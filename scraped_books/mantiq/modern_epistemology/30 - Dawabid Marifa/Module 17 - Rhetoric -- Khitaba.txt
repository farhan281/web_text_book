The Definition and Characteristics of the Rhetorical Argument
Author defines the rhetorical argument and describes its features.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the rhetorical argument and distinguish it from other types of arguments based on its reliance on preponderant probability from the arguer's perspective.

Core Idea
Rhetorical arguments convince with probability, not certainty, and do not require the addressee's acceptance.

In this passage, the author defines al-hujja al-khatabiyya (the rhetorical argument) and explains its key features. Unlike demonstrative or dialectical arguments, the rhetorical argument does not compel acceptance; it only provides a preponderant probability (zann rajih) from the viewpoint of the arguer. Understanding this concept is crucial for analyzing arguments in fiqh, judicial rulings, and everyday life.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of the rhetorical argument
In the Arabic passage, find where the author first defines al-hujja al-khatabiyya. Note that it does not obligate the other party.
The definition starts with 'الحجة الخطابية: هي الحجة التي لا تلزم الطرف الآخر...'
2
Identify the key characteristic: preponderant probability
The author emphasizes that from the arguer's perspective, the argument yields a preponderant probability (zann rajih). This is not certainty and does not require the addressee's acceptance.
Contrast this with dialectical arguments that require the opponent's premises.
3
Recognize the scope of rhetorical arguments
The author lists several fields where rhetorical arguments are used: instructions, addresses, juristic schools, judicial rulings, scientific theories, political opinions, etc. This shows their pervasive role in practical reasoning.
Most life affairs rely on such probable arguments.
4
Understand the degrees of probability
Note that zann rajih has degrees from near doubt to near certainty. This nuance helps in evaluating the strength of rhetorical arguments.
The author does not enumerate all degrees but indicates a range.

The rhetorical argument: It is the argument that does not obligate the other party to accept it, but it provides him with a preponderant and acceptable probability, or it is based on probable premises, whether the addressee accepts them or not, and whether it provides him with a preponderant probability or not, but from the point of view of the arguer it provides a preponderant probability. This argument is suitable in instructions and addresses, and is suitable for convincing with the viewpoint of the arguer, or for convincing of his excuse in what he adopts from a juristic school, or a judicial ruling, or what he concludes from a scientific theory, or what he decides from a political, administrative, or social opinion, or other matters of life. This rhetorical argument is presented in any method of direct or indirect inference, and its examples are found in all fields of thought. Most affairs of life and human issues rely on rhetorical arguments based on premises that contain a preponderant probability from the point of view of the one who argues with them; this probability does not reach the level of certainty, nor is it well-known among people as certainty is well-known, and it is not required that the addressee accept its premises or not. Juristic rulings, judicial rulings, and material scientific theories, most of them rely on arguments that are nothing more than rhetorical arguments, i.e., arguments that rely on adopting the preponderant probability. The preponderant probability has degrees that have not been counted, the lowest of which is close to doubt, and the highest is close to certainty. Among the rhetorical arguments mentioned in the Qur'an is an argument added to the demonstrative and dialectical arguments concerning the oneness of divinity (tawhid al-uluhiyya). The rhetorical argument on this subject is contained in the saying of Allah Almighty in Surah Ar-Rum (30): 'He strikes for you an example from yourselves: Do you have among your slaves partners in what We have provided you, so that you are equal in it, fearing them as you fear yourselves? Key Terms
الحجة الخطابية (al-hujja al-khatabiyya): An argument based on probable premises that yields a preponderant probability (zann rajih) for the arguer, without compelling the addressee. [Note: In Aristotelian logic, rhetoric is one of the five arts; here it is broadened to include everyday reasoning in fiqh and practical affairs.]
الظن الراجح (al-zann al-rajih): A preponderant probability that is more likely true than false, ranging from near-doubt to near-certainty. [Note: In usul al-fiqh, zann rajih is a technical term for a predominant probability used in ijtihad; here it is applied to rhetorical arguments.]
توحيد الألوهية (tawhid al-uluhiyya): The oneness of divinity, the belief that only Allah is worthy of worship, which is the subject of the rhetorical argument.

The Quranic Rhetorical Analogy on Tawhid al-Uluhiyya
Author provides a Quranic rhetorical argument against shirk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the analogical rhetorical argument in Surah Ar-Rum 30:28 and explain how it serves to disprove shirk.

Core Idea
The Quranic analogy refutes shirk by appealing to the logical inconsistency of accepting partners you would not accept among yourselves.

After defining the rhetorical argument, the author provides an example from Surah Ar-Rum (30:28) where Allah uses an analogy to challenge the polytheists. The verse strikes an example from human experience: just as you would not accept your own slaves as equal partners in your wealth, so how can you accept partners with Allah? This is a powerful rhetorical argument because it uses a universally accepted premise to undermine shirk.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic verse
Locate the verse in the passage: ﴿ضَرَبَ لَكُم مَّثَلًا مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ...﴾. Understand the structure: it begins with 'He strikes for you an example from yourselves'.
The verse is a complete ayah.
2
Identify the analogy (tamthil)
The verse compares two situations: (a) your relationship with your slaves and (b) your relationship with Allah's partners. The implicit argument is that if you would not accept slaves as partners, then you should not accept other beings as partners with Allah.
This is an analogical argument (qiyas tamthili).
3
Extract the premises and conclusion
The premise is that humans do not consider their slaves as equal partners in wealth. The conclusion is that it would be absurd to consider other beings as partners with Allah. The conclusion is not explicitly stated but implied.
The author's summary begins but is cut off; we can infer the conclusion.
4
Assess why it is rhetorical
The argument relies on a preponderant probability (zann rajih) that the polytheists would accept the first premise, but it does not compel them. It appeals to their common sense and sense of fairness.
This differs from a demonstrative argument which would use certain premises.

What Is a Rhetorical Argument? Definition and Quranic Example
Author defines rhetorical argument and illustrates with Quranic example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a rhetorical argument and distinguish it from demonstrative and dialectical arguments using the criterion of predominant opinion.

Core Idea
Rhetorical arguments persuade through predominant opinion, not certainty.

In Islamic logic, arguments are classified by their epistemic strength. The rhetorical argument (al-hujja al-khitabiyya) is the most common in daily life and even in scholarly disciplines like fiqh. This lesson defines its nature and illustrates it with a powerful Quranic analogy.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of rhetorical argument
Read the passage's opening: a rhetorical argument is used to convince based on an excuse or position in various fields. Note that it can be presented through any method of reasoning, direct or indirect.
Rhetorical arguments are not confined to a single form; they are defined by their purpose and basis.
2
Identify the role of predominant opinion (zann rajih)
The passage states that rhetorical arguments rely on premises containing a predominant opinion from the arguer's perspective. This opinion does not reach certainty, nor is it widely known. Recognize that this is the key distinguishing feature.
Predominant opinion (zann rajih) is a technical term in usul al-fiqh, meaning a probability that outweighs its opposite.
3
Recognize the range of certainty in rhetorical arguments
The passage notes that predominant opinion has degrees from near-doubt to near-certainty. Understanding this spectrum helps assess the strength of a rhetorical argument.
Not all rhetorical arguments are equally strong; their persuasiveness depends on how close the opinion is to certainty.
4
Apply the definition to the Quranic example
Analyze the verse from Surah Ar-Rum (30:28). Allah draws an analogy: just as you would not accept your slaves as partners, why do you associate partners with Allah? This is a rhetorical argument because it relies on a familiar comparison (an example from yourselves) to produce a predominant opinion that shirk is unreasonable.
The argument is not demonstrative (strict proof) but persuasive through analogy and common sense.
5
Contrast rhetorical with demonstrative and dialectical arguments
The passage mentions that this rhetorical argument is 'added to demonstrative and dialectical arguments.' Demonstrative arguments seek certainty, dialectical arguments rely on widely accepted premises. Rhetorical arguments, by contrast, rely on subjective predominant opinion. Categorize examples from Islamic disciplines accordingly.
This distinction is crucial in evaluating the epistemic weight of arguments in fiqh, kalam, and tafsir.

نظر صاحب الحجة، أو للإقناع بعذره فيما ذهب إليه من مذهب فقهي، أو حكم قضائي، أو فيما انتهى إليه من نظرية علمية، أو فيما قرره من رأي سياسي، أو إداري، أو اجتماعي، أو غير ذلك من شؤون الحياة. وتُقدَّم هذه الحجة الخطابية في أي طريق من طرق الاستدلال المباشر أو غير المباشر، وتُوجد أمثلتها في كل مجالات الفكر. ومعظم شؤون الحياة وقضايا الإنسان، تعتمد على الحجج الخطابية المستندة إلى مقدمات تشتمل على ظن راجح من وجهة نظر من يحتج بها؛ وهذا الظن لا يبلغ مبلغ اليقين، وليس مشهوراً بين الناس كشهرة اليقين، ولا يشترط فيه أن يكون المخاطب مسلماً بمقدماته أو غير مسلم بها. والأحكام الفقهية، والأحكام القضائية، والنظريات العلمية المادية، معظمها يستند إلى حجج لا تزيد عن كونها من قبيل الحجج الخطابية، أي الحجج التي تعتمد على الأخذ بالظن الراجح. والظن الراجح درجات لم تحصر، أدناها قريب من الشك، وأعلاها قريب من اليقين. ومن الحجج الخطابية الواردة في القرآن، حجة مضافة إلى الحجج البرهانية والحجج الجدلية حول توحيد الألوهية. والحجة الخطابية في هذا الموضوع ما تضمنه قول الله تعالى في سورة (الروم ٣٠): ﴿ضَرَبَ لَكُم مَّثَلًا مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ هَل لَّكُم مِّن مَّا مَلَكَتْ أَيْمَنُكُم مِّن شُرَكَاءَ فِي مَا رَزَقْنَكُمْ فَأَنتُمْ فِيهِ سَوَاءٌ تَخَافُونَهُمْ كَخِيفَتِكُمْ أَنفُسَكُمْ كَذَلِكَ نُفَصِّلُ الْآيَاتِ لِقَوْمٍ يَعْقِلُونَ ﴾. وتقرير هذه الحجة يتلخص بما يلي: أيها المشركون الذين تشركون بالله خلقاً من خلقه، وعبيداً من عبيده، فتجعلونهم آلهة تعبدونهم من دون الله، هل ترضون مثل ذلك لأنفسكم، فيما بينكم وبين ما تملكون من أرقاء؟ هل ترضون أن يكون عبيدكم شركاء لكم فيما تملكون من أشياء ينازعونكم فيها؟ هل تخافونهم كخيفتكم أنفسكم، فتستسلمون لمشاركتهم؟ . إذا كنتم لا ترضون شيئاً من ذلك لأنفسكم، لمنافاته مرتبة كمالكم في تصوركم، ولغضه من سلطانكم، أفترضون مثله لبارئكم، الذي هو خالقكم ومالككم، وخالق ما تشركونه به ومالكه؟ لو قسمتم الله على أنفسكم لرفضتم أن تجعلوا لله شريكاً، فتعالى الله عن ذلك علواً كبيراً.
The view of the author of the argument, or to convince with his excuse regarding the juristic school he followed, or a judicial ruling, or a scientific theory he arrived at, or a political, administrative, or social opinion he determined, and other such matters of life. And this rhetorical argument is presented in any method of direct or indirect reasoning, and its examples are found in all fields of thought. Most matters of life and human issues depend on rhetorical arguments based on premises that contain a predominant opinion from the perspective of the one who argues with them; and this predominant opinion does not reach the level of certainty, nor is it well-known among people like the fame of certainty, and it is not required that the addressee accepts its premises or not. Fiqh rulings, judicial rulings, and material scientific theories—most of them are based on arguments that are no more than rhetorical arguments, i.e., arguments that rely on adopting predominant opinion. Predominant opinion has degrees that are not limited, the lowest of which is close to doubt, and the highest of which is close to certainty. Among the rhetorical arguments found in the Qur'an is an argument added to the demonstrative and dialectical arguments regarding the oneness of divinity. And the rhetorical argument on this topic is what is contained in the saying of Allah Almighty in Surah (Ar-Rum 30): 'He strikes for you an example from yourselves. Do you have among those your right hands possess any partners in what We have provided for you so that you are equal therein? Do you fear them as you fear yourselves? Thus do We detail the signs for a people who reason.' And the presentation of this argument is summarized as follows: O polytheists who associate partners with Allah among His creation and His servants, making them gods worshipped besides Allah—do you accept such a thing for yourselves regarding what you own of slaves? Do you accept that your slaves be partners with you in what you own, disputing with you? Do you fear them as you fear yourselves, so you submit to their partnership? If you do not accept any of that for yourselves, because it contradicts your perceived perfection and diminishes your authority, would you accept the like for your Creator, who is your Creator and Owner, and the Creator and Owner of what you associate with Him? If you applied the standard to yourselves, you would refuse to make Allah a partner. Exalted is Allah above that, a great exaltation.

Commentary
This passage defines the rhetorical argument (al-hujja al-khitabiyya) and distinguishes it from other types. The author begins by stating that a rhetorical argument is used to convince based on an excuse or a position taken in various fields: jurisprudence, judiciary, scientific theories, politics, administration, social issues, etc. It can be presented through direct or indirect reasoning. The key characteristic is that its premises are based on predominant opinion (zann rajih) from the perspective of the arguer. This predominant opinion does not reach certainty, is not universally known, and does not require the audience's prior acceptance. The author notes that most fiqh rulings, judicial rulings, and scientific theories rely on such arguments. He then explains that predominant opinion has degrees ranging from near-doubt to near-certainty. Finally, he provides a Quranic example from Surah Ar-Rum (30:28), where Allah uses an analogy: just as people would not accept equality with their slaves, how can they associate partners with Allah? This is a rhetorical argument meant to persuade through a familiar comparison.

Key Takeaway
Rhetorical arguments are distinguished by their reliance on predominant opinion (zann rajih), which ranges from near-doubt to near-certainty, and they are the most common type of argument in practical life and Islamic jurisprudence.
