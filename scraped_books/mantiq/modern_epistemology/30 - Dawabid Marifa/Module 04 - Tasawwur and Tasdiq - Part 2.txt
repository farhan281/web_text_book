Distinguishing Theoretical and Intuitive Certainty
Author divides tasdiq into theoretical and intuitive and defines each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between theoretical and intuitive certainty and classify any given proposition as one of the two types.

Core Idea
Certainty is either earned through proof or self-evident.

Our knowledge is not all of the same kind. Some truths require a chain of reasoning to be affirmed, while others are immediately recognized as true. The Islamic logical tradition captures this distinction with the terms tasdiq nazari (theoretical certainty) and tasdiq badihi (intuitive certainty). In this lesson, you will learn to parse this foundational classification and apply it to examples from Kalam and geometry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions of tasdiq
Read the first two sections of the Arabic. The author explicitly labels 'al-qism al-awwal' (theoretical) and 'al-qism al-thani' (intuitive). Note the defining condition for each: theoretical requires investigation and inference, intuitive requires none.
2
Analyze the examples of theoretical certainty
Look at the examples given: 'the world is temporally originated', 'the Creator is eternal', 'sum of angles of a triangle equals two right angles'. Notice they are from cosmology, theology, and geometry—all requiring rational demonstration.
3
Understand the role of the reminder (tanbih)
The author says intuitive certainty may need only a tanbih—a reminder for someone unaware of its obviousness. This is not a proof; it simply removes obscurity. The footnote reinforces that intuitive truths need no proofs.
Tanbih is a compound (murakkab) meant to alert, not prove.
4
Apply the distinction to a new example
Now test a proposition like 'the whole is greater than its part'. Would this be theoretical or intuitive? According to the criteria, it is intuitive because the mind affirms it without inference. Compare with 'the world has a creator' which might be theoretical for some.

The Seven Types of Intuitive Certainty
Author subdivides intuitive certainty into seven types, first into clear and hidden.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven subtypes of intuitive certainty and classify each as clear or hidden.

Core Idea
Intuitive certainty is not monolithic—it has seven shades of obviousness.

The author does not stop at the simple dichotomy of theoretical vs. intuitive. He then reveals that intuitive certainty (tasdiq badihi) itself is subdivided into clear (jali) and hidden (khafi) categories, from which seven specific types branch. In this lesson, we will parse this taxonomy and understand what each type means, using examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main branches of intuitive certainty
From the tree diagram in the Arabic, note that badihi is first divided into jali (clear) and khafi (hidden). The clear type needs no reminder at all; the hidden type may need a reminder.
2
List the four types of clear intuitive certainty
The text enumerates: awwali (primary evident), fitri (innate), tajribi (empirical), and mushrik (shared among common people). Each has a specific meaning in Islamic epistemology.
For example, 'awwali' refers to first principles like 'the whole is greater than its part'.
3
List the three types of hidden intuitive certainty
The hidden types are: hadsi (intuitive), mutawatir (recurrently reported), and wijdani (sensory/emotional). These require a reminder to be activated.
Mutawatir is crucial in hadith studies: a report transmitted by so many narrators that it yields certain knowledge.
4
Match each type to a real Islamic example
For mutawatir, think of the knowledge of the Prophet's (SAW) pilgrimage rites—transmitted by countless companions. For hadsi, consider the geometer's ability to immediately see a principle. Try to assign an example to each of the seven.

The Primary Evident: Definition and Examples
Author defines awwali as propositions self-evident upon mere conception of terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a jali awwali proposition and distinguish it from propositions that require inference.

Core Idea
Awwali truths are known immediately upon understanding the terms — no intermediary needed.

In this lesson, we examine the first subtype of clear intuitive certainty: al-jali al-awwali. This type of proposition forms the foundation of all demonstrative knowledge because it is accepted by the mind directly, without any mediating concept or syllogism. The author defines it and provides classic examples to illustrate its self-evident nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining condition
The passage states: 'يحكم العقل بثبوت محمولها لموضوعها... بمجرد تصورهما'. This means the mind judges the proposition upon mere conception of its terms.
2
Recognize the absence of an intermediary
Key phrase: 'من غير احتياج إلى واسطة أصلاً'. There is no middle term or concept needed.
3
Analyze the examples
The examples: 'الواحد نصف الاثنين', 'الكل أكبر من الجزء', 'النقيضان لا يجتمعان ولا يرتفعان'. For each, note that the predicate is immediately seen to be true from the subject's meaning.
4
Test with a counterexample
Consider a proposition like 'النار حارة' (fire is hot). Is it awwali? It may require experience, not mere conception. So it is not.

Understanding al-Jali al-Fitri: Innate Evident Certainty
Author defines fitri as propositions known through an innate mental syllogism without conscious reflection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify propositions that are fitri (innate evident) and distinguish them from awwali (primary) propositions by recognizing the hidden innate syllogism.

Core Idea
Fitri knowledge is certain but requires an automatic syllogism, unlike purely intuitive axioms.

In the previous lesson, we learned about awwali (primary) propositions which are known directly without any mediation. Now, the author introduces a second type of clear intuitive certainty: al-jali al-fitri. These propositions are also certain, but they depend on an innate syllogism that runs subconsciously. This lesson unpacks that mechanism, showing how the mind arrives at judgements like 'four is even' and 'man is composite' through an automatic reasoning process.

Guided Path
1
Contrast fitri with awwali
Recognize that awwali needs no reasoning, while fitri involves a hidden syllogism. The author explicitly states that fitri is known 'by means of an innate logical syllogism'.
This is the key differentia.
2
Analyze the example 'four is even'
Identify the two premises: 'Evenness is every number divisible into two equal parts' and 'Four is a number divisible into two equal parts'. The mind automatically combines these to conclude 'Four is even'.
The syllogism is not consciously summoned; it runs instinctively.
3
Examine the example 'man is composite'
Similarly, the premise 'Composite is that which has parts' and 'Man has parts' leads automatically to 'Man is composite'.
This shows the universality of fitri reasoning.
4
Note the automaticity requirement
The author emphasizes that this syllogism does 'not require summoning or reflection'. It is part of the innate mental disposition.
If conscious effort is needed, it is not fitri but theoretical.

Commentary
This passage explains the second subtype of clear intuitive certainty: al-jali al-fitri (the innate evident). Unlike the awwali, fitri propositions require a mediating syllogism, but this syllogism is innate (fitri) and runs automatically without conscious effort or deliberation. The author gives two examples: 'Four is even' and 'Man is composite.' In the first, the mind implicitly knows the definition of evenness (divisible into two equal parts) and sees that four satisfies it, thus concluding 'four is even' instantly. In the second, knowing that man has parts, and that a composite is something composed of parts, the mind immediately infers that man is composite. The key point is that this syllogism is not summoned deliberately; it occurs by natural mental disposition. This distinguishes fitri from propositions that require explicit reasoning. In Islamic logic, fitri propositions form an important class of intuitive knowledge, often used in theological arguments. For instance, the argument for the existence of a Creator from the existence of the world may be considered fitri by some scholars.

Repeated experience yields self-evident judgments without causal reasoning.

In the Islamic logical tradition, self-evident propositions (badihiyyat) include those based on repeated sensory experience. This category, called al-mutakarrara, is crucial for understanding how empirical knowledge becomes certain. The passage below defines it and provides everyday examples, while also noting a scholarly debate about its status.

Distinguish from other badihiyyat

Types of Assent in Debate: Theoretical, Hidden Self-evident, Clear Self-evident
Author explains three types of assent in debate and their procedural implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between theoretical, hidden self-evident, and clear self-evident assents in debate, and identify the appropriate response for each.

Core Idea
In debate, clear self-evident truths must be accepted; hidden self-evident truths need a reminder, not a proof.

In Islamic dialectical tradition (munazara), not all propositions are equally open to challenge. This passage from the Dawabit al-Ma'rifa explains that debate properly occurs only about theoretical assents and, by extension, hidden self-evident assents that have been neglected. Clear self-evident assents, however, are beyond dispute. Understanding this classification is essential for knowing when a proof (dalil) is required and when a simple reminder (tanbih) suffices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of assent

The passage contrasts the response to an objection for each type.

Distinguish the response for each type
For theoretical assent, the mu'allil must provide a proof (dalil). For hidden self-evident, he must give a reminder (tanbih). For clear self-evident, no response is allowed because the proposition cannot be challenged.
The reminder points out the self-evident nature, not a logical proof.
