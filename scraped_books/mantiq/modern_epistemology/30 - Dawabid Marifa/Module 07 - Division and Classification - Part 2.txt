Rational Division: Logical Necessity in Disjunction
Author defines rational division with example and notes on contradictory equivalents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a rational division in a logical text by recognizing its disjunctive form and distinguishing between the proper contradictory (naqid) and its equivalent.

Core Idea
Rational division excludes other possibilities by logical necessity alone.

In logic, some divisions are rationally necessary: the divided concept must fall into one of two contradictory categories. This lesson explains how rational division is expressed and why even equivalents like 'odd' for 'not even' can be used.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction form
Look for the phrase 'إما ... وإما ...' (either ... or ...) in the Arabic. Rational division always uses this structure between affirmation and negation.
The disjunction must be between contradictory opposites (naqid) or their equivalents.
2
Locate the contradictory pair
Determine if the second term is the exact logical contradictory (naqid) of the first, e.g., 'even' vs 'not even'. If not, check if it is an equivalent (musawi al-naqid) like 'odd' for 'not even'.
The author notes that 'odd' is not the contradictory but is treated as equal for practical purposes.
3
Confirm no other division is logically possible
Rational division excludes any third possibility by the law of excluded middle. Reason alone suffices to enforce the division.
This distinguishes it from inductive division, which requires external observation.

The principle in rational division is that it is presented in the form of disjunction between affirmation and negation, like: 'Number is either even or not', considering that negation is the contradictory of affirmation. But sometimes what is equivalent to the contradictory is presented, so one says for example: 'Number is either even or odd', for odd is in reality not the contradictory, but it is equivalent to the contradictory. Inductive division: it is what does not prevent, by reason alone, the existence of another division besides what is mentioned in the division, but when one looks at the existing divisions actually in external existence, based on what sound induction establishes, then reason judges the non-existence of any other division. So reason's judgment about the restriction of the divided to its divisions here is dependent on what the induction of reality in external existence establishes, and is not based on its abstract view without consideration of external existence. Examples: 1. The word is divided into three divisions: noun, verb, and particle. Abstract reason does not preclude the existence of a fourth division, but induction of the reality of Arabic speech in external existence established the restriction of the word to these three divisions. 2. The planets subordinate to the sun are nine: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, Pluto. Reason does not preclude the existence of another planet or planets, but induction of the reality of the subordinates of the sun in external existence established their restriction to these nine planets, and a tenth planet is added between Mars and Jupiter, except that it exploded and became small scattered masses. The principle in inductive division is that it is not presented in the form of disjunction between affirmation and negation, assigning this form exclusively to rational division.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of division in logic: rational ('aqli) and inductive (istiqra'i). Rational division is expressed as a disjunction between affirmation and negation, for example 'a number is either even or not even'. The negation here is the proper contradictory (naqid). Sometimes something equivalent to the contradictory is used, like 'odd' instead of 'not even', because odd is not the contradictory but its equivalent. Inductive division does not rely on reason alone to exclude other divisions; rather, it looks at what actually exists externally based on sound induction. Reason then judges that no other division exists because induction has shown it. Two examples are given: (1) The word in Arabic is divided into noun, verb, and particle. Reason alone could allow a fourth type, but induction of actual Arabic speech shows only these three. (2) The nine planets (including Pluto, then considered a planet) are listed; reason does not preclude others, but induction shows only these nine. A note adds that a tenth planet between Mars and Jupiter exploded into asteroids. The key principle: inductive division should not be phrased as a disjunction; that form is reserved for rational division.

Inductive Division: Empirical Categories in Logic
Author defines inductive division with two examples and a principle about its phrasing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an inductive division by its reliance on empirical observation rather than a priori necessity, and explain why it should not be phrased as a disjunction.

Core Idea
Inductive division depends on what actually exists, not on pure reason.

Not all divisions are logically necessary. Some are discovered by examining the real world. This lesson introduces inductive division, where reason defers to induction (istiqra') to determine the actual categories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim of division
Look for a statement that divides a concept into specific parts, e.g., 'the word is divided into noun, verb, and particle'.
The division may seem comprehensive, but we must check its basis.
2
Note that reason alone does not preclude other divisions
The author emphasizes that 'abstract reason does not preclude the existence of a fourth division'. This is the hallmark of inductive division.
Contrast with rational division where reason forces only two options.
3
Find the evidence from induction
The text says 'induction of the reality of Arabic speech in external existence established the restriction'. Look for the empirical basis.
The division is accepted because observation shows no other categories exist.
4
Check that it is NOT phrased as a disjunction
The author explicitly states that inductive division should not be presented as a disjunction between affirmation and negation. That form is reserved for rational division.
If you see 'إما ... وإما ...' in a division, it is likely rational, not inductive.

Objections to Division of Whole into Parts
First category of objections: division of whole into parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and formulate objections to a division of a whole into parts based on the conditions of exhaustiveness and mutual exclusivity.

Core Idea
A valid division of a whole requires covering all parts and excluding all non-parts.

In logical debates, after a division is presented, the opponent (mustadill) can challenge its validity. The first category deals with dividing a whole into its parts. Two key conditions must hold: the division must be exhaustive (حصریت) and the parts must be mutually exclusive (تباین). This lesson explores the two corresponding objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the whole and its parts
Determine what is being divided (the whole) and what the proposed parts are. For example, 'human' divided into 'body' and 'soul'.
2
Check exhaustiveness
Does the division include all parts of the whole? Or does it allow anything that is not a part to be included? Both are violations of exhaustiveness.
Non-exhaustive can be due to missing a part (not جامع) or including a non-part (not مانع).
3
Check mutual exclusivity
Are the parts distinct from each other? Is any part not distinct from the whole? If a part is not distinct (i.e., it is essentially the same as the whole), the division fails.
The condition also requires that no part overlaps with another part.
4
Formulate the objection
State which condition is violated and how. For example: 'The division is non-exhaustive because it omits part X' or 'The parts are not mutually exclusive because part A and part B overlap.'

Objections to Division of Universal into Particulars
Second category: three objections to division of universal into particulars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and formulate the three types of objections to a division of a universal into its particulars.

Core Idea
A universal must be divided into particulars that are exhaustive, absolutely more specific, and mutually exclusive.

The second category of objections applies when a universal (كلي) is divided into its particulars (جزئيات). The conditions are stricter: the division must be exhaustive, each particular must be absolutely more specific than the universal, and the particulars must be mutually exclusive. This lesson unpacks the three corresponding objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal and its particulars
Determine the universal (e.g., 'animal') and the proposed particulars (e.g., 'human', 'horse').
2
Check exhaustiveness again
Is the division exhaustive? For rational divisions, the opponent can propose another mentally possible particular. For inductive divisions, the opponent can give an actual existing particular. Also, if one of the proposed particulars is not actually a particular of the universal (i.e., it does not fall under it), then the division is not exclusive (non-mani').
Non-exhaustive here can be omission or inclusion of a non-particular.
3
Check absolute specificity
Each particular must be absolutely more specific (أخص مطلقاً) than the universal. That is, every instance of the particular is an instance of the universal, but not vice versa. If a particular is coextensive with the universal or broader, it fails.
E.g., 'human' is absolutely more specific than 'animal'; but 'rational animal' might be equal to human, so it fails as a particular of 'animal' if 'animal' is already a universal.
4
Check mutual exclusivity
The particulars must be mutually exclusive (متباينة). No individual can belong to two different particulars. Overlap invalidates the division.
E.g., 'human' and 'horse' are mutually exclusive; 'human' and 'rational animal' are not.

Responding to Objections in Taqseem
Author explains the dialectical rules for taqseem objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules for responding to objections in taqseem within a munazara context, choosing the appropriate response based on the nature of the objection.

Core Idea
A flawed division can be rescued either by correction or by clarifying intent.

In scholarly disputation (munazara), divisions (taqseem) are common tools for clarifying concepts. But what happens when an objector points out a flaw? This lesson teaches you the two legitimate responses a proponent can make: either accept the objection and correct the division, or reject it by clarifying the intended meaning of key terms. You will also learn when the discussion shifts to debating new claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition violated
Look at the objection and determine which condition of a valid division is at stake: comprehensiveness (hasir), distinctness (tabayun), or proper specificity. For example, if the objector claims an overlap between two parts, the condition of tabayun is violated.
Refer to the footnote conditions in the passage.
2
Assess the proponent's first option: accept and correct
If the objection is valid and clearly shows a flaw, the proponent should agree and either modify the division or propose a new one that meets all conditions. This resolves the dispute.
This is the preferable response if the division is genuinely flawed.
3
Assess the proponent's second option: reject by clarifying
If the proponent believes the objection is based on a misunderstanding, he can reject it by clarifying his intended meaning of the divided thing (muqsam), the parts (aqsam), the division itself, or the doctrinal school (madhhab) underlying it. This may involve redefining terms or providing context.
The rejection must be substantive, not a mere denial.
4
Check for new claims from the objector
After the proponent's response, the objector's speech may contain unsupported claims (da'awa ghayr musallama). If so, the debate shifts from the division to those new claims, now requiring proofs for them. Recognize this shift.
This is a common escalation in munazara.

Two Types of Mu‘āraḍa: Targeting Evidence vs. Cause
Author begins classifying mu‘āraḍa into two types based on what it targets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mu‘āraḍa fī al-dalīl and mu‘āraḍa fī al-‘illa, and identify which type is being used in a given dialectical exchange.

Core Idea
Mu‘āraḍa attacks either the evidence or the cause – the two pillars of any argument.

In dialectical disputation (jadal/munazara), a questioner can oppose a proponent's argument in different ways. This passage introduces a fundamental classification: the opposition may target the very evidence (dalīl) presented, or it may target the underlying cause (‘illa) that links the evidence to the claim. Recognising which type is at play helps you evaluate the strength of the objection and craft an appropriate response. Let's explore this distinction with examples from Islamic legal theory and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of division
The passage divides mu‘āraḍa by what it is directed at. What two things can be targeted? Answer: the evidence (dalīl) or the cause (‘illa).
2
Define mu‘āraḍa fī al-dalīl
This type attacks the premises or the proof itself. For example, challenging the truth of a major premise in a syllogism, such as 'the world is temporally originated' in a kalam argument for God's existence.
Often called mu‘āraḍa bi-naqḍ al-dalīl in later texts.
3
Define mu‘āraḍa fī al-‘illa
This type attacks the causal link or rationale. In usul al-fiqh, for instance, one may argue that the ‘illa cited (e.g., intoxication) does not produce the claimed effect (prohibition) in all cases, or that the ‘illa is not present.
Crucial in qiyas debates.
4
Apply the distinction to a concrete case
Given a hypothetical debate: Proponent says 'Nabidh is forbidden because it intoxicates.' Opponent says 'Intoxication does not always entail prohibition; some intoxicants are permissible in small quantities.' Which type of mu‘āraḍa is this? Answer: mu‘āraḍa fī al-‘illa.
The opponent is attacking the causal link, not the evidence.
