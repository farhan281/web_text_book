Classification of Indications (Dalāla) – The Threefold Division
Author presents a tree diagram classifying indications into rational, conventional, and natural.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to draw the complete tree of indications and correctly classify any given example into its category.

Core Idea
All indications stem from reason, convention, or nature, and verbal indications have three modes.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational taxonomy of indications (dalāla) as presented in the summary tree. Understanding this classification is essential because every act of knowing in the Islamic sciences depends on signs — whether rational arguments, conventional word meanings, or natural effects. The tree shows how pure rational indication stands apart from conventional and natural, and how conventional verbal indication splits into correspondence, inclusion, and implication, with implication further divided into rational and conventional.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three root branches
Locate the top-level division: pure rational, conventional, and natural. Ask why each is distinct: pure rational depends solely on reason, conventional on human agreement, natural on causation.
2
Trace the conventional branch
Under conventional (waḍ‘iyya), note the split into verbal and non-verbal. Verbal indications are language-based; non-verbal include gestures and symbols.
3
Distinguish the three verbal modes
Under verbal, identify correspondence (muṭābaqiyya), inclusion (taḍammuniyya), and implication (iltizāmiyya). For each, think of an example: 'house' indicates the whole structure (correspondence), part of it (inclusion), and being a shelter (implication).
4
Understand the sub-branch of implication
Implication splits into rational and conventional. Rational implication is based on logic (e.g., a created thing implies a creator), conventional is based on cultural association (e.g., a turban implies a scholar).
5
Practice classification
Given an example like 'smoke indicates fire', determine which branch it belongs to (natural). Another: 'the word "sun" indicates the celestial body' – correspondence.

Commentary
This passage provides a summary tree of the division of indications (al-dalāla). The top-level division is into three: pure rational indication (dalāla ‘aqliyya baḥta), which is based solely on reason without any convention or natural connection; conventional indication (dalāla waḍ‘iyya), which depends on human convention; and natural indication (dalāla ṭabī‘iyya), like smoke indicating fire. Under conventional indication, it splits into verbal (lafẓiyya) and non-verbal (ghayr lafẓiyya). Under verbal, it splits into correspondence (muṭābaqiyya), where the meaning matches the word; inclusion (taḍammuniyya), where the word implies part of the meaning; and implication (iltizāmiyya), where the word implies something accompanying the meaning. Under implication, it splits into rational (‘aqliyya) and conventional (‘urfiyya). This tree is fundamental in logic for understanding how words convey meanings.

Key Terms
دلالة (dalāla): Indication; a sign that points to something else. [Note: Standard term in logic; in uṣūl al-fiqh it often refers to textual indication.]
عقلية بحتة (‘aqliyya baḥta): Pure rational indication: based on reason alone without convention or nature. [Note: Sometimes called 'dalāla ‘aqliyya' in kalām; here specified as 'baḥta' to exclude other types.]
وضعية (waḍ‘iyya): Conventional indication: based on human agreement or convention. [Note: Stable term.]
طبيعية (ṭabī‘iyya): Natural indication: based on natural causation like smoke indicating fire. [Note: Stable term.]
مطابقية (muṭābaqiyya): Correspondence indication: when the word indicates its entire meaning. [Note: Standard in logic.]
تضمنية (taḍammuniyya): Inclusion indication: when the word indicates part of its meaning. [Note: Standard.]
التزامية (iltizāmiyya): Implication indication: when the word indicates an accompanying meaning. [Note: Standard.]

The Definition of Contradiction
Definition of contradiction with conditions and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two conditions of contradiction (impossible to be both true together and impossible to be both false together) and correctly apply them to any pair of propositions in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Contradiction is the relation where two meanings cannot be both true nor both false together.

Contradiction (tanāquḍ) is one of the fundamental relations between meanings in logic. The author provides a precise definition that will help you distinguish genuine contradictions from mere opposites. Understanding this definition is crucial for constructing sound arguments and identifying fallacious ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
Look for the two conditions: (1) cannot be both true together, (2) cannot be both false together. The author explicitly states: 'لا يمكن اجتماعهما معاً' and 'لا يمكن ارتفاعهما معاً'.
These are jointly called the law of non-contradiction and the law of excluded middle.
2
Check the unity of subject and time
The definition specifies that these conditions apply 'في شيء واحد وزمان واحد' (in one thing and one time). This means the contradiction is relative to the same subject and aspect.
If the time or subject changes, the pair may not be contradictory.
3
Confirm the logical consequence
From the definition, we deduce that one must be true and the other false. The author says: 'فلا بد من وجود أحدهما أو ثبوت أحدهما وانتفاء الآخر.'
This is the hallmark of a contradiction.

The eleventh relation: (Contradiction). It is a relation between a meaning and another meaning with respect to the impossibility of them both coexisting together and the impossibility of them both being absent together, in one thing and one time. That is: they are opposed, so it is impossible for them to coexist in one thing at one time, and it is impossible for them both to be absent from that thing. So one of them must exist, or one of them must be established and the other negated. Examples: A - Like (existence and non-existence), for these two meanings are contradictory, and the relation between them is contradiction, because they do not coexist in one thing, nor are they both absent from that thing. A single thing by itself is either existent or non-existent. As for being existent and non-existent at the same time, that is rationally impossible; likewise it cannot be neither existent nor non-existent at the same time. Therefore they are contradictory and opposed: whenever one is established the other is negated, and whenever one is negated the other is established. B - Like (vision and no vision), these two are also contradictory meanings; the relation between them is contradiction. They do not coexist in one thing, nor are they both absent from that thing. A single thing by itself is either seeing or not seeing, with unity of aspect, time, and place. C - Like (life and no life), these two meanings are also contradictory, and the relation between them is contradiction, like the previous example. Note: The basis in contradiction is the use of the particle of negation in the contradictory, as came in the two examples: (vision and no vision) and (life and no life). If we use a word that conveys the same meaning without containing negation of the contradictory, then the logicians call it the equivalent of the contradictory, as came in the first example (existence and non-existence). For the two contradictories according to them are (existence and no existence), while the word (non-existence) is for them equivalent to the contradictory which is (no existence). And that is because verification of contradiction only appears between a thing and everything other than it, i.e., between a thing and its negation.

Commentary
This passage defines the eleventh logical relation, contradiction (tanāquḍ). The author begins by giving a precise definition: contradiction is the relation between two meanings such that they cannot both be simultaneously true of the same thing at the same time (social), nor can they both be simultaneously false (irtifā‘) – i.e., they cannot both be absent from that thing at that time. This means that for any given subject, one of the two must be true and the other false; there is no middle ground. The author then elaborates, explaining that the two are mutually opposed (mut‘ānidān). He provides three examples: (1) existence and non-existence, which are classic examples of contradictories in metaphysics; (2) vision and no-vision (e.g., a person is either seeing or not seeing); (3) life and no-life. These last two are phrased with the negation particle 'lā' directly attached to the positive term. The author then adds an important note (mulāḥaẓa): the proper form of a contradictory is a positive term and its negation with 'lā', like 'basar' and 'lā basar'. However, sometimes a single word like '‘adam' (non-existence) is used to mean the same as 'lā wujūd' (no existence). Logicians call such a word the 'musāwī al-naqīḍ' (equivalent of the contradictory). This is because the contradiction is essentially between a thing and its negation, not necessarily between two single words. This distinction is crucial to avoid conflating a simple negative term (e.g., ‘adam') with an actual contradictory consisting of a verb with a negation particle.

Examples of Contradiction in Logic
Three examples illustrating contradiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct and recognize examples of contradiction using the correct form: a positive term and its negation (or its equivalent).

Core Idea
The clearest way to express a contradiction is with a negation particle, but equivalent terms also work.

The author provides three concrete examples to illustrate contradiction: existence/non-existence, vision/no-vision, and life/no-life. These examples show how the definition applies across different domains. You will learn to identify the essential features that make these pairs contradictory.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the first example (existence/non-existence)
Notice that 'existence' and 'non-existence' are opposites. The author says they cannot coexist nor be both absent. This is a classic metaphysical contradiction.
Here 'non-existence' ('‘adam') is a single word, not a negation phrase.
2
Analyze the second and third examples (vision/no-vision, life/no-life)
These are formed with the negation particle 'lā'. Observe that 'no-vision' ('lā baṣar') is the contradictory of 'vision'. Similarly for life.
This is the standard form of contradiction in Arabic logic.
3
Extract the pattern
A contradiction consists of a positive term (S) and its negation (not-S). For any subject, S and not-S cannot both apply nor both fail to apply.
This applies to any predicate, not just these three examples.

Distinguishing True Contradictories from Their Equivalents
Note on the use of negation vs. equivalent terms in contradiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between a contradictory formed with a negation particle and an equivalent term (musāwī al-naqīḍ) that conveys the same meaning.

Core Idea
The true contradictory is a positive term and its negation; a word like '‘adam' is an equivalent, not the literal contradictory.

In the note, the author clarifies an important nuance: while the canonical form of contradiction uses the negation particle 'lā', sometimes a single word like '‘adam' stands in for the negation phrase. Logicians call such a word the 'equivalent of the contradictory' (musāwī al-naqīḍ). This distinction helps avoid confusion when analyzing arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the basis of contradiction in negation
The author states that the basis (al-aṣl) in contradiction is the use of the negation particle (ḥarf al-salb) to form the contradictory. For example, 'basar' and 'lā basar'.
Negation is the logical marker of contradiction.
2
Identify the equivalent term
When a word like '‘adam' is used without the negation particle but means the same as 'lā wujūd', logicians call it 'musāwī al-naqīḍ' (equivalent of the contradictory).
This term is not part of the original Aristotelian framework but is a later clarification.
3
Apply the distinction
Given a pair like (wujūd, ‘adam), recognize that the true contradictory is (wujūd, lā wujūd). The pair (wujūd, ‘adam) is still considered a contradiction because ‘adam is an equivalent.
This prevents confusion when interpreting texts that use such terms.

The eleventh relation: (Contradiction). It is a relation between a meaning and another meaning with respect to the impossibility of them both coexisting together and the impossibility of them both being absent together, in one thing and one time. That is: they are opposed, so it is impossible for them to coexist in one thing at one time, and it is impossible for them both to be absent from that thing. So one of them must exist, or one of them must be established and the other negated. Examples: A - Like (existence and non-existence), for these two meanings are contradictory, and the relation between them is contradiction, because they do not coexist in one thing, nor are they both absent from that thing. A single thing by itself is either existent or non-existent. As for being existent and non-existent at the same time, that is rationally impossible; likewise it cannot be neither existent nor non-existent at the same time. Therefore they are contradictory and opposed: whenever one is established the other is negated, and whenever one is negated the other is established. B - Like (vision and no vision), these two are also contradictory meanings; the relation between them is contradiction. They do not coexist in one thing, nor are they both absent from that thing. A single thing by itself is either seeing or not seeing, with unity of aspect, time, and place. C - Like (life and no life), these two meanings are also contradictory, and the relation between them is contradiction, like the previous example. Note: The basis in contradiction is the use of the particle of negation in the contradictory, as came in the two examples: (vision and no vision) and (life and no life). If we use a word that conveys the same meaning without containing negation of the contradictory, then the logicians call it the equivalent of the contradictory, as came in the first example (existence and non-existence). For the two contradictories according to them are (existence and no existence), while the word (non-existence) is for them equivalent to the contradictory which is (no existence). And that is because verification of contradiction only appears between a thing and everything other than it, i.e., between a thing and its negation.

Commentary
This passage defines the eleventh logical relation, contradiction (tanāquḍ). The author begins by giving a precise definition: contradiction is the relation between two meanings such that they cannot both be simultaneously true of the same thing at the same time (social), nor can they both be simultaneously false (irtifā‘) – i.e., they cannot both be absent from that thing at that time. This means that for any given subject, one of the two must be true and the other false; there is no middle ground. The author then elaborates, explaining that the two are mutually opposed (mut‘ānidān). He provides three examples: (1) existence and non-existence, which are classic examples of contradictories in metaphysics; (2) vision and no-vision (e.g., a person is either seeing or not seeing); (3) life and no-life. These last two are phrased with the negation particle 'lā' directly attached to the positive term. The author then adds an important note (mulāḥaẓa): the proper form of a contradictory is a positive term and its negation with 'lā', like 'basar' and 'lā basar'. However, sometimes a single word like '‘adam' (non-existence) is used to mean the same as 'lā wujūd' (no existence). Logicians call such a word the 'musāwī al-naqīḍ' (equivalent of the contradictory). This is because the contradiction is essentially between a thing and its negation, not necessarily between two single words. This distinction is crucial to avoid conflating a simple negative term (e.g., ‘adam') with an actual contradictory consisting of a verb with a negation particle.

Distinguishing Luzumiyya from Ittifaqiyya
Divides conditionals into necessary and accidental.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given conditional proposition as either luzumiyya (necessary) or ittifaqiyya (accidental) by checking whether the connection between antecedent and consequent is grounded in a real-world relation.

Core Idea
A conditional is luzumiyya if the consequent really follows from the antecedent; otherwise it is ittifaqiyya.

In the study of conditional propositions, logicians distinguish between those where the implication is necessary and those where it is merely coincidental. This distinction is crucial because only necessary conditionals can be used in valid logical inferences. The passage divides conditional propositions into two types: luzumiyya and ittifaqiyya, based on whether there is a real relation between the antecedent and consequent.

Guided Path
1
Understand the criterion
Look at the connection between the antecedent (muqaddam) and consequent (tali). Ask: Is there a real-world relation that necessitates the implication?
2
Identify luzumiyya
If the truth of the antecedent genuinely brings about the truth of the consequent (e.g., causation), it is luzumiyya. Example: 'If cold winds pass over saturated clouds, rain falls.'
3
Identify ittifaqiyya
If the conjunction is merely coincidental with no real relation, it is ittifaqiyya. Example: 'If the sun rises, then I wake up' — but I could wake up earlier.
4
Practice with Islamic examples
Apply the criterion to Islamic science examples, such as 'If nabidh is consumed, intoxication occurs' (luzumiyya) vs. 'If it is Friday, then the market is crowded' (ittifaqiyya).

Three Types of Real Relations in Luzumiyya
Explains three types of necessary conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the three relations (sabab, musabbab, 'illa) is present in a necessary conditional (luzumiyya) by analyzing the causal direction between antecedent and consequent.

Core Idea
The necessity in a luzumiyya conditional can arise from three causal patterns: antecedent as cause, antecedent as effect, or antecedent as determinant.

The passage goes beyond simply defining luzumiyya; it elaborates on three distinct types of real relations that can ground the necessity. Understanding these types deepens your ability to analyze the logical structure of conditionals, especially in Islamic sciences where causal relations are frequently invoked in arguments from Kalam to Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of luzumiyya
It requires a real relation between antecedent (muqaddam) and consequent (tali).
2
Identify the first relation: antecedent as sabab (cause)
The antecedent directly produces the consequent. Example from passage: cold wind causes rain. In Islamic terms, 'If nabidh is consumed, intoxication occurs' is of this type.
3
Identify the second relation: antecedent as musabbab (effect)
The antecedent is the effect of the consequent, meaning the consequent is the cause. Example from passage: rain indicates prior cooling. Example: 'If the ground is wet, then it rained.'
4
Identify the third relation: antecedent as 'illa (determinant cause)
The antecedent is the determinant cause that necessitates the consequent's existence. Example from passage: airplane's motion determines passengers' motion. Example: 'If the imam recites aloud, the followers listen.'
The difference between 'illa and sabab is subtle; 'illa often implies a logical or necessary connection, while sabab is more efficient causal.
5
Distinguish between the three
In sabab, antecedent → consequent; in musabbab, antecedent ← consequent; in 'illa, antecedent is the reason for consequent. Practice by identifying the direction.

In categorical logic, when one term is more general than another, the rules of predication determine which propositions are true. The Three Rational Judgments: Possible, Impossible, Necessary
Author classifies rational judgments and illustrates the possible with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish rationally possible from impossible and necessary, and identify whether a given state of affairs is rationally possible or merely contrary to custom.

Core Idea
Rational possibility is about logical non-contradiction, not about what usually happens.

In Islamic logic and theology, judgments about reality are not merely based on observation. The intellect can classify any conceived state into three categories: necessary, impossible, and possible. The possible is the broadest category – it includes everything that is not self-contradictory, even if it never occurs. This lesson will help you understand these categories and apply them to theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rational judgments
Read the first part of the passage. The author states that everything the intellect conceives falls into one of three categories: possible in existence and non-existence (mumkin), impossible in existence (mustaḥīl), or necessary in existence (wājib). Understand the defining feature of each: the possible can be conceived as existing or not existing; the impossible cannot be conceived as existing; the necessary cannot be conceived as non-existing.
2
Define the rationally possible (al-mumkin)
The passage defines the possible as 'that which the intellect accepts the possibility of its existence and non-existence, even in a state among the states that the mind conceives, under certain conditions, and according to special systems.' This means that for something to be possible, the intellect must be able to imagine it existing and also imagine it not existing without contradiction. Note the synonym 'al-jāʾiz'.
3
Analyze each example: distinguish rational from customary
For each of the five examples (human existence, fire burning, resurrection after death, traversing distances instantly, lifting a mountain), identify what is rationally possible according to the intellect. Then note the customary obstacle or observed regularity that makes it seem impossible. The key is to see that the intellect finds no logical contradiction in these events, even if they don't happen in everyday experience.
Example 2 on fire is especially important: the author explicitly says the intellect sees no necessary connection between fire and burning; it is purely from observation and the Organizer's grant.
4
Understand the role of sufficient power (al-quwwa al-mukāfiʾa)
In examples 3-5, the author mentions that for a rationally possible event to occur, a sufficient power must exist. This concept is crucial for kalām arguments about divine omnipotence: anything rationally possible can be brought about by God's power. Even if we have not observed it, it is not impossible in itself.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and definition of attributes (a'rad). The author begins with the physical observation that everything in the universe is in motion, and apparent rest is relative. This sets the stage for discussing the metaphysical concepts of conjunction and separation of two substances: if they meet with no third substance between them, it is conjunction; otherwise separation. The author then notes that modern science shows atoms have voids, so true conjunction is rare; what we see is proximity.

Next, the author lists general accidents common to many bodies: composition, heaviness, lightness, heat, cold, dryness, softness, moisture, color, odor, energy, hardness, flexibility, resistance, etc. These are qualities that can be analyzed by reflection.

Then the author turns specifically to living beings (al-ahya'), enumerating ten categories with definitions:
1. Hayat (Life): a power requiring sensation and movement.
2. Qudra (Power): an existential attribute affecting action according to will.
3. Irada (Will): attribute by which the agent prefers to do or leave an act.
4. Karahiya (Aversion): the soul's repulsion from something deemed harmful or ugly.
5. I'tiqad (Belief): a decisive judgment that can change; correct if matching reality, corrupt otherwise.
6. Zann (Conjecture): preferring one side of a judgment when it is preponderant; it has degrees down to illusion.
7. Tafakkur (Reflection): mental process leading to knowledge, belief, or conjecture.
8. Alam (Pain): perception of what nature dislikes.
9. Ladhdha (Pleasure): perception of what is suitable.
10. A list of further attributes: health, illness, joy, sadness, shame, dread, anger, love, satisfaction, hope, fear.

The author systematically defines each term, ensuring precise conceptual boundaries for use in logic and other sciences.
