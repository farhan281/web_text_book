The Poetic Argument: Definition and Function
Author defines poetic argument and gives an example from al-Ghazali.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a poetic argument and distinguish it from rhetorical and demonstrative arguments.

Core Idea
Poetic arguments move the soul, not the mind.

In this lesson, we explore a unique type of argument used in poetry and oratory. Unlike arguments that aim at truth, poetic arguments aim at emotional effect, even using false premises. The passage defines this and gives an example from al-Ghazali.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
What is being defined? The term is 'al-hujja al-shi'riyya' (poetic argument).
2
Extract the genus
It belongs to the broader category 'argument' (hujja).
All hujaj aim to convince, but poetic arguments do so through emotion.
3
Identify the differentia
What distinguishes it? It does not require certainty or predominant opinion; it relies on imaginary premises and manipulates emotions.
This contrasts with demonstrative (yaqini) and rhetorical (zanni) arguments.
4
Note the intended effect
The argument stirs emotions like desire, fear, attraction, or aversion, even if the addressee knows it is false.
The goal is emotional response, not epistemic belief.
5
Apply to the example
Al-Ghazali's example: inciting recklessness by relabeling 'caution' as 'cowardice' – this uses emotionally charged language to manipulate.
The example is incomplete in the text, but the mechanism is clear.

الحجة الشعرية : هي الحجة التي لا يشترط فيها أن تفيد ظناً راجحاً مقبولاً، بل قد تعتمد على مقدمات وهمية، وصور كاذبة لا تخفى على المخاطب، إلا أنها تشتمل على ما يتلاعب بمشاعر المخاطب النفسية، فيتأثر بها ويستجيب لمضمونها وقد يكون عالماً فكرياً بعدم صحتها. وطبيعي أن حجة من هذا القبيل، لا تفيد يقيناً، ولا تفيد ظناً راجحاً، وإنما تستخدم لتحريك مشاعر الرغبة أو مشاعر الرهبة، ولتحريك مشاعر الإقبال أو مشاعر النفور، ولتحريك مشاعر الجود أو مشاعر البخل، ونحو ذلك. والمخاطب تتحرك مشاعره، فتنبسط نفسه أو تنقبض، ويقبل طبعه أو ينفر، ولو كان عارفاً ببطلان الحجة الشعرية. وعلى هذا النوع من الحجج تعتمد صناعة الشعر، وعليها يعتمد الخطباء المتشدقون الذين يتلاعبون بمشاعر جماهير المستمعين. ومثاله كما قال الإمام الغزالي(١): أن من يريد أن يحمل غيره على التهور ويصرفه عن الحزم، يلقب (الحزم) بـ (الجبن) ويقبحه، ويذم صاحبه، فيقول:
The poetic argument: It is the argument that does not necessarily produce an acceptable predominant opinion; rather, it may rely on imaginary premises and false images that are not hidden from the addressee, but it contains something that manipulates the addressee's psychological emotions, so he is influenced by it and responds to its content, even though he may intellectually know its falsehood. Naturally, such an argument does not produce certainty nor predominant opinion; rather, it is used to stir emotions of desire or fear, to stir emotions of attraction or aversion, to stir emotions of generosity or stinginess, and so on. The addressee's emotions are stirred: his soul expands or contracts, his nature accepts or recoils, even if he knows the falsehood of the poetic argument. The craft of poetry depends on this type of argument, and also the bombastic orators who manipulate the emotions of masses of listeners. Commentary
This passage defines the poetic argument (al-hujja al-shi'riyya). Unlike rhetorical arguments, it does not even need to produce a predominant opinion; it can rely on entirely imaginary or false premises that the audience recognizes as false. Its power lies in manipulating emotions: stirring desire, fear, attraction, aversion, generosity, or stinginess. Even if the listener knows intellectually that the argument is false, his emotions may still be moved. The author notes that the art of poetry depends on such arguments, and also some orators use them to manipulate crowds. He gives an example from al-Ghazali: a person wanting to incite recklessness might relabel 'caution' as 'cowardice' to make it seem blameworthy. This shows how poetic arguments work by appealing to emotions rather than reason.
