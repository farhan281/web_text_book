Affirming the antecedent conditional syllogism
Author demonstrates affirming the antecedent with mercury example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid conditional syllogism using affirming the antecedent (wad' al-muqaddam) and verify its validity.

Core Idea
Affirming the antecedent is one of only two valid conditional inference forms.

In this lesson, we examine the first valid form of the conditional syllogism, where the antecedent is affirmed. Using the classic mercury example from the source, we see how affirming the truth of the 'if' clause logically compels the truth of the 'then' clause. This form is widely used in Islamic legal reasoning, such as deriving rulings from conditional premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional statement
Locate the major premise in the Arabic: 'إذا كان هذا السائل زئبقاً فهو يتمدد بالحرارة'. This is a conditional (shartiyya muttasila) with an antecedent (muqaddam) and consequent (tali).
2
Find the affirmative minor premise
The minor premise 'لكنه فعلاً زئبق' affirms the antecedent (wad' al-muqaddam). In the Arabic, the phrase 'وقد وضع فيها المقدم' indicates this explicitly.
Affirming the antecedent means asserting the 'if' part is true.
3
State the conclusion
The conclusion 'فهو يتمدد بالحرارة' affirms the consequent (tali). This follows necessarily because the conditional is true and the antecedent holds.
4
Distinguish from the fallacy of affirming the consequent
Note that affirming the consequent (e.g., 'it expands, so it must be mercury') is not valid. The lesson's form is valid only because we affirm the antecedent, not the consequent.
Affirming the consequent is a common fallacy in Islamic jurisprudence debates.

Valid Forms of Conditional Syllogism
Author uses weather example to show both valid forms of conditional syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify modus ponens and modus tollens in conditional syllogisms and construct valid examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Conditional syllogisms yield certainty only through affirming the antecedent or denying the consequent.

Conditional syllogisms (qiyas istithna'i muttasil) are central to Islamic reasoning, especially in kalam and usul al-fiqh. The author illustrates both valid forms using a simple weather correlation: clear sky implies no rain. This lesson shows you how to verify the validity of such arguments and avoid common fallacies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional major premise
Find the statement that expresses a constant correlation using 'if... then...' (in Arabic: 'idha... fa...'). In the example: 'If the sky is clear, then no rain falls.' This is the universal rule (da'iman).
The word 'da'iman' (always) marks a universal conditional.
2
Recognize the two valid forms
The author presents two valid ways to use this premise. First: affirm the antecedent (clear sky) to conclude the consequent (no rain). Second: deny the consequent (rain falls) to conclude the denial of the antecedent (sky not clear).
These are modus ponens and modus tollens respectively.
3
Check which part is asserted in the minor premise
Read the minor premise: does it affirm the antecedent (clear sky) or deny the consequent (rain falls)? Each leads to a valid conclusion. If it affirms the consequent or denies the antecedent, the argument is invalid.
Invalid forms: affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent.
4
Extract the conclusion
The conclusion always matches the other part. If antecedent affirmed, conclude the consequent. If consequent denied, conclude the denial of the antecedent. Verify that the conclusion follows necessarily.
This step tests logical necessity.

Core Idea
Affirm the antecedent or deny the consequent to reach a valid conclusion.

This passage presents a classic Islamic legal example of a conditional syllogism, demonstrating both valid forms: affirming the antecedent and denying the consequent. By studying this example, you will learn to identify the structure of conditional arguments and apply them to rulings in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and consequent
In the major premise 'If A then B', locate A (the condition) and B (the permission). Here, A = 'the item is his property and he is capable of disposal', B = 'it is permissible for him to sell and gift'.
Antecedent and consequent are the two parts of the conditional.
2
Recognize the two valid forms
The first minor premise affirms the antecedent (A is true), leading to the conclusion that B is true (modus ponens). The second minor premise denies the consequent (B is false), leading to the conclusion that A is false (modus tollens).
These are the only two valid forms for a conditional syllogism.
3
Verify logical necessity
Check that the conclusion follows necessarily. If the major premise is true, affirming A forces B; denying B forces not-A. The example shows both are logically sound.
If you affirm the consequent or deny the antecedent, the argument becomes invalid.
4
Connect to Islamic jurisprudence
This pattern appears widely in fiqh. For instance, 'If a person is bankrupt (muflis), then his disposal is restricted. This person is bankrupt, so his disposal is restricted' (modus ponens). Or 'This person's disposal is not restricted, so he is not bankrupt' (modus tollens).
Recognizing these forms helps evaluate legal arguments.

The Disjunctive Conditional Syllogism: Structure and Conditions
Author defines the disjunctive conditional syllogism, its premises, conditions, and three cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a disjunctive conditional syllogism and determine which exceptive premises are valid based on the type of disjunction.

Core Idea
The disjunctive syllogism relies on the type of disjunction to know which exceptions yield a valid conclusion.

In this passage, the author defines the disjunctive conditional syllogism (al-qiyas al-istithna'i al-munfasil), explaining its two premises and the crucial conditions for a valid inference. You will learn how the type of disjunction—whether it prevents both truth together, both falsehood together, or both—determines which part can be excepted to reach a sound conclusion. This lesson will equip you to analyze arguments like 'Either the act is wajib or mubah; it is not wajib; therefore it is mubah.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the two premises
The major premise is a conditional disjunctive proposition (must be affirmative contradictory). The minor premise is a categorical proposition introduced by 'but' (lakinna) called the exceptive premise. One of the two premises must be universal (or singular with universal force).
Look for the structure: major=disjunctive, minor=categorical exception.
2
Understand the three types of disjunction
Preventive of conjunction (mani'at al-jam') means both cannot be true together. Preventive of void (mani'at al-khuluw) means both cannot be false together. Preventive of both (mani'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw) means exactly one is true. Each type allows different exceptions.
This classification determines the valid exceptive operation.
3
Determine which exception is valid for each type
For preventive of both: you can except the antecedent, its contradictory, the consequent, or its contradictory. For preventive of conjunction only: except only the antecedent or consequent directly (not their contradictories). For preventive of void only: except only the contradictory of the antecedent or the contradictory of the consequent.
This is the key condition that avoids invalid inferences.
4
Apply the rule of affirmation and negation
In this syllogism, affirming one part yields negation of the other, and negating one part yields affirmation of the other (for complete disjunction). This is the logical operation that produces the conclusion.
Works only when the disjunction is exclusive (preventive of both).

In Islamic logic, disjunctions are classified by whether they allow both parts to be true or both false. This lesson focuses on the exclusive disjunction (man'iat al-jam' only), where the two parts cannot both be true but could both be false. This shows the practical importance of the distinction.

Rules for Exclusive and Inclusive Disjunctive Syllogisms
Author explains inference rules for exclusive and inclusive disjunctive syllogisms with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish exclusive from inclusive disjunctive syllogisms and apply their valid inference rules.

Core Idea
The type of disjunction decides whether positing or negating yields a conclusion.

Disjunctive syllogisms are common in Islamic legal reasoning, where a ruling may be one of two options. Knowing whether the two alternatives can coexist (inclusive) or cannot (exclusive) determines which inferences are valid. This lesson unpacks the rules with concrete examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of disjunction in the major premise
Determine if the disjunction is exclusive (man'iyya jami'), inclusive (man'iyya khuluww), or true (haqiqiyya). Exclusive means both cannot be true simultaneously; inclusive means both cannot be false; true means both properties hold.
The passage uses 'man'iyya jami' fakat' and 'man'iyya khuluww fakat' to indicate only one property.
2
Apply the inference rule for exclusive disjunction
If the disjunction is exclusive only, then positing (affirming) one disjunct yields negation of the other. Negating one does not yield positing the other because a third option might exist.
Example: Travel by land/sea/air shows that negating land does not imply sea.
3
Apply the inference rule for inclusive disjunction
If the disjunction is inclusive only, then negating one disjunct yields positing the other. Positing one does not yield negating the other because both could be true together.
Example: A place lit by natural and/or artificial light shows that positing natural light does not exclude artificial.
4
Check the conclusion against the rule
Verify that the inference matches the expected pattern: for exclusive, only positive affirmations give conclusions; for inclusive, only negations give conclusions.

In conditional syllogisms, the validity depends on whether the conditional is connected or disjunctive, and which part is affirmed or denied.

Core Idea
The validity of a disjunctive syllogism depends on whether the disjunction excludes conjunction, emptiness, or both.

In the previous lesson, we saw that the example of the poet is a disjunctive of type 'mana'at khuluw'. The author then provides a general summary listing three sub-types of disjunctive syllogisms and their specific production rules. In this lesson, we will dissect each sub-type.

Four Conditions for a Valid Conditional Syllogism
Author lists four conditions at the end of the passage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and apply the four conditions for the validity of conditional syllogisms as given by the author.

Core Idea
A conditional syllogism is valid only when the conditional premise is affirmative, implicative (not coincidental), one premise is universal, and a real disjunctive must be properly formed.

After explaining the types and production rules, the author gives four conditions that must be satisfied for any conditional syllogism to be productive (valid). These conditions apply to both connected and disjunctive syllogisms, with specific requirements for each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition
The conditional premise (either connected or disjunctive) must be affirmative. Negative conditionals do not yield valid conclusions.
Affirmative means the connection or disjunction is stated positively.
2
Identify the second condition
For connected, it must be implicative (luzumiyya), not coincidental (ittifaqiyya). For disjunctive, it must be adversative ('inadiyya), not coincidental. Coincidental conditionals (e.g., 'If the sun rises, then the river flows') are not valid because the connection is not necessary.
The connection must be real and necessary.
3
Identify the third condition
One of the two premises (major or minor) must be universal (kulliyya). This ensures the conclusion is universal or at least follows.
This is analogous to the categorical syllogism condition.
4
Identify the fourth condition
For the real disjunctive (haqiqiyya), it must be composed of a thing and the equal of its contradictory (musawi naqdihi), not the thing and its contradictory (naqdihi). For example, 'either even or odd' is valid; 'either standing or not standing' is not because it's a tautology (one is the negation of the other).
This ensures the disjunctive is exclusive and informative.
5
Apply the conditions to an example
Take the poet example: the premise is affirmative, it is adversative (since 'innovator' and 'imitator' are genuinely distinct), one premise is universal? The major premise 'The poet is either innovator or imitator' is universal (all poets are one or the other). The fourth condition applies to haqiqiyya, but this is not haqiqiyya; it's mana'at khuluw.
So the poet syllogism satisfies conditions 1,2,3 but not 4 because it's not haqiqiyya.

Four Conditions for Conditional Syllogism
Four conditions for conditional syllogism validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the four conditions for a valid conditional syllogism to a given Islamic argument.

Core Idea
Conditional syllogisms require implicative, affirmative, universal, and properly structured disjunctions.

In this passage, the author lays out the four strict conditions a conditional syllogism must meet to be valid. These rules ensure that the logical connection is necessary, not accidental, and that the syllogism is properly formed. Understanding these conditions is crucial when employing conditional arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition: affirmation
The first condition requires that the conditional premise—whether conjunctive ('if...then') or disjunctive ('either...or')—be affirmative. A negative conditional cannot serve as a premise in this syllogism.
2
Distinguish implicative from accidental conditionals
The second condition tells us that a conjunctive conditional must be luzumiyya (implicative), meaning the antecedent necessarily entails the consequent. Disjunctives must be 'anadiyya (contradictory), where the two parts are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. Accidental (ittifaqiyya) connections are invalid.
3
Check universality
The third condition requires that at least one of the two premises in the syllogism be universal (kulliyya). This ensures the argument has generality.
4
Understand the fourth condition on exclusive disjunction
The fourth condition specifies that the only valid type of exclusive disjunction is one that prevents both parts being true together (jam') and both being false (khuluw). It must be composed of the thing (shay') and its equal to its negation (al-musawi li-naqidihi), not the thing and its direct negation.
This technical point ensures the disjunction is properly exclusive and exhaustive.

Fallacy of Middle Term Identity (Circularity)
Author illustrates fallacy of middle term identity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a syllogism's middle term is identical in meaning to the minor term, rendering the argument circular and invalid.

Core Idea
A middle term that is a mere synonym of the minor term cannot produce new knowledge.

The first fallacy related to the middle term occurs when the middle term is not distinct from the minor term. The author gives an example where 'mortal' is synonymous with 'human', making the argument circular. We will learn to detect this by analyzing the relationship between the terms.

1
Extract the three terms
Identify the major term (predicate of conclusion), minor term (subject of conclusion), and middle term (appears only in premises). In the example: major = 'animal', minor = 'human', middle = 'mortal'.
2

Determine if the middle term and minor term have identical or near-identical meanings. Here, 'mortal' (بشر) and 'human' (إنسان) are synonymous in Arabic.
Synonymy may be context-dependent; check lexical relationships.
3
Assess the argument's informativeness
If the middle term is synonymous with the minor term, the conclusion is already implicit in the premises. The syllogism provides no new evidence.
4
Conclude invalidity due to circularity
Recognize that the syllogism is technically valid but practically fallacious because it begs the question. The middle term does not independently link the terms.
This fallacy is distinct from equivocation; the error is lack of distinction.

Commentary
This passage discusses two types of fallacies related to the middle term in a syllogism. The first (ب) occurs when the middle term is indistinguishable from the minor term, as in the example 'Every human is a mortal; every mortal is an animal; therefore every human is an animal' — here the middle term 'mortal' is synonymous with 'human', so the argument is circular. The second and more detailed fallacy (ج) is when the middle term is altogether absent because of equivocation or ambiguity. The author elaborates several sub-cases of this fallacy. First, a word that is equivocal (having multiple meanings) is used differently in the two premises, e.g., 'ayn meaning 'gold' in the minor premise and 'eye' in the major. The syllogism fails because there is no real common term. Second, a phrase may have two interpretations; the fallacious arguer uses one in the minor premise and the other in the major. The Quranic verse about the ambiguous verses (Āl ʿImrān 7) is cited: the phrase 'and those firmly rooted in knowledge' can be grammatically interpreted either as an object of knowing (i.e., they know the interpretation) or as a new sentence (they only affirm faith). By taking one interpretation in the minor premise and the other in the major, a false conclusion can be drawn. Third, ambiguity arises from an ambiguous pronoun referent. The story of the scholar on the minbar illustrates this: when asked who is better, Abu Bakr or Ali, he responds with a statement 'Whoever has his daughter under him is the best.' The pronoun 'his' can refer either to the Prophet (his daughter being his wife) or to the person being compared (e.g., Ali's daughter being his wife). Each faction interprets it to support their preference, but the scholar avoids directly answering. Fourth, ambiguity arises from an ambiguous description where the described term is not fixed. Fifth, the fallacy of composition and division: a statement true of parts is falsely claimed to be true of the whole, e.g., 'Five is even and odd' (because it is composed of an even and an odd number) versus 'It is impossible for a number to be even and odd at the same time' (treating 'five' as a whole). The author shows that in all these cases, the middle term is not truly shared, so the syllogism is invalid.

Burhan al-Tatbiq: Proof by Application Against Infinite Regress
Author presents and explains the proof by application against infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Burhan al-Tatbiq as a conditional syllogism (modus tollens) and identify the contradiction it exposes in the assumption of infinite regress.

Core Idea
Infinite regress forces the lesser to equal the greater, which is impossible by self-evidence.

Burhan al-Tatbiq is one of the most powerful arguments in Islamic thought against the possibility of an infinite temporal regress. It uses a simple conditional reasoning: if regress were possible, then a contradiction would follow (the lesser equals the greater). Since the consequent is impossible, the antecedent must be false. This lesson walks you through the logical structure and the intuitive application of the two chains.

Guided Path
1
Extract the conditional statement
Identify the 'if-then' structure in the Arabic: 'If this regress were intellectually permissible, then the lesser number would equal the greater number.' This is the conditional premise (P → Q).
The Arabic: 'لو كان هذا التسلسل جائزاً عقلاً، لكان العدد الأقل مساوياً للعدد الأكثر'.
2
Identify the denial of the consequent
The author explicitly states: 'the lesser number is in no way equal to the greater number.' This denies the consequent (¬Q).
Arabic: 'العدد الأقل لا يكون بحال من الأحوال مساوياً للعدد الأكثر'.
3
Apply modus tollens
From P → Q and ¬Q, we conclude ¬P: 'Therefore, the regress is intellectually impermissible.' Check that the reasoning is valid.
This is the standard rule of modus tollens.
4
Visualize the two chains
Imagine an existential chain from the present moment extending infinitely into the past, and another chain starting a million years earlier, also infinite. When applying them ring-by-ring backward, both chains become equal in number because they are both infinite, contradicting the obvious fact that one is longer by a million rings.
This thought experiment illustrates the contradiction that underlies the conditional.
5
Recognize the argument form
The proof is a conditional syllogism (al-istithna'i al-muttasil) of the modus tollens form. It is intellectually sound because denying the consequent requires denying the antecedent.
This form is widely used in Kalam to establish the impossibility of an infinite past.

And the analysis of this proof appears to us when we imagine that we have taken hold of an existential chain, starting from the present moment of time, and extending into the past without end. And we have taken hold of another chain exactly similar to it, but from one of its rings that existed a million years ago or more. Then we begin applied in imagination the rings of the two chains, this one from the present moment, and that one from a ring a million years ago, and we proceed backwards in a symmetrical application, following what was in the past side. We observe that no matter how far we go in the process of application, we find the two chains equal, as long as the past side is infinite, even though the self-evident reality is that one of them is longer than the other by the equivalent of a million years' rings. And this is an apparent contradiction, which is impossible, and that which entails the impossible is impossible. Or we say: If we allowed this regress, we would have to allow intellectually the equality of the lesser to the greater, but this is impossible, and whenever the necessary consequence is false, the necessitating premise is also false.

Commentary
This passage presents and explains the burhan al-tatbiq (proof by application) against the possibility of an infinite temporal regress. The author begins by stating that self-evident reason (badaha) rules the impossibility of regress, but additional proofs are given. The most apparent is the proof by application. The proof is formulated as a conditional syllogism: If regress were possible, then the smaller number would equal the larger number. But the smaller number never equals the larger. Therefore regress is impossible. The author then provides an intuitive analysis: imagine two chains extending infinitely into the past, one starting from the present and one starting from a point a million years earlier. When we apply them ring by ring backward, both are infinite so they become equal in number, yet one is intuitively longer by a million rings. This contradiction shows the assumption of regress is false. This is an argument by modus tollens (al-istithna'i al-muttasil): if P then Q, not Q, so not P.
