Applying the Method of Residues in Islamic Legal Reasoning
Introducing the use of the method of residues in religious matters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use the method of residues to identify the effective cause (عِلَّة) of a legal ruling in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Jurists eliminate possible causes of a ruling until only the true cause remains.

The method of residues is not just an abstract logical tool; it has direct application in Islamic jurisprudence, particularly in the identification of the 'illah (effective cause) for analogical reasoning (qiyas). The author hints at this by referencing الشرعيات. This lesson explores how the method is used in practice by jurists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal ruling and its potential causes
Start with a known ruling (e.g., prohibition of a substance) and list all plausible causes (intoxication, harm, addiction, etc.) that could be the effective cause.
The list must be exhaustive based on textual and rational evidence.
2
Find textual evidence for some causes
If the primary texts (Qur'an, Sunnah) explicitly mention a cause for the ruling, or if they rule out certain causes, use that to link some causes to the ruling in specific contexts.
For example, the prohibition of khamr is explicitly linked to intoxication in some hadith.
3
Subtract known causes
Eliminate causes that are not applicable to the new case under consideration. For instance, if the new substance does not intoxicate, intoxication is eliminated.
This subtraction is analogous to removing the wire pairs with known switches.
4
Attribute the remaining cause as the 'illah
After elimination, if only one cause remains, that is the effective cause for the new case. The ruling is then extended to the new case via qiyas.
This step mirrors the residue attribution in the general method.
5
Verify exhaustiveness and exclusivity
Ensure that the list of possible causes was complete and that they do not overlap. Overlapping causes would prevent a unique attribution.
In Islamic law, this verification often relies on scholarly consensus (ijma) that no other cause is relevant.

The Method of Residues: Discovery Tool, Not Proof
Author describes the Method of Residues, its origin, and its value in discovery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Method of Residues and distinguish its role in discovery from its role in proof.

Core Idea
The Method of Residues discovers causes by elimination, not by conclusive proof.

The Method of Residues is an inductive technique that eliminates possible causes to find the real one. This lesson explains its definition, its originator Mill's view that it is a discovery tool not a proof tool, and its successful applications in natural science. Understanding this method helps you see how induction works for generating hypotheses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Find how the passage defines the Method of Residues: considering all possible probabilities, discarding those unsuitable by evidence, and restricting the cause to the remaining one.
2
Understand Mill's distinction
Note that Mill says it is more useful for discovery than for proof, and it is not used to verify hypotheses. This distinction is key to its epistemological status.
3
Examine the examples
Look at the discoveries mentioned (Neptune, argon, radium) as illustrations of the method's power in generating new knowledge, not in confirming existing hypotheses.
4
Reflect on epistemological role
Consider why the method is not used for proof: it only eliminates alternatives, leaving the remaining cause as probable rather than certain. Its strength lies in discovery, not conclusive verification.

Analogy (Tamthil): A Method of Indirect Inference
Author defines tamthil and illustrates with four examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of an analogical argument and evaluate whether the shared cause ('illa) justifies the conclusion.

Core Idea
Analogy infers a common effect from a shared cause across two cases.

In Islamic logic, tamthil (analogy) is a powerful method of indirect inference. Unlike categorical syllogisms that work through middle terms, analogical reasoning transfers judgment from one case to another based on a shared cause ('illa). This lesson unpacks the definition of tamthil and shows how it operates across diverse fields, from materials science to social analysis. Mastering tamthil helps you recognise when an analogy is sound and when it is merely rhetorical.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and its genus
The passage defines tamthil as a type of 'indirect inference' (istidlal ghayr mubashir). Note the genus and what it shares with other indirect methods like induction.
The genus is 'intellectual operation' within the broader category of inference.
2
Extract the differentia: the role of 'illa
Tamthil is specifically based on likening one thing to another in the cause ('illa) that produces a phenomenon. Why is the 'illa the crucial element? Without a shared cause, the analogy is merely superficial.
In later Islamic logic, the strength of tamthil depends on the causal link.
3
Apply the pattern to the first example
Take the plastic-wood analogy: identify the 'illa (qualities making wood suitable for furniture) and the transferred judgment (plastic is suitable). Is the 'illa genuinely shared?
The author assumes the qualities are the same; in reality, one must verify.
4
Test the social and spiritual examples
For the nation and truth vs. falsehood analogies, do the shared 'illas (development steps, conflict dynamics) sufficiently justify the conclusions? Consider potential differences.
Social and spiritual analogies are often weaker due to unobservable factors.
5
Distinguish tamthil from qiyas in usul al-fiqh
In usul al-fiqh, qiyas is a specific form of analogy with strict conditions on the 'illa (must be from revelation or consensus). Tamthil as described here is broader and less demonstrative.
This distinction affects the certainty level of the conclusion.

This passage defines tamthil (analogical reasoning) as a method of indirect inference. Each example follows the same pattern: identification of shared cause/quality, then inference to shared effect. Defining Tamthil (Analogical Reasoning)
Author defines tamthil as an intellectual operation based on shared cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tamthil and distinguish it from direct deductive inference based on its genus and differentia.

Core Idea
Tamthil is inference by likening one thing to another in their shared cause.

Tamthil (analogy) is a core method of indirect inference in Islamic logic. In this lesson, we unpack its definition as presented by the author, identifying its genus (method of indirect inference) and differentia (likening based on a shared cause). This foundation is essential for understanding how analogical arguments function in Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Locate the term being defined in the Arabic. The author begins by naming 'al-tamthil' as the subject.
2
Extract the genus
The author states tamthil is 'among the methods of indirect inference' – this is its genus (jins). Note that it is not a direct inference.
This places tamthil under the broader category of indirect reasoning methods.
3
Identify the differentia
The definition continues: 'an intellectual operation based on likening one thing to another in the cause ('illa) that was the reason for a phenomenon'. This is the differentia (fasl) – what makes tamthil specific.
The key is the shared 'illa, which justifies the transfer of a property or ruling.
4
Contrast with direct inference
Compare this definition with a deductive syllogism (qiyas iqtirani). In tamthil, the inference is not necessary but depends on the strength of the likeness.
This contrast is implicit; the author stresses that tamthil is 'indirect'.

Al-Tamthil (Analogy)
Among the methods of indirect inference is the method of analogy.
And analogy is an intellectual operation based on likening one thing to another in the cause that was the reason for the occurrence of a phenomenon among its phenomena, and considering this similarity sufficient to analogize the thing to the other in that it has the same phenomenon.
When the researcher sees a similarity between (plastic) material and wood in many qualities that made wood suitable for making furniture from it, he concludes that (plastic) is also suitable for making furniture from it.
And when the researcher sees a similarity between a newly discovered metal and gold in its non-oxidizability by acids, for example, he concludes the suitability of this new metal for use in place of gold in all matters in which gold is used, because it is non-oxidizable by acids.
And when the social researcher sees a similarity between one nation and another in the steps of its course in life, he concludes that its end will be like the end of the other, due to the similarity between them in the steps of the course and the similarity between them in the origin of formation.
And when the deep researcher sees a similarity between the state of struggle between truth and falsehood and the state of struggle between water and the scum that rises in the movement of the torrent, he concludes that the end of the struggle between truth and falsehood will be coupled with the victory of truth over falsehood, its stability, firmness, and permanence, and the defeat of falsehood, its failure, and its disappointment, like the victory of water over the scum, its stability, firmness, and permanence in the earth, the extinguishing of the appearance of the rising scum, and its going as scum having no value or weight.
And when the jurist sees a similarity between one matter and another in the cause ('illa) of its ruling, he concludes that the ruling of this matter in Islamic law is similar to the ruling of the other matter, by virtue of its similarity to it in the cause of its ruling. Therefore he rules the prohibition of every intoxicating drink by analogizing to wine, because the cause of the prohibition of wine in Islamic law is intoxication.
And when the legal scholar sees a similarity between an incident not provided for in the law and another incident that is provided for in it, and the similarity resides in the cause that necessitated the enactment of this legal ruling, he concludes that the ruling of the incident not provided for in the law is like the ruling of the other incident provided for in the law, by virtue of their sharing in the cause that obligates the enactment of the ruling in the view of the legislator.
For example, if the law stipulates a penalty for the smuggler of hashish, cocaine, and opium, considering them to be among the narcotics, then new dangerous narcotic materials like heroin are discovered, then the penalty for smuggling this material not provided for in the law is like the penalty for the materials provided for in it, by virtue of its participation with them in the cause that obligated the enactment of the penalty ruling on smuggling.
And thus the reality of the method of analogy appears to us.
And analogy is called by the scholars of Usul al-Fiqh (Qiyas), and is called by the theologians—who are the scholars of Islamic creed—(inference by the witnessed from the unseen), or referring the unseen to the witnessed, except that the scholars of Usul al-Fiqh are the greatest of those who cared to set its controls, define its conditions, and explain everything related to it.

Pillars of Tamthil: Asl, Far', 'Illa
Author illustrates tamthil with examples and implicitly reveals its structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and label the original case (asl), new case (far'), and shared cause ('illa) in any analogical argument.

Core Idea
Every tamthil requires an original case, a new case, and a shared cause.

The passage provides vivid examples of tamthil from different fields. This lesson dissects the plastic-wood analogy to reveal the three essential pillars of analogical reasoning. Mastering these components allows you to construct and evaluate analogies in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case (asl)
In the first example, wood is the original case. It is the thing whose property (suitability for furniture) is known.
2
Identify the new case (far')
Plastic is the new case. It is being compared to wood to determine if it shares the property.
3
Identify the shared cause ('illa)
The 'illa is the set of qualities that make wood suitable for furniture. The author states plastic shares 'many qualities' with wood; these qualities constitute the cause.
The 'illa is not a single attribute but a cluster; however, in legal analogies, it is usually a single effective cause (like intoxication).
4
Formulate the conclusion
The conclusion is that plastic is also suitable for furniture because it shares the 'illa.
The conclusion is analogically transferred from the original.

The Four Pillars of Analogy
Author enumerates the four essential components of every analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four pillars of analogical reasoning in any given analogy from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Every analogy rests on four pillars: original, branch, cause, and ruling.

Analogical reasoning (tamthil) is a cornerstone of Islamic jurisprudence and many other disciplines. To properly evaluate an analogy, we must first understand its fundamental structure. This lesson breaks down the four essential components that every valid analogy must contain, as laid out in classical logic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case (al-asl)
Find the known case from which the analogy begins. In usul al-fiqh, this is often the case with an established ruling from the Qur'an or Sunnah. For example, wine is the original when analogizing to other intoxicants.
The original is also called al-mumaththal bihi (the thing represented) or al-maqis 'alayhi (the thing measured upon).
2
Identify the branch (al-far')
Locate the new case being compared to the original. This is the case whose ruling we want to determine. For example, nabidh (date wine) is a branch when analogized to wine.
The branch is also called al-mumaththal (the thing representing) or al-maqis (the thing measured).
3
Find the common cause (al-'illa al-jami'a)
Determine the attribute or reason that links the original and branch. This cause must be present in both and must be the effective reason for the ruling in the original. For intoxicants, the cause is intoxication (iskar).
The cause is the central element; without it, the analogy collapses.
4
Determine the ruling or phenomenon (al-hukm or al-zahira)
Identify what is being transferred from the original to the branch. This is the ruling (e.g., prohibition) or phenomenon that is generalized based on the shared cause.
The ruling is generalized 'by the evidence of analogy' (bi-dalil al-tamthil) and the shared cause.
5
Verify the completeness of the analogy
Check that all four pillars are present and that the cause is genuinely shared. If any pillar is missing or the cause is not common, the analogy is invalid.
This step is crucial for evaluating arguments in fiqh and kalam.

The Four Pillars of Analogical Reasoning
Author enumerates the four essential components of analogical reasoning.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Every qiyas rests on four pillars: asl, far', 'illa, and hukm.

In this lesson, we unpack the structure of analogical reasoning as defined by the author. Understanding these four components is the first step to evaluating any legal analogy in Islamic jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case (al-asl)
The original case is the standard that already carries the ruling. For example, wine (khamr) is the asl for the prohibition of intoxication.

The branch is the new case being compared. In the classic analogy, nabidh is the far' that is compared to wine.

The phenomenon or ruling (hukm) that exists in the original is generalized to the branch due to the shared cause. The prohibition of wine extends to nabidh.

Perfect induction covers all instances; analogy is probabilistic and never yields certainty.

Why Analogy Cannot Produce Certainty: The Three Impossible Conditions
Exposition of conditions for certain analogy and their impossibility

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Certainty from analogy requires complete cause knowledge and no particularities—impossible in reality.

The passage concludes with a rigorous set of conditions that must be met for analogy to produce certainty. These conditions are so stringent that they are impossible to verify in practice. This lesson dissects each condition and explains why analogy remains probabilistic.

Guided Path
1
Condition 1: Complete cause
The shared description must be the entire cause with no other participants or unknown conditions. This is impossible because we rarely know all causes of a phenomenon.
2
Condition 2: No particularity of the original
The unique features of the original case must not be conditions for the judgment. We cannot be sure that the judgment depends solely on the shared attribute.

The Value of the Analogical Argument:
Analogy in its reality is the mental process that completes imperfect induction, because the generalization of the judgment indicated by imperfect induction comes only through representing the unstudied by induction with the studied one, by the commonality of their sharing in the cause, then generalizing the judgment based on the similarity in the cause of the judgment.
Analogy is the basis of many scientific hypotheses that researchers propose to explain natural, psychological, social, legal, and other phenomena.
Through analogy, scientists hypothesized the possibility that Mars is inhabited by living beings like Earth, due to its similarity to Earth in phenomena indicating the existence of causes of life therein.
By its virtue, scientists deduced that heat is a type of motion, by analogy to light, which they had established is a type of motion.
And so on.
The analogical argument is not a path to reach certainties that are decisively established, but rather it is a path to reach probabilities, in material matters, natural matters, life affairs and actions, education and its means, ethics and morals, practical legal matters, preaching and reflection, in choosing what is beneficial and useful in the immediate and the afterlife, in politics and administrations, in judiciary and judgments, in conditions of peace and war, and the like.
To rely on the analogical argument, it is required that it yields preponderant probability in matters in which reliance on preponderant probability is valid, because it is the way of people's lives.
As for pure rational matters, analogy is of no use, and likewise analogy is not useful in judging the unseen world by the same judgment as the witnessed world, except under the condition of unity in characteristics and absence of difference in the fundamental system of existence, and this condition is impossible to attain knowledge of, therefore the unseen world cannot be analogized to the witnessed world, except in the great general rational truths that encompass both the unseen world and the witnessed world together, such as existence, contingency, impossibility, non-collection of contradictories, and the like.
And these do not require the analogical argument, but rather the logical syllogism applies to them.
For analogy to yield certainty in the judgment, conditions that are impossible to exist must be fulfilled, which are:
First: Certainty that the shared description is the complete cause or the complete reason necessarily, and that no other thing participates with it, and that there are no conditions for it that are unknown.
And fulfilling this condition is not feasible regarding the causes of things and their reasons.
Second: Certainty that the particularity of the original is not a condition for the judgment to be established for it, or for the phenomenon to exist in it.
Third: Certainty that the particularity of the branch is not an obstacle to the judgment being established for it, or to the phenomenon existing in it.
Since verifying the existence of these conditions is impossible, analogy does not yield certainty, so it is therefore not useful in rational matters that require certainty.

Commentary
This passage critiques analogical reasoning (tamthil), explaining its nature, scope, and ultimate inability to produce certainty.

The author begins by defining analogy as the mental process that completes imperfect induction. Imperfect induction examines some instances and finds a shared description (like intoxication in wine), but cannot generalize universally. Analogy extends the judgment to unexamined instances (like nabidh) based on shared cause ('illa). Thus, it is foundational for scientific hypotheses, e.g., proposing that Mars is inhabited due to similarities to Earth, or that heat is motion by analogy to light.

However, the author stresses that analogy yields only probability (zann), not certainty (yaqin). It is useful in practical matters—material, natural, social, legal—where preponderant probability suffices for daily life. In pure rational matters ('aqliyyat mahda), analogy fails. For example, judging the unseen world (al-ghayb) by the witnessed world (al-shahada) is invalid unless characteristics are identical and there is no difference in the fundamental system—a condition impossible to verify. Thus, except for universal rational truths (like existence, impossibility), analogy is inapplicable.

To produce certainty, three impossible conditions must be met: (1) the shared description is the complete cause with no other participant or unknown conditions; (2) no particularity of the original is a condition for the judgment; (3) no particularity of the branch prevents the judgment. Since these are unattainable, analogy cannot yield certainty, especially in rational matters requiring absolute certainty.
