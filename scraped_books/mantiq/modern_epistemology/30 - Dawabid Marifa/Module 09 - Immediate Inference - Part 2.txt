Conversion of Affirmative Propositions to Particular Affirmatives
Author states that all affirmative propositions convert to particular affirmatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert any affirmative proposition into its correct converse according to Islamic logic.

Core Idea
All affirmatives become particular, never universal.

In this passage, the author establishes a crucial rule for logical conversion: every affirmative proposition, regardless of its quantity (universal, particular, indefinite, individual), converts to a particular affirmative. This rule is essential for constructing valid arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence. Let's walk through the examples to see the pattern.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition's quality and quantity
Determine whether the proposition is affirmative or negative, and whether it is universal (كلي), particular (جزئي), indefinite (مهمل), or individual (شخصي). The rule only applies to affirmatives.
The original propositions in the examples are all affirmative.
2
Apply the conversion rule
Swap the subject and predicate (for categoricals) or antecedent and consequent (for conditionals), and change the quantity to particular (جزئي). The quality remains affirmative.
Exceptions: individual propositions with both terms individual convert as themselves, not to particular.
3
Verify the result
Check that the converse is a particular affirmative. For universal affirmatives, the converse must be particular; for particular affirmatives, the converse remains particular.
If the converse were universal, it could be false, e.g., 'Every human is an animal' does not imply 'Every animal is a human'.
4
Note the exception for individual propositions
The author notes that an individual proposition with two individual terms (شخصية الحدين) converts as itself, not to particular. The examples given are individual with one individual term (Imam al-Shafi‘i), which still converts to particular.
The exception was covered in the previous section.

Commentary
This passage explains the conversion rule for all affirmative propositions. The author states that every affirmative proposition—whether universal (كلية), particular (جزئية), indefinite (مهملة), or individual (مخصوصة)—converts to a particular affirmative. The only exception is the individual proposition with both terms individual (شخصية الحدين), which was covered in the previous section and converts exactly as itself. The author provides examples from categorical propositions: a universal affirmative like 'Every human is an animal' converts to 'Some animal is a human' (a particular affirmative); it cannot remain universal, as that would be invalid. A particular affirmative like 'Some plant is edible' converts to itself (still particular affirmative). An indefinite affirmative like 'The bird is an animal' converts to 'Some animal is a bird' (particular affirmative). An individual affirmative like 'Imam al-Shafi‘i is a jurist-mujtahid' converts to 'Among the jurist-mujtahids is Imam al-Shafi‘i' (a particular affirmative). The same principle applies to connected conditionals: a universal conditional like 'Whenever the Muslims hold fast to the rope of God, God succours them' converts to a particular conditional: 'It may be that if God succours the Muslims, they have held fast to His rope' (i.e., the converse is not universal but particular). The author also notes that if one tries to convert a universal affirmative to another universal affirmative, the result may be false (e.g., 'Every human is an animal' does not imply 'Every animal is a human').

Key Terms
القضية الموجبة (al-qadiyat al-mūjaba): An affirmative proposition, i.e., one that affirms the predicate of the subject (or the consequent of the antecedent). [Note: No significant shift; stable term.]
العكس إلى موجبة جزئية (al-‘aks ilā mūjaba juz’iyya): The conversion of an affirmative proposition into a particular affirmative (i.e., the converse is always a particular affirmative). [Note: In some texts, the conversion of particular affirmative is also particular; the rule here is general.]

Conversion of Affirmative Propositions to Particular Affirmatives
Author states the rule for converting affirmative propositions and provides examples from categoricals and conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert any affirmative categorical or conditional proposition into its correct converse using the rule that all affirmatives become particular affirmatives.

Core Idea
All affirmative propositions convert only to particular affirmatives.

This passage introduces the foundational rule for conversion in Islamic logic: swapping subject and predicate (or antecedent and consequent) while preserving quality yields a particular affirmative. This rule prevents the common error of converting 'All A are B' to 'All B are A'. We will examine the rule and its application to categorical and conditional propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
Determine the proposition's type (categorical or conditional), its quantity (universal, particular, indefinite, singular), and its quality (affirmative). Ensure it is affirmative.
The rule applies only to affirmative propositions.
2
Swap the terms
For categoricals, exchange subject and predicate. For conditionals, exchange antecedent (muqaddam) and consequent (tali).
This is the core of the conversion operation.
3
Change quantity to particular
Regardless of the original quantity, the converse must be a particular affirmative. For universal affirmatives, this means 'Some B is A'. For conditionals, add 'possibly' (قد يكون) to indicate particularity.
A universal affirmative cannot convert to universal affirmative; that would be invalid.
4
Preserve quality
Keep the proposition affirmative. Conversion does not change the quality.
Negative propositions have different rules.
5
Verify with examples
Check the provided examples: universal, particular, indefinite, and singular categoricals all become particular affirmatives. Conditionals likewise become particular conditionals with 'قد يكون'.
The singular with two singular terms is an exception.

Conversion of Universal Negatives to Universal Negatives
Rule 3: conversion of universal negatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that universal negative propositions convert to universal negatives, including the special case of singular negatives with general predicates.

Core Idea
Universal negatives convert to universal negatives, preserving both quality and quantity.

In immediate inference, conversion (aks) is the operation of swapping subject and predicate while preserving truth. A key rule is that universal negative propositions ('No A are B') convert to universal negatives ('No B are A'). This rule also covers singular negatives like 'Khalid is not a coward' which, after conversion, become universal negatives ('No coward is Khalid'). Understanding this rule allows you to derive logically equivalent propositions effortlessly.

Guided Path
1
State the rule for universal negatives
The author states: 'The universal negative converts like itself as a universal negative.' This means that when you swap subject and predicate, the result remains a universal negative proposition.
2
Examine the examples
Look at three examples: (a) 'No Muslim denies the message of Muhammad' converts to 'No one who denies the message of Muhammad is a Muslim'. (b) 'No act of adultery is permissible' converts to 'No permissible thing is adultery'. (c) 'Khalid is not a coward' (singular negative) converts to 'No coward is Khalid' (universal negative).
Examples (a) and (b) are standard universal negatives; (c) is a special singular case.
3
Understand the singular case
A singular negative like 'Khalid is not a coward' cannot remain singular after conversion because the subject 'Khalid' becomes part of the predicate. The only natural result is a universal negative: 'No coward is Khalid'. This works only if the original predicate (here 'coward') is a general term, not a singular.
If the predicate were also singular, conversion would not yield a universal negative; that case is excluded by the author.
4
Apply the rule yourself
Take a universal negative from Islamic jurisprudence, e.g., 'No intoxicant is halal'. Convert it: 'No halal thing is an intoxicant'. Verify that the conversion is a valid universal negative.
The truth is preserved: if the original is true, the converse is also true.

Rules of Conversion (al-'Aks) for Categorical and Conditional Propositions
Author explains conversion rules with examples and states why particular negatives cannot be converted.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of conversion to universal negative and particular negative propositions in categorical and conditional forms, and identify which conversions are valid.

Core Idea
Universal negatives convert cleanly; particular negatives cannot be converted at all.

Conversion (al-'aks) is a type of immediate inference where the subject and predicate of a proposition are swapped. This lesson examines the rules for converting universal and particular negatives in both categorical and conditional propositions. Understanding these rules prevents logical errors when interpreting Islamic legal and theological statements.

Guided Path
1
Understand conversion as immediate inference
Conversion swaps the subject and predicate of a proposition. The key condition is preserving truth value. Read the author's definition and examples to grasp the operation.
2
Identify the type of proposition
Look at the quality (affirmative/negative) and quantity (universal/particular). The passage provides examples: universal negative (e.g., 'No Muslim denies the message'), singular negative (e.g., 'Khalid is not a coward'), particular negative (e.g., 'Some animal is not human'). Note the abbreviations: ك س for universal negative, ج س for particular negative.
Abbreviations are standard in Arabic logic.
3
Apply conversion rule for universal negatives
The author states: the universal negative converts to itself as universal negative. Check the examples: 'No Muslim denies the message' becomes 'None who deny the message are Muslims' – both are universal negatives and true if original is true.
Similarly for conditional universal negatives.
4
Test conversion for particular negatives
The author demonstrates that converting a particular negative (e.g., 'Some animal is not human') to either a particular negative or a universal negative produces a false statement. Therefore, conversion is invalid.
The example shows the original true but the converted false, violating truth preservation.
5
Understand why conversion fails for particular negatives
The condition of conversion includes preserving quality. Converting a negative to affirmative would change quality, so it's not allowed. Moreover, attempts within the same quality fail because the truth value changes. The author mentions that logicians attribute this to one of three reasons (though not detailed here).
The three reasons are typically: change in quantity, change in truth conditions, etc.

Conversion Rulings for Propositions
Author enumerates five specific rulings for converting different proposition types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the five conversion rulings to any categorical or conditional proposition and determine its valid converse.

Core Idea
Conversion is not symmetric: affirmative and negative propositions behave differently, and particular negatives never convert.

This passage compiles all the conversion rules into a single reference. You will learn exactly which propositions can be converted and into what form. Mastering this summary is essential for manipulating propositions in syllogistic reasoning and for validating immediate inferences in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify proposition type
Determine whether the proposition is categorical or conditional, affirmative or negative, universal, particular, singular, or indefinite. Also note if it is a singular with singular predicate/consequent (shakhṣiyya al-ḥaddayn).
For conditionals, distinguish conjunctive (muttaṣila) from disjunctive (munfaṣila).
2
Check for the special singular case
If the proposition is singular and both subject and predicate (in categorical) or antecedent and consequent (in conditional) are singular, it converts identically in quantity and quality.
Example: 'Zayd is the author' converts to 'The author is Zayd'.
3
Apply the affirmative rule
If the proposition is affirmative (A, I, or indefinite affirmative, and singular outside the special case), it converts to a particular affirmative (I).
This includes universal affirmatives: 'Every S is P' becomes 'Some P is S'.
4
Apply the universal negative rule
If the proposition is a universal negative (E) or a singular negative without a singular predicate, it converts to a universal negative (E): 'No S is P' becomes 'No P is S'.
5
Identify non-convertible types
Particular negatives (O) and indefinite negatives, as well as disjunctive conditionals, have no valid conversion. Any attempt to convert them yields a statement that is not logically entailed.
Example: 'Some S is not P' does not imply 'Some P is not S'.

Three Reasons Why Conversion Matters in Islamic Logic
Author explains three reasons why knowledge of conversion is necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three uses of conversion in logical reasoning as presented in this passage.

Core Idea
Conversion is not a trick—it is a practical tool for solving problems in inference and syllogism.

This passage from our coursebook explains why mastering conversion (al-'aks) is essential for the logician. The author presents three distinct scenarios where knowing the converse of a proposition is not just useful but necessary. Understanding these reasons will help you see conversion as a practical skill, not a theoretical curiosity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first reason – Direct inference
The author says conversion is needed for 'direct inference' (istidlāl mubāshir). This means that from a single proposition, you can immediately derive another proposition without any middle term. For example, from 'No intoxicant is halal' you can directly infer 'No halal is intoxicant'. That's conversion.
Direct inference is immediate, not mediated by a third term.
2
Understand the second reason – Clarifying syllogisms
Some syllogisms, especially those outside the first figure, rely on conversion to reveal their validity. For instance, a second-figure syllogism like 'No intoxicant is halal; every khamr is intoxicant; therefore no khamr is halal' is actually valid only after converting the major premise. Why? Because without conversion, the middle term is not in the right position for the first figure.
The author alludes to this by saying 'some syllogisms have their mode of production clarified by conversion'.
3
Grasp the third reason – When the syllogism's conclusion is not exactly what we need
Sometimes we prove a proposition, but our desired conclusion (maṭlūb) is the converse of that proposition. For example, we might prove that 'No intoxicant is halal', but we need to prove that 'No halal is intoxicant'. Conversion gives us that. So we use the converse to establish our maṭlūb.
This shows conversion as a bridge between what is proven and what is needed.

Conversion Rules for Categorical Propositions
Lists conversion rules for categorical propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conversion rules to correctly transform categorical propositions into their converses.

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate but changes quantity for affirmatives.

Conversion (al-'aks) is an immediate inference that yields a new proposition by swapping the subject and predicate. The result depends on the type of proposition. These rules are essential for deriving new knowledge from given statements, such as when a mufti uses a known ruling to infer a related ruling. Let's learn the rules systematically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of original proposition
Determine whether it is singular (present-time or not), universal affirmative, universal negative, particular affirmative, particular negative, or indeterminate.
2
Apply the conversion rule for singular present-time
If the proposition is a singular about the present (e.g., 'Zaid is standing'), it converts to itself (same quality and quantity).
This is an exception.
3
Apply the conversion rule for universal negative
A universal negative (e.g., 'No intoxicant is halal') converts to a universal negative ('No halal thing is an intoxicant').
Quality and quantity preserved.
4
Apply the conversion rule for all affirmatives
Any affirmative (universal, particular, singular except present-time) converts to a particular affirmative. For example, 'Every intoxicant is haram' becomes 'Some haram things are intoxicants'.
Quantity changes to particular.
5
Recognize deficient propositions
Particular negatives, indeterminate negatives, and disjunctive conditionals are 'deficient' (qasira) and do not convert correctly.
They are not used in standard immediate inference.

Circularity: Definition and Logical Impossibility
Author defines circularity, gives two forms and examples, proving its impossibility by reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify circularity (al-dawr) in a causal argument and explain why it is logically impossible.

Core Idea
Circularity makes a thing depend on itself — it would need to exist before it exists.

The author declares circularity (al-dawr) an intellectual impossibility. He defines it as self-dependence, with or without an intermediary, and gives two vivid examples to expose the contradiction. By understanding this, you will spot circular reasoning in Islamic legal and theological arguments — a key skill for rigorous debate.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of al-dawr
Notice that the author defines circularity as 'the dependence of a thing upon itself' either directly or through an intermediary. This is the core idea: a thing is both cause and effect of itself.
2
Analyze the direct circularity example (universe)
The claim 'the universe came into existence by itself' forces the universe to be both cause (existent) and effect (non-existent) at the same time — an obvious contradiction. Check how the author uses the principle that cause must precede effect.
The contradiction violates the law of non-contradiction, which is foundational in Islamic logic.
3
Analyze the indirect circularity example (chicken-egg)
Here the circularity goes through an intermediary: the first chicken depends on the first egg, and the first egg depends on the first chicken. This creates a loop where each requires the other first, leading to the absurdity that the chicken must exist before itself.
Indirect circularity is trickier to spot but equally invalid.

Two-way Entailment (Talazum min al-Tarafayn)
Defines two-way entailment with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify two-way entailment relations and test whether a given pair of propositions are mutually entailing.

Core Idea
Two-way entailment: each implies the other; denial of one denies the other.

In Islamic logic, entailment (talazum) is a fundamental relation. This lesson introduces two-way entailment, where two propositions or entities are such that each necessarily follows from the other. This concept appears in discussions of correlatives (mutadayfat) and in immediate inferences. Understanding this helps in constructing sound arguments and avoiding fallacies in Islamic jurisprudence and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two items
Find the two entities or propositions said to entail each other. In the passage, examples include daytime and sunrise, and fatherhood and sonship.
2
Check forward implication
Verify that the existence of the first item implies the existence of the second. For example, existence of daytime implies sunrise.
3
Check reverse implication
Verify that the existence of the second item implies the existence of the first. For daytime and sunrise, both directions hold, making it two-way.
4
Check non-existence implications
Verify that the non-existence of each item implies the non-existence of the other. The passage states that negation of each mutual entailer entails negation of the other.
5
Determine if two-way
If all four conditions hold (forward, reverse, negation forward, negation reverse), the relation is two-way entailment. If only forward and negation forward hold, it is one-way.

One-way Entailment (Luzum min Taraf Wahid)
Defines and exemplifies one-way entailment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify one-way entailment relations and distinguish them from two-way entailment.

Core Idea
One-way entailment: the cause implies the effect, but the effect does not imply the cause.

While two-way entailment is mutual, one-way entailment (luzum min taraf wahid) is more common in everyday reasoning and Islamic legal analogy. This lesson explains that the existence of the necessitating (malzum) necessarily implies the necessary (lazim), but the absence of the malzum does not negate the lazim, because the lazim may have other causes. This is crucial for valid argumentation in Fiqh and Kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify malzum and lazim
Find the necessitating (الملزوم) and the necessary (اللازم) in the relation. In the passage examples: lamp lighting (malzum) and light (lazim); neck-cutting (malzum) and death (lazim); rain (malzum) and wetness (lazim).
2
Check forward implication
Verify that the existence of the malzum guarantees the existence of the lazim. For all examples, this holds.
3
Check reverse implication
Check whether the existence of the lazim guarantees the existence of the malzum. The passage shows it does not; e.g., light can come from sources other than the lamp.
4
Confirm one-way
If forward implication holds but reverse fails, the relation is one-way entailment. The passage emphasizes this with the principle: 'the non-existence of the malzum does not entail the non-existence of the lazim' due to alternative causes.
5
Apply to existence/non-existence variant
The passage also gives an example of one-way entailment between existence and non-existence: whiteness entails not-blackness, but not-blackness does not entail whiteness. Apply the same check: existence of whiteness implies non-blackness, but non-blackness does not imply whiteness.

Defining the General (al-A'am) and the Specific (al-Akhass)
Defines the general and specific with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a term is general or specific relative to another term using the criteria of subsumption.

Core Idea
Generality and specificity are relative: a universal is general relative to its species but specific relative to its genus.

In this passage, the author defines the key terms 'general' (al-a'am) and 'specific' (al-akhass) as relative concepts. Understanding these definitions is crucial because the subsequent rules depend on correctly identifying which term is broader. Let us see how the author distinguishes them using examples from everyday categories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms being compared
Look for two terms where one is a universal and the other is either a species or an individual. For example, 'animal' and 'human', 'human' and 'Zayd'.
2
Determine the relation between them
Check if one term is a genus (jins) of the other, or if one is a particular (juz'i) under a universal.
3
Apply the criterion of relativity
If a term is a universal and you consider it in relation to its species or particulars, it is the general (a'am). If it is a universal considered in relation to its genus, or a particular in relation to its species, it is the specific (akhass).
4
Practice with examples
Take 'animal' and 'human': animal is the general because it is a universal in relation to its species human. Human is specific because it is a universal in relation to its genus animal. Similarly, 'Zayd' is specific relative to 'human'.
These definitions are foundational for the rules that follow.

The Four Logical Relations Between General and Specific
States the four logical relations between general and specific.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the four rules of negation and affirmation between general and specific to determine valid and invalid inferences.

Core Idea
Negation of the general entails negation of the specific; affirmation of the specific entails affirmation of the general; the converse inferences are invalid.

Building on the definitions of general and specific, the author now states four logical rules governing their negation and affirmation. These rules are essential for correct reasoning about hierarchical classifications. They show us what follows and what does not follow when we deny or assert a general or specific term.

Guided Path
1
Understand the four cases
The passage lists four combinations: (1) negation of specific, (2) negation of general, (3) affirmation of general, (4) affirmation of specific. For each, we must determine whether the other term's status is entailed.
2
Examine the valid inferences
Case 2: Negation of general necessitates negation of specific (because the specific is a subset). Case 4: Affirmation of specific necessitates affirmation of general (because the specific is a member of the general).
3
Examine the invalid inferences
Case 1: Negation of specific does not negate the general (the general could be true of other specifics). Case 3: Affirmation of general does not affirm the specific (the general could be true of other things).

If 'animal' is negated, 'human' must be negated (valid). If 'human' is affirmed, 'animal' must be affirmed (valid). If 'animal' is affirmed, 'human' is not necessarily affirmed (invalid). If 'human' is negated, 'animal' is not necessarily negated (invalid).
These examples illustrate the rules in action.

The Third Rule: General and Specific in Immediate Inference
Defines the general and specific and their logical implications in negation and affirmation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the four logical entailments between general and specific to evaluate the validity of affirmations and negations in Islamic reasoning.

Core Idea
Negating the general forces negation of the specific, but affirming the general does not force affirmation of any specific.

In Islamic logic, understanding the relationship between the general (al-aʿamm) and the specific (al-akhass) is essential for valid argumentation. This rule, often used in kalam and usul al-fiqh, defines how negation and affirmation propagate. For instance, if you deny the existence of all animals, you necessarily deny humans; but denying humans does not deny all animals. This lesson breaks down the four implications and shows how to apply them.

Apply the affirmation implications
Check: if the specific is affirmed ('A human exists'), does the general have to be affirmed? Yes. Affirming the general ('An animal exists') does not affirm any specific.

Thus the universal in relation to some of its species or some of its particulars is always the general.

The Pitfall of Synonymy in Immediate Inference
Author illustrates how synonymous terms in premises affect the conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a conclusion merely repeats the premise via synonym substitution and distinguish it from genuine syllogistic inference.

Core Idea
Synonymy changes the wording without changing the meaning — it is not real inference.

In this passage, the author illustrates two examples where the conclusion is identical to the minor premise except that a term is replaced by its synonym. Although this is a form of immediate inference, it reveals no new information. Recognizing this helps you avoid confusing verbal substitution with valid logical deduction, a crucial skill in disciplines like Usul al-Fiqh where precise reasoning is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the minor premise
In example A, the minor premise is 'this is an asad (lion)'. In example B, it is 'this is a nuqla (transfer)'.
2
Examine the major premise
In A, the major premise is 'every asad is a layth'. In B, it is 'every nuqla is a haraka'. Note that these are not genuine universal statements connecting distinct categories; rather, they assert synonymy.
3
Compare the conclusion to the minor premise
In A, the conclusion is 'this is a layth'; in B, 'this is a haraka'. The conclusion is exactly the same proposition as the minor premise but with a synonym substituted for the subject term. No new predicate has been introduced.
4
Determine logical value
Because the major premise merely states synonymy, the conclusion adds no new information. The inference is valid but trivial—it does not advance knowledge. Genuine syllogistic inference requires the middle term to connect distinct concepts.
5
Apply the lesson to future arguments
When you encounter an argument where the conclusion seems to follow from premises, check whether the middle term is truly distinct from the subject or predicate terms. If it is merely a synonym, the argument is a verbal restatement, not a logical proof.
This is especially important in evaluating proofs in fiqh and kalam where subtle distinctions matter.

Commentary
This passage provides two examples illustrating a type of immediate inference where the conclusion is identical in meaning to the minor premise, differing only in synonymous wording. In the first example, 'this is an asad' and 'every asad is a layth' leads to 'this is a layth'. Since asad and layth are synonyms, the conclusion adds no new information. Similarly, 'this is a nuqla' and 'every nuqla is a haraka' yields 'this is a haraka' because nuqla and haraka are effectively synonyms. The author here demonstrates that such arguments are merely verbal changes and do not constitute genuine logical inference. This guards against mistaking synonymy for real syllogistic reasoning.

Key Terms
مقدمة صغرى (muqaddima sughra): The minor premise of a syllogism; here it is the first premise that is transformed. [Note: Standard term in Aristotelian logic; stable across texts.]
مترادفان (mutaradifan): Two synonyms; words that share the same meaning. [Note: Standard linguistic term; no shift.]
