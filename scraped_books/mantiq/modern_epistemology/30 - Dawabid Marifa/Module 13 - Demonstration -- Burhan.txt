Proof by Contradiction in Theoretical Reasoning
Author explains the utility of contradiction for proving truths by refuting their opposites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a proof by contradiction to establish a theological truth, such as the existence of the Necessary Existent.

Core Idea
If a claim's contradictory is false, the claim itself must be true.

Sometimes direct proof is impossible, but we can still demonstrate a truth by showing that its opposite leads to absurdity. This lesson explores how the concept of the contradictory (naqīḍ) is used in Islamic theology to prove foundational truths like the existence of the Necessary Existent (Wājib al-Wujūd). The author illustrates that when direct demonstration fails, refuting the contradictory suffices for proof.

Guided Path
1
Understand the role of the contradictory
Recognize that for any proposition, its contradictory is the exact opposite in truth value. In theoretical reasoning, if you cannot prove a proposition directly, you can prove it by showing that its contradictory is false.
The contradictory of 'existence is the original principle' is 'non-existence is the original principle'.
2
Analyze the example given
Examine the author's example: If 'non-existence is the absolute general principle' is false, then its contradictory 'existence is the principle' must be true. This establishes the Necessary Existent as that whose existence is essential, not derived.
The example relies on the law of excluded middle: either existence or non-existence is the principle; one must be true.
3
Formalize the argument step by step
Let P = 'Non-existence is the original principle'. The author assumes P is false. Since P and its contradictory (not-P, i.e., 'existence is the principle') are contradictories, if P is false, not-P must be true. Therefore, existence is the principle for what does not have a temporal beginning, i.e., the Necessary Existent.
This is a valid immediate inference by contradiction.
4
Apply the method to another theological claim
Choose a claim in Islamic theology that can be proven by contradiction, such as the impossibility of two gods. Assume the opposite (two gods) and derive a contradiction from their attributes (e.g., conflicting wills).
Practice ensures familiarity with the tool.

Burhan Limmi and Burhan Inni: Causal vs. Indicative Demonstration
Explains two types of demonstration: burhan limmi and burhan inni.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between burhan limmi and burhan inni by analyzing whether the middle term is the cause or merely a sign of the major term.

Core Idea
Burhan limmi explains why; burhan inni establishes that.

This passage introduces two fundamental kinds of demonstration in Islamic logic: burhan limmi (a priori), where the middle term is the cause, and burhan inni (a posteriori), where it is a sign. Recognizing the difference helps you evaluate the strength of arguments in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term in the syllogism
In the given Arabic examples, locate the term that appears in both premises but is absent from the conclusion. This is the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat).
For the first example (mercury), the middle term is 'معدن' (metal). For the second (Abu Lahab), it is 'خالد في النار' (eternal in the Fire).
2
Check if the middle term is the cause of the major term
Ask: Does the middle term explain why the major term applies? In burhan limmi, the answer is yes – the middle term is the 'illa (cause). In burhan inni, the middle term is not the cause; it is a sign (dalil).
In the mercury example, being a metal (middle) causes expansion with heat (major). In the Abu Lahab example, being eternally in the Fire (middle) does not cause dying in disbelief (major); rather, the opposite holds.
3
Determine the direction of causality
If the major term is actually the cause of the middle term, then the syllogism is burhan inni. If the middle term is the cause of the major term, it is burhan limmi.
In the Abu Lahab example, dying in disbelief (major) causes eternity in the Fire (middle), so it is inni.
4
Classify the syllogism as limmi or inni
Based on steps 2 and 3, assign the type. This classification affects how you interpret the argument's conclusion.
Always verify with the text's own labels: the author explicitly calls the mercury example 'burhan limmi' and the Abu Lahab example 'burhan inni'.

Commentary
This passage defines and distinguishes two types of demonstration (burhan): burhan limmi (a priori) and burhan inni (a posteriori). The author begins by explaining the name 'burhan limmi', which is derived from the interrogative 'lima' (why), because in such a demonstration the middle term gives the cause for the major term. The example given: 'Why does God not forgive him? Because he died as a polytheist.' The middle term (dying as a polytheist) is the cause of the major term (not being forgiven). Another example: 'Mercury is a metal, and every metal expands with heat, so mercury expands with heat.' Here, the middle term 'metal' is the cause of 'expansion with heat'. Such syllogisms are called 'qiyas al-'illa' (causal syllogism) or 'burhan limmi'.

Then the author discusses the second type, where the middle term is not the cause but merely an indication (dalil) that the major term holds for the minor term. The example: 'Abu Lahab is eternally in the Fire, and everyone eternally in the Fire has died as a disbeliever, so Abu Lahab died as a disbeliever.' In this case, the middle term 'eternity in the Fire' is not the cause of 'dying in disbelief'; rather, it is the opposite: dying in disbelief causes eternity in the Fire. Yet the fact that someone is eternally in the Fire indicates that they died as a disbeliever, without giving the cause. This type is called 'qiyas al-dalala' by jurists and 'burhan inni' by logicians. The name 'inni' is taken from the emphatic particle 'inna' (indeed), because the affirmation of the middle term strongly indicates the major term.

The author ends with another example: 'Everything changing has no permanence, and everything that has no permanence is temporally originated' (the conclusion is not fully stated). This example illustrates the burhan inni where the middle term 'no permanence' is not the cause of 'temporally originated' (rather, being temporally originated is the cause of having no permanence).

Core Idea
Burhān is the gold standard of proof: certain premises in a valid form yield certain knowledge.

Burhan al-Tatbiq: Refuting Infinite Regress with Conditional Logic
Author explains infinite regress and provides the Burhan al-Tatbiq to prove its impossibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Burhan al-Tatbiq as a modus tollens argument and evaluate its validity against a material example from Islamic Kalam.

Core Idea
Infinite regress fails because it forces the absurd conclusion that the lesser equals the greater.

The Burhan al-Tatbiq is one of the most celebrated arguments in Islamic logic and philosophy, used to prove the impossibility of an infinite chain of causes. It is a classic example of a conditional syllogism in the form of modus tollens. In this lesson, we will unpack its logical structure and see why it is considered demonstrative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premise
Find the 'if...then' statement. The passage says: 'If this regress were permissible, the lesser number would equal the greater number.' This is the conditional (If P, then Q).
The antecedent (P) is 'regress is permissible', the consequent (Q) is 'lesser equals greater'.
2
Identify the denial of the consequent
The passage then asserts: 'But the lesser number is by no means equal to the greater number.' This negates the consequent (not Q).
This is the crucial step: we deny what the conditional claims would happen.
3
Conclude the denial of the antecedent
From 'If P then Q' and 'not Q', we validly infer 'not P'. The passage concludes: 'Therefore, the regress is not permissible.' This is modus tollens.
Modus tollens is a valid form: if the consequent is false, the antecedent must be false.
4
Visualize the proof with two chains
Imagine two infinite causal chains: one from the present, another starting a million years earlier. Align them link by link backward. Since both are infinite, they have the same number of links, but intuitively one is longer. This contradiction shows the assumption of infinite regress is impossible.
The visualization makes the abstract contradiction concrete.
5
Check logical validity
The argument form is modus tollens, which is valid regardless of content. The proof's strength lies in the impossibility of the consequent (lesser equalling greater) being a clear absurdity.
This proof is not merely plausible; it is logically binding.

When Lack of Finding Justifies Negation: The Conditions of Valid Negative Inference
Author qualifies the principle with conditions for valid negative inference and provides Quranic proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions under which 'lack of finding' can legitimately imply 'lack of existence' and apply them to the case of Allah's knowledge.

Core Idea
Only exhaustive knowledge or certain proof legitimizes inferring non-existence from non-discovery.

The previous lesson established that lack of finding does not automatically mean non-existence. But are there exceptions? This lesson explores the precise conditions under which the inference becomes valid. The author identifies two: comprehensive certain knowledge (like Allah's knowledge) or a certain proof of non-existence. The Quranic verse cited provides a divine example where Allah's denial of knowing any intercessors proves their non-existence.

Guided Path

Find where the author explicitly states when 'lack of finding' can imply non-existence. The phrase is: 'لا يصح إلا إذا...' Extract the exact Arabic condition.
This step builds precise textual awareness.
2
Understand the first condition: comprehensive certain knowledge
What does 'الإحاطة الشاملة القطعية' mean? How does it apply to Allah? Explain why Allah's knowledge meets this condition, while human knowledge typically does not.
3
Understand the second condition: certain proof
What is an example of a 'دليل قطعي' that would prove non-existence? The passage mentions the Quranic verse as such proof. How does the argument work?
4
Analyze the Quranic proof

This connects epistemology to tafsir.

Why do human researchers rarely meet these conditions? Provide a scenario where a human claim of non-existence might be valid (e.g., a person genuinely knows every item in a small room and declares a specific object absent).
Distinguishes divine and human epistemic limits.
