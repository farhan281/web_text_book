Four Methods of Responding to a Naqd (Objection)
Author enumerates four possible responses to a logical objection in dialectical reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish and apply the four standard responses to a naqd (objection) in Islamic dialectical reasoning, using real examples from jadal practice.

Core Idea
In jadal, the defender has four tools to block a naqd: clarify the proof, deny the missing conclusion, deny absurd entailment, or show the impossibility is harmless.

When a questioner presents a naqd — a counterexample that appears to refute the defender's proof — the defender must respond effectively. This passage from the Dawabit al-Ma'rifa enumerates four classic responses. Understanding these responses is essential for anyone engaging in rigorous dialectical debate (munazara) in Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Open the source Arabic and identify the four numbered responses. Note the Arabic terms for each response.
Focus on the structure: the numbers 2, 3, 4 indicate the responses.
2
Identify the defender's action for each response
For each response, determine what the defender (al-mu'allil) does: clarifies intent (taḥrīr), denies the missing conclusion, denies absurd entailment, or denies impossibility.
Response 1 is implied at the start: clarifying the intended meaning of the proof.
3
Understand the conditions for each response
Note when each response is appropriate: responses 1 and 2 address the conclusion not following; responses 3 and 4 address alleged absurd consequences.
Response 4 involves distinguishing between vicious and harmless circularity.
4
Apply to a concrete jadal scenario
Take a naqd from usul al-fiqh: the objection that a qiyas on intoxication applies to date-wine (nabidh) which some permit. Choose the appropriate response and justify.
Practice defending the proof using one of the four methods.

Commentary
This passage enumerates four possible responses that a defender (al-mu'allil) can use when faced with a logical objection (naqd) from a questioner (al-sā'il) in dialectical reasoning. The four methods are: (1) clarify the intended meaning of the proof, thereby showing that the objection does not apply because the conclusion truly follows; (2) deny that the conclusion is absent in the counterexample, claiming instead that the conclusion is present; (3) deny that the proof entails an absurd consequence (muḥāl); (4) deny that the supposed absurdity (e.g., circular reasoning) is actually impossible, by distinguishing between types of circularity (e.g., simultaneous vs. temporal). The author notes that in some cases, the defender may resort to 'taḥrīr al-murād' (clarifying the intended meaning) of either the proof or the claim. This is a standard dialectical technique: by precisely defining terms, the defender can show that the objection misses the intended scope. For example, if the questioner objects that the proof also applies to a case where the conclusion is false, the defender might redefine the claim to exclude that case. The fourth response (denying impossibility) is particularly relevant when the questioner alleges a contradiction or infinite regress; the defender may argue that the alleged absurdity is not truly impossible, such as when the circularity is harmless (al-dawr al-mu'ayy) rather than vicious (al-sabqī). These four responses are foundational in Islamic dialectic (jadal) and are used to either block the objection or show that it does not refute the proof.

Key Terms
النقض (al-naqd): A logical objection in which the questioner presents a counterexample where the proof's condition is met but the conclusion is false. [Note: In earlier works like al-Isaghuji, naqd is not a central term; in later jadal texts it becomes a standard procedure.]
الدليل (al-dalīl): The proof or argument presented by the defender to support the claim.
المدلول (al-madlūl): The conclusion or purport of the proof; what the proof is intended to establish.
الدعوى (al-da'wā): The claim or thesis that the defender is trying to prove.
المحال (al-muḥāl): An impossibility or absurdity, such as a logical contradiction or infinite regress. [Note: In philosophical contexts, muḥāl often refers to logical impossibility; in dialectic it is used for any unacceptable consequence.]
الدور المعي (al-dawr al-mu'ayy): Simultaneous or mutual interdependence that is not vicious, e.g., two things that logically require each other at the same time. [Note: Distinguished from al-dawr al-sabqī (temporal circularity) which involves a vicious regress. This distinction is common in later jadal works.]

Man' al-Naqd via Taḥrīr al-Murād
An example of naqd and man' al-naqd in geometric reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the dialectical tactic of man' al-naqd through taḥrīr al-murād in a jadal exchange.

Core Idea
A well-placed naqd collapses a proof; clarifying your intended meaning can block it.

In the art of jadal (dialectical debate), a questioner can demolish a proof by showing that it leads to a false conclusion—this is called naqd. But the defender is not defenseless. One powerful response is to clarify the intended meaning of the claim (taḥrīr al-murād), thereby showing that the so-called counterexample actually falls under the claim. This lesson walks through a classic example from geometry to illustrate this move.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defender's claim and proof
Locate the defender's position (mudda'i) and his proof (dalil). In the passage, the defender says a shape is a square because it is enclosed by four equal lines, and anything with four equal lines is a square.
The proof is a categorical syllogism in the first figure.
2
Identify the questioner's naqd
The questioner points out that the same proof would apply to a rhombus, which has four equal lines but is not a square (missing right angles). This is a genuine counterexample (naqd haqīqī mashhūr) because the condition holds but the conclusion fails.
The naqd is strong because it uses the defender's own reasoning.
3
Analyze the defender's response: man' al-naqd via taḥrīr al-murād
The defender does not deny that his proof applies to the rhombus. Instead, he redefines 'square' to mean any equilateral quadrilateral, regardless of angles. Thus, the rhombus is indeed a square by his definition, so the objection is blocked.
This is a legitimate tactic in jadal, but it may conflict with standard definitions.
4
Evaluate the effectiveness and pitfalls
The defender's move succeeds dialectically because it clarifies his original intent. However, if the opponent insists on the conventional definition, the debate may shift to a dispute over definitions. The tactic works best when the defender's redefinition is consistent with his original claim.
In Usul al-Fiqh, similar moves occur when a mujtahid clarifies the scope of a legal term.

Refuting a Proof by Infinite Regress: The Basmala Example
Second example of naqd with infinite regress and self-evident exemption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a naqd based on infinite regress and analyse the defender's response of denying absurd entailment via self-evident exemption.

Core Idea
A proof leading to infinite regress is invalid unless the first instance is self-evidently exempt.

This passage continues our exploration of naqd (refutation) in dialectical debate. Here, the questioner attacks a defender's proof by showing it leads to an absurd infinite regress. The defender's counter-move is not to accept the regress but to deny that the proof actually entails it, introducing the crucial concept of self-evident exemption (al-istithnā' al-badāhah). Understanding this exchange sharpens your ability to both launch and parry objections in Islamic scholarly debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defender's claim and proof
Locate the defender's initial claim (muddaʿā) and the evidence (dalīl) he provides. Note that the proof is based on a hadith about the basmala for important matters.
The claim is that useful compositions must begin with basmala; the proof is the hadith.
2
Extract the questioner's objection (naqd)
The questioner argues that the proof leads to infinite regress (tasalsul). Understand why: if every important matter needs a basmala, then the basmala itself, being important, would need another basmala, and so on.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Analyse the defender's response
The defender does not accept the regress. He denies that the proof entails an absurdity, claiming that the basmala is self-evidently exempt from the hadith's generality. This exemption is known by necessity (ḍarūra), so no explicit text is needed.
The response uses the third method of replying to naqd: manʿ al-istilzām (denial of entailment).
4
Evaluate the exchange
Consider whether the defender's response is persuasive. In jadal, a self-evident exemption is accepted as a valid counter. The questioner's naqd fails because the regress only follows if the basmala is included, but it is not.
This shows that not all infinite regresses are vicious if the first element is exempt by nature.

Commentary
This passage provides a second concrete example of a naqd and the defender's response, this time using the third method (denying the absurd entailment). The defender claims that useful compositions must begin with the basmala, based on a hadith that every important matter should begin with basmala. The questioner objects that this leads to an infinite regress (tasalsul): if the basmala itself is an important matter, then it would need its own basmala, and so on infinitely. This is a classic absurdity. The defender's answer is to deny that the proof entails an absurdity. He argues that the basmala is self-evidently (badāhatan) exempt from the general ruling; it is the starting point of all important matters, so it does not require another basmala. This exemption is known by necessity (ḍarūra), so there is no need to state it in the proof. Thus, the apparent regress is not real because the basmala is not subject to the same requirement. This illustrates the method of denying the entailment of absurdity (man' al-istilzām). The defender's move is to show that the absurdity only follows if we include the basmala under the general rule, but the basmala is an exception by default, so the entailment fails.

Answering a Genuine Objection by Clarifying Intended Meaning
Author shows how a mu'allil refutes a naqd haqiqi by clarifying his definition of square

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze how a mu'allil refutes a naqd ḥaqīqī by clarifying the intended meaning of a key term, and apply this strategy in a jadal scenario.

Core Idea
Clarifying a term's scope can block a counterexample without conceding the claim.

In jadal, a sā'il may bring a genuine objection (naqd ḥaqīqī) that appears to invalidate the mu'allil's proof. The mu'allil has a powerful response: he can accept the specific instance but clarify that his claim was never about that instance, because his terms mean something different than what the objector assumed. This lesson dissects such an exchange, using the definition of 'square' as a case study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (naqd ḥaqīqī)
The sā'il claims that the mu'allil's proof fails because his general rule does not hold in a specific instance. Find that instance in the passage.
The objection is that the 'square' in question lacks right angles, so the proof's claim is contradicted.
2
Note the mu'allil's initial concession (taslīm)
The mu'allil does not deny that his proof applies to the specific case. He says 'I grant that my proof applies to the specific case.' This shows he accepts the factual scenario.
Taslīm is a strategic move to show integrity, then pivots to man'.
3
Extract the man' response and the clarified definition
The mu'allil blocks the objection by redefining the key term 'square'. He says he means any shape with four equal sides, regardless of right angles. This makes the objector's counterexample irrelevant.
The clarification is a form of man' (denial) because it shows the objection does not actually attack the claim as intended.
4
Assess why the objection fails
The objection fails because it relies on a narrower definition of 'square' (with right angles) that the mu'allil never intended. Once the intended meaning is clarified, the counterexample no longer conflicts with the claim.
This strategy works only if the clarification is consistent with the original claim and not a mere evasion.

Answering Infinite Regress with Badāhah Exemption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the dialectical move of man' (denial) by claiming self-evident exemption (badāhah) to refute an objection of infinite regress.

Core Idea
Common sense can exempt a case from a rule without breaking the proof.

In jadal, an opponent may argue that your proof leads to an infinite regress (tasalsul), making it invalid. However, you can deny the entailment by showing that the problematic case is self-evidently exempt from the rule. This lesson examines how the mu'allil uses badāhah to block the objection of tasalsul regarding the basmala.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and evidence
Find the mu'allil's claim: beneficial compositions must begin with the basmala. His evidence is a hadith about every important matter starting with the basmala.
2
Spot the objection (naqḍ)
The questioner objects that the basmala itself is an important matter, so the rule would require the basmala to be preceded by another basmala, leading to infinite regress. This is a genuine objection (naqḍ ḥaqīqī) because it shows the proof would entail an impossibility.
3
Analyse the mu'allil's response (man')
The mu'allil denies the entailment (luzūm). He argues that the basmala is exempted by common sense (badāhah) from the generality of the hadith. It is self-evident that the basmala does not need its own basmala, so no regress occurs.
This is called man' bi-bayān al-murād min al-dalīl – preventing the objection by clarifying the intended meaning of the proof.
4
Evaluate the strength of the response
Check whether the exemption is truly self-evident. In this case, it is reasonable: the basmala is the formula itself, so asking for its own basmala would be absurd. Thus, the mu'allil's response successfully blocks the objection without needing explicit textual proof.
If the exemption were not obvious, the mu'allil would need additional evidence.

Distinguishing Temporal and Simultaneous Circularity in Dialectic

Core Idea
Not all circularity is vicious: simultaneous relational dependence is permissible.

In dialectical debates, an opponent often tries to invalidate an argument by claiming it leads to circularity. However, not every circular dependence is logically impossible. This lesson examines a classic exchange where a mu'allil uses the distinction between temporal and simultaneous circularity to defend his proof. By mastering this distinction, you will be able to respond to common circularity objections in Islamic theological and jurisprudential debates.

First Steps in Disputation on Assent
Author outlines the first steps of a munazara regarding tasdiq, including handling ambiguous or unfamiliar terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recall and describe the first procedural steps in a munazara concerning tasdiq, focusing on how to handle ambiguous or obscure terms.

Core Idea
Clarity of terms precedes substantive argument in disputation.

Before any disputation on a proposition (tasdiq) can proceed, the terms used must be clear. This passage from the 'Dawabit al-Ma'rifa' outlines the initial steps: the sa'il (questioner) checks for ambiguity or obscurity in the mu'allil's (reasoner's) statement and may request clarification. Understanding this procedure ensures that debates start on firm ground and do not get bogged down by unclear language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial check
The first step in any munazara concerning tasdiq is to examine the wording of the proposition presented by the mu'allil. Two faults are to be looked for: ambiguity (ijmal) and obscurity (gharaba).
2
Recognize the sa'il's right to inquire
If the sa'il perceives either fault, he has the right to request clarification (istifsar). This is not an objection to the content but a request for clarification of the terms.
3
Understand the mu'allil's denial and the sa'il's burden
The mu'allil may deny that his statement is ambiguous or obscure. If he denies, the sa'il must then prove the fault. For ambiguity, he must show that the word oscillates between two or more equally probable meanings.
Proving obscurity would involve showing the term is unfamiliar or rare.
4
Recall the mu'allil's two response strategies
Once the fault is proven or acknowledged, the mu'allil has two options: (1) provide an interpretation (tafsir) to clarify, or (2) defend his original wording by denying obscurity with well-known evidence, denying multiplicity of meanings, invoking a contextual indicator (qarina), or asserting that his intended meaning is the predominant usage.
These strategies allow the mu'allil to maintain his original wording if it can be shown to be clear.

Initial Steps: Clarification and Transmission in Tasdiq Debate
Author enumerates the step-by-step procedure for dialectical examination of an assenting proposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the first two steps of the dialectical procedure for debating an assent: checking wording for ambiguity/obscurity and verifying the transmitter's commitment.

Core Idea
Before debating truth, clarify wording and confirm narrator stance.

In Islamic dialectic (munazara), debating an assent (tasdiq) follows a structured sequence. The proposer (mu'allil) presents a proposition. The first step is to inspect its wording: is it ambiguous (mujmal) or obscure (gharib)? If so, the questioner may request clarification. The second step is to determine whether the proposer is merely narrating (naqil) from others or is committed to the truth of the content. This lesson covers these initial checks, which prevent misunderstandings and ensure productive debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mu'allil's proposition
Locate the assent being debated. The mu'allil presents it as the subject of the dialectical exchange.
2
Check for ambiguity (ijmal) or obscurity (gharaba)
The questioner examines the wording. If ambiguous or obscure, he may request clarification (istifsar). He must then prove the ambiguity or obscurity (e.g., by showing multiple equal possibilities).
The mu'allil may deny ambiguity by providing evidence like common usage or contextual indicators.
3
Examine the mu'allil's response to clarification
The mu'allil can either explain (tafsir) if he concedes ambiguity, or deny it by offering proof (e.g., well-known usage, predominant meaning).
4
Determine the mu'allil's role: narrator or claimant
If the mu'allil is just narrating (naqil), the questioner can only demand verification of transmission (tashih al-naql). If he is committed to truth (claimant), the debate proceeds to substantive evaluation.
Special cases: if the questioner already knows the transmission is sound, or if the content is self-evident or a necessity of the school, verification cannot be demanded.

Evaluating the Assent: Self-Evidence, Proof, and Objection Functions
Author enumerates the step-by-step procedure for dialectical examination of an assenting proposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the third step of the dialectical procedure: determine whether a proposition is clearly self-evident, theoretical, or implicitly self-evident; and use the appropriate objection functions (man' and mu'arada) when evidence is provided.

Core Idea
Self-evident truths need no proof; theoretical claims may be challenged by demanding proof or by counter-claim.

Once the wording and transmission are settled, the questioner evaluates the assent itself. The passage distinguishes three categories: clearly self-evident (badihi jaliyy), theoretical (nazari), and implicitly self-evident (badihi khafiyy). For theoretical or implicitly self-evident propositions, the questioner may demand evidence if none is given, or if evidence is provided, may object using two specific functions: 'man'' (demanding proof for a premise) and 'mu'arada' (counter-claim). This lesson covers these options and their proper use within the dialectical framework.

Guided Path
1
Classify the assent type
The questioner determines whether the proposition is clearly self-evident (badihi jaliyy, e.g., 'the whole is greater than its part'), theoretical (nazari, requiring proof), or implicitly self-evident (badihi khafiyy, self-evident upon reflection).
2
If clearly self-evident, submit without objection
If the proposition is badihi jaliyy, the questioner must accept it. Denying it is obstinacy (mukabara), which is not permissible for truth-seekers.
Example: 'One is half of two' is clearly self-evident.
3
If theoretical or implicitly self-evident, check for evidence
The questioner checks whether the mu'allil has provided evidence (dalil) or at least an alert (tanbih) to the evidence. If not, proceed to step 4; if yes, proceed to step 5.
4
No evidence: use man' to demand proof
If no evidence is given, the questioner may demand evidence (man' al-dalil). He must not provide counter-evidence himself (ghasb), as that is prohibited.
5
Evidence provided: apply man' on a premise or mu'arada
If evidence is presented, the questioner may object by: (1) demanding proof for a specific premise (man' al-muqaddima), provided the mu'allil hasn't already proven it; or (2) presenting a counter-claim (mu'arada) to invalidate the original claim. Core Idea
Not all claims in a debate require proof; self-evident truths are accepted immediately.

The Three Functions of the Questioner: Man', Mu'arada, and Naqd
Author lists and defines the three functions of the questioner: prevention, counter-argument, and refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the three functions of the questioner—man', mu'arada, and naqd—and identify when each is appropriate in a debate.

Core Idea
The questioner has three precise tools to challenge an argument: demand proof, counter with evidence, or show the argument fails.

Once the respondent has provided evidence for his claim, the questioner may object using three distinct functions. Each function targets a different weakness in the respondent's argument. This lesson unpacks these tools, which are essential for any student of munazara to master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the respondent's argument structure
The respondent has presented evidence for a claim. Break down the evidence into premises and conclusion. Identify the specific premise or the overall reasoning.
2
Consider applying al-man' (demand for proof)
If the respondent has not provided evidence for a particular premise within his argument, the questioner may demand that evidence. This is a direct request for proof, not an assertion that the premise is false.
3
Consider applying al-mu'arada (counter-argument)
The questioner may bring a counter-claim that is the opposite of the respondent's claim, or equivalent to its opposite, or more specific, and support it with evidence. This can target the evidence itself or the cause ('illa).
4
Consider applying al-naqd (refutation by consequence)
The questioner shows that the respondent's evidence fails: either its consequence does not follow, or it leads to an absurdity (muhal). This can be real (haqiqi) or apparent (shabihi).

Core Idea
Musadara is a disguised tautology: the conclusion is the premise, just reworded.

Musadara, often translated as 'circular reasoning' or 'begging the question', is a prohibited tactic in Islamic dialectic (jadal). The author warns that it deceives the opponent by making the conclusion appear new when it is merely a repetition of a premise with different words. Recognizing musadara is crucial for anyone who wants to engage in honest argumentation and defend the truth without resorting to sophistry.

Grasp the definition of musadara

Quranic Debate: Conditional Refutation via Lazim Naqid
Author analyzes a Quranic debate using jadal terms to refute a false claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional argument (modus tollens) used in the Quranic refutation of the Jews' and Christians' claim and identify the roles of mu'allil and sa'il.

Core Idea
The Quran refutes false claims not by denial, but by proving their necessary opposite.

In this lesson, we examine a Quranic debate where the Prophet is instructed to refute the Jews' and Christians' claim of being God's beloved children. The author analyzes this refutation using the dialectical method of man' muqrin bi al-sanad al-qat'i, which involves proving the lazima naqid (necessary opposite) of the claim. Understanding this method is crucial for recognizing how logical argumentation is employed in the Quran and how it can be used in Islamic scholarly debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claimant and the Claim
Find the mu'allil and his mad'ā in the passage. The Jews and Christians claim: 'We are the children of Allah and His beloved.'
This is the initial thesis to be refuted.
2
Identify the Objector and the Rejection Method
The sa'il (Prophet and every Muslim) responds with 'mamnu'' (rejected), but this rejection is not a bare denial; it is supported by a definitive proof: the Quranic question 'Why then does He punish you for your sins?'
The rejection is al-man' al-muqrin bi al-sanad al-qat'i.
3
Extract the Conditional Premise
The question implies a conditional: 'If you were children of Allah and His beloved, then He would not punish you for your sins.' This is the implied antecedent-consequent relation.
This conditional is the core of the argument.
4
Formulate the Modus Tollens Argument
The observed fact is that Allah does punish them for their sins (denial of the consequent). Therefore, by modus tollens, the antecedent is false: 'You are not children of Allah and His beloved.'
This proves the necessary opposite (lazim naqid) of the claim.
5
Conclude the Refutation
Since the necessary opposite is established, the original claim is false. Thus the Quran refutes them logically, not merely rhetorically.
The argument is valid and sound given the observed punishment.

Identifying Sophistry in Dialectic: Nimrod's Equivocation
Author explains Nimrod's sophistry and its nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of equivocation in a dialectical exchange and explain why it constitutes sophistry.

Core Idea
Nimrod's sophistry equivocates on 'giving life' to evade Ibrahim's proof.

In the Quranic debate, Nimrod claims to give life and death by killing and sparing. But is that the same as what Ibrahim meant? This lesson explores the sophistical maneuver and how to spot it in Islamic dialectical contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original claim
Ibrahim says 'My Lord gives life and causes death.' Note that 'gives life' means creating life from lifeless matter, and 'causes death' means real removal of life. This is a claim about divine power.
2
Spot the sophistical objection
Nimrod says 'I give life and cause death' and illustrates by killing one prisoner and sparing another. Ask: does this action match Ibrahim's meaning?
3
Detect the equivocation
Nimrod replaces 'creation of life' with 'sparing' and 'real death' with 'killing'. This equivocation (ishtirak) makes his claim appear similar but is actually different. The fallacy is that two distinct meanings are treated as identical.
4
Understand why it is sophistry
Sophistry (maghalata) is an argument that seems valid but commits a fallacy, aiming to deceive. Here, Nimrod's argument superficially refutes Ibrahim but fails because the terms are ambiguous.

Ibrahim's Debate with Namrud: Fallacy and Strategic Shift in Jadal
Commentary analyzes the jadal exchange between Ibrahim and Namrud with fallacy and shift.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mughalata (fallacy) in a jadal exchange by analyzing the disanalogy between literal creation and instrumental causation, and explain why Ibrahim's shift to the second argument is a winning dialectical move.

Core Idea
A dialectical victory requires not only refuting the opponent’s fallacy but moving to grounds your proof cannot be challenged.

This lesson takes you into the heart of a Qur'anic jadal (dialectical debate) between Prophet Ibrahim and King Namrud. By studying this exchange, you will learn how to spot a classic fallacy—equivocation—and see how a skilled proponent can sidestep a sophistical objection by shifting to an irrefutable argument. This tool is essential for anyone who needs to defend truth against deceptive reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proponent's original proof
Ibrahim's first proof (ta'lil) is: 'My Lord is the One who gives life and causes death.' This implies that genuine lordship requires the power to create life and death ex nihilo. Find this in the Arabic: رَبِّيَ الَّذِي يُحْيِي وَيُمِيتُ.
The proof relies on the attribute of creating life and death, not on merely causing death by killing.
2
Identify the objector's fallacy (mughalata)
Namrud claims 'I give life and cause death' by killing one prisoner and sparing another. This is a fallacy of equivocation (ishtirak lafzi): he conflates 'causing death' with 'creating death' and 'sparing life' with 'creating life'. Locate this in the text: أَنَا أُحْيِي وَأُمِيتُ.
The commentary explicitly calls it a مغالطة.
3
Explain why the fallacy fails
Namrud's actions are merely instrumental; they do not involve creation of life or death. The commentary says: إبراهيم يريد خلق الحياة في المادة غير الحية. Ibrahim means creating life in non-living matter, which Namrud cannot do.
The fallacy exploits the ambiguity of 'giving life and causing death'.
4
Analyze Ibrahim's strategic shift
Instead of exposing the fallacy directly, Ibrahim moves to a second proof: 'Allah brings the sun from the east; so bring it from the west.' This argument is irrefutable by Namrud because it involves a cosmic act impossible for a human. The commentary says: انتقل إلى دليل آخر مماثل لا يستطيع النمروذ أن يغالط فيه.
The shift is a tactic: force the opponent onto ground where he cannot produce a counterexample.
5
Assess the outcome
Namrud is confounded (بُهت) and cannot respond, resorting to violence. The debate is won by Ibrahim. Reflect on why the second argument succeeded where the first was vulnerable.
A jadal ends when the sa'il is unable to refute the proof (al-sa'il yabhutu).

Prophet's Refutation by Counterexample
Commentary analyzes the jadal exchange where the Prophet refutes Jewish claim that God sent nothing to humans.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the dialectical move of 'manʿ' (prevention) using a counterexample in a jadal exchange.

Core Idea
A universal denial is refuted by a single accepted counterexample.

This lesson presents a Qur'anic example of dialectical argument (jadal) where the Prophet Muhammad (SAW) refutes the Jews' claim that God sent nothing to any human. The commentary analyzes how the Prophet's response constitutes a 'manʿ' (prevention) using the Torah, which the Jews themselves accept, as a counterexample. You will learn how to identify this refutation technique and apply it in theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proponent's claim (mudda'i)
Find the claim being made by the Jew. It is a universal negative: 'God has not sent down anything to any human.' This is the statement to be refuted.
The proponent is called al-muʿallil.
2
Locate the questioner's response (sā'il)
The Prophet (SAW) asks: 'Who sent down the Book that Moses brought?' This is not a request for information but a dialectical move.
The questioner (sā'il) here performs the refutation.
3
Recognize the counterexample
The Torah is a book sent down to a human (Moses). The Jews believe in the Torah as revelation. This particular instance contradicts the universal denial.
The counterexample is admitted by the opponent, making the refutation decisive.
4
Understand the dialectical move: manʿ (prevention)
The Prophet's response 'prevents' the claim by showing it is false. In jadal terminology, this is a 'manʿ' – rejecting the claim by pointing out an inconsistency or counter-instance.
The commentary says the response includes a 'definitive evidence' (sanad qaṭʿī).
5
Analyze the logical structure
The claim is 'No human has received revelation.' The counterexample is 'Moses received revelation.' Therefore the original claim is false. This is a simple refutation by counterexample.
The logical form: Universal negative vs. particular affirmative.

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical refutation (naqd) of a claim. The author argues that the opponent's own belief in the revelation of the Torah to Moses contradicts their assertion that God never sent down anything to any human. This serves as a model for dealing with dialectical opponents: after exposing the contradiction, one may disengage.
