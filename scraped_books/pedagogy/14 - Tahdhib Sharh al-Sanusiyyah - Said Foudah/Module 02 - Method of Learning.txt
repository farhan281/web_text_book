Commentary
This passage is a diagnosis of a contemporary problem and a call to action. The author emphasizes the great importance of the science of Tawḥīd (ʿilm al-tawḥīd) and insists that Muslims, especially in modern times, must increase their attention to it. The reason is twofold: first, the general public, and even those claiming to be specialists, no longer know their definitive creed (al-qaṭʿī min ʿaqīdatihim). Evidence that is acceptable in creed may not be acceptable in jurisprudence, and vice versa. The author then broadens the problem: the confusion is not limited to these two sciences but extends to all sharʿī sciences, such as principles of jurisprudence, taṣawwuf, ḥadīth, etc. Because this confusion is a sin (ithm), the author declares that it is obligatory (wājib) on anyone who has the ability to emerge from this sin to do so and to help others emerge from sin and error. This sets the stage for his own work in producing this book.

Commentary
This passage is an exhortation and lament about the current state of Sunni scholars. The author expresses astonishment that scholars of Ahl al-Sunna are now afraid to defend the creed against the materialistic anthropomorphic thought that has spread. Despite witnessing the dangerous infiltration, they remain passive and do not dare to speak out. The author contrasts this with the past, when Sunni scholars were leaders in criticism and refutation of innovators. He diagnoses the cause: their fear stems from genuine ignorance of their own madhhab and its principles. Most contemporary shaykhs are content with a summary (ijmāl) knowledge of the doctrines, not delving into the details of this noble science, which the verifying scholars have declared the foundation of all other sciences. The author criticizes this as reducing themselves to the level of the common people, who are only required to know the basics. He argues that such scholars cannot even be sure that their own beliefs are true, let alone identify the errors of others, because they lack certain knowledge (ʿilm al-yaqīn) and rely on mere conjecture (ẓann).

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by the author against contemporary scholars who fail to refute the Mujassimah (anthropomorphists). It functions as both a refutation of their excuses and a defense of the necessity of open criticism in 'ilm al-tawhid. The author begins by stating that the issue is about justifying these scholars' silence regarding the doctrine of the Mujassimah, whom he singles out because they are dominant in many Islamic lands. He then lists their excuses: first, that this is the era of the Mujassimah and they hold material power; second, that refuting them might cause a fitna; third, that the followers are unaware of their shaykhs' anthropomorphism, so one should guide them gently rather than refute them. The author refutes each by pointing out that even if the followers are ignorant, their beliefs are still false, and it is necessary to distinguish truth from falsehood and refute the leaders. He further criticizes scholars who claim to be Ahl al-Sunnah yet make excuses for the Mujassimah, arguing that such excuses do not absolve anyone from declaring falsehood false or from refuting it. He then emphasizes a fundamental principle: 'ilm al-tawhid requires examination of evidence (duh) and does not accept blind following (taqlid) of ancestors. Quoting the Quranic theme of following forefathers, he asserts that believing falsehood from one's parents does not change the truth. The author then gives his own analysis: the stance of these scholars is due to fear of being refuted themselves and ignorance of true Sunni beliefs. He concludes by stating the method of Ahl al-Sunnah: warning against errors of anyone except the Prophet, and that criticism is the foundation of tawhid because taqlid is not allowed for those capable of tahqiq. They do this to strengthen their own adherence to truth, seeking only Allah's pleasure.

Commentary
This passage is a lengthy footnote discussing the nature of knowledge of God (maʿrifat Allāh). The commentator begins by acknowledging that some scholars allow that knowledge of God may occur in some individuals through inspiration (ilhām), but it cannot be universal. For most people, knowledge of God is theoretical (naẓariyya), requiring reflection (naẓar). He then argues strongly against the claim that knowledge of God is innate (fiṭra), i.e., present at birth. The commentator then criticizes the Hashwiyya (a group often associated with anthropomorphism) for claiming that knowledge of God is only innate. Their motive, he says, is not to describe reality but to oppose the methods of rational inference used by the orthodox (ahl al-ḥaqq), because such rational arguments undermine their corporealist (tajsīm) doctrines. He accuses them of trying to turn people away from intellect and rational thinking.

The argument is that the rational objection that these attributes' opposites are deficiencies only holds for creatures, not for Allah. Therefore, scholars did not rely solely on reason for these attributes but followed the transmitted texts. This guards against the misunderstanding that rational proof is universally decisive for all attributes.
