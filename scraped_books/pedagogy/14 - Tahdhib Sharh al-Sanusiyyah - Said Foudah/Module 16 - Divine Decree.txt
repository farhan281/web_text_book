Involuntary Human Actions: Creation by God and Removal of Obligation
Defines involuntary actions and their properties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of involuntary actions, that God alone creates them, and that obligation is removed from the person compelled to perform them.

Core Idea
Involuntary actions are created by God; the person has no choice and no moral obligation.

Actions are divided into two categories: involuntary and voluntary. Involuntary actions, such as trembling from illness, occur without any choice or power on the part of the person. This lesson establishes that these actions are directly created by God, and that the compelled person is not held accountable for them.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the translation. Notice the clear classification and the footnote that explains the meaning of being forced.
2
Identify the two types of actions
The passage begins by dividing actions into two kinds. Find the terms 'اضطرارية' and 'اختيارية' and note their distinction.
Involuntary actions are those without choice (اختيار) or power (قدرة).
3
Understand the definition of involuntary actions
The passage gives the example of trembling (ارتعاش) and explains that the person is compelled both outwardly and inwardly. Ensure you grasp the concept of being forced even in the inner reality.
The footnote reinforces that the person's will has no effect.
4
Reflect on the removal of obligation
The passage states that God has graciously lifted obligation (تكليف) from the compelled person. Ask yourself why this matters for understanding divine justice and human accountability.

Commentary
This passage classifies human actions into two types: involuntary (idtirariyya) and voluntary (ikhtiyariyya). Involuntary actions are those in which the person has no choice or power, such as shaking from a fever. The text emphasizes that the person is 'compelled' both outwardly and inwardly. There is consensus that God alone creates such actions, and by His grace He removes legal obligation from the compelled person. The footnote clarifies the meaning of being forced: even if the person wills not to tremble, the trembling still occurs, showing that his will has no effect.

The Process of Voluntary Action: Creation, Choice, and Kasb
Sanusi explains the creation of voluntary actions and introduces kasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine of how God creates voluntary actions in humans, how humans exercise choice, and the concept of kasb (acquisition) where the action is related to human power without causal effect.

Core Idea
God creates the action and power; the human chooses and acquires, but does not create.

Imam al-Sanusi begins by explaining the creation of voluntary actions. He describes how God brings the action to the servant's mind, the servant chooses it, and then God creates the action along with a power that accompanies it without causing it. This introduces the key Ash'ari concept of kasb, where the action is acquired by the human but created by God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the sequence of events: God brings to mind, human chooses, God creates action and power.
The passage includes footnotes; focus on the main text first.
2
Identify the three components
Identify: (1) God's bringing the action to mind, (2) human choice, (3) God's creation of action and power. Understand the order.
Human choice is a cause in the sense of a condition, not a creator.
3
Understand the nature of power
Note that the created power is an existential attribute related to the action but has no effect. Compare to knowledge, which relates without affecting.
This distinction is crucial for the doctrine of kasb.
4
Reflect on kasb
The conjunction of power, will, and action is called kasb. The human acquires the action, but God creates it.

He creates them in the living being, like the servant, and he has choice in them, and God creates with them power. The explanation of that is that if God wants to create a voluntary action in the servant, e.g., standing, sitting, or walking, He brings it to the servant's mind and makes the cause for its creation his choice of that action, i.e., his will for it and his inclination towards it. So when he chooses it and directs himself to it, God creates it in him and creates with it a power, which is an existential attribute that is related to the voluntary action, i.e., it is connected and associated with it without having any effect in it (2). Thus it is like the customary causes: God creates the action with it, i.e., along with it, not by it. And its being related to the voluntary action does not necessitate its effect in it, because an attribute may be related without effecting, like knowledge, which is a relating attribute and has no effect on what it relates to (3). That relation, I mean the relation of the originated power to the action. (1) Note here that the will of God is dependent on His knowledge that this servant wills this voluntary action, for He knows in His eternal knowledge the servant's will for this action, so He creates it for him by creating a will in the servant and a power concomitant with that will. (2) Rather, the conjunction between the originated power and the originated will and the originated action subsisting in the servant is called kasb or action, i.e., not on the way of creation, because creation is bringing into existence from nothing, and bringing into existence from nothing is only permissible for God. (3) The meaning of that is that if we say that the servant's power is related to the voluntary action, it does not necessitate that this power is what brought the action into existence, because this matter is only by analogy with God's power, and it is known that this is not permissible, i.e., comparison between God and servants in any matter. Therefore, as long as attributing creation and effect to the servant's power entails anthropomorphism, and we have affirmed that it is related to the action, then necessarily its relation must be in a manner other than creation and effect, and this manner is what Ahl al-Sunnah called kasb. And it is not permissible to say that our statement of the dissimilarity of the reality of the relation of the servant's power and the relation of God's power is contrary to reason, on the grounds that the relation of power must necessarily be on the way of effect. Because we say: Your claim that the relation of power absolutely must be on the way of effect comes only from your analogy of the servant's power to God's power, and this is false. The principle is no analogy and no common aspect between the two powers, and consequently between the two relations of the powers. This is like what we say about knowledge that God is described with: it relates to known objects in a relation that differs from the relation of originated knowledge to known objects. The relation of originated knowledge may be an impression or a quality, or an imprinting of an image in the soul, etc., and it may be by necessity or after consideration, to the end of the ways of differentiation between the two knowledges. And it is known that God's knowledge cannot be attributed with any of these concepts. Yet we call what occurs in us 'knowledge', and we call the attribute subsisting in God 'knowledge'. So despite our affirmation of dissimilarity between the two realities, we have allowed the same name for both, and this is not lexically far-fetched. For the existence of God is not equal to any other existence, yet you see that we apply the term 'existence' to ourselves and apply the term 'existence' to God. Similarly for permanence. And we are certain that all these attributes in God are different from what they are in us. So learn from this that what we claim regarding the originated power is not strange, and it = V.

Commentary
This passage explains the nature of voluntary human actions in Ash'ari theology. It begins by stating that God creates both the action and the human power (qudra) that accompanies it. The process: God brings the action to the servant's mind, and the servant chooses it; then God creates the action in him along with a power that is related to the action but has no causal effect. This relation is like the customary causes—God creates the action with the power, not by it. The argument that relation does not imply causation is supported by the example of knowledge, which relates to objects without affecting them. The three footnotes expand the doctrine: First, God's will is based on His eternal knowledge of the servant's choice. Second, the conjunction of power, will, and action is called kasb (acquisition)—the servant acquires the action, but God creates it. Third, a detailed refutation of the notion that power must entail creation by analogy with God; the author argues for two different types of relation, drawing an analogy with the usage of 'knowledge' and 'existence' for both God and creatures despite essential differences.

Defending Kasb: The Rational Argument and Analogy with Knowledge
Sanusi defends kasb by refuting the objection that power must cause its effect and provides an analogy with knowledge and existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational defense of the doctrine of kasb, including the refutation of the claim that power must entail effect and the analogy with the use of terms like knowledge and existence for both God and creatures.

Core Idea
The human power's relation to action is unlike God's; the same term can apply to essentially different realities.

This passage continues the discussion of kasb by addressing a potential objection. The author argues that the relation of human power to action does not imply causation, as that would be an invalid analogy with God's power. He then draws a parallel with the use of terms like knowledge and existence, which are applied to both God and creatures despite essential differences.

Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Look for the objection raised and its refutation.

The objector argues by analogy with God's power.
3

The author rejects the analogy between God and creatures. He argues that the two powers are essentially different, so their relations differ.
No common aspect exists between the two powers.
4

The author uses the example of knowledge: we use the same term for God's knowledge and human knowledge, yet they are different in reality. Similarly, 'power' can be used for both without implying similarity.
Also uses existence as an example.
5

Understand that the doctrine of kasb is not unreasonable; it is consistent with how we use language for God and creatures.

(1) Note here that the will of God is dependent on His knowledge that this servant wills this voluntary action, for He knows in His eternal knowledge the servant's will for this action, so He creates it for him by creating a will in the servant and a power concomitant with that will. (2) Rather, the conjunction between the originated power and the originated will and the originated action subsisting in the servant is called kasb or action, i.e., not on the way of creation, because creation is bringing into existence from nothing, and bringing into existence from nothing is only permissible for God. (3) The meaning of that is that if we say that the servant's power is related to the voluntary action, it does not necessitate that this power is what brought the action into existence, because this matter is only by analogy with God's power, and it is known that this is not permissible, i.e., comparison between God and servants in any matter. Therefore, as long as attributing creation and effect to the servant's power entails anthropomorphism, and we have affirmed that it is related to the action, then necessarily its relation must be in a manner other than creation and effect, and this manner is what Ahl al-Sunnah called kasb. And it is not permissible to say that our statement of the dissimilarity of the reality of the relation of the servant's power and the relation of God's power is contrary to reason, on the grounds that the relation of power must necessarily be on the way of effect. Because we say: Your claim that the relation of power absolutely must be on the way of effect comes only from your analogy of the servant's power to God's power, and this is false. The principle is no analogy and no common aspect between the two powers, and consequently between the two relations of the powers. This is like what we say about knowledge that God is described with: it relates to known objects in a relation that differs from the relation of originated knowledge to known objects. The relation of originated knowledge may be an impression or a quality, or an imprinting of an image in the soul, etc., and it may be by necessity or after consideration, to the end of the ways of differentiation between the two knowledges. And it is known that God's knowledge cannot be attributed with any of these concepts. Yet we call what occurs in us 'knowledge', and we call the attribute subsisting in God 'knowledge'. So despite our affirmation of dissimilarity between the two realities, we have allowed the same name for both, and this is not lexically far-fetched. For the existence of God is not equal to any other existence, yet you see that we apply the term 'existence' to ourselves and apply the term 'existence' to God. Similarly for permanence. And we are certain that all these attributes in God are different from what they are in us. So learn from this that what we claim regarding the originated power is not strange, and it = V.

Commentary
This passage explains the nature of voluntary human actions in Ash'ari theology. It begins by stating that God creates both the action and the human power (qudra) that accompanies it. The process: God brings the action to the servant's mind, and the servant chooses it; then God creates the action in him along with a power that is related to the action but has no causal effect. This relation is like the customary causes—God creates the action with the power, not by it. The argument that relation does not imply causation is supported by the example of knowledge, which relates to objects without affecting them. The three footnotes expand the doctrine: First, God's will is based on His eternal knowledge of the servant's choice. Second, the conjunction of power, will, and action is called kasb (acquisition)—the servant acquires the action, but God creates it. Third, a detailed refutation of the notion that power must entail creation by analogy with God; the author argues for two different types of relation, drawing an analogy with the usage of 'knowledge' and 'existence' for both God and creatures despite essential differences.

Commentary
This passage classifies the theological positions on human power (qudrah ḥādithah) and affirms the Sunni doctrine of kasb (acquisition). It states that there are three schools: (1) Ahl al-Sunnah: power exists but accompanies the act without causal effect. (2) Muʿtazila: power exists and is effective in voluntary acts. (3) Jabriyya: power does not exist at all. The Sunni view is correct; the other two are false. The Muʿtazila view is refuted by reason (the proof of unity shows no creator but Allah) and by revelation (Qurʾan 39:62). The Jabriyya view is refuted by the obvious difference between involuntary trembling and voluntary movement. The passage then concludes that both the servant's power and his action are created by Allah. The servant is accountable because he is given choice, even though his power has no real effect. It quotes a poem defining kasb as the accompaniment of power to the act without effect. Finally, it denies causal efficacy to all natural causes—fire, water, etc.—asserting that only Allah is the true agent.

And they differed over the definition of qadar (destiny). The Ash'aris said: It is Allah's bringing things into existence according to what preceded of His knowledge and will, so it is an attribute of action and it is originated. Commentary
This passage explains the differing definitions of qadar (decree) between the Ash'aris and Maturidis. Ash'aris define qadar as Allah's bringing things into existence according to His prior knowledge and will, making it a created attribute. Maturidis define it as Allah's determining each creature eternally by its limit, relating to knowledge and will, thus being eternal. The author suggests the difference may be verbal: one looks at the act of creation, the other at the eternal determination. Similarly, qada is defined differently: Maturidis see it as the act with precision (created), Ash'aris as the eternal will (uncreated). Some say they are the same, and the author notes an inversion of terms between the two schools.
