The Supreme Importance of the Science of Beliefs
Author declares the science of beliefs the most important and individually obligatory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the science of beliefs (ʿilm al-ʿaqāʾid) is considered the most important Islamic science and why it is an individual obligation upon every Muslim.

Core Idea
Knowing your creed is the most important duty; without it you cannot be a true Muslim.

This lesson begins with the author's opening praise and his declaration that the science of beliefs is the supreme Islamic science. He gives three reasons: it clarifies what you must believe, your place in the world, and what your religion requires of you. Learning this is not optional—it is an individual duty. He uses a powerful analogy to show that without knowing your creed, you cannot truly belong to your faith.

3
Reflect on the analogy
The author ends with an analogy: just as a communist cannot be a true communist without knowing his doctrine, a Muslim cannot be a true Muslim without knowing his creed. Ask yourself why this analogy strengthens the argument for obligation.

Core Idea
Ilm al-kalam transforms beliefs into certainties through irrefutable proofs.

Sanusi opens his treatise by establishing what ilm al-kalam is. This is not merely a historical label; it is a precise definition that distinguishes kalam from mere speculation or blind imitation. The passage explains why this science is necessary: to secure religious beliefs against all doubt. Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion on the distinction between a prophet (nabī) and a messenger (rasūl). The author begins by addressing a potential contradiction: if a prophet is defined as someone commanded to convey, then what about prophets not commanded? He presents a strong alternative: prophethood essentially means being a conveyor of divine matters, regardless of explicit command. This view is attributed to al-'Izz ibn 'Abd al-Salam. Al-'Izz defines a prophet as a human who receives revelation from Allah, while a messenger additionally receives a command to convey that revelation to people. Thus, conceptually, messengership is separate from prophethood, though in reality they are inseparable—every messenger is a prophet. A messenger possesses both ranks, a prophet only one. From this, al-'Izz concludes that the rank of prophethood is superior to the rank of messengership because prophethood is directly connected to Allah from both sides (receiving and being in His presence), while messengership is connected only from the receiving side (since he also interacts with people). However, he does not say the prophet is superior to the messenger, because the messenger also possesses prophethood, and having two virtues is better than one. The author defends al-'Izz against criticism. He then explains the rationale for a prophet not commanded to convey: some revelations concern only Allah's essence and names, intended for the prophet's own increase in knowledge, not needed by the general public. Such secrets are like what some messengers received but were not ordered to publicize. However, prophets are still required to announce their prophethood itself so people honor them. The author concludes by quoting al-Shabrakhiti, who prefers messengership over prophethood, but notes that this does not contradict al-'Izz's position when properly qualified.

Commentary
This passage resolves the dispute over whether actions are part of Iman. It starts by noting that the disagreement among Ahl al-Sunna is minor. Then it presents Imam al-Halimi's analysis from his book al-Minhaj. Al-Halimi argues that those who include actions in Iman rely on scriptural usages where acts like prayer are called 'Iman'. Those who conflate actions with the core definition of Iman have missed this linguistic nuance, leading to many corrupt consequences detailed elsewhere. The passage thus upholds the distinction while acknowledging that actions can be called 'Iman' in a secondary sense.

A person with inner faith must fulfill outward obligations; one who performs outward acts without inner faith is a disbeliever. A person with inner faith but lacking outward actions is not a disbeliever but a sinner. In practice, we judge outwardly based on the shahada. We cannot label a specific person as 'believer but not Muslim' or 'Muslim but not believer' because of the correlation. This nuanced view preserves both the conceptual distinction and the practical unity.

Explains the meaning of correlation (talāzum) and its practical application.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the legal correlation (talāzum) between Iman and Islam, its meaning, and how it applies to practical judgment of individuals.

Core Idea
Iman and Islam are legally inseparable: one without the other is insufficient in the sight of the Sacred Law.

This passage is an extended commentary on the footnote (1) from the previous section. Sanusi explains what it means for Iman and Islam to be 'correlated in Sacred Law' (mutalāzimān sharʿan). He clarifies that inner faith does not excuse one from outward obligations, nor do outward actions suffice without inner faith. He then gives the practical ruling for judging others.

Guided Path
1
Understand the meaning of correlation
Read the first sentence: correlation means that one does not suffice for the other. Inner faith alone does not excuse from outward duties, and outward actions without inner faith are invalid.
This is a key principle in Sunni theology.
2
Examine the two cases
Sanusi presents two scenarios: (1) a person with inner faith but no outward actions is a sinner, not a disbeliever; (2) a person with outward actions but no inner faith is a disbeliever. Note the asymmetry.
This shows the priority of inner faith.
3
Apply the practical ruling
In practice, we judge based on apparent signs like the shahada. If someone says the shahada but does not perform any Islamic acts, we consider them Muslim outwardly and leave their inner state to Allah.
We cannot label a specific person as 'Muslim but not believer' or the reverse.

ومفهوم الإسلام شرعاً امتثال الأوامر واجتناب النواهي لبناء العمل على ذلك بالإذعان بالقلب وهما مختلفان مفهوماً، وإن تلازما شرعاً بحيث لا يوجد مسلم ليس بمؤمن ولا مؤمن ليس بمسلم. ملاحظة: إن أريد بالإسلام الإذعان بالقلب والقبول والانقياد فهو مرادف للإيمان وإن أريد به عمل الجوارح مثل الصلاة والصوم والحج ولفظ الشهادتين وغير ذلك من الأعمال فهما متغايران لكنهما متلازمان شرعاً (1)، وهذا التفصيل هو الحق. وفسر الإيمان بأنه حديث النفس التابع للمعرفة، والمراد بحديث النفس الإذعان والانقياد والقبول والتسليم الباطني كله معنى واحدا، فتصديق النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم في كل ما جاء به من عند الله والتسليم له والإذعان والانقياد له بقلبه هو تصديق خاص لا مجرد نسبة النبي للصدق من غير انقياد وإذعان وقبول وتسليم، فعلى هذا يكون الإيمان والإسلام مترادفين. والحاصل أن تصديق النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم في كل ما جاء به من عند الله، فهو مع الإذعان بالقلب والانقياد هو إسلام وإيمان، وأما عمل الجوارح كالصلاة والصوم والنطق بالشهادتين وغير ذلك فهو الإسلام.

ثم قال: (1) معنى أنهما متلازمان شرعاً أي إن أحدهما لا يغني عن الآخر، فمن صدق بقلبه وانقاد انقياداً نفسانياً، فإن الشرع لم يُعفه من الواجبات الشرعية الأخرى نحو الصلاة والزكاة والحج وغيرها، وكذلك فمن صلى وقام بالأعمال المأمور هو بها ولم يحقق أصل التصديق في نفسه فعمله باطل، لكفره. وأما الأول أي من صدق ولم يعمل فليس بكافر، ولكنه عاص، وحسابه على الله. أما عندنا فإن لم نره يعمل أي عمل من أفعال الإسلام سمعناه يتشهد بالشهادتين، فإننا نحكم عليه بالكفر ظاهرا، أي فيما يظهر لنا، ونوكل باطنه إلى الله تعالى. وكذلك فإنه في الواقع لا يجوز أن نقول عن إنسان معين أنه مؤمن غير مسلم ولا أن نقول إنه مسلم غير مؤمن، ولا حتى مؤمن، فهذا هو معنى التلازم. (س)
And the meaning of Islam according to the Sacred Law is compliance with commands and avoidance of prohibitions, in order to base action upon that with inner submission (idhʿān bi-l-qalb). And they (Iman and Islam) are different in concept, even though they are correlated (mutalāzimān) according to the Sacred Law, such that there is no Muslim who is not a believer nor a believer who is not a Muslim. Note: If by Islam is meant inner submission, acceptance, and compliance, then it is synonymous with Iman; but if by it is meant the works of the limbs such as prayer, fasting, pilgrimage, uttering the two testimonies, and other actions, then they are distinct but correlated according to the Sacred Law (1). This detail is the truth. And Iman is explained as 'inner speech' (ḥadīth al-nafs) that follows knowledge, and what is meant by 'inner speech' is inner submission, compliance, acceptance, and internal submission – all one meaning. So affirming the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) in everything he brought from Allah, and submitting to him, and inner compliance and submission with one's heart, is a specific affirmation, not merely attributing truthfulness to the Prophet without compliance, submission, acceptance, and submission. Therefore, on this basis, Iman and Islam are synonymous. The conclusion is that affirming the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) in everything he brought from Allah, along with inner submission and compliance, is Islam and Iman; as for the works of the limbs like prayer, fasting, uttering the two testimonies, and others, that is Islam.

Then he said: (1) The meaning of 'they are correlated (mutalāzimān) according to the Sacred Law' is that one does not suffice for the other. So whoever affirms with his heart and submits psychologically, the Sacred Law does not excuse him from other legal obligations such as prayer, zakāt, pilgrimage, etc. Similarly, whoever prays and performs the commanded works but does not realize the foundation of affirmation (taṣdīq) in himself, his work is invalid due to his disbelief. As for the first, i.e., whoever affirms but does not work, he is not a disbeliever, but he is disobedient, and his account is with Allah. As for us, if we do not see him performing any action of Islam, but we hear him utter the two testimonies, then we judge him outwardly as a believer, i.e., as far as appears to us, and we leave his inner state to Allah. Likewise, in reality we may not say about a specific individual that he is a believer but not a Muslim, nor may we say that he is a Muslim but not a believer, nor even 'believer'? This is the meaning of correlation (talāzum). (S)

Commentary
This passage defines Islam and clarifies its relationship with Iman. It opens by defining Islam as compliance with commands and avoidance of prohibitions, built upon inner submission. Though distinct in concept, they are correlated in Sacred Law: no Muslim is without Iman, and no believer is without Islam. Then a crucial observation is presented: if Islam is taken as inner submission, it is synonymous with Iman; if taken as outward acts (prayer, fasting, shahada, etc.), they are distinct but correlated. This detailed position is declared as the truth. The passage then interprets Iman as inner speech (ḥadīth al-nafs) meaning inner submission, compliance, acceptance. True affirmation (taṣdīq) is not mere acknowledgment of truthfulness but includes inner compliance. Hence, when Islam is understood as inner submission, it equals Iman. The conclusion summarizes: affirmation of the Prophet with inner submission is both Iman and Islam; outward acts are Islam specifically. The footnote expands on the legal correlation: one does not replace the other. A person with inner faith must fulfill outward obligations; one who performs outward acts without inner faith is a disbeliever. A person with inner faith but lacking outward actions is not a disbeliever but a sinner. In practice, we judge outwardly based on the shahada. We cannot label a specific person as 'believer but not Muslim' or 'Muslim but not believer' because of the correlation. This nuanced view preserves both the conceptual distinction and the practical unity.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Thinking that if someone has inner faith, they are excused from all outward obligations.
Correction: Inner faith does not excuse from outward duties; one who has faith but does not perform obligatory acts is still a sinner.

Key Takeaway
Iman and Islam are legally inseparable: both inner faith and outward actions are required, but in practice we judge by apparent signs.
