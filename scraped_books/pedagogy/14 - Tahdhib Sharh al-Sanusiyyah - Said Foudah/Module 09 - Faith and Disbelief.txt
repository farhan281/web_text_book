True faith is an inner conviction, not just outward words.

Sanusi establishes a clear chain: inquiry leads to knowledge, and knowledge leads to faith. But what exactly is faith? In this passage, Sanusi defines legal iman (al-iman al-shar'i) as an internal, heartfelt affirmation of everything the Prophet brought. Iman in the Heart, Shahada as Condition
Presents the preferred opinion that belief in the heart suffices before God, while utterance is a condition for legal treatment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between inner faith as belief in the heart and verbal utterance as a legal condition for worldly rulings, including the cases of refusal and incapacity.

Core Idea
Inner faith saves you with God; utterance is needed for worldly rulings.

This lesson explores the preferred opinion in the issue of Iman. It clarifies that faith is essentially internal affirmation, while verbal declaration is a condition for applying Islamic legal rulings. The passage also addresses exceptions for those who refuse or are physically unable to speak.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the full English translation. Let the author's argument come through clearly.
Pay attention to the distinction between inner faith and utterance.
2
Identify the key distinction
Notice that the passage states Iman is only affirmation of the heart, and utterance of the shahada is a condition for legal treatment, not part of Iman itself.

3

The passage addresses two scenarios: a person who refuses to speak despite ability is a disbeliever; a person unable to speak is excused.
Refusal negates the benefit of inner faith; incapacity preserves it.
4

The passage mentions that Imam Malik held a different view, requiring utterance once in a lifetime. Recognize that this stronger view differs from his school.

Commentary
This passage presents the preferred opinion (al-rajih) in the issue of Iman. It states that Iman is solely the affirmation (tasdiq) of the heart, and the verbal utterance of the two testimonies is not part of Iman itself but is a condition for the application of Islamic legal rulings to a person—such as the funeral prayer, burial in Muslim cemeteries, and so on. Therefore, a disbeliever who genuinely affirms in his heart but does not utter the shahada is considered a true believer in God's sight, provided he is not obstinate. However, if he is able to speak but refuses, his inner affirmation does not benefit him, and he is deemed a disbeliever. One who is physically unable to speak is excused. The passage then notes that this view conflicts with Imam Malik's opinion that the shahada is obligatory once in a lifetime like praising the Prophet. Finally, it clarifies that the earlier discussion (about the wording of the shahada) follows the Maliki school, not the Shafi'i school.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Thinking that verbal utterance is part of Iman itself.
Correction: The stronger view holds that utterance is only a condition for legal rulings, not part of Iman. Inner affirmation alone makes one a believer before God.

Checkpoint
According to the stronger view, what is the status of a person who affirms in his heart but refuses to utter the shahada when able?
A) He is a true believer and treated as Muslim.
B) He is a disbeliever.
C) He is excused due to inability.
D) He is considered a born Muslim.

Key Takeaway
The stronger view distinguishes between internal faith, which determines one's standing before God, and verbal utterance, which is a legal condition for being treated as a Muslim in society.
