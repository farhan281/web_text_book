Purpose of Pointing Out Scholars' Mistakes and Warning Against Mujassimah
The author explains why he points out certain mistakes and warns against anthropomorphists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author points out errors of Ahl al-Sunnah scholars and how this practice protects against deception by anthropomorphists.

Core Idea
Pointing out scholars' errors is a service to truth, not a belittlement.

In this passage, the author defends his practice of noting certain mistakes made by respected Ahl al-Sunnah scholars. He explains that these mistakes are in subtle matters and that the real purpose is to warn readers against the manipulation of such mistakes by the mujassimah (anthropomorphists). This lesson explores his reasoning and the high regard he still holds for those scholars.

Not All Hanbalis Are Anthropomorphists
The author clarifies two tendencies within the Hanbali school regarding anthropomorphism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the diversity within the Hanbali school regarding the attribute of transcendence (tanzih) versus anthropomorphism (tajsim).

Core Idea
The Hanbali school includes both anthropomorphists and those who uphold divine transcendence.

It is a common mistake to assume that all followers of Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal hold anthropomorphic views. The author of our text pauses to correct this misconception by showing that many prominent Hanbali scholars, including the imam himself, affirmed Allah's transcendence above any resemblance to creation. This lesson examines the two groups within the school.

Guided Path

Learn the key Arabic terms: مجسمة (anthropomorphists) and منزهون (those who transcend).
3
Understand the spectrum
The author notes degrees of anthropomorphism: the first group are less extreme than Ibn Taymiyyah and Ibn al-Qayyim. This shows the nuanced view.
4
Reflect on the purpose
This passage guards against overgeneralizing a theological stance to an entire school. It also teaches that a school's founder may not represent all later followers.

Contrast in Handling Errors and Degrees of Tajsim
Author contrasts error-handling and lists tajsim degrees.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Ash'aris openly correct scholars' errors while Mujassimah conceal them, and the varying degrees of tajsim among them.

Core Idea
Honest critique strengthens truth; hiding errors reveals systemic weakness.

This passage from al-Sanusi's student contrasts the methodologies of Ahl al-Sunnah and the Mujassimah towards scholarly errors. It explains why the former openly correct mistakes while the latter conceal them, and concludes with a classification of degrees of tajsim that illustrates the range of anthropomorphic beliefs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the contrast
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Observe the author's strong language in criticizing the Mujassimah's unwillingness to admit errors.
Pay attention to phrases like 'ميزة لأهل الحق' and 'إنما يتبعون الرجال'.
2

And know that this matter, i.e., pointing out the errors of scholars—though few among Ahl al-Sunnah—is a distinctive merit of the people of truth. For you will not find among the mujassimah in particular anyone who is fair to himself and others and admits even some of the atrocities that their leaders fell into. Rather, they constantly try to deny the attribution of that to them even if it is established, or to interpret it in a way that the speaker himself did not intend, or other such methods. Rather, they hasten to accuse others of not respecting the scholars of the nation, while the fact is that we do not criticize except the views of a very small group among those belonging to the nation. The matter is truly not one of respect or not, but rather a matter of truth or falsehood, error or correctness. They do this because they only follow men, and their state of affairs speaks of claiming infallibility for them and exalting them above what even the prophets were not exalted above. As for us, we only follow the truth that the scholars show us; we do not deify any of our great scholars, nor do we say that error is impossible for him, nor do we say that he is infallible as these people say, even by implication. And know that the secret behind their not declaring the errors of their virtuous ones and masters is that all their scholars have fallen into terrible atrocities. For if they passed judgment on one of them, then in fact they would be passing judgment on all of them, and this would be a judgment on their entire school as corrupt, vanishing, and incoherent. In contrast, for the Ash'ari Ahl al-Sunnah, those who erred among their scholars are very few in number, and also the rest of the scholars of the school did not leave him alone but rather pointed out what he fell into, while venerating him and acknowledging his virtue. Thus, his acknowledged virtue did not prevent them from judging that he was wrong in that instance. This is the reason for their not declaring correction for those who erred among the mujassimah. As for Ahl al-Sunnah, this obstacle does not exist at all, because they know that they are only commanded to defend the doctrine of Islam, not [for the sake of] the other [sect] of the mujassimah, except that their proportion among the Hanbalis is greater and larger. Therefore we pointed them out in our speech and did not mention others, because some heedless people might be deceived by their abundance among the Hanbalis and think that all Hanbalis are like them, while in this assumption he is mistaken. And know that the degrees of tajsim vary from one to another. Among these are those who explicitly deny contact (mumassa) while affirming direction (jiha) and limit (hadd) from one direction, like al-Qadi Abu Ya'la; and among them are those who affirm direction and contact, and affirm limit from all six directions, like Ibn Taymiyya. Commentary
This passage is an editorial critique by the author of the current state of Ahl al-Sunnah scholars and the rise of anthropomorphism (tajsim). The author begins by describing the fear among those called 'Shaykhs of Islam' who actually belong to the mujassima (anthropomorphists), afraid to speak the truth to preserve their worldly status. Then he turns to the 'great wonder': Ahl al-Sunnah scholars themselves have become fearful of defending their creed against the materialistic anthropomorphic thought that now dominates. They see the danger but do not dare to criticize, fearing criticism itself—unlike past Ahl al-Sunnah who were masters of refutation. The author attributes this fear to genuine ignorance of their own school and its principles; they content themselves with a summary knowledge of creed, which is only the way of the common people (al-'amma). Such people cannot recognize the corruptions of others because they lack certain knowledge (ilm al-yaqin). Consequently, many have adopted a method of 'taslim' (conceding to everyone), which has obvious corruptions. Then the author contrasts this with the opposite side: the once-weak school of tajsim and tashbih, whose followers used to hide their beliefs through ambiguous language. They would affirm direction (jiha) for God by using the term 'uluww (highness), and affirm anthropomorphism by using 'azam al-qadr (greatness of status) to imply size and space. In the past they feared openly affirming limbs; now they do so openly and even refute the people of truth, labeling them as Jahmiyya and Mu'attila. The author cites an example: a prominent figure called 'Shaykh al-Islam' claimed the Shari'a does not condemn tashbih. The author concludes that this reversal is due to the weakness of Ahl al-Sunnah, their lack of mastery of their own creed, and their attachment to worldly considerations, fearing human anger and pleasure rather than God's.

Taslim and the Rise of Anthropomorphism
Sanusi explains the taslim method and the historical ambiguity of tajsim terminology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how ignorance led to the method of taslim (conceding to everyone) and how anthropomorphists historically used ambiguous terminology to hide their true beliefs.

Core Idea
When scholars lack certain knowledge, they concede to all views—and falsehoods flourish.

The author continues his critique by showing the logical consequence of ignorance: those who do not have certain knowledge cannot refute falsehoods, so they resort to conceding to every opinion (taslim). He then contrasts this with the once-weak school of tajsim, which cleverly used ambiguous terms like 'uluww and qadr to conceal their anthropomorphic beliefs. This lesson unpacks that historical strategy and shows how it eventually gave way to open declaration.
