Distinguishing Categorical and Rhetorical Tamānuʿ Proofs
Maydani distinguishes categorical and rhetorical interpretations of the tamānuʿ proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between the categorical rational proof (burhān qaṭʿī) indicated by ishāra and the rhetorical proof (khiṭābī) indicated by ʿibāra in the tamānuʿ argument, and why only the categorical proof entails rational impossibility.

Core Idea
The same verse of tamānuʿ yields two proofs: one categorical for the elite, one rhetorical for the common.

In this lesson, we study a key distinction made by Imam Maydani regarding the famous proof of mutual hindrance (tamānuʿ) used to establish divine oneness. He argues that the Qur'anic verse can be read at two levels: one gives a categorical rational proof understood by the learned via indication (ishāra), and the other gives a rhetorical proof for the general public via expression (ʿibāra). Understanding this distinction prevents confusion between necessary rational consequences and merely customary ones.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two modes of Qur'anic proof
Read the passage to see how Maydani distinguishes between categorical rational proofs (istidlāl qaṭʿī) and rhetorical proofs (khiṭābī). Note that the categorical are for the elite (khāṣṣa) via ishāra, and rhetorical are for the common (ʿāmma) via ʿibāra.
This is a foundational hermeneutic principle in kalām.
2
Identify the two tamānuʿ proofs
Locate where Maydani explicitly names the categorical tamānuʿ proof (burhān al-tamānuʿ al-qaṭʿī) by consensus, and the rhetorical tamānuʿ that is merely customary.
3
Contrast the two corruptions
Notice the two different definitions of fasād: one is rational (one object between two agents, implying incapacity), the other is sensory (disruption of the observable order). Understand why the first is categorical and the second only rhetorical.
The rational corruption is logically necessary; the sensory one is only customary.
4
Reflect on the warning
Read Maydani's warning against calling a non-categorical proof categorical. Ask yourself why this is harmful to religion.
He says it provides a ladder for detractors.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained clarification and refutation concerning the proof of mutual hindrance (burhān al-tamānuʿ) used to establish God's oneness. The author begins by noting that the corruption (fasād) resulting from multiple gods is only necessary if disagreement is necessary, but disagreement is not necessary because agreement is possible; hence the corruption is only customary (ʿādī), not rational. He quotes Imam al-Rāzī to support this. Then he warns against calling a non-categorical proof categorical, as that harms religion. He explains that the Qur'an contains both categorical rational proofs (for the elite via indication, ishāra) and rhetorical proofs (for the common people via expression, ʿibāra). The categorical proof of mutual hindrance (according to the consensus of mutakallimūn) leads to the conclusion that a single object of power between two agents implies their incapacity, which is rationally impossible. This is the proof indicated by ishāra. The same verse also indicates a corruption via ʿibāra, namely the disruption of the observable order, which is only a customary consequence. These two meanings are different. Thus one should not confuse them: the categorical proof (ishāra) entails the impossibility of multiple gods rationally, while the rhetorical proof (ʿibāra) entails it only customarily. Customary entailment does not negate rational possibility. The author ends by noting a transmitted story about takfīr of someone who said the verse's indication is speculative.

Customary vs Rational Necessity in the Tamānuʿ Argument
Maydani explains that the corruption in the tamānuʿ argument is only customary, not rationally necessary, and warns against mislabeling proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the corruption (fasād) resulting from multiple gods is not rationally necessary but only customary, and how this distinction preserves the integrity of the categorical proof.

Core Idea
Corruption in the tamānuʿ argument is only customary, not rational, unless understood via indication.

In the previous lesson we distinguished the two levels of the tamānuʿ proof. Now we delve deeper into the nature of the necessity involved. The passage explains that disagreement among gods is not necessary because agreement is possible; therefore, the resulting corruption is only a customary (ʿādī) consequence, not a rational one. This insight goes back to Imam al-Rāzī and is crucial for avoiding the mistake of claiming a categorical proof where only a rhetorical one exists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise about disagreement
Read the opening lines: the corruption of both worlds only occurs on the assumption of necessary disagreement. But disagreement is not necessary because agreement is possible. Therefore, the corruption is customary, not rational.
This is the key logical move.
2
Understand 'customary necessity' (luzūm ʿādī)
Maydani says 'luzūm ʿādī' — a customary, not rational, necessity. Reflect on what this means: it is a pattern that Allah typically maintains, but not logically required.
He quotes al-Rāzī: 'Allah made the possible run its course according to the apparent.'
3
See the consequence for the proof
Because the corruption is only customary when understood via expression (ʿibāra), the verse does not provide a categorical proof for the common person. Only the indication (ishāra) yields a categorical proof.
4
Ponder the warning
Maydani warns that claiming a non-categorical proof as categorical harms religion. Reflect on why this might be: it makes the argument vulnerable to refutation and gives critics a foothold.

The Primary Division of Existence
Author introduces classification and defines accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fundamental division of all existents into the Necessary Existent (Allah) and the possible, and how possible existents are further divided into substances and accidents, with a focus on the definition of accident.

Core Idea
Everything that exists is either Allah or dependent on Him.

Before diving into detailed theology, the author lays out the basic categories of existence. This classification is the foundation for all subsequent arguments about God and creation. In this lesson, we will explore the primary division and understand what an accident (‛arad) is.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two main categories
Notice how the author first divides existents into necessary and possible, then immediately subdivides possible into substance and accident.
The necessary is exclusively Allah.
2
Focus on the definition of accident
Read the description of accident carefully: it requires a substrate to exist, like colors or motion.
The term 'dhat' (ذات) here means a substrate or essence.
3
Reflect on the theological significance
Ask yourself: Why is it important to distinguish between what is self-sufficient (Allah) and what is dependent?
This distinction safeguards tawheed.
