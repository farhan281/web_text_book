Facilitation by Creation and Deeds by Endings
Affirms principles of qadar: facilitation, deeds by endings, and divine decree.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the principles that everyone is facilitated to what they were created for, that deeds are judged by their endings, and that felicity or misery is by Allah's decree.

Core Idea
Allah facilitates each person to their destined path, and the final state determines their happiness or misery.

The concluding part of the passage introduces key principles of divine decree (qadar). It states that each person is made easy to do what they were created for, that deeds are evaluated by their final state, and that ultimate happiness or wretchedness is decided by Allah's decree.

Guided Path
1
Read the three statements
Read from 'وَكُلٌ مُيَسَّرُ' to the end, noting each distinct claim.
2
Understand facilitation
'Everyone is facilitated to what he was created for' means that Allah's decree aligns with a person's inclinations.
3
Understand deeds by endings
'Deeds are according to their endings' means that the final action determines the outcome.
4
Understand divine decree
The last sentence clarifies that felicity and misery are by Allah's decree.

Core Idea
Paradise and Hell are eternal creations, entered by grace or justice.

And Paradise and Hell are created, they never disappear nor perish, and indeed Allah Ta'ala created Paradise and Hell and created for them inhabitants; so whoever He wills from them He enters into Paradise by His grace, and whoever He wills from them He enters into Hell by His justice.
Source pages p. 221

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive statement of Sunni eschatological beliefs. It begins with belief in the recording angels (Kiram Katibin) who are appointed as guardians over deeds. Then it affirms belief in the Angel of Death, who is entrusted with taking the souls of all creatures. Next comes belief in the punishment of the grave for those who deserve it, and the specific questioning by the two angels Munkar and Nakir, who ask the deceased about their Lord, religion, and prophet, based on reports from the Prophet and Companions. The grave itself is described as either a garden of Paradise or a pit of Hell. The passage then moves to belief in the physical Resurrection, the recompense of deeds on the Day of Judgment, including the presentation (ard), accounting (hisab), reading of the book (qira'at al-kitab), reward and punishment, the Sirat (bridge), and the Scale (Mizan) which weighs deeds of believers encompassing good and evil, obedience and disobedience. Finally, it affirms that Paradise and Hell are created entities that will never cease to exist, and that Allah has created inhabitants for them. Entry into Paradise is by His grace, and entry into Hell is by His justice.

Benefit of Supplication and Charity for the Dead
Affirmation of benefit from supplication/charity and divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the living's supplications and charity benefit the dead, and that Allah's anger and pleasure are real attributes unlike human emotions.

Core Idea
The living can help the dead through prayer and charity; Allah's attributes are real but not like ours.

In this passage, Imam al-Tahawi affirms a key Sunni doctrine: the dead can benefit from the prayers and charity of the living. He also reminds us that Allah answers prayers, owns all, and that total dependence on Him is required. Finally, he affirms the divine attributes of anger and pleasure, but in a way befitting Allah, not like human emotions.

Commentary
This passage addresses several theological points. First, it affirms that the supplication (du'a') and charity (sadaqah) of the living benefit the dead – a key Ash'ari/Maturidi position against those who deny benefit for the dead. Second, Allah answers prayers and fulfills needs. Third, Allah is the possessor of all things and is possessed by nothing. Fourth, absolute dependence on Allah is emphasized: no one is free of need for Allah even for an instant; if someone thinks they are independent, they fall into disbelief and loss. Finally, divine attributes of anger (ghadab) and pleasure (rida) are affirmed, but they are not like those of any human – they are real attributes befitting His majesty.
