Why 'Abd' Precedes 'Rasul'
Commentator explains why 'abd' precedes 'rasul'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the multiple reasons why the attribute 'abd' (servant) is placed before 'rasul' (messenger) in Islamic creed, including prophetic instruction, theological priority, and Qur'anic usage.

Core Idea
The Prophet's servitude to Allah is his highest honor and precedes his messengership.

In this lesson, we explore the profound reason behind the order of words in the Islamic declaration of faith. The commentator al-Maydani explains why the attribute 'abd' (servant) is placed before 'rasul' (messenger) in the Tahawiyyah statement. This order is not accidental but rooted in prophetic instruction, theological priority, and the most noble contexts in the Qur'an.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Prophetic command
Read the hadith cited in the passage: the Prophet commanded to say 'His servant and His messenger' to avoid exaggeration. This is the primary reason for the order.
The hadith is authentic and emphasizes moderation in praise.
2
Grasp the existential precedence
Servitude precedes messengership in existence—the Prophet was a servant before being sent as a messenger. This shows the foundational nature of servitude.
3
Examine the Qur'anic evidence
The passage lists several verses where Allah describes the Prophet as 'His servant' in the highest contexts: revelation, calling to Allah, the Night Journey, and receiving revelation. This proves 'abd' is the most honored description.
Note the specific verses from Surah al-Kahf, al-Furqan, al-Jinn, al-Isra', and an-Najm.
4
Reflect on the theological implication
The description 'abd' guards against excess and affirms that divinity belongs only to Allah, while servitude is the highest station for creation. It highlights Allah's perfection and human need.
This connects to the core idea: servitude is honor, not degradation.

Commentary
This passage explains why the attribute 'abd' (servant) is mentioned before 'rasul' (messenger) in the Tahawiyyah statement. The commentator lists several reasons. First, it follows the command of the Prophet himself in the authentic hadith to say 'His servant and His messenger', not exaggerate as Christians did. Second, servitude precedes messengership in existence—the Prophet was a servant before he was sent as a messenger. Third, it indicates that the Prophet is not arrogant about his status as a servant; rather he takes pride in it. Fourth, the name 'servant' is the most beloved and elevated to Allah. The commentator then proves this by citing Qur'anic verses where Allah describes the Prophet as 'His servant' in the most honorable contexts: revelation of the Qur'an, calling to Allah, the Night Journey, and receiving revelation. If there were a more honorable description, Allah would have used it in those contexts. The commentator concludes by emphasizing that despite the Prophet's lofty ranks (selection, prophethood, messengership, finality, leadership), he never departed from servitude, because divinity belongs only to Allah. Describing the Prophet as servant points to Allah's complete perfection and everyone else's need for Him.

Core Idea
'Al-Mustafa' means the Chosen One, selected by Allah from the best creation.

The commentator begins by explaining the attribute 'al-Mustafa' (the Chosen), which highlights the Prophet's special status. He then supports it with a hadith that traces the divine selection from the descendants of Isma'il down to the Prophet himself. This lesson unpacks both the definition and the hadith, showing how the Prophet's lineage is part of his honor.

Reflect on the significance

The Prophet's Attributes: Mustafa, Mujtaba, Murtada
Author defines the titles Mustafa, Mujtaba, Murtada with a supporting hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meanings of the Prophet's titles al-Mustafa, al-Mujtaba, and al-Murtada, and the divine selection hierarchy that culminates in him.

Core Idea
The Prophet is the chosen one, selected by Allah from the best of creation.

This lesson explores three of the Prophet's noble attributes: al-Mustafa (the Chosen), al-Mujtaba (the Selected), and al-Murtada (the Approved). Author defines nabiyy and rasul quoting Ibn Hajar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between a prophet and a messenger according to Ibn Hajar, and why the classification matters for theology.

Core Idea
Every messenger is a prophet, but not every prophet is a messenger.

This passage from Sharh al-Tahawiyyah presents the classical definitions of nabiyy and rasul as explained by Ibn Hajar al-Haytami. It clarifies the key difference: both receive revelation, but only the messenger is commanded to convey it. This distinction is foundational for understanding the roles of Allah's chosen individuals.

Commentary
This passage is the concluding section of the Tahawiyyah creed, where Imam al-Tahawi summarizes key points of Sunni belief and dissociates from deviant groups. The passage begins by affirming the order of the caliphate after the Prophet: Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali, based on the consensus of the companions. These four are the rightly guided caliphs who ruled with justice. Next, he mentions the ten companions promised Paradise, listing them by name, and states that whoever speaks well of the Prophet's companions, wives, and offspring is free from hypocrisy. The scholars of the Salaf and later righteous people should only be spoken of positively; speaking ill of them is a deviation. Then the creed asserts that no saint is superior to any prophet; even a single prophet is better than all saints. Miracles (karamāt) of the saints are affirmed if reliably reported. Belief in the signs of the Hour is declared, specifically mentioning the Dajjal, descent of Jesus, rising of the sun from the west, and the Beast of the Earth. Soothsayers and diviners are rejected, along with any claim contradicting the Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus. The community (jamā'ah) is considered truth, and division is deviation and punishment. They declare themselves free from such sects, considering them misguided and wicked. The passage ends with a prayer acknowledging Allah's knowledge and the ultimate return to Him.

Speaking Well of the Companions and Scholars
Mandate to speak well of companions and scholars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the obligation to speak well of the Prophet's companions, his wives, his offspring, and the scholars of the Salaf, and that speaking ill of them is a sign of hypocrisy or deviation.

Core Idea
Love for the Prophet's household and companions is a sign of faith.

After affirming the status of the caliphs and the ten, Imam al-Tahawi turns to the general treatment of all companions, the Prophet's wives, and his offspring. He then extends this respect to the scholars of the Salaf and later righteous people. This passage draws a clear line: whoever speaks well of them is free from hypocrisy, and whoever speaks ill of them is on the wrong path.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on companions and scholars
Read the Arabic text that starts with 'وَمَنْ أَحْسَنَ الْقَوْلَ' and ends with 'فَهُوَ عَلَى غَيْرِ السَّبِيلِ'. Notice the strong language: 'فَقَدْ بَرِئَ مِنَ النِّفَاقِ' and 'فَهُوَ عَلَى غَيْرِ السَّبِيلِ'.
The passage uses a conditional structure: if-then.
2
Identify the groups mentioned
The passage mentions: companions (أصحاب), wives (أزواج), offspring (ذريات), scholars of the Salaf (علماء السلف), the Followers (التابعين), and later people of good and tradition (أهل الخير والأثر).
The Salaf includes the early generations.
3
Understand the result of good or bad speech
Good speech about these groups results in being free from hypocrisy. Bad speech results in being 'على غير السبيل' (on other than the path).
This shows the gravity of defaming them.

Prophets Are Superior to Saints; Belief in Miracles
Affirmation of prophetic superiority and saintly miracles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that no saint is superior to a prophet, even one prophet is better than all saints, and that miracles (karamat) of saints are to be believed if reliably reported.

Core Idea
Prophethood is the highest rank; saints are below prophets.

In this brief but important passage, Imam al-Tahawi clarifies the hierarchy between prophets and saints. While Ahl al-Sunnah honor the saints, they do not elevate them above any prophet. Moreover, they affirm the miracles (karamat) of saints that are authentically reported, which is a distinctive Sunni position against those who deny them.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on prophets and saints
Read the Arabic: 'وَلَا نُفَضِّلُ أَحَدًا مِنَ الأَوْلِيَاءِ عَلَى أَحَدٍ مِنَ الأَنْبِيَاءِ، وَنَقُولُ: نَبِيٌّ وَاحِدٌ أَفْضَلُ مِنْ جَمِيعِ الْأَوْلِيَاءِ، وَنُؤْمِنُ بِمَا جَاءَ مِنْ كَرَامَاتِهِمْ وَصَحَّ عَنِ الثِّقَاتِ مِنْ رُوَايَتِهِمْ.'
The passage has three parts: negation, statement, affirmation.
2
Understand the negation
'لا نفضل أحداً من الأولياء على أحد من الأنبياء' means we do not prefer any saint over any prophet. This is a general principle.
The word 'نُفَضِّلُ' indicates preference.
3
Understand the positive statement
'نبي واحد أفضل من جميع الأولياء' states that a single prophet is better than all saints combined. This emphasizes the superiority of prophethood.
This is a strong comparative.
4
Understand the affirmation of miracles
'ونؤمن بما جاء من كراماتهم' means we believe in the miracles (karamat) of saints that have come and are confirmed through trustworthy chains.
This distinguishes Sunni belief from those who deny saintly miracles.

And it affirms the caliphate after the Prophet (peace be upon him) first for Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, as a preference and precedence based on the consensus of the nation, then for Umar ibn al-Khattab, then for Uthman ibn Affan, then for Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with them all). And they are the rightly guided caliphs and the guided imams who judged with truth and by it they acted justly. And indeed the ten whom the Messenger of Allah named, we testify for them with Paradise just as the Messenger of Allah testified for them, and his word is truth. They are: Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, Talha, al-Zubayr, Sa'd, Sa'id, Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, and Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah, who is the trustee of this nation (may Allah be pleased with them all). And whoever speaks well of the companions of the Prophet (peace be upon him), his wives, and his offspring has indeed been free from hypocrisy. And the scholars of the Salaf among the righteous, the Followers, and those after them from the people of good and tradition, the people of jurisprudence and consideration, are not mentioned except with beauty. And whoever mentions them with evil is upon other than the path. And we do not prefer any of the saints over any of the prophets. And we say: One prophet is better than all the saints. And we believe in what has come of their miracles and that which is confirmed from trustworthy narrations. And we believe in the signs of the Hour, among them: the emergence of the Dajjal, the descent of Isa (peace be upon him) from the heaven, the rising of the sun from its west, and the emergence of the Beast of the Earth from its place. And we do not believe a soothsayer nor a diviner nor anyone who claims anything contrary to the Book, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the nation. And we see the community (jama'ah) as truth and correctness, and division as deviation and punishment. And the religion of Allah in the heavens and the earth is one, and it is the religion of Islam, as Allah the Exalted said: 'Indeed, the religion with Allah is Islam' (Surah Al 'Imran: 19), and He said: 'And whoever seeks a religion other than Islam, it will never be accepted from him' (Surah Al 'Imran: 85), and He said: 'And I have chosen for you Islam as religion' (Surah al-Ma'idah: 3). And it is between exaltation and inadequacy, anthropomorphism and negation, compulsion and free will, security and despair. So this is our religion and our creed outwardly and inwardly. And we disassociate to Allah the Exalted from whoever opposes what we have mentioned and clarified. And we ask Allah the Exalted to make us firm upon it and to seal it for us and to protect us from conflicting desires, divergent opinions, and evil sects, such as the Mushabbihah, the Jahmiyyah, the Kharijites, the Qadariyyah, and others who contradict the Sunnah and the Community and follow innovation and misguidance. And we are free from them, and they are in our view misguided and wicked. And Allah knows best what is correct, and to Him is the return and the final abode.

Commentary
This passage is the concluding section of the Tahawiyyah creed, where Imam al-Tahawi summarizes key points of Sunni belief and dissociates from deviant groups. The passage begins by affirming the order of the caliphate after the Prophet: Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali, based on the consensus of the companions. These four are the rightly guided caliphs who ruled with justice. Next, he mentions the ten companions promised Paradise, listing them by name, and states that whoever speaks well of the Prophet's companions, wives, and offspring is free from hypocrisy. The scholars of the Salaf and later righteous people should only be spoken of positively; speaking ill of them is a deviation. Then the creed asserts that no saint is superior to any prophet; even a single prophet is better than all saints. Miracles (karamāt) of the saints are affirmed if reliably reported. Belief in the signs of the Hour is declared, specifically mentioning the Dajjal, descent of Jesus, rising of the sun from the west, and the Beast of the Earth. Soothsayers and diviners are rejected, along with any claim contradicting the Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus. The community (jamā'ah) is considered truth, and division is deviation and punishment. The religion of Allah is one—Islam—supported by three Quranic verses. Islam is described as a middle path between extremes: between exaltation and inadequacy (regarding Allah's attributes), between anthropomorphism (tashbīh) and nullification (ta'tīl), between compulsion (jabr) and free will (qadar), and between security (from Allah's punishment) and despair (of His mercy). Finally, the imam declares that this is their creed outwardly and inwardly, and they dissociate from anyone who opposes it. They seek Allah's firmness upon this creed and protection from deviant sects, explicitly naming the Mushabbihah (anthropomorphists), Jahmiyyah (negators), Kharijites, Qadariyyah (free will proponents), and others who follow innovation. They declare themselves free from such sects, considering them misguided and wicked. The passage ends with a prayer acknowledging Allah's knowledge and the ultimate return to Him.

Belief in the Signs of the Hour
Affirmation of four major signs of the Hour

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four specific signs of the Hour that Imam al-Tahawi highlights: the Dajjal, the descent of Isa, the rising of the sun from the west, and the Beast of the Earth.

Core Idea
Belief in the major signs of the Hour is part of Sunni creed.

Imam al-Tahawi moves from the unseen miracles of saints to the unseen events of the end times. He affirms belief in the signs of the Hour, mentioning four specific signs that are well-known from authentic hadiths. This reaffirms the Sunni commitment to accepting what revelation has informed us about the future, without speculation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on signs of the Hour
Read the Arabic: 'وَنُؤْمِنُ بِأَشْرَاطِ السَّاعَةِ وَمِنْهَا: خُرُوجُ الدَّجَّالِ وَنُزُولُ عِيسَى عَلَيْهِ السَّلَامُ مِنَ السَّمَاءِ، وَطُلُوعُ الشَّمْسِ مِنْ مَغْرِبِهَا وَخُرُوجُ دَابَّةِ الأَرْضِ مِنْ مَوْضِعِهَا.'
The signs are introduced with 'مِنْهَا' meaning 'among them'.
2
Identify each sign
There are four: 1) Emergence of the Dajjal (Antichrist), 2) Descent of Prophet Isa from heaven, 3) Rising of the sun from the west, 4) Emergence of the Beast of the Earth.
These are all mentioned in authentic hadith.
3
Understand the phrase 'أشراط الساعة'
'أشراط' means signs or portents. 'الساعة' refers to the Day of Judgment.
Believing in these signs is obligatory.

Commentary
This passage is the concluding section of the Tahawiyyah creed, where Imam al-Tahawi summarizes key points of Sunni belief and dissociates from deviant groups. The passage begins by affirming the order of the caliphate after the Prophet: Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali, based on the consensus of the companions. Rejection of Soothsayers and False Claims
Rejection of soothsayers and anti-revelation claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Muslims must not believe soothsayers, diviners, or anyone who claims something contrary to the Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus of the nation.

Core Idea
True knowledge comes only from revelation, not from fortune-tellers.

Having affirmed belief in the unseen signs, Imam al-Tahawi now clarifies what we must not believe. He rejects all forms of divination and false claims that go against the primary sources of Islam. This protects the believer from superstition and innovation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage rejecting soothsayers
Read the Arabic: 'وَلَا نُصَدِّقُ كَاهِنًا وَلَا عَرَّافًا وَلَا مَنْ يَدَّعِي شَيْئًا بِخِلَافِ الْكِتَابِ وَالسُّنَّةِ وَإِجْمَاعِ الأُمَّةِ.'
The verb 'نُصَدِّقُ' means 'we do not believe/affirm'.
2
Identify the three categories rejected
1) Soothsayer (كاهن), 2) Diviner (عَرَّاف), 3) Anyone who claims something contrary to the Book, Sunnah, and Ijma'.
The third is broad.
3
Understand the criterion
The criterion for rejecting a claim is that it contradicts the Qur'an, Sunnah, or consensus. This shows that any belief not grounded in these sources is invalid.
Ijma' (consensus) is a key source.

The Community Is Truth; Islam Is the Middle Path
Affirmation of community and Islam as middle path

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the united community (jama'ah) is the correct path, division is punishment, and Islam is the middle path between various extremes as described by Imam al-Tahawi.

Core Idea
Unity is truth; division is punishment; Islam is the balanced path.

Imam al-Tahawi now turns to the social dimension of creed. He declares that the jama'ah (the main body of Muslims) is truth and division is deviation. Then he defines the religion of Islam as a single, divinely chosen path that avoids extremes — between exaggeration and neglect, anthropomorphism and negation, compulsion and free will, and security and despair. This middle path is the essence of Sunni Islam.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on jama'ah and Islam as middle path
Read from 'وَنَرَى الْجَمَاعَةَ حَقًّا' down to 'وَالْأَمْنِ وَالْيَأْسِ'. This is a comprehensive statement about the community and the nature of Islam.
The passage includes Quranic verses.
2
Understand the value of jama'ah
'ونرى الجماعة حقاً وصواباً والفرقة زيناً وعذاباً' — the community (jama'ah) is truth and correctness, while division is deviation and punishment. This encourages unity.
Note the word 'زيناً' means deviation or misguidance.
3
Understand Islam as one religion
'ودين الله في السماء والأرض واحد وهو دين الإسلام' — the religion of Allah is one and it is Islam, supported by three verses.
This emphasizes the universality of Islam.
4
Understand the middle path description
'وهو بين العلو والتقصير والتشبيه والتعطيل والجبر والقدر والأمن واليأس' — Islam is a balance between eight extremes: exaltation vs inadequacy, anthropomorphism vs negation, compulsion vs free will, security vs despair.
These are four pairs of opposing extremes.

Commentary
This passage is the concluding section of the Tahawiyyah creed, where Imam al-Tahawi summarizes key points of Sunni belief and dissociates from deviant groups. The passage begins by affirming the order of the caliphate after the Prophet: Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali, based on the consensus of the companions. These four are the rightly guided caliphs who ruled with justice. Next, he mentions the ten companions promised Paradise, listing them by name, and states that whoever speaks well of the Prophet's companions, wives, and offspring is free from hypocrisy. The scholars of the Salaf and later righteous people should only be spoken of positively; speaking ill of them is a deviation. Then the creed asserts that no saint is superior to any prophet; even a single prophet is better than all saints. Miracles (karamāt) of the saints are affirmed if reliably reported. Belief in the signs of the Hour is declared, specifically mentioning the Dajjal, descent of Jesus, rising of the sun from the west, and the Beast of the Earth. Soothsayers and diviners are rejected, along with any claim contradicting the Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus. The community (jamā'ah) is considered truth, and division is deviation and punishment. The religion of Allah is one—Islam—supported by three Quranic verses. Islam is described as a middle path between extremes: between exaltation and inadequacy (regarding Allah's attributes), between anthropomorphism (tashbīh) and nullification (ta'tīl), between compulsion (jabr) and free will (qadar), and between security (from Allah's punishment) and despair (of His mercy). Finally, the imam declares that this is their creed outwardly and inwardly, and they dissociate from anyone who opposes it. They seek Allah's firmness upon this creed and protection from deviant sects, explicitly naming the Mushabbihah (anthropomorphists), Jahmiyyah (negators), Kharijites, Qadariyyah (free will proponents), and others who follow innovation. They declare themselves free from such sects, considering them misguided and wicked. The passage ends with a prayer acknowledging Allah's knowledge and the ultimate return to Him.

وَالْفُرْقَةَ زَيْنًا وَعَذَابًا. وَدِينُ اللَّهِ فِي السَّمَاءِ وَالأَرْضِ وَاحِدٌ وَهُوَ دِينُ الإِسْلَامِ كَمَا قَالَ اللَّهُ تَعَالَى: ﴿إِنَّ الدِّينَ عِنْدَ اللَّهِ الْإِسْلَامُ﴾ [آل عمران: 19]، وَقَالَ تَعَالَى: ﴿وَمَنْ يَبْتَغِ غَيْرَ الإِسْلَامِ دِينًا فَلَنْ يُقْبَلَ مِنْهُ﴾ [آل عمران: 85]، وَقَالَ تَعَالَى: ﴿وَرَضِيتُ لَكُمُ الإِسْلَامَ دِينًا﴾ [المائدة: 3]، وَهُوَ بَيْنَ الْعُلُوِّ وَالتَّقْصِيرِ وَالتَّشْبِيهِ وَالتَّعْطِيلِ وَالْجَبْرِ وَالْقَدَرِ وَالأَمْنِ وَالْيَأْسِ. فَهَذَا دِينُنَا وَاعْتِقَادُنَا ظَاهِرًا وَبَاطِنًا، وَنَحْنُ نَبْرَأُ إِلَى اللَّهِ تَعَالَى مِمَّنْ خَالَفَ الَّذِي ذَكَرْنَاهُ وَبَيَّنَّاهُ، وَنَسْأَلُ اللَّهَ تَعَالَى أَنْ يُثَبِّتَنَا عَلَيْهِ وَتَخْتِمَ لَنَا بِهِ وَيَعْصِمَنَا مِنَ الأَهْوَاءِ الْمُخْتَلِفَةِ وَالآرَاءِ الْمُتَفَرِّقَةِ وَالْمَذَاهِبِ الرَّدِيَّةِ؛ كَالْمُشَبِّهَةِ وَالْجَهْمِيَّةِ وَالْخَارِجِيَّةِ وَالْقَدَرِيَّةِ وَغَيْرِهِمْ مِمَّنْ خَالَفَ السُّنَّةَ وَالْجَمَاعَةَ وَاتَّبَعَ الْبِدْعَةَ وَالضَّلَالَةَ، وَنَحْنُ مِنْهُمْ بَرَاءٌ وَهُمْ عِنْدَنَا ضُلَّالٌ وَأَرْدِيَاءُ. وَاللَّهُ أَعْلَمُ بِالصَّوَابِ وَإِلَيْهِ الْمَرْجِعُ وَالْمَآبُ.
And it affirms the caliphate after the Prophet (peace be upon him) first for Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, as a preference and precedence based on the consensus of the nation, then for Umar ibn al-Khattab, then for Uthman ibn Affan, then for Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with them all). And they are the rightly guided caliphs and the guided imams who judged with truth and by it they acted justly. And indeed the ten whom the Messenger of Allah named, we testify for them with Paradise just as the Messenger of Allah testified for them, and his word is truth. They are: Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, Talha, al-Zubayr, Sa'd, Sa'id, Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, and Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah, who is the trustee of this nation (may Allah be pleased with them all). And whoever speaks well of the companions of the Prophet (peace be upon him), his wives, and his offspring has indeed been free from hypocrisy. And the scholars of the Salaf among the righteous, the Followers, and those after them from the people of good and tradition, the people of jurisprudence and consideration, are not mentioned except with beauty. And whoever mentions them with evil is upon other than the path. And we do not prefer any of the saints over any of the prophets. And we say: One prophet is better than all the saints. And we believe in what has come of their miracles and that which is confirmed from trustworthy narrations. And we believe in the signs of the Hour, among them: the emergence of the Dajjal, the descent of Isa (peace be upon him) from the heaven, the rising of the sun from its west, and the emergence of the Beast of the Earth from its place. And we do not believe a soothsayer nor a diviner nor anyone who claims anything contrary to the Book, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the nation. And we see the community (jama'ah) as truth and correctness, and division as deviation and punishment. And the religion of Allah in the heavens and the earth is one, and it is the religion of Islam, as Allah the Exalted said: 'Indeed, the religion with Allah is Islam' (Surah Al 'Imran: 19), and He said: 'And whoever seeks a religion other than Islam, it will never be accepted from him' (Surah Al 'Imran: 85), and He said: 'And I have chosen for you Islam as religion' (Surah al-Ma'idah: 3). And it is between exaltation and inadequacy, anthropomorphism and negation, compulsion and free will, security and despair. So this is our religion and our creed outwardly and inwardly. And we disassociate to Allah the Exalted from whoever opposes what we have mentioned and clarified. And we ask Allah the Exalted to make us firm upon it and to seal it for us and to protect us from conflicting desires, divergent opinions, and evil sects, such as the Mushabbihah, the Jahmiyyah, the Kharijites, the Qadariyyah, and others who contradict the Sunnah and the Community and follow innovation and misguidance. And we are free from them, and they are in our view misguided and wicked. And Allah knows best what is correct, and to Him is the return and the final abode.

Commentary
This passage is the concluding section of the Tahawiyyah creed, where Imam al-Tahawi summarizes key points of Sunni belief and dissociates from deviant groups. The passage begins by affirming the order of the caliphate after the Prophet: Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali, based on the consensus of the companions. These four are the rightly guided caliphs who ruled with justice. Next, he mentions the ten companions promised Paradise, listing them by name, and states that whoever speaks well of the Prophet's companions, wives, and offspring is free from hypocrisy. The scholars of the Salaf and later righteous people should only be spoken of positively; speaking ill of them is a deviation. Then the creed asserts that no saint is superior to any prophet; even a single prophet is better than all saints. Miracles (karamāt) of the saints are affirmed if reliably reported. Belief in the signs of the Hour is declared, specifically mentioning the Dajjal, descent of Jesus, rising of the sun from the west, and the Beast of the Earth. Soothsayers and diviners are rejected, along with any claim contradicting the Qur'an, Sunnah, and consensus. The community (jamā'ah) is considered truth, and division is deviation and punishment. The religion of Allah is one—Islam—supported by three Quranic verses. Islam is described as a middle path between extremes: between exaltation and inadequacy (regarding Allah's attributes), between anthropomorphism (tashbīh) and nullification (ta'tīl), between compulsion (jabr) and free will (qadar), and between security (from Allah's punishment) and despair (of His mercy). Finally, the imam declares that this is their creed outwardly and inwardly, and they dissociate from anyone who opposes it. They seek Allah's firmness upon this creed and protection from deviant sects, explicitly naming the Mushabbihah (anthropomorphists), Jahmiyyah (negators), Kharijites, Qadariyyah (free will proponents), and others who follow innovation. They declare themselves free from such sects, considering them misguided and wicked. The passage ends with a prayer acknowledging Allah's knowledge and the ultimate return to Him.
