Divine Success and Abandonment
Maydani explains tawfiq and khidhlan and their relation to human capability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meanings of tawfiq and khidhlan in Ash'ari theology and their relation to human capability.

Core Idea
Allah is not obliged to grant success; He excuses Himself by showing the path and creating capability.

Maydani opens by describing obstacles that overwhelm human power, requiring divine help (tawfiq). He then clarifies that Allah owes no one success; rather, He establishes His case by providing guidance and capability. The concept of khidhlan (abandonment) is also defined as leaving the servant to his own resources without withdrawing capability.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic lines from 'موانع تشبه القواسر' to 'التي خلقها له' carefully. Then read the translation.
Notice the strong language about obstacles being like overwhelming forces.
2
Understand tawfiq and its necessity
The passage says obstacles cannot be overcome except with tawfiq. Ask: Why is tawfiq needed? Because human power alone is insufficient.
Tawfiq is a special divine aid.
3
Understand that Allah is not obligated to grant tawfiq
The phrase 'ليس لأحد على الله تعالى أن يوفقه' is crucial. Allah is under no obligation; He grants tawfiq purely by mercy.
This refutes any notion that humans can demand success from Allah.
4
Differentiate tawfiq from khidhlan
Khidhlan is the absence of tawfiq—Allah leaves the servant to his own self, but does not remove the capability already given.
Khidhlan is not the withdrawal of capability but the withholding of extra support.
5
Reflect on Allah's excuse
Allah excuses Himself by informing the path and creating 'makanah' (capability). This means no one can claim ignorance or inability as an excuse.
Makanah here refers to the basic capacity to choose and act.

موانع تشبه القواسر (۱) لقوة استيلائها؛ فلا تغلب إلا بمعونة التوفيق وليس لأحد على الله تعالى أن يوفقه بل إذا أعلمه طريق الخير والشر وخلق المكنة له فقد أعذر إليه، وعدم التوفيق وهو الخذلان وهو أن يدعه مع نفسه لا ينصره ولا يعينه عليها لا يسلبه المكنة من ذلك العزم التي خلقها له، وهذه غير القدرة التي ذهب أكثر أهل السنة إلى أنها لا تتقدم على الفعل حتى قد يقال: إن التكليف بغير المقدور واقع لأنه يكون قبل الفعل بالضرورة، ومقارن المتأخر غير موجود مع المتقدم؛ فإن المراد بتلك القدرة هو القدرة التي يقام بها الفعل، وهي قدرة جزئية مندرجة تحت مطلق القدرة الكلية تخلق مع الفعل، وقولنا: يقام بها الفعل تساهل، وإنما هي معه؛ إذ كان الفعل إنما هو أثر قدرة الله سبحانه وتعالى؛ قال القاضي أبو بكر (٢): إن الله تعالى لا يخلق تلك القدرة إلا ويخلق الفعل تحتها فهي من الفعل بمنزلة المشروط من الشرط فالقدرة كالمشروط والفعل كالشرط؛ فكما لا يوجد المشروط بلا شرط كذلك لا توجد القدرة بلا فعل، ويجوز أن يوجد الشرط بلا مشروط، وهذه القدرة شرط التكليف مقدمة عليه، وهي عبارة عندهم عن سلامة الآلات وصحة الأسباب بناء على أن من كان كذلك فإن الله تعالى يخلق له القدرة عند الفعل كذلك أجرى الله سبحانه وتعالى العادة، ومن مشايخنا من ذهب إلى أن القدرة تتقدم حقيقة على الفعل (۳) انتهى بحروفه.
Obstacles resembling overwhelming forces due to their strong domination; so they cannot be overcome except with the help of divine success (tawfiq). And no one has the right that Allah Almighty should grant him success. Rather, when He informs him of the path of good and evil and creates capability (makanah) for him, He has thereby excused himself. And lack of success (tawfiq) is abandonment (khidhlan), which is that He leaves him to himself, not helping him nor assisting him against it, not removing from him the capability of that resolve which He created for him. And this is other than the capacity (qudra) which the majority of Ahl al-Sunnah held does not precede the act, so much so that it is said: obligation with what is not within power actually occurs because it necessarily occurs before the act, and the subsequent concomitant does not exist with the antecedent. For what is meant by that capacity is the capacity by which the act is established, and it is a particular capacity included under absolute universal capacity, created with the act. And our saying 'by which the act is established' is a leniency; rather it is with it, since the act is only an effect of the power of Allah, exalted be He. Al-Qadi Abu Bakr (al-Baqillani) said: Verily Allah Almighty does not create that capacity except He creates the act under it. So it is with respect to the act like the conditioned to the condition: the capacity is like the conditioned and the act like the condition. Just as the conditioned does not exist without a condition, so too the capacity does not exist without an act. And it is permissible for the condition to exist without a conditioned. And this capacity is a condition of obligation (taklif) prior to it. And it is, in their view, an expression of the soundness of instruments and the validity of causes, based on the fact that whoever is like that, Allah Almighty creates capacity for him at the act. In such a way Allah Almighty has made it customary. And among our masters are those who held that capacity truly precedes the act. End of his words.

Commentary
This passage is a theological explanation of the nature of human capacity (qudra) and its relationship to the act. It begins by discussing obstacles that are like overwhelming forces, which cannot be overcome except by divine success (tawfiq). The author clarifies that Allah is not obligated to grant success; rather, when He shows the path and creates capability (makanah), He has excused Himself. Then he distinguishes this from the concept of capacity (qudra) which is the main subject. The majority of Ahl al-Sunnah hold that capacity does not precede the act. This leads to the apparent problem that taklif (obligation) would fall on what is not within power, since taklif occurs before the act. But the capacity meant here is the capacity that is created with the act, not before it. The author notes that saying 'by which the act is established' is a leniency; capacity is simply concurrent with the act, as the act is an effect of divine power. He quotes al-Baqillani who uses an analogy of conditioned and condition: capacity is like the conditioned (mashrut) and act is like the condition (shart). Just as a conditioned cannot exist without its condition, capacity cannot exist without an act. However, a condition can exist without a conditioned, meaning that the act can exist without capacity? Actually the text says 'it is permissible for the condition to exist without a conditioned'. So the act (condition) can exist without capacity (conditioned). This emphasizes that capacity is not a cause of the act; rather the act is a condition for capacity. Then the passage explains that this capacity is a condition for taklif prior to it, and it is defined as soundness of instruments and validity of causes. According to customary divine practice, for someone with sound instruments, Allah creates capacity at the time of the act. Finally, the author notes that some of their masters held that capacity truly precedes the act, indicating a difference of opinion.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that if Allah abandons a person (khidhlan), He removes all ability to do good.
Correction: The passage states that khidhlan does not remove the capability (makanah) already created; it merely withholds extra support.

Checkpoint

A) It removes the capability of resolve created by Allah

Key Takeaway
Tawfiq is a special divine aid not owed to anyone; khidhlan is the withholding of that aid without removing basic capability.

?

?

Is it a lack of tawfiq you haven't sought? Will you ask Allah earnestly for tawfiq right now, recognizing it is a gift you do not deserve? Pause and make that du'a.

Human Capacity Created with the Act
Maydani explains the Ash'ari view on human capacity (qudra) and its creation with the act, using al-Baqillani's analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine that human capacity (qudra) is created concurrently with the act, not before it, and al-Baqillani's conditioned-condition analogy.

Core Idea
Capacity is like the conditioned; the act is like its condition—capacity cannot exist without an act.

Maydani transitions to discuss the capacity (qudra) that Ahl al-Sunnah say does not precede the act. He addresses the apparent problem that taklif (obligation) occurs before the act, yet capacity comes later. He clarifies that the relevant capacity is a particular power created with the act itself, and quotes al-Baqillani's analogy to explain the relationship.

Guided Path
1
Read the rest of the Arabic passage
Read from 'وهذه غير القدرة' to the end. Then check the translation.
This part deals with the nature of qudra.
2
Understand the majority view on timing
The passage states that most Ahl al-Sunnah hold that qudra does not precede the act. This leads to the apparent difficulty that taklif (which comes before the act) would then command what is not yet in one's power.
This is a key theological point.
3
Distinguish the two types of capacity
There is a universal capacity (qudra kulliyya) and a particular capacity (qudra juz'iyya). The one that matters for the act is the particular capacity created with the act.
The universal capacity is a broader concept.
4
Study al-Baqillani's analogy
Al-Baqillani likens capacity to the conditioned (mashrut) and the act to the condition (shart). A conditioned cannot exist without its condition, but a condition can exist without the conditioned.
This emphasizes that capacity is dependent on the act, not vice versa.
5
Note the different opinion
The passage ends by noting that some of their masters held that capacity truly precedes the act. This shows an internal debate.
Remember that this is an Ash'ari text; the majority view is the one explained.
