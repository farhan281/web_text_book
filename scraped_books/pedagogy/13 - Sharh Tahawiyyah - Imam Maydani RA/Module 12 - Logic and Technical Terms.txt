Division of Existence: External and Mental
Maydani explains the division of existence and the debate over mental subsistence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between external and mental existence and the key debate among scholars regarding the reality of mental existence.

Core Idea
Existence is of two kinds: real external existence and shadow-like mental existence — but most theologians deny the latter.

Before delving into theological arguments, it is essential to clarify what we mean by 'existence.' Theologians and philosophers distinguish between the existence of things in the external world and their existence in the mind. This lesson introduces that distinction and the controversy surrounding mental existence, a foundational issue in Islamic epistemology and metaphysics.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once carefully, then read the literal translation. Let the original phrasing guide your understanding before analyzing.
Notice the use of 'shadow' as an analogy for mental existence.
2
Identify the two types of existence
The passage explicitly divides existence into external (wujud khariji) and mental (wujud dhihni). Note the characteristics given for each.
External existence is 'real' (asil) and 'agreed upon'; mental existence is 'non-real' like a shadow.
3
Understand the example of fire
Fire is used to illustrate external existence: it has real effects like light and burning. This example shows why external existence is uncontroversial.
The passage uses a concrete, observable example to ground the abstract concept.
4
Analyze the debate on mental existence
The passage presents two opposing views: philosophers and some theologians affirm mental subsistence; most theologians deny it, saying only knowledge exists in the mind. Compare the arguments.
This debate has implications for epistemology and the nature of knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal note explaining the division of existence. It classifies existence into two types: external (al-wujud al-khariji) and mental (al-wujud al-dhihni). External existence is the real, objective existence of a thing in the external world, as exemplified by fire and its effects. This is unanimously agreed upon. Mental existence, by contrast, is a metaphorical existence like a shadow; it is the mental image that corresponds to an external object. The author then presents a theological-philosophical debate: Do the meanings of things known by the intellect have actual subsistence in the mind, or does only the knowledge of them subsist? The philosophers (hukama') and some theologians affirm mental existence, while the majority of theologians (jumhur al-mutakallimin) deny it, asserting that only the knowledge exists in the mind. The passage ends with references to standard works in kalam and philosophy.

Division of Possible Existents: Substance and Accident
Maydani classifies possible existents into two categories: substance and accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam classification of possible existents into substance and accident, including their definitions and the reason why 'jawhar' is not applied to Allah.

Core Idea
The possible existent is either a self-subsisting substance or an accident needing a substrate.

In Islamic theology and philosophy, all possible entities fall into two fundamental categories: substance and accident. This classification helps us understand the nature of created things and also protects divine transcendence. Imam Maydani explains these terms and clarifies why the term 'substance' is not used for Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read the translation. Note the flow from the Necessary Existent to the possible existent and its two types.
The passage begins with the highest division before narrowing down.
2
Identify the definitions of 'arad' (accident)
Find how Maydani defines an accident: something that needs a self, position, or place to exist. Examples include colors and tastes.
Accidents are dependent on substances.
3
Understand the definition of 'jawhar' (substance)
Find the linguistic and technical definitions. Note the crucial point that 'jawhar' is not applied to Allah because it implies being an origin for others in a composite sense.
This guards against anthropomorphism.
4
Examine the subdivisions of substance
Substance is divided into simple (indivisible atom) and composite (body). Understand that these are subcategories of possible existents.
These categories are central to kalam cosmology.

الموجود إما واجب الوجود وهو الله تعالى، وإما ممكن، وهو قسمان: جوهر وعرض فالأعراض: هي ما تحتاج في وجودها إلى ذات؛ أي موضع أو محل تقوم به؛ فوجودها تابع للذوات القائمة بها؛ مثل الألوان والطعوم والحركة والسكون، فهي عبارة عن صفات قائمة بموصوفاتها أو معان زائدة على الذوات أو المحال التي قامت بها. والجوهر لغة: الأصل؛ لأنه أصل المركبات ومن هنا امتنع إطلاقه على الباري تعالى؛ لأنه ليس بأصل لغيره حتى يكون جزءا له، والجوهر اصطلاحًا ما قام بنفسه؛ فالجواهر هي تلك الذوات أو المواضع أو المحال التي لا تحتاج في وجودها إلى ما احتاجت إليه الأعراض، فالأعراض تقوم بالجواهر، والجواهر جنس واحد وإن اختلفت في الصور والهيئات، وهي :نوعان بسيط وهو ما يعبر عنه بالجوهر الفرد وهو ما لا يقبل التجزؤ، ومركب وهو الجسم وهو المؤتلف من جوهرين فردين فصاعدًا. انظر: الميين في شرح معاني ألفاظ الحكماء والمتكلمين ص ١٠٩-١١٠، فتح الرحمن شي مقدمة لقطة العجلان ص (٦٠-٦٢)، التعريفات ص (١٣٠)، عون المريد لشرخ جوهرة التوحید (۱/ ۲۳۲-۲۳۳).
The existent is either the Necessary Existent, which is Allah Almighty, or possible, which is two kinds: substance and accident. Accidents are what need in their existence a self, i.e., a position or place in which they subsist; their existence is dependent on the selves that support them, such as colors, tastes, motion, and rest. They are attributes subsisting in their subjects or meanings additional to the selves or places in which they subsist. The substance (jawhar) in language means 'origin'; because it is the origin of composites, and hence it is forbidden to apply it to the Creator Almighty, because He is not an origin for others such that He would be a part of them. In terminology, substance is what subsists by itself; so substances are those selves, positions, or places that do not need in their existence what accidents need. Accidents subsist in substances, and substances are one genus even if they differ in forms and configurations. They are of two types: simple, which is called the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard), which does not admit division, and composite, which is the body, which is composed of two or more individual atoms. See: al-Mubin fi Sharh Ma'ani Alfaz al-Hukama' wa al-Mutakallimin pp. 109-110; Fath al-Rahman … ; al-Ta'rifat p. 130; 'Awn al-Murid li-Sharh Jawharat al-Tawhid (1/232-233).

Commentary
This passage is a philosophical-theological classification of existents. It states that existence is divided into the Necessary Existent (Allah) and the possible existent. The possible existent is further divided into two categories: substance (jawhar) and accident ('arad). Accidents are defined as entities that require a substrate (a self, position, or place) in which to exist; they include qualities like colors, tastes, motion, and rest. They are attributes or additional meanings that inhere in their subjects. The term 'jawhar' (substance) is explained linguistically as 'origin' because it is the basis of compounds; for this reason, it is not applied to Allah, as He is not an origin for others in the sense of being a part. Technically, substance is what subsists by itself, not needing a substrate like accidents. Substances are a single genus despite differences in forms. They are further divided into simple substances (the indivisible atom that cannot be divided) and composite substances (bodies, which are composed of two or more individual atoms). The passage ends with references to standard works in kalam and philosophy.

Mental objects: do they exist in the mind or only our knowledge of them?

This passage introduces a key debate in Islamic theology and philosophy: whether the meanings of things we conceive have real existence in the mind, or whether only the act of knowing them is real. This disagreement has implications for epistemology and metaphysics. Let's examine the two views as presented by the author.

The Three Meanings of 'Mithl' and Their Theological Significance
Al-Maydani cites al-Misbah to explain the three meanings of 'mithl'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three distinct meanings of the Arabic word 'mithl' and how each meaning applies to Quranic verses about Allah, especially the verse 'laysa kamithlihi shay''.

Core Idea
The word 'mithl' can mean likeness, essence, or be redundant.

Before we explore how the Qur'an speaks about Allah, we must understand the precise meanings of key Arabic words. One such word is 'mithl', which appears in verses like 'laysa kamithlihi shay''. In this lesson, we examine the three possible meanings of 'mithl' according to Arabic usage, as explained by Imam al-Maydani, and see how this understanding protects our belief in Allah's uniqueness.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three meanings
Carefully read the Arabic text and its translation. Notice that the author lists three distinct meanings: likeness, self/essence, and redundancy. Pay attention to how each is introduced.
The three meanings are clearly enumerated: بمعنى الشبيه، وبمعنى نفس الشيء وذاته، وزائدة.
2
Examine the Quranic examples for each meaning
For each meaning, the author provides a Quranic verse. For 'likeness', he cites the verse about two human messengers. For 'self/essence', he cites 'laysa kamithlihi shay'' and explains that the 'kaaf' is not redundant but emphasizes that nothing is like His essence. For 'redundancy', he cites the verse about believing with 'mithl' of what believers believe.
Note the careful interpretation: 'laysa kamithlihi shay'' is understood as 'not like His description' or 'not like His essence anything'.
3
Understand the refinement about the redundant meaning
Ibn Jinni refines the understanding of redundancy in phrases like 'mithluka la yaf'alu kadha'. He argues that 'mithl' is not purely redundant; rather, it implies that the person belongs to a group characterized by that quality, making the statement more emphatic and stable.
This shows that even when 'mithl' seems redundant, it adds nuance.
4
Reflect on the theological implications
The correct understanding of 'mithl' protects the doctrine of tanzih (divine transcendence). If 'mithl' in 'laysa kamithlihi shay'' meant 'like', it would imply a likeness to something else. But if it means 'essence', the verse denies any comparison to Allah's essence entirely.
This lesson connects lexical analysis to core Islamic theology.

He said in al-Misbah: "Mithl" is used in three ways: meaning likeness, meaning the same thing and its essence, and as redundant. The plural is amthal, and it is used to describe masculine, feminine, and plural; so one says: huwa, hiya, huma, hum, hunna mithluhu. Qiyas is an expression composed of two or more propositions from which, by themselves, another statement necessarily follows; for example: 'The world is changing' and 'Everything changing is originated' yields 'The world is originated'. It is divided into two types: conjunctive (iqtirani) and conditional (sharti). The conjunctive is that in which the conclusion is not mentioned actually in its form, but potentially, i.e. in meaning, by mentioning its matter, like the previous example. It is called conjunctive due to the conjunction of its terms and their connection with each other without separation between them by the exception particle which is 'but' (lakin). As for the conditional, it is that in which the conclusion is mentioned actually, i.e. with its matter and form, like our saying: 'If this were a human, it would be an animal; but it is a human' yields 'so it is an animal'. This conclusion is mentioned in the syllogism with its matter and form. The figures are four; because the middle term if it is predicate in the minor premise and subject in the major, it is the first figure, like: 'The world is changing and everything changing is originated'. If it is predicate in both premises, it is the second, like: 'The world is changing and nothing eternal is changing'. If it is subject in both, it is the third, like: 'The world is changing [and] the world is originated'. If it is the reverse of the first, i.e. the middle term is subject in the minor and predicate in the major, it is the fourth, like: 'The changing is originated [and] the world is changing'. We love the Companions of the Prophet, plural of companion; al-Hafiz Ibn Hajar said: 'He is the one who met the Prophet, peace be upon him, believing in him and died upon Islam.' Commentary
This passage defines the term 'Companion' (Sahabi) and examines its conditions. It begins by stating the love for the Companions in the Tahawiyyah text, then the commentator explains the definition. He cites Ibn Hajar's definition: 'He is the one who met the Prophet, believing in him, and died upon Islam.' This definition is preferred over Ibn al-Salah's definition ('every Muslim who saw the Prophet') because Ibn al-Salah's definition is neither comprehensive (it excludes blind Companions like Ibn Umm Maktum who are unanimously Companions) nor exclusive (it includes those who saw the Prophet as disbelievers and later converted after his death, or saw him after death before burial, who are not Companions by consensus). The passage then addresses special cases: apostates who later return: there is a difference of opinion whether they retain Companionship. Al-'Iraqi notes that al-Shafi'i and Abu Hanifa hold that apostasy nullifies deeds, implying it nullifies previous Companionship, but exceptions are made for those who returned during the Prophet's lifetime. Next, it asks whether meeting must occur during the prophetic period or if meeting before prophethood counts (e.g., Zayd ibn 'Amr ibn Nufayl who died on the Hanifiyyah). Some include him because Ibn Mandah counted him as a Companion. Another issue is whether one who saw the Prophet before prophethood and then embraced Islam later but did not see him again counts. Al-'Iraqi says he did not see anyone address this. He cites evidence that the Prophet's son Ibrahim is listed among the Companions after his birth during prophethood, but not those who died before prophethood like al-Qasim. Finally, the passage discusses whether the one who sees must have reached the age of discrimination (tamyiz). For infants or children whom the Prophet did tahnik (chewing dates and putting them in the child's mouth) but who do not remember, the scholars did not address this explicitly, except al-'Ala'i who stated that 'Abdullah ibn al-Harith ibn Nawfal had no Companionship nor even sight. The passage ends with a reference to a work on the names of Badr.

Meeting Before Prophethood: Does It Count?
Debate on whether meeting before prophethood qualifies as Companionship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the debate over whether meeting the Prophet before he received prophethood qualifies one as a Companion.

Core Idea
Meeting before prophethood is generally not sufficient; the meeting must occur during the prophetic period.

This lesson examines a nuanced question: does meeting the Prophet before he became a prophet count towards Companionship? The passage discusses the case of Zayd ibn 'Amr ibn Nufayl, who recognized the truth before Islam and died on the Hanifiyyah. Some scholars, like Ibn Mandah, considered him a Companion. Others argue that the meeting must occur after prophethood, as evidenced by the fact that the Prophet's son Ibrahim is listed among the Companions but not his son al-Qasim who died before prophethood.

Misconception: Some think that any child who was brought to the Prophet and blessed is automatically a Companion.
Correction: Al-'Ala'i's statement indicates that without awareness or remembrance, such children are not considered Companions.

Checkpoint
According to al-'Ala'i, why is 'Abdullah ibn al-Harith not considered a Companion?
A) He was blind.
B) He did not see the Prophet.
C) He did not have discrimination (tamyiz) at the time.
D) He died as a non-Muslim.

Key Takeaway
A child who lacks discrimination at the time of seeing the Prophet is generally not considered a Companion.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Are you content with a passive relationship to the Prophet—one of mere cultural inheritance or association—or do you actively seek to 'see' him with the eyes of discrimination? What would it take for you to move from being someone 'brought to him' to someone who truly understands his message?
?
This lesson teaches that even being physically touched and prayed for by the Prophet did not guarantee companionship. How might you be guilty of assuming that external blessings or lineage automatically elevate your status before Allah? What does this demand of you today?
?
If the Prophet were to ask you about your comprehension of his teachings, would he find that you have 'discrimination' or that you are still an infant in your faith? What specific step will you take to grow in spiritual maturity?
