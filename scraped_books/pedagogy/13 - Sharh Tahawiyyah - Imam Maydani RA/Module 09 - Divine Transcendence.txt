Commentary

The plural form is used to encompass different categories, such as the world of birds, plants, inanimate objects, etc. Among these, rational beings (al-`uqala') are given precedence, so the plural is formed with the masculine sound plural (ya' and nun) to honor the noblest species. This demonstrates the comprehensiveness of Allah's lordship over all creation.

Core Idea
If Allah had a likeness, the eternal would become temporal or the temporal eternal – an impossibility.

Imam al-Tahawi asserts that nothing is like Allah. This lesson unpacks the rational proof behind this creedal statement. We will see how the definition of 'two likes' leads to a necessary contradiction if one is eternal and the other originated. Prepare to engage with a logical argument that reinforces Allah's absolute oneness.

Why Nothing Is Like Allah: A Rational Proof

The author shows that admitting any equal to Allah forces an absurd consequence: either the eternal becomes temporal or the temporal becomes eternal. You will see how this simple argument eliminates any possibility of anthropomorphism or likeness.

The Tahawiyyah opens with a powerful declaration of Allah's transcendence: 'Imaginations do not reach Him, nor do understandings grasp Him, nor do creatures resemble Him.' Commentary
This passage is an explanatory commentary on a key line from the Tahawiyyah: 'Imaginations do not reach Him, nor do understandings grasp Him, nor do creatures resemble Him.' The commentator proceeds phrase by phrase. First, he defines 'awham' (imaginations) as a bodily faculty in the brain that perceives partial meanings like bravery or generosity. He cites 'al-Sayyid' (likely al-Jurjani) from his Definitions. Next, he defines 'afham' (understandings) as the conceptualisation of meaning from words. He asserts that whatever is imagined or understood is contrary to Allah, and that Allah is the Creator of both the faculty and its objects. He then addresses the issue of waswasa (whisperings from Satan), citing a hadith from Muslim that hating such whisperings is a sign of pure faith. Then he explains 'anam' (creatures) as a collective noun with no singular from its own root, and states that the entire line is a negative attribute (sifah salbiyyah) signifying Allah's dissimilarity to temporal beings (mukhalafatuhu lil-hawadith). The footnotes provide additional definitions: the first footnote sources al-Ta'rifat; the second gives the full hadith; the third discusses the linguistic category of collective nouns; the fourth distinguishes between external and mental existence, though it is cut off. The commentator's method is to clarify each term linguistically and conceptually, then link them to Allah's transcendence.

Commentary
This passage is a theological explanation by Taftazani, transmitted via Qadi Zakariyya, clarifying the meaning of two key negations in Sunni creed: 'ليس كذاته شيء' (Nothing is like His essence) and 'ليس كمثله شيء' (There is nothing like unto Him). Taftazani states that these two phrases carry the same meaning: they negate any similarity (مماثلة) between God and anything else. The argument proceeds by logic: if similarity is negated regarding a being who has a likeness and attributes similar to him, then how much more is it negated regarding God, who has no equal? Taftazani then addresses a potential objection: this negation does not imply that there actually exists a similar thing to God. He gives an example: when someone says 'The like of the amir does not do such-and-such,' they are not admitting that the amir has an actual likeness; rather, they are negating any hypothetical similarity. Similarly, the Qur'anic phrase negates even the possibility of a likeness for God. Additionally, Taftazani uses a logical point: the like of a like is itself a like, so negating the like of the like entails negating both. The passage is significant because it defends the transcendence (tanzih) of God without falling into the error of anthropomorphism (tashbih) while also avoiding the extreme of denying God's attributes.

Negation of Tashbih and Affirmation of Tanzih
Imam Tahawi negates tashbih and affirms divine transcendence with positive attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct Islamic position on divine transcendence—negating anthropomorphism (tashbih) without falling into negation of attributes (ta'til), as taught by Imam Tahawi.

Core Idea
Allah is unlike anything in creation—transcendent beyond limits, directions, and physical attributes.

Imam Tahawi warns that true belief requires both negating any likeness to creation and affirming Allah's unique attributes. This passage establishes the balance between tanzih (transcendence) and affirmation of His attributes of oneness. It is a cornerstone of Sunni creed regarding Allah's nature.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the flow from warning to positive description to negation.
The Arabic is corrupted but the meaning is clear from the translation and commentary.
2
Identify the warning at the beginning
The passage starts with a warning: whoever does not properly negate tashbih and affirm tanzih has not attained correct belief. This shows the necessity of both negation and affirmation.
The exact wording is uncertain, but the doctrinal point is that balancing tashbih and tanzih is essential.
3
Note the positive description of Allah's attributes
Allah is described with 'attributes of oneness' (ṣifāt al-waḥdāniyya) and 'qualities of uniqueness' (nu‘ūt al-fardāniyya). This affirms that He has attributes but they are unique to Him.
This counters the view that negating tashbih means negating attributes entirely.
4
Understand what is negated
The passage explicitly negates limits (ḥudūd), endpoints (ghāyāt), pillars (arkān), tools (adawāt), and the six directions. This emphasises Allah's transcendence over any spatial or physical constraints.
The six directions (up, down, left, right, front, back) do not contain Him.
5
Reflect on the balance between negation and affirmation
Consider how the passage balances negation (tanzih) with affirmation of attributes. This is the middle path of Ahl al-Sunna, avoiding both tashbih and ta'til.

Commentary
This passage deals with the correct position regarding tashbih (anthropomorphism) and tanzih (transcendence). It warns that whoever fails to properly negate tashbih and affirm tanzih has not attained the correct understanding. It then positively describes Allah: He is described by attributes of wahdaniyya (oneness) and fardaniyya (uniqueness), and nothing in creation is like Him. Allah is exalted above limits (hudud), endpoints (ghayat), pillars (arkan), and tools (adawat). The six directions (the cardinal directions and up/down) do not contain Him, as is the case for all created things. The passage emphasizes that Allah is not confined by space or physical attributes, distancing Him from the attributes of contingent beings.

Correction: The passage affirms that Allah is described with attributes of oneness and uniqueness, showing that negating likeness does not negate attributes. Attributes are affirmed without modality.

The Throne, Kursi, and Allah's Independence
Affirms the Throne and Kursi as true, and declares Allah independent and all-encompassing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic belief regarding the Throne (al-‘Arsh) and the Kursi, that they are real entities, and that Allah is utterly independent of them and encompasses all things.

Core Idea
The Throne and Kursi are real, but Allah needs neither — He encompasses all.

This lesson examines a concise but profound statement from the Creed of Imam al-Tahawi. It affirms the reality of the Throne and the Kursi as part of the unseen creation, while firmly establishing Allah’s absolute independence from them. The passage also teaches that Allah encompasses everything, including His Throne, and that His creation cannot fully comprehend Him.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation. Notice the compact style and key terms.
The Arabic uses short, emphatic phrases.
2
Identify the two affirmations
The passage makes two main claims: the Throne and Kursi are true, and Allah is independent of them and everything below them. Find these in the text.
3
Reflect on 'encompassing' and 'incapable'
Consider what it means that Allah encompasses all things, including what is above the Throne, and that He has made creation incapable of encompassing Him. This highlights His transcendence.
Encompassing here refers to knowledge and power, not physical containment.
4
Connect to the broader creed
Understand how this passage guards against anthropomorphism (tashbih) and affirms tanzih (transcendence). It clarifies that Allah does not need the Throne for rule or location.
This is a key point in Sunni creed.

Commentary
This short passage affirms two important beliefs: first, that the Throne (al-ʿArsh) and the Kursi are real entities—they are true and exist. Second, it clarifies that Allah is utterly independent of the Throne and everything below it. This refutes any anthropomorphic notion that Allah needs the Throne or sits upon it in a physical sense. Rather, He encompasses all things, including the Throne. Furthermore, Allah has made His creation incapable of full comprehension of Him, emphasizing His transcendence.

The Reality of the Throne and Footstool
Imam Tahawi affirms the reality of the Throne and Footstool and clarifies Allah's relation to them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic creed regarding the Throne and Footstool as real created entities, Allah's absolute independence from them, and His encompassing of all creation.

Core Idea
The Throne and Footstool are real, but Allah is independent of them and encompasses all.

This lesson examines a key passage from Imam Tahawi's creed that affirms the reality of the Throne (al-'Arsh) and the Footstool (al-Kursi) as genuine created objects. At the same time, it clarifies that Allah is not in need of them nor confined by them. Instead, He encompasses everything, including what is above the Throne, and has made His creation incapable of fully comprehending His vastness.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage once to get a feel for its rhythm, then read the literal English translation. Notice the key terms: 'Arsh, Kursi, haqq, mustaghni, muhit.
The passage is one sentence but contains multiple doctrinal points.
2
Identify the two main affirmations
The passage first affirms that the Throne and Footstool are 'true' (haqq), meaning real. Then it describes Allah's attributes: independent (mustaghni), encompassing (muhit), and that He incapacitated creation from encompassing.
These affirmations are distinct: one about creation, the other about Allah.
3
Reflect on Allah's independence and encompassing
Ask yourself: Why does the author emphasize that Allah is independent of the Throne and what is below it? Why mention encompassing everything and what is above it? This refutes any idea that Allah needs the Throne or is limited by it.
This is a direct refutation of anthropomorphic interpretations.
4
Consider the final clause
The passage ends by stating that Allah has incapacitated His creation from encompassing (al-ihata). This highlights divine transcendence and human limitation—creation cannot fully comprehend Allah or His creation.
The pronoun 'it' in 'encompassing it' likely refers to Allah, but could also refer to the Throne or all creation.

Source Text

وَالعَرْشُ وَالكُرْسِيُّ حَقٌّ، وَهُوَ عَزَّ وَجَلَّ مُسْتَغْنٍ عَنِ العَرْشِ وَمَا دُونَهُ، مُحِيطٌ بِكُلِّ شَيْءٍ وَبِمَا فَوْقَهُ، وَقَدْ أَعْجَزَ عَنِ الإِحَاطَةِ خَلَقَهُ.
And the Throne and the Footstool are true, and He, may He be exalted and glorified, is independent of the Throne and what is below it, encompassing everything and what is above it, and He has incapacitated His creation from encompassing it.
Source pages p. 219

Commentary
This passage is a concise affirmation of two created entities: the Throne ('Arsh) and the Footstool (Kursi). The author declares that both are true (haqq), meaning they are real created objects. He then clarifies Allah's relation to them: Allah is independent (mustaghni) of the Throne and everything below it. This refutes any notion that Allah needs the Throne or is limited by it. Instead, Allah encompasses (muhit) everything, including what is above the Throne, meaning His knowledge and power encompass all creation. Finally, the author notes that Allah has made His creation incapable (a'jaza) of encompassing Him or His creation in the sense of full comprehension. This underlines divine transcendence.
