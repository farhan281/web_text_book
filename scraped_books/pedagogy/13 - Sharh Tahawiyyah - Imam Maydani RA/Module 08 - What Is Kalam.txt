What is Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah?
Al-Tahawi defines Ahl al-Sunnah and cites the hadith on sects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah as the prophetic path followed by the majority of Muslims, and the scriptural basis for the saved sect.

Core Idea
Ahl al-Sunnah is not a later school but the original prophetic path of the majority.

Al-Tahawi begins his creed by identifying whose belief he is expounding: Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah, the mainstream community of Muslims. This passage clarifies what that term means and provides a prophetic hadith about sect division as its foundation. Understanding this definition is key to recognizing the authority and continuity of Sunni Islam.

Guided Path
1

The passage includes a definition from al-Najm al-Ghazzi.
2
Identify the definition of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah
Find the commentator's explanation of what Ahl al-Sunnah means: the prophetic way and path, and who Ahl al-Jama'ah are: the Companions, Successors, and followers.
The term 'السواد الأعظم' (overwhelming majority) is used to describe them.
3
Understand the hadith on sect division
Read the hadith from Abu Hurayrah about the Jews, Christians, and Muslims splitting into sects. Note that the saved sect is identified as the one that follows what the Prophet and his Companions were upon.
This hadith is used as proof that the saved sect is Ahl al-Sunnah.
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this definition matters: it establishes Sunni Islam as the original, majority path, not a later development. Ask yourself how this relates to the concept of 'orthodoxy' in Islam.

He then quotes al-Najm al-Ghazzi to define the 'طريق أهل السنة والجماعة' as what the Prophet and his Companions were upon, and which the great majority of Muslims in every age adhere to. He provides multiple narrations: from Abu Hurayrah (the basic hadith), from 'Abdullah ibn 'Amr (stating that the saved sect is what the Prophet and his Companions are upon), from Mu'awiyah (naming the saved sect as al-Jama'ah), and from Ibn 'Abbas (emphasizing adherence to al-Jama'ah). The passage concludes by referencing Sharh al-Tariqah by 'Abd al-Ghani. The overall purpose is to establish the identity and authority of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah as the sole orthodox community.

Foundations of Religion and the Lord of the Worlds
Defines usul al-din and explains 'alamin'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between usul (foundations) and furu' (branches) of religion, the definition of din, and the meaning of 'alamin' as everything other than Allah.

Core Idea
Usul al-din are roots; furu' al-din are branches — and Allah is known through His creation.

Every discipline begins with clear definitions. In Islamic theology, the term 'usul al-din' refers to the foundational beliefs upon which the rest of the religion is built. This passage from the Sharh al-Tahawiyyah explains these foundational terms, distinguishing them from the branches of legal rulings, and then clarifies the meaning of 'Rabb al-Alamin' — the Lord of all worlds.

This is a key conceptual distinction in Islamic sciences.
2
Grasp the definition of din
The author quotes al-Sayyid al-Jurjani's definition: a divine institution that calls rational people to accept what comes from the Messenger. Reflect on how this definition incorporates reason and revelation.

Comprehend the meaning of 'alamin'
Read the explanation of 'alam' as everything besides Allah, including substances, bodies, and accidents. Notice that the plural 'alamin' covers different genera (birds, plants, minerals) and gives precedence to rational beings in its form.

And this is what they believe of the foundations of religion (usul al-din) — the plural of 'asl', in contrast to far' (branch). So al-asl is that upon which something else is built, and al-far' is that which is built upon something else; like the branches of a tree to its root. And the branches of religion are the legal rulings, while its foundations are the religious beliefs. And al-din, as in the Definitions of al-Sayyid: 'A divine institution that invites the possessors of intellects to accept what is from the Messenger.' And they practice religion to the Lord of the worlds (Rabb al-'Alamin). 'Alamin' is the plural of 'alam', a name for that by which Allah is known — and it is everything other than Him, consisting of substances, bodies, and accidents. Its plural is to encompass the different genera under it, for each genus is called a 'alam', like the world of birds, the world of plants, the world of inanimate objects, and so on. And the rational ones among them predominate, so the plural is with alif and nun (عالمين) giving preference to the noblest genus. Thus in al-Matalib. A reminder: it is incumbent upon everyone who embarks on something to conceptualize that thing by its definition (hadd) or description (rasm).

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of two key terms in the opening of the Tahawiyyah creed: 'usul al-din' (foundations of religion) and 'Rabb al-'Alamin' (Lord of the worlds). The author begins by defining 'asl' (foundation) in contrast to 'far'' (branch), using the analogy of a tree and its branches. He then identifies the branches of religion as the legal rulings (ahkam shar'iyya) and the foundations as the religious beliefs (aqa'id diniyya). He quotes al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani's definition of 'din' as a divine institution calling rational people to accept what comes from the messenger. Next, he explains 'alamin' as the plural of 'alam', which signifies everything other than Allah - including substances (jawahir), bodies (ajsam), and accidents (a'rad). The plural form 'alamin' covers the different species or genera, such as the world of birds, plants, minerals, etc. The masculine plural ending (with waw and nun) is used because rational beings (al-'uqala') are given precedence over non-rational, as the noblest genus. The passage ends with a methodological note (tanbih) that anyone embarking on a subject must first conceptualize it by its essential definition (hadd) or descriptive definition (rasm).

And is it stipulated [the meeting?] at the time of prophethood or more general than that, so that one who saw him before it and died upon the Hanifiyyah like Zayd ibn 'Amr ibn Nufayl is included? So it was said: yes; because Ibn Mandah counted him among the companions. Similarly, one who saw him, then the mission came, and he embraced Islam and did not see him; al-'Iraqi said: I have not seen anyone who addressed that. He said: and evidence for considering the sighting after prophethood is their mention among the companions of his son Ibrahim, not those who died before it like al-Qasim. He said: and is discrimination required in the seer, so that one who saw him while not discerning is not included, and the children for whom he performed tahnīk and they did not mention it after discernment, or is it not required? They did not mention that either; except that al-'Ala'i said in al-Marasil: 'Abdullah ibn al-Harith ibn Nawfal, the Messenger of Allah performed tahnīk for him and prayed for him, but he has no companionship and no sighting either.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the definition of a Sahabi (companion) and the conditions for being counted among the Sahaba. The author presents several questions from scholars. First, it asks whether seeing the Prophet prior to his prophethood is sufficient, or is seeing during prophethood required? It mentions Zayd ibn 'Amr ibn Nufayl, who saw the Prophet before prophethood and died a monotheist, and notes that Ibn Mandah counted him among the companions. Second, it asks about a person who saw the Prophet before prophethood, then lived to see his prophethood and embraced Islam, but did not actually see him after that. Al-'Iraqi said he did not find anyone who addressed this case. Third, it adduces that the Prophet's son Ibrahim is counted among the companions because he saw him after prophethood, while those who died before prophethood like al-Qasim are not. Fourth, it questions whether the seer must be a discerning adult; if a child was brought to the Prophet and had no later discernible memory, is the child counted? Some did not mention this, but al-'Ala'i stated that 'Abdullah ibn al-Harith ibn Nawfal received tahnīk and prayer from the Prophet but has no companionship nor sighting. Thus, the passage shows the nuanced criteria for the definition of a Sahabi.

Opening of the Tahawiyyah: Authority and First Declaration of Tawhid
Opening of the Tahawiyyah with attribution and first tawhid declaration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the authoritative transmission of the Tahawiyyah creed and the first foundational statement of Islamic monotheism (tawhid).

Core Idea
The Tahawiyyah creed is rooted in the Hanafi school and begins by affirming Allah's absolute oneness.

This passage opens one of the most widely studied Sunni creeds. Imam al-Tahawi begins by establishing that this creed is not his personal opinion but the transmitted belief of the early Hanafi imams and the broader Ahl al-Sunnah. He then states the first and most fundamental article of faith: the oneness of Allah, devoid of any partners, likeness, incapability, or other deities.

This is what the Imam Abu Ja'far al-Tahawi narrated in mentioning the exposition of the creed of the People of the Sunnah and the Community according to the school of the jurists of Islam: Abu Hanifa al-Nu'man ibn Thabit al-Kufi, Abu Yusuf Ya'qub ibn Ibrahim al-Ansari, and Abu 'Abdillah Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani — may the pleasure of Allah the Exalted be upon them all — and what they believe of the fundamentals of the religion and worship with it the Lord of the worlds. Commentary
This passage is the opening of the Tahawiyyah creed. It begins by establishing the authority of the text: it is transmitted by Imam al-Tahawi and represents the creed of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah according to the school of the three great Hanafi imams: Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The author then presents the first article of faith: the declaration of tawhid. It affirms that Allah is One, without partner, incomparable, not rendered incapable by anything, and that there is no deity besides Him. This is the foundational statement of Islamic monotheism.
