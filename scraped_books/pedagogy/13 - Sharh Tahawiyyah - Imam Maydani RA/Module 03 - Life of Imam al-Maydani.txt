Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the life and scholarly journey of Imam al-Tahawi, including his shift from the Shafi'i school to the Hanafi school and his classification as a mujtahid.

Core Idea
Imam al-Tahawi's journey from Shafi'i student to Hanafi mujtahid reflects his intellectual independence.

This lesson introduces the author of the famous Tahawiyyah creed. Imam al-Tahawi's biography shows how a scholar trained in one school can, through deep study, shift to another and become a leading authority. We'll explore his name, birth, teachers, and works.

3

Notice that he was the nephew of al-Muzani, a Shafi'i scholar, but he left him to study Abu Hanifa's books. This shows his independent thinking.

4

Ibn 'Abidin classified him in the third class of Hanafi jurists, capable of ijtihad. Note his major works listed.

Commentary
This passage provides a concise biography of Imam Abu Ja'far al-Tahawi, the author of the famous creedal text 'al-Aqidah al-Tahawiyyah'. It begins by giving his full name and lineage, noting his tribal affiliation (al-Azdi) and his legal school (al-Hanafi) and origin (Egyptian). The nisba 'al-Tahawi' refers to the village Tahiya in Upper Egypt. He was born in 230 AH. He initially studied with his maternal uncle al-Muzani, a prominent student of Imam al-Shafi'i, but he was drawn to the books of Abu Hanifa and eventually left his uncle to specialize in the Hanafi school. He studied fiqh under Abu Ja'far Ahmad ibn Abi 'Imran and met the chief judge Abu Khazim in Syria. He also heard hadith from many scholars. As a result, he became a renowned imam in fiqh, hadith, and creed. The passage notes that the scholar Ibn 'Abidin classifies al-Tahawi as belonging to the third of seven classes of Hanafi jurists, meaning he was capable of ijtihad in cases where no direct transmission from Abu Hanifa exists. His major works are listed, including famous hadith collections like Mushkil al-Athar and works on Quranic rulings. He died in 321 AH. Finally, the passage notes that a fuller biography will appear later in the commentary of al-Ghanimi.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical introduction to Imam al-Tahawi, detailing his teachers, his extensive scholarly output, and the praise of later scholars. It begins by stating that he studied under Abu Ja'far Ahmad ibn Abi 'Imran, then traveled to Sham (the Levant) in 268 AH, where he met and studied with Abu Khazim. This shows his dedication to seeking knowledge across regions. The passage then lists his numerous works, demonstrating his wide expertise in Qur'anic commentary (Ahkam al-Qur'an), hadith (Ma'ani al-Athar, Bayan Mushkil al-Athar), fiqh (al-Mukhtasar, commentaries on al-Jami' al-Kabir and al-Jami' al-Saghir), legal documents (al-Shurut, al-Mahadir wa al-Sijillat), inheritance law (al-Fara'id), refutations (Naqd al-Mudallisin, al-Radd 'ala 'Isa ibn Aban, al-Radd 'ala Abi 'Ubayd), history, merits of Abu Hanifa, qira'at, and even works on the rulings of Mecca's lands and distribution of fay' and ghanima. Importantly, it includes his 'Aqida, which is the famous Tahawiyyah. After listing the works, the passage cites Ibn Yunus, who praises al-Tahawi as thiqah (trustworthy), thabit (firm), faqih (jurist), and 'arif (knowledgeable), saying no one like him remained. Then it mentions that Ibn 'Asakir and Ibn al-Jawzi said similarly, and Ibn 'Abd al-Barr added that al-Tahawi served as a judge for twenty years, had excellent hadith memorization, and that Abu Khazim al-Qadi studied under him. The final line states that he died in Basra in 221 AH; however, the widely accepted death date of Imam al-Tahawi is 321 AH. The 221 AH date likely refers to his teacher Abu Khazim, as the context mentions him. This passage thus establishes al-Tahawi's scholarly pedigree and his monumental contributions, affirming his authority as a transmitter and interpreter of the Sunni creed.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive affirmation of the Sunni stance on the early Muslim community. It begins with the love of all Companions: we love them, do not exaggerate in love for any individual (avoiding extremism), nor do we disassociate from any. We hate those who hate them and only mention them well. Loving them is considered religion (din), faith (iman), and excellence (ihsan); hating them is disbelief (kufr), hypocrisy (nifaq), and transgression (tughyan). Next, the order of the caliphate is affirmed: Abu Bakr, 'Umar, 'Uthman, then 'Ali. They are the rightly-guided caliphs (khulafa' rashidun) who judged with truth and justice. Then the ten promised Paradise are listed by name, and we bear witness for them based on the Prophet's testimony. The passage further states that whoever speaks well of the Companions, the Prophet's wives, and his descendants is free from hypocrisy. Finally, the scholars of the Salaf – the righteous predecessors, the Tabi'un, and later scholars of fiqh and insight – are to be mentioned only with good; whoever mentions them with evil is on the wrong path.

Loving them is considered religion (din), faith (iman), and excellence (ihsan); hating them is disbelief (kufr), hypocrisy (nifaq), and transgression (tughyan). Next, the order of the caliphate is affirmed: Abu Bakr, 'Umar, 'Uthman, then 'Ali. They are the rightly-guided caliphs (khulafa' rashidun) who judged with truth and justice. Then the ten promised Paradise are listed by name, and we bear witness for them based on the Prophet's testimony. The passage further states that whoever speaks well of the Companions, the Prophet's wives, and his descendants is free from hypocrisy. Finally, the scholars of the Salaf – the righteous predecessors, the Tabi'un, and later scholars of fiqh and insight – are to be mentioned only with good; whoever mentions them with evil is on the wrong path.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think critiquing the Salaf scholars is acceptable in academic discourse.
Correction: This passage forbids speaking evil of them; they are to be mentioned only with good.

Key Takeaway
Respect for the Prophet's household and the Salaf scholars is an integral part of Sunni orthodoxy; speaking evil of them leads astray.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you hear someone criticize a Companion or a Salaf scholar, what is your immediate reaction? Do you remain silent, or do you defend them? Based on this passage, what does your silence or defense reveal about your own faith? Will you commit to speaking well of them even when it is uncomfortable?
?
Have you ever spoken ill of a scholar from the Salaf, even in jest or as a casual remark? How does the warning 'whoever mentions them with evil is on the wrong path' confront your past actions? What specific step will you take to repent and change your speech going forward?
?
Think of a person of knowledge from the Salaf whose teachings you find difficult or whose opinion differs from yours. How does this passage challenge you to still mention them only with good? Can you find a way to respect them while disagreeing, or does your disagreement mask an evil mention?
