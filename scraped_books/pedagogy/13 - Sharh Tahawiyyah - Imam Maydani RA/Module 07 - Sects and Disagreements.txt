Tahawi's Creed Is Ash'ari Across the Schools
Al-Subki states Tahawi's creed is Ash'ari with three exceptions; author comments on schools.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the creed of Imam al-Tahawi is fundamentally Ash'ari, with only three minor differences, and that Ash'arism is the dominant theology among the four Sunni schools.

Core Idea
Tahawi's creed is Ash'ari, and Ash'arism is the theology of the vast majority of Sunni scholars.

This passage from al-Subki and his father establishes a crucial historical fact: the creed of Imam al-Tahawi, a Hanafi scholar, is essentially the same as that of Imam al-Ash'ari. This counters the idea that Ash'arism is limited to Shafi'is, as the author shows it is the theological foundation of Malikis, Shafi'is, most Hanafis, and early Hanbalis. Understanding this helps you see the unity of Sunni theology across the madhhabs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that al-Subki quotes his father, then the author adds his own observation.
2
Identify the main claim and the exceptions
The passage states that Tahawi's creed is Ash'ari except for three issues (not specified here). Then the author examines each school. Identify which schools are entirely Ash'ari, which are mostly, and what deviations occurred.
The three issues are not named, so focus on the author's commentary on the schools.
3
Reflect on the theological unity
Ask yourself why this matters. It shows that Ash'arism is not a sect but the theology of the Sunni mainstream, with only a few deviations (anthropomorphism, Mu'tazilism) that are outside the fold.
This connects to the learning objective about the widespread nature of Ash'arism.

This underscores the widespread adherence to Ash'ari theology across the Sunni schools.

Origins and Naming of the Mu'tazila

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila were named for their founder's withdrawal over the grave sinner's status.

Before exploring Mu'tazilite doctrines, we must understand their historical origin. This passage explains how a disagreement about the status of a grave sinner led to the formation of a distinct theological school.

The Distinction Between Certainty and Despair in Ahl al-Sunna

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ahl al-Sunna distinguishes between affirming the deserved punishment of a sinner and the prohibited despair of Allah's mercy, and why the Mu'tazilite's position is not necessarily disbelief.

Core Idea
Certainty of punishment is not despair when repentance remains possible.

This passage from Sharh al-'Aqa'id presents a subtle theological objection raised by the Mu'tazilites. They claimed that affirming with certainty that a sinner will be in Hell constitutes despair of Allah's mercy, and that affirming that an obedient person will be in Paradise constitutes security from Allah's plan. The Ahl al-Sunna answer by showing a crucial distinction: such certainty is not despair or security because the final outcome depends on repentance and divine forsaking. Commentary
This passage is a historical proof and classification of the four rightly guided caliphs. It begins by affirming the Sunni doctrine that the caliphate after the Prophet (peace be upon him) goes first to Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali, and that they are the rightly guided caliphs. Then it provides detailed evidence for each succession. For Abu Bakr, it mentions his unique truthfulness in accepting the Prophet's message and the miracle of the Night Journey, his title al-Siddiq given by the Prophet, and his selection by the community at Saqifa, leading to consensus (ijma'). For Umar, it demonstrates that his caliphate was established by the explicit nomination (nass) of the previous caliph Abu Bakr through a written covenant, followed by the people's allegiance including Ali, thus forming consensus. For Uthman, it describes the shura council appointed by Umar on his deathbed, which led to Uthman's selection by Abdurrahman ibn Awf and the subsequent allegiance of the Companions, again establishing consensus. For Ali, it explains that after Uthman's murder, the senior Companions insisted on Ali's acceptance of the caliphate, and after initial reluctance, he received their allegiance, making it a consensus of the people of authority. The passage concludes by tying the thirty-year span of the Rashidun caliphate to the hadith of the Prophet about the caliphate lasting thirty years, with a note that Hasan ibn Ali's six-month caliphate completed that period. It ends with the prophetic command to adhere to the Sunnah of the rightly guided caliphs.

Umar's Caliphate: Nomination and Consensus
Details Umar's caliphate through appointment and consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Umar's caliphate was established by the explicit nomination of Abu Bakr and confirmed by consensus.

Core Idea
Umar's succession was both designated and unanimously accepted.

Imam al-Tahawi now turns to Umar ibn al-Khattab, showing that his caliphate had two pillars: the direct appointment (nass) by the previous caliph Abu Bakr through a written covenant, and the subsequent consensus of the community. This lesson examines the historical episode of Abu Bakr's illness and the document he dictated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two proofs for Umar's caliphate
The passage states: 'thubutat khilafatuhu bi-nass al-imam al-sabiq wa al-ijma'.' Note this dual basis.
This distinguishes Umar's succession from Abu Bakr's.
2
Read the text of Abu Bakr's covenant
Carefully read the document dictated by Abu Bakr. Notice its solemn language and the conditional clause about Umar's justice.
The document is a formal legal instrument.
3
Observe the public allegiance
After the document was sealed, the people gave allegiance, including Ali. This shows the consensus aspect.
4
Reflect on the principle of nass in leadership
This passage shows that a ruling caliph can designate his successor, and that designation gains legitimacy through community acceptance.

Commentary
This passage outlines the Sunni position on dealing with Muslim leaders and laypeople. We perform congregational prayer behind any Muslim imam, whether righteous (barr) or sinful (fajir), as long as they are from ahl al-qibla (i.e., not apostates). We also perform funeral prayer for them. We do not issue definitive statements about their final abode in paradise or hell (tanzil). We do not label them as disbelievers, polytheists, or hypocrites unless clear signs of that appear from them. Their inner state (sara'ir) is left to Allah.

Obedience to Rulers and Prohibition of Rebellion
The author forbids rebellion and mandates obedience with conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni position on rebellion and obedience to rulers, including the condition that obedience is not required if rulers command sin.

Core Idea
Obedience to rulers is obligatory unless they command sin.

This lesson addresses a critical aspect of Sunni political theology: the duty to obey rulers and the prohibition of rebellion. The passage from the Tahawiyyah clarifies that even unjust rulers must be obeyed in permissible matters, but this obedience is not absolute—it has a clear limit when they command disobedience to Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get its overall meaning, then read the English translation. Note the emphatic structure: 'we do not see...' repeated several times.
The passage begins with a prohibition of using the sword except in prescribed cases.
2
Identify the key prohibitions
List what the author forbids: rebellion, supplicating against rulers, and withholding obedience. Notice the exception: using the sword is permitted only when it is obligatory (e.g., legal punishment).
Rebellion (khuruj) is forbidden even if rulers are unjust.
3
Understand the condition for obedience
Find the phrase 'ما لم يأمروا بمعصية' (as long as they do not command disobedience). This is the crucial limit: obedience is an obligation from Allah, but it ceases when the ruler commands sin.
This prevents blind obedience and maintains the primacy of Allah's commands.
4
Note the positive commands
The passage also commands praying for the rulers' well-being and success, and following the Sunnah and the community. This shows a balanced approach: not rebelling, but also not cursing.
The prayer for rulers indicates concern for their rectification.

Note the final phrase: 'we consider love of them as religion and faith.' This shows that loving the Companions is not merely a feeling but an act of worship.

وَنُحِبُّ أَصْحَابَ النَّبِيِّ صَلَّى اللهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ وَلَا نُفْرِطُ فِي حُبِّ أَحَدٍ مِنْهُمْ وَلَا نَتَبَرَّأُ مِنْ أَحَدٍ مِنْهُمْ، وَنَبْغَضُ مَنْ يَبْغَضُهُمْ، وَبِغَيْرِ الحَقِّ لَا نَذْكُرُهُمْ، وَنَرَى حُبَّهُمْ دِينًا وَإِيمَانًا.
And we love the Companions of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and we do not go to extremes in love of anyone of them, nor do we disassociate from anyone of them. And we hate whoever hates them, and we do not mention them except with that which is right. And we consider love of them as religion and faith.
Source pages p. 222

Commentary
This passage establishes the balanced Sunni position towards the Companions. It begins by asserting love for all of them without exception. Then it warns against two extremes: first, going to extremes in love of any individual Companion (as the Rawafid do with 'Ali), and second, disassociating from any Companion (as the Khawarij do). The correct path is to love all. Furthermore, it commands hating those who hate the Companions. We are to speak of them only with truth and justice. Finally, love of the Companions is considered part of religion and faith itself.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Loving the Companions means we must consider them all infallible and cannot criticize any of their actions.
Correction: The passage does not claim infallibility; it commands love and refraining from speaking ill, but does not prevent objective scholarly discussion of historical events.

Loving all Companions and avoiding both excessive glorification and unjust criticism is an integral part of Sunni faith.
