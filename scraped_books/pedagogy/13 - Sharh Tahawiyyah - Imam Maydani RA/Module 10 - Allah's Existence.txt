Foundations of Belief and Divine Success
The commentator defines iʿtiqād and tawfīq from the opening of the Tahawiyyah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of belief (iʿtiqād) and divine success (tawfīq) as foundational concepts in Islamic creed.

Core Idea
True belief is a decisive judgment granted by Allah's success.

This passage from the commentary on the Tahawiyyah Creed clarifies two essential terms: iʿtiqād (belief) and tawfīq (divine success). The Imam emphasizes that our declaration of tawhid is made in a state of belief that is decisive and free from doubt, and that this belief itself is a gift from Allah. Let's explore these definitions.

Guided Path

The passage includes both the matn (original text) and the sharh (commentary).

Pay attention to how the commentary defines iʿtiqād as 'al-ḥukm al-jāzim alladhī lā yaqbalu al-tashkīk' (the decisive judgment that admits no doubt). Why is this definition important for creed?
It distinguishes true belief from mere opinion or hesitation.

See the definition of tawfīq: linguistically 'setting aright' and terminologically 'Allah making the action of His servant conform to what He loves and is pleased with.' Reflect on the implication that even the ability to believe is a gift from Allah.
This emphasizes dependence on Allah's grace.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the opening statement of the Tahawiyyah Creed. It begins by quoting the original text: 'The Imam said... "We say regarding the tawhid of Allah, believing with the success (tawfiq) of Allah Almighty."' Then the commentator explains the phrase. He clarifies that 'believing' (muʿtaqidīn) is a circumstantial accusative, indicating the state of the speakers. He defines iʿtiqād (belief) as a decisive judgment that does not admit doubt. Then he explains 'with the success (tawfiq) of Allah' meaning that the belief occurs by Allah's granting of success, not by their own power or ability. He defines tawfiq linguistically as 'setting aright' and terminologically as Allah making the action of His servant conform to what He loves and is pleased with. He attributes this definition to as-Sayyid (likely al-Jurjani). This shows the importance of affirming that true belief is a gift from Allah.

Imam Maydani explains that Allah's oneness is not numerical but negates partnership.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah's oneness is not numerical oneness, but rather negates any partner in His essence, attributes, or actions.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of divine unity (al-wahdāniyya). It begins with the foundational statement of tawḥīd from Imam al-Ṭaḥāwī: 'Indeed Allah is One, no partner for Him, nothing like Him, nothing incapacitates Him, and no god besides Him.' The commentator, Imam Maydānī, then explains the meaning of 'One' (wāḥid): it is not numerical oneness, but rather means that Allah has no partner in His essence, attributes, or actions. He states that divine unity is a negative attribute (ṣifa salbiyya) — it negates what is not befitting to Allah. This unity is of three types: unity of essence (waḥdāniyyat al-dhāt), unity of attributes (waḥdāniyyat al-ṣifāt), and unity of actions (waḥdāniyyat al-afʿāl). The footnote elaborates that comprehensive unity negates five specific negations, called 'quantities' (kamm): (1) continuous quantity in essence — composition of the essence from parts; (2) discontinuous quantity in essence — existence of a second god or more; both negated by unity of essence. (5) discontinuous quantity in actions — another creating independently; negated by unity of actions. The passage also clarifies that continuous quantity in actions, if understood as the multiplicity of actions, is affirmed because Allah has many actions; but if understood as participation in an action, it is negated by unity of actions. The author concludes by directing readers to Fath al-Murid for further detail.

The Three Types of Divine Unity and Their Negations
Imam Maydani details the three types of divine unity and the five quantities they negate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three types of divine unity (essence, attributes, actions) and the five quantities they negate.

Core Idea
Unity of essence, attributes, and actions together negate any form of multiplicity or resemblance in Allah.

Each category negates specific forms of multiplicity, called 'quantities' (kamm). This lesson unpacks those negations and clarifies a subtle point about actions.

The Argument of Mutual Prevention for Tawhid
Proof of tawhid using the argument of mutual prevention from the Qur'an.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument of mutual prevention (burhan al-tamana') and how the Qur'an uses cosmic order to prove that there is only one God.

Core Idea
Multiple gods would lead to conflict; harmony proves Oneness.

This lesson examines a powerful logical proof for tawhid based on the Qur'an. The argument of mutual prevention shows that if there were multiple gods, they would inevitably conflict, leading to cosmic disorder. Since the universe is orderly, there must be one God. We'll explore the verses and the reasoning that makes this argument so compelling.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the literal translation. Notice how the author quotes two Qur'anic verses and then states the conclusion.
The passage is concise; let the words sink in before interpreting.
2
Understand the logic of mutual prevention
The argument is: if there were two or more gods, they would either agree or disagree. If they disagree, one's will would override the others, causing corruption. If they always agree, they are not truly independent. Either way, multiple gods cannot coexist without leading to disorder. Since the universe is orderly, there must be only one God.
This is a classical logical argument known as burhan al-tamana'.
3
Examine the two Qur'anic verses
The first verse (21:22) states that if there were gods besides Allah, the heavens and earth would be corrupted. The second verse (23:91) says that each god would exalt himself over the other. Identify how each verse illustrates a different aspect of the impossibility of multiple gods.
The verses are not the argument itself but 'indications' (ishara) pointing to it.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage concludes: 'corruption is negated, and order exists, so oneness becomes manifest for the people of success.' Think about how the observable order of the universe (from the movements of celestial bodies to the regularity of seasons) testifies to a single, supreme Creator.
This conclusion is meant for those who reflect (ahl al-falah).

Commentary
This passage provides the proof of tawhid using the argument of mutual prevention (burhan al-tamana'). The passage also notes that the verse is an 'indication' (ishara) to the argument, implying that the explicit wording is not the full logical formulation but points to it.

The Declaration of Tawhid
Al-Tahawi opens the creed by affirming Allah's oneness and negating any partners, likeness, or incapacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the core declaration of tawhid in al-Tahawi's creed, including what it negates about God — that He has no partner, no likeness, and no incapacity.

Core Idea
Tawhid is the foundation: Allah is one, unique, and absolutely capable.

Al-Tahawi begins his creedal statements by affirming the oneness of Allah. This opening declaration sets the tone for the entire creed, establishing that Allah is unique in His essence, attributes, and actions. Before we can understand anything else about Allah, we must first affirm that He alone is God and that nothing shares His divinity or perfection.

3

Tawhid is not just saying 'God is one' but actively negating all forms of shirk, tashbih (likening God to creation), and inability.
4
Ask why this matters
Why does the creed begin with these negations? Think about what false beliefs they counter.

Learning Objective

Allah is without beginning or end, and nothing happens outside His will.

This passage teaches that Allah is pre-eternal (existing before time) and perpetual (never ceasing to exist). Moreover, everything that occurs is by His will alone. God's Transcendence: Beyond Imagination, Understanding, and Resemblance
Al-Tahawi affirms that Allah is beyond imagination, understanding, and resemblance to creatures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Tahawi affirms that Allah transcends all human faculties of perception and conception, and that nothing in creation resembles Him.

Core Idea
Allah is beyond imagination, beyond understanding, and beyond all resemblance to creation.

The creed concludes its opening section by emphasizing God's absolute transcendence (tanzih). This passage teaches that no human imagination can picture Him, no intellect can fully grasp Him, and no creature resembles Him. This is the logical conclusion of tawhid: if Allah is one and unique, then He must be beyond all comparison and comprehension.
