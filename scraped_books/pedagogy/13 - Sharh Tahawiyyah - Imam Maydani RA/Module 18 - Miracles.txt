Definition and Proof of Karamat
Defines karama and distinguishes it from other extraordinary events, with a proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of a karama, the five factors that distinguish it from other extraordinary events, and the proof of its reality through tawatur.

Core Idea
Karama: a righteous believer's miracle, not prophetic, proven by mass transmission.

The creed of Imam al-Tahawi includes belief in the miracles of saints (karamat). But what exactly is a karama? In this passage, the author provides a careful definition and distinguishes it from four other types of extraordinary events: prophetic miracles (mu'jiza), divine assistance (ma'una), gradual entrapment (istidraj), and false miracles that confirm liars. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correct theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic definition carefully
Begin by reading the Arabic definition of karama. Notice how each clause adds a condition. Then read the English translation to ensure you understand the precise wording.
The definition is rich: it includes being extraordinary, without challenge, at the hand of a righteous person following a prophet, with correct belief and action.
2
Identify the five distinctions
The passage lists four distinctions but actually covers five items: karama is distinguished from mu'jiza, ma'una, istidraj, and false miracles. Make sure you understand the basis of each distinction as stated in the text.
For example, karama lacks tahaddi, but mu'jiza has it; karama requires apparent righteousness, but ma'una does not.
3
Examine the example of Musaylimah
The author gives an example of a false miracle: Musaylimah's spitting into a well. Reflect on why this is not a karama or mu'jiza, but rather a sign of lying.
This example illustrates the distinction between karamat and false miracles.
4
Understand the proof by tawatur
The passage concludes with the proof for the reality of karamat: they are reported through tawatur (mass transmission) from many Companions and later generations. Consider why tawatur establishes certainty in Islamic theology.
The OCR cuts off here, but the meaning is clear: such widespread reporting confirms that karamat are real.

And we believe in what has come of their karamat — plural of karama — which is a matter extraordinary (khariq li'l-ada), not accompanied by challenge (tahaddi), appearing at the hand of a servant whose righteousness is apparent, committed to following a prophet from among the prophets, peace and blessings of Allah be upon them, accompanied by correct belief and righteous action; so it is distinguished by not being accompanied by challenge from the mu'jiza, and by being at the hand of a person of apparent righteousness from what is called ma'una (divine assistance) — and it is the extraordinary appearing at the hands of ordinary believers to rescue them from trials and tribulations — and by being accompanied by correct belief and righteous action from istidraj (gradual entrapment), and by following a prophet before it from the extraordinary [events] that confirm the lying of liars; like the spitting of Musaylimah into a well of sweet water so that its sweetness would increase, but it became bitter, salty. Mentioned by al-Laqqani, likewise in al-Matalib. And the proof for the reality of karama is what has been transmitted successively (tawatur) from many of the Companions and those after them

Commentary
This passage defines karama (miracle of a saint) and distinguishes it from other supernatural phenomena. It begins by affirming belief in the karamat of the awliya. The definition is: an extraordinary event (amr khariq li'l-ada) that is not accompanied by a challenge (tahaddi), occurs at the hand of a servant who is outwardly righteous (zahir al-salah), committed to following a prophet, and is accompanied by correct belief and righteous action. The passage then enumerates five distinctions: (1) from mu'jiza (prophetic miracle) by lacking tahaddi; (2) from ma'una (divine assistance) by requiring apparent righteousness (ma'una appears at the hands of ordinary believers for rescue); (3) from istidraj (gradual entrapment) by being accompanied by correct belief and righteous action; (4) from the extraordinary events that confirm liars (e.g., false prophets) by requiring following a true prophet. An example is given: the false prophet Musaylimah spat into a well hoping to make it sweeter, but it became salty. This definition is attributed to al-Laqqani and al-Matalib. Finally, the proof (dalil) for the reality of karama is given: the tawatur (mass transmission) of reports from many Companions and those after them, implying that so many reliable reports establish certainty.

Definition of Karama and Its Distinctions
Definition of Karama and Four Distinctions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of karama (the miracle of a saint) and how it is distinguished from mu'jiza, ma'una, istidraj, and false miracles.

Core Idea
Karama is a divine honor for saints, distinguished by humility and following a prophet.

The commentator begins by affirming belief in the karamat (miracles) of saints, then defines karama precisely. This passage clarifies what makes a saint's miracle distinct from prophetic miracles, divine assistance, entrapment, and false wonders. By understanding these distinctions, we avoid confusion and preserve the correct theology of prophethood and sainthood.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation

Focus on the flow: definition first, then distinctions.

Find the key conditions: khaariq lil-'aada (extraordinary), gheyr maqroon bi-tahaddi (not accompanied by challenge), on the hand of a servant outwardly righteous, committed to following a prophet, with correct belief and good deeds.
These conditions are essential for distinguishing karama from other extraordinary events.
3
Trace the four distinctions
List the four things karama is distinguished from: mu'jiza (by lack of challenge), ma'una (by appearing on a pious person), istidraj (by accompanying correct belief), and false miracles (by following a prophet).

Commentary
This passage provides a definition of karama (miracle of a saint) and distinguishes it from other types of extraordinary events. The commentator first defines karama as an extraordinary event (amr khaariq lil-'aada) not accompanied by a challenge (tahaddi), appearing at the hand of a servant who is outwardly pious (zaahir al-salaah), committed to following a prophet, and possessing correct belief and righteous deeds. Then he lists four distinctions: 1) From mu'jiza (prophetic miracle): by the absence of a challenge. 2) From ma'una (divine assistance): by being at the hand of an outwardly pious person (ma'una appears on ordinary believers). 3) From istidraj (gradual enticement/divine deception): by being accompanied by correct belief and righteous action. 4) From the extraordinary events that confirm the falsehood of liars (like the false miracles of Musaylima): by following a prophet. He gives an example: Musaylima spit into a sweet-water well to make it sweeter, but it became salty. The passage ends with citations from al-Laqqani and al-Matalib.

Evidence for the Reality of Karamat
The commentator provides two proofs for karamat from tawatur and Quranic stories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main proofs for the reality of karamat: tawatur from the Companions and explicit mention in the Qur'an.

Core Idea
Karamat are proven by widespread transmission and the Qur'an itself.

Many Muslims affirm that saints can perform miracles, but how do we know this is true? In this passage, the commentator presents two clear proofs: first, the sheer number of reports from the Companions and later generations makes denial impossible; second, the Qur'an itself recounts miracles from non-prophets like Maryam and Asif ibn Barkhiya. Let's examine these arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the passage once without overthinking. Focus on the flow of the argument.
2
Identify the two proofs
The passage gives two separate pieces of evidence. Find the first proof (tawatur) and the second proof (Quranic stories).
Notice that the commentator uses 'wa-aydan' (also) to introduce the second proof.
3
Understand the conclusion
The commentator concludes that once the occurrence is established, there is no need to debate whether karamat are possible. Reflect on why this is a strong argument.
This is a key logical move in kalam: 'thubut al-wuqu' yughni 'an ithbat al-jawaz'.

والدليل على حقيقة الكرامة ما تواتر عن كثير من الصحابة ومن بعدهم بحيث لا يمكن إنكاره؛ خصوصا الأمر المشترك وإن كانت التفاصيل آحادًا، وأيضًا الكتاب ناطق بظهورها من مريم ومن صاحب سليمان عليه الصلاة والسلام، وبعد ثبوت الوقوع لا حاجة إلى إثبات الجواز. كذا في شرح العقائد. (و) قد (صح عن الثقات من روايتهم) ما يضيق عن الحصر من كراماتهم؛ جعلنا الله من الصادقين في حبهم وأعاد علينا من بركاتهم.
And the evidence for the reality of karama is what has been transmitted mutawatir from many of the Companions and those after them, to the extent that it cannot be denied; especially the common aspect, even if the details are ahad (isolated). Also the Book speaks explicitly of their appearance from Maryam and from the companion of Sulayman, peace be upon him. And after the occurrence has been established, there is no need to prove the permissibility. Thus in Sharh al-Aqa'id. And (there) has been authenticated from trustworthy narrators of their narrations that which is too numerous to count of their karamat. May Allah make us among the truthful in their love and return to us from their blessings.
Source pages p. 194

Commentary
This passage presents the evidence for the reality of karamat (miracles of saints). The commentator offers two proofs: 1) Tawatur (widespread transmission) from many Sahabah and later generations, which reaches a level where denial is impossible. He notes that even if the details of specific miracles are ahad (solitary reports), the commonality of karamat is mutawatir. 2) The Qur'an explicitly mentions miracles occurring with non-prophets: from Maryam (peace be upon her) receiving fruit from heaven, and from the companion of Sulayman (Asif ibn Barkhiya) who brought the throne of Bilqis in the blink of an eye. Since these occurrences are established in the Qur'an (which is true), there is no need to prove that such things are possible. He then concludes with a reference to Sharh al-Aqa'id and the Tahawiyyah matn that karamat have been authentically reported from trustworthy narrators in abundance. The lesson ends with a prayer.
