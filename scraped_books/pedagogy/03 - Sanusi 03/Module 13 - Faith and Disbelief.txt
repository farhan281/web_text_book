The Insufficiency of Taqlid and the Majority View on Nazar
Sanusi defines taqlid and presents the majority view on its insufficiency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the majority Sunni position that taqlid is insufficient for true faith and that rational investigation (nazar) is obligatory, along with the three differing opinions on the status of a muqallid.

Core Idea
Taqlid cannot replace the rational knowledge required for valid faith.

Sanusi begins by defining taqlid as mere imitation without proof and declares it insufficient for the beliefs of faith. He then presents the unanimous view of the majority of scholars—including al-Ash'ari, al-Baqillani, and Imam al-Haramayn—that knowledge (ma'rifa) is obligatory. However, they differed on whether a muqallid is still a believer, giving rise to three distinct positions.

Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion about the role of rational investigation (nazar) in faith. It begins by defining taqlid as unquestioning acceptance of beliefs without proof, and states that the majority of scholars, including al-Ash'ari, al-Baqillani, and Imam al-Haramayn, hold that knowledge (ma'rifa) is obligatory and taqlid is insufficient. The majority then differ on the status of a muqallid: some say he is a believer but sinful for not seeking proper knowledge through nazar; others qualify belief based on intellectual capacity; a third group denies his belief entirely. The author comments that this division may apply only to those lacking any certainty. Finally, a minority view, championed by scholars like al-Ghazali, holds that nazar is not obligatory but only a perfection, and taqlid suffices. The passage thus maps the spectrum of classical Sunni positions on the necessity of rational inquiry for valid faith.

Commentary
This passage is an introduction to the study of the shahada (the testimony of faith). The author, Sanusi, begins by stating the title: 'Explanation of the encompassing of the two words of testimony of all the beliefs of faith.' He then asserts that the statement 'There is no god but Allah, Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah' encompasses all the previously mentioned beliefs. Crucially, he stresses that the scholars have declared that understanding its meaning is essential; without it, uttering the shahada does not save one from eternal fire. This is a guard against empty verbalism. Finally, he announces that he will discuss the shahada in seven chapters, beginning with its precise formulation. The passage thus serves both as a motivational preface and a clear statement that mere utterance is insufficient—true faith requires understanding.

This passage discusses the rulings concerning a disbeliever's utterance of the shahada (the word of tawhid) and its relationship to the validity of faith. The author then explains that the source of these three opinions is the disagreement over the status of the shahada itself: is it a condition (shart) for faith, a part (juz') of faith, or neither? He compares the case of a mu'min who disobeys by leaving an obligatory act like zakat.
