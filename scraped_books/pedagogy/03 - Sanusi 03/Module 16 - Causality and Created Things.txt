Customary Judgments: Conjunction, Not Causation
Sanusi explains that customary judgments indicate only constant conjunction, not causality, and refutes those who attribute causation to created objects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that customary judgments signify only regular conjunction, not causal efficacy, and why attributing causality to created objects is a theological error.

Core Idea
Custom only shows that two things happen together, not that one causes the other.

This lesson examines the true nature of customary judgments—those based on repeated observation—and clarifies a common misconception. While we say 'fire burns,' the reality is that custom merely indicates a constant conjunction between fire and burning. Sanusi explains that the real agent is known only through reason and revelation, and that attributing causality to created objects leads to serious error.

Guided Path
1
Read and understand the definition
Read the opening passage where Sanusi defines the meaning of customary judgments. Notice the emphasis on 'conjunction' (iqtirān) and the negation of 'effect' (athar).
Focus on the distinction between 'burning is conjoined with fire' vs. 'fire causes burning'.
2
Identify the error and its correction
Read where Sanusi refutes those who treat customary judgments as rational and attribute effects to objects by nature or deposited power. Understand why he calls it shirk.
3
Connect to the theological principle
Reflect on the claim that reason and revelation concur that God alone is the creator of all effects. This is the foundation of tawheed.
Consider how this principle affects your understanding of daily events.

Commentary
This passage explains the true nature of customary judgments (al-ahkam al-'adiyya) and refutes those who mistakenly ascribe causal efficacy to created objects. The author begins by clarifying that when we say fire burns, we only mean that burning constantly accompanies the contact of fire, based on observation. The word 'burning' does not imply that fire itself produces an effect; custom only tells us that two things occur together, not that one causes the other. Indeed, determining the actual doer (fāʿil) of the effect is outside the scope of custom; knowledge of the agent must be sought from reason and revelation (al-ʿaql wa al-naql). After asserting that both reason and religion agree that God alone is the sole Creator of all things and that nothing besides Him has any effect whatsoever, the author then criticizes those who erroneously treat customary judgments as rational necessities. Such people attribute each effect to its customary antecedent, either claiming that the antecedent acts by its own nature (bi-ṭabʿihi) or by a power deposited in it (bi-quwwatin ūdiʿat fīhi). The author condemns this as a blameworthy delusion, a hateful innovation, and a form of shirk (associating partners with God). The passage ends with a declaration of dependence on God.

Creation by Cause vs. Creation by Nature
Sanusi distinguishes creation via cause from creation via nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key difference between creation via cause and creation via nature, specifically regarding dependency on conditions and necessary conjunction.

Core Idea
Cause creates without conditions; nature creates with conditions.

Sanusi presents a subtle but important distinction for understanding how things come into existence without choice. While both cause and nature operate involuntarily, they differ fundamentally in whether they require external conditions. This distinction lays the groundwork for later discussions on divine attributes and creation.

Guided Path
1

2
Identify the shared feature
Both cause and nature lack choice (عدم الاختيار). This is the common ground between them.
3
Pinpoint the difference
The key difference is that creation by cause does not depend on the existence of a condition or the absence of an impediment, whereas creation by nature does. This is the central contrast.
4
Analyze the example
Consider the example of a finger moving a ring. The finger is the cause, and the movement of the ring necessarily accompanies the finger's motion. This illustrates the necessary conjunction of cause and effect.
The passage cuts off here, but we can infer that nature's conjunction is not necessary.
5
Reflect on implications
Ask yourself: Why does this distinction matter for theology? How does it protect Allah's transcendence?
This will be built upon in later lessons.

Commentary
This passage is a classification-and-distinction passage. It defines two ways in which something can be brought into existence without choice: through a cause (علة) and through nature (طبع). While both are non-voluntary, they differ in that creation by cause does not require any external condition or removal of obstacles; it is a direct, necessary emanation. In contrast, creation by nature depends on conditions and removal of impediments. Consequently, a cause is always conjoined with its effect (e.g., a finger moving a ring), whereas a natural agent is not necessarily always accompanied by its product (e.g., fire burning depends on contact). The passage ends abruptly, but likely contrasted nature's non-necessary conjunction.

Sanusi's Refutation of the Philosophers on the World as Effect
Sanusi refutes the philosophers' claim that the world is caused as an effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that the world is an effect of God leads to the eternity of the world and constitutes disbelief.

Core Idea
If God is a cause, the world must be eternal — but that denies God's will.

Sanusi exposes a critical flaw in the philosophers' reasoning: if the world is an effect of God in the way an effect follows its cause, then the world must be eternal. This contradicts Islamic teachings about creation and God's will. In this lesson, we will examine his argument and see why he labels it clear disbelief.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the English translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the strong language: Sanusi calls the philosophers atheists and their view clear disbelief.
2
Understand the consequence of cause-effect
Identify Sanusi's first point: if the world is an effect of God, then the cause must accompany its effect, implying the world is eternal.
This is because a cause cannot exist without its effect in this framework.
3
Grasp the absurdity of 'obtaining the obtained'
Reflect on why Sanusi says 'bringing into existence what already exists is impossible' – this is the principle of 'تحصيل الحاصل'. It contradicts God's will because an eternal world cannot be willed to exist if it already exists.
This shows that the philosophers' view undermines divine attribute of will.
4
See the final judgment
Recognize that the philosophers' belief leads them to deny all divine attributes like power and will, which Sanusi declares as clear disbelief (kufr).
The argument is a direct refutation of the philosophers' causal theory.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the philosophers' claim that the world's relationship to God is that of effect to cause. Sanusi first states the consequence: it would necessitate the eternity of the possible being (the world), because the cause must accompany its effect, and nature must accompany its impressed thing. This contradicts the very idea of will, because willing to bring into existence something that already exists is absurd—it is like trying to obtain what is already obtained. He then explains that this was the belief of the atheist philosophers: they thought the world's dependence on God is like the effect's dependence on its cause. As a result, they asserted the eternity of the world and denied all of God's attributes like power and will. Sanusi declares this to be clear disbelief (kufr). The argument hinges on the necessary concomitance between cause and effect, which would make the world co-eternal with God, thereby eliminating God's free choice and attributes.

Cause vs. Nature: The Key Difference
Sanusi distinguishes cause from nature and contrasts their conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the crucial distinction between creation by cause and creation by nature, and why the presence or absence of conditions makes all the difference.

Core Idea
Cause creates without conditions; nature requires conditions and may fail.

Sanusi now clarifies two types of non-voluntary causation: cause (ʿilla) and nature (ṭabʿ). Though both lack choice, they differ fundamentally in whether their effect depends on conditions. This distinction is essential for the upcoming proof that Allah's action is neither by cause nor by nature, but by will.

Guided Path

Conditions include both the presence of a condition (sharṭ) and absence of an impediment (māniʿ).
3

The finger-ring example shows cause: the ring moves with the finger without any extra condition. The fire-wood example shows nature: wood may not burn if wet (impediment) or not touching (missing condition). Reflect on why these illustrate the rule.
These examples are from everyday life to make the abstract distinction concrete.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sunnis affirm only one true agent: Allah, who acts by choice; other 'causes' are mere concomitance.

Sanusi begins by distinguishing three types of agents recognized by philosophers and naturalists. He then clarifies that among believers, only the first—agent by choice—is affirmed, and it belongs exclusively to Allah. He also warns about a common misinterpretation: when Sunni scholars speak of 'causation', they mean necessary concomitance, not real causal influence. This lesson unpacks these crucial distinctions.

Commentary
This passage provides definitions and an important clarification about causality. It begins by defining three types of agent according to philosophers: (1) agent by choice (fāʿil bil-ikhtiyār) – one who can either act or refrain; (2) agent by causation (fāʿil bil-taʿlīl) – one who acts necessarily without the ability to refrain, and whose action depends on no condition; (3) agent by nature (fāʿil bil-ṭabʿ) – one who acts necessarily but requires a condition and the absence of impediments. Sanusi then states that these three types are all recognized by philosophers and naturalists (whom he condemns), but among believers only the first – agent by choice – is affirmed, and it belongs exclusively to Allah. He then addresses a potential confusion: when Sunni theologians use the term 'causation' (taʿlīl) or speak of a 'cause' (ʿilla), they do not mean that the cause has any real effect. Rather, they intend mere concomitant relation (talāzum) between two things, either rationally necessary (like the impossibility of an effect without its cause, but without influence) or legally stipulated (like the relationship between wine and its prohibition). He concludes with a stern warning not to be misled by apparent meanings of expressions, which could lead to ruin.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb (acquisition). It begins by stating that human power (qudra) has no causal effect whatsoever on any of our voluntary acts. All acts—movements, stillness, standing, sitting, walking, etc.—are created directly by God without any intermediary. Human power itself is also an accident created by God, which accompanies the voluntary acts and is related to them, but it has no influence on them. The custom (ada) of God is that He creates acts at the time of that power, not by that power. He made the existence of this power a condition for moral responsibility (taklif) according to His sheer will. The technical term for this accompaniment and relation of the created power to the act without effect is 'kasb' or 'iktisab' (acquisition). It is on this basis that acts are attributed to the servant in a metaphorical sense, as in the Quranic verse {To it is what it has earned and against it is what it has acquired}. The actual creation (ikhtira') and bringing into existence (ijad) are exclusive to God. Then the passage explains two states of the servant: when God creates in him the power accompanying the act, he is called 'mukhtar' (chooser); when God creates the act without that accompanying power (like a tremor), he is called 'majbur' (compelled) or 'mudtarr' (constrained). The sign of the power's presence is the ease (taysur) of doing or leaving the act according to custom; the sign of compulsion is the lack of such ease. The difference between these two states is self-evident to every rational person. The Law confirms both states, and God graciously exempts from moral responsibility in the state of compulsion, as the verse {Allah does not burden a soul except with its capacity} indicates: capacity here means what one can do according to custom.

Commentary

فمنهم: مَنْ يعتقد أنَّ تلك الأمور تؤثر في تلك الأشياء التي تقارنها بطبعها وحقيقتِها، قالَ ابنُ دهاقٍ: ( ولا خفاءَ في كفر مَنْ يعتقدُ هذا ). ومنهم: مَنْ يعتقدُ أنَّ تلك الأمور لا تؤثر بطبائعها، بل بقوَّةٍ أودعها الله تعالى فيها، ولو نزعها منها لم تؤثر، قالَ ابنُ دهاق: ( وقد تبعَ الفيلسوفيَّ على هذا الاعتقادِ كثيرٌ مِنْ عَامَّةِ المؤمنينَ ولا خفاء في بدعةِ مَنْ يعتقد هذا، وقدِ اختُلِفَ في كفْرِهِ ).
والمؤمن المحقَّقُ الإيمانَ مَنْ لم يثبت لها تأثيراً ألبتة، لا بطبعها ولا بقوَّةٍ وُضِعَتْ فيها، وإنَّما يعتقد أنَّ مولانا جل وعلا قد أجرى العادة بمحض اختيارِهِ أنْ يخلقَ تلك الأشياء عندها لا بها، فهذا بفضل الله تعالى ينجو من جميع مهالكِ الآخرةِ.
By this is refuted the doctrine of the Qadariyya who claim the effect of the originated power in actions directly or by generation, and is refuted the doctrine of the philosophers who claim the effect of the celestial spheres and causes, and is refuted the doctrine of the naturalists who claim the effect of natures, temperaments, and the like; such as food satiating, water giving drink, causing growth, purifying, and causing to dry, fire burning, clothing covering the private parts and protecting from heat and cold, and the like which are countless. And they differ in their belief in the effect of these things: Among them are those who believe that these things affect those things which accompany them by their nature and reality. Ibn Dahaq said: 'There is no concealment of the disbelief of whoever believes this.' And among them are those who believe that these things do not affect by their natures, but by a power that Allah the Exalted deposited in them, and if He removed it from them they would not affect. Ibn Dahaq said: 'Many of the common believers have followed the philosopher in this belief, and there is no concealment of the innovation of whoever believes this, and there is disagreement concerning his disbelief.' And the true believer, whose faith is sound, is one who does not affirm any effect for them at all, neither by their nature nor by a power placed in them, but rather believes that our Master, Exalted and Glorified, has made it the custom by His sheer choice to create those things at the time of them, not by them. So this person, by the grace of Allah the Exalted, is saved from all the perils of the Hereafter.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of false beliefs about causality and a statement of the correct belief. It first refutes three groups: the Qadariyya (who claim the created power directly affects actions), the philosophers (who claim effects from celestial spheres and causes), and the naturalists (who claim effects from natures and temperaments). It then gives examples of mistaken causal attribution (food satiating, water quenching, etc.). Next, it explains two levels of erroneous belief: (1) those who believe things affect by their own nature and reality – Ibn Dahaq declares this disbelief; (2) those who believe things affect by a power Allah placed in them – Ibn Dahaq calls this innovation, with disagreement over its disbelief. Finally, it states the correct belief of the true believer: that no effect is attributed to created things at all, neither by nature nor by a deposited power, but that Allah by His sheer custom creates effects concomitant with those things, not through them. Such a believer is saved from all perils of the Hereafter.

Three Roots of Kufr: Reason Alone, Taqlid, and Customary Connection
Sanusi identifies three sources of error that lead to kufr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three fundamental errors that lead to disbelief according to Sanusi: over-reliance on reason, blind taqlid, and misattributing causality.

Core Idea
Three errors—reason alone, blind imitation, false causality—undermine true faith.

Sanusi identifies three roots of kufr that have plagued humanity. Each error represents a deviation from the proper reliance on revelation and recognition of Allah's exclusive causality. In this passage, he concisely enumerates them, providing a framework for understanding how disbelief arises.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the three distinct errors Sanusi lists.
The passage is a clear enumeration; look for the key phrases that introduce each root.
2
Understand the first root: reason alone
Sanusi criticizes those who make the intellect independent of revelation for deriving divine rulings. Why does this lead to misguidance?
This error ignores the need for revelation in legal and theological matters.
3
Understand the second root: bad taqlid
Blind following of ancestors is condemned with the Quranic verse. The verifying scholars say taqlid is insufficient for faith, equating a follower to a led animal.
Note the strong imagery: a mقلد (muqallid) is like a beast.
4
Understand the third root: customary connection
The naturalists observe regularities (eating → satiety, etc.) and wrongly think things cause effects by their own nature or an inherent power.
This error denies that Allah is the sole creator of all events.

And they made the intellect independent by itself without revelation to the legal rulings of Allah Almighty, to other such misguidances. And bad taqlid is the root of the kufr of idol worshippers and others, until they said: 'Indeed we found our fathers upon a religion, and we are upon their footsteps following.' And for this the verifying scholars said: Taqlid is not sufficient in the beliefs of faith. Some shaykhs said: There is no difference between a taqlid follower who submits and a beast that is led. And the customary connection is the root of the kufr of the naturalists, the ignorant among the believers, for they saw the connection of satiety with eating, quenching thirst with water, covering the private parts with wearing a garment, light with the sun, and such things that are unlimited. So they understood from their ignorance that those things that are effective in what is connected to their existence are with them, either by their nature or by a power that Allah placed in them.

Commentary
This passage enumerates three fundamental errors that lead to disbelief (kufr). First, Sanusi describes those who make human reason independent of revelation for deriving divine rulings, a position that leads to misguidance. Second, he condemns 'bad taqlid', which is blind following of ancestors out of tribal loyalty and prejudice, citing the Quranic verse where idolaters justify their shirk. He then reports that the verifying scholars (al-muhaqqiqun) rule that mere taqlid is insufficient for the beliefs of faith, quoting a shaykh who equates a blind follower to a led animal. Third, he identifies the 'customary connection' (al-ribt al-‘adi) as the root of kufr for the naturalists (al-tabai‘iyyun), who mistakenly attribute causality to natural phenomena based on observed regularities, failing to see that Allah is the true agent. The passage concludes by explaining their error: they thought things act by their own nature or an inherent power, rather than by Allah's creation.
