Definition of al-Ibāhah (Permissibility)
Sanusi defines permissibility as the Lawgiver's permission to do or leave an act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of permissibility (al-ibāhah) as a ruling from the Lawgiver, not an intrinsic quality of actions.

Core Idea
Permissibility is the Lawgiver's equal license to do or leave an act.

In this lesson, we examine the definition of permissibility as given by Imam Sanusi. This is a core concept in uṣūl al-fiqh and kalām, because it distinguishes acts that are spiritually neutral from those that carry a command or prohibition. Sanusi's definition centers on the permission of the Lawgiver, reminding us that even permissible acts are subject to divine authorization.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and Translation
Start by reading the Arabic sentence and its literal English translation. Notice the concise style: Sanusi gives a definition and then examples.
The term 'idhn (إذن) means permission or authorization.
2
Identify the Two Elements of the Definition
The definition has two components: the subject (the Lawgiver's permission) and the scope (both the act and its omission). Reflect on why both doing and leaving are mentioned.
This distinguishes permissibility from commands (which require action) and prohibitions (which require omission).
3
Analyze the Examples
The examples 'marriage' and 'sale' are given as instances of permissible acts. Consider why these are chosen: they are everyday transactions that are not inherently commanded or prohibited.
These examples show that permissibility covers a broad range of actions.
4
Consider the Implication
Sanusi's definition implies that an act is not permissible because of its own nature, but because the Lawgiver has granted permission. This counters the idea of independent moral evaluation.
This is a fundamental principle in Ashʿarī theology.

The definition implies that permissible acts are those for which the Lawgiver has given equal license to do or not do.

Definitions of Cause, Condition, and Preventer
Sanusi defines the three types of prescribed rulings: cause, condition, and preventer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of cause, condition, and preventer as prescribed rulings, and how each affects the existence or non-existence of a ruling.

Core Idea
Cause, condition, and preventer are the three gears of Islamic jurisprudence that determine when rulings apply.

After covering the five ruling types, Sanusi now explains the categories that govern how those rulings become actualized. These three categories—cause, condition, and preventer—are crucial for understanding how rulings relate to actions and events. The following passage defines each with clarity and examples.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice that each definition follows a pattern: 'that from whose existence/non-existence... necessarily follows.'
2
Focus on 'with consideration to its essence'
Pay attention to the phrase 'with consideration to its essence' in the definition of cause. Why does Sanusi add this qualification? What does it imply about causes in real life?
This clarifies that a cause is defined by its inherent nature, even if external factors block its effect.
3
Compare the three categories using the summary
At the end, Sanusi summarizes: cause affects both sides, condition affects only non-existence (from its non-existence), preventer affects only non-existence (from its existence). Use this summary to test your understanding of each definition.

وأما المانع : فهو ما يلزمُ مِنْ وجودِهِ العدمُ ، ولا يلزمُ مِنْ عدمِهِ وجود ولا عدم لذاتِهِ، مثالُهُ : الحيضُ ؛ فإنَّهُ يلزمُ مِنْ وجودِهِ عدمُ وجوب الصلاة مثلاً، ولا يلزمُ مِنْ عدمِهِ وجوب الصلاة ولا عدمُ وجوبها؛ لتوقف وجوبها على أسباب أُخَرَ قد تحصل عندَ عدمِ الحيضِ وقد لا تحصل .

[The prescribed rulings (al-aḥkām al-waḍʿiyyah)]
As for the positing (al-waḍʿ) for the two [i.e., for command and permissibility]: it is an expression for the Lawgiver's appointment of a cause (sabab), condition (sharṭ), or preventer (māniʿ) for what was mentioned of the five rulings that enter in our discourse under command and permissibility.
The cause (al-sabab): that from whose existence existence necessarily follows, and from whose non-existence non-existence necessarily follows, with consideration to its essence; like the noon declination (al-zawāl) for example; for the Lawgiver appointed it as a cause for the obligation of the noon prayer (ẓuhr), so from its existence the obligation of the noon prayer necessarily follows, and from its non-existence the non-obligation of it follows.
We only said 'with consideration to its essence' because existence of the caused may not necessarily follow from the existence of the cause, due to the occurrence of a preventer or the absence of a condition, and that does not impair calling it a cause; because if one considers its essence, disregarding the reason for the failure, its existence would necessitate the existence of the caused.
As for the condition (al-sharṭ): that from whose non-existence non-existence necessarily follows, but from whose existence neither existence nor non-existence necessarily follows with respect to its essence; its example is the [complete] year (al-ḥawl) with respect to the obligation of zakāh on gold, silver, and livestock; for from the non-completion of the year the non-obligation of zakāh on the mentioned [items] necessarily follows, but from the completion of the year neither the obligation nor non-obligation of zakāh necessarily follows, because the obligation of zakāh depends on full ownership of the niṣāb.
As for the preventer (al-māniʿ): that from whose existence non-existence necessarily follows, but from whose non-existence neither existence nor non-existence necessarily follows with respect to its essence; its example is menstruation (al-ḥayḍ); for from its existence the non-obligation of prayer, for example, necessarily follows, but from its non-existence neither the obligation nor non-obligation of prayer necessarily follows, because its obligation depends on other causes that may or may not be present when menstruation is absent.
From this it follows: that the cause (al-sabab) has effect in both its aspects, i.e., the aspects of its existence and non-existence; the condition (al-sharṭ) has effect only in the aspect of its non-existence, only in [producing] non-existence; and the preventer (al-māniʿ) has effect in the aspect of its existence, only in [producing] non-existence. And the place for exhaustive treatment of what pertains to the investigations of the legal ruling (al-ḥukm al-sharʿī) is in the discipline of legal theory (uṣūl al-fiqh).

Commentary
This passage defines three categories of prescribed rulings (al-aḥkām al-waḍʿiyyah) posited by the Lawgiver: cause (sabab), condition (sharṭ), and preventer (māniʿ). The author first explains al-waḍʿ (positing) as the Lawgiver's appointment of these for the five rulings mentioned earlier. Then each is defined and exemplified.

1. Cause (sabab): Something whose existence necessitates existence of the ruling, and whose non-existence necessitates non-existence, when considered in itself. The example is the noon declination causing the obligation of the noon prayer. However, the author clarifies 'in itself' because sometimes a cause's effect may be blocked by a preventer or missing condition, but that doesn't invalidate its causality.

2. Condition (sharṭ): Something whose non-existence necessitates non-existence of the ruling, but whose existence does not necessitate either existence or non-existence of the ruling. Example: the completion of a year is a condition for zakāh — without it, no zakāh is due, but with it, other conditions must be met.

3. Preventer (māniʿ): Something whose existence necessitates non-existence of the ruling, but whose non-existence does not necessitate either existence or non-existence. Example: menstruation prevents the obligation of prayer — when present, prayer is not obligatory, but when absent, prayer may or may not be obligatory depending on other causes.

The passage concludes by summarizing the effects: cause affects both existence and non-existence of the ruling; condition affects only non-existence (from its non-existence); preventer affects only non-existence (from its existence). The author notes that further details belong to uṣūl al-fiqh.

The Definition of Customary Judgment
Sanusi defines customary judgment and gives an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of customary judgment and how it differs from rational necessity.

Core Idea
Customary judgments are based on observed patterns, not real causation.

Sanusi now turns from rational and legal judgments to customary judgment (al-ḥukm al-ʿādī). This type of judgment is central to how we understand everyday causality—like fire burning—but the theologian insists that such judgments are merely habitual, not necessary. In this short passage, Sanusi gives a precise definition and an example.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the definition has three parts: establishing a link, by repeated conjunction, and the possibility of non-concomitance.
The parenthetical remark is crucial—it distinguishes customary from necessary judgments.
2
Identify the three components of the definition
Look for: (1) إثبات الربط (establishing a link), (2) بواسطة تكرر القران (by repeated conjunction), (3) مع صحة التخلف (while non-concomitance is possible).
3
Reflect on the example
The example of fire being burning is given. Why is this a customary judgment? Because we only know it from repeated observation, not from logical necessity.
This example shows the practical importance of customary judgments in daily life.

[Discussion on the customary judgment (al-ḥukm al-ʿādī)]
As for the customary judgment: its reality is the establishment of a connection (al-rabṭ) between one matter and another, in existence or non-existence, by means of the repeated conjunction (al-qirān) between them by sense-perception, (while the possibility of non-concomitance remains and neither one influences the other at all).
An example of that is the judgment that fire is burning – this is a customary judgment.
Source pages p. 118

Commentary
This passage defines the customary judgment (al-ḥukm al-ʿādī). It states that its reality is affirming a link (rabṭ) between two things – either in existence or non-existence – based on repeated observable conjunction (takrur al-qirān ʿalā al-ḥiss). The parenthetical addition clarifies that this link is not necessary: the conjunction can fail, and neither thing has any real causal influence on the other. The example given is the judgment that fire burns; this is a customary judgment because it is based on repeated observation, not on inherent causation. The definition distinguishes customary judgments from rational or necessary judgments.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Customary judgments imply real causal necessity between two things (e.g., fire necessarily causes burning).
Correction: Customary judgments only reflect repeated empirical conjunction; the possibility of non-concomitance remains, and neither thing influences the other essentially.

The State of the Successful Believer
Describes the intelligent person's response to prophetic asceticism and the state of the muwaffaq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the spiritual state of the successful believer who, through contemplating the prophets' asceticism, turns away from the world and strives for the vision of Allah.

Core Idea
Success is exchanging the worthless world for an eternal, indescribable reward.

This passage paints a vivid picture of the intelligent believer's response to the prophets' example. By recognizing the world's worthlessness, he redirects his entire heart toward the highest paradise and the vision of Allah. The author then describes the paradoxical state of the successful one—outwardly in distress, yet inwardly soaring in spiritual joy.

Guided Path
1
Reflect on the prophetic example
The passage begins by setting the stage: the intelligent person contemplates the prophets' asceticism and gains certain knowledge that the world is insignificant to Allah.
This knowledge is 'ilm al-yaqin'.
2
Understand the heart's response
Notice the complete turning away from the world with the heart ('a'rada 'anha biqalbihi bilkulliyya'). This is a total reorientation towards the hereafter.
3
Appreciate the believer's transaction
The author highlights the exchange: giving up something trivial for something immense. This is the 'profitable transaction' (ma arbah sifqat hadha al-muwaffaq).
4
Contemplate the believer's state
The description of tears, fear, and alienation is balanced by the soul's flight and flutter. This shows the tension between struggle and spiritual delight.
The fear is of missing Allah's pleasure, which is irreplaceable (la yumkin minhu khalf).

Commentary
This passage is a spiritual exhortation and description of the path of the seeker of the afterlife. The author begins by comparing this world to the provisions of a hasty traveler, emphasizing its transience. He then cites two hadiths: one advising believers to be like strangers or wayfarers in this world, and another stating that if this world had any value with Allah, He would not give even a disbeliever a sip of water. The author argues that when a rational person considers the asceticism of the prophets, he realizes the worthlessness of this world and therefore turns away from it completely, aiming for the highest paradises and the indescribable delight of seeing the Lord. He then describes the successful person's transaction: giving up the trivial world and gaining eternal blessings. The author vividly depicts the struggle of the seeker—humiliation, tears, fear, and longing—and the temporary relief from the 'breeze of connection'. Finally, he describes the reward: after death, the seeker becomes a king in Paradise, enjoying what no eye has seen, and concludes that this is the true kingdom for which one should sacrifice everything, though all is by Allah's grace. The passage ends with a poem urging patience in the pursuit of glory.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed grammatical analysis of the shahada 'lā ilāha illā Allāh', specifically the parsing of the word 'Allāh'. The discussion is presented as a commentary on the speech of earlier grammarians. The author begins by citing Sibawayh and al-Akhfash, who state that 'lā' is the operator ('āmil) in this construction. He then quotes al-Damāmīnī, who introduces an extensive discussion by Judge Muhyī al-Dīn Nāẓir al-Jaysh on the parsing of this noble word. The core analysis then begins: the people of knowledge (ahl al-‘ilm) state that the glorified noun (al-ism al-mu‘aẓẓam) in this construction is normally raised (marfū‘), which is the frequent case, and it is never otherwise in the Quran; occasionally it may be accusative (manṣūb). When it is raised, there are five opinions among grammarians, two of which are reliable (mu‘tabarān) and three unreliable. The two reliable opinions are that its raising is on substitution (badalīyah) — i.e., 'Allāh' is a substitute (badal) — or that it is on predication (khabarīyah) — i.e., it is the predicate of an implied subject. The author then elaborates on the substitution view, which is the common one among grammarians and the opinion of Ibn Mālik. Ibn Mālik's statement about the deletion of the predicate of 'lā' with 'illā' is cited as evidence that the raising is not on predication, so it must be substitution. Within substitution, the nearer view is that 'Allāh' is a substitute from the hidden pronoun in the implied predicate (the predicate of 'lā' which is deleted), not from the noun 'ilāha' in its original position before 'lā' entered. This is preferred because substitution from the nearer (the pronoun) is better than from the farther (the original noun), and because there is no need to follow the original case by place (i.e., the accusative case of 'ilāha' before 'lā') when followership by wording (i.e., the nominative case) is possible. The passage then compares two types of substitution: if the substitute is from the hidden pronoun, it resembles the substitution in 'mā qāma aḥadun illā zaydun' (where 'zaydun' is a substitute for the implied subject pronoun in 'qāma'), because both use followership by wording; if from the noun, it resembles the substitution in 'lā aḥada fīhā illā zayd' (where 'zayd' is a substitute for the original noun in the accusative after 'lā'), because both use followership by place. Finally, the passage mentions an objection to the substitution analysis in examples like 'mā qāma aḥadun illā zaydun': (1) it is a substitution of a part without a pronoun linking back to the substituted-from, and (2) the substitute is affirmative while the substituted-from is negated, causing inconsistency. The answer to the first objection is that 'illā' and what follows it are part of the first statement, and 'illā' itself indicates that the second item was included in the first, so no explicit pronoun is needed, unlike in 'qabaḍtu al-māla ba‘ḍahu' (where 'ba‘ḍahu' requires a pronoun to show the part-whole relationship).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed grammatical analysis of the shahada 'lā ilāha illā Allāh', specifically the parsing of the word 'Allāh'. The discussion is presented as a commentary on the speech of earlier grammarians. The author begins by citing Sibawayh and al-Akhfash, who state that 'lā' is the operator ('āmil) in this construction. He then quotes al-Damāmīnī, who introduces an extensive discussion by Judge Muhyī al-Dīn Nāẓir al-Jaysh on the parsing of this noble word. The core analysis then begins: the people of knowledge (ahl al-‘ilm) state that the glorified noun (al-ism al-mu‘aẓẓam) in this construction is normally raised (marfū‘), which is the frequent case, and it is never otherwise in the Quran; occasionally it may be accusative (manṣūb). When it is raised, there are five opinions among grammarians, two of which are reliable (mu‘tabarān) and three unreliable. The two reliable opinions are that its raising is on substitution (badalīyah) — i.e., 'Allāh' is a substitute (badal) — or that it is on predication (khabarīyah) — i.e., it is the predicate of an implied subject. The author then elaborates on the substitution view, which is the common one among grammarians and the opinion of Ibn Mālik. Ibn Mālik's statement about the deletion of the predicate of 'lā' with 'illā' is cited as evidence that the raising is not on predication, so it must be substitution. Within substitution, the nearer view is that 'Allāh' is a substitute from the hidden pronoun in the implied predicate (the predicate of 'lā' which is deleted), not from the noun 'ilāha' in its original position before 'lā' entered. This is preferred because substitution from the nearer (the pronoun) is better than from the farther (the original noun), and because there is no need to follow the original case by place (i.e., the accusative case of 'ilāha' before 'lā') when followership by wording (i.e., the nominative case) is possible. The passage then compares two types of substitution: if the substitute is from the hidden pronoun, it resembles the substitution in 'mā qāma aḥadun illā zaydun' (where 'zaydun' is a substitute for the implied subject pronoun in 'qāma'), because both use followership by wording; if from the noun, it resembles the substitution in 'lā aḥada fīhā illā zayd' (where 'zayd' is a substitute for the original noun in the accusative after 'lā'), because both use followership by place. Finally, the passage mentions an objection to the substitution analysis in examples like 'mā qāma aḥadun illā zaydun': (1) it is a substitution of a part without a pronoun linking back to the substituted-from, and (2) the substitute is affirmative while the substituted-from is negated, causing inconsistency. The answer to the first objection is that 'illā' and what follows it are part of the first statement, and 'illā' itself indicates that the second item was included in the first, so no explicit pronoun is needed, unlike in 'qabaḍtu al-māla ba‘ḍahu' (where 'ba‘ḍahu' requires a pronoun to show the part-whole relationship).

This passage examines the grammatical parsing of the shahada 'lā ilāha illā Allāh', specifically the case ending of 'Allāh' after 'illā'—whether it should be nominative (rafʿ) or accusative (naṣb)—and ties this linguistic analysis to the theological purpose of affirming divinity exclusively to Allah.
