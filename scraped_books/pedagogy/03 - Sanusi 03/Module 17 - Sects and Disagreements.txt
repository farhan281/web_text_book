Core Idea

Refutation of the Mu'tazila's Doctrine of al-Aslah
Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazila's principle of al-Aslah through three arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's rational refutation of the Mu'tazila's claim that God must always do what is most beneficial for creation.

Core Idea
Observed hardship disproves the claim that God must always do what is most beneficial.

The Mu'tazila argued that God is obligated to create the most beneficial circumstances for His creatures. Sanusi counters this with three clear arguments grounded in observable reality and logical necessity. This passage lays out why the principle of al-Aslah cannot be maintained, pointing to the existence of trials, the alternative of benefit without hardship, and the unequal distribution of benefit.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the English translation. Notice how Sanusi presents three distinct arguments against the Mu'tazila position.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the first argument: observation
Sanusi begins by stating the Mu'tazila claim and then refutes it by pointing to what we see in the world—trials and obligations exist, contradicting the claim.
This is the simplest and most direct refutation.
3
Understand the second and third arguments
The second argument says God could deliver benefits without hardship. The third notes that benefits are not universal, especially for disbelievers.
The third argument emphasizes divine justice and human accountability.
4
Reflect on the concluding prayer
Sanusi ends with a prayer for wellbeing and a good end without trial. This shows the proper adab (etiquette) of theological discussion.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Mu'tazila's principle of al-aslah (the most beneficial). The Mu'tazila argue that God must always do what is most beneficial for His creatures, but the author counters by pointing to observable reality. First, the Mu'tazila claim that if God were to do what is most beneficial, there would be no trials in this world or the next, and no obligations (commands or prohibitions), because these involve hardship. But this is contradicted by clear observation (al-mushahada) – we see trials and obligations exist. Second, even if one argues that these trials and obligations ultimately bring some benefit, God could have delivered those benefits without any hardship or burden. Third, these benefits are not universal: for those who die in disbelief, the trials and obligations are not beneficial but rather divine punishment and exposure to eternal destruction. The author ends with a prayer for well-being and a good end without trial.

The First Root: Ontological Necessitarianism
Sanusi explains the first root of disbelief: ontological necessitarianism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the error of ontological necessitarianism (al-ījāb al-dhātī) and why it leads to denial of God's attributes and the pre-eternity of the world.

Core Idea
Ontological necessitarianism makes God a blind cause, stripping Him of will and attributes.

Sanusi now explains the first root of disbelief: the belief that God acts by necessity of His essence, not by choice. This was the view of the philosophers, leading them to deny God's attributes and claim the world is pre-eternal. Sanusi refutes this by showing that the world is temporally originated and God acts by choice, using a rational argument based on simultaneity of cause and effect.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition and consequences
Read the passage defining al-ījāb al-dhātī. Notice how it leads to negation of God's attributes and the pre-eternity of the world.
The philosophers made God a 'cause' rather than a voluntary agent.
2
Understand the refutation
Sanusi provides a rational refutation: if the world were necessary from God, it would be simultaneous with Him (pre-eternal) or He would be temporal. Both are impossible, so God acts by choice.
The key premise: the effect must coexist with its cause.
3
Connect to the broader lesson
Reflect on how this root manifests in other contexts, e.g., denying God's power and will.

The Second Root: Rationalistic Moralism
Sanusi explains the second root of disbelief: rationalistic moralism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand rationalistic moralism (al-taḥsīn al-ʿaqlī) and how it leads to denying prophethood and obligating God to do what is best.

Core Idea
Rationalistic moralism makes human reason the arbiter of good and evil, even over God.

Sanusi explains the second root of disbelief: the belief that reason independently determines moral value. This leads to two major errors: the Brahmins denied prophethood because reason alone suffices for guidance, and the Mu'tazila obligated God to do what is best for creation, imposing human standards on divine actions. This lesson examines both branches and their refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition and branches
Read the passage defining al-taḥsīn al-ʿaqlī. Notice that it affects both the Brahmins and the Mu'tazila, though with different outcomes.
The Brahmins denied prophethood entirely; the Mu'tazila obligated God to act for the best.
2
Understand the error of the Brahmins
If reason can determine good and evil independently, then revelation is unnecessary. This led them to reject prophethood.
This is a form of rationalism that renders scripture superfluous.
3
Understand the error of the Mu'tazila
The Mu'tazila held that reason obligates God to do what is right and best, attributing purposes to His actions. This limits divine freedom.
They imposed human conceptions of justice on God.

This passage presents and refutes a dangerous deviant statement. First, the author mentions a person whose heart has been sealed by Allah, who interferes in matters of spiritual wayfaring without proper qualification. This person uttered a statement that is 'close to disbelief or is disbelief itself': that excessive remembrance of the Prophet (ṣallallāhu ʿalayhi wa-sallam) is a veil (ḥijāb) from Allah. Then another misguided individual rephrased this idea, claiming that if the tahlīl (lā ilāha illā Allāh) is uttered alone without the affirmation of prophethood, it is more effective and quicker in producing the meaning of tawḥīd. He argued that tahlīl and the testimony of prophethood have distinct meanings; when separate, the effect is stronger, and when combined, the meanings differ inwardly, weakening the effect and delaying the result. He concluded that joining the two formulas is only required at the moment of entering Islam. The author then quotes a firmly grounded imam (identified in the footnote as Muḥammad al-Sāḥilī al-Mālaqī) who condemns this statement unequivocally: it is a trial (fitna) that has no source other than the Fire and no end but destruction. It is nothing but deception (makr) and gradual seduction (istidrāj) leading to the rejection of the Sharia and dissolution (al-inḥilāl). The tone is severe, indicating that such an idea undermines the very foundation of the religion by separating the essential connection between tawḥīd and the prophethood of Muḥammad.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that excessive dhikr of the Prophet distracts from Allah, and that separating tawhid from prophethood strengthens tawhid.
Correction: The author and the imam clarify that this belief is close to kufr; it is a trial from the Fire that leads to rejection of Sharia and dissolution.

The claim that excessive dhikr of the Prophet is a veil from Allah is a dangerous innovation that separates the foundations of faith and leads to destruction.
