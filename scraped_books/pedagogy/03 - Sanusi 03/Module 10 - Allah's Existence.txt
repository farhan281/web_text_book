Proof of God's Existence from Contingency
Presents the proof of God's existence based on the contingency of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari proof for God's existence based on the contingency of the world and the necessity of a creator to tilt the balance from non-existence to existence.

Core Idea
The world's contingency demands a Creator to bring it from non-existence to existence.

This lesson presents the central proof for God's existence in the Ash'ari tradition: the argument from contingency (ḥudūth al-‘ālam). It argues that since the world came into being after not existing, it must have a Creator who chose to bring it into existence. Without a cause, no two equal possibilities would become actual. Let's examine the logic.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: a statement of the proof, then a conditional argument, then the conclusion.
The footnotes are editorial and can be read separately.
2
Identify the key premise: contingency
The proof is based on 'ḥudūth al-‘ālam' (contingency of the world). Understand that contingency means the world came into existence after not existing.
3
Trace the conditional reasoning
The author uses a conditional: 'if it had no originator... then it would necessitate...'. Follow this logic to see why a self-caused world leads to an impossibility.
The impossibility is that two equal things become actual without a cause.
4
Understand the need for a preponderant cause
For two equal possibilities (existence vs non-existence) to have one become actual, there must be a cause that tips the balance (murajjiḥ). Without it, both states would remain equally possible.
5
Connect to the conclusion
Therefore, the world must have a Creator (muḥdith) who brings it into existence. This is the proof of God's existence from contingency.

Commentary
This passage provides the central proof for God's existence in the Ash'ari system: the contingency argument (ḥudūth al-‘ālam). It states that the world is contingent (ḥādith), meaning it came into existence after non-existence. If it had no creator, it would have come into existence by itself. But for something to come into existence by itself, it would require that from two equally possible states (existence and non-existence), one becomes actual without any choosing cause. This is impossible, because without a cause (murajjiḥ) to tip the balance, both states would remain equally possible and no actualization would occur. Therefore, a Creator (muḥdith) must exist who brings the world into existence. The text then includes two footnotes: the first explains that the term 'karāhah' (aversion) in the impossible attributes section is interpreted in a specific sense to avoid confusion with legal aversion (al-karāhah al-shar‘iyya). The second notes a variant reading in the proof: 'li-nafsihi' (for itself) instead of 'bi-nafsihi' (by itself) in some manuscripts, but the established version is 'bi-nafsihi' as in the commentary copies. These footnotes are editorial and do not affect the main argument.

Proof of Allah's Existence: The Origin of the World
Sanusi presents a concise proof using the contingency of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam argument for Allah's existence based on the origination of the world and why the world cannot have originated by itself.

Core Idea
If the world originated, it must have an external originator.

Sanusi presents a classic kalam proof: the world is originated (hādith), meaning it came into existence after not existing. If it had originated without a cause, it would be an arbitrary preference for existence over non-existence. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the compact style of the argument.
2
Understand key terms
The terms 'burhān' (proof), 'ḥudūth al-ʿālam' (origination of the world), and 'muḥāl' (impossible) are central. Make sure you grasp their meanings.
Ḥudūth refers to temporal origination from non-existence.
3
Trace the logical sequence
Follow the conditional reasoning: if the world had no originator, then its existence rather than non-existence would be a preponderance without cause, which is impossible. Therefore, it must have an originator.
This is the principle of sufficient reason applied to contingency.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Ask yourself: How does this argument point to Allah? The originator must be external to the world, eternal, and powerful.
The proof does not prove all divine attributes, only existence.

Source Text

أَمَّا بُرْهَانُ وُجُودِهِ تَعَالَى : فَحُدُوثُ الْعَالَم ; لِأَنَّهُ لَوْ لَمْ يَكُنْ لَهُ مُحْدِثٌ بَلْ حَدَثَ بِنَفْسِهِ لَزِمَ أَنْ يَكُونَ أَحَدُ الْأَمْرَيْنِ الْمُتَسَاوِيَيْنِ مُسَاوِياً لِصَاحِبِهِ رَاجِحاً عَلَيْهِ بِلا سَبَبٍ ، وَهُوَ محال .
As for the proof of His existence, exalted: it is the origination of the world. For if it did not have an originator but rather originated by itself, it would necessarily follow that one of two equal possibilities would be equal to its counterpart but preponderant over it without cause, and that is impossible.

Commentary
This is a standard kalam proof for the existence of Allah based on the contingency of the world. The argument is: The world is originated (ḥādith). If it had originated by itself, then its existence rather than non-existence would be a preponderance without a cause, which is absurd. Therefore, it must have an external originator, who is Allah.

Proof of the World's Origination
Sanusi proves the world's origination by its inseparability from originated accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof for the world's origination based on its inseparability from originated accidents.

Core Idea
The world is originated because it cannot exist without originated accidents.

Sanusi now presents the key argument for the world's temporality. He shows that every part of the world is subject to change, and whatever changes must have come into existence. This passage lays out the proof in a concise logical chain that forms the foundation for establishing the existence of a Creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once carefully, then read the English translation. This allows you to absorb the argument as Sanusi intended.
Notice the two main parts: the first about the world, the second about accidents.
2
Identify the premises
The proof has two key premises: (1) the world is inseparable from originated accidents, and (2) whatever is inseparable from an originated thing is itself originated. Find these in the text.
3
Understand the proof for accidents
The text then proves that accidents themselves are originated by direct observation of their change. Ask yourself: how does this observation support the claim?
Change from non-existence to existence indicates origination.
4
Link premises to conclusion
Combine the premises: since the world always has accidents, and accidents are originated, the world must also be originated. Reflect on why this conclusion follows logically.
This is the core of the kalam cosmological argument.

The Exception of God from the Fourfold Division
Sanusi notes that the classification excludes God due to His necessary existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God and His attributes are not subject to the fourfold classification of opposite, contradictory, like, and different.

Core Idea
God's necessary existence places Him beyond all contingent categories.

Sanusi now adds a crucial clarification: the fourfold division covers all contingent things, but not God. Because God's existence is necessary and cannot be removed, He cannot be considered an opposite, contradictory, like, or different from anything. This short passage elevates our understanding of divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and then the English translation. Notice that Sanusi calls this a 'benefit' (فائدة), meaning an important corollary.
The passage assumes you already know the four categories from the previous lesson.
2
Identify the four categories mentioned
The passage refers to four categories: opposite (ضد), contradictory (نقيض), like (مثل), and different (خلاف). These are the classifications that apply to all things except God.
3
Understand the reason for exclusion
The key phrase is 'due to the impossibility of removal because of the necessity of existence.' God cannot not exist, so He cannot be paired or compared in the ways that contingent things can.
Contingent things can be removed (e.g., opposites can both be absent), but God's existence is necessary.
4
Reflect on divine transcendence
This teaching emphasizes God's absolute uniqueness (توحد) and distinction (تفرد). He is beyond all relational categories used for creation.
This is a cornerstone of Islamic theology: tanzih (transcendence).

Commentary
This short passage is a concluding benefit (فائدة) that clarifies an important exception to the fourfold division: God Almighty. Since the classification deals with pairs of contingent things that can coexist or be removed, it does not apply to God. God is unique (توحد) and distinguished (تفرد) from all things. He is not an opposite, contradictory, like, or different from anything, because all these categories imply the possibility of removal (ارتفاع) or change, but God's existence is necessary (واجب الوجود) and cannot not exist. This applies to His essence and attributes alike. Thus, the classification is exhaustive for everything other than God.

The First Three Impossible Attributes and Their Relation to the Necessary Ones
Sanusi explains the first three impossible attributes and their relation to the necessary ones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first three impossible attributes (non-existence, origination, occurrence of non-existence) and how they are the contradictions of the first three necessary attributes, and how the impossibility of non-existence entails the impossibility of the other two.

Core Idea
Non-existence is impossible for Allah, which logically excludes both origination and perishing.

Sanusi begins his discussion of the impossible attributes by pairing each necessary attribute with its logical opposite. The first three impossible attributes are direct contradictions of the first three necessary attributes. Understanding this pairing is essential for grasping the logical structure of the twenty necessary and twenty impossible attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the overall flow, then read the translation to understand the meaning.
The passage includes a footnote; focus first on the main teaching.
2
Identify the three pairs
Notice how each impossible attribute is the contradiction of a necessary attribute: non-existence contradicts existence, origination contradicts pre-eternity, and occurrence of non-existence contradicts everlastingness.
3
Understand the logical entailment
The passage explains that because non-existence is impossible for Allah, origination (which would imply non-existence before) and perishing (which would imply non-existence after) are also impossible.
This shows the interconnectedness of the attributes.

Commentary
This passage deals with the impossible attributes (مستحيلات) that are the negations of the necessary attributes. Specifically, it explains the first three: non-existence (عدم), origination (حدوث), and occurrence of non-existence (طروء العدم, also called فناء). These are the contradictions (نقيض) of the first three necessary attributes: existence (وجود), pre-eternity (قدم), and everlastingness (بقاء). The author notes that the arrangement of the twenty impossible attributes follows that of the twenty necessary ones. Then it is explained that the impossibility of non-existence in relation to Allah necessitates the impossibility of the other two (origination and perishing) because if non-existence is impossible, then there is no non-existence before or after. From this, one understands that the necessity of existence for Allah implies the necessity of pre-eternity and everlastingness. The footnote clarifies a technical point about the term 'contradiction' (نقيض): the author's statement that 'non-existence is the contradiction of existence' is considered imprecise by some, who note that the true contradiction of existence is non-existence (اللاوجود). The commentator argues that non-existence is more specific than the contradiction, based on the doctrine of states (أحوال). This is a subtle point but shows the depth of scholarship.

Proof of God's Existence through the Temporality of the World
Sanusi presents the proof of God's existence via the temporality of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classical kalam proof of God's existence based on the temporality of the world, including its three-part logical chain.

Core Idea
The world's temporality proves it needs a Creator.

This passage presents the classical kalam argument for God's existence known as the proof from temporality (huduth). Sanusi lays out a three-step logical chain: first, the world must be temporal because it is inseparable from changing accidents; second, those accidents are clearly temporal because we observe them change; third, a temporal world requires an originator. Let's examine this argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once carefully, then read the English translation. Notice the three distinct parts of the proof, each introduced by the word 'دليل' (proof) or 'برهان'.
The passage is dense but logically structured.
2
Identify the three sub-proofs
Find the three claims: (1) God's existence is proven by the world's temporality, (2) the world's temporality is proven by its association with temporal accidents, (3) the temporality of accidents is proven by observation of change.
Each claim is supported by a reason.
3
Understand the principle of preponderance without cause
In the first part, Sanusi argues that if the world occurred by itself, then one of two equally possible states would become preponderant without a cause. This is impossible, so the world must have an originator.
This is a key logical principle in kalam.
4
Trace the chain of reasoning
Follow the chain: observation shows accidents change -> accidents are temporal -> the world is temporal because it always accompanies accidents -> a temporal world needs a Creator.
Each link in the chain is necessary.
5
Reflect on the role of observation
The proof relies on empirical observation (mushahada) of change. This makes the argument grounded in everyday experience.
Contrast this with pure metaphysical arguments.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (burhan) of God's existence based on the temporality of the world (huduth al-‘alam). The argument proceeds in three steps. First, the proof of God's existence is the temporality of the world: if the world were eternal and self-originating, then given two equally possible states (existence or non-existence at a given time), one would become preponderant without a cause – which is absurd. Therefore, the world must have an originator (muhdith). Second, the proof of the world's temporality is its inseparable association with temporal accidents (a‘rad haditha) like motion and rest. Whatever is inseparable from a temporal thing is itself temporal. Third, the proof of the temporality of accidents is direct observation (mushahada) of their change: they pass from non-existence to existence and from existence to non-existence. The author thus establishes a chain: accidents are temporal because we see them change; the world is temporal because it always accompanies accidents; thus the world needs a Creator.

The Proof of God's Existence from the Contingency of the World
Sanusi presents the logical proof of God's existence from the contingency of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the contingency argument for God's existence as presented by Imam Sanusi.

Core Idea
The world's constant change proves it needs a Creator.

In this passage, Sanusi lays out one of the most famous arguments in Islamic theology: the contingency argument. He starts by stating that the world's very existence as something that came into being points to a Creator. Then he systematically proves that the world is indeed contingent—by showing that it is always linked to changing accidents, and those accidents are undeniably contingent. Follow the logical steps to see how he builds the case.

Guided Path
1
Understand the main claim
Read the opening statement where Sanusi says the proof is the contingency of the world, and the consequence if it had no creator.
Notice the phrase 'لَزِمَ أَنْ يَكُونَ أَحَدُ الْأَمْرَيْنِ الْمُتَسَاوِيَيْنِ مُسَاوِياً لِصَاحِبِهِ رَاجِحاً عَلَيْهِ بِلا سَبَبٍ' which introduces the impossibility of preponderance without cause.
2
Examine the evidence for contingency
Sanusi argues that the world is inseparable from accidents (motion and rest) that are observed to change.
The key principle: 'مُلازِمُ الْحَادِثِ حَادِثْ' (whatever is inseparable from a contingent thing is contingent).
3
Prove that accidents are contingent
Motion and rest cannot be eternal because they cease when their opposite appears; therefore they are contingent.
This uses the observation that each can be absent.
4
Apply the principle to bodies
Bodies are never free from motion or rest, so they are contingent as well.
Since motion and rest are contingent, their inseparable companion (body) must also be contingent.
5
Conclude the need for a creator
A contingent world requires a creator; otherwise, we would have an impossible situation of equality and preponderance without a cause.
This final step ties back to the initial impossibility.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the existence of God based on the contingency of the world. Sanusi begins by stating the main argument: the world's contingency is the proof, because if the world had no creator and originated by itself, then one of two equally possible states would become preferred without any cause, which is rationally impossible. He then provides the evidence for the world's contingency: the world is always accompanied by accidents (like motion and rest) that are observed to change—coming into existence and passing away. Since whatever is inseparable from a contingent thing is itself contingent, the world must be contingent. To establish that accidents are contingent, he points to direct observation: we see motion and rest appear and disappear. The world consists of celestial and terrestrial bodies, which are never free from motion or rest. He focuses on motion and rest because their inseparability from bodies is self-evident to every rational person. He then proves that both motion and rest are necessarily contingent: if either were eternal, it could never cease, but we observe that each ceases when its opposite appears. Therefore, motion and rest are contingent. Since bodies are never without motion or rest, and whatever is inseparable from something contingent is itself contingent, bodies must also be contingent. Thus the whole world is contingent. Finally, Sanusi concludes that a contingent world must have a creator. Without a creator, we would have to accept that a thing that is equally possible to exist or not exist comes into existence without any cause—a contradiction of equality and preponderance. Hence, a preponderator (مرجح) is necessary, and that is God.

Proof of God's Necessary Pre-Eternity
Sanusi presents the proof that God must be pre-eternal by reductio ad absurdum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that God must be pre-eternal, and why any alternative leads to an impossible circularity or infinite regress.

Core Idea
If God were not pre-eternal, He would need a creator — leading to an impossible chain.

Sanusi now proves that not only does God exist, but He must also be pre-eternal (qadīm). The proof is a critical step in establishing God's independence from all causes. By showing that the only alternative to pre-eternity is an impossible series of causes, Sanusi demonstrates that God cannot be originated. This passage uses a logical reductio ad absurdum argument, which you will follow step by step.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the necessity of God's pre-eternity (qidam). The author, Sanusi, begins by stating the proof: if God were not pre-eternal, He would be originated, and that would entail either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul), both of which are impossible. Thus, origination is impossible for God, so pre-eternity is necessary. This proof relies on the law of non-contradiction and the impossibility of an actual infinite. The conclusion is firmly stated: pre-eternity is necessary for God.

Proof of the Oneness of God (Burhan Wujub al-Wahdaniyya)
Sanusi proves God's oneness by the impossibility of two independent agents producing a single effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's rational proof that God must be one, based on the impossibility of two independent agents producing a single effect.

Core Idea
If there were two gods, nothing could exist – yet the world exists, so God is one.

This passage presents the central argument for tawḥīd from the impossibility of two equal creators. Sanusi begins with the established fact that God's power and will encompass all possibilities. He then constructs a hypothetical: if a second deity existed, what would happen when both direct their power at the same possible thing? The answer, as he shows, leads to a logical contradiction and thus the impossibility of two gods.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage and English translation. Notice how Sanusi structures his argument from a premise to a conclusion.
2
Identify the key logical move
Find where Sanusi states that a single effect cannot come from two agents. This is the core of the proof.
This is known as the 'impossibility of two independent agents' argument.
3
Understand the consequence of incapacity
Follow Sanusi's reasoning: if two deities cannot create one thing together, then one must be incapable. But if one is incapable, the other, being equal, must also be incapable.
4
Connect to observed reality
Sanusi concludes that if both were incapable, no contingent thing would exist. But we see contingent things existing, so the assumption is false. Therefore, God is one.

As for the proof of the necessity of oneness for Him, may He be glorified and exalted: it is that if He were not one, it would necessitate that nothing of the world would exist, due to the necessity of His incapability at that time. Meaning: if there were for Him, exalted, a similar being in His divinity, it would necessitate that nothing of the contingent events would exist, and the consequent is known to be false necessarily. And the explanation of the necessity of that: it has been established by decisive proof the necessity of the generality of His power and His will over all possible things. So if there were a being that had in terms of power and will for bringing into existence a certain possible thing the like of what our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, has, it would be necessary, when those two powers are attached to bringing that possible thing into existence, that it would not exist by them both together, due to the impossibility of a single effect between two agents, because of what would follow from that, of the single effect reverting to two effects, and that is inconceivable. Therefore, there must be incapability of one of the two agents, and that entails incapability of the other, the one similar to him in power over bringing into existence. And if their mutual incapability is necessary in this possible thing, then their incapability similarly is necessary in all other possible things, due to no distinction between them, and that entails the impossibility of the existence of all contingent events, and observation necessarily indicates the falsehood of that. And when the necessity of their mutual incapability is clear in the case of agreement on one possible thing, then in the case of disagreement about it in the way of opposition is even more apparent. So the necessity of the oneness of our Master, may He be glorified and exalted, in His essence, His attributes, and His actions is determined.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the oneness of God (burhān wujūb al-waḥdāniyya). The author presents an argument from the impossibility of two independent agents producing a single effect. He begins by stating the conclusion: if God were not one, nothing in the world would exist, because of the necessity of His incapability. He then explains: if there were another deity equal in power and will, then when both wills are directed to create one possible thing, two results cannot occur. A single effect cannot come from two independent causes, because that would require the one effect to be two effects, which is rationally impossible. Therefore, one of them must be incapable. But if one is incapable, the other, being identical in power, must also be incapable, because there is no distinction between them. This incapacity would then extend to all possible things, making the existence of all contingent events impossible. Yet we observe the existence of contingent events, so the assumption of two gods is false. The argument further notes that if incapacity follows even when the two agents agree on one possible thing, then it is even more evident when they disagree (i.e., in mutual opposition). Thus, the oneness of God in His essence, attributes, and actions is conclusively established.

Proof of Divine Oneness from the Impossibility of Two Gods
Sanusi concludes proof of divine oneness via impossibility of two gods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for the oneness of God based on the impossibility of two equally powerful gods.

Core Idea
Two gods would cancel each other out, making creation impossible.

Sanusi now concludes his argument for tawhid with a powerful reductio ad absurdum. If there were two gods with equal power, neither could act independently, rendering all events impossible. Since events clearly occur, there can only be one God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the literal translation. Let the flow of the argument come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage is concise but packed with logical reasoning.
2
Identify the two scenarios
The argument considers two cases: when the two gods agree on a single possible act, and when they disagree (mutual opposition). Identify each case in the text.
The term 'tamana‘' (تمانع) refers to mutual opposition or hindrance.
3
Trace the reductio ad absurdum
In the agreement case, if both gods are incapable regarding that one possible, they become incapable in all possibles, which would make events impossible. Observation shows events exist, so the assumption of two gods is absurd. The disagreement case is even more obviously absurd.
The argument does not rely on empirical proof but on rational necessity.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage concludes that the oneness of God (tawhid) in essence, attributes, and acts is necessary. Consider why this conclusion follows logically from the impossibility of two gods.
This is a cornerstone of Islamic theology (kalam).

And when the necessity of their incapability together in the case of agreement on a single possible is established, it is more evident in the case of disagreement therein in the way of mutual opposition (1). Commentary
This passage presents the final part of a proof for divine oneness (tawhid) based on the impossibility of two gods. The argument is a reductio ad absurdum. If there were two gods, each with equal creative power, then consider any possible thing that they might both want to create. If they agree on creating it, they would both attempt to create it, but then either both succeed (leading to two distinct creations, which is impossible) or both fail (since each would hinder the other). The text states that if they are both incapable in this one possible, then they are incapable in all possibles, because there is no difference between them. This would entail the impossibility of any events occurring, which is false as observation shows events exist. If they disagree—one wanting to create, the other wanting to prevent it—the mutual opposition (tamana') is even more obvious, leading to the same impossibility. Therefore, it is necessary that there is only one God, unique in essence, attributes, and acts.

The Valid Meaning of 'Ilah' as 'Al-Ma'bud bi Haqq'
Sanusi explains why the universal term 'Ilah' must mean 'the worshipped in truth' and not 'any worshipped being'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the universal term 'Ilah' must mean 'the worshipped in truth' (al-ma'bud bi haqq) and not merely 'any worshipped being' (mutlaq al-ma'bud).

Core Idea
The meaning of 'Ilah' must be restricted to the One truly deserving worship.

In the previous lesson, Sanusi established that the testimony 'La ilaha illa Allah' involves a universal term from which an exception is made. Now he examines which interpretation of that universal term makes the testimony true. This passage shows that if 'Ilah' meant any worshipped being, the testimony would be false, but if it means the one worshipped in truth, it is valid. He then clarifies that the great name 'Allah' is a proper noun for that unique individual.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic once, then the translation. Notice the two 'if' conditions.
2
Identify the two interpretations
Identify the two possible meanings of 'Ilah' as a universal: 'any worshipped being' vs 'the worshipped in truth'. Note why the first leads to falsehood.
3
Apply the conclusion
Understand that the only valid meaning is 'the worshipped in truth', and that 'Allah' is a proper noun for the individual who alone deserves worship.

And if what is meant by 'Ilah' is the worshipped in truth, it is valid. So then, none of these divisions is valid except that 'Ilah' be universal in the sense of 'the worshipped in truth', and the magnificent name (al-ism al-a'zam) is a proper noun for the existing individual from it. Commentary
This passage refines the valid fourth case identified in the previous lesson. The author considers two possible interpretations of the universal term 'إله' when it is taken as the thing from which exception is made (المستثنى منه). First, if 'إله' means 'any being worshipped' (مطلق المعبود), then the testimony becomes false because there are many false objects of worship—the negation would be false. Second, if it means 'the worshipped in truth' (المعبود بحق), then the testimony is true. Therefore, the only valid interpretation is that 'إله' is a universal term referring to the rightful object of worship, and the 'great name' (الاسم المعظم) in the excepted part is a proper noun (علم) for the specific individual who actually possesses that quality. The final meaning of the testimony is: none deserving of worship exists except the individual who is the Creator of the world.

The Superior Meaning of 'Ilah': Self-Sufficiency as the Foundation of Worship
Sanusi presents an alternative definition of 'Ilah' and explains why it is superior and how it enriches faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi considers the meaning of 'Ilah' as 'the Self-Sufficient, All-Needed' to be superior and how this enriches the believer's faith.

Core Idea
Worship is only due to the One who needs nothing and everything needs Him.

Sanusi has already defined 'Ilah' as 'the One truly worshipped.' Now he presents a deeper, more foundational meaning: the One who is absolutely self-sufficient and to whom all creation is in need. This definition, he argues, is clearer and serves as the basis for worship itself. In this passage, he explains why and describes the profound spiritual benefits that flow from grasping this meaning.

This connects self-sufficiency to the right to be worshipped.

Commentary
This passage introduces an alternative definition of 'إله' that is more profound and foundational. The author explains that 'إله' means the One who is absolutely self-sufficient (المستغني عن كل ما سواه) and to whom everything else is in need (المفتقر إليه كل ما عداه). This meaning is clearer and more fundamental than the previous definition ('المعبود بحق') because it provides the rational basis for worship: only one who is self-sufficient and all-needed deserves to be humbled before. The author then declares this second expression superior and describes its spiritual benefits: it reveals how all articles of faith are included under the testimony, expands the believer's heart with divine lights, and safeguards against errors. He uses poetic imagery of a garden to illustrate the richness of this understanding. Finally, the author mentions that for this reason he has chosen this interpretation in his creedal work, and cites al-Muqtarih's agreement.

The Kalima Proves Temporal Origination
Sanusi argues that the kalima implies the temporal origination of all creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the word of tawhid implies the temporal origination of the entire world and why any eternal part would be independent of Allah.

Core Idea
The word of tawhid proves that everything besides Allah is temporally originated.

In this passage, Sanusi demonstrates that the kalima not only asserts the oneness of God but also entails the temporality of all creation. He argues that if any part of the world were eternal, it would be self-sufficient and not need Allah, contradicting the word's implication of absolute dependence. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the heading indicates this is point 1 from the matn.
2
Identify the main claim
The passage states that the kalima indicates 'the temporal origination of the world as a whole'. This is the conclusion Sanusi wants to prove.
The phrase 'بأسره' emphasizes the entirety of creation.
3
Follow the conditional argument
Sanusi uses a conditional: if something from the world were eternal, it would be independent of Allah. But everything besides Allah must be dependent, so no part can be eternal.
This mirrors the logic of the kalima: 'لا إله إلا الله' implies all else is dependent.
4
Understand the supporting proof
He references a previous proof that whatever is eternal cannot cease to exist and is necessary of existence, thus needing no particularizer. This reinforces why eternal parts would be independent.
The terms 'قديم' and 'واجب الوجود' are key.
5
Conclude the necessity of temporality
The argument forces the conclusion that temporal origination is necessary for everything other than Allah. No part of the world can be eternal.
