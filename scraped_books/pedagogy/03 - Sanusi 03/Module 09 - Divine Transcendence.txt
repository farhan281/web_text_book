Introduction to Impossible Attributes and the First Four
Sanusi introduces the twenty impossible attributes and explains the first four.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah's impossible attributes are the logical negations of His necessary attributes, and you will be able to identify the first four impossibilities: non-existence, origination, need for non-existence, and similarity to created things.

Core Idea
Impossible attributes are the direct opposites of necessary attributes.

Sanusi now turns from what Allah must be to what He cannot be. The impossible attributes are not random; they are the exact opposites of the twenty necessary attributes. In this lesson, we see the first four of these impossibilities, with a detailed explanation of why similarity to creation is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening statement
Read the first sentence carefully: twenty attributes, opposites of the first twenty. This sets the entire framework.
The word 'أَزْدَادُ' is a variant spelling of 'أضداد' (opposites).
2
Identify the first four impossibilities
The text lists: non-existence, origination, need for non-existence, and similarity to originated things. Notice that these are negations of the first four necessary attributes (existence, pre-eternity, self-subsistence, distinctness from creation).
3
Understand the explanation of similarity
Read the subpoints under similarity: being a body, an accident, having direction, restriction by place/time, being subject to change, having size, having purposes. Each is a mode of similarity to created things, and all are impossible for Allah.
This long explanation shows how kalam thinkers specify what 'similarity' means.
4
Connect to necessary attributes
Each impossibility is the opposite of a necessary attribute. For example, non-existence opposes existence, origination opposes pre-eternity, similarity opposes distinctness from creation.
This is the key logical structure: the impossible are the negations of the necessary.

Commentary
This passage begins the section on impossible attributes (al-mustaḥīlāt) for Allah. It states that there are twenty impossible attributes, and they are the opposites (aḍdād, written as 'azdād' in the text) of the first twenty necessary attributes. The passage then lists some of these impossibilities: non-existence, origination, the need for non-existence [?], and similarity to created things. For the last, the author provides a detailed explanation: that similarity would mean Allah is a body (jirm) occupying space, or an accident subsisting in a body, or located in a direction, or restricted by place or time, or subject to change, or characterized by size or purposes in His actions. The passage then adds two more impossibilities: that Allah not be self-subsisting (i.e., that He be an attribute relying on something else or need a specifier), and that He not be one (i.e., that He be composite in essence or have a like in essence or attributes). These are direct negations of the necessary attributes of self-subsistence and uniqueness. The list is incomplete in the available text; the full enumeration of twenty is not provided here.

Impossible: Dependence and Multiplicity in Allah
Sanusi lists the impossibilities of not being self-subsisting and not being one.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that it is impossible for Allah to not be self-subsisting (which would mean depending on something) and impossible for Him to not be one (which would mean being composite or having a likeness).

Core Idea
Allah cannot be dependent or multiple in any way.

After listing the first four impossibilities, Sanusi adds two more that negate the necessary attributes of self-subsistence and oneness. These are crucial because they affirm that Allah depends on nothing and that He is absolutely unique. In this lesson, we examine each impossibility and its subpoints.

Guided Path
1
Read the first 'وكذا' section
Read the passage about the impossibility of not being self-subsisting. Notice it mentions two ways this could happen: being an attribute of something else, or needing a specifier.
2
Read the second 'وكذا' section
Read the passage about the impossibility of not being one. It mentions two ways: being composite in essence, or having a similitude in essence or attributes.
3
Relate to necessary attributes
These impossibilities are the opposites of qiyām bi-nafsihi (self-subsistence) and waḥdāniyya (oneness). If Allah were to lack self-subsistence or oneness, He would be like creation.
4
Summarize the list so far
Together with the previous lesson, we have covered six impossibilities: non-existence, origination, need for non-existence, similarity, not self-subsisting, and not one. The full list is twenty; the remaining are not given in this passage.

Non-Existence, Origination, and Likeness
Enumerates the first four impossible attributes and details the meaning of likeness to created things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first four categories of attributes that are impossible for God, including the detailed explanation of what it means for God to resemble created things.

Core Idea
God cannot be like anything in creation—not in essence, attributes, or actions.

Sanusi begins his enumeration of the twenty impossible attributes by establishing the fundamental principle: God cannot share any characteristic with contingent beings. The first four items in the list—non-existence, origination, the opposite of non-existence, and likeness to originated things—are closely linked. The explanation of 'likeness' is expansive, covering spatial, temporal, and qualitative limitations that all creatures experience. This lesson unpacks that foundational denial.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to get the full meaning.
2
Identify the first four impossibilities
Locate the four main attributes: non-existence, origination, opposite of non-existence, and likeness. Notice that the last is expanded with subpoints.
The opposite of non-existence is listed to avoid implying that existence is a contingent attribute.
3
Examine the subpoints of likeness
List the specific ways likeness is denied: being a body, accident, direction, restriction by space/time, having originated attributes, small/large size, and having motives.
4
Reflect on why these are impossible
Think about why each subpoint contradicts God's perfection. For example, being a body implies spatial limitation, which is impossible for the Creator.
This exercise deepens tawhid.

[The impossibles regarding Him, glory be to Him and exalted]
And from what is impossible regarding Him, exalted: twenty attributes; and they are additional to the first twenty.
They are: non-existence, origination, the opposite of non-existence, likeness to originated things; by His being a body; i.e., His exalted essence occupying a measure of space, or His being an accident subsisting in a body, or His being in a direction of a body, or His having a direction, or His being restricted by place or time, or His exalted essence being described with originated things, or His being described with smallness or largeness, or His being described with motives in deeds and judgments. And likewise it is impossible regarding Him, exalted, that He not be self-subsisting; by being an attribute subsisting in a locus, or needing a specifier. And likewise it is impossible regarding Him, exalted, that He not be one; by being composed in His essence, or having an equal in His essence or attributes, or having with Him in existence an effecter in any of the actions. And likewise it is impossible regarding Him, exalted: incapacity regarding any possible thing, and bringing into existence anything from the world while with His aversion to its existence; i.e., His lack of willing for it, or with heedlessness, or forgetfulness, or by causation, or by nature. And likewise it is impossible also regarding Him, exalted: ignorance and what is in its meaning of any known thing, and death, and deafness, and blindness, and dumbness. And the opposites of the attributes of meaning are clear from these.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed enumeration and explanation of the twenty attributes that are impossible (mustaḥīl) for God. It begins by stating that these are additional to the first twenty (which are the necessary attributes). The list includes: non-existence (al-‘adam), origination (al-ḥudūth), the opposite of non-existence (which is existence, but expressed as 'the opposite of non-existence' to emphasize that existence itself is impossible for God as a contingent attribute—only necessary existence is possible), likeness to originated things (al-mumāthala li-l-ḥawādith), and then an expansion explaining that likeness includes being a body (jism), an accident, having a direction, being restricted by space/time, being described with originated attributes, smallness/largeness, having motives. Then it lists impossibility of not being self-subsistent (i.e., being dependent on a locus or specifier), not being one (i.e., having composition, a peer, or a partner in effecting actions). Further impossibilities include: incapacity (al-‘ajz), creating while disliking existence (i.e., without will), creating with heedlessness (nuhūl?), forgetfulness, by causation (ta‘līl), or by nature (ṭab‘). Also included are ignorance, death, deafness, blindness, dumbness, and the opposites of the seven attributes of meaning (which are their negations). The passage systematically denies all imperfections and limitations from God, affirming His absolute transcendence (tanzīh).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed enumeration and explanation of the twenty attributes that are impossible (mustaḥīl) for God. It begins by stating that these are additional to the first twenty (which are the necessary attributes). The list includes: non-existence (al-‘adam), origination (al-ḥudūth), the opposite of non-existence (which is existence, but expressed as 'the opposite of non-existence' to emphasize that existence itself is impossible for God as a contingent attribute—only necessary existence is possible), likeness to originated things (al-mumāthala li-l-ḥawādith), and then an expansion explaining that likeness includes being a body (jism), an accident, having a direction, being restricted by space/time, being described with originated attributes, smallness/largeness, having motives. Then it lists impossibility of not being self-subsistent (i.e., being dependent on a locus or specifier), not being one (i.e., having composition, a peer, or a partner in effecting actions). Further impossibilities include: incapacity (al-‘ajz), creating while disliking existence (i.e., without will), creating with heedlessness (nuhūl?), forgetfulness, by causation (ta‘līl), or by nature (ṭab‘). Also included are ignorance, death, deafness, blindness, dumbness, and the opposites of the seven attributes of meaning (which are their negations). The passage systematically denies all imperfections and limitations from God, affirming His absolute transcendence (tanzīh).

The author then cites Quran 42:11, 'There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the Hearing, the Seeing,' and notes that the first part of the verse is a declaration of transcendence (tanzih) and the last part is an affirmation (ithbat) of attributes. He explains that the beginning of the verse refutes the anthropomorphists (mujassimah) and their like, while the end refutes those who negate all attributes (mu'attilah). Next, the author discusses the wisdom of placing the declaration of transcendence before the affirmation of hearing and sight. He acknowledges that normally negation precedes affirmation, but here the order is reversed for a specific reason: if the verse had begun with 'He is the Hearing, the Seeing,' it might have implied anthropomorphism, because in the witnessed world, hearing is associated with an ear and sight with an eyeball, and both are limited to specific objects and conditions (e.g., not being too far). Therefore, God began with the declaration of transcendence to make clear that even the hearing and sight mentioned afterward are not like creatures' hearing and sight. The author then explains God's hearing and sight: they are two attributes subsisting in His essence, which is impossible to have corporeality, physical organs, or their necessary concomitants. These attributes are necessary (wajib) with respect to pre-eternity and everlastingness – i.e., they are eternal. They relate to every existent being, whether pre-eternal or originated, whether an essence or an attribute, whether hidden or apparent. The passage ends with a footnote indicating that further verification of the relationship of these attributes to all existents will come later.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that God is not a body (jirm) nor an accident (ʿaraḍ), establishing His absolute transcendence (tanzīh). The author begins by referencing a prior discussion of 'the two similitudes' (al-mithlain), likely meaning body and accident, and then states that the entire world is confined to these two categories: bodies and accidents. Accidents are defined as meanings that subsist in bodies.

First, the author examines the essential attributes of a body. A body necessarily possesses tahayyuz (occupation of space), meaning it occupies a specific amount of empty space corresponding to its essence, enabling it to be still or move within that space. A body also essentially accepts accidents—originated attributes like motion, rest, combination, separation, colors, purposes, and similar qualities. Furthermore, a body is essentially specified by certain directions and places. All these attributes are impossible (mustaḥīla) for God, because they imply dependence and limitation. Therefore, God cannot be a body.

Second, the author examines the accident. An accident essentially subsists in a body (it cannot exist independently). Another essential attribute of an accident is that it necessarily ceases to exist in the second moment after its existence—that is, it does not persist. This is also impossible for God, because God necessarily subsists by Himself (qāʾim bi-nafsihī), as explained earlier. God necessarily possesses eternity (qidam) and permanence (baqāʾ); He does not accept non-existence. Hence, God is not an accident.

Finally, the author concludes by summarizing the general rule: everything other than God necessarily entails originatedness (ḥudūth) and dependence upon a Specifier (al-mukhaṣṣiṣ). In contrast, God necessarily possesses existence and absolute self-sufficiency (ghinā muṭlaq). Therefore, God is utterly distinct (mubāyin) from everything other than Him, whether that other is a body, an accident, or any other supposed entity (such as incorporeal substances, which some affirm). If such entities exist, they are also originated—just as the first two categories are originated—by proof of consensus (ijmāʿ). Thus, the passage firmly establishes God's uniqueness and dissimilarity to all created things.

The Impossibility of Muteness for Allah
Sanusi argues that muteness is impossible and that speech by letters/sounds entails two deficiencies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why muteness is impossible for Allah and why even speech composed of letters and sounds would entail deficiency.

Core Idea
Allah's speech is perfect, free from the limitations of letters, sounds, and temporal sequence.

After covering ignorance, death, deafness, and blindness, Sanusi now turns to muteness. He defines it and gives a profound argument: even if speech is eloquent and composed of letters and sounds, it contains two inherent deficiencies that are incompatible with divine perfection.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of muteness
Muteness is the absolute absence of speech due to an affliction. It includes silence and also the case of speech by letters and sounds.
Sanusi expands 'muteness' to cover speech that is composed of letters and sounds, arguing that such speech is deficient.
2
Understand the first deficiency: temporality
Letters and sounds are preceded by non-existence and followed by non-existence, meaning they are temporal (ḥādith). Any being described by temporal attributes is itself temporal, which is the greatest deficiency.
This shows that Allah's speech must be eternal and not composed of temporal elements.
3
Grasp the second deficiency: inherent muteness
Since two letters cannot occur simultaneously, a speaker using letters and sounds cannot indicate multiple pieces of information at the same moment. This is a form of muteness because the speaker is unable to express everything at once.
Thus, speech by letters and sounds implies a limitation that is incompatible with Allah's power.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
What is perfection for an animal can be degradation for a king — and infinitely more so for God.

Sanusi presents a powerful argument against those who dare to compare God's speech to the sounds of animals. He shows that even in human terms, such a comparison is insulting to a king, and then applies the logic to God, revealing the utter absurdity of the analogy. This lesson walks through the reductio ad absurdum and the principle of relative perfection.

Why Nothing Compares to God
Sanusi argues for the equality of accidents and God's exclusive role in differentiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why all accidents are essentially equal and how God's free choice differentiates them, demonstrating that comparisons between God and creation are invalid.

Core Idea
All accidents are equal in essence; God alone differentiates, so nothing compares to Him.

Sanusi presents a metaphysical argument against anthropomorphism. He begins by asserting that all accidents (like sounds, colors, etc.) are inherently equal—none has an intrinsic advantage over another. Any attribute of perfection or deficiency in one accident could equally exist in another. The differentiation we observe is solely due to God's free choice. Sanusi then escalates the argument: if even among accidents, what counts as 'perfection' for one is 'deficiency' for another, how absurd it is to attribute similar descriptions to the Creator, who shares no genus or species with creation.

3

This emphasizes God's absolute sovereignty.
4
Follow the logical escalation
Note how Sanusi moves from the relativity of perfection among accidents to the conclusion that comparing God to these accidents is even more absurd.
The argument uses a 'how much more' (fakayfa) reasoning.
5
Reflect on the conclusion

Sanusi states a general axiom about created things and argues for God's incomparability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the foundational principle that created things have no inherent superiority, and that God alone distinguishes them by deficiency or perfection, which leads to the absolute incomparability of God's attributes.

Core Idea
Created things have no inherent superiority; only God assigns qualities, so He is beyond all comparison.

Proof of God's Unlikeness to Originated Things
Sanusi proves God's unlikeness to originated things via the principle of similars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof (burhan) for the attribute of mukhalafa lil-hawadith — that God is unlike all originated things — based on the principle that similars must share necessary properties.

Core Idea
If God resembled any created thing, He would have to share its originated nature — impossible.

Sanusi now turns to proving one of the essential attributes of God: His unlikeness to all originated things (mukhalafa lil-hawadith). He gives a tight syllogistic argument based on the principle of similars: if two things are alike, they must share what is necessary, impossible, and possible for each. Since everything other than God is necessarily originated, any resemblance would force God also to be originated — but we already know He is pre-eternal and permanent. This short but dense passage is a model of kalam reasoning.

God's Transcendence from Purposes and Non-Obligation of Possible Things
Commentator derives God's transcendence from purposes and non-obligation of possible things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God cannot act for purposes and why nothing from possible things is obligatory upon Him, based on His absolute independence.

Core Idea
God's independence implies He has no needs, hence no purposes and no obligations.

The commentator now derives two important implications from the principle of God's self-sufficiency (al-ghinā). If God were to act for a purpose, He would need that purpose—but He is independent. Similarly, if anything were obligatory upon Him, He would need it for perfection. Let's see how these points are argued.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the two 'yukhadhu minhu' statements that introduce the two derived doctrines.
2
Identify the two derived doctrines
The passage explicitly presents two points: God's transcendence from purposes, and that nothing from possible things is obligatory upon Him. Find each one and note how they are argued.
3
Understand the argument for each
For the first, if God had purposes, He would depend on what fulfills them. For the second, if something were obligatory, He would need it for perfection. Both contradict His independence.
The key phrase is 'illa lazima iftiqāruhu' (otherwise His dependence would be necessary).
4
Define 'purpose' (al-gharad) as used here
The passage explicitly defines purpose as 'a motivator that motivates Him to bring into existence an action or a legal judgment.' Understand that this is what is negated.
This definition is crucial for understanding the negation.
5
Reflect on the footnote
The footnote says this transcendence from purposes is included under dissimilarity to originated things, or its proof is the impossibility of the reversal of realities. Consider why this explanation might be added.

Commentary
This passage explains two derived doctrines from the principle of divine independence. God's Actions Have No Purpose or Cause
Sanusi argues that God's actions and judgments are without purpose or cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's actions and judgments are completely unconditioned by purpose or motivating cause, based on His absolute self-sufficiency.

Core Idea
God's self-sufficiency means His actions have no purpose or cause.

Sanusi now applies the principle of divine self-sufficiency to the nature of God's actions and judgments. If God were to act for a purpose or be motivated by a cause, He would depend on something outside Himself for perfection. This lesson unpacks that argument, showing that all of God's acts are purely by His choice.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the logical flow from the initial proof to the two conclusions.
The passage begins by referencing a prior proof about 'repelling deficiencies'.
2
Identify the two main conclusions
The author draws two conclusions: (1) God is transcendent over purposes in His actions, and (2) nothing is obligatory upon God from contingent things. Find the sentences that state these.
The second conclusion is introduced by 'وَكَذَا يُؤْخَذُ مِنْهُ أَيْضاً'.
3
Understand the definition of 'negated purpose'
Sanusi defines the negated purpose as a motivating cause based on a benefit returning to God or to creation. Write down the Arabic definition.
The definition starts with 'الغرضُ المنفي عنه تعالى'.
4
Examine why both benefit possibilities are impossible
Follow the refutation: benefit to God implies need, benefit to creation implies repelling deficiency which is also a need. Both contradict self-sufficiency.
The conclusion is that all actions have no motivating cause.

Commentary
This passage is an extended argument establishing that God's actions and judgments are without any purpose or motivating cause. It begins by referencing the earlier proof from the attribute (or who repels deficiencies) to deduce God's necessary transcendence from all imperfections, including the necessity of His hearing, sight, and speech. From this principle, the author draws two major conclusions. First, God is transcendent over having purposes (aghrāḍ) in His actions and judgments, because any purpose would imply that He needs something to achieve that purpose, contradicting His absolute self-sufficiency. Second, nothing is rationally obligatory upon God from among contingent things, such as reward or punishment, because obligation would also imply need for perfection, and God is self-sufficient. The author then defines the negated purpose: it is a motivating cause that would prompt God to perform an action or give a religious judgment out of consideration for a benefit returning either to Him or to His creation. He then refutes both possibilities. If the benefit returns to God, He would be in need of His creation for perfection. If it returns to creation, the act of repelling deficiency from God (by creating that benefit) is itself a perfection, so again God would be in need of that benefit. Both are impossible for the Absolutely Self-Sufficient. The conclusion is that all of God's actions and judgments have no motivating cause ('illa bā'itha) and are purely by His choice (bi-mahḍ al-ikhtiyār). The text then begins a new point about what benefits God may observe for creation, but this sentence is incomplete within the page window.

Proof that divine acts have no motivating cause.

God's acts are pure choice, not motivated by any benefit.

Sanusi presents a crucial argument for divine transcendence: God's actions are not driven by any benefit. He examines two possibilities—benefit returning to God or to creation—and shows both are impossible. This establishes that all divine acts are by pure choice, not by any cause or motive.

Commentary
