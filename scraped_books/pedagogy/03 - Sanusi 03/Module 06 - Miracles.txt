Sanusi's Doctrine on Karamat of Awliya
Sanusi states his position on karamat and critiques a denial by some Sunni jurists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's affirmation of karamat and his critique of those who deny them, including some Sunni jurists.

Core Idea
Karamat are real wonders that nearly equal prophetic miracles, and denying them is a sign of innovation.

This lesson presents Imam al-Sanusi's definitive stance on the reality of karamat (extraordinary acts granted to saints). He affirms that karamat are so remarkable that they almost match the miracles of prophets, and he criticizes both innovators and even some Sunni jurists for rejecting specific reports. The passage showcases Sanusi's balanced yet firm approach to a debated topic.

2
Identify Sanusi's main claim

3
Understand the critique of Sunni jurists
Sanusi refers to a specific incident about Ibrahīm ibn Adham. Some jurists declared that believing in such simultaneous presence is kufr. Sanusi finds this extreme and wrong.

4
Reflect on the lesson's significance

This passage defends the orthodox view against both external and internal challenges.

Commentary
This passage presents Imam al-Sanusi's theological position on the reality and permissibility of karamat (wondrous acts granted to saints) and critiques a specific denial from some Sunni jurists. The passage begins by stating that Sanusi's view matches the consensus of the verifying scholars of Ahl al-Sunna. It notes that Sanusi discussed karamat in his works, both briefly and at length. Then it quotes his critical remark from 'Sharh al-Aqida al-Wusta'. Sanusi implies that this judgment is excessively harsh and unfounded, because the incident is a karamah and should be accepted by Ahl al-Sunna. Thus, Sanusi defends the doctrine of karamat against both external innovators and internal detractors.

Distinguishing Miracles from Sleight of Hand and Magic
Author differentiates miracles from sha'wadhah and sihr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how miracles differ from sleight of hand and magic, and why these differences preserve the unique role of miracles as divine proofs.

Core Idea
Miracles are divine; magic is learned; sleight of hand is just speedy fingers.

Prophets are supported by miracles that defy natural laws. But not every astonishing event is a miracle — some are tricks or learned skills. In this passage, Sanusi carefully distinguishes genuine miracles from two similar phenomena: sleight of hand (sha'wadhah) and magic (sihr), clarifying their natures and causes.

Commentary
This brief passage serves to distinguish genuine prophetic miracles from two phenomena that might be confused with them: sleight of hand (sha'wadhah or sha'badhah) and magic (sihr). The author defines sha'wadhah as a mere manual dexterity that makes things appear different from reality—it is an illusion, not a real change. Magic, on the other hand, is acknowledged to involve an actual transformation of the image (qalb al-sura), and the author affirms its reality (haqiqa). However, magic occurs through customary causes (sabab 'adi) that can be learned, implying it is a natural, trained skill rather than a divine intervention. This contrasts with miracles, which are beyond human ability and break natural customs. By clarifying these distinctions, the author protects the unique status of prophetic miracles as divine proofs.
