Spiritual Practices and Supplication
Stanzas on purifying the heart, remembrance, and a concluding supplication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the spiritual practices prescribed for purifying the heart and the concluding supplication that seeks closeness to Allah.

Core Idea
Purify your heart, watch God, and ask Him not to cut you off.

This final passage of the poem combines practical spiritual advice with a heartfelt supplication. The author instructs the seeker on how to purify the heart through effort, pre-dawn prayer, continuous thought and remembrance, and watchfulness of God. Then he models a humble prayer asking not to be separated from God and to be granted His secret that removes blindness. This lesson unpacks these practices and their significance.

Each practice builds towards spiritual ascent.
3

Examine the supplication starting with 'Qul bidhill...' What does the seeker ask for? Why is being cut off from God a concern?
The supplication shows humility and reliance on God's mercy.
4
Connect practices to the goal
The passage states that these practices lead to 'ascending to the landmarks of perfection.' Reflect on how each practice contributes to that ascent.
5
Appreciate the conclusion
The passage ends with praise and blessings on the Prophet. Notice how the poem closes with a traditional formula of gratitude and salawat.
This mirrors the structure of many Islamic poetic works.

Command, Will, and the Three Groups
Ibn 'Abd al-Salam analyzes divine command and will, categorizing three groups.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the relationship between divine command and will, and how the Sunni middle path avoids the extremes of the Mu'tazila and Jabariyya.

Core Idea
Balancing divine command and will is the key to understanding human agency and divine decree.

In this passage, the great scholar 'Izz al-Din ibn 'Abd al-Salam presents a profound analysis of how the concepts of divine command (amr) and divine will (irada) interact. He identifies three groups that have taken positions on this issue, two of which fall into error, while the third represents the balanced Sunni perspective. This lesson will guide you through his imagery and argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read Ibn 'Abd al-Salam's poetic description of command and will carefully. Notice the metaphor of the sphere revolving around the line of command and the center of will.
2
Identify the three groups
Look for the three groups: those who cling to command, those who cling to will, and those who combine them. Understand what each group believes based on the text.
3
Analyze the errors of extremes
Examine why clinging only to command (Mu'tazila) or only to will (Jabariyya) leads to error. The passage shows the flaws in each position.
The Jabariyya nullify Sharia, while the Mu'tazila deny divine will over evil.
4
Reflect on the middle path
Consider how combining command and will leads to guidance. Relate this to Sunni theology that affirms both divine decree and human responsibility.

قَالَ سَيِّدِي الْعِزُّ عَبْدُ السَّلَامِ: وَبَعْدُ، فَإِنِّي نَظَرْتُ فَرَأَيْتُ دَائِرَةَ الشَّقَاوَةِ وَالسَّعَادَةِ تَدُورُ عَلَى خَطِّ الْأَمْرِ وَمَرْكَزِ الْإِرَادَةِ، وَبَيْنَهُمَا تَدْقِيقٌ يَدِقُّ عَلَى التَّحْقِيقِ، وَمَضِيقٌ يَفْتَقِرُ سَالِكُهُ إِلَى رَفِيقِ التَّوْفِيقِ، فَالْأَمْرُ يَهَبُ وَالْإِرَادَةُ تَنْهَبُ، فَمَا وَهَبَهُ الْأَمْرُ نَهَبَتْهُ الْإِرَادَةُ، وَالْأَمْرُ يَقُولُ: افْعَلْ، وَالْإِرَادَةُ تَقُولُ: لَا تَفْعَلْ، وَالْفَعَّالُ الْمُرِيدُ لَا يُسْأَلُ عَمَّا يَفْعَلُ وَهُمْ يُسْأَلُونَ. فَقَوْمٌ عَلِقُوا بِالْأَمْرِ فَضَلُّوا، وَقَوْمٌ عَلِقُوا بِالْإِرَادَةِ فَزَلُّوا، وَقَوْمٌ جَمَعُوا بَيْنَ الْإِرَادَةِ وَالْأَمْرِ فَهُدُوا إِلَى الصِّرَاطِ الْمُسْتَقِيمِ وَاسْتَقَلُّوا. فَأَمَّا الَّذِينَ تَمَسَّكُوا بِالْأَمْرِ فَأَضَافُوا الْفِعْلَ إِلَى أَنْفُسِهِمْ، وَجَعَلُوا لِأَنْفُسِهِمْ تَقْدِيرًا وَفِعْلًا، وَقَالُوا: إِنَّ اللهَ لَمْ يَخْلُقِ الشَّرَّ، وَلَمْ يُقَدِّرْهُ، وَلَمْ يُرِدْهُ، وَإِنَّمَا هُوَ مِنْ خَلْقِ أَنْفُسِنَا وَفِعْلِهَا، لَيْسَ لِلَّهِ فِيهِ إِرَادَةٌ، وَزَعَمُوا بِجَهْلِهِمْ هَذَا أَنَّ فِي ذَلِكَ تَنْزِيهًا لِلَّهِ تَعَالَى. وَأَمَّا الَّذِينَ تَمَسَّكُوا بِالْإِرَادَةِ وَالْمَشِيئَةِ، فَأَحَالُوا فِعْلَهُمْ وَعَمَلَهُمْ عَلَى الْخَالِقِيَّةِ، وَقَطَعُوا نِطَاقَ الْعُبُودِيَّةِ، وَتَبَرَّءُوا مِنْ أَعْمَالِهِمْ، وَقَالُوا: نَحْنُ قَوْمٌ مَجْبُورُونَ بِحُكْمِهِ فِي قَبْضَةِ قَهْرِهِ، وَيَلْزَمُهُمْ عَلَى هَذَا إِبْطَالُ الْأَوَامِرِ وَالنَّوَاهِي وَسَائِرِ التَّكَالِيفِ الشَّرْعِيَّةِ.
My master al-'Izz 'Abd al-Salam said: To proceed, I have observed and seen that the sphere of misery and happiness revolves around the line of command and the center of will. Between them is a precision too fine for realization, and a narrow pass whose traveler needs a companion of divine success. Command bestows, and will plunders. What command bestows, will plunders. Command says: 'Do!', and will says: 'Do not do!'. The Efficacious Willer is not asked about what He does, but they are asked. Some people clung to command and went astray. Some people clung to will and slipped. Some people combined will and command and were guided to the straight path and became independent. As for those who held fast to command, they attributed action to themselves, made for themselves determination and action, and said: 'God did not create evil, nor decree it, nor will it; it is only from the creation of our selves and their action; God has no will in it.' In their ignorance they claimed that this is an exoneration of God Most High. As for those who held fast to will and volition, they referred their action and work to the attribute of creation, cut off the scope of servitude, disavowed their deeds, and said: 'We are a people compelled by His decree in the grasp of His overwhelming power.' Consequently, this necessitates the nullification of commands, prohibitions, and all legal obligations.

Commentary
This passage is an extended quotation from 'Izz al-Din ibn 'Abd al-Salam, analyzing the relationship between divine command (al-amr) and divine will (al-irada). He begins by stating that the realm of misery and happiness revolves around these two concepts. There is a subtlety and difficulty that requires divine assistance. He describes command as bestowing and will as plundering, indicating that what command demands, will may override. Command says 'Do!' but will perhaps prevents it. The Doer and Willer (God) cannot be questioned about His actions, but creatures are questioned. Then Ibn 'Abd al-Salam identifies three groups: (1) Those who cling only to command (the Mu'tazila) — they attribute acts to themselves, deny that God creates evil or wills it, thinking this is exoneration. (2) Those who cling only to will (the Jabariyya) — they attribute all actions to God, deny human agency, and consequently nullify commands and prohibitions. (3) Those who combine both command and will (Ahl al-Sunna) — they are guided to the straight path. The passage is a clear statement of the Sunni middle path between the extremes of Qadariyya/Mu'tazila and Jabariyya.

Gratitude, Praise, and the Seal of Prophethood
Praise for completion, gratitude for tawhid, and prayer upon the Prophet.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why praising the Prophet is an obligation of gratitude for the grace of tawhid, and the importance of seeking a good conclusion.

Core Idea
All blessings, especially tawhid, come through the Prophet, making praise upon him a duty of gratitude.

The commentator concludes the book with praise to God, but immediately links this gratitude to the Prophet, through whom the greatest blessing—tawhid—was conveyed. This passage reminds us that our faith is a gift mediated by the Prophet, and that our conclusion should be a prayer for a good end.

Guided Path
1
Read the praise for completion
The commentator says 'Al-hamdu lillah 'ala al-itmam' and describes the book as a unique gem.
This is the formal closing of the author's work.
2
Understand the obligation of gratitude
The passage states that gratitude to the Benefactor (shukr al-mun'im) is obligatory, and specifically mentions the grace of tawhid.
This sets up the rationale for praising the Prophet.
3
Reflect on the reason for praising the Prophet
The commentator argues that because every grace, especially tawhid, reached us through the Prophet, it became obligatory to pray upon him. He quotes a line of poetry: 'You are the gate of God...'.
This highlights the exclusive mediatory role of the Prophet.
4
Read the final supplication
The passage ends with a comprehensive prayer asking for a good ending, protection from trials, and to be gathered with the righteous.
This mirrors the earlier supplication and closes the book.
