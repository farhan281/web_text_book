Author's Biography: Name, Lineage, and Early Life
The biography introduces the author's full name, lineage, birth, and early scholastic career.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the background and scholarly lineage of the author, Ahmad Hasan al-Mashshāt.

Core Idea
Knowing the author's life illuminates the context of his theological work.

Before diving into the theological content of al-Bahja al-Saniyya, it is valuable to know something about its author. This biographical sketch introduces us to Ahmad Hasan al-Mashshāt, a Maliki scholar and judge from Mecca. His training under prominent scholars and his own teaching career shaped the commentary we are about to study.

Guided Path
1
Read the full biography
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get an overview of al-Mashshāt's life.
Pay attention to the many titles listed at the beginning.
2
Identify his scholarly titles
Notice the titles: 'al-Allama, al-Muhaddith, al-Usuli, al-Faqih, al-Qadi'. Each indicates a branch of Islamic learning.
These titles show the breadth of his scholarship.
3
Trace his lineage
His lineage goes back to Abd Manaf ibn Qusayy of Quraysh. Reflect on why lineage matters in Islamic scholarly tradition.
4
Map his education and teachers
List the schools and teachers mentioned: al-Sawlatiyya, Muhammad al-Khidr al-Jikani, etc. Consider the network of scholars he studied under.
5
Follow his career progression
He taught for 30 years, served on a commission, traveled, and became a judge. This shows his long dedication to learning and law.

Translation of the author. His name and lineage: He is the eminent scholar, hadith specialist, usuli, jurist, judge, Abu Ahmad Hasan ibn Muhammad ibn Abbas ibn Ali ibn Abd al-Wahid al-Mashshāt, al-Makki al-Maliki, al-Manafi, in relation to Abd Manaf ibn Qusayy al-Qurashi. His birth and upbringing: He was born on the third of Shawwal in the year 1317 of the Prophet's Hijra, in the al-Qarara district, and his family is one of the ancient Meccan scholarly families that produced a number of scholars. His father enrolled him in the kuttab for study, then in al-Sawlatiyya school in the year 1329, and he was appointed as an assistant to the teachers while close to graduation age, and after graduation he worked as a teacher in the same school for thirty years, and he obtained a certificate of merit from it in the year 1336, and he taught in the Sacred Mosque, and frequently attended its scholars, and met a large number of scholars coming to the sacred land. Among his teachers: Muhammad al-Khidr ibn Mayabi al-Jikani and Umar Hamdan al-Muharrisi, and he frequented the shaykhs Muhammad Habib Allah al-Shanqiti and his father Shaykh Muhammad al-Mashshāt. In the year 1361 he was appointed a member of the Discrimination Commission, and as was the habit of scholars, he was scrupulous regarding this matter, until the commission was dissolved in the year 1364, so he travelled thereafter on a journey of about a year to Sudan, Egypt, Syria, and Lebanon, and met its scholars. In the year 1365 he was appointed deputy head of the Sharia court, and remained there for two years until he became a judge there.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical sketch of the author, Ahmad Hasan al-Mashshāt. It begins by listing his full name, titles (eminent scholar, hadith specialist, usuli, jurist, judge), and lineage tracing back to Abd Manaf ibn Qusayy of the Quraysh, indicating his noble ancestry. He was born in Mecca's al-Qarara district on 3 Shawwal 1317 AH (approximately 1900 CE). His family was a scholarly family. His father sent him first to the kuttab (Quran school) and then to al-Sawlatiyya school in 1329 AH. He was made a teaching assistant before graduation, and after graduating he taught there for thirty years, earning a certificate of merit in 1336 AH. He also studied at the Sacred Mosque, attending the lessons of many scholars. The text then lists some of his teachers: Muhammad al-Khidr al-Jikani, Umar al-Muharrisi, Muhammad Habib Allah al-Shanqiti, and his own father Muhammad al-Mashshāt. Later, in 1361 AH, he was appointed to the Discrimination Commission, but he was cautious about the role, and after the commission dissolved in 1364, he traveled to Sudan, Egypt, Syria, and Lebanon for about a year, meeting their scholars. In 1365 AH he became deputy head of the Sharia court, and after two years became a judge.

Translation of the author. His name and lineage: He is the eminent scholar of hadith, usul, fiqh, judge, Abu Ahmad Hasan ibn Muhammad ibn Abbas ibn Ali ibn Abd al-Wahid al-Mishshāt, al-Makki al-Maliki, al-Manafi, attribution to Abd Manaf ibn Qusayy al-Qurashi. His birth and upbringing: He was born on the third of Shawwal in the year 1317 of the Prophetic Hijra, in the al-Qarara district, and his family is among the ancient scholarly Meccan families that produced a number of scholars. His father enrolled him for study in the kuttabs, then in the al-Sawlatiyya school in 1329, and he was appointed as a teacher's assistant, and he was close to graduation age, and after his graduation he worked at the same school as a teacher for thirty years, and obtained a certificate of merit from it in 1336, and he taught at the Sacred Mosque, and studied with its scholars, and met many scholars visiting the Sacred Land. Among his teachers: Muhammad al-Khidr ibn Mayabi al-Jikini and Umar Hamdan al-Mahrasi, and he attended the lessons of the shaykhs Muhammad Habib Allah al-Shinqiti and his father Shaykh Muhammad al-Mishshāt. In 1361 he was appointed a member of the Commission of Distinction, and as was the custom of scholars he was wary of this matter, until the commission was dissolved in 1364, whereupon he traveled for about a year to Sudan, Egypt, Syria, and Lebanon, and met its scholars. In 1365 he was appointed deputy to the head of the Sharia Court, and remained there for two years until he became a judge there. In 1372 he was appointed a member of the Consultative Council, and soon he was returned to the judiciary as assistant to the head of the court, and he remained there until his resignation was accepted in 1375, and he was very happy. He did not cease teaching at the al-Sawlatiyya school and teaching at the Sacred Mosque, and many scholars and notables graduated under him and attended his lessons. Among his authored works: (1) al-Thabt al-Kabîr, in which he mentions his shaykhs and narrations. (2) Inārat al-Dujā fī Maghāzī Khayr al-Warā, in which he mentions the military expeditions and campaigns of the Messenger of God, peace be upon him, and it is a commentary on the poem of Shaykh Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Badawi al-Shinqiti. (3) al-Jawāhir al-Thamīna fī Bayān Adillat 'Ālim al-Madīna, in the science of the principles of fiqh. (4) Raf' al-Asār 'an Muhayyā Mukhaddarāt Tal'at al-Anwār fī 'Ilm Āthār al-Nabī al-Mukhtār, which is a commentary on the poem 'Tal'at al-Anwār' of Sayyid 'Abd Allah ibn Ibrahim al-'Alawī, and it is an abridgment of the Alfiyya of al-Hāfiz al-'Irāqī in the science of hadith terminology. (5) al-Taqrīrāt al-Saniyya 'alā al-Manẓūma al-Bayqūniyya, which is a valuable commentary on the Bayqūniyya poem in hadith terminology. (6) Is'āf Ahl al-Islām bi-Waẓā'if al-Hajj ilā Bayt Allāh al-Harām, in which he mentions selected Quranic verses and hadiths related to the rites of Hajj. (7) Is'āf Ahl al-Īmān bi-Waẓā'if Shahr Ramaḍān, in which he explains a collection of authentic hadiths on issues of fasting. (8) al-Bahja al-Saniyya fī Sharḥ al-Kharīda al-Bahiyya, which is this book of ours. (9) Arba'ūn Hadīthan fī al-Targhīb wa al-Tarhīb, concluding with the hadith of al-Hasanayn on the virtues of their grandfather Muhammad.
