Defining the Intellect: A Spiritual Faculty
The commentator defines 'aql as a spiritual faculty with its loci and development.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how classical kalam defines the intellect as a spiritual faculty with specific loci and development stages.

Core Idea
The intellect is a spiritual secret, not merely the brain.

The commentator begins by defining one of the most important tools in kalam: the intellect ('aql). Without a clear understanding of what the intellect is, the subsequent arguments about knowledge of Allah would be confused. This passage gives a precise definition, locating the intellect both in the heart and the brain, and describing its origin and perfection.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the sequence: what the intellect is, what it does, where it resides, when it begins, when it becomes perfect.
The definition is compact but rich.
2
Identify the key components
Look at each part: the nature (spiritual secret), the function (perceiving sciences), the locus (heart) and light (brain), the beginning (spirit blown), and first perfection (puberty).
3
Reflect on why this matters
Consider why the commentator emphasizes that the intellect is spiritual and not purely physical. This shapes how we understand reason in Islamic theology.
This connects to broader kalam debates.

(۱) العقل: سر روحاني، تدرك به النفس العلوم الضرورية والنظرية، ومحله القلب، ونوره في الدماغ، وابتداؤه من حين نفخ الروح في الجنين، وأول كماله عند البلوغ. انظر شرح الخريدة» (ص١٤٤).
(1) The intellect: a spiritual secret, by which the soul perceives necessary and theoretical sciences; its locus is the heart, and its light is in the brain; its beginning is from the time the spirit is blown into the fetus, and its first perfection is at puberty. See Sharh al-Kharida (p. 144).

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'aql (intellect). It is described as a spiritual secret (sirr ruhani) by which the soul perceives necessary and theoretical sciences. Its locus is the heart, its light in the brain, and it begins at the blowing of the spirit into the fetus, reaching its first perfection at puberty. This definition clarifies that 'aql is not merely the brain but a spiritual faculty.

Distinction between Milla, Sharia, and Din
Author explains the subtle differences among the terms milla, sharia, and din.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the terms milla, sharia, and din are technically distinguished based on different aspects, despite being used synonymously in common speech.

Core Idea
Three key Islamic terms differ by the aspect of divine command they emphasize.

This lesson explores a subtle terminological clarification from the author. While dictionaries may treat milla, sharia, and din as synonyms, the author points out that each term highlights a distinct facet of divine legislation: transmission, worship, or legislation itself. Understanding this nuance deepens one's grasp of Islamic legal and theological vocabulary.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing; then read the translation. Let the author's rhythm come through before you interpret.
2
Identify the three aspects
Notice the three perspectives: rulings dictated (milla), worship (din), legislation (sharia). See how each term is defined.
3
Reflect on the distinction
Consider why such terminological precision matters in Islamic scholarship, especially in kalam and usul.
This connects to the learning objective.

Source Text

وكلام صاحب القاموس يشير إلى ترادف الملة والشريعة والدين حيث قال: «والملة بالكسر: الشريعة أو الدين...» إلى آخره. وهي في الحقيقة تختلف بالحيثيات، فمن حيث إن الأحكام الشرعية تُملى لتنقل فهي ملة، ومن حيث إنها يُتديّن بها أي يُتعبد بها فهي دين، ومن حيث إنها شُرعت أي بَيَّنها الشارع فهي شريعة.
And the statement of the author of al-Qamus indicates the synonymy of milla, sharia, and din where he said: 'Milla (with kasra): sharia or din...' etc. In reality they differ according to aspects: from the perspective that the legal rulings are dictated to be transmitted, it is milla; from the perspective that one practices religion through it, i.e., worships through it, it is din; and from the perspective that it is legislated, i.e., the Lawgiver explained it, it is sharia.
Source pages p. 52

Commentary
This passage is a terminological clarification. The author begins by citing the dictionary authority of al-Qamus, which states that milla, sharia, and din are synonymous. However, he then explains that in fact they are distinct when considered from different aspects. He gives three aspects: milla relates to the transmission of rulings; din relates to worship and religious practice; sharia relates to the act of legislation by the Lawgiver. The passage thus defines three key terms of Islamic law and theology, showing that although they may be used interchangeably in common speech, technical usage distinguishes them based on the specific relationship between the divine command and the human response.
