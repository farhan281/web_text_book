The Origin of the Mu'tazila and the Role of al-Ash'ari
Relates the story of Wasil ibn Ata and the emergence of Mu'tazila and al-Ash'ari.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the historical circumstances that led to the emergence of the Mu'tazila and how Imam al-Ash'ari returned to Sunni orthodoxy.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila began with a middle position on grave sinners; al-Ash'ari later refuted them.

This lesson tells the story of how a question about the status of a grave sinner sparked the formation of the Mu'tazila. It also traces the intellectual journey of Imam al-Ash'ari from Mu'tazilite student to defender of Sunni creed. Understanding this history helps us appreciate the context of later theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Read the narrative of Wasil ibn Ata
Notice how the questioner presents two extremes (Khawarij and Murji'a) and Wasil's response introduces a third position.
The term 'Mu'tazila' comes from al-Hasan's statement 'i'tazala 'anna'.
2
Understand 'al-manzila bayn al-manzilatayn'
Wasil's position that the grave sinner is neither believer nor disbeliever became the hallmark of Mu'tazila.
This was a response to the problem of determining the fate of sinners.
3
Learn about Abu 'Ali al-Jubba'i
He was a leading Mu'tazilite scholar and teacher of al-Ash'ari.
Al-Ash'ari later rejected his teacher's views.
4
Follow al-Ash'ari's return to orthodoxy
Al-Ash'ari realized the corruption of Mu'tazilite doctrine and adopted the creed of the Companions.
His genealogy links him to the Prophet's companion Abu Musa al-Ash'ari.
5
Read the footnotes on Khawarij and al-Jubba'i
These provide definitions and historical background.
They also show the scholarly apparatus.

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative explaining the origin of the Mu'tazila and the subsequent role of Imam al-Ash'ari in reestablishing Sunni orthodoxy. It begins with the story: a man came to al-Hasan al-Basri, describing two extreme groups—the Khawarij, who declare major sinners disbelievers, and the Murji'a, who claim that sin does not harm with faith. The man asks what the correct belief is. While al-Hasan reflects, Wasil ibn 'Ata, his student, answers by proposing a middle position: the grave sinner (ṣāḥib al-kabīra) is neither a believer nor a disbeliever, but in a station between the two (al-manzila bayn al-manzilatayn). If such a person dies without repentance, he is in that intermediate state. Al-Hasan responded, 'Wasil has withdrawn from us' (i'tazala 'annā), giving rise to the name 'Mu'tazila'. The Mu'tazila themselves claimed to be the people of justice and tawhid. The narrative then moves to the next generation: after Wasil came Abu 'Ali al-Jubba'i, a leading Mu'tazilite, and the young Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari was his student until he saw the corruption of the Mu'tazilite doctrine and returned to the creed of the Companions and Followers. The passage ends with a brief genealogy of al-Ash'ari, linking him to the Companion Abu Musa al-Ash'ari. Two footnotes are included: one on the Khawarij, defining them and their principles, and one on al-Jubba'i, providing his biography. This lesson thus traces the historical emergence of the Mu'tazila and establishes al-Ash'ari as the defender of Sunni orthodoxy.

The Birth of the Mu'tazila: Wasil's Middle Path
The author narrates the split of Wasil ibn Ata from Hasan al-Basri and the naming of the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila were named for 'withdrawal' after Wasil ibn Ata's innovative middle position.

This lesson recounts the dramatic moment when a student's bold answer to a theological puzzle led to the formation of one of Islam's most influential rationalist schools. The story centers on the question of the grave sinner and introduces the doctrine of the intermediate station. By studying this narrative, you will see how a single theological issue gave rise to a sect's identity.

Commentary
This passage is a historical account of the origin of the Mu'tazilite sect. The author narrates that when Hasan al-Basri was asked about the ruling on the perpetrator of a major sin (sāḥib al-kabīra), he paused to think, but his student Wasil ibn Ata interjected with an answer: he denied that such a person is either absolutely a believer or absolutely a disbeliever. Instead, he introduced an intermediate state, calling it 'al-manzilah bayn al-manzilatayn' (the station between the two stations). Wasil elaborated by classifying people into three categories: believer, disbeliever, and one who is neither—the grave sinner who dies without repentance. Upon hearing this innovation, Hasan al-Basri declared, 'I'tazala 'anna' (He has withdrawn from us), which gave rise to the name Mu'tazila (the withdrawers). The Mu'tazila themselves, however, adopted the titles 'People of Justice and Tawhid' to emphasize their core doctrines. This story highlights the theological divergence over the status of the grave sinner, which became a defining issue for early Islamic sects.

Commentary
This passage is a theological anecdote (fa'ida) from the commentary of 'Illish on Iḍāʾ al-Dujnah. It presents a simple yet powerful refutation of the Mu'tazili doctrine of the intermediate station (al-manzilah bayn al-manzilatayn). The Mu'tazili leader 'Umar ibn 'Ubayd asserts that there is a middle station between faith and disbelief. An old woman refutes him by quoting Quran 64:2, which states that people are divided into only two categories: believer and disbeliever. The Quranic verse directly contradicts the Mu'tazili claim, so the old woman declares his statement false. When Sufyan al-Thawri heard this, he praised her simple faith with the famous saying 'Aliykum bi-dīn al-ʿajāʾiz' (Hold to the religion of old women). The footnote provides a detailed explanation: the saying is attributed to al-Juwayni, and al-Subki interprets it not as anti-intellectualism but as encouragement to adhere to a clear, straightforward faith based on evidence, free from sectarian bias. The anecdote thus defends Sunni orthodoxy against Mu'tazili innovation and emphasizes the sufficiency of the Quran's explicit divisions.

وكلام صاحب القاموس يشير إلى ترادف الملة والشريعة والدين حيث قال: «والملة بالكسر : الشريعة أو الدين...» (١) إلى آخره. وهي في الحقيقة تختلف بالحيثيات، فمن حيث إن الأحكام الشرعية تُملى لتنقل فهي ملة، ومن حيث إنها يُتديّن بها، أي: يُتعبد بها، فهي دين، ومن حيث إنها سرعت، أي: بينها الشارع فهي شريعة.
(And whoever says) from the people of misguidance (by nature) i.e., by the effect of nature, that the aforementioned things affect by their nature, or says (by causal power) i.e., by a causal power inherent in them that they bring about the existence of something without Allah having choice in it, then that saying is disbelief, and the disbelief of its speaker is entailed, because he has affirmed a partner for Allah, (according to the people of the religious community) i.e., the community of Islam. Al-Raghib said: 'Al-milla is a name for what Allah has legislated for His servants upon the tongues of His prophets, so that they may arrive by it to His proximity. The difference between it and religion (al-din) is that milla is only attributed to the prophet upon whom it is based, and it is hardly ever found attributed to Allah, nor to individual members of the community, and it is not used except for the totality of laws, not for individual laws.' And the statement of the author of al-Qamus indicates the synonymy of milla, shari'a, and din, where he said: 'Al-milla, with a kasra, is al-shari'a or al-din...' etc. In reality they differ according to aspects: inasmuch as the legal rulings are dictated to be transmitted, it is milla; inasmuch as they are practiced as religion (yutadayyanu biha), i.e., worship is performed by them, it is din; and inasmuch as they have been enacted (shurri'at), i.e., the Lawgiver has made them clear, it is shari'a.
Source pages p. 51

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn line that declares disbelief upon anyone who says that things act by nature (tab') or by an inherent causal power ('illa). The commentator first explains the two positions: (1) saying by nature means believing that the mentioned things (fire, food, etc.) affect by their nature; (2) saying by causal power means believing that these things have an inherent power to bring about effects without Allah's choice. Such a saying is kufr (disbelief) and entails the kufr of its speaker because it attributes a partner (sharik) to Allah. This is the ruling according to the people of the milla (religious community) of Islam. The commentator then expands on the term 'milla' itself, quoting al-Raghib al-Isfahani, who defines milla as what Allah prescribes through prophets for attaining proximity to Him. The key difference between milla and din is that milla is only attributed to the prophet (its originator), not to Allah or to individual members; it is used for the whole set of laws, not individual rulings. Then he cites the author of al-Qamus (al-Fayruzabadi), who suggests synonymy among milla, shari'a, and din. The commentator reconciles by showing they differ in aspect: as dictated and transmitted rulings, it is milla; as practiced and worshipped with, it is din; as enacted and made clear by the Lawgiver, it is shari'a. This guards against the misunderstanding that milla is simply another word for religion in general.

The Ash'ari Response to the Mu'tazili Dilemma
The author narrates a debate between Abdul Jabbar and Abu Ishaq on divine will.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God's will does not make Him responsible for evil; what is His is His favor, not a right.

This lesson presents a famous debate that captures the core difference between Mu'tazili and Ash'ari views on divine will and human responsibility. As you read the back-and-forth, notice how the Ash'ari scholar uses a precise distinction to refute the Mu'tazili's dilemma. The passage demonstrates how theological reasoning defends divine transcendence while affirming human accountability.

A subtle point: It is narrated that Abdul Jabbar al-Mu'tazili said to the master Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini, one of the great imams of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah, alluding to him: 'Glory be to Him who is exalted above lewdness.' Commentary
This passage is a theological debate in objection-and-answer form. The Mu'tazili begins by implying that the Ash'ari view (that God creates all actions, including evil) contradicts divine transcendence. The Ash'ari replies that whatever occurs in God's dominion is by His will, affirming sovereignty. The Mu'tazili objects that this means God wills disobedience. The Ash'ari retorts: 'Is God disobeyed by force?' implying human acquisition of acts. Then the Mu'tazili poses a dilemma: if God prevents guidance and decrees perdition, is He good or evil? The Ash'ari distinguishes: what is rightfully due to the servant (guidance) is a right; preventing it would be evil. But guidance is not a right; it is God's favor. So preventing what is His is not evil. This distinction destroys the Mu'tazili argument.

The Three Groups that Are the Elite of the Ummah
The author classifies the three groups that constitute the elite of the Ummah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three groups that constitute the elite of the Muhammadan Ummah according to the author.

Core Idea
The elite of the Ummah includes scholars of fiqh, kalam, and tasawwuf.

In this passage, the author identifies the three groups that represent the highest levels of the Ummah. He begins with the four Imams of jurisprudence, then moves to the theologians who safeguarded correct beliefs, and finally the spiritual masters who practiced what they taught. This classification shows the unity of fiqh, aqida, and tasawwuf.
