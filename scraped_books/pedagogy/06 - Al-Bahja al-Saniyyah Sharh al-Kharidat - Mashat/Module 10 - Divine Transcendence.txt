Allah's Transcendence: Names and Negations
Sanusi lists Allah's names and negates limitations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah is described by beautiful names and is absolutely transcendent above all physical and spatial limitations.

Core Idea
Allah is beyond all creaturely categories — He does not dwell in creation, nor is He bound by space.

This lesson from Sanusi's poem presents a concise statement of divine transcendence. The author first lists some of Allah's majestic names, then negates five categories that would limit Him. Understanding this passage helps ground the Islamic belief that Allah is unlike anything in creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the four lines of Arabic poetry once. Then read the literal English translation. Let the rhythm and key terms settle before analyzing.
Notice the rhyme and compressed style typical of didactic poems.
2
Identify the names and the negations
The passage has two parts: first, a list of Allah's names (al-Jalil, al-Jamil, etc.); second, a list of negations (hulul, jiha, etc.). Mark each group.
The negations all begin with 'an' (from) after 'munazzah'.
3
Reflect on the meaning of each negation
Ask yourself: Why is it important to negate indwelling (hulul)? Direction (jiha)? Connection and separation? These negations safeguard tanzih (transcendence).
The word 'al-safa' at the end is uncertain; possibly a textual variant.

This passage affirms Allah's transcendence (tanzih). The author first lists some of Allah's beautiful names: al-Jalil (Majestic), al-Jamil (Beautiful), al-Wali (Protector), al-Tahir (Pure), al-Quddus (Holy), al-Rabb (Lord), al-'Ali (High). Then he declares Allah munazzah (transcendent) above: hulul (indwelling, i.e., dwelling within creation), jiha (direction, i.e., occupying space), ittisal (connection to created things), infisal (separateness from created things), and 'al-safa' (unclear). This affirms that Allah is not like any created thing, not subject to spatial or physical attributes.

The Proof Against Denying Attributes
Proof against infinite regress and circularity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that denying God's attributes leads to the impossible outcomes of infinite regress and circularity.

Core Idea
Denying God's attributes leads to rational absurdity.

This passage presents a compact reductio argument. The author argues that if we deny that God possesses perfect attributes, then we are forced to accept either an infinite chain of causes or a circular argument — both of which are manifestly impossible. This proves that affirming attributes is necessary.

Guided Path
1
Read the logical argument
Read the two lines of Arabic poetry that present the proof. Notice the conditional structure: 'if not described... then originated... impossible'
2
Identify the two impossibilities
The argument mentions 'infinite regress' and 'circularity' as the consequences of denying attributes. Recognize them as logical fallacies.
3
Understand why they are impossible
Infinite regress is impossible because it never ends, and circularity is impossible because it is self-referential and invalid in reasoning.
4
Connect to the conclusion
Since denying attributes leads to impossibilities, the denial is false. Therefore, God must possess attributes.
This is a form of proof by contradiction.

If He were not described with them [the attributes], then His being originated would be necessary, and it is impossible, so be upright. Because it leads to infinite regress and circularity, which is manifestly impossible. So He is the Majestic, the Beautiful, the Protecting Friend, the Pure, the Holy, the Lord, the Most High. He is transcendent above indwelling, direction, connection, separation, and foolishness.

Commentary
This passage presents a reductio argument against denying God's attributes. The author argues that if God were not described with the attributes (presumably of perfection), then He would be originated (ḥādith), which is impossible. The reasoning: denying attributes implies either infinite regress (tasalsul) or circularity (dawr), both of which are impossible. Therefore, God must possess perfect attributes. After establishing this, the author lists names of God: al-Jalīl, al-Jamīl, al-Walī, al-Ṭāhir, al-Quddūs, al-Rabb al-'Alī, and then negates all imperfections: He is above hulūl (indwelling in creation), above having direction (jiha), above connection (ittiṣāl) and separation (infiṣāl), and above foolishness (safah).

Affirming the Vision of God in Paradise
Affirmation of the vision of God in Paradise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the vision of God in Paradise is rationally possible and textually established in Sunni creed.

Core Idea
Seeing God in Paradise is a rational possibility confirmed by revelation.

This lesson begins with the affirmation of the beatific vision (ru'yat Allāh) in the eternal Garden. The author states that it is rationally possible and supported by textual evidence. This belief is a key component of Sunni eschatology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic lines
Read the first two lines of the poem carefully. Notice the imperative 'اجزم' (be certain) and the mention of reason and scripture.
2
Understand the rational and textual basis
The author says its occurrence is jā'iz (permissible) by reason and that a textual proof (dalīl al-naql) has come. This combines both rational and revealed evidence.
3
Reflect on the nature of vision
Consider that the vision is affirmed for the eternal Garden, without end, and is a blessing for the believers.
This counters those who deny the possibility of seeing God.

Transcendence from Indwelling and Direction
Sanusi negates indwelling and direction from Allah using rational arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Allah must be transcendent from indwelling (hulul) and direction (jihah), based on His eternity and non-materiality.

Core Idea
Direction is a property of matter; Allah is not matter, so He has no direction.

Sanusi begins by negating two fundamental physical attributes from Allah: indwelling (hulul) and direction (jihah). These are among the earliest topics in kalam because they directly oppose anthropomorphic conceptions of God. The passage uses a simple analogy to show that just as a stone cannot be described as knowing or ignorant, so too Allah cannot be described with direction because He is beyond matter and its properties.

Guided Path
1
Read the negation of hulul and jihah
Read the first two lines of the Arabic passage. Notice how the author immediately links these negations to Allah's eternity (qadim) and non-materiality.
2
Understand the analogy of the stone
The author compares the impossibility of attributing direction to Allah to the impossibility of attributing knowledge to a stone. Reflect on why this analogy works.
The stone lacks receptivity (qabiliyyah) for knowledge; Allah lacks receptivity for direction.
3
Clarify the misconception about non-existence
The author explicitly states that denying direction does not mean denying existence. This addresses a common fear that without spatial location, Allah would be nothing.
This clarification is crucial for beginners.
4
Affirm the transcendence from spatial relations
The passage concludes by negating all six spatial directions relative to Allah. Notice that this is not just a negation but a positive affirmation of His transcendence.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed proof of Allah's transcendence from physical and spatial attributes. The author begins with the poem's line 'Muzannahu 'an al-hulul...' and then explains each term. First, he negates 'hulul' (indwelling) and 'saryan' (permeation) in things, because these are attributes of contingent beings, and Allah is ancient in essence. Second, he negates 'jihah' (direction) by arguing that direction is a property of matter; Allah is non-material, so cannot have direction. He illustrates with the analogy of a stone that is not described as knowing or ignorant because it lacks receptivity. He then clarifies that negating direction does not imply non-existence, as that applies only to contingent things conditioned by receptivity. Third, he negates 'ittisal' (connection) and 'infisal' (disconnection) because these are contingent attributes; Allah is eternal, not contingent. Fourth, he negates 'safah' (foolishness) by affirming Allah's perfect wisdom in placing everything correctly. He then makes a concluding argument: the entire world, despite its size, is nothing before Allah's power; thus it is absurd to think Allah could be dwelling or connected in something so insignificant. Finally, he addresses ambiguous texts that seem to imply anthropomorphism: these are mutashabihat (ambiguous verses/hadiths) which must be believed in without attempting to interpret their literal meaning, following the approach of the righteous predecessors (salaf). He cites Quran 3:7, praising those who say 'We believe in it; all is from our Lord.' He ends with a prayer to be among those of understanding.

Approach to Ambiguous Texts (Mutashabihat)
Al-Mashat explains the Sunni approach to ambiguous texts: belief with tafwid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that ambiguous Quranic verses and hadiths about Allah's attributes must be believed in without literal interpretation, entrusting their meaning to Allah (tafwid).

Core Idea
We affirm what Allah said about Himself, but we do not interpret it literally.

After establishing that Allah is transcendent from physical and spatial attributes, the author addresses a potential problem: some Quranic verses and hadiths seem to imply anthropomorphism (e.g., Allah's hand, face). These texts are mutashabihat (ambiguous). The correct approach, taught by the Salaf, is to believe in them as true, but to entrust their exact meaning to Allah, avoiding interpretation that would contradict His transcendence.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed proof of Allah's transcendence from physical and spatial attributes. The author begins with the poem's line 'Muzannahu 'an al-hulul...' and then explains each term. Finally, he addresses ambiguous texts that seem to imply anthropomorphism: these are mutashabihat (ambiguous verses/hadiths) which must be believed in without attempting to interpret their literal meaning, following the approach of the righteous predecessors (salaf). Abu Ishaq replies with a counter-question: 'Is our Lord disobeyed by force?' (i.e., is the disobedience done by compelling God? No, the slave disobeys by his own choice, but that happens within God's will. The question challenges the Mu'tazili inference that willing sin implies approving or coercing it. The Ash'ari position is that God wills sin's occurrence but does not approve it; the human agent has free choice within God's overarching will.

The impossible with respect to Him, exalted. 'And the opposite of what preceded from the lofty attributes is impossible, so know.' 43. Commentary by Sheikh Hasan Muhammad al-Mishāt al-Makki al-Mālikī. When he finished the first section—which is what is obligatory for Allah—he intended to begin the impossible with respect to Him in the second section, which is what is impossible for Him, so he said: 'And the opposites of the attributes are impossible against what preceded with an alif of absolute negation, i.e., everything that contradicts what preceded for you from the essential attributes, the negative attributes, and the attributes of meanings (the lofty) meaning elevated and transcendent above temporality and its concomitants. (2) The difference between the agent by causality and the agent by nature is that causality does not depend on the existence of a condition or the absence of an impediment, while nature does. Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of the attributes that are impossible for Allah. The commentator explains the line from the poem that states the opposite of the previously mentioned necessary attributes are impossible. He clarifies that 'impossible' refers to everything that contradicts the essential, negative, and attributes of meanings, which are described as 'lofty' (shāmikhāt) meaning elevated and free from temporality. He then lists the impossible attributes in detail: non-existence, temporality, annihilation, similarity to contingent beings in being a body or accident, not being self-subsistent, lacking oneness (i.e., having multiplicity or a partner), ignorance, death, impotence, aversion (lack of will), causing things by natural necessity or blind causality, muteness, deafness, and blindness. The footnotes correct a printing error and explain the difference between an agent by causality and an agent by nature, both of which are impossible for Allah.

Believing in the Vision of Allah in Paradise
Al-Mashat defines the obligation to believe in seeing Allah in Paradise without limit or direction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic belief in seeing Allah in Paradise as a real but transcendent vision, free from limit, place, or direction.

Core Idea
Seeing Allah in Paradise is real but without limits, place, or direction.

Al-Mashat explains a core Sunni creedal point: the vision of Allah (ru'ya) in Paradise. This passage clarifies that the vision is obligatory to believe in, yet it is utterly transcendent—without finitude, without location, and without 'how.' The author uses analogies to help us grasp that we can see something without encompassing or confining it, pointing to the incomparability of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once through, then read the literal translation. Pay attention to the key terms: ru'ya, jannat al-khuld, and tanahi.
The passage is a commentary on a poetic line; notice how the author unpacks each word.
2
Identify the three negations
The passage states that the vision is without finitude, without place/direction, and without 'how'. Find these three negations in the text and see how they are argued.
These negations remove any physicality from the vision.
3
Reflect on the analogies
Consider the analogies: knowledge of Allah (without limit), seeing the sky/earth (without encompassing), and seeing night/day (without direction). How do these help understand the vision?
The analogies show that vision can be real without confinement or location.

And be certain, my brother, of the vision of the Divine * in the garden of eternity without finitude. Then the versifier began to mention the creed of the Muslims concerning the vision of Allah the Exalted in Paradise and its ruling, and he said: (And be certain) i.e., cut off and believe obligatorily (my brother) O my brother in religion of the vision (of the Divine the Exalted) i.e., that the believers will see their Lord, Blessed and Exalted, (in the garden of eternity) i.e., of permanent abode, and that by Allah, Mighty and Majestic, causing for whomever He wills vision to uncover a complete unveiling by the eye. And the meaning: it is obligatory upon you, O believer, to believe in the vision of Allah the Exalted in Paradise and its occurrence (without) i.e., in the state of the vision being without (finitude) of the seen, Exalted, i.e., without encompassing the limits and end of the seen, due to the impossibility of limits and ends for Allah the Exalted, because the vision is by Allah's creation, and it is a type of perception by His creation for whomever He wills in any place He wills, not in a place nor a direction, nor by the connection of a ray, and the like of that which is among the characteristics of contingent things, for Allah is transcendent above them. And one looks in that to knowledge, for they know Him the Exalted without limit, and without end, and without how, so similarly they see Him. And like the sky and the earth, for we know them and see them, but we do not encompass them, despite them being limited, so how about He of whom there is nothing like Him? And like the night and the day, for we see them, and they are not in a direction, so how about He of whom there is nothing like Him?

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of the Muslim belief regarding the vision of Allah (ru'ya) in Paradise. The author begins by citing the versified line that commands affirming this vision without finitude. He then unpacks each element: 'be certain' means to hold an obligatory belief; 'my brother' addresses the reader as a fellow believer; 'vision of the Divine' means that believers will see their Lord; 'in the garden of eternity' specifies the location as Paradise where believers dwell forever. The vision occurs by Allah creating a complete unveiling in the eye for whomever He wills. The commentary emphasizes that the vision is without limits because limits are impossible for Allah. It is a created perception, not occurring in a place or direction, nor by rays—these are attributes of contingent things. The author draws an analogy to how we know Allah without limit or how, and thus we see Him similarly. Further analogies compare seeing the sky and earth without encompassing them, and seeing night and day without them being in a direction, to show that Allah's vision is even more beyond our comprehension. The central point is that the vision is real but utterly transcendent, devoid of any physical or spatial limitations.
