Al-Mashat introduces himself, praises God, and explains his motivation for writing this commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the commentator's motivation and method in writing this abridged commentary on al-Dardir's poem 'al-Kharida al-Bahiyya'.

Core Idea
Commentary is an act of devotion and accessibility in a time of declining knowledge.

The commentary begins with a classical invocation and then reveals the author's heart: a humble scholar seeing the need to revive interest in theology. This introduction sets the stage for the entire work, showing us that the author writes not for fame but for benefit and proximity to God.

1
Read the passage and note its structure

This structure is a hallmark of Islamic scholarly works.

Al-Mashat observes that despite al-Dardir's excellent poem and commentary, ambitions have weakened and knowledge is declining. His solution is to write a simple, accessible commentary.

Commentary
This passage is the commentator's introduction, which follows the classical pattern of opening with the basmala, praise of God, and blessings on the Prophet. The commentator then identifies himself as 'the poor one hoping for success on the path' (al-faqir al-rajī al-fawz 'alā al-sirāt) — a humble title — and gives his full name: Hasan Muhammad 'Abbas al-Mashat. He asks God to be with him and to rectify his affairs in both worlds. The core of the introduction is the explanation of why he wrote this commentary. He tells us that he saw the poem of Ahmad al-Dardir, 'al-Kharida al-Bahiyya fi 'Ilm al-Tawhid,' and he extols it: it is sufficient in its subject matter, a blessed poem that covers everything required of the mukallaf (the responsible person) in the field of tawhid. Al-Dardir himself had already written a commentary on his own poem, which was excellent and delightful. But despite that, people's ambitions have weakened, knowledge is in decline, and people are turning away from it. Therefore, al-Mashat aimed to draw near to God and to make knowledge accessible (al-taqarrub wa al-taqrīb) by writing a simple commentary (ta'liq basīt) in the form of an abridged explanation (sharḥ mūjaz) of this blessed poem. He hopes for benefit both from the commentary and from its original author, and he wishes that this work be a provision for him on the Day of Judgment. The introduction thus establishes the commentator's humility, his respect for the original text and its author, his awareness of the sad state of contemporary scholarship, and his pedagogical intention to make tawhid accessible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three groups of scholars that must be followed after the Salaf, and that each group represents an essential aspect of the religion.

Core Idea
The elite of the nation are jurists, theologians, and spiritual masters.

This lesson explains how the imams after the Salaf split into three groups, each dedicated to a vital dimension of Islam: jurisprudence, theology, and spiritual practice. The passage names their representative leaders and affirms that all three groups are the elite of the Muslim community.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the poetic line that instructs the seeker to engage in reflection and remembrance of Allah perpetually while avoiding all sins. The commentator first defines the two key terms: 'al-fikr' (reflection) means contemplating the creation of Allah—the heavens, the earth, and other phenomena—and 'al-dhikr' (remembrance) means remembering Allah constantly, whether with the heart or with the tongue. He then explains that heart-based remembrance is the concern of the spiritually advanced, quoting Sayyidi Abu al-Hasan who states that a tiny amount of heart-work outweighs immense bodily works. The best form of verbal dhikr is that which is accompanied by striving for heart-presence, until such presence becomes natural. The author encourages persistence in dhikr even when heedless, because a heedless dhikr can elevate one to a present-hearted dhikr, and that in turn can lead to a state of witnessing the Remembrance beyond all else. He then declares that dhikr is the greatest pillar of the spiritual path: abundant dhikr causes the Remembrance to dominate the heart, leaving no room for other attachments, and it bestows a luminous light that fosters detachment from the world, whose love is the root of all sin. Those who are granted persistent dhikr have received the very patent of sainthood from Allah, while those deprived of it have been dismissed from sainthood. Finally, the commentator cites the Quranic verse 'Remember Me, I will remember you' and a hadith qudsi to show that the honor of being remembered by Allah is itself the greatest reward of dhikr.

The commentator defines two levels of certainty with proof and analogy.

Core Idea

This passage from al-Bahja al-Saniyya introduces two key terms in Islamic epistemology: 'ilm al-yaqin and haqq al-yaqin. The commentator uses the Qur'an and a vivid example to show that certainty is not a single state but a ladder. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping how theological proof works.
