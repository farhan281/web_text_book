The Three Rational Judgments and Their Application
The poet enumerates the three rational judgments and states the obligation to know them in God and prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational judgments (wujub, istihala, jawaz) and why it is obligatory to know them as applied to Allah and His messengers.

Core Idea
Knowing Allah requires knowing what is necessary, impossible, and permissible regarding Him.

The poet lays out the basic categories of rational judgment that underpin all theological reasoning. These three categories — necessity, impossibility, and permissibility — are not abstract; they directly determine what we can affirm or deny about Allah and His prophets. This passage introduces them and establishes the obligation to know them.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic poem carefully, then its literal translation. Let the structure and rhythm sink in before analyzing.
The poem uses rhyme and meter; pay attention to key terms.
2
Identify the three categories
Find the three divisions of rational judgment: wujub (necessity), istihala (impossibility), jawaz (permissibility). Notice they are listed explicitly.
3
Understand the obligation
The poet states that knowing these categories in relation to Allah and the prophets is legally obligatory (wajib shar'an). Reflect on why this knowledge is essential for a mukallaf.
The same three categories apply to the prophets.

The same obligation applies to knowing the messengers of God, i.e., one must know what is necessary, impossible, and permissible regarding them.

Definitions of Wajib, Mustahil, and Ja'iz
The poet defines the three rational judgments in terms of essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definitions of the three rational judgments (wajib, mustahil, ja'iz) as they relate to essence.

Core Idea
The three rational judgments are defined by whether essence can accept existence or non-existence.

This lesson establishes the foundational categories of rational judgment in Islamic theology, distinguishing between what is necessary, impossible, and possible in terms of essence. The poet provides crisp definitions that serve as the basic grammar for all subsequent theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the poetic definitions
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Notice the rhyme and rhythm. Then read the literal translation to understand the meaning.
The passage uses poetic meter, so some words are shortened.
2
Understand al-wājib al-ʿaqli
The first line defines the rationally necessary: that which does not accept non-existence in its essence. This means its existence is intrinsic and cannot be negated.
The phrase 'fa-ibtahil' (so supplicate) is a poetic exhortation to God.
3
Understand al-mustaḥīl
The second line defines the rationally impossible: that which does not accept existence in its essence. This is the opposite of the necessary.
4
Understand al-jāʾiz
The third line defines the rationally permissible: that which accepts both existence and non-existence in its essence. Neither is necessary or impossible.
5
Reflect on essence-based definitions
Notice that these definitions are absolute and based on the essence itself, not on external factors. This is key for applying them to Allah and creation.
This distinction prevents relativism in theological reasoning.

Commentary
This passage provides the precise definitions of the three rational judgments as they relate to essence. The rational necessary (al-wājib al-ʿaqli) is defined as that which cannot be conceived as absent from its essence, i.e., its non-existence is impossible. The impossible (al-mustaḥīl) is the opposite: that which cannot be conceived as existing in its essence, i.e., its existence is impossible. The permissible (al-jāʾiz) is that which can be conceived as either existing or not existing in its essence, i.e., neither existence nor non-existence is necessary or impossible. These definitions are the foundational tools for reasoning about God and creation.

Commentary
This passage classifies the three rational judgments (ahkam al-'aql) that are foundational in kalam. The author states the three divisions: necessary (wujub), impossible (istihala), and possible (jawaz). He then explains that it is a legal obligation for the mukallaf to know these judgments with respect to Allah and His messengers. Then he defines each: The rationally necessary (al-wajib al-'aqli) is what does not accept non-existence (al-intifa') in its essence; it must exist. The rationally impossible (al-mustahil) is what does not accept existence in its essence; it cannot exist. The rationally possible (al-ja'iz) is what accepts both existence and non-existence. Note: The word 'al-nubut' in the definition of mustahil is unusual; in standard kalam it is 'al-'adam' for non-existence or 'al-wujud' for existence. The author uses 'al-nubut' which might be a rhyme variant or a corruption. The sense is clear: the impossible is that which cannot exist.

The Three Rational Judgments: Wajib, Mustahil, Jaiz
Defines the three rational judgments and explains each

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three rational judgments (wajib, mustahil, jaiz) and their definitions, and why they are foundational to belief in Allah and His messengers.

Core Idea
The three rational judgments are the exhaustive categories for all that is conceivable.

This lesson introduces the foundational classification of rational judgments in Islamic theology. Imam al-Sanusi's poetic lines lay out the three categories that reason inevitably recognizes: the necessarily existent, the impossible, and the possible. The commentary explains each judgment succinctly, stressing that understanding these is essential for proper faith. Let us examine the passage and its explanation.

Guided Path
1
Read the poetic line and its explanation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the poetic line emphasizes inevitability ('la mahalah').
2
Identify the three judgments and their definitions
The passage presents three rational judgments: al-wujub, al-istihalah, and al-jawaz. Find each definition: wujub is 'adam qabul al-intifa' (non-acceptance of non-existence), istihalah is 'adam qabul al-thubut' (non-acceptance of existence), and jawaz is qabul al-thubut wa al-intifa' (acceptance of both existence and non-existence).
These definitions are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
3
Understand why these three are exhaustive and necessary
The commentator explains that it is inevitable (la mahalah) that the divisions are exactly three, no less, no more. This is because any rational judgment about existence falls into one of these categories.
This is a logical necessity, not a theological opinion.
4
Reflect on the connection to faith
The commentator states that true understanding of these divisions is the basis for belief in Allah and His messengers. The address is to anyone who can understand, and the ultimate source of understanding is Allah.
The note on al-'aql defines the intellect and its role.

Mashhat defines and explains the rationally necessary and its types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of al-wajib al-'aqli, its comprehensive scope covering essential attributes, and its two subdivisions.

Core Idea
Wajib 'aqli is what cannot be removed in essence, covering all divine attributes.

This lesson establishes the foundational concept of the rationally necessary in Islamic theology. He then proceeds to a classification: attributes are divided into those that have t'alluq and those that do not. Those that have t'alluq are further divided into three categories. The first category includes knowledge and speech, which relate to all three rational judgments: wajib (necessary), ja'iz (possible), and mustahil (impossible). The second category includes power and will, which relate only to all possible things (mumkinat). The third category includes hearing and sight, which relate to all existent things (mawjudat). This classification is foundational for understanding how each attribute relates to its objects.

Definition of al-Ja'iz and Its Subdivisions
The author defines the third division (al-ja'iz) and explains its types: ijad, tark, ishqa', is'ad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of al-ja'iz (the permissible) as it applies to Allah and its four main subdivisions: ijad, tark, ishqa', and is'ad.

Core Idea
Allah's actions are all permissible; He is not obligated to do anything.

This passage introduces the third and final rational judgment: al-ja'iz, or the permissible. The author defines it and then categorises the types of actions that fall under this category in relation to Allah. Understanding this concept is crucial for developing a correct theology of divine freedom and power.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Begin by reading the Arabic text carefully, then read the literal translation. Notice the key terms and their definitions.
2
Identify the definition of al-ja'iz
Find where the author defines al-ja'iz. Note that it includes both creating (ijad) and leaving (tark) possible things.
3
Distinguish between ijad and tark
Understand that ijad refers to the attachment of power to create, while tark is simply refraining from creating. Both are equally permissible.
4

Note that ishqa' (making miserable) and is'ad (making happy) are specific instances of ijad, emphasised because of their importance.

Commentary
This passage defines the third rational division (al-ja'iz) as it applies to Allah. The author explains that it includes creation (ijad) and leaving (tark), and then specifies two subtypes: making miserable (ishqa') and making happy (is'ad), which are included in ijad but emphasized because of their importance. The commentary also gives examples: sending messengers, rewarding sinners, punishing the obedient — all are permissible, not obligatory. The footnotes clarify that the names of Allah related to actions correspond to these attachments of power, and note a variant reading in the printed version.
