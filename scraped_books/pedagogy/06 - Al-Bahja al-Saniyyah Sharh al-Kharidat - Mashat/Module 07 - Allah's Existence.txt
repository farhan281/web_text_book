The Contingency of the World: Proof of a Creator
Sanusi proves the world is contingent (hadith) and thereby necessitates a Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the classical kalam proof that the world is temporally originated (hadith) and therefore requires a Creator, as articulated by Sanusi.

Core Idea
Change proves the world is not eternal; everything that begins needs a Maker.

Sanusi opens his proof by stating a fundamental truth: the world around us is not self-sufficient. It changes, it began, and it points beyond itself. In this passage, he lays out the logical steps from observing change to affirming the necessary existence of Allah. This is the cornerstone of the cosmological argument in Ash'ari theology.

Guided Path
1
State the thesis
Read the first line where Sanusi declares the world is without doubt temporally originated (hadith) and dependent (muftaqir). This is the claim to be proven.
2
Identify the evidence
Notice the reason given: 'because change has occurred in it.' Change is the observable proof that the world is not eternal.
3
Define key terms
Sanusi defines hadith as existence after non-existence, and qidam as its opposite. Understand these definitions clearly.
4
Connect effect to cause
The principle that every effect (athar) points to an effective cause (mu'aththir) is stated. Trace how the world as an effect necessitates a cause.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The cause (the Creator) must have the attribute of existence necessarily. Reflect on the logic that leads from contingency to necessity.

Commentary
This passage provides the classic kalam proof for the existence of God based on the contingency of the world. The author first asserts that the world (everything besides Allah) is without doubt 'hadith' (temporally originated) and 'muftaqir' (dependent/needy), because it undergoes change. Change implies that the world had a beginning. He defines 'huduth' as existence after non-existence, and its opposite as 'qidam' (pre-eternity). Since the world is hadith, its existence is not necessary in itself; therefore it requires a cause that gives it existence. That cause must be described with the attribute of existence necessarily. The author then states the principle that every effect (athar) leads to a cause (mu'aththir), which is the basis of the cosmological argument. This is a concise statement of the proof from contingency (dafil al-huduth).

God's actions are free; He is not obligated to do anything.

The poet lists four specific actions that are permissible for God, and then rebukes those who claim God must do what is right. Core Idea
Change in the world proves it is not eternal, but created.

This lesson introduces the central proof of kalam for God's existence: the argument from the contingency of the world. The poet defines the world as everything other than God and points to its constant change as evidence that it cannot be self-sufficient. Proof of the World's Temporality from Change
The commentator presents the syllogistic proof that the world is temporal because of change, concluding that all besides Allah is temporal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the temporality of the world is proven through the observation of change and the principle that whatever is inseparable from temporal things is itself temporal.

Core Idea
The world's constant change proves it cannot be eternal—only Allah is eternal.

The commentator presents the central proof for the temporality of the world: the argument from change. This proof is foundational in Islamic theology because it establishes that the universe had a beginning and therefore requires a Creator. By carefully examining the evidence of change in both observable and seemingly stable phenomena, the commentator builds a logical case that everything other than Allah is temporal.

Guided Path
1
Read the syllogism outline
Understand the structure of the deductive argument presented in the first figure: the world is temporal, every temporal thing depends on a cause, therefore the world depends.
Notice the middle term is 'temporal' and is repeated in both premises.
2
Examine direct evidence of change
See how the commentator uses observable examples like motion after rest and light after darkness to show change from non-existence to existence or vice versa.
These are empirical proofs that some things are temporal.
3
Consider inferential evidence for stable objects
Understand that objects like mountains or planets, even if they seem permanent, can be proven temporal because they could change—there is no essential difference between bodies in accepting motion or rest.
This extends the proof to everything in the world.
4
Trace the conclusion
Follow how the temporality of accidents leads to the temporality of all bodies, and finally that everything besides Allah is temporal.
The opposite of temporality, pre-eternity, is unique to Allah.

Commentary
This passage is a proof of the world's temporality (ḥudūth) based on change (taghayyur). The commentator explains that the poet's line 'because change has occurred in it' provides the evidence. He first outlines a syllogism in the first figure (the classical form): the world is temporal, and every temporal thing is dependent on a temporal originator, therefore the world is dependent. The proof that the world is temporal is change, which is a transition from non-existence to existence or vice versa. This change is observed directly in some things (e.g., motion after rest, light after darkness) and inferred in others by analogy. For things that appear constant, like mountains at rest or planets in motion, it is possible that they could change because there is no inherent difference between bodies in their capacity to accept motion or rest. If something could cease to exist, then it cannot be eternal; thus it must be temporal. Since all accidents (a‘rāḍ) are temporal, and bodies are never free from accidents, bodies must also be temporal. Therefore everything other than Allah—the entire world, both substances and accidents—is temporal, meaning it exists after not existing. Finally, the commentator explains the opposite of temporality: pre-eternity (qidam), which is unique to Allah. So the proof establishes that everything besides Allah is contingent and dependent on a creator.

Commentary
This passage consists of an introductory poetic line and a lengthy commentary that serves as an extended proof of God's existence and attributes through the observation of human creation and divine care. The commentator begins by stating the principle from the poem: that every observable effect (أثر) points to an effecter (مؤثر). The commentary then proceeds to argue this principle by tracing the stages of human life from conception to the afterlife, each stage being an effect that requires an intelligent and powerful agent. The commentary follows a chronological order. First, it describes how God prepares the human being as a sperm in the loins of fathers, then moves it to the womb, and combines the two gametes. This is linked to the divine wisdom that all existence is built on the secret of pairing (سر الازدواج). Next, the commentary details the development in the womb: clot, lump, bones, clothing with flesh, and the breathing of the spirit. Then it describes birth and the provision of milk through the mother's breasts, which are stimulated by mercy. It then speaks of the parents' care, which is ultimately from God's lordship and divinity. The commentary then moves to the stages of life: childhood, puberty, adulthood, old age, and then the events after death: being brought to God, standing before Him, being saved from punishment, admitted to Paradise, and ultimately seeing God without veil. The passage concludes with Quranic verses (Surah al-Qamar, Surah an-Nahl, Surah al-Mu'minun) that reinforce the argument that all blessings and the stages of creation are from God alone. The overall structure is a cumulative argument: the entire human life is a series of effects that point to a single, wise, and powerful effecter, namely God. The commentary does not present a separate formal proof but rather uses narrative and Quranic citation to build the case.
