The Three Spans of Knowledge
Al-Sha'bi's metaphor of the three spans of knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Sha'bi's metaphor of the three spans of knowledge and how it teaches humility in seeking knowledge.

Core Idea
True knowledge humbles, not inflates.

Before diving into theological arguments, Sanusi opens with a profound reminder about the nature of knowledge itself. Through al-Sha'bi's metaphor of three spans, he warns against intellectual pride and encourages a lifelong posture of humility. This short but powerful quote sets the tone for the entire book.

Guided Path
1
Read the quote in Arabic and English
Read the passage from 'فرحم الله الشعبي' to the end in both Arabic and translation. Let the words sink in before analyzing.
Notice the emotional progression from arrogance to humility to awe.
2
Identify each span
Al-Sha'bi describes three spans. Find the description of each: first span leads to arrogance, second to humility, third is unattainable.
The third span is compared to 'hيهات' (alas!), emphasizing its remoteness.
3
Reflect on the progression
Think about why the first span causes arrogance, the second brings humility, and the third is impossible. What does this say about the nature of true knowledge?
This mirrors the journey from ignorance to wisdom.
4
Apply the lesson to your own study
Consider how this teaching can shape your attitude as you begin studying theology. Are you at the first span? The second? How can you guard against pride?
Sanusi uses this to cultivate the right mindset in his readers.

Introduction to the Book
O Allah, we praise You with the praise of the bewildered after contemplating the beauty of Your essence, and infatuation with Your praiseworthy attributes, and drowning in the seas of Your benevolence. {And if not for the favor of Allah upon you and His mercy, none of you would ever have been purified, but Allah purifies whom He wills, and Allah is Hearing, Knowing} [An-Nur: 21]. And we send blessings and peace upon the greatest of Your manifestations in the mirror of Your existents, whose light permeates the particles of Your worlds, and who sits on the throne of Your disposal in Your creatures: our master and our liege Abu al-Qasim Muhammad, the bridegroom of Your kingdom, and upon his family, the seeds of his heart, and his companions, the trustees of his covenant, and every rightly guided one from his nation. {And how could you disbelieve while to you are recited the verses of Allah and among you is His Messenger?} [Al-Imran: 101]
And after:
•
May Allah have mercy on al-Sha'bi when he said: "Knowledge is three spans: whoever attains one span from it turns up his nose and thinks he has attained it; whoever attains the second span, his soul becomes small in his eyes and he knows that he has not attained it; as for the third span, alas! No one ever attains it." And he used to say, magnifying the importance of knowledge and that it cannot be encompassed: "I have never seen anyone like me!"

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to the book. It begins with a lengthy hamdalah (praise of God) and salawat (blessings on the Prophet), followed by a quote from the scholar al-Sha'bi about the nature of knowledge. The author uses the metaphor of three spans (ashbar) to illustrate the stages of learning: the first span leads to arrogance, the second to humility, and the third is unattainable. This sets a tone of humility and reverence for knowledge, indicating that even great scholars recognize their limits. The quote is attributed to al-Sha'bi, who reportedly said this to emphasize that true knowledge leads to humility, not pride.

Sanusi's Opening Doxology and Epistemological Framework
Sanusi opens his book with a praise-laden doxology that outlines his epistemological stance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi's opening doxology establishes his theological epistemology: true knowledge of God is a divine gift, derived from proofs, yet culminates in the recognition of God's incomprehensibility.

Core Idea
True knowledge of God is a gift that leads to seeing His hiddenness.

Sanusi begins his treatise not merely with formal praise but with a profound statement of his theological method. This opening passage sets the tone for the entire work: knowledge of God is both illuminated by proofs and humbled by divine grandeur. Let us read the passage to see how the author weaves together praise, epistemology, and spiritual awe.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without stopping. Let the rhythm and the author's ecstatic language wash over you. Then read the translation.
Notice the poetic style: it is dense with theological implications.
2
Identify the key figures mentioned
Note that Sanusi mentions himself with a chain of honorifics, then praises God for opening the breasts of the 'firmly grounded scholars' (al-ʿulamāʾ ar-rāsikhūn). This group is central to his epistemology.
3
Unpack the paradox
Focus on the paradox: God's manifestation is the essence of His concealment, and nearness is distance. This is a key theme: the scholars come to know that God is beyond full comprehension.
This paradox directly connects to the learning objective.

Our master, the Imam, the all-knowing, the unique leader, Abi Abdullah Muhammad ibn the Shaykh, the saint, the knower of God, the divinely taught, the sanctified, the deceased, Abi Ya'qub Yusuf ibn Umar al-Sanusi al-Hasani, may God have mercy on him and benefit us through his blessings, by His favor and generosity, said: Praise be to God who expanded the breasts of the firmly grounded scholars to accept the lights of gnosis derived from the shining proofs, and there appeared to them through the signs of His created things, for each according to what He apportioned to him by His grace in His prior decree, and He bestowed upon them therein right vision, so they came to know what cannot be encompassed nor qualified of His immense majesty and grandeur, and they became lost in that beauty and majesty until He distracted them from the wonders of His earth and heaven. Commentary
This passage is the author's preamble (khutba) of the book. It opens with the basmala and salawat upon the Prophet, then names the author with an extensive chain of honorifics, establishing his scholarly and spiritual lineage. Sanusi then praises God for granting deep insight to firmly grounded scholars ('ulama' rasikhun) by opening their hearts to accept the lights of knowledge derived from clear proofs. He emphasizes that this knowledge is a gift from God, apportioned according to His prior decree. The scholars are led to contemplate God's signs in creation, but they ultimately realize that God's majesty is beyond comprehension—His manifestation is inseparable from His concealment, and His nearness is inseparable from His distance. The inability to fully grasp Him is itself a sublime state of purity and perfection for the elect. Sanusi then sends special blessings on the Prophet Muhammad, who was elevated to the highest degree of gnosis, a station beyond the reach of intellects. The passage concludes with a prayer of satisfaction upon the Prophet's family and companions. The language is poetic and ecstatic, typical of Sufi-influenced kalam introductions, establishing a tone of awe and divine favor.

Sanusi's Preface to His Commentary
Sanusi opens with praise, salawat, and reason for writing the commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the purpose and context of Sanusi's commentary on his creed, as expressed in his preface.

Core Idea
Preface establishes a commentary's purpose: to guide students from blind following to clear understanding.

Sanusi opens his commentary with a traditional preface praising God and the Prophet, then explains why he wrote it. This lesson introduces the author's reverence and the goal of his work, setting the stage for the theological content to follow.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation

The language is ornate; don't worry about every word at first.
2
Identify the main components
Notice the structure: praise of God (hamd), blessings on the Prophet (salawat), introduction of self, and explanation of the occasion.
This is a standard format for Islamic scholarly works.
3
Understand the reason for writing
Sanusi states that after completing his creed 'Creed of the People of Tawhid', some readers asked for a concise explanation. He responded by writing this commentary.
4
Note the title and its significance
The commentary is named 'عمدة أهل التوفيق والتسديد' which means 'Support of Those Who Seek Success and Straightening'. Reflect on what this title suggests about its purpose.
The title emphasizes guidance and correctness.
5
Reflect on the author's humility
Sanusi refers to himself as 'the poor servant of his Lord' and asks for forgiveness. This shows his humility and reliance on God.
Such humility is a hallmark of traditional Islamic scholarship.

Our teacher, the imam, the scholar, the foremost leader, Abu Abdullah Muhammad bin al-Shaykh al-Wali al-Arif al-Rabbani al-Muqaddas al-Marhum Abi Yaqub Yusuf bin Umar al-Sanusi al-Hasani, may God have mercy on him and benefit us through his blessings by His grace and generosity, said: Praise be to God who expanded the breasts of the firmly grounded scholars to accept the lights of knowledge derived from the shining proofs. Commentary

The author extols the scholars' vision of God's majesty, which leads to a state of awe and wonder. He then sends blessings on the Prophet Muhammad, who attained the highest ranks of knowledge, and on his family and companions who were guided by his light. Next, the author introduces himself as Muhammad bin Yusuf al-Sanusi and explains the occasion for writing this commentary: after his earlier work 'Creed of the People of Tawhid' was completed, some readers requested a concise explanation. He obliged, seeking divine assistance, and named the commentary 'Support of Those Who Seek Success and Straightening, in Explaining the Creed of the People of Tawhid'. The language is ornate, reflecting the author's reverence and the importance of the subject.

وبعد : فيقولُ العبد الفقيرُ إلى ربِّهِ ، المشفقُ مِنْ حَبيثِ صن وكسبه ؛ محمَّدُ بنُ يوسفَ السنوسي الحسني ، غفر الله له بلا محنة ولأبويهِ ولإخوته وذريته وأحبَّتِهِ ، وجمع الجميع بفضلِهِ في أعالي الفردوس مع المقرَّبِينَ مِنْ أصفيائه وأهل محبتِهِ وشريف قُربته : لمَّا وَفَّقَ الله سبحانه وتعالى لوضعي العقيدةَ المُسمَّاةَ بـ ( عقيدة أهل التوحيد » ، المُخرِجةِ بعونِ اللهِ مِنْ ظلماتِ الجهل وربقة التقليد المرغمة بفضلِ اللهِ تعالى أنفَ كلَّ مبتدع عنيد .. طلب منِّي بعضُ مَنِ اعتنى بقراءتها : أنْ أضعَ عليها مختصراً يُكمل مقاصدها ، ويُسهل المَشْرَعَ إلى ما عَذَبَ مِنْ مواردِها . فأجبتُهُ إلى ذلك طالباً مِنَ المولى الكريم حسن المعونة ، والتسديد للصواب في الظواهر والبواطن التي هي عن كثيرٍ مِنَ العللِ غيرُ مصونة ، وسميته : « عمدة أهل التوفيق والتسديد ، في شرح عقيدة أهل التوحيد » والله أسألُ أنْ ينفعَ بهِ وبأصلِهِ ، ويمنَّ على مَنْ سعى في تحصيلها بنيل مراتبِ العرفانِ والفوز بكمال الدارين بحولِهِ وطَوْلِهِ ، والصلاة والسلام على سيدنا ومولانا محمد أفضل العالم بعضه وكله
And after: says the poor servant of his Lord, compassionate from [؟] his doing and earning; Muhammad ibn Yusuf al-Sanusi al-Hasani, may Allah forgive him without trial and his parents and his brothers and his children and his loved ones, and gather all by His favor in the highest paradise with the near ones from His chosen ones and people of His love and noble proximity: When Allah, glorified and exalted, enabled me to write the creed called 'Creed of the People of Tawhid', which brings out, with Allah's help, from the darkness of ignorance and the collar of blind imitation that humiliates, by Allah's favor, [؟] every stubborn innovator... some of those who took care to read it asked me to write a commentary on it that completes its purposes and makes easy the approach to what is sweet from its sources. So I responded to that, asking from the Generous Lord good assistance and direction to correctness in the apparent and hidden matters that are not protected from many defects, and I named it: 'Support of the People of Success and Direction, in Explaining the Creed of the People of Tawhid'. And I ask Allah that He benefit by it and by its original, and grant to whoever strives to acquire it attaining the ranks of gnosis and the success of the perfection of the two abodes by His might and bounty, and prayers and peace upon our master and lord Muhammad, the best of the world, part and whole.

Commentary
This passage is the author's preface. It begins with the conventional formula 'wa ba'd' (and after), indicating that this follows the opening praise of God and the Prophet. Sanusi then introduces himself humbly as 'the poor servant of his Lord' and mentions his lineage, asking forgiveness for himself and his family. He explains the occasion for writing this commentary: after Allah enabled him to write his earlier creedal work called 'Aqidat Ahl al-Tawhid' (Creed of the People of Tawhid), which he describes as a work that rescues from ignorance and blind imitation and which humiliates stubborn innovators, some of his students requested a concise commentary (mukhtasar) to complete its goals and make its contents easier to access. Sanusi agreed, asking God for help and correctness, and named this commentary 'Umdat Ahl al-Tawfiq wa al-Tasdid fi Sharh Aqidat Ahl al-Tawhid' (Support of the People of Success and Direction in Explaining the Creed of the People of Tawhid). He concludes with a prayer that God benefit others through this work and its original, and bless all who seek it with knowledge and success in this world and the next, and sends blessings on the Prophet Muhammad.
