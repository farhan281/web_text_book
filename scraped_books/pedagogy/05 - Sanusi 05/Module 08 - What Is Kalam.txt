Two Conditions of a Miracle: Concomitance and Challenge
Sanusi explains two conditions for a miracle: concomitance and prior challenge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two essential conditions that a true miracle must meet: concomitance with the claim of prophethood and challenge before occurrence.

Core Idea
A miracle is defined by its timing and connection to the prophetic claim.

In this passage, Sanusi unpacks two critical conditions from the classical definition of a miracle. A wondrous event is only a miracle if it accompanies the prophecy claim and is explicitly challenged beforehand. This lesson clarifies these conditions and what they exclude.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once, focusing on the two conditions introduced by each 'wa biqawlihi'.
Notice how the first condition excludes events without a claim or with a claim of sainthood.
2
Identify the first condition
Identify the phrase 'مقارن لدعوى الرسالة' and understand what it excludes: events without a claim or with a non-prophetic claim.
This condition ensures the miracle is tied specifically to prophethood.
3
Identify the second condition
Identify the phrase 'متحدى به قبل وقوعه' and understand that the challenge must precede the event, excluding irhas and post-event challenges.
This condition ensures the miracle is a proof, not a mere coincidence.

Commentary
This passage defines two conditions for a miracle (mu'jiza) by commenting on terms from a definition. First, a miracle must be 'concomitant with the claim of prophethood' (مقارن لدعوى الرسالة). This means it must occur in connection with a claim to prophethood, distinguishing it from events that happen without any claim (e.g., natural phenomena) or with a claim of sainthood (wilaya). This excludes preparatory signs (irhas) that occur before the claim, or challenges made after the event. The passage then discusses whether a miracle can be delayed until after the prophet's death. There are two opinions attributed to al-Ash'ari; al-Baqillani chooses the second (that it is not permissible). The apparent reason is that preserving the rulings revealed during his lifetime does not give a motive to accept his message after death, as the need for confirmation is immediate.

The Condition of Non-Contradiction in Miracles
Sanusi explains the condition that a miracle must not be contradicted by a contrary sign, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the condition that a miracle must not be contradicted by a contrary sign, and the scholarly disagreement about whether a revived dead person denying the prophet counts as contradiction.

Core Idea
A miracle must be consistent with the claim it is meant to prove.

In this passage, Sanusi clarifies a crucial condition for a miracle: it must not be contradicted by a contrary sign. He gives a vivid example of a hand speaking to deny the prophet, and then discusses a debated case among scholars regarding the revival of a dead person. This condition helps distinguish a true miracle from mere marvels.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and then the English translation to get an overview of the content.
Notice the opening parenthesis in the Arabic; it is likely a typo for 'غير مكذب'.
2
Understand the example of the hand
The passage gives an example: if a prophet claims that God will make his hand speak as a sign, but the hand speaks to deny him, then the miracle is invalid because it contradicts the claim.
3
Examine the debated case of the revived dead person
A more complex case: if a prophet raises a dead person as a miracle, but the dead person then denies the prophet's truthfulness, does this invalidate the miracle? Al-Qadi and Imam al-Haramayn had two opinions, and later scholars preferred that such contradiction (e.g., from the hand) does not damage the miracle.
The reasoning might be that the miracle itself is the act of revival, not the content of the revived person's speech.
4
Reflect on the condition
The condition 'غير مكذب' ensures that the sign directly supports the prophet's claim. If the sign itself produces a denial, it defeats the purpose.
This is distinct from other conditions like the miracle being beyond human capacity.

The First Duty and Refutation of Rational Obligation
Sanusi defines the first duty and refutes the Mu'tazila on rational obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first duty of the accountable person according to Sanusi, why it is established by sharia rather than reason, and how he refutes the Mu'tazila objection about rational obligation.

Core Idea
The first duty is to reflect on proofs of God, but its obligation comes from revelation, not reason.

Sanusi opens his creed by stating the very first thing every accountable person must do: employ the intellect to know Allah through evidence. He then addresses a crucial objection from the Mu'tazila who argue that reflection is obligatory by reason itself. In this lesson, you will see how he defines that duty and dismantles the rationalist argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of the first duty
Read Sanusi's statement: 'the first thing incumbent upon one who has reached puberty is to employ his intellect in what leads him to knowledge of his worshipped one through decisive proofs.' Notice the emphasis on active reflection (nazar).
2
Note the exception for prior knowledge
Sanusi adds that if knowledge is already obtained before puberty, then after puberty one should occupy oneself with the most important matters in order.
3
Grasp the difference: obligation by sharia, not reason
Sanusi clarifies that this duty is obligatory by sharia (shar'an), not by reason ('aqlan). This distinguishes Ahl al-Sunna from the Mu'tazila, who base all rulings on reason.
4
Analyze the Mu'tazila objection and Sanusi's answer
The Mu'tazila object that if reflection were not rationally obligatory, it would lead to a vicious circle disabling the messengers. Sanusi answers that the argument is 'shared' (mushtarak) – it applies equally to the Mu'tazila position, so it is not compelling.

And the reality of nazar: it is the arrangement of known matters in a manner that leads to knowledge of what is not known. Thus al-Baydawi and others defined it. And better and safer than it is to say: nazar is the setting down of one known [premise] or the arrangement of two or more known [premises] in a manner through which the goal is reached. And 'or' is for diversification, so it includes deficient definition and deficient description. Commentary
This passage is a definition (ta'rif) of nazar. Sanusi first gives the definition of al-Baydawi and others: nazar is the ordering of known things to discover the unknown. He then offers what he considers a better and safer definition: nazar is the placing of one known (premise) or the ordering of two or more known premises in a way that leads to the desired conclusion. The word 'or' allows for both deficient definitions (including the genus alone or the differentia alone) and deficient descriptions (like 'the laughing one' for human). Sanusi then classifies the results of nazar: if the ordered matters lead to knowledge of a single concept (tasawwur), they are called a definition (mu'arraf) or explanatory statement (qawl shar), e.g. 'human is the speaking animal'. If they lead to a judgment (tasdiq) – i.e., knowledge of the relation of affirmation or negation between two concepts – they are called a proof (hujja) or evidence (dalil). The example given is the classic kalam proof for the temporality of the world: 'The world is changing; everything changing is temporally originated; therefore the world is temporally originated'. The minor premise must be affirmative and the major universal for the conclusion to follow necessarily.

The Objection to Miracle as Act and Ash'ari's Addition
Sanusi presents the objection that miracles can be non-acts and Ash'ari's solution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that miracles can be non-acts and how Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari broadened the definition of miracle to include 'what stands in place of an act'.

Core Idea
Miracles can be non-acts; Ash'ari expanded the definition accordingly.

Theologians debated whether miracles must be positive acts of Allah. An objection arises from cases like prophetic immunity from harm, where the miracle is the absence of harm. In response, Sheikh Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari added the phrase 'or what stands in its place' to the definition, allowing non-acts to be considered miracles. This lesson examines that objection and Ash'ari's solution.

Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion responding to an objection against the definition of a miracle (mu'jiza) being strictly an act (fi'l) of Allah. The author (Sanusi) begins by presenting a counterargument: some accept the objection that a miracle could be a non-act, such as immunity from harm (ismah) in the cases of prophets like Muhammad and Noah (peace be upon them) where the challenger claims that no one will harm him – the miracle is the absence of harm, not a positive act. Or a prophet saying 'My sign is that no one will stand up in this region for a set time.' To include such cases, Sheikh Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari added to the condition 'act of Allah' the phrase 'or what stands in its place' (aw ma yaqumu maqamahu), thereby broadening the definition. Next, the author presents two specific answers to the objection. The first is from Ibn Dahaq, who holds that the miracle is effectively the knowledge and report of the event happening in accordance with reality – i.e., the miracle is in the cognitive and communicative aspect, not the physical act. The second is from Imam al-Haramayn (al-Juwayni), who argues that the continued sitting (qu'ud mustamirr) contrary to habit is itself an act, and similarly for the other examples: the absence is considered an act. Both answers are then critiqued with two reasons. The first reason: the challenge (tahaddi) did not in fact occur with what is mentioned in the answers; rather the challenge is based on the absence of the act. The second reason (specific to Imam al-Haramayn's view): if a prophet were to challenge by saying 'Allah causes this great mountain to cease to exist,' the challenged thing would be non-existence ('adam). If one then tries to argue that relative non-existence (al-'adam al-idafi) is an act in which divine power can operate – as per al-Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani and his followers – and that non-existence is not merely the cutting off of accidents, this would not hold because Imam al-Haramayn's own principle is that accidental non-existence (al-'adam al-tari') cannot be the object of power. So his attempted solution fails. As for Ibn Dahaq's answer, it is consistent (mut'tarid) in all cases, so it is good if it is safe from the first refutation (which was that the challenge is not based on knowledge/report but on the actual occurrence). A possible rejoinder is then considered: the challenge in a miracle is either by direct correspondence (mutabaqah) – clear – or by implication (luzum) like knowledge and report in the mentioned examples. But this rejoinder is itself open to consideration (fihi nazar). Thus the passage leaves the issue open, showing the complexity of defining miracles.
