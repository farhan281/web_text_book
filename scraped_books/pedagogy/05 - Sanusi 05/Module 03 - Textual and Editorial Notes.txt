The Rhetorical Meaning of 'حمار الشيخ'
Sanusi explains a phrase's rhetorical meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical and rhetorical function of the phrase 'حمار الشيخ' and why it is a metaphorical description rather than a literal reference.

Core Idea
The phrase 'حمار الشيخ' is a rhetorical metaphor, not a literal donkey.

In the previous anecdote, the phrase 'حمار الشيخ' appears. Sanusi pauses to explain its grammatical and rhetorical sense. He clarifies that the construct is explicative, meaning it describes the Sheikh's character, not his property. Let's see how this small note illuminates the wider lesson.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic phrase and its literal translation. Notice the technical term 'الإضافة بيانية' (explicative construct). This is the key to understanding the meaning.
2
Understand the term 'إضافة بيانية'
An explicative construct means the second noun describes the first, not that the first owns the second. For example, 'خاتم فضة' (ring of silver) means a silver ring, not a ring that owns silver. Here, 'حمار الشيخ' means 'the donkey-like Sheikh'.
This is a common rhetorical device in Arabic.
3
Note the meaning of 'عكاري' and 'حمارية'
The commentator says the word 'عكاري' (donkey) and 'حمارية' (donkey-ness) reinforce that the description is about character. The intended meaning is describing him as having donkey-like qualities (e.g., stubbornness).
4
Grasp the importance for the anecdote
This clarification prevents misinterpretation: the Sheikh is not literally a donkey owner; rather, the phrase is a metaphorical insult. Understanding this helps readers appreciate the tone of the original anecdote.
This connects the linguistic note to the broader lesson.

Commentary
This passage is a textual and rhetorical note. It explains the phrase "حمار الشيخ" (the donkey of the Sheikh) that appeared in the anecdote. The commentator clarifies that the construct (إضافة) is explicative (بيانية), meaning "donkey" is in the sense of describing the Sheikh as a donkey, not ownership. He references a manuscript number and states that the intended meaning is describing him as having donkey-ness (i.e., being stubborn or stupid). This is a rhetorical analysis of a key phrase in the previous lesson.

Al-Manjur's Critique of Qadi 'Iyad on Prophets Before Prophethood
Al-Manjur critiques Qadi 'Iyad's impossibility claim for prophets before prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Al-Manjur argues that it is possible to conceive of a prophet sinning before prophethood due to obligation under a previous law.

Core Idea
Prophets before prophethood can be bound by earlier laws, so sin is conceivable.

Qadi 'Iyad claimed that it is impossible to conceive of a prophet sinning before prophethood. However, the commentator Al-Manjur points out a critical flaw in this reasoning: the situation changes if the prophet is not the first messenger. Before prophethood, a person may be obligated by the law of a previous prophet. This lesson examines Al-Manjur's critique and his use of the example of Aaron.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage from Al-Manjur's gloss
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the flow from the reference to Al-Shifa to Al-Manjur's own words.
Al-Manjur begins by saying 'Look at this' indicating he is about to correct an oversight.
2
Identify the crux of Qadi 'Iyad's claim
From the context, Qadi 'Iyad claimed that it is impossible to conceive of a prophet sinning before prophethood. Identify why Al-Manjur says this only holds for the first messenger.
Think about what makes the first messenger different from subsequent prophets.
3
Understand Al-Manjur's counterargument
Al-Manjur argues that before prophethood, a prophet may be obligated by the law of a previous prophet, using Aaron (who followed Moses' law) as an example. Reflect on how this makes sin conceivable.
Al-Manjur ends with a note of humility, suggesting he might be the one misunderstanding.

Look at 'Al-Shifa' p. 673. The scholar Al-Manjur said in his gloss p. 223: 'Look at this; for it would only be valid if the discussion were about the first messenger only. How is it when the discussion is about the ruling on prophets before prophethood? The conceptualization of the issue then is not impossible, because the prophet before prophethood may be obligated by the law of one before him, as we say concerning Aaron before he became a prophet...' until he said: 'And it is amazing how this escaped the Qadi! Perhaps we did not understand.'
Source pages p. 573

Commentary
This is a marginal note by the commentator Al-Manjur criticizing Qadi 'Iyad's statement that it is impossible to conceive of a prophet sinning before prophethood. Al-Manjur argues that the conceptualization is not impossible because before prophethood, a person may be subject to a previous law (e.g., Aaron followed Moses' law). Thus Qadi 'Iyad's reasoning is flawed unless he meant only the first prophet.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: All prophets before prophethood are not bound by any divine law.
Correction: Al-Manjur clarifies that a prophet before prophethood may be obligated by the shari'a of a previous prophet, as in the case of Aaron under Moses' law.

Key Takeaway
Al-Manjur shows that before prophethood, a prophet can be under a previous law, making sin conceivable contrary to Qadi 'Iyad's claim.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Al-Manjur, a scholar, humbly says 'Perhaps we did not understand' even as he critiques Qadi 'Iyad. When you disagree with someone about a religious matter, do you leave room for the possibility that you might be the one misunderstanding? Can you recall a specific time you were too quick to assume you were right? What would it cost you to adopt a posture of epistemic humility in that situation?
?
This lesson shows that even prophets, before their prophethood, were bound by previous laws and could potentially sin. How does this reality affect your view of your own past mistakes and imperfections? Do you treat your failures before you had deeper faith as disqualifying, or as part of a story that God can redeem? What is one sin from your past that you need to trust God has already covered, and how can you let that go today?
