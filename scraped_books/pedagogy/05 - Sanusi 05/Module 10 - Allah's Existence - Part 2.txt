Objection and First Answer: The Impossible Partner
Sanusi presents an objection to tawhid and gives the first answer from al-'Aqida.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the proposal that two gods could divide the world between them is impossible according to the first answer in al-'Aqida.

Core Idea
Divine will and power must attach to all possibles, making a division impossible.

The proof of tawhid from mutual conflict (tamānuʿ) is challenged by an objector who suggests that two gods could simply divide creation into separate domains, thereby avoiding conflict. This seems plausible at first glance. However, Sanusi presents the first answer from al-'Aqida: the partner (qasim) is impossible because divine will and power are necessarily universal. In this lesson, we examine this objection and how the first answer refutes it decisively.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector proposes that two gods could avoid conflict by dividing the world into two parts, each ruling independently. Identify this proposal in the passage.
The objection hinges on the idea of a 'partner' (qasim).
2
Recall the requirement of universal will and power
The first answer relies on the principle that divine will and power must attach to every possible thing (wujub 'umum al-ta'allaq). Check the passage for this principle.
This was previously established in the proof of tawhid.
3
Apply the principle to the objection
If each god's will and power attach to all possibles, then each would will what the other wills, leading to conflict. See how this refutes the division proposal.
Thus, the partner (qasim) is impossible.
4
Connect to the broader argument
Understand that this first answer protects the proof of tawhid from a potential counterexample, reinforcing that only one God can exist.

Second Answer: Similar and Different Types
Sanusi analyzes two scenarios for the division of types between two gods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two scenarios in Sanusi's second answer and why both lead to logical impossibility for the existence of two gods.

Core Idea
Whether the types are similar or different, two gods cannot coexist without conflict.

Sanusi continues his refutation by considering the second objection from his opponents: what if the types assigned to each god are either similar or different? This passage shows that both possibilities lead to contradiction or impossibility, further strengthening the case for tawḥīd.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to understand the two scenarios Sanusi presents.
2
Analyze the similar case
If both types are similar (e.g., both substances), each god's power over one example implies power over its like, so both have power over both types, causing conflict.
Notice the logical principle: ability over one similar implies ability over its like.
3
Analyze the different case
If the types are different (e.g., substances vs. accidents), Sanusi gives two reasons why it's impossible: inseparability of substance and accident, and the possibility of conflicting wills.
Even if the opponent concedes the division, conflict can still arise.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Consider how this answer contributes to the proof of God's oneness by eliminating any possible division of domains.
This is part of a cumulative argument.

Second, even if allowed, conflict could still arise (e.g., one wills existence of substance, the other non-existence of accident), making both wills impossible and resulting in inability of one or both gods. The Problem of Specification in Dualism
Sanusi enumerates the logical possibilities for specification and refutes each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that any specification of types to distinct gods leads to impossibility, thereby refuting the idea of two gods each ruling separate realms.

Core Idea
Assigning different types to co-eternal gods requires unjustified specification, impossible or leads to conflict.

Sanusi presents a powerful additional argument against dualism. He shows that any division of the universe between two eternal beings requires unjustified specification, leading either to their temporality or internal conflict. This argument strengthens the case for tawhid.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and its translation
Read the passage from 'قلت: ويصحُ أنْ يُجابَ' to 'وكلا الأمرين محال'. Notice how Sanusi lays out the logical possibilities.
This is a typical kalam argument by exhaustion of possibilities.
2
Identify the three possibilities for specification
Track the three cases: (1) specification without any cause, (2) specification by an external specifier, (3) specification by the gods' own choice. See how each is shown to be impossible.
The argument uses a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Understand why each option fails
Option 1 leads to arbitrary specification without a specifier (impossible). Option 2 makes the gods temporal (impossible for eternal beings). Option 3 leads to conflict (tamānu') and is thus impossible.
Thus no valid specification exists, so two self-sufficient gods cannot coexist.

The Dualist Argument from Good and Evil
Sanusi presents the dualist syllogism from good and evil to two gods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dualists' argument that the coexistence of good and evil requires two distinct creators, and why this argument is considered invalid in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Observing good and evil in the world does not force belief in two gods; a single God can create opposites.

Dualists have long pointed to the existence of both good and evil as evidence for two rival deities. Sanusi presents their argument here, preparing to refute it. Understanding this argument is key to appreciating the Islamic response that attributes everything to one wise Creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the dualist argument and its translation
Read from 'وشبهتهم فى ذلكَ' to the end. Notice the structure: they observe good and evil, then infer opposite agents.
This is a common argument in Manichaean and Zoroastrian thought.
2
Identify the key premise: opposite effects require opposite causes
The dualists argue that good (order) and evil (corruption) are opposite effects, so they must come from opposite agents. This is a principle of causal reasoning.
Islamic theologians challenge this premise by pointing to the single omnipotent cause.
3
Understand why this argument fails
Sanusi does not elaborate here but the underlying refutation is that a single agent can produce opposite effects by will and wisdom, and that the observed differences do not necessitate multiple independent agents.
Good and evil are not fundamental opposites; they are contingent and can be attributed to God's wisdom.

The Impossibility of Two Gods: Argument from Specification without a Specifier
Sanusi refutes the possibility of two gods by analyzing the need for a specifier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument from specification without a specifier refutes the possibility of multiple gods.

Core Idea
Two gods would require a specifier, proving they are created—or lead to absurdity.

Sanusi addresses a subtle objection regarding the possibility of two gods. He demonstrates that even if two gods could theoretically exist, their distinct spheres of action would require a specifier. This leads to either dependence on a creator or a logical contradiction, reinforcing the necessity of a single, uncreated Creator.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the opening scenario where two gods will opposite things. Understand why this leads to impotence.
Notice the objection assumes conflict of wills.
2
Examine Sanusi's alternative answer
He points out that specifying each god to a type of action requires a specifier, otherwise it is arbitrary.
Key term: تخصيص (specification).
3
Consider the choice scenario
If specification is by their own choice, each could refrain, leading to mutual hindrance (تمانع). This is impossible.

Refuting Dualism: Good and Evil Are Relative
Sanusi presents the dualist argument and the response that good/evil are relative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that good and evil are relative attributes, not intrinsic, and that all actions depend on God as the Specifier.

Core Idea
Good and evil are relative, not absolute, and do not prove multiple gods.

The dualists argued that opposing effects imply opposing agents, so good and evil must come from different gods. This shows that the mere fact of being good or evil does not affect the attribution to God.

Killing a specific person is one action; it is evil for the victim's allies but good for his enemies. This illustrates that good and evil are relative (iḍāfiyān).
Thus the dualist argument from opposition in effects fails.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Since good and evil are not intrinsic, they cannot prove multiple agents. All actions depend on God equally.

The Shar'ī Basis of Good and Evil and God's Tawqīfī Names
Ibn al-Tilmisani gives the Shar'ī definition of good/evil and refutes the Mu'tazila naming argument.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Good and evil are defined by divine command for humans; God's actions are always good and His names only praiseworthy.

Having established that good and evil are relative, Ibn al-Tilmisani now turns to the Shar'ī definition: good is what is commanded, evil what is prohibited, and these apply only to human agents. For God, 'good' means He may do it and is praised for it. Furthermore, the Mu'tazila argument that an agent of evil is evil fails because God's names are restricted to what revelation permits.

This rejects the Mu'tazila view that good and evil are rational categories.
2
Apply to God
For God, 'good' means that He may do it and that praise has come for doing it. Since God can do any possible thing and is praised for all perfection, all His actions are good.
Thus, even actions humans call 'evil' are good when done by God.

The Disagreement on Proving God's Oneness
Sanusi categorizes the proof of oneness among rational judgments and outlines disagreements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the scholarly disagreement on whether God's oneness can be proven by revelation alone or only by reason, and the consensus that reason alone is sufficient.

Core Idea
Reason is universally accepted for proving oneness; revelation alone is debated.

Sanusi introduces a key epistemological question: does knowledge of God's oneness come from reason, revelation, or both? The Argument and Refutation on Prophethood and Tawhid

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Miracles only prove prophethood if we already know that only God can do them.

This passage presents a subtle debate: can we prove that a prophet is truthful without first knowing that God is one? Fakhr al-Din al-Razi argued that we can, but an objection shows that his reasoning is circular. Understanding this argument sharpens your grasp of how kalam establishes the foundations of religion.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument and its refutation. The Vicious Priority Circle in Scriptural Proof for Tawhid
Sanusi demonstrates the inevitable priority circle from using scriptural proof for tawhid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how granting a mutual circle between truthfulness and tawhid leads to an impossible priority circle when trying to prove tawhid through a scriptural proof.

Core Idea
Even a granted mutual circle makes tawhid prior to scriptural proof, causing a vicious regress.

Even if we accept a simultaneous circle between truthfulness and tawhid, inferring tawhid from a scriptural proof creates a contradiction: tawhid must be both prior and posterior to that proof. Sanusi shows the impossibility.

Guided Path
1
Recall the mutual circle
Assume a mutual circle between truthfulness and tawhid (as per the objector).
2
Apply it to scriptural proof
Since truthfulness is prior to the scriptural proof, and tawhid is not posterior to truthfulness (due to the mutual circle), tawhid must be prior to the scriptural proof.
3
Identify the contradiction
If we then use the scriptural proof to prove tawhid, tawhid would be posterior to the proof, creating a cycle: tawhid is both prior and posterior.
4
Conclude the impossibility
This vicious circle of priority (dawr taqaddum) shows that tawhid cannot be proved by scriptural proof alone.
The objector's position leads to an absurdity.

Commentary
This is another objection and answer. Existence cannot be a cause for vision because it is identical to the existent.

The formulation of the inference by existence as refined by Ibn al-Tilimsani is that it is said: The Creator, exalted, is existent, and every existent can be seen; therefore the Creator can be seen. Commentary
This passage is a critique of the rational proof commonly used to establish the possibility of seeing Allah, which argues that existence is the ground (musahhih) for visibility. Sanusi declares this proof weak because existence is identical to the existent, so it cannot be a separate cause. Then he presents a refined version by Ibn al-Tilimsani, which states the argument as a syllogism: The Creator exists; everything that exists can be seen; therefore the Creator can be seen. The major premise is defended by saying that visibility requires a ground; otherwise, vision could relate to non-existent things like knowledge does. Since vision relates to different things (substances and accidents), the ground must be something they share, not something that differentiates them. Differentiating causes would lead to different causes for the same type of judgment, which is impossible. So the ground must be a shared attribute, either positive (thubuti) or privative ('adami). Sanusi does not here complete the argument; he only shows the structure and implies that the shared positive attribute (like existence) is problematic because existence is identical to the existent. So the proof is not sound.

Objection: existence is equivocal to avoid composition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that existence is equivocal between God and creatures and why the objector believes univocal predication would lead to composition in God's essence.

Core Idea
If existence were univocal, God would need a differentia, making Him composite – impossible.

The seventh objection challenges the earlier claim that a common ruling needs a common cause. The objector argues that existence is not univocal but equivocal, citing Imam al-Ash'ari's authority. If existence were univocal, it would be a genus for God, requiring a differentia and leading to composition. This would break the analogy between our existence and God's existence as a cause for vision.

Commentary
This passage is the seventh objection (السابع) and its answer. The objector argues: Even if we accept that a common ruling needs a common cause, we do not accept that existence (الوجود) is univocal (مشترك معنوي) when predicated of God (الواجب) and creatures (الممكن). Rather, it is equivocal (مشترك لفظي). If it were univocal, existence would be a genus for God, requiring a differentia, leading to composition in God's essence, which is impossible. The objector cites Imam al-Ash'ari's doctrine that existence is equivocal and that the existence of each thing is identical with its essence. Consequently, if our existence causes the validity of our vision (of physical objects), it does not follow that God's existence causes the validity of seeing Him. The answer admits that this objection is difficult on al-Ash'ari's view. But the answer (presumably from the Matūridī or later Ash'arī position) commits to the position that existence is additional to quiddity (زائد على الماهية) even if inseparable, and that it is univocal. Evidence: existence can be divided into Necessary and possible, and the subject of a division must be common to all divisions. However, this does not make it a genus unless it is intrinsically shared (مشترك ذاتي), which is denied; because understanding the essence does not depend on understanding existence. This argument follows the choice of the Imām (likely al-Rāzī or al-Ghazālī, as indicated in the footnote).

Eleventh Objection: Existence as a Relative Cause Refuted
The commentator refutes the claim that existence could be a cause for visibility relative to human perspective.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the commentator rejects the notion that existence could be a cause for visibility relative to human perspective, and why rational causes cannot be relative.

Core Idea
Rational causes are absolute; they never fail to produce their effect, regardless of perspective.

In this passage, the commentator tackles an objection that tries to introduce relativity into rational causation. The objector suggests that existence might cause visibility 'relative to us' but not to God. The commentator's response reaffirms the absolute nature of rational causes and the uniformity of God's power over all possibles. Let's examine the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow. The objector uses an analogy about creating substances to argue that a cause may work in one context but not another.
Notice how the objector distinguishes between relative and absolute causation.
2
Identify the commentator's key principle
The commentator states that a rational cause never fails to produce its judgment in any state. This is the core rebuttal.
Rational causes are not like physical or conditional causes.
3
Contrast human and divine power
The commentator points out that human power has no real efficacy, while God's power is fully effective and relates equally to all possibles.
This shows that if existence were a cause, it would work universally, not relatively.
4
Understand the conclusion
The objection fails because rational causes cannot be relative. God's power is uniform, so no distinction between 'relative to us' and 'relative to God' is valid.
Therefore, existence cannot be a cause for visibility relative to us but not to God.

Commentary

A verse from Surah al-Nisa' (4:56) about skin replacement after being cooked is interpreted as a change in time, not identity, indicating that time is not restored.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think that everything, including time, is restored in the afterlife.
Correction: While God is capable of restoring time, no evidence from revelation confirms it; in fact, a Quranic verse indicates the opposite.

Key Takeaway
Accidents are restored with their identities, but time is not restored, as no report confirms it and a Quranic verse contradicts it.
