Refutation of Eternal Absolute Motion
Refutation of the philosophers' claim that absolute motion is eternal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers' claim that absolute motion is eternal is self-contradictory, and why the absolute cannot exist independently of particular instances.

Core Idea
The absolute has no existence apart from particular instances; therefore, it cannot be eternal if each instance is originated.

The philosophers of ancient Greece and the Islamic tradition held that motion as a universal is eternal, even though individual motions begin and end. This may seem plausible: the concept of motion is timeless, so why couldn't motion itself be eternal? In this passage, the author presents their view and then offers a decisive refutation based on the relationship between the absolute and the particular.

Guided Path
1
Understand the philosophers' claim
The philosophers define the eternality of originated movements as an infinite chain: every movement is preceded by another, with no first movement. This is their doctrine.
They do not claim any particular movement is eternal.
2
Note their concession
The philosophers concede that no particular movement is eternal. They only speak of 'absolute movement' as eternal.
3
Grasp the answer
The absolute movement has no existence except in and through particular movements. Since every particular movement is originated (hadith), the absolute cannot be eternal.
Thus, the philosophers' position is self-contradictory.

Core Idea
Space is not a real container; it is an imaginary void.

The response from the mutakallimun is not to deny the premise but to redefine what 'space' means. As you read, notice how a shift in definition resolves the apparent paradox.

The Proof and Infinite Regress
Objection and answer regarding infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the proof of a necessary Creator inherently refutes the possibility of an infinite regress of causes, and you will be able to articulate the logical argument that establishes this.

Core Idea
The proof of a necessary Creator inherently refutes infinite regress.

Some might think that the kalam cosmological argument can stand without addressing the problem of infinite regress. In this passage, the author corrects that misperception and shows that the argument itself provides a proof against infinite regress. By following the logic, we see why an endless series of contingent beings is impossible without a necessary external cause.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice how the author first states a potential misunderstanding, then corrects it, then gives the argument.
2
Identify the misconception
What is the false understanding that some might have? The author says some think the proof does not need to refute infinite regress. Why is this a problem?
3
Follow the logical argument
Trace the steps: if there were an infinite series of possible beings, it would need a cause. That cause cannot be the series itself or any part of it. So it must be external, and thus necessary, cutting off the series.
Pay attention to the phrase 'لا يجوز أن تكون نفسها ولا بعضها' – why can't the cause be part of the series?
4
Connect to kalam
Understand why this strengthens the cosmological proof. The proof does not bypass the issue of infinite regress; it actually relies on refuting it. This shows the coherence of the argument.
5
Reflect on the importance
Why does refuting infinite regress matter for tawhid? Without this, the existence of a necessary Creator might be doubted. This argument seals the case.

That cause cannot be the series itself or any part of it (since a thing cannot cause itself or its own causes), so it must be external, and thus must be necessary, which cuts off the series.

Mu'tazila Proof from Movement and Its Refutation
Mu'tazila objection and its refutation by distinguishing Ash'aris from Jabriyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila argument from voluntary vs. involuntary movement and why it does not refute the Ash'ari position.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila confused Ash'aris with Jabriyya.

The Mu'tazila argued that our intuitive distinction between voluntary and involuntary movement proves human free will, and that without it, moral responsibility collapses. This objection, however, only targets the Jabriyya who deny any human role. The Ash'ari position, with its doctrine of kasb (acquisition), remains untouched.

3
Study the answer

Kasb means the human acquires the action while God creates it.

Objection that divine creation of actions implies divine responsibility for sins.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God creates all actions, but humans acquire them and are responsible — the objection misunderstands this.

This lesson examines a critical objection raised against the Islamic doctrine of divine creation of all human actions. The objection claims that if God creates all deeds, He would be directly responsible for sinful acts like fornication and theft. The author dismisses this as ignorance, pointing to the key distinction between creation (khalq) and acquisition (kasb).

Commentary
This passage presents and answers the Mu'tazilite objection against karamat. The Mu'tazilites argue that if saints could perform extraordinary acts (khawariq al-'adat), these would be indistinguishable from the miracles (mu'jizat) that prove prophethood, thus making it impossible to distinguish a prophet from a non-prophet. The author responds by stating that the karamah of a saint is actually a miracle (mu'jizah) for the prophet (rasul) under whose message the saint follows. The karamah demonstrates that the person is a true saint, and a true saint is necessarily one who adheres to the religion of his prophet: he affirms with the tongue and believes with the heart, obeys the commands and prohibitions, and does not claim independence from following the prophet. If someone claimed to be self-sufficient and not a follower, he would not be a true saint and such extraordinary acts would not appear at his hand. Therefore, the extraordinary act is considered a miracle relative to the prophet, regardless of whether it appears from the prophet himself or from a member of his community. Relative to the saint, it is a karamah because it is free from any claim to prophethood. The author notes the differences: the prophet must have knowledge of his prophethood and intends to show miracles as proof, whereas the saint does not have these requirements. The passage concludes with the summary statement that every karamah of a saint is a miracle for that saint's prophet. Thus, karamat do not blur the distinction but rather confirm the truth of the prophet's message.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Karamat are independent proofs of saintliness that could rival or be confused with prophethood miracles.
Correction: Karamat derive their significance from being miracles of the prophet; they confirm prophethood rather than compete with it.
