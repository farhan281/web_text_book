The Prophet's Report: Certain Yet Inferential Knowledge
Explains why the prophet's report causes certain inferential knowledge through miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the prophet's report yields knowledge that is both certain (قطعي) and inferential (استدلالي), and how miracles underpin this certainty.

Core Idea
A miracle confirms the prophet's truthfulness, making his report certain, but the certainty is inferential.

This lesson unpacks a key argument in ʿilm al-kalām: how do we know that a prophet's message is true? The answer lies in the miracle—a sign from Allah that confirms the prophet's claim. But the knowledge we gain is not immediate; it requires reasoning. Let's see how the author explains this dual character of prophetic knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then its translation. Notice the two claims about the prophet's report: it produces certainty and it is inferential.
The passage is short but dense.
2
Identify the two components
The author first explains why the report gives certainty (the miracle guarantees truthfulness), then why it is inferential (it depends on recalling the miracle). Identify these two parts.
3
Reflect on the implication
Think about why this matters: if prophetic knowledge were necessary (direct), there would be no need for reasoning. But since it is inferential, it invites reflection on the miracle as evidence.
This connects to the broader kalam method of establishing prophethood.

Commentary
This passage provides the argument for why the report of the messenger yields knowledge that is both certain (قطعي) and inferential (استدلالي). First, the certainty is based on the miracle: if Allah manifests a miracle at the hands of a claimant to prophethood as a confirmation, then that person must be truthful in the rulings he conveys. Thus, knowledge of the content of his message is certain. Second, this knowledge is inferential because it depends on reasoning: one must infer that this report comes from someone whose prophethood is proven by miracles, and then conclude that the report is truthful and its content is real. The knowledge obtained is thus inferential, not immediate. The last line identifies 'the knowledge established by it' as knowledge from the messenger's report.

Necessary and Inferential Knowledge from Mutawatir Reports
Objection and answer distinguishing necessary and inferential knowledge from prophetic reports.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between necessary knowledge of a report's attribution to the Prophet and inferential knowledge of its content, and how this resolves an objection against inferential reasoning in theology.

Core Idea
Knowledge of the source is necessary; knowledge of the meaning is inferential.

A student of kalam might wonder: if a report is mutawatir or heard directly from the Prophet ﷺ, isn't the resulting knowledge necessary, not inferential? This would make the proofs of sam'iyyat collapse into necessary knowledge. The following objection-and-answer clarifies that only the report's attribution to the Prophet is known necessarily, while its content and implications require inference. This distinction is crucial for understanding how inferential knowledge operates in theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the passage twice: first to grasp the two rounds of question and answer, then to identify where the distinction between necessary and inferential knowledge is made.
Notice the objector's first point claims the discussion only applies to mutawatir.
2
Identify the two types of knowledge
Pinpoint where the passage defines necessary knowledge (علم ضروري) as knowing that this is the Prophet's report, and inferential knowledge (علم استدلالي) as knowing the content (مضمون) and its implication.
The example of the hadith about the burden of proof illustrates this.
3
Understand the resolution
See how the answer shows that even for mutawatir or directly heard reports, the derived ruling is inferential, not necessary. This preserves the category of inferential reasoning in theology.
This resolves the objector's concern without collapsing categories.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer (قيل...قلنا). The objector argues that the knowledge from the prophet's report is only relevant for mutawatir (recurrent) reports, and thus would belong to the category of necessary knowledge, not inferential. The answer distinguishes between two types of knowledge: First, the knowledge that a particular statement is indeed the report of the Messenger is necessary if it comes through tawatur or direct hearing from his mouth. Second, the knowledge of the content (maddūl) of that report—such as knowing that legal rulings follow from it—is inferential. The objector then presses: if the report is mutawatir or heard directly, the resulting knowledge is necessary like all other necessary knowledge. The reply clarifies that the necessary part is only the recognition that this is the prophet's report; the understanding of its implication requires inference. An example is given: the hadith 'The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the one who denies' is known by tawatur to be the prophet's statement (necessary), but deducing that the burden of proof must be on the claimant is an inferential step.

The Scriptural Argument from Moses' Request for Vision
Proof from Moses' request for vision with objections and answers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the request of Prophet Moses (peace be upon him) to see Allah serves as a proof for the possibility of vision, and how objections to this proof are answered within Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Moses' request shows vision is possible; objections fail on textual and logical grounds.

This lesson examines the second proof for the possibility of seeing Allah: the scriptural argument from Moses' request in the Quran. The argument hinges on the fact that prophets are infallible and would not ask for something impossible. However, there are objections that must be addressed. We will walk through the proof, the strongest objections, and their refutations based on the text.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once alongside the translation to get the overall argument.

Understand the proof
Moses asked to see Allah. Prophets do not ask for the impossible. God conditioned vision on the possible (mountain stability). Therefore, vision is possible.
This is a rational inference from the text and prophetic infallibility.
3
Identify the objection
The strongest objection says Moses asked on behalf of his people to teach them impossibility, and that the condition (stability) is impossible because it would require the mountain to be still while moving.
The objection tries to undermine the proof by reinterpreting the intent and the condition.
4
See the answer
The answer shows the objections go against the apparent meaning. Also, if the people were believers, Moses' word would suffice; if disbelievers, they wouldn't accept it. Stability at movement is possible because stillness could replace movement.
The answer defends the original interpretation and shows the objection is not necessary.
5
Reflect on the lesson
Consider how this proof establishes the possibility of vision through scriptural reasoning, and how theological arguments must handle objections.

Disapproval of a request is not proof of its impossibility.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of prophetic infallibility concerning sins. It begins by noting that prophets are infallible from disbelief (kufr) before and after revelation by consensus. Commentary
This passage discusses the truthfulness and infallibility of prophets. It begins by stating that all prophets are informers and conveyers from God, which is the essence of prophethood. They are truthful and sincere to prevent the purpose of their sending from being nullified. The passage then explains that prophets are infallible (ma'sūmūn) regarding lying, especially in conveying religious laws and rulings. Intentionally lying is impossible by consensus; forgetful lying is also impossible according to most scholars. The infallibility from other sins is detailed: they are protected from disbelief before and after revelation by consensus, and from intentional major sins (kabā'ir) according to the majority, contrary to the Jashwiyya (a deviant sect). The disagreement is whether this prevention is known by scriptural evidence (sam') or reason. Forgetful major sins are permitted by most. Minor sins (saghā'ir) are permitted intentionally by the majority, contrary to al-Jubba'i and his followers, and permitted forgetfully by consensus except those indicating baseness like stealing a morsel. However, the verifiers (muḥaqqiqūn) stipulate that prophets are alerted and then desist from such sins. All this applies after revelation. Before revelation, there is no proof preventing major sins. The Mu'tazila held they are prevented because they would cause aversion and nullify the benefit of sending. The truth is the prevention of what causes aversion, such as the fornication of mothers, licentiousness, and base minor sins. The Shi'a prevented minor and major sins before and after revelation but permitted manifesting disbelief under dissimulation (taqiyya). Commentary
This passage discusses the superiority between humans and angels. The author states the Ahl al-Sunna position: human messengers are superior to angel messengers, angel messengers are superior to ordinary humans, and ordinary humans are superior to ordinary angels. The first two points are agreed upon. Four proofs are given for the superiority of human messengers over angel messengers and ordinary humans over ordinary angels. First proof: God commanded angels to prostrate to Adam, indicating Adam's higher rank (quoting Quran 17:62 and 7:12). Second proof: The verse about teaching all names shows Adam's superior knowledge and honor. Third proof: The verse of election over worlds includes angels, so humans are chosen over them; however, consensus exempts the superiority of ordinary humans over angel messengers, so the verse applies otherwise. Fourth proof: Humans achieve perfection despite obstacles like desire and anger, making their worship harder and more sincere, hence superior.
