Definition of al-Haqq and Its Scope
Taftazani defines al-Haqq and its opposite al-Batil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of al-Haqq as the judgment that corresponds to reality, and its application to statements, beliefs, religions, and schools of thought.

Core Idea
Al-Haqq is defined by correspondence from reality to the judgment.

This lesson begins with the foundational premise of ilm al-kalam: reasoning from the observable world to the Creator. Taftazani then presents the definition of al-Haqq given by Ahl al-Haqq, which serves as the standard for truth in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic text from the beginning to the definition of al-Haqq. Focus on the context of kalam's methodology.
Notice that the definition is given by 'Ahl al-Haqq'.
2
Identify the definition
Find where al-Haqq is defined as 'al-hukm al-mutabiq li al-waqi‘'. Note the key terms: hukm (judgment) and waqi‘ (reality).
3
Consider the scope
The author states that al-Haqq applies to statements, beliefs, religions, and schools of thought insofar as they contain that correspondence.
Reflect on why this broad scope is important for kalam.

Then, since the foundation of ilm al-kalam is based on inferring from the existence of contingent things the existence of the Maker, His oneness, attributes, and acts, and from that to the other scriptural matters (sami‘iyyat), it was appropriate to begin the discourse by alerting to the existence of perceived substances and accidents, and the certainty of knowledge of them, so that through that one may arrive at knowledge of the most important objective. So he said: al-Haqq and what it is. The people of truth (ahl al-haqq) said that it (al-haqq) is the judgment (hukm) that corresponds to reality (al-waqi‘); and it is applied to statements, beliefs, religions, and schools of thought in consideration of their containing that. The opposite of judgment is falsehood (al-batil). As for truthfulness (al-sidq), its usage is prevalent in statements specifically, and its opposite is lying (al-kadhib). Commentary
This passage is an introduction and definition. The author explains that since ilm al-kalam is built upon reasoning from contingent created things (المحدثات) to the existence of the Creator, His attributes, and acts, and then to other scriptural matters (السمعيات), it is fitting to begin with a discussion of the existence of observable substances and accidents, so that knowledge of them serves as a means to gain knowledge of the ultimate goal. Then the author says: 'He said: al-Haqq and what it is.' Here, the commentator quotes the original text (المتن) of the Nasafiyyah: 'قال أهل الحق' (the people of truth said). The definition given is: al-Haqq is the judgment (الحكم) that corresponds to reality (الواقع). It applies broadly to statements, beliefs, religions, and sects insofar as they contain that correspondence. The opposite of 'judgment' here is falsehood (الباطل). Then the author distinguishes al-Haqq from al-Sidq (truthfulness). Sidq is commonly used for statements only, and its opposite is falsehood (الكذب). The distinction is based on the perspective of correspondence: for haqq, correspondence is considered from the side of reality (the reality must match the judgment), while for sidq, correspondence is considered from the side of the judgment (the judgment must match reality). So the meaning of a judgment being sidq is that the judgment corresponds to reality, and the meaning of it being haqq is that reality corresponds to the judgment.

Distinction between al-Haqq and al-Sidq
Taftazani distinguishes between al-Haqq and al-Sidq by direction of correspondence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between al-Haqq and al-Sidq based on the direction of correspondence.

Core Idea
Haqq and Sidq differ in which side of correspondence is considered.

After defining al-Haqq, Taftazani clarifies its relationship with al-Sidq, a term often used interchangeably. He highlights a subtle but important technical difference: in haqq, correspondence is viewed from the side of reality, while in sidq, it is from the side of the judgment.

Guided Path
1
Read the distinction
Read the Arabic text from 'وأما الصدق' to the end. Note the definitions of sidq and its opposite kadhib.
2
Understand the directionality
The author says: 'المطابقة تعتبر فى الحق من جانب الواقع وفى الصدق من جانب الحكم'. This means haqq considers correspondence from reality to judgment, and sidq considers it from judgment to reality.
Think of an example: if a judgment 'the sky is blue' matches reality, it is sidq; if reality matches the judgment, it is haqq? Actually both are equivalent, but the distinction is conceptual.
3
Apply to examples
Consider a statement that is true. It is both haqq and sidq? According to the distinction, it is sidq because the judgment matches reality, and it is haqq because reality matches the judgment. But the direction emphasizes different aspects.
The distinction becomes clearer when considering false statements: a false judgment is kadhib, and it is also batil.

The Realities of Things: Essence and Accidents
Taftazani defines haqiqah, mahiyyah, and related terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of haqiqah, mahiyyah, huwwiyyah, and the distinction between essential and accidental attributes in the context of kalam.

Core Idea
A thing's essence is what makes it what it is; accidents are separable.

Taftazani begins a key discussion on the nature of reality. Before we can talk about God's attributes, we need a clear vocabulary for what a 'thing' is, what its essence consists of, and what merely accidental. This passage lays that foundational terminology, distinguishing between essential and accidental attributes and clarifying that for Ahl al-Sunnah, 'thing' means 'existent'.

Commentary
This passage provides definitions related to the reality of things. It begins with the heading 'القول فى حقائق الأشياء' (the discourse on the realities of things). The author asserts that the realities of things are fixed (ثابتة) and objective. He defines the essence (حقيقة) and quiddity (ماهية) of a thing as that by which the thing is what it is (ما به الشيء هو هو). The author then clarifies the term 'thing' (الشيء) in the school of Ahl al-Sunnah: a thing is that which is existent (الموجود). Finally, he declares that the terms 'al-muthbut' (the established), 'al-tahaqquq' (realization), 'al-wujud' (existence), and 'al-kawn' (being) are synonyms whose meaning is self-evident (بديهي التصور).

Al-Haqq and Al-Sidq: Two Facets of Truth
Defines al-haqq and distinguishes it from al-sidq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of al-haqq and al-sidq, and how they differ in the direction of correspondence with reality.

Core Idea
Truth (haqq) and veracity (sidq) are mirror images: one from reality's side, one from our judgment's side.

In Islamic theology, precision in terminology is crucial. Al-Haqq and al-sidq both relate to truth, but they approach it from opposite directions. This passage from the Sharh al-Aqa'id al-Nasafiyyah clarifies their distinct meanings and shows how they apply to statements, beliefs, and religions.

Guided Path

2

3
Understand the distinction
The passage distinguishes them by the direction of correspondence. For haqq, correspondence is considered from the side of reality; for sidq, from the side of the judgment. Reflect on what this means: a judgment is sidq if it matches reality, but it is haqq if reality matches it.
This subtle difference shows that haqq is more about the inherent truthfulness of reality itself, while sidq is about the accuracy of our statements.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'al-haqq' (truth/reality) as understood by Ahl al-Sunnah. It begins by stating that al-haqq is the judgment (hukm) that corresponds to reality (waqi'). Next, it discusses al-sidq (veracity), which is commonly used for statements, opposite of al-kidhb. The key distinction is made by considering the direction of correspondence: for al-haqq, correspondence is considered from the side of reality (i.e., reality conforms to the judgment?), while for al-sidq, correspondence is considered from the side of the judgment (i.e., the judgment conforms to reality). Thus, a judgment is sidq if it matches reality, and it is haqq if reality matches it. This nuanced distinction refines the understanding of truth in theological contexts.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and polemical argument. It classifies substances (those with self-subsistence) into two types: composite (body) and non-composite (atom). For the body, the author (al-Nasafi) holds that two parts suffice, while some theologians require three parts (for three dimensions) and others require eight (for right-angled intersections). The commentator stresses that this is not a mere verbal dispute about terminology, but a real disagreement about the meaning of 'jism' (body): whether mere composition of two parts is enough for something to be called a body. The first group (al-Nasafi's) argues that if you add one part to a body, people say it is 'ajsam' (more jism), implying that the original body was already a body due to composition. However, the commentator criticizes this reasoning by noting that 'af'al' from 'jisama' means bigness, not body-ness; 'jism' is a noun for the thing itself, not an adjective for magnitude. The commentator notes that the author deliberately avoided the term 'jawharah' (atom) to prevent immediate denial from those who reject atoms.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument distinguishing between ma'rifa (knowledge) and tasdiq (assent) to establish that iman requires volitional assent. The author begins by citing scholarly consensus: the People of the Book knew Muhammad's prophethood with certainty yet were disbelievers because they lacked tasdiq. He then provides a definition from some mashayikh: tasdiq is the heart's binding to the informant's report, an acquired (kasbi) act chosen by the believer, earning reward. In contrast, ma'rifa may occur without choice, like perceiving a wall. Another verifier adds that tasdiq is a voluntary attribution of truthfulness; if it occurs involuntarily, it is not tasdiq but mere knowledge. The author notes a problem: if tasdiq is a psychic quality (kayfiyya nafsaniyya) like knowledge, how can it be voluntary? He resolves this by explaining that the cognitive result of proof — conviction and acceptance of a relation — is the meaning of tasdiq, and one can voluntarily seek its causes. He concludes that such tasdiq cannot occur in obstinate disbelievers; if it did, their disbelief would stem from external denial and persistence, not lack of inner assent.

The author emphasizes that preserving religion is the utmost goal (al-maqsud al-ahamm) and the greatest pillar (al-'umda al-'uzma). Therefore, a ruler must be a proper Imam who upholds religious duties, not merely a strongman.

The Thirty-Year Caliphate and Continuity of Imamate
Answers the objection that after the thirty-year caliphate no Imam exists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author defends the continuity of legitimate Islamic leadership after the thirty-year caliphate by distinguishing between the complete caliphate and the broader category of imamate.

Core Idea
The complete caliphate ends, but imamate continues.

This passage addresses a critical objection: if the true caliphate lasted only thirty years, does that mean later rulers are illegitimate? The author responds by clarifying that 'complete caliphate' is a specific term, while 'imamate' is more general and continues. Commentary
This passage is an objection and answer regarding the duration of the caliphate. The objector notes a previous claim that the caliphate lasts thirty years (a well-known hadith). If so, after the Rashidun caliphs (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali), there would be no Imam, making the entire later Muslim community sinful. The author responds: the previous statement referred to the complete (kamil) caliphate, not all caliphates. Even if we accept that, perhaps after that the role of caliphate ends but not imamate, since 'imam' is more general. However, the author admits this terminology (caliph vs. imam) is not found among the early scholars; only some Shia claim that 'caliph' is more general, saying that the first three caliphs (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman) were caliphs but not imams. Regarding the Abbasids, the matter is problematic (i.e., not clear whether they were proper imams or not).
