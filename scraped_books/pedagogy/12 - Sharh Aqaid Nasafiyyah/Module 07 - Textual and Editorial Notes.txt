Distinguishing Matn from Sharh
Editor clarifies authorship and bracket convention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how to identify the original text (matn) of al-‘Aqa’id al-Nasafiyyah from the commentary (sharh) of al-Taftazani based on the editorial brackets.

Core Idea
Brackets mark the author's words; everything outside is commentary.

Before diving into the creed itself, it is essential to know whose words you are reading. The editorial notes in this edition clearly separate the original matn by Imam al-Nasafi from the sharh by Sa‘d al-Din al-Taftazani. This lesson will help you navigate the text with confidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the editorial notes
Carefully read the Arabic passage that explains the bracket convention. Notice how it identifies the author of the matn and the commentator.
The passage has two separate footnotes.
2
Identify the convention
Focus on the key statement: 'what is between the brackets is his, and what is outside them belongs to the commentator.' Understand that the author's original text is inside the brackets.
The brackets are sometimes called 'خاصرتين' (parentheses).
3
Apply to a sample
Look at the provided example phrase: 'وبالذات مع المنكرين...' Is it inside or outside brackets? That tells you whether it is matn or sharh.
This phrase is outside brackets, so it is part of the commentary.
4
Test your understanding
Use the checkpoint question to confirm you can distinguish matn from sharh based on the bracket rule.

(1) And especially with the deniers of the fundamentals of religion, those who oppose the saved sect from among the righteous believers. (1) This is the beginning of the text of the author, Shaykh Najm al-Din ‘Umar al-Nasafi, author of al-‘Aqa’id al-Nasafiyyah. Commentary
These editorial notes provide essential information about the structure of the book. The first note (appearing on page 19) explains that one phrase—'وبالذات مع المنكرين لأصول الدين المخالفين للفرقة الناجية من صالحي المؤمنين'—is part of the commentary, specifically addressing the deniers of the fundamentals of religion. It then identifies the author of the original text (المتن) as Shaykh Najm al-Din Abu Hafs ‘Umar al-Nasafi. It clarifies a key editorial convention: the text enclosed within brackets (الخاصرتين) in bold black is the author's original wording, while the text outside the brackets is the commentary of the great scholar al-Taftazani. The second note (on page 20) reiterates that the beginning of the text proper (المتن) is by al-Nasafi, and everything outside the brackets or parentheses belongs to the commentator al-Taftazani. Together, these notes guide the reader on how to distinguish between the original creed and the commentary.

The Etymology of Sophistry: Adorned Wisdom
Taftazani explains the etymology of the Greek-derived term 'sophistry'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the etymological roots of the term 'sophistry' (السفسطة) in Islamic scholastic tradition and how it contrasts with philosophy.

Core Idea
Sophistry is 'adorned wisdom' — knowledge that appears truthful but is actually false.

This short but significant passage from Taftazani unpacks the Greek-derived term 'sophistry' (السفسطة). Understanding its etymology helps distinguish genuine philosophical inquiry (falsafa) from deceptive reasoning (safsata). The lesson clarifies the components of this term and its relation to 'philosophy'.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal English translation. Let the author's explanation sink in.
2
Break down the meaning of 'Sufista'
The author splits the Greek compound 'Sufista' into 'Sufa' (knowledge/wisdom) and 'ista' (embellished/error). Notice how this gives the meaning 'adorned wisdom'.
This breakdown is the core of the lesson.
3
Compare with 'philosophy'
Observe the parallel derivation: 'falsafa' comes from 'fila sufa' meaning 'lover of wisdom'. Contrast the two: one is genuine, the other is deceptive imitation.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
وسوفسطا اسم للحكمة المموهة والعلم المزخرف، لأن سوفا معناه العلم والحكمة واسطا معناه المزخرف والغلط. ومنه اشتقت السفسطة، كما اشتقت الفلسفة من فيلا سوفا أي محب الحكمة.
And 'Sufista' is a name for adorned wisdom and embellished knowledge, because 'Sufa' means knowledge and wisdom, and 'ista' means embellished and error. From it is derived 'safsata' (sophistry), just as 'falsafa' (philosophy) is derived from 'fila sufa', i.e., lover of wisdom.
Source pages p. 22

Commentary
This passage is a lexical note explaining the origin of the term 'sophistry' (السفسطة). The author states that the word 'Sufista' refers to 'adorned wisdom' and 'embellished knowledge'. He breaks it down: 'Sufa' means knowledge and wisdom, while 'ista' means embellished and error. From this composite word, the Arabicized term 'safsata' is derived. The author draws a parallel with the derivation of 'falsafa' (philosophy) from 'fila sufa', meaning 'lover of wisdom'. This note clarifies that sophistry is a corrupted form of wisdom.
