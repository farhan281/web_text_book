Commentary
This passage clarifies the semantic distinctions among the terms for divine action. The author begins by noting that takhliq, insha', fi'l, and san' are sometimes considered synonymous, meaning bringing something into existence after non-existence. Finally, tarziq is the creation of provision (rizq) for a thing and making it its sustenance. Thus each term has a nuance: ibda' emphasizes novelty, takhliq is general creation, insha' is first origination, fi'l is general transitive action, san' is skilled making, and tarziq is provision.

Multiple Meanings of 'Quran' and the Prohibition on Absolute Statements
Explains the multiple meanings of 'Quran' and the prohibition of absolute statements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different meanings of the term 'Quran' and why it is impermissible to say 'the Quran is created' without qualification.

Core Idea
The word 'Quran' is ambiguous—it can refer to created things or the eternal attribute, so absolute statements are dangerous.

The term 'Quran' carries multiple meanings in Islamic theology. While in one sense it refers to the created recitation or physical mushaf, it primarily denotes Allah's eternal speech. The author explains these nuances and warns against making absolute statements about the Quran being created, as ambiguity can lead to disbelief.

Guided Path

Understand the problem

This ruling protects faith from the implication that Allah's speech is temporal.

The Doctrine of Kasb and the Two-Agents Objection
Explanation of kasb and response to the two-agents objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the concept of kasb (acquisition) resolves the apparent conflict between divine creation and human free will, and how the objection of two independent agents on one effect is answered.

Among what should be pointed out here is that firasa (spiritual insight) is of three types: (1) Imani firasa (insight of faith): its cause is a light that God Almighty casts into the heart of His servant, and its reality is that it is a thought of the heart that pounces upon him like the pouncing of a lion upon its prey—it is from this that [the word] is derived. This firasa is according to the strength of faith: whoever is stronger in faith is sharper in firasa. Abu Sulayman al-Darani, may God have mercy on him, said: 'Firasa is the unveiling of the soul and the witnessing of the unseen, and it is one of the stations of faith.' End quote. (2) Riyadi firasa (ascetic insight): it is acquired through hunger, sleeplessness, and solitude. When the soul is stripped of hindrances and attachments to creatures, it obtains firasa and unveiling according to its degree of detachment. This firasa is shared between the believer and the disbeliever; it does not indicate faith or sainthood, nor does it unveil beneficial truth or a straight path. Rather, its unveiling is of the same kind as the firasa of rulers, dream interpreters, physicians, and the like. (3) Khulqi firasa (physiognomic insight): it is that about which physicians and others have written, inferring character from physical constitution due to the connection between them that God’s wisdoms require—such as inferring smallness of intellect from a head that is abnormally small, greatness of intellect from its largeness, generosity of character from breadth of chest, its narrowness from narrowness of chest, and dullness of the person and weakness of his heart's heat from fixed eyes and their dull gaze, and so on.

Commentary
This passage classifies firasa (spiritual insight) into three types. The author begins by stating that firasa is of three kinds. First, imani firasa (faith-based insight): its cause is divine light, its reality is a sudden thought, and it is proportional to one's faith. He supports this with a quote from Abu Sulayman al-Darani defining firasa as unveiling and witnessing the unseen. Second, riyadi firasa (ascetic insight): it is acquired through ascetic practices and is shared by believers and unbelievers, so it is not a sign of faith or sainthood. Third, khulqi firasa (physiognomic insight): it is based on physical traits and is used by physicians to infer character. The passage thus distinguishes what is truly spiritual from what is merely natural or acquired.
