Refutation of the Murji'ah on Hell's Punishment
Refutes the Murji'ah claim that Hell's inhabitants will not be punished.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Murji'ah claim that Hell's inhabitants are without punishment, why it is false according to Quran and Sunnah, and the correct interpretation of the hadith about Hell becoming empty.

Core Idea
Hell's punishment is real and eternal for disbelievers; emptiness hadith refers only to the level for sinful believers.

This lesson examines a controversial Murji'ah belief that Hell's inhabitants will not experience torment. The author refutes this using clear Quranic verses and addresses a problematic hadith that might seem to support it. By the end, you'll see how Islamic orthodoxy affirms eternal punishment for disbelievers.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Murji'ah position
Read the opening description of what the Murji'ah claim: that people in Hell are like fish in water, without pain or pleasure. Only believers enjoy Paradise, but disbelievers have no enjoyment in Hell.
This is an extreme view that denies any punishment in Hell.
2
Examine the Quranic refutation
Look at the four Quranic verses cited. Each one describes suffering: crying out, skin burned, punishment not lightened, and increase in punishment. These are decisive proofs against the Murji'ah claim.
The author says the Murji'ah belief is false by Book, Sunnah, and consensus.
3
Interpret the problematic hadith
The Jahmiyyah cite a hadith that Hell will one day be empty. The author explains that even if authentic, it refers only to the level for sinful Muslims who are purified and then enter Paradise, not to the eternal Hell for disbelievers.
This resolves the apparent contradiction without denying eternal punishment.

Tarawih is Sunnah, Not Innovation
Explains sunnah status of tarawih and interprets Umar's 'innovation'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that tarawih prayer is a confirmed sunnah, and why Umar's description of it as an 'excellent innovation' does not make it a religious innovation.

Core Idea
Tarawih is sunnah; Umar's 'innovation' refers to reviving the sunnah, not creating a new act.

This passage from the sharh clarifies the status of tarawih prayer in Ramadan. The author asserts its sunnah origin based on the Prophet's practice, and addresses the common misconception arising from Umar's famous statement. The lesson will explore the reasoning and the evidence used.

Look for the hadith about following Abu Bakr and Umar.

Commentary

The author then addresses Umar's famous statement calling tarawih an 'excellent innovation' (ni'mat al-bid'ah). He explains that Umar meant it as a good innovation in the sense of reviving it after it had been abandoned in congregation, or gathering the people for it. The author points out that this is not a bid'ah in the religious sense; rather, it is in accordance with the Prophet's command to follow the sunnah of the Rightly Guided Caliphs. The Prophet specifically instructed to follow Abu Bakr and Umar. Therefore, the tarawih prayer as performed in congregation is a revival of the sunnah, not an innovation. The author concludes that this argument refutes the Rawafid (Shi'a) who reject tarawih.

Affirms the order of superiority among the four caliphs with Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective

Imam Abu Hanifa's treatise 'al-Wasiyyah' contains a clear statement on the ranking of the four caliphs. This passage not only establishes the order but also roots it in a Quranic verse about the 'foremost.' Interpreting Imam Abu Hanifa's Statements on Aisha
Interpretation of Imam Abu Hanifa's statements on Aisha's superiority and the simile of 'child of adultery'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the interpretive nuances in Imam Abu Hanifa's statements about Aisha's superiority and the phrase 'child of adultery' as an extreme simile.

Core Idea
Imam Abu Hanifa's words are open to interpretation; 'child of adultery' is a rhetorical simile.

This lesson delves into two points from Imam Abu Hanifa's al-Wasiyya that require careful interpretation. First, his claim that Aisha is the best of women—does it include Khadija, Fatima, and Maryam? Second, his statement that a slanderer is a 'child of adultery'—which is explained as a powerful simile meaning he is the worst of three. Understanding these nuances prevents misunderstanding of his teachings.

This shows that Imam Abu Hanifa's words should be understood figuratively.
3
Understand the rhetorical device

The Nuanced Distinction Between Polytheists and People of the Book
Author explains the subtle distinction that Jews and Christians are not considered polytheists despite their creeds, due to Quranic text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Jews and Christians are legally distinguished from polytheists despite their beliefs, based on Quranic text overruling rational principle.

Core Idea
Revelation can override rational judgment in theological rulings.

This passage tackles a subtle but important legal distinction: why are Jews and Christians not considered polytheists even though their creeds include elements of shirk? The author explains that pure reason (qadiyyat al-‘aql) would classify them as polytheists, but the Quran itself refers to them as 'People of the Book' and distinguishes them from polytheists (mushrikin). Therefore, we submit to the revealed text. This lesson unpacks that subtle distinction.

Guided Path

Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the author's argumentative flow: he first states the rational principle, then presents the exception.
Pay attention to the phrase 'gadiyyat al-‘aql' (rational principle).
2

Look for the sentence that defines what reason alone would conclude about anyone who worships other than Allah. The author says such a person is a polytheist (mushrik) definitively.
3

I say, and with Allah is success: Here there is a subtle distinction, which is that the rational principle is the absolute dichotomy between [believing in] the Book and worshipping other than Allah, Exalted and Majestic, for that is truly polytheism (shirk), while the People of the Book (kitabi) are not considered polytheists (mushrik) according to the Shari‘ah. So everyone we see worshipping other than the Truth, may He be glorified, we rule that he is a polytheist definitively, even if he acknowledges scriptures and prophets, peace be upon them. But we have deviated from this rational principle in the case of Jews and Christians by virtue of the revealed text (nass). For we find that the Mighty Quran narrates about them evil beliefs but then rules that they are 'People of the Book' and distinguishes them from the polytheists. So submission is obligatory due to the presence of the text. As for the Sabians, since no such text has come regarding them, it is not permissible to analogize them to these or to abandon the rational principle in their case.

Commentary
This passage is a juridical-theological clarification regarding the status of Jews and Christians compared to polytheists. The author identifies a 'subtle distinction' (farq daqiq). According to pure reason (qadiyyat al-‘aql), anyone who worships other than Allah is a polytheist (mushrik), regardless of their belief in scriptures. However, the Shari‘ah does not treat Jews and Christians as polytheists — they are called 'People of the Book' (Ahl al-Kitab) in the Quran, and are distinguished from polytheists (mushrikin). For the Sabians, no such Quranic text exists to remove them from the rational ruling, so they remain polytheists by default, and it is not permissible to analogize them to Jews and Christians.
