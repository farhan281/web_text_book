Two Classical Definitions of Ilm al-Kalam
Two scholars define Ilm al-Kalam and its method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two classical definitions of Ilm al-Kalam given by al-Taftazani and al-Iji, and how they complement each other.

Core Idea
Ilm al-Kalam is both knowledge of beliefs from proofs and the power to defend them.

This passage presents two foundational definitions of Ilm al-Kalam from two of its greatest scholars. Al-Taftazani defines it as knowledge of religious beliefs based on certain proofs, while al-Iji emphasizes the capability it gives to establish those beliefs by presenting proofs and repelling doubts. Together, they show that kalam is both a body of certain knowledge and a practical skill of argumentation.

3
Identify the key terms

4
Reflect on the complementarity
Ask yourself: Why might both definitions be needed? How does knowledge of beliefs differ from the ability to prove them?
This connects to the learning objective.

Before diving into specific arguments, Sanusi first clarifies the scope of kalam itself. He defines its subject matter as 'the known thing' in relation to religious beliefs. This seemingly simple definition has profound implications: it means that kalam is not limited to God's essence but also studies creation, because everything in creation points back to the Creator.

The Subject Matter of Ilm al-Kalam: Al-Ma'lum
Sanusi defines the subject of kalam as the known in its relation to establishing beliefs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the subject of kalam is defined as 'the known' (al-ma'lum) and how it encompasses both divine attributes and contingent realities in service of establishing Islamic beliefs.

Core Idea
Kalam’s subject is everything known, but only in its role as evidence for belief.

Before we can truly engage in kalam, we must know what it studies. The author gives a precise yet comprehensive definition: the subject of kalam is 'the known' — but not in an absolute sense. Rather, it is the known insofar as it can help us establish religious beliefs. This passage explains that definition and shows how kalam investigates both God’s attributes and the properties of created things, always with the goal of proving creedal truths.

Explanation of al-Fiqh al-Akbar. Ilm al-kalam. First: Its importance with regard to the person in his theoretical faculty, which is ascent from the abyss of taqlid (blind imitation) to the zenith of yaqin (certainty). He says, Most High: 'Allah will raise those who have believed among you and those who were given knowledge, by degrees' [al-Mujadila: 11]. As for his doctrinal faculty, its goal is to adorn iman (faith) with certainty, so that faith and belief in the legal rulings become firmly certain, not shaken by the doubts of falsifiers. Perhaps fortifying the Muslim's beliefs with this science is of utmost importance, especially in this difficult time in which deviations and false calls abound, because the scientific strength of the Islamic creed and the rest of the religious sciences has a great effect on people's adherence to their religion and their steadfastness upon it. Second: Its importance with regard to completing others. It is guiding the seekers by clarifying the proof for them to the beliefs of the religion, and compelling the obstinate by establishing the proof against them. This is what Hujjat al-Islam al-Ghazali expressed by his saying: 'The basis of that is that removing doubts concerning the fundamentals of beliefs is obligatory, and the occurrence of doubt is not impossible. Then calling to the truth by proof is important in the religion, and that is not possible except by this science. So there must be those who undertake this science, who resist the callers of the innovators, and attract the one who inclines away from the truth, and purify the hearts of Ahl al-Sunna from the accidents of doubt.' Third: Its importance with respect to the foundations of Islam, which is preserving the rules of the religion from being shaken by the doubts of falsifiers. The basis of that is that no time is free from the existence of an innovator or an obstinate one who sets out to misguide the people of truth by pouring doubts upon them. So there must be someone who resists his doubt by uncovering it and his misguidance by condemning it, and that is not possible except by this science. Sayings of the scholars concerning the legality of ilm al-kalam. The goal of this science in its origin goes back to the existence of a need that requires occupying oneself with the beliefs of faith from this aspect. There is no doubt that this need is not free from being either a religious need, a da'wa need, or a scientific need; and Islam in its reality and what branches from it is based on these three pillars. Knowledge is the origin, faith is necessary from it and follows upon it, and da'wa and what is necessary from it branch from both of them. Therefore, the call of the prophets was coupled with proofs indicating their truthfulness, which are miracles, and indicating the truth of what they say from the evidences and proofs that are based on consideration, thought, and reflection on the secrets of the universe and the wonders of creation, along with argumentation and refuting opponents. Hence, the Noble Qur'an was filled with verses that carry within them proofs that cannot be counted and hardly ever end, and whose pivot is establishing proofs for that. And the Noble Qur'an narrates to us many of the arguments that took place between the messengers, peace be upon them, and their peoples, and what they had of strong argumentation and refutation of opponents. Rather, Allah Most High has urged us to consider in many verses and desired it for us, and condemned taqlid and those who imitate. This is besides the fact that the Qur'an is almost from its beginning to its end an argument with the disbelievers and a refutation of idol worshippers and atheists, and an explanation of tawhid (monotheism) and prophethood. And if we exclude the verses concerning legal rulings, which are less than six hundred verses, we find that the entire Qur'an is about tawhid, prophethood, establishing all chapters of beliefs, arguing against the disbelievers and polytheists, and indicating ways to compel the obstinate, even the verses concerning stories are intended for knowing the wisdom and power of Allah Most High and the necessity of submission to the authority of His divinity. Hence, the need to establish Islamic beliefs by presenting proofs and repelling the doubts and objections of the obstinate is an obligatory matter, in accordance with the nature of Islam and its principles and realizing its goals, as you have already known. And it is known that ilm al-kalam in this sense has existed since the time of the Messenger, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, and the Companions, may Allah be pleased with them. For this reason, Imam al-Taftazani stated that the knowledge of the Companions concerning beliefs is part of ilm al-kalam, where he said: 'And the knowledge of the Companions enters into that, for it is kalam, even if it was not called by this name at that time, just as their knowledge of practical matters is fiqh, even if this codification and arrangement did not exist.' But the difference between the era of the Companions and those after them is, in summary, the appearance of sects that disturbed the believers' beliefs, corrupted their unity, and stirred up conflict among them, which made the need to pay attention to beliefs from this aspect more urgent. Thus, the call to establish the true beliefs and to refute those who oppose them became a necessary matter, to protect Islam and the beliefs of the believers.

Commentary
This passage presents the importance and legitimacy of ilm al-kalam (Islamic theology). The author begins by stating a title, then lists three aspects of importance. First, for the individual: it elevates a person from blind imitation (taqlid) to certainty (yaqin), citing the Quranic verse on the elevation of those who have faith and knowledge. It strengthens faith against doubts. Second, for guiding others: it involves guiding seekers and compelling obstinate opponents through proof, quoting al-Ghazali that removing doubts is obligatory and requires this science. Third, for preserving the foundations of Islam: it protects religious principles from being shaken by the doubts of innovators and obstinate people, which inevitably appear in every age. Then the author transitions to a discussion of the legality of this science, arguing that the goal of ilm al-kalam is to meet a need—whether religious, da'wa, or scientific—and that Islam is built on knowledge, faith, and da'wa. The prophets used proofs and arguments, and the Quran is full of argumentation and calls to reflection, condemning taqlid. The author concludes that ilm al-kalam existed since the Prophet's time, as al-Taftazani noted, and that the later appearance of sects made it even more necessary.

Thus, the call to establish the true beliefs and to refute those who oppose them became a necessary matter, to protect Islam and the beliefs of the believers.

Then he further clarifies: whatever Allah brings into existence without coupling His power with the servant's power and will is an attribute of Allah and not an act of the servant, like involuntary trembling. Whatever He brings into existence while coupling His power and the servant's choice is described as an attribute of Allah, an act of the servant, and an acquisition for the servant, like voluntary movements. Finally, he mentions generated effects (mutawallidat) such as pain in a struck person or breakage of glass: these are creation by Allah, but the Mu'tazila attribute them to the servant's creation.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation. It then explains 'they differ' (yatafāwatūn) as referring to believers differing in matters below Iman, i.e., in actions other than the core belief and affirmation, such as performing the pillars of Islam (with variation among the pious) and degrees of disobedience (among the wicked). The Rule for Uncertainty in Tawhid
The author states the ruling for when a subtle point of tawhid is unclear.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct approach when encountering unclear points in tawhid: to affirm the truth in a general way while seeking knowledge, rather than suspending belief or falling into doubt.

Core Idea
When tawhid is unclear, affirm the truth briefly and seek a scholar.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario every student of faith may face: encountering a subtle theological point that is not fully understood. The author provides a clear ruling that balances intellectual honesty with steadfast faith. Instead of suspending belief or hastily rejecting the point, one should immediately affirm the correct truth in a general sense, until a knowledgeable teacher can provide clarity. This ruling protects the believer from doubt while encouraging the pursuit of deeper knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation together. Notice the structure: first a general rule, then an explanatory gloss that clarifies each term.
Pay attention to the repetition and expansion in the gloss.
2
Identify key elements

Understand the obligation
The gloss clarifies that 'should' (yanbaghi) actually means 'obligatory' (yajib). This is not a mere recommendation but a duty.
4
Grasp the 'general belief'
The phrase 'what is correct according to Allah' is to be believed in a general way (ijmalan), without necessarily having detailed understanding.
This preserves correct faith while acknowledging one's current ignorance.
5
Reflect on the wisdom
Why is this ruling important? It prevents doubt and suspension of faith, while still motivating the seeker to find a scholar. It shows the balance between certainty and inquiry.
This connects to the broader methodology of Ahl al-Sunna in dealing with ambiguous matters.

Commentary
This passage gives a practical ruling for a believer who encounters a subtle point in tawhid that is unclear. The ruling is not merely recommended but obligatory (yajibu 'alayhi). The belief should be general (ijmalan), not necessarily detailed. This preserves correct belief while awaiting proper instruction. The passage guards against the danger of abandoning belief or falling into doubt when confronted with complex theological questions.
