Proof That God Has No Likeness
A proof from necessary existence that God cannot have a likeness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof from necessary existence that God cannot have a likeness, and why this excludes both anthropomorphism and figurative interpretation.

Core Idea
God's necessary existence logically excludes any likeness.

This lesson examines a concise yet powerful rational argument that 'there is nothing like Him' is not just a claim but a necessary conclusion from God's attributes. The author shows that if God is Necessary of Existence, then having a like would lead to absurdity, and that divine attributes are inherently unshareable.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow of the argument. Then read the literal translation to clarify each phrase.
Notice the two distinct formulations: the first from necessary existence, the second from unique attributes.
2
Trace the two lines of reasoning
Identify the two proofs: (1) If God had a like, that like would also be necessary, leading to a regress and the impossibility of God's non-existence. (2) Divine attributes cannot be shared, so a like would share them, which is impossible.
Both proofs end with the conclusion that God has no like.
3
Understand the doctrinal conclusion
The author ends by stating that this establishes no addition (tashbih) and no interpretation (ta'wil) — the literal negation stands. Reflect on why this settles the matter.
This connects to the broader topic of divine transcendence (tanzih).

Commentary
This passage is a proof of divine transcendence (tanzih). It begins by citing the Qur'anic verse 'There is nothing like Him' and states that this is a claim with evidence. The evidence is that God is wajib al-wujud (necessary of existence), meaning His non-existence is impossible. If He had a like (mithl), then that like would also be necessary (since it would share the attribute of necessity), but then it would be a like of His like, which is a regress. Since there is no like of the divine like, God cannot have a like. Otherwise, it would imply the non-existence of the Necessary Being, which is absurd. Another formulation: divine attributes are such that the intellect does not allow sharing them; if God had a like, it would share those attributes, but that is impossible. Therefore, there is neither anthropomorphism (tashbih) nor figurative interpretation (ta'wil) needed—the literal negation of likeness stands.

Necessary existence implies absolute uniqueness.

This lesson presents a rational argument grounded in the concept of necessary existence (wajib al-wujud) to demonstrate that God has no peer. The proof is not merely scriptural but logical, starting from the nature of God's existence and attributes. As you read, notice how the argument unfolds in two complementary formulations.

Commentary
This passage presents a rational proof for God's absolute uniqueness (tawhid). It begins by referencing the Qur'anic verse 'There is nothing like unto Him' (42:11), treating it as a claim supported by rational evidence. The proof proceeds as follows: God is necessarily existent (wajib al-wujud), meaning His non-existence is impossible. If God had a peer (mithl), then that peer would necessarily be exactly like Him. But if the peer were like Him, then the peer would itself have no peer—a contradiction because then God would be the peer of something that has no peer, implying God is not unique. More directly: God's attributes are such that reason cannot conceive of them being shared by two beings. If He had a peer, that peer would share these attributes. But then the peer would be like God, and God would be like the peer—yet God's attributes are unique and cannot be shared. Therefore, the notion of a peer is absurd. Hence, no literal interpretation (ta'wil) or addition is needed; the verse stands as is. The argument is drawn from the source 'Al-Mu'tamad al-Mustanad'.

The Preserved Tablet and Erasure and Confirmation
Reconciles preservation of the Tablet with erasure and confirmation

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Tablet itself is eternally preserved; erasure happens on its covers.

This passage tackles a subtle theological point: if the Preserved Tablet is unchangeable, how can Allah erase and confirm decrees? The answer lies in distinguishing the Tablet's essence from its two covers, where change occurs. Let's explore the proof from Ibn Abbas.

Commentary
This passage addresses a theological issue concerning the Preserved Tablet (al-lawh al-mahfuz) and the divine actions of erasure and confirmation (al-mahw wa al-ithbat) mentioned in the Quran (Surah al-Ra'd 13:39). The author begins by stating a commonly held view: the Tablet itself is preserved, and the erasure and confirmation occur only in the scrolls of the angels. However, he notes that some reports indicate that confirmation also occurs in the Tablet itself. To reconcile these, he presents a narration from Ibn Jarir al-Tabari's Tafsir, quoting Ibn Abbas. According to Ibn Abbas, the Preserved Tablet is a huge white pearl, and it has two covers (daftan) which are themselves tablets. Allah performs acts of erasure and confirmation three hundred and sixty times each day on these covers, while the essence of the Tablet remains unchanged. The Mother of the Book (umm al-kitab) is the source that is never altered. Thus, the author concludes: the Tablet itself (nafs al-lawh) is preserved (mahfuz), and the erasure and confirmation occur in its two covers. The passage ends with 'And Allah knows best.'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah's oneness is not numerical but absolute uniqueness with no partner in essence or attributes.

Core Idea
Allah's oneness is about being absolutely unique, not being first in a series.

The Great Imam begins by clarifying a common misunderstanding: when we say 'Allah is One', we do not mean He is the first of many. Rather, His oneness is unique—He has no partner, no equal, and no likeness. This lesson unpacks that foundational distinction.

Commentary
This passage is a theological definition and explanation of the term 'thing' (shay') as applied to Allah, based on the text of Fiqh al-Akbar. It begins by stating the core definition: the meaning of 'thing' (when referring to Allah) is the affirmation of His existence while negating any association with body, substance, accident, limit, opposite, or peer. This is the foundational negation of all creaturely attributes.

The commentary then provides a linguistic analysis: the word 'thing' in origin is a verbal noun (masdar), but it is sometimes used in the sense of the passive object (maf'ul) – as in the Quranic verse 'Allah over all things is All-Powerful' – and in that sense it cannot be applied to Allah because it implies objecthood and limitation. However, it is also used in the sense of the active agent (fa'il), as in 'Which thing is greatest in testimony?', and in that sense it may be applied to Allah. Furthermore, the word can refer to absolute existence, but a distinction is made between the Necessary Existent (Allah) and possible existents; thus applying the term to Allah is more appropriate than to others.

Next, the passage expands the negation of physical attributes: Allah is not a body (jism), which is composite and occupies space – signs of temporality (huduth). He is not a substance (jawhar), which is an indivisible part of a body and occupies space. He is not an accident ('arad), which subsists in another and is transitory, such as colors, modes, tastes, and smells. The passage summarizes that the created world consists of entities (a'yan) and accidents: entities are self-subsisting (either composite bodies or indivisible substances), and Allah is transcendent above all of that.

A quote from al-Razi is given to emphasize that an anthropomorphist (mujassim) does not truly worship Allah but rather a mental image. An anecdote about Abu Hanifa is cited: when asked about discoursing on accidents and bodies, he cursed Amr ibn Ubayd for opening that door (implying that such speculative theology is blameworthy when it leads to anthropomorphism).

Finally, the passage reaffirms that Allah has no limit (hadd), no opposite (didd), no peer (nidd), and no like (mithl). The verse prohibiting rivals (andad) is used as proof. This establishes a complete negation of any comparison or similarity between Allah and created things.

The commentator then presents the famous classification of Imam al-Shafi'i, which divides people into three categories regarding tawhid: the anthropomorphist (mushabbih), the negator (mu'attil), and the true monotheist (muwahhid) who acknowledges his inability to fully perceive Allah. Returning to the main statement, the passage elaborates on human incapacity to maintain constant remembrance and gratitude, citing the verse about counting blessings. It then discusses the abrogation of the verse 'Fear Allah as He should be feared' by 'Fear Allah as much as you are able,' citing the Prophet's explanation of true taqwa. The key point is that while ma'rifa (knowledge) is continuous, worship demands perpetual attention which humans cannot sustain, leading to moments of forgetfulness—which is considered kufr by spiritual masters but excused for the general public by the Shari'ah as a mercy. Allah's Nearness and Distance: Beyond Space and Honor
Imam Abu Hanifa defines qurb and bu'd as non-spatial and without quality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah's attributes of nearness and distance are not spatial or based on honor/disgrace, but are affirmed without modality (bila kayf) as part of the ambiguous verses.

Core Idea
Allah's nearness is not spatial or about honor—it is without how.

Imam Abu Hanifa begins by clarifying a subtle point about divine attributes that might otherwise be misunderstood. When we hear 'Allah is near' or 'far', we might think of physical distance or social standing. But the Imam teaches us that these attributes belong to the category of the ambiguous (mutashabihat), and must be affirmed without asking how. This lesson unpacks his definition and the commentator's explanation.

Resolving Contradictions: The Correct View on Divine Nearness
Critique of contradictory interpretation and resolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct Islamic view that divine nearness (qurb) is an attribute without how (bila kayf), and how the commentator refutes a contradictory interpretation.

Core Idea
Divine nearness is described without how, not literal or merely metaphorical.

In this continuation, the commentator critiques an earlier scholar who made contradictory statements about nearness and distance: first applying them literally to creation, then metaphorically as honor/disdain, then citing a verse that seems to affirm real proximity for God. The commentator resolves this by presenting the chosen view of the Imam: both nearnesses (God to creation and creation to God) are attributes without how (bila kayf). He also notes the majority's metaphorical interpretation and the mystics' spiritual understanding. This lesson focuses on the reconciliation and the correct doctrine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criticized commentator's position

The commentator points out the contradiction: if literal for creation, then metaphorical for God, but then the verse implies literal for God.
2
Understand the contradiction
The criticized commentator's statements are internally inconsistent: he shifts between literal and metaphorical, and the verse citation seems to affirm real proximity for God.
The author says 'the relation of proximity and distance is equal between Lord and servant'—if taken literally, it would imply both are subject to spatial relations.
3
Learn the correct view (mukhtar al-Imam)
The commentator states: 'the chosen view of the Imam is that the proximity of the Truth to creation and the proximity of creation to the Truth is a description without how and an attribute without unveiling.'
This is the central thesis: both are affirmed but without modality.
4
Consider the majority and mystical interpretations
The majority (jumhur) interpret them metaphorically as nearness of mercy/distance of favor. The mystics (ashab al-isharat) define nearness as witnessing God's grace and being absent from self.
These are secondary interpretations that preserve transcendence while explaining the terms.
