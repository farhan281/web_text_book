Commentary
This passage is an explanation of how Moses heard the speech of Allah. It begins by quoting the verse 'And Allah spoke to Moses with [direct] speech' (4:164), noting that the emphatic verbal noun 'taklīman' is used to confirm that the speech is literal, not metaphorical. Also, he might have heard it from inside the fire or through Jibrīl or other angels. However, the author of this commentary (the one writing the current text) points out that the last two possibilities are questionable because they would not confer any special distinction (khuṣūṣiyya) or excellence (maziyya) upon Moses over other prophets. As for the earlier notion (of hearing from the cloud/veil), perhaps the speech occurred to Moses on multiple occasions and in different states. But the first instance of revelation to Moses was when he was called from the blessed tree (the burning bush) that he thought was a fire. In reality, that tree was a source of spiritual lights and secrets.

The concept of prophetic infallibility (ʿiṣmah) is central to Islamic theology, but how does it actually work? Scholars have debated whether God protects prophets by completely overriding their free will or by guiding them while leaving choice intact. In this passage, al-Qunawi reports two contrasting views, including the position favored by Imam al-Maturidi.

Commentary
This passage addresses the Ascension (Mi'raj) of the Prophet. It begins by affirming that the report is true and that rejecting it makes one 'astray and an innovator'. The author clarifies that the Ascension was bodily and awake. He then cites 'al-Khulasah' to distinguish between the Night Journey (Isra) from Mecca to Jerusalem, which is established by a definitive Quranic verse, and the Ascension from Jerusalem to heaven, which is established by the Sunnah which is speculative in transmission and interpretation. Hence, denying the Isra is disbelief, but denying the Ascension does not necessarily entail disbelief. The author notes that he himself wrote a treatise on the matter. Then he critiques the commentary of 'al-Aqa'id' on Aisha's statement that the Prophet's body was not absent. The commentator claimed it meant the soul never left the body, but the author says this is far-fetched. The correct interpretation is that the Ascension occurred in Mecca early in the prophethood, before Aisha was born (or before she was married?), or that there were multiple ascensions, explaining differing reports about the endpoint. The author concludes by rejecting Ibn al-Qayyim's objection that the prayer obligation would need to be decreed multiple times.
