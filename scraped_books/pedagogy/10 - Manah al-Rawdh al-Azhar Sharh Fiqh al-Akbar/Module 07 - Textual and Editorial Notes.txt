Defending the Authorship of al-Fiqh al-Akbar
Defends the attribution of al-Fiqh al-Akbar to Imam Abu Hanifa with citations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the scholarly evidence that establishes the attribution of al-Fiqh al-Akbar to Imam Abu Hanifa and the refutation of opposing claims.

Core Idea
Al-Fiqh al-Akbar is authentically Imam Abu Hanifa's foundational creedal work, defended by a chain of scholars.

This passage is a robust defense of the authorship of one of the earliest Sunni creedal texts. The author presents a chain of authorities, from early scholars to later commentators, to affirm that al-Fiqh al-Akbar was written and dictated by Imam Abu Hanifa himself. You will see how the author counters doubts raised by the Mu'tazila and explains the significance of the book's title.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Read the opening sentence which states the disagreement and the correct view: that al-Fiqh al-Akbar is the composition and dictation of Imam Abu Hanifa.
2
Examine the early authorities
Look at the citations from al-Baghdadi, al-Isfara'ini, al-Zabidi, and Ibn al-Nadim. Each affirms that Abu Hanifa authored a book in kalam called al-Fiqh al-Akbar.
Note how the author uses these to build a strong chain.
3
Understand the Mu'tazila objection
Read al-Kardari's refutation of the Mu'tazila claim that Abu Hanifa had no kalam works. Understand their motive: to deny that the Sunni content of al-Fiqh al-Akbar could be from him.
The author calls this claim a 'clear error'.
4
Appreciate the cumulative evidence
Review the later confirmations from Shams al-Milla, the author of Miftah al-Sa'ada, and Ibn Abi al-Izz. Notice how they explain the name 'al-Fiqh al-Akbar' and reaffirm the attribution.
The passage ends with a direct quote from the book itself.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained defense of the attribution of the book al-Fiqh al-Akbar to Imam Abu Hanifa. The author begins by stating that there is disagreement on this point, but the correct view is that it is indeed the composition and dictation of Abu Hanifa. He then marshals a series of authoritative citations to support this position. First, he quotes Imam Abd al-Qahir al-Baghdadi, who asserts that Abu Hanifa was among the first theologians among jurists and wrote a book titled al-Fiqh al-Akbar in refutation of the Qadariyya. Next, he cites Imam Abu al-Muzaffar al-Isfara'ini, who confirms the book's authenticity through a reliable chain of transmission. Al-Zabidi is then referenced from al-Tabsira al-Baghdadiyya, again affirming Abu Hanifa as the first theologian of Ahl al-Sunna among jurists and that he wrote al-Fiqh al-Akbar. Ibn al-Nadim is quoted listing among Abu Hanifa's works: al-Fiqh al-Akbar, a letter to al-Batti, the book al-Alim wa al-Muta'allim, and the book al-Radd ala al-Qadariyya. The author then presents the argument of al-Kardari, who refutes the Mu'tazila claim that Abu Hanifa had no kalam works. The Mu'tazila allegedly made this claim to deny that the content of al-Fiqh al-Akbar and al-Alim wa al-Muta'allim—which contain Sunni principles—could be from him, and instead asserted that these books were by another Abu Hanifa (al-Bukhari) and that the Imam himself was a Mu'tazili. Al-Kardari calls this a clear error, because the views in these epistles match the theological school of Abu Hanifa known through other sources, and there is no evidence against their authenticity. He further notes that many shaykhs, including Shams al-Milla wa al-Din al-Barr al-Taqi al-Umadi, have concurred on these two books being by Abu Hanifa. The author then adds the testimony of the author of Miftah al-Sa'ada wa Misbah al-Siyada, who calls the denial of authorship a Mu'tazili fabrication. Next, Ibn Abi al-Izz is quoted to explain why the book is named al-Fiqh al-Akbar (the Greater Jurisprudence): because it deals with the noble science of usul al-din, as opposed to fiqh al-furu' (branches). Finally, the author lists further statements of support from various scholars, each affirming that al-Fiqh al-Akbar is by Abu Hanifa, and includes a Persian/Urdu citation from the book itself, followed by a direct quote from the opening of al-Fiqh al-Akbar: 'The foundation of tawhid and what correct belief is based upon.' The overall argument is cumulative and emphatic, using multiple authorities to rebut any doubt about the book's attribution.

Reconciling Obligation and Punishment Before Revelation
A reconciliation that obligation does not necessitate punishment, thereby harmonizing with Quran 17:15.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ash'ari scholars reconcile the concept of obligation (wujub) before revelation with the Quranic principle that punishment requires a revealed messenger.

Core Idea
Obligation does not automatically imply punishment; divine mercy precedes obligation.

In Ash'ari theology, there is a tension between the idea that humans can be obligated by reason and the Quranic statement that punishment only comes after sending a messenger. How can both be true? This passage offers a precise reconciliation by distinguishing obligation from its punitive consequence.

The key is that obligation does not necessarily entail punishment.

Commentary
This passage offers a reconciliation between two seemingly conflicting views regarding the nature of obligation before revelation. The author states that the term 'obligation' (wujub) does not necessarily imply that punishment will follow its non-fulfillment. Therefore, there is no contradiction with the Quranic verse 'We would not punish until We send a messenger' (17:15). This interpretation removes the need to either limit punishment to this world (instead of the afterlife) or to extend the concept of 'messenger' to include both intellect (aql) and revelation (naql). The reconciliation preserves the Ash'ari principle that punishment without a revealed warning is not just, while still allowing for a metaphorical obligation prior to revelation.

Textual Corruption Regarding the Prophet's Parents and Abu Talib
The author argues that the statement about the Prophet's parents is a textual corruption and provides scholarly evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the arguments that the statement in Fiqh al-Akbar about the Prophet's parents dying as disbelievers is a textual corruption, and the evidence from manuscripts and internal inconsistency.

Core Idea
The claim that the Prophet's parents died as disbelievers is a textual corruption, not authentic.

This lesson examines a controversial passage in Fiqh al-Akbar regarding the faith of the Prophet's parents and his uncle Abu Talib. Several scholars argue that the statement about the parents is an interpolation (madhsus) based on manuscript evidence and textual inconsistency. We will explore their arguments and understand how such corruption could occur.

Guided Path
1
Read the disputed passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note the two parts: the statement about Abu Talib and the additional phrase about the Prophet's parents.
The passage begins with 'And Abu Talib died as a disbeliever' and then introduces the disputed phrase.
2
Examine scholarly testimonies
Identify the scholars quoted—al-Kawthari, al-Tahtawi, al-Bajuri—and note their common conclusion: the phrase about the parents is interpolated (madhsus) and not part of the original.
Al-Kawthari mentions seeing 'ma mata' (did not die) in old copies.
3
Analyze the internal inconsistency
Consider the author's argument: if both parents and Abu Talib were disbelievers, the text should mention them together, not separately. This suggests the parent phrase is an addition.
This is a logical criticism of the text as transmitted by Mulla Ali al-Qari.
4
Understand the paleographic mechanism
Learn how the corruption likely occurred: in Kufic script, 'fitrah' can easily be misread as 'kufr', and 'ma mata' (did not die) could be altered to 'mata' (died).
Al-Kawthari's observation about the ease of alteration is key.

(مقدمة العالم والمتعلم للكوثري ص: ٧). ويقول الحافظ محمد المرتضى الزبيدي شارح الإحياء والقاموس في رسالته "الانتصار لوالدي النبي المختار": وكنت رأيتها بخطه عند شيخنا أحمد بن مصطفى العمري الحلبي مفتي العسكر العالم المعمر. معناه: إن الناسخ لما رأى تكرر «ما» في «ما ماتا» ظن أن إحداهما زائدة فحذفها فذاعت نسخته الخاطئة، ومن الدليل على ذلك سياق الخبر لأن أبا طالب والأبوين لو كانوا جميعاً على حالة واحدة لجمع الثلاثة في الحكم بجملة واحدة لا بجملتين مع عدم التخالف بينهم في الحكم. (مقدمة العالم والمتعلم للكوثري ص: ٧)
Know that in some copies before this statement there is another statement: 'And the parents of the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, died upon disbelief.' So is it the statement of Fiqh al-Akbar or not? The correct view is yes, it is his statement, but it is distorted, as in the unreliable copies. As for the old reliable copies, Imam Zahid al-Kawthari said about them: 'I, by the praise of Allah, saw the wording "ma mata" (they did not die) in two old copies in the Egyptian National Library, as some of my friends saw the two wordings "ma mata" and "mata 'ala al-fitrah" in two old copies in the library of Shaykh al-Islam. And Ali al-Qari based his commentary on the erroneous copy and behaved improperly, may Allah forgive him.' (Muqaddimat al-‘Alim wa al-Muta‘allim, p. 7) And the scholar al-Tahawi said: 'What is in Fiqh al-Akbar that his parents, peace be upon him, died upon disbelief is interpolated upon the Imam, and evidence for that is that the reliable copies contain nothing of that.' (Hashiyat al-Tahawi ‘ala al-Durr, Kitab al-Nikah, Bab Nikah al-Kafir, 2/80) And Shaykh Muhammad ibn Ibrahim al-Bayjuri, who died 1276 AH, said: 'What is transmitted from Abu Hanifah in Fiqh al-Akbar that the parents of al-Mustafa died upon disbelief is interpolated upon him, and far be it from him to say that. And Mulla ‘Ali al-Qari, may Allah forgive him, erred in a vile word he said.' (Tuhfat al-Murid Sharh Jawharat al-Tawhid, p. 69) The wording is flawed: He who reads 'And Abu Talib died a disbeliever' after the text that Mulla ‘Ali al-Qari transmitted says objecting immediately: 'If the parents of the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, died upon disbelief and Abu Talib likewise, then the correct speech should be: "And the parents of the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, and Abu Talib died disbelievers," not that he mentions the disbelief of his parents alone and mentions the disbelief of Abu Talib separately.' One of the aspects of distortion: Imam Zahid al-Kawthari said: 'In some of those copies: "mata" (died) upon fitrah, and "al-fitrah" is easily distorted to "al-kufr" in Kufic script, and in most of them: "ma mata ‘ala al-kufr".' (Muqaddimat al-‘Alim wa al-Muta‘allim, p. 7) And al-Hafiz Muhammad al-Murtada al-Zabidi, commentator of al-Ihya' and al-Qamus, says in his treatise 'al-Intisar li-Waliday al-Nabi al-Mukhtar': 'I saw it in his handwriting with our shaykh Ahmad ibn Mustafa al-‘Umari al-Halabi, the mufti of the army, the long-lived scholar.' Its meaning: 'The copyist, when he saw the repetition of "ma" in "ma mata", thought that one of them was superfluous and deleted it, so his erroneous copy spread. And evidence for that is the context of the report, because if Abu Talib and the two parents were all in the same state, he would have gathered the three in a single sentence, not in two sentences with no difference between them in the ruling.' (Muqaddimat al-‘Alim wa al-Muta‘allim, p. 7)

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that the Prophet's parents died as disbelievers, arguing that the text in Fiqh al-Akbar is a corruption. The author begins by noting a variant reading in some manuscripts: 'ووالدا رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم ماتا على الكفر'. He then presents the issue: is this the original wording? He says the correct view is that it is but it is distorted. He cites Imam Zahid al-Kawthari who saw copies that read 'ما ماتا' (they did not die) and 'ماتا على الفطرة' (they died upon their natural disposition). Al-Kawthari criticizes Mulla Ali al-Qari for relying on the erroneous copy. Next, al-Tahawi states the phrase is interpolated and absent from reliable copies. Al-Bayjuri also declares the statement an interpolation and condemns al-Qari's harsh words. The passage then analyzes the grammatical inconsistency: if both the parents and Abu Talib were disbelievers, the text would group them in one sentence, not separate sentences. Al-Kawthari points out that 'al-fitrah' can easily be misread as 'al-kufr' in Kufic script, and that most copies read 'ما ماتا على الكفر'. Al-Zabidi's observation about the copyist deleting a repeated 'ما' explains the corruption. The concluding argument from context: if all three were the same, they would be mentioned together. This multi-layered argument defends the orthodox view that the Prophet's parents died as monotheists.

The Scribal Error in 'ما ماتا' and Its Correction
Explanation of a scribal error and its contextual correction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the scribal error in the phrase 'ما ماتا' and how the context of the report is used to correct it.

Core Idea
A copyist's misjudgment of a repeated word can corrupt a text, but context restores the original meaning.

This passage from the commentary explains a specific textual corruption that occurred in a report about the Prophet's family. The copyist mistakenly thought a repeated word was redundant, leading to a widespread erroneous version. Through contextual analysis, the author demonstrates why the repetition is necessary and how it affects the theological conclusion.
