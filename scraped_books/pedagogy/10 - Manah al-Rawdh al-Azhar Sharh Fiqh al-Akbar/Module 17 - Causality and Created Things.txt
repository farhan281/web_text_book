The Prophet's Throwing: Kasb and Khalaq
Author interprets Q.8:17 on kasb and quotes Abu Hanifa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between acquisition (kasb) and creation (khalaq) in the verse of throwing and its implication for human actions.

Core Idea
The Prophet's action was a real acquisition, but Allah alone creates.

The verse 'And you did not throw when you threw, but Allah threw' is a key text in Sunni theology on human action. This lesson unpacks its meaning in light of the kasb theory, as articulated by Imam Abu Hanifa.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once. Notice how the author distinguishes between two types of 'throwing'.
The verse negates creation but affirms acquisition.
2
Identify the two actions
The author says 'you did not throw by creation when you threw by acquisition'. Understand that the Prophet's action is real but not creative.
Kasb means the servant's action is real but dependent on Allah's creation.
3
Reflect on Abu Hanifa's quote
Abu Hanifa states that the servant and all his actions are created. Why does he say 'so his actions are more fitting to be created'?
Because the agent is created, so the effect is even more dependent.

And as for His saying, Exalted is He: {And you did not throw when you threw, but Allah threw} [Al-Anfal: 17], its meaning is: you did not throw by creation when you threw by acquisition, but Allah threw by creating the acquisition of the throwing in the Chosen One, may Allah bless him and grant him peace. Al-Imam al-A'zam said in his book al-Wasiyya: 'We affirm that the servant, with all his actions, his confession, and his knowledge, is created. So since the agent is created, his actions are more fitting to be created.' End. And its elucidation in a manner that shows its proof is that the cause of the need of things for existence in their existence is their contingency (imkan). And everything that enters into existence, whether substance or accident, is contingent in the observable world. So if the servant, who is subsistent by himself due to his contingency, obtains existence in his affair from the Creator, may His majesty be exalted, then his actions that subsist in him are more fitting to obtain existence from his Creator. And this is the meaning of His saying: {And Allah is the Self-Sufficient} i.e., in His essence and attributes from all His creatures, {and you are the poor} [Muhammad: 38], i.e., you are needy in your essences, attributes, actions, and states to Allah, i.e., to His bringing into existence in the beginning and His sustaining in the interim before the end.

Commentary
This passage interprets the verse 'And you did not throw when you threw, but Allah threw' from Surah al-Anfal. The author explains that the Prophet's throwing was an acquisition (kasb), not a creation (khalaq); Allah created the acquisition of the throwing in the Prophet. He then quotes Abu Hanifa's al-Wasiyya affirming that the servant and all his actions are created. Since the agent is created, his actions are even more fitting to be created. Then the author provides a rational proof (burhan) based on contingency (imkan): the cause of all things needing a creator is their contingency. Everything that enters existence, whether substance or accident, is contingent. Since the servant himself obtains existence from the Creator due to his contingency, his actions are even more deserving of receiving existence from the Creator. This is the meaning of the verse 'And Allah is the Self-Sufficient, and you are the poor'—the servants are needy in every aspect, including their actions, and require Allah's origination and sustenance.

The Contingency Argument for the Creation of Actions
Author gives a rational proof from contingency for creation of actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof from contingency (imkan) that all things, including human actions, depend on Allah for existence.

Core Idea
Because everything contingent needs a creator, and we are contingent, our actions are even more contingent.

The author builds a rational argument to support the teaching of the verse. He grounds the need for a creator in the nature of contingency (imkan) and extends it to actions. This lesson walks through that logical proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof passage
Read the Arabic passage from 'وبيانه' to the end. Focus on the logical structure.
The author uses a clear 'if-then' reasoning.
2
Identify the cause of need
The passage states that the cause (illa) of everything needing a creator is its contingency (imkan). Understand what contingency means: not necessary, possible to exist or not.
Contingency is the opposite of necessity.
3
Apply to actions
Since the servant himself is contingent and receives existence from Allah, his actions, which are even more dependent, must also receive existence from Allah.
This is a 'from the greater to the lesser' argument.

The Simultaneity of Capacity with Action
Abu Hanifa affirms capacity simultaneous with action, using Quran and reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why capacity must be simultaneous with the action in Sunni theology, based on scripture and reason.

Core Idea
Capacity is with the action — not before (independence) nor after (impossibility).

This lesson examines the precise timing of human capacity to act. The early Sunni tradition, articulated by Abu Hanifa, insists that capacity is created exactly when the action occurs. This position avoids two fatal errors: making the servant independent of Allah, or making the action impossible. Let's see how this reasoning is anchored in the Quran and rational necessity.

This verse is key to the argument.

The Simultaneity of Human Capacity with the Act
Affirms that the servant's will, power, and capacity are created simultaneously with the act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that human capacity (istita'a) is created by Allah simultaneously with the act, not before nor after, and the theological implications of this belief.

Core Idea
Capacity is a divine gift at the moment of action, not a pre-existing possession.

This passage from the creed of Imam Abu Hanifa addresses a central question: when does a person possess the ability to act? Some think ability precedes action, implying independence from Allah. Others think it follows, making action impossible. The correct view is that capacity is created by Allah at the very time of the act, affirming total dependence on Him. The passage uses Quran and Hadith to support this position.

The Difference with Mu'tazila on Rational Good and Evil
Refutation of Mu'tazila claim that reason independently obligates God and man

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key difference between the Maturidi and Mu'tazila positions on rational good and evil, specifically regarding the role of reason and the source of obligation.

Core Idea
Reason is a tool that recognizes moral qualities, not an independent source of obligation.

The Mu'tazila argued that reason itself imposes obligations on God and humans once it perceives good and evil. The Maturidi scholars, following Abu al-Maturidi, countered that only God is the obligator, and reason functions as a tool to recognize the moral qualities that God has placed in actions. This lesson explains that crucial distinction and its implications for Sunni theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

So the difference between us and the Mu'tazila, who affirm rational good and evil, is what the teacher Abu al-Maturidi and the general scholars of Samarqand, may Allah have mercy on them, mentioned: that the intellect, according to them, when it perceives good and evil, by itself makes their consequences obligatory upon God and upon the servants. According to us, the obligator is Allah Almighty; He makes obligations upon His servants, and nothing is obligatory upon Him, may He be exalted, by consensus of Ahl al-Sunnah. Commentary
This passage is a comparative refutation. It first states the Mu'tazila position: reason, upon perceiving good and evil, itself imposes obligations on both God and humans. The Maturidi counter-argument, attributed to Abu al-Maturidi and the scholars of Samarqand, is that only God is the obligator; He imposes duties on His servants, and nothing is obligatory upon God Himself. This is a core Sunni principle: God's actions are not subject to external moral obligation. Reason is not an independent source of obligation but a tool ('āla) that recognizes the good or evil quality that God has placed in actions. Thus, the Mu'tazila err by granting reason autonomous normative power.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think that Maturidis deny any rational knowledge of good and evil.
Correction: Maturidis affirm rational knowledge of good and evil, but they see it as recognition of qualities created by God, not as independent obligation.
