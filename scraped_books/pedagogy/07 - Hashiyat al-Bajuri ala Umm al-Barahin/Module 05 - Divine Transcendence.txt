The Impossible Attributes: Opposites of Perfection
Sanusi enumerates the impossible attributes as opposites of the necessary ones, giving examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah's impossible attributes are the direct opposites of His necessary attributes, and you will recognize several key examples that emphasize His transcendence.

Core Idea
Allah's perfection is known by what He cannot be — twenty opposites of necessity.

Sanusi continues his systematic exposition of divine attributes by turning to what must be denied of Allah. Just as there are twenty attributes necessary for Him, there are twenty that are impossible for Him. Each impossible attribute is the exact opposite of a necessary one, reinforcing the principle of divine transcendence (tanzih). This lesson introduces the concept and lists the first several pairs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of opposition
Recognize that impossible attributes are defined as the opposites (aḍḍād) of the necessary attributes. Each impossibility directly negates a perfection.
2
Read the list of impossible attributes
Carefully read the Arabic passage and translation. Identify each impossible attribute mentioned: non-existence, origination, passing-away, similarity, being subject to originated events, size, and purposes.
Notice that 'similarity' includes several sub-attributes like being a body or occupying space.
3
Connect to necessary attributes
Pair each impossible attribute with its opposite necessary attribute. For example, non-existence is opposite of existence; origination is opposite of pre-eternity.
This helps you see the structure of Sanusi's argument.
4
Reflect on tanzih
Consider how these negations protect Allah from any resemblance to created things. This is the core of divine transcendence.

Proof of God's Incomparability to Creatures
Sanusi proves that God is unlike all contingent things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God must be unlike all contingent beings and how this is proven through a simple conditional argument.

Core Idea
God cannot resemble any creature without becoming contingent.

This lesson presents Sanusi's concise proof for God's incomparability. Using a conditional argument, he shows that any resemblance to contingent beings would entail contingency, which contradicts God's necessary pre-eternity and everlastingness. This foundational doctrine safeguards divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully

Notice the structure: 'if...then...' with a justification.
2

The proof consists of a conditional statement (if resemblance then contingency) and a justification (contingency is impossible given God's pre-eternity). Identify each part.
3
Understand the underlying premises
Sanusi assumes you already know that God is necessarily pre-eternal and everlasting. How does this support the conclusion?
These attributes were proven in previous lessons.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that God must be completely unlike all contingent beings. What does this imply about the relationship between God and creation?
5
Consider the common misunderstanding
Some might think this negates any shared attributes, but conceptual attributes like 'existence' can be shared in a different mode. Reflect on the nuance.
This distinction is crucial for avoiding anthropomorphism.

Therefore, God is utterly dissimilar to all created things in His essence, attributes, and actions.

Core Idea
If God resembled any created thing, He would be temporal—impossible for the Eternal.

In this brief but powerful argument, Sanusi establishes the attribute of mukhalafa — God's absolute difference from all originated beings. The logic is simple yet decisive: resemblance would entail temporality, which contradicts God's necessary eternity.

Identify the conditional argument

This is a classic proof by contradiction.
3
Connect to previous knowledge
Recall that God has been proven to be eternal and perpetual. The conclusion (temporality) is impossible, so the premise (resemblance) must be false.
This builds on the earlier lesson about God's eternity.

Proof of God's Self-Subsistence
Sanusi proves God's self-subsistence via two conditional arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof that God is self-subsistent (qiyam bi-nafsihi) and why He cannot be an attribute or need a determiner.

Core Idea
God's self-subsistence means He needs no substrate and no external cause.

After establishing that God exists, Sanusi now demonstrates His absolute independence. This lesson unpacks two concise arguments showing that God cannot be an attribute (which needs a locus) nor temporal (which needs a determiner). Pay close attention to how each premise leads to a necessary conclusion about the Divine nature.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text aloud or silently, noting its compact structure. Then read the English translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice that the proof is presented as a single continuous sentence in Arabic.
2
Analyze the first argument (locus)
Identify the conditional statement: 'if He needed a locus, He would be an attribute.' Then understand why an attribute cannot possess the attributes God must have.
This refutes the idea that God could inhere in a substrate.
3
Analyze the second argument (determiner)
Examine the conditional: 'if He needed a determiner, He would be temporal.' Connect this to the previously established proof of God's eternity.
A determiner would imply a prior cause, which contradicts timelessness.
4
Synthesize the conclusion
Combine both arguments to form the complete proof of self-subsistence. Reflect on how Sanusi builds his case without relying on scripture alone.
Self-subsistence here means independence from both substrate and external specification.

وأَمَّا بُرهانُ وُجوبِ قِيامِهِ تعالى بِنَفْسِهِ: فلأنه لو احتاج تعالى إلى مَحَلّ لكانَ صفةً، والصفةُ لا تَتَّصف بصفاتِ المعاني ولا المعْنَويَّةِ، وَمَوْلانَا جلّ وعزّ يجبُ اتِّصافُهُ بِهِما ، فَليْسَ بِصفةٍ، ولو احتاجَ إِلى مُخَصِّص لكانَ حادثاً، كيف وقد قامَ البُرْهَانُ عَلى وُجُوبِ قِدَمِهِ تعالى وبَقَائِهِ.

And if He needed a determiner, He would be temporal, especially since proof has been established for the necessity of His eternity and perpetuity.

Commentary
This passage proves that God is self-subsistent (qiyam bi-nafsihi), meaning He does not need a locus (mahall) or a determiner (mukhassis). The argument has two parts: First, if God needed a locus, He would be an attribute (sifa). But attributes do not themselves possess the essential attributes (sifat al-ma'ani) and mental attributes (sifat al-ma'nawiyya) that God must have. Therefore, God cannot be an attribute. Second, if God needed a determiner (i.e., something that specifies His existence), He would be temporal. Since His eternity and perpetuity have already been proven, He cannot be temporal. Thus, He is self-subsistent.

Meanings of Salam in Blessings upon the Prophet
Al-Bajuri explains the various interpretations of 'salam' in the blessing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the various interpretations of 'salam' in the blessings upon the Prophet and why some interpretations are preferred.

Core Idea
Salam in the blessing means security and awe, not just a name of Allah.

When we send peace upon the Prophet, what does 'peace' mean? Al-Bajuri examines several interpretations, from security to greeting to a divine name. This lesson walks through his analysis and shows why some meanings are closer to the intended reverence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Get the full picture of Al-Bajuri's discussion.
Notice the flow from primary meaning to rejected interpretation.
2
Identify the primary meaning
Al-Bajuri states that 'salam' primarily means 'al-aman' (security). Understand that it secures the Prophet from what he fears for his nation.
3
Understand the Prophet's fear
The Prophet does not fear harm but fears Allah out of awe. Also note the interpretation about fear on Judgment Day.
This shows the depth of the Prophet's reverence.
4
Examine alternative interpretations
Look at the greeting interpretation and the far-fetched interpretation of 'salam' as a name of Allah. Understand why Al-Bajuri rejects the latter.
He affirms the name but not in this context.
5
Reflect on the lesson
Consider why precise theological language matters. The meaning of 'salam' is not arbitrary but rooted in reverence and protection.
This connects to broader themes of divine transcendence.

Commentary
This passage explains the meaning of 'salam' (peace) in the context of sending blessings upon the Prophet. Al-Bajuri begins by stating that the meaning of 'salam' is 'al-aman' (security/safety). The intended meaning is that Allah secures the Prophet from what he fears for his nation. Since Allah is infallible, how could he fear for himself? However, the Prophet does fear Allah, but it is a fear of awe and veneration, not fear of harm. This is supported by the Prophet's saying: 'Indeed, I am the most fearing of Allah among you.' Then Al-Bajuri presents another interpretation: the Prophet fears for himself at the time of distress in the gathering (Day of Resurrection) because he forgets his infallibility like other prophets. Another interpretation is that 'salam' means a greeting from Allah to His messenger, indicating honor. Finally, some mistakenly think 'salam' is a name of Allah, meaning 'Allah is pleased' or 'Allah is a Protector.' Al-Bajuri rejects this as far-fetched, though he affirms that 'Salam' is indeed one of Allah's names, but it is not appropriate in this context. He then notes that there are other investigations related to prayer and peace that are not suitable to discuss here.
