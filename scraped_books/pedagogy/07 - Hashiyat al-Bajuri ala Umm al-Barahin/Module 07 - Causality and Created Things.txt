The Proof from the Originatedness of the World
Sanusi presents the proof of Allah's existence via the world's originatedness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's three-step argument for Allah's existence based on the world's originatedness and the logical principle of preponderance without a cause.

Core Idea
The world's constant change proves it originated, requiring an Originator.

This lesson presents a classical kalam proof for God's existence. Sanusi demonstrates that the world's dependence on changing accidents leads to the necessity of a Creator. The argument hinges on the logical principle that if something could equally be or not be, something external must tilt the balance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the translation. Notice the three distinct parts of the argument.
The first word 'بزهان' is a variant spelling of 'برهان' (proof).
2
Understand the main argument
The passage first states that if the world had no originator, it would require one of two equal states to preponderate without cause—impossible. Therefore, an Originator exists.
This is an application of the principle of sufficient reason.
3
See how the world's origination is proved
The world is inseparable from originated accidents (like motion and rest), and whatever is inseparable from an originated thing is itself originated.
4
See how accidents are proved originated
Accidents are observed to change from non-existence to existence and vice versa, proving they are originated.
This relies on empirical observation (mushāhada).

As for the proof of His existence, exalted: it is the origination (ḥudūth) of the world; because if it had no originator, but rather originated by itself, it would necessitate that one of two equal things be preponderant over its counterpart without a cause, and that is impossible. And the proof of the origination of the world is its inseparability from originated accidents (aʿrāḍ ḥāditha) such as motion, rest, and others, and that which is inseparable from something originated is itself originated. And the proof of the origination of accidents is the observation of their changing from non-existence to existence and from existence to non-existence.

Commentary
This passage is a concise argument for the existence of God, structured as a proof (burhān). Sanusi states that the proof of Allah's existence is the origination (ḥudūth) of the world. He gives the logical reason: if the world had no originator and came into being by itself, then given two equally possible states (existence vs. non-existence), one would have to preponderate over the other without a cause, which is impossible. Therefore, there must be an external cause. He then proves that the world is originated: because it is always accompanied by originated accidents (like motion and rest), and whatever accompanies something originated is itself originated. Finally, he proves that accidents are originated by observing that they change from non-existence to existence and vice versa. Thus the world is originated and needs an Originator.

No Effect for Customary Causes and Two Erroneous Views
Al-Bajuri negates causal efficacy and details the first two erroneous beliefs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that customary causes have no real effect on their apparent effects, and that attributing effect to them—whether independently or by a deposited power—is a serious theological error with specific rulings.

Core Idea
Only God truly causes; nature is powerless.

In this passage, al-Bajuri draws a crucial deduction from the statement about God's sole agency. He affirms that things like fire and food do not cause burning or satiety, but only God does. He then lays out two incorrect beliefs and their consequences: belief in independent causal power leads to clear disbelief, while belief in a deposited power makes one a wicked innovator, though not necessarily a disbeliever. This lesson explores these first two positions and their implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the passage from the beginning to the analogy with the servant creating actions. Pay attention to the precise wording.
Notice the deduction from 'or that there be with Him in existence an effecter'.
2
Identify the core denial
Find where al-Bajuri states that no customary cause has any effect. Understand the examples he gives.
3
Examine the first erroneous view
Look at the person who believes a cause acts by itself. Note the ruling: definitive disbelief.
This is the most severe error.
4
Examine the second erroneous view
Look at the person who believes a cause acts by a power deposited by God. Note the description: wicked innovator, with a debated ruling on disbelief, but preponderantly not disbelief.
Al-Bajuri compares this to the Mu'tazilite view on human action.
5
Reflect on the spectrum
Consider why the second view is less severe but still innovated. Think about the underlying principle: all true causality belongs to God alone.

And it is known from His statement: 'or that there be with Him in existence an effecter... etc.' that there is no effect for customary causes in their effects, so there is no effect for fire in burning, nor for food in satiety, nor for the knife in cutting, and so on. So whoever believes that any of them effects by itself—there is no dispute about his disbelief. And whoever believes that any of them effects by a power that God deposited in it—he is a wicked innovator, and there are two opinions regarding his disbelief, and the preponderant is his non-disbelief, like one who believes that the servant creates his voluntary actions by a power that God created in him. And whoever believes that there is no effect for any of them, and that the effecter is only God, but there is a rational concomitance between them and their effects, so that when fire for example exists, burning exists—he is ignorant of the reality of the judgment, and perhaps that drags him to disbelief; because it may lead him to deny extraordinary things such as the miracles of the prophets, peace be upon them, and the resurrection of bodies. So no one is saved except one who believes that there is no effect for any of them, and that there is no concomitance between them and their effects, by believing in the validity of dissociation, so that the cause may exist while the effect does not. And God is the grantor of success.

Commentary
This passage explains a deduction from the statement 'or that there be with Him in existence an effecter' concerning causality. The author asserts that customary causes (e.g., fire, food, knife) have no real effect in their apparent effects; only God is the true agent. He then categorizes people based on their belief: (1) Whoever believes a cause acts by itself is undoubtedly a disbeliever. (2) Whoever believes a cause acts by a power God placed in it is a wicked innovator (fasiq mubtadi‘); regarding his disbelief there are two opinions, with the preponderant view being that he is not a disbeliever, similar to the Mu'tazila who say the servant creates his actions by a power from God. (3) Whoever believes no cause has effect but asserts a rational concomitance (talazum ‘aqli) between cause and effect is ignorant of the true ruling, and this may lead to disbelief because it implies denial of miracles and bodily resurrection. (4) The saved one is he who believes that there is no effect and no rational concomitance, accepting that the cause can exist without the effect. The passage ends with 'And God is the grantor of success.'

Why Rational Concomitance Leads to Denial of Miracles
Al-Bajuri contrasts rational concomitance with the correct belief of dissociation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that believing in a rational necessity between cause and effect can lead to denying miracles and resurrection, and that the correct belief is to accept that God can dissociate cause from effect.

Core Idea
Causes can exist without effects; God is not bound by 'laws' of nature.

Having rejected causal efficacy, al-Bajuri addresses a more subtle error: assuming a rational connection between cause and effect. He warns that this seemingly innocuous belief can undermine faith in miracles and bodily resurrection. The only safe position is to affirm that God may create the cause without the effect, preserving His absolute power. This lesson examines the third and fourth beliefs in the passage.

Guided Path
1
Read the second part of the passage
Read from 'ومن اعتقد أنه لا تأثير لشيء منها' to the end. Notice the contrast between the third and fourth beliefs.
The third belief includes a rational concomitance.
2
Understand the third belief
This person says no effect but asserts a rational necessity (talazum 'aqli) between cause and effect. Al-Bajuri calls this person ignorant and says it may lead to disbelief.
Why? Because it denies the possibility of dissociation.
3
Connect to miracles
Realize that if cause must produce effect, then miracles where fire does not burn become impossible. This person would have to deny miracles.
Also resurrection of bodies.
4
Learn the saved belief
The fourth person believes no effect and no rational concomitance, accepting that cause can exist without effect. This is the only safe position.
This allows for the possibility of miracles.
5
Reflect on divine freedom
Consider how this belief upholds God's absolute power and freedom, and the importance of not binding Him to 'natural laws'.

Defining al-Taghleel: Causation by Nature
Al-Bajuri defines al-taghleel and gives an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of al-taghleel (causation by nature) as a deterministic form of causality that negates divine will and choice, and why it is rejected in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Al-taghleel is a deterministic causality that denies divine will and choice.

Before explaining the full emanation scheme, al-Bajuri defines the type of causality the philosophers employ. 'Al-taghleel' is a necessitarian causation where one thing necessarily arises from another without will or choice, and without dependence on conditions. This sets the stage for understanding how the philosophers derive the multiplicity of existents from a single principle.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition and example
Read the first part of the Arabic passage where al-Bajuri defines 'al-taghleel' and gives the example of the finger and ring. Notice how the example illustrates a direct, necessary causal connection.
The phrase 'may Allah disfigure them' indicates the author's rejection of this view.
2
Identify the key elements of the definition
The definition includes: (1) the effect arises from the cause without the cause having will or choice, and (2) no condition is required and no obstacle is present. This makes it a strictly deterministic process.
3
Recognize the theological implication
Notice that the philosophers extend this to call God the 'cause of causes' ('illat al-ilal'), implying that all existence emanates from Him necessarily, which contradicts the Islamic emphasis on divine will and choice.
This is the point of refutation.
4
Reflect on the key takeaway
Summarize: al-taghleel is a deterministic causality that Islamic theology rejects because it excludes divine will. This concept is foundational to understanding the philosophers' emanation theory.

The conclusion: that if you estimate that the influence of any of the beings is by its nature, then its non-existence (of being derived from that) is taken from the dependence of all other than Him upon Him—exalted is He—; otherwise it would entail that the effect is independent of our Master—glorified and exalted—although He is the One upon whom all else depends. And if you estimate that the influence of any of the beings is by a power that God—exalted is He—placed in it, then its non-existence is taken from His independence—exalted is He—from all other than Him; otherwise it would entail that He—exalted is He—in need of an intermediary in creating some actions, despite the fact that He—glorified and exalted—is independent of all other than Him. And the difference between these two estimations: that influence in the first does not depend on the will and choice of God—exalted is He—because that which is by nature does not depend on that; so it entails that the effect is independent of God—exalted is He—and it does not entail that He—exalted is He—is in need of an intermediary, unlike the second, for it depends on the will and choice of God—exalted is He—until He creates the power in the customary causes. Thus the action from this perspective becomes desired by God—exalted is He—and it entails that He—exalted is He—is in need of an intermediary in creating some actions, and it does not entail that the effect is independent of God—exalted is He. So reflect. (His saying: 'Because it') i.e., the state and affair. (And His saying: 'It becomes then') i.e., when you have estimated it as influencing by a power that God placed in it. And from that is taken the major premise of the syllogism which says: if you estimate that any of the beings influences by a power that God placed in it... [marginal note from al-Ajhuri:] (His saying: 'some actions') are the customary effects like satiety and thirst. (His saying: 'to an intermediary') are the customary causes like food and water.

Commentary
This passage explains the impossibility of creatures having real causal power. It begins by answering the conditional: it is impossible for any being to influence by a power placed in it by God (as a real power independent of God's will?). Actually the text says that if influence is by nature, then the non-influence of creatures is derived from the dependence of all else on God; otherwise the effect would be independent of God. If influence is by a power placed by God, then the non-influence is derived from God's independence; otherwise God would need an intermediary. The key distinction: influence by nature does not depend on God's will, so the effect becomes independent of God (since nature acts necessarily). Influence by a power placed by God does depend on God's will, because God must create that power in the cause; thus God's action passes through an intermediary, which implies a need. However, since God is independent, both scenarios are rejected: creatures cannot have any real causal power. The passage ends with an exhortation to reflect. The final notes clarify that 'some actions' refer to customary effects like satiety and thirst, and 'intermediary' refers to customary causes like food and water, confirming that the discussion is about the physical world.
