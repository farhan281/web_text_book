The Six Types of Extraordinary Events (Khariq al-‘Adah)
Al-Bajuri defines mu'jizah and enumerates the six types of khariq al-‘adah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six categories of extraordinary events (khariq al-‘adah) according to classical Islamic theology, including the distinction between miracles and other types.

Core Idea
Extraordinary events are classified into six types, each with a specific context and purpose.

Al-Bajuri begins by defining what a miracle (mu'jizah) is and then expands on five other types of extraordinary events. This classification helps us understand that not every supernatural event is a miracle; some are tests, punishments, or signs. The passage includes a vivid example to illustrate the last category, ihanah (humiliation).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing to analyse. Then read the English translation. Let the author's flow come through before diving into details.
2
Identify the six categories
As you read, note each type of khariq al-‘adah mentioned. There are six in total: mu'jizah, irhas, karamah, ma'unah, istidraj, and ihanah. Observe the definitions and the condition that distinguishes mu'jizah (after prophethood).
3
Understand the purpose of each type
For each type, reflect on its purpose: confirmation of prophethood (mu'jizah), foundation for prophethood (irhas), honor for a saint (karamah), assistance for common believers (ma'unah), deception for a sinner (istidraj), and humiliation for a liar (ihanah).
The example of Musaylimah powerfully illustrates ihanah.
4
Test your understanding
Answer the checkpoint question to see if you can recall the total number of categories. Then try the activity to reinforce the definitions.

His saying: 'by the miracle' – i.e., it is the extraordinary affair, with the condition that it occurs after the message, unlike before it, for it is then irhas, i.e., a foundation for it. Remaining from the divisions of the extraordinary are: al-karamah: it is what appears at the hand of a worshipper outwardly righteous; and al-ma‘unah: it is what appears at the hand of the common people to deliver them from a severe difficulty that has befallen them, for example; and al-istidraj: it is what appears at the hand of a sinner by way of deception and guile; and al-ihanah: it is what appears at his hand as a discrediting of him, as happened to Musaylimah the liar, for he spat into the eye of a one-eyed man to heal it, so the healthy eye went blind, and he spat into a well to increase its water, so it dried up, and he spat into a well to make its water sweet, so it became bitter salty water! So it follows that the divisions of the extraordinary affair are six divisions.

Poetic Summary of the Six Types of Extraordinary Events
Al-Bajuri cites a poem enumerating the six types of extraordinary events.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six categories of extraordinary events (khawariq al-'adat) and their distinguishing conditions as summarized in the poem cited by al-Bajuri.

Core Idea
Extraordinary events are classified by who performs them: prophets, saints, common believers, or sinners.

Al-Bajuri presents a concise poem that summarizes the six types of events that break natural custom. Each type has a specific source and purpose, from miracles confirming prophethood to humiliations for sinners. This mnemonic poem helps students memorize the categories and their distinguishing features.

Guided Path
1
Read the poem and translation
Read the Arabic poem once, then read the English translation. Notice the rhyme and rhythm that aids memorization.
The poem is cited from another scholar, not part of the original matn.
2
Identify the six terms
From the poem, list the six Arabic terms for extraordinary events. For each, note the type of person from whom it originates.
The terms are: معجزة, إرهاص, كرامة, معونة, استدراج, إهانة.
3
Understand the conditions
For each term, understand the condition mentioned in the poem: e.g., معجزة from a prophet, إرهاص before prophethood, etc.
Pay attention to the condition for a sinner: if in accord with desire = استدراج, otherwise = إهانة.
4
Reflect on the classification
Consider why this classification is important for distinguishing genuine miracles from other extraordinary events.
This helps in refuting false claims of prophethood or sainthood.

The Addition of Magic and Trial as Extraordinary Events
Al-Bajuri glosses additional extraordinary event types with scholarly opinions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the scholarly debate over whether sihr (magic) is extraordinary or customary, and how ibtila' (trial) is defined as a category of khariq al-‘adah.

Core Idea
Not all unusual events are miracles—magic and trials are debated categories.

Al-Bajuri continues refining the concept of khariq al-‘adah by noting two more categories that some scholars include: sihr (magic) and ibtila' (trial). The main text states these were added and invites review. The commentary reveals a key disagreement about whether magic is truly extraordinary or merely customary. This lesson explores that debate and the definition of ibtila'.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Note that the text includes both a main statement and glosses explaining two key additions.
The passage is short but dense with scholarly references.
2
Identify the two positions on sihr
Focus on the gloss explaining sihr. It presents two views: Ibn 'Arafah and the author of al-Maqasid consider it extraordinary, while al-Qarafi considers it customary. Understand the reasoning behind each.
Al-Qarafi argues that magic's strangeness is due to ignorance of its causes, not any violation of natural law.
3
Understand the definition of ibtila'
The second gloss defines ibtila' as an increase of illness occurring contrary to habit. This is another instance of an event that is not a miracle but still deviates from the norm.
Ibtila' is a trial, often afflicting saints and prophets.

The Indicative Meaning of Miracles: Two Views
Al-Bajuri explains the two views on what a miracle indicates regarding a prophet's truthfulness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two theological views on what a miracle indicates about a prophet's truthfulness and the implications of each view.

Core Idea
A miracle is either God's implicit statement of a prophet's truthfulness or a created indication of it.

Al-Bajuri examines the fundamental question: what does a miracle actually indicate? Does it function like a divine declaration that the prophet is truthful, or does it merely create an indication of truthfulness without being a direct statement? This distinction has important consequences for understanding divine speech and the nature of prophetic proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and then the literal translation. Pay attention to the flow of the argument.
The passage includes a marginal note on ibtila' which is a separate discussion.
2
Identify the two views
Find the two theological views: the miracle as khabar (informative statement) and as insha' al-dalala (creation of indication). Notice how each view is introduced.
The first view uses the phrase 'نازلة منزلة قوله تعالى: صدق عبدي'.
3
Understand the entailment problem
Consider the consequence of the first view: if the prophet is false, God would be lying. Then see how the commentary resolves the potential objection from the Mu'tazila.
The resolution involves a distinction in definitions of truth and falsehood between creatures and God.
