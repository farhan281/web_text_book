The Three Types of Lam: Istihqaq, Ikhtisas, and Mulk
Al-Bajuri defines the three types of lam with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three types of lam (istihqaq, ikhtisas, and mulk) and the distinction between them based on whether the governed is a meaning or a substance and whether it can be owned.

Core Idea
Lam al-ikhtisas differs from lam al-mulk in that its object cannot be owned.

Al-Bajuri, in his commentary on Umm al-Barahin, explains the three types of lam that appear in Arabic constructions. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for interpreting theological statements about praise and ownership. In this lesson, we will explore the definitions and examples of each lam as presented by Al-Bajuri.

3
Understand the distinction
Note that lam al-ikhtisas connects two substances where the governed cannot be owned, while lam al-mulk connects two substances where the governed can be owned.
Lam al-istihqaq connects a meaning to a substance.
4
Reflect on ikhtisas
Al-Bajuri clarifies that ikhtisas here means attachment (ta'alluq) and connection (irtibat), not restriction (hasr). Understand why this matters.
This prevents misunderstanding of the term.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the main text's phrase 'أو مختص أو مملوك له تعالى' (or specific or owned by Him, exalted). The commentator first explains the meaning of 'mukhtass' (specific) by stating that ikhtisas here means attachment (ta'alluq) and connection (irtibat), not restriction (hasr) as mentioned by al-Sabban. He clarifies that the object of praise in all categories is either an attribute of God or something that originates from Him. Then he discusses 'or owned' (mamluk), explaining that the apparent meaning is that all praises are within God's power. He notes that this leads to the later point that the eternal cannot be owned. He gives a detailed explanation: if we interpret 'owned' to mean that all praises return to God and that He is the ultimate object of praise, either explicitly or implicitly, then making the lam for mulk (ownership) is valid in all cases. He provides examples: explicit praise like God praising Himself and contingent things praising Him, and implicit praise like God praising His prophets and saints, which includes praise of Himself. He then moves to the three types of lam. Lam al-istihqaq occurs between a meaning (ma'na) and a substance (dhat), e.g., 'al-hamdu lillah' and 'waylun lil-mutaffifin' (with the note that 'wayl' is a name for punishment). Lam al-ikhtisas occurs between two substances, and its governed (madhkul) is not owned, e.g., 'the saddlecloth is for the beast', or between two substances and the companion of its governed is not owned, e.g., 'for Zayd a son'. The preponderant view is that ikhtisas here means attachment, not restriction. Lam al-mulk occurs between two substances, and its governed is owned, and the companion of its governed is owned, e.g., 'the wealth is for Zayd'. The commentator notes that sometimes lam al-ikhtisas is applied to the first and third types as well, and lam al-istighraq (inclusiveness) is applied to the second type. This summary is from al-Ashmuni and the gloss of al-Sabban.

Nine Combinations of Lam and 'Al' in 'Al-Hamdu Lillah'
Al-Bajuri enumerates nine linguistic possibilities for lam and 'al' in 'al-hamdu lillah' and explains their validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the nine possible combinations of lam (istihqaq, ikhtisas, mulk) and 'al' (jins, istighraq, 'ahd) yield only eight valid interpretations of 'al-hamdu lillah', and why one combination is theologically impossible.

Core Idea
Nine linguistic possibilities, one rational impossibility: the eternal cannot be owned.

In this passage, al-Bajuri analyzes the phrase 'al-hamdu lillah' by exploring the intersection of two Arabic grammatical particles: the lam (with three possible meanings) and the definite article 'al' (with three possible meanings). Multiplying these yields nine logical combinations. Al-Bajuri identifies exactly one combination that is impossible on theological grounds, and explains why the rest are valid. This lesson unpacks his reasoning, showing how grammar, logic, and theology intersect in Islamic kalam.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three meanings of lam
The lam in 'lillah' can mean istihqaq (deserving), ikhtisas (specification), or mulk (ownership). Each changes the meaning of the praise being for Allah.
These are standard categories in Arabic grammar and kalam.
2
Understand the three meanings of 'al'
The definite article 'al' in 'al-hamdu' can be for jins (genus), istighraq (inclusiveness of all individuals), or 'ahd (definiteness by prior mention).
'Al' for 'ahd refers to a specific praise already known.
3
Multiply to get nine combinations
Crossing three lam meanings with three 'al' meanings gives nine possible interpretations. The passage states this explicitly.
4
Identify the one impossible combination
The combination of lam for mulk and 'al' for 'ahd, when the covenanted praise is only the eternal praise (Allah's own praise), is impossible because the eternal cannot be owned.
If the covenanted includes contingent praises (e.g., prophets'), it becomes valid because the composite is contingent.
5
Follow the valid combinations for istighraq and jins
When 'al' is for istighraq or jins, the valid lam meanings depend on whether we consider eternal or contingent individuals, or the sum/generic essence.
The passage also notes that if the sum is considered, lam for mulk is valid because the sum includes contingent parts.

Commentary
This passage is an analysis of the possible combinations of the three meanings of the lam (istihqaq, ikhtisas, mulk) with the three possible meanings of the definite article 'al' in the phrase 'al-hamdu lillah'. The three meanings of 'al' are: for jins (genus), for istighraq (inclusiveness of all individuals), and for 'ahd (definiteness by prior mention). Multiplying the three lam possibilities by the three 'al' possibilities gives nine combinations. The commentator states that one of these nine is impossible: when the lam is for mulk (ownership) and 'al' is for 'ahd, and the covenanted (the specific praise referred to) is solely the eternal praise (i.e., God's own eternal praise). This is impossible because the eternal cannot be owned. However, if the covenanted praise is taken to be the composite of eternal and contingent praises (e.g., God's praise of Himself and the praise of His prophets and saints), then it is valid because the composite is contingent (since it includes contingent parts). Next, the commentator explains the other combinations. When 'al' is for istighraq (inclusiveness of all individuals, both eternal and contingent), then: the lam for mulk is valid when considering the contingent individuals, while the lam for istihqaq or ikhtisas is valid when considering the eternal individuals. If the sum (all individuals collectively) is considered, then the lam for mulk is also valid (because the sum includes contingent parts). When 'al' is for jins (genus), the lam for mulk is valid with regard to the genus being realized in the contingent individuals, while lam for istihqaq or ikhtisas is valid with regard to the genus being realized in the eternal individuals, as long as the sum is not considered. Then the commentator adds two explanatory notes. The first defines mulk (ownership) as possessing a thing with the ability to dispose of it exclusively. The second explains that 'what is composed of both is contingent' means that the observed combination of eternal and contingent individuals is a contingent thing; if no actual combination occurs, there is no real composition. It further notes that if by 'composite' is meant the mere aggregate, it is not valid because the contingent and eternal remain distinct in their essences. The passage ends mid-sentence discussing an alternative interpretation (hay'a/form).

Al-Ajhuri's Objection: Lam is Only for Istihqaq
Al-Ajhuri critiques the earlier analysis using al-Sabban's definitions, concluding only istihqaq applies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Ajhuri argues that the lam in 'al-hamdu lillah' must be for istihqaq only, based on al-Sabban's precise definitions.

Core Idea
Precise definitions force the lam to be for istihqaq, not mulk or ikhtisas.

This lesson examines a critical objection from al-Ajhuri that challenges the earlier analysis allowing three possible meanings for the lam in 'al-hamdu lillah'. Using al-Sabban's precise definitions, al-Ajhuri shows that only one interpretation is valid. Understanding this objection refines our grasp of how technical definitions can rule out apparent possibilities.

Understand the three lam definitions
Identify the three types of lam: lam of mulk, lam of ikhtisas, and lam of istihqaq. Note the key distinction: mulk and ikhtisas involve two substances, while istihqaq involves a meaning and a substance.
This distinction is crucial for the argument.
3
Apply the definitions to 'al-hamdu lillah'
Recognize that 'al-hamd' (praise) is a meaning (ma'na), and 'Allah' is a substance (dhat). Therefore, according to the definitions, only istihqaq fits.

(His words: 'Whenever the rememberers remember him and the heedless are heedless of his remembrance') This is with the pronoun of absence in both, and in a narration with the pronoun of address in both, and in a narration with the pronoun of address in the first and absence in the second, and in a narration with the opposite, so the forms are four. According to the first, the first and second pronouns refer to the Prophet, and the opposite is possible, and it is correct that each could be [referring to] Allah or to the Prophet. These possibilities are the first [strongest] because the rememberers of Allah are more than the heedless of Him, and [on the other hand] the heedless of the Prophet are more than the rememberers of him, for the believers in relation to the disbelievers are like a white hair on a black ox, and mentioning the greater [number] on the side of Allah and the greater [number] on the side of the Prophet is more eloquent in the abundance of blessings upon him.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the phrase 'kullama dhakarahu al-dhakirun wa ghafala 'an dhikrihi al-ghafilun'. Al-Bajuri discusses its four variant readings: both pronouns in the third person, both in the second person, first in second person and second in third, and the opposite. According to the first variant (both third person), the pronouns refer to the Prophet, but it could also be the opposite or each refer to Allah or to the Prophet. He then notes that the preferred interpretation (al-awwal) is based on the fact that the rememberers of Allah are more numerous than the heedless of Him, whereas the heedless of the Prophet are more numerous than those who remember him, because believers are few compared to disbelievers. Therefore, mentioning the greater number in the context of Allah and the greater number in the context of the Prophet makes the blessing more abundant and eloquent. The passage thus shows careful attention to the rhetoric of supplication.

The Colophon of al-Ajhuri's Hashiyah
Colophon concluding al-Ajhuri's Hashiyah on al-Bajuri.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the significance of the colophon in concluding al-Ajhuri's gloss and the historical context of its completion.

Core Idea
A colophon marks the completion of a scholarly work with a prayer.

This brief passage is a colophon—a closing note from the author—that marks the completion of a major gloss on al-Bajuri's commentary. It records the date and the author's full name, serving as a historical anchor for the text.

Guided Path
1
Read the colophon passage
Read the Arabic text and its translation carefully. Notice that it begins by identifying the work as a gloss of al-Ajhuri.
The phrase 'قال مؤلفه' indicates the author is speaking.
2
Identify the author and date
Find the full name of the author and the exact date of completion. Note the Hijri year 1291 and the month Sha'ban.
The date helps locate the work in Islamic intellectual history.
3
Reflect on the purpose of a colophon
