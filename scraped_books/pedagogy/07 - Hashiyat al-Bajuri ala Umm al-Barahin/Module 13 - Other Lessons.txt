Inherent vs Accidental Rulings in Islamic Law
Al-Anbābī defines and exemplifies the fourfold classification of rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Islamic jurists distinguish between rulings that are inherently prohibited/disliked and those that become prohibited/disliked due to an accidental cause, and why this distinction matters.

Core Idea
A ruling is inherent if it stays even when its cause is gone; accidental if it follows the cause.

Islamic rulings are sometimes rooted in the act itself, sometimes in an external factor. This passage explains the key difference between inherent (li-dhātihi) and accidental (li-ʿāriḍ) prohibitions and dislikes. By working through clear examples from adultery to onions, you will grasp a foundational analytical tool used in uṣūl al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions carefully
Identify the two opposite conditions: inherent rulings do not revolve around a cause; accidental rulings do revolve around a cause.
The cause (ʿilla) is the reason that makes the ruling apply.
2
Examine the prohibition examples
Notice why adultery and wine are inherently prohibited (cause absent, ruling remains), while using usurped water is accidentally prohibited (cause present, ruling applies; cause absent, ruling lifts).
3
Examine the dislike examples
See the same pattern: looking at wife's private part is inherently disliked; eating onion is accidentally disliked because its cause is harming others.
Cooking the onion removes the cause and thus the dislike.
4
Understand the refutation
The passage answers an objection that the distinction is not real. It shows that the same act (drinking) can be permissible in itself but prohibited when connected to wine.
This confirms the need to consider the accidental attribute.
5
Apply the concept to a new case
Think of another act, such as praying in a stolen garment. Which category does it fall into? Use the logic of the passage.

Annotations of the scholar Muḥammad al-Anbābī: (His saying: 'such that it is not inherently prohibited nor inherently disliked') it appears that what is meant by inherently prohibited (ḥarām li-dhātihi) and inherently disliked (makrūh li-dhātihi) is that whose prohibition and dislike are not due to a cause (ʿilla) that revolves with it in existence and non-existence, while accidentally prohibited (ḥarām li-ʿāriḍ) and accidentally disliked (makrūh li-ʿāriḍ) are that whose prohibition and dislike are due to a cause that revolves with it in existence and non-existence. Thus adultery (zinā) and drinking wine (khamr) are of the type of inherently prohibited; because the prohibition of adultery does not revolve with its cause – which is mixing of lineages – in existence and non-existence; for indeed the cause may be absent and the prohibition exists, as when a man has intercourse with a minor girl. Similarly, the prohibition of drinking wine does not revolve with its cause – which is intoxication; for indeed intoxication may be absent and the prohibition exists, as when a person becomes accustomed to drinking wine such that it does not affect his mind at all, or drinks an amount that does not intoxicate. And performing ablution (wuḍūʾ) with usurped water is of the type of accidentally prohibited; because its prohibition revolves with its cause which is violent seizure of another's right – in existence and non-existence. And looking at the private part of one's wife is of the type of inherently disliked; because its dislike does not revolve with its cause – which is fear of blinding combined with lack of need; for indeed the cause may be absent and dislike exists, as when a trustworthy person informs him that he will not suffer blinding if he looks at his wife's private part. And eating onion is of the type of accidentally disliked; because its dislike revolves with its cause – which is causing harm to others, even to an angel – in existence and non-existence; so if the cause is absent, such as when it is cooked, the dislike is absent. And by this is refuted what is said: 'A distinction between inherently and accidentally prohibited/disliked is not intelligible'; because if one considers the drinking in itself, it is permissible, but if one considers it as being connected to wine...

Commentary
This passage explains the distinction between rulings that are inherent (li-dhātihi) and those that are accidental (li-ʿāriḍ). It opens by defining the two categories: the ruling is inherent if it does not revolve around a rational cause (ʿilla) such that the ruling remains when the cause is absent; it is accidental if the ruling revolves around a cause, being present when the cause is present and absent when the cause is absent. The author then provides examples: adultery (zinā) is inherently prohibited because even if the cause (mixing lineages) is absent (e.g., with a minor girl), it is still prohibited. Similarly, wine is inherently prohibited because even if it does not intoxicate (e.g., due to tolerance or a small amount), it is still prohibited. In contrast, using usurped water for ablution is accidentally prohibited because its prohibition is due to aggression; if that cause is absent (i.e., water is not usurped), it is permissible. The same pattern applies to disliked acts: looking at one's wife's private part is inherently disliked because the dislike remains even if the cause (fear of blinding) is not present (e.g., if informed by a reliable person that no harm will occur). Eating raw onion is accidentally disliked because the dislike is due to causing harm to others; if the onion is cooked and no longer causes harm, the dislike vanishes. The passage ends by refuting the objection that there is no real difference between inherent and accidental categories, noting that when considering the act itself (e.g., drinking water), it is permissible, but when considered with an accidental attribute (e.g., being wine), it becomes prohibited.

The Hadith of the Prophet's Parents Revived
Sanusi cites and defends a hadith about the Prophet's parents being revived.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the content and significance of the hadith about the revival of the Prophet's parents, and the arguments for its authenticity.

Core Idea
A hadith's authenticity can be supported by theological reasoning and scholarly consensus.

This passage presents a specific hadith that reports a miracle involving the Prophet Muhammad's parents. The author, al-Sanusi, cites it with support from al-Suhayli and a poetic affirmation. You will explore how scholars defend this hadith and what it reveals about divine power and prophetic honor.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith narration
Begin by reading the hadith as narrated from 'Urwa and 'A'isha. Notice the key elements: the Prophet's request, the revival, and the parents' belief.
The hadith is reported by 'Urwa ibn al-Zubayr.
2
Understand al-Suhayli's principle
Al-Suhayli asserts that Allah is omnipotent and can grant special favors to His Prophet. This provides theological justification for the miracle.
This principle is central to accepting the hadith.
3
Analyze the poetic verse
Read the poetic lines that affirm the hadith and criticize those who reject it. Consider how poetry was used to reinforce scholarly positions.
The poet claims certainty about the revival.
4
Recognize al-Suyuti's contribution
Al-Suyuti wrote works on the salvation of the Prophet's parents, which shows the importance of this topic in Islamic theology.

The World's Lowliness: A Lesson for Others
Al-Bajuri explains the phrase 'or to alert to the baseness of the world' with hadith proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the act of turning away from worldly attachments serves as a powerful lesson to others about the world's insignificance before Allah.

Core Idea
Turning away from the world itself becomes a lesson about its worthlessness.

Al-Bajuri unpacks the phrase 'or to alert to the baseness of the world' by showing that the very act of turning away from worldly attractions is a visual lesson for others. Through three prophetic hadiths, he drives home the point that the world is contemptible in Allah's sight, and that believers should view themselves as strangers or wayfarers, not settlers. This passage connects our ascetic practice to its impact on those around us.

Guided Path
1
Read the explanation and the first two hadiths
Notice how Al-Bajuri defines 'alerting' as making others aware by one's own attitude. Then read the hadiths about the world being a carcass and worth less than a mosquito's wing.
These two hadiths emphasize the world's low value.
2
Understand the stranger and wayfarer analogy
Read the third hadith and Al-Bajuri's commentary distinguishing between a stranger (who may settle temporarily) and a wayfarer (who never stops). Reflect on which example fits a believer better.
3
Consider the marginal note by al-Ujhuri
Read the short gloss that links turning away and seeking consolation to difficult circumstances. Ask yourself how this connects to the main point.
This note shows that asceticism often arises from hardship, not just virtue.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory gloss on the phrase 'or to alert to the baseness of its worth with Allah Most High'. The commentator first clarifies that the purpose of turning away from the world is to alert others to its contemptible status before Allah. He explains that when people see those who are truly devoted turning away from the world with the same disgust a sensible person shows a carcass, they themselves become aware of the world's lowliness. He then provides three hadith proofs to confirm this lowly status: 1) The world is like a foul carcass; 2) It is not even worth a mosquito's wing to Allah; 3) The believer should be like a stranger or wayfarer, not taking permanent residence. He elaborates on the difference between a stranger (who may settle temporarily) and a wayfarer (who does not stay at all), emphasizing that the true believer should be like a wayfarer, always heading to the hereafter. The marginal note by al-Ujhuri adds that both turning away and seeking consolation are motivated by scarcity of wealth and other hardships, connecting the action to the experience of difficulty.

Short Hope: Counting Among the Dead and Sending Wealth Ahead
Al-Bajuri explains the metaphor and provides two hadith on hope and love of death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic stance on lengthy hope and the practical remedies to cultivate love for meeting Allah.

Core Idea
Lengthy hope is a disease; counting death and sending charity are its cure.

Al-Bajuri unpacks the instruction to count yourself among the dead, explaining it as a tool to cut lengthy hope. He then recounts two hadith: one rebuking Usama for long-term planning, and another prescribing charity to change one's attachment to life. Together they form a comprehensive attitude for the believer.

Guided Path
1
Understand the metaphor
Read the first sentence: 'And count yourself among the people of the graves' is a metaphor for constantly observing death and avoiding lengthy hope. Notice how this mindset counteracts the tendency to plan far ahead.
The metaphor is meant to be a practical daily reminder.
2
Examine the first hadith
Read the hadith about Usama bin Zayd. The Prophet criticized him for buying a slave with payment deferred for a month, calling that 'lengthy hope'. Then the Prophet described his own state: never taking a step or opening his eye without expecting death.
This shows that even moderate future planning can be blameworthy if it reflects attachment to this world.
3
Learn the remedy for not loving death
Read the second hadith. A man complained he did not love death. The Prophet asked if he had wealth, and told him to send it ahead (give charity). The explanation: a believer's heart is with his wealth; if he sends it ahead, he loves to meet it (death).
The marginal note defines 'lengthy hope' as assuming extended life, and notes a variant: 'with him' instead of 'about him' in the text.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory commentary by al-Bajuri on a section of Sanusi's Umm al-Barahin. It has been narrated that Muhammad ibn Abd al-Hakam said: I saw our Imam al-Shafi‘i (may Allah have mercy on him) in a dream, and I asked: What did Allah do with you, O Imam? He said: He had mercy on me and forgave me, and I was escorted to Paradise as a bride is escorted. I asked: By what did you reach this state? He said: By what is in the book 'al-Risala' of sending blessings upon the Messenger of Allah. I asked: And how is that prayer? He said: 'O Allah, bless our master Muhammad as many times as the rememberers remember You and the heedless are heedless of Your remembrance.' When I awoke, I took 'al-Risala' and looked, and found the matter as I had seen. And some righteous people said: I saw the Prophet (may Allah bless him and grant him peace) in a dream, and I said: O Messenger of Allah, what is the reward of al-Shafi‘i with you for what he said in the book 'al-Risala': 'And may Allah bless our master Muhammad whenever the rememberers remember You and the heedless are heedless of Your remembrance'? He said: His reward with me is that he will not be stopped for the reckoning.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal discussion about the reward for reciting a specific formula of salawat (blessings upon the Prophet) that was mentioned by al-Shafi‘i in his book al-Risala. The author first presents a scholarly dispute: some say each utterance yields a reward equal to the number of times it is said, while the verifiers (المحققون) say it yields a single but greater reward. He then supports the latter view through two dream visions. In the first, Muhammad ibn Abd al-Hakam sees al-Shafi‘i in Paradise and learns that his high rank is due to the salawat formula. In the second, a righteous person sees the Prophet himself, who declares that al-Shafi‘i will not be held for account because of this formula. The passage thus emphasizes the immense virtue of this particular form of salawat, while also acknowledging the scholarly opinion that the reward is one great reward rather than multiple rewards.

According to the 'verifiers' (المحققون), what is the reward for praying with the specific salawat formula?
A) A reward equal to the number of prayers said with that formula.
B) A single reward that is greater than the reward of prayer without the formula.
C) No reward at all.
D) A reward equal to the number of angels in the heavens.

Key Takeaway
The specific salawat formula attributed to al-Shafi‘i is so virtuous that it can lead to exemption from the reckoning, and the scholarly consensus of the verifiers holds that its reward is a single, immense reward.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you send salawat upon the Prophet, do you truly ponder the weight of your words, or do you repeat them mechanically? If al-Shafi‘i’s formula carried such immense reward partly because of its profound meaning and sincerity, what does your own habit of salawat reveal about your heart? Will you commit today to reform one utterance of salawat with full presence and understanding?
?
The Prophet declared that al-Shafi‘i would not be stopped for reckoning due to his salawat formula. If you were to stand before Allah tomorrow, would your salawat be a witness for you or against you? What specific change will you make this week to ensure your prayers upon the Prophet are not empty phrases but deeds that can lift you to that station?
?
Al-Shafi‘i attributed his entire bliss in the grave to a single passage he authored. If your deeds were shown to you in a dream, what would they say about the state of your worship? Are you satisfied with performing acts of devotion without depth, or will you now seek to infuse them with the same sincerity and reflection that made al-Shafi‘i’s words so powerful?
