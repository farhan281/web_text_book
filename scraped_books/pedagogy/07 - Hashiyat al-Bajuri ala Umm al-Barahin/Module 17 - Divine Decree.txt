Human Acts and the Middle Path of Ahl al-Sunna
Al-Bajuri explains divine unity in acts and refutes Mu'tazila and Jabriyya, affirming the Sunni position of kasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni doctrine of kasb (acquisition) and how it avoids the extremes of Mu'tazila (human creation of acts) and Jabriyya (compulsion).

Core Idea
The servant acquires, but Allah creates — the middle path that preserves both divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

The issue of human acts is one of the most contested in Islamic theology. In this passage, al-Bajuri explains that Allah is the sole creator of all acts, while the servant only ‘acquires’ them. This sets the Sunni position apart from two extreme sects: the Mu‘tazila, who give humans creative power, and the Jabriyya, who deny human agency entirely. The passage uses a vivid simile — pure milk from between dung and blood — to illustrate the balanced Sunni doctrine.

(And His saying: AND NOT IN HIS ACTS) One might initially understand from it that acts are of two kinds: one is His acts—exalted is He—and the other the acts of others, and the first kind is that in which the unity of acts lies, but that is not intended. Rather, the attribution is to clarify reality, because all acts that exist are wholly attributed to Him—exalted is He—and there is no second to Him in them, since the servant has no part in them except acquisition (al-kasb), contrary to the Mu'tazila in their statement that the servant creates his own voluntary acts by a power that God created in him, and contrary to the Jabriyya in their statement that the servant is compelled in his act like a feather suspended in the air, with no acquisition at all. So the Mu'tazila fell short when they said the servant creates his voluntary act. The Jabriyya exceeded bounds when they said he has no acquisition in it. And the Ahl al-Sunna took the middle path, saying that the servant does not create his act, but he has acquisition in it. The best of matters is the middle one, because it comes out from between dung and blood as pure milk, palatable to drinkers. Marginal note by the scholar Ahmad al-Ajhuri: (His saying: FROM BETWEEN DUNG) refers to the school of the Mu'tazila. (His saying: AND BLOOD) refers to the school of the Jabriyya with blood, because the school of the Jabriyya entails removal of obligation, so it necessitates unbelief. He compared the school of the Mu'tazila to dung, and the school of the Mu'tazila does not necessitate that, but rather only necessitates sin. Reports of the scholar Muhammad al-Anbabi: For the servant, dung is a metaphor for the school of the Mu'tazila, and blood is a metaphor for the school of the Jabriyya, because dung is said to be pure, unlike blood, so blood is worse. Yes, if one considers dung to be more repulsive than blood to the soul, then the matter is reversed. In any case, milk is a metaphor for the school of Ahl al-Sunna.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of deviant views on human acts and a defense of the Sunni doctrine of kasb (acquisition). It begins as a commentary on Sanusi's phrase 'and not in His acts.' Al-Bajuri notes that one might erroneously think that acts are divided into God's acts and others' acts, with unity of acts only applying to God's acts. He clarifies that the attribution of all acts to God is to indicate reality, since every existing act is wholly by God's creation. The servant only has acquisition (kasb) in his act, not creation. This is contrary to the Mu'tazila who claim the servant creates his own voluntary acts by a power God created in him, and contrary to the Jabriyya who claim the servant is compelled like a feather in the air with no acquisition. The Mu'tazila are said to have fallen short (farraṭu) by exaggerating human power, while the Jabriyya exceeded bounds (afraṭu) by denying human agency. Ahl al-Sunna take the middle path: the servant does not create his act but has acquisition. He then uses the simile of pure milk emerging from between dung and blood, indicating the Sunni position is the best and purest. The marginal notes explain the simile: dung represents the Mu'tazila, blood represents the Jabriyya, and milk represents Ahl al-Sunna. Blood is worse because it entails unbelief (kufr), while dung only entails sin (fisq). There is also a nuance about which is more repulsive, but the milk remains the best. The lesson affirms divine sovereignty while preserving human moral responsibility through kasb.

Commentary
This passage defines the positions of three theological groups on human action (kasb) using a vivid metaphor. The author Sanusi begins by stating: the Jabariyya went to excess by denying any acquisition (kasb) to the servant, while the Mu'tazila (implied in the metaphor) are represented by dung, and Ahl al-Sunna by pure milk. Then al-Ujhuri's marginal note clarifies: the dung refers to Mu'tazila, blood to Jabariyya; the Jabariyya view entails disbelief because it removes moral responsibility, while the Mu'tazila view only entails sin. Al-Anbabi adds: dung is considered pure, blood more repulsive, but if one finds dung more repulsive, the metaphor reverses; still, milk represents the Sunni position of affirming kasb without creating the action. The passage thus guards against two extremes: denying all human agency (Jabariyya) and exaggerating it (Mu'tazila), affirming the Sunni middle path.

His saying: 'Or there be with Him in existence an effecter, etc.' contains a refutation of the Mu'tazila in their saying that the servant creates his own voluntary actions by a power that Allah created in him. Commentary
This passage addresses the refutation of the Mu'tazila, who claim that humans create their own voluntary actions by a power created in them by Allah. Al-Bajuri states the correct view: the Mu'tazila are not to be declared disbelievers for this, because they do not equate human creation with divine creation—they still see humans as dependent on causes. The scholars of Transoxiana, however, held a stricter view, declaring them disbelievers and even considering the Magians better off, since the Mu'tazila posit many partners to Allah. The passage then explains a key principle derived from the same phrase: ordinary causes (fire, food, knife) have no inherent effect; all effect is from Allah alone. Al-Bajuri distinguishes four positions: (1) Belief that a cause has effect by itself—clear disbelief. (2) Belief that a cause has effect by a power Allah placed in it—this is sinful innovation, but not necessarily disbelief (there are two opinions, the preponderant being non-disbelief). (3) Belief that there is no effect from any cause, but a rational necessary concomitance exists (fire always causes burning)—this leads to denial of miracles and resurrection, so it leads to disbelief. (4) The correct belief: no effect, no necessary concomitance; a cause may exist without its effect, preserving miracles and resurrection. The passage ends with a supplication.

Al-Bajuri explains the principle that ordinary causes have no effect and categorizes four beliefs about causality with their rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four positions on the effect of customary causes and why only the belief in no effect and no necessary concomitance preserves Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Only the belief that causes have no effect and no necessary concomitance is sound, as it alone allows for miracles.

From the same phrase, al-Bajuri derives the principle that ordinary causes have no real effect. He then outlines four distinct beliefs regarding causality, grading them from outright disbelief to the correct Ash'ari position. This lesson is crucial for understanding how Muslim theologians safeguard the possibility of miracles and the resurrection.

قوله: أَوْ يَكُونَ مَعَهُ فِي الْوُجُودِ مُؤَثَرٌ إلخ) فيه رَدُّ على المعتزلة في قولهم: بأن العبد يخلق أفعال نفسه الاختيارية بقُدْرَةٍ خلقها الله فيه، والصحيح: عدمُ كُفْرِهم بذلك؛ لأنهم لم يجعلوا خالقيَّةَ العبد كخالقية الله تعالى، حيث جعلوا العبدَ مُفْتَقِراً إلى الأسباب والوسائط بخلافه تعالى. وذهب علماء ما وراء النهر إلى تكفيرهم، بل جعلوا المجوس أَسْعَدَ حالاً منهم؛ لأنهم لم يثبتوا الله إلا شريكاً واحداً، وهؤلاء أثبتوا الله شُرَكَاءَ كثيرة. ويُعلم من قوله: (أو يكون معه في الوجود مؤثر... إلخ): أنه لا تأثير للأسباب العادية في مسبباتها، فلا تأثير للنار في الحرق ولا للطعام في الشبع، ولا للسكين في القطع، وهكذا. فمن اعتقد أن شيئاً منها يؤثر بنفسه؛ فلا نِزَاعَ في كُفره، ومن اعتقد أن شيئاً منها يؤثِرُ بقوةٍ أَوْدَعَها الله فيه؛ فهو فاسق مبتدع، وفي كفره قولان، والراجح عدمُ كُفْره، كمن اعتقد أن العبدَ يَخْلُقُ أفعال نفسه الاختيارية بقوة خلقها الله فيه ومن اعتقد أنه لا تأثير لشيء منها، وإنما المؤثر هو الله تعالى، لكن بينها وبين مسبباتها تلازُمٌ عقليّ، فمتى وُجِدَتِ النارُ مثلاً؛ وُجِدَ الحرقُ، فهو جاهِلٌ بحقيقة الحكم، وربما جَرَّهُ ذلك إلى الكفر؛ لأنه قد يؤدّيه إلى إنكار الأمور الخارقة للعادة كمعجزات الأنبياء عليهم الصلاة والسلام وكبَعْثِ الأجسام، فلا ينجو إِلَّا مَن اعتقد أنه لا تأثير لشيء منها، وأنه لا تلازم بينها وبين مسبباتها بأن اعتقد صِحَّةَ التخلف، فيمكن أن يُوجَدَ السبب، ولا يُوجَدَ المسبب. والله هو الموفّق.
His saying: 'Or there be with Him in existence an effecter, etc.' contains a refutation of the Mu'tazila in their saying that the servant creates his own voluntary actions by a power that Allah created in him. The correct view is their non-disbelief due to that; because they did not make the creatorship of the servant like the Creator-ness of Allah, since they made the servant dependent on causes and intermediaries unlike Him. And the scholars of Transoxiana went to declaring them disbelievers, rather they considered the Magians more fortunate than them; because they did not assign to Allah except one partner, while these assigned to Allah many partners. And it is known from His saying 'Or there be with Him in existence an effecter... etc.' that there is no effect for the customary causes in their effects: no effect of fire in burning, nor of food in satiety, nor of knife in cutting, and so on. So whoever believes that any of them has effect by itself, there is no dispute about his disbelief. And whoever believes that any of them has effect by a power that Allah placed in it, he is a sinful innovator, and there are two opinions about his disbelief, the preponderant being his non-disbelief, like one who believes that the servant creates his own voluntary actions by a power created in him by Allah. And whoever believes that there is no effect from any of them, and that the only effecter is Allah, but between them and their effects there is a rational necessary concomitance, so whenever fire, for example, exists, burning exists, he is ignorant of the reality of the judgment, and perhaps that will lead him to disbelief; because it may lead him to denial of extraordinary matters like the miracles of the prophets (peace and blessings be upon them) and like the resurrection of bodies. So none is saved except one who believes that there is no effect from any of them, and that there is no necessary concomitance between them and their effects, by believing the possibility of non-concomitance, so that the cause may exist without the effect. And Allah is the Grantor of success.

Commentary
This passage addresses the refutation of the Mu'tazila, who claim that humans create their own voluntary actions by a power created in them by Allah. Al-Bajuri states the correct view: the Mu'tazila are not to be declared disbelievers for this, because they do not equate human creation with divine creation—they still see humans as dependent on causes. The scholars of Transoxiana, however, held a stricter view, declaring them disbelievers and even considering the Magians better off, since the Mu'tazila posit many partners to Allah. The passage then explains a key principle derived from the same phrase: ordinary causes (fire, food, knife) have no inherent effect; all effect is from Allah alone. Al-Bajuri distinguishes four positions: (1) Belief that a cause has effect by itself—clear disbelief. (2) Belief that a cause has effect by a power Allah placed in it—this is sinful innovation, but not necessarily disbelief (there are two opinions, the preponderant being non-disbelief). (3) Belief that there is no effect from any cause, but a rational necessary concomitance exists (fire always causes burning)—this leads to denial of miracles and resurrection, so it leads to disbelief. (4) The correct belief: no effect, no necessary concomitance; a cause may exist without its effect, preserving miracles and resurrection. The passage ends with a supplication.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: A common misunderstanding is that believing that fire burns by a power from Allah is acceptable, but this passage shows it is sinful innovation.
Correction: Correct belief is to deny any effect from fire, even by a power placed in it, as that still attributes effect to a creature.

Key Takeaway
Only believing in no effect from causes and no necessary concomitance preserves the possibility of miracles and resurrection.
