The Six Necessary Attributes of Allah
Sanusi lists the six necessary attributes of Allah, divided into two categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six attributes that are necessarily true of Allah and their division into the psychological attribute and the attributes of negation.

Core Idea
Allah's necessary attributes define His exclusive perfection and transcendence.

Imam al-Sanusi begins his discussion of Allah's necessary attributes by listing them in a concise yet profound statement. These attributes are foundational to Islamic theology, establishing that Allah is necessarily existent, eternal, unlike creation, self-subsistent, and absolutely one. This lesson unpacks these key concepts.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow. Notice the author's name and salutation, then the list of attributes.
The attributes are presented in a list after 'اعلم' (know).
2
Identify the two categories
The passage divides the attributes into two types: the psychological attribute (الصفة النفسية) which is 'واجب الوجود' (necessary of existence), and the attributes of negation (صفات السلوب) which include five attributes. Find each one.
Psychological here means essential, not psychological as in modern usage.
3
Understand each attribute
For each attribute, consider its meaning and significance. The commentary explains that 'قائم بنفسه' means independent of place and specifier.
These attributes are all about what Allah is not (negations) except for the first which is positive.
4
Reflect on the order
Notice the logical order: existence, then eternity, then difference, then self-subsistence, then unity. Why might this order be chosen?

Commentary
This passage is an introductory definition of the necessary attributes (الصفات الواجبة) of Allah. The author, Imam al-Sanusi, begins with the basmala and praise, then states his topic. He lists six attributes that are obligatory for Allah, which are divided into two categories: the psychological attribute (الصفة النفسية) which is 'necessary of existence' (واجب الوجود), and the attributes of negation (صفات السلوب) which are: pre-eternity (القدم), everlastingness (البقاء), being different from creation (مخالف لخلقه), being self-subsistent (قائم بنفسه) which means independent of place and specifier, and oneness (واحد) in essence, attributes, and actions. The author explains that 'self-subsistent' (قائم بنفسه) means Allah is not in need of a locus or a specifying agent. This sets the stage for the detailed discussion of each attribute. The order reflects a logical progression: first establishing that Allah's existence is necessary, then that He is eternal and everlasting, then that He is unlike anything created, then that He is self-sufficient and not dependent on anything, and finally that He is one and unique in every respect. These are foundational to Islamic theology.

The Meaning of ‘Ālam: From Language to Theology
Sanusi defines ‘ālam linguistically and theologically.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the term ‘ālam (world) is defined linguistically and how it was adopted by kalam theologians to refer to everything created.

Core Idea
The word ‘ālam points to creation’s distinction from the Creator.

Sanusi begins by unpacking the word ‘ālam (world) as it appears in the Quranic phrase “Lord of the worlds.” He traces its linguistic roots, explains its plural form, and shows how theologians repurposed it to refer to every originated thing. The key insight is that everything created bears signs that set it apart from its Creator.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic origin of 'ālam
Read the explanation of the root tarbiya and the plural 'ālamūn. Notice how language evolves.
"Tarbiya" originally meant nurturing, then came to mean ownership.
2
Grasp the lexical definition of 'ālam
Sanusi defines 'ālam as every species or genus with a distinguishing mark. Internalize the examples (humans, birds, animals, etc.).
The distinguishing mark is what makes it known.
3
See why theologians repurposed the term
The mutakallimūn transferred 'ālam to every created thing because each creation has signs that distinguish it from the eternal Creator.
This prevents any confusion between Creator and creation.
4
Connect to the broader purpose
Reflect on how this definition lays the groundwork for proving that the world is originated (hādith) and thus points to a Creator.
This will be developed later.

Discourse on the world, regarding its naming and its indication of God Almighty. Lord of the worlds. The origin of 'tarbiya' is: transferring something from one state to another until it reaches the goal intended by the one who raises it. Then it was transferred to the owner and the reformer because raising is usually necessary for them. And 'al-ʿālamūn' is a sound masculine plural of 'al-ʿālam' irregularly. And 'al-ʿālam' in language is: every species or genus in which there is a sign by which it is distinguished from other originated species and genera. So it is said concerning species: the world of humans, the world of birds, the world of horses; and it is said concerning genera: the world of animals, the world of bodies, the world of growing things. And it is possible that the appropriateness in naming the species and genus as 'ʿālam' is that they have distinguishing features and properties by which they are known. And the mutakallimūn transferred it to every originated thing. And the appropriateness in this naming is that every originated thing has signs that distinguish it from its Maker, the Eternal Lord, so that it is never confused with Him. And for this reason our Lord, Mighty and Majestic, refuted the astray ones who made for Him...

Commentary
This passage is a linguistic and technical analysis of the term 'ālam' (world). Sanusi begins with the Quranic phrase 'Lord of the worlds' and then explains the root 'tarbiya' (nurturing) and how it came to mean ownership and reform. Then he discusses the plural 'ālamūn' as an irregular sound masculine plural. In language, 'ālam' refers to any species or genus that has a distinguishing mark. He gives examples: species like humans, birds, horses; genera like animals, bodies, growing things. The appropriateness is that they are known by their differentiating features. Then the mutakallimūn (theologians) transferred the term to every originated thing (hādith), i.e., everything created. The appropriateness here is that every created thing has signs that distinguish it from the eternal Creator, preventing any confusion. Finally, he mentions that God refuted those who set up partners with Him, implying that the signs of creation point to the sole Creator.

The Meaning of 'Seal of the Prophets'
Sanusi defines 'Khatam an-Nabiyyin' and deduces its logical implication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the literal meaning of 'Khatam an-Nabiyyin' and its necessary implication that no messenger can come after Prophet Muhammad.

Core Idea
The Seal of Prophecy closes the door to all prophets and messengers.

Sanusi begins by defining the term 'Khatam an-Nabiyyin' as the last of the prophets. He then derives an important logical consequence: since prophethood is a broader category than messengerhood, the end of prophethood necessarily means the end of messengerhood as well. This lesson unpacks that definition and the principle behind it.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the literal meaning.
Notice the logical structure of definition then implication.
2
Identify the logical principle
The passage uses a logic rule: negation of the more general (prophet) implies negation of the more specific (messenger). Understand why this is valid.
This principle is central in Islamic theology and law.
3
Reflect on significance
Consider why this distinction matters: it settles the finality of prophethood and messengerhood.

ومعنى خاتم النبيين أنه آخرهم، وبه كمل عددهم الذي هو مئة ألف وأربعة وعشرون ألفا، فلا نبي بعده، ومن لازمه: أن لا رسول بعده؛ لأن النبي أعم من الرسول على الصحيح، ونفي الأعم يستلزم نفي الأخص؛ فكمل سبحانه لنبينا ومولانا محمد عليه الصلاة والسلام جميع المحاسن التي تفرقت في الأنبياء والرسل قبله، وشرع شريعته السمحة؛ بأن جعل أحكامها متصلة بالآخرة، لا ناسخ لها ولا مبدل لها، وأطلع أمته المشرّفة على مساوي الأمم الذين خلوا وعلى العقوبات التي نزلت بهم؛ ليعتبروا بذلك ويرتدعوا عن المعاصي ولا يغتروا بالمهلة ومتعة الدنيا كما اغتر بذلك الذين هلكوا قبلهم، فجعلهم مولانا بفضله معتبرين لا معتبرا بهم، ومتعظين وشاهدين على غيرهم لا مشهودا عليهم، وأظهر سبحانه محاسنهم لمن مضى من الأمم وستر مساوئهم، بل نوه المولى الكريم بقدرهم وقدر نبيهم سيدنا ومولانا محمد عليه الصلاة والسلام تنويها تمنى بسببه كليم الله تعالى موسى صلى الله عليه وسلم أن يكون من هذه الأمة. وبالجملة: فنعم مولانا الكريم جل وعلا ومواهبه الاختصاصية التي خص بها نبينا ومولانا محمدا صلى الله عليه وسلم دنيا وأخرى لا يمكن إحصاؤها، نسأله سبحانه أن يجعلنا من خيار أمته، الفائزين بشرف قربه ومتابعته، المتحصنين من كل محنة وهول وخوف دنيا وأخرى بحرم محبته وولايته.
And the meaning of 'Seal of the Prophets' is that he is the last of them, and with him their number, which is one hundred twenty-four thousand, was completed, so there is no prophet after him, and from its necessary implication: that there is no messenger after him; because 'prophet' is more general than 'messenger' according to the correct view, and negation of the more general necessitates negation of the more specific. Commentary
This passage defines the term 'Khatam an-Nabiyyin' (Seal of the Prophets) and explains its theological implications. The author begins by stating the literal meaning: that the Prophet Muhammad is the last prophet, and with him the total number of prophets (124,000) is completed. He then draws a necessary implication: since prophethood is a more general category than messengerhood, denying that any prophet comes after him also denies that any messenger comes after him. The argument relies on the principle that negating the broader category (prophets) necessarily negates the narrower (messengers). The author then highlights the perfection bestowed upon the Prophet Muhammad: all excellences that were scattered among previous prophets were combined in him. His law (shari'a) is made continuous until the Hereafter, without abrogation or replacement. The community of Muhammad is given a privileged role: they are witnesses over other nations, as indicated in the Quranic verse (2:143). They are admonishers, not admonished; witnesses, not witnessed against. God also showed the virtues of this community to previous nations while concealing their faults. In fact, Prophet Moses (as) wished to be part of this community due to its high status. The passage concludes with a general statement that the favors and special gifts bestowed upon Prophet Muhammad (s) are innumerable, followed by a supplication to be among the best of his community.

Commentary
This passage presents a definition of 'knowledge' (ma'rifa) as required by the Law (sharʿ). It begins by stating the obligation for every morally responsible person to know what is obligatory, impossible, and permissible concerning Allah and the messengers—a classification that will be detailed later. Commentary
This passage defines what every legally responsible person (mukallaf) must know about Allah and the prophets, and then provides a precise definition of true knowledge (ma'rifa). The author begins by stating the obligation to know the three rational judgments (obligatory, impossible, permissible) concerning Allah and likewise concerning the messengers. He then defines 'originated knowledge' (al-ma'rifa al-haditha) as 'decisive belief conforming [to reality] from necessity or proof'. He unpacks each condition: First, 'decisive belief' (jazzm) excludes conjecture (zann), doubt (shakk), and illusion (wahm). Second, 'conforming' (mutabiq) excludes compound ignorance (al-jahl al-murakkab), which is a firm but false belief, giving examples from philosophers and previous religions. Third, the source must be 'necessity or proof' (darura or burhan), which excludes the decisive belief of the imitator (muqallid) even if it happens to be true; such a belief is merely called 'belief' (i'tiqad) in terminology, not knowledge. Then the author explains each source: 'necessity' is when Allah compels the soul to a certain belief without reflection, like self-awareness or basic mathematical truths. 'Proof' is a composite argument from certain premises that are either self-evident or ultimately reducible to necessary knowledge. He illustrates proof with a worked example: determining the price of a commodity requires combining necessary premises (e.g., a quarter of four is one) with non-necessary premises that nevertheless lead to necessity through repeated reasoning (e.g., four is a tenth of forty). The example shows how demonstration builds certainty from foundational necessary truths.

He then explains that this judgment of possibility can be made either without reflection (bila ta'mul) – like the whiteness of a body, which is known necessarily through observation – or after reflection (ba'da al-ta'mul), like wishing for death. The author argues that the more general validity of existence and non-existence (the possibility itself) is even more certainly necessary than the specific observed case (e.g., whiteness). Then he turns to the example of wishing for death. For healthy people who have not suffered severe calamities, the possibility of a rational person wishing for death is a theoretical judgment: they initially think it impossible, but after considering that there are calamities worse than death, they realize it is possible (not obligatory or impossible). However, for one who has experienced such causes (fear, hope, yearning), the permissibility is known necessarily, without reflection. The author then cites poetry from Labid and another poet expressing weariness with life, and the famous hadith about Abu Bakr understanding that the Prophet was given the choice between worldly splendor and what is with Allah, and chose the latter – illustrating that wishing for death (or preferring the afterlife) can be permissible. The overall purpose is to clarify that the classification of a judgment as necessary or theoretical can depend on the observer's knowledge state.

Why the Phrase Attaches to 'Know' Not 'Must'
Sanusi explains why the phrase about events depends is attached to 'know' not 'must'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the phrase 'for the dependence of events' is grammatically attached to 'know' (اعلم) rather than to 'must' (يجب) in the creed, and the theological reason behind it.

Core Idea
The phrase explains our knowledge of God's necessity, not God's necessity itself.

Sanusi addresses a subtle grammatical point in the creed that has deep theological implications. The phrase 'for the dependence of events on His existence' might seem to explain why God's existence is necessary, but attaching it to 'must' would imply that God's necessity depends on events. Sanusi clarifies that it is attached to 'know', making the events a cause of our knowledge, not of God's necessity.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and theological clarification about a phrase in the creed: 'for the dependence of events on His existence'. Sanusi explains that the prepositional phrase ('for the dependence') is attached to the verb 'know' (اعلم) not to 'must' (يجب).
