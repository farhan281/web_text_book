Wisdom of the Gradual Revelation of the Quran
Al-Bayli answers why the Quran was revealed gradually rather than all at once.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two main wisdoms behind the gradual revelation of the Quran as explained by al-Bayli: strengthening the Prophet's heart and facilitating memorization.

Core Idea
The Quran was revealed gradually to fortify the Prophet's heart and make it easy to memorize.

Why was the Quran sent down piece by piece rather than all at once like previous scriptures? This question was posed by the disbelievers and answer directly given in the Quran itself. In this passage, al-Bayli draws from that Quranic response and adds a second wisdom, showing that God's method of revelation was deliberate and full of mercy.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that the passage begins with a question and then cites the Quran directly.
2
Identify the question being answered
The passage starts by stating that previous books were revealed all at once, then asks: what is the wisdom in the Quran being revealed gradually?
This sets up the need for a divine answer.
3
Find the Quranic verse and its explanation
Al-Bayli quotes Quran 25:32, where Allah responds to the same objection. The key phrase is 'li-nuthabbita bihi fu'adaka' (to strengthen your heart). This is the primary wisdom.
The primary wisdom is spiritual: to fortify the Prophet.
4
Note the secondary wisdom
After the Quranic answer, al-Bayli adds 'or: to make it easy to memorize.' This is a second wisdom, contrasting with previous scriptures that were not memorized due to their size.
Gradual revelation aids preservation of the Quran in memory.
5
Reflect on the verse count
The passage ends with the total number of Quranic verses (6,666) and mentions that a thousand are commands. This gives a sense of the scale of revelation.
While the text cuts off, it shows the completeness of the Quran.

Commentary
This passage is a question-and-answer about the wisdom of the gradual revelation (tanzil mufarraq) of the Quran, attributed to al-Allama al-Bayli. It notes that previous scriptures were revealed all at once (jumlatan wahidatan), raising the question of why the Quran was revealed piecemeal. The answer is drawn directly from the Quran: Allah says in Surah al-Furqan (25:32) that He sent it gradually 'that We may strengthen thereby your heart' (li-nuthabbita bihi fu'adaka). This is the primary wisdom: to fortify the Prophet's heart. A secondary reason suggested is to make it easy to memorize (li-yahfazahu bi-suhula), unlike other books which are not memorized due to their size. The passage then gives the total number of Quranic verses as 6,666, with a thousand being commands, though the text breaks off.

Wisdom of Gradual Revelation and Verse Categories
Al-Bayli explains the wisdom of gradual revelation and enumerates the categories of Quranic verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the wisdom behind the gradual revelation of the Quran and the detailed categorization of its verses.

Core Idea
The Quran was revealed gradually to strengthen the Prophet and make memorization easy.

Al-Bayli addresses a common question: why was the Quran revealed piecemeal while other scriptures were sent down all at once? He provides the answer from the Quran itself and then offers a detailed breakdown of the types of verses in the Quran, showing its comprehensiveness.

Read the opening where the question is posed: why did the Quran come down piece by piece while other books were revealed all at once?
This question was actually asked by the disbelievers, and Allah answered it.
2
Identify the divine answer
Find the two wisdoms given: to strengthen the Prophet's heart and to make memorization easier.
The answer is directly from Quran 25:32.
3
Study the verse categories

These categories show the comprehensive nature of the Quran.
4
Reflect on the purpose of the statistics
Consider why al-Bayli provides this breakdown. It demonstrates the Quran's completeness and that every type of content has a purpose.
This also helps in understanding the scope of the Quran's guidance.

Ruling on Denial of Quran and Refutation of Ibn Mas'ud Claim
Al-Bayli rules on denial of Quranic verses, addresses the false claim about Ibn Mas'ud, and provides scholarly refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ruling on denying any part of the Quran and the truth about the attribution to Ibn Mas'ud regarding the mu'awwidhatayn.

Core Idea
Denying even a single letter of the Quran is kufr, and the claim that Ibn Mas'ud rejected the mu'awwidhatayn is false.

This passage addresses two important topics: the severe consequence of denying any part of the Quran, and a historical claim about a Companion. It clarifies that Ibn Mas'ud's omission of the mu'awwidhatayn from his personal mushaf was due to a methodological principle, not because he denied they were Quran.

Guided Path
1
Read the ruling on denial
Start from 'فمن أنكر آية منه أو حرفاً كفر' and understand that denying any verse or letter makes one a disbeliever.
This includes both rejection and addition with the belief that it is from the Quran.
2
Understand the case of denying a revealed book
Note that denying any of the revealed books also constitutes kufr.
This is a separate category from denying the Quran.
3
Examine the claim about Ibn Mas'ud
The passage says the attribution that Ibn Mas'ud considered the mu'awwidhatayn not from the Quran is false.
4
Learn the true explanation
Al-Baqillani explains that Ibn Mas'ud did not write them in his personal mushaf because he only included what the Prophet commanded to be written, not because he denied them.
This is a crucial distinction: omission for a methodological reason is not rejection.
5
Confirm with later scholars
Al-Nawawi and Ibn Hazm explicitly state that the report is a lie and fabricated.
This solidifies that the claim is baseless.

The Quran's Comprehensive Content and the Ruling of Denial
Sanusi enumerates types of Quranic verses and states rulings on denial and addition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the types of verses in the Quran and the severe ruling that denying any verse or letter is disbelief, as well as the rulings on denying the Basmala, adding a letter, and denying a revealed book.

Core Idea
The Quran's every letter is protected; denying even one is kufr.

The Quran contains various types of verses, from commands to stories, and it is complete in every way. Imam Sanusi begins by enumerating these categories to emphasize the Quran's comprehensiveness, then lays down unmistakable rulings: whoever denies a single verse or letter commits disbelief. This lesson establishes the inviolability of the Quranic text.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage carefully, then its literal translation. Pay attention to the numbers and categories mentioned.
Notice the precise enumeration of verse types.
2
Identify the enumerated categories
The passage lists several types of verses: prohibitions, promises, threats, parables, stories, lawful/unlawful, supplications, and abrogating/abrogated. Count them as you go.
3
Note the four rulings on denial and addition
The passage then states rulings: (1) denying a verse or letter, (2) denying the Basmala, (3) adding a letter while believing it is from the Quran, and (4) denying a revealed book. All result in kufr.
The brief mention of Ibn Mas'ud is a dismissal; the detailed refutation comes later.

And their saying: 'with Your praise' means: we declare transcendence while we are praising You; that is, praising You with every perfection in every state; so the 'ba' is for accompaniment. Or they declare transcendence due to the grace of Your success which necessitates Your praise and gratitude, not by our own power or strength; so the 'ba' on this is causative, and it is from the expression of the effect for the cause, because praise in the sense of gratitude is caused by blessings. And it is possible that the meaning is: we declare transcendence by Your praise itself; that is, by praising You with every perfection, because praising with perfection is transcendence from its opposite; so the 'ba' is instrumental. And Allah knows best. And their saying: 'and we sanctify You' means, and Allah knows best, we sanctify ourselves; that is, we purify ourselves from every bad thought, for Your sake; that is, for Your pleasure and independence from all other than You. And it is possible that the meaning is: we purify our hearts for Your service and worship, for service and worship are only proper with a heart pure from all impurities. And as for His reply, Glorious and Exalted, to them: 'Indeed I know what you do not know' — its meaning, and Allah knows best, is: Even if I place on earth one who causes corruption and sheds blood, I know in that wisdom and interests that occur by pure choice, not by necessity and obligation, which you cannot encompass with knowledge. And the remainder of the meanings in the verse belong to exegesis. And with Allah is success.

Commentary
This passage explains the angels' question in Surah al-Baqarah. The author first identifies the passage as a clarification of the angels' intent. He states that their question was purely for inquiry (istifham li-mujarrad al-isti'lam), not for denial or objection (inkar or i'tirad), which would have been disbelief. To prove this, he points to the angels' immediately following statement: 'while we exalt You with Your praise and sanctify You.' This serves as a precaution (ihtiras) preventing any misinterpretation. Then the author explains the meaning of 'nusabbihu bihamdika'. He gives three possible interpretations for the 'ba' in bihamdika: (1) accompaniment (li al-musahabah): they declare transcendence while praising; (2) causative (sababiyyah): they declare transcendence due to the grace of His success; (3) instrumental (al-ala): they declare transcendence by praising itself. Similarly, he explains 'nuqaddisu laka' as purifying themselves for God's sake, or purifying their hearts for service. Finally, he explains God's reply: it means that God knows wisdom and interests behind placing a vicegerent that are based on free choice, not necessity, and that humans and angels cannot fully comprehend. The author notes that further meanings belong to tafsir.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The angels' question was an objection to God's plan, implying they knew better.
Correction: The passage clarifies it was an inquiry for knowledge, and their immediate praise and sanctification show their absolute submission and reverence, refuting the idea of objection.
