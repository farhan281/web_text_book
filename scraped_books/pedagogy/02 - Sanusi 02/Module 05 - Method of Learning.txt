The Necessity of Rational Proof for Creedal Matters
Sanusi argues that rational proof is indispensable for some creedal matters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that some creedal matters cannot be properly believed without rational proof, and that one must grasp the three rational judgments (wujub, istihalah, jawaz) through active reflection.

Core Idea
Rational proof is necessary for creed; mere hearing of revelation is not enough.

In this passage, Imam Sanusi addresses the student directly, warning that passive acceptance of revelation does not constitute proper belief in certain creedal matters. He introduces the three fundamental rational judgments—obligation, impossibility, and possibility—that one must actively perceive. The passage then grounds this need in Quranic injunctions to reflect and reason.

Guided Path

2

Find the sentence where Sanusi states that some creedal matters cannot do without rational proof. What does he mean by 'cannot do without'?
This claim is radical: rational proof is not optional.
3
Understand the three judgments
The text mentions three judgments: wujub, istihalah, jawaz. Consider how these apply to creedal statements (e.g., Allah exists is wajib; Allah is created is mustahil).
These are the building blocks of kalam reasoning.
4
Reflect on the Quranic evidence
Read the two verses cited. How do they support the need for rational reflection? The first rebukes lack of tadabbur, the second condemns lack of 'aql.
The Quran itself commands reflection.
5
Consider the implication
This does not deny revelation; it asserts that revelation must be understood rationally. Discuss how this balances transmitted knowledge with reasoned conviction.

Commentary
This passage is a direct admonition to the reader about the necessity of rational proof ('dalil 'aqli) in certain creedal matters. The author states that some beliefs cannot be correctly held without understanding the rational basis—specifically, grasping the judgment of obligation (wujub), impossibility (istihalah), or possibility (jawaz) concerning the matter. Mere passive hearing of divine revelation or authentic hadith is insufficient; one must actively reflect (tafakkur) on the proof. The author supports this with two Quranic verses: one condemning those who do not reflect on the Quran, and another comparing those who do not use reason to the worst of creatures. Thus, rational engagement is mandatory.

The Quran's Rational Appeal
Sanusi explains why non-Muslims accepted Islam and the necessity of kalam scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quran addresses the intellect and why the mutakallimun are essential guides in matters of creed.

Core Idea
The Quran speaks to the mind, not just the heart.

This lesson explores a powerful observation by Sanusi: non-Muslims were drawn to Islam because the Quran directly addressed their intellect. He explains how clear proofs combined with sound reason lead to faith, and warns that without proper guidance, even believers can be shaken by doubts. The passage sets the stage for the role of kalam scholars as navigators in the wilderness of confusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on Quran's appeal
Read the Arabic passage from 'وإنما عظُمَ شأن القرآن' to 'مشاعل الهداية إلى طرق الرشاد'. Then read the translation to grasp the flow.
Notice how Sanusi describes the Quran as 'speaking to the ears of their minds' – a metaphor for rational appeal.
2
Identify the two lights
Find the phrase 'نوري العقل السليم والدين القويم' (the two lights of sound reason and upright religion). Understand that Sanusi sees both as complementary.
This is a key principle: faith and reason are allies.
3
Understand the role of mutakallimun
Sanusi says the mutakallimun are guides in the wilderness of doubts. Ask yourself: why do people need such guides?
The passage implies that without kalam, people can easily fall into deviation.

Commentary
This passage combines praise for the Quran's rational appeal with a sharp criticism of those who reject kalam under the banner of Ahl al-Hadith. First, the author explains that non-Muslims were attracted to the Quran because it addresses the intellect ('uqul); they found clear proofs and were guided to faith by the combination of sound reason and true religion. He then warns that many who think they have strong certainty are quickly swayed by doubts. The role of mutakallimun and creed scholars is to guide such people and refute deviations. Second, he criticizes a group that calls itself Ahl al-Hadith and Athar but actually replaces the science of creed with anthropomorphism (tashbih) and misguidance. They accuse the great scholars (a'lam al-umma) of deviation, yet these scholars have a more comprehensive knowledge of the revealed texts than they do. The author emphasizes that the real issue is not recitation or memorization but deep understanding (fiqh) of the meanings and subtle indications of the texts. He likens these superficial adherents to a child who merely repeats what the teacher recites, lacking true comprehension. The passage thus defends the science of kalam and its practitioners as the true heirs of prophetic understanding.

Critique of Pseudo-Ahl al-Hadith
Sanusi criticizes those who claim to follow hadith but reject kalam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's criticism of those who reject kalam while claiming to be Ahl al-Hadith, emphasizing the importance of deep understanding (fiqh) over mere memorization.

Core Idea
Recitation without understanding is like a child's parrot.

In this passage, Sanusi turns a critical eye on a group that calls itself Ahl al-Hadith but actually rejects the science of kalam, accusing its scholars of deviation. He argues that true scholarship lies not in memorizing verses and hadiths but in grasping their deep meanings and subtle indications. The lesson defends the mutakallimun as the true inheritors of prophetic understanding.

Read the passage on pseudo-Ahl al-Hadith

2
Identify the accusation
Sanusi says this group 'exchanges the science of creed for the speech of the people of anthropomorphism and misguidance.' What does he mean?
They have abandoned true kalam for a distorted literalist approach.
3
Understand the criterion of true knowledge
Sanusi emphasizes that the mutakallimun know the texts more comprehensively ('أجمع'). The real issue is fiqh, not sarda (recitation).
This is a key distinction: deep understanding vs. surface-level repetition.

Editorial Warning: Interpreting Revelation with Discipline
Editorial passage warns against capricious interpretation and introduces the Sanusiyyat series.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the importance of disciplined interpretation of the Quran and Sunnah guided by reason, language, and usul, and the recognition of the Sanusiyyat series as an accepted scholarly work.

Core Idea
Interpreting revelation requires disciplined tools—reason, language, and usul—not caprice or fanaticism.

This editorial passage from Dar al-Taqwa sets the stage for the Sanusiyyat series by warning against common misinterpretations of the Quran and Sunnah. It critiques those who rely on caprice, fanaticism, or a few deviant scholars, and instead emphasizes the need for sound methodology. The passage also introduces the Sanusiyyat series as a trusted resource for learning Islamic creed.

Methodology of work in the book. The books of Imam al-Sanusi are generally widespread and well-known, as is not hidden, and his 'Sanusiyyat' are among the creedal books that a researcher in fundamentals can hardly do without. Effort has been made to revive this blessed series in a well-dressed edition, with solid text, no ambiguity nor confusion, flexible [in style], with its paragraphs titled, helping the student of knowledge when examining it to understand it. Commentary
This passage provides the author's justification (munāsaba) for including the invocation of blessings and peace upon the Prophet immediately after the praise of God (ḥamd) at the beginning of his creed. He enumerates three complete reasons and begins a fourth that is cut off. The first reason (al-awwal) characterizes the ṣalāh and salām as a specific praise (ḥamd khāṣṣ) following the general praise (ḥamd ʿāmm). The second reason (al-thānī) emphasizes gratitude (shukr) for the means through whom favors are conveyed: after thanking God for all favors, we thank the Prophet as the intermediary, because the Prophet commanded thanking people, and since we cannot adequately reward him ourselves, we ask God to reward him by sending ṣalāh (as a form of honor) and salām (as magnification, including hearing God's speech). The third reason (al-thālith) connects the ḥamd to the Prophet's role in teaching true monotheism: before his mission, people misdirected praise to idols and false causes; the Prophet taught that all praise belongs solely to God, and this teaching is encapsulated in Sūrat al-Fātiḥa, which is called the Mother of the Qurʾān. The fourth reason is only begun and appears incomplete.

Commentary

The passage thus carefully distinguishes between levels of certainty and the role of demonstration.

Demonstrative Proof and the Degrees of Certainty
Sanusi defines demonstrative proof and uses an example to classify types of certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how demonstrative proof (burhan) produces certain knowledge from necessary premises, and how that differs from blind taqlid and mere probability.

Core Idea
Real knowledge comes from proof; believing a fact on authority is not knowledge.

The first lesson gave us necessary knowledge. But many truths require reasoning. Sanusi now introduces demonstrative proof (burhan) as a compound of certain premises. He uses a detailed arithmetic example to show how we can reach certain knowledge through demonstration, and he distinguishes that from accepting a result on trust (taqlid) or having only a probable opinion. Let's follow his careful analysis.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of burhan
Read the definition: a proof composed of certain necessary premises, either immediately or ultimately. This is the core idea.
2
Walk through the arithmetic example
Follow the example of the commodity bought for 'a quarter of a tenth of forty dirhams'. Notice how two premises are used: one immediate (one is a quarter of four) and one that becomes necessary after verification (four is a tenth of forty). See how the conclusion is reached.
This illustrates the structure of a demonstration.
3
Understand the three epistemic categories
Sanusi then distinguishes three outcomes: (1) certain knowledge from demonstration (called 'ilm/ma'rifa), (2) certain belief from trusting an authority (called 'i'tiqad sahih), and (3) a preponderant belief without certainty (just probability).
Only the first qualifies as true knowledge.

The First Division: Articles Requiring Rational Proof
Sanusi defines the first division and explains the circularity argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why certain foundational articles of faith—like God's existence and attributes—can only be known through rational proof, not scripture.

Core Idea
Some truths must be known before we can trust any messenger.

Sanusi begins his classification by identifying the foundational beliefs that make prophecy possible. If these were proven by scripture, a vicious circle would result. This lesson explores that crucial first division and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice how Sanusi defines the first division and gives examples before presenting the circularity argument.
The circularity is key: scripture depends on miracle, miracle depends on God's existence, so scripture cannot prove God's existence.
2
Identify the examples
List the attributes Sanusi gives as examples: existence, power, will, knowledge, life. Ask: why are these essential for a miracle to be possible?
These are necessary attributes of God that a miracle presupposes.
3
Understand the circularity
Trace the argument: if we tried to prove God's existence using scripture, we would need to verify the miracle that attests the messenger, but the miracle already requires God's existence. This is a logical circle.
The Arabic term 'دور' (dawr) means circular reasoning.
4
Reflect on the principle
Ask yourself: Why does Sanusi start with this division? What does it tell us about the role of reason in theology?
It establishes that reason is independent and necessary for grounding revelation.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the articles of faith (ʿaqāʾid al-īmān) according to the method of proof. Sanusi divides them into three categories. Even necessary truths can be disputed in their method of proof.

The two imams, Imām al-Ḥaramayn and al-Fakhr [al-Rāzī], held the first view, and some investigators, among them the scholar Sharaf al-Dīn al-Tilimsānī, held the second, and this is what the author [held] in al-Kubrā, so refer to it.)
Source pages p. 174

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the articles of faith (ʿaqāʾid al-īmān) according to the method of proof. Sanusi divides them into three categories. First: those that can only be known by rational evidence (al-dalīl al-ʿaqlī). These are doctrines upon which the very validity of a miracle depends—specifically, God's existence, power, will, knowledge, and life. If one tried to prove these by scriptural evidence, which depends on the veracity of the messenger, which in turn depends on a miracle, a vicious circle (al-dawr) would result. Thus rational proof is indispensable. Second: those that can be validly inferred by scriptural evidence (al-dalīl al-sharʿī). These are doctrines that do not affect the validity of a miracle—like the attributes of hearing, sight, and speech, and the resurrection and afterlife details. These are known from revelation. Third: those about which there is disagreement, such as the oneness of God (al-waḥdāniyya). The dispute is whether it falls into the first or second category. One argument says scriptural evidence is sufficient because the investigator's knowledge of the miracle does not depend on knowing God's oneness, even though in reality a miracle cannot occur if there were a partner with God. The other argument requires rational evidence because the miracle's validity itself depends on the impossibility of a partner. A footnote adds that Imām al-Ḥaramayn and al-Fakhr al-Rāzī held the first opinion (scripture suffices), while some investigators like Sharaf al-Dīn al-Tilimsānī and the author in his larger work al-Kubrā held the second (rational proof is needed).
