The Sixth Creed of Sanusi and Its Attribution
Author lists names and discusses attribution of the sixth creed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the various names of the sixth creed attributed to Imam al-Sanusi and the scholarly debate surrounding its attribution.

Core Idea
The sixth creed is widely known but its attribution is cautiously debated.

In this lesson, we explore a short creed often called the 'sixth creed' of Imam al-Sanusi. You will learn its alternative names, its description as a concise and clear text, and the careful scholarly discussion about whether it was actually written by al-Sanusi. This lesson highlights the importance of source criticism in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the names of the creed
Read the passage and list all the names given to this creed. Notice that it is called 'al-Sughra sughra al-sughra' (the smallest of the small), 'al-Hafida', 'Women's Creed', 'Women and Children's Creed', and 'al-Wajiza'.
Each name highlights a different aspect: size, audience, or conciseness.
2
Understand the description of the creed
The author describes the creed as very short (two pages), easy in expression, and clear in proofs. Reflect on why such a simple text might be attributed to a great scholar like al-Sanusi.
3
Analyze the attribution debate
Focus on the author's discussion: there is no definitive evidence, only a report from al-Hafnawi. The multiplicity of early commentaries supports probabilistic certainty, but scholars like al-Shakani remained cautious. Consider the balance between confidence and skepticism.
This shows scholarly honesty in the face of uncertain evidence.

The sixth creed: al-Allamah al-Hafnawi al-Disi mentioned it in 'Ta'rif al-Khalaf bi Rijal al-Salaf' in the biography of Muhammad ibn al-Buni, where he said: 'He composed the sixth creed of Sanusi, and it is a creed unknown to many people, and its author al-Allamah Sayyidi Abd al-Rahman al-Jami'i explained it. It is said that the Shaykh wrote it for women and children.' And other designations are associated with this creed, which it is appropriate to mention before discussing it; they are: al-Sughra sughra al-sughra, al-Aqida al-Hafida, Aqida al-Nisa', Aqida al-Niswan wa al-Sibyan, al-Aqida al-Wajiza. All these titles are for a very fine creed, not exceeding two pages, easy in expression, clear in proofs, close – as described – to all minds despite the variation of their understandings. But there is no definitive evidence establishing the attribution of this blessed creed, whose content is agreed upon, to Imam al-Sanusi. Rather, the text transmitted to you from al-Allamah al-Hafnawi is what later scholars relied upon to prove it. Yes, the multiplicity of its commentaries, the agreement of their expressions, and the historical precedence of these commentaries – many of them dating from the beginning of the eleventh century AH to its middle – may make us trust its attribution to Imam al-Sanusi, although Imam al-Shakani – one of its commentators – sufficed with his statement: 'Among what was attributed to him – meaning Imam al-Sanusi – was the creed called al-Hafida.' This from Imam al-Shakani is a word of fairness and verification. If he had at hand then what confirmed its attribution to Imam al-Sanusi, he would have stated it decisively and definitively, but he sufficed with this expression of uncertainty, and he did not object to this skepticism.

Commentary
This passage is a textual-historical discussion about a creed known as 'the sixth creed' attributed to Imam al-Sanusi. The author begins by citing al-Hafnawi, who reports that al-Sanusi composed a sixth creed, unknown to many, and that it was commented upon by Abd al-Rahman al-Jami'i, and that it was said to have been written for women and children. The author then lists several alternative names for this creed: 'al-Sughra sughra al-sughra' (the smallest of the small), 'al-Aqida al-Hafida' (the Granddaughter Creed), 'Aqida al-Nisa' (Women's Creed), 'Aqida al-Niswan wa al-Sibyan' (Women and Children's Creed), and 'al-Aqida al-Wajiza' (the Concise Creed). The author describes the creed as very short (no more than two pages), easy in expression, and clear in proofs, accessible to all. He then addresses the issue of attribution: there is no definitive proof (mustanad) establishing this creed as al-Sanusi's own. Later scholars relied solely on al-Hafnawi's transmitted report. However, the author notes that the multiplicity of commentaries, their agreement in content, and their early dates (from the 11th century AH onward) give confidence in the attribution. He exemplifies this dilemma with Imam al-Shakani, a commentator who only said the creed was 'among what was attributed' to al-Sanusi, not certifying it. This shows scholarly caution and the lack of absolute certainty. The passage thus provides a balanced view: while the creed is widely accepted and has strong circumstantial evidence, its attribution remains probabilistically but not definitively proven.
