Allah's Will for Disbelief and Sin: Adab and Theology
Sanusi discusses the permissibility of applying 'will' to disbelief and sin, with consensus and disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why some scholars avoid saying 'Allah wills disbelief and sin' strictly out of adab and how the distinction between creator and acquirer prevents misattribution of evil to Allah.

Core Idea
Allah wills all things, but moral descriptions attach to the servant, not the Creator.

This lesson addresses a nuanced theological and linguistic question: Is it proper to say that Allah wills disbelief and sin? While Ahl al-Sunna agree that everything occurs by Allah's will, they disagree on the specific wording. The passage explains that the prohibition is purely a matter of adab to avoid the false implication that Allah is described by sin. Sanusi then clarifies the theological distinction between Allah as creator and the servant as acquirer.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the consensus
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Sanusi first mentions the consensus of Ahl al-Sunna that all things occur by Allah's will, including faith and disbelief.
This consensus is the foundation.
2
Identify the disagreement
The passage then states that scholars differed on applying the term 'will' specifically to disbelief and sin. Find the description of those who forbade it and the reason given.
3
Understand the rationale of adab
The reason for forbidding is purely linguistic politeness (adab) to prevent the misconception that Allah deserves the name of disbelief or sin when the act is attributed to Him.
This is a sophisticated theological precision.
4
Analyze the analogy of the container
Sanusi provides an analogy: if you put a foul-smelling object in a container, the container becomes foul, not you. Similarly, the servant is like the container that acquires the moral quality, while Allah is like the one who placed it.
This illustrates the distinction between creator and acquirer.
5
Reflect on the broader principle
Consider the key principle: Allah is not described by temporal events (al-ḥawādith). The moral description attaches to the servant even though Allah is the creator of the act.
This guards against attributing evil to Allah.

Commentary
This passage addresses a theological and linguistic question: Is it appropriate to say 'Allah wills disbelief and sin'? The author first notes the consensus that all things occur by Allah's will, including faith and disbelief. Despite that, scholars differed on applying the term 'will' specifically to disbelief and sin. Some prevented it purely out of adab (linguistic politeness) to avoid the misunderstanding that Allah deserves the blame for those acts. The author explains that the names 'disbelief' and 'sin' apply to the act as attributed to the servant, not to Allah as creator. The servant is the one who acquires (kasb) the act and is described by it, while Allah creates it and wills it but is not described by it, because it is impossible for His essence to be characterized by temporal events. He gives a simile: if you put something foul-smelling into a container, the container becomes foul, not you. Similarly, the servant is like the container that acquires the quality of the act, while Allah is like the one who placed it, not affected by it.

All Actions Relative to Allah Are Good
Sanusi states the principle that all actions are good relative to Allah and differ only by acquisition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari principle that all actions are good relative to Allah and how human acquisition accounts for moral differentiation without attributing any causal effect to humans.

Core Idea
All actions are good from Allah; human acquisition distinguishes them legally and customarily.

Sanusi lays down a fundamental principle of Ash'ari theology: everything Allah does is good. But we see good and evil in the world. How is that possible? This passage explains that the difference comes from how humans acquire actions according to divine law or custom, while humans themselves have no real causal effect. This preserves divine goodness while accounting for human responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get its rhythm. Then read the English translation. Notice the key terms: الأفعال, حسنة, العباد, اكتسبوا.
The passage is a single sentence, but packed with theological meaning.
2
Identify the main claim
The passage begins by stating that all actions relative to Allah are good (حسنة). This is the foundational claim. Understand that 'relative to Him' means from the perspective of Allah's creation and ownership.
This does not mean that everything humans do is good in a moral sense.
3
Understand the differentiation
Actions differ only 'by consideration of their occurrence in servants according to what they acquired from them, legally or customarily.' The differentiation is not in the act itself as a divine act, but in how it is acquired by humans under law (شرعا) or custom (عرفا).
The Arabic word اكتسبوا (acquired) refers to the Ash'ari theory of kasb (acquisition).
4
Note the negation of human effect
The passage explicitly says humans have no effect (أثر) in any of the actions at all. This reinforces that Allah is the sole creator of all actions, including human deeds.
This preserves divine omnipotence while still allowing for human accountability through acquisition.
5
Reflect on the theological significance
This principle reconciles divine goodness with the existence of evil in the world: evil actions are not evil relative to Allah's creation, but become evil when acquired by humans in violation of law or custom.
This is a key Ash'ari solution to the problem of evil.

Commentary
This passage presents a foundational principle in Ash'ari theology: everything Allah does is good (ḥasan). The apparent differentiation of actions into good and evil arises only when those actions are considered as they occur in human beings (fi l-ʿibād), based on whether humans 'acquire' (iktisāb) them in accordance with revealed law (sharʿan) or custom (ʿurfan). Crucially, humans have no real effect (athar) on the actions themselves; the actions are entirely created by Allah. The distinction is merely in the description of the act relative to the human subject's acquisition, not in the act as a divine act.

The Objection: Restricting Will to Good as an Excuse for Sin
Sanusi presents the objection that restricting will to good gives sinners an excuse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sunni theologians argue that attributing evil actions to Allah's will is necessary to prevent sinners from using a false excuse.

Core Idea
Denying Allah's will over evil gives sinners a false excuse.

Sanusi now presents a powerful objection against those who restrict the term 'will' only to good actions. If disbelief and sin are not willed by Allah, then sinners could argue that they are not responsible for acts Allah did not intend. This lesson examines that objection and its refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation. Notice the key terms: takhṣīṣ (specifying), kufr (disbelief), maʿṣiyah (disobedience), irādat Allāh (will of Allah), iʿtidhār (excuse), dam lāḥiq (legal claim), ʿudhr (excuse).
This is a short but dense objection.
2
Understand the objection
Identify what the objection is saying: that restricting the attribution of will to good only makes it seem like an excuse for sinners to avoid punishment. Ask yourself: why would that be a problem?
The objection is not the author's own view; it is a critique against a certain position.
3
Grasp the refutation
The passage itself refutes the objection by stating that such an excuse is not valid in Islamic law and that Allah is not questioned about His actions. Focus on how the objection is dismissed.
This shows the strength of the Ashʿarī position: all things are from Allah's will.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection (wujjiha) against a particular view. The view in question is one that restricts the word 'will' (irāda) to good actions only, refusing to say that Allah wills disbelief and sin. The objection argues that such a restriction would effectively serve as an excuse for the disbeliever or sinner: 'Since Allah did not will my sin, I am not responsible.' But this excuse has no weight in Islamic law (sharʿ); Allah is not questioned about His actions or judgments. Therefore, one cannot avoid attributing evil actions to Allah's will merely out of politeness, because that would grant sinners a false pretext.

Quranic Evidence for Polite Speech About Divine Will
Sanusi cites two Quranic verses demonstrating polite expression about divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quran itself models a reverent way of speaking about Allah's will, using indirect verbal constructions for evil and direct attribution for good.

Core Idea
The Quran teaches us to speak indirectly about evil while directly attributing good to Allah.

This passage from Sanusi's commentary brings two powerful Quranic examples that show how careful expression about divine will is not just human politeness but is modeled by Allah Himself in the Qur'an. By examining these verses, we learn a practical adab for talking about qadar, balancing theological truth with reverence.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that Sanusi presents two Quranic examples to support the principle of observing etiquette in speech about Allah's will.
Look for the shift from active to passive voice in the first example.
2
Analyze the Fatiha example
Examine how Allah attributes grace directly to Himself ('an'amta 'alayhim) but uses a passive participle for wrath (al-maghdūbi 'alayhim) instead of an active verb. Reflect on the difference.
This shows that even Allah's own speech models indirect expression for undesirable things.
3
Analyze the Jinn example
Look at the contrast: 'evil is intended' (passive, no agent) versus 'their Lord intends for them right guidance' (active, agent specified). Note the change in grammatical construction.
The first part leaves the agent unspecified for evil; the second part explicitly attributes good to Allah.
4
Understand the underlying principle
Synthesize the two examples. The lesson is that one should use reverent language, avoiding direct active statements that link evil to Allah in everyday speech, while still acknowledging that all things are willed by Him.
This is an etiquette (adab), not a denial of divine will over evil.

Commentary
This passage provides Quranic support for the principle of polite speech about Allah's will. In Sūrat al-Fātiḥa (1:7), Allah first says 'those upon whom You have bestowed favor' (anʿamta ʿalayhim), directly attributing grace to Himself. Then He says 'not of those who have incurred wrath' (ghayri l-maghdūbi ʿalayhim) using the passive participle, rather than 'those whom You have wrath upon' (alladhīna ghaḍibta ʿalayhim). The active verb is avoided for evil to maintain adab. In Sūrat al-Jinn (72:10), when speaking of evil (sharr), the phrase is 'evil is intended for those on earth' (ashar un urīda) using the passive, without specifying the agent. But when speaking of good (rashad), Allah says 'their Lord intends for them right guidance' (arāda bihim rabbuhum rashadan), directly attributing the action to Himself. This shows that it is acceptable to avoid directly saying 'Allah wills evil' in ordinary speech, while affirming that He wills good. The lesson is that one can use indirect expressions for evil as a form of reverence, without denying the theological truth that all things are willed by Allah.

The Best Statement on Specifying Will

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The best statement combines theological accuracy with respectful context.

After establishing the first view on proper etiquette when speaking of Allah's will, Imam Sanusi now presents two additional positions from the imams. One view permits specifying 'will' to sin without concern for ill-manners, while another distinguishes between teaching contexts and ordinary speech. Sanusi then declares that the third view, which balances context, is the best.

Guided Path

Identify the two views

The first additional view focuses on the distinction between Allah as originator and humans as described.
2
Understand the rationale
For the first view, consider why some imams allowed explicit specification: because the context makes clear the difference between the Creator and the creature. For the second view, note the distinction between teaching and ordinary speech.
3
Appreciate the best statement
Reflect on why Sanusi declares the third view (the one that distinguishes contexts) as 'aḥsan al-aqwāl' (the best of statements). How does it reconcile theological precision with respectful speech?
This third view is not a compromise but a contextual differentiation.

This passage is a theological argument establishing that human actions have no causal effect on reward or punishment. The author begins by drawing a conclusion from earlier principles: the actions God creates in His servants (obedience and disobedience) have absolutely no effect on reward or punishment; if they did, creatures would be partners with God in influence. Moreover, God could reward or punish directly without any preceding indicator, and that would be good, since indicators have no effect at all; His eternal decree precedes all created things, even the indicators. He supports this with transmitted evidence: God will create people directly for Paradise and Hell without prior obedience or disobedience. All this is permissible for God and entails no deficiency; every divine action manifests His perfect attributes—knowledge, power, will, oneness, sovereignty, and freedom from purposes. No possible thing can resist Him, and nothing restricts His disposal.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Obligation targets your voluntary actions that are the usual cause, not the external effect.

Kalam theology insists that obligation (taklif) only applies to what the servant can acquire. Read the objection
The objector claims that we find obligations in actions that are not within the servant's kasb, such as striking in jihad. The objection's basis is that contingent power only relates to actions in its own locus.

This solves the objection without violating the principle.

The Jabriyya Objection to Created Capacity
Presents Mu'tazila view and Jabriyya objection to created capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Jabriyya argument that created capacity is useless unless it produces actions, and why the Ash'ari response requires rethinking its function.

Core Idea
Created capacity's only benefit cannot be to create actions, else it would compete with Allah.

The Jabriyya argued that if humans had a created capacity, it would only be useful if it actually produced actions, but that would make humans creators—impossible. This forced the Ash'ari theologians to clarify that capacity serves a different, legal purpose. In this lesson, we examine the Mu'tazila background and the Jabriyya challenge.

Commentary
This passage is a complex theological-legal argument that defends the Ash'ari concept of created capacity (qudra haditha) against the Jabriyya denial of it. The passage begins by describing the Mu'tazila position that humans create their own actions, noting that early Mu'tazila avoided calling humans 'creators' due to Salafi consensus, while later ones boldly did so. Al-Qadi and al-Ustadh replied that capacity has a different benefit: legally, its conjunction with the action is a 'cause' (sabab) for the action being classified as obedience or sin. They used the word 'influence' (ta'thir) metaphorically to emphasize that without capacity, the action cannot be legally categorized. The author then supports this metaphorical usage by showing that in usul al-fiqh, jurists commonly say 'intoxication is the influence in prohibition' or 'menstruation influenced the prohibition of prayer,' even though they hold that legal rulings have no real cause (illa) in the sense of producing the ruling. Rather, these are merely indicators (amarat) set by the Law, with no necessary rational connection between them and the rulings. Thus, the passage clarifies that the Ash'ari position does not ascribe any creative power to human capacity; it only serves as a legal marker.

Refuting the Mu'tazilite Doctrine of Al-Aslah Regarding Children
The author raises and refutes the Mu'tazilite claim about children dying before puberty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari objection to the Mu'tazilite claim that death before puberty is most beneficial and the logical refutation based on consistency.

Core Idea
Divine foreknowledge does not justify inconsistent treatment of disbelievers.

The Mu'tazilites claimed that it is most beneficial for God to cause children to die before they reach puberty, to save them from potential disbelief. In this passage, the author presents a powerful objection: if that logic holds, then why didn't God kill all known disbelievers as children? This exposes the inconsistency of the Mu'tazilite argument.

Commentary
This passage presents a classic Ash'ari objection to the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-aslah. The author first poses the problem: if obligation is beneficial, why does God allow children to die before they can be obligated? Then he reproduces the Mu'tazilite response: it is more beneficial to kill them because God knows they would have disbelieved. The author then refutes this by pointing out that the same applies to known disbelievers like Pharaoh; if God's foreknowledge of their disbelief justifies killing them young, then He should have killed them too. But He did not, so the Mu'tazilite argument is inconsistent.
