Second Rational Proof That Lying is Impossible for Allah
Sanusi presents a second rational proof for the impossibility of lying.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second rational proof for the impossibility of lying for Allah, based on the principle that possible attributes become necessary for Him.

Core Idea
Possible attributes become necessary for Allah due to His simplicity.

This lesson presents a second proof that lying is impossible for Allah, based on the logical extension of the principle that contingent attributes cannot apply to Him. By examining the nature of truthful speech and its possibility for a knower, Sanusi shows that truthfulness becomes a necessary attribute of Allah, thereby rendering lying impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Notice the logical flow from premise to conclusion.
The argument uses a conditional chain.
2
Identify the premises
Identify the major premise (every knower can report truthfully) and minor premise (Allah is a knower).
3
Understand the pivot
Focus on the sentence: 'كلُّ ما صحّ أنْ يتّصفَ بهِ وجَبَ لهُ'. This is the key principle: possibility entails necessity for Allah due to the impossibility of contingent attributes.
This applies to all attributes, not just speech.
4
Apply to the conclusion
Since truthful speech is possible for Allah, it becomes necessary; its opposite (lying) is therefore impossible.
The proof relies on the three rational judgments: necessary, possible, impossible.
5
Reflect on the logic
Consider why this proof is considered 'second' — it uses the same principle regarding contingent attributes but from a different angle (knowledge-based).

Commentary
This passage presents a second rational proof for the impossibility of lying for Allah. The argument proceeds: every knower is capable of reporting in accordance with his knowledge. Allah is a knower, so He can report truthfully. However, in Allah's case, whatever is possible (i.e., can be attributed) becomes necessary because contingent attributes (al-ḥawādith) are impossible for Him. Therefore, since truthfulness is possible, it becomes necessary for Him. Consequently, its opposite, lying, is impossible. The proof relies on the principle that Allah cannot have accidental or contingent attributes; all His attributes are necessary. Thus, if truthful speech is possible, it must be necessary, and lying is precluded.

Third Proof Against Lying and the Anthropomorphist Dilemma
Sanusi presents a reductio proof and a dilemma for anthropomorphists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the reductio proof that lying is impossible for Allah and why this proof is especially challenging for anthropomorphists.

Core Idea
Lying in Allah would force either contradiction or change—both impossible.

Sanusi now offers a third, more intricate proof that lying is impossible for Allah. This argument not only builds on earlier logical principles but also exposes a weakness in the position of the anthropomorphists (al-mushabbiha), who allow contingent attributes in Allah. By following the chain of reasoning, you will see why lying cannot even be considered a possibility for the Divine.

Guided Path
1
Read the full Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing. Let the flow of the argument settle in your mind before you dissect it.
Notice the strong logical connectors: 'لو... لكان...' and the emphatic conclusion.
2
Trace the reductio argument
Identify the initial assumption (lying is possible), and follow how Sanusi derives a contradiction. Ask yourself: why would lying become necessary if it were possible?
The key move is that contingent attributes are impossible for Allah, so any possibility becomes necessity.
3
Analyze the footnote's additional reasoning
The footnote considers the scenario where both truthfulness and lying are attributed. Understand why succession (change) would prove temporality, and why co-eternal opposites are impossible.
This closes off the last loophole.
4
Reflect on the anthropomorphist problem
Sanusi concludes that the proof is difficult for anthropomorphists because they admit contingent attributes. Consider why this forces them into absurdity.
They end up having to say lying is necessary for Allah—which is absurd.

Commentary
This passage presents a third proof and concluding remarks. The third proof is a reductio: if lying were possible for Allah, it would become necessary (since contingent attributes are impossible), making truthfulness impossible. But truthfulness is necessary because Allah knows all things infinitely, and a knower cannot but report truthfully (as known necessarily). Hence lying is impossible. The footnote explains that if both truthfulness and lying were attributed, succession (temporal change) would follow, proving temporality; if both are eternal, they are contradictory and cannot coexist. The conclusion states that this rational proof is difficult for anthropomorphists (al-mushabbiha), because they allow contingent attributes for Allah, so they cannot rule out lying as possible. However, for attributes of essence, whatever is possible becomes necessary, so for them, lying would become necessary, which is absurd.

The Rule of Whole and Parts in Tawatur and Consensus
Sanusi answers the doubt about whole vs parts judgments in tawatur and consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the judgment of the whole can differ from the judgment of its parts, and why accumulated probabilities can yield certainty in mutawatir reports and consensus.

Core Idea
The whole can be certain even when each part is only probable.

Sanusi addresses a key doubt raised by those who deny the certainty of consensus and mutawatir reports. The doubt is simple: if each individual report is only probable, how can the combined group be certain? In this lesson, you will see Sanusi's answer, which invokes an external factor—the impossibility of collusion on a lie—that transforms probable parts into a certain whole.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt
Read the passage and locate the doubt: if each individual report is probable (dhann), how can the aggregate be certain (yaqeen)?
The doubt is stated explicitly in the middle of the passage.
2
Understand Sanusi's answer
Sanusi asserts that the judgment of the whole may differ from the judgment of its parts due to an external meaning (ma'na khaarij). Find this key phrase.
The external meaning is the impossibility of collusion on a lie.
3
Recognize the implication
Note that the certainty does not come from the aggregation of parts but from the external factor. This refutes the doubt.

And its answer: that the judgment of the whole may differ from the judgment of the parts, and this is precisely the doubt of one who denies the certainty of consensus and the mutawatir report (1). They claimed that probability is the judgment of each individual of the aggregate that has reached the level of tawatur; so how can accumulated probabilities be certainty? Commentary
This passage is a response (jawaab) to a doubt (shubhah) raised by those who deny the certainty of consensus and the mutawatir report. The hypothetical argument (law sahha dhalika) shows that the conclusion is definitively false (qat'i al-batlan).

Reductio: If Whole Were Sum of Parts, Anyone Could Produce Poetry
Sanusi uses a reductio ad absurdum to show the falsehood of the sarfah theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the absurd consequence of claiming that the judgment of the whole is simply the sum of the judgments of its parts.

Core Idea
If the whole were just the sum of parts, every Arab could match the Qur'an's eloquence.

Sanusi employs a powerful reductio ad absurdum to reinforce his point. If the judgment of a whole were merely the aggregate of its parts, then every individual—since they can produce the individual words and short sentences of eloquent speech—would be able to produce poetry as magnificent as that of Imru' al-Qais. Since this is clearly false, the premise is false.

Guided Path
1
Read the hypothetical scenario
The passage begins with 'If that were true' (law sahha dhalika). Identify what 'that' refers to: the idea that the whole judgment is just the sum of parts.
2
Identify the absurd consequence
If the whole judgment were simply the sum of parts, then every Arab could produce poetry like Imru' al-Qais, because they can produce the individual words and short sentences.
The consequence is absurd because clearly not every Arab is a great poet.
3
Understand the conclusion
The conclusion is that the premise (that the whole judgment is the sum of parts) leads to a false and impossible result, so the premise must be false.
The author states 'the consequent is definitively false' (اللازم قطعي البطلان).

The Three Fruits of Knowledge: Detachment, Sincerity, Contentment
Sanusi enumerates three fruits of knowledge of usul al-din and their result.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three spiritual fruits that knowledge of the principles of religion yields according to Sanusi, and how they lead to patience with creatures.

Core Idea
Knowledge of God's oneness and decree frees the heart, purifies actions, and brings contentment.

In this passage, Sanusi explains what reason indicates about the fruits of knowledge of the principles of religion. He shows that true understanding of creation's temporality, divine unity, and God's preordination transforms the believer's heart, actions, and attitude. These fruits are not mere theory but practical outcomes that shape how we relate to the world and others.

Guided Path

Find each 'fruit' in the text: detachment from the world, exclusive devotion to God, and contentment with divine decree. Notice the cause-effect relationship.

Commentary
This passage enumerates three fruits that knowledge of the principles of religion (usul al-din) yields, as indicated by reason. The first fruit: knowing that the entire world is temporally originated (hadith) causes the heart to detach from worldly things—neither fearing loss nor hoping for gain—because one realizes everything is in God's power. The second fruit: knowing divine unity (tawhid) leads to exclusive devotion to God in all actions, since lordship cannot tolerate any form of shirk. The third fruit: knowing that God's foreordainment (qadar) preceded all events results in contentment (rida) with whatever happens, fearing neither loss of what is decreed nor hoping for what is not decreed. From these three fruits emerges kindness (rifq) towards creatures and pardoning them when they cause harm—this patience flows from the detachment, sincerity, and contentment described.

The Fourth Fruit: Perpetual Fear and Piety (Wara')
Sanusi describes the fourth fruit: perpetual fear leading to wara' and several specific fears.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how perpetual fear of one's eternal destiny leads to scrupulous piety (wara') and the various manifestations of this fear.

Core Idea
Awareness of the unseen destiny turns fear into a shield of piety.

Sanusi presents the fourth fruit of faith: a state of constant fear that prevents spiritual complacency. This fear is rooted in the uncertainty of pre-eternal decree, and it manifests as vigilance against sin, hypocrisy, and even the loss of one's good deeds. Understanding this fruit helps the believer maintain a sincere and cautious devotion.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth fruit: a state of perpetual fear (khawf) that prevents any tranquility of the soul. The reason for this fear is uncertainty about one's eternal destiny—whether one is predestined for happiness or misery. To guard against misery, the believer examines its causes (sins) and avoids them; this avoidance is wara' (scrupulous piety). Moreover, even when one performs good deeds, there is fear that they may be nullified (habt) by opposites like hypocrisy (riya') or seeking reputation (sum'a). There is also fear that one's knowledge may turn into ignorance, that one cannot adequately thank God, and that one's soul may deceive. Finally, there is fear that debts or obligations owed to human beings may cause one's good deeds to be transferred to their accounts on Judgment Day. The overall effect is a life of vigilant piety.

Commentary
