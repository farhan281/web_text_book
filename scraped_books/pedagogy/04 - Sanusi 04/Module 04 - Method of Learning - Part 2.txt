Sanusi gives examples of three types of arguments and identifies demonstration as reliable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three types of arguments illustrated by Sanusi and why demonstration is the only reliable one.

Core Idea
Not all arguments are equal – only demonstration yields certainty.

Sanusi provides concrete examples to distinguish between valid and fallacious reasoning. He shows a correct syllogism, a deceptive fallacy based on a false premise, and a sophistical argument based on superficial resemblance. This helps the student recognize that while many argumentative forms exist, only demonstration (burhān) is trustworthy for establishing truth.

Commentary
This passage provides examples of three types of logical arts, likely from the five classical arts (brāhān, jadal, khaṭābah, shiʿr, and sophistry). The passage thus clarifies that while various argumentative forms exist, only demonstration provides certainty.

The Proof of the World's Contingency: Foundational Role and Logical Summary
Sanusi highlights the foundational status of the proof and summarizes its logical core.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the proof of the world's contingency is the cornerstone of Islamic theology and how its logical structure is based on the inseparability of bodies from motion and rest.

Core Idea
Two things that always go together cannot have existed eternally.

Sanusi now steps back to show the big picture. Having outlined the proof earlier, he emphasizes that this is not just one argument among many—it is the very foundation of all other beliefs. Then he distills its logical essence into a crisp, undeniable inference that anyone can grasp.

Guided Path
1

Read the opening sentence where Sanusi calls this knowledge a 'great foundation' and 'large basis' for all beliefs. Notice the strong language.
This sets the stakes for the entire proof.
2
Understand the inference from inseparable concomitants
The proof's summary: infer the contingency of one inseparable concomitant from the other. What are these two things? They always occur together.
The two are motion and rest, which always accompany bodies.
3
Trace the applied example on bodies
Bodies cannot be conceived without motion or rest. Since both motion and rest cannot be eternal, bodies cannot be eternal either. Follow the logical necessity.
If a body were eternal, it would have to be without motion or rest, which is impossible.
4
Note the conclusion that bodies cannot be eternal
The passage ends by stating that a body cannot exist in pre-eternity in any state, as its possible states are only motion, rest, or an impossible third.
This completes the proof that the world is contingent.

Seven rational principles anchor the proof that the world had a beginning.

This lesson presents the core framework of the classical kalam argument for the world's createdness. Before engaging with the proof itself, Sanusi lays out seven indispensable premises. He then begins explaining the first premise—that bodies have inherent accidents—and shows it is so obvious that even denying it is self-refuting.

Commentary
This passage lists the seven foundational principles required for the classical kalam proof of the world's temporal origination (ḥudūth al-ʿālam). Sanusi first notes that establishing the second principle (which is disproving that the accident is self-subsistent) depends on four principles—likely those discussed earlier regarding emergence, cessation, and the impossibility of self-subsistence, transfer, and latency. Then he states the full set of seven: (1) establishing the existence of accidents (zā'id ʿalā al-ajrām)—attributes or qualities inhering in bodies; (2) disproving that these accidents are self-subsistent (i.e., they must inhere in a body); (3) disproving that accidents can transfer from one body to another; (4) disproving that accidents can be latent (kāmin) and then appear—a view held by some natural philosophers; (5) proving that an eternal thing cannot cease to exist; (6) proving that bodies are never free from accidents; (7) proving that it is impossible for events to go back infinitely without a beginning. Sanusi then briefly explains the first principle: it is self-evident (ḍarūrī) and requires no proof, because every rational person intuitively knows that their own self has additional meanings (like knowledge, will, etc.) beyond the mere substance. He cites an unnamed intelligent scholar (likely al-Ghazali) who responded to those who deny the existence of accidents with a rhetorical question: 'Is your dispute with us about the existence of accidents: is it existent or not?'—implying that the denial is absurd because even the denial itself is an accident.

Five principles from one statement: the power of concise theological reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of how the seven principles underlying the proof of the world's occurrence are derived from a single statement. Sanusi begins by noting that the proof of the world's occurrence in the creed is actually a reply to these seven principles. He then takes a specific statement used to refute the third division (the case where a body is neither stationary nor moving in pre-eternity). From this statement, he extracts two principles: first, that there is an accident (meaning) superadded to the body (such as motion or rest), and second, that it is impossible for the body to be devoid of such an accident. He then shows that the same statement also yields three more principles: the invalidation of an accident subsisting by itself (i.e., accidents must inhere in a body), the invalidation of an accident transferring from one body to another, and the invalidation of latency and manifestation (that accidents can be hidden and then appear). He argues that the definitions of rest (fixation in place) and motion (departure from place) imply that if these three things were possible, the very definitions would be overturned, which is impossible. Therefore, this single statement contains five of the seven principles.

Are the Three Additional Principles Necessary for the Proof?
Sanusi argues the three invalidations are not necessary for the creed's proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi argues that the three additional principles (invalidating accident self-subsistence, transmission, and latency) are not necessary for the proof of the world's occurrence.

Core Idea
The proof of the creed does not depend on refuting three extra possibilities.

In this passage, Sanusi addresses a potential objection about the necessity of three principles for proving the world's occurrence. He argues that the core proof is based on a different foundation—the impossibility of change in an eternal body—and therefore these additional invalidations are not required. However, he acknowledges a counterargument and provides a nuanced answer.

Guided Path
1
Identify Sanusi's main claim.
Read the first line to see that Sanusi states the three principles may not be necessary for the proof of the creed. Note his careful phrasing 'قد لا يتوقف'. This sets up his thesis.
2
Understand the reasoning behind the claim.
Follow the explanation: if a body existed in pre-eternity in a state (motion or rest), that state would be eternal and necessarily unchangeable. This reasoning avoids needing the three invalidations.
3
Examine the objection from a specific statement.
Sanusi quotes a statement about eternal rest not accepting non-existence, which seems to imply the rest is not eternal. Understand why this appears to contradict the proof.
4
Follow Sanusi's response to the objection.
He reinterprets 'does not accept non-existence' to mean the state does not cease to belong to the body, not that the state itself is non-removable. This defuses the objection.
5
Recognize the conclusion.
Sanusi concludes that addressing the three invalidations is not necessary. If it were needed, guidance from the creed already suffices.

Commentary
This passage discusses whether the three principles – the invalidity of an accident subsisting by itself, its transmission, and its latency/manifestation – are required for the proof of the creed. Sanusi argues that they may not be necessary because the proof is originally based on a different approach: if a body existed in pre-eternity with a certain state (motion or rest), that state would be eternal and thus unchangeable. Therefore, the proof does not depend on refuting those three possibilities. However, there is a potential objection from a statement that says 'its rest is eternal, so it does not become non-existent'. This might imply that the rest cannot be eternal because it does accept non-existence. Sanusi responds by explaining that the phrase 'does not accept non-existence' refers to the non-existence of the state from the body, not the non-existence of the state itself. That is, since the state is necessary for the body when assumed eternal, it cannot be removed from the body. Thus, the three additional invalidations are not essential for the proof, and if they were needed, the creed already provides guidance.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer format followed by the author's own conclusion. The objector argues that the proof of the world's temporal origination (ḥudūth) only covers bodies (ajrām) and their attributes (ṣifāt), so it does not prove the temporality of incorporeal substances (al-jawāhir al-mufāriqa) such as those posited by philosophers and al-Ghazālī. The author responds by stating that the theologians (mutakallimūn) hold the world to be entirely composed of bodies and attributes, so according to them the objection does not arise. However, the author immediately notes that the theologians' proofs for this confinement are weak; he refers to his own commentary where he refuted their strongest argument. Therefore, the correct position is to suspend judgment (al-waqf) about such extra entities, neither definitively affirming nor denying them, because the proofs of those who deny them are weak and the proofs of those who affirm them are false. The author then provides a proof for the temporality of such an entity if it does exist: it cannot be God (since God's existence is proven elsewhere), so it must be contingent and thus temporal. This argument follows the order: objection, initial answer (theologians' view), critique of that view, conclusion of suspension, and a hypothetical proof.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses the analogy of eyesight and light to explain the complementary roles of reason and revelation in gaining knowledge of Allah.

Core Idea
Reason is like eyesight; revelation is like light – both are needed to see clearly.

In this passage, Sanusi unpacks the Quranic phrase 'light upon light' to illustrate the partnership between rational proof and divine guidance. He presents a vivid analogy from everyday experience: reason functions like healthy eyesight, but without the light of revelation, it cannot perceive spiritual truths. This lesson walks through that analogy and its profound implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two 'lights'
The passage begins by explaining that 'light upon light' refers to the proof of reason and the path of revelation. Notice how each serves a distinct purpose: reason distinguishes the Eternal from created things, and revelation clarifies divine rulings.
The verse is from Surah An-Nur, 35.
2
Understand the analogy
The scholars liken reason to eyesight and revelation to light. See how this clarifies their relationship: just as eyesight needs light to see, reason needs revelation to know Allah's commands.
3
Apply the analogy to two cases

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the verse 'light upon light' and an analogy clarifying the relationship between reason and revelation. The author explains that 'light upon light' points to two lights: the light of rational proofs ('burhan al-aql') and the light of revealed law ('nahj al-shar'). By the light of reason, one distinguishes the Eternal (al-Qadim) from originated things (al-hawadith). By the light of revelation, one discerns the rulings of Allah. Then the scholars drew an analogy: reason is like eyesight, and revelation is like light. A person with sound reason (like good eyesight) can know the truth of the messengers when revelation comes (like light). Without reason, one is blind in darkness. But even with reason, before revelation, one is like a person with healthy eyes in pitch darkness—unable to perceive. The Quranic verse (As-Sajda 13) is quoted to show that guidance is from Allah. Then the author compares two types: the one with reason (sight) who sees when light comes, and the one without reason (blind) who remains blind, even worse off in the light. This underscores the necessity of both reason and revelation for proper knowledge.

The Chain from Guidance to Taqwa
The author explains the causal relationship between intellect, knowledge, and taqwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the causal chain that links intellect, reflection, knowledge, and taqwa, and why genuine knowledge is necessary for true God-consciousness.

Core Idea
Taqwa is the fruit of a chain that begins with a sound intellect.

This passage from Sanusi presents a powerful causal argument: human guidance and taqwa are not arbitrary but result from a specific sequence. The author traces the path from intellect to reflection to knowledge to taqwa, showing that each step depends on the previous. Without intellect, reflection fails; without reflection, knowledge falters; without knowledge, taqwa cannot exist. As you read, notice how the chain works both positively and negatively.

Read the passage and translation

2
Identify the positive chain
Look for the sequence: intellect → reflection → knowledge → taqwa. Notice the phrase 'taqwa is the fruit of knowledge, knowledge is the fruit of reflection, reflection is the result of intellect.'
This chain is the core of the argument.

Core Idea
The 'illa of a ruling motivates the servant, not the Lord.

This lesson presents a classic reconciliation by Imam al-Zarkashi between two schools of thought: the mutakallimun (theologians) who say God's rulings have no cause, and the jurists who say they do. Al-Zarkashi shows that the contradiction is only apparent, arising from a difference in what each group means by 'illa. By carefully defining terms, he harmonizes the positions without compromising the Ash'ari principle of God's absolute freedom.

Thus the two groups are speaking different languages about the same concept.

This passage is a reconciliation by al-Zarkashi of the apparent contradiction between the mutakallimun (theologians) and the jurists on whether divine rulings have 'illa (causes). Al-Zarkashi explains that there is no contradiction because the term 'illa is used differently. Al-Amr bi-l-Ma'ruf is Not Exclusive to Rulers
Sanusi argues that al-amr bi-l-ma'ruf is not limited to rulers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the duty of enjoining good and forbidding evil applies to all Muslims, not just rulers, based on the practice of the early community.

Core Idea
Enjoining good is everyone's duty, not just the ruler's.

One common misconception is that only those in authority can advise rulers. Sanusi refutes this by pointing to the early Muslim community, who routinely admonished their leaders without anyone objecting. This passage establishes the general obligation for all qualified individuals.

Authority of the Ruler in Armed Conflict
Sanusi explains ruler's authority in armed conflict and quotes Imam al-Haramayn on knowledge levels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why authority to wage armed conflict in commanding good and forbidding evil rests with the ruler, and how Imam al-Haramayn distinguishes between general and specific knowledge in this context.

Core Idea
Armed conflict in enjoining good is the ruler's domain, not the individual's.

This passage introduces a critical limit on the duty of commanding good and forbidding evil: when it escalates to armed confrontation, authority transfers to the ruler to avoid civil strife. Imam al-Haramayn's quote then clarifies who may enjoin or forbid based on the nature of the ruling—general knowledge or specific ijtihad—and forbids mujtahids from rebuking each other on valid disagreements.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation to grasp the flow from the general rule to the detailed quote.
2
Understand the ruler's authority
Note the condition: when it comes to setting up fighting and drawing weapons, it is tied to the ruler to prevent fitna. This is the main point of the first part.

Commentary
This passage introduces a crucial qualification: when the action of enjoining good and forbidding evil escalates to the point of armed confrontation (nasb al-qital) and drawing weapons (shahr al-silah), authority reverts to the ruler (sultan) to avoid fitna (civil strife).
