The Imam's View on the Muqallid and His Defense of Kalam
Report of the Imam's view on the muqallid and defense of kalam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Imam insisted that mere blind imitation (taqlid) does not constitute true knowledge, and how he defended the discipline of kalam against its detractors.

Core Idea
True knowledge requires knowing how you know; taqlid is conjecture, not knowledge.

The passage below describes the position of the Imam regarding the muqallid—the one who blindly follows authority without understanding. The Imam not only critiques the muqallid's epistemic status but also defends the science of kalam as essential for attaining certainty in creed. Read this passage to see how the Imam ties together epistemology, the rejection of taqlid, and the necessity of reasoned inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then review the literal translation. Pay attention to the key claims made by the Imam.
The passage is a report of the Imam's view, not his own words.
2
Identify the Imam's key claim about the muqallid
Notice that the Imam equates taqlid with conjecture, not knowledge. The muqallid who knows but does not know how he knows is only imagining himself knowledgeable.
3
Understand the defense of kalam
The Imam defends kalam by showing that the Companions and Salaf were at the highest degree of knowledge and far from taqlid. This contrasts with the Hashwiyya who reject such inquiry.
The refutation of Hashwiyya is also part of this defense.

What is Kasb? The Ash'ari Doctrine of Acquisition
Sanusi defines kasb and its role in taklif

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari definition of kasb as an act created by Allah accompanied by a non-effective contingent power in the servant.

Core Idea
Kasb is Allah's creation with the servant's accompaniment, not causation.

This passage from Sanusi establishes the fundamental Ash'ari concept of kasb, which reconciles divine omnipotence with human moral responsibility. It defines kasb precisely and distinguishes it from both compulsion and independent human power. The following will present the definition and its implications for Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic definition and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the literal translation to grasp the meaning.
Pay attention to the phrase 'with no effect from it at all'.
2
Identify the key components
Find the four elements: Allah's creation, the act (e.g., motion), the contingent power, and the relation of non-effect.
The contingent power 'relates' to the act but does not cause it.
3
Understand the role of kasb in taklif
Note that kasb is the basis for legal obligation and a sign for reward and punishment, but only by divine law, not reason.
This shows the Ash'ari emphasis on revelation for moral accountability.
4
Contrast with the Jabriyya
Though the refutation comes later, note that the definition already implies a rejection of pure compulsion by affirming some power in the servant.

And the acquisition (kasb) of the servant is an expression of Allah’s creating the act in him—such as motion and rest, for example—accompanied by a contingent power (qudra ḥāditha) in him that relates to that act with no effect from it at all in it. And this acquisition is the object of legal obligation and a sign of reward and punishment by law, not by reason. And what indicates the accompaniment of this contingent power to the act—even though it has no effect in it at all—is our necessary perception of the difference between the movement of tremor and the like from involuntary movements, and other than them from voluntary movements; and there is no difference between them after thorough examination except that these voluntary ones are conjoined with a contingent power in the servant by which he feels that the act is made easier upon him, unlike the first involuntary ones. So from this, it emerges for you that by our saying: 'Indeed, with the act in which its doer does not feel compulsion, there is a contingent power in the servant, which is an accident among accidents, like knowledge and the like, relating to the act even if we see no effect from it in it at all'—we have separated from the school of the Jabriyya, who say there is no contingent power in the servant absolutely.
Source pages p. 105

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof concerning the concept of kasb (acquisition), a key doctrine in Ash‘arī theology that reconciles divine omnipotence with human responsibility. The author first defines kasb: it is Allah’s creation of the act (e.g., motion or rest) in the servant, accompanying a contingent power (qudra ḥāditha) that exists in the servant but has no effect on the act; it merely relates to it. This kasb is the basis for legal obligation (taklīf) and serves as a sign for reward and punishment by divine law, not by rational necessity. Then the author provides evidence for the existence of this contingent power by pointing to the perceived difference between involuntary movements (e.g., tremor) and voluntary movements. Our necessary perception (idrākunā ḍarūratan) distinguishes them even though both are created by Allah. After thorough examination (ba‘d al-sabr al-tāmm), the only difference is that voluntary movements are accompanied by a contingent power in the servant that he feels makes the act easier (yutayyir al-fi‘l ‘alayhi). Thus, the author concludes that by affirming this contingent power that accompanies the act without effect, they distinguish themselves from the Jabriyya, who deny any such power in the servant.

Evidence for Kasb and Refutation of Jabriyya

Linguistic and Terminological Definitions of Nabi and Rasul
Sanusi defines 'Nabi' and 'Rasul' linguistically and terminologically with Qadi Iyad's authority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic roots of 'Nabi' and 'Rasul' and the debate over their equivalence or distinction.

Core Idea
The words 'Nabi' and 'Rasul' have deep linguistic roots that shape their theological meanings.

Before we can discuss the roles of prophets and messengers, we must first understand what these terms mean in the Arabic language and in Islamic terminology. This lesson unpacks the derivations given by al-Qadi 'Iyad and the key debate over whether the two terms are synonymous or distinct.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation to grasp the flow of definitions.
Notice the two linguistic derivations for 'Nabi' and the one for 'Rasul'.
2
Identify the two linguistic derivations for 'Nabi'
Al-Qadi 'Iyad presents two derivations: one from 'naba'' (news) for those who pronounce hamza, and one from 'nabwa' (height) for those who do not. Understand how each shapes the meaning.
The first derivation gives 'one who is informed' or 'one who informs'; the second gives 'one of elevated station'.
3
Understand the derivation for 'Rasul'
Sanusi defines 'Rasul' as 'one who is sent' (mursal), noting the rare fa'ūl pattern. He also gives a secondary derivation from 'sequential succession', indicating repeated delivery of the message.
The term 'Irsal' means God's command to convey, implying obligation.

The passage ends by noting that some consider 'Nabi' and 'Rasul' synonymous. Reflect on how this impacts the understanding of prophethood and messengership.

Commentary
This passage is a terminological and linguistic definition section. It begins by stating the necessity of clarifying the meanings of 'nabi' (prophet), 'nubuwwa' (prophethood), and 'rasul' (messenger) both legally and in terms of linguistic derivation, and also to clarify whether the two terms are synonymous or different. The commentator then quotes al-Qadi 'Iyad. He explains two linguistic derivations for 'nabi'. First, for those who pronounce the hamza, it derives from 'naba'' (news), so the prophet is called 'nabi'' in the sense of either 'one who is informed' (maf'ūl) or 'one who informs' (fā'il). Second, for those who do not pronounce the hamza, it derives from 'nabwa' (height), meaning an elevated station, indicating the prophet's lofty rank before God. The commentator notes that both derivations are harmonious. Then he defines 'rasul' (messenger) as 'one who is sent' (mursal), noting that the pattern fa'ūl in the sense of muf'al is rare. He explains that 'irsāl' (sending) means God's command to convey (tabligh) to creation. He also gives a secondary derivation from 'tataabu'' (sequential succession), as in the phrase 'jā'a al-nāsu arsālan' (the people came one after another), likening the messenger to one who is obligated to repeatedly deliver the message, and the community is obligated to follow him. Finally, he mentions an opinion that 'nabi' and 'rasul' are synonymous (mutarādifān).

Commentary
This passage is a historical and doctrinal defense of the science of kalam as practiced by the Ash'ari school. The author begins with an anecdote about Imam al-Shafi'i, who upon being visited by a known heretic named Hafs al-Qard, cursed him and ordered him to be beaten with palm branches. The author clarifies that this condemnation was directed at early heretical mutakallimun who engaged in innovation (bida'), casting doubts, and opposing reason and revelation. These people deserved severe punishment. The author then distinguishes them from the orthodox mutakallimun of his own time: the Ash'ari school led by Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari, and including great scholars like Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini, al-Baqillani, and Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni. They fought the heretics with conclusive proofs stronger than any physical weapon, exposing their falsehoods throughout history. The author states that these scholars undertook the jihad of distinguishing truth from falsehood, presenting truth clearly and exposing falsehood's darkness. To further legitimize them, the author cites a hadith: 'This knowledge will be carried by the upright of every generation; they will remove from it the distortions of the extremists, the false claims of the falsifiers, and the interpretations of the ignorant.' The author then imagines that if the Companions and early righteous imams had witnessed these later Ash'ari scholars, they would have greatly praised them and directed all Muslims to seek their guidance in correcting religious foundations and fortifying against trials. The footnote provides the chain of the hadith and an explanation from al-Qari that 'distortions of the extremists' refers to innovators like the Qadariyya, Jabriyya, and Mushabbiha, and 'false claims' refers to those who falsely claim adherence to the Sunnah.

Commentary

Commentary

The Necessity and Qualities of the Imam
Sanusi argues for the obligation of the imamate and lists its required qualities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational basis for the obligation of the imamate and the specific qualities required in an imam according to Sanusi.

Core Idea
The imamate is obligatory because fully establishing good requires a qualified leader.

This lesson explores Sanusi's argument for the necessity of a single leader for the Muslim community. He establishes that the comprehensive implementation of commanding good and forbidding evil depends on appointing an imam with specific qualifications. The passage then lists those qualities and concludes that appointing such a person is a legal duty.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the English translation. Pay attention to the logical flow: a condition, a list, and a conclusion.
Notice the opening phrase 'ولما كان' which introduces a cause.
2
Identify the conditional premise
Find the statement that something is dependent on something else. What is the goal? What is it dependent on?
The full establishment of good depends on appointing an imam.
3
List the qualities of the imam
The passage enumerates several qualities. Write them down. Why do you think each one is necessary?
Qualities include being Muslim, sane, male, just, sound opinion, courage, knowledge.
4
Understand the conclusion
The passage ends with a legal obligation. What is the obligation and on whom does it fall?
It is obligatory on Muslims to appoint someone with these qualities.
5
Reflect on the reasoning
Consider why Sanusi uses this teleological argument (means-to-an-end). How does this justify the imamate?
This is a common kalam reasoning: if the end is obligatory, the necessary means is also obligatory.

وجبَ على المسلمين شرعاً تقديمُ مَنْ هذا صفته ، وتجنُّبُ مَنْ عَرِيَ عن هذهِ الصفات المذكورة أو عن بعضها بقدر الإمكان .
[ Discourse on the Greater Imamate ]
And since the ability to fully establish good and command it, support truth and its people, suppress falsehood and adhering to it in a complete manner is dependent upon appointing an imam for the Muslims who is a Muslim, sane, male, characterized by abundant justice and soundness of opinion, firmness of heart and courage, possessing strength of knowledge in the fundamentals of religion and its branches, not shaken by difficult calamities nor bewildered by their darknesses. Then it is obligatory upon the Muslims, by the Law, to put forward one who has this description, and to avoid one who is devoid of these mentioned qualities or some of them as much as possible.

Commentary
This passage provides a proof for the necessity of the imamate (caliphate) and enumerates the required characteristics of the imam. It begins by stating a conditional: the full and complete implementation of commanding good and forbidding evil, supporting truth and its people, and suppressing falsehood is dependent upon the appointment of an imam. This imam must have specific qualities: Islam, sanity, male gender, abundant justice, sound judgment, firmness, courage, and deep knowledge of both the principles (usul) and branches (furu') of the religion. He must also be unshaken by crises. Because these matters depend on such a person, it becomes a legal obligation (wajib shar'i) for the Muslim community to appoint someone with these attributes and to avoid those lacking them. The argument is teleological: the goal of establishing good requires a qualified leader, so the leader is necessary for fulfilling the duty.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think the imam's knowledge is limited to general scholarship, but Sanusi specifies 'strength of knowledge in the fundamentals of religion and its branches', indicating comprehensive religious expertise.
Correction: The passage defines knowledge as covering both usul (principles) and furu (branches), meaning the imam must have deep expertise in all aspects of Islamic learning.
