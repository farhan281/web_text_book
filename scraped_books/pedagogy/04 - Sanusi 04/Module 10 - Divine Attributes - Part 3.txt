The Three Categories of Tawhid: Essence, Attributes, Actions
Sanusi defines the three categories of divine unity and explains each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of tawhid (oneness of essence, attributes, and actions) and how each affirms God's uniqueness in a distinct way.

Core Idea
Tawhid is threefold: essence, attributes, and actions — each negates a different form of multiplicity.

In this passage, Sanusi presents the foundational classification of tawhid in Sunni theology. He explains that affirming God's oneness is not limited to His essence but extends to His attributes and actions. By defining each category, he provides a precise framework for understanding what true monotheism entails.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the author's direct meaning.
Notice that Sanusi uses a clear three-part structure explicitly marked by 'أما'.
2
Identify each definition
For each type of oneness, note what it negates: essence negates multiplicity (connected and separate), attributes negates similarity, actions negates any effect from other than God.
The negation is different for each: composition, likeness, independent causation.
3
Reflect on the conclusion
Read the final sentence: these three divisions are mandatory for every believer, and knowing them along with their proofs makes one a true monotheist.
True tawhid requires knowledge and rational demonstration.

Commentary
This passage provides a clear classification of tawhid into three types. The author first states the general obligation of affirming God's oneness in essence, attributes, and actions. Then he defines each: (1) Oneness of essence (waḥdāniyyat al-dhāt) negates all multiplicity, both internal composition (connected) and external similarity (separate). The author notes that the creed focuses on negating connected multiplicity, from which the separate is understood. (2) Oneness of attributes (waḥdāniyyat al-ṣifāt) means God alone possesses His attributes uniquely; no other being can have attributes like His. (3) Oneness of actions (waḥdāniyyat al-afʿāl) means God alone creates all things directly, and no other has any effective power. The author concludes that every believer must know these divisions and their proofs to be a true monotheist (muwaḥḥid) and a knower (ʿārif).

God's Oneness in Attributes from Impossibility of Multiple Gods
Sanusi deduces God's oneness in attributes from impossibility of multiple gods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the impossibility of multiple gods implies God's oneness not only in essence but also in attributes, meaning He has no equal.

Core Idea
God's oneness in attributes logically follows from the impossibility of multiple gods.

This lesson presents a concise rational proof from Sanusi's 'Umm al-Barahin. Having established that multiple gods are impossible, Sanusi now shows that this very impossibility also demands that God is unique in all His attributes—He has no like. The reasoning hinges on a simple logical step: if there were two beings with identical attributes, they would need an external specifier to distinguish them, which leads to contingency. This short passage is a powerful reminder that tawhid encompasses both essence and attributes.

Read the Arabic passage and translation

The Arabic is a single sentence but carries a complete argument.

This shows God's oneness in attributes is necessary.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument defending the Ash'ari position that human acts (obedience and disobedience) are not causes of reward and punishment but merely signs ('amarat) created by Allah. The passage begins by stating the thesis: obedience and disobedience are signs, not causes, of reward and punishment. It clarifies that reward and punishment have no rational cause; rather, the acts are signs created directly by Allah without any effective intermediary from the servant. These signs indicate legally (shar'an) what Allah has chosen of reward and punishment. The author then supports this by citing the Quranic verse (21:23) that Allah is not questioned about what He does, implying His absolute sovereignty. The following 'meaning' (ya'ni) expounds the underlying principle: all creatures—their essences, attributes, and acts—are acts of Allah, created with no intermediary, purpose, or effect from anything else. Therefore, all acts are equal relative to Allah and among themselves, with no causal influence. Reward and punishment are also acts of Allah, so they must occur by His sheer choice, with no other essence or act having any effect in them. The passage then deduces that human acts have no effect whatsoever in reward or punishment; otherwise, creatures would be partners with Allah in effect (ta'thir). This would contradict the previously established principle of Allah's sole exclusivity in effect and management, with no helper or deputy. The only relation between works and reward/punishment is their mere indication (dalala). Next, the author distinguishes this indication from rational evidence (adilla 'aqliyya), where the connection between evidence and evidenced is intellectual and independent of a maker's appointment (like the world's temporality indicating God's existence). Instead, the indication of works is that they are created signs appointed by Allah according to His will, with no intrinsic rational connection between the act and its reward or punishment. Because the connection is purely legal and based on Allah's choice, it is intellectually possible for a sign of reward to be made a sign of punishment and vice versa. Finally, the passage asserts that Allah could reward or punish without any preceding sign, since the sign has no effective role; His decree is pre-eternal, before the existence of the creature. This underscores the utter sovereignty and freedom of Allah in granting reward and punishment.

The Creation of Human Will and Actions
Sanusi explains that the servant's will and thoughts are created by God, citing Qushayri.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that human will and actions are created by God alone, and that even internal thoughts and intentions are subject to divine power, as affirmed by al-Qushayri's commentary.

Core Idea
Even our thoughts and intentions are created by God, not by ourselves.

Sanusi continues to build the case that God is the sole creator. After establishing that human power is created and has no independent effect, he now turns to the will and thoughts that precede action. These, too, are created by Allah. To support this, he quotes a chain of authority from al-Qushayri through al-Wasiti, making the point that just as substances are created by God alone, so are accidents—including human actions.

Proof that Lying is Impossible for God
Sanusi proves the impossibility of lying for God using multiple rational arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proofs for the impossibility of lying for God, based on His simplicity, knowledge, and the impossibility of temporal accidents.

Core Idea
God cannot lie because He is not composite and His knowledge necessitates truthfulness.

This passage from Sanusi's work presents multiple rational arguments establishing that lying is impossible for God, not merely impermissible. It contrasts the composite nature of humans with God's absolute simplicity, and shows how divine attributes necessitate truthfulness. The proofs are foundational for understanding key aqeedah points about divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Read the entire passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to get the flow of the arguments. Notice the structure: first a contrast with humans, then two proofs, then a footnote, then a point about anthropomorphists.
The passage is dense; read it slowly.
2
Analyze the first proof from God's simplicity
Identify how the author derives the impossibility of lying from God's lack of composition. Why does composition allow for different attributes to be in different 'places'?
This grounds the argument in the attribute of truthfulness.
3
Trace the second proof from possibility
Follow the reasoning: if lying were possible, it would become necessary (due to no temporal accidents), which would contradict the known necessity of truthfulness. Understand the logical steps.

Commentary
This passage proves the impossibility of lying for God through rational arguments. It begins by contrasting the composite human nature, where knowledge and truthfulness occupy a place and lying another, with God's simplicity. Then it presents a proof based on God's knowledge: since God is knowing, it is possible for Him to inform according to His knowledge; but whatever is possible for God becomes necessary because He cannot be subject to temporal accidents; thus truthfulness is necessary and lying impossible. A second argument: if lying were possible for God, it would become necessary, making truthfulness impossible, which is absurd because we know truthfulness is necessary for the All-Knowing. Then a footnote addresses the case of assuming both attributes simultaneously, showing that it leads to temporal succession or impossibility. Finally, the author notes the difficulty of proving this for anthropomorphists (al-mushabbiha) who allow temporal accidents in God, as they would have to allow lying in God's speech, which then becomes necessary if it is an attribute of essence.

Four Opinions on the Nature of I'jaz and Their Refutations
Sanusi enumerates four opinions on i'jaz and refutes each.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Qur'an's miracle cannot be reduced to a single feature.

Muslim scholars have debated what exactly makes the Qur'an miraculous. This passage presents four different opinions—ranging from its unique composition to its predictions of the unseen—and refutes each one. By examining these refutations, we learn that no single aspect fully accounts for the Qur'an's i'jaz.

Commentary
This passage lists four opinions (aqwaal) held by different scholars on what constitutes the miraculous nature (i'jaz) of the Qur'an, and refutes each. (1) Some said i'jaz lies in its unique composition (nazm) unlike any Arab speech. Refutation: even the absurd speech of the false prophet Musaylimah also has a certain composition. (2) It is due to freedom from contradiction. Refutation: many eloquent speeches are also free from contradiction. (3) It contains deep sciences and truths. Refutation: wise people's speech often includes such. (4) It informs of unseen events. Refutation: only a few verses contain such predictions; thus i'jaz would be limited to those verses, which is contrary to the widespread miraculous nature. The phrase 'خلافُ، وهو الظاهر' indicates that this fourth opinion is actually contrary to the evidence, and the correct view is that i'jaz is a combination of these aspects? The text ends by stating the last refutation but does not present the correct alternative, leaving the implication that the correct explanation is more comprehensive.
