Imam al-Shafi'i's Answers on Priorities and Proximity
Imam al-Shafi'i answers questions about the most necessary, wondrous, difficult, and near things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the comparative priorities in religious obligations, wonders, difficulties, and nearness as taught by Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Not all obligations are equal – the most obligatory is abandoning sins.

This lesson presents a famous exchange in which Imam al-Shafi'i was asked to identify the most extreme forms of four categories. His answers reveal deep insights into human heedlessness, the difficulty of patience, and the imminence of death. As you read, consider how these priorities shape the spiritual life.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the literal translation. Let the rhythm of the author's responses come through.
Note that the passage has four distinct pairs.
2
Identify each of the four pairs
The passage presents four categories: obligatory/wondrous/difficult/near, each with a base and a more intense version. Find each pair and notice the contrast.
The pairs are: repentance vs abandoning sins, time vs heedlessness, patience vs abandoning patience, hoped things vs death.
3
Reflect on why the 'more' version is indeed more important
For each pair, ask yourself why the second item is considered more extreme. Consider the spiritual implications.
For example, abandoning sins is more obligatory because repentance can be delayed or incomplete, but leaving sin is immediate.
4
Consider practical implications
Think about how these teachings apply to your own life. What does it mean that death is nearer than everything we hope for?

سئل الإمام الشافعي رضي الله عنه وأرضاه عن واجب وأوجب منه، وعجيب وأعجب منه، وصعب وأصعب منه، وقريب وأقرب منه . فأجاب بقوله : واجب على الناس أن يتوبوا، لكن ترك الذنوب أوجب، والدهر في نفسه عجيب، وغفلة الناس عنه أعجب، والصبر على النائبات صعب، لكن ترك الثوب أصعب، وكلما يُرتجى قريب، والموت من كل ذا الشر أقرب.
Imam al-Shafi'i, may God be pleased with him and grant him contentment, was asked about an obligatory act and something more obligatory, about a wondrous thing and something more wondrous, about a difficult thing and something more difficult, and about a near thing and something nearer. He answered with his saying: 'Repentance is obligatory upon people, but abandoning sins is more obligatory. Time itself is wondrous, but people's heedlessness of it is more wondrous. Patience with calamities is difficult, but abandoning the garment [possibly: patience/suffering] is more difficult. Everything hoped for is near, but death is nearer than all of these.'
Source pages p. 498

Commentary
This passage recounts a question posed to Imam al-Shāfiʿī asking him to identify the most obligatory, most wondrous, most difficult, and nearest things. He answers by contrasting a base with a more extreme version: obligatory (wājib) vs. more obligatory (awjab): repentance is obligatory, but actually leaving sins is more obligatory. Time is wondrous, but people's heedlessness is more wondrous. Patience in calamities is difficult, but abandoning the garment (possibly patience or reward) is more difficult. Finally, everything hoped for is near, but death is nearer. The lesson emphasizes priorities and the imminence of death.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think that repentance alone suffices for forgiveness, without actively abandoning sins.
Correction: Imam al-Shafi'i teaches that while repentance is obligatory, abandoning sins is even more obligatory, indicating that the proactive cessation of sin is of higher priority.

Checkpoint
According to al-Shafi'i, what is more obligatory than repentance?
A) Charity
B) Abandoning sins
C) Fasting
D) Pilgrimage

Key Takeaway
Imam al-Shafi'i teaches that beyond every obligation, wonder, difficulty, or nearness lies a more intense reality: abandoning sins, heedlessness, abandoning patience, and death.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
You know repentance is obligatory, but are you actively abandoning the sins you repent from? If not, why is 'leaving sin' not your immediate priority today?
?
You marvel at the wonders of the world, but when was the last time you were genuinely shaken by your own heedlessness of time slipping away? What will you do this week to become more mindful?
?
When calamity strikes, you find patience difficult. But have you considered that abandoning patience altogether is even harder in the long run? Will you cling to patience, or will you let go and suffer a greater loss?

The Supreme Virtues: A Kalam Guide to Ultimate Goods and Evils
A series of superlative comparisons ranking virtues and vices.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how classical scholars used superlative comparisons to rank spiritual and moral values, and why ignorance, harshness, and complaining are considered the worst.

Core Idea
The worst poverty is ignorance; the best worship is contemplation.

This passage from a classical theological work presents a series of striking comparisons that serve as a moral compass. Each statement declares the ultimate form of a virtue or vice, offering a clear hierarchy of values. Before diving into the Arabic, consider how these aphorisms might shape a believer's priorities.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage twice
First read silently to absorb the rhythm, then aloud to feel the parallel structure.
Notice the repeated pattern 'لا ... أشد من ...'.
2
Identify each comparison
List the seven pairs: poverty/ignorance, wealth/intellect, piety/restraint, pleasant life/health, goodness/righteousness, evil/harshness, worship/contemplation.
3
Reflect on the final advice
Consider why leaving complaint to God's creatures is included as a capstone. How does it tie the list together?
This emphasizes reliance on God alone.

Source Text

لا فقر أشد من الجهل ولا مال أكثر من العقل، ولا ورع أحسن من الكف عن محارم الله، ولا معيشة أهنأ من العافية، ولا حسن أعظم من البر وحسن الخلق ولا سيئة أشد من الحدة وسوء الخلق، ولا عبادة كالتفكر في مصنوعات الله تعالى، وترك الشكوى بالمصائب لخلق الله تعالى. اهـ.
There is no poverty more severe than ignorance, nor wealth greater than intelligence, nor piety better than refraining from what God has forbidden, nor life more pleasant than health, nor goodness greater than righteousness and good character, nor evil more severe than harshness and bad character, nor worship like contemplation of God's creations, and leaving off complaint about calamities to God's creatures. End.
Source pages p. 498

Commentary
This final passage presents a series of superlative comparisons. Each statement declares that a particular virtue is the greatest in its category: the worst poverty is ignorance, the best wealth is intellect, the best piety is avoiding forbidden things, the most pleasant life is health, the greatest goodness is righteousness and good character, the worst evil is harshness and bad character, and the best worship is contemplating God's creation. It concludes with the advice to refrain from complaining about trials to people, implying one should complain only to God. The term 'اهـ' marks the end of the manuscript's added benefits.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: These statements are literal factual rankings that apply in all circumstances.
Correction: They are rhetorical superlatives meant to highlight the immense value of these virtues and vices, not to deny that other forms of poverty or wealth exist.

Checkpoint
According to the passage, what is the best form of worship?
A) Fasting
B) Prayer
C) Contemplation of God's creations
D) Charity

Key Takeaway
The passage uses superlative comparisons to teach that the most valuable inner qualities are intellect, piety, good character, and contemplation, while the worst are ignorance, harshness, and complaining to people.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you experience a trial, do you find yourself complaining to others before turning to God? What would it mean for your relationship with Him if you truly practiced leaving complaint to people?
?
If you honestly examined your life, where do you invest more time and energy: in accumulating material wealth or in cultivating intellect and contemplation? Which does this passage say is the greater wealth?
?
Think of the last time you acted with harshness or bad character. In light of this lesson, what does that say about the state of your soul—and what concrete step can you take today to replace that with righteousness and good character?
