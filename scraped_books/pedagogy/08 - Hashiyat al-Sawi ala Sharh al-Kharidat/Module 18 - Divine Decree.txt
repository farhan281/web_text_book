Allah Alone Creates Human Acts
Sawi presents the doctrine and defends against an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position that Allah alone creates human voluntary acts and how the objection of human accountability is answered.

Core Idea
Allah creates all actions; human power has no effect.

Sawi begins by asserting a fundamental Ash'ari principle: Allah is the sole creator of all things, including human voluntary acts like movement and stillness. This directly challenges the Mu'tazila who claim humans create their own acts. He then anticipates an objection: if we don't create our acts, how can we be held responsible? The reply cites Quranic and Sunnah evidence that actions are attributed to us even though Allah creates them.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening assertion
Read the first sentence where Sawi states that Allah alone has influence and our power has no effect in voluntary acts. Notice the use of 'Al-Qahhar' to emphasize divine dominance.
This is the core Ash'ari stance.
2
Find the proof verse
Identify the Quranic verse (37:96) cited as evidence. Note that it is interpreted as 'He created your deed'.
3
Spot the objection
Look for the phrase 'If you say:' which introduces the Mu'tazila-like objection: if we have no power, how can actions be attributed to us and how is obligation valid?
The objection is a common challenge to predestinarian views.
4
Examine the reply
Read the answer that references Quran 9:105 and the many similar verses and hadith. Understand that attribution of acts to humans is a valid scriptural language even though Allah creates them.
This establishes the basis for human responsibility (kasb).

This passage presents the Ash'ari position on human acts: that Allah alone is the Creator of all actions, including voluntary human acts. The author begins by stating that Al-Qahhar (the Subduer) alone has influence, and our power has no effect in any voluntary actions like movements or postures. Interpreting the Quranic Argument Against the Mu'tazila
Sawi analyzes Quran 37:96 to refute Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical analysis of Q37:96 and why the masdariyyah reading strengthens the argument that Allah creates all actions.

Core Idea
Grammar clarifies creed: the masdariyyah 'ma' proves creation of acts.

Sawi delves into the precise meaning of 'wa ma ta`malun' in the Quranic verse. He compares two grammatical interpretations—masdariyyah (gerundive) and mawsulah (relative pronoun)—and argues that the masdariyyah is superior because it makes the argument clearer and avoids needing a pronoun. This refined analysis is used as a decisive refutation of the Mu'tazila doctrine.

Guided Path
1
Read the grammatical discussion
Read the passage from 'قوله : ( وَاللهُ خَلَقَكُمْ وَمَا تَعْمَلُونَ' to the end. Note the two interpretations of 'ma'.
This is a technical linguistic analysis.
2
Identify the two readings
Find where Sawi explains 'masdariyyah' (gerundive) and 'mawsulah' (relative pronoun) interpretations. Understand the difference.
Masdariyyah turns the phrase into a verbal noun; mawsulah takes it as 'that which'.
3
Understand the preference
Read why Sawi prefers the masdariyyah: it makes the argument more apparent and avoids assuming a pronoun.
This is a matter of clarity and economy.
4
See the refutation
Notice the final sentence: 'في الآية حجة لنا... ورد على المعتزلة'. The verse, properly understood, refutes the Mu'tazila claim that humans create their own acts.
The grammatical analysis is not just academic; it has theological stakes.

Objection to Verse and Answer
Refutes an objection against using the verse to prove God creates actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ash'ari theologians defend the verse 'Wa khalaqa alladhi ta'malun fihi' against a linguistic objection and why improbable interpretations do not invalidate evidence.

Core Idea
Linguistic improbability does not make a valid proof invalid.

The objection argues that the verse 'He created that which you do' might refer to the materials, not actions, thus failing to refute the Mu'tazila. The rebuttal shows this reading violates Arabic syntax. Let's examine the argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Understand the opponent's claim that the verse could mean bodies, not actions.
The objection relies on a possible alternative reading.
2
Study the answer
Examine the linguistic rule about omitting the resumptive pronoun (al-'a'id) and why it fails here.
3
Apply the principle
Reflect on how this shows that not all probabilities are equal in kalam reasoning.
Improbable interpretations do not undermine a clear proof.

And 'He created that which you do' meaning the bodies in which your work occurs, so the meaning would be: He created us and the bodies in which our actions take place — stones for building, sheep for slaughter, wood for carpentry, etc. So then the verse does not prove that God creates human actions, so there is no basis to refute the Mu'tazila with it, because when an argument is susceptible to probability, it is invalid as evidence. Commentary
This passage presents a classical objection to the Ash'ari proof from the Qur'anic verse 'وخلق الذي تعملون فيه' (Surah as-Saffat). The opponent interprets 'ما تعملون فيه' as referring to the material objects in which actions occur, so the verse only says God creates those objects, not the actions themselves. Thus the verse does not prove divine creation of actions, and the proof based on it fails. The answer refutes this interpretation on linguistic grounds: in Arabic, when a relative pronoun is governed by a genitive preposition, the resumptive pronoun (al-‘a’id) cannot be omitted. Here the relative pronoun 'ما' is not in the genitive, so the omission cannot be justified by this rule. Even if the condition were met, omitting a direct object pronoun is common, but not a genitive one. Hence the interpretation is linguistically invalid.

Divine and Human Power: Ijad and Kasb
Distinguishes divine and human power attachments via ijad and kasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between divine creation (ijad) and human acquisition (kasb) and why human power has no causal effect in Ash'ari theology.

Core Idea
Human power accompanies God's creation without effect—like fire's presence at burning.

The Ash'ari view of human action is subtle: humans have power and choice, yet only God creates. This passage explains how divine and human powers attach to the same act, with human power serving as a non-causal accompaniment. Through this distinction, scholars preserved both human responsibility and divine unity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the parallel structure: 'تعلق إيجاد' and 'تعلق كسب'.
2
Understand the two attachments
The passage defines two types of attachment for divine and human power. Divine power attaches as creation (ijad); human power attaches as acquisition (kasb). Grasp that kasb is explicitly not creation.
3
Explore the concept of accompaniment
Human power has no effect; it merely accompanies God's creation of the action. The fire analogy illustrates: fire does not cause burning but is present when God creates it.
This analogy is key to understanding the non-causal role of creatures.
4
Accountability despite non-causality
God creates inclination, intention, and power in humans. Thus actions appear as the servant's, and we are held accountable. Notice how this preserves human responsibility without attributing creation to us.
5
Connect to tawhid
The proof of tawhid forces the conclusion that only God creates; otherwise polytheism results. See how the entire theory safeguards divine unity.
This is a central argument against dualism in action.

This passage is a theological explanation of the relationship between divine power and human power, specifically the doctrine of kasb (acquisition) and the unity of acts (wahdat al-af'al). The author comments on the text's phrase 'attachment of creation,' noting that the annexation is explicative (bayaniyya). He then identifies the concept of 'unity of acts' as the gnostic's perspective: the gnostic does not see any act as truly from other than Allah. Reward is pure favor because the servant deserves nothing; punishment is just because Allah is the absolute Owner who may dispose of His property as He wills—so punishing is not injustice. Commentary
This passage is a theological explanation of the relationship between divine power and human power, specifically the doctrine of kasb (acquisition) and the unity of acts (wahdat al-af'al). The author, al-Sawi, begins by defining the two attachments: Divine power attaches to actions as creation (ijad), while human power attaches as acquisition (kasb), not creation. This means that the human power has no effective role in bringing the act into existence; its attachment is merely concomitant (muqarana), like the contact of fire with wood that coincides with burning but does not cause it (Allah creates the burning at the moment of contact). The author then explains how voluntary acts are attributed to the servant: Allah creates in the servant an inclination and intention, and a power that accompanies the divine creation of the act. In outward appearance, the act seems to be the servant's, but the proof of tawhid leads one to conclude that only Allah creates. The author comments on the text's phrase 'attachment of creation,' noting that the annexation is explicative (bayaniyya). He then identifies the concept of 'unity of acts' as the gnostic's perspective: the gnostic does not see any act as truly from other than Allah. Two poetic illustrations follow: one compares worldly actions to shadow-play figures that move while the mover remains unseen; the other compares creation to a snowball that appears solid but is only water, melting away when the sun (the light of divine existence) appears. Both poems emphasize that all apparent acts are really from Allah alone.

The second part begins with the heading 'Acts of servants and the disagreement concerning them.' Al-Sawi summarizes the doctrine: the attachment of human power is mere concomitance without effect, and acts are attributed to the servant accordingly, as the Quranic verse indicates. Reward and punishment follow from pure divine favor or justice. The servant who has concomitant power is called 'chooser' (mukhtar); if Allah creates an act in the servant without any accompanying power, the servant is 'compelled' (majbur) and obligation is removed by divine grace, though Allah could still obligate if He willed (because obligation with what is unbearable is possible in principle). The author then comments on the text's mention of reward and punishment as an ordered disclosure (laf wa nashr murattab): favor applies to reward, justice to punishment. Reward is pure favor because the servant deserves nothing; punishment is just because Allah is the absolute Owner who may dispose of His property as He wills—so punishing is not injustice. The passage concludes by affirming that obligation with what is unbearable is possible (ja'iz), which is a key Ash'ari position.

Fourth: contentment with divine decree and destiny. The fourth: contentment, which is exiting from one's own pleasure by entering into the pleasure of his Lord, by submission to eternal judgments and entrustment to everlasting arrangements, without turning away or objection. And he [the poet] alluded to it, branching from what preceded: 'So be' O seeker of the pleasure of your Master, 'to Him Exalted, submitting' in everything He has measured and decreed, or commanded of religious rulings or prohibited, by being pleased with that without turning away or objection, 'so that' i.e., in order that 'you may be safe' from the afflictions of this world and the Hereafter. His statement: 'for what He measured' i.e., and decreed. His statement: 'without any alleviation for him nor any helper to aid him' contains an allusion to the parable that Allah struck for those who do not endure His judgments in His statement: 'Whoever thinks that Allah will never help him in this world and the Hereafter, let him stretch a rope to the ceiling, then cut it, and see if his plan removes that which enrages him.' (22:15) His statement: 'in everything He has measured and decreed' i.e., of good and evil. His statement: 'without turning away' i.e., from what He has commanded and prohibited. And his statement: 'and without objection' i.e., to what He has measured and decreed. In it is a disordered wrapping and spreading.

Commentary
This passage defines the fourth principle of the spiritual path: contentment (al-riḍā) with divine decree. Al-Sawi begins by stating the station and then defines it: contentment is exiting from one's own pleasure and entering into the pleasure of God. This is expressed through two actions: submission (al-taslīm) to eternal judgments and entrustment (al-tafwīḍ) to everlasting arrangements, all without turning away (iʿrāḍ) or objection (iʿtirāḍ). He then references the poet's line that commands the seeker to be submissive to God in everything He has measured, decreed, commanded, or prohibited, so that one may be safe from worldly and otherworldly afflictions. Al-Sawi then unpacks key phrases from the poem. 'For what He measured' means 'what He decreed.' The line about 'no alleviation and no helper' alludes to the Quranic verse (22:15) about those who think God will not help them, implying that those who do not patiently accept God's decrees will be utterly lost. 'In everything He measured and decreed' includes both good and evil. 'Without turning away' refers to not turning away from God's commands and prohibitions, while 'without objection' refers to not objecting to His decree. Al-Sawi notes that this phrasing involves a disordered wrapping and spreading (laff wa nashr mushabbash), meaning the order of explanation is jumbled but the meaning is clear. Essentially, contentment requires complete surrender to God's will in all matters.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think contentment means passive resignation without any action or effort.
Correction: Al-Sawi defines contentment as active surrender through submission (taslīm) and entrustment (tafwīḍ), which involve fulfilling obligations and accepting decrees willingly, not passivity.

According to Al-Sawi, what does contentment (al-riḍā) with divine decree involve?
A) Passively accepting whatever happens without action
B) Exiting one's own pleasure and entering into God's pleasure through submission and entrustment
C) Objecting to evil decrees while accepting good ones
D) Only turning away from prohibitions, not commands

Key Takeaway
Contentment with divine decree means actively surrendering your own will to God's will through submission and entrustment, avoiding both turning away from His commands and objecting to His decree.
