The Benefit of Creed and Tawhid for Actions
Author explains benefit of creed and role of tawhid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that creed initiates action whereas tawhid ensures its success, as illustrated by practical examples.

Core Idea
Creed starts the engine; tawhid steers it to success.

The author begins by stating the worldly benefit of studying creed: correctness and success of deeds. He distinguishes between creed as a condition for beginning an action and tawhid as a condition for its success. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its real-world implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key distinction
Read the passage and note the two different roles: creed enables starting, tawhid ensures success.
The author uses the same examples for both.
2
Understand the examples
Think about ships, cars, planes, kingdoms: they all need unity of purpose and leadership to succeed.
This is a universal principle, not just religious.
3
Connect to your own life
Ask yourself: where do I have multiple goals or leaders causing failure? How does tawhid bring focus?

In the name of God, the Merciful, the Compassionate. Praise be to God, the Bestower of blessings and gifts, and the pure blessed prayers upon our master and lord Muhammad, the master of creation, and upon his family, possessors of glory and honor, and abundant peace until the Day of Judgment, Amen. To proceed: My master, the scholar, the knower of God, Muhammad al-Hashimi – may God have mercy on him and be pleased with him – was asked about the benefit that the reader of the science of Islamic creed reaps and what is the calamity that befalls him from not reading it? So he answered, may God be pleased with him: The scholars, indeed all rational people, said: Its benefit in this world is the correctness of deeds and their success; legally, customarily, and rationally. For creed, whether sound or corrupt, is a condition for the beginning of action, and tawhid is a condition for its success. Creed is a strong motivator for action, whether good or evil, and tawhid is a condition for the success of that action. For it is impossible to begin an action with determination, strength, and earnestness except with creed and tawhid. Thus, the safety of a ship, car, plane, kingdom, school, factory, town, or house is impossible without unity of goal and leadership, and the success of an action whose leaders and goals are multiple is impossible. As for the calamity that befalls the one lacking creed regarding something by which he brings benefit or repels harm: it is perpetual confusion, regret, disappointment, hesitation, and obsessions. He is unable to begin an action with determination and resolve, and if he begins, he cannot persevere in it. As for the calamity that befalls the one lacking tawhid in all his deeds: their corruption and lack of success. If it is a ship, it sinks; or a car, it breaks down; or a plane, it falls and crashes; or a kingdom, it disintegrates; or a school, factory, town, or house, it becomes ruined and perishes. What calamity is greater and clearer than this? This is concerning absolute creed and absolute tawhid. As for the calamity of corrupt creed: it is the hatred of rational people for him, his fall from the virtues of human morals, his deprivation of civil rights, and his exposure to worldly punishments such as humiliation, bankruptcy, beating, imprisonment, and execution. What calamity is greater than this? The sound creed is that which conforms to reality and is supported by transmitted evidence and reason. The corrupt, defective creed is that which does not conform to reality and is not supported by transmitted evidence or reason. In summary: the benefit of a corrupt creed, if attained, is winning some immediate, psychological, lustful, animal, temporary pleasures, followed by regret and punishment in this world and the Hereafter. The benefit of the sound, safe creed is winning in this world correctness of deeds, beautiful praise, security, trust in its possessor, wealth, comfort, tranquility, and victory over enemies; and in the Hereafter, winning the pleasure of the Merciful and entering Paradise. Thus, the science of creed is considered among the noblest and highest sciences, the most obligatory and deserving, and among the most important to acquire and venerate. Therefore, the scholars of the early and later generations gave great attention to this science and its teaching to all segments of society. They authored texts, commentaries, glosses, lengthy works, and abridgments to suit all levels. (1) Miftah al-Jannah (pp. 23-24).

Commentary
This passage is an introduction (between yaday al-kitab) that explains the importance of studying Islamic creed ('aqidah). It begins with the author citing a question put to the scholar Muhammad al-Hashimi: what benefit does the reader of the science of Islamic creed reap, and what calamity befalls him if he does not read it? The answer is then given in several stages. 

First, the general benefit of creed in this world is the correctness and success of actions, considered legally, customarily, and rationally. The answer makes a key distinction: creed itself (whether sound or corrupt) is a condition for beginning an action, while tawhid (monotheism) is a condition for its success. Creed motivates action, but tawhid ensures that the action succeeds. To illustrate, the author gives examples: a ship, car, plane, kingdom, school, factory, town, or house cannot be safe without unity of goal and leadership. Multiple leaders with conflicting goals lead to failure. 

Second, the calamity of lacking creed entirely: perpetual confusion, regret, disappointment, hesitation, and obsessions. Such a person cannot start an action with determination, and if he starts, he cannot persist. 

Third, the calamity of lacking tawhid specifically: all deeds become corrupt and unsuccessful. The examples are repeated with explicit consequences: a ship sinks, a car breaks down, a plane crashes, a kingdom disintegrates, a school or factory or town or house is ruined. This is said to be the greatest and clearest calamity, applying to both absolute creed and absolute tawhid. 

Fourth, the calamity of having a corrupt creed: the person is hated by rational people, loses human moral virtues, is deprived of civil rights, and suffers worldly punishments like humiliation, bankruptcy, beating, imprisonment, and execution. Again, what calamity is greater? 

Fifth, the author defines sound creed and corrupt creed. Sound creed conforms to reality and is supported by both transmitted evidence (naql) and reason ('aql). Corrupt creed does not conform to reality and is supported by neither. 

Sixth, the benefit of corrupt creed (if any) is only temporary animal pleasures followed by regret and punishment in both worlds. In contrast, the benefit of sound creed includes in this world: correctness of deeds, praise, security, trust, wealth, comfort, tranquillity, and victory over enemies; and in the Hereafter: God's pleasure and entrance into Paradise. 

Finally, the author concludes that the science of creed is among the noblest and highest sciences, the most obligatory and deserving, and the most important to acquire and venerate. Hence, scholars of all generations paid great attention to it, authoring texts, commentaries, glosses, long and short works to suit all levels. The passage ends with a footnote reference to Miftah al-Jannah.

Throughout, the argument is practical: creed is not abstract theology but a necessary foundation for successful worldly and religious life.

To proceed: My master, the scholar, the knower of God, Muhammad al-Hashimi – may God have mercy on him and be pleased with him – was asked about the benefit that the reader of the science of Islamic creed reaps and what is the calamity that befalls him from not reading it? So he answered, may God be pleased with him: The scholars, indeed all rational people, said: Its benefit in this world is the correctness of deeds and their success; legally, customarily, and rationally. For creed, whether sound or corrupt, is a condition for the beginning of action, and tawhid is a condition for its success. Creed is a strong motivator for action, whether good or evil, and tawhid is a condition for the success of that action. For it is impossible to begin an action with determination, strength, and earnestness except with creed and tawhid. Thus, the safety of a ship, car, plane, kingdom, school, factory, town, or house is impossible without unity of goal and leadership, and the success of an action whose leaders and goals are multiple is impossible. As for the calamity that befalls the one lacking creed regarding something by which he brings benefit or repels harm: it is perpetual confusion, regret, disappointment, hesitation, and obsessions. He is unable to begin an action with determination and resolve, and if he begins, he cannot persevere in it. As for the calamity that befalls the one lacking tawhid in all his deeds: their corruption and lack of success. If it is a ship, it sinks; or a car, it breaks down; or a plane, it falls and crashes; or a kingdom, it disintegrates; or a school, factory, town, or house, it becomes ruined and perishes. What calamity is greater and clearer than this? This is concerning absolute creed and absolute tawhid. As for the calamity of corrupt creed: it is the hatred of rational people for him, his fall from the virtues of human morals, his deprivation of civil rights, and his exposure to worldly punishments such as humiliation, bankruptcy, beating, imprisonment, and execution. What calamity is greater than this? The sound creed is that which conforms to reality and is supported by transmitted evidence and reason. The corrupt, defective creed is that which does not conform to reality and is not supported by transmitted evidence or reason. In summary: the benefit of a corrupt creed, if attained, is winning some immediate, psychological, lustful, animal, temporary pleasures, followed by regret and punishment in this world and the Hereafter. The benefit of the sound, safe creed is winning in this world correctness of deeds, beautiful praise, security, trust in its possessor, wealth, comfort, tranquility, and victory over enemies; and in the Hereafter, winning the pleasure of the Merciful and entering Paradise. Thus, the science of creed is considered among the noblest and highest sciences, the most obligatory and deserving, and among the most important to acquire and venerate. Therefore, the scholars of the early and later generations gave great attention to this science and its teaching to all segments of society. They authored texts, commentaries, glosses, lengthy works, and abridgments to suit all levels. (1) Miftah al-Jannah (pp. 23-24).

Commentary
This passage is an introduction (between yaday al-kitab) that explains the importance of studying Islamic creed ('aqidah). It begins with the author citing a question put to the scholar Muhammad al-Hashimi: what benefit does the reader of the science of Islamic creed reap, and what calamity befalls him if he does not read it? The answer is then given in several stages. 

First, the general benefit of creed in this world is the correctness and success of actions, considered legally, customarily, and rationally. The answer makes a key distinction: creed itself (whether sound or corrupt) is a condition for beginning an action, while tawhid (monotheism) is a condition for its success. Creed motivates action, but tawhid ensures that the action succeeds. To illustrate, the author gives examples: a ship, car, plane, kingdom, school, factory, town, or house cannot be safe without unity of goal and leadership. Multiple leaders with conflicting goals lead to failure. 

Second, the calamity of lacking creed entirely: perpetual confusion, regret, disappointment, hesitation, and obsessions. Such a person cannot start an action with determination, and if he starts, he cannot persist. 

Third, the calamity of lacking tawhid specifically: all deeds become corrupt and unsuccessful. The examples are repeated with explicit consequences: a ship sinks, a car breaks down, a plane crashes, a kingdom disintegrates, a school or factory or town or house is ruined. This is said to be the greatest and clearest calamity, applying to both absolute creed and absolute tawhid. 

Fourth, the calamity of having a corrupt creed: the person is hated by rational people, loses human moral virtues, is deprived of civil rights, and suffers worldly punishments like humiliation, bankruptcy, beating, imprisonment, and execution. Again, what calamity is greater? 

Fifth, the author defines sound creed and corrupt creed. Sound creed conforms to reality and is supported by both transmitted evidence (naql) and reason ('aql). Corrupt creed does not conform to reality and is supported by neither. 

Sixth, the benefit of corrupt creed (if any) is only temporary animal pleasures followed by regret and punishment in both worlds. In contrast, the benefit of sound creed includes in this world: correctness of deeds, praise, security, trust, wealth, comfort, tranquillity, and victory over enemies; and in the Hereafter: God's pleasure and entrance into Paradise. 

Finally, the author concludes that the science of creed is among the noblest and highest sciences, the most obligatory and deserving, and the most important to acquire and venerate. Hence, scholars of all generations paid great attention to it, authoring texts, commentaries, glosses, long and short works to suit all levels. The passage ends with a footnote reference to Miftah al-Jannah.

Throughout, the argument is practical: creed is not abstract theology but a necessary foundation for successful worldly and religious life.

Commentary
This passage is an introduction (between yaday al-kitab) that explains the importance of studying Islamic creed ('aqidah). It begins with the author citing a question put to the scholar Muhammad al-Hashimi: what benefit does the reader of the science of Islamic creed reap, and what calamity befalls him if he does not read it? The answer is then given in several stages. 

First, the general benefit of creed in this world is the correctness and success of actions, considered legally, customarily, and rationally. The answer makes a key distinction: creed itself (whether sound or corrupt) is a condition for beginning an action, while tawhid (monotheism) is a condition for its success. Creed motivates action, but tawhid ensures that the action succeeds. To illustrate, the author gives examples: a ship, car, plane, kingdom, school, factory, town, or house cannot be safe without unity of goal and leadership. Multiple leaders with conflicting goals lead to failure. 

Second, the calamity of lacking creed entirely: perpetual confusion, regret, disappointment, hesitation, and obsessions. Such a person cannot start an action with determination, and if he starts, he cannot persist. 

Third, the calamity of lacking tawhid specifically: all deeds become corrupt and unsuccessful. The examples are repeated with explicit consequences: a ship sinks, a car breaks down, a plane crashes, a kingdom disintegrates, a school or factory or town or house is ruined. This is said to be the greatest and clearest calamity, applying to both absolute creed and absolute tawhid. 

Fourth, the calamity of having a corrupt creed: the person is hated by rational people, loses human moral virtues, is deprived of civil rights, and suffers worldly punishments like humiliation, bankruptcy, beating, imprisonment, and execution. Again, what calamity is greater? 

Fifth, the author defines sound creed and corrupt creed. Sound creed conforms to reality and is supported by both transmitted evidence (naql) and reason ('aql). Corrupt creed does not conform to reality and is supported by neither. 

Sixth, the benefit of corrupt creed (if any) is only temporary animal pleasures followed by regret and punishment in both worlds. In contrast, the benefit of sound creed includes in this world: correctness of deeds, praise, security, trust, wealth, comfort, tranquillity, and victory over enemies; and in the Hereafter: God's pleasure and entrance into Paradise. 

Finally, the author concludes that the science of creed is among the noblest and highest sciences, the most obligatory and deserving, and the most important to acquire and venerate. Hence, scholars of all generations paid great attention to it, authoring texts, commentaries, glosses, long and short works to suit all levels. The passage ends with a footnote reference to Miftah al-Jannah.

Throughout, the argument is practical: creed is not abstract theology but a necessary foundation for successful worldly and religious life.

His saying: (In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful, praise be to Allah): The discussion on the basmalah and hamdalah will come clarified in the words of the commentator when he mentions the text for them. His saying: (Who illuminated our hearts... etc.): In it is a good opening and an excellent beginning, which is that the author or orator, for example, begins his speech with what indicates his purpose. Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the opening formulas of the matn. The author, al-Sawi, first notes that the full discussion of the basmalah and hamdalah will come later in the commentary on the matn proper. He then focuses on the phrase 'alladhi nawwara qulubana' (Who illuminated our hearts). He explains that this is a good opening (husn iftitah) and an excellent beginning (bara'at matla'), where the author signals his purpose from the start. He then defines 'alladhi' grammatically: it is a relative pronoun (ism mawsul) that is 'juz'i' (particular) both in definition (wad'an) and in usage (isti'malan), according to al-'Adud and al-Sayyid, opposing al-Sa'd who considered it kulli (universal) in definition but juz'i in usage. The pronoun is used to connect a descriptive sentence to a known referent. Here it describes Allah with His attribute of illumination, which is known from the Qur'an. The purpose is to praise Allah and indicate that He alone deserves praise. Al-Sawi then anticipates an objection: since an adjective (na't) should be derived (mushtaqq) but the relative pronoun is uninflected (jamid), it cannot serve as an adjective. The answer is that the relative pronoun is interpreted as a derived term, i.e., 'Praise be to Allah who is described as being a light.' This defends the usage.
