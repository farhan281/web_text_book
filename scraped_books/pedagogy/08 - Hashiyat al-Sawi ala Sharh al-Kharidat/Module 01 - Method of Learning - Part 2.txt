The Hidden Predicate: Two Interpretations of the Shahada
Al-Sawi discusses two interpretations of the predicate of 'la ilaha' and their objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the predicate of 'la ilaha' is omitted and how scholars estimate it as 'existing' or 'possible', along with the objections and answers.

Core Idea
The shahada's predicate is hidden; its estimation shapes theological meaning.

This lesson tackles a subtle grammatical issue in the shahada. The predicate of 'la' is not stated, so scholars supply it. The choice of word—'existing' or 'possible'—affects theological implications. Al-Sawi presents both views and their defenses.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice that the predicate of 'la' is missing and scholars supply it.
The text uses the word 'تقدير' meaning estimation.
2
Identify the two predicates
Look for two proposals: one estimates the predicate from 'وجود' (existence), the other from 'إمكان' (possibility). Understand each phrasing.
3
Understand the objections
For the first (existence), the objection is that it implies other gods are possible. For the second (possibility), the objection is that it implies God is merely possible.
4
See the answers
Both objections are answered by saying that the theological truths (negation of possibility for others, necessity of existence for God) are known from rational proof, not from the shahada's wording alone.
The exception clause 'إلا الله' only affirms existence, not negates possibility.
5
Reflect on the significance
Think about why this matters: the shahada is not a standalone theological argument; it relies on rational proof for its meaning.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Al-Sawi carefully analyzed the meaning of a simple prayer he included in his book. When you recite common Islamic phrases like 'رضي الله عنهم' or 'بارك الله فيك', do you ever stop to consider their precise meaning? What would change in your heart if you did?
?
The author deliberately ended his work by emulating the Quran's conclusion. In your daily life, do you consciously imitate the Quran's language and spirit, or does your speech and behavior follow convenience? What specific step can you take today to align more closely with the Quran?
?
Al-Sawi sought blessing (tabarruk) by connecting his writing to a Quranic verse. How often do you intentionally seek Allah's blessing in your ordinary tasks—studying, working, even resting? If you were to treat every activity as an opportunity for tabarruk, what would feel different?
