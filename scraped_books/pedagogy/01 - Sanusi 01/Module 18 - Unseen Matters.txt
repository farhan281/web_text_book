Children and the Command to Command
Sanusi applies the uṣūlī debate on command-to-command to children's legal responsibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the uṣūlī debate about 'the command to command' affects whether children are considered legally addressed by the Law, and how the example of prayer illustrates the nuanced application of taklīf to minors.

Core Idea
Whether a parent's command counts as Allah's command hinges on a fine point in legal theory.

This passage builds on the earlier principle that children are not normally subject to sharʿī rulings. But what about when a parent tells a child to pray? Is that command from the parent actually a command from the Law? Sanusi brings in a key debate from uṣūl al-fiqh to show that the answer determines whether the child is obligated before Allah or merely following a guardian's instruction. Understanding this nuance helps clarify the nature of religious training for children.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then its translation. Note the logical flow: the uṣūl question, its two possible answers, and the implications for children.
This passage is a short but dense legal analysis.
2
Identify the two positions
The passage starts with a disagreement in uṣūl: does 'the command to command something' count as a command for that thing? Identify the two views and their consequences for children.
Think of this as a key principle in legal theory.
3
Follow the analogy with nadb
Sanusi compares the case of children to voluntary recommendations (nadb) for adults. Notice how he argues that if nadb is considered a charge (taklīf) for adults, then children's obligation for prayer is even stronger because they face a punishment for leaving it.
This is a qiyās (analogy) argument.
4
Distinguish age groups
The passage distinguishes between children over ten (who get punished for missing prayer) and under ten (whose request is like a recommended act). Mark this distinction and its rationale.
The age of ten is based on hadith.
5
Consider the consensus caveat
Sanusi ends with a caution: unless there is consensus that puberty is a condition for taklīf. This shows the debate is not settled.
This keeps the discussion open.

And consider this with what was mentioned in uṣūl of the disagreement concerning the command to command something: is it a command for that thing or not? If it is said: it is not a command, then the children remain, the Lawgiver has not commanded them, so what pertains to them is not the ruling of the Law but the ruling of their guardians. And if we say: it is a command for it, then the more likely is that children are responsible from the Law with such a command. And if voluntary recommendation (nadb) is a charge (taklīf) with respect to adults according to a saying, while no legal penalty follows from abandoning it either in this world or the Hereafter, then commanding children to pray is more likely to be a charge, because they deserve a legal penalty in this world for abandoning it. This applies to those who have reached ten years among them, and whoever has not reached that, the request for prayer from him is like a voluntary recommendation with respect to one who has reached, and it is a charge according to a saying—unless there is consensus that puberty is a condition for responsibility, so consider that.

Commentary
This passage addresses the legal status of children with respect to sharʿī rulings, building on the earlier conclusion that a child is not normally subject to rulings. It brings in a controversial issue from uṣūl al-fiqh: whether the command to command something (al-amr bi-l-amr bi-l-shay') constitutes a command for that thing itself. If it does not, then when a parent tells a child to pray, the child is not commanded by the Law—only by the parent. If it does, then the child is charged with that command from the Law. The author then strengthens the latter view by analogy: even voluntary recommendations (mandūb) are considered charges (taklīf) for adults in one opinion, even though no penalty results from omission. Since children who abandon prayer incur a legal penalty in this world (according to the ḥadīth about beating at ten), their obligation is even more plausible. The passage distinguishes between those ten years and older (who are punished) and younger children, whose prayer request is like a recommended act. However, the author cautions that if there is consensus that puberty is a condition for taklīf, then the whole discussion is moot. This shows the nuanced debate in uṣūl about the nature of legal address to minors.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Children have no religious obligations whatsoever before puberty.
Correction: This passage shows that there is a view that children can be legally charged (mukallaf) with some acts, like prayer, especially after the age of ten, based on the command to command and the analogy with nadb.

Key Takeaway
The legal status of children in sharīʿa is nuanced; while they are not fully responsible, certain commands (like prayer after age ten) can be considered obligations from the Law, depending on how one interprets the 'command to command'.

Learning Objective

The author begins by affirming the legitimacy of seeking intercession through Allah's chosen beings. He then presents a series of Quranic verses and a prophetic tradition that demonstrate how the Law encourages considering specific places, traces of the righteous, times, and persons in supplication. And tawassul is not worship of the one through whom one seeks intercession, rather it is a means that the Law has approved, just as it approved taking means through actions in His saying, Mighty and Majestic: {Enter Paradise for what you used to do} [An-Nahl: 32], despite the fact that entering it is purely by the grace of God Almighty. And whoever among the kings knows that, he destroys him and his partner if he is pleased with that partnership. So it has become clear to you: it is an arrow and a disturbance of their intellects in this polytheism in every aspect. We seek refuge in the face of our Generous Master from every doubt, polytheism, hypocrisy, and evil morals until death; by the rank of His prophet and the most honorable of His creation, our master Muhammad, may God bless him and grant him peace.

Commentary
This passage is a combined proof and refutation. The author first establishes the legitimacy of tawassul (seeking intercession) through prophets, angels, and saints, citing extensive evidence from the Quran and hadith regarding the observance of place, traces of the righteous, time, and persons. Then, he refutes a false analogy that compares tawassul to the practice of polytheists who associate others with a king. He explains that the Law does not permit treating the intercessors as equal to God in sovereignty and service. A long footnote further clarifies the distinction: shirk al-taqreeb is worship of other than God, whereas tawassul is a mere means (tassabub) sanctioned by the Sharia, similar to the principle that entering Paradise is by grace yet attributed to deeds. The passage ends with the conclusion that the polytheists' analogy is utterly misguided, and a dua seeking protection from shirk and evil.

Refutation of False Analogy and Distinction from Shirk
The author refutes the false analogy and differentiates tawassul from shirk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's refutation of the false analogy comparing tawassul to polytheistic practices and the clear distinction between tawassul and shirk al-taqreeb.

Core Idea
Tawassul is a means, not worship; confusing them is a grave error.

The author now addresses a common objection: that tawassul resembles the polytheists who set up partners with a king. He explains why this analogy fails and clarifies that tawassul is a sanctioned means (tassabub), while shirk al-taqreeb involves worship. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Two Interpretations of the Hypocrites' Testimony
Sanusi presents two possible ta'wil for why the hypocrites were called liars in Quran 63:1.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two possible interpretations (ta'wil) offered by Sanusi for why the hypocrites in Quran 63:1 were called liars, focusing on the difference between their insincere testimony and their denial of other statements.

Core Idea
The hypocrites were called liars either because they testified insincerely or because they denied their own divisive words.

The first interpretation focuses on their insincerity in the testimony itself, while the second focuses on their cover-up of earlier statements. The footnotes clarify the referenced verses.

Commentary
This passage is an exegetical analysis of the concept of takdhib (accusation of lying) in the context of the hypocrites' statements in Sūrat al-Munāfiqūn. The author presents three possible interpretations. First, the takdhib may be directed at the hypocrites' own testimony of faith (their saying 'you are the Messenger of Allah') because in their hearts they did not believe it; thus they are accused of lying in their own profession of faith. Second, the takdhib may be directed at their subsequent denial of having uttered the harmful statements (such as 'Do not spend...' and 'the mightier will expel the meaner'); Allah confirms that they did indeed utter those statements. Third, it is possible that the term 'kadhib' is used metaphorically (tajawwuz) to refer not to deliberate falsehood but to their mistake (ghalat) in using the testimony of faith incorrectly—they uttered the words without corresponding belief. The author notes that this metaphorical interpretation explains their misuse of the testimony as an error rather than outright lying. The discussion moves through these possibilities in the order presented, each building on the preceding to clarify the precise charge against the hypocrites.
