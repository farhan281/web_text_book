Definitions of Trust and Betrayal
Sanusi defines trust and betrayal in the eighth introduction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definitions of trust (al-amanah) and betrayal (al-khiyanah) as taught by Imam al-Sanusi, encompassing both external and internal limbs.

Core Idea
Trust guards every limb from sin; betrayal is the failure to do so.

In this passage, Imam al-Sanusi presents the eighth introduction of his work, defining two central ethical concepts: trust (al-amanah) and betrayal (al-khiyanah). These definitions are foundational for understanding Islamic moral responsibility, as they encompass both outward actions and inner states. Let's explore the text directly to see how the author distills this profound idea into a few lines.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing. Let the rhythm of Sanusi's concise style sink in. Then read the translation.
Notice the prayer at the end – 'And with Allah is success' – it frames the entire discussion.
2
Identify the two definitions
The passage gives two definitions: one for trust and one for betrayal. Find them and note how they are opposites.
Trust is defined positively (safeguarding), betrayal negatively (failure to safeguard).
3
Understand 'all limbs'
Sanusi specifies 'all external and internal limbs'. Reflect on what internal limbs are (heart, mind, intention) and why they are included.
This shows that trustworthiness is not just outward obedience but inner purity too.
4
Examine the two types of prohibitions
The text mentions 'prohibition of tahrim' (forbidden) and 'prohibition of karahah' (disliked). Understand the difference and why both matter in safeguarding.
Betrayal includes not only haram acts but also makruh ones – showing the high standard of trust.
5
Connect to daily life
Think of an example from your own life where you safeguard your tongue (external) or your heart (internal) from something prohibited or disliked.
The lesson is practical: trust is a constant state of vigilance.

وَالأَمَانَةُ : حِفْظُ جَمِيعِ الْجَوَارِحِ الظَّاهِرَةِ وَالْبَاطِنَةِ مِنَ التَّلَبْسِ بِمَنْهِي عَنْهُ نَهْيَ تَحْرِيمٍ أَوْ كَرَاهَةٍ

The eighth introduction: On Trust. And trust (al-amanah) is the safeguarding of all the external and internal limbs from engaging in that which is forbidden by a prohibition of prohibition or detestability. And betrayal (al-khiyanah) is the failure to safeguard them from that. And with Allah Most High is success.
Source pages p. 103

Commentary
This passage is a definition-and-contrast passage. The author, Imam al-Sanusi, presents the eighth introduction of his work, which is on the concept of trust (al-amanah). He defines trust as the preservation of all the limbs—both the external ones like hands and tongue, and the internal ones like the heart and mind—from becoming involved in anything that is prohibited either by a definitive prohibition (nahy tahrīm) or by a detestability prohibition (nahy karāhah). Then he immediately gives the opposite definition: betrayal (al-khiyanah) is the failure to preserve those limbs from such forbidden or disliked acts. The passage concludes with a humble acknowledgment that success in this comes only from Allah. The definitions are concise and cover the full scope of ethical responsibility in Islam, emphasizing that trustworthiness is not merely about external compliance but also internal purity.

Checkpoint

Key Takeaway
Trust is a comprehensive safeguarding of all limbs from both forbidden and disliked acts, while betrayal is the neglect of that duty.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?

?
The text ends by attributing success to Allah. If true trust depends on divine help, where in your life are you relying on your own strength instead of asking Allah for tawfiq? What will you change to humble yourself before Him?

Refuting the Doubt of Shirk and Defining Shirk
Sanusi refutes the doubt of polytheists and defines shirk via Taftazani

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between tawassul (seeking nearness) and shirk, and the precise definition of shirk according to Taftazani.

Core Idea
Tawassul is not shirk because shirk requires affirming a partner in divinity.

This passage addresses a common doubt: that seeking intercession through prophets or saints is shirk. Sanusi refutes this by showing the doubt only leads to seeking nearness, not worship of another deity. He then provides Taftazani's definition of shirk to clarify the boundary.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the doubt
Read the Arabic passage once, then its translation. Notice how the author begins with the doubt of those who associate partners through 'taqrib' (seeking nearness).
The doubt is that seeking nearness to God through intermediaries amounts to shirk.
2
Understand Taftazani's definition of shirk
Focus on the quote from al-Taftazani. He defines shirk in two ways: affirming necessity of existence to another, or attributing worship to another. Memorize these two meanings.
3
See why tawassul is not shirk
The author argues that tawassul does not include either meaning of shirk. Reflect on why seeking intercession is merely asking a honored person to pray, not worshiping them.
This is the key takeaway: tawassul is permissible and not shirk.

وإذا نظرت إلى شبهة هؤلاء الذين أشركوا بالتقريب . . وجدتها غير مقتضية للشرك (1)، وإنما تقتضي مجرد التقرب إلى الملك بمن هو شريف عنده إن علم أن الملك يأذن في ذلك ويحبه.

And if you consider the doubt of those who associated partners by way of approximation, you find it does not necessitate shirk (1), but rather it necessitates merely drawing near to the King by one who is honored with Him if it is known that the King permits and loves that. (1) The eminent Imam Sa'd al-Taftazani said in Sharh al-'Aqa'id al-Nasafiyya (p.212): 'Shirk is affirming a partner in divinity; either in the sense of necessity of existence as the Magians do, or in the sense of deserving worship as the idolaters do.' And you know that the meaning of tawassul in itself does not include either of these two meanings; because the reality of shirk is attributing a property specific to God to other than the creation; as the author says in Sharh al-Jaza'iriyya (p.156), but they detailed the discussion in some issues, such as affirming effective power for the originated among the Mu'tazila, and they did not declare them disbelievers on the soundest of the two opinions.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a doubt. It begins by presenting the doubt of those who associate partners through 'taqrib' (seeking nearness). The author states that this doubt does not actually entail shirk, but only seeking nearness to God through someone who is honored, with the condition that God permits and loves it. Then the author quotes Taftazani's definition of shirk: affirming a partner in divinity either in terms of necessity of existence (as Magians do) or in terms of deserving worship (as idolaters do). Then the author argues that tawassul does not include either meaning; shirk is attributing a specific property of God to others. The mention of Mu'tazila serves to illustrate that even in controversial issues, scholars do not hastily declare disbelief, further distancing tawassul from shirk.

Ruling on Shirk al-Aghrād (Riyā')
Sanusi defines the sixth category (showing off) and states its ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the ruling on performing righteous deeds for worldly purposes (shirk al-aghrād), the exception when the goal is to aid obedience, and how to interpret hadiths about worship increasing provision.

Core Idea
Not all worldly intentions in worship are forbidden; seeking aid for obedience is exempt.

Sanusi continues his enumeration of categories by addressing a subtle form of shirk: performing good deeds for worldly gains. He clarifies the ruling, an important exception, and explains how certain hadiths about worship causing provision are to be understood as spiritual expansion rather than material reward.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then its literal translation. Notice how Sanusi defines the sixth category and states its ruling.
The passage is a continuous explanation; pay attention to the exception.
2
Identify the definition and ruling
Find where Sanusi defines 'shirk al-aghrād' and what he says about its ruling. Identify the conditions that make it forbidden riyā'.
The ruling is 'disobedience' (ma'siya).
3
Understand the exception and its application
Notice the exception: seeking a worldly goal to aid in obedience is not riyā'. Then see how this principle is applied to interpret hadiths about worship and provision.
The interpretation leads to spiritual expansion through contentment and asceticism.

Source Text

قوله : ( وحكمُ السادس : المعصية ) يعني بالسادس : شرك الأغراض ؛ وهو أن يعمل عملاً مِنَ الأعمال الصالحة بنيّة الوصولِ بهِ إلى غرض دنيوي ؛ وهو رياء محرَّم ، سواء طلبَ ذلكَ الغرضَ مِنَ الخلْقِ أو مِنْ مولانا جلَّ وعزَّ ، إلا أن يطلب ذلك الغرض الدنيوي ليستعين به على طاعتِهِ تبارك وتعالى ، فلا يكون ذلكَ حينَئِذٍ رياءً . وعلى هذا يُحمَلُ ما ورد في بعض الطاعات أنَّها سبب للتوسع في الرِّزْقِ ، وقد يُحمَلُ ذلكَ على التوسعة المعنوية ؛ بخلْقِ القناعة ، والزهد ، والغَناءِ بالمولى تبارك وتعالى عن كلَّ ما سواه ، وهذا هو الغناء الأكبر والتوسعة الحقيقية
His statement: 'The ruling of the sixth is disobedience.' He means by the sixth: association of purposes (shirk al-aghrād); it is that one performs an act from righteous deeds with the intention of attaining through it a worldly goal; it is forbidden showing off (riyā'), whether he seeks that goal from creation or from our Master, Glorious and Exalted, unless he seeks that worldly goal to seek aid through it in His obedience, Blessed and Exalted, so that is not then showing off. And upon this is interpreted what is reported concerning some acts of obedience that they are a cause for expansion in provision; and it may be interpreted as spiritual expansion, through the creation of contentment (al-qanā'a), asceticism (al-zuhd), and self-sufficiency (al-ghinā') with the Master, Blessed and Exalted, from everything other than Him, and this is the greater self-sufficiency and the true expansion.
Source pages p. 187

Commentary
This passage explains the ruling for the sixth category of shirk, which is shirk al-aghrād: performing a righteous deed for a worldly purpose. The author states that this is forbidden riyā' (showing off), whether one seeks the worldly goal from people or directly from Allah. However, there is an exception: if one seeks the worldly goal to aid in obedience to Allah, it is not riyā'. The author then applies this principle to interpret certain hadiths that describe acts of worship as causes for increased provision; these should be understood as spiritual expansion through contentment, asceticism, and reliance on Allah, which is the true self-sufficiency and expansion.
