Sanusi defines cause (sabab) and explains its logical properties.

A cause (sabab) is defined by its intrinsic logical necessity: existence implies effect, non-existence implies non-effect.

This lesson explores the foundational definition of cause (sabab) in Islamic jurisprudence, as articulated by Imam Sanusi. Understanding this definition is crucial for correctly applying legal rules, as it distinguishes a cause from other legal indicators like conditions and proofs. The passage provides a precise logical formulation and clarifies nuances about when a cause may fail to produce its effect due to external factors.

Commentary
This passage defines the cause (sabab) and explains its logical properties. The definition states that a cause is that from whose existence the existence of its effect necessarily follows, and from whose non-existence the non-existence of its effect necessarily follows, by itself (lidhatihi). The second clause ('from whose non-existence the non-existence follows') excludes mere proofs (adilla) from scripture or reason, because a proof implies the truth of what it indicates by its existence, but its absence does not necessarily imply the absence of the indicated matter. The phrase 'by itself' includes causes that may fail to produce their effect due to the absence of a condition or presence of a preventer; the cause remains a cause in itself, but the effect is not realized because of external factors. The author then gives examples: if a condition (like sanity) is absent, or a preventer (like menstruation) is present, the cause (like the sun's setting) does not produce its effect. Finally, he discusses cases where a cause's non-existence does not lead to the effect's non-existence because another cause produces the same effect, as with urine and stool both being causes for the obligation of purification. This nuanced definition distinguishes cause from other types of indicators in legal theory.

Core Idea
A condition is necessary but not sufficient for its effect.

In Islamic legal reasoning, distinguishing between a condition (shart) and a cause (sabab) is crucial. The condition is a factor whose absence guarantees the absence of the effect, but whose presence does not guarantee the effect's presence. This lesson from Sanusi's text defines the condition and illustrates it with the example of the completion of the year for zakat.

Understand the logical properties

This is the core of the definition.

Consider the completion of the lunar year for zakat. If the year does not complete, zakat is not obligatory. But if it completes, zakat may still not be obligatory if other conditions are missing or preventers exist.
The example shows the condition's logical behavior.

Core Idea
Causes are not all alike: reason, law, and custom define different types.

Al-Bannani's classification of causes is a helpful framework for understanding how Islamic theology views causality. By identifying three distinct types—rational, legal, and customary—he shows that causation can arise from intellectual necessity, divine command, or habitual observation. Commentary
This passage is a beneficial note from al-Bannani classifying causes into three categories. First, a rational cause ('aqli) is based on intellectual necessity, such as bodies causing accidents (like shape or color) or meanings causing spiritual effects; but he notes that these are mutual implications (talazum). As for according to Ahl al-Sunnah: the movement of the pen in the first example is one of two movements – conditionally inseparable – created by Allah, may He be exalted, without the mediation of an originated power in terms of bringing into existence. In the second example, the writing of the pen on paper is by the direct creation of Allah, and the condition may exist and the obstacle may be absent but the writing does not occur at all, for there is no necessary connection rationally between them.
Source pages p. 195

Commentary
This passage defines and contrasts cause (علة) and nature (طبيعة). A cause necessarily brings about its effect; the effect cannot be separated from the cause. Nature, on the other hand, produces its effect only when conditions are met and obstacles are absent; the effect may fail if a condition is missing or an obstacle exists. Two examples illustrate: (1) The movement of a pen held in the hand – according to proponents, the hand's movement is the cause of the pen's movement, and the pen must move when the hand moves. (2) The writing of the pen on paper – this is a natural product: it must occur when the pen touches the paper and there is no obstacle like dry ink or slippery paper; but if an obstacle exists, writing does not occur. Then the author presents the Ahl al-Sunnah view: In the first example, the pen's movement is simply one of two conditionally correlated movements, both directly created by Allah without any originated power as intermediary. In the second example, the writing is directly created by Allah, and even if conditions are present and obstacles absent, writing may not occur because there is no rational necessary connection.
