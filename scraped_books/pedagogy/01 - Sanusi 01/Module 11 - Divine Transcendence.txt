Habitual Rulings Overcome Rational Rulings in Believers
The author warns that habitual thinking can overshadow rationally proven shari'a rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how habitual thinking about secondary causes can lead believers to forget that God alone is the true creator and agent of all effects.

Core Idea
Habitual causes are mere occasions; only God creates the effect.

Imam Sanusi alerts us to a subtle but dangerous mental habit: even believers, despite possessing rational proofs, often slip into thinking that ordinary causes (like recitation, clothing, wind) independently produce their effects. This lesson examines that error through three clear examples from the Qur'an, and reminds us to constantly attribute all action to Allah alone.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the rhythm, then read the literal English translation. Notice the three examples and the prayer at the end.
The passage is a warning (tanbīh) – the author is alerting us to a common mistake.
2
Identify the three examples
The passage uses three verses: faith from recitation, covering from clothing, clouds from wind. For each, identify what people naturally assume and what the correct belief is.
3
Understand the concept of 'habitual cause'
A 'habitual cause' is something that God has made a regular pattern, but it has no power in itself. The real agent is Allah alone. Compare this with the shari'a rulings that are supported by rational proofs.
The key term is 'sabab 'ādī' – habitual cause.
4
Reflect on the prayer
The author ends by praising God for pardoning such thoughts and asking for guidance. Consider why the author includes this prayer and what it teaches about humility.
This shows that even thinking incorrectly about causes is a deficiency that requires God's pardon.

Refuting Dualism: Conflict Between Gods
Sanusi refutes dualism and affirms God's transcendence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Sanusi's logical refutation of dualism through the concept of mutual opposition (tamānu') and the necessity of God's absolute transcendence.

Core Idea
Two opposing gods cannot coexist; God is beyond all motives.

Sanusi concludes his critique of dualism by presenting a devastating logical argument: if two gods with conflicting wills existed, the world would be impossible. This leads to the affirmation of tawhīd and God's transcendence over any external purposes, exposing the Magian doctrine as madness.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the logical structure: first the hypothetical scenario, then the conclusion about transcendence.
2
Understand 'tamānu''
Tamānu' means mutual opposition or conflict. Sanusi argues that if two gods had opposite wills at the same time, conflict would inevitably arise.
This is the core of the refutation.
3
Connect to the real world
Since the world exists in an orderly fashion, the scenario of two conflicting gods cannot be real. This refutes dualism.
4
Grasp God's transcendence
Sanusi then states the correct belief: God is free from purposes or motives that impel Him, and no action affects His essence. This distinguishes Islamic monotheism from flawed dualist conceptions.
5
Reflect on the Magians
The passage ends by labeling the Magian belief as madness, emphasizing the clarity of tawhīd in comparison.

The Origin and Refutation of Idol Worship
Sanusi recounts the origin of idol worship and refutes the need for intermediaries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the historical origin of idol worship according to Sanusi and the rational refutation of the need for intermediaries in worship.

Core Idea
Idol worship originates from misguided attempts to reach God through intermediaries.

Sanusi begins by recounting how idol worship began as a well-intentioned but deeply misguided attempt to connect with God through representations of absent beings. He then delivers a powerful refutation, affirming God's direct control and the absolute incapacity of all creatures.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to grasp Sanusi's narrative and argument.
2
Understand the origin of idol worship
Notice how Sanusi describes the making of idols as likenesses of absent objects of worship (angels, celestial bodies, Jesus) and the intention to draw near to God through them.
The worshippers intended to reach God, but Sanusi labels this clear misguidance.
3
Grasp the refutation of intermediaries
Focus on the argument: all creatures are equal in incapacity and neediness before God. God directly creates and sustains everything, with no helpers, viziers, agents, or intermediaries. Nothing is absent from His knowledge.
No creature can benefit or harm itself or others except by God's pure bounty.
4
Reflect on the theological importance
Consider why this refutation is crucial for tawhid: it removes any basis for worshipping anything other than Allah directly, affirming His absolute transcendence and power.

He then presents the refutation: if they had thought, they would realize that all creatures, regardless of rank, are equally incapable and dependent on God alone. God directly creates and sustains everything with no intermediaries. He grants honor or its opposite as He wills. He has no helper, vizier, agent, or intermediary. Nothing escapes His knowledge and control. Refutation of Necessary Causation, Affirmation of Divine Choice
Sanusi proves that God creates the world by free will, not by necessity of cause or nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why divine creation must be by choice and not by necessity, based on Sanusi's proof against infinite regress and eternal obstacles.

Core Idea
Creation by choice alone avoids the absurdities of infinite regress and changeless obstacles.

This lesson examines Sanusi's decisive argument against the view that God creates by necessity. He shows that both causal necessity (لزوم تعليل) and natural necessity (لزوم طبع) lead to impossibilities. The only coherent alternative is that God creates by free will (اختيار).

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to hear Sanusi's rhythm. Then read the literal translation. Notice the strength of his opening claim ('This doctrine is clearly corrupt').
2
Identify the two types of necessity
Sanusi names two forms of necessary causation: one by way of cause (تعليل) in pre-eternity, and one by way of nature (طبع) as continuous necessity. Find where these appear.
The word لزوم appears for both, but with temporal qualifiers 'في الأزل' and 'فيما يزال'.
3
Understand the condition argument
If a condition for the world's existence were absent in pre-eternity, then that condition itself would need a condition, leading to an infinite regress (تسلسل). Since infinite regress is impossible, condition absence cannot explain failure.
Sanusi says 'نقل الكلام إلى ذلك الشرط فيلزم فيه التسلسل'.
4
Understand the obstacle argument
If an obstacle existed in pre-eternity, it would be eternal and therefore unchangeable. Since the world depends on the obstacle's non-existence, the world could never come to be. Yet it exists, so no obstacle existed.
This refutes natural necessity (طبع) which postulates a perpetual impediment.

Commentary
This passage provides a decisive refutation of the doctrines of necessary causation (both by way of cause and by way of nature). The author states that the doctrine in question is clearly corrupt. He then appeals to two decisive proofs: (1) the proof that God alone is necessarily eternal (قديم) and everything else is originated (حادث), and (2) the impossibility of an infinite regress of originated events. From these, it follows necessarily that God created the world by choice (اختيار), not by necessity. The author presents a double argument: (a) If the world required a condition that was absent in pre-eternity, then that condition would need another condition, leading to an infinite regress – impossible. (b) If an obstacle to the world's existence existed in pre-eternity, that obstacle would be eternal and therefore cannot cease to exist; but the world's existence depends on the removal of that obstacle, so the world could never come into existence – yet it does exist. Therefore, necessity in creation is false, and divine choice is affirmed.

Refuting the Dualists' Literalism and the Principle of Impossibility
Sanusi refutes dualists' literal interpretation and states the rule of impossibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Dualists' literal interpretation of the Light Verse is impossible and how the impossibility of the apparent meaning necessitates either tafwid or ta'wil.

Core Idea
A literal reading that implies imperfection in God is impossible and must be reinterpreted.

Commentary
This passage is a combined refutation and methodological explanation. It begins by identifying a specific instance of disbelief: the Dualists (those who believe in two gods, light and darkness) misinterpreting the Quranic verse 'Allah is the light of the heavens and the earth' (24:35). They took it literally, concluding that light is one of the two deities and its name is Allah. Sanusi immediately points out their error: they did not consider the impossibility (istiḥāla) of such an interpretation. Light is contingent—it changes, originates, and ceases to exist. The divine nature (ulūhiyya) precludes change and requires pre-eternity (qidam) and everlastingness (baqā'). Since the literal meaning leads to an impossibility (something that cannot be true of God), the verse must not be taken at face value. The author then states the rule: when the apparent meaning (ẓāhir) is impossible, one must resort either to tafwīḍ (consigning the true meaning to God) or taʾwīl (interpretation in a valid linguistic sense). Tafwīḍ is the way of the Salaf (early Muslims) regarding such ambiguous passages. Taʾwīl, specifying a meaning that is linguistically valid, is the way of Imām al-Ḥaramayn (al-Juwaynī) and many other scholars. Sanusi then provides an example of taʾwīl: the verse is a metaphor (istiʿāra) or emphatic simile (tashbīh balīgh). The representation is that non-existence (ʿadam) is likened to darkness, and the existence of all creatures is hidden within it. Their emergence from non-existence to existence depends entirely on God's creation, just as hidden things become visible when light spreads upon them. Therefore, by this consideration, God is called 'the light of the heavens and the earth' meaning He is the Manifestor (al-muẓhir) of all things—by initially creating them and then sustaining them through continuous provision. If God were to withdraw His sustaining power, everything would return to the darkness of non-existence. At the resurrection, He will once again bring forth existence by His power, will, and knowledge. Thus, the metaphorical interpretation is perfectly valid in the Arabic language. The passage concludes with a note attributed to al-Taftazānī about the metaphor being similar to 'Zayd is a lion'.

Proof that God is Self-Sufficient from Particularizer
Commentary proves God's self-sufficiency from particularizer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that God, being necessarily existent, does not need a particularizer (mukhaṣṣiṣ) because requiring one leads to circularity or infinite regress.

Core Idea
A necessary being cannot depend on an external cause; therefore God needs no particularizer.

The commentator now addresses the second aspect of God's self-sufficiency: independence from a particularizer. Since God is wājib al-wujūd (necessary of existence), He cannot be contingent; if He were, He would need a cause, leading to an impossible regress or circularity. This lesson examines that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on particularizer
Pay attention to the distinction between necessary and contingent existence.
Contingent beings require a cause; necessary beings do not.
2
Understand the impossibility of contingency for God
If God were contingent, He would need a particularizer to bring Him from non-existence to existence.
The particularizer would then need another, leading to a chain.
3
Analyze the dilemma
The chain of particularizers either ends (circularity) or goes on infinitely; both are impossible.
Also the alternative absurd consequence is mentioned.

The Fourfold Existence of the Quran and the Distinction Between Recitation and Recited
Sanusi enumerates the four existences and distinguishes created acts from eternal speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four modes of existence of the Quran and why the acts of recitation and writing are created while the recited speech is eternal.

Core Idea
The eternal Quran is indicated, not indwelt, in created forms.

This passage clarifies a common confusion about the nature of the Quran. When we say the Quran is in our hearts or on our tongues, we do not mean that the eternal speech of Allah literally dwells in created things. Sanusi explains the four modes of existence and draws a crucial distinction between the act of recitation and the recited speech itself.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof of the Ash'ari doctrine regarding the speech of Allah (kalam Allah). The author begins by affirming that the Quran is realized in four modes of existence: in external reality (a'yān), in minds (adhhān), on tongues (alsina), and in writing (binān). He clarifies that when the Salaf described Allah's speech as 'preserved in the chest, recited on the tongue, written in copies,' they meant this in a real (haqīqa) rather than metaphorical sense, but they did not imply that the eternal speech of Allah (al-qadīm) actually indwells in created beings such as chests, tongues, or paper. Rather, the speech is indicated by these means and is understood through them. The first existence (in external reality) is the true essential existence of Allah's speech, while the other three are existences of indication and understanding. From this distinction, the author draws an important conclusion: recitation (tilāwa) is not the same as the recited (matlū), reading (qirā'a) is not the same as the read (maqrū'), and writing (kitāba) is not the same as the written (maktūb). In each pair, the first is originated (ḥādith) and the second is eternal (qadīm). The passage concludes with a citation from Imam Abu al-Hajjaj al-Darir's poem, which reinforces the distinction between the temporally originated act of recitation and the eternal recited speech. The commentary in the poem also denies any likeness or transition in Allah's attributes.

First Division: Reports Necessarily True by Their Source
Sanusi defines the first division of reports: those necessarily true due to their source.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why reports from God and His Prophet are considered necessarily true according to Sanusi, and how this is established through rational and textual proofs.

Core Idea
The truth of divine and prophetic reports is not just probable but necessary.

Sanusi begins by defining the first of three divisions of reports. He explains that reports in their linguistic form can be true or false, but when we consider the source — God and His infallible Messenger — the possibility of falsehood is removed and truth becomes necessary. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the rational and textual proofs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice how Sanusi contrasts the two perspectives.
Pay attention to the technical terms like 'عقلاً' and 'نقلاً'.
2
Understand the two perspectives
Sanusi first looks at reports in their mere linguistic form, then adds the consideration of the informant's identity. Understand why the second perspective makes truth necessary.
The key is the infallibility of the source.
3
Examine the rational proofs
In the footnotes, Sanusi gives rational arguments: God cannot lie because lying would contradict His nature; the Prophet's infallibility is supported by miracles.
Rational proofs are based on impossibility of contradiction.
4
Examine the textual proofs
Sanusi cites Quranic verses affirming God's truthfulness and the Prophet's truthfulness. These are textual evidence.
Textual proofs come from revelation itself.
