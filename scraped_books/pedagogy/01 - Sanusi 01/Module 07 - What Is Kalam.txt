Commentary
This passage is an editorial introduction describing the book al-Muqaddimat by Imam al-Sanusi. It begins by noting that the book requires some familiarity with Arabic grammar (e.g., knowing the subject, object, circumstantial accusative, and basic morphology), and likewise its commentary and similar preparatory works define the terminologies of the science of kalam. The book is rightly called 'Introductions' (al-Muqaddimat) because it prepares the beginner to learn creed according to the Ash'ari method, which combines rational proof (burhān ʿaqlī) and transmitted evidence (dalīl naqlī). Just as transmitted evidences have their own terminologies, so do rational ones; books of uṣūl generally explain both, and this book focuses on the most important matters for both the beginner and the advanced student. The passage then quotes al-Bannānī stating that the commentary on al-Muqaddimat is the text itself. It explains the name 'al-Muqaddimāt': its letters are given with vowels, and it denotes a body of knowledge placed before another so that the beginner practices delving into further study. The relationship between al-Muqaddimat, its commentary, and al-Sanusi's other work al-ʿAqīda al-Ṣughrā is clarified: al-Mallālī says the Muqaddima was composed as an explanation of the ʿAqīda al-Ṣughrā, but this book remains an independent creed suitable for any student of Ahl al-Sunna, not necessarily a preface to the ʿAqīda al-Ṣughrā; rather, it is a general introduction. Finally, al-Bannānī enumerates the eight introductions (mukadimāt) as follows: (1) Introduction on rulings (aḥkām), (2) introduction on schools (madhāhib), (3) introduction on types of polytheism (anwāʿ al-shirk), (4) introduction on foundations of disbelief (uṣūl al-kufr), (5) introduction on existing things (mawjūdāt), (6) introduction on possible things (mumkināt), (7) introduction on eternal attributes (ṣifāt azaliyya), and (8) introduction on trustworthiness (amāna) regarding the messengers. These eight titles confirm the name 'al-Muqaddimāt' in manuscripts, though it may also be called 'al-Muqaddima' in the singular, considering its general content.

Definition of Shar‘ī Ruling
Sanusi defines the shar‘ī ruling and explains the genus 'address'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of a shar‘ī ruling and its key components: address, legally responsible persons, demand, permission, and setting.

Core Idea
A shar‘ī ruling is Allah's address pertaining to our actions.

Sanusi begins by defining the central term of uṣūl al-fiqh: the shar‘ī ruling. This definition establishes the framework for all legal reasoning. He uses a precise technical definition that distinguishes Allah's address from all other speech.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal ruling (al-hukm al-shar‘ī). The definition given is: 'the address of Allah the Exalted that pertains to the acts of the legally responsible persons, with demand, permission, or setting.' Sanusi then unpacks each term. The word 'address' is the genus in the definition. He explains that 'address' means speech that is intended for someone capable of understanding. There is a dispute whether calling it 'address' requires an actual addressee; this relates to whether Allah's speech is called address in eternity before there are addressees. Sanusi notes that the more correct position according to Imam Ash‘ari is that it is permissible because the attachments of speech are eternal, while al-Baqillani disagreed. Then he says that with the condition that it is the address of Allah, it excludes the addresses of others like kings and parents. The sentence is cut off but continues to explain that the definition excludes the speech of anyone other than Allah.

Four Divisions of Customary Judgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four logical divisions of customary judgment and how cause, condition, and obstacle define different types of connections.

Core Idea
Customary connections are not just cause-effect; they include condition and obstacle with specific rules.

Sanusi now deepens the discussion of customary judgment (al-hukm al-'adi) by showing how every connection between two things can be classified into one of four types based on existence or non-existence. He then links these types to three key concepts: cause, condition, and obstacle. This classification is essential for understanding how theologians analyze causal relationships in the world.

Read the passage and translation

Understand the rule for cause

The True Meaning of Kasb (Acquisition) in Sunni Theology
Sanusi defines kasb and refutes erroneous interpretations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of kasb according to Sanusi, why the concept is necessary, and how it preserves divine omnipotence while affirming human accountability.

Core Idea
Kasb means human power attaches to actions without creating them.

Sanusi tackles a delicate theological balance: how can humans be responsible for their actions if Allah creates everything? The concept of kasb (acquisition) provides the answer. In this passage, he defines kasb precisely and warns against interpretations that compromise divine omnipotence.

Some ignorant people interpret kasb as the originated power having some effect in actions. Sanusi rejects this as a confusion arising from lack of tawhid.
This error compromises Allah's exclusive creative power.

The correct definition states that human power attaches to the action without any effect (tathir). This preserves the principle that Allah alone creates.
The phrase 'without any effect' is crucial.

Sanusi adds that by saying 'originated' (al-haditha), he excludes the attachment of Allah's eternal power, so that no one confuses the two.

Commentary
This passage defines kasb, explains the need for the concept, criticizes erroneous interpretations, and restates the correct definition. Sanusi begins with the definition: kasb is the attachment (ta'alluq) of the originated power (al-qudra al-haditha) to the possible (al-maqdur) in its locus (the body part) without any effect (ta'thir). He then explains that it is necessary because it is established by reason and revelation that Allah alone creates all things, yet the servant is said to acquire actions, and is rewarded and punished for them. This requires a precise meaning of kasb that does not conflict with divine omnipotence. Some ignorant people interpret kasb as the power having some effect, which is wrong. The correct interpretation, verified by rational principles, sunna, and consensus, is the definition given. He adds a precaution: by saying 'originated' (al-haditha), he excludes the attachment of the eternal power, so that it is not said that the eternal power attaches in the same way. The passage ends mid-sentence, but the meaning is clear.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of shirk al-aghrāḍ (shirk of purposes), which is essentially riyā' (showing off) or minor shirk. Sanusi first defines it: performing commanded acts (obligatory and recommended) or avoiding forbidden and disliked acts for motives other than compliance with Allah's command. These motives include seeking praise, love, leadership, wealth, or avoiding blame from people. This also includes performing acts solely to attain Paradise or avoid Hellfire. He then provides footnotes: one refers to a previous page, another states that this is called riyā' and minor shirk, and a third quotes al-Nasrabādhi's saying that Paradise is sustained by Allah's sustenance (qā'im bi-iqbā'ihi) while Allah's remembrance and mercy are by His own subsistence (baqā'ihi), highlighting the distinction between contingent and eternal. Next, Sanusi explains the underlying cause: forgetting tawḥīd. The person imagines that benefit or harm can come from other than Allah, so he considers creatures in his obedience, and even imagines his obedience has independent causal effect. If he truly understood that Allah alone creates all things without any intermediary or effect from anything else, including his own obedience, then his intention would be pure compliance with Allah's command, and he would hope for reward purely by Allah's grace, not because it is obligatory or deserved. Finally, Sanusi clarifies that the 'action' in this discussion refers to actions required by the Law, because that is where riyā' is unlawful. The footnote then provides two stories from al-Qushayri: one illustrating that creation are mere forms subject to divine decree, and another where Ibrāhīm shows that even intercession is insignificant because all creation is just a piece of clay, thus emphasizing the need for pure tawḥīd.

Sanusi explains the cause of shirk al-aghrāḍ and cites sayings of the arifs.

Then he hopes with it for what the Master, Majestic and Exalted, promised of good in connection with it, by pure grace without obligation or entitlement. So what is meant by 'action' in our speech is the action required by the Law; for it is in that that riya' is unlawful. (Footnote 1: Al-Qushayri narrated in his 'Risala' p.90: that Abu Uthman al-Maghribi was asked about the creation, and he said: 'Molds and forms upon whom the decrees of divine power flow.' And he also narrated p.744: that the knower of Allah Abu Yazid al-Bistami said to his companions one day: 'Let us go and welcome a saint from the saints of Allah.' Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of shirk al-aghrāḍ (shirk of purposes), which is essentially riyā' (showing off) or minor shirk. Sanusi first defines it: performing commanded acts (obligatory and recommended) or avoiding forbidden and disliked acts for motives other than compliance with Allah's command. These motives include seeking praise, love, leadership, wealth, or avoiding blame from people. This also includes performing acts solely to attain Paradise or avoid Hellfire. He then provides footnotes: one refers to a previous page, another states that this is called riyā' and minor shirk, and a third quotes al-Nasrabādhi's saying that Paradise is sustained by Allah's sustenance (qā'im bi-iqbā'ihi) while Allah's remembrance and mercy are by His own subsistence (baqā'ihi), highlighting the distinction between contingent and eternal. Next, Sanusi explains the underlying cause: forgetting tawḥīd. The person imagines that benefit or harm can come from other than Allah, so he considers creatures in his obedience, and even imagines his obedience has independent causal effect. If he truly understood that Allah alone creates all things without any intermediary or effect from anything else, including his own obedience, then his intention would be pure compliance with Allah's command, and he would hope for reward purely by Allah's grace, not because it is obligatory or deserved. Finally, Sanusi clarifies that the 'action' in this discussion refers to actions required by the Law, because that is where riyā' is unlawful. The footnote then provides two stories from al-Qushayri: one illustrating that creation are mere forms subject to divine decree, and another where Ibrāhīm shows that even intercession is insignificant because all creation is just a piece of clay, thus emphasizing the need for pure tawḥīd.

The Three Divisions of Khabar and the Necessity of 'Li Dhatihi'
Sanusi classifies khabar into three divisions based on possibility of truth/falsity and explains the need for 'li dhatihi'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi classifies khabar into three types based on their truth/falsehood potential and why the qualifier 'li dhatihi' is essential to include all divisions and exclude insha'.

Core Idea
A report may be possible, necessarily true, or necessarily false depending on evidence, so its definition needs 'in itself'.

Sanusi begins by examining the nature of reports (khabar) and their truth-value. He demonstrates that not all reports are equally truth-apt; some become certain only when external evidence is considered. This leads to a careful refinement of the definition of khabar, showing why the phrase 'li dhatihi' is necessary for both comprehensiveness (jami') and exclusiveness (mani').

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation to grasp the overall flow of Sanusi's argument. Notice how he uses examples.
Pay attention to the footnote numbers; they are explained at the end.
2
Identify the three divisions
The passage presents three divisions of khabar: (1) that which is inherently possible for truth and falsehood, (2) that which becomes necessarily true with proof, (3) that which becomes necessarily false with proof. Find each one and note their examples.
Sanusi also adds a fourth example (report contradicting necessary knowledge) which is a subtype of the third division.
3
Understand the role of external factors
For each division, notice how truth or falsehood is not determined by the report's form alone but by something additional (zā'id): necessary meaning, decisive proof, or universal proofs. This is key to the definition.
This shows why the definition must allow for external factors.
4
Analyze the necessity of 'li dhatihi'
Sanusi concludes that without 'li dhatihi', the definition would only cover the first division, leaving out the other two. Also, it excludes insha' (non-reportive speech) which has truth-value only indirectly. See how this preserves both comprehensiveness and exclusiveness.
The footnotes clarify the terms 'invalid in converse' (fasad al-'aks) and 'corrupted in comprehensiveness' (fasad al-tard).

Commentary
This passage classifies khabar (proposition/report) into three divisions based on its potential for truth (sidq) and falsehood (kidhb). The author uses examples to illustrate each division and then explains why the phrase 'li dhatihi' (in itself) is necessary in the definition of khabar.

First, he gives an example of a necessary truth: 'Two is more than one.' In its verbal form, this report could be true or false if one ignores its obvious meaning. But when one looks at its necessarily known meaning, truth becomes certain. This shows that some reports are not inherently truth-apt but become certain truths when additional factors (like necessary knowledge) are considered.

Second, he gives reports of essential doctrines (e.g., 'The world is originated') which are possible in themselves but become necessarily true when proven by decisive proofs (burhan qat'i).

Third, he gives false doctrines of the Mu'tazilites (e.g., that God's will does not apply to evil) which in themselves could be true or false linguistically but become necessarily false when considered against universal proofs of God's will and power.

Then he adds a fourth example: reporting what contradicts necessary knowledge (e.g., 'Four is less than three') which becomes necessarily false.

He concludes that the phrase 'li dhatihi' in the definition of khabar is essential to include not only the first division (which is inherently possible) but also the second and third, which only become determined true or false when external factors are considered. Without this phrase, the definition would be invalid in its converse (i.e., not including all its species). It also excludes insha' (non-reportive speech), which has truth-value only indirectly through its implications. Thus the qualifier 'li dhatihi' preserves both the comprehensiveness (jami') and exclusiveness (mani') of the definition. The footnotes clarify the technical meaning of 'invalid in converse' and 'corrupted in comprehensiveness'.

Definition of Insha': Speech Without Truth-Value
Sanusi defines insha' as speech that does not admit truth or falsity in itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of insha' as speech that does not admit truth or falsehood in itself and how it contrasts with khabar.

Core Idea
Insha' is speech that is neither true nor false by its own nature.

Sanusi has already defined khabar as speech that admits truth or falsehood in itself. Now he turns to its counterpart, insha', which includes commands, prohibitions, and other constructions. This lesson establishes the precise definition of insha' and the crucial qualifier 'li dhatihi' that distinguishes it from khabar.

Commentary
This passage defines insha' (constructive speech, such as commands and prohibitions) as the opposite of khabar. The definition is: 'that which does not admit truth nor falsehood in itself'. The commentator explains that this means insha' is speech that, when considering its own linguistic form and composition, is not assessed as true or false. For example, the command 'Do this!' is neither true nor false in its own right; its truth-value only arises from the implications of the command (e.g., whether the command actually exists or not). This definition uses the phrase 'li dhatihi' (in itself) to make clear that insha' is not truth-apt by its very nature, unlike khabar which is defined as speech that does admit truth and falsehood 'in itself'. The previous lesson showed that the qualifier 'li dhatihi' in the definition of khabar excludes insha', and now we see that insha' is defined precisely by the absence of that property.

Why Add 'By Essence' to the Definition of Insha'?
Sanusi explains why the insha' definition needs 'by essence'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definition of insha' must be qualified with 'by essence' (bi al-dhāt) to achieve proper coextensiveness and inverse-coextensiveness.

Core Idea
The definition needs 'by essence' to avoid including khabar and excluding real insha'.

Sanusi has defined insha' as that which does not admit truth or falsehood. But without qualification, this definition fails in two ways: it allows some khabar to slip in, and it excludes some insha' that carry informative implications. In this passage, he shows exactly why the qualifier 'by essence' is needed to fix both defects.

Understand why a simple definition (without 'by essence') would incorrectly include some types of khabar. This is called fāsid al-ṭard, meaning the definition is not exclusive enough.
3
See how 'by essence' excludes those khabar

Consider the example of a hypocritical command. Conventionally it implies truth/falsehood, so it would be excluded from insha' without 'by essence'. That is fāsid al-ʿaks: the definition is too narrow.

Understand how the qualifier fixes both
The addition of 'by essence' ensures that only insha' in their essential nature are included, and only khabar in their essential nature are excluded. This achieves proper ṭard and ʿaks.

Commentary
This passage refines the definition of insha' (creation/command) by adding the qualifier 'by essence' (bi al-dhāt). The author begins by noting that if we define insha' simply as 'that which does not admit truth or falsehood', two types of khabar (news) — those that admit truth/falsehood in their essence — would wrongly be included, making the definition 'fāsid al-ṭard' (defective in coextensiveness: it allows entry of what is not part of the defined). By adding 'by essence', those two types are excluded because they do admit truth/falsehood in their essence, so they remain khabar. Next, the author shows how this same qualifier also prevents the definition from being 'fāsid al-ʿaks' (defective in inverse-coextensiveness: it excludes some instances that should be included). He gives the example of a command issued hypocritically: conventionally, such a command implies an informative content (e.g., 'I want you to eat'), which can be judged true or false. Without the 'by essence' qualifier, such commands would be excluded from insha', but they should be included because, in their essential imperative reality, they do not admit truth/falsehood. Thus, adding 'by essence' makes the definition both coextensive (ṭard) and inverse-coextensive (ʿaks) — it includes all instances of insha' and excludes all non-instances. The footnote explains that 'fāsid al-ṭard' means the definition fails to exclude what is not part of the defined.

Commentary
This passage, appearing as a footnote, clarifies the technical definitions of several epistemological terms within the framework of Ahl al-Sunna. The author distinguishes between three levels: verbal existence (wujud lafzi), mental existence (wujud dhihni), and external existence (wujud khariji).
