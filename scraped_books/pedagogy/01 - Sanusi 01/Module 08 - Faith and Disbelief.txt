And the ruling of the fifth is: detailed; for whoever says concerning customary causes that they affect by their nature, consensus has been reported on his disbelief; and whoever says that they affect by a power that Allah Almighty has deposited in them, then he is a sinful innovator (fasiq mubtadi'), and concerning his disbelief there are two sayings.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and classification passage. Sanusi introduces the Third Introduction on the types of shirk (associating partners with God). He lists six types. The first is 'shirk al-istiqlal' (independence shirk): positing two independent gods, exemplified by the Magians (Zoroastrians). The second is 'shirk al-tab'id' (fractionalization shirk): composing the deity from multiple gods, exemplified by the Christians (Trinity). The third is 'shirk al-taqrib' (approximation shirk): worshipping other beings to draw near to God, as done by early pre-Islamic Arabs. The fourth is 'shirk al-taqlid' (imitation shirk): worshipping other deities out of blind following of others, as done by later pre-Islamic Arabs. The fifth is 'shirk al-asbab' (causes shirk): attributing causal influence to natural causes, exemplified by philosophers and naturalists. The sixth is 'shirk al-aghrad' (purposes shirk): performing actions for other than God (i.e., showing off or worldly motives). Sanusi then explains the rulings for these categories. The first four types are unanimously considered kufr (disbelief). The sixth type is unanimously considered a sin (ma'siya) but not kufr. The fifth type (causes shirk) has a detailed ruling: if one says that customary causes produce effects by their own inherent nature, that is unanimously kufr. If one says they affect by a power God has deposited in them, then the person is a sinful innovator (fasiq mubtadi'), and there are two opinions on whether that constitutes disbelief.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the Fourth Introduction, where Sanusi enumerates the seven roots (uṣūl) of disbelief (kufr) and innovations (bidaʿ). These are not actions but underlying intellectual and methodological errors that lead people astray.

1. Essential necessitation (الإيجاب الذاتي): This is the belief that things occur from Allah necessarily, by way of causal necessity or nature, without divine choice. This denies the free will of Allah and implies determinism, which is a root of disbelief.

2. Rationalistic approval of good (التحسين العقلي): This is the Mu'tazilite concept that human reason can independently determine moral good and evil, and that Allah's actions are bound by purposes like acquiring benefits and repelling harms. This imposes rational constraints on Allah and is an innovation.

3. Vicious imitation (التقليد الرديء): Following others blindly out of zeal or bigotry without seeking the truth. This leads to persistence in error.

4. Habitual conjunction (الربط العادي): Affirming a necessary connection between cause and effect based on repeated observation, without recognizing that Allah is the true agent. This is similar to the error of natural causality.

5. Compound ignorance (الجهل المركب): Being ignorant of the truth and simultaneously ignorant of one's own ignorance. This prevents learning and correction.

6. Adherence to mere outward meanings (التّمسّك بمجرّد ظواهر الكتاب والسنة): Taking scriptural texts literally without distinguishing between what is possible and impossible for Allah. This leads to anthropomorphism and other errors.

7. Ignorance of rational principles (الجهل بالقواعد العقلية): Lack of knowledge of the three rational judgments (necessity, contingency, impossibility) and of the Arabic language (including grammar and rhetoric). This undermines proper theological reasoning.

The passage emphasizes that sound theology requires both correct rational principles and proper understanding of revelation. The seven roots represent common pitfalls that the Ash'ari tradition seeks to avoid.

Bad Taqlid and Its Two Outcomes
Sanusi classifies bad taqlid into two types: leading to clear kufr or to bid'ah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two distinct outcomes of bad taqlid—clear kufr and bid'ah with disagreement—and how Sanusi classifies them.

Bad taqlid leads either to undisputed kufr or disputed bid'ah.

After discussing the first two causes of deviation, Sanusi turns to the third: bad taqlid. This is the practice of blindly following others in falsehood. He shows that it produces two different types of error—one unequivocally disbelief, the other a matter of dispute among scholars. Let's read his classification.
