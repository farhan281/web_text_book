Refuting Attacks on the Ash'ari Creed
Defense of Ash'ari creed against charges of innovation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Ash'ari school is not an innovation but a faithful articulation of the Salafi creed, and how it rejects anthropomorphism.

Core Idea
The Ash'ari school did not innovate—it systematized the creed of the Salaf.

In an era of criticism, some groups claim that the Ash'ari creed deviates from the path of the Salaf. This passage from Imam Dardir's commentary defends the Ash'ari school, clarifying that it faithfully represents the beliefs of the Companions and early Muslims, and that its opponents promote anthropomorphism.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on modern attacks
Read the Arabic and translation of the first part of the passage to understand the accusations against the Ash'aris.
Notice the strong language: 'amazing wonders' and 'biased calls'.
2
Identify the defense of al-Ash'ari
The author asserts that Imam al-Ash'ari did not invent a new madhhab but rather systematized the creed of the Salaf, as al-Subki confirmed.
3
Reflect on the takfir issue
Consider the gravity of declaring disbelief over creedal differences, as the passage mentions.
This shows the seriousness of the debate.

And among the amazing wonders is that in our time there have appeared biased calls, adopted by sects, groups, and movements, claiming that the beliefs of the noble Ashʿarīs are innovations outside the methodology of the Salaf, and influenced by the statements of the philosophers and Muʿtazila. And these sects and movements were not content with that only, but they claimed that the principles of the Mujassimah (anthropomorphists) and Mushabbihah (assimilationists) are the correct beliefs that people must follow, calling for adherence to them and founding belief upon them. Moreover, they rushed to declare disbelief (takfīr) upon whoever opposes the creed of the Mujassimah or refutes their doubts and responds to their callers. And the clear truth is that Imam Abū al-Ḥasan al-Ashʿarī did not innovate a new madhhab in belief, but rather he is a consolidator of the madhhab of the Salaf, a defender of what the Companions of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) were upon. Thus, affiliation to him is only because he tied the way of the Salaf in a systematic framework, adhered to it, and established proofs and evidences for it, as al-ʿAllāmah al-Subkī stated. And when the beliefs of the noble Ashʿarīs are presented, it appears clearly to us that they are based on tanzīh (transcendence), and that it is the foundation upon which their madhhab in tawḥīd and the principles of Islamic creed rests, and that they present clear proofs from reason and transmission for the truth of their belief and its establishment in minds and hearts. And tanzīh is manifested in the Ashʿarī creed in the following: - Transcendence of the Creator, may His glory be exalted, from similarity to creation in an absolute manner, for Allah, glorified be He, is unlike everything that is imagined in minds, and every apparent meaning that suggests anthropomorphism must, in their view, be turned away from its literal sense to a meaning that negates anthropomorphism and befits Him Who is like unto Him nothing. - Transcendence of the Creator, may His glory be exalted, from being a body or a state in a body. - Transcendence of the Creator, may His glory be exalted, from being bounded by a limit, or confined to a direction, or being a state in a place. (Reference: Ṭabaqāt al-Shāfiʿiyyah 3/367)

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of contemporary attacks on the Ashʿarī school and a statement of its core principles. The author first expresses astonishment at modern movements that accuse Ashʿarīs of innovation and being influenced by philosophers and Muʿtazila, while instead promoting anthropomorphism (tajsīm and tashbīh) as correct belief and even declaring takfīr against those who oppose it. The author then defends al-Ashʿarī, clarifying that he did not invent a new creed but systematically articulated the creed of the Salaf (the early Muslims) and the Companions, as affirmed by al-Subkī. Following this, the passage explains that Ashʿarī creed is fundamentally built on tanzīh (transcendence). Three explicit principles of tanzīh are enumerated: (1) absolute negation of similarity between God and creation — any scriptural expression that seems to imply anthropomorphism must be interpreted metaphorically in a way befitting God, because nothing is like Him; (2) God is not a body nor does He inhere in a body; (3) God is not limited by boundaries or directions, nor does He dwell in a place. These principles guard against any form of corporealism or spatialization of God. The footnote cites al-Subkī's Ṭabaqāt as an authoritative reference.

Principles of Divine Transcendence in Ash'ari Creed
Enumeration of three principles of divine transcendence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three fundamental principles of tanzih (divine transcendence) that form the basis of Ash'ari theology.

Core Idea
God is absolutely unlike creation—no body, no place, no limit.

The Ash'ari creed is built on the principle of tanzih, or divine transcendence. In this passage, the author enumerates three clear principles that safeguard God from any resemblance to creation, ensuring that all scriptural expressions are understood in a way befitting His majesty.

Guided Path
1
Read the three principles
Read the Arabic and translation that enumerate the three principles of tanzih.
The principles are introduced with a colon, so look for the bulleted list.
2
Understand the first principle
The first principle states absolute negation of similarity: God is unlike anything imaginable, and any apparent anthropomorphism must be reinterpreted.
This is the basis for ta'wil (interpretation) of ambiguous verses.
3
Understand the second and third principles
The second principle denies that God is a body or dwells in a body; the third denies that He is bounded by limits, directions, or places.
These principles directly counter anthropomorphic and corporealist views.

The Ash'ari school is built upon the principle of tanzih — affirming God's transcendence above all creation. The Beatific Vision of Allah in Paradise
Commands certainty on the beatific vision with rational and revealed proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari doctrine of the beatific vision (ru'yat Allāh) in Paradise and its twofold basis in reason and revelation.

Core Idea
Seeing Allah in Paradise is rationally possible and divinely revealed.

This lesson examines a key passage from al-Kharīda al-Bahiyya on the beatific vision. Imam al-Dardīr commands certainty that believers will see Allah in the eternal Garden, grounding this belief in both rational possibility and scriptural proof. Let's unpack the theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse and translation
Read the Arabic verse once without analysis. Then read the literal translation to grasp the author's direct command and its justification.
Notice the imperative 'ijzam' (be certain) – this signals a non-negotiable belief.
2
Identify the two justifications
The passage gives two reasons for the beatific vision: rational possibility (jā'iz bil-'aql) and transmitted proof (dalīl al-naql). Mark these in the text.
Rational possibility means it is not impossible, not that reason alone proves it occurs.
3
Reflect on why certainty is required
Ask yourself: Why does the author command certainty? How does this belief relate to divine transcendence and the afterlife?
The Ash'ari position balances God's transcendence with His power to grant vision.

Source Text

واجزم أخي برؤية الإله * في جنَّةِ الخُلْدِ بِلا تَناهي * إذ الوقوع جائز بالعَقْلِ * وقد أتى فيه دليلُ النَّقْلِ
And be certain, my brother, of seeing God * in the garden of eternity without end. * For its occurrence is possible by reason * and the transmitted proof has come concerning it.
Source pages p. 18

Commentary
This passage asserts the beatific vision (ru'yat Allāh) in Paradise. It commands the reader to be certain (ijzam) that believers will see God in the eternal garden (jannat al-khuld), without end. It then provides the theological justification: the occurrence (wuqūʿ) is possible (jāʾiz) according to reason (al-ʿaql), meaning it is not rationally impossible. Additionally, a transmitted proof (dalīl al-naql)—i.e., from revelation—has come to confirm it. This is the standard Ash'ari position affirming vision in the afterlife, based on Quranic verses like 'Wujūhun yawmaʾidhin nāḍiratun ilā Rabbihā nāẓirah' (75:22-23).

Commentary
This passage explains why God's occurrence (ḥudūth) is impossible.
