Learning Objective

Core Idea
The basmalah's grammar safeguards divine transcendence by preferring accompaniment over instrumental use.

The author begins by analyzing the opening phrase of his work, 'In the Name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful.' This passage reveals how careful grammatical choices reflect deeper theological commitments. Commentary
This passage is a detailed grammatical analysis of the basmalah as it appears at the beginning of the work. The author explains that the prepositional phrase 'bismillah' has a deleted verb that is the real action. He argues that the verb must be estimated (1) as a verb (not a noun) because verbs are the original in action, (2) as delayed (after the basmalah) because advancing the object indicates exclusivity, and (3) as specific to the work being begun (this commentary) so that the blessing of the basmalah encompasses all parts. This shows the author's concern both with grammatical precision and with theological propriety.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of the term al-hamd (praise). The author, Imam Dardeer, begins by giving the linguistic definition: hamd is praising a beautiful action that is voluntary and done with glorification, whether the action is a virtue (benefiting oneself) or a benefaction (benefiting others). The final footnotes detail the concept of partial overlap with an example of an iron ring, and explain that the source (mawrid) is the starting point and the scope (muta´allaq) is the endpoint, showing how the linguistic and legal definitions differ in source and scope.

The author states that there is no incompatibility between two different things that are not opposites (like whiteness and motion), and also between two likes (like two whitenesses). However, the verifiers (muḥaqqiqūn) assert that two likes are incompatible in the same substrate. They argue using a reductio: if a substrate could accept two likes, it could also accept two opposites. Because the substrate must be either occupied by the thing, its opposite, or its like. If two likes could coexist, one might be absent, letting its opposite enter, resulting in simultaneous opposites—an impossibility. Thus, two likes cannot coexist, supporting the view that only one attribute of a kind can inhere in a subject.
