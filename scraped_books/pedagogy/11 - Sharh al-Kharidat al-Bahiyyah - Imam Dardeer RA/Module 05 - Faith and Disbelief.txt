The creed of Ahl al-Sunna is moderate, forbids takfir of Muslims who sin, and its followers are known by unity behind the imam or the majority.

Covering Sins: Erasure or Non-Accountability?
Dardir explains the two meanings of sincere covering of sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two interpretations of sincere covering of sins as explained by Imam al-Dardir.

Core Idea
Sincere covering of sins means either erasing them from the record or not being held accountable for them.

Imam al-Dardir begins his commentary by explaining what it truly means for Allah to 'cover' sins. He presents two possibilities supported by the Sunnah: either the sins are erased from the records, or they remain but are not taken into account. The first is more hopeful from Allah's vast generosity.

The Ruling on Taqlīd in Matters of Faith
Dardeer defines taqlīd and enumerates five opinions on its sufficiency for faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of taqlīd, the differing scholarly opinions on whether it suffices for faith, and the correct position that while a muqallid's faith is valid, he is sinful for not engaging in rational inquiry (naẓar).

Core Idea
Taqlīd may save one's faith but not excuse neglecting inquiry.

This passage from Imam Dardeer's commentary addresses a critical question in Islamic theology: can a person be considered a believer if they simply follow the beliefs of others without evidence? Dardeer defines taqlīd, presents five major opinions on the issue, and clarifies the correct stance. As you read, pay attention to how the different opinions are weighed and why inquiry remains essential even for a valid faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of taqlīd
Identify how Dardeer defines taqlīd: accepting another's statement without proof. Note it describes a firm belief based on another's statement.
This is the baseline for the entire discussion.
2

List the five views: 1) taqlīd suffices (correct), 2) does not suffice (disbelief), 3) suffices only if following Qur'an and definitive Sunnah, 4) inquiry is obligatory, 5) inquiry is prohibited (refuted).
Notice how each opinion affects the status of the muqallid.
3
Understand the correct position
The correct view is that taqlīd suffices for valid faith, but the person is sinful for neglecting inquiry. Distinguish between validity of faith and obligation of inquiry.
This reconciles the two concerns.
4
Reflect on the refuted opinion
One opinion forbids inquiry entirely. Dardeer dismisses it as baseless. Why? Because it contradicts the obligation to seek knowledge and understand.

The Divisions of Rational Judgment: As for taqlīd, which is taking the statement of another without proof, i.e., a firm belief based solely on another's statement, there is disagreement about it. Commentary
This passage addresses the controversial issue of taqlid in matters of faith. It begins by defining taqlid as accepting another's statement without evidence. Then it presents five opinions: (1) Taqlid suffices for faith and the muqallid's faith is valid, but there is a further question: is inquiry (naẓar) obligatory upon him? The author indicates that the correct view is that inquiry is obligatory, so the muqallid is sinful for neglecting it while still being considered a believer. (2) Another opinion says inquiry is a condition of perfection, not obligation. (3) Another holds that taqlid does not suffice and the muqallid is a disbeliever. (4) Another conditions that it suffices only if one follows the Qur'an and definitive Sunnah, but the author finds this questionable. (5) A fifth opinion forbids inquiry altogether, considering it a cause of doubt, but the author dismisses this as baseless. Thus the preferred position is that taqlid is valid in the sense that the person is a believer, but he is sinful for not engaging in rational inquiry.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of faith (iman). It begins by stating that the tasdiq (affirmation) of previously mentioned matters is the legal iman. Then it distinguishes between the linguistic meaning of iman (absolute tasdiq) and the legal meaning (affirmation of the Prophet with the heart regarding all that is necessarily known to be from his religion). The phrase 'necessarily known' is explained: it refers to matters so widely transmitted that common folk know them without need for evidence, even if originally they required inference, e.g., God’s oneness and prayer’s obligation. The passage then clarifies that the tasdiq meant is not mere mental assent but idh‘an (submission) and qabul (acceptance), such that the term taslim (surrender) applies, and that without this, even those who knew the truth like disbelievers would be considered believers. Next, it summarizes that legal faith is hadith al-nafs (inner disposition) following knowledge, and confirms the validity of the muqallid’s (blind follower’s) iman. Finally, it equates idh‘an, qabul, tasdiq, and taslim as one thing—the inner disposition—concluding that faith is an act of the soul, not from the category of sciences and cognitions. The footnotes elaborate on the meaning of darura (necessity), the nature of idh‘an, and the scriptural basis for hadith al-nafs.

The Nature of Iman: Act or Knowledge?
Dardeer presents al-Taftazani's view that iman is perception, a quality, not an act or passion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the debate on whether iman is an act of the soul or a type of knowledge, and how al-Taftazani argued it is a quality obligated through its causes.

Core Idea
Iman is either an act of the soul or a type of knowledge; al-Taftazani says it is a quality obligated through reflection.

This lesson introduces a central debate in Islamic theology: is faith (iman) an act of the soul, or is it a form of inner knowledge? After presenting both sides, the passage focuses on the view of al-Taftazani, who defined iman as perception (idrak), which is a quality (kayf) that is not directly under our control yet is obligated through its causes such as reflection and utterance. Understanding this distinction is key to resolving later objections about the judgment of disbelief.

Commentary

Thus, outward acts can determine the ruling of kufr even if inner faith exists. The passage concludes that understanding this resolves many objections about faith. Tasdiq in Logic: Conception vs. Affirmation
Al-Taftazani explains the logical meaning of tasdiq as submission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the logical term tasdiq (affirmation) relates to the theological concept of iman, and why it is defined as submission (idh‘an).

Core Idea
In logic, tasdiq is submission and surrender, which is the same meaning as iman in theology.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The verbal dispute masks a deeper disagreement about whether faith is simple or composite.

Some scholars argue that the debate over whether faith increases or decreases is merely verbal: they reconcile contradicting texts by distinguishing the essence of faith from its perfection. Commentary
This passage is a detailed theological argument on the relationship between Islam and Iman. It begins by asserting that while the two are conceptually distinct (مختلفان), they are necessarily conjoined in Islamic law (تلازما شرعا) such that one never finds a Muslim who is not a believer (مؤمن) nor a believer who is not a Muslim. The author explains that inner conviction (إذعان) necessarily entails outward compliance (امتثال), and vice versa, and calls for careful reflection. The first objection raises the case of the hypocrite (منافق) who outwardly appears Muslim but inwardly disbelieves, citing the Quranic verse where Bedouins claim faith but are told they have only submitted. The answer distinguishes between mere outward Islam (الانقياد الظاهري) and the legally valid Islam (المعتبَر شرعا) that saves from eternal fire; the hypocrite's Islam is only outward and thus not the same as Iman. The second objection cites a hadith where the Prophet defines Islam through specific actions like prayer and fasting, seemingly equating Islam with those works. The answer, attributed to At-Taftazani, explains that the Prophet was listing signs (علامات) of Islam, not its essence, just as he defined Iman through outward actions for a delegation. This shows that Iman is inner affirmation (تصديق) and submission (إذعان), not the actions themselves. The passage then presents a reconciliation between the Maturidi and Ash'ari positions: if 'Islam' is understood as mere outward compliance without inner conviction, it differs from Iman; if understood as inward surrender and acceptance, it is identical with Iman. Finally, a clarifying note points out that the phrase 'without regard to inner conviction' refers to the concept (مفهوم) of outward Islam, not to the practical requirement of being built upon inner conviction, which is necessary for the legal connection.

The Three Levels of Certainty (Yaqin)
The author explains the meaning of 'sirr al-abha' and describes the three levels of certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three levels of certainty in Islamic epistemology and how they relate to direct experiential knowledge of Allah.

Core Idea
Certainty has three ascending levels: proof, sight, and direct experience.

This passage from Imam Dardir's commentary explains a profound supplication: asking Allah for His 'most beautiful secret' (sirr al-abha). He unpacks this as the divine light that grants discernment, and then distinguishes three levels of certainty—ranging from intellectual inference to direct experiential knowledge. Understanding these levels helps us appreciate the goal of spiritual knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Understand the supplication
The author begins by explaining the plea 'do not deprive me of Your most beautiful secret.' Learn its linguistic meaning and the concept of divine light (nur ilahi).
The word 'sirr' here refers to a divine light, not a hidden fact.
2
Learn the three levels of certainty
The author defines 'ilm al-yaqin, haqq al-yaqin, and 'ayn al-yaqin with an analogy of fire. Each level is a distinct mode of knowing.
The levels are hierarchical: each is more luminous than the previous.
3
Reflect on the analogy
Notice how each level differs: proof (smoke), distant observation (seeing fire), and direct mixing (being in the fire). This illustrates the superiority of direct experience.
The analogy is meant to show that knowledge through direct contact is the most certain.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. It continues the commentary on the poet's line 'do not deprive me of Your most beautiful secret'. The author first gives the linguistic options for 'tahrimni' (from harama or ahrama) meaning to prevent. Then defines 'sirr' (secret) as the divine light (nur ilahi) that gives furqan (discernment) between truth and falsehood, citing Quran 8:29. Next, he explains 'al-abha' as the most luminous light. He then classifies three levels of certainty (yaqin): (1) 'Ilm al-yaqin: knowledge obtained through rational proof, e.g., inferring fire from smoke. This is light. (2) Haqq al-yaqin: knowledge through direct witnessing but without mixing, e.g., seeing fire from afar. This is more luminous. (3) 'Ayn al-yaqin: knowledge through direct contact and mixing, e.g., being in the fire and experiencing it. This is the most luminous. The author concludes with an analogy comparing these three levels: the person who infers fire from smoke is not like the one who sees it from afar, and the one who sees it is not like the one who mixes with it and knows its essence. The point is that direct experiential knowledge is superior to intellectual or visual knowledge.
