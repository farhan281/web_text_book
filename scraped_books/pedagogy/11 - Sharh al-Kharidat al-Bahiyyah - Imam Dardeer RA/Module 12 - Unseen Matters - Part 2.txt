The Nature of Tasdiq and the Overriding Power of Outward Signs of Disbelief
The author defines tasdiq as cognition and discusses the effect of outward signs of disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that shar'i tasdiq (assent) is a cognition (idrak) that constitutes iman, and that outward acts of disbelief can override inner faith for worldly rulings.

Core Idea
Inner faith is not enough; outward signs of disbelief override it.

The author now turns to a critical distinction: while faith is essentially inner assent (tasdiq), outward actions can legally override that inner state. This principle resolves many apparent contradictions, such as when someone internally believes but commits acts of open disbelief. The passage explains the nature of tasdiq as a cognition and sets the rule for applying the label of disbelief.

Commentary
This passage begins by marking the third section of the book, which deals with sam'iyyat (things known through revelation) and cognitions. The author then presents a view that is considered preponderant according to some. He then states the position of al-Taftazani and many other verifying scholars that the shar'i tasdiq, which is called iman, idh'an, and taslim, is identical to idrak (cognition). This means it falls under the category of knowledge (sciences and cognitions), not action. The author clarifies that the most correct view regarding idrak is that it is a quality (kayf), meaning it is a state of the soul, not an act or passive affection. Obligation to have this tasdiq is by way of its causes, namely reflection and reasoning that lead to it. Then he explains that this is the same tasdiq that logicians contrast with tasawwur: knowledge is either conception or assent. Assent (tasdiq) among logicians is the same as idh'an, and is also called taslim. The key point is that if disbelievers possessed this inner assent, they would still be called disbelievers because of external signs of denial and rejection that they exhibit. The author gives an example: if someone internally believes in everything the Prophet brought and even acts on it, yet deliberately ties the zunnar (a distinctive belt of non-Muslims) or prostrates to an idol, we judge him a disbeliever because the Prophet made those actions signs of disbelief. The author concludes that understanding this principle helps solve many problems related to the issue of iman, such as cases where outward actions contradict inner belief.

Faith Increases and Decreases with Deeds
Imam Dardir presents three proofs that iman increases and decreases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the preponderant view that faith increases and decreases with deeds, supported by rational, Quranic, and prophetic proofs.

Core Idea
Faith changes with deeds – it grows by obedience and diminishes by sin.

In this section on sam'iyyat (matters known through revelation), Imam Dardir presents the established view that faith is not static. He argues that the faith of the righteous cannot be equal to that of sinners, and supports this with clear evidence from the Quran and Sunnah. This lesson will walk through his reasoning and proofs.

The rational proof is based on the clear inequality between the faith of the wicked and the righteous.
3

Read the summary statement about inward and outward deeds increasing the radiance of faith in the heart.
The author uses the metaphor of illumination (شراقه وضيائه).

This shows that even the core of faith (tasdiq) can be stronger or weaker.

Iman and Islam are two sides of the same coin, yet not identical.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the phrase 'al-muzīl li-l-ʿamā' (the remover of blindness). The author states that it refers to ignorance, and that the supplication (duʿā) mentioned in the poem is beneficial. He affirms that duʿā is certainly beneficial according to Ahl al-Ḥaqq (the people of truth), citing the Quran and Sunna, and refutes the Muʿtazila who deny its benefit. He then lists conditions: the duʿā must not be impossible by reason (e.g., making the number three even), by Sacred Law (e.g., forgiveness for a polytheist), or by custom (e.g., having a child without a spouse). It should be accompanied by humility, performed at noble times like pre-dawn and after prayers, and should not impose restrictions on Allah (e.g., demanding a specific need at a specific time) unless distress is extreme. Finally, he explains that duʿā in itself is the essence of worship (mukhkh al-ʿibāda) because it manifests one's neediness before Allah. If the response is not seen, it is due to the absence of a condition or because Allah knows that non-response is better for the supplicant.

Seal Our Lives with Good
Dardeer explains the supplication 'ikhtim lanā bi-khayr'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of the supplication for a good end and the ultimate goal of dying upon perfect tawhid with longing for Allah.

Core Idea
The best end is to be taken by Allah in perfect tawhid with desire for Him.

This lesson focuses on a short but profound supplication from the text. Imam Dardeer explains that the true good ending is not merely worldly success but being sealed with the most complete tawhid and a heart longing for Allah. Let's delve into its meaning.
