Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how deviant groups like the Mujassima and Mushabbiha promoted their erroneous beliefs and used takfir to silence opposition.

Core Idea
Deviant groups weaponize takfir to enforce anthropomorphic beliefs.

Before we proceed with the correct creed, we must first recognize the false claims made by deviant groups. This passage exposes the tactics of those who were influenced by philosophers and the Mu'tazila, how they declared their flawed principles as obligatory, and how they excommunicated anyone who disagreed.

The Three Sects of Sophists
Imam Dardeer classifies the three groups of Sophists and briefly describes each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of sophists and their core doctrines as classified by Imam Dardeer.

Core Idea
Sophism denies objective truth in three distinct ways: obstinacy, subjectivism, and universal doubt.

This passage from Imam Dardeer introduces a classification of sophists—those who deny the possibility of certain knowledge. Understanding these three sects is crucial for recognizing and refuting skeptical arguments that persist in various forms.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get a sense of the classification and key terms.
Pay attention to the three names: ʿinādiyya, ʿindiyya, and lā-adriyya.
2
Identify each sect and its core claim
For each sect, note the core claim: the obstinate deny all reality, the subjective say truth is relative to belief, and the agnostic claim total ignorance.
Think about how each claim undermines objective knowledge.
3
Reflect on why these doctrines are problematic
Consider how these positions lead to contradictions and are self-refuting. The passage hints that refutations exist in detailed works.
Why can't the subjective or agnostic positions be consistently held?

وهم فرَقٌ ثلاثة: عناديّة (۳)، يقولون: لا ثبوت الحقيقة مِنَ الحقائق، وإنما هي أوهام وخيالات كالذي يُرى في المنام. وعِنْديّة، يقولون: الشخصُ عِندَ اعتقاده، حتى لو اعْتَقَدَ أَنَّ النارَ جنة أو بالعكس لكان كذلك. ولا أذرية، يقولون في كُلِّ شَيءٍ: لا أدري، حتى إنه يشُلُّ في نفْسِه وَفي شَكٍّه. وتوضيح الرد عليهم مذكور في المُطَوَّلات).

And the subjective ones, who say: a person is according to his belief, so if he believes that fire is paradise or the opposite, it would be so. And the unknowing ones, who say about everything: I do not know, so much so that he is paralyzed in himself and in his doubt. And the clarification of the refutation of them is mentioned in the detailed works.

Commentary
This passage classifies the Sophists (al-sūfisṭāʾiyya) into three groups. The first is the 'obstinate' (ʿinādiyya) who outright deny any fixed reality, claiming all perceived realities are mere illusions and fantasies, like dream images. The second is the 'subjective' (ʿindiyya) who assert that truth is relative to individual belief: if one believes fire is paradise, then it becomes paradise for him, and vice versa. The third is the 'agnostic' (lā-adriyya) who profess ignorance about everything, even about their own state of doubt, thus falling into perpetual uncertainty. The author concludes that the detailed refutation of these positions is found in extensive works (al-muṭawwalāt), indicating that while their doctrines are false, a thorough rebuttal requires deeper study.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the eight origins of disbelief (kufr) that stem from intellectual errors, as enumerated by Imam Dardeer.

Core Idea
Intellectual errors are the roots of disbelief.

Imam Dardeer warns that innovation often stems from foundational mistakes in reasoning. He lists eight origins of disbelief that can afflict even those who claim to follow Islam. These origins are not major sins but subtle intellectual missteps that lead to deviation. By identifying them, you can guard your own faith.

Some origins are conceptual (e.g., essential necessitation), others are behavioral (e.g., prejudice).

Understand the danger

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of the origins (uṣūl) of disbelief, listed as a warning. Dardeer explains that the first origin is the very claim of deposited power mentioned earlier. He then lists seven more: (2) essential necessitation, i.e., making God a cause without choice; (3) rational good and evil, i.e., making God's rulings dependent on human reasoning; (4) blind imitation of forefathers; (5) prejudice; (6) compound ignorance; (7) literalism without reflection; (8) ignorance of the necessary/possible/impossible. He notes some count nine, but eight are listed here. The purpose is to caution students against these subtle errors that lead to kufr.

God's power does not cover impossibilities like having a son by birth.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an objection raised by Ibn Hazm. The objection is reported by al-Sibāʿī: Ibn Hazm argued that God must be able to take a son, because if He were not able, He would be incapable—which is impossible. Dardir responds by analyzing a Quranic verse: 'If Allah had intended to take a son, He could have chosen from what He creates whatever He wills' (Az-Zumar 4). He explains that the verse does not imply that God's power attaches to the impossible (having a son in reality). The impossible is that God should have a son by birth or derivation. What the verse speaks of is 'taking a son' by selection and adoption, which is a possible act because it refers to creating a being and treating him as a son. Dardir uses the example of Pharaoh's wife, who said, 'Perhaps he may benefit us or we may take him as a son' (Al-Qasas 9). Moses was not her biological son, so 'taking him as a son' meant adoption, not real birth. Thus, the objection fails because it confuses the possible act of adopting with the impossible act of having a real son. God's power does not extend to the impossible, and this objection does not prove otherwise.

Core Idea
A simple story exposed the logical flaw of 'God must do the best for everyone.'

This lesson presents a famous historical debate that led Imam al-Ash'ari to abandon the Mu'tazila school. Through a story of three brothers, al-Ash'ari forced his teacher into a logical corner, demonstrating the bankruptcy of the doctrine that God is obligated to provide the most beneficial outcome for each individual.

Commentary
This passage is a historical argument and a refutation of the Mu'tazila by means of a thought experiment. - وفرقة نصبت نفسها للاشتغال بالعَمَلِ والمجاهدات على طبق ما ذهب إليه الفرقتان المتقدمتان، وهم أبو القاسم الجنيد ومَنْ تبعه .
فهؤلاء الفِرَقُ الثلاثةُ هُم خواص الأُمِّةِ المحمدية، ومَنْ عداهم مِنْ جَميعِ الفِرَقِ على ضلال، وإنْ كانَ البَعْضُ منهم يُحكَمُ له بالإسلام، فالناجي مَنْ كان في عقيدته على طبق ما بيَّنَه أهلُ السُّنّة، وقلد في الأحكام العَمَليّةِ إمامًا مِنَ الأئمة الأربعةِ المرضيّة، ثُمَّ تمامُ النّعمةِ والنجاة في سُلوكِ مسْلكَ الجُنَيدِ وأتباعه بَعدَ أَنْ أَحْكَمَ دينَه على طبق ما بيَّنَه الفريقان المتَقَدِّمان.
Conclusion on Sufism: A group devoted themselves to expounding the beliefs that the predecessors were upon, and they are al-Ash'ari and al-Maturidi and those who follow them. And a group devoted themselves to action and spiritual exertions in accordance with what the two aforementioned groups held, and they are Abu al-Qasim al-Junayd and those who follow him. So these three groups are the elite of the Muhammadan ummah, and whoever is other than them from all the sects is in misguidance, even if some of them are judged to be Muslims. So the saved one is he whose creed is in accordance with what Ahl al-Sunnah explained, and he followed in practical rulings one of the four approved imams, then the completion of grace and salvation is in following the path of al-Junayd and his followers after he has established his religion according to what the two aforementioned groups explained.

Commentary
This passage classifies the three groups that constitute the saved elite of the Muslim ummah. Dardir begins by identifying two groups as they appear in the text: first, the theologians (Ash'ari and Maturidi) who devoted themselves to explaining the creed of the Salaf; second, the spiritual practitioners (Sufis) like Junayd who focused on action and spiritual struggle. The text states there are three groups, and the missing third group is the jurists (the four imams and their followers), implied by the later reference to the 'four approved imams'. Dardir then declares that these three groups are the elite of the Muhammadan ummah, and all other sects are in misguidance, though some may still be considered Muslims. He then gives the condition for salvation: one must have a creed in agreement with Ahl al-Sunnah (the first group), follow one of the four imams in practical rulings (the second group), and then complete the blessing by following the path of Junayd and his followers (the third group) after mastering the religion. This threefold path integrates correct 'aqidah, fiqh, and tasawwuf.

A conclusion on Sufism. The divine pole, my master Muhammad al-Khalwati and his followers, and the divine pole, my master Abdullah al-Naqshbandi and his followers — all of these are the masters of the Muhammadan community, may Allah be pleased with us and with them, amen. The sheikh who guides to Allah must have walked upon the path of a sheikh from the masters of the path, and labored and struggled against himself until it became refined and the human follies departed from it, otherwise it is obligatory to avoid him. Indeed, many among the people have emulated an imam from the four imams, may Allah be pleased with them, but in his beliefs he deviated from their belief, so he did not believe the belief of the people of the Sunnah, and they are various sects who have gone astray in their beliefs like the Qadariyya and others. And among the people is one who was not content with emulating an imam from the four imams, nor with the belief of the people of the Sunnah — he is more astray than those before them. And among the people is one who claims that he is a traveler on the path of the people of Allah, so he dresses in their garb and speaks in a way that makes people think he is from them, while in fact he is a good-for-nothing, who fills his stomach with food, whether lawful or unlawful, and his night with sleep, and pounces on the world like a beast pouncing on its prey, and perhaps he makes himself a sheikh, and he has followers who hunt for him with the trap of his sheikhdom the filth of decaying rubble, and they claim that they are upon something; those are the liars. The knower of Allah, my master Umar ibn al-Farid, may Allah be pleased with him, alluded to them with his saying: 'They were content with wishes and afflicted by their portions, and they waded into the seas of love in pretense, so they were not tested. They are in the night-journey, they have not moved from their place, nor have they departed in the journey from it, and they are exhausted.' Nay, they fell back and retreated because they followed their own desires, and Satan leads them to everything he loves from them, as he said: 'And from my madhhab, when they preferred blindness over guidance out of envy from their own selves, they went astray, until it became from the most generous of them, by the nobility of their character, that if someone gave them charity, they made that a habit, and they demanded it from the one who did good to them until they narrowed his paths, and they say: Give us our custom or else we will put you to shame, making people think they are masters of spiritual states and that Allah confirms them in their speech. No! This is not the way of the poor, the people of Allah! Their way is humility, brokenness, love of burdens, chastity, asceticism, piety, altruism, and reliance on Allah. As for these, they are the worst of people, consuming people's wealth in falsehood, and claiming high stations while they are in the lowest depths. They have multiplied in this age until they filled the layers of the earth in every region and place; we seek refuge in Allah from them. Our teacher al-Sayyid al-Bakri said in his Alfiyya al-Tasawwuf: 'Indeed their evil has increased in this age, so much that their bitterness has risen to the utmost among people. And there is no one here to deter them, because of this Hanafi religion, they left.' And when the people of Allah looked at their abundance, the abundance of their corruption, and the confusion of their beliefs, they closed the doors of the corners of guidance and delegated the matter to the Lord of servants, and they differed concerning the people, for none know them except one whom Allah has singled out with divine lights and eternal felicity. They traveled a handspan but returned in the night-journey a mile.

Commentary
This passage is a conclusion (khātima) on Sufism (taṣawwuf). It begins by invoking revered Sufi figures (al-Khalwati, al-Naqshbandi) as true masters of the Ummah. Then it defines the condition for a legitimate spiritual guide: he must have undergone disciplined training under a qualified sheikh, struggling against his own soul until its human flaws are removed. Without this, such a person must be avoided. Imam Dardeer then distinguishes three categories of misguided people: (1) Those who follow one of the four imams but deviate in creed, belonging to various sects like the Qadariyya; (2) Those who reject emulation of the imams and the Sunni creed altogether, being even more astray; (3) Those who falsely claim to follow the path of the friends of Allah, dressing and speaking like them but actually being gluttons, sleepers, and greedy for worldly gain — they even set themselves up as sheikhs, trapping people for material benefit. He quotes the Sufi poet Ibn al-Farid, whose lines show that these claimants are stuck in their desires and never progress. The author adds his own commentary: such people follow their whims and are led by Satan; they become accustomed to receiving charity and demand it, pretending to have spiritual states. Then Imam Dardeer emphatically contrasts this false way with the true way of the poor (fuqarā') of Allah: it consists of humility, brokenness, bearing burdens, chastity, asceticism, piety, altruism, and reliance on God. The false ones are evil, consuming people's wealth unjustly, and pretending to high ranks while being low. He notes that they are widespread in his time, and seeks Allah's refuge. Finally, he quotes his own teacher al-Sayyid al-Bakri's poem describing how the evil of these impostors has become rampant and no one deters them, leading the genuine friends of Allah to close guidance centers and entrust the matter to Allah, as only those specially illuminated can recognize the true from the false. The passage ends with a poetic line that they barely move forward but fall back far.

A conclusion on Sufism. The divine pole, my master Muhammad al-Khalwati and his followers, and the divine pole, my master Abdullah al-Naqshbandi and his followers — all of these are the masters of the Muhammadan community, may Allah be pleased with us and with them, amen. The sheikh who guides to Allah must have walked upon the path of a sheikh from the masters of the path, and labored and struggled against himself until it became refined and the human follies departed from it, otherwise it is obligatory to avoid him. Indeed, many among the people have emulated an imam from the four imams, may Allah be pleased with them, but in his beliefs he deviated from their belief, so he did not believe the belief of the people of the Sunnah, and they are various sects who have gone astray in their beliefs like the Qadariyya and others. And among the people is one who was not content with emulating an imam from the four imams, nor with the belief of the people of the Sunnah — he is more astray than those before them. And among the people is one who claims that he is a traveler on the path of the people of Allah, so he dresses in their garb and speaks in a way that makes people think he is from them, while in fact he is a good-for-nothing, who fills his stomach with food, whether lawful or unlawful, and his night with sleep, and pounces on the world like a beast pouncing on its prey, and perhaps he makes himself a sheikh, and he has followers who hunt for him with the trap of his sheikhdom the filth of decaying rubble, and they claim that they are upon something; those are the liars. The knower of Allah, my master Umar ibn al-Farid, may Allah be pleased with him, alluded to them with his saying: 'They were content with wishes and afflicted by their portions, and they waded into the seas of love in pretense, so they were not tested. They are in the night-journey, they have not moved from their place, nor have they departed in the journey from it, and they are exhausted.' Nay, they fell back and retreated because they followed their own desires, and Satan leads them to everything he loves from them, as he said: 'And from my madhhab, when they preferred blindness over guidance out of envy from their own selves, they went astray, until it became from the most generous of them, by the nobility of their character, that if someone gave them charity, they made that a habit, and they demanded it from the one who did good to them until they narrowed his paths, and they say: Give us our custom or else we will put you to shame, making people think they are masters of spiritual states and that Allah confirms them in their speech. No! This is not the way of the poor, the people of Allah! Their way is humility, brokenness, love of burdens, chastity, asceticism, piety, altruism, and reliance on Allah. As for these, they are the worst of people, consuming people's wealth in falsehood, and claiming high stations while they are in the lowest depths. They have multiplied in this age until they filled the layers of the earth in every region and place; we seek refuge in Allah from them. Our teacher al-Sayyid al-Bakri said in his Alfiyya al-Tasawwuf: 'Indeed their evil has increased in this age, so much that their bitterness has risen to the utmost among people. And there is no one here to deter them, because of this Hanafi religion, they left.' And when the people of Allah looked at their abundance, the abundance of their corruption, and the confusion of their beliefs, they closed the doors of the corners of guidance and delegated the matter to the Lord of servants, and they differed concerning the people, for none know them except one whom Allah has singled out with divine lights and eternal felicity. They traveled a handspan but returned in the night-journey a mile.

Commentary
This passage is a conclusion (khātima) on Sufism (taṣawwuf). It begins by invoking revered Sufi figures (al-Khalwati, al-Naqshbandi) as true masters of the Ummah. Then it defines the condition for a legitimate spiritual guide: he must have undergone disciplined training under a qualified sheikh, struggling against his own soul until its human flaws are removed. Without this, such a person must be avoided. Imam Dardeer then distinguishes three categories of misguided people: (1) Those who follow one of the four imams but deviate in creed, belonging to various sects like the Qadariyya; (2) Those who reject emulation of the imams and the Sunni creed altogether, being even more astray; (3) Those who falsely claim to follow the path of the friends of Allah, dressing and speaking like them but actually being gluttons, sleepers, and greedy for worldly gain — they even set themselves up as sheikhs, trapping people for material benefit. He quotes the Sufi poet Ibn al-Farid, whose lines show that these claimants are stuck in their desires and never progress. The author adds his own commentary: such people follow their whims and are led by Satan; they become accustomed to receiving charity and demand it, pretending to have spiritual states. Then Imam Dardeer emphatically contrasts this false way with the true way of the poor (fuqarā') of Allah: it consists of humility, brokenness, bearing burdens, chastity, asceticism, piety, altruism, and reliance on God. The false ones are evil, consuming people's wealth unjustly, and pretending to high ranks while being low. He notes that they are widespread in his time, and seeks Allah's refuge. Finally, he quotes his own teacher al-Sayyid al-Bakri's poem describing how the evil of these impostors has become rampant and no one deters them, leading the genuine friends of Allah to close guidance centers and entrust the matter to Allah, as only those specially illuminated can recognize the true from the false. The passage ends with a poetic line that they barely move forward but fall back far.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That true Sufis are always in high spiritual states and perform miracles, so they can demand payment for their services.
Correction: Imam Dardeer strongly denies this. The true people of Allah are characterized by humility, selflessness, and reliance on God, not by demanding charity or claiming miracles.

Key Takeaway
The true people of Allah are defined by humility, asceticism, and reliance on God, not by demanding worldly benefits or claiming exalted status.
