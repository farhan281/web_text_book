Linguistic Origin of 'Al' in the Opening Praise
Dardeer explains the linguistic origins of 'Al' and its usage conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two linguistic theories for the origin of the word 'Al' and the specific rule for its usage in idafa.

Core Idea
Al has a special origin and is only used for honorable men.

This lesson examines a seemingly small word, 'Al', as it appears in the opening praise of the Prophet's family. Linguists debate its etymology, and there is a precise condition for when it can be used. Understanding this helps us appreciate the precision of Arabic in theological contexts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage carefully, then the literal translation. Notice the two views and the examples.
2
Identify the two derivations
Find where Sibawayh and al-Kisa'i derive 'Al' from. Note the phonetic changes: ha to hamza to alif for Sibawayh, and waw to alif for al-Kisa'i.
Compare with the examples 'Adam' and 'jamal'.
3
Understand the usage restriction
The passage states a rule: 'Al' is only used for honorable rational males. Look at the prohibited examples to internalize the rule.
Think about why the author includes this condition.

And its origin according to Sibawayh is 'ahl', its ha' was turned into a hamza, then the hamza became an alif due to its sukun and the fat-ha before it, as in 'Adam'. And according to al-Kisa'i: 'awalun' like 'jamal', from 'ala ya'ulu' meaning he returned, so the waw was turned into an alif due to its haraka and the fat-ha before it. And it is only added to someone who has honor among rational males; thus one does not say: Al al-Iskani, nor Al Fatima, nor Al al-Hidn.

Commentary
This passage is a lexical definition of the word 'Al' (آل) as used in the praise of the Prophet's family. The author, Imam Dardeer, presents two linguistic views. First, according to Sibawayh, the word originally is 'ahl' (أهل), where the ha' was changed to a hamza, and then the hamza became an alif because it is preceded by a fat-ha and is itself sukun, similar to the derivation of 'Adam' (آدم). Second, according to al-Kisa'i, the origin is 'awal' (أَوَل), like 'jamal' (جمل), derived from the verb 'ala ya'ulu' (آل يؤول) meaning 'he returned.' In this case, the waw (و) was turned into an alif because it was moving and preceded by a fat-ha. After explaining the etymology, Dardeer states a usage rule: the word 'Al' is only used in idafa (construct phrase) for people who have honor and are rational males. Therefore, it is not said for non-honorable or non-rational beings, nor for females. The examples given: 'Al al-Iskani' (a place name), 'Al Fatima' (a female), and 'Al al-Hidn' (possibly a tribe or meaning 'lap'), are all incorrect uses.

Grammatical Analysis of 'La Siyyama' and Abu Bakr's Distinction
Dardeer analyzes the grammar of 'la siyyama' and identifies Abu Bakr as the special companion in the cave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the grammatical flexibility of 'la siyyama' and how Imam Dardeer uses it to highlight Abu Bakr's unique status among the Companions.

Core Idea
'La siyyama' is not a fixed phrase; its grammar reveals Abu Bakr's supreme rank.

In this passage, Imam Dardeer unpacks the grammar of a single phrase from the opening praise: 'la siyyama rafiqahu fi al-ghar'. He shows that the phrase can be read in three ways, each grammatically valid. Beyond the grammar, he explains that the phrase singles out Abu Bakr as the companion in the cave, giving him special mention despite being already included among all companions.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing; then read the translation. Get the overall sense of Dardeer's grammatical discussion.
Notice the detailed focus on a single phrase.
2
Identify the three grammatical cases
Dardeer states that the noun after 'ma' can be in jar, rafa', or nasb. Find where he explains each and what construction underlies it.
The jar case treats 'ma' as extra; rafa' treats 'ma' as relative; nasb treats 'ma' as kaffa.
3
Understand the significance of the identification
After the grammar, Dardeer clarifies the meaning and singles out Abu Bakr as the companion. Recognise that this grammatical excursus serves to honour him.
He is mentioned separately though already included in 'al-sahb'.
4
Reflect on the takeaway
Consider how attention to linguistic detail in praise poetry elevates the subject. This shows the high regard for Abu Bakr in Islamic tradition.

لا سيما رفيقه في الغار) «لا» من «لا سيما» نافية للجنس، و«سي» كـ «مثل» وزنا ومعنى اسمها وخبرها محذوف وجوبًا، أي ثابت وأصله «سوي»، فقلبت الواو ياء لاجتماعها مع الياء وسبق إحداهما بالسكون وأدغمت في الياء. ويجوز في الاسم الواقع بعد «ما» الجر والرفع مطلقًا، والنصب إن كان نكرة، وقد روي بالأوجه الثلاثة قوله: «ولا سيما يوم بدارة جلجل». والجر أرجحها، وهو على إضافة «سي» إليه، و«ما» زائدة بينهما، مثلها في «أيَّا الأجلين». وأما الرفع فهو على أنه خبر لمبتدإ محذوف، و«ما» موصولة أو نكرة موصوفة بالجملة بعدها والتقدير: ولا مثل الذي هو رفيقه، ولا مثل شيء هو رفيقه، و«سي» مضاف، و«ما» مضاف إليه، فعلى كل من وجهي الجر والرفع تكون فتحة «سي» فتحة إعراب؛ لأن اسم «لا» النافية للجنس إذا كان مضافًا يكون منصوبًا. وأما نصب النكرة بعدها فعلى التمييز، و«ما» كافة عن الإضافة، والفتحة فتحة بناء، مثلها في «لا رجل». والمعنى: والصلاة والسلام على الصحب، لا مثل الرفيق، فإن الصلاة عليه أنم منها عليهم، يعني أطلب ذلك من الله تعالى. والمراد برفيقه في الغار أبو بكر الصديق رضي الله عنه، خصه بالذكر بعد دخوله في عموم الأصحاب تنويها بعظم شأنه إذ هو شيخ الصحابة وأفضلهم على الإطلاق.
'La siyyama rafiqahu fi al-ghar': 'La' from 'la siyyama' is the negative of genus, and 'siyy' is like 'mithl' in measure and meaning; its subject and predicate are necessarily omitted, meaning 'thabit'. Its origin is 'suway', so the waw was turned into a ya' due to its meeting with the ya' and one of them being preceded by sukun and it was assimilated into the ya'. And it is permissible in the noun after 'ma' to have jar and rafa' absolutely, and nasb if it is indefinite. It has been narrated with all three ways in his statement: 'Wa la siyyama yawmi bi-darati juljul'. The jar is the strongest of them, and it is based on adding 'siyy' to it, with 'ma' being extra between them, like it in 'ayya al-ajalayn'. As for rafa', it is as a predicate of an omitted subject, and 'ma' is a relative pronoun or an indefinite described by the sentence after it, the estimation being: 'wa la mithla alladhi huwa rafiqahu' or 'wa la mithla shay'in huwa rafiqahu', and 'siyy' is mudaf and 'ma' is mudaf ilayh. So in each of the two ways of jar and rafa', the fat-ha of 'siyy' is a declensional fat-ha because the ism of 'la' the negator of genus when mudaf is mansub. As for the nasb of the indefinite after it, it is on tamyiz, and 'ma' is kaffa from idafa, and the fat-ha is a building fat-ha, like it in 'la rajula'. The meaning: and the prayers and peace be upon the sahb, not like the companion, for indeed the prayer upon him is more complete than upon them, meaning: I ask that from Allah the Exalted. And what is meant by his companion in the cave is Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, may Allah be pleased with him; he singled him out for mention after including him in the generality of the companions to make great his status, for he is the elder of the Companions and the best of them absolutely.
Source pages p. 35

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed grammatical analysis of the phrase 'La siyyama rafiqahu fi al-ghar' (especially his companion in the cave) from the opening praise. The author, Imam Dardeer, breaks down the components. 'La' is the negation of genus, and 'siyya' is like 'mithl' (like/similar). The subject and predicate of 'la' are obligatorily omitted. The word 'siyya' originally was 'suway', where the waw changed to ya' due to assimilation with the following ya'. Then Dardeer explains three possible cases for the noun that comes after 'ma' (i.e., 'rafiqahu'). It can be in jar (genitive), rafa' (nominative), or nasb (accusative) if the noun is indefinite. He cites a line of poetry as evidence that all three are used, but jar is preferred. The analysis then details each case: Jar is by adding 'siyy' to it with 'ma' as an extra letter zida, like in the Quranic 'ayya al-ajalayn'. Rafa' treats 'ma' as a relative pronoun or indefinite described by a sentence, with an estimated subject. In both jar and rafa', the fat-ha on 'siyy' is an inflectional fat-ha because 'la' negating genus requires a mansub noun when it is mudaf. Nasb of the indefinite is as a tamyiz (specification) with 'ma' as a kaffa (preventive) making 'siyy' mabni (indeclinable), like in 'la rajula'. The overall meaning is that prayer and peace are upon the companions, but especially upon the companion in the cave, for the prayer upon him is more complete. Finally, Dardeer identifies the companion as Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, explaining that he is singled out despite being included in the general category of companions to honor his great status, as he is the leader of the companions and the best of them absolutely.
