Customary Judgment: Habit, Not Cause
Dardeer defines customary judgment and explains it does not imply causal efficacy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ashʿarī concept of customary judgment (al-ḥukm al-ʿādī) and why it does not imply causal efficacy in created things.

Core Idea
Customary judgment links events, but only Allah produces effects.

In Islamic theology, especially within the Ashʿarī tradition, the concept of 'custom' (ʿāda) plays a key role in understanding causality. Imam Dardeer now defines what a customary judgment is, distinguishing it from any claim of inherent causal power in created objects. This passage clarifies that when we say 'fire burns,' we are only describing a habitual pattern, not asserting that fire is an agent.

The passage is concise; read it twice.

This is a key point: custom only indicates correlation, not causation.

Commentary
This passage defines the customary judgment (al-ḥukm al-ʿādī). The author states that when the judge (al-ḥākim) is custom (al-ʿāda), the judgment is called customary. Its definition is the affirmation or negation of one thing concerning another based on repeated sense-perception (al-ḥiss). For example, we judge that fire burns because we repeatedly observe that contact with fire results in burning, and that food satiates because we repeatedly observe that eating leads to satiation. However, the author immediately clarifies that this does not mean fire itself is the true agent or cause of burning. Customary judgment only indicates a constant conjunction or correlation between two events, not a causal relationship. The actual effect (taʾthīr) is not known by custom; only Allah creates effects. Custom merely indicates a habitual link. Therefore, assigning the role of agent (fāʿil) is outside the scope of custom. This is a crucial point in Ashʿarī theology, which denies inherent causal efficacy in created things and affirms that Allah is the sole creator of all actions and effects.

Kasb: How Human Acts Are Acquired, Not Created

Core Idea
Human acts are acquired by choice but created by Allah alone.

This passage addresses a key question in Islamic theology: if Allah creates all things, including our actions, how can we be held responsible? The Ash'ari answer is the doctrine of kasb (acquisition). The author presents the objection and then clarifies that human acts are attributed to us by virtue of our choice and intention, not by any creative power. This subtle distinction preserves divine omnipotence while making moral accountability meaningful.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ashʿarī doctrine of occasionalism — that natural objects and customary causes have no real causal power, and that only Allah directly creates effects.

Core Idea
Fire does not burn — Allah creates burning alongside the fire.

Imam Dardeer now draws a crucial conclusion from the proofs of divine unity: that nothing in creation has inherent causal power. He lists familiar examples — fire, food, water, planets, knives — to show that what we call 'causes' are merely occasions for Allah's direct act of creation. This lesson presents this foundational doctrine of occasionalism.

This passage is a declaration of the Ashʿarī doctrine of occasionalism: the denial of natural causality. Dardeer states that it is also known (from the proof of divine unity) that customary matters (al-umūr al-ʿādiyya) have no real effect upon the things with which they are habitually associated. He lists numerous examples: fire has no effect in burning, food does not cause satiety, water does not cause quenching or plant growth, planets do not ripen fruits, celestial spheres do not affect anything, knives do not cut, and nothing has inherent power to repel or bring heat or cold. He specifies that this lack of effect is not due to natural disposition (ṭabʿ), nor due to causation (ʿilla), nor due to a power that Allah has deposited in them. Rather, all effects are produced solely by Allah Most High, by His pure choice, at the moment these customary things are present. In other words, Allah habitually creates burning upon contact with fire, but the fire does not cause it; Allah could create the opposite. This reinforces tawḥīd and refutes any notion of secondary causes.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation and classification. Imam Dardeer explains the philosophers' view: they believe that God is a necessary cause (illa) of the world, which implies that the world is eternal (qadīm) because the cause and effect are co-eternal. This entails that God lacks choice (ikhtiyār) and power (qudra) to do otherwise, which is shirk and kufr. Then he summarizes the three possible types of agents:
1. Agent by nature (fā‘il bi'l-tab‘): acts by inherent nature, conditional.
2. Agent by cause (fā‘il bi'l-‘illa): acts necessarily and unconditionally.
3. Agent by choice (fā‘il bi'l-ikhtiyār): acts freely, can do or refrain.
The philosophers affirm all three: they say humans are agents by choice, but God is an agent by cause (compulsion). Muslims affirm only the third type (agent by choice) and attribute it solely to God, the One, the Overwhelming. Footnotes add that the philosophers rely only on reason, not revelation, so they fell into error; that lack of power means inability to refrain; and that the Quran confirms God's freedom to create and choose.

Commentary
This passage defines a doctrinal position and its ruling. It begins by quoting the matn line: 'Whoever says by the deposited power, that one is an innovator, so do not pay attention.' The commentary then explains that the phrase 'whoever says' refers to one from the people of deviation who claim that customary things (like fire) have an effect through a power that Allah deposited in them, analogous to the servant's effect through his own acquired power. The commentary then affirms the ruling that such a person is a 'bid'ī' (innovator), because his statement contradicts the way of the Salaf. However, he is not considered a disbeliever according to the sounder opinion, for reasons already mentioned. Because he is an innovator, his statement is to be ignored, and one must instead adhere to the Sunni position: no effect belongs to anything besides Allah, whether by nature, cause, or deposited power. Effect is solely Allah's act by His pure choice.

Commentary
