Refuting Jabriyya and Qadariyya on Human Action
Dardeer refutes Jabriyya and Qadariyya on human action and rules on disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Dardeer's refutation of both the Jabriyya who deny human power and the Qadariyya who claim human power creates actions, and the ruling on their disbelief.

Core Idea
Actions are neither purely forced nor purely independent; voluntary versus involuntary is self-evident, and extreme views are refuted.

Dardeer continues his discussion on human action by clarifying involuntary actions—where Allah creates an action without concomitant power—and then refutes two extreme schools: the Jabriyya who deny any power in the servant, and the Qadariyya who claim the servant's power creates actions. He also addresses the theological rulings of disbelief for each group. This passage shows the balanced Sunni position that preserves both divine omnipotence and human accountability.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation once to get the flow of Dardeer's argument.
Notice the compact style; each sentence carries doctrinal weight.
2
Identify the two refuted groups
Note how Dardeer first describes involuntary action, then refutes both Jabriyya and Qadariyya in a single sentence.
3
Understand the ruling on disbelief
Pay attention to why the Jabriyya are definitively disbelievers, while there is disagreement on the Qadariyya.
The key phrase is 'لأن مذهبهم ينفي التكليف' for the Jabriyya.
4
Reflect on the distinction
Consider why the difference between voluntary and involuntary movement is obvious to every rational person, and how this anchors the middle position.
This distinction is the foundation of the refutation.

Commentary
This passage continues from the previous by discussing involuntary actions, then refutes both Jabriyya and Qadariyya, and finally gives the ruling on their kufr. Dardeer explains that when Allah creates an action in the servant without a concomitant power, the servant is called constrained (majbūr) and compelled (muḍṭarr). Allah has graciously removed obligation in such cases, though He could have obligated us. The distinction between voluntary and involuntary movement is obvious to every rational person. Then he refutes two extreme views: (1) The Jabriyya, who say the servant has no power at all accompanying any action, making him completely constrained like a thread moved by wind—this is false because we do experience a sense of choice and power, and it would negate accountability altogether. (2) The Qadariyya, who claim that the originated power has actual effect in actions according to the servant’s will—this is false because it implies a partner with Allah in creation. Dardeer then rules: The Jabriyya (who deny human power entirely) are unequivocally disbelievers because their view nullifies the obligation brought by the messengers. As for the Qadariyya (who affirm effect of human power), there is disagreement; the correct position is that they are not disbelievers, because even though their position logically implies a partner for Allah, they nonetheless affirm that Allah creates the servant, his power, and his will, so in reality the action is created by Allah (since they attribute creation of the agent to Allah).

Refuting the Mu'tazilite Objection on Divine Will
Dardeer refutes the Mu'tazilite claim that willing evil is itself evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Mu'tazilite argument that willing evil is evil fails, and how the Ash'ari response distinguishes between divine will and human acquisition.

Core Idea
Willing evil is not evil for Allah; ugliness pertains to acquisition, not creation.

The Mu'tazila objected that if Allah wills evil acts, He would be evil. Imam Dardeer refutes this by clarifying that ugliness lies in the act of acquisition (kasb) and self-description, not in the sheer act of divine willing or creating. This lesson unpacks that rebuttal and its implications for divine justice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazilite objection
Read the first part of the passage where the Mu'tazila argue that willing the ugly is ugly, just like creating it. This leads them to deny that most human actions are willed or created by Allah.
The objection is based on an analogy between divine action and human action.
2
Recognize the two-part rebuttal
The author first denies that will and command are identical, citing the hadith 'Whatever Allah wills occurs' to prove that all occurring things are willed by Allah. Then he distinguishes between ugliness in acquisition (kasb) vs. creation/will.
This shows that the Mu'tazilite premise is flawed.
3
Distinguish between kasb and creation
Understand the key distinction: ugliness (qabih) is only in the act of acquiring (kasb) ugly deeds and describing oneself with them, not in creating or willing them. This protects Allah from attributed ugliness.
The term 'كسب' (kasb) is central to the rebuttal.
4
Appreciate the role of the hadith principle
The hadith 'ما شاء الله كان، وما لم يشأ لم يكن' is used as a proof that Allah's will encompasses all that occurs, including actions that are ugly from a human perspective.
This is a foundational Ash'ari principle.

The text notes this position with 'wa huwa shanabuhu' (perhaps 'it is ugly' or a corruption). Then he clarifies that ugliness pertains to acquisition (kasb) of ugly acts and self-description with them, not to the act of creating or willing them in the divine sense. Therefore, willing evil is not evil for Allah. Finally, he asserts that this doctrine is falsified by both rational and transmitted proof.

The Four Categories of Occurring Things
Dardeer enumerates the four divisions of things relative to divine will and command.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four logical categories of things based on divine will and command, with their examples from Abu Bakr and Abu Jahl.

Core Idea
Not everything commanded is willed, and not everything willed is commanded.

Imam Dardeer builds on the earlier principle that everything occurring is willed by Allah. He now lists four categories that logically follow, using the examples of Abu Bakr's faith and Abu Jahl's disbelief to illustrate the relationship between divine command and will.

Guided Path
1
Read the enumeration passage
Read the Arabic passage that lists the four divisions. Notice the structure: first division, second (its opposite), third, fourth (its opposite).
The passage refers back to the earlier verse 'And every thing occurring He wills'.
2
Identify each category
Match each division to its description: commanded and willed, neither commanded nor willed, commanded but not willed, willed but not commanded.
The second division is the opposite of the first, so it is neither commanded nor willed.
3
Understand the examples
Abu Bakr's faith is commanded and willed; his disbelief (hypothetical) is neither commanded nor willed; Abu Jahl's faith (hypothetical) is commanded but not willed; his actual disbelief is willed but not commanded.
These examples show that command and will are not identical.
4
Check your understanding
Confirm that the four categories are exhaustive and mutually exclusive. Ask yourself: why is Abu Jahl's disbelief willed but not commanded?
Because Allah wills all things that occur, but He does not command disbelief.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the four categories of occurring things in relation to divine will (irada) and command (amr). The author refers back to the earlier statement 'And every thing occurring He wills' to show that the four divisions follow from the logical implications of that principle. The four divisions are: 1) commanded and willed (e.g., Abu Bakr's faith), 2) its opposite, i.e., not commanded and not willed? Actually the text says 'al-thani 'aksuhu ka al-kufr minhu' meaning the opposite, i.e., not commanded and not willed? But careful: The second division is 'cakasuhu' = its opposite. Since the first is commanded and willed, the opposite would be not commanded and not willed. However, later the third is commanded but not willed, and fourth is willed but not commanded. So actually the second should be neither commanded nor willed. But the example given is 'like the disbelief from him' i.e., from Abu Bakr. Abu Bakr's disbelief would be neither commanded nor willed (since disbelief from a believer is not willed). So the four divisions are: (1) commanded and willed, (2) neither commanded nor willed, (3) commanded but not willed, (4) willed but not commanded. The author then stresses the importance of this classification because the Mu'tazila erred here, and holding it correctly is the Sunni doctrine of the Salaf and Khalaf. A hadith is cited supporting the principle that whatever Allah wills occurs and whatever He does not will does not occur.

The Sunni Position: Against Mu'tazila Error
Dardeer warns against Mu'tazila error and cites a hadith on divine will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the four categories are crucial for distinguishing Sunni doctrine from Mu'tazilism, and how the hadith supports the principle that nothing occurs except by Allah's will.

Core Idea
Correct belief about will and command saves one from the error of the Mu'tazila.

After laying out the four categories, Imam Dardeer warns that the Mu'tazila have slipped here. He affirms that holding this classification correctly is the way of Ahl al-Sunna, and supports it with a hadith from Abu Dawud that underscores Allah's absolute will: 'What Allah wills occurs, and what He does not will does not occur.'

Guided Path
1
Read the warning passage
Read from 'So preserve this station' to the end of the hadith. Notice the strong language: 'the feet of the Mu'tazila have slipped therein.'
The Mu'tazila denied that Allah could will evil, so they rejected the fourth category.
2
Understand the Sunni stance
The correct belief according to the Salaf and Khalaf is to affirm all four categories without restricting Allah's will. This distinguishes Sunni from Mu'tazila.
3
Analyze the hadith evidence
The hadith says 'ما شاء الله كان وما لم يشأ لم يكن' (Whatever Allah wills occurs, and whatever He does not will does not occur). This proves that everything that happens is by His will, including disbelief.
The hadith supports the fourth category (willed but not commanded).

المزيل للعمى) يعني: الجهل، وفي كلامه إشارة إلى أنَّ الدُّعاءَ يَنْفَعُ، وهو تما لا شك فيه عِندَ أهْل الحقِّ، والقُرآنُ العظيم مشحون به، وهو في السُّنّةِ أكثرُ مِنْ أَنْ يُحصى، خلافًا للمعتزلة)، (۱) الدعاء ينفع الأحياء والأموات، ولو صدر من كافر، لحديث: (دعوة المظلوم مستجابة، وإن كان كافراً)، وفي لفظ أبي هريرة: (وإن فاجرا ففجوره على نفسه). ويرجح هذا كلام الفقهاء في باب الاستسقاء. وقيل: دعاء الكافر لا يستجاب، لقوله تعالى: ﴿وَمَا دُعَاءُ الْكَافِرِينَ إِلا فِي ضَلَال [الرعد: ١٤]، والله تعالى يقضي باستجابته الحاجات تفضلا، إذ القضاء على قسمين: مبرم ومعلق، فالمعلق لا استحالة فيما عُلق رفعه منه على الدعاء، ولا في نزول ما علق نزوله منه على الدعاء، ضرورة وجوب ترتب المشروطات على شروطها، والمسببات على أسبابها، وأما المبرم فالدعاء وإن لم يرفعه لكن ربما أثاب الله العبد على دعائه برفعه، أو أنزل بالداعي لطفاً فيه، والمدعى ترتب نفع للداعي أو لغيره على دعائه عاجلا أو آجلا يخرجه عن العبث، فإذا علمت ما تقرر، تعلم رد ما احتج به المعتزلة بأن ما دُعِيَ به إما أن يكون مما قدره وقضاه أو لا، والأول تخلفه محال، والثاني غير محال، فانتفت فائدته فكان عبثا. [سباعي، ص ٦٨٠]
('The remover of blindness') means ignorance, and in his words is an indication that supplication benefits, and it is something about which there is no doubt among the people of truth, and the great Qur'an is filled with it, and it is in the Sunnah more than can be counted, contrary to the Mu'tazila. (1) Supplication benefits the living and the dead, even if it comes from a disbeliever, according to the hadith: 'The supplication of the oppressed is answered, even if he is a disbeliever,' and in the wording of Abu Hurayra: 'Even if he is a wicked person, his wickedness is against himself.' This is supported by the speech of the jurists in the chapter on seeking rain. And it was said: The supplication of the disbeliever is not answered, due to His statement: 'And the supplication of the disbelievers is only in error' [al-Ra'd: 14]. Rather, Allah the Exalted decrees the answering of needs as a favor, for decree is of two types: irrevocable (mubram) and suspended (mu'allaq). As for the suspended, there is no impossibility in its repulsion being suspended on supplication, nor in its descent being suspended on supplication, due to the necessity of the arrangement of conditioned things upon their conditions, and effects upon their causes. As for the irrevocable, even if supplication does not repel it, perhaps Allah rewards the servant for his supplication by repelling it, or He sends down gentleness to the supplicant through it. The claim is that the arrangement of benefit for the supplicant or others, sooner or later, upon his supplication removes it from futility. So if you know what has been established, you will know the refutation of what the Mu'tazila argued: that what is supplicated for is either something He has destined and decreed or not; the first is impossible to fail, the second is not impossible, so its benefit is negated and it becomes futile.

Commentary
This passage is a combined statement of doctrine and a refutation of an opposing view. The author begins by defining a phrase from the poem being commented on: 'al-muzīl li-l-ʿamā' (the remover of blindness) means ignorance. He then indicates that the poet's words imply that supplication (duʿā) is beneficial. This benefit is unquestionable among the people of truth (ahl al-ḥaqq), is extensively mentioned in the Qur'an, and is even more abundant in the Sunnah. The author notes that this contradicts the Mu'tazila, who deny the benefit of duʿā. The accompanying footnote (1) provides a detailed proof and refutation. It first asserts that duʿā benefits the living and the dead, even if from a disbeliever, citing relevant hadiths and the consensus of jurists. It then addresses a counter-argument from the Mu'tazila by distinguishing between two types of divine decree: mubram (irrevocable) and muʿallaq (suspended). For suspended decrees, the outcome is conditioned upon causes like duʿā, so duʿā can affect it. For irrevocable decrees, duʿā may still bring reward or divine gentleness. Thus, duʿā never lacks benefit. The author concludes that this refutes the Mu'tazila's syllogism: if duʿā is for something decreed, it is superfluous; if not, it is impossible. The answer shows that duʿā remains beneficial in both cases.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Du'a is futile because if Allah has decreed something, it will happen regardless and du'a cannot change it.
Correction: Du'a is part of the causal chain for suspended decrees, and even for irrevocable decrees, it brings reward and divine kindness. Thus du'a is never futile.

Key Takeaway
Du'a is never futile because it operates within the causal order Allah has established for suspended decrees and still yields reward for irrevocable ones.
