Definition of ‘Aqa’id al-Tawhid’
Imam Dardeer defines the terms ‘aqa’id and tawhid in the opening phrase.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that 'aqa'id al-tawhid refers to universal principles affirming divine perfection and transcendence, not merely belief in one God.

Core Idea
Tawhid means affirming every divine perfection and negating every imperfection.

Imam Dardeer begins by clarifying the key phrase 'aqa'id al-tawhid'. This is important because it sets the scope of the entire work. The commentator explains that 'aqa'id' are universal rules, and 'tawhid' is the comprehensive belief in Allah's perfection. This lesson unpacks that definition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then read the English translation. Notice how the commentator defines each term before connecting them.
2
Understand the two examples
The commentator gives two universal rules: 'every perfection is obligatory for Allah' and 'everything the Prophet informed of is true'. Reflect on how these are examples of 'aqa'id'.
These are not exhaustive; they illustrate the type of principles meant.
3
Grasp the 'idafah bayaniyya' concept
The commentator explains that the construct 'aqa'id al-tawhid' is an explicative genitive, meaning the creeds themselves are tawhid. This shows that tawhid is not just a single belief but a comprehensive system.
This resolves the possible misunderstanding that 'aqa'id' and 'tawhid' are separate.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of the phrase 'عقائد التوحيد' (the creeds of tawhid) found in the author's opening praise. The commentator clarifies the meaning of 'al-'aqa'id' (the creeds) as universal rules and principles (qawa'id wa dawabit kulliyya), giving two examples: 'every perfection is obligatory for Allah' and 'everything the Prophet informed of is true'. He notes that the plural form 'aqa'id' is used because these are multiple universal principles. Then he explains 'al-tawhid' (the oneness of Allah): it is not merely the attribute of being one (unicity), but rather the entire belief that Allah possesses every perfection and is free from every imperfection. Thus, the construct 'aqa'id al-tawhid' is an 'idafah bayaniyya' (explicative genitive), meaning that these 'aqa'id themselves constitute tawhid. Definitions and Pillars of Hamd
Dardir defines hamd linguistically and in Islamic legal custom, noting the five pillars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic and Shari'ah definitions of 'hamd', how they relate, and the five pillars that constitute a complete act of praise.

Core Idea
Hamd is more than just speech—it includes heart and action.

In this passage, Imam Dardir clarifies the meaning of 'hamd' (praise) from two perspectives: its linguistic root and its usage in Islamic law. He then explains how these two definitions relate through the logical relation of general and specific per aspect, and outlines the essential components of a complete act of praise.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once carefully, then read the English translation. Pay attention to the structure: first the linguistic definition, then the Shari'ah definition, then comparison, then pillars.
2
Identify the two definitions
Notice that the linguistic definition specifies the tongue and voluntary good acts. The Shari'ah definition broadens the act to any indication of exaltation and ties it specifically to benefaction.
The Shari'ah definition includes belief and physical service, not just speech.
3
Understand the comparison
The text says there is a general-specific per aspect relationship. The linguistic definition has a specific source (tongue) but general scope (any good act); the Shari'ah definition has a general source (any action) but specific scope (benefaction).
This shows that neither definition fully encompasses the other.
4
Memorize the five pillars
The five pillars are: praiser, praised, that by which praise is given, reason for praise, and formula. Commit them to memory as they are foundational for understanding praise in Islamic theology.
5
Reflect on common misunderstanding
Many think hamd is only verbal. But the Shari'ah definition includes heart and limbs. Consider how this broadens your understanding of worship.

"And hamd linguistically is: praise with beautiful words for a voluntary good act in a manner of exaltation, whether it pertains to virtues or favors. And in the custom of the people of Shari'ah: it is an act that indicates exaltation of the benefactor because of his being a benefactor, even if the praise is not from the one who received the benefit, and whether the act is speech by the tongue, or belief by the heart, or service by the limbs. And in both there is a general and specific relationship per aspect; because the domain of the linguistic is specific, which is the tongue, and its scope is general; and the domain of the customary is general, and its scope is specific, which is benefaction. And the pillars of hamd are five: the praiser, the praised, that by which praise is given, the reason for praise, and the formula."
Source pages p. 30

Commentary
This passage provides a comprehensive definition of 'hamd' (praise) in two contexts: linguistic (lugha) and in the custom of the people of Shari'ah ('urf ahl al-shar'). Linguistically, hamd is praise (thana') with beautiful words for a voluntary (ikhtiyari) good act, done out of exaltation (ta'zim), whether the good act is a virtue (fadila, which benefits oneself) or a favor (fadila, which benefits others). In Shari'ah, hamd is defined as any action (not just speech) that indicates exaltation of the Benefactor (al-mun'im) because of His being a Benefactor, even if the praise is not from the one who directly received the favor. This action can be speech by the tongue, belief in the heart, or physical service by the limbs. The passage then compares the two definitions using the logical relation of 'umum wa khhusus wajhi (general and specific per aspect). The linguistic definition has a specific source (the tongue) but a general scope (any voluntary good act). The customary (Shari'ah) definition has a general source (any action—tongue, heart, limbs) but a specific scope (only beneficial acts). Finally, the passage enumerates the five pillars (arkan) of hamd: the praiser (al-hamid), the praised (al-mahmud), the attribute or thing by which praise is given (al-mahmud bihi), the reason for praise (al-mahmud 'alayhi), and the verbal formula (al-sigha). These pillars are essential for a complete act of praise.

The Meaning of Salawat and Taslim upon the Prophet
Dardir explains the meaning of salawat and taslim and identifies the Prophet.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of 'afḍal al-ṣalāh wa al-taslīm' as used in Islamic praises of the Prophet and how the terms are defined linguistically and theologically.

Core Idea
Salāh upon the Prophet is Allah's favor and veneration, not mere supplication.

The poem begins with a salutation to the Prophet. Dardir unpacks the terms 'ṣalāh' and 'taslīm', showing that ṣalāh is not just prayer but a divine bestowal of honor and exaltation. Let's explore the layers of meaning before moving to the definition of prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the opening lines of the commentary. Notice how Dardir breaks down each word of the poem. Focus on his linguistic definitions.
The phrase 'afḍal al-ṣalāh' is identified as 'the most complete prayer'.
2
Understand the meaning of 'ṣalāh'
Dardir explains that 'ṣalāh' linguistically means supplication for good, but when attributed to Allah, it means an increase of favor accompanied with veneration.
This shows that divine ṣalāh is not a prayer (since Allah does not pray) but an act of honoring.
3
Understand the meaning of 'taslīm'
'Taslīm' is defined as greeting (taḥiyyah). Recognize that the combination 'ṣalāh and taslīm' is a standard formula for sending blessings upon the Prophet.
4
Identify the Prophet mentioned
Dardir specifies that the Prophet referred to is the well-known one, Muhammad ibn Abdullah ibn Abdul-Muttalib. Appreciate how the poem uses the generic term 'al-nabī' to point to him.
This clarification avoids ambiguity.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the third line of the poem, explaining the meaning of the salutation upon the Prophet and providing a detailed definition of the term 'nabī' (prophet) along with its etymological analysis. The author begins by explaining 'afḍal al-ṣalāh' as the most complete prayer, defining ṣalāh linguistically as supplication for good, and when attributed to God, it means an increase of favor accompanied with veneration. 'al-Taslīm' means greeting. Then he identifies 'al-nabī' as the well-known Prophet Muhammad. He then gives a technical definition: a prophet is a free male human who is inspired with a sharia (religious law), whether or not he is commanded to convey it; if commanded to convey, he is also a messenger (rasūl). Thus 'nabī' is more general than 'rasūl'. This is a standard Ash‘arī definition. The author proceeds to discuss the etymology of the word 'nabī'. Three possibilities are given: 1) It is derived from 'al-naba’’ meaning news, with the original form 'nabī’' (with hamza), and it is in the sense of a passive participle (one who is informed) or active participle (one who informs). 2) It is derived from 'al-nubuwwa' meaning elevation, with the original 'nabīw', and it means elevated in rank. The author then explains the adjectives 'al-Muṣṭafā' (the chosen one) and 'al-Karīm' (the generous/noble). For 'Karīm', he mentions two interpretations: an attribute that necessitates giving without return, or the act of giving itself. He prefers the interpretation that 'Karīm' here means 'ṭayyib' (pure/good), because it is more comprehensive in praise—encompassing pure origin, creation, and character. The footnotes add that the tashahhud was specifically mentioned because even those who do not know the Qur'an know it, and that 'karam' is the opposite of 'lu’m' (baseness), so it comprises all good.

Definition and Etymology of Nabi
Dardir defines 'nabī' technically, distinguishes it from 'rasūl', and discusses its etymology and the adjectives al-Muṣṭafā and al-Karīm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the technical definition of a prophet (nabī) and its distinction from a messenger (rasūl), along with the linguistic origins of the word 'nabī' and the meanings of the adjectives al-Muṣṭafā and al-Karīm.

Core Idea
A prophet is more general than a messenger; both receive revelation, but a messenger must convey it.

After the salutation, Dardir provides a precise definition of 'nabī' and its linguistic roots. This clarifies an important theological distinction and enriches our understanding of prophethood. The passage also explains the honorifics 'al-Muṣṭafā' and 'al-Karīm' attached to the Prophet.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of 'nabī'
Dardir defines a prophet as a free male human who receives inspiration (waḥy) with a sharia (religious law). Note the conditions: free, male, human, and recipient of revelation.
This definition excludes angels and jinn from prophethood.
2
Distinguish between nabi and rasul
The text explicitly states: if the prophet is commanded to convey the revelation, he is also a messenger. Therefore, a messenger is a prophet with an additional duty. So nabi is more general.
Memorize: 'al-nabī aʿammu min al-rasūl' (the prophet is more general than the messenger).
3
Explore the three etymologies of 'nabī'
Dardir offers three possibilities: 1) from 'al-naba'' (news) as passive (informed) or active (informer), 2) from 'al-nubuwwa' (elevation) meaning elevated in rank. Understand each derivation.
The different origins enrich the concept of prophethood.
4
Understand 'al-Muṣṭafā' and 'al-Karīm'
'Al-Muṣṭafā' means the chosen one. For 'al-Karīm', Dardir prefers the meaning 'pure/good' (ṭayyib) over 'generous', as it encompasses purity of origin, creation, and character.
This shows the comprehensive praise of the Prophet.

This passage combines definition, classification, and refutation. It begins by introducing the first section of the book: Divine Matters (al-Ilahiyyat). The Swift Path to Allah: Death by Will

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The straight path is to attach your heart to Allah while opposing your soul's desires.

After defining the spiritual states, Dardeer turns to the journey itself. He explains the inner meaning of hastening to Allah, the nature of the straight path, and the radical foundation of this path: death by will. A poem by Umar ibn al-Farid illustrates the struggle with the soul.

Commentary
This passage defines three key spiritual states—fear, sadness, and hope—and then describes the path to Allah. First, fear is defined as worry about future events, sadness as worry about past events, and hope as the heart's attachment to a desired future outcome while taking the necessary means; hope without effort is blameworthy wishful thinking. Next, the author comments on the line 'hasten with swift travel to your Lord,' explaining that travel means attaching one's heart to Allah while opposing the soul's desires, and that this is the straight path. This path is specifically the way of 'ash-shuttar'—the swift ones among the lovers of Allah. Its foundation is 'death by will,' based on the prophetic saying 'die before you die,' meaning voluntary death through renouncing desires. The author then quotes a poem by Umar ibn al-Farid illustrating the struggle with the soul. Finally, he states that this path has ten foundations, though they are not enumerated here.

Du'a as the Marrow of Worship
Imam Dardir explains that du'a is the marrow of worship due to manifesting poverty before Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why du'a is considered the essence of worship, even when it appears unanswered.

Core Idea
Du'a's true worth lies in expressing our need for Allah, not in getting what we ask.

This passage from Imam Dardir's commentary redirects our focus from the results of du'a to its intrinsic value. Many measure du'a by whether it is answered, but the author teaches that du'a itself is the very core of worship because it reveals our complete dependence on Allah. Let's read and unpack this profound teaching.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the English translation. Notice the author's direct claim about du'a being the 'marrow of worship.'
:The Arabic term 'مخ العبادة' literally means 'marrow' — the innermost core.
2
Identify the core reasoning
Find the sentence that explains why du'a is the marrow of worship. What does du'a manifest?
Look for the phrase 'because in it is the manifestation of poverty and neediness.'
3
Reflect on unanswered du'a
The author addresses why some du'as are not answered. Identify the reasons he gives.
He mentions a missing condition, Allah's knowledge that non-response is better, or other reasons.
4
Examine the example du'a
Read the supplication the author provides. Notice what it asks for and the state of heart it describes.
It asks for a good end and emphasizes dying in the best state of tawḥīd with longing for Allah.

Then indeed supplication in its essence is the marrow of worship; because in it is the manifestation of poverty and neediness to Allah the Exalted, and that Allah is the Self-Sufficient, the Powerful over all things, even if response is not obtained. And the lack of response is due to the absence of a condition, or to Allah's knowledge that non-response is better for him, or other than that. Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal explanation of the intrinsic value of du'a. The author states that du'a itself (fī dhātihi) is the very core (mukhkh) of worship, regardless of whether it is answered. The reason is that du'a manifests one's poverty (faqr) and neediness (fāqa) before Allah, while acknowledging that Allah is the Self-Sufficient (al-Ghanī) and Powerful over all things. If du'a is not answered, it is either because a condition has been unfulfilled, or because Allah knows that not answering is better for the person, or for some other reason. The author then provides an example of a proper supplication: to say with humility, 'O Lord, seal our deeds, circumstances, and lives with good,' so that one dies in the best state of tawḥīd, with longing and desire for Allah. This illustrates how du'a combines recognition of divine sovereignty with personal devotion.
