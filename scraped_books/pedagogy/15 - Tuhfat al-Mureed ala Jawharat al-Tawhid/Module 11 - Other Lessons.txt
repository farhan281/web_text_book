Knowledge as a Condition for Hadd Penalties
Ibn Abidin on ignorance as an excuse in hadd penalties

Learning Objective

Ignorance may excuse from hadd, but not if you grew up knowing the prohibition.

He cites the eminent jurist Ibn Abidin to clarify that a person raised in isolation or among ignorant people cannot be punished for fornication if they were unaware of its prohibition. However, this excuse does not apply to those raised in Muslim lands where the prohibition is common knowledge. The passage also references a historical precedent from Caliph Umar.

Ignorance of non-essential historical details is not kufr until after teaching.

This passage from the commentary explains that ignorance of matters not known by reason alone, such as the names of certain prophets, can be excused. It is not considered disbelief unless there is obstinacy after receiving knowledge.

The proof: for he is excused by ignorance, because knowledge of that is not attained by reason nor by reflection and thought. And as an appendix to this discussion: the verifying scholar Ibn 'Abidin said in his Hashiyah (6/4): 'Knowledge of the prohibition is a condition for one who claims ignorance of it and shows signs of that — such as if he was raised alone on a mountain top, or among ignorant people like himself who do not know its prohibition — meaning zina — or who believe it is permissible; for the existence of that is not denied. Commentary
This passage is a legal and theological discussion about the excuse of ignorance. It begins with a proof (al-hujja) that ignorance excuses in matters not known by reason alone, such as particular historical details about prophets (like al-Yasa'). Such ignorance does not constitute kufr unless there is obstinacy after receiving teaching. Then the text turns to a lengthy appendix on the hadd punishment for zina, quoting Ibn 'Abidin's Hashiyah. The key point is that knowledge of the prohibition is a condition for the hadd punishment to be applied. If a person was raised in isolation or among ignorant people who do not know zina is haram, they are excused. However, if they were raised in the Abode of Islam where the prohibition is widespread, or in the Abode of War where it is believed forbidden, then ignorance is not accepted, and they are flogged. The author concludes by reconciling differing opinions and emphasizing that splitting over this issue is not commendable.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the meaning of two categories of angels: the guardian angels (hafazah) and the recording angels (katabah). It begins by quoting two lines of the poem (verses 85-86) that state that every servant has guardians and writers who never neglect. The commentary then explains the phrase 'بكل عبد حافظون وكلوا' (with every servant guardians and they are delegated), noting that the verb 'وكّلوا' (they are delegated) indicates that Allah has commissioned these angels to every servant, including humans, jinn, and angels themselves. There is a discussion about whether jinn and angels have their own guardians. Al-Juzuli hesitated, then asserted that jinn have guardians, but found the idea that angels have guardians improbable, and the author of the text (Sanusi) says he has not found it elsewhere; the apparent view is that angels do not have guardians. Next, the passage examines what the author means by 'hafizun' (guardians): are they protectors from physical harm, or are they recorders of deeds (i.e., the same as the 'katibun')? This depends on the grammatical conjunction ('atf) of the word 'wa-katibun'. If 'wa' is for differentiation (taqhayur), then 'hafizun' means protectors, as in Sanusi's Small Commentary. If it is for explanation (tafsir), then 'hafizun' means recorders, as in his Large Commentary. The preponderant view is that they are different, supported by the Quranic term 'mu'aqqibat' (successive angels) which are separate from the writers, and by al-Qurtubi's statement that guardians never leave the servant, while writers leave at three times: urination/defecation, intercourse, and bathing. Even in those states, the writing of deeds continues because Allah provides the angels with a sign to record. In all other states, the guardians never leave, even if the house contains a bell, dog, or image.
