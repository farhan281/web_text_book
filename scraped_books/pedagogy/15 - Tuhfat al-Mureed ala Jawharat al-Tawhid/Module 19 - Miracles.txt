Defining Miracles: The Qur'an as the Greatest
Author defines miracles and notes their abundance with the Qur'an as the greatest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of a miracle (mu'jiza) and why the Qur'an is considered the greatest miracle of the Prophet.

Core Idea
A miracle is any extraordinary event from a prophet, even without a challenge.

After establishing that Allah supports prophets with miracles, the author now turns to the miracles of Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ). He emphasizes their abundance and highlights the Qur'an as the foremost miracle. In this passage, he also clarifies what exactly constitutes a miracle.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse and translation
Read the Arabic verse (line 73) and its literal translation. Notice that the verse declares the miracles to be many and brilliant, with the Qur'an as the prime example.
The number ٧٣ is the verse number from the poem.
2
Understand the commentary's purpose
The commentary explains the context: earlier the author discussed Allah's support for prophets through miracles. Now he focuses on the abundance and clarity of miracles for our Prophet specifically.
3
Grasp the definition of a miracle
The commentary defines a miracle as extraordinary matters that appear at the Prophet's hand, whether or not accompanied by a challenge (tahaddi). This is the literal meaning of the term.
This clarifies that miracles are not limited to those with a challenge.
4
Reflect on the challenge condition
Consider the common misunderstanding that a miracle requires a challenge. The author explicitly includes events without challenge, expanding the scope of what counts as a miracle.

The Quran as the Greatest Miracle
Author defines the Quran as the greatest miracle and its comprehensiveness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Quran is considered the greatest miracle of the Prophet and how it encompasses all other miracles implicitly.

Core Idea
The Quran is the most enduring miracle; all other miracles are subsumed within it.

The author begins by clarifying that the Quran—the revealed word—is the best and most lasting miracle of Prophet Muhammad. It encompasses all other miracles, even those not explicitly mentioned, through verses affirming Allah's absolute power and the comprehensiveness of the divine book. This lesson establishes the Quran's primacy before listing specific examples.

This links to the subsequent lessons.

Commentary
This passage enumerates several miracles of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) that are not explicitly mentioned in the Quran but are authentically narrated in hadith. The author begins by clarifying that the Quran itself is the greatest miracle, calling it 'the speech of Allah' in the sense of the revealed word, not the eternal attribute. The Quran encompasses all miracles implicitly by Allah's omnipotence and comprehensive decree. Then he gives specific examples: first, the splitting of the moon, narrated by Ibn Mas'ud, where the Quraysh called it magic but others confirmed it. Second, the greeting of trees and stones, from Ali's narration that every mountain and tree greeted the Prophet. Third, the glorification of pebbles in the Prophet's hand, a miracle witnessed by companions where the pebbles glorified in his hand and in the hands of Abu Bakr, Umar, and Uthman but not in the hands of others. Fourth, the moaning of the palm trunk, which is described in detail: the trunk groaned like a child when the Prophet moved to the pulpit; the Prophet gave it a choice to return to its original place or be planted in Paradise; it chose Paradise, and the Prophet ordered it to be buried under the pulpit. The author concludes with the comment of Hasan al-Basri, who wept when narrating this, emphasizing that if a piece of wood yearns for the Prophet, humans should yearn even more. This passage serves to defend the authenticity and significance of these miracles as part of the Prophet's signs.

The Splitting of the Moon
Author narrates the splitting of the moon miracle with details.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the miracle of the moon splitting as narrated in hadith and how it was witnessed and disputed.

Core Idea
The moon split in two as a clear sign, confirmed by distant witnesses.

This lesson examines the well-known miracle of the moon splitting. The author presents the hadith of Ibn Mas'ud, describing how the moon visibly split into two halves—one behind the mountain, one before it—while the Prophet was present. The Quraysh attempted to discredit it as magic, but reports from far-off lands confirmed the event.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith of the moon splitting
Read the Arabic account carefully, noting the description of the split and the Prophet's command to bear witness.
The splitting was witnessed by the companions and later by others afar.
2
Analyze the Quraysh reaction
Notice how the disbelievers called it 'magic' and sought confirmation. When others confirmed, they called it 'continuous magic.'
This shows the persistent denial despite evidence.
3
Recognize the miracle's significance
Reflect that this miracle occurred in the sky, visible to all, and is a major proof of prophethood.
The moon is a celestial body; its splitting is extraordinary.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument for why karamat (saintly wonders) are permissible within Islamic theology.

Core Idea
If no impossibility follows from assuming karamat, then they are possible.

Before examining specific accounts of saintly wonders, the author establishes a foundational rational principle: that anything whose occurrence does not entail a logical contradiction is deemed permissible. This sets the stage for accepting karamat without resorting to blind faith.

This is a standard argument in Sunni theology for establishing possibility.
3

Consider why this argument is necessary: to counter those who claim karamat are impossible because they might be confused with miracles of prophets.

Commentary
This passage provides proofs for both the possibility and the actual occurrence of karamat (wonders of saints). It begins with a general rational argument: since no logical impossibility is entailed by assuming that karamat occur, they are deemed possible (ja'iz). The author then shifts to scriptural and historical evidence for their actual occurrence, citing several examples from the Quran and early Muslim history. First is the miracle of Mary (peace be upon her) having out-of-season fruit brought to her by God, a clear marvel. Second is the story of the Companions of the Cave (Ashab al-Kahf), who slept without food or drink for 309 years. Third is the story of Asif ibn Barkhiya, Solomon's vizier, who brought the throne of the Queen of Sheba in the blink of an eye using the Greatest Name. Fourth are examples from the Companions and Successors, including the incident of Umar ibn al-Khattab calling out to his general Sariyah from a long distance to take refuge at the mountain, and Abdullah al-Shashiq commanding clouds to rain by the power of an oath. These examples demonstrate that God grants extraordinary occurrences to His righteous servants, and the fact that such stories are transmitted in reliable sources confirms that karamat do indeed happen.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think that any extraordinary event must be a miracle of a prophet, and therefore karamat are impossible.
Correction: The rational argument shows that as long as the occurrence does not imply a contradiction (e.g., denying prophethood), karamat are possible. They are distinct from prophetic miracles.

Scriptural and Historical Evidence for Karamat
Enumeration of Quranic and historical proofs for the occurrence of karamat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key examples from the Quran and early Islamic history that demonstrate the actual occurrence of karamat.

Core Idea
The Quran and reliable historical reports confirm that God grants extraordinary wonders to His righteous saints.

The examples range from Mary (peace be upon her) receiving out-of-season fruit, to the Companions of the Cave sleeping for centuries, to the miraculous transport of the throne of Bilqis, to the Companions' experiences. These accounts ground belief in karamat in authoritative sources.
