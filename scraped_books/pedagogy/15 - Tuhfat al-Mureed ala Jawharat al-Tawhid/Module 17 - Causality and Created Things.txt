Refutation of Natural Causality
Refutation of causal theories affirming direct divine action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari refutation of natural causality and the affirmation of Allah as the sole direct agent in all events.

Core Idea
Allah is the only true agent; no creature has independent causal power.

The passage tackles a fundamental debate in Islamic theology: are created things true causes of their effects? The author presents two common causal theories and refutes them, affirming instead that Allah alone creates every movement and effect. This lesson will unpack this occasionalist argument and its implications for divine will.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once, focusing on the two theories presented. Notice the contrast between 'they say' and 'we say'.
The passage uses concise argumentation typical of kalam.
2
Identify the two refuted theories
The first theory is about the finger moving the ring (philosophical causality). The second is about nature as a necessary agent (naturalist causality). Find these two examples.
3
Understand the refutation
For each theory, notice that the author asserts Allah as the sole creator and denies any real influence to the creature. Ask: what does this imply about causality?
This is the core of occasionalism.
4
Connect to divine will
The final sentence says these views oppose divine will (irada). Reflect on why causal power would conflict with Allah's absolute will.
This ties the argument to the broader topic of tawhid.

The commentator argues God alone creates all actions and refutes those who attribute efficacy to causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni doctrine that God alone creates all actions, the refutation of natural causality, and the rulings on those who attribute efficacy to causes.

Core Idea
Only God creates; causes are mere occasions, not creators.

This passage from Tuhfat al-Mureed explains a key branch of tawheed: that God is the sole creator of all actions, including human deeds. The commentator then addresses misconceptions about causality, clarifying which beliefs constitute disbelief and which are merely innovation. We'll explore the 'Unity of Acts' concept and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the poetic verse and its explanation
Start by reading the opening verse and the commentator's explanation of the branching (tafri') from the principle of God's exclusive origination.
The fa' (so) indicates a logical consequence.
2
Understand the concept of 'Unity of Acts'
The commentator introduces 'wahdat al-af'al', the notion that all acts are ultimately attributed to God alone. Grasp how this refutes any real causal power in creatures.
This is a key Ash'ari concept.
3
Examine the classification of beliefs about causality
The passage distinguishes three groups: those who believe in causal efficacy by nature (kufr), by a power God created (innovation), and the Mu'tazila who say humans create their own acts (innovation). Note the different rulings.
Only the first group is unanimously considered disbelievers.
4

Consider why the second and third groups are not declared kafir but rather fasiq mubtadi'. This teaches a nuanced application of takfir.

"So He is Creator of His servant and what he does, and an aid to whomever desires to reach."
His statement: "So He is Creator... etc." This is a branch on what is known from what preceded of His exclusiveness in origination. The fa' is for branching, and it is permissible to be the fa' of perspicuity, because it makes manifest a deleted condition, the تقدير: if the obligation of His exclusiveness in origination is established, then He is Creator... to the end. And "Creator" (khaliq) is a predicate of a deleted subject, the origin is: "So God is Creator... to the end." Commentary
This passage is a proof and classification. It begins with the poetic verse affirming that God is the creator of His servant and his deeds, and that He grants success (tawfiq) to whomever desires to reach Him. The commentator explains that this is a branching (tafri') from the previously established principle that God alone is the originator. The letter fa' (so) indicates this branch, and can also be the fa' of perspicuity (fa' al-fasiha) that makes manifest a deleted condition: 'if the obligation of God's exclusivity in origination is established, then He is Creator...'. The word 'khaliq' (Creator) is the predicate of a deleted subject 'Allah'. This doctrine is called among the gnostics 'Unity of Acts' (wahdat al-af'al), and it shows the falsehood of any claim that something has influence by its own nature or by a power within it. The commentator then classifies four groups regarding causality: (1) Whoever believes that natural causes (fire, knife, eating, etc.) affect their effects (burning, cutting, satiety, quenching) by their own nature and essence is a disbeliever by consensus. (2) Whoever believes they affect by a power that God created in them—there are two opinions on whether this disbelief; the correct view is that he is not a disbeliever but a fasiq (corrupt) and mubtadi' (innovator). (3) The Mu'tazila are similar to this second group—they believe the servant creates his own voluntary actions by a power God placed in him, and the correct opinion is that they are not disbelievers. This lesson presents the correct Sunni doctrine of exclusive divine causality and the rulings on various false views.

The Danger of Asserting Necessary Causal Connection
Warning against belief in necessary connection between causes and effects leading to kufr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why believing in a necessary causal connection between causes and effects, even while affirming God as the true agent, is a dangerous error that can lead to disbelief by denying the possibility of miracles.

Core Idea
Even with God as ultimate cause, asserting necessary links between causes and effects leads to denying miracles and can cause kufr.

The commentator continues his classification of errors regarding causality. Having addressed those who deny God's agency entirely and those who attribute causality to natural forces, he now turns to a more subtle mistake: believing that God is the true cause but that there is a rational necessary connection between everyday causes and their effects. This position may seem moderate, but the commentator warns it is ignorant and can ultimately lead to disbelief.

Commentary
This passage is a warning about a subtle error. It addresses a fourth group: those who affirm that God is the true agent (al-mu'aththir huwa Allah) but still assert a rational necessary connection (talazum 'aqli) between ordinary causes and their effects, meaning that effects cannot be separated from their causes. The commentator judges such a person as ignorant (jahil). Moreover, this belief can lead to kufr (disbelief) because it entails denying the miracles of the prophets, which by definition are events that break the normal causal habits (khilaf al-'ada). Miracles involve fire not burning, sea parting, etc., so positing an inescapable link between cause and effect would make such miracles impossible. Thus, this intermediate position, while less severe than outright naturalism, is still dangerous and can result in rejection of prophethood. The lesson highlights the importance of maintaining that causal relations are not necessary in themselves but are contingent on God's will.

Sanusi distinguishes two erroneous beliefs about causal efficacy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between believing causes act by their own nature and believing they act by a power God created in them, and the rulings on each.

Core Idea
Attributing causal power to nature is kufr; attributing it to God's gift is sinful innovation.

Sanusi begins classifying beliefs about causation by addressing how causal efficacy is attributed. Some attribute it to the nature of the cause itself, while others attribute it to a power God placed in the cause. This lesson unpacks these two positions and their consequences.

Sanusi contrasts rational necessary connection with customary connection between causes and effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between a rational necessary connection and a customary connection between causes and effects, and why only the latter preserves faith in miracles.

Core Idea
Rational necessity denies miracles; customary connection affirms divine freedom and power.

The final two categories in Sanusi's classification focus on the relationship between cause and effect. One view posits a rational necessary bond, the other a habitual or customary one. This lesson explains why only the customary view protects belief in miracles and leads to salvation.

The passage says such a person is 'ignorant' and may be led to kufr because they would deny miracles. Why? Because miracles break the normal pattern.

The one who believes in customary connection but affirms God as the true effecter is the successful believer. Delay or interruption of effects is possible, which allows miracles.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the dispute (ikhtilaf) regarding the superiority (afdaliyya) of acquisition (iktisab) versus trust (tawakkul). The commentator first notes that the disagreement is only about which is more virtuous. He presents the arguments of the group that favored acquisition: it involves direct engagement in means by choice (like buying/selling for profit, taking medicine for health) and it restrains the soul from looking to others, prevents humiliation, and enables one to help others. Then he presents the arguments of the group that favored trust: it means relying on God and disregarding means while being capable of them; it leaves distractions, fosters desire for God, and provides safety from the trials of wealth. He then quotes a narration from al-Quda'i and a statement of Sulayman al-Khawwas to support the virtue of trust. Next, he mentions that in the author's commentary, there is a preference of the rich who give thanks over the patient poor.
