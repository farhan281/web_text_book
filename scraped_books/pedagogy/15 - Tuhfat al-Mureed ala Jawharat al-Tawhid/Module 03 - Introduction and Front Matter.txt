The Legacy of Jawharat al-Tawhid and Its Commentaries
Author praises the poem and its commentaries, especially al-Bajuri's.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the significance of al-Laqani's Jawharat al-Tawhid and the role of al-Bajuri's commentary in the tradition of Sunni creed.

Core Idea
The poem Jawharat al-Tawhid became the foundation of a school in Sunni creed, perfected by al-Bajuri's commentary.

This preface introduces the poem Jawharat al-Tawhid by Imam al-Laqani, which became a central text for teaching Ahl al-Sunnah creed. It highlights the scholarly tradition around it and praises al-Bajuri's commentary as the most refined. As you read, note how the author emphasizes the poem's acceptance and the chain of scholars who contributed to its exposition.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it, then read the translation. This will give you a sense of the author's praise and the scholarly lineage.
Notice the Quranic verse at the start; it sets the tone for the reminder.
2
Identify the key scholars mentioned
List the scholars in the order they appear: al-Laqani, his son 'Abd al-Salam, Ibn 'Abd al-Barr al-Ujhuri, as-Suhaymi, etc. Understand their role in the commentary tradition.
The author emphasizes that al-Bajuri came later and synthesized earlier works.
3
Reflect on why al-Bajuri's commentary is singled out
Consider what made al-Bajuri's commentary special: it distilled the essence of previous works, removed obscurities, and focused strictly on creed.
This explains why al-Bajuri's commentary became the standard.

Commentary
This passage is a preface (بين يدي الكتاب) that introduces the book by praising the foundational poem 'Jawharat at-Tawhid' by al-Laqani. The author begins by citing the Quranic verse about reminding, indicating that scholars have taken up the task of reminding through this work. He then praises al-Laqani's poem, stating that it gained widespread acceptance across the Islamic world, from east to west, Syria, Iraq, Yemen, and Najd. The poem became a cornerstone for a school of thought in presenting the creed of Ahl as-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah. Numerous commentaries and glosses were written on it, starting with the author himself (al-Laqani), then his son 'Abd as-Salam, and then a series of other scholars. Among the important glosses was that of Muhammad al-Amir al-Kabir as-Sanbawi on the commentary of Ibn an-Nazim. However, the author highlights that the commentary of al-Bajuri ash-Shafi'i superseded all previous ones by distilling the best of earlier statements, resolving difficult issues, removing repetitiveness, and focusing strictly on creed (fard kifayah). Al-Bajuri's commentary became the most popular and widespread. The preface concludes with prayers for the scholars who defended the creed and for the brother who provided scholarly annotations, followed by the standard praise of Allah and blessings on the Prophet.

Opening Praises and Testimonies
Al-Bajuri's opening praises and testimonies of faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Bajuri's opening praises and shahadah establish the theological framework for his commentary.

Core Idea
Every commentary on tawhid begins by praising Allah for His unique attributes and testifying to His oneness and His Prophet's mission.

Al-Bajuri opens his commentary with the traditional basmalah and hamdalah, but he carefully chooses words that highlight key theological points: Allah's unique control over existence and nonexistence, His freedom from imperfection, and His role as the imposer of obligations. The two shahadahs then affirm the foundations of faith. This opening is not mere formality—it sets the doctrinal tone for the entire work.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening lines
Start with the basmalah, hamdalah, and the two shahadahs. Notice the specific attributes mentioned: 'المنفرد بالإعدام والإيجاد', 'المنزه عن شوائب النقص والإضداد', 'القديم المكلف الأعلام'.
The term 'المكلف' may seem odd; it likely refers to Allah as the imposer of obligations.
2
Identify the three parts
The opening consists of three clear sections: praise of Allah, shahadah of tawhid, and shahadah of risalah. Each has a specific theological emphasis.
3
Understand the attributes
Why does al-Bajuri say 'المنفرد بالإعدام والإيجاد'? This affirms that only Allah brings things into being and causes nonexistence. Then 'المنزه عن شوائب النقص والإضداد' declares His perfection and incomparability.
This combats any anthropomorphic or imperfect conception of Allah.
4
Reflect on the shahadah
Notice the shahadah includes 'القديم المكلف الأعلام'. The word 'المكلف' is a key term—some might misinterpret it, but it emphasizes Allah as the Legislator.
This guards against the misconception that obligation arises from anything other than Allah's command.

Commentary
This passage is the author's preamble (muqaddimah) to his commentary. It begins with the basmalah and hamdalah, praising Allah for His unique attributes of bringing things into existence (ijad) and nonexistence (i'dam), and declaring Him free from any imperfection. The author then performs the two shahadahs, affirming Allah's oneness and the prophethood of Muhammad. He describes the Prophet as the truthful and trustworthy conveyer of all religious duties. The specific term 'al-mukallaf' (the one who obligates) might refer to Allah as the imposer of duties. After this, al-Bajuri introduces himself humbly as 'afqar al-wara' (the most needy of people) and acknowledges his shortcomings. He explains the motivation for writing this gloss: al-Laqani's poem 'Jawharat al-Tawhid' beautifully summarized the essential matters of theology, but its symbols and secrets needed explanation. Therefore, some brethren requested a commentary. Initially reluctant, al-Bajuri eventually found divine assistance and produced this work, collecting precious insights and arranging them as a hashiah (gloss) on the poem. He named it 'Tuhfat al-Mureed 'ala Jawharat al-Tawhid' (The Gift of the Seeker on the Jewel of Tawhid). Finally, he prays for its acceptance and seeks forgiveness for any errors.

The Purpose and Naming of the Commentary
Al-Bajuri's narrative of the commentary's origin, naming, and prayer for acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Bajuri's motivation for writing this commentary, his initial hesitation, and why he named it Tuhfat al-Mureed.

Core Idea
A commentary born from scholarly need and divine facilitation, named as a gift for seekers of tawhid.

After the opening praises, al-Bajuri introduces himself humbly and explains the circumstances that led to this commentary. He describes how the poem Jawharat al-Tawhid, though already popular and beneficial, contained symbols and secrets that required explanation. A group of brothers requested a gloss, and after initial reluctance, he found himself guided to produce this work, which he named 'Tuhfat al-Mureed' (The Gift of the Seeker).

Guided Path
1
Read al-Bajuri's self-introduction
He introduces himself as 'أفقر الورى إلى ربه الرحيم' (the most needy of people to his Merciful Lord) and acknowledges his shortcomings. This sets a tone of humility.
This humility is a common trope but also reflects sincerity.
2
Understand the value of Jawharat al-Tawhid
Al-Bajuri praises the poem as a tightly strung necklace containing precious gems that astonish minds. He notes that people were eager to engage with its gardens and pick its fruits.
This shows the poem's high status and the need for a commentary.
3
The request for a gloss
A group of brethren asked him to write a gloss that would unveil the symbols and secrets of the poem. They made the request purely for Allah's sake.
The sincerity of the request is emphasized.
4
Al-Bajuri's initial hesitation
He says: 'فلم ينشرح صدري لذلك والله أعلم بما هنالك' (My heart did not expand for that, and Allah knows what is there). This shows his reluctance and reliance on Allah's knowledge.
Hesitation is a sign of humility and caution.
5
Divine assistance and writing process
Then he raised his determination toward the poem and directed his thought into its life and paper, collecting precious insights. He says some of the fruits came from the author and from checking the poem.
The text has some ambiguous words ('واش النظم') but the sense is clear: he gathered material from various sources.
