Al-Laqani's three completed works with a story of his poem's origin.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Al-Laqani's Jawharat al-Tawhid was composed in one night and quickly spread.

This lesson introduces the scholarly legacy of Ibrahim al-Laqani by examining his three completed works. The most famous, Jawharat al-Tawhid, was written in a single night at the request of his spiritual master. Understanding the author's context helps appreciate the text we are studying.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to get an overview of the content. Then read the translation to ensure full comprehension.
Notice the structure: a general statement about his works, then a numbered list of three completed works.
2
Focus on the story of Jawharat al-Tawhid
Pay attention to the anecdote: composed in one night, presented to his teacher, teacher's praise and instruction to downplay himself, and the rapid spread (500 copies in one day).
This story highlights the baraka (blessing) of the work and the teacher-student relationship.
3
Note the status of the other two works
Identify the second work as a large commentary (still in manuscript) and the third as a medium commentary (unfinished/unpublished). Understand that only Jawharat al-Tawhid is widely available today.
This explains why only the poem is studied extensively.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography of al-Laqani's works. It first notes that he authored beneficial writings that people desired to copy and read; he completed many but left others unfinished. Then it lists his three completed works. The first and most famous is 'Jawharat al-Tawhid', a didactic poem on creed composed in one night at the request of his spiritual teacher, Shaykh Ahmad al-Sharnubi. The story highlights the teacher's approval and his instruction to al-Laqani to downplay his own merit (ghamt haqq nafsihi) and not actively promote the work out of scrupulousness (tawaru'). It also reports that five hundred copies were made in a single day when he began teaching it. The second work is 'Umdat al-Mureed', a large commentary on Jawharat al-Tawhid, completed in 1019 AH but still in manuscript. The third is 'Talkhis al-Tajrid', a medium commentary known as Qadi Zadih, completed in 1035 AH but left unedited and unpublished.
