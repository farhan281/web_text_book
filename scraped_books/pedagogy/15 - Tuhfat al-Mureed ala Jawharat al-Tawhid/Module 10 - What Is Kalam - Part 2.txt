Definitions and Nature of the Intellect
The author presents multiple definitions of intellect and discusses its substance and locus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the various definitions of the intellect in Sunni theology and the correct view regarding its locus and nature.

Core Idea
The intellect is an accident, not a substance; its locus is the heart.

This passage from the commentary on Jawharat al-Tawhid presents a rich tapestry of definitions and debates surrounding the intellect (al-'aql). As a foundational concept in Islamic theology, understanding what the intellect is and where it resides is crucial for grasping how humans attain knowledge of God. The author guides us through the consensus of Ahl al-Sunna and the differing opinions of other schools.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get an overview of the different definitions and arguments presented.
2
Identify the Ahl al-Sunna consensus
Notice that the passage begins by stating that Ahl al-Sunna agree the intellect is accidental ('arad). This sets the stage for the subsequent definitions.
The shared foundation is that the intellect is not a substance.
3
Explore the definitions
Work through each definition: knowledge of necessary sciences, instinct, distinguishing quality, spiritual light, and divine subtlety. Understand the nuances.
The author considers the 'spiritual light' definition the best.
4
Examine the substance debate and locus
Contrast the Sunni view with that of the Mu'tazila and philosophers who consider it a substance. Then, analyze the correct view that its locus is the heart, with a light connected to the brain.
The rebuttal against the brain-locus argument shows the importance of proper reasoning.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why these definitions matter for kalam: they determine how we understand human capacity for knowledge and accountability.

Commentary
This passage is a collection of definitions and views on the nature of the intellect. It begins by instructing the reader to examine the interpretations of scholars, noting that Ahl al-Sunna agree that the intellect is accidental ('arad). It then presents several definitions: some say it is a kind of knowledge (al-ilm bi ba'd al-ulum al-daruriyya) – this is the view of Imam al-Haramayn; others say it is an instinct (ghariza) that leads to knowledge of necessities when the bodily instruments are sound; al-Shirazi defines it as a quality that distinguishes good from evil. The passage then gives a footnote explaining that 'when the instruments are sound' excludes cases like sleep where the intellect remains but perception is impaired. The best definition, according to the author, is that the intellect is a spiritual light (nur ruhani) by which the soul perceives necessary and theoretical sciences. Another view holds that it is a divine subtlety (latifa rabbaniyya) that is called intellect, spirit, or soul depending on its function, but all are one essence. The passage then states that the Mu'tazila, Khawarij, and philosophers consider it a substance (jawhar), some defining it as a substance that perceives unseen things via intermediaries (like syllogisms) and sensible things directly. Al-Ghazali says it is an abstract substance. Finally, there is disagreement on its locus (mahall): the correct view is that it is located in the heart with a light connected to the brain, as held by al-Shafi'i, Malik, and the majority of mutakallimun. The philosophers and some jurists place it in the brain because it is affected by brain damage, but the author refutes this by noting that brain health may be a condition for its continuation even if its locus is the heart. A footnote explains that 'unseen things' means intelligibles, and 'intermediaries' refers to syllogisms for assertoric propositions and definitions for conceptual ones.

Additionally, the passage lists other factors that can give a minor sin the ruling of a major one: taking the sin lightly (istikhfāf), neglecting it, rejoicing in it, boasting about it, and the sin being committed by a scholar who is followed as an example. The author's order flows from the special case of lying on the Prophet to the general definition of minor sins, then to the conditions under which a minor sin takes the ruling of a major sin, with a nuanced discussion of persistence.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think 'لهولاء' refers to something else, like individuals rather than categories.
Correction: The commentator specifies that 'لهولاء' means the three sources of fitna (the extremists, the negligent, and the deviant).

Checkpoint
According to the commentary, what does 'فَمَن يَمِل لِهَوْلاء' mean?
A) Whoever inclines to these has found guidance
B) Whoever inclines to these has fallen into error
C) Whoever inclines to these is excused
D) Whoever inclines to these is a hypocrite

Key Takeaway
Inclining to any of the three sources of fitna is a departure from right guidance and uprightness.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Are there any sources of fitna in your life—extreme views, negligence in duties, or exposure to deviant ideologies—that you are subtly inclining towards? How does this lesson call you to repent and re-center on the straight path today?
?
Have you ever justified a small inclination toward a fitna as harmless, thinking it wouldn't affect your guidance? How does the commentator's explanation that 'فَمَن يَمِل لِهَوْلاء قَد غَوَى' (whoever inclines has definitely gone astray) pierce that rationalization?
?
What concrete step will you take this week to guard yourself from inclining towards any of the three sources of fitna, and whom will you hold yourself accountable to for staying on the path of right guidance and uprightness?
