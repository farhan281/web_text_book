The Four Types of Divine Decree and Their Responses
Samarqandi classifies divine decree into four types with Quranic instructions for each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four categories of divine decree and the prescribed response for each, supported by Quranic verses.

Core Idea
Every divine decree has a divinely appointed response: strive in obedience, repent from sin, thank for blessings, persevere through hardship.

Imam Samarqandi teaches that divine decree is not a monolithic concept but consists of four distinct types, each requiring a specific response from the believer. Instead of passive fatalism, the believer is called to active engagement: striving in obedience, repenting from sin, thanking for blessings, and persevering through trials. Let's examine this practical theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
Read the Arabic passage and note how Samarqandi categorizes divine decree into four aspects: obedience, disobedience, blessing, hardship.
Each category is named explicitly.
2
Match each decree with its response
For each decree type, identify the prescribed action and the Quranic proof cited. Notice the pattern: effort for obedience, regret for disobedience, gratitude for blessing, patience for hardship.
3
Reflect on the theological balance
Consider how this teaching avoids both fatalism (doing nothing) and self-reliance (denying Allah's role). The responses are acts of worship that acknowledge Allah's decree while requiring human action.
This is the core lesson.

Know that the decree of Allah Most High is of four aspects: the decree of obedience, the decree of disobedience, the decree of blessing, and the decree of hardship. The correct way in that is: when Allah decrees obedience for a servant, he meets it with effort and sincerity so that Allah honors him with success (tawfiq), by His saying: 'And those who strive in Us, We will surely guide them to Our ways.' And when Allah decrees disobedience for a servant, he meets it with seeking forgiveness (istighfar), repentance (tawba), and regret (nadama) so that Allah provides him with repentance and forgiveness, by His saying: 'Indeed Allah loves those who repent and loves those who purify themselves.' And when Allah decrees blessing for a servant, upon him is gratitude (shukr) and generosity (sakha') so that Allah honors him with increase, by His saying: 'If you are grateful, I will surely increase you.' And when Allah decrees hardship for a servant, he meets it with patience (sabr) and contentment (rida) so that Allah gives him the honor of the Hereafter, by His saying: 'Indeed the patient will be paid their reward without account' and Allah said: 'And Allah loves the patient.' And it is incumbent upon you, when you fall into disobedience, to see the decree of the occurrence as just from Allah and not to be pleased with yourself for falling into it, and to repent and seek forgiveness from it, because the Qadari does not see the decree of the occurrence as just from Allah, and the Jabri does not see blame in himself, and the Munzali[؟] does not see forgiveness without repentance. So if you see the decree of the occurrence as just from Allah, you have disassociated from the way of the Qadariyya; and if you repent and seek forgiveness from Allah, you have disassociated from the way of the Munzala[؟]; and if you see the decree of the occurrence as just from Allah, you have acted upon this verse: 'Say: All is from Allah'; and if you see blame in yourself, you have acted upon this verse: 'Our Lord, we have wronged ourselves, and if You do not forgive us and have mercy upon us, we will surely be among the losers'; and if you repent and seek forgiveness from Allah, you have acted upon this verse: 'Ask forgiveness of your Lord; indeed He is ever Forgiving.' And know that whoever does not believe in the decree (qada) and does not see the determination (taqdir) of good and evil from Allah, he is an innovator (mubtadi'), and this proof is sufficient for the intelligent.

Commentary
This passage is a practical theological instruction on how a believer should understand and respond to divine decree (qada). It begins by classifying divine decree into four types based on what is decreed: obedience, disobedience, blessing, and hardship. For each type, the correct response is prescribed with supporting Quranic verses. For obedience, the response is effort and sincerity. For disobedience, it is seeking forgiveness, repentance, and regret. For blessing, it is gratitude and generosity. For hardship, it is patience and contentment. The author then addresses the specific case of falling into sin. He instructs the believer to see the decree of the occurrence as just (adl) from Allah, not to be pleased with oneself, and to repent. He contrasts this with the positions of three deviant sects: the Qadariyya (who deny that Allah decrees evil), the Jabriyya (who deny human responsibility), and the Munzala (possibly the Murji'a or Mu'tazila, who hold a middle position on forgiveness). By seeing the decree as just, one disassociates from Qadariyya; by repenting, one disassociates from Munzala; and by blaming oneself, one acts on the verse 'Say: All is from Allah' (4:78) and on the verse 'Our Lord, we have wronged ourselves' (7:23), while repentance aligns with 'Ask forgiveness of your Lord' (71:10). The passage concludes by stating that denial of Allah's decree and predetermination of good and evil constitutes innovation, and this argument suffices for the rational.

The Middle Path Between Qadariyya, Jabriyya, and Munzala
Samarqandi contrasts the correct believer's stance with three deviant sects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the errors of three deviant sects regarding divine decree and the correct balanced position taught by the author.

Core Idea
True belief in divine decree requires affirming Allah's justice, human responsibility, and the need for repentance.

After laying out the proper responses to divine decree, Samarqandi addresses three distorted views that emerged in early Islamic history: the Qadariyya (who deny Allah's decree of evil), the Jabriyya (who deny human free will), and the Munzala (who hold a middle position on forgiveness). He shows how the correct believer avoids all three by simultaneously affirming Allah's justice and blaming oneself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sects and their errors
Read the passage where Samarqandi names the Qadari, Jabri, and Munzala. Understand what each one gets wrong: Qadari denies Allah's decree of evil as just, Jabri denies self-blame, Munzala denies forgiveness without repentance.
The name 'Munzala' is uncertain; it may refer to Murji'a or Mu'tazila.
2
Learn how to disassociate from each sect
Samarqandi provides a threefold formula: see the decree as just (avoid Qadari), repent and seek forgiveness (avoid Munzala), and blame yourself (avoid Jabri). Notice how he uses Quranic verses to support each action.
3
Apply the balance
Understand that the correct path is a combination: affirming Allah's decree as just while taking moral responsibility for your actions. This avoids extremes of fatalism and denial of divine power.
This balance is key to Sunni orthodoxy.

The Necessity of Believing in Divine Decree
Samarqandi warns that denial of divine decree is innovation and states the proof is sufficient.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that belief in Allah's comprehensive decree (qada and qadar) is obligatory, and that this teaching provides a rational proof against those who deny it.

Core Idea
Denial of divine decree is innovation, and the arguments presented suffice for the rational mind.

Samarqandi concludes his teaching with a strong warning: whoever does not believe that all good and evil are determined by Allah is an innovator (mubtadi'). He then asserts that the proofs he has provided are sufficient for those who reason. This lesson underscores the fundamental importance of this belief.

Guided Path
1
Understand the gravity of denying decree
Read the final sentence. Note that the author equates denial of taqdir of good and evil with innovation. Reflect on why this is so central.
This shows the seriousness of the belief.
2
Consider the sufficiency of the proof
The author says this argument is enough for the intelligent. Think about the arguments he has presented: the fourfold decree, the balanced response, the refutation of deviant sects, and the Quranic verses. They collectively form a comprehensive case.

Rather, He does what He wills and judges what He desires. He is not asked about what He does, but they are asked. Know and be certain that the fortunate one may become wretched and the wretched one may become fortunate. If it were not so, obedience would not benefit the obedient, disobedience would not harm the disobedient, and the disbelievers would be excused before their Lord for their disbelief. The proof of the correctness of what we say is His statement: {Allah erases what He wills and establishes, and with Him is the Mother of the Book} and His statement: {And Allah judges; there is no reverser of His judgment, and He is swift in reckoning} and His statement: {When He wills something, He only says to it 'Be' and it is.} And the Prophet, peace be upon him, said: 'A man may be as close as a span to Paradise, then evil deeds overtake him and he ends up wretched; and a man may be as close as a span to Hellfire, then good deeds and righteous work overtake him and he ends up fortunate.' And it is narrated from Umar ibn al-Khattab, may Allah be pleased with him, that he used to supplicate: 'O Allah, my Lord, if You have written my name in the register of the wretched, transfer it to the register of the fortunate by Your favor, my Lord.' And it is narrated from Abdullah ibn Mas'ud similar to this. And know that Allah does not waste the reward of the doers of good. And Allah said: {Whoever does righteous deeds, it is for his own soul; whoever does evil, it is against it.} Whoever says 'the pen has dried with what is to be and Allah does what He wills' is an innovator. And the one who says 'the fortunate is fortunate in his mother's womb and the wretched is wretched in his mother's womb' – this is with regard to provision, lifespan, and life, for the provision of some servants is narrow and the provision of some servants is ample, and the life of some servants is shorter and the life of some servants is longer. And he, peace be upon him, said: 'Every child is born upon the fitrah' – meaning the religion – except that his parents make him a Jew, a Christian, or a Zoroastrian. So whoever dies among the children of the disbelievers, Jews, Christians, Zoroastrians, or believers – their destination is Paradise, because the Prophet, peace be upon him, said: 'The pen is lifted from my community concerning mistakes, forgetfulness, and what they are coerced into' – upon the sleeper until he wakes, upon the insane until he recovers, and upon the child until he reaches puberty. So if someone was fortunate in his mother's womb, when he reaches the age of men and does the deeds of the fortunate, Allah makes him fortunate by His grace. If it were not so, obedience would not benefit anyone and disobedience would not harm anyone. And this is the doctrine of the Jabriyya. And in this is sufficient for the intelligent.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument affirming God's absolute will and the possibility of change in a person's final state (fortunate or wretched). Samarqandi begins by stating that God does whatever He wills, has sole judgment, and is not questioned about His actions. He then asserts that the fortunate (sa'id) may become wretched (shaqi) and vice versa; otherwise, obedience and disobedience would be meaningless and disbelievers would be excused. He provides three Quranic proofs: (1) Allah erases and establishes what He wills from the Preserved Tablet (13:39); (2) Allah's judgment is irreversible (13:41); (3) Allah creates by simply saying 'Be' (36:82). He then cites a hadith about a man close to Paradise ending in Hell, and another close to Hell ending in Paradise. The supplications of Umar and Ibn Mas'ud show that the Companions prayed for change in their written destiny. He then refutes two groups: (1) those who say 'the pen has dried' (jaf al-qalam) and (2) those who say that one's fate is fixed in the womb. He explains that statements about provision and lifespan are predestined, but a person's final state depends on their actions. He cites the hadith on fitra and the hadith on lifting the pen from children, concluding that children of disbelievers go to Paradise. He states that if someone were predetermined to be fortunate, when he reaches adulthood and does good deeds, God grants him fortune. If the opposite, then obedience would be futile – but that is the doctrine of the Jabriyya (extreme predestination). The passage thus upholds both divine decree and human responsibility.
