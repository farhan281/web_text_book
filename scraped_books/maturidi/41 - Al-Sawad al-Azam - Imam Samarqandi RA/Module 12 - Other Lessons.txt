Ruling on Wudu with Stagnant Water
Ruling on the permissibility of wudu with stagnant water versus flowing water.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the conditions for valid wudu regarding water types, specifically why stagnant water is impermissible and flowing water permissible.

Core Idea
Stagnant water cannot purify itself, so wudu with it is invalid.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi tradition regarding the types of water acceptable for wudu. Imam Samarqandi explains that small stagnant pools are not permissible due to their inability to flow and thus become impure more easily, while flowing water remains pure unless impurity is visible. The ruling has serious implications for one's prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the water types and their signs
Read the passage and distinguish between stagnant water (small pools that move as one) and flowing water (streams or rivers). Note the sign given for stagnant water.
The sign is a physical test: if moving one side moves the other, it is stagnant.
2
Understand the condition for permissibility
Contrast the rulings: stagnant water is never permissible for wudu (when small), while flowing water is permissible even in small amounts unless impurity is visibly present.
3
Grasp the consequence for dissent
The author declares that anyone who permits wudu with stagnant water is not to be prayed behind, implying their wudu is invalid. Reflect on the seriousness of this ruling.
This underscores the importance of correct fiqh in practice.

المسئلة الثالثة والخمسون - وما قلناه ان الوضوء لا يجوز بالماء القليل الراكد وعلامته اذا حرك جانبه تحرك الجانب الاخر فلا يجوز الوضوء منه وان كان الماء جاريا يجوز الوضوء منه وان قل اذا لم يربه اثر النجاسة ومن قال يجوز الوضوء من الماء الراكد لا تجوز الصلاة خلفه لانه لا يتوضأ ابدا وهذا كفاية للمعاقلى
The fifty-third issue: And what we said that wudu is not permissible with a small amount of stagnant water. Its sign is that if one side is moved, the other side moves – so wudu from it is not permissible. But if the water is flowing, wudu from it is permissible even if it is small, as long as he does not see a trace of impurity in it. And whoever says that wudu from stagnant water is permissible, it is not permissible to pray behind him, because he never performs wudu properly. And this is sufficient for the intelligent.
Source pages p. 32

Commentary
This passage is a legal ruling (mas'alah) on the conditions for valid wudu regarding water types. The author states that wudu is not allowed using a small amount of stagnant water – defined as water so still that moving one side causes the other side to move (i.e., a pool that does not flow). The reason is that such water becomes impure by mere contact with an impure thing, as it cannot purify itself. However, flowing water (e.g., a river or stream) is permissible for wudu even in small quantity, provided no visible trace of impurity is present. The author then declares that anyone who permits wudu from stagnant water is not to be prayed behind, because he never performs a valid wudu (due to using inherently impure water). This reflects the Hanafi view prevalent in the author's tradition. The passage ends with a statement of sufficiency for the intelligent.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: All stagnant water is impure and cannot be used for wudu.
Correction: This ruling applies specifically to small stagnant pools that are not flowing; large quantities of standing water (e.g., a lake) are not necessarily impure.

Key Takeaway

Ruling on wudu breaking from blood and pus
The author states that blood and pus from a wound break wudu and that neglecting to renew it makes one an innovator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that blood and pus breaking wudu is a matter of obligation, and neglecting to renew it is considered innovation.

Core Idea
Failing to renew wudu after blood or pus flows makes you an innovator.

This lesson examines a specific fiqh ruling within an aqeedah context: the obligation to renew wudu when blood or pus exits the body. The author emphasizes that disregarding this duty, whether intentional or not, leads to innovation and invalidates prayer behind such a person.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis. Then read the translation to grasp the author's straightforward ruling.
Notice the author's direct style: he states the rule and then the consequence of ignoring it.
2
Identify the condition that breaks wudu
The author says that if blood, pus, or similar substances flow from a wound, wudu is broken. He then generalizes: anything that exits the interior to the exterior breaks wudu.
This includes intentional and unintentional flow.
3
Understand the consequence of not renewing wudu
The author warns that anyone who does not repeat wudu after such flow is an innovator, and it is not permissible to pray behind them.
The ruling is strict: even unintentional flow requires renewal.
4
Reflect on the aqeedah dimension
Consider why this fiqh issue is included in a creedal text: neglecting an obligatory act of worship can lead to innovation, which affects one's faith and community standing.
Innovation here means inventing a practice contrary to the sunnah.

المسئلة السادسة والخمسون وما قلنا أنه ينبغي له أن يعلم أنه إذا سال الدم والقيح وما أشبه ذلك من جرح فقد انتقض الوضوء ويرى إعادة الوضوء حقا. فاعلم أن كل شيء في باطن الإنسان إذا بين في ظاهره أو سال من الباطن إلى الظاهر فقد انتقض به الوضوء، وكل ظاهر إذا دخل في الباطن يفسد صومه إلا أن يكون ناسيا. فمن احتجم أو سال من بدنه دم أو قيح وما أشبه ذلك متعمدا أو غير متعمد ولم يعد وضوءه فهو مبتدع لا يجوز الصلاة خلفه لأنه يصلي بغير وضوء. وهذا القدر كفاية للعاقل والله أعلم.
The fifty-sixth issue: And what we say: It is necessary for him to know that if blood and pus and what resembles that flow from a wound, then the ablution is broken, and he holds the repetition of ablution as obligatory. So know that everything inside a person when it appears on his exterior or flows from the interior to the exterior, then the ablution is broken by it; and every external thing when it enters the interior spoils his fast, unless he is forgetful. So whoever cups himself or blood or pus or what resembles that flows from his body intentionally or unintentionally and does not repeat his ablution, he is an innovator; it is not permissible to pray behind him because he prays without ablution. And this amount is sufficient for the intelligent, and Allah knows best.
Source pages p. 34

Commentary
This passage is a ruling (mas'ala) on ritual purity (wudu). The author begins by stating that if blood, pus, or similar substances flow from a wound, the wudu is broken and must be renewed. He then generalizes: anything that exits from inside the body (from the interior to the exterior) breaks wudu. Conversely, anything external that enters the body breaks the fast, except in cases of forgetfulness. He then warns that anyone who experiences such a flow—whether intentionally or unintentionally—and does not repeat the wudu is an innovator (mubtadi'), and it is forbidden to pray behind such a person because they are praying without valid ablution. The passage thus establishes a clear fiqh ruling within an aqeedah context, emphasizing that disregarding this obligation leads to innovation.
