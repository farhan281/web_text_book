Affirming Saints' Miracles: The Three Cases
Samarqandi states the ruling and enumerates three logical possibilities for those who deny karamat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why denying the miracles of saints is considered innovation and the three logical possibilities for those who claim to defend prophetic miracles.

Core Idea
Denying saints' miracles does not protect prophethood; it leads to disbelief.

Samarqandi opens this topic by stating that affirming the miracles of saints (karamat) is obligatory; denial makes one an innovator. He then addresses the concern that affirming karamat might undermine the unique miracles of prophets (mu'jizat). By analyzing three possible positions, he shows that only correct understanding avoids both disbelief and error.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening ruling
Understand the initial claim: denying karamat makes one an innovator. The author then examines the motive behind such denial.
2
Identify the three possibilities
Look for the three cases: deny the Quranic verses, accept them but claim those were prophets, or accept them without claiming prophethood. Note the consequence of each.
The first two lead to disbelief; the third is correct.
3
Study the first Quranic example
Samarqandi provides the story of Asaf ibn Barkhiya (Quran 27:40) as evidence that a non-prophet can perform a miracle. Reflect on how this supports the third case.
Asaf was a saint, not a prophet, yet he brought the throne of Bilqis.

Whoever denies miracles of saints thinking thereby he is undermining the miracles of prophets does not escape one of three cases: either he denies the signs that are in the Book of God Almighty; if he denies the signs, he has disbelieved. If he does not deny the signs and believes in them but says they were prophets, he has disbelieved as well. And if he does not deny the signs and believes in them and does not say they were prophets, then it is confirmed for him that this miracle (karama) was for other than prophets, and that is permissible. Because God Almighty said: {The one who had knowledge of the Book said, 'I will bring it to you before your glance returns to you'} (Quran 27:40). And this was Asaf ibn Barkhiya, and he was among the saints and was not a prophet, and he was from the people of Solomon son of David. So when it was permissible to have miracles of saints from the people of Solomon, is it not permissible to have miracles of the Ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him)? And Muhammad is better than Solomon, and his Ummah is better than his Ummah. If the opponent says: 'That miracle was through Solomon,' you say: 'This miracle is through Muhammad (peace be upon him).' And His saying: {And shake towards you the trunk of the palm tree} (Quran 19:25) – God Almighty brought forth from the dry tree fresh dates for Mary, honoring her with that, and Mary was not a prophet. And His saying: {Whenever Zachariah entered upon her in the sanctuary, he found with her provision. He said, 'O Mary, from where is this for you?' She said, 'It is from God'} (Quran 3:37). Similarly in the story of the People of the Cave, God Almighty honored them and they were not prophets. So when it was permissible among the earlier peoples, why should it not be permissible in the Ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him) to have miracles of saints? Commentary
This passage is a definition and refutation with objection-and-answer. Samarqandi begins by stating that one must affirm the miracles (karamat) of saints; denial makes one an innovator. He then analyzes the position of someone who thinks that affirming karamat undermines the prophets' miracles (mu'jizat). He lists three possibilities: 1) Deny the Quranic verses about such miracles (e.g., Asaf, Mary, People of the Cave) – that is disbelief. 2) Accept the verses but claim those individuals were prophets – also disbelief. 3) Accept the verses and not claim prophethood – then it is established that these were karamat, and it is permissible. He then provides examples from the Quran: Asaf ibn Barkhiya bringing the throne of Bilqis (prophet Solomon's associate), the story of Mary and the dates, Zachariah's provision, and the People of the Cave. He argues that if such honors were given to non-prophets in past communities, then in the Ummah of Muhammad, which is the best community, it is even more appropriate. He then answers an objection: if someone says that such a feat (like traveling to Mecca in one night) is impossible, he cites the Isra and Mi'raj of the Prophet, which is even greater. The passage ends with an incomplete argument comparing believers and disbelievers in terms of travel speed.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Affirming karamat of saints reduces the uniqueness of prophetic miracles.
Correction: Samarqandi shows that karamat are different in kind and do not compete with mu'jizat; the Quran itself records such events for non-prophets.

Checkpoint
According to Samarqandi, what is the consequence for someone who accepts the Quranic verses about miracles but claims those individuals were prophets?
A) They are correct, as long as they believe in the verses.
B) They become disbelievers.
C) They are innovators but not disbelievers.
D) They are excused because of ignorance.

Key Takeaway
The correct position on saints' miracles is to affirm them as distinct from prophetic miracles, avoiding both denial and false claims of prophethood.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Have you ever been hesitant to accept the miracles of righteous people because you feared it might lessen your reverence for the prophets? If so, what would it take for you to trust the Quran's testimony over your own caution?
?
When you hear of extraordinary spiritual experiences or miracles among Muslims today, do you immediately dismiss them or seek to explain them away? How does Samarqandi's argument challenge that impulse?
?
This lesson shows that denying karamat can lead to disbelief. Is there any area of your faith where you hold back from accepting what the Quran teaches because you think you are protecting something more important? What would it mean to surrender that fear and submit fully?

Quranic Proofs and Refuting Objections on Saints' Miracles
Samarqandi provides Quranic evidence and refutes an objection using Isra.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Samarqandi uses Quranic examples and the superiority of Muhammad's Ummah to argue for karamat, and how he responds to the objection of impossibility.

Core Idea
The best community deserves even greater miraculous honors than previous ones.

After establishing the principle, Samarqandi provides multiple Quranic proofs: the dates for Mary, provision for Mary, and the People of the Cave. He then argues from the virtue of this Ummah and answers the specific objection that traveling to Mecca in one night is impossible, citing the Isra and Mi'raj. The passage ends with an incomplete comparison between believers and disbelievers.

Guided Path
1
Examine the Quranic examples
Read the stories of Mary (dates, provision) and the People of the Cave. Note that none of them were prophets.
2
Understand the superiority argument
Samarqandi argues that if such honors occurred in earlier communities, they should certainly occur in the best Ummah (Quran 3:110).
3
Analyze the objection and answer
The opponent suggests that traveling long distances in one night is impossible. Samarqandi counters with the Isra and Mi'raj, which is far greater.
The passage ends before the argument about believers vs. disbelievers is complete.
