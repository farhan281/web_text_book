Allah's Will Encompasses Good and Evil
Imam Samarqandi affirms absolute divine will encompassing good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that Allah's will is absolute, covering both good and evil, and that human understanding is not a condition for His actions.

Core Idea
Allah's will is absolute—He does as He wills, whether we understand or not.

The first article in this passage affirms a core tenet of Sunni predestination: Allah's will is unconditioned. He acts as He pleases, and His deeds include both what we perceive as good and evil. This lesson unpacks Article 37 and its implications for divine sovereignty.

Guided Path
1
Read Article 37
Read the Arabic sentence and its translation. Notice the phrasing 'فعل ماشاء ويفعل مايشاء'—past and future tense, indicating continuity.
The phrase 'فهم الحق اولم يفهم' underscores that human comprehension is irrelevant.
2
Understand the scope
The article says Allah does what He wills, and that includes both good and evil. This is a strong predestinarian statement.
This does not mean Allah approves of evil, but that nothing occurs outside His will.
3
Reflect on human understanding
The phrase 'whether they understood the truth or not' emphasizes that divine wisdom may be beyond human grasp.
This counters the idea that we must understand the wisdom behind every event.

The Qur'an: Uncreated Speech, and Faith as Reality
Imam Samarqandi affirms the uncreated Qur'an and the reality of faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the Qur'an is the uncreated speech of Allah in reality, and that faith (iman) is a real attribute, not a metaphor.

Core Idea
The Qur'an is Allah's uncreated speech, and faith is a real, not metaphorical, reality.

Articles 38 and 39 address two foundational concepts: the nature of the Qur'an and the reality of faith. The first affirms that the written Qur'an is the actual, uncreated speech of Allah; the second asserts that faith is a real attribute, not merely a label. This lesson explains the distinction between literal and metaphorical understanding.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic for articles 38 and 39
Notice the phrases 'غير مخلوق بالحقيقة لا بالمجاز' and 'الايمان بالحقيقة لا بالمجاز'. Both emphasize reality over metaphor.
2
Understand 'ghayr makhluq'
This rejects the Mu'tazili view that the Qur'an is created. The phrase 'bil haqiqa la bil majaz' emphasizes that the uncreatedness is literal, not figurative.
This is about the essence of the Qur'an, not just its recitation.
3
Understand faith as reality
Article 39 says faith is 'bil haqiqa'—it is a genuine state, not just a label or outward show. This counters those who say faith is merely verbal profession.
Faith is a real attribute in the heart, confirmed by the tongue.

Justice on Judgment Day, Divine Enablement, and the Two Limbs of Faith
Imam Samarqandi presents three articles on divine justice, divine assistance, and the locus of faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand three interconnected beliefs: how Allah settles grievances on Judgment Day, the relationship between obedience and divine enablement, and that faith resides on the heart and tongue.

Core Idea
Allah settles grievances with good deeds, obedience requires divine enablement, and faith is on heart and tongue.

The final three articles in this passage deal with divine justice, human responsibility, and the definition of faith. Article 40 describes how Allah will satisfy those wronged by believers; Article 41 links obedience to divine enablement (tawfiq) and disobedience to divine abandonment (khidhlan); Article 42 defines faith as active on two limbs: the heart and the tongue. This lesson explains these concepts and their interrelation.

Guided Path
1
Read articles 40-42
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the structure: each begins with 'an ya'lam' (that he know) except article 39 (an yara), but here it returns to 'an ya'lam'.
2
Understand the ruling on grievances
Article 40 says that if a believer has wronged someone and dies without reconciliation, Allah will transfer good deeds from the wrongdoer to the victim on Judgment Day. This shows Allah's perfect justice.
This is based on hadith about qisas.
3
Understand tawfiq and khidhlan
Article 41 states that obedience (ta'a) and divine enablement (tawfiq) are equivalent, and disobedience (ma'siya) and divine abandonment (khidhlan) are equivalent. This means that no good deed occurs without Allah's help, and no sin occurs without His leaving the person.
This emphasizes Allah's control over actions while preserving human responsibility.
4
Define faith on two limbs
Article 42 says faith is on the two limbs: the heart (qalb) and the tongue (lisan). This includes inner conviction and verbal confession, countering extremists who say faith is only in the heart.
Some later theologians add actions; here only heart and tongue are mentioned.

The Middle Path in Qadar
Samarqandi states the obligation of belief in predestination and defines the three positions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the obligation to believe that both good and evil are predestined by Allah, the middle path between denial and fatalism, and why both extremes lead to disbelief.

Core Idea
The middle path in qadar is faith: neither denying Allah's decree nor using it as an excuse for sin.

Samarqandi begins the sixth issue by establishing a foundational truth: the believer must know that the predestination of good and evil is from Allah. He cites the famous hadith of Jibril, where the Prophet includes qadar in the definition of iman. Then he clarifies the logical consequences: nothing occurs without Allah's decree, the servant cannot undo it, yet the decree is not a justification for sin. He warns that both rejecting the decree (Qadari) and relying on it to abandon action (Jabri) lead to disbelief, while the middle path is true faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three positions
The passage presents three stances on qadar: denial (Qadari), reliance (Jabri), and the middle path. Find them and note how each is defined.
The middle path is described as 'what is between these two is iman'.
2
Understand the hadith of Jibril
Contemplate why the Prophet included qadar in the definition of iman. This shows that belief in predestination is not optional but integral to faith.
The hadith is cited in the opening lines.
3
Analyze the principle: nothing without decree, but decree is not a proof
Samarqandi states that everything happens by Allah's decree, but the decree is not a justification ('hujja') for human actions. Why does he insist on this distinction?
This is crucial to avoid Jabrism.
4
Reflect on the middle path
The author says the middle path is 'the firm handhold' and 'the straight path'. Why is this balance essential for faith?
It preserves both divine sovereignty and human accountability.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal exposition on the sixth issue: the correct belief in predestination (qada and qadar). The author begins by stating the obligation for the believer to know that the predestination of good and evil is truly from Allah. He supports this with the famous hadith of Jibril, where the Prophet defined iman and included qadar. Then he gives a fundamental principle: nothing occurs without Allah's decree, and the servant cannot remove it. He warns that relying solely on the decree (as the Jabriyya do) or denying the decree (as the Qadariyya do) both lead to disbelief. The middle path is faith. He then identifies the two extreme sects: the Qadariyya (who claim good and evil originate from themselves, denying Allah's role) and the Jabriyya (who claim all good and evil are from Allah, denying human agency). He calls them the Magians of this ummah. He then presents several hadiths: one defining the true believer as one who attributes actions to himself and predestination to Allah; a hadith Qudsi from Ibn Abbas where Allah says He created good and evil; a hadith about new good deeds erasing old sins; and a hadith to Abu Bakr explaining that if Allah had wanted no disobedience, He would not have created Iblis. The author concludes that reward and punishment are based on actions, not on predestination (citing 2:53/46:19?), and then critiques both sects again: the Jabri sees himself and disbelievers as excused, while the Qadari denies Allah's will. Both are declared disbelievers because the Jabri attributes servitude to Allah (making Allah responsible for sin) and the Qadari attributes lordship to himself.

Tawfiq and Human Capacity: Avoiding Extremes
Samarqandi explains the simultaneity of tawfiq and action and refutes Qadari and Jabri views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the correct Islamic position on divine enabling (tawfiq) and human capacity (istita'a), and why both Qadari and Jabri views are errors.

Core Idea
Tawfiq and action are simultaneous; capacity is also simultaneous—neither before nor after.

This passage from Imam Samarqandi clarifies a central debate about free will and divine action. The extremes of the Qadariyya (human independence) and Jabriyya (absolute compulsion) are both rejected in favor of a balanced position that upholds both divine sovereignty and human responsibility. Understanding the simultaneity of tawfiq and capacity is key to avoiding these errors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two extreme positions
The passage lists the Qadari view (tawfiq before action) and Jabri view (tawfiq after action). Note how each is labeled as an error and what they imply about human and divine roles.

2
Learn the correct relationship of tawfiq
The author states that tawfiq and action are simultaneous (مستويان). Understand that divine enabling accompanies the action, not precedes or follows it.
This simultaneity avoids both pre-creation of tawfiq (Qadari) and post-action tawfiq (Jabri).
3
Review the prophetic evidence
The Prophet said the servant has strength for action but not for tawfiq, which is from Allah. This establishes that tawfiq is a divine gift, not a human acquisition.
The servant is charged with action and given the power to act, but tawfiq is solely from Allah.
4
Understand the doctrine of capacity
The passage also affirms that capacity (istita'a) is simultaneous with action, neither preceding nor following. This refutes both the Qadari and Jabri positions on human ability.

The Obligation of Kasb and Its Limits
Imam Samarqandi states the ruling on kasb and its denial.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that kasb (acquisition) is obligatory at certain times, that denying it is innovation, and that attributing provision to one's own earning is disbelief.

Core Idea
Kasb is obligatory but provision comes only from Allah.

Imam Samarqandi opens this topic by establishing that kasb (acquisition) is not merely permissible but becomes obligatory in specific contexts, drawing on Qur'anic examples. He then warns against two extremes: denying the obligation of kasb (which is innovation) and attributing provision to one's own effort (which is disbelief). This lesson sets the foundational ruling.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the literal translation to get the gist.
The Imam uses a compressed style; take your time.
2
Identify the three rulings
Find the statements about kasb being obligatory at times, leaving it being a concession, and denying it being innovation.
Note the strong warning about those who see provision from kasb.
3
Understand the warning
Reflect on why seeing rizq from kasb is kufr and shirk – it contradicts tawheed in lordship.

And what we said: that he should know that acquisition (kasb) becomes obligatory at some times, because Allah the Exalted gestured to Maryam and said: 'And shake towards you the trunk of the palm tree' (19:25), and He made the day a means of livelihood. And know that abandoning kasb is a concession, and denying kasb is an innovation. So whoever denies it, he is a Karrami? (or: he is disliking?) And whoever sees provision (rizq) from kasb has disbelieved and is called a polytheist. And it is necessary that kasb be under certainty (yaqin) and trust (tawakkul). If kasb is not under certainty, it is disbelief. Because He the Exalted said: 'Allah is the One who created you, then provided for you, then will cause you to die, then will give you life' (30:40). And know that earning does not increase provision nor does it decrease the provision of the one who abandons kasb. And Allah the Exalted does not decrease the provision of the evil-doer because of his evil, nor does He increase the provision of the good-doer because of his goodness. Because Allah the Exalted said: 'And He measured therein its provisions in four days, equal for the seekers' (41:10). And He said: 'But by the Lord of the heaven and the earth, it is the truth' (51:23). And reliable narrators narrated from Ibn Abbas from the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, that he said: 'Whoever does not consider kasb an obligation upon himself in the manner of prayer and fasting, he is an innovator.' It was said to Ibn Abbas: 'Which kasb is best?' He said: 'Carrying stones from the tops of mountains?' And reliable narrators informed us from the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, that he said: 'Seeking lawful kasb is an obligation.' And reliable narrators narrated from Ibn Mas'ud that he said: 'I do not like a man who is empty, neither is he in the work of this world nor in the work of the Hereafter.' And reliable narrators narrated from Umar, may Allah be pleased with him, that he said in his sermon: 'Whoever among you works, we will strive with him; and whoever does not work, we will suspect him. Effort is from the servant, and success is from Allah the Exalted.' And this is sufficient for the intelligent. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage discusses the concept of kasb (acquisition or earning) which is a key Ash'ari term for human action as acquired from Allah's power. The Imam begins by stating that kasb becomes obligatory at certain times, citing the example of Maryam being commanded to shake the palm tree (Quran 19:25) and the general principle that day is for livelihood. Then he clarifies: abandoning kasb is a concession (rukhsa) but denying its obligation is innovation (bid'a). However, there is a danger: one who sees provision (rizq) as coming from his own earning (rather than from Allah) has committed kufr and is called a mushrik. The correct balance is that kasb must be done with certainty (yaqin) and trust (tawakkul) in Allah. If kasb is not accompanied by this certainty, it becomes disbelief. The Imam cites Quranic verses to show that Allah is the sole provider and that human effort does not increase or decrease the provision Allah has decreed. The Prophet (peace be upon him) is reported to say that whoever does not consider kasb an obligation upon himself like prayer and fasting is an innovator. Various companions give practical advice: Ibn Abbas says the best kasb is carrying stones from mountain tops (i.e., hard labor), and the Prophet declared seeking lawful kasb an obligation. Ibn Mas'ud disapproves of a person who is neither engaged in worldly work nor work for the Hereafter. Umar's sermon emphasizes that effort is from the servant, while success (tawfiq) is from Allah. The passage thus establishes the middle path: kasb is a religious duty, but one must attribute the actual provision to Allah alone.

The Necessity of Certainty and Trust in Kasb
Imam Samarqandi on yaqin and tawakkul in kasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that kasb must be accompanied by yaqin (certainty) and tawakkul (trust) in Allah, and that earning does not increase or decrease provision.

Core Idea
Kasb without tawakkul is disbelief.

Having established the obligation of kasb, Imam Samarqandi now turns to the inner dimension: kasb must be performed with certainty that Allah is the sole provider. He warns that kasb without this certainty becomes kufr, and he supports this with Qur'anic verses affirming that Allah created and provides for all.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage focusing on the relationship between kasb, yaqin, and tawakkul.
Notice the conditional statement: 'if kasb is not under yaqin, it is kufr.'
2
Grasp the principle: kasb under yaqin and tawakkul
Understand that the Imam says kasb must be under certainty and trust. Without that, it becomes disbelief.
This is the core of the Ash'ari perspective on human action.
3
Understand the Quranic proofs
Reflect on the verses cited: Allah creates, provides, and has measured provisions. Effort does not change the decree.
The verses show that provision is from Allah alone.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand the statements of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and his companions regarding the obligation and virtue of seeking lawful kasb.

Core Idea
Seeking lawful kasb is an obligation like prayer and fasting.

The Imam now supports the previous rulings with authentic narrations. The Prophet (peace be upon him) declares that whoever does not consider kasb an obligation upon himself like prayer and fasting is an innovator. The companions further elaborate on the best forms of earning and the balance between this world and the Hereafter.

This shows the importance of being active in both spheres.

Consider Umar's sermon

Iblis Was a Believer, Abu Bakr and Umar Were Disbelievers: Refuting Fatalism
Refutation of fatalism using the examples of Iblis and the prophets' parents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author claims that Iblis was a believer while worshipping Allah, and how this refutes the fatalist view that everything is predetermined.

Core Idea
Faith and disbelief are based on one's actual actions, not on eternal decrees.

The author directly challenges the Jabariyya by examining the examples of Iblis and the early Meccan polytheists. He argues that if we take predestination to mean that people are fixed in their states, then the Qur'anic command to fight polytheists becomes meaningless. Through this, he demonstrates that human choices are real and that divine knowledge does not compel actions.

Guided Path
1
Read the initial claim
Read the opening statement: Iblis was a believer when worshipping Allah, and Abu Bakr and Umar were disbelievers when worshipping idols. This is in the sight of Allah, angels, and the Preserved Tablet.
Notice that the author asserts this as a necessary belief.
2
Understand the Ibn Abbas quote
The narration from Ibn Abbas states that caution does not avert destiny, but supplication repels it. This shows that actions like prayer can change outcomes, implying human agency.
3
Analyze the argument from fighting
The author asks: Did Allah command fighting believers or disbelievers? If polytheists were predetermined as believers, fighting them would be wrong. But Allah commanded fighting until they say 'La ilaha illallah', so they were actual disbelievers at that time.
This refutes the idea that their unbelief was fixed from eternity.
4

Adam's Disobedience and the Erasure of Names in the Preserved Tablet
A logical demonstration that divine records change with actions, using Adam and Iblis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author uses the examples of Adam and Iblis to show that divine records can change based on human actions, refuting the idea of fixed predestination.

Core Idea
Human actions cause changes in divine records; predestination does not mean immutability.

The author continues his refutation of fatalism by addressing the case of Adam. If Adam was created obedient, he could not disobey; if created disobedient, he could not have been commanded. Yet the Qur'an says he disobeyed, proving he was obedient before. Similarly, Iblis was a believer until his refusal to prostrate led to his name being erased from the register of believers. These examples demonstrate that the Preserved Tablet records outcomes based on choices, not predetermined static states.

Guided Path
1
Read the questions about Adam
The author asks: Was Adam disobedient before eating, or obedient? If created obedient, he cannot disobey; if created disobedient, he cannot obey. This sets up a logical dilemma.
The point is to show that Adam's state changed.
2
Follow the argument about Iblis's prostration
When Allah commanded the angels to prostrate, was Iblis a believer or disbeliever? If disbeliever, Allah would not command him. But He did command, so Iblis was a believer then.
This shows Iblis was not eternally condemned.
3
Understand the concept of erasure
After Iblis refused, his name was erased from the register of believers and written as a disbeliever. Similarly, Adam's name was erased from the obedient and written as disobedient, then restored after repentance.
This demonstrates that the Preserved Tablet is dynamic.
4
Relate to refutation of Jabariyya
If everything were fixed, there would be no point in repentance or command. The changes in records prove human actions are real and have effects.

Refuting fatalism with supplication and Iblis's example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that supplication can alter divine decree and that a person's spiritual status is not eternally fixed, as shown by the example of Iblis and the command to fight polytheists.

Core Idea
Supplication repels decree; Iblis proves faith can change.

Imam Samarqandi begins by citing Ibn Abbas that caution does not avert decree, but supplication does. He then confronts a common fatalist objection: if everything is predestined, why bother praying? The example of Iblis—once a believer who worshipped God—demonstrates that one's state of faith can change. This passage lays the groundwork for understanding the efficacy of human action within divine decree.

1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Notice the opening quote and the story of Iblis.
2
Identify the two key claims
The passage makes two main points: (1) supplication can repel decree, and (2) Iblis was initially a believer then became a disbeliever. Why does this matter?
3
Understand the refutation of fatalism
The author uses God's command to fight polytheists to argue that their status is not fixed. If they were eternally disbelievers, fighting would be futile.
This is a rational argument against fatalism.
4
Reflect on the definition of true faith
The author defines true faith as actual belief in God, not mere appearance.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained theological argument refuting fatalism and affirming that God's decree is not immutable in a way that negates human action and supplication. The author begins by citing Ibn Abbas that caution does not avert decree but supplication repels it, establishing that human actions (like supplication) have genuine effect. He then addresses a key objection: If faith and disbelief are eternally decreed, then fighting polytheists is futile. He refutes this by arguing that Iblis was initially a believer who worshipped God, but due to his disobedience, his status changed. Similarly, Adam was obedient, then disobedient, then forgiven. The author provides multiple examples from the Quran and early Islamic figures to demonstrate that God has the power to change people's eternal records (محو وإثبات). He concludes with the hadith that a person can be born a disbeliever and die a believer, emphasizing the possibility of change. The argument follows a dialectical pattern: he presents an objection, then refutes it with scriptural and rational proofs. The key point is that God's decree is conditional and subject to change based on human actions, and that the 'Preserved Tablet' contains records that can be altered by God's will.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That the Preserved Tablet is immutable and cannot be changed.
Correction: The passage shows multiple examples of names being erased and rewritten, demonstrating that the Tablet records are subject to change based on actions.

Checkpoint
According to the passage, what happened to Adam's name after he repented?
A) It remained as disobedient.
B) It was erased from obedient and written as disobedient, then after repentance rewritten as obedient.
C) It was permanently written as obedient.
D) It was erased and never rewritten.

Key Takeaway
God erases and establishes names in the Preserved Tablet; actions like obedience and repentance change one's eternal record.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Your present actions are writing your eternal record. If your name were to be rewritten today based on your current state, would it be among the obedient or the disobedient? What one action can you take right now to begin changing that?
?
Adam and Iblis both knew Allah’s command, but one stumbled and rushed back in repentance while the other persisted in arrogance. Is there a sin you have normalized or justified, assuming it doesn’t affect your standing with Allah? What would it mean for you to treat it as seriously as Adam treated his?

The sorcerers of Pharaoh were once disbelievers, but their sincere prostration rewrote their names among the believers. Do you truly believe that no matter how far you have strayed, a single act of genuine submission can erase your past and rewrite your future? If so, why are you holding back from that prostration today?

God's Absolute Power Over the Preserved Tablet
Concluding examples and the principle of divine power over destiny.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that God has absolute power to change anyone's destiny, as shown by the examples of Abu Bakr and Umar, and the hadith that a person can be born disbeliever and die believer.

Core Idea
God can make the disbeliever a believer and the believer a disbeliever.

Imam Samarqandi concludes his argument by citing the examples of Abu Bakr and Umar, who were originally idol worshippers but later became the greatest believers. He also mentions other figures like Bal'am, Qarun, and Tha'laba. He then states the general principle: God erases and establishes what He wills, and He can change a person's state from happiness to wretchedness and vice versa. A final hadith emphasizes that a person's end can be different from their beginning, reinforcing the doctrine of changeability.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the examples of Abu Bakr and Umar.
2
Understand the principle
God's power is absolute: He can change anyone's status.
3
Reflect on the hadith
The hadith 'born a disbeliever, dies a believer' shows that change is possible throughout life.

Ibn Abbas, may God be pleased with him, said: 'Caution does not avert divine decree at all, but supplication repels decree.' Know that Iblis, may God curse him, was a believer as long as he worshipped God Almighty in the sight of God and the angels, because whoever believes in God is truly a believer, and whoever disbelieves and worships an idol is truly a disbeliever. And whoever considers himself truly a believer is likewise truly a believer in God's sight, and whoever considers himself truly a disbeliever is likewise truly a disbeliever in God's sight. Do you not see that God commanded His Prophet, peace be upon him, to fight the polytheists until they say 'There is no god but God, Muhammad is the Messenger of God'? So what do you say, O opponent: Did God Almighty command His Prophet to fight the believers or the disbelievers? Then your statement would be that God commanded fighting the believers, and that is impossible. And if the disbelievers were believers while worshipping idols, then fighting would not be appropriate, and Islam would not be necessary for them, and they would be believers in some aspects and disbelievers in others? God Almighty said: 'O Muhammad, do not fight the believers, but fight the polytheists.' And if the believers are those who were truly believers in eternity and did not change from their state, and nothing else changes them, then if that is the case, what is the benefit of God's command to fight until they say 'There is no god but God'? And what is the benefit of the sickness of Islam? For if the disbeliever is a disbeliever in the Preserved Tablet and never becomes Muslim by your saying, then fighting him is impossible because he was written in the Tablet as a disbeliever. This is the doctrine of those who consider disbelievers and people of major sins excused in their actions, and this is disbelief. And say to the opponent: Was Adam, peace be upon him, disobedient before eating from the tree, or obedient? Or did God create him obedient or disobedient? If he says 'God created him obedient,' then he would not disobey by your saying; and if he says 'God created him disobedient,' then he would not obey by your saying, and there would be no meaning or benefit to this verse, which is His saying: 'And Adam disobeyed his Lord and erred' (Quran 20:121). And say to him: When God commanded the angels to prostrate to Adam, was Iblis at that time a disbeliever or a believer? If he was a disbeliever, then God would not have commanded him to prostrate to Adam by your saying, because God commanded the angels, not the disbeliever, to prostrate; and Iblis, may God curse him, would be excused for leaving the prostration by your saying. And God Almighty said: 'What prevented you from prostrating when I commanded you?' He said: 'I am better than him; You created me from fire and created him from clay' (Quran 7:12). Know that God commanded him to prostrate; if he was a disbeliever, He would not have commanded him to prostrate, since the disbeliever has no work with the angels. So it is clear that Iblis was a believer and used to worship God Almighty. But when he did not prostrate and disbelieved in God, his name was erased from the register of believers and written as a disbeliever. And Adam, peace be upon him, was written in the Tablet as obedient before he ate from the tree. When he ate from the tree and disobeyed, his name was erased from the obedient and written as disobedient. Then when God had mercy on him, accepted his repentance, and forgave him, God wrote his name among the obedient. Similarly, Harut and Marut, and similarly Qabil son of Adam: he was a believer in the Tablet, but when he killed his brother and was not satisfied with God's judgment, his name was erased from the believers and written as a disbeliever. And the sorcerers of Pharaoh: as long as they practiced sorcery, their names were in the Tablet among the sorcerers and disbelievers; when they believed and prostrated, they were written among the believers. And Abu Bakr and Umar: as long as they worshipped the idol, their names were in the Tablet among the disbelievers; when they embraced Islam, their names were written in the Tablet among the believers. Similarly, Bal'am bin Ba'ura, Qarun, and Tha'laba. And God Almighty is able in all circumstances to do what He wills and does what He wills. He erases what He wills and establishes; and with Him is the Mother of the Book. He makes the wretched one happy and the happy one wretched; He makes the disbeliever a believer and the believer a disbeliever. And the Prophet, peace be upon him, said: 'A person is born a disbeliever, lives a disbeliever, and dies a believer.' There are many reports about this, but we have limited ourselves. And in this is sufficient for the intelligent. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained theological argument refuting fatalism and affirming that God's decree is not immutable in a way that negates human action and supplication. The author begins by citing Ibn Abbas that caution does not avert decree but supplication repels it, establishing that human actions (like supplication) have genuine effect. He then addresses a key objection: If faith and disbelief are eternally decreed, then fighting polytheists is futile. He refutes this by arguing that Iblis was initially a believer who worshipped God, but due to his disobedience, his status changed. Similarly, Adam was obedient, then disobedient, then forgiven. The author provides multiple examples from the Quran and early Islamic figures to demonstrate that God has the power to change people's eternal records (محو وإثبات). He concludes with the hadith that a person can be born a disbeliever and die a believer, emphasizing the possibility of change. The argument follows a dialectical pattern: he presents an objection, then refutes it with scriptural and rational proofs. The key point is that God's decree is conditional and subject to change based on human actions, and that the 'Preserved Tablet' contains records that can be altered by God's will.
