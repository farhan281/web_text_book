Colophon of Bahr al-Kalam: Date and Copyist
Colophon of Bahr al-Kalam with completion date and copyist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how to read and interpret a classical Islamic manuscript colophon, including resolving date ambiguities due to scribal errors or OCR corruption.

Core Idea
Colophons are historical anchors for manuscripts.

At the end of many classical works, scribes left a colophon recording the completion date and their own name. This passage concludes Bahr al-Kalam with such a note, but the OCR has introduced errors in the month and year. Let's carefully reconstruct the intended information and learn what colophons can tell us about the transmission of texts.

Guided Path
1
Read the colophon text
Read the Arabic colophon once, noting the formulas of praise and the scribe's self-description. Then read the translation to get the gist.
The scribe calls himself 'the poor, humble one in need of the care of his mighty Lord'.
2
Identify the date elements
Look for the month, day, and year. The text mentions 'شهر ربيع النعير' (likely a scribal error) and 'الثامن والعشرين من شهر جمادى الأولى'. The year is given as 'اثنين وثلاثين وثمانمائة' (832 AH).
The correct month is Jumada al-Ula, not the corrupted 'ربيع النعير'.
3
Understand the copyist
The copyist is Darwish Mustafa. He adds a supplication that he may die loving the Prophet's Companions and enter paradise.
This shows the religious context of manuscript copying.
4
Resolve the textual corruption
Compare the OCR output with the likely original. 'ربيع النعير' is probably a mistake for 'ربيع الأول' or 'جمادى الأولى'. The phrase 'شهس دسته' seems to be a misreading of 'سنة' (year). The corrected date is 28 Jumada al-Ula 832 AH.
OCR errors like these are common in digitized manuscripts.

Ibn Hajar reconciles by suggesting that the 'foot' causes enlargement to fill Hell, and those brought out are replaced by disbelievers.

Critique of a Distorted Edition of Imam Abu Hanifa's Will
Nasafi criticizes a modern editor's distortion of Imam Abu Hanifa's Will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Nasafi exposed a textual distortion in a modern edition of Imam Abu Hanifa's Will and why manuscript evidence is crucial for preserving creedal texts.

Core Idea
A single word change can invert a creedal statement from tanzih to tashbih.

Imam Abu Hanifa's al-Wasiyya is a foundational creedal text. In this lesson, we examine a modern editor's attempt to alter a key phrase about Allah's istiwa', and see how Nasafi used the editor's own manuscript reproductions to refute the distortion. This story underscores the importance of textual integrity in Islamic theology.

I say: The professor Abu Muadh Muhammad ibn Abd al-Hayy Uwayna undertook the edition of the aforementioned Will of Imam Abu Hanifa. But unfortunately, he distorted the saying of the imam (may Allah be pleased with him): 'We affirm that Allah, exalted, is established (istawa) upon the Throne without need and without settlement (istiqrar) upon it' so that in his edition it became: 'We affirm that Allah, exalted, is established upon the Throne without need and settled (istaqarra) upon it.' Abu Muadh claimed that this is the text of the written sources he consulted and relied upon in publishing the 'Will', but Allah refused except to expose him by his own hand. Commentary
This passage is a critical textual note by Nasafi regarding a modern edition of Imam Abu Hanifa's famous theological work known as al-Wasiyya. Nasafi accuses the editor, Abu Muadh Muhammad ibn Abd al-Hayy Uwayna, of tampering with the text. The original wording, as preserved by Nasafi, states: 'We affirm that Allah is established (istawa) upon the Throne without need and without settlement (istiqrar) upon it.' The editor changed 'without settlement' to 'and settled' (wa istaqarra), which entirely reverses the meaning and introduces explicit anthropomorphism. Nasafi points out the contradiction: the editor claimed his edition relied on manuscript originals, but the photographic reproduction of the manuscript he himself included in the book actually shows the original correct wording, condemning his own alteration. This lesson highlights the importance of manuscript verification and the dangers of ideological tampering with creedal texts.
