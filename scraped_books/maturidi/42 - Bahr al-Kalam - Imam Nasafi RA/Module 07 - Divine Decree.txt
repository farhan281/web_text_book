Ibn al-Arabi's Refutation of Taklif Ma La Yutaq
Ibn al-Arabi refutes the claim that verse 4:129 proves the permissibility of obligating the unbearable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ibn al-Arabi refutes the claim that Quran 4:129 allows obligating the unbearable, by distinguishing between outward justice (possible) and inner inclination (not obligated).

Core Idea
What God obligates is always possible; the impossible is never commanded.

Some argue that verse 4:129 proves that God can obligate what is impossible, because He commands justice between wives but says 'you will never be able to be just.' Ibn al-Arabi exposes this as a great illusion. In this passage, he carefully distinguishes between the outer justice God commands and the inner feelings He does not, using the Prophet's own example to settle the matter once and for all.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the full translation. Let the author's argument flow naturally before you dissect it.
The passage includes a Quranic verse and the Prophet's supplication.
2
Identify the opponent's claim and Ibn al-Arabi's response
Locate the opponent's argument (the 'great illusion') and then Ibn al-Arabi's clarification. Notice how he distinguishes outward justice from inner inclination.
The key distinction is between what is commanded (outward justice) and what is impossible (inner inclination).
3
Understand the decisive cutter
Focus on the final sentence: God has informed us that He lifted difficulty from us as a favor. This is the conclusive answer to the problem.
This ties back to the core idea: God does not obligate what is unbearable.

Imam Abu Bakr ibn al-Arabi says in his book Ahkam al-Quran, page 504, in discussing the saying of the Most High: And you will never be able to be just between women, even if you are eager; so do not incline completely [to one] and leave her like one suspended; and if you make peace and fear Allah, then Allah is Forgiving and Merciful [4:129]. Its form: 'The master Abu Bakr said: In this verse there is a proof for the permissibility of obligating what cannot be borne, because Allah the Glorified and Exalted obligated men to be just between women and informed that they cannot do it. But this is a great illusion, for what He obligated them of that is the outward justice which He indicated by His saying: That is more likely that you will not be unjust [4:3] and this is something possible. And that which He informed them they cannot do, He never obligated them with it at all, and it is the inclination of the soul. For this reason the Prophet used to be just between his wives in division, yet he found himself more inclined to Aisha in love and said: O Allah, this is my power in what I possess, so do not ask me about what You possess and I do not possess (meaning his heart). And the decisive cutter of this problem is that Allah, glorified and exalted, has informed that He has lifted from us the difficulty in obligating what we cannot do as a favor, although He has the right to oblige us with it as a right and as creation.' End.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a claim made by some that the Quranic verse 4:129 provides evidence for the permissibility of obligating what cannot be borne (taklif ma la yutaq). Ibn al-Arabi first states the opposing argument: God obligated justice between wives and then said 'you will not be able to be just', implying that He obligated something impossible. Ibn al-Arabi calls this a great illusion. He clarifies that what God obligated is outward justice (al-ʿadl fī al-ẓāhir), which is possible, as indicated in verse 4:3. What God informed that they cannot achieve is the inner inclination of the soul (al-nisba fī mayl al-nafs), which was never obligated. He supports this with the Prophet's example: the Prophet was just in his division but admitted his heart's inclination towards Aisha, and he prayed acknowledging that the heart is beyond his control. Ibn al-Arabi concludes with the decisive point: God has informed that He lifted from us the burden of obligating what we cannot do, as a favor; nevertheless, He has the right to oblige us with it if He willed. This refutation defends the Ash'ari/Maturidi position that God does not obligate the impossible, against the opposite claim.

Ibn al-Arabi's Refutation of Taklif Ma La Yutaq
Ibn al-Arabi refutes the claim that a Quranic verse supports taklif ma la yutaq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ibn al-Arabi refutes the claim that Quran 4:129 supports imposing obligations beyond one's capacity (taklif ma la yutaq) by distinguishing between outward justice and inner inclination.

Core Idea
What seems impossible may not be commanded; the command is for what is possible.

In this passage, Ibn al-Arabi addresses a theological controversy: does the Quranic verse about justice among wives prove that God can command the impossible? He shows that a careful reading reveals the command is for outward justice, which is attainable, while the impossibility refers to inner feelings that were never commanded. This lesson breaks down his exegetical argument.

Refuting the Partnership Objection in Faith
Nasafi refutes the objection that faith would be shared between God and servant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Nasafi refutes the objection that faith being from both God and servant implies impermissible partnership, by distinguishing between cause and effect.

Core Idea
Faith is not shared; God provides the cause, the servant produces the effect.

A common theological objection argues that if faith involves both divine and human action, it would be a shared act between God and the servant, which is impermissible. In this passage, Imam Nasafi clarifies that God's role is causal, not participatory. He uses analogies from provision and ablution to show that the cause is distinct from the effect. This lesson will walk through the objection and its refutation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection carefully: if faith were partly from God and partly from the servant, it would be shared between the Lord and the servant, which is not permissible. Identify the underlying assumption.
The objector assumes any divine involvement creates partnership.
2
Analyze the cause-effect distinction
Nasafi's answer distinguishes between the cause (sabab) from God and the effect (musabbab) from the servant. The servant is the caused (musabbab), God is the causer (musabbib). The caused is not the causer.
This distinction is central to the refutation.
3
Examine the analogies
Nasafi provides two analogies: provision (rizq) is a cause for survival, not part of survival; ablution (wudu) is a cause for prayer's permissibility, not part of prayer. Understand how these clarify the cause-effect relationship.
These analogies show that a cause is not identical to its effect.
4
Apply to the case of faith
The definition (ta'rif) from God is a cause for salvation, not a part of faith. The light of knowledge in the heart is created, as everything other than God is created. Thus, the objection fails.
Faith remains the servant's act, caused by divine guidance.

Just as provision is a cause for the servant's survival, and similarly, ablution is a cause for the permissibility of prayer, and it is not said that it is part of the prayer, so likewise the definition from God Most High is a cause of salvation, and it is the giving of light in the believer's heart, so it is not shared. And the light of knowledge in the believer's heart is created, because whatever is other than God Most High is created.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument. The objector claims that if faith is partly from God and partly from the servant, then faith would be a composite act shared between the divine and human, which is impermissible because it would imply partnership in an act of worship. The answer distinguishes between the cause (sabab) and the effect. God's role is to provide the definition (ta'rif) which is a cause for salvation, not the act of faith itself. The servant's response is the caused effect. The analogy of rizq and wudu' clarifies: God provides provision as a cause for survival, but the survival is not attributed to God as an act. Similarly, wudu' is a cause for prayer's permissibility, but it is not part of prayer. Thus, the definition from God is a cause, not a part of faith. The light of knowledge in the heart is created, as everything other than God is created. Therefore, the objection fails because there is no direct partnership; God's action is causal, not participatory in the servant's act.

Refutation of Mu'tazila on Qadar and Human Action
Nasafi refutes the Mu'tazila claim that evil is from the servant alone.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni response to the Mu'tazila claim that evil is from the servant alone, and how divine decree (qada) does not compel human action.

Core Idea
Divine decree does not compel; it merely knows – the servant acts by choice.

The Mu'tazila argued that if Allah decreed evil and then punished for it, that would be unjust. They therefore attributed all evil to the servant. Imam Nasafi responds by affirming that the servant has choice and ability, and that the decree is simply an attribute of Allah – like knowledge – that does not force the action. This lesson unpacks the argument and the key analogies he uses.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila objection
Start by reading the first part where the Mu'tazila present their reasoning: All evil is from the servant because Allah does not decree evil, otherwise He would be unjust.
Notice how they call themselves 'people of justice and tawhid'.
2
Identify the Sunni response
Read the 'but we say' section. Identify the main points: the servant is given choice, the decree does not compel sin, and the decree is like knowledge.
3
Analyze the analogies
Look at the two analogies: the tailor and the master's oath. Understand how each illustrates that the decree (or condition) does not force the action.
The oath analogy shows that the condition (entering the house) triggers the consequence but does not compel the entry.
4
Connect to human responsibility
Reflect on why this matters: If the decree compelled, punishment would be unjust. But since the servant acts by choice, he deserves reward or punishment.
This preserves both divine sovereignty and human accountability.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer argument. It first presents the Mu'tazila objection: they argue that all evil comes from the servant, not from Allah, because if Allah decreed evil and then punished for it, that would be unjust. They thus call themselves 'people of justice and tawhid'. The Sunni response (answered by 'we say') has multiple points: (1) The servant has choice and ability (mukhayyar mustati'). Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer section. The objection presents a classic problem: If all human actions are ultimately caused by Allah's capacity, decree, and will, then how can the slave deserve punishment or reward? The answer is developed in two parts. When the servant exerts effort, intention, and acquisition, strength is granted to him concurrently by Allah. Thus, the servant deserves reward or punishment based on his own action. The author then applies this same framework to various aspects of the religious life: faith, guidance, sin, repentance, blessing, and gratitude. In each case, there is a divine grant and a human response. The servant's intention and resolve in sin attract Allah's abandonment (khidhlān), while intention and resolve in obedience attract Allah's success (tawfīq). Therefore, reward and punishment are based solely on the servant's own effort, intention, and acquisition, which are his own acts and attributes. Anyone who denies this is considered astray and an innovator. The author then provides a second, complementary answer: the servant deserves punishment simply for abandoning what is commanded and what is prohibited, and these matters are evident. Finally, the author defines istitā'a (capability) as the true power that is the basis of accountability, citing Quran 11:20. This capability, along with sound causes and instruments, is what makes actions count, and it is the subject of discussion among the scholars of this field.

Commentary
This passage is a series of objections and answers that refute apparent proofs of human ability. The author addresses four texts that some might use to argue that humans have power over actions. For each, he provides a reinterpretation that shows the text actually establishes human incapacity. First objection: Quran 2:31 where God asks angels to inform Him of names. The author says this was to demonstrate the angels' inability to know what Adam knew, since they were not punished for not answering. Second objection: Quran 68:42 about being called to prostrate on the Day of Resurrection and being unable. The author says this refers to prostration in this world; they are punished for abandoning it in the Hereafter, not for inability in the Hereafter. Third objection: Quran 2:286 asking not to be burdened beyond capacity. The author cites exegeses that the 'burden' refers to specific calamities like being turned into apes or swallowed by the earth, which were lifted from this community, not that humans lack capacity for obligations. Fourth objection: a hadith about image-makers being commanded to blow spirit into their creations on Judgment Day. The author says this also establishes their incapacity (since they cannot give life), and the command is a punishment, not a duty they are able to perform. Throughout, the author maintains that these texts, properly understood, support the doctrine of human incapacity.

Believers' sins burn away like dross from gold, leading to salvation, not eternal fire.

Imam al-Nasafi here refutes a deviant claim that extreme love exempts a believer from any punishment. He establishes that all sinful believers are under God's absolute will, and explains how punishment for believers is purifying, not eternal. Commentary
This passage presents another objection-and-answer. Imam al-Nasafi refutes this with a nuanced theological response. He cites the Qur'anic verse (al-Fath 48:14) that declares God's sovereignty in forgiving or punishing whom He wills. The hadith about entering Paradise only after becoming like a pure garment supports this. He ends with a contrast: the Fire removes impurity from the believer, but Paradise does not remove purity from its inhabitants, so they remain forever. This refutes the claim that love prevents all punishment, affirming instead that punishment may occur but is temporary and purifying for believers.
