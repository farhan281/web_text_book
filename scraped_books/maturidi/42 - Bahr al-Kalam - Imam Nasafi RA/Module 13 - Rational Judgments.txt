The First Obligation: Knowledge of God
Defines the first obligation and presents three views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why knowledge of God (ma'rifa) is considered the first obligation on every morally responsible person (mukallaf), and the debate over whether it is the knowledge itself, inquiry (nazar), or intention (qasd) that is primary.

Core Idea
The first duty of every Muslim is to know God—but how it is defined shapes the entire approach to faith.

This passage marks the beginning of the actual theological discussion in the book. The author clarifies that the term 'ma'rifa' is used synonymously with 'ilm for this purpose. He then explains why the author of the primary text started with belief in knowledge of God: because it is the very first obligation on every responsible person. However, scholars have differed on whether the obligation is the knowledge itself, the act of inquiry that leads to knowledge, or even the intention to inquire. Let's explore this foundational debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the author's opening statement
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Notice how the author begins with an explanation of the primary text.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Understand the clarification of 'ma'rifa'
The author says that 'ma'rifa' here means 'ilm (knowledge). This is important because some might think they are different. See how he explains the usage of the ancients.
3
Identify the three views on the first obligation
The passage mentions three opinions: some say the first obligation is knowledge of God itself, others say it is inquiry (nazar), and others say it is the intention (qasd) to inquire. Note that the author says the dispute is verbal.
Reflect on why these distinctions might matter.

Commentary
This passage begins the actual discourse of the book. The author quotes a statement: 'Know that I believe in knowledge of God and tawhid.' Then he comments that it would be better to phrase it differently, explaining that the term 'ma'rifa' here means 'ilm as used by the ancients. He explains that 'اعلموا' is an address to all, and that 'i'tiqad' (belief) is affirmation. Then he discusses why the author started with knowledge of God: because the first obligation on the mukallaf is knowledge of God. However, there is a dispute: some say the first obligation is nazar (inquiry) that leads to knowledge, others say it is the intention to inquire. The disagreement is claimed to be merely verbal. He clarifies that by 'first obligation' is meant that which is intended essentially; there is no doubt that intention, if taken as the determining cause, is prior, and then nazar leads to knowledge. Then he mentions that some say it is wajib sam'an (based on revelation), which is the school of al-Ash'ari, while others say it is 'aqli (rational), which is the school of our companions (the Hanafi/Matridi?) and the Mu'tazila. The evidence for those who say sam'an includes the verse: 'And we would not punish until we send a messenger' (19:15) and 'Lest people have an argument against God after the messengers' (4:165).

Is the Obligation Based on Revelation or Reason?
Presents the dispute over the basis of the obligation: revelation vs reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between those who say the obligation to know God is based on revelation (sam'an) and those who say it is based on reason ('aqli), and the Quranic evidence used by the former.

Core Idea
Is knowing God a command from revelation or a demand of reason? This debate shapes how we understand religious obligation.

After establishing that the first obligation is knowledge of God, the author turns to a key question: Is this obligation derived from revelation (sam'an) or from reason ('aqli)? The Ash'ari school holds that it is from revelation, while the Maturidi and Mu'tazili schools hold it is rational. The passage presents the evidence from the Quran for the first view, citing verses that delay punishment until after messengers are sent. Let's examine this crucial disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the two positions
Read the Arabic and translation. Identify the two schools: Ash'ari (sam'an) and our companions/Mu'tazila ('aqli).
The passage is short but dense.
2
Understand the evidence for the sam'an view
Those who say the obligation is from revelation cite two verses: 'And we would not punish until we send a messenger' (17:15) and 'Lest people have an argument against God after the messengers' (4:165). Reflect on how these verses support their position.
3
Reflect on the implications
Consider why this distinction matters. If the obligation is from revelation, then prior to revelation there is no duty; if it is rational, then reason itself imposes the duty.
This connects to broader debates in usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Superiority of Knowledge and Variation in Intellects
Statement on knowledge's superiority and variety of intellects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why knowledge is superior to intellect and how intellects vary among prophets and saints, contrary to the Mu'tazila claim of equality.

Core Idea
Knowledge outranks intellect, and not all intellects are equal.

Imam Nasafi begins by establishing a key theological principle: knowledge (ʿilm) is superior to intellect (ʿaql). He then addresses a related issue—the variation in intellectual capacity among humans, especially prophets and saints. This directly contradicts the Mu'tazila who asserted that all people are equal in intellect. The passage sets the stage for understanding the unique rank of prophethood and the value of revealed knowledge.

Universal Obligation of Rational Inference
Obligation of rational inference for all mature sane persons.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that every sane adult is obliged to use reason to infer the existence of a Creator, regardless of whether they have received revelation.

Core Idea
No one is excused from the duty to rationally infer God's existence.

Having affirmed the value of knowledge, Imam Nasafi now turns to a practical imperative: every mature, sane person must use their intellect to deduce that the universe has a Creator. He provides Qur'anic examples—Abraham and the People of the Cave—as models of this rational inference. Then he makes a strong claim: even those untouched by revelation are not excused. This position distinguishes the Maturidi school from other theological groups.

Nasafi states the superiority of knowledge over intellect and the inequality of intellects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between knowledge and intellect, the hierarchy of intellects among prophets and saints, and why this contradicts the Mu'tazilite position of equal intellects.

Core Idea
Knowledge ranks above intellect, and intellects are not equal — prophets and saints differ.

This short passage from Nasafi makes three important claims that shape the Sunni understanding of knowledge and intellect. First, it establishes that knowledge (ʿilm) is superior to intellect (ʿaql). Mu'tazila's Rational Proof Against Vision of Allah
Mu'tazila's rational proof against vision of Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila's rational argument that affirming the vision of Allah would imply direction, which is impossible.

Core Idea
Vision of Allah would require direction, but Allah transcends direction.

The Mu'tazila used a concise rational argument to deny the possibility of seeing Allah. They argued that vision necessarily involves a direction between the seer and the seen, and since Allah is beyond direction, vision cannot occur. This short passage presents their core reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
The Mu'tazila begin with a conditional: if we say Allah can be seen, then it leads to establishing direction.
2
Identify the contradiction
Direction is negated from Allah by consensus; thus the consequence is impossible. Therefore the antecedent (vision being possible) is also impossible.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Reflect on the argument's strength
Consider why the Mu'tazila think vision requires direction. Does their argument hold? What do Ahl al-Sunna say?
This lesson only presents the Mu'tazila side.

Commentary
This passage states the rational proof of the Mu'tazila against the vision of Allah. They argue that vision (ru'ya) necessarily requires the existence of direction (jiha) between the seer and the seen. Since Allah is transcendent and has no direction, direction is negated from Him. Therefore, if we affirm that Allah can be seen, we would have to affirm direction, which is impossible. Thus, vision is impossible. This is a concise logical argument based on the incompatibility of vision with divine transcendence (tanzīh).
