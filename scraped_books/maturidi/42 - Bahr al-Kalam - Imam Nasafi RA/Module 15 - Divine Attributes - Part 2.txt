The Benefit of Abrogation as Mercy
Imam al-Nasafi explains that the benefit of abrogation is mercy and ease for believers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that abrogation (naskh) is an expression of divine mercy, not divine indecision or caprice, and that both the abrogating and abrogated rulings hold value.

Core Idea
Abrogation is mercy, not contradiction: God lightens burdens for believers.

Imam al-Nasafi responds to the question: What is the point of abrogation? Is it not arbitrary? He clarifies that far from being arbitrary, abrogation is a manifestation of divine affection and mercy, lightening the obligations upon His servants. The passage provides a concrete example from the Quranic ruling on fighting to show how God explicitly calls it a 'lightening.'

Guided Path
1
Read the question and answer
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the overall argument.
2
Identify the three benefits listed
Notice that the author lists three benefits: affection/compassion, lightening, and mercy. These are not three separate things but facets of divine care.
3
Examine the Quranic example
See how the example of the fighting obligation illustrates the concept: the initial heavy burden was lightened, and God called it a lightening.
4
Distinguish the present status
Understand that the abrogating ruling is acted upon now, while the abrogated ruling is believed in as having been a previous command. Both have a place.
This counters the misconception that abrogation makes earlier rulings worthless.

فإن قيل: أيش الفائدة في النسخ؟ قلنا: الفائدة في النسخ التحنن [أو الشفقة] والتخفيف والرحمة على عباده، كما أمر الله تعالى المسلمين في الابتداء بأن يقاتل منهم كل واحد العشرة من الكفرة والفجرة بقوله تعالى: ﴿ وَإِنْ يَكُنْ مِنْكُمْ عِشْرُونَ صَابِرُونَ يَغْلِبُوا مِائَتَيْنِ ﴾ [الأنفال: 65] ثم خفف بعد ذلك وأسقط عن كل عشرة ثمانية بقوله تعالى: ﴿ الْآنَ خَفَّفَ اللَّهُ عَنكُمْ وَعَلِمَ أَنَّ فِيكُمْ ضَعْفًا ﴾ [الأنفال: 66] سماه تخفيفًا، كذلك ههنا الناسخ أنفع في الحال؛ لأنه يوجب العمل به في الحال والإيمان به واجب والمنسوخ لا يجب العمل به في الحال، ولكن يجب الإيمان به.
For if it is said: What is the benefit in abrogation? We say: The benefit in abrogation is affection [or compassion], and lightening, and mercy upon His servants. Just as Allah the Exalted commanded the Muslims in the beginning that each one of them should fight ten of the disbelievers and transgressors by His saying: 'And if there are among you twenty steadfast, they will overcome two hundred' [Al-Anfal: 65]. Then He lightened after that and removed from every ten, eight, by His saying: 'Now Allah has lightened your burden and knows that among you is weakness' [Al-Anfal: 66]. He called it a lightening. Similarly here: The abrogating ruling is more beneficial in the present moment because it necessitates acting upon it in the present, and believing in it is obligatory. The abrogated ruling need not be acted upon in the present, but believing in it is obligatory.

Commentary
This passage is a question-and-answer about the benefit (fā'idah) of abrogation (naskh). The question asks: What is the point of abrogation? The author responds by stating that the benefit is divine affection, compassion, lightening, and mercy upon God's servants. He illustrates with the example of the obligation to fight: initially, each believer was required to fight ten disbelievers (based on Al-Anfal: 65). Later, God lightened this obligation to one against one (Al-Anfal: 66), explicitly calling it a 'lightening' (takhfīf). The author then explains that the abrogating (nāsikh) ruling is more beneficial in the present because it must be acted upon, while the abrogated (mansūkh) ruling is no longer to be acted upon but still must be believed in as having been revealed for its time. The key point is that abrogation is not arbitrary but a manifestation of divine mercy, easing burdens for believers.

The Fate of the Sinful Believer: Purification Through Punishment
Nasafi explains the fate of sinful believers: punishment, purification, and salvation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic doctrine regarding the fate of sinful believers: they are subject to Allah's will, may be temporarily punished to purify sins, then saved by mercy or intercession, unlike disbelievers who remain eternally in Hell.

Core Idea
Sinful believers are purified like gold in fire, not eternally damned like disbelievers.

In this passage, Nasafi addresses the crucial question of what happens to believers who commit sins. He explains that their fate rests in Allah's will, and if punished, it is temporary purification leading to eventual salvation. This lesson unpacks the key analogies and scriptural proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
Recognize that this passage is an answer to an objection about the fate of sinful believers. Nasafi begins by affirming that all sinful believers, whether saints or not, are under Allah's will.
The objection likely claimed that sinful believers would be punished forever; Nasafi refutes this.
2
Identify the three outcomes
Notice the three categories: 1) the believer forgiven by grace, 2) the believer punished then saved by mercy or intercession, 3) the disbeliever eternally in Hell. Contrast these with the righteous who enter Paradise purified.
3
Analyze the analogies and hadith
Study the gold analogy: like gold purified by fire, the believer's sins are burned away. Contrast with the disbeliever as firewood. Reflect on the hadith about Paradise not being easily attained.
The gold analogy emphasizes temporary purification, while firewood shows eternal purpose.

والجواب عنه قلنا : إذا أذنب العبد ذنبا - وليَّاً كان أو غير ولي ـ فهو في مشيئة الله تعالى، إن شاء غفر له بفضله، وإن شاء عذبه ،بعدله، قال الله تعالى: ﴿يَغْفِرُ لِمَن يَشَاءُ وَيُعَذِّبُ مَن يَشَاءُ) [الفتح: ١٤] وإذا عذَّبه بقدر ذنوبه [ ثم يخرجه من النار برحمته أو بشفاعة الأنبياء، كالذهب يدخل النار ليزول عنه غشّه ، فإذا زال عنه يخرج منها ولا يترك فيها، بخلاف الكافر لأنه كالحطب أعد لإيقاد النار والإحراق فيها لا لمعنى آخر، بخلاف أهل الجنة لأنه لا يدخل في الجنة إلا الطَّاهر من الوسخ والحوبة إما برعاية النفس أو بالتوبة. ألا يرى أن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم قال: «أتحسبون أن الجنة مرابض الغنم والله لن يدخلوها حتى يصيروا كالبردة». والنار تحرق نجاسة الذُّنوب وتزيلها عن المؤمن العاصي فيخرج منها بعد زوالها، بخلاف الجنة لأنها لا تزيل طهارة الداخل ليخرج منها ويدخلها ثانية برحمة الله تعالى وبشفاعة الأنبياء عليهم السلام].
And the answer to it we say: When the servant commits a sin — whether a saint or not — he is under the will of Allah Almighty: if He wills, He forgives him by His grace, and if He wills, He punishes him by His justice. Allah Almighty said: {He forgives whom He wills and punishes whom He wills} [Al-Fath: 14]. And if He punishes him according to his sins, then He brings him out of the Fire by His mercy or by the intercession of the prophets, like gold that enters the fire to remove its impurity, and when its impurity is removed, it comes out and is not left in it. Unlike the disbeliever, for he is like firewood prepared for kindling the Fire and burning in it, for no other purpose. Unlike the people of Paradise, for none enters Paradise except the pure from filth and sin, either by self-discipline or by repentance. Do you not see that the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, said: 'Do you think that Paradise is a pasture for sheep? By Allah, they will not enter it until they become like the white garment.' And the Fire burns the impurity of sins and removes them from the disobedient believer, so he comes out after their removal, unlike Paradise, for it does not remove the purity of the one who enters so that he would come out of it and enter it a second time by the mercy of Allah Almighty and by the intercession of the prophets, peace be upon them.
Source pages p. 249

Commentary
This is an answer to an objection regarding the fate of sinful believers. The passage argues that the sinful believer is subject to Allah's will: either forgiven by grace or punished by justice. If punished, the punishment is proportionate to sins, after which the believer is brought out of Hell by mercy or prophetic intercession. This is compared to gold purified by fire: the gold (the believer) loses impurities (sins) and exits, never to remain. The disbeliever, however, is like firewood — created only to burn eternally. The righteous enter Paradise because they have been purified of filth and sin either through self-discipline or repentance. The Prophet's hadith emphasizes that Paradise is not easily attained. Thus, fire removes sin from the believer but does not remove purity from Paradise's dwellers.
