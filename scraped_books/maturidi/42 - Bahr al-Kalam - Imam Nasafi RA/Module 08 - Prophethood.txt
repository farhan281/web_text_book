Three Views on Accountability Without Revelation
Nasafi presents three views on whether one not reached by revelation is excused for not knowing God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three major theological positions on whether a rational person who has not received revelation is excused for not knowing God, and the reasoning behind each view.

Core Idea
Revelation or reason? Three schools disagree on accountability without divine message.

How do we judge someone who never heard of Islam but is intelligent and doesn't believe in God? Nasafi frames this question by presenting three answers: one insisting on rational inference, one obligating knowledge through reason, and one excusing until revelation comes. This lesson breaks down each position and its Qur'anic foundation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the author's flow. Then read the English translation carefully, noting the three distinct positions.
The passage is structured as three answers to one question.
2
Identify Nasafi's own view
Look for the first answer after the question. Nasafi states his position (Hanafi view) and supports it with two Qur'anic examples.
3
Compare the Mu'tazilite view
Find the Mu'tazilite position. Note how they differ: they do not require inference but say reason directly obligates knowledge of God.
This is a subtle but important distinction.
4
Examine the Ash'ari/Hanbali view
Read the third position. They say the person is excused and support it with the verse 'We would not punish until We send a messenger.'
This view relies on a literal reading of the Qur'an.
5
Synthesize the implications
Consider why this debate matters: it affects our understanding of human responsibility, the role of reason, and God's justice.
Think about real-world scenarios.

Commentary
This passage is a school-by-school classification of the ruling on a rational person who has not received revelation (waḥy) and does not know his Lord. The author (Nasafi, a Hanafi) begins by stating his own position: such a person is not excused ('indanā lā yakūnu maʿdhūran) and must use reason (istidlāl) to infer that the world has a Creator. He cites the People of the Cave and Prophet Abraham as examples of rational inference without prior revelation. Next, he presents the Mu'tazilite view: they deny that reason imposes an obligation to infer (lā yajibu ʿalayhi an yastadilla bi-l-ʿaql), but they assert that reason itself (al-ʿaql) directly obligates knowledge of God. Finally, he presents the Ash'ari and some Hanbalite view: such a person is excused (yakūnu maʿdhūran) and not obligated to infer, supporting this with the Qur'anic verse (17:15) that punishment only comes after sending a messenger. The passage thus delineates three positions on the interplay between reason and revelation in the obligation to know God.

Interpreting the Prophet's Vision of His Lord
Objection and answer interpreting the Prophet's vision hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two figurative interpretations of the Prophet's vision in the Mi'raj hadith and why misreading a divine name can lead to disbelief.

Core Idea
The Prophet's vision of his Lord was not literal; it was a vision of Jibril or a state of perfection.

The hadith of the Mi‘raj describes the Prophet seeing his Lord 'in the most beautiful form.' Some might take this literally, but Islamic theology insists Allah is not a body. This lesson explores two interpretations that safeguard tanzih, along with a stern warning about mishandling divine names.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The passage begins with an objection citing the hadith. Recognize that the objection relies on a literal reading of the Prophet's words.
The objection is a common challenge: how can the Prophet see Allah if Allah is not a body?
2
Learn the two interpretations
The author provides two figurative meanings: first, 'my Lord' refers to Jibril; second, the phrase means the Prophet was in a beautiful state. Compare these explanations.
Both avoid attributing a form to Allah.
3
Grasp the Quranic support and ruling
The author cites Quran 59:24 and adds a ruling: deliberately reading 'al-Musawwir' in the accusative is disbelief. Understand why this is serious.
This ruling shows the importance of correct theological beliefs in recitation.

The Brides of the Devils and the Refutation of a Myth about Prophet Sulayman
Nasafi enumerates three 'brides' of devils and refutes a story about Sulayman's wives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the story that devils had access to Prophet Sulayman's wives is theologically impossible.

Core Idea
Devils cannot physically interact with humans; such stories are false.

This lesson examines a narration from Ibn Abbas about the three 'brides' of the shayateen, which illustrates the limits of their power. Then it addresses a false story about Prophet Sulayman's wives, showing why it must be rejected. The key principle is that shayateen have no ability to commit physical acts like intercourse with humans.

Note that Nasafi explains devils can only embrace and kiss, but not have intercourse. Why? Because they have no 'amal (action/power) over humans.
This is the theological basis for rejecting the story.

This passage is a kalam argument about whether the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) is still a Messenger after his physical death. The author begins by posing the question. He then presents the view of the Mutashabbiha (or Mutafalsifa) and the Karramiyya, who hold that prophethood is an accident (‘arad) that does not subsist for two consecutive moments; therefore, since the Prophet's death, the accident of prophethood has ceased, so he is no longer a Messenger. Next, the author reports the position of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash‘ari: although the accident itself may not persist, the ruling (hukm) of messengership remains. Al-Ash‘ari supports this using analogies: the waiting period (‘idda) of a divorced woman still carries rulings from the marriage even after the marriage has ended; a person who breaks his ablution during prayer is considered 'in the ruling of prayer' when he goes to renew it, though he is not performing the actual acts. Similarly, prophethood is an accident, but its ruling continues. The author then provides a proof that accidents do not subsist: after finishing the Zuhr prayer, one is no longer considered 'in prayer' because otherwise eating, drinking, and talking would be prohibited; thus accidents have no persistence across different times. Despite this, the author asserts his own conclusion: 'We say: He is a Messenger in the present (fi al-hal).' He argues that if the Prophet were not a Messenger now, then the faith of those who believe in him would be invalid, and the testimony in the adhan—'I bear witness that Muhammad is the Messenger of God'—would be false if said in the present tense. The passage ends by extending this to all prophets: they remain prophets even after death. Thus, the author maintains that prophethood is not merely a transient accident but has a continuous reality, or at least that we affirm actual prophethood in the present.
