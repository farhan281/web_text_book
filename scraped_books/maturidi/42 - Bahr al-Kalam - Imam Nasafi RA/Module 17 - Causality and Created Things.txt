The Ruling on Earning and Seeking Wealth in Light of Tawakkul
Nasafi compares three theological positions on earning and tawakkul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different theological rulings on earning (kash) and tawakkul according to the Qadariyya, Ahl al-Sunna, and extreme ascetics.

Core Idea
Earning is not forbidden; its ruling depends on one's situation and need.

This passage from Nasafi presents three contrasting views on the Islamic ruling of earning wealth and relying on God. The Qadariyya saw earning as obligatory, while extreme ascetics saw it as forbidden. Ahl al-Sunna took a nuanced position, differentiating based on one's circumstances. Let's explore each view and see how the Sunni school balances trust in Allah with personal responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three groups
Read the passage and identify the three theological groups: Qadariyya, Ahl al-Sunna, and the extreme ascetics (mutaqashshifa) along with the Karamiyya. Notice the different rulings they assign to earning.
2
Examine the Sunni position in detail
Ahl al-Sunna gives a graded ruling depending on one's financial situation. Highlight the three conditions: having sufficiency (sunna/permissible), having a dirham for sufficiency (license), and being in need (obligatory).
This nuanced view avoids extremes.
3
Understand the ascetics' argument
The ascetics argue that earning is haram because it contradicts tawakkul. They quote Qur'anic verses to support their view. Ask yourself: How does the Sunni position still honor tawakkul while allowing earning?
The key is that tawakkul does not negate taking means.

A chapter explaining the reality of earning and seeking wealth and explaining the reality of tawakkul (reliance on God). The Qadariyya said: Earning and seeking wealth are obligatory upon the servant. Ahl al-Sunna wa al-Jama'a said: If he has sufficiency, then earning is for him a sunna and permissible; [and if he does not have sufficiency and has a dirham to buy sufficiency, then earning is a license for him], and if he is in dire need or has a family and dependents, then earning is obligatory upon him. The mutaqashshifa (extreme ascetics) and the Karamiyya said: Earning is forbidden, and putting aside wealth (hoarding) is forbidden, because tawakkul on God is obligatory. God Most High said: 'And upon God let the believers rely' [Al-Imran 122] and 'And upon God rely, if you are believers' [Al-Ma'ida 23]. And they said: Earning invalidates tawakkul and that is not permissible, because God Most High provides for him from where he does not expect.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of three theological positions on the ruling of earning (kash) and tawakkul. It opens with the title stating the chapter's purpose. First, the Qadariyya hold that earning and seeking wealth are obligatory (fard). Second, Ahl al-Sunna differentiate based on the person's situation: if one already has sufficient provisions, earning is a sunna (recommended) and mubah (permissible); if one has no provision but has a dirham to buy food, earning is a rukhsa (license, meaning it is allowed but not required); if one is in dire need or has dependents, earning is fard (obligatory). Third, the mutaqashshifa (extreme ascetics) and Karamiyya hold that earning is haram and hoarding wealth is haram, arguing that tawakkul is obligatory and earning contradicts it. They cite Quranic verses commanding tawakkul to support their view. The passage ends with their argument that earning nullifies tawakkul and is therefore impermissible, because God provides from unexpected sources. The author presents all views without explicit endorsement, but the detailed differentiation from Ahl al-Sunna is characteristic of Ash'ari kalam.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Tawakkul (reliance on God) forbids all forms of earning and seeking wealth.
Correction: The Sunni position shows that earning is permissible or even obligatory depending on one's circumstances, and it does not contradict tawakkul; rather, taking means is part of trusting God.

Checkpoint
According to Ahl al-Sunna in this passage, what is the ruling on earning for a person who does not have sufficiency but has a dirham to buy food?
A) It is obligatory.
B) It is a sunnah.
C) It is a license (rukhsa).
D) It is forbidden.

Key Takeaway
The Sunni position balances reliance on God with the obligation to earn for one's needs, rejecting both extremes of compulsory earning and ascetic rejection.
