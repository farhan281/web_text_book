Abu Hanifa's Ship Analogy for the Existence of God
Abu Hanifa uses the analogy of a ship without a captain to prove the necessity of a Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Abu Hanifa uses a simple analogy from everyday experience to prove the necessity of a Creator for the universe.

Core Idea
A sailing ship without a captain is as impossible as a universe without a Creator.

Imam Abu Hanifa, one of the great scholars of Islam, often used rational arguments accessible to all. In this passage, he presents a powerful analogy that appeals to common sense. The analogy of the ship without a captain leads the listener to recognize that the universe must have a Maker, Preserver, and Originator.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage in Arabic and English
Start by reading the original Arabic passage and its literal translation. Let the words of Imam Abu Hanifa resonate without interpretation.
Notice the dialogue format: question and response.
2
Understand the hypothetical scenario
Abu Hanifa describes a ship fully loaded with cargo, sailing smoothly amid stormy seas, with no captain or crew. Imagine the absurdity of such a claim.
Why would this be impossible according to common sense?
3
Identify the rational conclusion
The listeners immediately reject the scenario as irrational. The intellect does not accept a ship without a captain. This instinctive response is the foundation of the argument.
The same logic applies to the universe.
4
Apply the analogy to the universe
Abu Hanifa makes the connection: if a simple ship needs a captain, how much more does the entire world, with all its complexity and order, need a Creator? Reflect on the terms: Maker (Sani'), Preserver (Hafiz), Originator (Muhdith).
This is the argument from design in its simplest form.

Commentary
This passage presents a famous analogy from Imam Abu Hanifa to prove the existence of God. The Imam poses a hypothetical: if someone claimed to see a fully laden ship sailing smoothly amidst stormy seas without a captain or anyone to steer it, would the intellect accept that? The audience answers no. Then Abu Hanifa makes the point: if such a simple case is impossible without an agent, how can the entire universe, with all its complexity and order, exist without a Creator (Sani'), Preserver (Hafiz), and Originator (Muhdith)? This is a classic argument from design, using common sense to lead to the necessity of a divine creator. The analogy is designed to be easily understood and compelling.

Imam Nasafi's Opening Creed of Tawhid
Imam Nasafi declares his creed of tawhid and divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the foundational creedal statement of Imam Nasafi, including the essential affirmations and negations regarding Allah that form the basis of his work.

Core Idea
Tawhid is the declaration that Allah is One, Singular, and transcendent above all limitations.

Imam Nasafi begins his book with a formal creedal declaration that sets the agenda for the entire work. This passage is not merely a personal profession but a teaching on the essential beliefs about Allah that every Muslim must know. As we read, notice how the author carefully balances positive affirmations of Allah's attributes with negations of any imperfection or limitation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic with translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Pay attention to the structure and the repeated phrases.
The Arabic uses parallel negations (لا... له).
2
Identify the three sections
Break the passage into: opening formula (basmalah, hamdalah, salawat), author introduction, and creedal statement. Notice how the creed begins with 'اعلموا أني أعتقد'.
3
List the negations
Write down all the things that are denied of Allah: partner, likeness, resemblance, form, opposite, equal. Reflect on why each is necessary for tawhid.
4
List the affirmations
Note what is affirmed: oneness, singularity, eternity, self-sufficiency, perfection, knowledge, dominance, pre-existence before place.
5
Reflect on 'before place'
The passage ends by affirming Allah's existence before the creation of place, reinforcing His transcendence beyond space.
This counters anthropomorphic ideas.

Commentary
This passage is the opening declaration of the author's creed (aqidah). It begins with the basmalah, hamdalah, and salawat, establishing the religious context. Then the author introduces himself as 'the Shaykh, the greatest Imam' etc., and begins his creedal statement: 'Know that I believe in knowledge of Allah Most High and tawhid.' This indicates that the purpose of the book is to articulate and defend tawhid (divine oneness) and ma'rifah (knowledge of Allah). He then affirms Allah's oneness (wahid) and singularity (fard), and asserts eternity (qadim azali?)—that Allah is eternal without beginning. He then describes Allah as Samad (Self-Sufficient, Eternal Refuge), denying any partner (sharik), likeness (mithl), resemblance (shabah), form (shakl), opposite (didd), or equal (nidd). This refutes anthropomorphism (tashbih) and polytheism (shirk). He affirms that Allah has always been and always will be One, Samad, Singular, and Unique—denying any change or multiplicity in the divine essence. Finally, he states that Allah is perfect in His essence (dhat) and attributes (sifat), transcendent (munazzah) above all deficiency, and that He is the All-Knowing, the Dominant, the Everlasting, and that He existed before place—affirming divine transcendence beyond spatiality. This opening establishes the key topics of the book: tawhid, divine attributes, and transcendence.

Imam al-Nasafi begins his theological treatise by laying down the bedrock of Islamic belief: the absolute oneness and uniqueness of Allah. This lesson walks through his opening declarations, clarifying the key terms and negations that define Tawhid.

وقال عليه السلام: «إن الله تعالى عادةً جميلة في تكذيب المنجمين». وقد قيل: المنجم كالكاهن والكاهن كالساحر، والساحر كالكافر، والكافر في النار. والدليل على بطلان علم النجوم والطب قوله تعالى: ﴿ما أَشْهَدتُهُمْ خَلَقَ السَّمَوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضِ وَلَا خَلْقَ أَنفُسِهِمْ وَمَا كُنتُ مُتَخِذَ الْمُضِلَّينَ عَضُنَا ﴾ [الكهف: ٥١] لأن العلم لا يحصل إلا بشيئين: إما المعاينة، أو بخبر المخبر الصادق، والنبي يا الله ما أخبر عنها والناس في المعاينة كلهم سواء إلا أن بعض الناس وكلوا بآرائهم وخذلوا بعقولهم فضلوا ضلالا بعيدًا وخسروا خسرانا مبينا.
And he, peace be upon him, said: 'Indeed Allah the Exalted has a beautiful habit in disbelieving astrologers.' And it has been said: The astrologer is like the soothsayer, and the soothsayer is like the sorcerer, and the sorcerer is like the disbeliever, and the disbeliever is in the Fire. And the evidence for the invalidity of the science of the stars and medicine is the saying of the Exalted: 'I did not make them witness the creation of the heavens and the earth nor the creation of their own selves, and I was not taking the misguiders as assistants' [al-Kahf 51]. Because knowledge is obtained only through two things: either direct observation, or through the report of a truthful informant. And the Prophet, by Allah, did not inform about them. And people are all equal in direct observation, except that some people are entrusted to their opinions and forsaken by their intellects, so they went astray far away and lost a manifest loss.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-part refutation of astrology (and medicine). First, a hadith of the Prophet (ﷺ) states that Allah has a 'beautiful habit' (ʿādatun jamīla) of disbelieving (takdhīb) astrologers, i.e., He consistently makes their predictions false. Then a chain of equivalence is given: an astrologer (munajjim) is like a soothsayer (kāhin), a soothsayer is like a sorcerer (sāḥir), a sorcerer is like a disbeliever (kāfir), and a disbeliever is in the Fire. Next, Nasafi presents a Qur'anic proof from al-Kahf 51: Allah says He did not make the stars or other beings witness the creation, implying they have no knowledge of unseen things. The logical argument follows: knowledge can only be obtained through two means: direct observation (muʿāyana) or a report from a truthful informant (khabar al-mukhbi al-ṣādiq). The Prophet (ﷺ) did not inform about astrological matters, and people are equal in observation—none have special access to stellar knowledge. Those who rely on their own opinions (ārā') and are forsaken by their intellects go astray.

Correction: The passage shows that astrology fails the two tests of valid knowledge: direct observation and truthful report. The Prophet (ﷺ) did not affirm it, and people have no special observational access.
