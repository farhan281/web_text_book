The Epistemological Proof against Astrology and Medicine
Nasafi presents the transmitted and rational proofs for the invalidity of astrology and medicine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two sources of certain knowledge (tawatur and truthful report) and why astrology and medicine fail to meet these criteria.

Core Idea
True knowledge requires unbroken transmission or prophetic report – astrology has neither.

This lesson examines the first part of Imam Nasafi's refutation, where he establishes a clear epistemological standard: certain knowledge comes only from tawatur (concurrent transmission) or the report of an infallible truthful person. He then applies this standard to astrology and medicine, showing that they possess neither, and therefore cannot be relied upon. The Quranic verse he cites reinforces that astrologers have no witness to the unseen.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening proof statement
Notice that the author declares the proof against astrology and medicine to be both transmitted and rational. This sets up a dual argument.
2
Examine the transmitted (Quranic) proof
Read the verse from Surah al-Kahf (18:51) and reflect on how it denies astrologers any witness to creation – thus they cannot know the unseen.
The verse is a direct refutation of astrological claims.
3
Understand the rational proof: two sources of knowledge
Identify the two paths to certainty: tawatur (widespread reliable transmission) and khabar al-sadiq (report of a truthful, infallible person). The author asserts that only these yield knowledge.
'Tawatur' means reports from so many independent sources that collusion is impossible.
4
Apply the standard to astrology and medicine
The Prophet never informed about these sciences, and no tawatur exists for them. Therefore, they cannot produce certain knowledge, and acting on them is impermissible.
This logical step is the core of the refutation.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the sciences of astrology and medicine, arguing that they are invalid based on both transmitted and rational evidence. The author begins by stating that the proof against these sciences is twofold. First, the transmitted proof: he cites Quran 18:51, where Allah says He did not make the astrologers witness creation, implying their claims to knowledge of the unseen are false. Second, the rational proof: knowledge can only be obtained through two means – tawatur (concurrent transmission from many reliable sources) or the report of a truthful person who is infallible (the Prophet). Since the Prophet did not inform us about the science of stars or medicine, and since people are equal in the possibility of tawatur (i.e., no such tawatur exists for these sciences), these fields cannot yield certain knowledge. Therefore, acting upon them is impermissible. The author then highlights that some people follow their own opinions and are led astray. He supports his argument with hadith: the Prophet stated that happiness lies in rejecting astrologers, and that believing a soothsayer, augur, or physician constitutes disbelief in revelation. A hierarchical condemnation follows: the astrologer is like the soothsayer, the soothsayer like the sorcerer, the sorcerer like the disbeliever, and the end is the Fire. Finally, he includes a saying about reliance: whoever clings to his intellect is guided (but limited), while only clinging to the Lord brings exaltation. This underscores the theme that true knowledge and guidance come from revelation, not human conjecture.

The Prophetic Warnings against Astrology and Medicine
Nasafi cites hadith and sayings to warn against believing astrologers, soothsayers, and physicians, and ends with a proverb about reliance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the severe warnings from hadith against believing astrologers, soothsayers, and physicians, and the principle of relying solely on Allah.

Core Idea
Believing an astrologer or physician is tantamount to disbelief.

After establishing the epistemological invalidity of astrology and medicine, Imam Nasafi reinforces his argument with prophetic hadith and wise sayings. These warnings show that not only are these sciences futile, but believing in them leads to spiritual harm, even kufr. The lesson concludes with a powerful reminder that true exaltation comes from clinging to Allah alone, not to human intellect or stratagems.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith about happiness and disbelief
The Prophet says happiness is in disbelieving astrologers, and believing a soothsayer, augur, or physician is kufr. Note the strong language.
These are severe warnings, not mere discouragement.
2
Examine the chain of equivalence
The saying equates the astrologer to the soothsayer, the soothsayer to the sorcerer, and the sorcerer to the disbeliever – ending in the Fire. This shows the gravity.
This chain highlights the spiritual danger.
3
Understand the final saying on reliance
The last proverb contrasts reliance on intellect, tradition, cunning, and reliance on the Lord. Only the last leads to exaltation.
This serves as a conclusion: put trust in Allah, not human means.

Defining Pre-eternity and Temporal Origination
Nasafi defines qadim and muhdath and proves that God alone is qadim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between qadim and muhdath, the proper meaning of qadim for God, and why denying God's pre-eternity leads to absurdity.

Core Idea
God alone is qadim — without beginning, not merely old.

This passage from Imam al-Nasafi lays the foundation for all discussions of God's eternity. Before we can speak of creation or divine attributes, we must define what it means for something to be pre-eternal (qadim) versus temporally originated (muhdath). Nasafi clarifies that God's qidam is not about being ancient in time but about having no beginning at all.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the clear two-part division of existents.
2
Understand the two meanings of qadim
Nasafi first gives the linguistic meaning (preceding others) and then the theological meaning for God (never ceased to be). Note the shift and why the linguistic meaning is inadequate for God.
The linguistic meaning implies a relation to other beings, which is not appropriate for the Creator.
3
Follow the proof for God's pre-eternity
The author uses a reduction: if God were not qadim, He would be muhdath, which is incompatible with being the Lord and Creator. Therefore, negating huduth necessitates affirming qidam.
This is a simple but powerful argument from the impossibility of an eternal creation.
4
Connect to divine names and the term mawjud
Nasafi cites al-Awwal and al-Akhir as scriptural support, and explains that 'mawjud' for God means the same as 'lam yazal'.
This shows that all terms for God's existence point to His timelessness.

A chapter on explaining the meaning of pre-eternity (al-qidam) and temporal origination (al-huduth). Know that existent things are of two types: pre-eternal (qadim) and temporally originated (muhdath). The temporally originated is what is other than God the Exalted, and the pre-eternal is God the Exalted. Al-qadim in language means: the one preceded in existence over others, and this is in the attributes of creatures. As for the attributes of God the Exalted, qadim means 'has never ceased to be' (lam yazal), and God the Exalted is qadim without beginning or end, and He has never ceased to be and will never cease to be, not in the sense that He precedes others in existence. And this is evidenced by the fact that if we do not say that God the Exalted is qadim, we would be forced to assert temporal origination (al-ahdath) and divestment (al-ta'til), because the contrary of qadim is muhdath, and the muhdath cannot be a lord, maker, creator. So from the necessity of negating temporal origination follows the affirmation of pre-eternity. And upon this the revealed text (al-nass) has come with these two names: He is al-Awwal and al-Akhir, meaning 'has never ceased to be without beginning and end.' And it is permissible to say that God the Exalted is existent (mawjud), because al-mawjud means 'He has never ceased to be.'
Source pages p. 107

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal exposition of the key concepts of qidam (pre-eternity) and huduth (temporal origination). The author begins by dividing all existents into two kinds: qadim and muhdath. The muhdath is everything other than God; the qadim is God alone. He then explains the linguistic and technical meanings of qadim. Linguistically, qadim means "that which precedes others in existence," but this applies only to creatures. In God's attributes, qadim means "lam yazal" (never ceased to be) and "la yazal" (will never cease)—without beginning or end, not in the sense of being prior to others. He provides a proof: if we deny that God is qadim, we would have to say He is muhdath (originated), which would contradict His being Lord, Maker, and Creator. Therefore, negating huduth necessitates affirming qidam. Moreover, the revealed text (Qur'an) affirms this by giving God the names al-Awwal and al-Akhir, which mean the same: He never ceases to be without beginning and end. Finally, the author states that it is permissible to apply the term "mawjud" (existent) to God, because it means the same as "lam yazal"—He has never ceased to exist. This guards against the misconception that "existent" implies temporal origination.

Types of Sins and Conditions for Their Repentance
Nasafi classifies sins and explains when repentance is accepted or requires additional conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different categories of sins and the specific conditions required for repentance to be accepted according to Nasafi.

Core Idea
Repentance conditions vary: sins against Allah alone differ from sins against others.

Imam Nasafi provides a clear classification of sins and their repentance requirements. Not all sins are forgiven by simply turning to Allah; some require the forgiveness of those we have wronged, while others demand making up missed duties. This lesson unpacks these distinctions directly from the source.

Read the Arabic passage and translation

Commentary
This passage is a classification of sins and their conditions for repentance. The author first states that sins are of various types. He identifies sins between the servant and his Lord—such as adultery, sodomy, drinking wine, lying, backbiting, and slander—and explains that if the news of the sin has not reached the victim, repentance suffices. However, if the news has reached the victim, repentance alone is not enough; the victim must forgive the sinner (make it permissible). He gives the example of adultery with a married woman: if the husband learns of it, repentance requires the husband's forgiveness. Then he discusses sins of omission (abandoning prayer, zakat, fasting): these are not removed by repentance alone but require making up the missed obligations. The passage ends with a remark that if Allah wills to punish, it is just, and cites a Quranic verse indicating accountability for what the heart earns.

Interpretation of the Hadith on the Fire Becoming Empty

Core Idea
The Fire will become empty only for sinful believers, not for all.

This passage from Imam Nasafi's work tackles a challenging hadith about the Fire eventually becoming empty. Critics have used it to argue that punishment is not eternal, but Nasafi shows that the report is limited to sinful believers. Let's examine his reasoning step by step.
