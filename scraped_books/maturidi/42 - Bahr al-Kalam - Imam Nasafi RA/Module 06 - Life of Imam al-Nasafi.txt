Commentary
This passage covers the caliphate of Umar and the election of Uthman. First, the author explains that Abu Bakr chose Umar based on a prophetic hadith encouraging emulation of the two after him. Umar's reign was marked by military expansion, including the conquest of Khurasan and the peaceful submission of Balkh under Ahnaf ibn Qays. Notably, Umar refrained from crossing the Oxus, saying that region would be under Uthman's caliphate, showing his respect for the future. After Umar's assassination by a Persian slave, he left the succession to a shura (consultative council) of six senior Companions. Talha and al-Zubayr withdrew, leaving Uthman, Ali, and Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf. Abd al-Rahman waived his own candidacy to act as an arbiter. He canvassed public opinion for three days and found it favored Uthman, so he selected him. Ali and all other Companions then gave bay'ah to Uthman. The text then mentions Uthman's assassination by rioters and gives the total years of both Umar and Uthman's caliphates as twenty-two years, with Abu Bakr's figure cut off. This narrative reinforces the legitimacy and consultative nature of the early caliphate.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some may think Uthman's selection was controversial or imposed.
Correction: The text shows a proper consultative process: a council was formed, members withdrew willingly, and Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf surveyed public opinion before choosing Uthman.
