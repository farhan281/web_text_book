Commentary
This passage details Abu Bakr's extensive support for the Prophet during the Meccan period. It begins by stating his financial assistance, citing the hadith 'No wealth has benefited me as the wealth of Abu Bakr has benefited me.' It mentions that he gave all his wealth when asked, leaving nothing for his family except reliance on Allah. It also notes his role in ransoming those being tortured. Then it describes his missionary work during pilgrimage seasons, where he accompanied the Prophet and urged the tribal leaders to embrace Islam. The author contrasts this with 'Ali, who was young, not heeded, without wealth or following. The passage then praises Abu Bakr's pre-Islamic reputation for sound judgment and leadership, which caused the great figures of Quraysh to follow him. It emphasizes his steadfastness: his resolve never weakened, he never grew weary or regretted his efforts despite relentless opposition. Instead, he increased in his support for the religion and defense of the Prophet and weak believers. The author concludes by noting that none of these hardships fell upon 'Ali due to his youth and the enemies' perception that his efforts would not spread the religion.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous argument by Imam al-Nasafi enumerating multiple proofs of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq's (may Allah be pleased with him) precedence and virtue over all other Companions. The author proceeds in a chronological and thematic order, building a cumulative case. He begins with Abu Bakr's financial support during the Meccan period, noting the Prophet's famous acknowledgment that no wealth benefited him as much as Abu Bakr's, and highlights that he gave all his wealth for the sake of Allah. Then he describes his da'wah efforts during pilgrimage seasons, his persuasion of the nobles of Quraysh who embraced Islam through his efforts, contrasting this with Ali's young age and lack of influence. Next, he praises Abu Bakr's steadfastness and unwavering resolve in the face of hardships, noting his continuous increase in support for the religion. He then mentions his virtue of being chosen by the Prophet for the Hijra (emigration) and as a companion on that journey. The author then refers to the two pledges of allegiance (Bay'at al-Aqaba and Bay'at al-Ridwan), stating that Abu Bakr was the first among them and most eager. He cites the Qur'anic verse about divine pleasure with those who pledged under the tree. Following that, he describes Abu Bakr's role as the Prophet's constant right-hand companion, advisor, and vizier, and relates the incident at Badr where the Prophet affirmed his status. He then notes that the Companions themselves held Abu Bakr in the highest esteem, as evidenced by Abu Ubayda's response to Umar during the Saqifa event. Finally, he argues that even if there were no other evidence, the fact that the hearts of the Companions turned to him and their unity after the Prophet's death, despite the fear and confusion, is sufficient proof of his merit. The passage concludes by asserting that this is among the strongest proofs.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous argument by Imam al-Nasafi enumerating multiple proofs of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq's (may Allah be pleased with him) precedence and virtue over all other Companions. The author proceeds in a chronological and thematic order, building a cumulative case. He begins with Abu Bakr's financial support during the Meccan period, noting the Prophet's famous acknowledgment that no wealth benefited him as much as Abu Bakr's, and highlights that he gave all his wealth for the sake of Allah. Then he describes his da'wah efforts during pilgrimage seasons, his persuasion of the nobles of Quraysh who embraced Islam through his efforts, contrasting this with Ali's young age and lack of influence. Next, he praises Abu Bakr's steadfastness and unwavering resolve in the face of hardships, noting his continuous increase in support for the religion. He then mentions his virtue of being chosen by the Prophet for the Hijra (emigration) and as a companion on that journey. The author then refers to the two pledges of allegiance (Bay'at al-Aqaba and Bay'at al-Ridwan), stating that Abu Bakr was the first among them and most eager. He cites the Qur'anic verse about divine pleasure with those who pledged under the tree. Following that, he describes Abu Bakr's role as the Prophet's constant right-hand companion, advisor, and vizier, and relates the incident at Badr where the Prophet affirmed his status. He then notes that the Companions themselves held Abu Bakr in the highest esteem, as evidenced by Abu Ubayda's response to Umar during the Saqifa event. Finally, he argues that even if there were no other evidence, the fact that the hearts of the Companions turned to him and their unity after the Prophet's death, despite the fear and confusion, is sufficient proof of his merit. The passage concludes by asserting that this is among the strongest proofs.

Commentary
This passage is a continuous eulogy presenting multiple proofs for the superiority of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA) and his rightful caliphate after the Prophet (SAW). The author begins with ornate praise, then lists specific evidences: (1) The Prophet's trust in him for emigration and companionship. (2) He was the first in the two pledges of allegiance (Bay'at al-`Aqabah and Bay'at al-Ridwan) and most eager. (3) Allah revealed the verse about pleasure with the believers under the tree, and Abu Bakr was part of that. (4) He was always at the Prophet's right side, his consultant and vizier, notably at Badr in the arish where the Prophet declared him an intimate friend, companion, and vizier. (5) The Companions considered him greatest and most majestic; Abu 'Ubaydah rebuked 'Umar for suggesting someone else for caliphate while Abu Bakr was present. (6) After the Prophet's death, when the Companions were perplexed, Abu Bakr remained firm, recited the verse about mortality, united them, and dispatched Usamah's army despite fears. (7) He fought apostates and zakat-withholders, bringing the Arabs back to Islam. (8) The Prophet appointed him to lead prayer, which is a strong indication of his precedence. (9) 'Ali refused to accept Abu Bakr's resignation, saying the Prophet put him forward. (10) The remark of Abu Sufyan (though incomplete here) also shows Abu Bakr's high status. Each of these proofs collectively establishes Abu Bakr's excellence and his deserved position as the first caliph.

First in the Two Pledges and Divine Pleasure
Demonstrates Abu Bakr's precedence through his initiative in the two pledges and divine approval.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Abu Bakr's precedence in the two pledges and the verse about the pledge under the tree establish his virtue.

Core Idea
Abu Bakr's priority in pledging allegiance earned him Allah's pleasure and immense reward.

After highlighting personal trust, the author now turns to Abu Bakr's public acts of allegiance. He was the first in two historic pledges and the most eager. The Qur'anic verse that followed confirms Allah's pleasure with those present, and Abu Bakr receives the reward of all.

Guided Path
1
Read the short passage
Focus on the two claims: being first in the pledges and the citation of the Qur'anic verse.
2
Understand 'the two pledges'
The two pledges refer to Bay'at al-`Aqabah (before Hijra) and Bay'at al-Ridwan (after peace treaty of Hudaybiyya).
Both are landmark events in early Islam.
3
Connect the verse
Allah's pleasure in Surah al-Fath includes all who pledged under the tree. As the first and most eager, Abu Bakr's inclusion is certain, and he gets reward like the whole group.
This is a direct divine endorsement.
4
Reflect on the implication
Being first in both pledges shows initiative and sincerity, leading to highest ranks.

Firmness and Leadership After the Prophet's Death
Describes Abu Bakr's exemplary response to the crisis of the Prophet's death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Abu Bakr's composure, decisive actions, and military campaigns after the Prophet's death proved his superior intellect and courage.

Core Idea
Abu Bakr's unwavering faith and decisive leadership in the face of crisis established him as the rightful successor.

The most severe test came with the Prophet's death. The companions were in disarray; some denied it, others clung to hope. But Abu Bakr remained calm, recited the Quranic verse about mortality, and took firm action. He dispatched Usamah's army and fought the apostates, reuniting the community.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
This is a long passage describing the crisis and Abu Bakr's response. Read it carefully.
2
Understand the Companions' state
Note the confusion: some denied the Prophet's death, others claimed he was alive. This shows the gravity of the situation.
The author says they were perplexed.
3
Observe Abu Bakr's qualities
He was firm, not perplexed, recited the verse, dispatched Usamah's army, and fought apostates. These demonstrate intellect, courage, and leadership.
Each action is a proof of his virtue.
4
Reflect on the outcome
Through his efforts, the Arabs were guided back to Islam. This shows that Allah blessed his leadership.

Presents the prayer leadership as decisive evidence of Abu Bakr's virtue.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Prophet's command that Abu Bakr lead prayer was a clear indication of his superiority.

The final category of proof is perhaps the most direct: the Prophet himself appointed Abu Bakr to lead the congregational prayer during his illness. This act, combined with the hadith that the most learned should lead, shows Abu Bakr was the best. Moreover, when Abu Bakr offered to resign, 'Ali refused, emphasizing that the Prophet had put him forward.

Its beauties and adornments and his noble stations which [؟] in the fields of [؟] beyond what others have of exploits and glories, their traces and the attack of their stars and tails follow him. The Prophet (peace be upon him) had such trust in him that he singled him out for his emigration and chose him for his companionship, and commanded him to assist him with what his hand contained in his journey. Then he (Abu Bakr) was the first of them in the two pledges and the most eager for them. Then Allah Almighty said: 'Indeed Allah was pleased with the believers when they pledged allegiance to you under the tree' (48:18). So he had in them the reward equal to that of whom we found (from him?). Then throughout the Prophet's life, he was on his right in his assembly, and in calamities he was his consultant, and in important matters his vizier, until on the day of Badr he was with the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) in the arish. A bedouin turned away from the battle line and said: 'The war has become fierce among the companions of the Messenger of Allah; come out, O Abu Bakr.' So the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: 'Indeed Allah has made Abu Bakr for His Prophet an intimate friend, a companion, and a vizier.' The bedouin departed saying: 'Bravo, bravo, O son of Abu Quhafah.' Then indeed he was the greatest of people in the eyes of the Companions and the most majestic in their hearts. For this reason Abu 'Ubaydah said to 'Umar (may Allah be pleased with them both) when 'Umar said to him: 'Extend your hand so that I may pledge allegiance to you': 'Do you say this while Abu Bakr is present? By Allah, you have no [excellence?] in Islam except this.' So he considered him the most deserving of the community for leadership. Then if there were no proof of his virtue and precedence over all the Companions except what was achieved of (uniting) hearts, gathering the scattered, and uniting the word after the death of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) with the prevalence of fear and terror among the Companions, that would be a sufficient proof. And likewise that is among the strongest proofs of his strong intellect, correct judgment, firmness of heart, utmost courage, and disregard for the blame of blamers. Indeed the Companions (may Allah be pleased with them) when the great matter and tremendous, grave calamity of the Prophet's death befell them, they were perplexed about it, until some of them denied his death out of dislike for splitting the stick of the Muslims and dispersing their word, and some of them claimed his life (peace be upon him) because they thought he would not die since he is the seal of the prophets. But he (Abu Bakr) at that time his heart was firm and he was not perplexed in his affair, and he did not become heedless of his opinion at the occurrence of the calamity which would normally cause distress and at its arrival. (He recited) 'Indeed you are mortal, and indeed they are mortal' (39:30). Then he dispatched the army of Usamah (may Allah be pleased with him) despite the Muslims' fear of division and reversal, and said: 'I will not untie a banner that the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) tied.' Then he did what he did with the people of apostasy and those who withheld zakat, so Allah guided the Arabs through the blessing of his effort after they had turned back from the religion on their heels and apostatized on their backs. And Allah is the granter of success. Likewise, the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) entrusting the matter of prayer to him, with his saying: 'The most reciter of the Book of Allah among you should lead you in prayer,' the hadith, indicates that he was the best of them. For this reason 'Ali said to him when he (Abu Bakr) said: 'Relieve me' — so 'Ali stood and said: 'We will not relieve you nor seek your relief; the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) put you forward, so we will not put you back. He was pleased with you for our religion, so we are pleased with you for our worldly affairs.' Likewise when Abu Sufyan said to 'Ali when Abu Bakr was given the pledge: 'What is this matter (going) to the lowest tribe of Quraysh? If you wish...'

Commentary
This passage is a continuous eulogy presenting multiple proofs for the superiority of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA) and his rightful caliphate after the Prophet (SAW). The author begins with ornate praise, then lists specific evidences: (1) The Prophet's trust in him for emigration and companionship. (2) He was the first in the two pledges of allegiance (Bay'at al-`Aqabah and Bay'at al-Ridwan) and most eager. (3) Allah revealed the verse about pleasure with the believers under the tree, and Abu Bakr was part of that. (4) He was always at the Prophet's right side, his consultant and vizier, notably at Badr in the arish where the Prophet declared him an intimate friend, companion, and vizier. (5) The Companions considered him greatest and most majestic; Abu 'Ubaydah rebuked 'Umar for suggesting someone else for caliphate while Abu Bakr was present. (6) After the Prophet's death, when the Companions were perplexed, Abu Bakr remained firm, recited the verse about mortality, united them, and dispatched Usamah's army despite fears. (7) He fought apostates and zakat-withholders, bringing the Arabs back to Islam. (8) The Prophet appointed him to lead prayer, which is a strong indication of his precedence. (9) 'Ali refused to accept Abu Bakr's resignation, saying the Prophet put him forward. (10) The remark of Abu Sufyan (though incomplete here) also shows Abu Bakr's high status. Each of these proofs collectively establishes Abu Bakr's excellence and his deserved position as the first caliph.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some argue that prayer leadership does not imply political leadership.
Correction: The Companions, including 'Ali, explicitly linked the Prophet's appointment for prayer to the caliphate.
