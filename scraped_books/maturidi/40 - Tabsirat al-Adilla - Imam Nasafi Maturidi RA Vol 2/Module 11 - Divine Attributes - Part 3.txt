Al-Maturidi's Doctrine on 'Umum, Khass, and Bayan
Al-Nasafi states al-Maturidi's view on 'umum, khass, and bayan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Maturidi's position that delayed specification (khass) clarifies the intended meaning (bayan) rather than abrogating (naskh) the original general statement.

Core Idea
Delayed specification is clarification, not abrogation.

This passage from al-Nasafi's Tabsirat al-Adilla presents the core of al-Maturidi's methodology in interpreting general and specific texts. It distinguishes his view from that of the Mu'tazila, who treat delayed specification as abrogation. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping early Hanafi-Maturidi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the main claim
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that al-Nasafi states the view of al-Maturidi and other shaykhs: generality may intend specificity, and delayed evidence of specification is a clarification, not an abrogation.
Focus on the contrast between 'bayan' and 'naskh'.
2
Analyze the evidence for the claim
The passage clarifies that evidence of restriction (qayd) is also considered bayan. The phrase coined from such evidence does not indicate that the original was intended to be general.
3
Understand the polemical context
Al-Nasafi reports that this view was contrasted with the Mu'tazila, and that later scholars in his region deviated from it, inclining toward the Iraqi Hanafis. This shows the doctrinal stakes.
Consider why 'umum and khass mattered in kalam debates.
4
Reflect on the implication for usul
The distinction between bayan and naskh affects how later revealed texts are used to interpret earlier ones. Al-Maturidi's position preserves the priority of the original general statement while allowing specification without abrogation.
This is a key methodological principle in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage defines the position of Abu Mansur al-Maturidi and the early Hanafi-Maturidi scholars on the relationship between 'umum and khass. They held that when a general statement is made, it may be intended to refer to a specific meaning. If evidence specifying the general meaning appears later, that evidence is not an abrogation (naskh) of the original statement but rather a clarification (bayan) of what was always intended, unless the specifying evidence is a restriction (qayd). Consequently, a phrase that is derived from a specific evidence or restriction does not serve as proof that the original statement was meant to be general and absolute. The passage then notes that these scholars considered the opposite view—that delayed specification indicates naskh—to be the doctrine of the Mu'tazila. Al-Nasafi reports that in his region, the shaykhs would censure and ascribe to i'tizal anyone who held that view. However, a later scholar emerged among them who inclined toward the Iraqi Hanafis in usul al-fiqh (i.e., the followers of Abu Hanifa's school in Iraq) and followed them on the issue of 'umum, even attributing the original Hanafi position to al-Shafi'i, while avoiding the issue of wa'id. Al-Nasafi leaves the judgment of that scholar's belief to Allah.

Can God Break His Threat? The Debate on Khulf in Wa'id and Wa'd

Learning Objective

Core Idea
God's truthfulness requires that He never breaks a promise or a threat.

This passage delves into a nuanced debate among early Muslim theologians about the nature of divine threats and promises. Some scholars argued that breaking a threat is a noble act, while breaking a promise is base. Others, including leading theologians, rejected any breach of divine word. The discussion also features a linguistic analysis of the term 'khulf' and a famous anecdote with Abu 'Amr ibn al-'Ala'.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on the threat (al-wa'id). It begins by mentioning that al-Husayn and others claimed that the general threat applies to every individual, but then they say that God can break His threat, and breaking a threat is considered noble (karam) while breaking a promise is base (lu'm). Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that God may break His threat (الوعيد). It begins by presenting the view attributed to al-Husayn and others: that a general threat applies to all individuals, yet God may leave it unfulfilled, and this is considered generosity (كرم), while breaking a promise would be baseness (لؤم). Many jurists held this, distinguishing between lying (كذب) in the past and breaking (خلف) in the future, claiming blame only for breaking a promise but not a threat. The author notes that Abd al-Qahir al-Baghdadi attributed this to al-Qalanisi, but the author found in al-Qalanisi's book that he held it by way of specification (تخصيص), not general permissibility. However, the leading mutakallimun reject this absolutely, arguing that breaking (خلف) is impermissible for God in both promise and threat, because if it were allowed, one could call Him 'breaker of the threat' (مخلف الوعيد), which is not permissible.

Then a story is narrated from Abu Uthman al-Mazini: Muhammad ibn Mish'ar related that they were with Abu Amr ibn al-Ala, who questioned another person about the threat. The person claimed God fulfills both promise and threat. Abu Amr rebuked him, saying he is ignorant of the Arabs' usage: they do not consider leaving a threat unfulfilled as lying, but rather praise it. He recited lines of poetry to illustrate that breaking a threat is called 'مخلف' and is praiseworthy. When asked if God would be called 'مخلف' if He did not fulfill His threat, the person said no. Abu Amr concluded he had invalidated his own evidence. This shows that what the Arabs call 'مخلف' in praise of humans is not applicable to God.

The author then cites more poetry: al-Sari al-Mawsili praises one who fulfills good promises but pardons when threatening punishment; Ka'b ibn Zuhair praises the Prophet for being hoped to break threats (i.e., pardon). Another poet criticizes those who always fulfill threats. But the author immediately clarifies: this applies only to human beings (في العباد). As for God, it is impossible to call Him 'مخلف' because change (التبدل) is impossible for Him.

Finally, the decisive proof is given: informing contrary to knowledge is lying, whether past or future. This is supported by two Quranic verses (59:11-12 and 48:15) where God declares the hypocrites liars because they promised to go out and aid but would not fulfill it. Thus, if God were to break His threat, He would be a liar, which is impossible. The argument is complete: breaking a threat is equivalent to lying, and lying is impossible for God.

Learning Objective

Key Takeaway
Because God's speech is eternal, His threats must be fulfilled; otherwise, His report would be false, which is impossible.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Do you ever find yourself assuming that God's threats are not as certain as His promises? How does the fact that His speech is eternal and cannot be false force you to take His warnings more seriously?
?
When you sin, do you sometimes comfort yourself with the thought that God will forgive you anyway, without considering the consequences He has threatened? What would change in your life if you truly believed that every threat from God must be fulfilled?
?
If you realized that the only way for God to be truthful is for His threats to be carried out, how would that reshape your view of repentance and the urgency of turning back to Him now?
