Challenging the Analogy from Witnessed to Unseen
Dialectical refutation: author challenges the universal analogy from witnessed to unseen foolishness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author challenges the opponents' analogy from the witnessed to the unseen by questioning its universal applicability.

Core Idea
An analogy is only as strong as its proof of universality.

This passage presents a classic kalam dialectical exchange. The opponents argue that since an action is foolish in the witnessed world, it must be foolish in the unseen world as well. The author responds not by denying the analogy outright, but by forcing the opponents to defend the universal scope of their analogy. He asks whether every instance of foolishness in the witnessed retains that judgment in the unseen, and whether an act's foolishness depends on the agent. This strategic questioning exposes the weakness of the opponents' proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation once to follow the dialectic. Note the back-and-forth structure.
Notice the conditional questions at the end.
2
Identify the opponents' claim
The opponents say: this action is foolishness in the witnessed, so it is foolishness in the unseen. That is their analogy.
They assume the analogy holds universally.
3
Analyze the author's counter-questions
The author asks two conditional questions: (1) Is everything that is foolish in the witnessed also foolish in the unseen? (2) Is an act that is foolish from one agent also foolish from another?
These questions target the scope and agent-dependence of the judgment.
4
Understand the dilemma
If the opponents answer 'No' to either, they invalidate their own proof. If they answer 'Yes', they must provide evidence for that universal claim.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
5
Reflect on the methodology
Consider how this dialectic uses logic without yet introducing positive arguments about God's transcendence.
This is a methodological move, not a doctrinal assertion.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation by argument. It records an objection posed to the author's opponents: 'Why did you consider this action foolishness?' The opponents respond by arguing from analogy: whatever counts as foolishness in the witnessed world must also be foolishness in the unseen, so God's action would be foolish. The author replies by challenging the universal applicability of this analogy. He asks a series of conditional questions: Is everything considered foolishness when done by a particular agent in the witnessed also foolishness in the unseen? And is an act that is foolish when done by one agent still foolish when done by another agent? If the opponents answer 'No', they undermine their own proof; if they answer 'Yes', they are required to provide evidence for that blanket claim. Thus the author exposes the weakness of the initial analogy without yet introducing a positive counterargument.

Refuting the Charge of Foolishness in Divine Creation
Refutation of the charge of foolishness in divine creation using the example of hidden parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the objection that God's creation without benefit would be foolish, using the distinction between the witnessed and the unseen and the example of hidden parts of creation.

Core Idea
God's creation without benefit is not foolish; the rules of the witnessed do not apply to the unseen.

This passage addresses a common objection: if God created the world without benefit to Himself, doesn't that make Him foolish like a human who does pointless acts? The author shows that the analogy between human and divine actions fails, especially when we consider parts of creation that no one can ever see or benefit from.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the argumentative flow: the author presents an objection, gives an initial response, then a counter-response from opponents, followed by a final refutation with a concrete example.
2
Identify the objection and initial response
Find where the opponents claim that an act without benefit to the agent is foolish in the witnessed. Then see how the author points out that God is not foolish despite creating without self-benefit.
The key distinction is that God is not subject to need, so the analogy fails.
3
Examine the counterexample and conclusion
The opponents refine their claim: an act is only foolish if no one benefits. The author provides a strong counterexample: parts of the earth and mountains that no creature can access or benefit from. Yet God is not foolish. This shows the difference between witnessed and unseen.
Think about why this example is effective in breaking the analogy.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that God's creation of the world without benefit to Himself would be foolish. The author begins by reaffirming the distinction between the witnessed and the unseen. He then presents an objection: in the witnessed, a person who acts without any personal benefit is called foolish. God created the world without benefit to Himself because benefit would imply need, which is a deficiency and a sign of being created. Yet God is not foolish, so the analogy between witnessed and unseen breaks down. The opponents respond by refining the criterion: in the witnessed, an act without benefit to the agent is only foolish if no one else benefits either; if others benefit, the agent is not foolish. Since God's creation benefits others, it is not foolish. The author counters by giving a concrete example: God created parts of the earth and mountains that no one sees or benefits from. If the opponents' rule were applied, such creation would be foolish—but God is not foolish. Therefore, the difference between the witnessed and the unseen leads to differing judgments, invalidating the opponents' argument.

Why Acts Benefiting Others Can Still Be Foolish
Author rebuts claim about acts benefiting others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the argument that God's acts cannot be foolish because they benefit others is invalid, and how self-interest in praise and reward underlies human judgments of foolishness.

Core Idea
Human judgments of foolishness depend on expected personal benefit, which is absent in God.

The opponents argue that any act benefiting others cannot be foolish, using examples like feeding the hungry. But the author shows that these acts are only deemed non-foolish because the agent expects praise or reward. By constructing a hypothetical where no praise or reward is expected, he demonstrates that the same act would be considered foolish. This exposes the flaw in analogical reasoning about God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the structure: claim, examples, response, hypothetical, conclusion.
2
Identify the opponents' examples
The opponents give three examples: feeding the hungry, clothing the naked, and building a fort in the desert. Note that in each, the agent does not personally benefit from the act itself.
3
Examine the author's hypothetical
The author constructs a scenario where the agent knows he will receive no praise (no one knows he built it) and no reward (prophet tells him). Despite benefiting others, the agent would be considered foolish. This is the key move.
This shows that personal benefit expectation is necessary for the act to be considered non-foolish.
4
Understand the conclusion
God cannot benefit from praise or reward, so the analogy fails. The author extends this to divine commands and prohibitions.
The argument undermines any attempt to judge God's actions by human standards.

Commentary
This passage is a direct refutation of the opponents' claim that acts benefiting others are never foolish. The author concludes that the entire basis of analogical reasoning from the witnessed to the unseen is invalid, extending this to divine commands and prohibitions.

The Wisdom of Divine Commands: An Analogy
The author uses an analogy to defend the wisdom of divine commands.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author uses a human analogy to defend the wisdom of God's commands and prohibitions, even though God does not benefit from them.

Core Idea
A wise command does not require personal benefit to the commander.

This passage presents a key argument in Islamic theology: that God's commands and prohibitions are inherently wise because they benefit His servants, even though He has no need of them. The author uses a simple analogy from everyday life to demonstrate that a command can be wise without benefiting the commander. By drawing a parallel between a human warning a blind man to avoid danger and God's guidance to humanity, the author refutes the claim that only commands that benefit the commander can be wise.

The passage uses a rhetorical question to force agreement.
2
Identify the analogy
Find the comparison: how is a human commanding a blind man to avoid harm similar to God commanding His servants? What is the key difference?
The commander gains no personal benefit in either case.
3
Understand the refutation
See how the analogy invalidates the opponents' principle that all wise acts must benefit the agent. The conclusion demolishes their foundational rule.

Commentary
This passage is an argument from analogy defending the wisdom of divine commands and prohibitions. The author presents a scenario from everyday life: if someone commands another to do something beneficial or forbids him from something harmful—like warning a blind man to avoid a well or a house with a dangerous lion—that person is not considered foolish, even though he gains no personal benefit. The opponents are forced to agree, as denial would be stubborn. The author then applies this to God: His commands and prohibitions bring benefit and repel harm from His servants, which is wisdom and not foolishness, even though God does not benefit from it. Therefore, the opponents' principle—that an act without benefit to the agent must be foolish—is entirely invalidated. The argument demolishes their foundational rule and shows that divine actions can be perfectly wise without any benefit accruing to God.

Ash'ari's Argument for Permissible Impossibility
Ash'ari argues for the permissibility of taklif ma la yutaq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Ash'ari's key argument that imposing the impossible is permissible for God because of His absolute sovereignty and the nature of commands as signs.

Core Idea
God's commands are not bound by human standards of benefit or harm.

This passage from Ash'ari's al-Nawadir presents a bold theological claim: God can command what is impossible for His servants. To justify this, Ash'ari distinguishes between human and divine command, basing his argument on God's absolute ownership of creation and the role of commands as mere signs of reward or punishment.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get a feel for Ash'ari's argument structure.
Notice the logical connectors like 'فإن' and 'لأن' that signal reasoning.
2
Identify the main claim and example
Find where Ash'ari states that taklif ma la yutaq is permissible and gives the example of commanding two opposites.
This example illustrates the extreme case of impossibility.
3
Understand the divine sovereignty principle
Ash'ari says this is consistent with his principle that God could punish eternally without sin, because He acts in His own dominion.
This is the foundational premise: God's absolute ownership.
4
Examine the contrast with human commands
Note how Ash'ari explains that in the witnessed world (al-shahid), commanding the impossible is bad because humans act for benefit or to repel harm, and they don't own the person commanded.
This contrast highlights God's transcendence.
5
Grasp the theory of signs and conclusion
Understand that obedience and disobedience are signs (amaratani) for reward and punishment, not causes of desert. Hence, God can make impossible command a sign of punishment.
The final sentence notes that both types are equally permissible but only one is revealed in law.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari position on the nature of evil. The Ash'aris define evil (sharr) as harm (ḍarar) that occurs when the one harmed is transgressing (mujāwiz) against the command of his commander. If the harm occurs without such transgression, it is not evil. To illustrate, Nasafi gives the example of a man who kills another intentionally: this inflicts harm (evil) on the victim's young child. But if the killer kills in retaliation (qiṣāṣ), even though the harm to the child is the same, he is not considered evil because he is not transgressing a command—retaliation is commanded. Applying this to God: Allah brings disbelief (kufr) into existence, but He does not transgress anyone's command (since there is no one above Him). Therefore, He is not evil by this act, just as He was not evil by creating the disbeliever (who is a greater evil), again because there is no transgression of another's command. In contrast, the servant (the human) acquires (iktisāb) disbelief and thereby transgresses the command of his Maker (God), so the servant becomes evil. Thus, the Ash'aris attribute the evil of disbelief to the servant's acquisition, not to God's creation.

Maturidi View: Evil Is the Servant's Act, Not God's

Core Idea
Evil is in the act, not in God's bringing it into existence.

The Maturidi school addresses a key theological problem: if God creates everything, how is He not responsible for evil?
