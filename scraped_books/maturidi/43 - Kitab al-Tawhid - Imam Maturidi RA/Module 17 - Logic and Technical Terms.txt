Reports: The Self-Refuting Denial
Classification of reports and argument against wholesale denial.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Maturidi classification of reports into two types and the argument that denying all reports is self-refuting and leads to ignorance.

Core Idea
Denying all reports is self-refuting because the denial itself is a report.

Maturidi opens this section by dividing reports (akhbār) into two types, but immediately focuses on the first—those who deny all reports entirely. He argues that such blanket denial is internally contradictory, because the act of denial is itself a report. Moreover, rejecting reports would strip a person of basic knowledge about themselves and the world, and it would be ingratitude for the very faculties of speech and hearing that make us human.

3
Consider the consequences of denying reports
List the things the denier would be ignorant of: his lineage, name, quiddity, substance, and everything else that comes through reports. Also note the ingratitude for speech and hearing.
This shows how essential reports are for all knowledge.

Maturidi's Proof That Accidents Are Distinct from the Body
Maturidi enumerates accidents distinct from the body and critiques the Mu'tazila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's rational proof that accidents such as motion, rest, composition, separation, annihilation, and duration are distinct from the body itself.

Core Idea
Bodies can acquire opposite states; therefore, those states are not the body itself.

Maturidi begins by establishing a common-sense observation: a body can be separated and then come together, or move and then rest. If these states were identical to the body, the body could not change without ceasing to be itself. This leads to the conclusion that motion, rest, composition, and separation are accidents distinct from the body's substance. He then extends this reasoning to annihilation and duration, and critiques the Mu'tazila for accepting part of this argument while rejecting the rest.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the logical flow from observable changes to the conclusion that accidents are distinct.
2
Identify the key states and the proof
Find the four states: motion, rest, composition, separation. Understand the argument: if a body can be moving and then resting while remaining the same body, then movement and rest cannot be identical to the body. They must be additional attributes (accidents).
Maturidi also extends this to annihilation and duration using similar reasoning.
3
Examine the critique of the Mu'tazila
Read the section where Maturidi points out the inconsistency of the Mu'tazila: they accept that motion etc. are accidents, but deny that annihilation and duration are accidents. He refutes their position by showing that it leads to the corruption of tawhid.
Consider why denying that bodies depend on God for their continuation undermines tawhid.

The Conventional Nature of Theological Terms: 'Arad vs Sifat
Maturidi discusses the correct terminology for the attribute of creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Maturidi argues that the term 'sifat' is more appropriate than 'arad' for describing the attribute of creation, based on methodological principles and Quranic usage.

Core Idea
Names in theology are conventional, not derived by reason alone.

Imam Maturidi enters a debate among theologians about whether to call the attribute of creation an 'accident' (arad) or an 'attribute' (sifat). He does not simply pick a side; instead, he sets out a principle about how theological terms should be chosen. His argument draws on the conventional nature of names, a critique of a specific claim by al-Ka'bi, and the Quran's own usage of the word 'arad. This lesson walks through his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

The passage is dense; read slowly.

This principle is key to his approach.

Maturidi criticizes al-Ka'bi's automatic conclusion that whatever is not a body must be an accident. He then shows that the Quran uses 'arad to mean worldly goods, making 'sifat' a more appropriate Islamic term.
Notice he appeals to scripture over pure reason.

There is also a fourth meaning mentioned: that it is interpreted as qaṣd (intent/purpose), and some literary scholars have directed this understanding. These multiple acceptable interpretations demonstrate that istiwa can be understood in various ways without implying anthropomorphism.

If obligation required the actual power (which occurs at the moment of action), then no one would be obligated until that power arises, which would lead to a regress. For example, Hajj is not obligatory until the power to perform it arrives, but that power only comes after traveling, yet travel itself is not obligatory if Hajj is not obligatory. Similarly, jihad is obligatory even if at the time of the call a person does not yet have the means to reach the battlefield; the obligation was binding on those who stayed behind. Likewise, prayer and fasting can be substituted (e.g., by substitution in prayer for the sick), showing that the real power may be achieved by effort, but the obligation is established by conditions. Maturidi then notes that powers do not persist, and what is borne by them is not existent—yet obligation remains. Zakat is obligatory on property and conditions even if payment might be hindered. The widespread practice of asking God for help in worship proves that if power were already fully present, asking would be unjust; rather, the command to worship is a command to exert effort. Thus, obligation is fixed regardless of the actual power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi argues that attributing effective will to Iblis or any sinner is rationally and theologically impossible.

Core Idea
If evil's will were effective, good would be absent from the world, but good exists.

Maturidi next addresses a potential challenge: what if someone claims that Iblis's will can bring about events? He shows the absurd consequences: if Iblis's will were effective, then evil would be limitless and good would disappear from existence. This leads to the conclusion that all realities are by Allah's will alone, and attributing effective will to anyone else is rationally and theologically unsound.
