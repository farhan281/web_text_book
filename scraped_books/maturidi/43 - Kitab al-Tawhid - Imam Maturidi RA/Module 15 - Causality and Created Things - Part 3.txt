Human Actions as Stronger Evidence for God's Power
Maturidi argues that human actions are stronger evidence than the cosmos.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Maturidi considers the creation of human actions to be stronger evidence for God's power than the creation of the heavens and earth.

Core Idea
Your own limitations prove God's power more clearly than the cosmos.

Maturidi presents a powerful rational argument: the evidence from the creation of each person's act is greater than the evidence from the creation of the universe. This is because every human being directly experiences their inability to fully control their own actions, revealing that the act is determined by God. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the comparison
Maturidi claims that the evidence from human acts is greater than from the heavens and earth. Ask yourself why that might be.
2
Analyze the rational limitation
He argues that no one has examined all the powers of substances to know the possibility of creating like them. So the evidence from the cosmos is indirect.
3
Reflect on personal experience
Everyone knows that they cannot perform acts beyond their capacity, and their acts often fall short of their intentions. This shows the act is created by God.
4
Link to practical example
Consider things like flying or swimming—substances have capacities that others do not. Similarly, your own act is limited, pointing to the Creator.

Commentary
This passage presents a sustained argument by Maturidi that the creation of human actions provides stronger evidence for God's power than the creation of the heavens and earth. It begins by citing several Quranic verses where God attributes the creation of various things—like compassion in hearts, faith, houses, and hardness of hearts—to Himself, establishing that all such acts are His creation. Maturidi then argues that the evidence from the creation of every individual's act is greater, because reason cannot fully comprehend the creation of the cosmos, but every person experiences the limitation of their own power: they cannot produce an act at will, and their acts often fall short of their intentions. This proves that the act is determined and brought forth by God. The passage then addresses the Mu'tazila position, which holds that God only creates existence from non-existence and that human acts are independent. Maturidi argues that without the framework of command and prohibition, reason would not attribute any act to another agent. He further contends that denying God's power over human acts would lead to unacceptable consequences, such as making God's power dependent on another, which is blasphemy.

Refuting the Mu'tazila on Human Acts
Maturidi refutes Mu'tazila on human acts and warns against limiting God's power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's refutation of the Mu'tazila claim that human acts are independent of God's power, and the logical consequences of denying God's power over such acts.

Core Idea
Without God's direct creation of acts, reason would not attribute any act to another.

Maturidi now directly addresses the Mu'tazila position, which holds that God only creates existence from non-existence, and that human acts are independent. He argues that this view collapses because without the framework of divine command and prohibition, reason would never attribute an act to anyone other than God. Moreover, denying God's power over human acts leads to the blasphemous notion that God's power is dependent on another.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazila position
Maturidi summarizes the Mu'tazila view: God only brings things into existence after non-existence, and that meaning exists in every act. Recognize this as the background.
2
Analyze the role of command and prohibition
Maturidi argues that without command and prohibition, reason would not tolerate excluding anything from God's power. So the Mu'tazila rely on revelation to limit God's power, but that is inconsistent.
3
Consider the consequence
If denying God's power over human acts were allowed, then one could also deny command and prohibition. The argument shows the contradiction in the Mu'tazila position.
4
Grasp the final warning
Maturidi concludes that denying God's power over a specific act would make God like one who is able by another, not by Himself—which is blasphemy. God is exalted above that.

Causes and Their Necessary Effects
Al-Maturidi argues that causes in the witnessed world necessitate their effects, whether by choice or necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam al-Maturidi argues for the necessary connection between causes and their effects in the witnessed world.

Core Idea
Causes and effects are inseparable in the witnessed world.

Imam al-Maturidi presents an argument from empirical observation: causes in this world always produce their effects simultaneously. He gives concrete examples to show that cause and effect are not separate events but occur together. This lesson explores that argument and its theological implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice how the author presents a series of paired examples to illustrate cause and effect.
The passage is an argument from the witnessed world (al-shahid).
2
Identify the examples
List the pairs of cause and effect that al-Maturidi mentions: exit with taking out, removal with removing, pain with hitting, pleasure with what is pleasant, fatigue with action. Observe that each cause is immediately followed by its effect.
These examples show that cause and effect are simultaneous.
3
Understand the implication
Reflect on why this observation matters. Al-Maturidi uses it to support the idea that power and action are not separate but occur together. The final example about friendship of God with faith suggests a similar inseparability in spiritual matters.
This connects to the broader theme of divine power and action.

Commentary
This passage is an empirical argument from the witnessed world (al-shahid/الشاهد). The author observes that causes are always accompanied by their effects: for example, exit (al-khuruj/الخروج) is simultaneous with taking out (al-ikhraj/الإخراج), removal with removing, pain with hitting, pleasure with what is pleasant, fatigue and exertion with action. This shows that cause and effect are not separate; they occur together. The author then mentions that choice (al-ikhtiyar/الاختيار) from that is like the friendship of God (wilayat Allah/ولاية الله) with faith (al-iman/الإيمان). The passage appears to be incomplete, but it reinforces the idea that power is not prior to action but simultaneous with it.

Commentary
This passage is a complex dialectical argument about the relationship between prior inability (ʿajz mutaqaddim), power (qudra), and the act (fiʿl). The author responds by interrogating the meaning of 'existence' (wujūd): does 'existence' mean the act completed or the act in process? Then he questions about the timing of God's seeing (yara) of the act of friendship (wilāya) and enmity (ʿadāwa): if before, impossible; if after, invalid; if during, then cause and effect exist together. Finally, he provides a tripartite summary: the act has three temporal states—non-existence before, passing away after, and existence during. These states correspond to the times when God sees the act and its causes. Power, like the act, is seen as non-existent before, passing away after, and existent simultaneously with the act. This resolves the problem by showing that power and act can coexist in the present moment of occurrence.

The passage ends with a rebuttal involving a 'feeble' (masāwil) person who says he cannot fulfill a need despite having no defect, implying that the opponent's logic is flawed. This lesson shows Maturidi's method of using analogies and reductio ad absurdum to defend the possibility of simultaneous causation between ability and act.

Human Actions: From Creatures or from Allah?
Maturidi argues that human actions are not truly their own based on subjective experience.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that human actions are not truly from creatures because they often contradict one's own judgment, knowledge, and intention, pointing to divine agency.

Core Idea
Our own actions surprise us — proving they are not entirely ours.

Maturidi addresses a key theological question: if actions appear to be from us, how can they be attributed to Allah? He responds by examining what we know about our own agency. The passage presents a subtle argument that from certain sensory and rational aspects, human actions are not truly our own, which protects divine omnipotence without negating human choice.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the literal translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
The Arabic has OCR issues; focus on the argument's logic.
2
Identify the question and response
The passage begins with a rhetorical question: 'How does this not indicate that what you mention is not from Allah?' Then Maturidi responds by referring to previous proofs.
3
Examine the basis: possible agency
Maturidi states that everyone knows they are a possible agent (fāʿil mumkin) who chooses between opposites. This self-knowledge is as certain as sight or sense.
Possible agent means one who can act but is not the ultimate creator of the action.
4
Note the three contradictions
Observe how one's actual action contradicts (a) one's own estimation of good and evil, (b) one's knowledge of place and time, and (c) one's intention for ease. These are the key evidence.
These contradictions show the action is not entirely from the agent.
5
Conclude: actions are not from creatures
From these aspects, it is established that actions are not from the creature. Denying this is to contradict one's intellect and sense.
This does not deny human responsibility; it limits the attribution of action.

Whoever seeks to affirm them from these aspects or deny them from the previous aspects is contradicting his intellect and opposing his sense, and there is no power except with Allah.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof that human actions are not truly their own from certain aspects. It begins with a question about why certain actions are not attributed to Allah, then argues that everyone knows themselves as a possible agent (fāʿil mumkin) of their actions. The argument points out that one's actions often occur contrary to one's own estimation of good and evil, contrary to one's knowledge of place and time, and contrary to one's intention of ease, while one's ability remains intact. These aspects make the actions almost sensory and visual, yet they are not truly from the agent. To deny this is to contradict one's intellect and sense.

Proof that Iman is an Action and Thus Created
Maturidi proves iman is an action through commands, prohibitions, and Qur'anic description, concluding it is created.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that iman is an action of the servant and therefore created, based on commands, prohibitions, and Qur'anic usage.

Core Idea
Iman is a divinely commanded action, not a static belief, so it must be created.

Maturidi presents a compelling proof that iman (faith) is an action performed by the servant, not merely a mental state. Drawing from commands, prohibitions, promises, and threats in revelation, he shows that such directives only apply to actions. Furthermore, the Qur'an itself describes iman as 'amal (action). From this, he concludes that iman is created, just like all other human actions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the impossibility of eternal iman
Read the opening lines where Maturidi argues that iman cannot be described as eternal because that would require it to exist before the servant.
This sets up the need for iman to be an originated action.
2
Identify the evidence from divine address
Notice the list: command to iman, prohibition of abandoning it, promise for doers, threat for rejecters. Consider why these only apply to actions.
This is the core of the proof.
3
Examine the Qur'anic description
Maturidi points out that the Qur'an calls iman 'action' and calls the believer 'one who acts'. Reflect on the literal usage.
This reinforces the rational point.
4
Conclude that iman is created
If iman is the servant's action, and all actions are created, then iman is created. Maturidi adds that it is the most noble action, so most deserving of being called created.
This ties back to broader Maturidi theology.

ثم لا يخلو من أن يكون إيمان كل أحد يوصف في الأزل بالكون بعد أن لم يكن فإن لزم الوصف له بالكون فى الأزل لزم الوصف بما في العقل دفعه وفي السمع إحالته ؛ لإحالة كون إيمان أحد فعلا له قبل كونه . والدليل أنه فى العبد ، الأمر به ، والنهي عن تركه ، ومجيء الوعد لمن أتى به ، والوعيد على من أعرض عنه ، ومحال كون ذلك كله على غير فعل. ثم الأخبار في القرآن عن الذي جاء به وتسمية ذلك عملا وتسمية صاحبه به ، والمعقول فى ذلك أن يكون هو الذي يشهد بوحدانية الله ويؤمن برسله ويعتقد ذلك ، وذلك أنه فعله . على أنه لو لم يكن [؟] بفعله فيكون سائر ما له مما لا صنع له فيه خلقا عند الجميع ، وإن كان فعله فهو عند القائلين بهذا إن كل فعل العبد مخلوق ، وقد بينا ذلك فيما نقدم ، فعلى ذلك الإيمان ، بل هو أحق أن يوصف بالخلق من سائر أفعال العبد ؛ إذ هو أعلا أفعاله وأجلها .
Then it is unavoidable that the iman of everyone be described in eternity as coming into existence after not having been. For if the description of it with coming into existence in eternity is necessary, then description with that which reason repudiates and revelation deems impossible would be necessary; because it is impossible for the iman of anyone to be an act of his before his existence. The evidence is that it is concerning the servant: the command to it, the prohibition of abandoning it, the coming of the promise for whoever performs it, and the threat on whoever turns away from it. It is impossible for all of that to be about something other than an action. Then the reports in the Qur'an about the one who brings it and the naming of that as action and the naming of its possessor by it, and what is rationally understood in that is that it is the one who testifies to the oneness of Allah and believes in His messengers and believes that, and that is his action. And if it were not [؟] by his action, then all his other things that he has no hand in would be creation in the view of everyone. And if it is his action, then according to those who say that every act of the servant is created; we have already explained that in what preceded. So on that basis, iman is, rather, more deserving of being described as created than the other acts of the servant, because it is the highest and most noble of his acts.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof that iman is an action of the servant and therefore created. Maturidi first argues that iman cannot be described as eternal (existing from eternity) because that would require it to exist before the servant, which is impossible. He then provides evidence: there are commands to perform iman and prohibitions from abandoning it, as well as promises and threats. Such directives only apply to actions. The Qur'an calls iman 'amal (action) and refers to the believer as one who acts. Moreover, if iman were not the servant's action, then all other things that the servant does not produce would be considered created by God, but everyone agrees those are not the servant's actions. If iman is his action, then according to the view that all actions of the servant are created, iman is created. In fact, it is more deserving of being called created because it is the highest and most noble action of the servant.
