Divisions of Hearing and the Trial of Stopping
Maturidi divides hearing into four categories and explains the trial of stopping.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four divisions of hearing (clear, ambiguous, explained, obscure) and how they guide the believer to act or refrain, culminating in the trial of stopping.

Core Idea
Hearing is divided into clear and ambiguous; the obscure must be interpreted in light of the explained, and stopping is a trial.

Maturidi further subdivides hearing into four categories: unambiguous (muḥkam), ambiguous (mutashābih), explained (mufassar), and obscure (mubham). This classification shows the limits of knowledge: some texts require action, others require stopping. The one who interprets the obscure by the explained adheres to the clear and submits the ambiguous to it. But there is also a trial in stopping – knowing when not to delve further.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic and translation, noting the four terms: محكم, متشابه, مفسر, مبهم.
These are technical terms in Quranic interpretation.
2
Identify the four divisions
Maturidi lists: muḥkam (clear), mutashābih (ambiguous), mufassar (explained), mubham (obscure).
Some count these as two pairs: muḥkam/mutashābih and mufassar/mubham.
3
Understand the rule for interpretation
The obscure (mubham) should be carried to the explained (mufassar); then one commits to the unambiguous (muḥkam) and presents the ambiguous (mutashābih) to it as much as possible.
This is a method for resolving ambiguity.
4
Grasp the trial of stopping
If knowing the reality of something is not necessary, one may refrain from delving, and that refraining becomes a trial (mihnat al-wuqūf).
This shows that not all questions need to be answered.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the divine test and the means of knowledge. Maturidi begins by stating that God tested humans because their intellects were unwilling to bear certain things, and they naturally inclined to good deeds and avoided evil. He then explains that God made the test consist of two pairs: difficult and easy, hard and soft, so that humans would engage both with effort, not passively. The ultimate goal is to attain virtue through knowledge (al-‘ilm). God made knowledge two types: manifest (ẓāhir) and hidden (khafī), so that people of intellect (awā’il al-‘aqli) could excel according to their striving and endurance against natural aversion. The path to knowledge is also two: direct observation (al-‘iyān) which is infallible and serves as the foundation for hidden knowledge, and hearing (al-sam‘) which requires verification through observable signs. Hearing is further divided into muḥkam (clear) and mutashābih (ambiguous), and mufassar (explained) and mubham (obscure). This division is meant to guide action: when the obscure is interpreted in light of the explained, one adheres to the unambiguous and submits the ambiguous to it as far as possible, either pursuing necessary knowledge or abstaining when the reality is not needed. This abstention itself becomes a trial of stopping (mihnat al-wuqūf).

The Quran's Divine Origin and Human Reasons for Disagreement
Maturidi argues the Quran's divine origin and explains why people disagree over its clear verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's argument that the Quran's internal consistency proves its divine origin and that human shortcomings—not the text—cause disagreement over its clear verses.

Core Idea
Disagreement over the Quran reveals human flaws, not divine ambiguity.

Maturidi begins by citing a Quranic verse that challenges any claim of human authorship: if the Quran were from other than God, it would contain many contradictions. But since it is free of contradiction, it must be from God. He then tackles a natural objection: if the Quran is clear, why do people disagree about it? His answer is a masterclass in theological psychology—the fault lies not in the scripture, but in the human heart and mind. In this lesson, we will unpack his argument and examine the six human factors that cause people to mistake the clear (muḥkam) for the ambiguous (mutashābih).

1

The passage is dense; don't worry about every detail on the first read.
2

He references Quran 4:82 indirectly.
3

Some factors overlap slightly; try to distinguish them.

This shows the deeper theological stake.
5
Reflect on the misconception

The passage directly addresses this.

Commentary
This passage is a combined proof and explanation. Maturidi begins by citing Quran 4:82, arguing that the Quran's lack of contradiction proves it is from God. He then addresses the problem of why people disagree over its clear verses (muḥkam). He states that the disagreement is not due to any deficiency in the Quran itself, but rather due to a set of human factors. These include: (1) the natural inclination of one's substance toward pleasure-seeking, (2) attachment to custom and habit, (3) blind following (taqlid) of someone trusted, (4) laziness in seeking knowledge, (5) overconfidence in one's own intellect leading one to equate human reason with divine wisdom instead of submitting to revelation, and (6) negligence in thorough investigation. Because of these, people mistake muḥkam for mutashābih. Maturidi then connects this to the universal testimony of all things to God's oneness (tawḥīd), indicating that those who turn away from tawḥīd are misled by these factors.

Intellect as Proof Over Nature
Maturidi establishes intellect as God's proof and obligates following it over nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Imam Maturidi considers the intellect to be God's proof (ḥujja) for moral obligation, superior to natural inclination.

Core Idea
Intellect is God's proof; nature is not the measure of good and evil.

Maturidi begins by establishing that God made intellects a proof, not natural dispositions. He argues that those with sound intellect are obligated to follow what reason shows as good, even if nature recoils, and to avoid what reason deems ugly, even if nature accepts it. This sets the intellect as the decisive authority in moral judgment.

Commentary
This passage is an argument establishing the intellect ('aql) as the primary criterion for moral and rational judgment, in contrast to nature (tab') or natural disposition. The author, Imam Maturidi, begins by stating that God made intellects a proof (hujja), not the inclination of natures. He explains that although humans share a common nature with animals (or those lacking sound intellects), those with intellect are obligated to follow what reason shows as good, even if nature dislikes it, and to avoid what reason shows as ugly, even if nature accepts it. The intellect perceives the true reality of things, while nature (specifically the nature of substance) only perceives the present and cannot represent the absent. Intellect can bring the absent to nature, making it like a witness, easing the trial of enduring what nature dislikes. Similarly, words and expressions vary in beauty to the ears but do not change the actual rights (haqq) because the good is intrinsic. Therefore, the goodness of things is not judged by natural disposition or eloquence of speech, but by the intellect, which never sees good as ugly. The intellect is the principle (asl) to which all matters must be referred, akin to immediate knowledge ('ilm al-'iyan) that is unchanging and cannot be contradicted by ignorance. It is the foundation for every hidden matter and for every natural matter. Maturidi then notes a consequence: because natures often oppose what reason shows as good or ugly, many people fail to perceive correctly. Thus, the clear (muhkam) becomes ambiguous (mutashabih) to them, and vice versa. The passage ends with a supplication to be protected from confusing truth and falsehood. The argument emphasizes the primacy of intellect over subjective nature in discerning objective moral and rational truths.

Commentary
This passage is an argument establishing the intellect ('aql) as the primary criterion for moral and rational judgment, in contrast to nature (tab') or natural disposition. The author, Imam Maturidi, begins by stating that God made intellects a proof (hujja), not the inclination of natures. He explains that although humans share a common nature with animals (or those lacking sound intellects), those with intellect are obligated to follow what reason shows as good, even if nature dislikes it, and to avoid what reason shows as ugly, even if nature accepts it. The intellect perceives the true reality of things, while nature (specifically the nature of substance) only perceives the present and cannot represent the absent. Intellect can bring the absent to nature, making it like a witness, easing the trial of enduring what nature dislikes. Similarly, words and expressions vary in beauty to the ears but do not change the actual rights (haqq) because the good is intrinsic. The first aspect: the threat in the verse could be interpreted as condoning sin and taking the command and prohibition lightly, which would undermine the motivation for forgiveness. Threat verses are definitive and should not be diverted. The three aspects collectively defend the orthodox position that the verse on forgiveness applies to those God wills, not automatically to all minor sins, and that the threat remains serious.
