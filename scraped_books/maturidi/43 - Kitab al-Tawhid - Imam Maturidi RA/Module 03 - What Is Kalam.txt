The Three Ways of Knowledge and Testimony as Proof for Contingency
Maturidi introduces the three ways of knowing and explains the first way: testimony.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The universe is contingent because revelation and human experience both confirm that nothing claims eternity.

Imam al-Maturidi begins his proof for the contingency of all entities by identifying three fundamental sources of knowledge. The first of these is testimony, which includes both divine revelation and the universal human observation that no living being has ever claimed to be eternal. This lesson unpacks that argument and shows how it establishes the createdness of all things.

Definitions of Hadath and Baqa' and the Twofold Argument
Maturidi defines hadath and baqa' and presents a twofold argument against a critic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definitions of hadath and baqa' as used by Maturidi and how he constructs a twofold argument against a critic who claims pre-eternity for certain entities.

Core Idea
Hadath defined as 'existence after non-existence' makes any claim of pre-eternity self-contradictory.

This passage introduces two central terms in Islamic theology—hadath (temporal origination) and baqa' (remaining). Maturidi defines them not merely as lexical terms but as logical premises for a powerful argument. He then deploys a twofold attack against an opponent who attempts to use revelation to argue for the pre-eternity of some entities. By exposing a contradiction and forcing a dilemma, Maturidi strengthens the case for the temporality of all entities (a'yan).

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic text once to get a feel for its structure. Then read the literal translation. Notice the concise, argumentative style.
Pay attention to the terms 'حدث' and 'بقاء'.
2
Understand the definition of hadath
Maturidi defines hadath as 'existence after non-existence'. This definition implies that anything that has this property cannot be pre-eternal (qadim). If something has the 'reality' of hadath (i.e., its essence involves being preceded by non-existence), then affirming qidam for it is contradictory.
This is the first prong of the argument.
3
Examine the definition of baqa' and the second argument
Baqa' is defined as 'existence in future time', irrespective of whether it is accompanied by something else or not. The opponent's claim about baqa' is based on revelation (sam'i). Maturidi turns this against the opponent by offering a dilemma: either accept that revelation proves baqa', then it also proves the temporality of entities (a'yan) through the same method; or reject revelation, then the opponent's own argument collapses.
This is the second prong, a classic dilemma.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Consider how these two arguments together support the position that all entities (a'yan) are temporally originated. The first argument is rational (based on the definition), the second is dialectical (turning the opponent's own method against them).

The Term 'Thing' as Applied to God
Maturidi defines 'thing' as mere affirmation and applies it to God

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Maturidi's definition of 'thing' as mere affirmation and how it applies to God without implying composition.

Core Idea
'Thing' is pure affirmation, not a description of essence.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that faith (imān) is only verbal confession (iqrār bi-l-lisān) with no role for the heart. Maturidi begins by stating the opposing view: some say faith is only confession with the tongue and nothing in the heart. Then he presents his own position: faith properly belongs to the hearts, through both hearing (samʿ, i.e., revelation) and reason (ʿaql). He then provides a series of Quranic proofs to support this. First, he cites the hypocrites who said 'We believe' with their mouths but whose hearts did not believe (Q 2:8? actually 2:8 is about hypocrites, but he also quotes Q 49:14 about the Bedouins: they say 'We believe' but faith has not entered their hearts). God invalidates their claim as faith. Next, Q 49:17: they consider it a favor that they submitted, but God says He guided them to faith if they are truthful—implying that if they were truthful in their claim, they would be guided, but the hypocrites are not truthful. Maturidi argues: if faith were only tongue, then uttering it would make them truthful, but God denies that. Then Q 60:10: concerning believing women emigrants, God says He knows best their faith—if faith were only speech, everyone would know it equally. Then Q 9:56: they swear they are from you (the believers), but they are lying. Then Q 4:65: 'But no, by your Lord, they will not believe until they make you judge...'—God negates their faith due to the presence of distress in their souls, which is internal, showing that internal state matters. Then Q 4:25: 'from your believing slave-girls' and then 'God knows best your faith'—showing faith is a hidden reality known only to God. Then Q 2:8: some people say 'We believe in God and the Last Day' but they are not believers—their speech alone does not make them believers if their hearts differ. Then Maturidi uses a theological argument: God promised believers eternal reward and hypocrites the lowest depth of fire. If their outward show were real faith, they would deserve paradise, not increased punishment. Then Q 2:9: they deceive God and the believers—their manifest faith is actually deception. So claiming that the religion of Islam and faith can be attained through deception is ignorance. Finally, he cites Q 63:6: it is all the same whether you ask forgiveness for the hypocrites or not, and Q 9:54: their expenditures are not accepted because they disbelieved. This shows that their outward acts are not accepted because of lack of inner faith. The passage thus systematically refutes the notion that faith is merely verbal, establishing that true faith resides in the heart and is verified by God.

The Consequence of Verbal-Only Faith: Hypocrisy and Divine Justice
Maturidi uses divine reward and punishment to argue against verbal-only faith.

Learning Objective

If mere words were faith, hypocrites would be promised Paradise, but God promises them Hell.

Since God promises believers eternal reward and hypocrites the lowest depth of Hell, their outward show cannot be real faith, otherwise they would deserve Paradise. He then warns against thinking that the religion of Islam can be attained through deception.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that faith (imān) is only verbal confession (iqrār bi-l-lisān) with no role for the heart. Maturidi begins by stating the opposing view: some say faith is only confession with the tongue and nothing in the heart. Then he presents his own position: faith properly belongs to the hearts, through both hearing (samʿ, i.e., revelation) and reason (ʿaql). He then provides a series of Quranic proofs to support this. First, he cites the hypocrites who said 'We believe' with their mouths but whose hearts did not believe (Q 2:8? actually 2:8 is about hypocrites, but he also quotes Q 49:14 about the Bedouins: they say 'We believe' but faith has not entered their hearts). God invalidates their claim as faith. Next, Q 49:17: they consider it a favor that they submitted, but God says He guided them to faith if they are truthful—implying that if they were truthful in their claim, they would be guided, but the hypocrites are not truthful. Maturidi argues: if faith were only tongue, then uttering it would make them truthful, but God denies that. Then Q 60:10: concerning believing women emigrants, God says He knows best their faith—if faith were only speech, everyone would know it equally. Then Q 9:56: they swear they are from you (the believers), but they are lying. Then Q 4:65: 'But no, by your Lord, they will not believe until they make you judge...'—God negates their faith due to the presence of distress in their souls, which is internal, showing that internal state matters. Then Q 4:25: 'from your believing slave-girls' and then 'God knows best your faith'—showing faith is a hidden reality known only to God. Then Q 2:8: some people say 'We believe in God and the Last Day' but they are not believers—their speech alone does not make them believers if their hearts differ. Then Maturidi uses a theological argument: God promised believers eternal reward and hypocrites the lowest depth of fire. If their outward show were real faith, they would deserve paradise, not increased punishment. Then Q 2:9: they deceive God and the believers—their manifest faith is actually deception. So claiming that the religion of Islam and faith can be attained through deception is ignorance. Finally, he cites Q 63:6: it is all the same whether you ask forgiveness for the hypocrites or not, and Q 9:54: their expenditures are not accepted because they disbelieved. This shows that their outward acts are not accepted because of lack of inner faith. The passage thus systematically refutes the notion that faith is merely verbal, establishing that true faith resides in the heart and is verified by God.

Commentary
This passage is a multi-faceted argument defending the position that iman (faith) is primarily an inner reality of the heart, not merely a verbal declaration by the tongue. The author, Imam al-Maturidi, begins by citing Qur'anic verses about fighting polytheists based on their outward manifest shirk, not their inner beliefs. He argues that the command to fight is not proof that shirk or kufr is in the heart; rather, fighting is prevented when people verbally profess faith even if the true place of faith is the heart. Next, he provides a rational argument: faith is a religious bond, and religions are held in the heart; linguistically, iman means tasdiq (affirmation), which is an act of the heart. He asserts that Allah has placed the highest degree of iman in the hearts, making it the basis for good deeds and the validity of worship. Only hearts, not tongues, can bear this reality. The address of faith is directed to intellects, and the reality of iman is known through thought and reflection—acts of the heart. He then employs scriptural proofs: the verse 'There is no compulsion in religion' shows that iman cannot be located in something subject to coercion. Similarly, in the verse about disbelieving in taghut and believing in Allah, the disavowal is not merely by the tongue, so the same applies to iman. He references the verse about those who claim to believe but turn to taghut, demonstrating that their verbal claim is contradicted by their actions, thus iman is not just speech. Finally, he highlights the Qur'anic address 'O you who have believed,' used numerous times. Those addressed were already believers in their hearts at the time of revelation, even if not actively reciting the shahada. This proves that the reality of iman must be in the heart. He concludes that these verses refute the Mu'tazila, Khawarij, Karramiyya, and Hashwiyya, who in various ways err on the nature of faith.

He concludes that the established principle is that knowledge is not identical to affirmation; rather, it is a cause that leads to affirmation. The passage ends with 'And Allah knows.'

Question: Is iman affirmation by the heart or knowledge? A group thought that there is no affirmation by the heart, but only specific knowledge. The origin is that it is, even if one cannot point to that with a letter except by way of indication through what is well-known from speech: that iman is affirmation in language, and kufr is denial, or covering; the superiority of knowledge is in reality over ignorance and denial. And [someone] would not be ignorant of something or a denier of it from the perspective of knowledge, as a group said: deniers, meaning they do not know. Likewise, every true ignorance is not described as denial of it. Thus it is established that iman by the heart in reality is other than knowledge. However, knowledge is a cause that impels to affirmation just as ignorance sometimes impels to denial; thus each has a meaning that the other does not have in reality. On this is based the saying of those who say: iman is knowledge – rather it is affirmation, because knowledge is what impels to it, so [iman] is named after it, just as iman is described as a gift of Allah, His favor and mercy, and the like, by that which is attained, not that in reality it is the act of Allah, but its reality does not lack that, so it is attributed to Him. Similarly is the matter of attribution to knowledge and cognition. And that is also just as every sin of the believer is called ignorance and every misdeed of the disbeliever is called forgetfulness, and likewise the believer, because of ignorance, magnifies what befalls him or forgetfulness, or because every forgotten thing is abandoned, so it is named after that, not that it is the name of its reality. And Allah is the granter of success. And on this, it is permissible to say: iman in all the messengers, without saying: knowledge of all the messengers in the hearts. And on that is His saying: "Whoever disbelieves in Allah after his iman, except one who is coerced while his heart is tranquil in iman" – if there were in the heart only knowledge, disbelief would not remove it, nor would the condition be useful in that. And a person may choose for repelling coercion other than what is true in his view to repel that from himself, so the condition of tranquility of the heart is required. And likewise the saying to Ibrahim: "Did you not have iman?" He said: "Yes." It would only be said: "Did you not have iman in my report?" or "in what I said?" He said: "Yes." It was not said: "Did you not know?" And there is no power except through Allah. For cognitions sometimes occur about things without causes that cannot be described as having iman in them. And likewise His saying: "Whoever disbelieves in the taghut and has iman in Allah" – it is denial of the taghut in what they claim, iman in Allah, not as a statement, but as the reality of rejection and denial in the heart, and acceptance and affirmation of Allah. And the basis in that is more established than the common matter: that not every knowledgeable person about something is described as having denial of it, nor every knower of it is described as having affirmation of it; rather, knowledge impels to affirmation and ignorance impels to denial, so it is named after the cause, not the reality. Commentary
This passage is a definitional argument and proof. Maturidi begins by stating the question: Is iman (faith) tasdiq (affirmation) by the heart or ma'rifa (knowledge)? He reports the view of a group that says there is no tasdiq in the heart, only ma'rifa. Maturidi then asserts the correct position: iman is tasdiq. He provides linguistic evidence that iman means tasdiq in language and kufr means takdhib (denial) or covering. He argues that ma'rifa is distinct from tasdiq because one can know something without affirming it as true (e.g., a person may know a fact but deny it). He explains that ma'rifa is a cause that impels to tasdiq, just as ignorance impels to takdhib. Therefore, tasdiq and ma'rifa have different realities. Maturidi then addresses those who say iman is ma'rifa: they mean tasdiq, but they name iman after its cause (ma'rifa), just as iman is sometimes called a gift or mercy of Allah, even though those are not the reality of iman but attributes related to it. He analogizes this to calling a believer's sin 'ignorance' or a disbeliever's misdeed 'forgetfulness'—these are labels based on cause, not essence. He then provides Quranic proofs: First, the verse about compulsion (Q 16:106) shows that iman can be retained in the heart under coercion, but if iman were merely ma'rifa, it could not be removed by outward disbelief, and the condition of 'tranquility of the heart' would be meaningless. Second, the verse where Allah asks Ibrahim 'Did you not have iman?' (Q 2:260) uses 'iman' not 'knowledge', indicating iman is more than knowledge. Third, the verse about kufr bi al-taghut and iman billah (Q 2:256) shows that kufr means denial in the heart and iman means acceptance and affirmation. He concludes that the established principle is that knowledge is not identical to affirmation; rather, it is a cause that leads to affirmation. The passage ends with 'And Allah knows.'

Quranic Evidence that Iman is Affirmation
Maturidi provides Quranic evidence and a concluding principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Maturidi uses three Quranic verses to prove that iman is the heart's affirmation, distinct from mere knowledge.

Core Idea
The Quran shows that faith can remain when knowledge is suppressed.

Maturidi now turns to the Quran to solidify his case. He cites three key verses: the compulsion verse (Q 16:106), Ibrahim's question (Q 2:260), and the taghut verse (Q 2:256). Each highlights that iman involves the heart's tranquil affirmation, not just intellectual awareness. This lesson unpacks these proofs and concludes with the established principle.
