The Virtues of Mecca as Signs of Prophethood
Nasafi enumerates miraculous signs of Mecca as proofs of prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the miraculous signs and historical honors of Mecca serve as evidence for the prophethood of Muhammad.

Core Idea
Mecca's miraculous signs prove prophethood, not the merits of its people.

Imam Nasafi presents the various honors and miracles associated with Mecca, the Prophet's birthplace, as clear signs of his prophethood. He lists its religious significance—from Ibrahim's vestiges to the destruction of the Elephant army—and then clarifies that these signs do not reflect the virtue of Meccans, who were polytheists. This passage shows how Allah uses place-based miracles to support His prophets.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the continuous Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Notice the flow from general honors to specific miracles and then the concluding remark about the polytheism of the Meccans.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the categories of signs
The passage lists multiple types of signs: historical honors (Ibrahim's vestiges, birthplace of Ismail), natural phenomena (safety of wild animals, birds avoiding the roof), and a historical event (the Elephant incident). Identify each category.
Nasafi calls these 'clear signs, manifest proofs, and successive lessons.'
3
Analyze the Elephant incident as a sufficient sign
Nasafi singles out the destruction of the Elephant army as sufficient alone to show Mecca's greatness. Consider why this event is especially miraculous.
The birds carried stones of baked clay from the sea—a clear divine intervention.
4
Understand the concluding point
Nasafi concludes that these signs are for the sake of the Prophet, not for the Meccans themselves, because they were polytheists. Reflect on how this reinforces prophethood.
This prevents the misconception that Mecca's virtues are independent of the Prophet.

Commentary
This passage is a description of the miraculous signs associated with Mecca, the Prophet's birthplace, as evidence of his prophethood. Nasafi begins by listing the historical and religious honors of Mecca: it is the place of Ibrahim's vestiges, birthplace of Ismail, pilgrimage site, pride of Arabs, the center of Arabia, and the first house of worship. He emphasizes its sanctity as the sanctuary of Allah and the qibla. Then he mentions phenomenal signs: wild animals are safe there, birds avoid the roof of the Kaaba, the Station of Ibrahim, and the greatest sign—the destruction of the army of the Elephant by birds carrying stones. Nasafi argues that even this one sign is sufficient to show Mecca's exalted status. He then notes that despite these signs, the people of Mecca at the time of the Prophet were polytheists and disbelievers, demonstrating that these signs are for the Prophet's sake, not for their own merit.

The Long Period Between Prophets as Proof of Prophethood
Imam Nasafi presents the long interval between prophets as proof of Muhammad's prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the long interval between prophets and the world's desperate state serve as proof of the Prophet's divine mission.

Core Idea
The world's corruption before the Prophet proves he was sent by God.

This passage from Imam Nasafi presents a distinctive proof of prophethood based on historical conditions. He argues that the timing of the Prophet's mission—during a period of profound spiritual and moral decay—demonstrates that it was a divine response to human need. Understanding this argument deepens one's appreciation of how kalam thinkers used historical reasoning alongside rational and scriptural proofs.

3
Understand the argument

Commentary
This passage is a proof based on the condition of the world at the time of the Prophet's mission. Imam Nasafi presents the long interval (fatrah) between prophets as a key argument. He describes the era as one of complete religious decay: the entire legal systems and divine judgments had been forgotten, guides to the true (ḥanīfī) religion were nonexistent, injustice was rampant, and people were ruled by beastly nature. The unqualified were given leadership, and oppression was everywhere. It was in such a context that Allah sent His Prophet as a mercy and as a means to establish the truth, remove excuses, and provide a definitive proof (ḥujjah) for all people. The implication is that the coming of the Prophet at such a time, when guidance was utterly lost, indicates that it was a divine response, not a human invention. This is a common kalam argument: the timing and conditions of the Prophet's appearance demonstrate that he was sent by God to reform a corrupt world.

The Elephant Incident as Proof of Makkah's Honor and Prophethood
Imam Nasafi cites the Elephant incident to argue for Makkah's honor and the truth of the Prophet's mission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Imam Nasafi uses the historical event of the Elephant to argue for the special status of Makkah and the truth of the Prophet's mission.

Core Idea
If God honored a city with clear signs, then a prophet from that city must be truthful.

Imam Nasafi presents a unique argument: the well-known story of the Elephant, confirmed by the Quran and eyewitnesses, proves that Makkah is divinely honored. Therefore, any prophet emerging from such a city cannot be a liar. This lesson unpacks that argument, showing how historical consensus and divine signs work together to support prophethood.

Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic and English to get the flow of the argument. Notice the opening poetry and the reference to Surah Al-Fil.

Identify the historical proof

The Quranic citation reinforces that this is not just a story but divine revelation.
3
Follow the logical link to prophethood
The argument connects the honor of Makkah (shown by the sign) to the truthfulness of any prophet from that city. The rhetorical question drives the point home.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (istidlal) using the historical incident of the Elephant to establish the special honor of Makkah and, by extension, the truth of the Prophet's mission. The author begins by quoting a line of poetry from Umayyah ibn Abi al-Salt, which refers to the clear signs of the Lord, and then mentions the Elephant stopping at al-Mughammas as if crippled. He states that this event is so well-known that it cannot be denied. He then cites the Quranic surah Al-Fil, noting that even the polytheists did not reject or falsify the story because they knew its truth; some eyewitnesses were still alive. Building on this, he argues that since Makkah is a town of such high status and clear divine signs, it is inconceivable that a person from that town—especially one who calls to truth—would be a liar. Anyone claiming prophethood from such an honored place must be truthful, because God would not allow a false prophet to arise from a place He has so clearly marked with honor. The argument concludes by stating that this is obvious to anyone who abandons obstinacy and follows guidance.

Core Idea
The state of the world itself is a clear proof for prophethood.

After describing the corruption, the author poses a rhetorical question: could God leave people in such bewilderment without guidance? He answers with a strong denial. This lesson examines that argument and its conclusion: that only Muhammad (s) could have been sent as a guide, and his coming is itself a proof for those who seek truth.

The passage concludes that this argument—the world’s corruption and the need for a prophet—is a clear proof in itself, sufficient to establish the obligation of following him.

Commentary
This passage provides historical support for the necessity of prophethood. It begins by stating that people needed both religious teachers to guide them in spiritual and worldly matters, and just rulers to maintain order and justice. Then the author examines the Persian empire: once the mightiest, but weakened due to the blasphemy of their king Abruwayz (who claimed divinity), leading to God's punishment through his son Sheerwayh. This caused a chain of decline with women and children ruling, and oppression spreading. Similarly, Yemen fell to Abyssinians, Hira collapsed after the killing of Nu'man, and the Ghassanids weakened.
