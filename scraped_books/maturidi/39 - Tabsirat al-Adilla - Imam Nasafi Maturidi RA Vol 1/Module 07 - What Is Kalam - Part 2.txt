The Two Divisions of Capability in Kalam
Definition of istita'a and its two divisions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that capability (istita'a) in Islamic theology has two distinct divisions: one as the safety of causes and instruments, and another as a created accident that directly causes actions.

Core Idea
Capability is twofold: a condition and a cause.

Theologians often use terms like capability, power, and strength interchangeably when referring to human capacity for action. But beneath these synonymous terms lies a crucial distinction: capability has two aspects, one that precedes action and one that is directly involved in producing it. The following passage from Imam al-Nasafi defines these two divisions and clarifies their roles in voluntary actions.

Guided Path
1
Read and understand the synonymous terms
The passage begins by equating istita'a, quwwa, qudra, and taqa as synonyms in kalam. Note how they are used interchangeably for servants.
This sets the stage for the division.
2
Identify the first division: safety of causes
Look for the description of the first type: safety of causes and soundness of instruments. Understand that it precedes actions and is a preparation, not a cause.
It is a blessing from Allah.
3
Comprehend its definition
The first division is defined as 'preparedness to execute the action when the chooser wills.' Recognize that this is a condition for action.
4
Identify the second division: created accident
The second type cannot be defined plainly; it is an accident created by Allah in the living being, which directly causes voluntary actions.
It is called the 'cause' (illa).
5
Compare the two divisions
Note that the first is a condition (safety of means) that may exist before action, while the second is the actual cause that exists at the time of action.
This distinction is key to Maturidi theology.

Commentary
This passage defines capability (istita'a) and its divisions according to the theologians. First, it clarifies that terms like istita'a, quwwa, qudra, and taqa are essentially synonymous when applied to servants in kalam discourse, much like synonyms in language. Then it states that what is called power and capability is divided into two types. The first type is 'safety of causes and soundness of instruments' – a state of health and means that may exist before the action. This is not a cause but a condition for actions; it is a blessing from God for which gratitude is due. This type is defined as 'preparedness to execute the action when the chooser wills'. The second type is a meaning that cannot be pointed to directly; it is an accident created by God in the living being by which he performs voluntary actions, and it is the cause (illa) of the action. Thus, capability has a precursory aspect (safety of means) and a concurrent aspect (the created power that causes the action).

Al-Isfarayini's Argument Against Taklif Ma La Yutaq
Nasafi records al-Isfarayini's distinction on impossibility of taklif ma la yutaq based on the meaning of obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini's argument that taklif ma la yutaq is impossible because the concept of obligation inherently requires the possibility of deserving punishment for violation.

Core Idea
Taklif is impossible for the incapable because obligation loses its meaning without the threat of punishment.

In this passage, Nasafi records a subtle argument from Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini, an influential Ash'ari theologian. Al-Isfarayini agrees with the people of truth that obliging the impossible is impossible, but he bases this not on divine justice or ugliness, but on the very logic of what 'obligation' means. Let's explore his reasoning and see how he defends the Ash'ari position against common objections.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Isfarayini's Position
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp al-Isfarayini's main claim and the structure of his argument.
Notice he begins by stating the impossibility and then explains why.
2
Identify the Core Argument
Identify that al-Isfarayini defines obligation (taklif) as entailing deserving punishment for violation, and argues that this cannot coexist with incapacity.
He explicitly distinguishes his reason from ugliness (qubh).
3
Distinguish the Two Cases
Notice the distinction between obliging something inherently impossible and obliging something possible that becomes impossible due to being occupied with its opposite.
In the second case, the idea of deserving punishment is still conceivable.
4
Understand the Defense
See how al-Isfarayini's framework allows for commands as subjugation (taskhir) or notification, repelling the Mu'tazilite accusation.
This clarifies that the Ash'aris do not believe in obliging the impossible in the usual sense.

وقال أبو إسحاق الأسفرائيني: قال أهل الحق يستحيل تكليف ما لا يطاق لاستحالة وجود المعنى الذي يقتضيه التكليف مع العجز لا للقبح والسفه، وهذا لأن الذي يقتضيه التكليف استحقاق نوع من العقوبة على ضرب من المخالفة، لأن التكليف إنما يتميز عما ليس بتكليف بهذا قال ولا يتوهم ذلك بغير استحقاق نوع من العقوبة على ضرب من المخالفة فعلى هذا ظهر الفرق له بين تكليف المعجوز عنه وتكليف ما ليس بمعجوز عنه إلا أنه عجز عنه لاشتغاله بضده فإن استحقاق نوع من العقوبة عليه على ضرب من المخالفة موهوم قال: ويجوز ورود ذلك على التسخير والإعلام بالعجز وإظهار القدرة. فعلى هذا يدفعون هذا التشنيع عن أنفسهم.
And Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini said: The people of truth said: It is impossible to oblige what cannot be borne because of the impossibility of the existence of the meaning that the obligation requires together with incapacity, not because of ugliness or foolishness. And this is because what obligation requires is deserving of a type of punishment for a type of violation. For obligation is distinguished from what is not obligation by this. He said, and it is not imagined without deserving a type of punishment for a type of violation. On this basis, the distinction appeared for him between obliging what one is incapable of and obliging what one is not incapable of except that he becomes incapable of it due to being occupied with its opposite, for the deserving of a type of punishment for a type of violation is imagined. He said: And it is permissible that such (obligation) occurs as subjugation (taskhir), notification of incapacity, and display of power. On this basis, they repel this accusation from themselves.

Commentary
This passage presents the view of Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini, a prominent Ash'ari theologian, who offers a nuanced position on taklif ma la yutaq. He agrees with 'the people of truth' (ahl al-haqq) that obliging what cannot be borne is impossible (mustahil), but his reasoning differs from those who say it is 'ugly' (qabih). Instead, al-Isfarayini argues that the very meaning (ma'na) of 'taklif' (obligation) necessarily entails the idea of deserving some punishment for violation. If the obligee is inherently incapable, then the threat of punishment loses its logical basis because the violation is not a free act. Thus, the impossibility is conceptual, not moral. He then distinguishes two cases: (1) something inherently impossible (ma'juz 'anhu) and (2) something possible but the person becomes unable due to being occupied with its opposite (e.g., being commanded to pray while already in prayer). In the second case, the idea of deserving punishment is still conceivable (mawhum) because the person could have chosen otherwise. Finally, al-Isfarayini notes that even the impossible type of command can occur as 'subjugation' (taskhir), notification of incapacity, or display of power—but not as genuine obligation. With this, the Ash'aris defend themselves against the Mu'tazilite accusation that they believe in taklif ma la yutaq.

Enumerates the six topics of rational proofs concerning the acts of servants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the six rational topics that structure the Maturidi discussion on the acts of servants.

Core Idea
Six rational topics frame the debate on divine and human action.

This passage presents the backbone of the Maturidi argument on human acts. Imam al-Nasafi outlines six distinct topics that must be addressed to establish a coherent position on the relationship between divine power and human action. Each topic addresses a specific challenge or misconception, and together they form a complete rational framework.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's enumeration come through.
Notice the numbered structure: first, second, third, etc.
2
Identify the six topics

Understand the flow
The first three topics deal with power and action, the fourth with methodology, the fifth with moral categories, the sixth with shirk. See how they progress from divine power to human acquisition to moral and theological concepts.

Introduction to Kasb: Beyond the Quiddity to the Act's Reality
States the need to establish the servant's act and then introduces the differing definitions of kasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Maturidi scholars prioritized establishing the servant's act over defining the quiddity of kasb, and how they distinguished kasb from khalq by the locus of power.

Core Idea
Kasb is not about defining its essence; it is about affirming the servant's act without compulsion.

After laying the foundation with two proofs—that the servant cannot create and that he indeed has an act—the author steps back from asking 'what is kasb?' He argues that the more urgent task is to show that the servant is not compelled and that his power genuinely relates to his act. Only then does he turn to the differing definitions of kasb among the scholars, beginning with one that ties kasb to the locus of power.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the shift
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Observe how the author moves from reflecting on the sufficiency of previous proofs to introducing the definition of kasb.
The passage is cut off, so focus on the first part.

Identify what is unnecessary vs. necessary
The author says there is no need to explain the quiddity (māhiyya) of kasb. Instead, he emphasizes three necessary points: the servant is not compelled, he has an act, and his power relates to his maqdūr.
This prioritization is key to Maturidi methodology.

The definition contrasts kasb with khalq, which does not occur in a locus.

Reflect on the reason for this approach

Two Definitions of Qabih in Ahl al-Hadith
Nasafi presents two definitions of qabih from Ahl al-Hadith and Abu Ishaq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two definitions of qabih (ugly/evil) held by the majority of Ahl al-Hadith and by Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini, and how they distinguish between divine and human actions.

Core Idea
Qabih is not an inherent property but depends on prohibition or harm, both absent in God.

Before rejecting the Mu'tazili use of analogy, Nasafi establishes what 'ugly' (qabīḥ) means within the Ahl al-Hadith tradition. Two definitions are given: one based on divine prohibition, another based on harm. Both ensure that God's actions can never be called ugly, while human actions can be. Understanding these definitions is key to refuting the analogy between God and creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the first definition

God is not subject to prohibition, so His creating disbelief is not ugly.
2

Core Idea
Iman by tongue alone has three versions: conditional, conditional with speech as the defining act, and unconditional.

This passage from Tabsirat al-Adilla presents three distinct theological positions that locate iman in verbal confession (iqrar). Although all three agree that iman is essentially an utterance, they differ sharply on whether inner knowledge or affirmation is required. Understanding these differences is crucial for grasping the spectrum of views on the relationship between faith and inward assent in Islamic theology.

Guided Path

This passage reports three views that define iman primarily as verbal confession (iqrar), but with significant differences. First, al-Raqqashi held that confession is iman only if there is knowledge (ma'rifa) in the heart. For him, knowledge is a necessary (daruri) thing that inevitably exists in every human; but this knowledge itself is not called iman because iman is an acquired act (fi'l muktasab), not a necessary one. Excluding Works from Iman: Al-Ash'ari's Argument
Al-Ash'ari argues against including works in iman, using the basis of loyalty as evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Ash'ari's argument for excluding works from the reality of iman, based on the proper basis of religious loyalty.

Core Idea
Works are not part of iman; loyalty is based on belief, not deeds.

In the second part of the passage, al-Ash'ari explains why works like prayer and fasting are not included in the definition of iman. He uses a practical argument: those who include works base their loyalty on verbal agreement rather than on true obedience to Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice that al-Ash'ari first distinguishes between literal and figurative usage, then gives a pragmatic consequence.
2
Note the use of tawassu'
Al-Ash'ari says that applying 'iman' to acts of worship is by way of extension (tawassu'), not literal. This is key.
Tawassu' means figurative or extended usage.
3
Understand the consequence of including works
If works were part of iman, then loyalty would depend on agreeing with that claim rather than on actual piety. This is absurd.
The conclusion: iman is solely about belief (i'tiqad).

Commentary
This passage reports the position that iman is tasdiq bi al-qalb (affirmation by the heart), which is attributed to major Sunni authorities: Abu Mansur al-Maturidi, Abu Hanifa (through transmission), al-Husayn ibn al-Fadl al-Bajali, and Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari. It notes a nuance: al-Ash'ari in some works expressed a preference for the view of al-Salihi (that iman is ma'rifa), but his well-known (mashhur) position is tasdiq. Then the passage explains why works (a'mal) like prayer and fasting are not part of the reality of iman. Al-Ash'ari argues that applying the word 'iman' to such acts is merely figurative or extended (tawassu'), not literal. The core argument is that those who include works in iman base their loyalty and enmity on agreement with their verbal formulation (the saying that works are iman) rather than on actual piety. They befriend someone who agrees with them even if he neglects God's commands, and they hate someone who disagrees with them even if that person is scrupulous in obeying God. Therefore, the proper basis for love and hate in religion should be belief (i'tiqad) itself, not the performance or non-performance of deeds. Hence iman is properly defined by inner conviction alone, and judgments about iman in this world are made on that basis, not on the observation of works.

Faith as Assent: From Opposites and Language
Nasafi presents two initial arguments: from opposites and from language.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two initial proofs that faith (iman) is essentially assent (tasdiq): the argument from its opposite (disbelief as denial) and the argument from linguistic usage.

Core Idea
Faith is defined by its opposite: disbelief is denial, so faith is affirmation.

Imam Nasafi begins by establishing the foundational meaning of faith. He points to what faith is opposed to—disbelief—which is denial and rejection, implying that faith must be the opposite: affirmation. He then strengthens this with linguistic evidence: the Arabic language itself defines iman as tasdiq, and altering this meaning would destroy language itself.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opposite argument
Nasafi argues that faith's opposite (kufr) is denial (takdhib) and rejection (juhud). By logical necessity, faith must be the opposite, which is affirmation (tasdiq).
This is a common logical technique in kalam: define by opposites.
2
Analyze the linguistic argument
Nasafi then cites the people of language (ahl al-lisan) who define iman only as tasdiq. He argues that changing this meaning would invalidate all language.
Consider why this is a strong argument for the Maturidi school.
3
See how both arguments support the same conclusion
Both arguments converge on the same point: iman is fundamentally assent, not deeds.

Faith as Assent: Qur'anic Evidence and the Status of Sinful Believers

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Qur'an separates faith from deeds and calls sinners 'believers', showing faith is distinct and unconditional.

He points out that Allah consistently distinguishes between iman and righteous deeds in the Qur'an (e.g., 'those who believe and do righteous deeds'), indicating they are separate. Moreover, Allah addresses sinners who commit major sins as 'believers', proving that iman does not require sinlessness. This is a key argument in the Maturidi position.

Analyze the address of sinners as believers
Allah addresses those who commit sins (e.g., murder) as 'O you who have believed', which would be meaningless if iman required avoiding all sins.
This is a key refutation of the view that deeds are part of iman.
3
Conclude that faith is only assent
Both evidences confirm that iman is purely affirmation, independent of actions.

Nasafi presents two concise arguments that cut to the heart of the debate: if iman were simply another name for every good deed, we would face logical absurdities. Refuting the Hadith Objection on Iman and Islam
The author refutes the hadith-based objection by distinguishing the topic of the hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the objection based on the hadith of the pillars of Islam by showing it pertains to the laws of Islam, not to iman.

Core Idea
The hadith about pillars of Islam does not conflate iman with actions.

Opponents might cite the famous hadith where the Prophet defined Islam by its pillars as evidence that actions are part of faith. The author responds by clarifying that the question was about the 'laws of Islam' (shara'i' al-Islam), not about iman. He further supports his argument by citing multiple chains of transmission and mentioning Imam al-Maturidi's record that the Prophet was asked about iman separately.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument defending the distinction between iman (faith) and Islam (submission), likely in response to those who equate the two or consider actions sufficient. The author begins by posing a question: Is a person a Muslim (and thus saved) merely by performing Islamic actions? If the opponent says yes, the author points out that this would make a heart-denier a Muslim, which contradicts the Quranic verse about hypocrites being in the lowest depth of Hell. If the opponent says no, the author asks: why not, since the person has performed what is Islam? He argues that actions cannot define the actor—for example, a person can sit without being a 'sitter'—so performing Islamic acts does not automatically make one a Muslim. He then addresses an objection about a hadith where the Prophet defined Islam by its pillars. The author responds that he is not obligated to explain the hadith; rather, he only needs to refute the opponent's argument. He asserts that in the hadith, the question was about the 'laws' (shara'i') of Islam, not about iman, and thus it does not conflate the two. Finally, he cites multiple chains of transmission to support the hadith narration, including from Abu Hanifa, and mentions that Imam al-Maturidi also recorded that the Prophet was asked about iman (implying a separate question). The passage thus serves to uphold the Ash'ari/Maturidi view that iman is solely affirmation by the heart, while Islam includes actions.
