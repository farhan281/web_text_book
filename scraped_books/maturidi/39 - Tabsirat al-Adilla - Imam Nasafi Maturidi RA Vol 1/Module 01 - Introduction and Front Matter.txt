Tabsirat al-Adilla by Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi, volume 1. Words about al-Nasafi and about his life. The biographical books that I have consulted agree on naming him Maimun ibn Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Nasafi, and on his teknonym being Abu al-Mu'in. As for his birth, I did not find mention of it in the books that dealt with that except from al-Zirikli and Kahhala, for they mentioned that he was born in the year 418 AH, and I do not know from where they obtained this date. As for his birthplace, I did not find a single word indicating it. However, all who have written his biography attributed him to Nasaf. Was he born there and lived? Or was he born elsewhere then moved to it, living there most of his life, so he was attributed to it just as his forefathers were attributed to it? I did not find an answer to this during my research, but I do not rule out that Nasaf was his birthplace, since his second, third, and fourth forefathers resided there. Nasaf, to which he was attributed and in which he lived – with the opening of the nun and sin then fa – is a large, populous city between the Jihun River and Samarkand; many scholars came from it.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical introduction to Imam Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi, the author of Tabsirat al-Adilla. The modern editor begins by stating that biographical sources agree on the author's name: Maimun ibn Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Nasafi, and his teknonym (kunya) Abu al-Mu'in. He then addresses the issue of Nasafi's birth year, noting that only al-Zirikli and Kahhala give a specific date (418 AH), but he doubts its accuracy. After investigation, he found only the death date recorded by Ibn Qutlubugha in Taj al-Tarajim: 25 Dhu al-Hijjah 508 AH at age 70, which would imply birth in 438 AH. The editor observes that omitting birth dates is common among Hanafi biographers, citing similar cases like Imam al-Sabuni and Imam al-Maturidi. Finally, he discusses Nasafi's birthplace. While all biographers attribute him to Nasaf (a city in Transoxiana), it is uncertain if he was born there or lived there. The editor inclines to think Nasaf was his birthplace because his ancestors lived there, and he describes the city's location and scholarly reputation.

A brief word about the environment in which al-Nasafi lived
Political and religious aspect
With the beginning of the third century AH, weakness began to creep into the Abbasid state, and it intensified with the Abbasid caliph al-Mu'tasim Billah adopting the Turks in the year 218 AH, hoping for protection through them, but these in turn took control of the affairs of the caliphate, and here the schism from the caliphate began, and a number of small states emerged, such as the Hamdanid state in the Jazira and the Samanid state in Transoxiana. We are concerned with the latter, as their ancestor was Asad ibn Saman from the people of Khurasan. When al-Ma'mun assumed the caliphate, he drew the Banu Asad close to him and raised their status. At the beginning of the year 261 AH, Nasr ibn Ahmad al-Samani took over the emirate of Transoxiana, so he appointed his brother Isma'il over Bukhara and Abu Ishaq ibn al-Sabuktakin over Ghazna. This Nasr was religious, intelligent, and a poet, and his brother Isma'il was good, loving the people of knowledge and religion, and bringing them close to him. With these qualities lived most of the rulers of the Banu Saman until their state became one of the best states in conduct, policy, and justice.
In the year 389 AH, the Samanid state was extinguished at the hands of Mahmud ibn Sabuktakin and Ilek Khan the Turk. As for Mahmud, he ruled Khurasan, and the lands of Transoxiana remained in the hands of Abd al-Malik al-Samani. When he was defeated by Mahmud ibn Sabuktakin, he headed to Bukhara and gathered there with some of the commanders, and they prepared themselves to reclaim Khurasan, but they were suddenly surprised by the death of the leader of their force, Fa'iq, so their resolve weakened, and news of his death reached Ilek Khan. He saw that the opportunity was opportune to swoop down on Abd al-Malik and his army, so he marched with a group of Turks to Bukhara, and showed affection and loyalty to Abd al-Malik and his people, so they thought him sincere. When they gathered, he attacked them and threw their multitudes into prison, headed by Abd al-Malik, the last king of the Samanid state. Not much time passed until Abd al-Malik died, and thus the Samanid state ended.
From here it becomes clear that with the extinction of the Samanid state, control was established in Khurasan for Mahmud ibn Sabuktakin, and in the lands of Transoxiana for Ilek Khan, both of whom were Sunni in sect. The rule of Transoxiana continued for Ilek Khan and his brothers after him until it ended in the year 482 AH, with the beginning of the Seljuk state and the control of Malik Shah over Transoxiana. We conclude from this that Transoxiana was, before the separation, under the control of the Sunni Abbasid state, and after the separation it came under the control of the Turks, who were also Sunnis. Then if we look at the Islamic nation, we find that the second authority, opposing the Abbasid state, was the authority of the Fatimids in Egypt, the Maghreb, and the Levant, which was a Shiite authority. Hence conflict, strife, and bloodshed were a fundamental feature of the fifth century between the Abbasid and Fatimid authorities.

Commentary
This passage is a description of the historical and political backdrop of the region where Imam Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi lived. The author begins by noting the decline of the Abbasid caliphate from the third century AH, especially after al-Mu'tasim's reliance on Turkish soldiers. This led to fragmentation and the emergence of small dynasties, among them the Samanids in Transoxiana, which is the focus. He then details the rise of the Samanids under Nasr ibn Ahmad and his brother Isma'il, praising their justice and support for scholars. The narrative continues with the end of the Samanid state in 389 AH at the hands of Mahmud of Ghazni and Ilek Khan, both Sunni rulers. Finally, the author contrasts the Sunni-controlled Transoxiana with the Shiite Fatimid state, highlighting the sectarian tensions of the fifth century. Throughout, the passage emphasizes the consistent Sunni affiliation of the rulers in Transoxiana, setting the stage for the theological orientation of the region.

Read the praise section starting with 'أحمد الله تعالى' and note the specific attributes: limitless favors, uncountable blessings, seeking guidance for speech and action, protection from deviation, and trust in Allah.
The praise is not generic; it lists theological and practical needs.
3
Examine the salawat (blessings) on the Prophet and his family/companions
Look at the prayer on Muhammad as the one who saves from polytheism, sin, and blindness, and then the mention of his family (lamps of darkness) and companions (leaders and role models).
The Prophet is described as 'master of mankind' and 'prophet of mercy'.
4
Note the transition marker 'وبعد'
Observe that 'وبعد' signals the end of the doxology and the beginning of the main content. It is a common classical Arabic transition.
Do not confuse 'وبعد' with part of the blessings; it is a separate structural element.

Commentary
This passage is the opening doxology of the book. The author begins with the basmala, then expresses reliance on Allah, then states the chapter heading. He then praises Allah in a series of clauses: enumerating the limitlessness of His favors, seeking guidance, protection, and support. He then sends blessings on the Prophet Muhammad and his family and companions. The passage ends with 'وبعد' signaling the transition to the main content.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The phrase 'وبعد' is often misunderstood as part of the doxology or blessings.
Correction: It is a common classical Arabic transition marker, here signaling the end of the opening and the start of the first chapter.
