The editor documents citations of Tabsirat al-Adilla by later scholars and its chain of transmission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how later scholars' citations and the chain of transmission in manuscripts establish the authenticity of al-Nasafi's Tabsirat al-Adilla.

Core Idea
The book's fame and its continuous sanad confirm its authorship by Imam al-Nasafi.

In this editor's introduction, we see two complementary arguments for the authenticity of Tabsirat al-Adilla. First, a long list of later scholars from the 8th to 14th centuries explicitly mention the book and identify al-Nasafi by it. Second, to address any doubt about reliance on secondary sources, the editor introduces the manuscript chain of transmission (sanad) that connects directly back to the author. This passage exemplifies scholarly methods for verifying textual attribution.

This argumentative structure moves from external recognition to internal manuscript evidence, showing the careful scholarly method of the editor.

Attribution of the response to Ash'ari and Maturidi's stance
Historical note on the transmission of the response and al-Maturidi's relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the chain of transmission for this response and how al-Maturidi's stance relates to it.

Core Idea
The response is attributed to Ash'ari via a chain, not from his own books.

This passage provides important historical context for a theological response often attributed to al-Ash'ari. The author traces its transmission and notes that while al-Maturidi references it, his school’s apparent doctrine aligns with the early Hanafis. Understanding this helps situate the response within both Ash'ari and Maturidi traditions.

Commentary
This passage is a historical note on the authorship and transmission of the preceding response. This situates the response within the Ash'ari tradition and shows that Maturidi's position, while not identical, contains hints of it.

The Colophon: A Window into Manuscript History
Scribal colophon recording the completion of the copy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the purpose and typical contents of a manuscript colophon, and how this colophon provides critical data about the copy's date, place, and scribe.

Core Idea
A colophon is a scribe's signature that fixes a manuscript in time and place.

Colophons are scribal notes at the end of manuscripts, often overlooked but invaluable for textual criticism. This particular colophon from a copy of Tabṣirat al-Adilla records when and where it was written, and by whom. It reminds us that every manuscript has a human story behind it.

Guided Path
1
Read the colophon carefully
Read the Arabic text and its translation. Notice the standard phrases: the date, the place, the scribe's name, and prayers.
The scribe uses pious formulae common in Islamic scribal tradition.
2
Identify the key elements
Look for: (1) ownership note, (2) date (day, month, year), (3) place, (4) scribe's name, (5) prayers for forgiveness. These are typical colophon components.
The date is Saturday, 9 Dhu al-Hijjah 659 AH.
3
Reflect on why colophons matter
Think about how this information helps scholars verify the manuscript's age, provenance, and relationship to the author. Without colophons, we would know much less about textual transmission.
This colophon confirms the copy was made just over a century after the author's death (d. 537 AH).

Commentary
This passage is a colophon – a scribal note at the end of the manuscript. It begins with an ownership statement indicating that the manuscript was owned by a person named Abu Bakr Anma, who describes himself as 'the poor to Allah' (al-faqir ila Allah). The scribe then records the completion details: the date is Saturday, the 9th of Dhu al-Hijjah, 659 AH (corresponding to November 1261 CE), completed at dawn in the city of Bukhara. The scribe identifies himself as Muhammad ibn al-Hasan ibn al-Husayn, and he prays for forgiveness for himself, his parents, and all believing men and women and Muslim men and women. He concludes with the salutation upon the Prophet Muhammad and his family. This colophon provides critical data for textual criticism, confirming the provenance and date of the copy, and also reflects the pious conventions of Islamic manuscript culture.
