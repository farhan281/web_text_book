The Author's Purpose and Method
Author explains purpose, inspiration, and method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's purpose in writing the book and his methodological approach to presenting proofs of God's existence as clear and accessible to all rational minds.

Core Idea
The truth is manifest; barriers are fabricated by desires.

The author begins the second edition by expressing gratitude and explaining the inspiration behind his work. He emphasizes that the proofs of God's existence are clear and accessible to reason, with only artificial veils creating an illusion of distance. This introduction sets the stage for a universal, reason-based approach to faith.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing. Then read the English translation. Notice the author's tone of gratitude and conviction.
2
Identify the main claims
Find where the author states what God inspired him to write, and how he describes the relationship between reason and the proofs.
Look for phrases like 'لا حاجز' (no barrier) and 'غشاوات مصطنعة' (fabricated veils).
3
Contrast the author's method with alternatives
Notice that the author did not invent new proofs but pointed to existing clear evidences. He avoided specialized arguments that only some people understand.
This reflects his commitment to universal accessibility.
4
Reflect on the reception
Read the last paragraph about the book's positive reception among youth and scholars. Consider why this might be significant for the author's purpose.

Introduction to the Second Edition: Praise be to God, the Guardian of all success, the Inspirer of all good, the Guide to all truth. I say praise be to God with my full mouth and all my feelings and heart... Indeed, the Truth inspired me and endowed me with insight into a way of indicating it, and carried me with a pen in defending it. I was not worthy of any of this without His abundant grace and immense favor. From Him and to Him is all grace. To Him and through Him is all praise. About two years ago I wrote what God inspired me with: proofs of His existence as the Necessary Eternal (al-Wājib al-Azalī), and proofs of man's servitude to Him and his connection to a grave, great responsibility towards Him. I clarified that between reason and any of these proofs there is no barrier requiring effort to penetrate, nor a long distance needing effort to cross. But fabricated veils motivated by objectives, passions, and hatreds are what suggested the existence of a barrier, and what made some imagine a distant gap. So it became necessary for someone to arise to draw attention to the fact that the barrier is illusory and the distance imaginary, and that the truth is manifest to the eye and near to understanding. My work in what I wrote was to point out manifest, clear, and near evidences, not to invent unknown and distant proofs. All my effort was directed to dealing with those scientific proofs prevalent among all intellects and in all places and times, not with a specific type that only a certain class of people might understand or that only the people of a particular civilization in a narrow historical period dealt with. And indeed God Almighty - and praise be to Him, of which my tongue is incapable of giving due - willed that this work bear fruit. So the youth flocked to it from every direction and type, and many intellectuals and researchers of different orientations and persuasions contemplated it. As for the believers, it increased them in faith, and they continued with it their constant striving in calling to the truth.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to the second edition. It is a personal and methodological statement. He begins with praise of God, acknowledging that all guidance and success come from Him. He then explains the motivation behind his book: God inspired him to write proofs of His existence as the Eternal Necessary Being (الواجب الأزلي) and proofs of human servitude to God and accountability. The author emphasizes that there is no real barrier between human reason and these proofs; any perceived barrier is due to fabricated veils from selfish motives, passions, and hatreds. Therefore, his role was not to invent new obscure arguments but to point out clear, accessible evidences that are present and understandable to all minds across all times and places. He rejected relying on specialized arguments that only a specific group or civilization could grasp. Finally, he notes the positive reception of the work, especially among youth and scholars of various backgrounds, and that it strengthened the faith of believers.

The Free-Hearted Truth-Seekers
Describes the first type of reader: free-hearted seekers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first category of readers described by the author: those with free hearts who arrive at faith through decisive rational proofs, not emotional attachments.

Core Idea
Sincere seekers find faith through reason, not emotion.

The author begins by describing a category of readers who, though not yet believers, approach the book with intellectual honesty. They have not been enslaved by desires or preconceptions. Through their turbulent search, they find in this book the lost truth they were seeking, arriving at faith through definitive scientific proofs, not comforting illusions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first category
Read the passage describing 'owners of free hearts'. Notice the qualities: not yet believers, but minds free from inclinations and objectives.
The author emphasizes that their minds are not enslaved.
2
Understand their journey
The passage says they found in the book a 'lost item'. Understand that they were searching and concluded their intellectual journey with faith.
They arrived at faith through decisive scientific proofs.
3
Note the contrast
The author mentions that some of these were previously callers to materialist beliefs. This shows the transformative power of rational proofs.

As for the owners of free hearts whose hearts have not yet been inhabited by faith, but whose minds have not been enslaved by any inclinations or objectives, nor have these taken possession of their right guidance, many of them found in it a lost item. Commentary
This passage is a classification of two types of readers. First, the author describes those with free hearts who are not yet believers but are sincere seekers. They find in the book the truth they were searching for, having arrived at faith through rational proofs, not emotional comfort. Some of these were formerly materialists or atheists. Second, he describes those who prioritize their psychological desires over reason. For them the book is futile because they have subjugated intellect to their desires. The author notes that such people do not admit this, as doing so would undermine their own rationalization.

Praise of God and Purpose of Creation
Author opens with praise and states man's honored status and duty to know God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the author's opening praise and his statement of man's honored status and duty to know God through intellect and revelation.

Core Idea
Man is honored with intellect to hear the universe's signs and know God.

In this opening passage, the author sets the stage for the entire discussion by praising God as Creator and establishing the unique role of human beings. He describes how the universe itself speaks of God's existence, and how man is endowed with intellect to receive that message. The passage concludes with a powerful reminder of man's responsibility not to be the first to disbelieve or ignore the truth.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the English translation. Let the author's praise and flow of ideas sink in.

2

Identify the three logical sections: praise of God, creation and honor of man with intellect, and the sending of prophets with the concluding statement.
Each section builds on the previous to establish man's duty.
3
Reflect on the intellect's role
The passage describes intellect as both a 'listening ear' and a 'vessel'. Consider how these metaphors convey receptivity and containment of divine signs.
The intellect is not merely a tool for reasoning but a trust (amāna).
4
Understand the conclusion
The author ends with a rhetorical statement: the most honored creature should not be the first disbeliever. Ask yourself why this conclusion is so forceful given the earlier points.
It ties back to the honor bestowed by intellect and prophethood.

الحمد الله فاطر السموات والأرض . أنطق الكون بآيات وجوده ، وكشف به عن عظيم سلطانه . خلق الإنسان وشرفه بحمل أمانة العقل ، ليكون الأذن الصاغية لما ينطق به الكون من الآيات ، ثم الوعاء الحاوي لما يتجلى فيه من الدلائل والعظات . أرسل الرسل والأنبياء ، يتوالون مع الزمن، في كل أمة وبقعة ومحيط : أن ذكروا الإنسان بما أوليته من أمانة العقل وما رفعته إليه من شرف السيادة والرياسة في الكون . فليس لائقاً بأشرف مخلوق فيه أن يكون أول كافر أو جاهل بالحق
Praise be to God, the Creator of the heavens and the earth. He made the universe speak with the signs of His existence, and through it revealed the greatness of His authority. He created man and honored him by entrusting him with the burden of intellect, to be a listening ear to what the universe utters of signs, and then a vessel containing what manifests from proofs and admonitions. He sent messengers and prophets, successive with time, to every nation, region, and environment: that they remind man of what I (God) have bestowed upon him of the trust of intellect and what I have raised him to of the honor of sovereignty and leadership in the universe. So it is not befitting for the most honored creature in it (the universe) to be the first disbeliever or ignorant of the truth.

Commentary
This passage is an opening praise and an introduction to the theme of the book. The author begins by praising God as the Creator of the heavens and the earth, who makes the universe articulate signs of His existence and reveals His mighty authority through it. He then focuses on man: God created man and honored him by giving him the trust (amāna) of intellect. The intellect is described as an ear that listens to the signs of the universe, and a vessel that holds the proofs and lessons that appear in it. God sent messengers and prophets to every nation over time to remind man of the intellect's trust and the honor of sovereignty and dominion in the universe. The conclusion is a rhetorical statement: it is not suitable for the most honored creature to be the first to disbelieve or be ignorant of the truth. The author is establishing the high dignity of man and the natural obligation to recognize God, setting the stage for the discussion of belief and proofs.
