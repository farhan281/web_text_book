Prophethood is Divine Revelation, Not Acquired Effort
Buti refutes the acquired view of prophethood and affirms it as revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why prophethood cannot be attained by human striving and effort, but is solely a gift of divine revelation from Allah to whom He wills.

Core Idea
Prophethood is a divine gift, not a human achievement.

In this passage, Buti targets a deep misunderstanding: that prophethood is an acquired status like sorcery or reform. He exposes the flawed reasoning behind this view and then reaffirms the truth established by earlier proofs—that prophethood is exclusively revelation from Allah. This lesson helps you see why prophethood cannot be reduced to personal effort and how to recognize the error of those who deny its divine origin.

Guided Path
1
Understand the misconception
Read how Buti describes those who think prophethood is attained by striving, equating it with sorcery and reform. Notice his tone of refutation.
He says they 'only deceive themselves'.
2
Identify the motive behind the error
See why they insist on an acquired concept: to avoid accepting the Qur'an as divine, miracles as real, and revelation as more than thought.
Their worldview forces this interpretation.
3
Trace the conclusion from prior proofs
Buti recalls the earlier investigations into revelation, miracles, and the Necessary Existence, then states the inevitable conclusion: prophethood is revelation, not sorcery or effort.
This is the central thesis.
4
Recognize the warning against denial
He warns that only those already in disbelief reject this truth, and they deceive their intellects with baseless illusions.
The passage ends with 'All-Knowing' as a seal.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the view that prophethood (nubuwwa) is an acquired status (ma'na iktisabi) attained by human effort. The author, Ramadan al-Buti, first describes the misconception: those who deny the divine origin of prophethood imagine it as a goal reachable through striving and acquisition, similar to how sorcerers acquire sorcery. They reduce the prophethood of Muhammad (peace be upon him) to a personal effort, equating 'prophet' with 'reformer'. Buti notes that such people deceive themselves.

He then explains the motive behind this misconception: they refuse to accept that the Qur'an is divine speech, that miracles occur at the hands of prophets, and that revelation (wahy) is more than thought and reflection. They insist on an acquired concept of prophethood to fit their worldview, even if it is not logically analyzable.

After reviewing the previous proofs—revelation, miracles, and the existence of the Necessary Being—Buti affirms the conclusion: prophethood is solely revelation from God to whomever He chooses, to warn of the meeting (with Him) and to remind of rights. It has nothing to do with soothsaying (kihana), sorcery (sihr), or astrology (tanjim), nor is it attained by cunning and effort.

Finally, Buti warns that only those who are already disbelievers (kafir) would reject this truth and resort to baseless interpretations. They deceive their own intellects with illusions. The passage ends with the divine attribute 'All-Knowing' ('Alim).

The Validity of Tawassul through the Prophet as a Mercy
Author argues for tawassul using Quran, sunnah, and comparison to medicine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Islamic rationale for the validity of tawassul (seeking intercession) through the Prophet Muhammad, based on Quranic evidence, prophetic practice, and logical consistency.

Core Idea
Tawassul does not attribute power to the Prophet; it recognizes Allah as the sole cause of all effects.

In this lesson, we explore a key passage from Shaykh Ramadan al-Buti defending the practice of tawassul. The author addresses a common misconception that tawassul implies shirk by clarifying the nature of causality in Islam. He uses the Qur’an, the example of the Companions, and a comparison with medicine to demonstrate that seeking blessings through the Prophet is both permissible and consistent with tawhid.

Commentary
This passage defends the validity of tawassul (seeking intercession) through the Prophet Muhammad. The author begins by citing Quran 21:107, which states that the Prophet was sent as a mercy to the worlds, arguing that Allah made him a cause for mercy; thus, there is no harm in a Muslim using him as a means to seek blessings. He then notes that the Companions consistently practiced tawassul and tabarruk (seeking blessings) through the Prophet's relics, and this is well-established in authentic hadith. The author then criticizes those who condemn tawassul as shirk, pointing out their inconsistency: they accept that medicine causes healing and ask doctors for treatment, yet they reject the same logic when applied to the Prophet. He declares that there is no real causality anywhere—all causes are merely formal or contingent. However, the contingency attributed to the Prophet for mercy and forgiveness is far superior to that of medicine. The key point is that tawassul does not attribute any independent effect to the Prophet; rather, it acknowledges Allah as the sole true cause.

Refutation of the Objection that Tawassul Ceased After the Prophet's Death
Author refutes the claim that tawassul ceased after the Prophet's death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that tawassul ended with the Prophet's death is based on a flawed understanding of causality and the Prophet's role.

Core Idea
The Prophet's honor from Allah never ends; tawassul is based on that, not his life.

This passage tackles a common objection: that seeking intercession through the Prophet was only valid in his lifetime. The author argues that this objection misunderstands the basis of tawassul. He clarifies that the Prophet's influence is not physical but derived from Allah's eternal honoring, and that all causes are merely formal, with Allah as the sole true cause.

Commentary
This passage addresses a specific objection: that tawassul through the Prophet was valid during his life but ceased after his death. The author refutes this by exposing its flawed presupposition: that the Prophet's physical existence was the source of his influence. He asserts that the Prophet has no inherent influence whatsoever; all influence belongs to Allah. Therefore, the basis of tawassul is not the Prophet's life or bodily presence, but Allah's eternal honoring of him, which does not diminish with death. The author then summarizes the broader principle: all causes are merely formal; the only true cause is Allah. Using causal language in everyday life, including tawassul, is permissible as long as one maintains certainty that Allah is the sole causer. However, tawassul through the Prophet is distinct from other causes because it is an act of worship: it follows the Companions' example and venerates the Messenger. Thus, the objection is based on a misunderstanding of causality and a failure to recognize that Allah's honoring of the Prophet is ongoing.

We said: What makes you laugh, O Messenger of Allah? He said: Just now a Surah was revealed to me. Then he recited: 'In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Commentary
This passage describes the Pond (al-Hawd) of the Prophet Muhammad, a great honor from Allah. The author begins by stating that the Pond is mentioned in the Quran through Surah al-Kawthar, where Allah gives the Prophet the Kawthar, which is explained by the Prophet as a river and a pond. He then provides two hadiths. First, from Imam Muslim via Anas: the Prophet, after Surah al-Kawthar was revealed, explains that al-Kawthar is a river promised by Allah, and it is a pond (hawd) at which his nation will drink on the Day of Resurrection, with vessels as numerous as stars. The hadith also includes that some of his followers will be taken away, and he intercedes but is told they changed after him. Second, from Muslim, Malik, and others via Abu Huraira: the Prophet goes to the cemetery and speaks about his brothers (later generations), saying he is their forerunner at the Pond. He will recognize them by the marks of ablution (white blazes and stockings). Some will be driven away, and when he calls them, it will be said they changed after him, and he will say 'Away'. The author concludes that the water of the Pond and al-Kawthar are the same thing, as stated by scholars (the text cuts off with 'as stated by').

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think al-Kawthar is only a river in Paradise and not the Pond on the Day of Resurrection.
Correction: The Prophet explicitly identified al-Kawthar as a river and as a pond (Hawd) at which his nation will arrive on the Day of Resurrection, meaning they are the same.
