God's Actions and the Definition of Injustice
The author presents an objection about God's supposed injustice and answers it by defining injustice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's actions, including punishing the obedient and afflicting people, are not considered injustice according to Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Injustice is violating another's rights; God owns everything, so He cannot be unjust.

A common objection claims that if God forbids injustice, then He cannot do what looks like injustice to us. This lesson examines that objection and the precise definition of injustice in Islamic thought, showing that God's absolute ownership of all things means His actions are beyond the category of injustice.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the objector's reasoning: God made injustice evil, so He cannot be unjust, therefore He should not punish the obedient or afflict people without sin.
The objection seems logically sound at first glance.
2
Understand the definition of injustice
The author defines injustice as disposing of something that belongs to another without their consent. This definition is key to resolving the objection.
The definition comes from the law itself.
3
Apply the definition to God
Since everything belongs to God, He never disposes of what belongs to another. Therefore, His actions—even punishing the obedient or sending calamities—are not injustice.
This shows that the objection is based on a misunderstanding of injustice.

The author answers by defining injustice: it is disposing of something that belongs to another without their consent. Since everything belongs to God, His actions—even punishing the obedient or sending calamities—are not injustice. The footnote directs the reader to Taftazani's commentary for a fuller discussion of the distinction between creating evil and acquiring it.

Two Types of Movement: Compulsory and Voluntary
Buti introduces the problem and classifies human movements into two types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between compulsory and voluntary movements in humans, and how this distinction frames the relationship between human will and God's will.

Core Idea
God created humans with two types of movement, one compulsory and one voluntary.

Before tackling the puzzle of free will and divine will, Buti sets the stage by describing two categories of human action. This foundational distinction clarifies what is at stake when we speak of human choice under God's absolute will.

4 - The Fate of Human Will Before the Will of God, Majestic is His Majesty

Now, having known that the will of God Almighty is absolute and perfect, and suitable to attach to all possible things, how can we conceive that man also has a will alongside it? And we have known through proofs of experience and observation that man wills and chooses in much of his behavior and conceptions. So what is the type of this will and its reality, rather what is its fate in relation to the will of God? The answer is that God Almighty and Glorified, when He created man, established him upon two types of movement and action. As for one of them, man is equal in it with other existing things from animals, inanimate objects, plants, and celestial spheres: compulsory movements and automatic functions in which man has no acquisition or volition, like the movement of growth and what follows it of strength, aging, and weakness, and like birth and death, and like various emotions of love, hatred, hunger, thirst, fear, and fright. As for the second type, they are actions arising from a special wondrous secret that God Almighty and Glorified deposited in man, which we call: choice and will. For the will of God Almighty and Glorified attached to planting in man's being this secret which is the pivot of religious obligation in him, and to make him act in much of his actions according to this secret by which he is called free and choosing. And the meaning of that is that the will of God Almighty attached to you being a willing agent, so the will of God Almighty and Glorified extended thereby to all the actions that you will and choose. Then it is impossible that any contradiction occurs between the will of God Almighty and what you choose through your own will, because if we suppose that God does not will an action that you have chosen by your will, then the meaning of that is that He, glorified and exalted, does not will your will that directed you to that action, and that contradicts what has been established that God Almighty and Glorified willed for you to be a willing agent and willed to create in you this secret. So the supposition that God may not will the action you choose is proven false.

And I will give you an example to bring this truth closer to you: A servant in your house, you want to know the extent of his honesty and trustworthiness in service and dealings. And to achieve this purpose, you give him an amount of money and send him to the market to buy some needs, and you allow him room to act as he wishes without placing a watcher over him or narrowing the path for him. So by this arrangement you willed that he be free in what he does and leaves, responding only to the call of his conscience and inner thought, such that he enjoys a will untainted by compulsion, so that by that you may know his inner nature. If he returns having betrayed the trust in the money you gave him and what he brought back of goods, then you in reality are willing this result. And if he returns having achieved the utmost honesty in his work (1), then you are also willing this result, since you did not will to release his hand to act as he wishes except while you are willing the appearance of the result of that, whatever the result may be, whether you love it and are pleased with it or not. If this becomes clear to you, you know that the fate of the human will in relation to the will of God is nothing but like the fate of the servant's will in relation to the will of his master, and to God belongs the highest attribute. So your will pertaining to your voluntary actions is subsumed under the will of God Almighty, but not by way of compulsion and coercion – as is the case of His will pertaining to the first type of movements and functions – but by way of implanting the secret of will and choice in your being. And His wisdom in that is that you may earn by virtue of it everything you love, without compulsion or coercion, so that your inner nature may be manifested in your behavior, and thereby you become deserving of God's reward or punishment. And it is clear that this your behavior becomes, as a result, from the volitions of God Almighty and Glorified. Thus you know that nothing occurs in His dominion except what He wills and intends, and that does not contradict that He also gave you will and volition, just as His knowledge of all things does not contradict that He also gave you knowledge of some small part of them.

(1) Noting the difference, which is that God knows the inner nature of the servant and knows what he will choose by his mere will.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation and defense of how human free will coexists with God's absolute will, resolving a potential contradiction. The author begins by posing the problem: since God's will is absolute and perfect, how can humans also have will? He states that we know from experience that humans will and choose. The answer is that God created man with two types of movement: (1) involuntary movements shared with other creatures—compulsory and automatic functions like growth, birth, death, and emotions—where man has no acquisition or volition; (2) voluntary actions arising from a special secret God placed in man, which we call 'choice and will'. God's will attached to implanting this secret in man, making him a free and choosing agent, and this secret is the pivot of religious obligation (taklif). Thus, God's will extends to everything man wills and chooses, because God willed that man be a willing agent. Therefore, there is no contradiction: if God did not will a particular action chosen by man, that would imply God did not will man's will that directed him to that action, contradicting God's willing to create man as a free chooser. The author then illustrates this with an analogy: a master sends a servant to the market with money, giving him full freedom to act as he wishes to test his honesty. The master wills both possible outcomes—honesty or dishonesty—because he willed to give the servant free rein. The result, whatever it is, is willed by the master. Similarly, human will is subsumed under God's will, but not by coercion; rather, God instilled the secret of will and choice in humans for the purpose of accountability. Human actions become from God's volitions, but this does not negate human responsibility. Finally, the author concludes that nothing occurs in God's dominion except what He wills, and this does not contradict God giving humans will and knowledge, just as God's comprehensive knowledge does not negate human knowledge of some things.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained analogy (tamthīl) to explain the relationship between human will and divine will. The author, Ramadan al-Buti, invites the reader to imagine a master who wishes to test his servant's honesty. The master gives the servant money and sends him to the market with complete freedom to act as he wishes, without supervision. The master's intention is that the servant should act freely, based on his own conscience and inner thought, so that his true character is revealed. If the servant cheats, the master has effectively willed that result because he set up the test precisely to see what the servant would do; similarly if the servant acts honestly, the master has willed that result as well. The key point is that the master's will extends to the outcome, whether good or bad, because the purpose of the test is to bring that outcome to light. Al-Buti then applies this analogy to God and human beings. God's will encompasses all human voluntary actions, but not by way of compulsion (qasr). Instead, God instills in humans the 'secret of will and choice' (sirr al-irāda wa-l-ikhtiyār), allowing them to act freely and earn reward or punishment. This is distinct from the first type of movements (e.g., involuntary bodily functions) over which humans have no control. The author emphasizes that nothing occurs in God's dominion except what He wills, yet this does not negate that He has also given humans a real will and volition. Similarly, God's all-encompassing knowledge does not negate that humans possess some knowledge. A footnote adds that God knows the servant's inner self and what he will choose by his own free will, highlighting the difference between divine omniscience and human free will.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-answer unit. The objector claims that even if God's knowledge does not coerce, His creation and will of human actions would still imply coercion. The author accepts the premise that everything occurs by God's creation and will, but he refutes the conclusion of coercion by distinguishing two types of creations. 
1. Compelled creations (qasr): events where humans have no acquisition, like natural phenomena and involuntary bodily functions. These are not subject to moral accountability, so no problem arises. 
2. Acquired creations (iktisab): human voluntary actions, such as studying or eating, which involve human effort and choice. 
The author then asserts that these voluntary actions are created by God, but through the human's acquisition (kasb). He provides rational and scriptural proofs: rationally, if actions were not created by God, that would imply a deficiency in His power; scripturally, Qur'an 25:2 states that Allah created all things, and actions are things. The author concludes that this is the standard Ash'ari position of Ahl al-Sunnah. The argument preserves human responsibility by affirming that while God creates the action, the human acquires it through his choice.

Commentary
This passage is an argument defending human free will against the charge that God's creation of all actions entails compulsion. The author begins by stating the claim: God's creation of your actions does not necessarily make you compelled. He then explains that involvement in an act requires two factors: (1) the existence of the act with all its components (created by God), and (2) your acquisition (kash) of that act through your own impulse. You are morally accountable as an acquirer, not as a creator. To illustrate, the author gives a concrete example: writing. God creates the hand, its abilities, the paper, the pen, and the ink; He even causes the meeting of these elements to bring about the written line. Yet, for you to be called a writer, you must intend to write and your will must impel you to act. At that point God's permission activates the means, and the act is attributed to you as acquisition. The author then draws a social analogy: in legal systems, a person is prosecuted not for the physical act itself (e.g., the car killing someone) but for the acquisition (intention and choice). Similarly, with digging a pit or placing poison, liability rests on the acquirer. Finally, the author cites Quranic verses that consistently tie reward and punishment to kasb, i.e., the psychic impulse toward the commanded or forbidden act. The conclusion is that God's creation of all things does not imply any coercion, because human responsibility lies in the intention and choice (kasb), not in creating the act. The passage is a clear exposition of the Ash'ari doctrine of kasb (acquisition) as a middle ground between absolute predestination and absolute free will.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory argument about two categories of people and the divine law (sunnat Allah) regarding guidance and misguidance. Buti begins by describing the first category: a person who from the outset resolves to stubbornly oppose whatever he dislikes, even if it is true, and to ignore his own reason, following only his desires. Such a person makes it known that he will not accept any reminder of the truth. Buti then states the divine sunna concerning such people: they are increased in misguidance and intellectual darkness, and their wills are driven [unclear phrase] into the flames of desire. Additionally, they are afflicted with greater aversion to the reminders of preachers and the signs of God. Buti supports this with Quranic verses: al-Kahf 18:57, al-A'raf 7:146, al-Baqarah 2:26, and al-Tawba 9:115, all of which describe how God seals the hearts of those who turn away. Then Buti interprets these verses: the divine sunna is the practical explanation of the general statements that God leads astray whom He wills and guides whom He wills. Buti clarifies that God is not powerless to forcibly grant guidance to the most deviant or to cause misguidance in the most righteous. However, out of grace and beneficence, He has decreed that He will only lead astray those who expose themselves to the causes of misguidance and turn away from means of guidance, and He draws near the means of guidance and success to those who resolve to obey His commands, who extend the hand of servitude asking for His aid and support.

The Effect of a Single Deed in Changing One's Destiny
Buti illustrates how a single good or evil deed can redirect a person's entire life trajectory, citing a hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a single good or evil deed can alter a person's entire life trajectory, as illustrated by a prophetic hadith.

Core Idea
A single deed can be the turning point that changes your destiny.

Buti highlights the profound impact of individual actions, showing that even one good deed from a wicked person or one evil deed from a righteous person can redirect his entire life. He then supports this with the well-known hadith about the last deed determining one's final abode. This lesson explores that concept.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory statement with a prophetic proof illustrating how a single deed can alter a person's final destiny. Buti begins by stating that a single good deed may proceed from a wicked sinner, at a moment when his humanity and innate disposition (fitra) awaken, and that deed becomes a cause of God's guidance for him and a great factor in changing his life's course. Conversely, a single reprehensible deed may proceed from a righteous man, committed carelessly without subsequent remorse leading to repentance, and that deed becomes a cause of God's displeasure and a great factor in changing his life's course as well. Buti then cites the authentic hadith in which the Prophet swears by God that a person may do the deeds of the people of Paradise until only a cubit separates him from it, but then the divine decree overtakes him and he does the deeds of the people of Hell and enters it, and the opposite can also happen. This hadith illustrates that final outcome depends on the end of one's life, and a single deed can be the turning point.

Refuting the Extreme Predestinarian View of Some Sufis
Buti warns against extreme jabri view among Sufis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that humans are mere feathers in the wind, driven by divine decree, is a distortion of Islamic doctrine, and how the verse 'وما رميت إذ رميت' is misinterpreted by some Sufis.

Core Idea
Humans are not puppets; divine sovereignty does not negate human agency.

Buti has established the sunna of Allah and human responsibility. Now he addresses a dangerous misconception circulating among some contemporary Sufis: that humans have no real will or action. This lesson examines their claim and their proof text. You'll see why this view contradicts the balanced Islamic understanding of qadar.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an extreme predestinarian (jabri) view that Buti attributes to some contemporary Sufis. Buti notes that these Sufis often cite the Quranic verse from al-Anfal 8:17, 'And you did not throw when you threw, but God threw,' to support their view. By placing this refutation after his earlier explanations, Buti intends to show that such a literal and extreme interpretation of the verse is misguided and contradicts the balanced understanding of divine predestination and human responsibility he has laid out.
