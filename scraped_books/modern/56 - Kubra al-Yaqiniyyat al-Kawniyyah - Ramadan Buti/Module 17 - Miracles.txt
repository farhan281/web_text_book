On Ecstatic Utterances and the Miracle of the Pebbles
Critique of unjustified ecstatic utterances and correct exegesis of Quran 8:17.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proper basis for spiritual utterances according to Sunni orthodoxy and the correct interpretation of Quran 8:17 as a specific miracle.

Core Idea
Ecstatic utterances are not proof; true Sufis adhere to apparent meanings and consensus.

This passage from al-Buti clarifies a common confusion regarding ecstatic utterances (shathiyyat) attributed to some Sufis. He distinguishes between those who speak from genuine bewilderment and those who merely imitate, and emphasizes that the great Sufis never relied on such speech. He then provides the correct understanding of the verse 'You did not throw...' as referring to a specific miraculous event at the Battle of Hunayn.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the author's argument flow.
Notice the author's strong tone in criticizing imitators.
2
Identify the two groups
The passage distinguishes two groups who utter ecstatic speech: the righteous ones (from genuine bewilderment) and the imitators. Note the author's judgment on each.
The author excuses the first group but not the second.
3
Understand the correct exegesis
Learn how the great Sufis interpreted Quran 8:17 as a specific historical miracle, not a general negation of human agency.
This refutes misinterpretations that deny human causality.

Know that this speech is not based on any of the proofs, knowledge, or divine report from which the pure Law was formed, but rather it is based (among the righteous of them) on states of intense contemplation of the greatness of Allah, so they drown thereby in bewilderment and obliviousness to themselves, which makes them utter these words. Commentary
This passage is a critique and clarification regarding certain ecstatic utterances (kalam) attributed to some Sufis. The author begins by stating that such speech is not grounded in proofs (barahin), knowledge, or divine revelation (al-khabar al-ilahi) that constitute the pure Sharia. Rather, among the righteous ones, it stems from intense contemplation of Allah's greatness, leading to a state of bewilderment and self-oblivion that causes them to utter these words. The author emphasizes that this is not a scientific statement of their intellectual conclusions but a psychological description of their awe. However, among others, it is mere imitation and mimicry. The author then excuses the former group due to their genuine state but condemns the latter for deliberate imitation. He then highlights that the great Sufis (kibar al-sufiyya) never uttered such things; they adhered only to the apparent meaning of the texts and what the consensus of Muslims accepts as rational proof. They understood the verse 'And you did not throw when you threw, but Allah threw' (8:17) as referring to a specific historical miracle: the Prophet's handful of pebbles multiplied in the air during the Battle of Hunayn. Thus, the verse describes a supernatural event (khariqa) that Allah granted His Messenger, not a general negation of human agency. The author concludes by asserting that this clarification is sufficient and no further explanation is needed.

Definition of a Miracle and Its Components
Buti defines the miracle and unpacks its four components.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise definition of a miracle (muʿjizah) and how each component of the definition distinguishes it from other extraordinary events.

Core Idea
A miracle is a sign of prophethood, not a violation of reason.

Buti begins by defining the miracle as a supernatural event tied to prophethood and challenge. This definition is carefully crafted to exclude coincidental wonders, saintly miracles, and even events that disprove the claimant. Understanding each clause is essential for grasping the theological significance of miracles.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic definition of muʿjizah and its translation. Notice that it consists of four main clauses.
The definition is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the four clauses
The definition has four parts: (1) khāriq li-l-ʿādah, (2) appears at the hand of a claimant, (3) at time of challenge, (4) in a manner showing truth. Each excludes something.
3
Understand the purpose of each clause
Work through the commentary to see what each clause excludes: contradicts custom not reason, excludes saintly miracles, excludes coincidences, excludes falsifying events.
4
Reflect on the necessity of challenge
Note that challenge (taḥaddī) is key; it can be explicit or implied by context. This shows miracles are responses to denial.

Commentary
This passage provides a formal definition of a miracle (muʿjizah) in Islamic theology. It begins by stating the definition: a miracle is a supernatural event (khāriq li-l-ʿādah) that occurs at the hand of a claimant to prophethood at the time of challenge (taḥaddī) to the deniers, in a way that proves his truthfulness. The author then explains each part of the definition. First, 'breaking custom' means it contradicts the normal course of events, not reason or possibility. Second, 'at the hand of a claimant to prophethood' distinguishes miracles from the extraordinary acts of saints (karāmāt). Third, 'at the time of challenging the deniers' excludes coincidental extraordinary events, which would be considered divine honor (ikrām ilāhī) rather than miracles; explicit challenge is not required as contextual clues suffice. Fourth, 'in a manner that shows truth' excludes events that actually disprove the claimant, such as an inanimate object speaking to refute him. After the definition, the author explains why miracles are rationally possible: the constancy of natural phenomena is not a logical necessity but a result of habit and created causes. Thus, miracles are merely unfamiliar, not impossible. The intellect does not reject them because they break habit, not reason. This addresses a common objection that miracles violate natural law; the author argues that natural law is not absolute but contingent on God's will.

Rational Possibility of Miracles
Buti argues that miracles are rationally possible because natural regularity is habitual, not necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why miracles are rationally possible and how the habitual nature of phenomena allows for divine intervention.

Core Idea
Miracles are only against habit, not against reason.

After defining miracles, Buti addresses a common objection: how can a miracle be possible if nature follows fixed laws? He argues that the constancy of nature is not a logical necessity but a product of habit and created causes. Therefore, a miracle is simply an unfamiliar event, not an impossible one.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument for rational possibility
Read the passage where Buti explains that the intellect does not deem miracles impossible. Notice his reasoning about the habitual pattern of nature.
Key phrase: 'ليست شيئاً ضرورياً يفرضه العقل'
2
Understand the distinction between necessity and habit
The author says the continuation of natural phenomena is not a logical necessity but is formed by habit and created causes. This is crucial for accepting the possibility of miracles.
3
Reflect on the emotional reaction to miracles
People wonder or disapprove of miracles because they are strange and unfamiliar, not because they are intellectually impossible. This reinforces the point.

The Quran's Repeated Challenge to the Disbelievers
Author recounts historical challenge and Quranic verses demanding a similar composition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quran repeatedly challenged the disbelievers to produce a similar composition and why their failure serves as a proof of its divine origin.

Core Idea
The Quran's challenge is a living miracle: no human or jinn can produce its like.

This lesson takes us back to the time of the Prophet ﷺ when the disbelievers demanded a miracle. Allah pointed them to the Quran itself, and when they boasted they could imitate it, the Quran issued a series of escalating challenges. Their inability to respond, despite their eloquence and motivation, confirms the Quran's divine authorship.

Guided Path
1
Read the historical context
Read the opening lines that set the scene: the Arabs asked for a sign, and Allah declared the Quran as the greatest sign.
2
Identify the three challenges
As you read, locate the three different forms of the challenge: produce a surah, gather all humans and jinn, produce a discourse like it.
Each challenge is more comprehensive.
3
Consider the disbelievers' situation
Notice the author's argument: despite their eloquence, hatred, and constant search for a way to discredit the Prophet, they did nothing.
This highlights the miraculous nature.
4
Reflect on the lesson
Ask yourself: why was the challenge effective? How does this prove the Quran is from Allah?
This connects to the broader proof of prophethood.

Commentary
This passage presents a multi-step inductive proof (istiqrā’ tāmm) for the divine origin of the Quran. The author, Ramadan al-Buti, begins by noting that the Arab opponents of the Prophet shifted their stance: initially they claimed they could produce something like the Quran if they wished, but later they resorted to calling it sorcery, soothsaying, or poetry (citing Quran 43:30). He then points to the historical fact that the Quranic challenge remained unmet across centuries, despite the abundance of skilled poets and orators among the Arabs. Next, Buti applies the method of complete induction: the inability of all Arabs to produce a single surah like the Quran proves that it cannot be the work of any human, including famous figures like Waraqah ibn Nawfal or Bahira the monk. He argues that if a jinn had inspired it, that jinn would be capable of producing similar speech, and it is inconceivable that no other jinn would have rivaled him over the centuries by inspiring a similar Quran to someone else who wished to produce it. Allah has also challenged both humans and jinn (citing Quran 26:210-212). Since no such rival speech has appeared, experience again disproves jinn authorship. Thus, the induction is complete: the Quran cannot be from any human or jinn. The only remaining logical possibility is that it is the speech of Allah, brought by the Trustworthy Spirit (Jibril) to Muhammad, confirming his prophethood. The passage concludes with the verse from Surah Hud (11:14) that calls for submission.

Commentary
This passage presents a multi-step inductive proof (istiqrā’ tāmm) for the divine origin of the Quran. The author, Ramadan al-Buti, begins by noting that the Arab opponents of the Prophet shifted their stance: initially they claimed they could produce something like the Quran if they wished, but later they resorted to calling it sorcery, soothsaying, or poetry (citing Quran 43:30). He then points to the historical fact that the Quranic challenge remained unmet across centuries, despite the abundance of skilled poets and orators among the Arabs. This empirical evidence (tajriba mushāhada) itself is a clear proof of the Quran’s inimitability (i‘jāz). Next, Buti applies the method of complete induction: the inability of all Arabs to produce a single surah like the Quran proves that it cannot be the work of any human, including famous figures like Waraqah ibn Nawfal or Bahira the monk. Moreover, the Quran contains references to events after their deaths, which rules out their authorship. He then addresses the hypothetical possibility of jinn inspiration. He argues that if a jinn had inspired it, that jinn would be capable of producing similar speech, and it is inconceivable that no other jinn would have rivaled him over the centuries by inspiring a similar Quran to someone else who wished to produce it. Allah has also challenged both humans and jinn (citing Quran 26:210-212). Since no such rival speech has appeared, experience again disproves jinn authorship. Thus, the induction is complete: the Quran cannot be from any human or jinn. The only remaining logical possibility is that it is the speech of Allah, brought by the Trustworthy Spirit (Jibril) to Muhammad, confirming his prophethood. The passage concludes with the verse from Surah Hud (11:14) that calls for submission.

How Miracles Are Proven and the Ruling on Denying Them
Buti argues that miracles are proven by tawatir and denial is disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Prophet's miracles are authenticated through mutawatir transmission and the different rulings for denying them based on the strength of their narration.

Core Idea
Denying all miracles is kufr; denying a specific one (if ahad) is fisq, not kufr.

Buti addresses a critical question: how do we know the Prophet's miracles are true, and what happens if someone rejects them? He first shows that the Quran itself mentions clear miracles, thereby refuting skeptics. Then he sets a methodological principle: in creedal matters, we only accept reports that reach the level of mutawatir (mass transmission). This principle leads to a nuanced ruling that distinguishes between rejecting the entire set of miracles (kufr) versus rejecting a particular miracle reported through a single chain (fisq).

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic evidence
Notice how Buti starts by citing three verses that explicitly mention miracles: the Night Journey, splitting of the moon, and angels at Badr. This establishes that miracles are at the core of the Qur'anic message.
These verses are directly from the Quran, so their authenticity is beyond doubt.
2
Understand the methodological principle
Buti then states that in creed ('aqida), we only rely on certain proofs (barahin yaqiniyya). Hence, only miracles reported through mutawatir transmission are required for belief.
Mutawatir means reported by so many narrators at each level that collusion to lie is impossible.
3
Grasp the two-level ruling
The passage distinguishes between: (a) denying the totality of miracles known by tawatur – this is kufr by consensus; (b) denying a specific miracle known only through ahad (isolated) reports – this is not kufr but is fisq (transgression).
This nuance prevents hasty takfir (excommunication) while maintaining respect for the Sunnah.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this principle protects the integrity of creed: it requires high certainty for core beliefs but avoids declaring disbelief on matters where certainty is lower.
This is a hallmark of Sunni methodology: balanced and evidence-based.

إذا تبين لك هذا ، فلا بد أن نعرض لمسألتين تتعلقان بهذا الصدد ، نكشف فيهما عن وجهة الحقيقة العلمية التي لا ينبغي أن يصار إلى غيرها المسألة الأولى : أن بعض الكاتبين من تلامذة مدرسة الشيخ محمد عبده أنكروا أن يكون عيسى بن مريم عليه الصلاة والسلام قد رفع بجسمه إلى السماء قالوا : وإنما هو ارتفاع الروح أو الدرجة ، ومن تم فإنهم أنكروا نزوله إلى الأرض أيضاً قرب قيام الساعة . وقد كتب في ذلك الشيخ محمود شلتوت مقالاً في مجلة الرسالة » عدد ٤٦٢ أعقبه بكتابة مقالات أخرى في نفس المبحث ؛ وكان الذي انتهى إليه في مقالاته تلك ، هو تأويل الآيات الواردة ، والإعراض عن الأحاديث الكثيرة الثابتة، زاعماً أنها أحاديث آحاد لا يصلح أن يقام عليها الاعتقاد !! . ولعلك قد قرأت ما كتبناه عن موقف العلم والعقل من المعجزة في بحث النبوات ، ووقفت إذ ذاك على قصة المدرسة التي قامت تنكر المعجزة دون أن تنكر الدين صراحة ، كما وقفت على سر انبعاث تلك المدرسة والظروف المكونة لها فإذا ذكرت ما قد قلناه إذ ذاك ، فاعلم أن إنكار نزول عيسى عليه الصلاة والسلام على الرغم مما ثبت في ذلك من الأدلة القاطعة ، ليس إلا صدى طبيعياً من أصداء تلك المدرسة وثمرة تتفق كل الاتفاق مع مبادئها بيد أن كلاً من الاضطراب والتخبط الأعمى اللذين لا يبالي أن يقع فيهما دعاة تلك المدرسة وأتباعها ، يتمثل في محاولة التوفيق والجمع بين التمسك بالإسلام والتمسك بإنكار الخوارق والمعجزات !! ونحن لانشك في أنها محاولة من حيث الظاهر فحسب ، اتقاء لغضب المسلمين وتدرجاً في التسلل إلى موضع العقيدة من قلوبهم . فهذا التخبط في محاولة التوفيق ، هو الذي جعل واحداً مثل الشيخ شلتوت لا يبالي أن يكذِّب سبعين حديثاً مع رواتها ، وأن يخطئ عامة المفسرين لكتاب الله منذ عصر الصحابة إلى بزوغ مدرسة أستاذه - في سبيل أن تسلم له عقيدة إنكار الخوارق والمعجزات !! والأعجب من ذلك أن يكذب الأحاديث بدون شاهد ، أو صورة شـاهـد
Now that this is clear to you, we must present two issues related to this subject, in which we uncover the truth of the scientific reality that should not be turned away from. The first issue: that some writers from the disciples of the school of Sheikh Muhammad Abduh denied that Jesus son of Mary (peace be upon him) was raised bodily to heaven. They said: it is only the elevation of the spirit or rank, and consequently they also denied his descent to earth near the establishment of the Hour. And Sheikh Mahmoud Shaltut wrote an article on this in the magazine 'al-Risala' issue 462, followed by other articles on the same topic. And what he concluded in those articles was to misinterpret the revealed verses and turn away from the many established hadiths, claiming that they are ahad hadiths upon which belief cannot be based!! And you may have read what we wrote about the position of science and reason towards miracles in the discussion of prophethood, and you learned then the story of the school that began to deny miracles without explicitly denying religion, and you learned the secret of the emergence of that school and the circumstances that formed it. So if you recall what we said then, know that the denial of the descent of Jesus (peace be upon him), despite the conclusive evidence established on it, is nothing but a natural echo of that school and a fruit that is entirely consistent with its principles. However, both the confusion and blind recklessness that the advocates of that school and their followers do not mind falling into, is manifested in the attempt to reconcile and combine adherence to Islam with adherence to the denial of supernatural events and miracles!! And we have no doubt that it is only an outward attempt, out of fear of the anger of Muslims and a gradual infiltration into the place of creed in their hearts. So this recklessness in the attempt at reconciliation is what made someone like Sheikh Shaltut not mind denying seventy hadiths along with their narrators, and declaring the common error of the interpreters of the Book of Allah from the time of the Companions until the rise of his teacher's school - in order to preserve for himself the creed of denying supernatural events and miracles!! And even more astonishing is that he denies the hadiths without any witness or any semblance of a witness.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the modernist denial of Jesus's bodily ascension and subsequent descent. Buti begins by introducing two issues (though only the first is detailed in this window). The first issue concerns a group of writers from the school of Muhammad Abduh who denied that Jesus was raised bodily; they claimed only his spirit or rank was elevated, thus also denying his future physical descent. Buti then focuses on Mahmoud Shaltut, who published articles arguing this position by reinterpreting Quranic verses and dismissing the many authentic hadiths as ahad (single-narrator) and therefore insufficient for belief. Buti reminds the reader of his earlier discussion on miracles in the context of prophethood, where he explained the existence of a school that denies miracles while ostensibly maintaining adherence to Islam. He asserts that Shaltut's denial is merely an echo of that school, and that the real issue is an attempt to reconcile Islam with the denial of miracles—a contradiction that leads to intellectual confusion. Buti accuses them of only pretending to be Muslim to avoid backlash, while gradually undermining the faith. He specifically criticizes Shaltut for arbitrarily rejecting seventy hadiths and contradicting the consensus of Quranic interpreters from the Companions onward, all to preserve his rejection of miracles. The passage ends by highlighting the absurdity of denying hadiths without any evidence.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The belief that ahad hadith (solitary narrations) cannot be used as evidence for matters of belief.
Correction: Buti refutes this by showing that the many hadiths on Jesus's ascension are established (ثابتة) and that rejecting them without valid grounds is arbitrary. The consensus of scholars across generations affirms their reliability.
