The Vision of Allah in This World and the Two Shahadas
Conclusion of vision debate and transition to prophethood via shahadas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the debate on seeing Allah in this world is resolved, why the majority view is preferred, and how this topic connects to the two shahadas and the study of prophethood.

Core Idea
Seeing Allah in this world is possible for the Prophet, and all divine knowledge is contained in the first shahada.

This passage marks a pivotal transition in the text. After addressing the controversial issue of seeing Allah in this world, Buti concludes with the preferred view of the majority of Companions and scholars. He then emphasizes that all divine truths are encapsulated in the first shahada, and the second shahada introduces the study of prophethood.

Guided Path

Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Notice the flow from discussing the vision to concluding divine matters and introducing prophethood.
The passage contains a brief argument from opponents and the author's response.
2
Identify the two main parts
The passage has two distinct sections: first, the conclusion of the vision of Allah in this world; second, the transition to prophethood via the two shahadas. Mark where the shift occurs.
The shift happens at 'وهذا آخر ما يجب أن تعلمه'.
3
Understand the vision conclusion
Note that the author presents an objection from those who deny the vision, then states that since the Companions differed, no absolute certainty is required. However, the preferred view is that hearing (scripture) indicates both possibility and actual occurrence for the Prophet.
The word 'عروسه' is unclear but likely refers to the Prophet (peace be upon him).
4
Connect to the two shahadas
The author states that all divine matters are contained in 'la ilaha illa Allah', and prophethood topics are contained in the second shahada. This shows the structure of Islamic creed.

Commentary
This passage concludes the discussion on the vision of Allah in this world. The author begins by mentioning an argument from those who deny the vision: that Allah called it a trial (fitna) for people, and it is unreasonable to base a trial on a dream vision. But then he notes that the Companions themselves differed on this issue, so there is no call for dogmatism in creed regarding either opinion. However, he states that the preferred view is that of the majority of Companions and later scholars: that hearing (samʿ, i.e., scriptural evidence) indicates both the possibility and actual occurrence of seeing Allah in this world, specifically for the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The passage ends by stating that this is the last of the divine matters to be learned, all contained in the first shahada, and then transitions to the second shahada, which covers prophethood.

Purpose and Accountability from God's Wisdom
Buti argues from God's wisdom that human life cannot be purposeless.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why belief in a wise Creator necessarily implies that human life has purpose and that we are accountable for our actions.

Core Idea
A wise God does not create in vain; human intellect implies purpose beyond mere survival.

Having established God's existence and attributes, Buti now draws out the necessary implications. If God is wise and all-knowing, then creating humans with intellect and free will cannot be without purpose. In this passage, he argues that accepting a Creator means accepting that life has meaning and that we have responsibilities.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening rhetorical questions
Notice how Buti uses questions to force the reader to think: after believing in God, can we really imagine we have no purpose? Then read his reasoning that a wise God cannot create absurdly.
2
Identify the key attributes of God used
Buti refers to God as 'Hakim' (Wise) and states that 'absurdity is inconceivable regarding Him'. Underline these points.
3
Reflect on the intellect as evidence
Buti points out that humans are distinguished by intellect, which allows us to explore the universe. If that intellect has no ultimate purpose, it would be absurd. Consider how this connects to the idea of accountability.
4
Summarize the argument
Write in your own words: why does belief in a wise God lead to belief in human purpose? The passage provides a clear logical chain.

Commentary
This passage is a rhetorical argument from wisdom and justice. The author, having previously established belief in God's existence and attributes, now poses a series of rhetorical questions to demonstrate that such belief necessarily implies human purpose and ultimate accountability. He begins by asking whether, after recognizing God as the Wise Disposer of the universe, we can imagine that humans have no function or responsibility, and whether a wise God would create humans with intellect only for them to live like animals and then die into nothingness. The author asserts that any rational person who believes in God cannot accept such absurdity, because God's wisdom precludes futility. He then contrasts the tyrant and the oppressed in this world, asking if their story can end with death without a sequel that restores justice. He uses the analogy of an incomplete play to argue that a wise author would not leave his story unresolved. Similarly, a rational child would not compose a play with no conclusion; how then could God, the All-Wise, create such a grand universe with no ultimate purpose? The author then criticizes those who acknowledge a 'supernatural power' but then stop thinking about the implications, comparing them to a man who finds evidence of a predator in a cave but then goes to sleep without further investigation. Finally, he distinguishes between those who have not yet found God (though still blameworthy) and those who recognize a higher power but fail to pursue the moral and eschatological consequences. The passage thus serves as a powerful appeal to reason and conscience to affirm the necessity of divine justice and a life beyond death.

Justice Requires an Afterlife
Buti argues from divine justice that an afterlife is necessary to rectify worldly injustices.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the existence of injustice in this world, combined with God's justice, necessitates a final reckoning beyond death.

Core Idea
Injustice in this world demands a sequel; a just God will restore balance in the afterlife.

Buti continues his argument by turning to the problem of injustice. If there is no life after death, then oppressors escape punishment and the oppressed never receive justice. But if God is just, this cannot be the end. Through the analogy of an unfinished play, Buti demonstrates that a wise author does not leave his story incomplete. This passage invites us to see the world as a story that must have a just conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the contrast between oppressor and oppressed
Notice how Buti describes two types of people: one who lives in tyranny and luxury, another who is oppressed and miserable. He asks if their story can end with death without a sequel that restores justice.
2
Examine the play analogy
Buti compares the world to a play with only one act. A rational child would not write such a play; how then could God, the All-Wise, leave His story unfinished? Reflect on why this analogy is powerful.
3
Consider the cave analogy
Buti then criticizes those who acknowledge a supernatural power but stop thinking. He compares them to a man who finds evidence of a predator in a cave but goes to sleep. This shows that recognizing God requires us to pursue the implications, including justice.
4
Summarize the necessity of afterlife
Write down the logical steps: if God is just, then injustice must be addressed; since it's not fully addressed in this life, there must be a life after death where justice is served.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a practical application of the general rule on apostasy to a specific contemporary issue: judging by other than Allah's law (al-hukmu bighayri ma anzala Allah). Buti emphasizes that the core criterion is belief (i'tiqad): apostasy hinges on inner conviction, but that conviction is inferred from outward evidence. Thus, in the absence of clear indicators of denial or contempt, one must give the benefit of the doubt and refrain from takfir.

ثانياً : صفات الله تعالى : صفة ( الوجود ) - الفرق بين وجود الله ووجود الإنسان - الخبل الذي وقع فيه الفلاسفة ( الوجوديون ) والوهم الذي انجرف فيه بعض الصوفية - لا يحملنك ما نقول على تقليد بعض الناس في تكفير من نسب إليهم القول بوحدة الوجود - الصفات السلبية : ١ ( الوحدانية ومعناها ودليلها ٢ ( القدم : معناه ودليله - سؤال بعضهم : من خلق الله والجواب عليه - ٣ ) البقاء :
Secondly: The attributes of God Almighty. The attribute of existence (al-wujud). The difference between the existence of God and the existence of humans. The madness that the (existentialist) philosophers fell into and the delusion that some Sufis were carried away by. Do not let what we say lead you to imitate some people in declaring disbelief (takfir) against those who are attributed with the statement of the unity of existence (wahdat al-wujud). The negative attributes: 1) Oneness (al-wahdaniyyah): its meaning and its proof. 2) Eternity (al-qidam): its meaning and its proof. The question of some: 'Who created God?' and the answer to it. 3) Permanence (al-baqa'):

Commentary
This passage discusses divine attributes. It begins with the attribute of existence (al-wujud), explaining the difference between God's existence and human existence. Buti warns against the error of existentialist philosophers and some Sufis who were deluded about unity of existence (wahdat al-wujud). He cautions against imitating those who declare takfir against people associated with this view. Then he moves to the negative attributes (al-sifat al-salbiyyah). He lists three: oneness (al-wahdaniyyah) with its meaning and proof, eternity (al-qidam) with its meaning and proof (and answers the question 'who created God?'), and permanence (al-baqa'), but the text cuts off.
