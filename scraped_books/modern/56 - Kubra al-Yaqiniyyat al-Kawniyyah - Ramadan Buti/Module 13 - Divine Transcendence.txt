The Dangerous Qualities Placed in Humans
Buti explains the dangerous nature of human qualities as attributes of lordship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how qualities like knowledge, power, and authority, though divine in origin, become dangerous intoxicants that lead to oppression and conflict when not guided by religion.

Core Idea
Human qualities are divine in origin but become destructive without divine guidance.

Buti begins by referencing the Quranic verse about the trust to highlight the immense potential and danger of human qualities. He explains that these qualities are essentially attributes of lordship, which can intoxicate man and cause him to forget his true nature. This lesson examines the nature of these qualities and their destructive consequences when left unchecked.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic verse and explanation
Read the verse about the trust and Buti's comment on its importance. Notice how he emphasizes the gravity of the qualities entrusted to humans.
The verse is from Surah Al-Ahzab, 72.
2
Identify the qualities as divine attributes
Buti lists qualities: knowledge, power, authority, ownership, omnipotence. He says they are components of divinity. Understand why he calls them dangerous.
These are attributes of rububiyyah (lordship).
3
Observe the consequences of unchecked qualities
Buti describes how these qualities lead to oppression, exploitation, and bloody conflict. Reflect on historical examples he alludes to.
He mentions the constant events of history as evidence.

وتصبح نزعة القوة والبطش سبيلاً إلى حراسة الحقوق وحفظ العدالة.
For that reason, Allah gave these weapons with which this creature was entrusted with the trust, and He showed the extent of their importance and great significance in His saying: 'Indeed, We offered the trust to the heavens and the earth and the mountains, and they refused to bear it and feared from it, and man bore it. Indeed, he was unjust and ignorant.' [Al-Ahzab: 72]. And the source of the danger of these qualities is that in their reality they are nothing but qualities of lordship: knowledge, power, authority, ownership, and omnipotence — all are components of divinity and attributes of the Lord, glorified be He. It is the nature of these qualities when found in a human to intoxicate him, seize his mind, make him forget his reality, and cause him to stretch towards the level of lordship and divinity, even though in truth man possesses of them only shadows and traces that have nothing of the reality of the divine attributes except the name alone. Among the dangerous consequences of these qualities is that they cause their possessor to use the quality of power to oppress others, to satisfy his inclination to control and authority by extending his influence and dominion over the weak among communities, and to direct his possessive inclination towards others' wealth to plunder and destroy it. Then among their consequences is that groups of people, driven by these qualities, race in a field of bloody conflict over authority, status, possessions, rule, and leadership; and the constant events of history clearly indicate this. Thus these qualities turn into a factor of disorder and misery in human life, while they were only placed in him to be a factor of happiness, progress, and order. Therefore, there must be another force that directs these qualities to the righteous direction and prevents man from using their weapons except within their beneficial limits. What then is this force that controls the evil of all those faculties and qualities and pushes them solely towards righteousness? True religion is the bridle that protects man from the danger of these qualities. That is the need of all humanity for religion, that is, for the correct creed about man, the universe, life, and what lies beyond all that. And the correct creed to which reason and knowledge guide is: faith in the existence of Allah and His oneness, that there is no true authority in the universe other than His authority, no overwhelming power other than His power, no ownership other than His ownership, and everything beyond that is a creation of Allah, may He be exalted, who grants it when He wills and takes it away when He wills, and that He is the Watcher over all His servants, and He will resurrect them after death and will hold each accountable for what he earned, so whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it. When man contemplates all this and believes in it with firm belief based upon free, thoughtful intellectual inquiry, he feels in the depths of his entire being that he is a servant of this one, great God. And these dangerous yet important qualities he possesses become less than to cause him to exceed the limit of his servitude, and they are nothing but transformed into a great means for his happiness as an individual and for the happiness of his fellow humans as a community, and the bond of brotherhood and equality before their servitude to God is established among people, after there had been among them unworthy competitions and rivalries in a field where forces clash, spears rattle, and the weak become victims of the desires of the strong and the intoxication of his madness. Then the possessive tendency in man becomes a natural means for establishing a just, comfortable life in which civilization flourishes, gardens and paradises become green in its areas, and good things multiply in its surroundings. And the tendency of power and violence becomes a path to protect rights and preserve justice.

Commentary
This passage is an argument demonstrating the necessity of religion to control the dangerous qualities inherent in human nature. Buti begins by referring to the Quranic verse about the trust (al-amanah) to highlight that humans carry immense potential. He then explains that these qualities—knowledge, power, authority, ownership, omnipotence—are essentially attributes of lordship (rububiyyah). When found in humans, they intoxicate and cause him to forget his place, even though he possesses only shadows of these attributes. This leads to oppression, conflict, and misery. Buti argues that these qualities were intended for happiness and order, but without guidance they cause disorder. Therefore, a restraining force is needed. He identifies this force as true religion (al-din al-haqq), which provides the correct creed (al-'aqidah al-sahihah) about God, the universe, and the afterlife. This creed includes belief in God's oneness, absolute sovereignty, and accountability. When a person truly believes through rational inquiry, he realizes his servitude to God, and the dangerous qualities are transformed into means for individual and communal happiness, brotherhood, and justice. The passage thus presents a coherent argument: the problem (human dangerous qualities), the solution (religion), and the outcome (transformation and societal harmony).

True Religion as the Solution
Buti presents true religion as the solution to control human destructive qualities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how true religion acts as a bridle to control dangerous human qualities and transforms them into tools for justice and harmony.

Core Idea
True religion redirects dangerous human qualities towards righteousness and establishes a just society.

After describing the destructive potential of human qualities, Buti explains that a restraining force is necessary. He identifies true religion and the correct creed as that force. This lesson explores how belief in God's oneness, sovereignty, and accountability transforms human behavior and establishes brotherhood and justice.

Negative Attributes and Divine Unity
Buti defines negative attributes and explains the first: al-waḥdāniyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the category of negative attributes and the meaning of al-waḥdāniyya as the negation of all quantity from Allah's essence and attributes.

Core Idea
Negative attributes strip away all imperfection from Allah.

In this passage, Buti introduces the five 'mother' negative attributes, beginning with the first: absolute unity (al-waḥdāniyya). He explains that it negates any notion of quantity—whether composition or multiplicity—from Allah's essence and attributes. This foundational concept is key to understanding divine transcendence in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of negative attributes
Start by reading the first few lines where Buti defines negative attributes. Note that they negate what is unbefitting to Allah and that there are five comprehensive ones.
This sets the context for the specific attribute being discussed.
2
Focus on the explanation of al-waḥdāniyya
Read the explanation of unity carefully. Notice that it negates two kinds of quantity: connected (parts) and disconnected (multiplicity of instances).
Buti gives examples like 'no two knowledges' and 'no one has knowledge like His'.
3
Understand the terms 'part' and 'particular'
Examine how Buti defines 'part' (juz') and 'particular' (juz'ī). Reflect on how these concepts reinforce the denial of composition and similarity in Allah.
This clarifies the precise meaning of the negation.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument about the impossibility of imagining Allah and the value of bewilderment. This belief distinguishes believers from atheists; if the unseen were visible, there would be no merit, because obligation (taklif) requires an element of the unseen.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage refutes a classic theological objection: the question 'Can God create another god like Himself?' The author, Ramadan Buti, identifies this as huthyan (meaningless speech) rather than a genuine question. The argument proceeds as follows: A genuine question requires that the questioner conceive a coherent meaning for his question. If the question has no intelligible meaning, it has no mental form. The author compares it to asking 'Can you be absent and present before me at the same time?'—this has no conceivable meaning, so any answer would be arbitrary. He then addresses the special case of a child asking such a question. The author clarifies that this is teaching the ignorant, not answering a questioner. The passage concludes with another example of such huthyan: 'Can God create a rock He cannot carry?' This reduces to 'Can God be incapable?'—a contradiction in terms.

Core Idea
Ambiguous verses must yield to clear ones when interpreting God's attributes.

After establishing that God is beyond all imperfections, we encounter verses and hadiths that seem to contradict this. How can 'Your Lord comes' or 'the hand of God' be understood without compromising tanzih? Buti addresses this tension by introducing the categories of muhkamat (clear) and mutashabih (ambiguous) and showing how the former governs the interpretation of the latter.

Commentary
This passage argues against leaving ambiguous Quranic verses (mutashabihat) on their literal, apparent meaning without any interpretation (ta'wil). The author begins by stating that such a literal approach is impermissible and was not adopted by the righteous predecessors (salaf) or later scholars (khalaf). He then provides examples: the singular 'eye' in one verse and plural 'eyes' in another would imply contradiction if taken literally; similarly, the verses about Allah being 'established on the Throne' and being 'closer than the jugular vein' would conflict; also, 'in heaven' and 'in earth' would contradict. The proper method, he explains, is to affirm transcendence (tanzih) — negating any resemblance to creation — and then affirm what Allah has affirmed for Himself in a way befitting His majesty. This involves entrusting (tafwid) the detailed meaning of these texts to Allah. This is the way of the salaf, who said 'pass them on without how' (amirruha bila kayf). The author notes that this statement itself implies a general interpretation: they recognized that the apparent meanings are not what is intended, due to the definitive verses (muhkamat) that establish transcendence. However, they refrained from specifying a particular modality (kaifiyya), which is the abstention from detailed interpretation (tafwid). The passage concludes by urging reflection, calling this the precise truth.

Khalaf's Method and Ta'wil of 'Istawa'
Buti explains the khalaf's method and their ta'wil of 'istawa'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the khalaf's general method of ta'wil and their specific interpretation of 'istawa' as domination of power and authority.

Core Idea
The khalaf interpret ambiguous verses to preserve tanzih by assigning metaphorical meanings.

The later scholars (khalaf) did not simply relegate ambiguous verses to Allah's knowledge. Instead, they actively interpreted them in ways that harmonized with the definitive texts affirming Allah's transcendence. This lesson introduces their method and examines their interpretation of the verse about Allah's 'istawa' on the Throne.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the khalaf's method
Read the Arabic passage once to grasp the general approach: they interpret ambiguous texts to align with definitive texts that assert tanzih.
Notice the key terms: تأويل, نصوص محكمة, تنزيه.
2
Identify the specific ta'wil of 'istawa'
Find how they interpret 'istawa' in the verse. They say it means 'tasallut al-quwwa wa al-sultan' (domination of power and authority).
This is a valid linguistic meaning, not a forced interpretation.
3
Reflect on the purpose
Ask yourself: Why did they choose this meaning? How does it avoid anthropomorphism while remaining within the Arabic language?
This connects to the core idea of preserving divine transcendence.

What the successors specifically did: And the doctrine of the successors who came after them is the interpretation of these texts in a way that places them on a single path of agreement with the other definitive texts that decisively assert Allah's transcendence over direction, place, and limb. So they interpreted 'istawa' in 'The Most Merciful firmly established upon the Throne' (Ta-Ha: 5) as domination of power and authority, which is a meaning established in the language and known. And they interpreted 'the hand' in the other verse as power or generosity, and 'the eye' as care and protection, and they interpreted 'the two fingers' in the hadith as will and power. And they said concerning the hadith 'Verily Allah created Adam upon His image' that the pronoun refers to Adam, not to Allah's essence, meaning that Allah created Adam from the moment He brought him into existence upon his own image and form which he would later possess, so he did not develop from one form to another. Commentary
This passage presents the method of the later scholars (khalaf) regarding ambiguous verses (mutashabihat). After the section on the salaf's tafwid, the author now describes what the khalaf uniquely did: they engaged in specific interpretation (ta'wil) to harmonize these verses with the definitive verses (muhkamat) that affirm Allah's transcendence (tanzih) beyond direction, place, and limb. They interpreted 'istawa' as 'domination of power and authority' (istila' al-quwwa wa al-sultan), which is a valid linguistic meaning. They interpreted 'hand' as power or generosity, 'eye' as care and protection. For the hadith about 'two fingers' (referring to the hearts being between two of Allah's fingers), they interpreted it as will and power. For the famous hadith 'Allah created Adam upon His image', they offered two possibilities: either the pronoun refers to Adam (i.e., Adam was created in his own human form that he would have throughout life), or it refers to the 'brother' mentioned in a variant of the hadith (i.e., do not strike the face because Allah created Adam in the image of that brother). This shows the khalaf's willingness to assign specific metaphorical meanings to avoid anthropomorphism, while the salaf generally refrained from such detailed interpretation.

Ta'wil of the Hadith: Allah Created Adam upon His Image
Buti presents the khalaf's two ta'wil of the hadith about Adam's image.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two interpretations offered by the khalaf for the hadith about Allah creating Adam upon His image.

Core Idea
The ambiguous pronoun 'his' is interpreted as referring to Adam or the brother, not Allah.

One of the most debated hadiths concerns Allah creating Adam upon His image. The khalaf offered two possible interpretations to avoid attributing a form to Allah. This lesson presents both possibilities in detail, showing their reasoning and the supporting narration.

Guided Path
1
Read the first interpretation
The khalaf said the pronoun 'his' refers to Adam himself. This means Adam was created in his own human form from the beginning, not that he evolved from another shape.
This interpretation avoids saying Adam was created in Allah's image.
2
Read the second interpretation
Alternatively, the pronoun refers to the 'brother' mentioned in the full hadith: 'When one of you fights his brother, let him avoid the face, for verily Allah created Adam upon his image.' Here 'his' refers to the brother being fought.
This relies on a variant narration in Sahih Muslim.
3
Understand the principle
Both interpretations show the khalaf's effort to preserve tanzih by reinterpreting ambiguous wording. The pronoun cannot refer to Allah.
This is consistent with their method of ta'wil.

If one seeks a hidden motive behind every creation and will, that means one assumes a driving final cause, which contradicts what has been established. The passage concludes that the universe indeed operates on a system of causality (fi manhajihi wa mazharihi ʿala nizam al-ʿilla) and that this system aims to alert minds to the Creator, which negates absurdity. However, this does not mean that God uses intermediaries as independent agents; He is the ultimate cause and the Creator of all means.
