The Ontological Status of Imkan and Its Relation to External Existence
Analysis of whether imkan exists externally and its dependency on a subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the debate over whether imkan (possibility) exists externally or is a mental concept that requires an external subject.

Core Idea
Imkan is not a thing in the external world—it is a mental relation that points to a real subject.

The question of whether possibility (imkan) is a real entity outside the mind or merely a conceptual tool has deep implications for how we understand causation and divine action. This lesson examines the argument that imkan is a mental attribute that nevertheless depends on an external reality, much like blindness requires a subject even though blindness itself is not a physical object.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Begin by reading the complete Arabic passage and its translation. Let the author's argument unfold naturally without overanalyzing.
The passage is dense; read at a steady pace.
2
Identify the main claim
Look for the statement that imkan is a mental matter (أمر عقلي) but relates to an external thing. This is the central thesis.
3
Follow the analogy of blindness
The text compares imkan to blindness: both are not externally existent but require an existing locus (e.g., a person). This illustrates the relation.
Reflect on how an attribute can be non-existent yet depend on reality.
4
Understand the objection and resolution
The passage addresses an objection about regress and explains that imkan is a relation between event and cause, which exists only in the mind, avoiding the problem.
The resolution clarifies that imkan does not need an external locus beyond the relata.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of the ontological status of imkan (possibility), specifically whether it exists externally or is purely mental. The author begins by setting the context: the First Principle's effusion is general, and differentiation arises only from differing preparedness of recipients. Then a view is presented that imkan does not exist externally but is a mental concept related to an external thing. The explanation then parses the commentator's statements: imkan is a mental attribute, but it requires an external subject (like whiteness requiring a body). The analogy of blindness is used to show that some attributes are not existent externally but necessitate a locus. The passage then addresses an objection from the Imam (al-Razi) that if imkan existed it would lead to a regress. The author explains that imkan is a relation between the event and the cause in the mind, and that the talk of nearness and distance refers to conditions of existence, which do not require an external locus. The conclusion is that imkan itself is not externally existent; rather, it is the imkan of existence that relates to an external thing. The final sentence notes a debate: if the relation is mental-external, it does not prove the external existence of the subject, implying a potential flaw in the argument.

Unity Nullifies Multiplicity Directly
Sanusi refutes the objection that unity destroys multiplicity accidentally.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Sanusi rejects the objection that unity only accidentally destroys multiplicity, and how he proves that nullifying the parts is identical to nullifying the whole.

Core Idea
Nullifying the parts of multiplicity is exactly nullifying multiplicity itself.

An objector challenges that a contrary should destroy its opposite directly, not by first removing its components. Sanusi responds by showing that removing the components is the same as removing the whole, because a whole is nothing but its parts. This lesson unpacks that crucial argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection

2
See Sanusi's direct reply
Read Sanusi's rebuttal: 'إبطال الوحدات المقومة عين إبطال الكثرة' — it is identical.
This is the core thesis.
3
Examine the part-whole principle
He argues that removing a part is the same as removing the whole in actuality, unlike the relation of necessary concomitant.
4
Relate to contraries
Note that this makes unity a proper contrary of multiplicity, acting directly.
Thus the objection fails.

The Fourth Observatory - The Third Aim: Unity and Multiplicity are not Essential. It cannot be said: When unity occurs upon a subject, it does not destroy multiplicity essentially, but rather it nullifies the constituent unities, then from their nullification necessarily follows the nullification of multiplicity accidentally. And it is the property of a contrary to nullify its contrary essentially, not accidentally; for we say: The nullification of the constituent unities is identical to the nullification of multiplicity, because the removal of the part is the removal of the whole itself, unlike the removal of the necessary concomitant, for it necessitates the removal of the subject. Therefore it is possible to conceive the removal of the concomitant with the subject, even if the conceived is impossible. And it is not possible to conceive the removal of the part while the whole remains, for the conception here is impossible, like the impossible. His statement: (It cannot be said, etc.) meaning what you mentioned is conceded regarding multiplicity, but regarding unity it is denied. His statement: But it nullifies the unities, etc.) meaning their essences and existences; and when every unity of them is removed, the multiplicity composed of them is removed. His statement: (Because the removal of the part is the removal of the whole, etc.) i.e., in actuality; for there are not in external existence two removals one of which is consequent upon the other; rather the difference between them is according to the concept in the mind, and by this consideration the intellect judges between them by causality, and the entry of the particle 'fa' between them is valid. Therefore the verifiers said: The causality of non-existence is not in reality except the absence of the causality of existence in the external, and the consideration of causality between two non-existences is only in the mind. And by this the contradiction between his two statements is repelled.

Commentary
This passage presents a proof that unity eliminates multiplicity directly, not indirectly. The objection assumed that unity, as a contrary, should destroy its opposite essentially, but here it is argued that unity destroys multiplicity by destroying the unities that constitute it. The core analogy: removing a part is identical to removing the whole in actuality (صدقاً), whereas removing a necessary concomitant (لازم) only implies removing the subject (ملزوم) but can be conceived separately. The commentary clarifies that the relation between part and whole is not one of causal succession in external existence; rather, the mind distinguishes them conceptually. Thus, the nullification of the constituent unities is exactly the nullification of multiplicity, establishing direct opposition.

The Metaphysical Distinction: Part vs. Necessary Concomitant
Sanusi explains the metaphysical basis of the part-whole principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the metaphysical principle that distinguishes the part-whole relation from the relation of necessary concomitant, and how this principle underpins Sanusi's argument.

Core Idea
Part and whole are existentially identical; necessary concomitant and subject are only conceptually linked.

Sanusi's commentary elaborates on the distinction between nullifying a part and nullifying a necessary concomitant. He clarifies that in external reality, there is only one removal — removing the part is removing the whole. The relation of causality between them is only in the mind. This lesson explores that deep conceptual clarification.

Guided Path

Read the commentary

Understand 'صدقاً'
The removal of the part is the removal of the whole in actuality (صدقاً); they are not two separate events.
This is the key metaphysical claim.
3
Grasp the mental causality
The apparent causality between the two removals is only in the mind; in reality, there is no sequential causation.
This resolves the apparent contradiction with the earlier claim about اللازم.
4
Appreciate the resolution
The objector's confusion arises from mistaking a mental distinction for an existential one.
Sanusi's argument remains sound.

The Fifth Observation - The Third Objective: Attribution of multiple effects to a single cause. Composition in the truly simple [entity] is impossible; this is a contradiction. (Otherwise) if neither of these two [concepts] nor one of them enters into it, then that truly simple [entity] would be a source for their two source-hoods (مصدرية), i.e., for the source-hoods of (a) and (b), just as it was their source; for it is not permissible that the two source-hoods be attributed to something else, otherwise it would not be the sole source of (a) and (b), and the supposition is contrary. So these two concepts: if they enter into it or one of them, composition follows; otherwise it would be a source for them also, and a regress in source-hoods would follow. And this line of reasoning may be presented in a simpler way, by saying: If each of the two concepts of source-hood of (a) and source-hood of (b) were the truly simple entity itself, then a simple thing would have two different essences. If both enter into it, or one enters and the other is identical, composition alone follows. If both exit, or one exits and the other is identical, regress alone follows. If one enters and the other exits, composition and regress together follow. So the cases are six, and all are impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents the first argument (الوجه الأول) that a single simple entity (الواحد الحقيقي) cannot be the source of two distinct effects. The argument proceeds by analyzing the possible relationships between the concepts of 'being the source' for each effect and the simple entity itself. The author considers whether these concepts (مفهومي المصدرية) are identical to the simple entity, enter into it, or are outside it. Six possible cases are enumerated: 1) both concepts are the simple entity itself (leads to two essences for one thing), 2) both enter into it (composition), 3) one enters and the other is identical (composition), 4) both exit (regress), 5) one exits and the other is identical (regress), 6) one enters and the other exits (both composition and regress). All six are impossible, thus the initial assumption that a single simple cause produces two distinct effects is false.

The Contradiction of Distinct Effects from a Simple Source
Al-Jurjani presents the third argument from contradiction against a simple source producing two distinct effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why a single simple cause cannot produce two distinct effects without contradiction.

Core Idea
A truly simple entity cannot be the source of diversity without contradiction.

Al-Jurjani presents a concise logical argument against the idea that a single simple entity can produce multiple distinct effects. This argument highlights the necessity of composition in sources of multiplicity, pointing to the uniqueness of the divine. Let's examine the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the conclusion that it is a contradiction.
The phrase 'لما ليس (۱)' is a textual note; the intended meaning is 'for what is not (a)'.
2
Understand the assumption
The argument assumes a truly simple entity (al-wahid al-haqiqi) as the source of two distinct effects, labeled (a) and (b).
3
Follow the logical deduction
Since (b) is not (a), the source would be a source for (a) and for what is not (a). Similarly for (b). This is a contradiction because the source would be the origin of both a thing and its negation.
The contradiction is explicit in the text: 'وأنه تناقض'.
4
Reflect on the significance
This argument shows that any source of multiplicity must have some internal differentiation. Therefore, the First Cause, being absolutely one, cannot be the direct source of multiple distinct effects.
This is a key proof for divine transcendence in Islamic theology.
5
Address a common misunderstanding
One might object that a single cause can produce different effects through different instruments or modes. However, the argument targets a simple entity without any internal complexity, which is precisely the nature of the First Cause.
The argument reinforces the need for intermediaries or multiple aspects in causation.
