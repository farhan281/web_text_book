Refuting the Denial of Tamāthul
Refutes denial of tamāthul by denying the second conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument against tamāthul is refuted by distinguishing essential from accidental attributes.

Core Idea
Difference in non-essential attributes does not negate essential similarity.

Some thinkers deny tamāthul (likeness) altogether, arguing that any difference precludes similarity. However, this argument overlooks the distinction between essential and accidental attributes. The passage provides a concise refutation that preserves the possibility of tamāthul while acknowledging differences in non-essential qualities.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first part of the passage where the denial of tamāthul is presented with its two conditional scenarios.
Notice the logic: if identical → no twoness; if different → no tamāthul.
2
Identify the key premise
The second conditional (if differ → no tamāthul) is the premise that is refuted.
3
Understand the refutation
The answer denies the second conditional by pointing out that two things may differ in non-essential attributes while sharing all essential ones, so tamāthul is still possible.
Essential attributes are ṣifāt al-nafs, non-essential are others.

Commentary
This passage addresses two challenges. First, there are those who deny tamāthul altogether. Their argument: if two things are identical in every aspect, they would not be distinct, so there would be no twoness and hence no tamāthul. If they differ in any aspect, they are not similar. Therefore, they claim the classification of the 'two' into three types (similar, different, and identical?) collapses. The answer refutes the second premise: two things can differ in non-essential attributes while sharing all essential attributes, so tamāthul is possible. Second, the Mu'tazila define tamāthul as sharing in the most specific attribute of the essence (akhaṣṣ waṣf al-nafs). The passage criticizes this: if they mean sharing only in the most specific without including the more general attributes, that is impossible because the specific presupposes the general. If they include the general as well, then their definition reduces to the earlier definition that uses the definite plural 'the two similars share in all necessary, possible, and impossible attributes' (al-jam‘ al-muḥallā bi-lām). Thus the Mu'tazila definition is either incoherent or redundant.

The Mu'tazila Definition of Tamāthul
Critiques the Mu'tazila definition of tamāthul as sharing the most specific essential attribute.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila definition of tamāthul is logically flawed or unnecessary.

The Mu'tazila offer a more restrictive definition of tamāthul: two things are similar if they share the most specific attribute of the essence. This passage examines their definition and shows it leads to either an impossibility or a redundancy with the standard definition that uses the definite plural 'the two similars share all essential attributes'.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila definition
Read the passage where the Mu'tazila state that similars share the most specific attribute of the essence.
Note the phrase 'akhaṣṣ waṣf al-nafs'.
2
Consider the first interpretation
If they mean sharing only the most specific without the general, it is impossible because the specific cannot exist without the general.
Example: 'rational' presupposes 'living'.
3
Consider the second interpretation
If they mean the most specific includes the general, then it reduces to the standard definition (sharing all essential attributes).
The standard definition uses 'al-jam‘ al-muḥallā bi-lām'.

Mu'tazila on the Opposition of Knowledge and Ignorance
Mu'tazila on knowledge and ignorance as opposites without requiring unity of subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila argument that two opposites can exist without a single subject, and how they apply this to both human attributes and divine attributes.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila allow opposites in different parts of a thing, even for God's attributes.

This passage explores a key Mu'tazila position: that opposition does not require the two opposites to be in the same subject. They use the example of knowledge and ignorance subsisting in different parts of the heart, and then extend this to God's will and hatred. By studying this, we see how the Mu'tazila theorize about attributes without requiring unity of subject.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Notice the structure: first the Mu'tazila claim about knowledge and ignorance in the heart, then the general principle about attributes dependent on life, and finally the application to God's will and hatred.
2
Understand the heart example
The Mu'tazila say knowledge and ignorance are opposites even if they subsist in different parts of the heart. If they were not opposites, the whole heart would be both knowing and ignorant of the same thing, which is absurd.
This shows that unity of subject is not necessary for opposition.
3
Examine the extension to divine attributes
Some Mu'tazila apply the same reasoning to God's will and hatred. These are two originated attributes that do not subsist in a subject, yet they are opposites because their judgments cannot coexist in God's essence.
This is a further step: opposition can exist even without a subject at all.
4
Note the clarification about compound ignorance
The passage clarifies that the discussion concerns compound ignorance (ignorance that is an actual opposite of knowledge), not simple ignorance which is mere privation. This is important to avoid confusion.

The Seat of Knowledge: The Heart According to the Millians
Commentary on the Millian view of knowledge's location in the heart.

Core Idea
Knowledge resides in a physical heart made of atoms, say the Millians.

The commentator begins by explaining a reference to 'a part of the heart' from the matn. He clarifies that this aligns with the Millian school, which takes the apparent meaning of Quranic verses to affirm the heart as the locus of knowledge, further holding that the heart is composed of indivisible particles. This atomic view of the heart is a key point in their theological physics.
