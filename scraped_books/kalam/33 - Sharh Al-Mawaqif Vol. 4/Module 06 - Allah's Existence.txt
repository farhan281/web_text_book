The Regress Argument for Origination from the Necessary Being
Sanusi argues that the possible either is eternal or requires an infinite series of originated conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that the possible cannot be originated from the Necessary Being unless an originated condition exists, and why this leads to an infinite regress.

Core Idea
Either the possible is eternal or it requires an infinite chain of originated conditions.

Sanusi presents a key argument about the relationship between the Necessary Being and the possible. He examines whether the possible's own essential possibility is sufficient for its existence. This argument is foundational for understanding how kalam thinkers ruled out the existence of anything eternal besides God.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once, focusing on the logical flow. Notice the conditional structure: 'if... if not...'
2
Understand the two cases
Identify the two main cases: (1) essential possibility is sufficient, leading to eternal existence; (2) essential possibility is insufficient, requiring a condition.
3
Analyze the condition's nature
If a condition is needed, it must be either eternal or originated. If eternal, the possible remains eternal. If originated, the possible is originated but that condition itself needs another, leading to regress.
This regress is the key point.
4
Reflect on the implication
The argument shows that the only way a possible can be originated is through an infinite regress of conditions, which is impossible. Thus, the possible cannot be originated from the Necessary Being unless we accept regress.
The commentator then addresses objections to this reasoning.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument about the origination of the possible (al-mumkin) from the Necessary Being (al-wajib). The commentator clarifies the scholars' statement by examining whether the essential possibility of the possible is sufficient for its emanation from God. If it is sufficient, then since God is perfect in agency and the receiver is ready, the possible would be eternal, like the first effect. If it is not sufficient, a condition (shart) is needed for the emanation. If that condition is eternal, the possible remains eternal. If it is originated (hadith), then the possible is originated, but then that originated condition itself needs another originated condition, leading to a potential regress. The commentary then addresses several objections: it refutes the idea that the condition could be a non-existent, because non-existence cannot be a condition for existence; it also discusses the case of a condition that is non-existent but renewing, and argues that such cannot be valid. The commentator also considers the possibility that the non-existence of a thing after existence could be due to its own essence, avoiding the need for a cause of removal. Finally, he questions whether the originated condition could be a non-existent thing, but the text cuts off.

Refuting Simultaneous Conditions for Temporal Origination
Refutes simultaneous conditions via proof of application and composite argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument that the conditions for a temporal event cannot exist simultaneously, and why this leads to a contradiction via the composite argument.

Core Idea
Simultaneous conditions for a temporal event create a vicious infinite regress and a self-contradictory composite.

This lesson presents a key argument in Islamic theology against the possibility that the conditions for a temporal event could all exist at the same time. The argument uses the proof of application and the composite argument to show that such simultaneous existence is impossible. Understanding this refutation is crucial for grasping the nature of temporal origination and causation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
Read the opening of the passage: if a temporal event requires a condition that is also temporal, this leads to an infinite chain. The two possibilities are simultaneous or successive existence.
2
Examine the simultaneous case
The passage argues that simultaneous existence is false using two proofs: the proof of application and the composite argument. Focus on how the composite of all conditions would itself be temporal.
3
Analyze the composite argument
The composite argument states that if all conditions exist simultaneously, they form a composite which is temporal and therefore requires another condition, leading to a contradiction because that condition must be both inside and outside the composite.
4
Consider objections
The passage addresses objections, such as that the composite does not have real existence or that the condition could be outside. Note how the commentator refutes these.
5
Conclude
The passage ends with 'and that is impossible.' Understand why simultaneous conditions are logically impossible.

Commentary
This passage refutes the possibility that the conditions for a temporal event exist simultaneously. It begins by stating the problem: if the condition for a temporal event is itself temporal, it leads to an infinite regress. The two possibilities are simultaneous or successive existence. The simultaneous is false for two reasons: the proof of application (برهان التطبيق) shows the impossibility of infinite regress in ordered coexisting things; and the composite formed by simultaneous conditions would itself be temporal and require another condition that is both inside and outside it, which is contradictory. The commentator then expands on the composite argument, explaining why the composite has a distinct temporality and exists externally. He also addresses objections about the condition being outside the composite and the possibility of the argument applying to successive events. He concludes that simultaneous conditions are impossible ('وأنه محال').

Successive Conditions Need a Specific Substrate
Argues that successive conditions need a substrate with infinite successive dispositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why successive conditions for a temporal event require a substrate with successive dispositions to avoid preponderance without a preponderant.

Core Idea
Without a specific substrate, the relation of conditions to events is equal, making any occurrence arbitrary.

This passage presents a key argument in kalam cosmology: if temporal events require successive conditions, those conditions must inhere in a substrate that is specific to the event. Otherwise, the occurrence of one event over another would lack any decisive reason. This lesson unpacks that argument and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the main argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Identify the logical chain: conditions are successive, so they need a substrate specific to the event. Otherwise, arbitrariness results.
Pay attention to the phrase 'ترجيح بلا مرجح' (preponderance without preponderant).
2
Understand the problem of arbitrariness
The commentator argues that if the composite of conditions has no substrate or a neutral substrate, its relation to any particular event is equal. Therefore, the occurrence of one event over another would be a preponderance without a preponderant.
3
Trace the conclusion about dispositions
Because arbitrariness is impossible, the substrate must have successive dispositions (استعدادات متعاقبة) that condition each other ad infinitum.
Each prior disposition is a condition for the next.
4
Review the objections and replies
The commentator addresses objections: (1) conditions might have intrinsic specificity; (2) proving a substrate alone suffices. Understand how the replies defend the need for dispositions.
The reply to the second objection emphasizes that the substrate could be the event's essence, so dispositions must be proven actual.

Why Questions About Priority Cease at Time Itself
Explains why questions of priority stop at time itself

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why, in the chain of asking about the cause of priority among events, questions stop when we reach time itself, as temporal parts are inherently ordered.

Core Idea
Questions of 'why prior?' can recur until we hit time; time's parts need no external cause.

In this passage, Sanusi (or al-Iji) uses the simple example of two births and their caliphates to show how questioning about priority can be pushed back repeatedly. He demonstrates that the only way to finally stop the regress is if the items in question are themselves parts of time, like years, because their order is intrinsic to time.

Guided Path
1
Read the example of Zayd and Umar

Identify the point where the question stops

The passage states: 'if each of the prior and posterior is time itself, then that is the case.'
3
Understand the principle
The core principle: priority between temporal things must be explained by something else, but priority between time-parts themselves is not caused by something external.
4

Consider how this argument is used in kalam to show that the world's temporal nature points to a beginning of time, and ultimately to a Creator beyond time.

Commentary
This passage is a proof of the impossibility of infinite regress (tasalsul) in a chain of possible beings. The author, al-Iji, begins by stating the conclusion: infinite regress is impossible. He then argues that if a possible being (mumkin) is caused by another possible being, that chain of possibles is itself possible and requires an external cause. He reasons that the entire chain (jami') is possible because all possible beings require a cause. This cause must be external to the chain, because the cause of a thing cannot be the thing itself (otherwise it would precede itself) nor any of its parts (otherwise that part would cause itself). Then he argues that this external cause must produce some part of the chain. If it did not produce any part, then the entire chain would exist without that external cause, so it would not be a cause of the chain. Now, if the external cause produces a part, then that part is not caused by any internal part of the chain (otherwise two causes would concur on one effect). This means that part is not dependent on another part in the chain, which contradicts the assumption that each part is caused by a preceding part ad infinitum. Moreover, if that part is not caused by an internal cause, it becomes a terminus of the chain, making the chain finite, contradicting the assumption of infinity. Thus, the assumption of infinite regress leads to a contradiction, so infinite regress is impossible.
