Third Distinction: Condition May Be Multiple or Composite
Al-Jurjani states the third difference: condition may be multiple or composite.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A condition can be either multiple individually necessary factors or a single composite of jointly sufficient factors.

Al-Jurjani now presents the third difference between condition and conditioned: the condition may be multiple (muta'addid) or composite (murakkab). This distinction is crucial for analyzing how multiple factors can be necessary or sufficient for a single effect. The passage uses the example of knowledge requiring both life and the absence of opposites.

Fourth Distinction: Condition as Subject, Cause as Attribute
Al-Jurjani states the fourth difference: condition may be the subject, cause its attribute.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The subject enables the judgment; the attribute causes it.

Al-Jurjani continues his analysis of the differences between condition and cause by presenting a fourth distinction: the condition can be the subject (mahall) of the judgment, while the cause is its attribute (sifa). This distinction refines our understanding of causality in theological judgments—the subject itself is not the efficient cause; rather, the attribute within the subject is what truly causes the judgment. Let's examine this precise point.

Cause vs Condition: The Permissibility of Mutual Condition
Distinguishes cause from condition and argues for the permissibility of mutual condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key difference between cause and condition in kalam and why mutual dependency without temporal priority is permissible.

Core Idea
A cause cannot be an effect of its effect, but a condition can be conditioned by its conditioned.

In kalam discussions of causality, it's crucial to distinguish between a cause ('illa) and a condition (shart). While a cause produces its effect and cannot be reciprocally dependent, a condition can be mutual — as long as there is no temporal priority. This lesson clarifies that distinction and explains the concept of concomitant circularity (dawr ma'iyya) using the vivid example of two leaning bricks.

Guided Path

Read the passage and its translation

2
Identify the definition of condition
Note how the author defines condition in contrast to cause. Look for the phrase 'not what affects'.
Condition does not affect but is necessary for the effect's existence.
3
Understand the two leaning bricks analogy
Consider the example of two leaning bricks. Each brick's standing depends on the other. This illustrates concomitant circularity, which is permissible.
Contrast with circular priority which is impossible.

Commentary
This passage distinguishes between cause and condition, and then addresses the fifth point regarding reciprocal dependence. The author begins by noting that the judgment accompanies the condition in some cases just as it accompanies the cause, using the example of will (irāda) which accompanies power (qudra) as its condition, and also accompanies will itself. Thus a distinction is needed. He reiterates that after the cause exists, the effect cannot depend on anything else; therefore, what is a condition for the judgment is a condition for the existence of the cause. This is called 'concomitant circularity' (dawr maʿiyya), which is possible, unlike circularity of priority (dawr al-taqaddum) which is impossible. The commentary addresses a potential objection that there is no circularity because each brick's dependence is not on the particularity of the other, but the author dismisses this, asserting that the particular standing of each depends on the particular standing of the other.

Sixth Point: Condition May Not Persist While Conditioned Persists
Explains the sixth point: condition may be temporary, cause is permanent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that a condition may be required only at the beginning of existence, not for continuance, and how this differs from a cause.

Core Idea
A condition can be temporary; a cause is always necessary.

In kalam, conditions and causes are distinct. The sixth point clarifies that a condition may be necessary only for the initial existence of a thing, not for its persistence. This is illustrated with the attachment of power to originated things. Meanwhile, a cause is constantly present with its effect. Understanding this helps avoid the mistake of thinking that everything required for existence must persist.

The sixth point states that a condition may be required only at the beginning of the existence of the conditioned, not for its continuance. (الثامن): الحكم الواجب لم يتفق على عدم شرطه، بل اتفق على أنه لا يوجد بدون شرط كالعالمية لله، فإنها مشروطة بكونه حياً، وقد اختلف في كون الحكم الواجب معللاً بعلة. (التاسع : العلة مصححة لمعلولها اتفاقاً، وفي كون الشرط مصححاً لمشروطه خلاف، قال به القاضي كالحياة للعلم، فإنه ذهب إلى أن الحياة وإن لم تكن علة للعلم، بل شرطاً له، لكنها علة في تصحيحه، ومؤثرة في صحته، وموجبة له . ومنعه المحققون ؛ لجواز توقفه أي توقف العلم في صحته على شروط أخر، كانتفاء أضداده، ووجود محله، وحينئذ فلا يمكن أن تكون الحياة مستقلة بالتصحيح. ولما كانت هذه المباحث مع ركاكتها في أنفسها مبنية على أصل فاسد، أعرضنا عن تفاصيلها، والله تعالى الموفق والمرشد .
الشرط قد يكون معلولاً، فظهر الفرق بين علة الحكم وشرطه بأن العلة لا تكون معلولة أصلاً، والشرط قد يكون معلولاً. وإنما لم يكتف على ما يستفاد من المتن؛ لأن وجود الشرط لعلة الحكم، وعدم وجود العلة لها لا يفيد الفرق بين علة الحكم وشرطه إذ الفرق إنما يحصل بأن يكون لأحدهما حكم لا يكون لآخر.
قوله : (بل اتفق إلخ) أضرب عن عدم الاتفاق، لأنه يجامع الاختلاف، فلا يحصل الفرق بخلاف الاتفاق .
قوله : ( وقد اختلف إلخ ) فإن مثبتي الأحوال من الأشاعرة يعللونه بصفات موجودة، ومن المعتزلة ينفونه سوى البهشمية، فإنهم يعللون الحال بالحال؛ بناء على ما نقل عن أبي هاشم .
The fifth observation - the tenth intention: On explaining the cause and the effect. The living being is a condition for his being knowing, even though his being living is an effect of life. (Eighth): The necessary ruling is not agreed upon the absence of its condition, but it is agreed that it does not exist without a condition, like the knowledge attribute of God, for it is conditioned by His being living, and there is disagreement on whether the necessary ruling is caused by a cause. (Ninth): The cause is a corrector of its effect by agreement, and regarding the condition being a corrector of its conditioned there is disagreement; the judge (al-Qāḍī) held it, like life for knowledge, for he held that life, even though it is not a cause for knowledge, but a condition for it, yet it is a cause in its correction, effective in its validity, and necessitating it. The verifiers (al-muḥaqqiqūn) denied this, because of the possibility of its dependence—that is, the dependence of knowledge in its validity on other conditions, like the absence of its opposites and the existence of its locus, and then life cannot be independent in correction. Since these investigations, despite their weakness in themselves, are built on a corrupt foundation, we turned away from their details, and God Almighty is the Success and Guide. The condition may be an effect, so the difference between the cause of a ruling and its condition appears: that the cause is never an effect, and the condition may be an effect. And it was not sufficient according to what is understood from the text, because the existence of a condition for the cause of a ruling and the non-existence of a cause for it do not yield the difference between the cause of a ruling and its condition, for the difference is only obtained by one of them having a ruling that the other does not. His statement: 'but it is agreed etc.'—he turned away from the lack of agreement, because it is compatible with disagreement, so the difference is not obtained, unlike agreement. His statement: 'and there is disagreement etc.'—for the affirmers of states (aḥwāl) among the Ashʿarīs cause it by existing attributes, and the Muʿtazila deny it except for the Bahshamiyya, for they cause the state by a state, based on what was transmitted from Abū Hāshim.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a section of al-Mawāqif dealing with the distinction between cause (ʿilla) and condition (sharṭ) in the context of divine attributes and rulings. The commentator first presents the original text's eighth and ninth points, then criticizes them as weak, and finally offers a simpler, more fundamental distinction.

1. The passage begins with the heading: 'The fifth observation - the tenth intention: On explaining the cause and the effect.' Then an example: the living being's being alive is a condition for his being knowing, even though his being alive is an effect of life itself.
2. The eighth point: 'The necessary ruling is not agreed upon the absence of its condition, but it is agreed that it does not exist without a condition.' For instance, God's knowledge attribute is conditioned by His being living. There is disagreement on whether such a necessary ruling is caused by a cause.
3. The ninth point: The cause is unanimously agreed to be a corrector (muṣaḥḥiḥ) of its effect, but there is disagreement on whether the condition is a corrector of its conditioned. Al-Qāḍī (probably al-Bāqillānī) held that life, though not a cause of knowledge, is a cause in its correction, effecting its validity and necessitating it. The verifiers (al-muḥaqqiqūn) rejected this because knowledge's validity depends on other conditions (absence of opposites, existence of its locus), so life cannot be independent in correction.
4. The commentator then dismisses these discussions as based on a corrupt foundation, turning away from their details.
5. He presents a clearer distinction: 'The condition may be an effect, so the difference between the cause of a ruling and its condition appears: that the cause is never an effect, and the condition may be an effect.' This is the core insight.
6. He explains why the text's earlier distinction (based on existence and non-existence of causes and conditions) was insufficient: the difference is not about existence but about having a different ruling—the cause is never an effect, while the condition may be one.
7. Finally, he clarifies two phrases from the original text: (a) The phrase 'but it is agreed' (bal ittafaqa) turns away from the absence of agreement, because the absence of agreement does not provide a clear difference, unlike agreement. (b) The phrase 'and there is disagreement' (wa qad ikhtalafa) refers to the Ashʿarīs (who affirm states/causes) and the Muʿtazila (who deny them except for the Bahshamiyya who cause a state by a state according to Abū Hāshim).

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Condition and cause are the same in effecting a ruling.
Correction: The passage shows that cause corrects by agreement, while condition's correction is disputed.

Checkpoint

A) It must have a cause.

C) It has no condition.

Key Takeaway
The eighth and ninth points show that while a condition is necessary, its role as a corrector is disputed, and these discussions are ultimately dismissed as weak.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Do you tend to attribute success in your life to a single condition, ignoring the multiple factors that God has ordained? This lesson reminds us that no condition is the sole corrector. Where in your life have you made a 'condition' into a 'cause'?
?
God's knowledge is conditioned by His life, but not caused by it. Does your understanding of God's attributes ever reduce them to being effects of something else? Examine your heart: do you truly believe that God's attributes are not causally dependent on anything?
?
The verifiers denied that life is the independent corrector of knowledge because other conditions are needed. In your faith, are there 'conditions' you treat as sufficient without recognizing the other conditions God has set? This lesson calls you to humility—what condition in your spiritual life do you need to reevaluate?

The Fundamental Distinction: Cause Never an Effect, Condition May Be
Commentator gives clear difference: cause not effect, condition may be effect; clarifies text phrases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the clear distinction between cause and condition in kalam: the cause is never an effect, while the condition may be one, and why earlier formulations were insufficient.

Core Idea
The cause is never an effect; the condition may be an effect.

After dismissing the earlier discussions as weak, the commentator offers a simpler and more fundamental distinction between cause and condition. He also clarifies two phrases from the original text to show why they were insufficient. Let's break down his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the core distinction
Start with the sentence: 'الشرط قد يكون معلولاً...' until 'الفرق إنما يحصل بأن يكون لأحدهما حكم لا يكون لآخر.' This is the basis.
The key: cause never effect, condition can be effect.
2
Examine why the text's earlier approach was insufficient
The commentator explains that focusing on existence of condition for cause vs. non-existence of cause for condition does not yield the difference. The difference lies in the property of being an effect or not.
3
Understand the clarifications of 'bal ittafaqa' and 'wa qad ikhtalafa'
Read the two 'قوله' sections. The first clarifies that agreement is needed to establish a difference. The second explains the positions of Ashʿarīs and Muʿtazila on causing states.
These technical clarifications connect to broader kalām debates.
