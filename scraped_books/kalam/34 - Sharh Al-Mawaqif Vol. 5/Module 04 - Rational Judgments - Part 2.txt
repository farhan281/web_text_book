Are Tastes Real? Al-Razi on Sharpness, Sourness, and Astringency
Al-Razi questions whether certain tastes are real or imaginary due to combination with tactile qualities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's argument that certain tastes may not be distinct real qualities but rather composites of taste and tactile sensations, and the skeptical implication this raises.

Core Idea
Some tastes blend with touch, making them seem unique but maybe just imagined.

Imam al-Razi, a towering figure in Islamic theology and philosophy, often probed the reliability of sensory perception. In this passage, he examines whether the tastes we call sharpness, sourness, and astringency are truly distinct realities or merely the result of a basic taste mixing with a tactile quality like heat or dryness. His analysis challenges us to question how we distinguish between what is real and what is a mental construction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation carefully without analyzing. Let the argument flow naturally.
Notice that the passage begins with a question and then presents examples.
2
Identify the three examples of compounded tastes
Al-Razi gives three examples: sharpness (ḥarāfah), sourness (ḥumūḍah), and astringency (ʿufūṣah). For each, identify the tactile quality that accompanies the taste.
The tactile qualities are dispersion (tafrīq), heating (isḫān), thickening (taktīf), and drying (tajfīf).
3
Understand the skeptical twist
After giving examples, Al-Razi argues that if compounding occurs in some cases, we cannot be sure it doesn't happen in all cases. This raises doubt about the independent reality of these tastes.
4
Reflect on the implication for sensory knowledge
Consider how this argument fits into broader discussions in kalam about the reliability of the senses and the nature of accidents.
Al-Razi is not denying all tastes, only those that are mixed with tactile effects.

Commentary
This passage presents Imam al-Razi's argument that certain tastes (sharpness, sourness, astringency) may not be distinct real qualities but rather the result of a combination of a basic taste with a tactile quality (like dispersion, heat, thickening). The argument begins by questioning whether these tastes are real or imagined. Al-Razi suggests that the apparent multiplicity of tastes arises because a taste sensation can be accompanied by a tactile sensation, and the two blend into a single percept that is not distinguished by the senses. For example, sharpness (ḥarāfah) is a basic taste accompanied by dispersion (tafrīq) and heating (isḫān); sourness (ḥumūḍah) is a basic taste accompanied by dispersion without heat; astringency (ʿufūṣah) is a basic taste accompanied by thickening (taktīf) and drying (tajfīf). Thus, the multiplicity of tastes may be merely apparent, not real. The author of the Mawaqif (al-Iji) summarizes this in his own words, explaining that when a tactile quality joins a taste, the sense does not distinguish them, and they appear as a single distinct taste. Al-Razi then raises a skeptical point: if this blending occurs in some cases, what guarantee is there that sharpness and astringency are always of this imagined kind? The passage ends by noting that one might mistakenly think from al-Iji's phrasing that they are real tastes, but confusion can occur. The argument is a subtle exploration of the psychology of perception and the classification of sensible qualities.

Commentary
This passage defines the fifth type of sensible qualities: those perceived by the sense of smell. It states that in our language (Arabic) there are no specific names for smells themselves; instead they are described from three aspects. The first aspect is by their agreeableness or disagreeableness: pleasant smells are called 'fragrant' (ṭayyib) and unpleasant ones 'fetid' (muntin). The passage concludes that the varieties of smells are not fixed, and their levels of intensity and weakness are not delimited, similar to tastes and other qualities. This classification is a concise summary of how smells are categorized in the Aristotelian framework, emphasizing that smells have no independent names but are described relationally.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the chapter on psychological qualities (al-kayfiyyat al-nafsaniyya), which is one of the four divisions of qualities. It begins by defining psychological qualities as those specific to the souls of elemental bodies. A view is mentioned that what is meant is animal souls, meaning these qualities exist only in animals, not plants or inanimate objects. The commentator then raises a problem: if restricted to animal souls, then qualities like life, knowledge, power, and will, which are attributed to God (al-Wājib) and the separate intellects (al-mujarradāt), would not be specific to animals. The commentator notes that the restriction to elemental bodies suggests these qualities do not exist in souls that are not elemental bodies, but that is false because celestial bodies also have some of these qualities (e.g., voluntary motion). Therefore, it is better to interpret 'souls' as including celestial souls as well, as indicated by a passage from al-Shifa (Avicenna's Healing) that calls them 'dispositions' (malakāt) and 'states' (ḥālāt). The ultimate explanation is that the restriction is not for specification but to indicate that the specificity relative to some elemental bodies. The commentary then addresses the wording of the text, suggesting that the phrase 'upon this' should be omitted or rephrased to avoid confusion. Next, it discusses the examples (life, knowledge, power, will) and notes that the affirmation of these qualities for separate intellects is debatable. It also explains that the theologian who affirms additional attributes for God does not include them under 'quality' (kayf), as they belong to the division of substance and accident. Finally, it transitions to a discussion about the difference between a 'state' (ḥāl) and a 'disposition' (malaka), raising an objection from the Peripatetics: if intensity and weakness cause a specific difference, then the same individual quality cannot be both a state and a disposition because they differ in intensity. The commentator's answer (incomplete) is that the cause of specific difference is intensity and weakness in the occurrence of the universal in its particulars.

Ibn Sina's Proof: Life is Not Sensation and Movement
Ibn Sina's evidence from the paralyzed limb to distinguish life from sensation and movement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why life cannot be identified with the powers of sensation and movement, using Ibn Sina's evidence from the paralyzed limb.

Core Idea
A paralyzed limb is alive but lacks sensation – life is distinct.

A common misunderstanding is to equate life with the powers of sensation, voluntary movement, and nutrition. Ibn Sina, in his Canon of Medicine, refutes this by pointing to a simple observation: a paralyzed or numb limb still lives, even though it lacks sensation and movement. This lesson unpacks Ibn Sina's proof and the deeper principle that different effects indicate different powers.

Guided Path

2

Focus on the paralyzed limb versus the dead limb. Note that the paralyzed limb lacks sensation and movement but is still alive, while the dead limb lacks them and decays.
3
Understand the logical inference
The proof relies on the idea that different effects (remaining alive vs. decaying) imply different causes or powers. Ibn Sina uses this to show that life is a distinct power.
4
Review the commentator's clarification
The commentator stresses that sensation and movement are not essential to life. Life is the specific equilibrium that preserves the body.

Commentary
This passage refutes a common misunderstanding: that life is identical to the powers of sensation, voluntary movement, and nutrition. The author cites Ibn Sina's argument from the Qanun: a paralyzed or numb limb lacks sensation and movement at the moment due to an obstruction, yet it remains alive, whereas a dead limb also lacks these powers but decays.
