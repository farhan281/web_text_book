The Second Face Argument Against Time and Ibn Sina's Response
Al-Jurjani presents Ibn Sina's logical critique of the second face argument against time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second face argument against the existence of time and Ibn Sina's logical response, including the distinction between specific (a-khass) and general (a-amm) existence.

Core Idea
Denying time's tenses does not prove time's non-existence.

Theologians argued that if time exists, it must be present, past, or future. Since none of these are externally real, time must be unreal. Ibn Sina counters that these three categories are not exhaustive of existence—there can be existents that are none of them, like eternal things. This lesson unpacks the argument, the response, and the logical nuances of the debate.

Guided Path
1
Read the second face argument
Read the opening passage where the mutakallimun state that if time existed, it would have to be present, past, or future. Since these are all false, time is false.
Notice the argument is a disjunctive syllogism.
2
Understand Ibn Sina's objection
Ibn Sina asks: why must time be one of these three? He points out that 'present', 'past', and 'future' are more specific than absolute existence. Their falsity does not falsify time, which is more general.
This is a logical distinction between specific and general.
3
Examine the formulation issue
The commentator explains that the argument can be formulated as a conjunctive or disjunctive syllogism, and Ibn Sina's response matches the latter. There is also a distinction between 'containment' (zarfiyya) and 'individuality' (fardiyya) formulations.
The response's validity depends on which formulation is used.
4
Address the counter-objection
An objector says: if something exists not in present, past, or future, its existence is impossible. The commentator shows this objection fails when the response is properly understood.
The commentator defends Ibn Sina's logic.

Commentary
This passage presents the second argument ('second face') by which the theologians (mutakallimūn) sought to prove the non-existence of time (al-zamān). The argument is: if time existed, it would have to be present, past, or future; but none of these divisions actually exist in an external sense, therefore time does not exist. Ibn Sīnā responds by questioning the assumption that time must fall under one of these three categories. He points out that 'present,' 'past,' and 'future' are more specific than absolute existence – they are not coextensive with existence itself. So even if they were false (as the mutakallimūn claim), it does not imply that time (the more general concept) is false. The commentator then elaborates: the original argument can be formulated either as a conjunctive syllogism (two conditionals) or a disjunctive syllogism (a three-part disjunction). Ibn Sīnā's answer is intended to match the latter formulation. The commentator explains that when Ibn Sīnā says 'each of them is more specific than absolute existence,' he means there are existents (like eternal things) that are not in any of the three divisions, and time might be among them. This is because the divisions are mental constructs resulting from partitioning, whereas time is a single continuous existent with no division. The commentator then addresses an objection (the 'problem' – mushkil): if something exists neither in the present, past, nor future, its existence is impossible or unimaginable. He argues that this objection does not apply when Ibn Sīnā's answer is understood in accordance with the author's formulation. The objection would only apply if the answer were framed in terms of 'containment' (zarfiyya) – i.e., that time is contained in one of these three containers. But that is not the author's formulation. The commentator notes that the author's wording may appear problematic, but if one interprets 'answered him' as a response to the second face based on the containment formulation, it leads to absurdity. Ultimately, he asserts that even on the containment formulation, the problem is still repelled. The passage concludes with a remark about the author's possible disorder.

The Apparent Contradiction in Ibn Sina on Time and Motion
Al-Jurjani presents the charge of contradiction and a possible defense.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the charge of self-contradiction against Ibn Sina concerning the necessity of existing in one of the three times, and the defense that distinguishes temporal things from time itself.

Core Idea
Ibn Sina's two statements appear to conflict on whether everything must exist in time.

In his response to the proof of congruence, Ibn Sina argued that infinite past motions cannot exist because they would have to be in the past, present, or future. Yet elsewhere, he denies that time itself must be in any of these three divisions. This appears as a direct contradiction. However, a defense can be made by distinguishing between temporal entities like motion and time itself, which exists in its own right.

Guided Path
1
Read the charge of contradiction
Read the opening lines where al-Jurjani states that Ibn Sina contradicted himself. Note the two statements: one from the burhan al-tatbiq context and the other from his denial about time.
Pay attention to the phrase 'ناقض ابن سينا نفسه'.
2
Identify the two statements
The first statement says that infinite past movements do not exist because they would have to be in one of the three times (past, present, future). The second statement (from another context) denies that time must be in any of these. Spot both in the passage.
3
Understand the defense
The defense argues that motion is temporal (zamānī) and requires a container, while time is not temporal in the same way—it exists in itself. Read the defense carefully and see how it attempts to resolve the contradiction.
The defense is presented after 'فإن قلت'.
4
Reflect on the distinction
Think about the key distinction: temporal things exist in time, but time exists as a continuum that is not contained within another time. Does this successfully remove the contradiction?
This sets up the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage discusses an apparent self-contradiction in Ibn Sīnā's thought. On the one hand, in his response to the argument known as 'burhān al-taṭbīq' (proof of congruence) against the infinite regress of successive events, Ibn Sīnā reportedly said that all past infinite motions do not exist at all, and if they did, they would have to be in the past, present, or future – but all three are false, so they do not exist. In that statement, he implicitly accepted the trichotomy of time as exhaustive: whatever exists must be in one of the three tenses. On the other hand, in his response to the 'second face' argument against the existence of time (discussed in the previous lesson), he denied that time must be in one of these tenses. The commentator presents this as a clear contradiction (tanāquḍ ṣarīḥ). He then considers a possible defense: motion is a temporal thing (zamānī), and if it is not in any of the three times, it cannot exist; but time itself (al-zamān) is not temporal in the same way – it exists in itself and is the measure of motion. Time can exist without being in a time, whereas motion cannot. So Ibn Sīnā's two statements are consistent if one distinguishes between temporal things and time itself. However, the commentator rejects this defense, arguing that time also cannot exist without being in some time; to say time exists 'in itself' means it is not in another time, but it still must be in some time. Ibn Sīnā's position is therefore based on a sophistry (mugālata). The passage thus critically examines the consistency of Ibn Sīnā's views on time.

The Refutation of Ibn Sina's Defense and the Sophistry Charge
Al-Jurjani refutes the defense and charges Ibn Sina with sophistry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Jurjani's rebuttal of the defense of Ibn Sina and why he ultimately accuses Ibn Sina of sophistry.

Core Idea
Time itself cannot escape the requirement of existing in a time; the defense fails.

Al-Jurjani does not accept the defense of Ibn Sina. He argues that time also must exist in some time, not merely in itself. The idea that time exists 'in itself' is ambiguous: it means not in another time, but time cannot exist entirely outside time. Therefore, Ibn Sina's position is based on a sophistical misunderstanding, and the apparent contradiction stands.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Jurjani's rebuttal
Read the section beginning with 'والتحقيق أن هذا الجواب غير تام'. Notice how he directly challenges the defense.
Al-Jurjani uses the word 'مغالطة' (sophistry) at the end.
2
Understand the key objection
Al-Jurjani argues that the defense is incomplete. He points out that time also cannot exist without being in some time—if time were not in any time, it would be non-existent. Grasp this reasoning.
He concedes that time exists in itself, but interprets 'in itself' correctly.
3
Grasp the sophistry charge
Al-Jurjani concludes that Ibn Sina's statement is built on sophistry (muḡālaṭa). Understand what he means: Ibn Sina uses an ambiguous phrase to avoid the contradiction.
The sophistry lies in the phrase 'exists in itself' being misapplied.

Commentary
This passage discusses an apparent self-contradiction in Ibn Sīnā's thought. The commentator presents this as a clear contradiction (tanāquḍ ṣarīḥ). However, the commentator rejects this defense, arguing that time also cannot exist without being in some time; to say time exists 'in itself' means it is not in another time, but it still must be in some time. Ibn Sīnā's position is therefore based on a sophistry (mugālata). The passage thus critically examines the consistency of Ibn Sīnā's views on time.

Hearing Through a Thick Wall: Objection and Response
Al-Iji presents the objection and his response using pores and quality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that sound cannot pass through a solid barrier is refuted by the concept of air carrying its quality (kayfiyya) through narrow pores, and the metaphorical use of shape (shakl).

Core Idea
Sound travels through a wall because air carries its quality through narrow pores.

The objection claims that since sound requires air to reach the ear, a thick wall should block all sound. Al-Iji refutes this by pointing to the narrow pores in the wall and the nature of sound as a quality in the air. The key is that the air does not need to pass through as a whole; it only needs to carry the sound's quality through the pores. This lesson unpacks that argument and the metaphorical language used.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection: we hear sound through a thick wall that surrounds us completely. Why does the opponent think this disproves the need for a carrier?
The opponent argues that if sound needs air to reach the ear, then a wall should stop all air and thus all sound.
2
Analyze the response about pores
The response states that the wall has narrow pores (masām) and experience shows sound is heard. The more pores, the stronger the sound. This suggests the air carrier passes through.
The condition for hearing is that the air retains its quality (kayfiyya) even when passing through narrow openings.
3
Clarify the metaphorical shape
The term 'shape' (shakl) is used metaphorically for quality. The air does not need to preserve a physical shape, only its qualitative state (the undulation of sound).
This explains how air can penetrate narrow pores without losing the sound's properties.
4
Consider the tube comparison
The passage compares the wall to a tube: air under pressure can travel through a narrow tube, just as in the wall where air has no other exit but the pores.
The difference is that in a tube air exits freely, while in a wall it must force through small openings.

Objection from Silent Letters and the Undulation Response
Al-Iji presents the objection from silent letters and the response that air propagates the quality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection from silent letters is resolved through the theory of sound propagation by undulation of air.

Core Idea
Sound travels by successive undulation of air, not by movement of the same air.

This passage presents a subtle objection against the view that sensation does not require the physical arrival of the object. If silent letters exist only at the moment of utterance, how can we hear them after that moment? The response refines our understanding of sound propagation: it is the quality of undulation that is transmitted, not the air itself.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the full passage once to grasp the objection: silent letters have no duration, so hearing them seems impossible after their moment.
The objection targets the idea that sound arrival is not necessary for hearing.
2
Understand the problem with silent letters
Silent letters (consonants) are articulated at an instant; they do not persist. If hearing requires the air carrying the sound to reach the ear, then the sound would have vanished before arrival.
This challenges the earlier position that hearing does not depend on arrival.
3
Follow the response about undulation
The response explains that sound propagates by undulation: the first air becomes qualified, then adjacent air is qualified similarly, and so on until the ear. The original air does not travel.
Thus, the quality of the silent letter is transmitted through layers without requiring the original air to reach the ear.
4
See how this solves the temporal issue
Since the undulation transmits the quality sequentially, the ear can receive the sound after its moment of production. No temporal contradiction arises.
This preserves the non-dependence of hearing on physical arrival.

Commentary
This is a passage from the third type, first section, third objective of Sharh al-Mawaqif, discussing the existence of sound outside the ear canal and how we perceive its direction and distance. The author begins by stating that sound exists outside the ear canal, and we perceive something from it that continues until it ceases. At that point we perceive the arriving sound and its source, what remains of it, its direction, the distance and nearness of its source, and the remaining strength or weakness of its waves. If there is no effect left in the intervening space to indicate the origin, we only know the amount of distance by what remains of the sound. For example, we cannot distinguish between thunder from the sky and the sound of a nearby millstone, but we can distinguish between two unseen speakers at different distances. This shows that the sound itself conveys information about distance and direction. Then Imam al-Razi presents an objection: even if the hearer traces the sound back, hearing only perceives the sound itself, not its direction. Therefore the direction is not perceived, and being in a direction is not perceived either. Thus the perceived sound does not vary with direction, so it cannot cause perception of direction. The author responds by pointing out the weakness of this objection: if sound is perceived in a direction, one knows it is in that direction, even if direction is not directly perceived by hearing, just as we know a smell is in a body even though we do not perceive the body or the inherence of the smell by smell. Then the author considers two possible counterarguments (la yuqal): first, that we perceive direction because the air striking the ear comes from that direction, not because the sound exists in it; second, that we distinguish near from far because the effect of the near is stronger. The author provides answers to both. The first answer references the experiment of blocking one ear: if you block the ear on the side of the sound source and hear with the other ear, you still know the direction even though the air must come from the opposite direction by bending, so it cannot be due to the direction of the air. The second answer points out that if strength indicated nearness, then two sounds equal in distance but different in strength would be confusing, but we can still correctly identify the distant strong sound from the near weak sound. The marginal notes further refine grammatical and logical points about the arguments.

Undulating Air and the Production of Echo
Al-Iji explains how undulating air carries sound and produces echo through reflection from smooth surfaces, with a discussion on the necessity of smoothness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how undulating air carries sound and how echoes are formed through reflection from smooth surfaces, including the debated necessity of smoothness.

Core Idea
Echo is the return of undulating air maintaining its wave form after striking a smooth surface.

In this passage, al-Iji explains the physical process behind sound and echo. He compares the reflection of undulating air to a ball bouncing off a wall, and then addresses a key condition: the striking surface must be smooth. However, he also presents a counterexample from distant mountains, showing that echo can occur without a perfectly smooth reflector. Answer to the Second Objection: Strong Distant vs. Weak Near Sounds
Al-Jurjani answers the second objection about sound distance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the ability to distinguish between a strong distant sound and a weak near sound refutes the claim that near sounds are always stronger.

Core Idea
Proximity does not guarantee loudness; distance and strength are independent.

In this passage, al-Jurjani directly refutes a critical objection to the theory of sound perception. The objector claimed that we cannot reliably distinguish between a loud far sound and a soft near sound. Al-Jurjani shows that we do distinguish them, and if the objection were true, we would be confused when two equally distant sounds differ in intensity—yet we are not. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Definition of Echo
Al-Jurjani defines echo and its cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Jurjani defines echo as the reflection of undulating air carrying sound when it strikes a smooth body.

Core Idea
Echo is a new sound produced by reflected undulating air, similar to the original.

In this passage, al-Jurjani lays down the natural cause of echo. He describes how the air that carries sound, when it meets a smooth obstacle, is reflected back with its undulating pattern intact, producing a second sound. This foundational definition underlies the subsequent discussions on echo.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition carefully
Read the Arabic passage once to grasp the core statement. Focus on the chain: undulating air → strikes smooth body → returns with form → new sound.
Notice the analogy of a ball thrown at a wall.
2
Identify the key components
Identify the three key elements: the undulating air (الهواء المتموج), the smooth body (جسم أملس), and the resulting sound (الصدى). Understand how they relate.
3
Reflect on the necessity of smoothness
Ask yourself: why must the surface be smooth? The text implies that only a smooth surface allows the air to retain its undulation pattern when reflected.
This connects to the later debate about smoothness.

Commentary
This passage defines echo (الصدى) and its cause. The main text states that when undulating air strikes a smooth body (like a mountain or wall), it reflects back with its form, producing a sound similar to the first – the echo. The condition of smoothness is discussed: commonly smoothness is required for walls but not mountains, but the commentary argues it should be required for both, because an uneven surface would distort the reflection. A problem is raised: in a desert, echo is heard even with a distant mountain, which implies the undulation cannot reach it; thus the reflector may not be necessary, as Imam al-Razi held. A final note clarifies the verb 'raja'a' is transitive here (the body returns the air) versus intransitive in the main text, avoiding redundancy.

Condition of Smoothness for Echo
Commentary on the condition of smoothness in echo.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the debate about whether smoothness is required for both walls and mountains, and the problem of distant echo.

Core Idea
Smoothness is essential for echo because it preserves the undulation pattern.

The commentary now examines the condition of smoothness more deeply. It questions whether a mountain must be smooth like a wall, and raises a natural puzzle: if echo can be heard from faraway mountains, does it even need a reflector? These discussions show how kalam reasoning engages with physical phenomena.

Reflect on the implications

First Branch: Echo as New Air Oscillation
Al-Jurjani explains the first branch: echo is caused by a new oscillation of air, not the return of the original.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the apparent view is that echo results from a new oscillation of air, not the return of the original sound wave.

Core Idea
Echo is not the original sound returning; it is a newly generated sound.

This lesson tackles a common natural phenomenon: the echo. The question is whether the echo is the same sound wave bouncing back or a new sound produced by the collision. Al-Jurjani presents the first branch, arguing that the echo is a new oscillation of air, not the return of the first. Let's see how he reasons.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the overall argument.
The passage is continuous; notice the parentheses indicate the main thesis.
2
Identify the two explanations
The passage presents two competing views: the echo as a new undulation vs. the return of the original undulation. See how al-Jurjani frames the first as 'apparent'.
3
Understand the reasoning
The key point: when undulating air hits a resistant body, the original undulation is lost, and a new one arises from the collision. This new undulation is the echo.
Notice the analogy: just as the first undulation had impacts and stillnesses, so does the second.

When Do We Hear an Echo? Al-Jurjani's Qualifications
Al-Jurjani qualifies the claim that every sound has an echo.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why not all sounds produce a perceptible echo, according to al-Jurjani's analysis of al-Razi's view.

Core Idea
An echo always exists, but may be imperceptible due to distance or reflectors.

Al-Jurjani examines the claim that every sound has an echo. While Imam al-Razi argues that every displaced air creates a compensating undulation that causes an echo, al-Jurjani points out that the echo may not be heard. He gives two reasons: the reflector may be too close (causing overlap) or too soft (resulting in a weak reflection). This lesson unpacks those qualifications.

Reflect on the conditional

The Nine Basic Tastes: Agents and Patients
Enumerates nine basic tastes from agents (heat, cold, moderate) and patients (dense, subtle, moderate).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the nine basic (simple) tastes are derived from three agents (heat, cold, moderate) and three patients (dense, subtle, moderate).

Core Idea
The nine simple tastes arise from multiplying three possible agents by three possible patients.

This lesson focuses on the enumeration of basic tastes. The author explains that every taste has an active factor (heat, cold, or moderate) and a passive factor (dense, subtle, or moderate). Their combination yields nine simple tastes, which are the building blocks of all tasted qualities.

Guided Path
1
Locate the enumeration
Find the sentence stating that the basics of tastes are nine, resulting from multiplying three by three.
This is a key mathematical formulation.
2
Identify the agents
The author lists three agents: heat, cold, and the intermediate quality (moderate). These are the active factors that affect taste.
Think of how temperature influences taste.
3
Identify the patients
The three patients are: dense (thick, coarse), subtle (fine, delicate), and moderate between them. These are the passive factors that receive the agent.
These describe the consistency or texture of the tasted substance.
4
Combine agents and patients
Multiply the three agents by the three patients to get nine combinations. Each combination yields a simple taste.
This classification is systematic and logical.
