In this passage, the commentator quotes Ibn Sina's candid admission that his division of categories is merely an approximation, not a guaranteed proof. He also discusses the role of induction as the primary method for classifying existents. This lesson explores that key methodological insight.

By this it becomes clear that the commentator's saying 'before everything' is the intended meaning with respect to the context, for the chosen act here is returning to induction before claiming rational confinement and arguing for it.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the response to the previous objections. The commentator explains the phrase 'and if it were said' (ولو قيل), which presents a hypothetical objection: if someone claims that induction (istiqrā') only found these categories, then the whole division is useless and one should simply rely on induction from the start. The commentator then quotes a crucial passage from Ibn Sina's al-Shifā', where Ibn Sina admits that his division is merely an approximation (taqrīb) for testing the method, not a guaranteed proof. Ibn Sina states that he does not guarantee its correctness and that if he found a better division, he would use it. The commentator emphasizes that Ibn Sina's goal was merely to prevent dispersion, and therefore objecting to his division is unfair. The rest of the passage is dedicated to philological explanation of the phrase 'āthara dhī athīr', which means 'before everything', indicating that induction should be the first resort. A final gloss on 'manẓūr fīhi' (open to scrutiny) discusses the possibility that the principle of effect could be a substance, not only a quality, but this requires that the substance itself be the principle without mediation of quality.

A careful grammatical reading can remove apparent contradictions in authoritative texts.

The author of the matn uses a phrase that, at first glance, seems to contain an inaccuracy. Al-Isfahani, the commentator, does not dismiss it but instead offers a careful grammatical reinterpretation. This lesson walks you through that resolution, showing how precision in Arabic grammar preserves the coherence of the argument.

Confirm the corrected meaning

Commentary
This passage addresses an apparent inaccuracy in the author's phrase 'for any part of the line assumed' (fa-inna ayyu juz’in min al-khaṭṭi fardun). The Moon Shadow Example and Its Defense
Commentator discusses the moon shadow example and defends the definition of shadow.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the commentator acknowledges the moon shadow example as a concession and how he defends the definition of shadow against an objection.

Core Idea
The moon shadow example is a concession to the author, not a real shadow.

The commentator begins by addressing the example of the moon's shadow given in al-Mawāqif. While the author presents it as an example of shadow, the commentator notes it is contrary to common usage and even contrary to the view of a major commentary. He then defends the definition of shadow by addressing a potential objection that would classify the moon's light as shadow. This passage clarifies the boundaries of the technical term 'shadow'.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the moon shadow example
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice how the commentator first states the example and then immediately distances himself from it by referencing common usage and Sharh al-Maqāṣid.
The commentator is not rejecting the example outright but is explaining its basis.
2
Understand the objection to the definition of shadow
Identify the objection: if shadow is defined as 'second light' (light from a non-self-luminous source), then the moon's light—which is also reflected sunlight—would be shadow. The commentator presents two possible responses.
3
Analyze the two responses
The first response is to accept that the moon's light is indeed a shadow (commitment). The second response, if rejected, would force considering the moon as self-luminous, which is false. Discuss why this defense works.
Notice that the commentator uses the objection to clarify the definition.
4
Note the qualification on 'falling on its sides'
Read the short additional comment about the qualification 'al-wāqiʕa fī jānibihi'. This strengthens the example relative to the house. Understand how it refines the example.
This qualification ensures that only the light falling on the sides of the window is considered shadow.

Commentary
This passage contains two comments. First, the example of shadow from the moon is acknowledged to be according to the author's formulation but is contrary to common usage and the view in Sharh al-Maqāṣid that it is not a shadow.
