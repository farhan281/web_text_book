Ad Hominem Argument and the Philosopher's Objection
Explains ad hominem nature and philosopher's denial of instantaneous motion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the argument for vacuum is ad hominem rather than demonstrative, and why the philosopher rejects it based on the impossibility of instantaneous motion.

Core Idea
The argument fails because the philosopher denies motion in an instant.

This passage clarifies the nature of the previous argument for vacuum. It is not a demonstrative proof but an ad hominem strategy that relies on premises accepted by the mutakallim. The mutakallim offers ways to avoid the conclusion, but the philosopher's decisive objection is that the required rise of a surface in an instant is motion, which cannot be instantaneous. Understanding this deepens our grasp of how theological arguments are evaluated.

1
Identify the type of argument
The passage opens by stating that the argument is إلزامي (ad hominem) rather than برهاني (demonstrative). Understand the difference: it depends on the opponent's premises, not absolute truth.
This sets the stage for why the philosopher can reject it from his own principles.
2

The mutakallim shows that even on his own view, vacuum does not follow: God can create air instantly, or surfaces can be atomic with pores that allow air to fill gaps.
These alternatives prevent the argument from proving vacuum.
3
Focus on the philosopher's objection
The philosopher denies that the rise can happen in an instant because all motion requires time. This is the core reason the argument fails against him.
The argument assumes instantaneous motion, which is impossible for the philosopher.
4
Reflect on the methodology
Notice how arguments in kalam often depend on what the opponent accepts. This passage illustrates the importance of understanding the opponent's worldview in theological debates.
This is a key methodological lesson for engaging with different schools.

Commentary
This passage presents three empirical arguments used to demonstrate the impossibility of vacuum (khala'). The author enumerates them as the third, fourth, and fifth signs (amarat). Each relies on observed physical phenomena and the principle that nature prevents vacuum.

The third proof involves the cupping glass (mihjama). When applied to flesh and suction is applied, the flesh rises. The explanation given is that the extracted air necessarily draws in flesh to fill the space, preventing a vacuum. If the cupping glass is placed on smooth iron with no air passage, suction does not lift the iron because either no air is removed or the remaining air expands. On an anvil, strong suction can lift it, showing the same principle.

The fourth proof uses a tube in water. Suction at one end causes water to rise despite its weight. This is because the air surface is continuous with the water surface; when the air is drawn up, the water follows to avoid vacuum.

The fifth proof is a flask experiment. A sealed tube or stick partially inserted into a sealed flask is pushed in or pulled out. If pushed in, the flask breaks outward; if pulled out, it breaks inward. The explanation: the flask is full of air and the tube, so it cannot accommodate more volume without breaking (interpenetration) or less volume without creating a vacuum. The breaking indicates both the impossibility of interpenetration (tadakhul) and of vacuum.

Commentary
This passage presents three empirical arguments used to demonstrate the impossibility of vacuum (khala'). The author enumerates them as the third, fourth, and fifth signs (amarat). Each relies on observed physical phenomena and the principle that nature prevents vacuum.

The third proof involves the cupping glass (mihjama). When applied to flesh and suction is applied, the flesh rises. The explanation given is that the extracted air necessarily draws in flesh to fill the space, preventing a vacuum. If the cupping glass is placed on smooth iron with no air passage, suction does not lift the iron because either no air is removed or the remaining air expands. On an anvil, strong suction can lift it, showing the same principle.

The fourth proof uses a tube in water. Suction at one end causes water to rise despite its weight. This is because the air surface is continuous with the water surface; when the air is drawn up, the water follows to avoid vacuum.

The fifth proof is a flask experiment. A sealed tube or stick partially inserted into a sealed flask is pushed in or pulled out. If pushed in, the flask breaks outward; if pulled out, it breaks inward. The explanation: the flask is full of air and the tube, so it cannot accommodate more volume without breaking (interpenetration) or less volume without creating a vacuum. The breaking indicates both the impossibility of interpenetration (tadakhul) and of vacuum.

Commentary
This passage presents the views of the ancients on color gradation and Ibn Sina's critique. It begins by describing three ways from whiteness to blackness: through weak blackness, through redness and qatama, and through greenness and niliyya. Ibn Sina then argues that if only black and white existed, there would be only one way of transition, varying only in intensity according to the mixture of black and white. The observed multiplicity of ways (e.g., through redness, greenness) requires a third element that is neither black nor white nor a composite of them. He identifies this as light. Mixing black with light produces red or yellow depending on dominance; mixing yellow with black without brightness produces green. The commentary then refines these points: the mixture of visible and non-visible may not be visible, but the transparent mixed with opaque may explain it. The composite colors are artificial (i.e., produced by mixing), not essential. The doctrine assumed is that black and white are the primaries. The commentary also addresses an apparent contradiction about brightness in greenness, resolving it by distinguishing parts from whole. Finally, it notes that yellowness is implicitly involved in the third way as generating greenness.

The Fourth Argument: Light and the Reflection Experiment
Presents the fourth empirical argument and its objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the reflection experiment demonstrates the existence of a visible entity (light) that is neither black nor white.

Core Idea
Reflection from black proves a third color is needed.

The argument deduces that if only black and white existed, reflected light would always be white. Since we see red and green reflections, there must be another visible element—namely, light itself. Commentary
This passage presents two empirical arguments against the theory that black and white alone suffice for all colors. The fourth argument is based on experiment: light reflected from a black polished surface does not turn the receiving body black. If only black and white existed, reflected light would only produce white (since reflection comes from transparent parts, not black). But we observe red and green reflections, so there must be something other than black and white. Imam al-Razi raises a subtle objection: perhaps different subjective qualities arise from a single physical state, just as a single color can appear different. The fifth argument contrasts cooking with grinding: cooking whitens plaster and lime more than grinding, even though grinding also divides particles. This suggests whiteness is not merely due to air entering loosened particles (as grinding might imply), since cooking produces more whiteness without as much division. The commentary elaborates: mixture of transparent and opaque produces the imagined colors; reflection from black is impossible because black is opaque; therefore reflected light must come from something else, leading to the conclusion that light is an independent color element. The commentary also refines the definition of whiteness as not originally light, but as a composite involving light. Finally, it defends the fourth argument against the objection that composition might affect reflection, asserting that reflection is imaginary or real only from light.

Commentary
This passage presents two empirical arguments against the theory that black and white alone suffice for all colors. The fourth argument is based on experiment: light reflected from a black polished surface does not turn the receiving body black. If only black and white existed, reflected light would only produce white (since reflection comes from transparent parts, not black). But we observe red and green reflections, so there must be something other than black and white. Imam al-Razi raises a subtle objection: perhaps different subjective qualities arise from a single physical state, just as a single color can appear different. The fifth argument contrasts cooking with grinding: cooking whitens plaster and lime more than grinding, even though grinding also divides particles. This suggests whiteness is not merely due to air entering loosened particles (as grinding might imply), since cooking produces more whiteness without as much division. The commentary elaborates: mixture of transparent and opaque produces the imagined colors; reflection from black is impossible because black is opaque; therefore reflected light must come from something else, leading to the conclusion that light is an independent color element. The commentary also refines the definition of whiteness as not originally light, but as a composite involving light. Finally, it defends the fourth argument against the objection that composition might affect reflection, asserting that reflection is imaginary or real only from light.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological defense by the commentator of the author's arrangement of topics concerning darkness and light. The commentator begins by explaining the phenomenon of perceiving weak light (e.g., dust illuminated by a window) versus strong light (sunlight overpowering vision). Therefore, it is inappropriate to make the explanation of the essence of its opposite (darkness) an original goal; instead, conditionality should be the goal, and essence a branch. This refutes any preference for a method that places essence before states.

Tastables: Definition, Placement, and Mixed Perception
Defines tastables and explains their placement after sight and hearing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of tastables, the reasons for their placement after sight and hearing in the kalam order, and how taste and touch can combine into a single sensation.

Core Idea
Tastables are not less important, but treated briefly and placed after sight and hearing.

In this section, Sanusi turns to the fourth kind of sensible qualities: tastables. He not only defines them but also explains why they appear after sight and hearing in his systematic presentation. The passage reveals a nuanced view of sensation, including the dependence of taste on touch and saliva, and even the phenomenon where taste and touch merge into one perception. Let's explore these points as the author lays them out.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage slowly, then follow with the literal translation. Notice the key terms: المذوقات, القوة الذائقة, اللمس, الرطوبة اللعابية.
Pay attention to the phrase 'وإنما أخرها' – this signals the reason for ordering.
2
Identify the definition of tastables

The author gives multiple reasons why tastables are placed after sight and hearing: (1) brevity of discussion, (2) natural pairing with touch based on animal needs, (3) dependence of taste on touch and saliva, and (4) combined sensation. Note how each reason builds.
Reasons 2 and 3 are intertwined; reason 4 adds a fascinating complexity.

Consider the example of pungent taste
The passage uses the example of a pungent substance (الحريف) to illustrate how touch and taste produce an indistinguishable single sensation. Reflect on how this supports the author's point about the close relationship between touch and taste.
This example shows practical application of the theoretical idea.

Consolidate understanding
