How Cold Produces Astringency and Its Gradation
Explains astringency from cold in moderate substances and its gradation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how cold produces astringency in moderate substances and how it differs from the intense astringency of 'afas.

Core Idea
Astringency is an intermediate unpleasantness between sourness and intense astringency.

Having explained sourness, the passage now turns to astringency. Cold acting on a moderate receptacle produces a taste that is more unpleasant than sourness but less than the intense astringency of 'afas. The distinction is based on the degree of condensation and the effect on the tongue.

Guided Path
1
Read the astringency passage
Read the Arabic passage on astringency. Note how the cold agent condenses the moderate receptacle and the resulting quality is described as 'intermediate' between sourness and strong astringency.
The passage compares the unpleasantness of astringency to that of 'afas and sourness.
2
Understand the middle ground
Recognize that the condensation in a moderate receptacle is less than in a dense one but more than in a light one, leading to a taste with intermediate unpleasantness—astringency.
The text explicitly states the analogy: less than in dense, more than in light.
3
Contrast the two astringents
Focus on the final part: 'afas contracts both inner and outer tongue, causing strong aversion, while astringent (qâbidh) contracts only the outer surface, causing less aversion. This clarifies why astringency is less unpleasant.
The author uses the tongue test to differentiate the two.

Commentary
This passage defines how the cold element produces two distinct tastes—sourness (hâmidh) and astringency (qâbidh)—depending on the nature of the substance it acts upon. The author begins by explaining sourness: when cold acts on a light (latîf) substance, it condenses it less than it would a dense substance, resulting in a quality whose unpleasantness is intermediate, which is sourness. He then provides an example: the astringent fruit (al-'afas) becomes more sour as it becomes more watery and slightly warmed by the sun. Next, he discusses astringency: when cold acts on a moderate (mu'tadil) substance, the condensation is intermediate—less than in dense, more than in light—yielding a taste whose unpleasantness lies between astringency (higher) and sourness (lower). This is astringency. He clarifies the difference: astringent substances contract both the inner and outer of the tongue, causing strong aversion, while astringent (qâbidh) contracts only the outer, causing less aversion. The passage follows the author's commentary on a base text, explaining the rationale behind the classification.

Resolving Avicenna's Contradiction: Heat and Cold in Causation of Sourness
Addresses and resolves an apparent contradiction in Avicenna regarding the cause of sourness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Avicenna's apparent contradiction on the cause of sourness is resolved by distinguishing essential and accidental agency.

Core Idea
The same effect can have both heat and cold as causes, but at different causal levels.

This passage tackles a seeming inconsistency in Avicenna's medical writings: in one place he says heat causes sourness, in another cold. The resolution lies in a crucial philosophical distinction: essential versus accidental causation. By examining the example of sweet phlegm turning sour, you will see how heat indirectly sets the stage for cold to act as the true, direct cause.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Notice the structure: objection, answer, verification.
The passage ends with a Quranic analogy that may seem disconnected; focus on the causal argument first.
2
Identify the apparent contradiction
Find where Avicenna attributes sourness to heat and then to cold. Understand why this seems contradictory.
3
Understand the answer: essential vs. accidental
The solution uses the distinction between 'essential agent' (fāʿil bi-dh-dhāt) and 'accidental agent' (fāʿil bil-ʿaraḍ). Heat is accidental; cold is essential.
This is a key philosophical tool in Islamic thought.
4
Trace the causal process
Follow the example of sweet phlegm: foreign heat causes boiling, which draws off innate heat, then cold dominates and produces sourness. See how heat only triggers the chain.
This step clarifies why heat is not the direct cause.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer. The objection is that some think sourness comes from weak heat, but the author denies this. He notes an apparent contradiction in Avicenna: in his Canon of Medicine, he attributes sourness to heat in one place and to cold in another. The answer, provided by 'some virtuous one', resolves this by distinguishing primary and accidental causation: heat causes sourness accidentally (bil-'arad) by setting off processes that ultimately lead to cold's dominance, while cold causes it essentially (bi-dh-dhât). Thus, heat is only an indirect cause. The passage ends with a grammatical note equating the causal structure to the Quranic verse about ilaf of Quraysh, where 'li-'ilâfi' functions as a conditional explanation. The argument maintains that the primary agent of sourness is cold, not heat.

The Moderate Element's Role in Sweetness, Greasiness, and Insipidity
Classification of three tastes by moderate element on dense, light, and moderate receptacles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the moderate element produces different tastes (sweetness, greasiness, insipidity) depending on the density of the receptacle, and why insipidity is not directly sensed.

Core Idea
The same moderate agent produces three different tastes depending on the receptacle's density.

This lesson explores how Galenic physiology explains the production of tastes by a moderate element acting on substances of varying density. It distinguishes three outcomes—sweetness, greasiness, and insipidity—and explains why insipidity, though intermediate, is not directly sensed, along with the distinction between real and sensory insipidity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three receptacles and tastes
Read the passage and list the three types of receptacles (dense, light, moderate) and the tastes they produce (sweetness, greasiness, insipidity).
2
Understand the mechanism for each taste
For each receptacle, note how the resistance between agent and receptacle determines the intensity of the resulting taste. Strong resistance yields sweetness, weak resistance yields greasiness, intermediate resistance yields insipidity.
3
Explain why insipidity is not sensed
The passage states that insipidity is a real quality but is not sensed because its carrier (the moderate receptacle) does not dissolve into the saliva, and the quality itself is too weak.
Contrast with greasiness, which is sensed because its carrier is subtle.
4
Distinguish real vs. sensory insipidity
Real insipidity is complete tastelessness (like simple bodies), while sensory insipidity is a taste that is not sensed due to density but can be revealed by dissolution (e.g., brass turned into verdigris).
This addresses a common misunderstanding.

Commentary
This passage classifies the three tastes produced by the moderate element (al-mu'tadil) acting on three types of receptacles: dense, light, and moderate. It defines each taste and explains why one is sensed and another is not. First, when the moderate agent acts on a dense substance (kathîf), there is strong resistance, causing the agent's parts to combine and produce a perfect, pleasant effect: sweetness (halâwah), which is the most pleasant taste. Second, on a light substance (latîf), resistance is weak, so the agent penetrates and produces a weak pleasant effect: greasiness (dusûmah). Third, on a moderate substance (mu'tadil), the effect is intermediate, yielding a pleasant quality weaker than sweetness but stronger than greasiness—insipidity (tafâhah). However, this quality is not sensed because the carrier substance is too dense to dissolve into the saliva and stimulate the taste buds, and the quality itself is too weak. The passage distinguishes real insipidity (tafâhah haqîqiyya) from sensory insipidity (tafâhah hissiyya): real insipidity is tastelessness like in simple bodies; sensory insipidity occurs in bodies that have taste but it is not sensed due to density, but if dissolved (e.g., brass turned into verdigris) it yields a strong sharp taste. The author also notes a common misunderstanding: some wrongly think insipidity as tastelessness is a taste; he compares it to the 'unconditional' in logic, which is counted among the moodals. He mentions that Imam al-Razi omitted it for this reason.

المقصد الثاني : الحياة مشروطة بالبنية المخصوصة ] في شرط الحياة ( الحياة عند الحكماء مشروطة بالبنية المخصوصة وهو جسم) مركب من العناصر ) له صورة) نوعية (مخصوصة و لذلك الجسم (كيفيات تتبعها ) أي تتبع هذه الكيفيات تلك الصورة المخصوصة ) من اعتدال ) مزاجي ( خاص وغيره ) . فإنهم زعموا أنه لا بد في الحياة من جسم مؤلف من العناصر الأربعة، ومن مزاج معتدل مناسب لنوع من الحيوانات حتى يفيض عليه صورة نوعية حيوانية مستتبعة للحياة، ولا بد فيها من اعتدال الروح الحيواني المتولد من بخارية الأخلاط قوله : ( أي تتبع هذه الكيفيات التي من جملتها الحياة فتكون مشروطة بالصورة النوعية المشروطة بالنية المخصوصة وبهذا ظهر فائدة قوله : ولذلك الجسم كيفيات تتبعها، وأن تفسيره بأنه تتبع هذه الكيفيات تلك الصورة النوعية المخصوصة لغو من الكلام لا دخل له في المقصود على أنه ليس لها سوى الاعتدال النوعي كيفية تتبع الصورة النوعية إياها فكيف يصح قوله : من اعتدال خاص وغيره . قوله : ( من اعتدال مزاجي خص الاعتدال بالمزاجي لأن الاعتدال الروحي ليس لذلك الجسم المركب من العناصر بل للروح الحاصل من الأخلاط . قوله : ( فإنهم زعموا إلخ يريد أنّ الحياة مشروطة بالنسبة لوجهين. أحدهما : من حيث الفاعل فإن الحياة تابعة للصورة النوعية المقتضية لها التابعة للاعتدال المزاجي الذي لا يحصل إلا بالبنية المخصوصة وثانيهما باعتبار الحامل فإنَّ الحياة لا تفيض إلا على الروح الحيواني من لطافة الأخلاط التي لا تحصل إلا بالبنية قوله : (المتولدة من بخارية الأخلاط إلخ) أي من صيرورة الاخلاط بخاراً فإنه، جسم لطيف قوله: (أي تتبع هذه الكيفيات تلك الصورة (المخصوصة) المناسب لقوله الآتي يفيض عليه صورة نوعية أن يجعل هذه الكيفيات مفعول تتبع وتلك الصورة فاعلها، لكن الكلام في وجود كيفية غير الاعتدال متبوعة للصورة كما يدل عليه الكيفيات أيضاً، ويمكن أن يعكس حديث الفاعلية والمفعولية أن يراد بالتبعية التبعية باعتبار البقاء كما سيحققه فليتأمل. قوله : (اعتدال الروح الحيواني الروح الحيواني جسم لطيف بخاري يتكون من الأخلاط ينبعث من التجويف الأيسر من القلب ويسري إلى البدن في عروق ثابتة من تسمى بالشرايين.
Second objective: Life is conditioned by the specific structure. Regarding the condition of life: Life, according to the philosophers, is conditioned by a specific structure, which is a body) composed of the elements, having a specific specific form, and therefore the body has qualities that follow it, i.e., these qualities follow that specific form, such as a specific temperamental balance and other things. For they claimed that life requires a body composed of the four elements, and a balanced temperament suitable for a type of animal so that a specific animal form may emanate upon it, entailing life; and it requires the balance of the animal spirit generated from the vapor of the humors. (His statement:) 'i.e., these qualities follow' — the qualities of which life is one, so life becomes conditioned by the specific form which is conditioned by the specific structure. By this, the benefit of his statement 'and therefore the body has qualities that follow it' becomes apparent, and that interpreting it as 'these qualities follow that specific form' is meaningless talk that has no bearing on the objective, especially since it has nothing besides the specific temperament as a quality following the specific form — so how can his statement 'such as a specific temperament and other things' be valid? (His statement:) 'a specific temperamental' — he specified 'temperamental' because the spiritual balance is not for that body composed of elements, but for the spirit resulting from the humors. (His statement:) 'For they claimed etc.' — he intends that life is conditioned in two respects. One: from the aspect of the efficient cause, for life is consequent upon the specific form that necessitates it, which is consequent upon the temperamental balance that is only obtained through the specific structure. The second: from the aspect of the substrate, for life emanates only onto the animal spirit from the subtlety of the humors, which is only obtained through the specific structure. (His statement:) 'generated from the vapor of the humors etc.' — i.e., from the transformation of humors into vapor, for it is a subtle body. (His statement:) 'i.e., these qualities follow that specific form' — it is appropriate to his later statement 'so that a specific form may emanate upon it' that these qualities are made the object of 'follow' and that form is its agent; but the discussion concerns the existence of a quality other than temperament that follows the form, as the plural 'qualities' indicates. It is also possible to reverse the question of agent and object, meaning that 'following' is intended in terms of subsistence, as he will verify — so let one reflect. (His statement:) 'balance of the animal spirit' — the animal spirit is a subtle vaporous body formed from the humors, emanating from the left cavity of the heart and flowing through the body in fixed veins that are called arteries.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory unit on the second objective: that life is conditioned by a specific bodily structure. The commentator begins by stating the philosophers' view: life requires a body composed of the four elements, a specific type-specific form, and qualities (like a specific temperamental balance) that follow that form. He then elaborates that life depends on a balanced animal spirit generated from the vapor of the humors. The commentary then clarifies the phrase 'qualities that follow it' by noting that life itself is one of those qualities, making life conditional on the specific form and thus on the specific structure. He dismisses a misinterpretation that would render the phrase meaningless. Next, he explains that the balance spoken of is temperamental, not spiritual, because spiritual balance belongs to the spirit, not the elemental body. The commentator then explains that life is conditioned in two respects: (1) from the efficient cause (الفاعل): life depends on the specific form, which depends on the temperamental balance, which only comes from the specific structure; (2) from the substrate (الحامل): life only emanates onto the animal spirit, which arises from the subtlety of humors that only come from the specific structure. Finally, he describes the animal spirit as a subtle vapor emanating from the left heart chamber and traveling through the arteries.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That life is a simple power not requiring a specific body or temperament.
Correction: The passage shows that life requires a specific bodily structure, a balanced temperament, a specific form, and a vital spirit.

Key Takeaway
Life is not self-sufficient; it requires a specific bodily structure, a balanced temperament, and a vital spirit, and it is conditioned both by its efficient cause and its substrate.
