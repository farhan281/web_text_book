Early Mu'tazilite and Philosopher Positions on Accident Persistence
Lists supporters of non-persistence and persistence of accidents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand which early Mu'tazilites agreed with the Ash'aris on the non-persistence of accidents, and which groups affirmed persistence with exceptions.

Core Idea
Not all Mu'tazilites agreed on persistence; some early ones sided with Ash'aris.

The question of whether accidents persist divided theologians. Some early Mu'tazilites like al-Nazzam and al-Ka'bi joined the Ash'ari view that they do not persist. Others, including philosophers and most Mu'tazilites, affirmed persistence except for certain accidents. This passage introduces these positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main positions
Read the passage and find the two opposing views: one against persistence (al-Nazzam and al-Ka'bi) and one for persistence (philosophers and majority Mu'tazilites).
The 'them' in the passage refers to the Ash'aris.
2
Note the exceptions for persistence
Observe that the philosophers and majority Mu'tazilites excluded times, motions, and sounds from persistence.
3
Reflect on the significance
Think about why early Mu'tazilites would agree with the Ash'aris on this point.
This shows the diversity within the Mu'tazilite school.

The passage lists who says accidents cannot persist at all, who says most persist, and who limits persistence to certain types.

Commentary
This passage classifies the various positions on the persistence of accidents. It begins by noting that al-Naẓẓām and al-Ka'bī from the early Mu'tazila agreed with the Ash'aris that no accidents persist. Then it states that the philosophers and the majority of Mu'tazila hold that accidents persist, except for times, movements, and sounds. Next, it mentions the views of Abū 'Alī al-Jubbā'ī, his son Abū Hāshim, and Abū al-Hudhayl: they held that colors, tastes, and smells persist, but sciences, wills, sounds, and types of speech do not. However, there is a discrepancy: the commentator notes that in another section (the sixteenth objective on psychic qualities), Abū 'Alī was reported to have said that necessary and acquired sciences not involving obligation persist, while obligated acquired sciences do not; and his son Abū Hāshim said all sciences persist. The commentator tries to reconcile this by suggesting that the present passage means Abū 'Alī only affirmed persistence for some sciences (the non-obligatory ones) and that the text's negation of sciences is not absolute but relative to the three examples (colors, tastes, smells). But he finds this reconciliation forced and not fully consistent, especially for Abū Hāshim, and calls the effort a 'cold contrivance' (ta'assuf bārid) that a sound mind would not accept. The passage ends with an invitation to reflect.

Commentary
This passage presents al-Razi's critique and the response of the commentators. It begins with an analogy: place exists in itself even if not in a place, but a spatial thing must be in a place to exist. This is called a verbal dispute because the issue is whether time would be identical to past, present, or future. Then the principle 'the falsity of the specific does not necessitate the falsity of the general' is invoked; the response qualifies that if the general is confined to specific individuals and none exist, then the general does not exist. The passage ends with a remark that al-Razi's own work might not treat the proof as binding.

The General-Specific Principle and Its Qualification
Sanusi explains the logical relationship between general and specific claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical rule that the falsity of the specific does not necessarily entail the falsity of the general, and when it does.

Core Idea
The general exists only through its specific instances; if all specifics are false, the general is false.

This passage discusses a key logical principle: the falsity of a specific claim does not automatically falsify the more general claim. However, if the general is confined to a set of specifics and all those specifics are false, then the general must also be false. This is used in the debate about time: if time would have to be one of three specifics (past, present, future) and all are invalid, time cannot exist.

Guided Path
1
Read the principle
Read the statement that 'the falsity of the specific does not necessitate the falsity of the general.' Understand this as a general logical maxim.
2
Understand the qualification
Notice the response: 'We say: If the general is confined to several things each of which is more specific than it... and nothing of those things exists, then the general does not exist.' This is the key qualification.
The qualification depends on confinement (انحصار).
3
Apply to the time argument
The argument against time uses this qualified principle: time would be confined to past, present, future; none exist, so time does not exist.

Al-Razi's Critique by Motion and the Comprehensive Response
Al-Razi's critique by motion and the multifaceted response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's argument that the same reasoning used against time would also deny motion, a self-evident reality, and the responses that defend the original argument.

Core Idea
Al-Razi uses motion to show the argument against time is self-defeating, but the responses argue motion's temporal divisions are conceptual, not real.

This passage presents al-Razi's powerful critique: if the argument that time cannot exist because it would have to be one of three non-existent divisions is applied to motion, motion would also be unreal—yet motion is perceptible. The commentators respond by clarifying that the divisions are merely conceptual, arising from analysis of something that exists as a whole. They also note that the original argument was 'binding' (ilzāmi), not a direct demonstration, so the critique misses the point.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Razi's critique
Read the part describing al-Razi's argument: applying the same reasoning to motion leads to absurdity because motion is perceptible.
Look for the structure: motion is confined to three parts, present part is indivisible, leading to composition of indivisibles, which is false.
2
Understand the first response: verbal dispute and conceptual divisions
The commentators say the dispute is over the word 'in', and the divisions of time/motion are conceptual, not real parts. So negating them does not negate the whole.
The phrase 'these divisions are conceptual' (هذه الأقسام اعتبارية) is key.
3
Examine the second response: the proof is binding
The original proof is ilzāmi; it aims to show a contradiction in the opponent's view, not to prove time's non-existence. Thus al-Razi's critique is misplaced.
Ilzām means binding or forcing a conclusion.
4
Note the discussion about al-Razi's own work
The passage ends with a remark that al-Razi's Mabāhith might not treat the proof as binding, so the critique might still be valid in that context.
This is a nuanced point.

Commentary
This passage presents al-Razi's critique and the response of the commentators. It begins with an analogy: place exists in itself even if not in a place, but a spatial thing must be in a place to exist. This is called a verbal dispute because the issue is whether time would be identical to past, present, or future. Then the principle 'the falsity of the specific does not necessitate the falsity of the general' is invoked; the response qualifies that if the general is confined to specific individuals and none exist, then the general does not exist. The commentators respond that motion, like time, is applied to time and exists in its entirety; its division into past, present, future is conceptual after its external existence, so the critique does not hold. They also note that the proof in question is 'binding' (ilzāmī) and not a direct demonstration, so al-Razi's critique misses the mark. The passage ends with a remark that al-Razi's own work might not treat the proof as binding.

Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari refutation of the conditionality of life on a specific bodily structure, followed by a response. It begins by stating the Ash'ari position: they do not condition life on a specific structure; rather, Allah can create life in a single indivisible part.
