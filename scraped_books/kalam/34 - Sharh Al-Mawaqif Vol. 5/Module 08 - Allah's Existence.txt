Proving the Existence of Accidents
Al-Isfahani proves accidents and refutes deniers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that accidents exist as real entities known by sensory necessity, and you will be able to refute the main objections against their existence.

Core Idea
Accidents are directly perceived and cannot subsist independently.

Al-Isfahani begins by addressing the question of accidents. He notes that only a few deny their existence, such as Ibn Kaysan, while the majority agree that accidents do not subsist by themselves. The strongest proof is sensory necessity: we perceive colors, sounds, etc., and we know intuitively they require a substrate. He also rebuts Abu al-Hudhayl's claim that volition is an accident not in a substrate, calling it outright prevarication.

Commentary
This passage is a proof of the existence of accidents (a'rad) and a refutation of those who deny that they subsist in a substrate. It begins by noting that the only known denier of accidents altogether is Ibn Kaysan al-Asamm, who held that everything is substance (jawhar). The majority affirm accidents, agreeing that accidents do not subsist by themselves, except for a small group like Abu al-Hudhayl al-Allaf, who allowed that a volition ('irada) could occur as an accident not in a substrate, and that God is volitional by that volition. The author then asserts that sensory necessity (al-darura) is enough proof: we directly perceive colors, sounds, etc., and we know they do not subsist by themselves. To claim that volition subsists by itself and that God is volitional by it, while its relation to God and to others is equal, is sheer prevarication (mukabara). The commentary explains that the author abbreviated his argument by relying on the indication of necessity. It also refutes Abu al-Hudhayl: even if he does not call volition an 'accident' in the technical sense, it is still a temporal attribute (sifa haditha), and the reality of accident is precisely a temporal attribute; however, accidentally also requires subsistence in a substrate, which volition as he conceives it lacks, so his 'temporal attribute' is broader than accident. Finally, the commentary rejects the claim that volition's relation to God and others is equal, because Abu al-Hudhayl considers it a proper attribute of God.

Ash'ari Doctrine: Accidents Are Continuously Renewed
States Ash'ari position on non-persistence of accidents and its proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari position that accidents are continually renewed and how this preserves the world's constant need for God.

Core Idea
If accidents persisted, the world would be independent of God after creation.

The Ash'ari school holds that accidents do not endure for two moments but are constantly created anew by God. This doctrine is not merely a metaphysical curiosity; it is a direct response to a theological problem: if the world could continue existing without new acts of creation, it would be independent of its Creator. In this passage, al-Ash'ari and his followers explain their reasoning and why they affirm the perpetual renewal of accidents.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the key claim: accidents do not persist. Understand the terms: 'accident', 'substance', 'origination'.
Pay attention to the phrase 'by His mere will' — this is crucial.
2
Identify the theological reason
The passage gives a specific reason for this doctrine: to avoid the implication that the world is independent of God after its initial creation. Find that argument in the text.
The key is that if accidents persisted, the cause for needing God (origination) would cease.
3
Connect accident renewal to dependence
Understand the logical chain: substance continues via accidents; accidents need constant creation; therefore substance always depends on God. This is the proof.
This shows how the doctrine directly upholds tawhid.
4
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself: Why does this matter for Islamic theology? How does it differ from other views? The commentary will help.
This doctrine is a cornerstone of Ash'ari occasionalism.

Commentary
This passage presents the Ash'ari doctrine that accidents do not persist for two moments but are constantly renewed by God. The author explains the reasoning: if accidents persisted, the world would be independent of God after its initial creation, because the cause for needing God is origination (حدوث). To avoid this impious consequence, they assert that the accident is the condition for the substance's continuance, and since the accident is renewed and depends on God, the substance also remains dependent on God. Thus, the world is always in need of the Creator.

The Ash'ari Argument for Non-Persistence of Accidents
Al-Ījī states the Ash'ari claim that accidents do not persist and gives their argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari argument that accidents do not persist for two moments and how this ensures the world's continuous need for God.

Core Idea
Accidents must be recreated every moment to keep the world dependent on God.

This passage lays out a central Ash'ari doctrine: that accidents (like color, motion, or life) last only an instant and are immediately replaced by new ones. The purpose of this view is to avoid a theological problem: if anything in the world could persist without God's ongoing action, the world would be independent of its Maker. Al-Ījī presents the claim and the reasoning behind it, showing how the constant renewal of accidents ties every moment of existence directly to the Creator's will.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the claim
Begin by reading the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the first part states the doctrine: accidents do not endure for two moments. Each accident is replaced by a similar one, and the timing of each is determined solely by God.
The phrase 'القادر المختار' emphasizes God's absolute freedom in specifying times.
2
Understand the problem of independence
The Ash'aris reason that if accidents persisted, the world after its first moment would no longer need God for its continued existence. This would imply that the world is independent of the Creator, which is impossible.
The key premise: 'the cause necessitating the effect is origination (al-huduth).' Once something exists, it would need no further cause if it could persist.
3
See how the condition of endurance solves it
The solution is to say that the persistence of substances (like atoms) depends on accidents. Since accidents are always new and require a cause each moment, the substance also needs God through its condition.
The term 'شرط البقاء' (condition of endurance) means the accident is a necessary condition for the substance to continue existing.
4
Note the role of the Omnipotent Chooser
The argument emphasizes that God specifies each accident to its exact moment by His mere will, even though He could have created it earlier or later. This highlights God's complete control over every instant.
5
Reflect on the overall argument
Consider how this argument ties together: the non-persistence of accidents solves the theological problem of the world's independence, and it reinforces the continuous dependence of all creation on God's creative act.
The commentary clarifies that this view is supported by three proofs (to be given later) and that the term 'origination' is taken in its apparent sense.

Commentary
This passage states the Ash'ari doctrine that accidents do not persist for two moments and presents their core argument. The text first asserts the position: each accident exists for only one moment, is replaced by a similar accident, and the specific timing of each is determined solely by God's will. Then it explains why the Ash'aris adopted this view: they hold that the only reason anything needs a cause is its origination (ḥudūth). If accidents persisted, the world after its origination would no longer need God for its continued existence, implying independence from the Creator—an impossible conclusion. To avoid this, they argue that the persistence of a substance (jawhar) depends on accidents. Since accidents are constantly renewed and require a cause at every moment, the substance too, through its dependence on accidents, remains perpetually dependent on God. The commentary then clarifies that the motivation for this view is not only this argument but also three proofs (to be given later) which support the general ruling that no accident persists. It also clarifies that the term 'origination' is taken in its apparent sense (being preceded by non-existence), and that even on a different interpretation the world's need for a cause during endurance is established without the contrivance of positing an accident of persistence. Finally, it notes that 'condition for endurance' means the accident is a necessary part for the substance's continued existence, not contradicting the fact that all possibles are attributed to God from the outset.

Al-Nazzam's Proof for Non-Persistence of Bodies
Al-Nazzam's proof applied to bodies with circularity discussion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Nazzam's reductio proof that bodies do not persist, including the three possible modes of cessation and how he avoids circularity regarding the condition of substance.

Core Idea
Al-Nazzam argues bodies must cease because persistence would make cessation impossible.

Al-Nazzam extends his earlier argument against the persistence of accidents to bodies themselves. He presents a reductio: if bodies persisted, they could never cease to exist, yet we observe cessation by consensus and sense. The passage then analyzes how cessation could occur—by itself, by an agent, or by removal of a condition—and tackles a potential circularity involving the condition when it is a substance. This lesson unpacks that dense reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof statement
Read the Arabic and translation of the initial claim: if bodies persisted, cessation would be impossible. Notice the reductio structure and the appeal to consensus and sense.
This is the core of the argument.
2
Examine the three possibilities for cessation
Identify the three ways a body could cease: by itself, by an agent, or by removal of a condition. Understand why the first two are rejected and the third is pursued.
The third path leads to the discussion of circularity.
3
Follow the circularity objection and reply
Trace how the objection arises: persistence of substance depends on the type of accidents and vice versa. Then see how Al-Nazzam avoids it by distinguishing the type from specific instances.
The resolution hinges on 'the type does not require this specific substance'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Number is not a real external thing; it is a mental construct dependent on units.

Al-Jurjānī presents a sophisticated proof that number (ʿadad) is not a real entity in the external world but a mental consideration (iʿtibārī). The argument hinges on the nature of multiplicity and its relationship to units. If multiplicity were real as a whole, it would lead to an infinite regress; instead, its existence mirrors the existence of its units. Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the lemma 'And it does not need in their occurrence' (wa lā yaḥtāju fī 'urūḍihimā). The author explains that while priority and posteriority depend on motion for their existence (thubūt), motion is a mediator in existence, not in occurrence (al-‘urūḍ). Then, commenting on the lemma 'Unlike what is other than it' (bikhilāfi mā 'adāhā), the author discusses motion. Motion's reality is the perfection of what is in potentiality (kamāl mā bil-quwwa). It is not intrinsically continuous; even if we suppose three indivisible parts, the motion in the middle is not continuous. Therefore, motion itself is not divisible, and its parts do not have priority and posteriority by themselves. Rather, division, priority, and posteriority accrue to motion because it coincides with the spatial extension (masāfa), which is continuous and has priority and posteriority. The author elaborates by citing Avicenna (al-Shaykh) from al-Shifā': motion can be divided into prior and posterior based on the parts of space. The prior of motion is that which occurs in the prior part of space, and the posterior occurs in the posterior part. However, the prior and posterior of motion do not coexist, unlike those of space. Thus, priority and posteriority in motion have a special characteristic, and they are counted by motion. Since motion, by its parts, lacks priority and posteriority, it has a number due to its relation to space, and it also has a magnitude corresponding to the space's magnitude. Time (al-zamān) is precisely this number and magnitude.

The author presents the second proof that present time exists and is indivisible, with responses to objections.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The present time must exist and be indivisible, otherwise time itself would be impossible.

This lesson presents a key kalam argument: if time exists, the present moment must be real and indivisible. The proof hinges on the fact that past and future are defined relative to the present; without a present, no time would exist. Further, the present cannot have parts without creating contradictions. We'll explore the argument and the responses to common objections.

Guided Path

If the present had parts, those parts would either be simultaneous (making all time coexist) or successive (making only a part present). Both lead to contradictions.

Likewise, it resembles that in time and in motion in the sense of traversal there is something like that, and something like the point entering the line which it did not do. Until he said: So if there is something like this existent, then what is said is true: the now by its flowing produces time, and this is not the now that is assumed between two times connecting them, to the end of his speech. The thing that coincides with it is the point; so then the part is not necessary. We said: The thing that coincides with it in motion in the sense of traversal is also the point, so the part is not necessary. But you may say: The part is only necessary from the succession of the point in the distance, which is necessary from the composition of motion from indivisible parts, because the mover from a point to a third then traverses in an instant a point of the parts of the body also an indivisible thing, so the indivisible part is necessary, not from the existence of the point. Since the point's being of its locus indivisible is not necessary, rather its locus is the line, and no harm follows from the coincidence of motion in the sense of intermediateness with a point on a successive basis, for no two points are assumed but between them there is a divisible thing that the mover traverses, and the discussion after is a place of reflection.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the mutakallimun's denial of time. It begins by presenting their objection: if time were continuous like motion, then the time of the Flood would have to exist now, which is absurd, so time cannot be continuous. The response, drawn from al-Mabahith al-Mashriqiyya, distinguishes two meanings of time: first, an existing indivisible thing corresponding to motion 'in the middle' (the flowing now); second, an imaginary extension produced by the flow of the first. The passage then quotes a commentary on the base text, explaining that the boundaries assumed on a line do not imply composition because motion is also coincident with points. The argument from al-Shifa is cited: just as the moving point generates a line by its flow, the flowing now generates time. The discussion concludes that no indivisible part is necessary because between any two points there is a divisible interval.

The flowing now is like a moving point that generates a line without being composed of parts.

Time as Past and Future: Refuting Atomic Composition
Presents the view that time is only past and future, refuting the second objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosopher's view that time consists only of past and future, with the present being an imaginary boundary, and how this refutes the objection that time is composed of atomic nows.

Core Idea
The present is not a part of time but a boundary between past and future.

The philosophers distinguish time as purely past and future, denying a real present part. This radical view has profound implications: if the present is merely a common boundary like a point on a line, then time cannot be composed of successive atomic nows. In this passage, the author defends this position and shows how it dissolves the second objection against the existence of time.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Note the key terms 'past time', 'future time', 'flowing now', and 'imaginary present'.
The passage is dense; read it twice to grasp the flow of argument.
2
Identify the main claim about time
Focus on the statement 'time according to the philosophers is either past or future'. Ask: What is the present? How is it described?
The present is compared to a point on a line – a boundary, not a segment.
3
Understand the consequences for atomic composition
If two nows cannot meet like two points on a line, then time cannot be made of indivisible nows. This refutes the objection that time is composed of atomic parts.
The passage also extends this to motion: motion is not composed of indivisible parts.
4
Address the potential counterargument
The commentary anticipates an objection: if past and future are non-existent and the present is not a real part, then time is non-existent. The reply is that the 'flowing now' is the real existent, so the objection fails.
This clarifies that the existence of time as a continuous, flowing now is affirmed.

Proof that Time is a Necessary Substance
A reductio argument that time is necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how some ancient philosophers used a reductio argument to prove that time is a necessary substance, not an accident.

Core Idea
Time's non-existence leads to a contradiction, so it must be necessary.

This passage presents a fascinating argument from some ancient philosophers that time is not a mere accident but a self-subsisting necessary substance. The proof employs a logical contradiction: if time did not exist, we would still need time to describe that non-existence, forcing us to conclude that time exists necessarily. Let's examine the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get an overview of the argument. Notice the structure: claim, proof, conclusion.

Walk through the reductio
Follow the argument: assume time does not exist. Then its non-existence would be posterior to its existence. That posteriority (ba'diyya) must be temporal, implying time exists at that moment. Contradiction.
The key is that non-existence cannot be described without time.

Since assuming non-existence leads to existence, non-existence is impossible. Thus time exists necessarily, and being necessary, it must be a substance.

Three Rational Proofs for the Existence of Place
Presents three proofs for place's existence: transference, divisibility, and variation, and concludes they cannot apply to non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three kalam proofs for the existence of place based on transference, divisibility, and variation, and why these properties cannot belong to pure non-existence.

Core Idea
Place exists because bodies move, divide, and vary — nonexistence cannot do that.

The author now presents three compelling proofs that place must exist, drawing from everyday observations: bodies move from one location to another, places can be divided into halves and thirds, and places vary in size. These properties are incompatible with pure non-existence. In this lesson, we will examine each proof and see how they collectively establish the reality of place.
