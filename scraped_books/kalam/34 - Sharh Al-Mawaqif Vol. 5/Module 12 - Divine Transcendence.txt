The First Proof Against Space as a Self-Subsistent Dimension
Refutes the view that space is a self-subsistent dimension by arguing that dimension requires a subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical argument that a dimension requires a subject, and why the proof against a self-subsistent dimension relies on an unproven assumption about the similarity of material and immaterial dimensions.

Core Idea
A dimension cannot be self-subsistent because it needs a subject—but that conclusion relies on a hidden premise.

This passage presents a key argument against the idea that space is an independent dimension. The argument seems straightforward, but the commentator reveals it rests on a controversial assumption. By examining the logical steps, we can see why the proof is not conclusive without further support.

Guided Path

2

3
Identify the hidden assumption

This is the key to evaluating the proof.

Consider why the assumption is denied. Material dimensions (in bodies) may differ from immaterial ones. Does that undermine the proof?
Think about whether the proof would still work without that assumption.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof against the view that space (al-makan) is a self-subsistent dimension (bu'd qā'im bi-nafsihi). The argument proceeds as follows: A dimension (bu'd) by its very essence requires a subject (mahall) in which it subsists and to which it is related; otherwise it would be independent. If a dimension were independent, it could not subsist in a subject because what does not need something cannot be conceived to enter into it. But we observe that dimensions exist in bodies, e.g., the dimensions of a body subsist in that body. Therefore, no dimension can be self-subsistent; all dimensions are accidents needing a subject. Hence space cannot be a self-subsistent dimension. The commentator then explains that this argument is based on the assumption that all dimensions are alike (tamāthul) — i.e., material dimensions (such as those in bodies) and immaterial dimensions (if such exist) are considered similar. But this assumption is denied (mamnū'), as material and immaterial dimensions differ fundamentally: one is in a subject, the other might not be. Thus the proof fails.

Two Dimensions, One Body: Distinguishing Material from Immaterial
Distinguishes material from immaterial dimensions to answer an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the interpenetration of a material dimension and an immaterial dimension is not necessarily impossible, based on the distinction between their natures.

Core Idea
Not all dimensions are alike: material and immaterial dimensions differ in reality, so their interpenetration is not impossible.

In the debate about space as a self-subsistent dimension, a key objection argued that two dimensions cannot coexist in one body. The response hinges on a crucial distinction: there are two types of dimensions—one that is material and inseparable from the body, and another that is immaterial and separable. This passage explains that their interpenetration is not ruled out by necessity, because the impossibility applies only to two material dimensions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the opening: the opponent claims that if space is a dimension, then a body would contain two dimensions (its own and space), which is impossible. This sets up the need for a reply.
The objection is based on a presumed impossibility of two dimensions coexisting.
2
Identify the answer's move
The answer begins by rejecting the claim that two dimensions cannot be in one body 'on that supposition.' It then offers a distinction that breaks the objection's premise.
The key is that the two dimensions are not of the same kind.
3
Grasp the distinction
There is a material dimension that is in the body and accompanies its matter, and an immaterial dimension (space) that is self-subsistent and separable from the body. Notice the contrast: one is a state in matter, the other is not.
The material dimension is 'in the body,' the immaterial dimension is 'in which the body is.'
4
See the conclusion
Because of this difference in reality, the interpenetration of a material and an immaterial dimension is not impossible. The claim of necessary impossibility is denied; only material-material interpenetration is necessarily impossible.
The passage ends by affirming the shared property of being dimensions but stressing the underlying difference.

Corollaries of Permissible Interpenetration and Final Refutation
Derives consequences from permissibility of material-immaterial interpenetration and refutes opposing arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the permissibility of interpenetration between material and immaterial dimensions does not lead to absurd consequences, and how the final refutation rests on exposing a false assumption of similarity.

Core Idea
Not all interpenetration leads to absurdity; material and immaterial are fundamentally different.

In this passage, the author continues to defend the permissibility of interpenetration between a material body and an immaterial dimension. He shows that this does not imply any absurd consequences, such as a single cubit being two cubits, or the entire world fitting into a mustard seed. The key is recognizing the fundamental difference between material and immaterial dimensions. Finally, he summarizes by pinpointing the false assumption underlying all opposing arguments.

Grasp the concluding refutation
