Introduction to Audible Things and Two Research Topics
Al-Taftazani introduces audible things and lists two research topics on sound.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that audible things are the third type of perceptible phenomena and that two research topics—creation and differentiation by modality—define the study of sound in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Audible things: two research topics on sound, creation and differentiation by modality.

Al-Taftazani now turns to the third category of perceived phenomena: audible things. This brief passage sets the agenda for the discussion by announcing two specific research topics. Before diving into details, it is essential to grasp the overall structure and the two core issues that will be examined.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once. Then read the literal translation. Notice the concise structure: a classification followed by two numbered topics.
2
Identify the two research topics
The passage lists two research topics: (1) sound is created by God, and (2) differentiation of sound by modality. Make sure you know what each topic means.
The first topic addresses the createdness of sound, a key theological point. The second is about what distinguishes sound from other similar things.
3
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself why these topics matter. Sound is often connected to the issue of divine speech and its nature. Understanding whether sound is created or not has implications for the attributes of God.
This passage sets the stage for deeper arguments in the following lessons.

Definition of the Letter and Three Views on Its Nature
Taftazani defines the letter and presents three views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the technical definition of the letter (ḥarf) and the three scholarly views on what it actually is.

Core Idea
The letter is both a quality of sound and the sound itself—a combination is closest to the truth.

Taftazani begins by establishing what a letter is in the context of phonetics and kalam. He presents the definition and immediately notes that scholars differ on whether the letter is the quality, the sound, or both. This lesson unpacks that foundational definition and the three positions.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition and its components
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that the letter is defined as a quality that distinguishes sound from similar sounds in pitch and weight, with distinction occurring in the heard.
The definition hinges on the phrase 'tamayyuzan fi al-masmū‘'.
2
Identify the three views
The passage mentions three views on what the letter is: (1) Ibn Sina: the occurring quality, (2) a group of investigators: the sound itself, (3) some: the combination of quality and sound, called the closest to truth.
3
Reflect on the significance
Ask yourself why defining the letter matters for understanding speech and divine attributes. The commentary will explain that this is not just a linguistic but a theological concern.

تمايز الصوت بكيفية عما يماثله
قال : المبحث الثاني : قد تعرض للصوت كيفية بها يمتاز عما يماثله في الحدة والثقل تميزاً في المسموع وهو الحرف. يتميز عن صوت آخر يماثله في الحدة والثقل تميزاً في المسموع والحرف وهي تلك الكيفية العارضة في عبارة ابن سينا، وذلك الصوت المعروض في عبارة جمع من المحققين، ومجموع العارض والمعروض في عبارة البعض وكأنه أشبه بالحق وقيد المماثلة بالحدة والثقل أي الزيرية والبمية احترازاً عنهما، فإن كلا منهما يفيد تميز صوت عن صوت آخر تميزاً في المسموع لكن في صوتين يكونان مختلفين بالحدة والثقل ضرورة.
وقيد التميز بالمسموع احترازاً عن مثل الطول والقصر والطيب وغيره فإن التميز بها لا يكون تميزاً في المسموع لأنها ليست بمسموعة، إلا أن في كونها من الكيفيات نظراً. فالأولى : أنه احتراز عن مثل الغنة والبحوحة .
مع
بقى النظر في دلالة :قولنا تميزاً في المسموع على أن يكون ما به التميز مسموعاً، وفي أن الحدة والثقل من المسموعات بخلاف الغنة والبحوحة، والحق أن معنى التميز في المسموع ليس أن يكون ما به التميز مسموعاً، بل أن يحصل به التميز في نفس المسموع بأن يختلف باختلافه، ويتحد باتحاده كالحرف بخلاف مثل الغنة والبحوحة وغيرها. فإنها قد تختلف اتحاد المسموع وبالعكس، وما وقع في الطوالع من أن الحروف كيفيات تعرض للأصوات فيتميز بعضها عن البعض في الثقل والخفة كلام لا يعقل له معنى، وكأنه جعل قوله في الثقل متعلقاً بمحذوف أي عن البعض المماثل له في الثقل، وأراد بالخفة الحدة وترك قيد التميز في المسموع لشهرته، وكفى بهذا اختلالاً . والحق أن تعريف الحرف بما ذكر تعريف بالأخفى ، وكان المقصود مزيد تفصيل للماهية الواضحة عند العقل، وتنبيه على خصوصياتها .
The second section. Distinction of sound by a quality from that which resembles it. He said: The second section: A quality may occur to sound by which it is distinguished from what resembles it in pitch and weight, a distinction in the heard, and it is the letter. It is distinguished from another sound that resembles it in pitch and weight, a distinction in the heard, and the letter is that occurring quality in Ibn Sina's expression, and that sound which is the substrate in the expression of a group of investigators, and the combination of the occurrence and the substrate in the expression of some, and it is as if it is closer to the truth. And the qualification of resemblance with pitch and weight, i.e., highness and lowness, is a safeguard against them, for each of them yields a distinction of a sound from another sound, a distinction in the heard, but necessarily in two sounds that differ in pitch and weight. And the qualification of the distinction as in the heard is a safeguard against such as length, shortness, pleasantness, etc., for distinction by them is not a distinction in the heard because they are not heard, except that there is consideration whether they are qualities. So the better [view] is that it is a safeguard against such as nasality and hoarseness. Then there remains consideration of the implication of our saying 'a distinction in the heard' as to whether that by which the distinction occurs is heard, and that pitch and weight are among heard things unlike nasality and hoarseness. And the truth is that the meaning of distinction in the heard is not that that by which the distinction occurs is heard, but that distinction occurs by it in the heard itself, such that it differs with its difference and is uniform with its uniformity, like the letter, unlike nasality, hoarseness, and others. For they may differ while the heard is uniform, and vice versa. And what occurs in al-Tawali' that letters are qualities that occur to sounds by which some are distinguished from others in weight and lightness is speech that cannot be given a meaning, and as if he made his phrase 'in weight' attached to a deleted [phrase] i.e., from the other that resembles it in weight, and he intended by lightness pitch, and he omitted the qualification of distinction in the heard due to its fame, and this is sufficient as a defect. And the truth is that the definition of the letter with what was mentioned is a definition by what is more obscure, and the purpose was a further elaboration of the essence that is clear to the intellect, and a reminder of its specificities.

Commentary
This passage defines the letter (ḥarf) and explains its distinguishing quality. It begins by stating that a quality occurs to sound by which it is distinguished from another sound similar in pitch (ḥidda) and weight (thiqal), resulting in an auditory distinction. The author then notes differing views on what the letter is: Ibn Sīnā defines it as the occurring quality itself; a group of investigators define it as the sound that is the substrate; and some combine both, which the author suggests is closer to the truth. The qualification 'similar in pitch and weight' excludes sounds that differ naturally in these properties, as those distinctions are necessary and not what is meant. The qualification 'distinction in the heard' (tamayyuz fī al-masmū‘) excludes non-auditory qualities like length or pleasantness, although there is debate about whether these are qualities. The author prefers treating it as a safeguard against nasality (ghunna) and hoarseness (baḥḥa). Then a potential objection arises: does the phrase 'distinction in the heard' imply that the distinguishing quality itself must be heard? The author clarifies that pitch and weight are heard, unlike nasality and hoarseness, but the true meaning is that the distinction occurs within the heard sound itself—the sound varies with the letter and is uniform with it. Nasality and hoarseness can vary while the sound remains the same, so they are not true distinguishing qualities. The author sharply criticizes a definition from al-Ṭawāli‘ that speaks of distinction in weight and lightness, calling it meaningless and defective. Finally, he acknowledges that the given definition is not a proper definition from the unknown to the known but rather an elaboration of an essence already clear to the mind.

The Qualifications: Pitch, Weight, and Distinction in the Heard
Taftazani explains the two qualifications and addresses an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definition of the letter includes qualifications about resemblance in pitch/weight and distinction in the heard, and what they exclude.

Core Idea
The qualifications ensure that the letter is not confused with non‑auditory qualities like length or nasality.

The definition of the letter contains two important qualifications: 'resembling in pitch and weight' and 'distinction in the heard'. These serve to exclude other types of sound distinctions that are not relevant to letters. Taftazani explains each safeguard and addresses a potential objection about whether the distinguishing quality itself must be heard.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first qualification
Read the part about 'qayd al-mumāthala' and note that it excludes sounds that differ naturally in pitch or weight, because those differences are inevitable and not what defines a letter.
2
Understand the second qualification
Read about 'qayd al-tamayyuz bi al-masmū‘' and see how it excludes qualities like length, shortness, pleasantness, and also nasality and hoarseness.
There is a brief debate about whether these are qualities at all.
3
Grasp the objection and resolution
The passage then considers whether the phrase 'distinction in the heard' implies the distinguishing quality itself must be heard. Taftazani clarifies that it does not; rather, the distinction occurs in the heard sound itself, varying with the letter.
This resolves a potential logical issue.

Critique of al‑Ṭawāli‘ and Concluding Remarks
Taftazani critiques al‑Ṭawāli‘ and gives a final assessment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Taftazani's critique of the definition from al‑Ṭawāli‘ and his final assessment that the presented definition is an elaboration, not a proper definition from the unknown to the known.

Core Idea
The definition from al‑Ṭawāli‘ is meaningless and defective; the true definition is an elaboration of an already clear concept.

After laying out the definition and qualifications, Taftazani turns to critique an alternative definition from the work al‑Ṭawāli‘. He finds it incoherent and points out its flaws. Finally, he acknowledges that the definition he has provided is not a true definition in the formal sense but rather a detailed explanation of something already understood by the intellect.

Guided Path
1
Read the critique of al‑Ṭawāli‘
Read the Arabic text criticizing al‑Ṭawāli‘. Notice that Taftazani says the definition there is 'kalam lā yu‘qal lahu ma‘nā' (speech that cannot be given a meaning).
He identifies a grammatical issue: the phrase 'in weight' is left dangling without a proper referent.
2
Understand the defect
Al‑Ṭawāli‘ omitted the qualification of distinction in the heard and used 'lightness' instead of 'pitch'. Taftazani calls this a sufficient defect.
3
Grasp the final assessment
Taftazani concludes that the definition he has been discussing is actually a definition by what is more obscure (al‑akhfā), but its purpose is to elaborate an essence already clear to the intellect.
This shows the pedagogical value of the definition.

Commentary
This passage defines the letter (ḥarf) and explains its distinguishing quality. It begins by stating that a quality occurs to sound by which it is distinguished from another sound similar in pitch (ḥidda) and weight (thiqal), resulting in an auditory distinction. The author then notes differing views on what the letter is: Ibn Sīnā defines it as the occurring quality itself; a group of investigators define it as the sound that is the substrate; and some combine both, which the author suggests is closer to the truth. The qualification 'similar in pitch and weight' excludes sounds that differ naturally in these properties, as those distinctions are necessary and not what is meant. The qualification 'distinction in the heard' (tamayyuz fī al-masmū‘) excludes non-auditory qualities like length or pleasantness, although there is debate about whether these are qualities. The author prefers treating it as a safeguard against nasality (ghunna) and hoarseness (baḥḥa). Then a potential objection arises: does the phrase 'distinction in the heard' imply that the distinguishing quality itself must be heard? The author clarifies that pitch and weight are heard, unlike nasality and hoarseness, but the true meaning is that the distinction occurs within the heard sound itself—the sound varies with the letter and is uniform with it. Nasality and hoarseness can vary while the sound remains the same, so they are not true distinguishing qualities. The author sharply criticizes a definition from al-Ṭawāli‘ that speaks of distinction in weight and lightness, calling it meaningless and defective. Finally, he acknowledges that the given definition is not a proper definition from the unknown to the known but rather an elaboration of an essence already clear to the mind.

Commentary
This passage classifies letters (ḥurūf) into two main categories: sāmit (consonant) and muṣawwit (vowel). The vowels are further divided into maqṣūra (short vowels), which are the ḥarakāt (movements: fatḥa, kasra, ḍamma), and mamdūda (long vowels), which are the maddāt (the letters alif, wāw, yā’ when lengthened). Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quranic verse 'He created death and life' is used to argue for death's existence, and how the author refutes it by interpreting 'creation' as predestination or ellipsis.

Core Idea
A Quranic proof can be refuted by exegetical nuance.

Now that we have seen the Mu'tazila claim that death is an existent act, we turn to a scriptural argument: the verse 'He created death and life' (Quran 67:2). Proponents of death's existence say non-existence cannot be created, so death must be existent. The author refutes this by showing that 'creation' here can mean 'predestination' (taqdir), which applies to both existent and non-existent things, or it can be understood elliptically as 'creating the causes of death'. This lesson walks through that rebuttal.

2

3

Even if creation implies existence, the verse may be interpreted as 'creating the causes of death', with 'causes' omitted. This is a common figure of speech. This interpretation, though less apparent, is sufficient to refute the proof.

Notice how the author uses linguistic and exegetical principles to counter a proof. This shows the role of language in kalam arguments.

وجودياً، وعلى هذا ينبغي أن يحمل ما ذكره المعتزلة من أن الموت فعل من الله تعالى، أو من الملك يقتضي زوال حياة الجسم من غير جرح. واحترز بالقيد الأخير عن القتل وحمل الفعل على الكيفية المضادة مبني على أن المراد به الأثر الصادر عن الفاعل إذ لو أريد به التأثير على ما هو الظاهر لكان ذلك تفسيراً للإماتة لا للموت، وقد يستدل على كون الموت وجوداً بقوله تعالى (خلق) الموت والحياة. فإن العدم لا يوصف بكونه مخلوقاً، ويجاب بأن المراد بالخلق في الآية التقدير، وهو يتعلق بالوجودي والعدمي جميعاً، ولو سلم فالمراد بخلق الله تعالى الموت إحداث أسبابه على حذف المضاف وهو كثير في الكلام. مثل هذا وإن كان خلاف الظاهر كاف في دفع الاحتجاج.
Existent, and upon this should be carried what the Mu'tazila mentioned, that death is an act of God Almighty, or from the angel, which necessitates the removal of the body's life without a wound. And he guarded with the last restriction against killing, and the interpretation of the act as the opposite quality is based on the intention that what is meant by it is the effect emanating from the agent; for if what was intended by it was the effecting (al-ta'thir) as is apparent, that would be an interpretation of causing death (imata), not of death. And it may be argued for death being existent by His saying Exalted (He created) death and life. For non-existence cannot be described as being created. And it is answered that what is meant by creation in the verse is predestination (taqdir), and it pertains to both the existent and the non-existent. And if it is conceded, what is meant by God's creation of death is the bringing about of its causes, with the omitted object (al-mudaf) being ellipted, and this is frequent in speech. The like of this, even if contrary to the apparent meaning, is sufficient to repel the argument.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion about the ontological status of death: whether it is an existent entity or a pure non-existence. The author begins by referencing the Mu'tazila, who hold that death is an act of God or an angel that removes life from the body without a wound. The author notes a qualification that excludes killing, and then analyzes the Mu'tazila's interpretation: they treat 'act' (fi'l) as the quality opposite to life, meaning the effect (athar) produced by the agent. If 'act' were taken in its usual sense of effecting (ta'thir), it would define 'causing death' (imata), not 'death' itself. The author then presents a proof that death is existent: the Quranic verse 'He created death and life' (Quran 67:2). Since non-existence cannot be described as created, death must be existent. The rebuttal follows: 'creation' here means 'predestination' (taqdir), which can apply to both existent and non-existent things. Even if one accepts that creation implies existence, the verse may be understood elliptically as 'creating the causes of death', which is a common figure of speech. The author concludes that this interpretation, though a departure from the apparent meaning, is sufficient to refute the proof. The passage thus demonstrates a typical kalam dialectic: presenting a rational argument and then undermining it by appealing to language and exegesis.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Many assume that the verse 'He created death and life' definitively proves death is an existent entity.
Correction: The author shows that 'creation' can mean predestination, which applies to non-existence, or can be understood elliptically, thus the verse does not prove existence.
