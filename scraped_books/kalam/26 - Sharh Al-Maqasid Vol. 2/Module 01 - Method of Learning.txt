Objection and answer on defining time by argument
Objection that time's definition cannot be proven by argument, and the answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that even if a thing's true essence is not directly known, rational demonstration can prove some of its essential attributes, as illustrated with the example of time.

Core Idea
Arguments can prove attributes even when a thing's true essence is unknown.

This lesson presents a common objection in kalam: if time is defined by its essential attributes, how can we argue for that definition? The answer shows that demonstration can establish attributes even when the true reality (ḥaqīqa) is not directly perceived. The example of time clarifies that the initial concept (priority/posteriority) is not the full reality, and quantities can be proven by argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the structure: an objection (qīla) followed by an answer (qulnā).
The objection questions how definition can be sought by argument.
2
Identify the objection's assumption
The objection assumes that if something is defined by its essential attributes (dhātiyyāt), those attributes should be known without argument. Ask yourself why this assumption might be challenged.
3
Understand the answer's key point
The answer distinguishes between the initial concept of a thing and its true essence. Even if the true essence is not conceived, demonstration can prove some of its parts. For time, we only conceive it as that which gives priority/posteriority, but measurability is an essential attribute of its reality, not of the initial concept.
Examples like the substantiality of the soul and composition of body from matter and form illustrate the principle.

And here, time is not conceived except as something in virtue of which things are described by priority and posteriority, and measurability is not among the essential attributes of this concept, but rather among the essential attributes of its reality.
Source pages p. 189

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its answer. The objection (qīla) questions how one can provide a demonstrative proof (ḥujja) for a definition that details the essential attributes (dhātiyyāt) of time, since definition is supposed to be known a priori or by immediate conception. The answer (qulnā) is that even if the true essence of a thing is not directly conceived, one can still prove some of its parts or attributes through demonstration (burhān). Examples are the substantiality of the soul (jawhariyyat al-nafs) and the composition of the body from matter and form (hayūlā wa ṣūra), which are proven by argument. In the case of time, our initial concept is merely that it is something by which things are described as prior and posterior (qabliyya wa ba‘diyya); the property of being a quantity (al-miqdāriyya) is not essential to this initial concept but is an essential attribute of its true reality (ḥaqīqa). Hence, it is legitimate to prove that time has measurement through rational demonstration.

Then it reports al-Qadi al-Baqillani's view allowing the reverse (necessary becoming theoretical) based on the homogeneity of sciences, arguing that all sciences share the same essence and differ only by accidental relations. The passage then mentions a rebuttal: even if homogeneity is accepted, the types of knowledge still differ, so the argument fails. The majority rejected the reverse transformation absolutely because one cannot be free of necessary knowledge while attending to the object. Some restricted the impossibility to what is a condition for reasoning to avoid circularity. Finally, al-Imam al-Ghazali rejected both directions entirely, arguing that whether the concepts are sufficient or require reasoning is essential to the judgment, and essentials do not change. However, this reasoning is specific to primary propositions (awwaliyat) and may be subject to objection on its first premise.

Commentary
This passage discusses whether necessary (daruri) knowledge can be based on theoretical (nazari) knowledge. The author notes that the disagreement may be verbal. He presents two positions. The one who forbids (mani') argues: if necessary knowledge depends on theoretical knowledge, and theoretical knowledge depends on consideration (nazar), then necessary knowledge would depend on consideration, making it theoretical, which is a contradiction (khulf). The proper answer is to deny that conjunction being a branch of existence requires that knowledge of its impossibility depends on knowledge of existence; rather it depends on conceiving the two opposites in some way, which may not require theoretical consideration.

The author presents an objection, then the two opposing positions, their arguments, and finally a resolution suggesting the dispute may be verbal.

The objector says this is false because theoretical knowledge is impossible without some necessary basis. The author responds: This objection only prevents the complete transformation of all necessary knowledge into theoretical, not the possibility of some necessary knowledge changing individually.

Then the author frames the main issue: 'The scholars' disagreement on the dependence of the necessary upon the theoretical resembles being verbal.' He states that scholars differ over whether necessary knowledge can be based on theoretical knowledge.

The preventer (al-māniʿ) argues: If necessary knowledge depended on theoretical knowledge, which itself depends on reflection (naẓar), then necessary knowledge would depend on reflection and thus be theoretical, not necessary—a contradiction.

The permissive (al-mujawwiz) argues: Knowledge of the impossibility of two opposites coexisting (e.g., black and white) is necessary, but it depends on knowledge of the existence of those opposites, because coexistence is a branch of existence. The author then gives an answer: Denying that this knowledge is necessary is weak. Rather, the proper answer is to deny the premise: that being a branch of existence necessarily makes knowledge of impossibility depend on knowledge of existence. It depends only on a conception of the two opposites, which does not require reflection. However, sometimes a judgment independent of reflection may depend on reflection in conceiving its terms. Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the definitions of health (ṣiḥḥa) and disease (marad) as psychological qualities (kayfiyyāt nafsāniyya). The author first distinguishes between different types of causes, then addresses the nature of disease, stating that it is sometimes considered a firm quality (malaka) or a non-firm quality (ḥāla) opposite to health, and sometimes a privation of firm quality (ʿadam malaka). He notes that if they are opposites, both belong to the genus of psychological qualities. Then he presents Ibn Sina's definition of health from the Canon: 'a firm quality or a non-firm quality from which the actions proceed from the subject soundly.' He clarifies that health is a psychological quality whether well-established or not, and it is not limited to the well-established, contrary to some claims. He then discusses the distinction between ḥāla and malaka, explaining that the only difference is the accident of being well-established (rusūkh). He explains why Ibn Sina prioritized mentioning malaka over ḥāla: because malaka is more noble due to its firmness, and it is more prevalent in health. He also cites the Imām (likely al-Rāzī or al-Āmidī) who argued that malaka is preferred because there is no disagreement about it being health, unlike ḥāla, and because malaka is the goal of ḥāla, and the goal is prior in causality. The author then notes that this definition includes health of humans and other animals, but rejects the Imām's extension to plants because ḥāla and malaka are specifically psychological qualities pertaining to animal souls. He criticizes the definition from al-Shifāʾ as potentially repetitive unless interpreted as absolute quality. Next, he presents another definition from the Canon: health is a disposition (hayʾa) of the human body such that all actions proceed soundly. He explains this definition avoids circularity because health in actions is sensible while health in the body is non-sensible, and defining the non-sensible by the sensible is permissible. Finally, he addresses an objection concerning the phrasing 'the actions proceed from it' and 'from the subject.' He gives two answers: (1) health is an agent principle and the subject is a receiver, so actions proceed from the subject by means of the quality; (2) health is an intermediary cause for the subject's being a source of action, analogous to fire and heat. The passage concludes that health is a cause for the body to become a source of sound action.

He notes that if they are opposites, both belong to the genus of psychological qualities. Then he presents Ibn Sina's definition of health from the Canon: 'a firm quality or a non-firm quality from which the actions proceed from the subject soundly.' He clarifies that health is a psychological quality whether well-established or not, and it is not limited to the well-established, contrary to some claims. He then discusses the distinction between ḥāla and malaka, explaining that the only difference is the accident of being well-established (rusūkh). He explains why Ibn Sina prioritized mentioning malaka over ḥāla: because malaka is more noble due to its firmness, and it is more prevalent in health. He also cites the Imām (likely al-Rāzī or al-Āmidī) who argued that malaka is preferred because there is no disagreement about it being health, unlike ḥāla, and because malaka is the goal of ḥāla, and the goal is prior in causality. The author then notes that this definition includes health of humans and other animals, but rejects the Imām's extension to plants because ḥāla and malaka are specifically psychological qualities pertaining to animal souls. He criticizes the definition from al-Shifāʾ as potentially repetitive unless interpreted as absolute quality. Next, he presents another definition from the Canon: health is a disposition (hayʾa) of the human body such that all actions proceed soundly. He explains this definition avoids circularity because health in actions is sensible while health in the body is non-sensible, and defining the non-sensible by the sensible is permissible. Finally, he addresses an objection concerning the phrasing 'the actions proceed from it' and 'from the subject.' He gives two answers: (1) health is an agent principle and the subject is a receiver, so actions proceed from the subject by means of the quality; (2) health is an intermediary cause for the subject's being a source of action, analogous to fire and heat. The passage concludes that health is a cause for the body to become a source of sound action.

قال : وقد يقال : التخلخل والتكاثف للانفشاش والاندماج، وهما مداخلة الهواء بتباعد الأجزاء وضده، وقد يكون ازدياد المقدار بورود الماء المادة على نسبة طبيعية، وهو الورم أو عليها، لكن لا في جميع الأقطار وهو السمن ويقابله الهزال. يعني قد يراد بالتخلخل الانغشاش أي تباعد أجزاء الجسم بحيث تداخلها جسم غريب كالهواء، وبالتكاثف الانداماج أي تقارب الأجزاء بحيث يخرج ما بينها من الجسم القريب، وهما من قبل الوضع لرجوعهما إلى هيئة نسبة الأجزاء بعضها إلى البعض ثم لا يخفي أن هذا الانتقال بالنظر إلى الأجزاء حركة أينية، وأما بالنسبة إلى الكل فحركة في الكم على طريق النمو وإن لم يكن نمواً، وفي الوضع بحسب الداخل حيث تبدلت نسبة الأجزاء بعضها مع البعض، كما للفلك بحسب الخارج حيث تبدلت نسبتها إلى الأمور الخارجة. فإن قيل : فعلى الأول لا تنحصر الحركة في الكم في اعتبارات الأربعة . قلنا : لا كلام في عدم الانحصار وفي أن قولنا الانتقال من النقصان إلى الزيادة، لورود المادة نمو ليس على إطلاقه، وإلى هذا يشير قولنا وقد يكون ازدياد المقدار بورود المادة لا على تناسب طبيعي، وهو الورم أو على تناسب طبيعي لكن لا في جميع الأقطار وهو السمن، فإنه وإن كان ازدياد طبيعياً بانضياف مادة الغذاء إلى المغتذي كالنمو لكنه لا يكون في الطول على تلك النسبة ولا يختص بوقت معين ولا يكون له غاية ما يقصدها الطبع بخلاف النمو، ومقابل السمن هو الهزال، فيكون انتقاضاً طبيعياً لكن لا في جميع
He said: It may be said that rarefaction and condensation are for expansion and contraction, and they are the interpenetration of air by separation of parts and its opposite. And the increase of measure may be by the coming of water matter on a natural proportion, which is swelling, or on it but not in all dimensions, which is obesity, and its opposite is emaciation. That is, sometimes by rarefaction is meant expansion, i.e., the separation of the parts of the body such that a foreign body like air enters them, and by condensation is meant contraction, i.e., the coming together of the parts such that what is between them of the near body exits. Both are from the perspective of position because they revert to the configuration of the relation of the parts to each other. Then it is not hidden that this transition with respect to the parts is locomotion in space, but with respect to the whole it is motion in quantity along the path of growth even if not growth, and in position according to the interior where the relation of the parts to each other changes, as for the celestial sphere according to the exterior where their relation to external matters changes. If it is said: Then on the first case, motion in quantity is not limited to the four considerations? We say: There is no discourse about the lack of limitation, and that our saying 'the transition from deficiency to increase by the coming of matter is growth' is not absolute. And this is indicated by our saying: And the increase of measure may be by the coming of matter not on a natural proportion, which is swelling, or on a natural proportion but not in all dimensions, which is obesity. For even though it is a natural increase by the addition of food matter to the nourished like growth, it is not in length on that proportion, nor is it specific to a particular time, nor does it have an end intended by nature, unlike growth. And the opposite of obesity is emaciation, so it is a natural decrease but not in all...

Commentary
This passage defines alternative meanings of rarefaction and condensation as expansion and contraction involving air. It then classifies types of increase: growth (nmu) is natural proportional increase over time with a specific end; swelling (waram) is increase without natural proportion; obesity (siman) is increase on natural proportion but not in all dimensions. Emaciation is the opposite of obesity. The author then addresses an objection that motion in quantity would not be limited to the four types (increase, decrease, etc.), and responds that the concept of transition from deficiency to increase is not always growth. This shows the nuanced distinction.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Any increase in size or quantity is considered growth.
Correction: The passage shows that only natural increase in all dimensions with a specific time and end is growth; swelling and obesity are distinct types of increase.

Key Takeaway
True growth is a natural proportional increase in all dimensions with a specific time and end, unlike swelling and obesity which lack one or more of these attributes.
