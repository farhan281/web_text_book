The Four Arguments for the Emission Theory of Vision
Al-Taftazani enumerates the four arguments for the emission theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four arguments used to support the emission theory of vision and why they seemed plausible to its proponents.

Core Idea
Proponents of emission theory used empirical observations to argue that a ray leaves the eye.

Al-Taftazani begins by fairly presenting the arguments of those who hold that vision occurs by a ray emitted from the eye. These arguments are based on everyday experiences: differences in near and far vision, day-blindness and night-blindness, the clarity of the center of gaze, and subjective visual phenomena. Understanding these arguments is essential before seeing how they are refuted.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage of the four arguments
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow of the four arguments. Then read the translation carefully.
Notice each argument is introduced with 'أحدها', 'ثانيها', etc.
2
Identify each argument's reasoning
For each argument, identify the observation it relies on and the conclusion it draws about emission.
3
Consider the plausibility
Think about why each argument might seem convincing to someone who believes in emission. What everyday experience does it explain?
The first argument: weak-eyed see near better, strong-eyed see far better.
4
Note the structure
Observe that this is an enumeration. The author lists four distinct points before providing a response.
The structure helps in following the subsequent refutation.

Those who hold the emission theory also adduced arguments:

First: That one whose visual ray is weak perceives the near more correctly than the far, because the ray disperses in the far; and one whose visual ray is abundant with its thickness perceives the far more correctly, because motion over a long distance gives it fineness and clarity. If vision were by intromission, the situation would not differ.

Second: That the day-blind person sees at night but not during the day, because his visual ray, due to its scarcity, is dissolved during the day by the sun's ray, so he does not see, and it gathers at night and strengthens for vision; the night-blind is the opposite, because his visual ray, due to its thickness, does not become strong for vision unless the sun gives it fineness and clarity.

Third: That when a person looks at a paper on which he has written, nothing appears to him except the line at which he stares. This is only because the projection of the arrow of the cone of the ray is more correct in perception than its sides.

Fourth: That a person sees in darkness as if a light has separated from his eye and shone on his nose; and when he closes his eyes over a lamp, he sees as if radial lines are connected between his eyes and the lamp.

The answer concerning all of them is that they do not prove what is sought, namely that vision is by emission of a ray, but rather that there is light in the eye. And we do not deny that in the organs of vision there are luminous, radiating bodies called the visual spirit, from which an imaginary cone is traced between the eye and the visible object. The visible object is perceived by means of its angle at the crystalline lens; its motion intensifies when seeing the far, so its subtle part dissolves, and it needs refinement if thick, and condensation if weak, and becoming too fine. And from it, in the receptive counterpart, rays and lights are generated, whose strength is in that which is opposite the center of the eye, which is like the vertex of the imaginary cone. Due to the intensity of its illumination, the imprinted image in it is more apparent, and its perception is stronger and more complete. It is probable that this is what those who hold the emission theory mean metaphorically, as Ibn Sina stated; otherwise it is absolutely false. If what is meant is the reality of the ray, which is of the category of accidents, that is obvious [in its falsehood]; and if what is meant is a radiating body that moves from the eye to the visible object, then we are certain that it is impossible for a body to emerge from the eye and spread in an instant over half the sphere of the world, then when the eyelid closes, return to it, or cease to exist, then when it opens, another like it emerges, and so on; and that the radiating body should move without a compeller or will to all directions; and that it should penetrate the spheres and tear them to see the planets; and that it should not be disturbed by the blowing of winds, nor connect with other than the opposite, as in the case of sounds where winds deflect them to directions; and because it would necessitate that the moon be seen before the fixed stars by a time proportional to the difference in distance between them, but it is not so; rather the spheres with the planets in them are seen at once. Also, it would necessitate that what is in earthenware be seen due to the abundance of pores in it, as evidenced by seepage, unlike what is in glass or water. If vision of what is in them were through pores, it would have to be in proportion to them without the thing being seen as a whole. And by such proofs and indications...

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the emission theory of vision (شعاع). The author, al-Taftazani, first presents four arguments commonly adduced by proponents of the emission theory, then gives a comprehensive response.

The four arguments are:
1. Weak-eyed people see near objects better; strong-eyed see far objects better. This would not differ if vision were by intromission.
2. Day-blindness and night-blindness are explained by the strength or weakness of the visual ray in different lighting.
3. When reading, only the line stared at is clear, because the central ray of the cone of emission is stronger.
4. Subjective experiences like seeing light from one's eye in darkness or lines when closing eyes over a lamp suggest emission.

Al-Taftazani then answers: These arguments only prove that there is light in the eye, not that vision occurs by emission. He concedes that there are luminous bodies in the eye (الروح الباصرة) that produce an imaginary cone, but this is not the same as a ray leaving the eye. He cites Ibn Sina to suggest that emission theorists may speak metaphorically. If taken literally, the emission theory is absolutely false due to numerous absurd consequences:
- A body cannot emerge from the eye and spread instantly over the entire visible hemisphere, then return when the eyelid closes.
- It cannot move to all directions without a cause.
- It would contradict observational facts such as seeing celestial bodies simultaneously despite vast distance differences, and seeing through porous materials differently than expected.

Thus, the refutation is both logical and empirical.

Refutation of the Emission Theory and Its Absurd Consequences
Al-Taftazani refutes the emission theory with logical consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Taftazani refutes the emission theory by showing it leads to logical and empirical absurdities.

Core Idea
The emission theory is impossible because it violates basic physics and common observation.

Al-Taftazani now answers the four arguments collectively, conceding only that there is light in the eye. He then demolishes the literal emission theory by pointing out several impossible consequences: a body cannot emerge and spread instantly, cannot move without a cause, cannot penetrate celestial spheres, and would predict incorrect observations. This is a classic example of reductio ad absurdum.

Guided Path
1
Read the answer passage
Read the Arabic from 'والجواب عن الكل' to the end. Then read the translation. Notice the shift from concession to refutation.
The passage begins with a concession: the arguments only prove there is light in the eye.
2
Identify the positive account
Al-Taftazani admits there is a 'visual spirit' (الروح الباصرة) and an imaginary cone. Understand what he accepts vs. rejects.
He cites Ibn Sina to suggest that emission theorists may speak metaphorically.
3
List the absurd consequences
Find each consequence that Al-Taftazani lists as impossible if the ray were a literal body leaving the eye.
Examples: spreading instantly over half the world, returning when eyelid closes, moving without cause, etc.
4
Understand the reductio
Reflect on why each consequence is impossible and how they together refute the theory. This is a logical argument, not just empirical.
The refutation combines physical impossibility, contradiction with observation, and logical necessity.

The Common Sense and Responses to Objections
Refutes objections against the common sense by explaining the role of the soul and external senses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how scholars defend the existence of the common sense (al-ḥiss al-mushtarak) by explaining the soul's role in perception and the necessity of an integrating faculty for judgment between sensibles.

Core Idea
The common sense is the inner faculty that unites sensory impressions, making integrated judgment possible.

In this passage, the author addresses key objections to the common sense. The first objection argues that if the soul directly perceives both universals and particulars, no common sense is needed. The second, from Imam al-Razi, questions the brain's involvement in taste and touch. By responding, the author clarifies that the common sense serves as the final integrator of sensory data, reconciling bodily faculties with the soul's judgment.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Let the author's argument structure become clear.
Notice the two distinct objection-answer pairs.
2
Identify the first objection and answer
The first objection claims that if the soul perceives and judges between universals and particulars, then particular forms must be in the soul, eliminating the need for a common sense. The answer explains that particulars are imprinted in the external sense organs, not in the soul, requiring a shared instrument for judgment.
3
Examine the additional consideration
The text raises a potential counter: perhaps judgment occurs through the eye alone. The response argues that external senses are insufficient for judgment in both absence and presence, necessitating an inner sense.
This reinforces the need for the common sense.
4
Analyze the second objection and answer
Imam al-Razi objects that taste and touch are not in the brain, so the common sense cannot be there. The answer shows that the brain is involved: an affliction in the brain affects taste and touch, and the full process involves the tongue, nerves from the brain, and the common sense as the final integrator.
This solidifies the common sense as the seat of sensory integration.

Commentary
This passage presents a rigorous logical proof (the third proof in the series) that the rational soul (al-nafs al-nāṭiqa) cannot be in a body. The author (al-Taftazani in his Sharh al-Maqasid) frames it as a dilemma: if the soul is in the body, then its awareness (taʿaqqul) of the body would have to be either perpetual or never occur at all, because the soul's own presence in the body would either be sufficient for awareness or not. The consequent is false by self-evident experience (al-wijdān): we are sometimes aware of our bodies and sometimes not. The author then explains the entailment in detail. If presence itself suffices, then awareness should be constant, like the soul's awareness of itself and its essential attributes. But it is not constant. If presence does not suffice and an image (ṣūra) is needed, then the soul's awareness of the body would be occasional, requiring the acquisition of a new image of the body. But if the soul is material and located in the body, that new image would also be in the same body, leading to two images of the same thing in one matter, which is impossible. The author also clarifies the underlying epistemology: perception is not just a relation (iḍāfa) but requires the presence of a form from the perceived object in the perceiver. This argument is a standard kalam proof for the immateriality of the soul.

The conclusion: the immediate movers of the spheres are corporeal powers (like animal souls) acting under the direction of immaterial souls (like rational souls). The passage then records an objection: after granting the tripartite division of motion and the distinction between universal and particular perception, the opponent challenges the premise that what is desired by nature is necessarily fled from by nature. The answer to the objection is not provided here, leaving the argument open.

And if it is a specific relation, then why is that not sufficient for perception of universals without needing the occurrence of the form in the soul? In summary: perception has been allowed without the impression of an image in the perceiver. So why have you made that necessary in the perception of universals, while you say that perception is one meaning that differs according to the sense or the intellect?

Commentary
This passage presents a remaining problem and a final rebuttal. The problem: if the soul perceives particulars through a form in the instrument, either that form also occurs in the soul (which leads to the former objection of a particular image in an abstract soul) or it does not occur in the soul (only in the instrument, which is a part of the body). In the second case, we must inquire what state in the soul constitutes perception. It cannot be merely the existence of the thing or its form in matter. If it is a specific relation, then why is that not enough for universals? The conclusion: Since perception can occur without an image in the perceiver (as shown earlier), there is no need to require an image for universals, especially since perception is a single concept differing only by relation to sense or intellect. Thus the objection against the soul's perception of universals is invalid.

The Theological Warning: Philosophical Premises Not Universally Accepted
Al-Taftazani warns that the attributes rely on philosophical premises not accepted by theologians.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Taftazani's caution that the attributes of intellects discussed are based on philosophical premises that are not accepted by the theologians (mutakallimun).

Core Idea
The elaborate theory of intellects rests on philosophical assumptions that theologians reject.

After presenting the intricate philosophical attributes of intellects, al-Taftazani issues a crucial methodological caution. He reminds the reader that these conclusions are built upon premises from Greek philosophy that are not granted by the theological tradition. This warning situates the discussion within the broader conflict between falsafa and kalam, showing that the author is aware of the contested nature of these claims.

Guided Path
1
Read the final sentence of the passage
Read the Arabic: 'ثم إنك خبير بابتناء هذه الفروع على مقدمات فلسفية غير مسلمة عند المتكلمين، فلا حاجة إلى التنبيه'. This is the caution.
2
Identify what is being criticized
The 'branches' (furu') refer to the attributes just discussed. The 'philosophical premises' (muqaddimat falsafiyya) are the foundational ideas from Aristotle and Ibn Sina that underpin them.
3
Understand the theological stance
Theologians (mutakallimun) do not accept these premises, so they would not accept the conclusions. Thus, the whole discussion is conditional on a philosophical worldview.
4
Reflect on the purpose of this warning
Al-Taftazani is being careful to distinguish between what philosophers assert and what is doctrinally sound for Muslims. He does not need to argue further; he simply flags the issue.

Commentary
This passage is a list of attributes of intellects (al-'uqul) followed by a methodological caution. The author states that intellects are not preceded by matter and are free from it, unlike forms and accidents that reside in matter. Then three attributes are enumerated: (1) each intellect is a species confined to a single individual, meaning there is only one instance of each type, because multiplicity requires matter; (2) their perfections are actual, not potential, because potentiality is tied to matter; (3) they are intelligent of themselves and of all universals. The author explains self-intellection: the intellect's essence is present to itself, so no representation is needed. For intellection of others, because it is immaterial, it can grasp all universals without being hindered by matter. A proof is given: if an immaterial intellect can cognize, it can cognize with others because intellectual forms are mutually supportive. The validity of accompaniment does not depend on the intellect's substance already containing them, otherwise circularity would result. Therefore, since the immaterial actually exists, it actually cognizes all universals. The author then notes that these claims are based on philosophical premises not accepted by theologians, so no further rebuttal is necessary.
