The Mu'tazilite Definition of Body as Long, Wide, Deep
Al-Taftazani presents the Mu'tazilite definition of body and explains its justification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite definition of body (jism) as 'the long, wide, deep' and why it is considered a description by a particular characteristic rather than a true definition.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazilites define body not by genus and differentia but by its distinctive property of accepting three dimensions.

When the Mu'tazilites define a body as 'the long, the wide, the deep', they are not providing a logical definition in the Aristotelian sense. Instead, they are giving a description based on a necessary and comprehensive property. This passage from al-Taftazani unpacks the reasoning behind this definition, addressing potential objections and distinguishing it from the mathematical body.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that al-Taftazani uses a very condensed style with many technical terms.
2
Identify the definition and key terms
The Mu'tazilite definition is 'the long, wide, deep' (al-ṭawīl al-ʿarīḍ al-ʿamīq). Note that 'jawhar' (substance) and 'jism taʿlīmī' (mathematical body) are crucial for understanding why no mention of substance is needed.
The definition is not a ḥadd (true definition) but a rasm bi-l-khāṣṣa (description by particular characteristic).
3
Understand the justification for capacity
The definition refers to the capacity (qubūl) for these dimensions, not their actual existence. This explains why a sphere (which lacks actual lines) is still considered a body.
Alteration of dimensions, as in a candle melting, does not affect bodyness.
4
Examine the exclusion of intermediate composites
By including width and depth, the definition excludes composites that are arranged in only one or two dimensions, or have fewer atoms than the minimum for a body (8, 6, or 4, depending on the Mu'tazilite school).
This ensures only true bodies, not atoms or intermediate aggregates, are covered.
5
Reflect on the significance of denying the mathematical body
The Mu'tazilites do not posit a mathematical body (jism taʿlīmī) that is a quantity with three dimensions. If they did, the three dimensions would be a common accident requiring the mention of substance to exclude it. By denying it, the combination of dimensions becomes a particular characteristic of the natural body.
This is a key difference from other schools in kalām.

He said: (And according to the Mu'tazilites) it is the long, the wide, the deep. And this definition is by a necessary, comprehensive particular characteristic, based on the fact that the intended meaning is the acceptance of those dimensions absolutely. So their non-actuality does not harm, as in the sphere, nor their alteration with the remaining of bodyness, as in the candle, given that according to them this reverts to the arrangement of parts without positing quantities additional to bodyness. And they made it free from needing to mention substance (jawhar). As for the restriction of width and depth, it is a precaution against the composite that is an intermediary between the body and the indivisible atom (jawhar fard), such that its parts are arranged in one or two directions only, or their number is less than the minimum from which a body is composed, namely eight, six, or four according to their differences in that. The well-known among them regarding the definition of body is that it is the long, wide, deep, and there is no dispute among them that this is not a definition (hadd) but a description by a particular characteristic. And the basis for its being a particular characteristic is that they do not posit the mathematical body (jism ta'limi), which is a quantity possessing the three dimensions, so that these would be a common accident including it, thus needing to mention substance to avoid it. And the combination becomes a composite particular characteristic for the natural body, like 'the flying, birthing' for the bat. And it does not harm that substance is a genus, because the compound from the inside and outside is outside. It is possible that by 'long' is meant that which has length, i.e., the supposed extension first as an accident for it, so it does not include the mathematical body because these dimensions are its parts.

Commentary
This passage presents the Mu'tazilite definition of body (jism) as 'the long, wide, deep' (al-tawīl al-'arīḍ al-'amīq). Al-Taftazani explains that this definition is by a 'particular characteristic' (khāṣṣa) that is necessary and comprehensive, not a true definition (ḥadd) by genus and differentia. The Mu'tazilites argue that the definition refers to the capacity for these dimensions, not their actual existence, so a sphere lacking actual lines is still a body. They do not need to mention substance (jawhar) because they deny the mathematical body (jism ta'līmī) that would be a common accident subsumed under substance. Instead, their definition applies to the natural body. The restriction of width and depth excludes intermediate composites that are not full bodies. The passage also notes differences among Mu'tazilites regarding the minimum number of atoms (8, 6, or 4) for a body, but this is secondary to the definition itself.

The First Objection: Comprehensiveness and Necessity
Al-Taftazani presents the first objection and its answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Taftazani refutes the objection that the Mu'tazilite definition of body is not comprehensive or necessary.

Core Idea
The definition refers to the capacity for dimensions, not their actual possession.

Al-Taftazani presents a critique of the Mu'tazilite definition of body, arguing that its particular characteristic is neither comprehensive nor necessary. He then provides a nuanced answer, clarifying that the definition concerns potential dimensions, not actual ones. This lesson unpacks that exchange.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first part of the passage where the objection is raised: the characteristic is not comprehensive (no line in sphere, no surface in infinite body) and not necessary (candle's dimensions change).
Notice the two separate prongs: comprehensiveness and necessity.
2
Understand the answer
Read al-Taftazani's answer: the definition means capacity (qubūl) and possibility (imkān) of dimensions, which is comprehensive and necessary. Changes only affect particular dimensions, not the capacity.
The answer hinges on reinterpreting 'particular characteristic' as potential, not actual.
3
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this distinction matters for defining a body. It saves the definition from the counterexamples.
This clarifies that the definition is about inherent property, not accidental state.

واعترض بأن الخاصة إنما تصلح للتعريف إذا كانت شاملة لازمة. وهذه ليست كذلك أما الشمول فلأنه لا خط بالفعل في الكرة ولا سطح فيما يعرض من الجسم الغير المتناهي فإنه جسم وإن امتنع بدليل من خارج بخلاف ما إذا فرض أربعة لیست بزوج. فإن الزوجية من لوازم الماهية ، وأما اللزوم فلأن الشمعة المعينة قد يجعل طولها تارة شبراً وعرضها أصابع، وتارة ذراعاً وعرضها إصبعاً، فيزول ما فيها من الأبعاد مع بقاء الجسمية . وأجيب بعد تسليم أن انتفاء الخط والسطح لفعل يستلزم عدم اتصاف الجسم بالطول والعرض والعمق . بأن المراد قبول تلك الأبعاد وإمكانها، وهذه خاصة شاملة لازمة على أن ما ذكر من زوال مقدار وحدوث آخر مما لا يثبت له عند المتكلمين، بل الجواهر الفردة هي التي تنتقل من طول إلى عرض . ولو سلّم فالمراد مطلق الأبعاد وهي لازمة، وإنما الزوال للخصوصيات . فإن قيل : على تقدير نفي المقادير، فالطويل خاصة للجسم، وعلى تقدير إثباتها فالجوهر الطويل، فأي حاجة إلى ذكر العرض والعمق. قلنا : إنما يصح ذلك لو كان منقسم جسماً حتى المؤلف من جزءين، وهم لا يقولون بذلك بل عند النظام أجزاء كل جسم غير متناهية، وعند الجبائي أقلها ثمانية، بأن يوضع أربعة بحيث يحصل مربع ثم فوقها أربعة كذلك، وعند أبي الهزيل ستة ، بأن يوضع ثلاثة ثم فوقها ثلاثة، وقيل أربعة بأن يوضع جزءان، وبجنب أحدهما في سمت آخر فرد واحد ، وفوق أحد الثلاثة جزء آخر، وإنما لم يفرض بالثلاثة على وضع المثلث والثالث على ملتقاهما بحيث يحصل مكعب لأن جواز ذلك عندهم في حيز المنع لاستلزامه الانقسام على ما سيجيء : وبالجملة فالجوهر المركب الذي يكون عدد أجزائه أقل من أدنى ما يصح تركب
It was objected that the particular characteristic is only suitable for definition if it is comprehensive and necessary. And this is not so. As for comprehensiveness, because there is no actual line in the sphere, nor surface in what appears from the infinite body—for it is a body, even if impossible by an external proof—unlike if four is supposed not to be even, for evenness is among the necessities of the quiddity. And as for necessity, because a particular candle may sometimes have its length a span and its width fingers, and sometimes a cubit and its width a finger, so the dimensions in it vanish while bodyness remains. And it was answered, after conceding that the non-existence of actual line and surface entails that the body is not described by length, width, and depth, that the intended meaning is the acceptance of those dimensions and their possibility. And this is a comprehensive, necessary particular characteristic, given that what was mentioned of the disappearance of one quantity and the occurrence of another is not established according to the theologians; rather, the individual atoms are what transfer from length to width. And if it is conceded, then the intended meaning is absolute dimensions, which are necessary, and the disappearance is only of particularities. Then it was said: On the supposition of negating quantities, then 'long' is a particular characteristic for the body; and on the supposition of affirming them, then it is the 'long substance'. So what need is there to mention width and depth? We say: That would only be correct if a body were divisible up to [a compound of] two parts, but they do not say that. Rather, according to al-Naẓẓām, the parts of every body are infinite; according to al-Jubbā'ī, its minimum is eight, by placing four so that a square results, then four above them likewise; according to Abū al-Hudhayl, six, by placing three then three above them; and it was said four, by placing two parts, and beside one of them in another direction one single, and above one of the three another part. And the reason they did not suppose three in a triangular arrangement and the third at their meeting point to produce a cube is that permissibility of that according to them is in the realm of prevention because it necessitates division, as will come. In summary, the composite substance whose number of parts is less than the minimum from which a body can be composed...

Commentary
This passage presents two objections to the Mu'tazilite definition of body and their answers. The first objection is that the particular characteristic (khāṣṣa) used in the definition is not truly comprehensive (shāmila) nor necessary (lāzima). The objection argues that a sphere has no actual line, so it lacks length, and an infinite body has no surface, so it lacks width/depth. Also, a candle's dimensions change while its bodyness remains, showing the characteristic is not necessary. The answer clarifies that the definition refers to the capacity (qubūl) and possibility (imkān) of these dimensions, not their actuality. This makes the characteristic comprehensive and necessary. The change of actual dimensions is merely change of particularities, not of the capacity. The second objection questions why width and depth need to be mentioned if length alone suffices, since either quantities are negated (making 'long' a characteristic of body) or affirmed (making it a characteristic of substance). The answer argues that this logic would only apply if bodies could be composed of two atoms, but Mu'tazilites differ on the minimal number—al-Naẓẓām says infinite parts, al-Jubbā'ī says 8, Abū al-Hudhayl says 6, and some say 4. This diversity shows that width and depth are necessary to exclude intermediate composites.

The Second Objection: Why Mention Width and Depth?
Al-Taftazani presents the second objection and its answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection regarding the redundancy of mentioning width and depth, and al-Taftazani's response about the minimal composition of bodies.

Core Idea
Width and depth are necessary to exclude intermediate composites that are not yet bodies.

The second objection questions why the definition includes both width and depth if length alone suffices. Al-Taftazani's answer delves into the Mu'tazilite disagreements on the minimal number of atoms required to form a body, showing the need for all three dimensions to distinguish bodies from smaller composites.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the second objection: if quantities are negated, 'long' is a characteristic of body; if affirmed, it is a characteristic of substance. So why mention width and depth?
The objection assumes that length alone could define body.
2
Understand the answer's logic
Al-Taftazani says that would only be correct if a body could be composed of two parts, but Mu'tazilites differ on the minimal number. He then lists various views (infinite, eight, six, four).
The diversity shows that without width and depth, intermediate composites might be misclassified as bodies.
3
Reflect on the significance of the minimal parts
Consider why the minimal number matters: it shows that a body requires all three dimensions to exclude smaller aggregates.
This argument reinforces the necessity of including all three dimensions in the definition.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of al-Nazzam's view that accidents like blackness are composed of self-subsistent atoms. Taftazani first notes that al-Nazzam calls these accidents 'substances' or even 'bodies', agreeing with al-Najjar but differing from the mainstream. The standard argument against such a view is that an accident cannot subsist by itself; it must be supported by a substance. Additionally, if atoms are all similar, then bodies composed of them would be similar, contradicting observed differences. Taftazani points out that this argument fails against al-Nazzam because he denies that bodies endure (baqa') as commonly understood. Al-Nazzam's own position is that each kind of accident is composed of a unique type of atom, so atoms differ across species. Taftazani then considers and rejects the dialectical answer of simply denying the similarity of atoms, as it only works as a forced argument (ilzam). The preferred approach is to deny that bodies differ by essence; rather, differences arise from accidents caused by God's will. Al-Nazzam's view, however, makes difference essential to bodies. Taftazani then warns that if one tries to escape by making accidents part of the body's reality, it leads to a fatal problem: since accidents (according to al-Nazzam's own admission) do not endure, any body containing them would also not endure. Taftazani cites al-Nazzam's own statement that 'accidents do not endure, and atoms endure' and concludes that al-Nazzam should have said 'bodies endure' if he meant to include the body as a composite of atoms and accidents.

He refers to what some theosophers (al-muta'allihin) of the philosophers went to, and which was attributed to the ancients, that between the world of the sensible and the world of the intelligible there is an intermediary called the world of images ('ālam al-mithāl). It is neither in the complete abstraction of the incorporeal entities (al-mujarradāt) nor in the mixture of material things (al-mādiyyāt). In it, for every existent among the incorporeal entities, bodies, and accidents—even movements, rests, positions, shapes, tastes, and smells—there is a self-subsisting exemplar, suspended not in matter or place, appearing to the sense with the help of a medium such as a mirror, imagination, water, air, and the like. It may pass from one medium to another, and it may cease, as when the mirror or imagination is corrupted or when the facing or imagination is removed. In summary: it is a vast, infinite world that parallels the sensory world in the perpetual motion of its ideal spheres, the acceptance of its elements and compounds of the effects of the motions of its spheres, and the illuminations of the intellectual world. This is what the ancients said—that in existence there is a quantitative world other than the sensory world, whose wonders are endless and whose duration is incalculable. Among those cities are Jābalqā and Jābarṣā, two great cities each having a thousand gates, containing countless creatures of this kind. From this world come the angels, jinn, devils, and ghouls, because they belong to the category of images, or because they are the separate rational souls which appear in it. By means of it, the incorporeal entities appear in various forms—beautiful or ugly, subtle or dense, etc.—according to the preparedness of the recipient and the agent. Upon this they founded the matter of bodily resurrection, for the ideal body (al-badan al-mithālī) in which the soul acts has the same status as the sensory body in that it possesses all external and internal senses, so it experiences pleasure and pain from physical pleasures and pains. Also, it consists of suspended luminous forms in which is the bliss of the blessed, and dark forms in which is the torment of the wretched. Likewise is the matter of dreams and many perceptions, for all that is seen in dreams or imagined while awake, even what is witnessed in illnesses and when fear predominates, etc., of quantitative forms that have no reality in the sensory world—all of it comes from the world of images. And likewise many of the wonders and extraordinary events, as it is related of some saints (awliyā') that while residing in his town, he was among those present at the Sacred Mosque during the days of Hajj, and that he appeared from some walls of the house, or emerged from a house with locked doors and windows, and that he brought certain persons or fruits or other things from a distant place in a short time, etc. Those who affirm this world—some of them claim intuitive knowledge (al-mukāshafa) and sound experiments, and others argue that what is witnessed of those particular forms in mirrors and the like is neither sheer non-existence, nor from the material world (as is obvious), nor from the intellectual world because they have magnitude and are not imprinted in the cerebral parts (due to the impossibility of imprinting the large in the small). But since the claim is lofty and the suspicion is weak, as stated earlier, the verified scholars (al-muḥaqqiqūn) among the philosophers and theologians did not pay attention to it. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a combined explanation and refutation of the concept of the 'World of Images' (ālam al-mithāl). The author first attributes this theory to some theosophers (al-muta'allihin) and to the ancients. He defines it as an intermediate realm between the sensible and intelligible worlds, distinct from both pure incorporeality and material mixture. In this world, every existent has a self-subsisting exemplar that appears to the senses via a medium. The description includes detailed features: it is parallel to the sensory world, containing celestial spheres, elements, cities like Jābalqā and Jābarṣā, and from it come angels, jinn, and other beings. It explains apparitions in dreams, illnesses, and miracles of saints. The author then presents the arguments of its proponents: they rely on mystical experience and a logical argument that the images seen in mirrors are neither nothing nor material nor intellectual. However, the author dismisses the whole theory by stating that the claim is lofty and the argument is weak (shubha wāhiya), and therefore the verified scholars (al-muḥaqqiqūn) among both philosophers and theologians ignored it. He ends with 'And God knows best', indicating his rejection. The passage thus serves to inform the reader of this theory while clearly marking it as untenable.
