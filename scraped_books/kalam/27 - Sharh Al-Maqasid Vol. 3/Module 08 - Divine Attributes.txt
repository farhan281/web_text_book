First Proof: God's Power to Separate Parts
First of three arguments based on God's power over opposites to prove indivisible atoms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the first argument for the existence of the indivisible atom, based on God's power over opposites.

Core Idea
God's power to separate parts completely proves indivisible atoms must exist.

This lesson presents the first of three classical Kalam arguments for atomism. The argument uses a thought experiment: if God can completely separate the parts of a body, then there must be ultimate indivisible units. By following the logical steps—from God's omnipotence to the contradiction of divisible parts—you will see why early Muslim theologians affirmed the existence of the atom.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to understand the flow.
Notice the word 'خلف' (contradiction) at the end.
2
Identify the key premises
Identify three parts: (1) God's ability to replace union with separation, (2) the basis of equal relation to opposites, (3) the inference to the indivisible part.
3
Trace the contradiction argument
Follow the khalf reasoning: if the part were divisible, union would remain, contradicting the assumption of complete separation.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Consider the broader significance
Reflect on why this argument matters for the atomistic worldview and how it supports the existence of ultimate particles.
This argument does not require actual separation, only logical possibility.

The first: that God Most High is able to create in the parts of the body, in place of their union, separation, such that no union remains at all. This is because the relation of power to two opposites is equal. And when separation occurs, the part that does not divide (al-juzʾ alladhī lā yatajazzā) is established, for if it were divisible, the union would remain — this is a contradiction.

Commentary
This passage presents the first of three arguments (wujūh) in support of the existence of the indivisible atom, based on the principle that division requires the actual existence of parts. The argument is: God is all-powerful and can replace the union (ijtimāʿ) of the parts of a body with separation (iftirāq) such that no union remains. Since God's power relates equally to opposites, He can cause separation. If separation occurs, then there must be parts that are not further divisible, because if those parts were themselves divisible, the union would not entirely disappear (since each divisible part still contains smaller parts that could remain united). The last part 'this is a contradiction' (khalf) means that assuming the part is divisible leads to the preservation of union, contradicting the assumption of complete separation. Therefore, indivisible parts must exist.

Refutation of the Objection on Hylomorphism
Refutes the objection that matter cannot exist by denying that shared attributes imply similarity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the objection against the existence of prime matter (hayula) and the response that sharing a necessary attribute does not necessitate similarity.

Core Idea
Sharing an attribute does not make two things similar — only essential properties count.

In this passage, Sanusi presents a powerful objection against the existence of prime matter (hayula) and then refutes it. The objection argues that matter must either be spatially extended or not, both leading to absurdities. The response clarifies the limits of similarity (tamathul) and shows that sharing a necessary attribute does not entail being similars.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its two horns
First, read the objection: if matter exists, it is either extended or not. Both are impossible. Understand why each horn is problematic.
The first horn (non-extended) is impossible because corporeality cannot inhere in non-extended substratum.
2
Analyze the consequence of independent extension
If matter is independently extended, it shares the most specific property of the self (essential spatial extension) with body, making them similars. This leads to several absurdities listed in the text.
The absurdities include impossibility of coexistence, inability to distinguish locus and inherence, and infinite regress.
3
Examine the response and its key points
The response denies that independent spatial extension is the most specific property of the self. Even if it were, similars are equal only in essential necessary properties, not accidental ones. Therefore, sharing that attribute does not necessitate similarity.
This response saves the possibility of matter being a distinct substance from body.

It may be by inherence in it, that sharing in the necessary attribute does not necessitate similarity. Reference to an objection presented by the Imam, its formulation: if hylomorphy (al-hayula) existed, then either it is spatially extended (mutahayyiza) or not; both are impossible. As for the second, because it is impossible for corporeality (al-jismiyya), which is specific to space and direction, to inhere in what is not spatially extended at all; for this reason there is no doubt about the impossibility of some immaterial entities being loci for bodies. As for the first, because its spatial extension (tahayyuzuha) is either by way of independence (al-istiqlal) or dependence (al-tabaiyya). On the first, it follows that it and corporeality are two similars (mithlayn) because they share the most specific property of the self (akhass sifat al-nafs), namely spatial extension by essence (al-tahayyuz bi al-dhat), so it is impossible for them to coexist, because of the impossibility of the conjunction of two similars; and that one of them should be distinguished as locus (mahalliyya) and the other as inhereing (haliyya), because the rule for similars is one; and that corporeality should be distinguished by need for matter (al-madda); rather, either both are independent, so corporeality cannot inhere in matter, or both are dependent, so matter would have another matter and an infinite regress would result. On the second, it follows that matter is an attribute (sifa) of corporeality, inhering in it, not the opposite, because the meaning of inherence (al-hulul) is dependence in spatial extension, and because if the opposite were allowed, it could be said that the body is an attribute of color, inhering in it. The answer: that its spatial extension not being independent does not necessitate its being an attribute and inhering, because it is possible that this is due to the fact that the inherence of corporeality in it is a condition for its spatial extension; and we do not accept that everything whose individuation is conditioned by something is an attribute of that thing, inhering in it—rather, sometimes the opposite is the case. Moreover, sharing in independent spatial extension does not necessitate their similarity, for we do not accept that this is the most specific property of the self. And even if it were granted, similars are only equal in the necessary properties of the essence (lawazim al-mahiyya), not in every necessary property, because it is possible that it pertains to accidents (al-awarid). According to the philosophers (al-hukama'), substance (al-jawhar) may be spatially extended by essence or not spatially extended at all, such as immaterial substances (al-jawahir al-mujarrada). Thus it is derived from what is mentioned in Sharh al-Mawaqif in the introduction to universal matters and the discussion of substance and accident. The author of al-Muhakamat said: The spatially extended (al-mutahayyiz) is of three types: either it is spatially extended independently, like form (al-sura) and body; or it is spatially extended dependently, either by way of its inherence in another, like accidents (al-awarid), or by way of the inherence of another in it, like al-hubut (the substratum?) — for it is individuated by the condition of the inherence of form in it.
Source pages p. 64

Commentary
This passage presents a full objection-answer unit and then a concluding classification. It begins by introducing a refutation: sharing in a necessary attribute does not imply similarity. Then it reports an objection raised by the Imam (likely Fakhr al-Din al-Razi or another authority): if hylomorphy (matter) exists, it must either be spatially extended or not; both options are impossible. The impossibility of not being extended is explained by the fact that corporeality, which is essentially tied to space and direction, cannot inhere in something non-extended. The impossibility of being extended is then argued: if matter is extended, it is either independently or dependently. If independently, matter and body are similars (mithlayn) because they share the most specific property of the self—essential spatial extension. This leads to several absurdities: they cannot coexist (since similars cannot coexist), they cannot be distinguished as locus and inhereing, and a regress arises. If dependently, matter would be a quality in body, which is absurd and reverses the proper relation. The answer rejects the premise that independent spatial extension is the most specific property of the self. It argues that even if it were, similars are equal only in essential necessary properties, not in all properties; some properties are accidental. The passage then shifts to a classification from the philosophers: substance may be essentially extended or not (e.g., immaterial substances). The spatially extended is of three types: independent (form and body), dependent by inherence in another (accidents), and dependent by inherence of another in it (a substratum like hylé).

Classification of Spatial Extension
Classifies spatially extended entities into three types and notes that immaterial substances are non-extended.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three categories of spatially extended entities: independent, dependent by inherence in another, and dependent by inherence of another.

Core Idea
Spatial extension can be essential or derived; immaterial substances are non-extended.

After completing the refutation, Sanusi presents the philosophers' classification of spatial extension (tahayyuz). This taxonomy helps clarify the nature of substances and accidents. He also notes that some substances, like immaterial ones, are not spatially extended at all.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish independent vs. dependent spatial extension
First, note the fundamental division: some entities are spatially extended by themselves (bi al-istiqlal), others only dependently (bi al-tabaiyya). Independent examples are form and body.
Independent extension means the entity's own essence entails occupation of space.
2
Identify the two types of dependent extension
Dependent extension has two subtypes: by inherence in another (like accidents inhering in a body) and by being a locus for another (like hyle/hubut, which is extended only because form inheres in it).
The second subtype is often discussed in the context of prime matter.
3
Contrast with non-extended substances
Note that according to the philosophers, some substances (al-jawahir al-mujarrada) are not spatially extended at all. This shows that extension is not a necessary property of all substances.
Immaterial substances like intellects are non-extended.

The Truth about Substantial Forms and the Human Soul
States the truth that substantial forms are not proven and discusses the human soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author argues that the hierarchy of substantial forms is not proven and how this relates to the rational soul.

Core Idea
The proof of a hierarchy of substantial forms is not established; the rational soul is a separate, simple substance.

This passage presents the author's considered position on substantial forms. He begins by affirming what is known with certainty—that different things like water and fire share corporeality yet differ in reality—but then argues that the further claim of a layered hierarchy of forms within bodies remains unproven. He also refutes a key argument for such forms and concludes with a reference that suggests the human form has a special, instantaneous existence from the world of command.

Guided Path

How does your understanding of the greatness of Allah expand when you consider that He created such lofty beings? Are you in awe of Him, or have you become complacent?
