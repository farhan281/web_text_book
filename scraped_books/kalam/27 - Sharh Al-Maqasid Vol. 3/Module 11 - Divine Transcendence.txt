The Shortest Argument for Matter and Its Rebuttals
Sanusi presents the shortest proof for matter and two refutations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the shortest rational argument for the necessity of matter for forms and the two key refutations offered against it.

Core Idea
The shortest argument for matter is refuted by questioning essential implications.

Sanusi presents a compact argument that attempts to prove that bodily forms require matter, but then immediately provides two sharp refutations. This lesson walks through the argument and its rebuttals, showing how careful reasoning about essence and accidental properties unsettles the proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the overall flow of the argument and refutations.
Notice the structure: first the argument, then two separate rebuttals.
2
Identify the two premises of the argument
The argument consists of two parts: (1) if forms were abstract they would be self-sufficient and cannot inhere; (2) forms entail imaginary division leading to separative division and matter. Identify each premise.
3
Understand the two refutations
The first refutation questions whether the essence of a form necessarily implies abstraction or inherence; the second refutation denies the implication from imaginary to separative division. Study how each refutation undermines the argument.
The second refutation refers to a previous discussion on division.

The first rebuttal says that the essence of a form might not entail abstraction or inherence; these could be due to external factors. The second rebuttal denies that imaginary divisibility necessarily leads to separative divisibility, referring to a previous discussion. The structure is: argument, then two separate objections.

Bodily Form Necessitates Shape and Matter
Sanusi presents the famous proof for matter from form and shape.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the famous kalam argument that bodily form necessarily leads to shape and shape necessarily leads to matter, and why shape requires matter.

Core Idea
Shape is a boundary; boundaries need matter to exist.

Sanusi presents one of the most celebrated arguments in kalam: that the mere existence of a body with a form implies it has boundaries (shape), and having boundaries cannot occur without a material substrate. This argument, though abstract, establishes a fundamental component of physical reality—matter—through pure rational analysis.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Let the argument's structure become clear.
2
Understand the first premise
Bodily form necessitates shape, defined as a configuration with a boundary. Sanusi references the finitude of extensions.
Focus on the definition of shape.
3
Understand the second premise
Shape necessitates matter. The argument gives two alternatives if shape were not due to matter: either uniform shapes from mere extension, or diversity from an external agent requiring connection/separation and receptivity/affection—all impossible without matter.
4
Consider the dilemma
Sanusi shows that both alternatives lead to contradictions, so shape must involve matter. This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
Notice the logical structure.
5
Conclude the necessity of matter
Thus, from bodily form we deduce the existence of matter. This is the famous proof.

As for the second: because the attainment of shape, if it were not by participation of matter and matter had no role in it, then either it would be by mere extensional nature, leading to equality of bodies in shapes, or due to an external agent, then the difference of magnitudes and shapes depends on connection and separation and on receptivity and affection, and it has already been established that that is impossible without matter.

Commentary
This passage presents the most famous argument for the necessity of matter. It has two premises: (1) bodily form necessitates shape, meaning a configuration with boundaries; this is supported by the future discussion on the finitude of extensions. (2) Shape necessitates matter. The reasoning: if shape occurred without matter's participation, either it would arise from mere extension, which would make all bodies equal in shape, or from an external agent, which would require differences in magnitudes and shapes to depend on connection/disconnection and receptivity/affection, all of which are impossible without matter. Thus matter is necessary.

The Quranic Proof and the Nature of Simples
The author presents Quranic proof and distinguishes compounds from simples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quranic verses about perishing are used as a proof for annihilation and why simple substances can only perish through complete non-existence.

Core Idea
Simples cannot dissolve; they require absolute annihilation.

The author begins by citing Quranic verses that declare everything perishing. But what does 'perishing' actually mean for substances that have no parts to dissolve? This lesson clarifies the crucial distinction between compounds and simples in the context of annihilation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic proof
Read the verses cited by the author: 'Everything is perishing except His Face' and 'Everyone upon it will pass away.' Notice how these are general statements.
The author uses these as evidence for the possibility of annihilation.
2
Distinguish compounds and simples
The author explains that for compounds, perishing can occur through dissolution of composition or removal of forms. For simples (individual atoms or matter-form composites), perishing is only conceivable as complete non-existence.
This distinction is key to understanding the argument.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this distinction matters: it shows that the Quranic verses apply to all things, including the simplest parts of the universe, and their annihilation must be absolute.

The author contrasts the views of an-Nazzam and al-Karamiyya on annihilation and proposes a distinction.

Beliefs about permanence determine views on annihilation.

How did they arrive at these positions? The author also provides a possible distinction based on the nature of accidents and substances.

Understand the shared premise

Contrast the two positions
An-Nazzam accepted the proof, concluding that bodies do not persist (they are annihilated). Al-Karamiyya insisted that bodies must persist, so they denied annihilation is possible.
Notice how they prioritize different assumptions.

Examine the author's distinction
The author suggests a distinction: accidents depend on substances and are interdependent, but substances can be preserved by God through successive accidents or annihilated directly.
