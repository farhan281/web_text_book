Circular Definitions of Necessary, Contingent, and Impossible
Al-Jurjani defines wujub, imkan, imtina' and demonstrates their circularity, then resolves it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definitions of wujub, imkan, and imtina' appear circular and how they are reconciled as harmless verbal definitions.

Core Idea
The three kalam categories are mutually defining, but their circularity is harmless.

Al-Jurjani begins by presenting the standard definitions of the necessary, contingent, and impossible. He then demonstrates that each definition relies on the other two, creating an apparent logical circle. This raises a critical question: are these definitions valid? The passage explores this problem and offers a resolution based on the innate character of these concepts.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to grasp the definitions of wujub, imkan, and imtina'.
Notice how each definition references the other terms.
2
Identify the circularity
Trace how each concept is defined by negating or affirming another, forming a cycle. See that the necessary is defined by the impossible, the impossible by the necessary, and the contingent by both.
This circularity is 'apparent' (zahir) and needs resolution.
3
Understand the resolution
The passage explains that these are not real definitions (haqiqiyya) but verbal definitions (lafziyya) for concepts that are already intuitively known. The innate clarity of wujub makes it the best starting point.
Circularity in verbal definitions is harmless because they only assign words to known meanings.

Commentary
This passage defines the three fundamental concepts of kalam: the necessary (wajib), the contingent (mumkin), and the impossible (mumtani'). The text first demonstrates that each is defined circularly in terms of the others. For example, the necessary is defined as that whose non-existence is impossible, the impossible as that whose existence is necessary, and the contingent as that which is neither necessary nor impossible. The author then notes that if these definitions are considered real definitions (ta'rifat haqiqiyya) aiming to reveal the essence, they are circular and thus invalid. However, the commentator clarifies that there is a qualification: the conceptions of these three are innate (daruriyya), though they differ in clarity, with wujub being the clearest. Therefore, alerting to the meanings of imkan and imtina' through wujub is more fitting. The remainder of the passage addresses an objection: if these definitions were real, they would require that the concepts be intuitive, leading to absurd consequences. The response is that these are not real definitions but verbal definitions (ta'rifat lafziyya) intended simply to affirm the linguistic usage of the terms for already known meanings, so circularity is harmless.

Defending the Circle: Objections and Replies
Al-Jurjani addresses objections to the charge of circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Jurjani defends the charge of circularity in defining the necessary, contingent, and impossible against two objections, and why the distinctions do not break the circle.

Core Idea
The circle of definitions remains even if the possibility is distinguished as general or particular.

Al-Jurjani anticipates objections to his claim that defining the three modal concepts leads to circularity. The first objection suggests that the term 'possibility' in the definitions might refer to different kinds, thus avoiding the circle. The second objection argues that the definitions may lead to definition by the unknown rather than a circle. In this passage, he shows why both objections fail and the circle persists.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse. Then read the translation. Notice the technical terms and the structure of objections and replies.
2
Identify the two objections
The first objection distinguishes general vs. particular possibility. The second objection claims definition by the unknown rather than a circle.
The text uses 'قد يناقش' for each objection.
3
Understand Al-Jurjani's responses
For the first, the reply is based on the established discussion of the modes. For the second, he insists the circle is absolute and occurs even without direct circularity between definer and defined.
The phrase 'فإن المدعى لزوم الدور مطلقاً' is key.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Ask yourself why both objections fail to save the definitions from circularity. The core idea is that the circle is not easily broken by such distinctions.

Commentary
This passage deals with objections to the charge of circularity. The first objection is that the term 'imkan' (possibility) used in defining one of the other two concepts might be 'particular possibility' while the one used in their definitions is 'general possibility', so no circle occurs. The commentator says the refutation of this is clear from what was established about the modes (al-jihaat). A second objection is that if we define the contingent as 'that whose existence is not necessary nor its nonexistence', define the necessary as 'that whose nonexistence is impossible', and the impossible as 'that whose existence is impossible', then there is no circle in defining possibility, but rather definition by the unknown. The answer is that the claim is that there is a circle absolutely, and indeed it does occur even if between the definer and the defined (the possible) there is no direct circle. So the charge stands.

The First Objection to Necessity of Necessity
Al-Jurjani's objection and answer regarding wujub al-wujub.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that wujub al-wujub cannot be identical to wujub and how the commentator answers by distinguishing the necessity of the necessary from its essence.

Core Idea
Wujub al-wujub is a relation, not identical to wujub itself.

Al-Jurjani presents an objection against the identity of wujub al-wujub (the necessity of necessity) with wujub (necessity). The objector argues that since wujub al-wujub is a relation between wujub and its existence, it cannot be the same as wujub. The answer clarifies that even if wujub is possible, the necessary being's necessity comes from its own essence, not from wujub. This lesson explores this dialectical exchange.

The objection is based on the relational nature of wujub al-wujub.
2
Identify key terms

These terms are central to the argument.
3
Analyze the answer
The answer argues that the necessity of the necessary (الواجب) comes from its own essence, not from the possibility of wujub al-wujub. Distinguish between the two levels of necessity.
The answer prevents the slide from possibility of wujub to possibility of the necessary being.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer concerning the claim that the necessity of necessity (wujub al-wujub) is identical to necessity itself. The answer is that even if necessity is possible (mumkin), the possibility of the necessary (al-wajib) does not follow, because necessity may accrue to the necessary due to its very essence, while necessity may not accrue to its own necessity due to that necessity's essence. The Identity of Wujub and Wajibiyya
Al-Jurjani argues for the identity of wujub and wajibiyya and refutes the causal objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Jurjani insists that wujub (necessity) and wajibiyya (the state of being necessary) are identical, and why making wujub a cause of wajibiyya leads to impossibility.

Core Idea
Wujub and wajibiyya are the same reality—not cause and effect.

In this passage, al-Jurjani tackles a subtle objection: if wujub is a real attribute, how can it be identical to wajibiyya which involves a relation? He shows that the objection fails because identity holds regardless of whether they are real or conceptual, and that separating them would imply dependence on a possible, which is impossible for the Necessary Being.

4

Understand the logical consequence if wujub were a cause: the Necessary Being would depend on a possible, which is impossible (خلف).
This is the decisive argument for identity.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a claim that if wujub (necessity) is a real (ghayr i'tibari) attribute, then it cannot be identical to wajibiyya (the state of being necessary) because wajibiyya involves a relation (nisba) while wujub does not. The author acknowledges that if wujub were a real attribute, the relation would be external to it, and wajibiyya would be an intentional matter including a relation. However, the author asserts that they (wujub and wajibiyya) are identical in reality, whether they are existent or intentional. Therefore, the objection that wujub would then cause wajibiyya fails; rather, if they are identical, wujub does not cause wajibiyya. If wujub were a cause, then the necessary being would depend on a possible matter, which is impossible (khulf).

The Infinite Regress Argument for Considerational Things
Al-Jurjani defines amur i'tibariyya and explains the regress they entail.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why considerational things (al-umur al-i'tibariyya) cannot have external existence and how the infinite regress argument proves their mental status.

Core Idea
Considerational things exist only in the mind; real existence would trigger an endless chain.

Al-Jurjani introduces a subtle but powerful argument that shapes the Islamic theological understanding of abstract concepts. He identifies certain notions—like 'descriptiveness,' 'unity,' and 'determination'—that seem to refer to something but cannot actually exist outside the mind. The argument is that if they did exist externally, they would require an infinite series of similar entities, which is impossible. This lesson unpacks the logic of that regress and its resolution.

Attributes that come after their subject's existence are purely mental, not real.

B) The dependence of four is mental/imaginary, while the dependence of the world is real.
C) The number four is a necessary being.
D) God's essence is not like the essence of four.

Key Takeaway
The example of the number four does not prove real eternal dependence because its dependence is merely mental, not real.

The Five Ancients Doctrine and a Subtle Indication
Discussion of the five ancients school, including void and time, and a note on the hidden indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the historical context of the 'five ancients' doctrine and the meaning of the subtle indication mentioned by the author.

Core Idea
The five ancients doctrine was hidden until Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi brought it to light.

This passage concludes a section on the eternal, discussing void and time as neither agents nor patients. It recounts how the physician Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi revealed the previously hidden school of the five ancients. The author also addresses a potential objection about the impossibility of a necessary concomitant, explaining that it is material in a specific sense. Finally, he clarifies his earlier 'subtle indication' as a summary one.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage carefully, then its literal translation. Notice the mention of void and time, and the quote from Imam al-Razi.
Pay attention to the names: Imam al-Razi (Fakhr al-Din) and Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi (the physician) are different people.
2
Identify the historical point
The passage reports that Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi the physician authored a book on the five ancients. Understand that this doctrine was hidden before him.
The five ancients are typically: God, soul, matter, void, and time.
3
Understand the objection and response
The passage addresses an objection: the impossibility of a necessary concomitant is not granted. The response clarifies that this impossibility is 'material' in a specific sense related to the body.
This is a technical distinction about how the concomitant relates to the body.
4
Clarify the 'subtle indication'
The author explains that his earlier subtle indication was a summary one. Reflect on what this means for the reader: it is a brief hint to be expanded later.
This connects to the author's style of referencing arguments elsewhere.

The Third Observatory - The Fifth Quest: On Investigations of the Eternal. Its non-existence and these two, namely void and time, are neither agents nor patients. Commentary
This passage is a concluding section of the Third Observatory of the Book of Stations, discussing a specific doctrine about the five pre-eternal entities (void, time, etc.). It begins by mentioning that void (al-khala') and time (al-zaman) are neither agents nor patients (i.e., they do not act nor are acted upon). Then it reports a statement from Imam al-Razi (Fakhr al-Din) that this school was hidden among the various schools, until Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi the physician (Abu Bakr al-Razi) adopted it, publicized it, and wrote a book titled 'The Discourse on the Five Ancients' (al-Qawl fi al-Qudama' al-Khamsa). The author notes that the reader will encounter these arguments later in the book, and that he (the author) has already made a subtle reference to them. The passage then addresses a potential objection: one might argue that the impossibility of a necessary concomitant (istihalat al-lazim) is not granted. The response is that this impossibility is 'material' (madiyya) in the sense that it is related to the body which is its matter, even if it is not material in the sense of inherence. The concomitant of temporal origination (al-huduth) is material in a comprehensive sense covering both meanings, as will be explained later. Finally, the author clarifies the phrase 'a subtle indication' (ishara khafiyya) as meaning an elliptical or summary indication. The volume ends with the colophon noting the completion of the third part and the beginning of the fourth part with the Sixth Quest.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Confusing Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (the commentator) with Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi the physician.
Correction: The text quotes Imam al-Razi (Fakhr al-Din) reporting about Ibn Zakariyya al-Razi. The book 'The Discourse on the Five Ancients' was authored by the physician, not the Imam.
