Author introduces the fourth introduction and explains two Quranic terms implying creation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Quranic terms imply creation, but they require rational interpretation.

This lesson begins the fourth introduction of al-Matalib al-Aliyah, where the author addresses a subtle point: the divine scriptures do not explicitly state that the world is created in its matter and form. Instead, they contain terms that strongly indicate this, and the author sets out to explain how. We will focus on the first two terms: 'Rab' (Lord) and 'Khalq' (creation).

Commentary
This passage is an introductory enumeration and explanation of Quranic terms that imply the world's createdness. The author first states that the divine books (al-kutub al-ilāhiyya) do not explicitly declare that the world is originated (ḥādith) in both its matter and form. Instead, there are a countable number of expressions that indicate this meaning. He then explains the first term: 'Lord' (rabb). The first verse of the Quran, 'Al-ḥamdu lillāhi rabbi l-ʿālamīn', does not by itself entail that Allah originated the essences of the worlds, because 'lord' linguistically means the one who nurtures and cares for a thing. However, if one adds the rational belief that Allah originated the world from parts and arranged it perfectly, then one acknowledges that He is the powerful Lord. Thus the term 'rabb' requires rational interpretation to prove createdness. The second term is 'creation' (khalq). The verse 'al-ḥamdu lillāhi lladhī khalaqa s-samāwāti wa l-arḍ' uses 'khalq', which in the language means 'estimation' (taqdeer). The author provides scriptural proof from the verse about Jesus and Adam (Surah Al-Imran 3:59), where 'khalaqahu min turāb' shows that 'khalq' can mean bringing something into existence from nothing, as with Adam. The passage follows a pattern: each term is given, then its linguistic meaning is clarified, and then a rational or scriptural argument is made to connect it to the world's createdness.

Al-Razi's Refutation of the Objection on Sphere's Parts
Al-Razi refutes the objection that a sphere's depth part is identical in essence to its surface part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that a part in the sphere's thickness shares the same essence as a part on its surface does not apply according to the philosophers.

Core Idea
The celestial sphere is a single continuum, not a composite of actual parts.

Al-Razi addresses a subtle objection: if a part in the thickness of a sphere and a part on its surface have the same quiddity, why can't they be interchanged? His answer hinges on the philosophers' view that the celestial body is a single uninterrupted continuum. Understanding this requires grasping the nature of continuous bodies and the impossibility of splitting the celestial spheres.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let Al-Razi's response unfold naturally.
Notice the compact style of argumentation.
2
Identify the objection
Find the sixth objection. It says that a part in the thickness and a part on the surface share the same quiddity, implying they should be interchangeable.
Quiddity means essence or 'whatness'.
3
Understand Al-Razi's answer
Al-Razi claims the objection is not necessary because the sphere is a single continuum without actual parts. Parts only arise potentially due to external causes.
The continuum view is key.
4
Grasp the reason for impossibility
The form of the whole prevents separation, and splitting (al-kharq) of the celestial spheres is impossible. Therefore, the assumed part in the depth cannot occur on the surface.
This shows the logical consistency of the philosophers' position.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The sixth objection states that a part in the thickness (depth) of a sphere and a part on its surface share the same quiddity (essence), so they should be interchangeable, yet they are not. Al-Razi answers that this objection does not apply to the philosophers (al-hukama') because they hold that the celestial sphere is a single continuous body, not composed of actual parts. Parts only arise potentially due to external causes such as imagination or different forces. But on this assumption, the part is conceived only after the whole exists. The form of the whole prevents the actual separation of those parts, because splitting (kharq) of the celestial spheres is impossible. Therefore, the part assumed in the depth cannot occur on the surface, and vice versa.

Core Idea
The diversity of organs points to an intelligent agent, not blind nature.

This passage presents Al-Razi's response to a key objection against divine creation. The objection asks how homogeneous semen can produce vastly different organs like the heart and liver. Al-Razi counters by invoking an angelic agent who forms the organs according to divine wisdom, thereby preserving the role of a free, purposeful actor.

Reincarnation and the Great Resurrection
Explains the process of reincarnation and the eventual liberation of souls leading to the great resurrection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical concept of reincarnation and the process leading to the great resurrection as described in a classical Islamic critique.

Core Idea
Souls trapped in matter reincarnate until they recognize bodily pleasures as pains and break free.

This passage presents a philosophical view on the cycle of reincarnation and the ultimate liberation of all souls. It describes how souls attached to material bodies keep returning until they attain spiritual insight, leading to the final dissolution of the soul-body composite—the great resurrection. Understanding this helps distinguish this concept from Islamic eschatology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Focus on the overall narrative of the soul's journey.
The passage is a continuous answer to a question about the duration of the corrupt composition.
2
Identify the stages of reincarnation
Notice the sequence: souls with love for matter die and reincarnate into new bodies repeatedly. Identify the turning point when the light of reason strengthens and the soul becomes averse to bodily ties.
The turning point is when the soul realizes that material pleasures are actually pains.
3
Understand the great resurrection
See how the process culminates: all souls eventually achieve this aversion, leading to the dissolution of all soul-body composites and the return to the primordial state.
This great resurrection is different from the Islamic concept of bodily resurrection.

The sixth question: If it dies, how long does this corrupt composition remain? He answered: These souls that love this matter (hayula), when they die while this love remains, they return to attachment to another body, and this repeats from body to body until it attaches to a specific body, and it happens for it in that body that the light of reason strengthens and it appears to it that all material ties are pains in the form of pleasures and wounds in the guise of comforts. Then its nature recoils from them and it has no inclination to them. Then this soul departs with aversion to these bodily things and with desire for those spiritual things, and then that soul does not return to attachment to the body. Then this repetition continues cycle after cycle until this truth becomes manifest to all souls attached to bodies, so all souls become separate from all bodies, and the composition dissolves, and the great resurrection occurs, and the matter returns to what it was at first: the souls remaining in their world free from attachment to bodies, and the matter remaining free from mixture with souls. This is the sum of the account of this school.

This is presented as the doctrine of a certain school (probably the philosophers mentioned earlier).
