The Three Categories of Existence
Definition and classification of existents into three categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the foundational classification of existents into space-occupiers, inhering in space-occupiers, and neither, and how this sets the stage for theological discussions.

Core Idea
All existents in the world are either space-occupiers, inhering in them, or neither — but what about God?

The author begins by laying the conceptual groundwork for the entire work. He presents a tripartite classification of existents that will be used to analyze the world. Understanding this classification is essential before delving into deeper theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the structured opening 'اعلم أن' which signals an important foundational point.
2
Identify the three main categories
The passage divides all existents into three categories. Find each one and notice the Arabic terms: متحيز, حال في المتحيز, and neither.
These categories are mutually exclusive and cover all created beings.
3
Examine the subdivisions
Look at how the first two categories are further divided: space-occupiers into body and indivisible part; inhering things into accidents, with quality as the first genus, then its two types.
The passage mentions that accidents are nine genera, but only quality is discussed here.
4
Reflect on the purpose
Consider why this classification is important for Islamic theology. It provides a precise vocabulary for discussing the nature of created things and their relationship to the Creator.
This classification excludes God, who is neither a space-occupier nor an accident.
5
Address the common misunderstanding
Many assume these three categories cover all existents, including God. But the terms are only for created beings. The passage sets the stage for arguments about God's existence and attributes.
This will be clarified in later lessons.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. It begins by stating that the world is to be interpreted through the classification of existents. The author divides all existents into three categories: those that occupy space (mutahayyiz), those that inhere in space-occupiers (hall fi al-mutahayyiz), and those that are neither space-occupiers nor inhering in them. The first category is then subdivided into bodies (jism), which are divisible, and indivisible parts (al-juz' alladhi la yatajzza), which are affirmed only by those who accept atomism. The second category—things inhering in space-occupiers—is identified as accidents (a'rad) that subsist in bodies and atoms. The author notes that according to the philosophers, accidents are of nine genera, and he begins with the first genus: quality (kayf). Quality is then divided into two types: accidents that can inhere in non-living things, and accidents that can inhere only in living things. This classification provides the foundational ontology for subsequent discussions in the work.

This is analogous to what later philosophers say about the elements: each element's part has its own specific attribute and position. Thus, the atomist can also resolve the objection by appealing to pre-assigned positions.

The author distinguishes two types of demonstration: from cause to effect and from effect to cause.

In this passage, the author contrasts the method of the philosophers with that of the mutakallimun. Third Type of Quranic Proof: The Four Ranks in Surah an-Nahl
Al-Razi enumerates four ranks of Quranic argument in Surah an-Nahl and raises an objection about natural causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Al-Razi structures the Quranic arguments in Surah an-Nahl into four ranks and the objection from natural causality.

Core Idea
The Quran builds arguments from creation in four progressive ranks, inviting reflection.

Al-Razi introduces the third type of Quranic proofs by examining Surah an-Nahl, where the argument proceeds through four levels: the heavens, humanity, animals, and plants. Each level points to divine power and wisdom. But an objection arises: why not attribute these wonders to natural causes like seasons and celestial motions? Al-Razi begins the answer, but the passage is incomplete, leaving us to reflect on the necessity of thoughtful contemplation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the progression from heavens to plants and the objection that follows.
The passage is incomplete; the answer cuts off.
2
Identify the four ranks
List the four ranks Al-Razi mentions: 1. heavens and earth, 2. human being (body and soul), 3. animals, 4. plants. Observe how each rank is supported by a Quranic verse.
The verse for plants ends with 'they reflect' (يتفكرون).
3
Understand the objection
The objector asks why natural causes (seasons, sun and moon motions) cannot explain the diversity of plants. This challenges the argument from design.
Al-Razi notes that the proof is incomplete without answering this, hence the call to reflection.
4
Reflect on the incomplete answer
The first answer begins: 'Suppose that the changes of the lower world are connected to the states of the motions...' but is cut off. Consider why Al-Razi might start with a hypothetical concession.
This teaches that theological arguments must address counterarguments.

The Four Ranks of Proofs in Surah Nahl
Razi enumerates the four ranks of verses in Surah Nahl as proofs for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Surah Nahl presents a four-rank argument from the cosmos to human, animals, and plants, culminating in reflection on God's existence.

Core Idea
Four ranks of creation lead the mind from heavens to self to animals to plants, all pointing to God.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi unpacks the rhetorical structure of Surah an-Nahl, showing how God arranges four successive proofs from different realms of creation. Each rank builds on the previous, and the final verse explicitly calls for reflection. Understanding this structure reveals the Qur'an's method of reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the four ranks
Read the Arabic passage once carefully, then review the translation. Notice that Razi enumerates four distinct ranks of creation as proofs.
The ranks are: heavens and earth, human, animals, plants.
2
Observe the progression and connection
Notice how the ranks move from the largest (cosmos) to the human (body and soul), then to animals, and finally to plants. Each rank is introduced with a verse from Surah Nahl.
The human rank is split into body (from a drop) and soul (disputant).
3
Reflect on the concluding phrase 'who reflect'
The plants rank ends with 'yatafakkarun' (who reflect). Ask yourself why this specific ending is chosen. Razi explains it is because the argument from plant diversity to God requires intellectual reflection.
The other ranks end with different phrases; this one emphasizes thought.
4
Connect the argument to divine proof
Consider how these four ranks together constitute a cumulative proof for a powerful, free-choosing God. The systematic arrangement is deliberate, not random.
This is a key method in Qur'anic argumentation.

The Mutakallimūn's Argument from Origination
States the mutakallimūn's proof that every body is contingent based on its origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the mutakallimūn prove the contingency of bodies through their origination.

Core Idea
Origination proves contingency: every body is originated, hence contingent.

The mutakallimūn offer a concise proof for the contingency of bodies that relies on a prior demonstration of their origination. This passage presents the syllogism that forms the backbone of their argument, distinguishing their approach from philosophical proofs. Let's examine the structure of this reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once and its literal translation. Notice the compactness of the argument.
The passage is just one sentence.
2
Identify the two premises
The argument contains two premises: (1) every body is originated, (2) every originated thing is possible in itself. Find them in the text.
The second premise is 'every originated thing is possible in itself'.
3
Understand the argument's significance
Ask why the mutakallimūn chose this approach. How does it differ from philosophical proofs? The commentary will help.
This argument depends on prior proof that bodies are originated.

Commentary
This short passage presents the alternative argument of the mutakallimūn (Islamic theologians). Instead of the philosophical proofs from divisibility or composition, they base the contingency of bodies on the fact that bodies are originated (muḥdath). Their reasoning is a syllogism: every body is originated, and every originated thing is contingent (possible). Therefore, every body is contingent. This argument relies on the prior demonstration that bodies are originated, which the mutakallimūn establish through arguments about accidents and temporal existence.

This impossibility of chance occurrence points back to the need for a purposeful Creator. The author introduces a forgotten view without endorsing it, laying groundwork for systematic critique.

The Scholar's Humble Supplication After Theological Inquiry
Author reports a skeptical view, then offers a humble prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a great theologian like Fakhr al-Din al-Razi humbly submits to God after presenting complex theological arguments, recognizing the limitations of human reason.

Core Idea
True scholarship ends in humble supplication, not arrogant certainty.

After summarizing various theological schools and their proofs, al-Razi reports a reaction from 'the people of confusion and astonishment' and then offers his own heartfelt prayer. This passage reveals the author's deep piety and recognition that ultimate truth rests with God alone. As you read, notice the shift from analytical discourse to personal supplication.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify its two sections
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once. The passage has two clear parts: first, the report of what 'the people of confusion and astonishment' said; second, the author's personal supplication beginning with 'At this point I supplicate and say'.
Notice the change of tone from impersonal report to intimate prayer.
2
Understand the claim of the 'people of confusion'
What is their evaluation of the proofs? They say the proofs lack sufficient clarity and strength to remove doubt and fill the mind, and that each proof has some obscurity. They argue that the Merciful and Generous God should excuse those who err in such difficult matters.
This represents a skeptical or fideistic position that al-Razi neither fully endorses nor rejects.
3
Analyze the supplication's structure and content
Al-Razi presents his supplication: his proof is his need, his provision is his poverty, his means is God's grace, his intercessor is God's kindness. He describes his knowledge as a mirage, his heart as ruined, his problems as numerous. Yet he hopes to be among the beloved.
Key phrases: 'my proof is my need' (حجتي: حاجتي) – a profound inversion of normal theological argument.
4
Reflect on the theological posture
How does this supplication reconcile intellectual effort with divine grace? Al-Razi does not abandon reason; he affirms his intention to seek truth. But he places ultimate confidence in God's bounty and mercy, not in the power of proofs alone.
This is a model of humility for students of theology: work hard, then trust God.

Commentary
This passage follows the conclusion of the theological arguments. The author reports that after summarizing the various schools and showing the merits and defects in each, some people (called 'the people of confusion and astonishment') claim that these proofs are not sufficiently clear and powerful to remove doubt and fill the mind. They say that each proof has some obscurity, and that the Merciful and Generous God should excuse those who err in such difficult matters. Then the author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, speaks in the first person, presenting a heartfelt supplication. He states that his proof is his need, his provision is his poverty, his means to God is God's grace, and his intercessor is God's kindness. He acknowledges that no one can reach God except through His bounty, and that separation from God is due to His justice. He describes his knowledge as a mirage (sarāb), his heart as ruined by fear, and his problems as numerous as sand and dust. Despite this, he hopes to be among God's beloved. He affirms that his intention in all his words and writings was only to attain truth and avoid error. He asks God to accept his work if correct by His bounty, and to pardon him if mistaken by His mercy and forbearance. The prayer ends with praises to God as the Possessor of Generosity and the Effuser of Existence.
