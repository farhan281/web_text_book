The Three Categories of Existents in Kalam
Al-Razi enumerates the three categories of existents and subdivides the first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how classical kalam divides all existents into three fundamental categories: occupier, inhering entity, and neither.

Core Idea
Everything that exists is either a thing that occupies space, inheres in one, or is neither.

Al-Razi opens his discussion with a foundational classification that sets the stage for all later debates about substance, accident, and the nature of God. Before we can argue about atoms or bodies, we need to know what kinds of things exist. This short passage gives us the map.

Guided Path
1
Read the classification
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the threefold division introduced by 'إما أن يكون...'.
The author uses a logical disjunction to exhaust all possibilities.
2
Identify the subdivisions
The first category, occupier, is further divided into body and indivisible part. Note that the indivisible part is only accepted by some theologians.
This reflects the atomic theory debate in kalam.
3
Reflect on why this matters
Ask yourself: What does this classification allow us to say about accidents, substances, and God? This framework is essential for later proofs.
It's the basic ontology of kalam.

Defining al-Muhdath: Preceded by Non-Existence
An argument for defining contingency by non-existence precedence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the definition of al-muhdath as 'preceded by non-existence' is more fundamental than defining it as 'preceded by another existent'.

Core Idea
Contingent existence must be preceded by non-existence — that is its defining feature.

In kalam, precise definitions are essential. The term 'al-muhdath' (contingent/originated) is central, but how should we define it? This passage from al-Matalib al-Aliyah presents a key argument: the best definition focuses on precedence by non-existence, not by another existent. Let's explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once for overall meaning, then read the translation. Notice the logical flow: the author presents two possible definitions, argues for one, and gives the reason.
Pay attention to the phrase 'لا شك' — it signals certainty.
2
Identify the two definitions
The author contrasts two interpretations: 'preceded by non-existence' and 'preceded by another existent'. Mark these in the text.
3
Understand the preference argument
The author argues the first definition is 'more appropriate' because precedence by non-existence is necessary for contingency, while precedence by another existent requires proof.
Consider why 'necessity' vs. 'proof' is a strong argument.
4
Reflect on universality
The author notes that regardless of one's view on causation (self-caused, other-caused, etc.), all agree on precedence by non-existence. This makes the definition more fundamental.
This shows the definition is independent of controversial proofs.

نقول : لا شك أنه لا يمكن تفسير المحدث، إلا بأنه الذي سبقه غيره ثم إما أن يكون ذلك السابق عدماً أو وجوداً ، فإن كان عدماً فحينئذ يقال : إن المحدث هو الذي يكون وجوده مسبوقاً بالعدم ، وإن كان ذلك السابق وجوداً فحينئذ يقال : إن المحدث هو الذي سبقه وجود شيء آخر والتفسير الأول أولى ، لأن المحدث من حيث إنه محدث يجب أن يكون وجوده مسبوقاً بعدمه ، ولا يجب من حيث هو محدث أن يكون وجوده مسبوقاً بوجود غيره ، لأنه مالم يثبت بالدليل أن المحدث إنما يحدث إلا لمحدث موجود ، فإنه لا يثبت أن المحدث لا بد وأن يكون مسبوقاً بوجود غيره . أما سواء قلنا : إن المحدث يحدث لنفسه أو لغيره ، أو لا لنفسه ولا لغيره ، فإنه يجب أن يكون مسبوقاً بالعدم . فيثبت : أن التفسير المحدث بأنه الذي يجب أن يكون مسبوقاً بالعدم أولى من تفسيره بأنه الذي يجب أن يكون مسبوقاً بغيره.
We say: There is no doubt that it is not possible to interpret 'al-muhdath' (the contingent/originated) except as that which is preceded by something else. Then that something else is either non-existence or existence. If it is non-existence, then it is said: 'al-muhdath' is that whose existence is preceded by non-existence. If that something else is existence, then it is said: 'al-muhdath' is that which is preceded by the existence of something else. The first interpretation is more appropriate, because al-muhdath, insofar as it is muhdath, must have its existence preceded by its non-existence, and it is not necessary, insofar as it is muhdath, that its existence be preceded by the existence of something else. For as long as it is not proven by proof that al-muhdath only occurs from a muhdath existent, it is not established that al-muhdath must be preceded by the existence of something else. Whether we say that al-muhdath occurs by itself or by another, or neither by itself nor by another, it must be preceded by non-existence. So it is established that interpreting al-muhdath as that which must be preceded by non-existence is more appropriate than interpreting it as that which must be preceded by something else.

Commentary
This passage defines 'al-muhdath' (the contingent/originated) and argues for the superiority of one definition over another. The author begins by stating that the definition of 'al-muhdath' must involve being preceded by something else, and that something else can only be non-existence (ʿadam) or existence (wujūd). If it is non-existence, then 'al-muhdath' is 'that whose existence is preceded by non-existence'. If it is existence, then it is 'that which is preceded by the existence of something else'. The author then argues that the first definition is better (awlā). The reason is that the very concept of 'muhdath' (contingent) necessarily entails that its existence is preceded by its own non-existence; it does not necessarily entail being preceded by another existent. The latter would require proof (dalīl) that every contingent is caused by an existing cause, which is not a priori. The author emphasizes that regardless of whether one believes the contingent occurs by itself, by another, or neither, it must still be preceded by non-existence. Thus, the definition based on precedence by non-existence is more fundamental.

The Four-Fold Classification of Existence
Philosophers' classification of existence into four categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophical classification of existence into four categories based on the criteria of affecting and being affected.

Core Idea
Existence is divided into four: Creator, hyle, soul, and void/time.

This passage presents a foundational classification used by certain philosophers (like al-Razi) to categorize all existing things. It is not the mainstream Islamic view but a philosophical framework that later theologians engaged with. The criteria are simple: does a thing affect others, and is it affected by others? Based on this, four categories emerge.

قالوا : الموجود إما أن يكون مؤثراً ، لا يتأثر، وهو البارى تعالى . أو متأثراً لا يؤثر ، وهو الهيولى . أو يكون متأثراً ومؤثراً وهو النفس . فإنها تقبل الأثر عن عالم الإله ، وتقوى على التأثير في الهيولى . وإما أن لا يكون مؤثراً ولا متأثراً ، وهو الفضاء والدهر .

Commentary
This passage is a classification of existence into four categories. The author begins by quoting 'they said', referring to the philosophers (likely al-Razi and his school). The classification is based on two criteria: whether something affects (ؤثر) and whether it is affected (تأثر). The first category is the Creator (الباري تعالى), who affects but is not affected. The second is hyle (الهيولى), the prime matter that is affected but does not affect. The third is the soul (النفس), which both receives effects from the divine world and affects the hyle. The fourth is void (الفضاء) and time (الدهر), which neither affect nor are affected. This division provides the foundation for the doctrine of the five eternals (since void and time are two distinct principles).

In this classification, the Creator alone is unaffected. Is there any area of your life where you have been living as if you are unaffected—refusing to be genuinely changed by Allah's words or guidance? What would it mean for you to fully acknowledge that you are constantly affected by Him?
?
The soul is described as both affected and affecting. In your daily interactions, do you see yourself more as a passive receiver of influences (from media, people, environment) or as an active shaper of your surroundings? Where have you neglected your responsibility to affect the hyle—the material world—for good?
?
Void and time are categorized as neither affecting nor being affected. Have you allowed any part of your spiritual life to become like a void—unresponsive to divine call and producing no effect on others? What step can you take today to break out of that state and become a soul that both receives from Allah and impacts the world?
