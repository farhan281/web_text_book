Why No Medicines in the Fourth Clime: Objection and Answer
The author answers the first objection about medicines.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rationale behind the lack of medicines in the moderate fourth clime, as a response to an objection against its superiority.

Core Idea
Medicines require imbalance; the fourth clime's moderation produces only balanced nutrition.

In defending the superiority of the fourth clime, the author anticipates an objection: if it is the best, why are useful medicines not produced there? He explains that medicine by nature requires imbalanced qualities, which only occur outside the moderate zone. This passage reveals the link between climate, physical constitution, and the production of remedies.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and identify the assumption
Read the objection carefully. The objector assumes that the absence of medicines implies a defect. Notice that the author does not deny the fact; he provides a reason.
2
Understand the definition of medicine
The author defines medicine as requiring one dominant quality and solar power dominance. This means medicine is inherently imbalanced.
Contrast with food, which is balanced.
3
Link to the fourth clime
The fourth clime is perfectly moderate, so it only produces balanced nutritive bodies, not imbalanced medicinal bodies.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
The answer turns the objection on its head: the lack of medicines is a sign of balance, not inferiority.
How does this defend the superiority of the fourth clime?

For if it is said: You claimed that the fourth clime is the best of the climes. It has two questions: First, that beneficial medicines are not generated at all in the fourth clime, but only in the other climes. Second, that none of the prophets [and the Messengers] have come forth from this clime. The answer to the first: Medicine is only medicine when one of the qualities is dominant and the solar power dominates it. This is only generated in the climes outside moderation. As for the fourth clime, since it is moderate, necessarily only moderate nutritive bodies are generated in it. As for medicinal bodies, they must necessarily be outside moderation. For this reason medicines are not generated in this clime. The answer to the second question: The intellects of the people of the fourth clime are perfect, their [innate disposition/abundance] is abundant, and some of them approximate others in perfection, so necessarily the prophets did not come forth. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer pair. The objector raises two difficulties against the claim that the fourth clime is best: (1) beneficial medicines are not produced there, and (2) no prophets came from it. The author answers each. First, medicines require imbalanced qualities (like extreme heat or cold) and solar dominance, which occur in non-moderate climes; the moderate fourth clime only produces balanced nutritive food, not medicinal extremes. Second, the people of the fourth clime are intellectually perfect and close to one another in perfection, so there is no need for prophets to correct them; in deficient climes, when a perfect person appears, his superiority becomes so evident that it reaches the level of a miracle (i'jaz), which is why prophets appear there. The author concludes with 'And God knows best' as a pious disclaimer.

Al-Razi's Claim: Prophets Confirm Reincarnation
Al-Razi presents his claim that philosophers and prophets support reincarnation, with a proof from the prophets' condemnation of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's argument that the prophets' condemnation of the world supports the doctrine of reincarnation.

Core Idea
Prophets condemn the world because it is not from God's original creation.

Al-Razi's reinterpretation of prophetic teachings is a bold attempt to align them with his philosophical views on reincarnation. He argues that if the world were created by God as good, He would not condemn it. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the claim and proof
Read the Arabic passage where al-Razi states his claim and gives the proof. Notice how he frames the argument as a conditional.
The proof contrasts God's hypothetical action with actual prophetic warnings.
2
Identify the logical structure
Break down the argument: if God created the world, He would not condemn it; but prophets do condemn it; therefore the world's creation is not from God in a good way.
This implies that the soul's attachment is accidental.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this reasoning supports reincarnation. Al-Razi is trying to show that the soul's fall into matter is not part of God's original plan.
This is key to his entire system.

Answering the Objection of Adorned Desires
Al-Razi addresses an objection based on the Qur'anic verse about adorned desires and resolves it through the soul's accidental attachment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's response to the objection that God Himself adorns desires, and his alternative explanation for the soul's attachment.

Core Idea
If God adorns desires, His command to avoid them is inconsistent; al-Razi says the attachment is accidental.

Al-Razi anticipates an objection from the Qur'anic verse 'Adorned for men is the love of desires.' If God is the one who adorns, why would He then command avoidance? This lesson examines al-Razi's resolution through the idea of accidental soul attachment.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answers
Read the Arabic passage. Identify the verse and the two possible answers al-Razi considers: one involving Iblis, the other involving God.
Notice how each leads to a problem.
2
Analyze the infinite regress argument
If Iblis adorns, then who adorned Iblis? This leads to an infinite chain. Understand why this is problematic.
Al-Razi uses this to reject the Iblis explanation.
3
Understand the inconsistency charge
If God adorns, then commanding avoidance seems contradictory to His attributes of mercy and wisdom.
Al-Razi argues this is unbefitting.
4
Grasp al-Razi's solution
Al-Razi proposes that the soul's love for matter is an accidental occurrence, not created by God. Thus, God can rightfully warn against it.
This solution preserves God's consistency.

Commentary
This passage presents al-Razi's argument that the prophets' condemnation of the world supports the doctrine of reincarnation. Al-Razi claims that if God had created the world and placed people in it, He would not condemn it. The fact that prophets warn against the world indicates that the soul's attachment to the world is an accident not intended by God. Al-Razi then addresses an objection based on the Qur'anic verse 'Adorned for men is the love of desires.' If Iblis is the adorner, the chain would be infinite; if God is the adorner, it would be inconsistent for Him to then command avoidance. Al-Razi resolves this by saying that the love is not from God's creation but is an accidental attachment of the soul. This makes the prophets' admonition fitting.

Razi's Doctrine of Souls and Hyle: Proof from Prophetic Blame
Razi summarizes his doctrine and provides a proof from the prophets' condemnation of the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that the soul's attachment to the body is accidental, based on the prophets' universal condemnation of the world.

Core Idea
The prophets blame the world because the soul's attachment to it is an accidental affliction, not God's design.

Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi advanced a distinctive doctrine about the original separation of souls and matter, and he claimed that all prophets agreed with him. His primary proof is that the prophets unanimously condemned the world—something God would not do if He had created our attachment to it. This lesson unpacks that argument and its implications for understanding the soul's predicament.

Guided Path
1
Understand the doctrine's foundation
Begin by grasping the core of Razi's doctrine: souls and hyle (prime matter) were originally separate, free from attachment to each other.
This sets up the idea that attachment is not original.
2
Recognize Razi's claim of universal agreement
Razi claims that all ancient divine philosophers before Aristotle, as well as all prophets, held this same doctrine.
This is a key rhetorical move to bolster his position.
3
Examine the proof from prophetic blame
The prophets blamed the world, disparaged its states, and warned against it. If God had created the world and our inclination toward it, He would not then order us to avoid it.
This is the central logical argument.
4
Analyze the Quranic example and the infinite regress objection
The verse 'Adorned for people is the love of desires' (3:14) is discussed. Who adorned it? If Satan, infinite regress; if God, inconsistency. This leads to the resolution.
The objection sharpens the argument for accidental attachment.
5
Grasp the resolution: accidental attachment
The soul's love for matter is an accidental, necessary property that became attached to it. Thus God's warning is consistent with His mercy and wisdom.
This completes the proof.

Commentary
This passage presents a doctrine (mazhab) attributed to Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi and provides its proof. It begins by describing the primordial state: souls exist in their own world free from attachment to bodies, and hyle (prime matter) exists free from intermingling with souls. This is the core of the doctrine. Then Razi claims that all ancient divine philosophers before Aristotle, as well as all prophets, were on this doctrine. The proof is based on the prophets' universal condemnation of the world: they blamed it, disparaged its conditions, and warned against it. The argument proceeds by reductio: if God had created the world and all creatures in it, made them needful of it, and placed in them inclinations and desires for it, then it would be inconsistent for Him to then command them to keep away from it. The inconsistency is resolved if the soul's attachment to the body is not originally from God but is an accidental love that the soul acquired, leading to suffering. Then God, being wise and merciful, warns against it. The author supports this with the Quranic verse about the adornment of desires (3:14). The question arises: who adorned it? If Satan, then infinite regress arises (Satan would need another Satan to adorn him). If God, then it is inconsistent for God to adorn and then forbid. But if the soul's love for matter is an accidental necessary property, then it makes sense for God to warn. This completes the establishment of the doctrine.
