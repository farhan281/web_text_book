Prophethood Does Not Require Prior Knowledge of Oneness
Sanusi presents the tenth argument: prophethood and scriptural evidence are independent of rational proof of oneness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why scriptural evidence for prophethood is sufficient on its own and does not require prior rational proof of God's oneness, and you will grasp the critical distinction between the existence of a thing and knowledge of its existence.

Core Idea
Prophethood can be proven through scripture alone; rational oneness is not a prerequisite.

This lesson presents a key argument from Sanusi's Sharh al-Maqasid. The author asserts that the truth of prophethood and the miracles of the prophets can be established through scriptural evidence (such as the consensus of all prophets on tawheed and the definitive texts of the Quran) without first proving God's oneness by rational means. He then refutes an objection that would require prior rational knowledge of God's necessity, and he diagnoses the root error: confusing the existence of something with the knowledge of its existence. Understanding this argument clarifies the independence of scriptural proofs in theology.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Circularity arises if prophethood presupposes divine attributes.

He presents a weak answer—denying that dependency—and dismisses it as stubborn denial. However, he notes that this circularity does apply to the attribute of speech. Moses heard Allah's speech in a way that breaks all normal patterns of hearing.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objection is that if the speech of Allah is defined either as the composition of heard letters (from the perspective of unity of type) or as the eternal meaning understood from sounds, then everyone who recites the Quran or understands its meaning is hearing Allah's speech. Both explanations preserve Moses' unique distinction by emphasizing the extraordinary, miraculous manner of his hearing, which is not the ordinary hearing of revelation.

The Two-Aspect Proof from Moses' Request for Vision
Sanusi enumerates two proofs from Moses' request for vision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two logical arguments derived from Moses' request to see Allah and why they demonstrate the possibility of vision.

Core Idea
Moses' request proves vision's possibility because prophets do not ask for the impossible.

In this passage, Sanusi presents a two-aspect proof based on the Quranic story of Moses asking to see Allah. The first aspect argues that Moses, being a prophet, would not request something impossible. The second uses the conditional statement in the verse to show that vision must be possible. Let's examine these arguments closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Sanusi uses enumeration: 'one of them... the second of them'.
2
Identify the two aspects
The passage presents two distinct arguments. Find each one and understand the core claim and its justification.
The first aspect uses a conditional argument; the second uses the logic of conditionals (ta'liq).
3
Understand the logical structure
For each aspect, trace the reasoning: what is the premise, what is the implication, and what conclusion is drawn. Pay attention to terms like 'mumkin' (possible) and 'muhal' (impossible).
The second aspect relies on the principle that 'what is made conditional upon the possible is possible'.
4
Reflect on the theological impact
Consider how these proofs establish the possibility of seeing Allah, which is a key point in Sunni theology regarding the Beatific Vision in the afterlife.
This connects to broader debates about God's attributes and human perception of Him.

And the consequent is false by text, consensus, widespread transmission, and the opponent's admission. The aspect of the implication is that if he knew Allah the Exalted and what is permissible for Him and what is not, then his request for vision would be frivolous and idle talk, unbefitting of prophets. The second of them: that he made vision conditional upon the mountain's settling, which is possible in itself necessarily, and what is made conditional upon the possible is possible, because the meaning of conditioning is that the conditioned occurs on the supposition of the condition, and the impossible does not occur under any supposition.

Commentary
This passage presents the two-aspect proof from the story of Moses (peace be upon him) requesting to see God. The first aspect uses a conditional argument: if seeing God were impossible, Moses would not have requested it. The consequent (that he would not request) is false by several evidences: text (the Quranic verse shows he did request), consensus (the ummah agrees), tawatur (widespread transmission), and even the opponent's admission. The logical implication is explained: if Moses knew God's attributes and what is possible for Him, then requesting the impossible would be frivolous, which is unbefitting of a prophet. If he were ignorant, he would not be fit to be a universal prophet (nabiyyun kulliyyun), i.e., one sent to all. The second aspect is based on the conditional statement in the verse: God says, "You shall not see Me, but look at the mountain; if it remains in its place, then you shall see Me" (Quran 7:143). Making vision conditional upon the mountain's stability implies that vision is possible, because the mountain's stability is possible, and what is made conditional upon a possible thing is itself possible. The reasoning is that the conditioned occurs only if the condition occurs, and an impossibility does not occur under any condition. Therefore, vision must be possible.

In this lesson, we examine a specific objection raised against the view that prophethood is necessarily obligatory. The objection argues that if it were obligatory, then God would have to provide prophets in every age and a just caliph in every land – which is not observed. The Sixth Objection: Death of Prophets and Survival of Iblis
The author presents the sixth objection against al-aslah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the death of prophets and the survival of Iblis poses a logical problem for the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-aslah.

Core Idea
If al-aslah were binding, even evil would be considered beneficial.

The author presents a sixth objection to the Mu'tazilite principle that God must always do what is most beneficial (al-aslah). This objection uses a striking example: the early death of prophets and the prolonged life of Iblis. If al-aslah were obligatory, then this state of affairs would have to be considered most beneficial, which is obviously repugnant. The objection serves as a reductio ad absurdum.
