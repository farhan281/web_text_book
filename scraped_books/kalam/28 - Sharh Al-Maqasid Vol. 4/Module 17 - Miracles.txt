Fourth Objection: Miracles on Liars and the Ash‛ari Response
Al-Taftazani presents and answers the fourth objection about miracles and lying prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fourth objection against the Ash‛ari denial of rational good and evil, and the Ash‛ari response that rational possibility does not imply actual occurrence.

Core Idea
Rational possibility does not undermine certainty of God's customary practice.

Al-Taftazani presents a critical objection: if God could perform evil, then miracles could appear on false prophets, making prophethood unprovable. His answer draws a key distinction between logical possibility and actual occurrence, relying on God's established habit. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage once and its translation. Notice the structure: objection beginning with 'لو' and answer with 'والجواب'.
The objection is short but powerful.
2
Identify the core logical move
The answer states that rational possibility does not contradict certainty of non-occurrence, likening it to natural habits. Understand that this separates logical possibility from actual occurrence.
This is a key Ash‛ari principle.
3
Connect to broader theology
Reflect on how this defends Ash‛ari theology without relying on intrinsic good/evil. It preserves the ability to prove prophethood through miracles while maintaining that God is not constrained by moral obligations.
This argument safeguards the rational basis for belief in prophets.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth objection against the Ash‘ari denial of rational good and evil. The objection argues that if nothing is inherently bad for God, then it would be logically possible for God to display a miracle through a false prophet, thus making it impossible to distinguish true prophets from impostors, closing the door to proving prophethood. The answer is that rational possibility does not entail actual occurrence; we can be certain that miracles will not appear on liars due to God's established practice (habit), just as we are certain of other regularities in nature despite their logical possibility. Thus, denial of intrinsic moral qualities does not lead to skepticism about prophethood.

Fourth Objection: Miracles on Liars and the Ash'ari Response
Objection and response on miracles and prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'ari respond to the objection that their position leads to skepticism about prophethood by distinguishing rational possibility from actual occurrence.

Core Idea
Rational possibility does not imply actual occurrence; custom provides certainty.

This lesson addresses a powerful objection against the Ash'ari view that nothing is objectively ugly for God. If God could do anything, why couldn't He allow a miracle to be performed by a false prophet? Wouldn't that make it impossible to know who is a true prophet? The Ash'ari answer is subtle: logical possibility does not mean something will happen. Like other customary matters, we can be certain that God will not do such a thing, even though it is not logically impossible. Let's examine the objection and its response.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the Arabic and translation of the objection: 'If nothing were ugly from God, then it would be permissible for a miracle to be shown at the hand of a liar, and in this there is the closing of the door of establishing prophethood.' Notice the logical chain: no ugliness → possible miracle on liar → cannot prove prophethood.
This objection assumes that without objective ugliness, there is no reason for God to restrict miracles to true prophets.
2
Study the Ash'ari answer
Read the Arabic and translation of the answer: 'That rational possibility does not contradict certainty of non-occurrence at all, like other customary matters.' Understand the key distinction between what is logically possible and what actually happens based on God's established custom (sunnah).
The Ash'ari rely on the concept of 'adiyyat (customary matters) to ground certainty.
3
Reflect on the analogy
Consider the example of other customary matters: we are certain the sun will rise tomorrow, even though it is logically possible that God could prevent it. Similarly, we are certain that God will not support a liar with a miracle, because His custom is to only support truth. This is a certainty based on experience and divine promise, not logical necessity.
This shows how rational possibility and practical certainty can coexist.
4
Connect to the broader debate
Ask yourself: does this response fully satisfy the objection? The objector might say that without an intrinsic prohibition, there is no guarantee. The Ash'ari response acknowledges that but asserts that God's custom is reliable enough for certainty. This is a key move in Ash'ari epistemology.
