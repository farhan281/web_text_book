Objection and two-part refutation on preponderance without a preponderator

Learning Objective

Imam al-Haramayn's Proof: The Impossibility of an Infinite Past
Imam al-Haramayn demonstrates the impossibility of an infinite past and contrasts it with an infinite future.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Imam al-Haramayn's rational argument that an infinite past is impossible due to the need for a completed infinite series, whereas an infinite future is logically permissible.

Core Idea
An infinite past is a contradiction; an infinite future is simply endless.

Imam al-Haramayn presents a powerful rational argument against the possibility of an infinite series of past events. He contrasts this with the endlessness of Paradise, showing that the two are fundamentally different. This lesson will walk through his proof, his examples, and the logical conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then read the literal translation. Let the author's flow come through before interpreting.
2
Identify the main claim and its basis
Imam al-Haramayn states that the entry of an infinite number of events into existence sequentially is 'known to be false by the first principles of intellects.' Ask yourself why this is self-evident to him.
The phrase 'awā'il al-'uqūl' refers to primary rational intuitions.
3
Analyze the example of cycles and conditionals
He gives two types of examples: the atheist claim of eternal cycles (dawrāt) and a conditional promise involving dirhams and dinars. Understand how each illustrates the impossibility of a beginningless series.
The conditional example shows that if each gift requires a prior gift, no gift can ever be given first.
4
Contrast infinite past with infinite future
Notice how he distinguishes the case of Paradise (endless future) from the past series: an infinite future does not require a completed infinite sequence; it is simply never-ending.
This contrast is crucial to avoid confusion.
5
Summarize the conclusion
The final sentence: 'temporality (ḥudūth) negates the absence of a beginning, but does not negate the absence of an end.' This means created things must have a beginning but can have no end.
This is a key theological principle in kalām.

Imam al-Haramayn (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'The entry of an infinite number of events sequentially into existence is known to be false by the first principles of intellects. For how can what has no end be completed one after another? This is like the cycles (dawrāt) before this cycle that we are in, according to what the atheists claim—that the world has always been as it is, and there has always been a cycle before a cycle without a beginning, a father before a son, a seed before a crop, a hen before an egg. This is unlike the affirmation of events with no end, like the bliss of Paradise, for that is not a judgment of the existence of what is infinite. Similarly, if someone says: "I will not give you a dirham unless I give you a dinar before it," or "I will not give you a dinar unless I give you a dirham before it," then it is inconceivable that he should give him a dirham or a dinar under his condition. In contrast, if he says: "I will not give you a dirham unless I give you a dinar after it," and "I will not give you a dinar unless I give you a dirham after it," [then it is possible]. In summary, temporality (ḥudūth) negates the absence of beginning, but does not negate the absence of end.'

Commentary
This passage is a direct quotation from Imam al-Haramayn, providing a powerful argument against the possibility of an infinite series of past events. He begins by asserting that it is self-evident (ma'lūm al-buṭlān bi-awā'il al-'uqūl) that an infinite number of events cannot occur sequentially in existence. The reason is that if there is no beginning, then to reach the present would require completing an infinite number of events, which is impossible. He gives an example from the atheists' claim of eternal cycles (dawrāt) without a first cycle, and the chain of parent-child, seed-crop, hen-egg. This is an infinite regress without a beginning. He then contrasts this with the idea of infinite future events, such as the endless bliss of Paradise, which does not require completing an infinite number from the past; it is simply an endless future, which is logically permissible. To further illustrate, he uses a conditional promise analogy: if someone conditions that every dirham must be preceded by a dinar and every dinar preceded by a dirham, then no payment can ever begin—illustrating the impossibility of a beginningless series. Conversely, if the condition is that each dirham is followed by a dinar and each dinar followed by a dirham, then an infinite series can be begun. The passage concludes with a summary: ḥudūth (temporality) negates the lack of a beginning (nafy al-awwaliyya), but does not negate the lack of an end (nafy al-ākhirayya).

Fifth Objection: Action Preferable or Futile
Objection and refutation about God's free action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth philosophical objection against divine free will and its refutation based on the distinction between personal benefit and intrinsic appropriateness.

Core Idea
An act without personal benefit is not necessarily futile for God.

This lesson tackles the fifth objection from the philosophers, which presents a dilemma about God's actions: either they are preferable and thus imply dependence, or they are not and thus are futile. The author's response clarifies the meaning of futility, showing that an act can be meaningful even if it does not benefit the agent. By the end, you will see how the objection fails to disprove divine free will.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Start by reading the objection in Arabic and English. Notice the two horns of the dilemma: if the action is more appropriate for God, then He is completed by something external; if not, the action is futile.
Both outcomes are presented as impossible for the Necessary Being.
2
Analyze the key terms
Focus on the terms 'أولى' (more appropriate) and 'عبث' (futility). The objection assumes that an action without personal preference is futile. Identify this assumption.
The refutation will challenge this assumption.
3
Read the answer
Read the answer: the author denies that lack of personal preference makes an action futile. An action can be appropriate in itself or for the sake of another, without benefiting the agent.
Even if called futility because it lacks personal benefit, it is not impossible for God.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
Consider how the refutation upholds divine free will: God's actions are not constrained by personal benefit; they are meaningful in their own right or for creation.

Sixth Objection: Eternal Possibility and Divine Power
Taftazani presents the sixth objection and refutes it by distinguishing essential possibility from actual occurrence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Taftazani's refutation of the objection that divine freedom entails either a change in possibility or an eternal effect.

Core Idea
Essential possibility does not require actual occurrence; the eternal object depends on the agent for temporal actualization.

In this lesson, we examine a subtle objection raised against the Ash'ari doctrine of divine free choice. The opponent argues that if God is free, His eternal act must be either impossible then possible or always possible but dependent on the agent—both seen as absurd. Taftazani shows that the second horn is not absurd by distinguishing between essential possibility and the impossibility of occurrence due to dependence on the temporal act of the agent.

The first horn involves change; the second involves dependence.
3

Taftazani denies the second implication by distinguishing between essential possibility and the impossibility of occurrence due to dependence on the agent. Understand how this distinction resolves the dilemma.
A thing can be possible in itself yet impossible to occur in pre-eternity because its actualization depends on a temporal act.

Al-Jubba'i's Argument Against Shared Power
Al-Jubba'i's argument against shared power and its logical consequence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Jubba'i's argument that one act cannot be within the power of two agents, and why it leads to the logical problem of two creators.

Core Idea
Al-Jubba'i argued that shared power would imply two creators.

Al-Jubba'i and his followers held a strict view about divine power: God cannot be capable of the same act as a human. In this passage, the author presents their reasoning, which hinges on a principle that two efficient causes cannot concur on one effect. Understanding this argument sets the stage for the Ash'ari response.

Core Idea
God's power alone brings about all that He wills; otherwise, absurdities follow.

This passage presents a second sub-argument from the section on divine power. It uses a disjunctive syllogism to eliminate any possibility of created beings bringing about objects of God's power. Guided Path
1
Understand the disjunctive structure

2
Examine possibility (a): another's power alone

The preponderance argument relies on the independence of both powers.
3
Examine possibility (b): both powers together
If both powers bring about the act, it would require the convergence of two independent agents on one effect, which is impossible.
This is a standard kalam argument against cooperation in creation.
4

If neither power brings about the act, then the act would not occur at all, contradicting the supposition that it occurs. Also, non-fulfillment would require an obstacle, which would be the second power—leading to circularity.
The obstacle argument shows that if only the second power prevented the first, then the act would still occur by the second.

If another's power alone brought about the act, God would no longer have power to create it (since it already exists), implying the servant incapacitates God. This is absurd. Thus, only God's power alone is coherent.
This reinforces the conclusion and shows that God's acting alone confirms His power.

Sanusi elaborates the third proof, explaining the absurdity of a relative weight from the servant.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The servant's act cannot be independent because its relative weight must come from God, not the servant.

The Fifth Proof and Its Initial Answer
Sanusi presents the fifth proof and its first answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logic of the fifth proof against human freedom and the first answer that appeals to the strength of God's power.

Core Idea
Two independent powers cannot conflict without a stronger one prevailing.

The fifth proof attempts to show that if human power were truly independent, a conflict with divine will would lead to a logical dead end. The first answer rescues the possibility of divine determinism by asserting that God's power is stronger, even when both are independent. This lesson walks through the proof and this initial response, preparing you for the more subtle refutation that follows.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof structure
Identify the conditional: if human power is independent, then a conflict of wills between servant and God leads to either agreement or disagreement of outcomes. Both are declared impossible, forcing a contradiction.
The proof assumes that both powers are equally independent.
2
Understand the impossibility of agreement
Agreement would require both motion and stillness to occur together or neither to occur, which is impossible for a single body.
3
Understand the impossibility of disagreement
Disagreement would mean one will prevails without a preponderator, because both powers are assumed equal in independence.
4
Examine the answer
The answer denies that equal independence implies equal strength. God's power is stronger, so His will prevails without logical flaw.
This answer opens the door to al-Razi's criticism.

Al-Razi's Refutation: Power Admits No Degrees
Sanusi presents a restated proof and al-Razi's refutation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Commentary

The Mu'tazila Doctrine of Al-Tawallud and Its Refutation
Definition, internal disputes, and refutation of Mu'tazila tawallud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila concept of al-tawallud (generated acts), the internal disagreements among them, and the Sunni argument that invalidates it based on divine causality.

Core Idea
The Mu'tazila theory of generated acts collapses because all contingent acts depend directly on God.

The Mu'tazila proposed that human acts can generate other acts through a causal chain, a theory known as al-tawallud. They differed on which acts are truly the servant's, but ultimately the theory is refuted by the principle that all contingent things depend on God from the beginning, leaving no room for independent generated effects. This passage presents the definition, variants, and refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of tawallud
Start with the second paragraph where the Mu'tazila define tawallud as an act necessitating another act in a different locus.
Note the two cases: within the locus of power or outside it.
2
Examine the branches of tawallud
The Mu'tazila derived several branches, including that a generated act cannot occur directly but only through a cause, and disagreements about whether death is generated from a wound.
These branches show the practical implications of the theory.
3
Understand the different Mu'tazila positions
Al-Nazzam, Ma'mar, and others held different views on which acts are directly the servant's act vs. generated by nature.
Al-Nazzam limited the servant's act to the locus of his power; Ma'mar excluded even volition.
4
Grasp the central refutation
The passage opens and closes with the argument that since all things depend on God, tawallud is invalid. The specific argument about two efficient causes is also given.
The refutation focuses on the impossibility of independent generated effects alongside God's causality.

Commentary
This passage discusses the concept of al-tawallud (generation) as held by the Mu'tazila. It begins by stating that when it is established that all things depend on God, the Mu'tazila doctrine of generation is invalidated. It then explains the Mu'tazila position: most of them say the generated act is the servant's action, but there are variations. Al-Nazzam says the servant only has an act in the locus of his power. Ma'mar says only volition is his act. Others say only thought, and the rest is due to nature. An objection is raised about the arrow: it may not be valid for the archer not to cause the arrow to move, but it is responded that there is an impediment. The author then presents the argument that if the generated act were the servant's, it would require two efficient causes or preponderance without a preponderator. The Mu'tazila define generation as an act necessitating another act. They derived several branches, such as that a generated act cannot occur directly by the same power but only through a cause, and disagreements about whether generated acts are in God's act or direct. Finally, the passage concludes that since all contingent things depend on God from the beginning (without intermediaries), the whole doctrine of generation is invalidated. The last line appears incomplete.

Definition of Tawleed and the Mu'tazila's View
Defines tawleed and states the Mu'tazila's general position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what the Mu'tazila mean by tawleed (generation) and why the Ash'ari tradition rejects it.

Core Idea
Tawleed is the claim that an act can generate another act attributable to the human agent.

The concept of tawleed is central to the Mu'tazilite understanding of human agency. They believe that a human act can generate a second act that is still the human's responsibility. This lesson introduces the basic definition and the Ash'ari rejection.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once and then the translation. Pay attention to how the author first declares the doctrine invalid before defining it.
The Ash'ari position is stated at the very beginning, showing the conclusion before the definition.
2
Identify the two key parts
The passage first states that tawleed is invalid, then defines it. Note the definition: an agent's act may necessitate another act for that agent.
The definition uses 'وجوب' (necessitation) which is a strong causal link.
3
Connect to the broader debate
Reflect on why this matters: if tawleed were true, humans would be responsible for acts they did not directly perform, challenging divine omnipotence.

Internal Mu'tazili Disagreements and the Ash'ari Refutation
Records the views of al-Nazzam, Ma'mar, and others, and presents the Ash'ari counterargument and the branches of tawleed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the different views among Mu'tazila on generated acts and the key arguments for and against tawleed.

Core Idea
Mu'tazila disagreed on what counts as a human act, but all forms of tawleed are rejected by Ash'aris.

The Mu'tazila were not unanimous on the details of tawleed. Al-Nazzam, Ma'mar, and others proposed different limits on human agency. The Ash'ari refutation uses the problem of two causes and the arrow example. This lesson unpacks those debates.

Mu'tazila's Proofs for Tawleed and the Ash'ari Response
Lists Mu'tazila's arguments from praise/blame and intention, then the Ash'ari response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila's two main arguments for attributing generated acts to humans and the Ash'ari's short response referencing the earlier debate on the creation of actions.

Core Idea
Generated acts are praised and blamed, and they follow motives—so they must be human acts.

The Mu'tazila extend their earlier reasoning from the creation of actions to the case of generated acts (al-mutawalladāt). They argue that because generated acts receive praise and blame and occur according to the agent's motives, they must be truly human acts. The Ash'ari response is not repeated in full but refers back to the refutation already given in the earlier discussion, reminding us of the consistency of the Ash'ari position.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice how the Mu'tazila present their arguments and the brevity of the Ash'ari reply.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the two arguments
The passage gives two reasons: (1) praise and blame apply to generated acts, even more clearly than to direct acts; (2) generated acts occur according to motives and intentions, which is a sign of human agency.
These are analogical extensions from the earlier 'creation of actions' debate.
3
Understand the Ash'ari response
The response is very brief: 'similar to what has passed.' This refers to the earlier refutation where the Ash'ari denied that praise/blame or intention necessarily prove human causation, asserting instead that God creates all acts.
The response is not elaborated here; it relies on prior knowledge.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author of Sharḥ al-Maqāṣid refutes the Mu‘tazilite claim that causing a child's death to prevent future misguidance is consistent with the principle of al-aṣlaḥ (the most beneficial).

Core Idea
If God kills a child to prevent evil, why didn't He kill Pharaoh and other tyrants as children?

The Mu‘tazilites held that God always does what is most beneficial for His creatures. In this objection, they argue that God may cause a child to die early because He knows that child would later go astray and lead others astray, thus preventing greater harm. The author responds with a devastating counterexample: if that logic holds, then why were the greatest misleaders in history—Pharaoh, Haman, Mazdak, Zoroaster—allowed to live and cause immense harm? This short but powerful argument exposes the inconsistency of the Mu‘tazilite position.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Note that the objection is presented as a hypothetical ('If it is said...') and the answer begins with 'We say...'.
2
Analyze the logical force
Ask yourself: why is the list of tyrants so effective? The author does not need to prove that God actually kills children; he simply shows that the Mu‘tazilite principle cannot be applied consistently.
Consider what the author implies by asking 'how would it not be foolishness and injustice?'

Commentary
This is another objection-answer pair. The Mu'tazilite objector says: God knows that a particular child, if allowed to live, would go astray (ḍalla) and lead others astray (aḍalla ghairah), so God causes that child to die early for the benefit (maṣlaḥah) of others—this is consistent with al-aslah because it prevents greater evil. The author responds by asking why then did God not cause Pharaoh, Haman, Mazdak, Zoroaster, and other notorious misleaders (ḍāllīn muḍillīn) to die as children? They caused immense harm, so if the principle holds, God should have killed them early to prevent that harm. Furthermore, the author argues that depriving an innocent child (lā janāyata lahu) of the most beneficial outcome (preventing his misguidance by killing him) for the sake of others is actually foolishness (safah) and injustice (ẓulm). This shows the Mu'tazilite position is inconsistent: they cannot consistently apply al-aslah across all cases.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The author is arguing that God should have killed Pharaoh and other tyrants as children.
Correction: The author is not prescribing what God should have done; he is using a hypothetical to show that the Mu‘tazilite principle, if applied consistently, would lead to absurd conclusions. The point is to expose the inconsistency in the Mu‘tazilite argument, not to advocate for killing.

Key Takeaway
The author uses a reductio ad absurdum to show that the Mu‘tazilite principle of al-aṣlaḥ leads to inconsistency and injustice when applied to a child's early death.
