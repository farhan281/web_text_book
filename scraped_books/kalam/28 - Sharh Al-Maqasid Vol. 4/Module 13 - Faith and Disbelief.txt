Fana' in Tawhid: Experiential Absorption
Author describes fana' as absorption in God, distinct from actual hulul or ittihad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what fana' in tawhid means and why it is distinct from literal incarnation or union (hulul/ittihad).

Core Idea
Fana' is experiential absorption in God, not literal union.

In this passage, the author introduces the first of two doctrines that may be mistaken for incarnation or union. He describes the concept of fana' (annihilation) in tawhid, where the seeker becomes so immersed in the oneness of God that their own sense of self dissolves. The author clarifies that any utterances that sound like union are due to the limitations of language, and that this state is known through direct experience, not rational proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get the author's description of fana'.
Notice the author's cautious tone at the end.
2
Understand the concept of fana'
Identify how the author describes the seeker's essence dissolving in God's essence and attributes.
The phrase 'يضمحل ذاته في ذاته' is key.
3
Distinguish from hulul/ittihad
The author emphasizes that this is not actual incarnation or union, only an impression ('يوهمان بالحلول والاتحاد، وليسا منه في شيء').
4
Examine the hadith reference
Consider the divine hadith where God becomes the servant's hearing and sight. How does it support the author's point about experiential closeness?
The hadith shows intimacy without literal union.
5
Note the author's caution
The author distances himself from this path ('نحن على سبيل التمني نفرق') and acknowledges it is experiential, not provable.
This reflects the Ash'ari cautious approach to mysticism.

Al-Nasafi's Balanced View on Faith
Al-Taftazani presents al-Nasafi's view on the components of faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Nasafi's distinction between the divine and human components of faith, and why this avoids both jabr and qadar extremes.

Core Idea
Faith has uncreated divine gifts and created human acts—both are needed.

In this passage, al-Taftazani introduces a nuanced view of faith from Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi that reconciles divine sovereignty and human responsibility. Instead of saying faith is entirely from God (jabr) or entirely from the servant (qadar), al-Nasafi identifies specific elements from each side. This framework preserves the balance central to Sunni theology.

Guided Path
1
Read al-Nasafi's statement
Read the Arabic carefully. Identify the components attributed to God and those attributed to the servant.
Notice that God's aspects are called 'uncreated' and the servant's are 'created'.
2
Understand the divine components
List the four gifts from God: definition (al-ta'rif), success (al-tawfiq), guidance (al-hidaya), and bestowal (al-i'ta'). They all refer back to His creative act (al-takwin), which is uncreated.
These are not created because they are acts of God.
3
Understand the human components
List the four acts from the servant: knowledge (al-ma'rifa), intention (al-qasd), acceptance (al-i'tidad), and reception (al-qubul). They are created because they are human actions.
4
See the balance
Reflect on how this view avoids the extremes: it is not jabr because the servant contributes genuine acts, nor qadar because God grants the essential enabling gifts.

قلنا وجهه ما أشار إليه أبو المعين النسفي رحمه الله من أن الإيمان ليس كله من الله إلى العبد على ما هو الجبر، ولا من العبد على ما هو القدر، بل من الله التعريف والتوفيق والهداية والإعطاء ومرجعها إلى التكوين وهو غير مخلوق ومن العبد المعرفة والقصد والاعتداد والقبول وهي مخلوقة. هذا ويمحى من الكتاب ويثبت ما هو الصواب، ثم لا يخفي ما في الوجوه المذكورة من وجوه الضعف، والأولى التمسك بالكتاب والسنة وإجماع أهل الحق من الأمة لا بمعنى إثباته في نفسه بمحض الإجماع، ليرد أن الحقائق العقلية مثل حدوث العالم، وقدم الصانع لا يثبت بالإجماع، بل بمعنى أن إجماعهم عليه يدل على أن لهم قاطعاً فيه وإن لم نعرفه على التفصيل.
We said: Its aspect is what Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi, may God have mercy on him, indicated: that faith is not entirely from God to the servant, as is jabr (compulsion), nor entirely from the servant, as is qadar (free will), but rather from God is definition (al-ta'rif), success (al-tawfiq), guidance (al-hidaya), and bestowal (al-i'ta'), and their reference is to creation (al-takwin), which is uncreated (ghayr makhluq); and from the servant is knowledge (al-ma'rifa), intention (al-qasd), acceptance (al-i'tidad), and reception (al-qubul), which are created (makhluqa). Commentary
This passage presents a specific view on the nature of faith and then critiques alternative approaches, advocating for reliance on scripture and consensus. The author begins by quoting Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi's balanced position: faith is not purely from God (which would be jabr or compulsion) nor purely from man (which would be qadar or free will), but has components from both. From God come uncreated aspects: definition (ta'rif), success (tawfiq), guidance (hidaya), and bestowal (i'ta'), all rooted in His creative act (takwin). From the servant come created aspects: knowledge (ma'rifa), intention (qasd), acceptance (i'tidad), and reception (qubul). The author remarks that this view erases incorrect positions and establishes the truth. However, he notes that the 'mentioned faces' (i.e., other arguments) suffer from weakness. Therefore, the best approach is to cling to the Book, Sunnah, and consensus of Ahl al-Haqq. But the author clarifies that consensus is not used to prove rational truths like the world's origination or God's pre-eternality, because those are established by reason. Rather, consensus indicates that the scholars have a decisive proof for those truths, even if we do not know the specific proof. This avoids the objection that rational doctrines cannot be based on consensus.

Preferred Methodology: Scripture, Sunnah, and Consensus
Al-Taftazani critiques alternative views and endorses scripture and consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Taftazani advocates for the Book, Sunnah, and consensus as the primary methodology, and how consensus functions as an indicator of decisive proof.

Core Idea
The best method is to rely on revelation and the consensus of scholars.

After presenting al-Nasafi's view, al-Taftazani notes weaknesses in other arguments and concludes that the safest approach is to adhere to the Quran, Sunnah, and the consensus of Ahl al-Haqq. He clarifies that consensus does not establish rational truths in themselves, but rather indicates that scholars possess a decisive proof for them. This lesson unpacks that methodological stance.

Understand the preferred sources
The best approach is to cling to the Book (Quran), the Sunnah, and the consensus of Ahl al-Haqq (the people of truth). This is a classic Sunni methodology.

Clarify the role of consensus
Consensus does not itself prove rational truths (like the world's origination or God's pre-eternality) because those are established by reason. Rather, consensus indicates that the scholars have a decisive proof (qati') for those truths, even if we don't know it in detail.

Refuting the Hiring Analogy and the Goodness of Obligating the Disbeliever
Author refutes hiring analogy and defends obligation of disbeliever.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why obligating the disbeliever is good despite apparent harm, and how the author refutes the analogy to a hiring contract.

Core Idea
Obligating the disbeliever is an opportunity for reward, not a trap.

The objector argues that rewarding work is like a hiring contract requiring consent. But the author turns this around: the rationalist view of divine justice faces a difficulty with the disbeliever's obligation. Yet he shows that the harm is self-inflicted and the obligation itself is a good because it enables reward—provided the outcome is not predetermined.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and analogy
Start by reading the first part of the passage where the objector compares work and reward to a hiring contract. Notice the key term 'ijāra' and the requirement of consent.
This analogy sets up the problem: if reward requires consent, how can God obligate the disbeliever?
2
Identify the problem for rationalist doctrine
The author mentions that the doctrine of rational evil (al-qabḥ al-ʿaqlī) creates a difficulty: obligating the disbeliever seems harmful without benefit.
The harm is the imposition of difficult actions leading to eternal punishment.
3
Compare with the believer's case
Notice the contrast: the believer's obligation brings immense benefits, while the disbeliever's seems only harmful. This highlights the need for a defense.
The distinction is key to understanding the author's answer.
4
Understand the author's defense
The author argues that the obligation is good because it is for the purpose of exposure (taʿrīḍ) and enabling (tamkīn)—an opportunity for reward. It is only truly good if the disbeliever's failure is not definitely known.
The punishment is due to the disbeliever's own choice, not the obligation itself.

Definitions of infallibility must preserve moral responsibility.

After presenting these views, it raises a critical objection that any definition which makes sin impossible also eliminates the basis for praise, reward, and obligation. Let's examine each definition and the objection.

The sixth objection against the Mu'tazilite principle of al-aslah.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This lesson presents the sixth objection against the Mu'tazilite claim that God must always do what is most beneficial for His servants. The Eighth Objection: Supplication Contradicts al-Aṣlaḥ
Eighth objection: supplication contradicts al-aslah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite dilemma regarding supplication and why it seemingly implies injustice.

Core Idea
If al-aslah is obligatory, supplication becomes a request for injustice.

The eighth objection targets the universal practice of supplication. If God must always do what is most beneficial, then asking Him to change a situation is asking Him to abandon the obligatory. This raises a serious contradiction for the Mu'tazilite position.

Guided Path
1
Read the eighth objection passage
Carefully read the Arabic and its translation. Notice the agreed practice of prophets, saints, and rational people: they all pray for relief from affliction.
This universal consensus is the starting point.
2
Identify the Mu'tazilite premise
Recall that the Mu'tazilites hold that God must do what is most beneficial (al-aslah) for His servants. This is the 'your' in the passage — referring to their principle.
You may need to revisit the lesson on al-aslah.
3
Extract the contradiction
If supplication is legitimate, it asks God to alter His decree. Under al-aslah, that would mean changing what is most beneficial and preventing the obligatory, which is inherently unjust. Hence the objection.
The conclusion 'that is injustice' is the key.
