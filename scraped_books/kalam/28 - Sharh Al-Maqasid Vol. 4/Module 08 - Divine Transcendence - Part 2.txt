Choosing Between Pain and Pleasure as Grace
Contrasts majority and Abu Hashim on choosing between pain and pleasure as grace.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the disagreement between the majority and Abu Hashim on whether pain can be chosen as grace when equal to pleasure.

Core Idea
When pain and pleasure are equal as grace, the majority prefers pleasure, but Abu Hashim allows choice.

The author presents a nuanced debate: if both pain and pleasure can serve as divine grace (lutf), which one should be chosen? The majority argues for pleasure because pain is only good when it is a necessary means for compensation and grace. Abu Hashim counters that pain, when serving as compensation and grace, is no longer futile or unjust, so one may choose freely.

Guided Path

Read the passage

2
Analyze the majority view
The majority holds that pleasure is determined and pain is ugly unless it becomes a path to compensation and grace.
They see pain as only good under necessity.
3
Analyze Abu Hashim's view
Abu Hashim argues that pain, when it serves as compensation and grace, is no longer futile or unjust, so one can choose between pain and pleasure as between two benefits.
This view elevates pain to the level of pleasure when it has a purpose.

And among them: That inflicting pain by God's command, as in the use of animals, or by His permission, as in their slaughter, or by His enabling while delaying justice to the abode of recompense, as in the case of the oppressed person whose compensation is upon God Almighty due to His being above injustice. And among them: That if pleasure and pain are equal in both being grace, then the majority holds that pleasure is determined, and pain is ugly, because it is only good if it is determined as a path to compensation and grace. And Abu Hashim said: Rather, one chooses from them as between two benefits, because inflicting pain, being compensation and grace, has departed from being futile and unjust.

Commentary
This passage presents two related points. First, the author notes that inflicting pain can occur by God's direct command (like using animals for work), by His permission (like slaughtering animals), or by His enabling while postponing justice to the afterlife, as in the case of the oppressed who will receive compensation from God, for God is above injustice. Second, when pain and pleasure are both equally capable of being a grace (lutf), the majority (al-jumhur) holds that pleasure is determined and pain is deemed ugly (qabih). Pain is only considered good when it becomes a necessary means for compensation and grace. Abu Hashim disagrees, arguing that one may choose between them as between two benefits, since inflicting pain, now having the purpose of compensation and grace, is no longer futile or unjust.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Pain can never be chosen over pleasure because it is always evil.
Correction: Abu Hashim argues that pain can be chosen when it serves as compensation and grace, as it then ceases to be futile or unjust.

Checkpoint
According to Abu Hashim, why is it permissible to choose pain over pleasure when both are equal as grace?
A) Because pain is always more beneficial
B) Because pain, as compensation and grace, is no longer futile or unjust

Key Takeaway
Abu Hashim's position is that pain, when purposeful as compensation and grace, can be freely chosen alongside pleasure.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
The majority argues that when pain and pleasure are equal as grace, pleasure must be chosen because pain is inherently ugly unless necessary. Abu Hashim says both are permissible. When you look back at a painful trial in your life, did you assume it was simply punishment or meaningless suffering? How might your faith deepen if you saw that trial as a potential grace from Allah, carrying a hidden compensation? Will you commit to asking Allah for the insight to recognise purposive grace in hardship?
?
Abu Hashim teaches that pain, when it serves as compensation and grace, sheds the qualities of futility and injustice. Is there any area of your life where you feel God has been unjust or futile in allowing your suffering? How does this lesson challenge you to reinterpret that experience? What step can you take this week to embrace the possibility that your pain may be a path to a greater gift from Allah?
