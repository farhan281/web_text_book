How Veneration of Images Leads to Shirk
Al-Taftazani cites a hadith and the story of Nuh's people regarding images leading to idolatry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the veneration of images of righteous people can gradually lead to idolatry, as shown by the hadith and the story of the people of Nuh.

Core Idea
Honoring the righteous can become worshipping them when images are venerated.

This lesson presents two pieces of evidence from Islamic tradition that demonstrate a dangerous progression: from honoring righteous figures to eventually worshipping them. The first is a hadith condemning the building of places of worship over graves with images. The second is the historical account of how the idols of Nuh's people originated as memorials of righteous men.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text carefully, noting the two separate narrations: the hadith from Aisha and the story from Ibn Abbas.
The passage is a single continuous argument, but it contains two distinct pieces of evidence.
2
Understand the hadith
The hadith criticizes those who build mosques over graves and place images. Ask: Why does the Prophet call such people 'the worst of creation'?
The condemnation is severe because it involves a practice that can lead to shirk.
3
Analyze the story of Nuh's people
Ibn Abbas explains that the idols of Nuh's people began as memorials to righteous men. The process from memorial to idol occurred when the original generation died and knowledge was lost.
This shows how good intentions (remembering the righteous) can be corrupted over time.
4
Connect to the lesson's core idea
Reflect on how both examples illustrate the same danger: venerating images of the righteous can lead to their worship when the original intent is forgotten.
This is the fifth interpretation mentioned earlier in Sharh al-Maqasid.

Core Idea
The challenge of the Quran is an eternal attribute, not a temporal property.

This lesson addresses a common objection to the doctrine of the Quran's eternity. If the Quran is eternal, how can a challenge (tahaddi) be a property of something temporal? The author answers by clarifying that the challenge itself is an eternal attribute, and it is impossible for the Arabs to meet it.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Objection
The objector argues that since the eternal is permanent, the challenge would be eternal and thus not a property of the temporal Quran. Notice the logic: eternal → permanent → challenge must be permanent too.
The objector assumes that if the Quran is eternal, its attribute of challenge must also be eternal.
2
Understand the Answer
The author clarifies that the challenge means Allah's call to the Arabs to produce something like the Quran. This call is impossible for them, and that impossibility is an attribute of the eternal speech, not of the created text.
The answer redefines what the challenge is: a divine call, which is an eternal act.

Abrogation Applies Only to the Temporal Aspect of Revelation
The author qualifies that abrogation applies only to the temporal, not the eternal speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that abrogation (naskh) applies only to the temporal aspect of revelation, not to the eternal divine speech itself.

Core Idea
Abrogation changes the temporal, not the eternal.

This short passage addresses a critical objection: if abrogation can affect both the wording and the meaning of a verse, does that imply change in God's eternal speech? The author agrees with the premise but restricts its scope to what is temporal, preserving the immutability of the divine attribute of speech.

Read the objection
Read the objection carefully: it claims that abrogation applies to both word and meaning. This sets up the challenge that abrogation might be seen as altering the eternal speech.
The objector uses the general principle of naskh.
2
Examine the response
The author agrees but immediately qualifies: abrogation is only for the temporal. Notice the concession 'نعم' followed by the limiting 'لكن'.
This is a classic kalam move—acknowledging the premise but restricting its domain.
3
Understand the reason
The author gives the reason: the eternal does not cease or come to an end. This is the core theological point: the eternal attribute of speech is immutable.
Consider the contrast between حادث (temporal) and قديم (eternal).

The Second Doubt: Ray and Impression

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The ray/impression doubt fails because it only applies to physical vision, not divine vision.

This passage presents the second major objection raised by those who deny that God can be seen. The objection relies on two classical theories of vision: the emission of a ray from the eye, or the impression of an image on the eye. The answer shows that even if these theories are accepted for physical objects, they do not apply to God, for the vision of God is different in kind or occurs through a different means.

The Third Doubt: Obstacle Objection and Its Answer
Refutes the third objection that if God were seeable, He would always be seen

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the objection that if God were seeable, He would always be seen, is refuted by distinguishing between intrinsic possibility and habitual certainty.

Core Idea
Not seeing God does not prove impossibility; it may be habitual knowledge.

This passage tackles the third doubt against the possibility of seeing Allah (beatific vision). The doubt claims that if seeing God were possible, every healthy person would see Him always. Since we do not see Him now, and the people of Paradise are not perpetually engaged in seeing Him, it must be impossible. The response cleverly distinguishes between intrinsic possibility and intellectual certainty based on habitual knowledge, showing that the absence of seeing is not a logical necessity but a matter of divine habitual practice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the doubt carefully: if seeing God were possible, it would be perpetual for every sound-sensed person in this world and the Hereafter. Since we don't see Him now and the people of Paradise are not always seeing Him, it seems impossible.
The objection relies on the principle that for unseen objects, only a healthy sense and the object's visibility are sufficient.
2
Analyze the implied consequence
Notice the argument: if the two conditions are met and seeing does not occur, then it would be possible for huge mountains to exist right in front of us but unseen, which is considered absurd. This is supposed to prove that seeing God must be impossible.
This is called the 'doubt of obstacles' because it claims conditions are sufficient.
3
Examine the answer's first part
The answer distinguishes two meanings of 'possibility'. If the opponent means intrinsic possibility (that such a scenario could happen by divine power), then it is indeed possible, not absurd. God could create us ignorant of the mountains. The conditional is not a logical necessity.
4
Examine the answer's second part
If the opponent means intellectual certainty (that the intellect would not be sure of the mountains' non-existence), then the implication is denied. The non-existence of such mountains is known by habitual necessary knowledge, not by reason alone.
This habitual knowledge comes from Allah creating certainty, not from logical proof.
5
Apply the distinction to the original doubt
The original doubt fails because the absence of seeing God in this world is a matter of habitual divine practice, not a logical impossibility. Similarly, the people of Paradise can see Him intermittently while enjoying other pleasures, as texts indicate.

The Generality of Negation and the Definition of Idrāk
First two responses to the objection that the verse negates ruʾya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ashʿarīs defend the possibility of seeing Allah by arguing that the negation in 'Vision perceives Him not' is not absolute, and that idrāk means encompassing, not mere vision.

Core Idea
Negating idrāk does not negate ruʾya; they are not the same.

Before we accept that the verse rules out seeing Allah, we must examine two key objections. The first concerns whether the negation is permanent across all states, and the second, more crucial, concerns the meaning of 'idrāk'. This lesson unpacks these two responses and shows how they open the door for ruʾya.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the two distinct responses: one about time and one about meaning.
The passage includes a further objection and answer after the second response.
2
Understand the first response
The first response argues that the negation may be limited to this world. Self-praise through negating vision can be temporary because it relates to acts, not essence.
Vision is an act Allah may create or not; its absence now does not imply impossibility later.
3
Grasp the second response
The second response distinguishes idrāk (encompassing) from ruʾya (vision). Negating idrāk does not negate ruʾya, just as negating encompassing knowledge does not negate knowledge.
The linguistic analysis shows idrāk implies reaching and encompassing, which is impossible for Allah, but vision without encompassing is possible.

The objection asks: if idrāk is impossible for Allah, why mention it? The answer: it is self-praise (tamadduḥ) by negating deficiencies, and Allah's perceiving the sights means He sees them or knows them.
This shows the verse is about transcendence (tanzīh), not impossibility of ruʾya.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained theological argument defending the Ashʿarī position that the Qur'anic verse 'لا تدركه الأبصار' (Vision perceives Him not) does not negate the possibility of seeing Allah (ruʾya) in the Hereafter, but rather negates comprehensive perception (idrāk) which implies encompassing. The passage contains two main parts: a three-part response to an objection, and then a positive argument from the verse that it actually implies the permissibility of ruʾya.

The first part begins with the objection: the verse is self-praise, and what brings praise must be permanent. The answer distinguishes between essential attributes (which are permanent) and acts (which may be created or not). Vision is an act, so its absence in this world does not negate its possibility later. Even if the negation were general, it would only yield probability, not certainty in theoretical matters.

The second response defines idrāk as encompassing (iḥāṭa), not mere vision. Linguistic examples show that one can see something without 'perceiving' it if obscured. Thus, negating idrāk does not negate ruʾya. This also refutes the claim that negating idrāk implies ruʾya is a deficiency.

The third response distinguishes between 'perception of the sights' (idrāk al-abṣār) and 'perception of the seers' (idrāk al-mubṣirīn). Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of rizq by addressing the Mu'tazila objection that unlawful things (haram) cannot be considered rizq. First, the author clarifies the term 'servant' in the definition: it includes animals by overriding. He then criticizes the definition of rizq as 'ownership' because it is not universal (it includes God's ownership, which is not rizq) and not exclusive (it excludes provision of animals). He suggests an alternative: the owned in the sense of permission for legal disposal, which includes a relation to the Provider. Then he turns to the Mu'tazila position: since rizq is attributed to God alone, the unlawful that is benefited from cannot be rizq because it is repulsive (qabih). The author refutes this by noting the corruption of the Mu'tazila principle: it would imply that someone who only eats unlawful his whole life has not been provided by God, contradicting the Qur'anic verse 'And there is no creature on earth except that upon God is its provision' (11:6). The Mu'tazila further object: if unlawful were rizq, it would not be permissible to repel it, nor would there be blame or punishment for it. He then categorizes the ruling of seeking provision: it can be obligatory (in need), recommended (to expand comfort), permissible (to increase without sin), or prohibited (when done through unlawful means like usurpation, theft, usury).

Refuting Mu'tazila on Haram as Rizq
Al-Taftazani refutes Mu'tazila on haram not being rizq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila argument that haram cannot be rizq due to its repulsiveness, and the Sunni refutation using the Qur'an and logical consequences.

Core Idea
Mu'tazila error: if haram is not rizq, then those who only eat haram were not provided by God.

After clarifying definitions, al-Taftazani directly confronts the Mu'tazila claim that haram is not rizq. They argue that because rizq is from God alone, something repulsive (qabih) cannot be attributed to Him. The author refutes this by showing its absurd consequence and citing a Qur'anic verse that affirms God's provision for all creatures.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila claim and refutation
Read from 'ولما كان الرزق مضافاً' to 'فجوابكم جوابنا'. The Mu'tazila say haram is not rizq due to repulsiveness. Al-Taftazani refutes by noting the corruption of their principle and citing the verse.
The verse is Q11:6 (Hud: 6).
2
Understand the logical consequence
If only lawful is rizq, then a person who only eats haram his whole life was not provided by God. This is absurd because God provides for all creatures.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Analyze the counter-objection and response
An opponent says: God provided much permissible but the person turned away. Al-Taftazani counters with the case of one who dies without eating anything at all—then God didn't provide? This refutes the objection.
The phrase 'فجوابكم جوابنا' means 'your answer is our answer'.

Answering Mu'tazila Objections and Rulings of Provision-Seeking
Al-Taftazani answers Mu'tazila objection and classifies rulings of seeking provision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazila objection that if haram is rizq, it would not be blameworthy, and al-Taftazani's response, including the various rulings on seeking provision.

Core Idea
Blame and punishment are for the prohibited means, not for the rizq itself.

The Mu'tazila raise a further objection: if haram is truly rizq, then it should be permissible to defend it, and there should be no blame or punishment for it. Al-Taftazani responds by distinguishing the rizq from the means of acquiring it. He then elaborates on the rulings of seeking provision, showing when it is obligatory, recommended, permissible, or prohibited.

Guided Path
1
Read the Mu'tazila objection
They say: if haram is rizq, then it cannot be repelled, nor blame or punishment for it. Read the Arabic carefully.
The objection assumes that rizq is something good that should be accepted.
2
Understand al-Taftazani's response
He says 'denied' (ممنوع) and explains that blame and punishment are due to committing the prohibited act and acquiring evil, not due to the rizq itself.
The rizq is what sustains; the sin is in how it is obtained.
3
Learn the rulings of seeking provision
Al-Taftazani categorizes seeking provision: obligatory (need), recommended (expanding comfort), permissible (increase without sin), prohibited (through forbidden means).
Examples: usurpation, theft, usury.

Understanding the Concept of Al-Jaza' (Recompense)
Author announces the third section and defines al-jaza'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of al-jaza' as reward for obedience and punishment for disobedience, and where it fits in the broader structure of theological topics.
