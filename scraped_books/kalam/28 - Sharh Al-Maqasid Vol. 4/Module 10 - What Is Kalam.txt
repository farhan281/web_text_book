Definition of Tanzeehat and the First Research Topic
Commentator defines tanzeehat and elaborates on the first research topic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the meaning of tanzeehat and the argument for negating composition from the necessary being.

Core Idea
The necessary being cannot be composite because composition implies need and contingency.

In this passage, the commentator introduces the second chapter on transcendences (tanzeehat), defining them as negating from Allah what is unbecoming of Him. He then begins the first research topic: negating multiplicity from Him, both in terms of parts and in terms of individuals. He provides a rational proof against composition, showing that any composite is dependent on its parts and therefore contingent, which cannot apply to the Necessary Being.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of tanzeehat
Read the passage where the commentator states 'tanzeehat' means negating what is unbefitting of the necessary being. This sets the scope of the chapter.
Tanzeehat is a key term in kalam referring to God's transcendence.
2
Identify the two aspects of multiplicity
Notice that the first investigation covers two types of multiplicity: composition from parts (internal) and multiple necessary beings (external). Both are negated.
3
Analyze the proof against composition
Trace the argument: every composite needs its parts, which are other than itself; anything needing another is contingent because its essence alone is insufficient for existence. Thus, the necessary being cannot be composite.
The proof works even if the parts are not external agents.

And he argued for the negation of composition, by saying that every composite is in need of the part that is other than itself, and everything in need of another is possible, because its essence without consideration of the other is not sufficient for its existence, even if that other is not an external agent for it.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining the meaning of 'tanzeehat' (transcendences) and elaborating on the first research topic. The commentator defines 'tanzeehat' as the negation of anything unbefitting the necessary being. Then he clarifies the first investigation: negating multiplicity both in terms of parts (composition) and in terms of individuals (multiple gods). He then presents the proof against composition: any composite entity is dependent on its parts, which are other than itself; anything dependent on another is contingent (possible), because its own essence without considering the other is insufficient for existence. This holds even if the other is not an external agent. Thus, the necessary being cannot be composite.

The Reality of Tawhid and Its Essential Properties
Al-Taftazani defines tawhid and enumerates agreed divine properties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Taftazani's definition of tawhid and the four properties of divinity agreed upon by all Muslims.

Core Idea
Tawhid means believing that no partner shares in divinity or its unique properties.

Al-Taftazani begins by defining tawhid not merely as belief in one God, but as acknowledging that all exclusive properties of divinity belong to Him alone. He then lists the properties that all Muslims agree belong exclusively to Allah, forming the foundation for further theological discussion.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic definition
Read the first part of the passage where al-Taftazani gives the definition of tawhid.
Notice that he includes 'khawass' (specific properties) as part of the definition.
2
Identify the agreed properties
Find the four properties that all Muslims agree are exclusive to Allah.
They are: management of the world, creation of bodies, deserving worship, and eternity of what subsists by itself.
3
Reflect on the scope of tawhid
Think about how this definition goes beyond essence to include actions and attributes.
This sets the stage for later disagreements about attributes.

We intend by 'eternity' the meaning of not being preceded by non-existence. And as for the meaning of not being preceded by another, it is divinity itself and necessary existence. We only affirm eternal attributes, not essences, yet we do not make the attribute other than the essence. Yes, their delegation of the management of a portion of the world's events, namely evils and abominations, to Satan, contrary to the will of God—even if by His empowering and enabling—is a difficult matter. And more difficult than that is the saying of the philosophers about the eternity of intellects, their bringing into existence of souls and some bodies, and the delegation of the management of the world of elements to them and to the celestial spheres. And the Ahl al-Sunna [exaggerate] concerning the negation of multiplicity of the Creator. And all agree on negating multiplicity of the Necessary Being, the One deserving worship, and the Bringer-into-existence of the body.

Commentary
This passage is a definition of tawhid followed by a comparative analysis of the positions of Ahl al-Sunna, Mu'tazila, and philosophers. Al-Taftazani begins by defining the reality of tawhid as the belief that there is no partner in uluhiyya (divinity) and its particular properties (khawass). He clarifies that all Muslims agree on certain properties belonging exclusively to God: management of the world, creation of bodies, deservingness of worship, and eternity of what subsists by itself. He then explains the meaning of 'eternity' (qidam): literal precedence in nonexistence, and relational precedence in being uncaused. The latter is divinity itself. He then distinguishes the Sunni position: they affirm eternal attributes but not eternal essences, and they do not separate attribute from essence. The Mu'tazila affirm that servants create their own actions but not other accidents or bodies. Al-Taftazani criticizes the Mu'tazila for delegating some events to Satan, though he acknowledges it is a difficult matter. He then says that the philosophers' view is even more problematic: they affirm eternal intellects, which bring souls and bodies into existence, and delegate the management of the sublunar world to intellects and spheres. The conclusion is that tawhid for all these groups reduces to the oneness of the Necessary Being, but the Mu'tazila focus on denying multiplicity of the eternal, while Ahl al-Sunna focus on denying multiplicity of the Creator. Ultimately, all agree on negating multiplicity of the Necessary, the Worshipped, and the Creator of bodies.

The Meaning of Qidam and the Sunni Position on Attributes
Al-Taftazani explains qidam and the Sunni position on attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two meanings of qidam (eternity) and how Ahl al-Sunna affirm eternal attributes without making them separate entities.

Core Idea
Qidam means not preceded by non-existence; the Sunni position affirms eternal attributes but not eternal essences.

Al-Taftazani clarifies the term 'qidam' (eternity) which appears in the list of divine properties. He distinguishes two meanings: not being preceded by non-existence, and not being preceded by another (i.e., being uncaused). He then presents the Sunni view on attributes: they are eternal but not distinct essences, and the attribute is not other than the essence.

Guided Path

Read the clarification on qidam

2
Identify which meaning pertains to divinity
Notice that the second meaning (uncaused) is identified as divinity itself and necessary existence.
3
Understand the Sunni position on attributes
Read the sentence where al-Taftazani states the Sunni view: we affirm eternal attributes, not eternal essences, and attribute is not other than essence.
This contrasts with Mu'tazila who deny eternal attributes.

Mu'tazila on Human Actions and Delegation to Satan
Al-Taftazani presents and critiques the Mu'tazila position on human action and delegation to Satan.

Learning Objective

Al-Taftazani turns to the Mu'tazila, who hold that servants create their own actions (but not other things) and that some events are delegated to Satan. He criticizes this as a difficult matter, preparing the ground for his concluding comparison.

Philosophers' Position and Concluding Comparison of Tawhid
Al-Taftazani criticizes philosophers and compares tawhid conceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' view on eternal intellects and delegation, and how tawhid is conceived by Sunnis, Mu'tazila, and philosophers.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument defining and defending the concept of inner speech (kalam al-nafs) as a meaning in the soul distinct from knowledge and will. The author begins by responding to the second objection against attributing speech to God—that speech consists of arranged audible letters, which cannot subsist in God's essence. He then quotes the matn (the base text) stating that one who commands, forbids, or informs finds in himself a meaning other than knowledge and will, which is commonly called speech. The author explains that when a person utters commands, prohibitions, etc., he finds meanings in himself, then expresses them through sensory words. That meaning, which does not change with different expressions, is what is called inner speech. He notes that Abu Hashim (a Mu'tazili) may acknowledge it as 'thoughts' (khawatir). The distinction from knowledge and will is especially clear in informative and non-demanding utterances. A potential misconception is raised: that inner demand is identical to will, and that it would be contradictory to say 'I want from you this action but I do not demand it from myself'—but the author postpones this to the chapter on actions. He then provides two proofs: (1) that a person can inform about something he knows to be false, showing inner speech is distinct from knowledge; (2) that a master can command a slave while not actually wanting the action, showing inner speech is distinct from will. He cites al-Mawaqif, stating that if the Mu'tazila had said that inner speech is a will that causes belief, it would be plausible, but he did not find that in their texts; however, al-Zahidi's statement suggests it. The author then refutes the objection that what one finds in himself is the will to make the utterance a command: this is false because the word perishes while the demand remains, and one can regret a past action, which is not the same as wanting it; moreover, the word translates the inner meaning of requirement or obligation. Finally, he cites common linguistic usage and a line of poetry by al-Akhtal to show that speech is said to be in the heart, with the tongue as its guide.
