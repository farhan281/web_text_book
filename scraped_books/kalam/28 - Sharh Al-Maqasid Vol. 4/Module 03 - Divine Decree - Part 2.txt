Refutation of Ka'bi's Denial that the Slain is Dead
Ka'bi's objection and refutation on the distinction between killing and death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Sunni reply to al-Ka'bi distinguishes between killing as a human act and death as a divine act, and how this preserves both human responsibility and divine omnipotence.

Core Idea
Killing is human; death is divine—both occur in the same event.

Al-Ka'bi, a Mu'tazili theologian, argued that the slain person is not truly dead because killing is a human action while death belongs exclusively to God. This objection challenges the coherence of divine predestination and human agency. In this lesson, we examine the classical refutation that distinguishes between the act of killing (in the killer) and the event of death (in the victim), showing how God creates death following the killing by His custom.

Commentary
This passage presents a complex objection-and-answer discussion regarding the appointed term (ajal) of a person who is killed. The author clarifies the Ash'ari position that the killed person dies at his single, unchangeable term determined by God's knowledge, addressing possible objections. First, an objection is raised: if God knows someone will not be killed, then non-killing is possible, but if He knows he will be killed, then killing is necessary, leading to an impossibility? The answer is that no impossibility results because the term is determined by God's knowledge and is not changed by the act. Then a further objection is considered: if the term is the time of death in God's knowledge, does the killed person die at his term? The author explains that the term is the moment when life necessarily ends, without any possibility of delay or advance, as indicated by Quranic verse. The disagreement among scholars is whether this applies exactly to the killed person or whether his term is conditional on being killed. Three Arguments Against the Obligation of al-Aslah
Three rational arguments against the obligation of al-aslah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand three rational arguments that challenge the view that God is obligated to do the most beneficial for His servants.

Core Idea
Claiming God must always do what is best leads to impossible contradictions.

This passage presents three powerful objections to the doctrine that God is obligated to do the most beneficial (al-aslah) for His creatures. Even if we accept that something can be obligatory upon God, the arguments show that applying this notion consistently leads to absurdities. The first argument points to the existence of suffering disbelievers; the second argues from analogy to human duties; the third uses the case of disbelievers' punishment. Together, they undermine the claim that al-aslah is a divine obligation.

Guided Path
1
First Argument: The existence of suffering
Read the first argument carefully. Notice how the author uses the existence of disbelievers and afflicted people to show that if God were obligated to do the most beneficial, He would not have created them.
This argument appeals to observable reality against the doctrine.
2

Analyze the second argument. The author draws an analogy: if God must do the most beneficial for His servants, then every person must do the most beneficial for his own dependents. This leads to an infinite regress or contradiction. Consider the objection and response about rewards outweighing hardships.
The objection about reward is answered by symmetry: the same logic applies to God's actions.
