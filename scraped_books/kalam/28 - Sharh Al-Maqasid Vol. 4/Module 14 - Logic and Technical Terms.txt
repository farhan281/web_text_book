Sufi Definitions of Fanā'
Author enumerates definitions and signs of fanā'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key Sufi definitions of fanā' (annihilation) and its associated signs, distinguishing it from literal extinction.

Core Idea
Fanā' is transformed awareness, not ontological annihilation.

The Sufi concept of fanā' is often misunderstood as complete annihilation of existence. In this passage, the author presents several definitions from Sufi tradition that clarify fanā' as a state of spiritual transformation: loss of self-awareness, replacement of human attributes with divine ones, and cessation of blameworthy qualities. The signs and final stage further illuminate this mystical journey.

The definitions are introduced by 'or it is said' (وقيل).
3
Study the signs and final stage
Examine the signs of fanā' given in the passage, including loss of worldly share and detachment from causes. Understand the final stage: seeing nothing but Allah.
4
Reflect on the common misconception
Consider how these definitions show that fanā' is not physical annihilation but a transformation of perception and attributes.

Defining Otherness: The Two Others (Al-Ghayran)
Al-Taftazani defines al-ghayran and critiques the view that a part is other than the whole.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalām definition of al-ghayran and the controversy over whether a part is other than the whole.

Core Idea
Otherness means two things are not identical, but applying it to parts and wholes is problematic.

This lesson examines the technical meaning of al-ghayran in Islamic theology. Al-Taftazānī presents the standard definition—two things considered from the perspective that one is not the other—and then addresses the unusual view of the Mu‘tazilite Ja‘far ibn Ḥarb, who claimed that a part is other than the whole. By analyzing the example of ten and one, the passage shows why this view leads to contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition
Read the opening definition of al-ghayran as two things not identical.
It's a relational definition.
2
Examine the whole-part example
See how the example of ten and one illustrates the definition and the problem with Ja'far's claim.
The argument uses the concept of self-reference.
3
Note the linguistic criticism
Read the strange statement about the usage of al-ghayran and al-ithnan.
It shows how careful theologians are with terms.

قال صاحب التبصرة، وكذا تفسيرهما بالشيئين من حيث إن أحدهما ليس هو الآخر، لصدقه على الكل مع الجزء كالعشرة مع الواحد، وزيد مع رأسه مع أنه لم يقل أحد يكون الجزء غير الكل إلا جعفر بن حرب من المعتزلة، وعد هذا من جهالاته، لأن العشرة اسم للمجموع لا يتناول لكل فرد مع أغياره فلو كان الواحد غير العشرة لصار غير نفسه، لأنه من العشرة؛ ولن تكون العشرة بدونه وقال أيضاً: كل الشيء ليس غيره، لأن الشيء لا يغاير نفسه. وأعجب من هذا من قال : لو كان الغيران هما الاثنان لكان الغيران والاثنان ليس بمستعمل، والغير مستعمل . والقول ما قال إمام الحرمين : إن إيضاح معنى الغيرين مما لا يدل عليه قضية ولا دلالة قطعية سمعية، فلا يقطع ببطلان قول من قال : كل شيئين غيران . نعم يقطع بالمنع من إطلاق الغيرية في صفات الباري وذاته لاتفاق الأمة على ذلك. ثم قال : ولا يتحاشى من إطلاق القول بأن الصفات موجودات ، والعلم مع الذات موجودان وكذا جميع الصفات، فظهر أن القول بالتعدد لا يتوقف على القول بالتغاير. فقولنا : ولو سلم معناه لو سلم التغاير، أو التعدد بدون التغاير.
The author of al-Tabsira said, and likewise their interpretation as two things from the perspective that one is not the other, because it applies to the whole with the part, like ten with one, and Zayd with his head, although no one said that the part is other than the whole except Ja'far ibn Harb of the Mu'tazila, and this is counted among his ignorances, because 'ten' is a name for the totality, it does not apply to each individual with its others; so if the one were other than ten, it would become other than itself, because it is part of ten, and ten would not exist without it. And he also said: Everything is not other than it, because a thing does not differ from itself. And more astonishing than this is the one who said: If 'the two others' (al-ghayran) means the two, then 'the two others' and 'the two' are not used, while 'the other' (al-ghayr) is used. And the correct statement is what Imam al-Haramayn said: Clarifying the meaning of 'the two others' is something that neither a categorical proof nor a definitive transmitted proof indicates, so one cannot definitively judge the falsehood of the one who says 'every two things are two others'. Yes, one definitively prohibits applying otherness (al-ghayriyya) to the attributes of the Creator and His essence, due to the consensus of the nation on that. Then he said: And one does not refrain from stating absolutely that the attributes are existents, and knowledge together with the essence are two existents, and likewise all attributes, so it becomes clear that the statement of multiplicity does not depend on the statement of otherness. So our statement 'and if it is conceded' means: if otherness is conceded, or multiplicity without otherness.
Source pages p. 81

Commentary
This passage discusses the concept of al-ghayran (the two others) and its application to the divine attributes. The passage begins by presenting the interpretation of al-ghayran as two things considered from the aspect that one is not the other. Examples are given: the number ten and the number one, or Zayd and his head. The point is that in such pairs, one is not identical to the other. The author then notes that Ja'far ibn Harb, a Mu'tazilite, claimed that a part is other than the whole, but this is considered ignorance. The reason is that 'ten' is a collective noun that does not refer to each individual part separately; if one were other than ten, it would be other than itself because it is part of ten, and ten cannot exist without the one. Then a strange statement is mentioned: someone said that if al-ghayran means 'two', then the word 'al-ghayran' and 'al-ithnan' are not used while 'al-ghayr' (the other) is used. This appears to be a linguistic criticism. The passage then quotes Imam al-Haramayn, who says that the exact meaning of al-ghayran cannot be proven by a definitive rational proof (qadiyya) or a definitive transmitted proof (dalala qat'iyya sam'iyya). Therefore, one cannot definitively reject the view that every two things are 'two others'. However, he affirms that it is definitively prohibited to apply otherness (al-ghayriyya) to God's attributes and essence due to the consensus of the ummah. Then he adds that one may freely state that the attributes are existents, and that knowledge and the essence are two existents, and similarly all attributes, showing that multiplicity (al-ta'addud) does not depend on otherness (al-taghayur). The passage concludes by explaining the phrase 'wa law sallim' (and if it is conceded) as meaning either conceding otherness itself, or conceding multiplicity without otherness.

Multiplicity of Attributes Without Otherness
Imam al-Haramayn states that otherness cannot be definitively proven nor disproven, but its application to God is forbidden by consensus, and multiplicity does not require otherness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that affirming multiple divine attributes does not imply otherness among them, and why otherness is prohibited in God.

Core Idea
We affirm many attributes but deny that they are 'other' than the essence.

This lesson continues from the definition of otherness to its application to God. Al-Taftazānī cites Imam al-Haramayn, who asserts that while the exact meaning of 'two others' cannot be definitively proven or disproven rationally or scripturally, it is absolutely forbidden to apply otherness to God and His attributes due to consensus. He then makes the key point: one can affirm that the attributes are many existents without claiming they are 'other' than the essence. Thus multiplicity does not depend on otherness.

Guided Path
1
Understand Imam al-Haramayn's position
Read the passage from 'al-qawl ma qala Imam al-Haramayn' to understand that the meaning of al-ghayran is not subject to definitive proof.
This is a crucial epistemological claim.
2
Note the consensus on God
Although the definition is uncertain, there is a definitive prohibition against applying otherness to God's attributes and essence, based on consensus.
This safeguards divine simplicity.
3
Grasp the conclusion
Read the final part where it says multiplicity does not depend on otherness. Understand that 'multiplicity without otherness' is the correct stance.
This resolves the apparent tension between many attributes and divine unity.

Linguistic Analysis of the Term 'Shaytan'
The author defines shaytan and discusses its etymology and the three interpretations of the verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the linguistic definition of shaytan, its etymological roots, and the three interpretations of the Quranic phrase 'heads of the shayateen'.

Core Idea
Shaytan refers not only to Satan but to any rebellious being, including snakes and plants.

In this passage, the author provides a linguistic and exegetical analysis of the word 'shaytan'. He begins with a broad definition encompassing insolent beings from humans, jinn, and animals. Then he explores a Quranic verse where the fruit of the Zaqqum tree is likened to the heads of devils, citing three interpretations from al-Farra'. Finally, he delves into the etymology, debating whether the nun is a root letter or an addition. This lesson will unpack each of these layers.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of shaytan
Read the opening sentence carefully: shaytan is defined as any insolent, rebellious being among humans, jinn, and animals. Note that even snakes are called shaytan in Arabic.
This broad definition sets the stage for the later interpretations.
2
Examine the three interpretations of the verse
The author cites al-Farra' on the Quranic phrase 'heads of the shayateen'. There are three views: likening the fruit to ugly heads of devils, referring to an ugly snake, or referring to an ugly plant. Identify each view.
These interpretations show how the term 'shaytan' is used in Arabic to describe ugliness.
3
Understand the etymology debate
The passage discusses whether the nun in 'shaytan' is a root letter or extra. If from 'tashayyana', it follows the pattern fa'yal and is declined; if from 'tashayyata', it follows fa'lan and is not declined. Grasp the two possibilities and their grammatical implications.
This linguistic analysis demonstrates the importance of word patterns in Arabic.
4
Reflect on the broader meaning

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer followed by a detailed linguistic analysis and conclusion. The objection states that if the definite plural 'al-jam‘' indicates generality, then entering negation upon it yields 'negation of generality' (salb al-‘umum), i.e., partial negation, not 'generality of negation' (umum al-salb), i.e., total negation. Thus the verse 'لا يراه أحد' (no one sees Him) would mean 'not everyone sees Him', which is true because disbelievers do not see Him. The answer begins by asserting that just as the definite plural is used for negation of generality, it is also used for generality of negation, citing Quranic examples such as 'وما الله يريد ظلماً للعباد' (Allah does not desire injustice for the servants) – which means no injustice at all – and 'ولا تطع الكافرين والمنافقين' (do not obey the disbelievers and hypocrites) – meaning do not obey any of them. The author then provides a rigorous linguistic principle (tahqīq): if the relation to the whole is considered first and then negated, it is salb al-‘umum; if negation is considered first and then related to the whole, it is umum al-salb. This applies to all restrictions. He illustrates with examples of negation with purpose clauses ('I did not strike him as discipline' meaning I struck him but not for discipline, vs 'I did not strike him to honor him' meaning I left off striking for honor), and with modal clauses ('He did not come riding' meaning he came but not riding, vs 'He performed Hajj while able' is not given directly but analogous). He then extends this to the indefinite in negative context, noting that an indefinite like 'rajul' in 'ma ja'a rajul' (no man came) indicates generality only when the indefinite is attached to the verb as subject; but in constructions like 'la yahsin min al-fatiha harfan' (does not know a letter of the Fatiha), the negation is attached to the verb, and the indefinite is not general. He distinguishes real vs. metaphorical attribution of a negated verb to non-agent/object. Further, he discusses the object of prohibition (mutā‘allaq al-nahy) – whether it restricts the prohibited thing or restricts the prohibition itself – and similarly for commands. He then concludes that this principle, though not fully elaborated by earlier scholars, is essential. Finally, he states that once this is established, the definite plural with the definite article in a negative context is used in the Quran exclusively for generality of negation (umum al-salb), which is the correct interpretation for the verse about seeing God.

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer followed by a detailed linguistic analysis and conclusion. The objection states that if the definite plural 'al-jam‘' indicates generality, then entering negation upon it yields 'negation of generality' (salb al-‘umum), i.e., partial negation, not 'generality of negation' (umum al-salb), i.e., total negation. Thus the verse 'لا يراه أحد' (no one sees Him) would mean 'not everyone sees Him', which is true because disbelievers do not see Him. The answer begins by asserting that just as the definite plural is used for negation of generality, it is also used for generality of negation, citing Quranic examples such as 'وما الله يريد ظلماً للعباد' (Allah does not desire injustice for the servants) – which means no injustice at all – and 'ولا تطع الكافرين والمنافقين' (do not obey the disbelievers and hypocrites) – meaning do not obey any of them. The author then provides a rigorous linguistic principle (tahqīq): if the relation to the whole is considered first and then negated, it is salb al-‘umum; if negation is considered first and then related to the whole, it is umum al-salb. This applies to all restrictions. He illustrates with examples of negation with purpose clauses ('I did not strike him as discipline' meaning I struck him but not for discipline, vs 'I did not strike him to honor him' meaning I left off striking for honor), and with modal clauses ('He did not come riding' meaning he came but not riding, vs 'He performed Hajj while able' is not given directly but analogous). He then extends this to the indefinite in negative context, noting that an indefinite like 'rajul' in 'ma ja'a rajul' (no man came) indicates generality only when the indefinite is attached to the verb as subject; but in constructions like 'la yahsin min al-fatiha harfan' (does not know a letter of the Fatiha), the negation is attached to the verb, and the indefinite is not general. He distinguishes real vs. metaphorical attribution of a negated verb to non-agent/object. Further, he discusses the object of prohibition (mutā‘allaq al-nahy) – whether it restricts the prohibited thing or restricts the prohibition itself – and similarly for commands. He then concludes that this principle, though not fully elaborated by earlier scholars, is essential. Finally, he states that once this is established, the definite plural with the definite article in a negative context is used in the Quran exclusively for generality of negation (umum al-salb), which is the correct interpretation for the verse about seeing God.

Indefinite in Negative Context and Attribution
Al-Taftazani explains the generality of indefinite in negative context and real vs. metaphorical attribution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand when an indefinite noun in a negative context indicates generality, and the distinction between real and metaphorical attribution of a negated verb to a non-agent.

Core Idea
An indefinite in a negative context only generalizes when attached to the verb as subject; otherwise it does not imply generality.

Al-Taftazani extends his principle to the case of indefinite nouns in negative sentences. He also clarifies a subtle point about attributing a negated verb to something other than its agent, distinguishing between real and metaphorical usage.

Guided Path
1
Read the rule about indefinite
Notice that an indefinite like 'rajul' in 'ma ja'a rajul' (no man came) indicates generality because it is attached to the verb as subject.
But in 'la yuhsinu min al-Fatiha harfan' (does not know a letter), the indefinite is not attached to the verb directly and does not indicate generality.
2
Understand real vs metaphorical attribution
When a negated verb is attributed to a non-agent (e.g., 'ma nama al-laylu' meaning the night did not sleep), it can be real (denying the attribution) or metaphorical (attributing the negation to the subject).
Example: 'ma nama layli' (my night did not sleep) is metaphorical, meaning 'I stayed awake'.
3
Apply the distinction
Recognize that the interpretation depends on whether the speaker intends to negate the action or to attribute the negation to the subject.
This affects the scope of negation.

Meaning: It is not said: If the definite plural is for generality, then entering negation upon it yields negation of generality and denial of inclusiveness, according to the meaning of partial negation, not generality of negation and inclusiveness of denial, which is total negation. So it would not be an announcement that no one sees Him, but rather that not everyone sees Him, and the matter is so because the disbelievers do not see Him. Its verification is that if the relation to the whole is considered first and then negated, it is for negation of generality; and if negation is considered first and then related to the whole, it is for generality of negation. And likewise all restrictions, even that speech comprising a negation and a restriction may be for negating the restriction or for restricting the negation. For example: 'I did not strike him as discipline' – i.e., but as insult – is a negation of the cause and the action applies [to the verb]; and 'I did not strike him to honor him' – i.e., I left off striking him for honor – is a causation of the negation and the action applies to the negation. And 'He did not come to me riding' – i.e., but walking – is a negation of the modality; and 'He did not perform Hajj while able' – i.e., he left Hajj despite ability – is a modalization of the negation. Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer followed by a detailed linguistic analysis and conclusion. The objection states that if the definite plural 'al-jam‘' indicates generality, then entering negation upon it yields 'negation of generality' (salb al-‘umum), i.e., partial negation, not 'generality of negation' (umum al-salb), i.e., total negation. Thus the verse 'لا يراه أحد' (no one sees Him) would mean 'not everyone sees Him', which is true because disbelievers do not see Him. The answer begins by asserting that just as the definite plural is used for negation of generality, it is also used for generality of negation, citing Quranic examples such as 'وما الله يريد ظلماً للعباد' (Allah does not desire injustice for the servants) – which means no injustice at all – and 'ولا تطع الكافرين والمنافقين' (do not obey the disbelievers and hypocrites) – meaning do not obey any of them. The author then provides a rigorous linguistic principle (tahqīq): if the relation to the whole is considered first and then negated, it is salb al-‘umum; if negation is considered first and then related to the whole, it is umum al-salb. This applies to all restrictions. He illustrates with examples of negation with purpose clauses ('I did not strike him as discipline' meaning I struck him but not for discipline, vs 'I did not strike him to honor him' meaning I left off striking for honor), and with modal clauses ('He did not come riding' meaning he came but not riding, vs 'He performed Hajj while able' is not given directly but analogous). He then extends this to the indefinite in negative context, noting that an indefinite like 'rajul' in 'ma ja'a rajul' (no man came) indicates generality only when the indefinite is attached to the verb as subject; but in constructions like 'la yahsin min al-fatiha harfan' (does not know a letter of the Fatiha), the negation is attached to the verb, and the indefinite is not general. He distinguishes real vs. metaphorical attribution of a negated verb to non-agent/object. Further, he discusses the object of prohibition (mutā‘allaq al-nahy) – whether it restricts the prohibited thing or restricts the prohibition itself – and similarly for commands. He then concludes that this principle, though not fully elaborated by earlier scholars, is essential. Finally, he states that once this is established, the definite plural with the definite article in a negative context is used in the Quran exclusively for generality of negation (umum al-salb), which is the correct interpretation for the verse about seeing God.

Core Idea
Confusing truth as a state of a judgment with truth as a judgment creates paradoxes.

Logical paradoxes can arise when statements refer to themselves across time. Al-Taftazani presents a famous example involving utterances about 'tomorrow' and 'yesterday' that leads to a contradiction. His resolution hinges on a subtle distinction in how truth and falsehood function in propositions—either as properties of the judgment's relation or as predicates applied to statements. This lesson unpacks both the fallacy and its solution.

This distinction is key to resolving the paradox.

Distinction between Naming and Attributing
Taftazani distinguishes naming from attributing and cites Ghazali's view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key difference between naming God and describing Him with attributes, and why naming requires authority while describing does not.

Core Idea
Naming requires authority; describing requires truthfulness.

In Islamic theology, how we speak about God matters. There is a crucial distinction between giving God a name (tasmiya) and applying an attribute to Him (ijra' al-sifat). This passage, from Taftazani's Sharh al-Maqasid, clarifies that naming is a disposal requiring specific authority, whereas describing is an informative act governed by truthfulness.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the English translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the contrast between 'tasmiya' and 'ijra' al-sifat'.
2
Understand Ghazali's rule
Imam al-Ghazali states that applying attributes is an informative act about the establishment of their meanings. This is permissible as long as the meaning is established, because truthfulness is permissible—even desirable—in speech.
The key condition: the attribute must have a real meaning that applies to God.
3
Distinguish naming from describing
Taftazani contrasts the two: naming is a disposal over the named entity, requiring specific authority (like a father or owner). Describing does not require such authority; it only requires the description to be true.
This is why we can say God is 'Knowing' but we cannot call Him by a name He has not revealed.
