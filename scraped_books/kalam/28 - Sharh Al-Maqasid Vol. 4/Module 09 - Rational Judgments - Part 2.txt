Ash'ari Definitions of Tawfiq and 'Isma
Ash'ari definitions of tawfiq and 'isma with the basis of power accompanying the act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari definition of tawfiq as the creation of power for obedience, the identification of 'isma with tawfiq, and the basis that power accompanies the act.

Core Idea
Tawfiq is the power to obey; 'isma is tawfiq in specific contexts.

The author begins by revisiting the Ash'ari understanding of divine assistance (tawfiq) and protection ('isma). He clarifies that 'isma is not a separate concept but a specific form of tawfiq. This lesson unpacks the core definitions and the rational basis that power is simultaneous with the act.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice how it defines tawfiq as the creation of power for obedience and abandonment as the creation of power for disobedience.
The phrase 'خلق قدرة الطاعة والخذلان خلق قدرة المعصية' captures the essence.
2
Understand the identification of 'isma with tawfiq
The passage states that 'isma is the same as tawfiq. It can be general (for all obligations) or specific (for particular acts).
Imam al-Haramayn is cited for this view.
3
Grasp the basis: power accompanies the act
The reason the one given tawfiq cannot sin is that power is created simultaneously with the act and is not equally related to opposites. This is the foundation of the Ash'ari position.
Contrast with the Mu'tazilite view that power exists before the act.
4
Note the additional Ash'ari definition
Some Ash'aris define 'isma simply as Allah not creating sin in the servant, which highlights divine causation.
This is a simpler formulation.

Commentary
This passage refines the earlier definitions and includes a refutation. It begins by reiterating that tawfiq is the creation of power for obedience and khidhlan for disobedience, adding that 'isma is identical to tawfiq; if general, it is general tawfiq, and if specific, it is specific tawfiq. This is attributed to Imam al-Haramayn, who further explains that the one given tawfiq does not sin because he lacks the power to sin, and the basis is that power is simultaneous with the act and not equally related to opposites. Then it reports some Ash'aris who define 'isma as Allah not creating sin in the servant. The philosophers define it as a disposition preventing evil despite ability. Another definition says it is a property in the person or body preventing sin. This last definition is refuted because such a person would not deserve praise or reward for refraining from sin, nor would he be obligated. The passage then returns to the Mu'tazilite view, repeating the classification of lutf into muqarrib and muhassil, specifying that muhassil for obligations is tawfiq and for avoiding evil is 'isma. Some Mu'tazilites define tawfiq as the creation of grace by which Allah knows the servant will obey, khidhlan as withholding grace, and 'isma as a grace without any motive to disobey, with the ability to both. Grace is the act by which Allah knows the servant will obey.

?

Refutation of the Property-Based Definition of 'Isma
Refutation of the property-based definition of 'isma from the philosophers and others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the property-based definition of 'isma is rejected, and how the refutation centers on the absence of merit and obligation.

Core Idea
If 'isma were a property preventing sin, it would nullify praise, reward, and moral obligation.

After presenting the Ash'ari definitions, the author reports two alternative views: the philosophers' definition of 'isma as a disposition (malaka) and another definition as a special property (khaassiyya) in the person or body. He then refutes the property definition with a concise but powerful argument: such a person would not deserve praise, reward, or even obligation. This lesson focuses on that refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions presented
Read the philosopher's definition: 'isma is a disposition that prevents evil despite ability. Then the property definition: a special property in the person or body that prevents sin.
Both definitions imply an inherent quality in the person.
2
Understand the refutation
The author refutes the property definition by stating that if sin is prevented by a property, the person would not deserve praise or reward for avoiding sin, nor would he be obligated to avoid it.
The refutation is given in one sentence: 'ورد بأنه لا يستحق المدح...'
3
Reflect on the implication
Why does the refutation matter? It preserves the role of free will and moral responsibility. 'Isma must be by divine aid, not by a natural property.
Contrast with the Ash'ari view that 'isma is tawfiq (divine creation of power).

Mu'tazilite Classification of Lutf, Tawfiq, and 'Isma
Mu'tazilite classification of lutf into muqarrib and muhassil, and definitions of tawfiq and 'isma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite classification of divine grace (lutf) into approximating and effecting types, and how tawfiq and 'isma are subcategories of effecting grace.

Core Idea
Grace is what leads to obedience; tawfiq secures obligations; 'isma secures avoidance of evil.

The author now turns to the Mu'tazilite perspective, which organizes divine aid under the concept of grace (lutf). This lesson unpacks their detailed classification: lutf is divided into approximating (muqarrib) and effecting (muhassil), and then tawfiq and 'isma are identified as specific forms of effecting grace. Alternative definitions within the school are also presented.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of lutf
Lutf is that which makes the servant choose obedience (either doing or abandoning) or brings him close to it, while maintaining ability in both states.
Contrast with Ash'ari view where power is created with the act.
2
Learn the two categories of lutf
If lutf brings one close to the obligatory or to abandoning evil, it is muqarrib (approximating). If it actually effects the result, it is muhassil (effecting).
Effecting grace is further subdivided.
3
Identify tawfiq and 'isma as subcategories
Muhassil for obligations is called tawfiq; muhassil for abandoning evil is called 'isma.
Note the parallel with the Ash'ari view but with different basis.
4
Examine alternative Mu'tazilite definitions
Some say tawfiq is the creation of lutf with knowledge that the servant will obey; khidhlan is withholding lutf; 'isma is a lutf that leaves no motive to disobey while still having ability.
Grace is defined as an act by which Allah knows the servant will obey.

The First Proof: Will to Obedience
First proof and refutation of the obligation of grace from God's will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first argument that God's will to obedience necessitates grace and why it is refuted by denying that every commanded act is willed.

Core Idea
God's will does not automatically obligate grace for every commanded act.

The first proof attempts to derive the obligation of grace from God's will for obedience. It claims that if God prevented the means to obedience, He would contradict His own will. However, the refutation challenges this by distinguishing between what God commands and what He wills. Let's examine this argument closely.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof
Read the first proof: 'He is willing obedience, so if preventing what brings about it were permissible, He would not be willing it, which is a contradiction.'
2
Identify the refutation
Read the refutation: 'Refuted by denying the implication and denying that every commanded thing is willed.'
3
Analyze the logical structure
The proof assumes that if God commands something, He must will it in a way that requires providing means. The refutation denies this link.
4
Consider the implications
Reflecting on this shows that divine grace is not rationally necessary; it is a free gift.

Source Text

الأول : أنه مريد للطاعة، فلو جاز منع ما يحصل أو يقرب منها لكان غير مريد لها، وهو تناقض ، ورد بمنع الملازمة ومنع أن كل مأمور به مراد. الثاني : أن منع اللطف نقض لغرضه الذي هو الإتيان بالمأمور به، ونقض الغرض قبيح يجب تركه ورد بمنع المقدمتين لجواز أن لا يكون نقض المأمور به مراداً أو غرضاً، ويتعلق بنقضه حكم ومصالح . الثالث : أن منع اللطف تحصيل للمعصية أو تقريب منها وكلاهما قبيح يجب ترکه ورد بالمنع فإن عدم تحصيل الطاعة أعم من تحصيل المعصية وكذا التقريب ، ولا ثم أن إيجاد القبيح قبيح وقد مرّ . الرابع أن الواجب لا يتم إلا بما يحصله أو يقرب منه فيكون واجباً. وردّ بعد تسليم القاعدة بأن ذلك وجوب على المكلف بشرط كونه مقدوراً له فلا يكون مما نحن فيه.
First: [God] is willing obedience. If it were permissible to prevent what brings about or approaches it, He would not be willing to it, which is a contradiction. It is refuted by denying the implication and denying that every commanded thing is willed. Second: Preventing grace is a violation of His purpose, which is the performance of the commanded act, and violation of purpose is ugly and must be avoided. It is refuted by denying both premises, because it is possible that the performance of the commanded act is not willed or intended, and by violating it there are rulings and interests. Third: Preventing grace is bringing about sin or approximating it, both of which are ugly and must be avoided. Objection and Answer: Why Children Are Not Charged
Al-Ash'ari refutes the Mu'tazilite doctrine of al-aslah with the case of children.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari refutation of the Mu'tazilite principle of al-aslah using the case of children.

Core Idea
If taklif is always most beneficial, why do children not receive it?

The Mu'tazilites argued that God must always do what is most beneficial for His creatures, and that the divine charge (taklif) is the most beneficial because it leads to paradise. But Imam al-Ash'ari famously challenged his teacher al-Jubba'i with a simple question: if taklif is indeed the best, why does God not impose it on children who die before adulthood? This question exposes a contradiction in the Mu'tazilite position. In this lesson, you will examine that argument and its historical significance.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Notice the structure: an objection (fīla qīla) followed by an answer (qulnā).
This is a classic kalam dialectical format.
2
Identify the Mu'tazilite premise and the counter
The Mu'tazilite claims taklif is al-aslah because it leads to the higher station. Al-Ash'ari responds with a counterexample: children. Ask yourself: why is this a powerful refutation?
Think about the logical inconsistency: if something is always best, it should apply universally.
3
Reflect on the historical significance
The passage states that this subtle point (nukta) forced al-Jubba'i to recant. Consider how a single logical question can overturn a whole theological system.
This event marks a turning point in al-Ash'ari's intellectual journey away from Mu'tazilism.

Commentary
This passage contains an objection and answer in kalam style. The Mu'tazilite (al-Jubba'i) argues that the most beneficial (al-aslah) is the divine charge (taklīf) and exposure to eternal bliss because that gives the highest station (a'lā al-manzilatayn) – the best possible outcome for believers. The author (al-Ash'ari) answers with a logical question: if taklif is so beneficial, why did God not impose it on children who die before puberty? Such children are not charged (mukallaf), so according to the Mu'tazilite view, God would be withholding the higher station from them, contradicting the claim that He must do what is best. This famous argument (nukta) forced al-Jubba'i to admit defeat and abandon his position (rujū' 'an madhhabih). The incident is historical: it is said that al-Ash'ari debated his teacher al-Jubba'i on this point and caused him to recant, which led al-Ash'ari to abandon Mu'tazilism.
