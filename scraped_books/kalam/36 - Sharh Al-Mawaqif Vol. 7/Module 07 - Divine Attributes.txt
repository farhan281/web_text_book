Proof of the Divisibility of the Indivisible Part from Motion
Sanusi constructs a thought experiment with two lines to prove divisibility via motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the kalam argument from motion demonstrates that the supposed indivisible part must be divisible by tracking two simultaneous motions.

Core Idea
Two simultaneous motions force the indivisible part to split.

Sanusi presents a clever thought experiment using motion to challenge the assumption of an indivisible part. By setting up two lines and tracking a point's motion, he shows that the point must traverse different distances, implying divisibility. We'll walk through this proof step by step to grasp its logical force.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
Read the description of the stationary line ABJ and the moving line KH. Note the positions of points K, H, and Z.
The setup is crucial for tracking motions.
2
Track the motion components
Identify the two components of Z's motion: its own motion along KH (essential) and the motion of KH itself (accidental).
This dual motion is key to the proof.
3
Compare distances traversed
Observe that Z moves two parts while K moves only one part in the same time interval.
This comparison shows the part is divisible.
4
Draw the conclusion
The existence of two simultaneous motions (fast and slow) contradicts the assumption of indivisibility.
5
Reflect on the proof's structure
Consider how the argument is a hypothetical proof by contradiction, not requiring actual motion in the world.
It's a thought experiment.

Argues against the indivisible part using the square and its diagonal.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The square's diagonal argument shows that indivisible parts lead to geometric absurdity.

This lesson presents the first of six geometrical arguments against the indivisible part. By assuming a square composed of indivisible parts, the author demonstrates that the diagonal would have to equal the side, contradicting known geometry. He then refutes a potential objection about voids, showing that the assumption leads to an even greater contradiction.

Commentary

The refutation shows that if the void is exactly a part, the diagonal becomes seven parts (equal to the sum of two sides), which is still false; if the void is smaller, it implies the part is divisible because something smaller exists. Thus the assumption of indivisible parts is untenable.

Circularity of Geometrical Proofs
Objection that geometrical proofs against atomism are circular.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Geometrical proofs against atomism assume what they aim to disprove.

In this passage, the author presents a powerful objection: that geometrical arguments against the indivisible part are circular. The objection traces the dependence of geometrical figures on the concept of continuous magnitude, which itself assumes the denial of discrete parts. Understanding this circularity helps evaluate the strength of such arguments.

Commentary
This passage is an objection (and its answer) raised in the margin. It argues that using geometrical figures to disprove the indivisible part is circular. Second Geometrical Face: Right Triangle with Legs of 10
Shows that the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs 10 is irrational, implying divisibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a geometrical argument from a right triangle demonstrates the necessity of divisible parts in atomist theory.

Core Idea
Geometry forces the part to be divisible.

This passage presents a mathematical proof used by theologians to challenge the concept of indivisible atoms. By considering a right triangle with both short sides equal to 10, the Pythagorean theorem forces the hypotenuse to be irrational, implying that the unit of length must be divisible to accommodate fractional amounts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.

Identify the given lengths and theorem
The triangle has legs of 10. The Pythagorean theorem is applied: square of hypotenuse equals sum of squares of legs.
The theorem is referred to as 'شكل العروس'.
3
Follow the calculation
Each leg squared is 100, sum is 200, so hypotenuse is √200. Compute squares of 14 and 15 to show √200 lies between them.
14²=196, 15²=225, so √200 is between 14 and 15.
4
Understand the conclusion
Since the hypotenuse length is not an integer number of parts, the part must be divisible to account for the fractional remainder.
This refutes the idea of a truly indivisible atomic unit.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the nature of the moon's light and the colors of planets, drawn from the commentary on al-Mawaqif. It begins by stating that all planets are transparent and colorless, luminous by themselves—except the moon, which is dim in itself (its darkness appears during lunar eclipse). The moon's light comes from the sun, as inferred from its changing phases based on distance from the sun. Two opinions are given on how the moon receives light: (1) by reflection (inʿikās) from its surface, like a mirror, without the moon's substance becoming intrinsically illuminated; (2) by the moon's substance itself becoming illuminated (yastanīru jawharuhu). Imam al-Razi prefers the second view because on the reflection view, not all parts of the moon would be equally illuminated, yet observation shows that the entire visible face is luminous at full moon. The passage then notes that some astronomers argue that planetary eclipses (e.g., moons of Jupiter?) prove that planets have colors, albeit weak: Mercury is yellowish, Venus pure white, Mars red, Jupiter impure white, Saturn darkish with turbidity. The section then states that there are five goals (maqāṣid) in this section, but only the first goal is treated here: the moon and its illumination from the sun. The commentator then explains the geometry of crescent and full moon: the half facing the sun is always lit. When the moon is below the sun and in conjunction (new moon), it is dark. The rest of the passage consists of marginal glosses (aqwāl) that elaborate on the earlier points: (i) clarifying that 'luminous by themselves' opposes those who say planets derive light from the sun; (ii) explaining reflection as similar to a mirror; (iii) arguing against the reflection view by noting that not all parts would reflect light equally; (iv) countering an objection that if the moon's substance were illuminated, it would be visible from all sides—this is denied, as illumination is not the same as self-luminosity.

Commentary
This passage states the seventh proposition regarding simple intellects: they do not know particulars qua particulars. Two reasons are given: (1) particulars require corporeal instruments for perception, which immaterial intellects lack; (2) particulars change, so knowledge of them would be changeable, but simple intellects do not admit change. The author then notes that this is subject to an objection that will appear later in the discussion of God's attributes, specifically His knowledge: God knows particulars without any corporeal instrument or change in His essence, so the objection will be addressed there.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some might think that if intellects cannot know particulars, then God also cannot know particulars.
Correction: The passage explicitly notes that this does not apply to God, who knows particulars without instruments or change—this will be explained later.

Key Takeaway
Simple intellects cannot know particulars because they lack material instruments and cannot accommodate change, but God's knowledge is an exception.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
You rely on your senses and physical instruments to know the details of this world. What does this truth about the limitations of intellects say about the limits of your own knowledge, and how does that humble you in your pursuit of certainty?
?
The lesson shows that only God can know every changing particular without change in Himself. When you worry that He does not see your specific struggles or changing circumstances, does this passage reassure you? How does it demand that you turn to Him with all the details of your life?
?
If intellects cannot accommodate change in knowledge, how does that challenge your own desire to fully comprehend the mutable world? Are you seeking to know things in a way that exceeds your nature? What does it mean to trust that God's knowledge is sufficient even when your own understanding is limited?
