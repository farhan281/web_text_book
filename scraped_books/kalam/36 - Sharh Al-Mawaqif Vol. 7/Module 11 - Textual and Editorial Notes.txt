Defending the Third Theory: The Ray as an Accident
Marginal gloss defends the third theory by distinguishing ray as accident and rebutting objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the third theory (ray as accident) avoids the rational impossibilities that plague the second theory, and how the gloss defends it against objections.

Core Idea
The third theory's ray is an accident, so it doesn't change the medium—saving it from two key objections.

In the margin, the commentator clarifies and defends the third theory against attacks from the main text. By showing that the cone-shaped ray is an accident rather than a bodily substance, he argues that it can be created by God in the air without disturbing other qualities, and that analogies with sound fail.

Guided Path
1
Clarify the pronoun references
The gloss begins by explaining what 'between it' and 'in it' refer to. Understand this textual basis before diving into the argument.
2
Distinguish the two theories
The second theory treats the ray as a bodily substance; the third as an accident. Recognize how this distinction changes the possibilities.
3
See the third theory's advantage
The gloss argues there is no rational impossibility in the ray being an accident created by the bountiful origin. Notice that it does not change other qualities of air/spheres.
4
Refute the sound analogy
The gloss denies the objection that the ray must be disturbed, and rejects the analogy with sound because sound is a quality while the ray is a substance.
5
Address remaining objections
The gloss shows that other objections (e.g., affecting stars, sparrow organ) only apply to the second theory, not the third.

Commentary
This passage is a marginal gloss (hashiya) that comments on and defends the third theory against the refutations presented in the main text. It begins by clarifying pronouns in the main text. It then distinguishes the second theory, which treats the cone-shaped ray as a bodily substance (jawhar jismānī), from the third theory, which treats it as an accident ('araḍ). The gloss argues that there is no rational impossibility in the third theory: the ray can be created by the bountiful origin (al-mabda' al-fayyāḍ) in the air or spheres given appropriate conditions like turning the pupil. Its existence does not alter other qualities of the air and spheres. The gloss then refutes the wind argument: the analogy with sound is rejected because sound is a quality in air, while the ray here is a substance. The gloss also states that the argument from the impossibility of affecting the fixed stars can be repelled from the third theory, implying that an accident does not require transformation of the medium. Similarly, the statement about the organ (sparrow, etc.) applies only to the second theory. The gloss concludes by advising to analogize to what the Imam (al-Razi) said.

Editorial Glosses on Key Terms
Glosses explaining word meanings and textual variants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how scholars clarify textual variants and difficult vocabulary in classical texts through editorial glosses.

Core Idea
Precise wording is crucial for transmitting knowledge accurately.

This passage shows the meticulous work of editors who explain ambiguous words and variant readings. Understanding these glosses is essential for correctly interpreting the original text. Here, the commentator addresses terms like 'al-khalkhala al-māzija' and 'dhu'āba' to ensure no confusion remains.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation together. Notice how each gloss targets a specific word or phrase from the main text.
The Arabic uses technical terms like 'al-ḥā' al-muhmala' (unpointed ḥā) etc.
2
Identify the terms being glossed
List the key terms: al-khalkhala al-māzija, al-mundamija, dhu'āba, al-niyāzak, bukhār ladakh dhihnī, and dā'ira. Observe how each is explained.
3
Understand variant readings
Note the two versions of 'al-khalkhala al-māzija' discussed. This shows that textual variants exist and must be reconciled.
The difference in pointing (ḥā vs. khā, rā vs. zāy) changes meaning.
4
Appreciate the purpose of glosses
Reflect: without these glosses, a reader might misinterpret the original text. The glossator clarifies definitions and citations from authoritative sources like al-Ṣiḥāḥ.

Commentary
This passage is a set of editorial glosses clarifying terms appearing in the main text. The glossator explains two readings of al-khalkhala al-māzija, noting one with ḥās and rā' and jīm, meaning agitation or mixing, and a variant with khās and zāy. He then glosses al-mundamija as the opposite of al-takhalkhul (looseness). The phrase wa lā yuḥriqihā is explained as meaning it does not burn the purse unless the purse itself is burned by the melting of its contents. The word dhu'āba is identified as a tuft of hair, with its plural dhawā'ib. Al-niyāzak are spears. The phrase bukhār ladakh dhihnī is interpreted as a smoky oily vapor, with a possible reading of ladakh as transitive. A variant of dukhān (smoke) from al-Ṣiḥāḥ is cited. Finally, the description of a stick as 'circular' is explained: a stick rotating rapidly on its axis deflects arrows in various directions, illustrating the analogy in the main text about the rebound of smoke. These glosses serve to ensure precise understanding of the technical terminology and textual variants.
