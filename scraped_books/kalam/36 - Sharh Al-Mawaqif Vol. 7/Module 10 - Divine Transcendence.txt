The Will of the Omnipotent Agent Determines Location
Al-Jurjani answers the objection that only form determines the position of hyle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the objection that form alone determines location fails, and how al-Jurjani affirms divine will as the ultimate determinant of a body's location.

Core Idea
Location is not determined by form alone, but by the will of the all-powerful God.

In the proof of hyle, an objection is raised that only form determines where a body is. Al-Jurjani answers by reminding us that without a free agent, form would be the only determinant, but since God exists as omnipotent, it is His will that causes the location. This passage clarifies the role of divine choice in the specification of matter.

The objection is presented as a conditional: 'if there were no omnipotent agent...'

Understand the logical flow

This shows the dependence of the objection on a false premise.
4

This connects to broader themes in kalam about causality.

The Proof for the Necessity of Matter in Body
The author argues that if form were immaterial, it would entail contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the kalam argument that abstract form cannot be immaterial because it entails contradiction regarding indication and causation of shape.

Core Idea
Form without matter cannot be indicated, yet still has shape — a contradiction that proves matter is necessary.

This passage presents a key proof for the existence of hyle (matter) in bodies. The author argues that if the form of a body were abstract and immaterial, it would either be subject to indication or not — both options lead to contradiction. The argument is refined to show that shape itself forces a material substrate.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's rhythm and the flow of the argument come through.
2
Identify the two options for abstract form
The text considers whether an abstracted form is subject to indication (ishara) or not. Notice the two different consequences.
The first option leads to being in a direction and specific to matter; the second leads to being a purely intellectual entity.
3
Understand the disjunction about shape
The author adds that if form were abstracted, it would be finite and shaped. Then ask: what causes that shape? Either corporeality alone or another cause.
This disjunction is key because it shows the need for matter regardless of indication.
4
See how the argument is completed
The final summary says: if the separated form accepts indication, it is material; if not, it is not the form we originally indicated while it was material. This reinforces the necessity of matter.
This ties back to the conclusion that form cannot be immaterial in a body.

Natural Place and its Refutation by Atomists and Surface Theorists
Al-Jurjani presents the sixth branch that every body has a natural place and refutes it from atomist and surface theorist perspectives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the concepts of natural place are rejected by atomists and surface theorists in Islamic kalam.

Core Idea
Nature and place are interdependent; reject nature, and natural place becomes meaningless.

In this passage, al-Jurjani presents a key debate about whether bodies have natural places determined by their inherent natures. The atomists, who deny distinct natures, argue that place is arbitrary and depends on divine will. The surface theorists, meanwhile, collapse place and location, leaving no room for a natural place. This lesson explores these critiques and their implications for kalam cosmology.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to understand the claim that every body has a natural place.
Notice the immediate assertion that this depends on nature inhering in hyle.
2
Identify the atomist objection
The atomists (aṣḥāb al-juz') argue that bodies are similar, so no inherent nature exists; place is assigned by divine will.
This undermines the very basis of natural place.
3
Examine the commentator's additional refutation
The commentator discusses an argument that place cannot be a pure dimension because it would prevent rest and motion; the reply invokes compatibility (mulā'ama) and incompatibility.
This shows another dimension of the debate.
4
Understand the surface theorist position
For surface theorists (qā'ilīn bi-l-saṭḥ), place and location are synonymous, and the limited has no place, so natural place is impossible.
This completes the two refutations.
5
Reflect on theological implications
Consider why these debates matter for divine causality and the nature of bodies.
They relate to divine transcendence and the role of will.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed definition and analysis of the concept of al-ḥayz (dimension/region) and its relation to natural place. It begins by stating a general definition: al-ḥayz is that by which bodies are distinguished in sensory indication (al-ishāra al-ḥissiyya). It is more general than place (makān) because it includes position (waḍ'). Even if a limited thing (al-muḥaddad) is not in a place, it can still have a ḥayz. The author then discusses an objection: since al-ḥayz is attributed to the body by the word 'fīhi', it might imply containment, but it also allows transfer. The main point is that a body cannot have two natural ḥayz-s, so position cannot be part of al-ḥayz in that sense. The correct view is from Ibn Sina's al-Shifā', where al-ḥayz is either place or an ordering position. The passage then links the definition to the earlier claim that nature necessitates being in a particular ḥayz. It refutes a misinterpretation from a commentary that restricts natural ḥayz to what is obtained for the body in purposes, and also responds to an objection: how can place as a surface be natural if it depends on a container? The answer: natural place requires that the body by its nature necessitates being there, even if actual presence depends on conditions. The passage ends with a quotation from Ibn Sina's al-Ishārāt and its commentary, emphasizing that the nature provides a principle of requiring (mabda' istījāb) a specific position and shape, and that the body returns to this when compulsion is removed.
