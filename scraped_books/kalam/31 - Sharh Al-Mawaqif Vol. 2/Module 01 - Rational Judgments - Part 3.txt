The Second Proof: Possibility as a Positive Attribute
Argument that possibility is a positive attribute, then refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof for the fixedness of nonexistents and its refutation, specifically the claim that possibility (imkan) is a positive attribute and the response that it is merely a mental consideration.

Core Idea
Possibility is not a real attribute; it is a mental relation.

This lesson examines a key argument in the debate over the status of nonexistents. Proponents argue that since the nonexistent possible is described as 'possible,' and possibility is a positive attribute, the nonexistent must itself be positive (i.e., have some reality). The refutation denies that possibility is an objective attribute, classifying it instead as a mental consideration (amr i'tibari). Understanding this debate sharpens your grasp of how theologians analyze concepts like existence and essence.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic passage once slowly. Notice the structure: a claim, a proof, then an answer. Also note the reference to a later observatory.
The passage uses technical terms like صفة ثبوتية and أمر اعتباري.
2
Identify the proof's premises
The proof has two main premises: (1) the nonexistent possible is qualified by possibility, (2) possibility is a positive attribute. From these it concludes that the nonexistent is positive (fixed).
3
Understand the refutation
The refutation denies the second premise: possibility is not a positive attribute but a mental consideration. Consider what 'mental consideration' means—something that exists only in the mind, not in external reality.
The passage also mentions that this proof is refuted by the same objections as the first proof.
4
Consider the additional doubt
The passage hints at another argument based on 'otherness' (taghayur). Notice how it is presented and why it might reduce to earlier proofs.
This part is less developed; focus on the main proof and refutation.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof used by proponents of the fixedness of nonexistents, followed by its refutation. The proof proceeds as follows: the nonexistent possible (al-maʿdūm al-mumkin) is qualified by possibility (imkān). Since possibility is a positive attribute (ṣifa thubūtiyya) – as will be argued in the third observatory (al-marṣad al-thālith) – that which is qualified by a positive attribute must itself be positive (thubūtī), i.e., existent. The reasoning is that it is impossible for a non-fixed thing (ghayr thābit) to be described by a positive attribute. The answer to this proof is to deny that possibility is a positive attribute; instead, it is a mental consideration (amr iʿtibārī), having no external reality. This is also stated to be refutable by the same objections used against the first proof. The passage then mentions that the opponents have additional doubts (shubah) beyond these two proofs, including the argument that the nonexistent possible in pre-eternity is not God, so it is other than God. Otherness (taghāyur) implies two distinct things; hence both must be something. The commentator notes that this argument reduces to the previous ones and requires careful handling.

Two Objections to the Proof of the Non-Existent as a Thing
Explains the naqd argument and the critique of analogy lacking a common factor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two key objections—the naqd using the impossible and the critique of analogy lacking a common factor—and how the commentator defends the original proof.

Core Idea
The impossible is known but not real, so being known does not make something a thing.

The commentator presents two objections that challenge the claim that the non-existent is a thing because it is known. The first objection, naqd bil-mustahil, points to the impossible as a known but non-real entity, breaking the link between knowledge and reality. The second objection argues that the analogy between sensible and intellectual knowledge lacks a true common factor. Let's examine these objections and the responses.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the two objections
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice that the commentator first explains 'naqd bil-mustahil' and then moves to the second objection about the analogy lacking a common factor (jami).
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Understand the naqd argument
Focus on the first part: how the impossible (al-mustahil) nullifies the implication. The impossible is known but not established (thabit) and not sensible. This serves as a counterexample to the claim that all known things are real.
The commentator says 'fi-mustahil' is 'known not established'.
3
Understand the critique of analogy
The second part argues that the analogy between the sensible and the intellectually known is flawed because it lacks a common factor effective in the ruling. Sensation and intellection differ in their properties (e.g., requiring existence), so the commonality of 'being known' is insufficient.
The commentator defends against objections by clarifying that difference in type does not automatically provide a common factor.
4
Reflect on the responses
Notice how the commentator repels counter-arguments: the naqd is valid because it directly refutes the implication; the analogy is invalid because the commonality is not proven. This strengthens the original proof.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on two objections to a previous proof that the non-existent is a thing because it is known. The first objection is the 'naqd bil-mustahil' (refutation using the impossible). The commentator explains that this refutation nullifies the implication of the conditional statement by pointing to the impossible: it is known but not established and not sensible. This shows that being known does not necessarily imply being a thing (established). The second objection is that the analogy (tamthīl) between the intellectually known and the sensible is invalid because it lacks a common factor (jāmi‘). The commentator argues that sensation and intellection differ in their rulings (e.g., requiring existence), so the commonality of being known is not sufficient to transfer the ruling of establishment from the sensible to the intellectual. He then repels counter-arguments: difference in type does not preclude a common factor, but here the commonality itself is not proven. The passage defends the original proof against these objections.

The Four Attributes of Substances (Jawahir)
Al-Jurjani enumerates the four attributes of substances and explains each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four attributes that substances possess according to the kalam tradition, and the logical relationships between them.

Core Idea
Substances have only four attributes: substantiality, existence, occupying space, and location.

In classical kalam, the concept of substance (jawhar) is fundamental. Al-Jurjani explains that a substance is defined by four specific attributes, each with a distinct origin and function. This passage clarifies what attributes are intrinsic to the substance itself versus those that come from an external agent, and it resolves a common misconception about the attributes of color or other accidents.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Note the dense style and numbered items.
The passage uses terms like jawhariyya, wujud, tahayyuz, kawn, and ka'iniyya.
2
Identify the four attributes
The passage lists four attributes in order. Find and name each one: substantiality, existence, occupying space (tahayyuz/kawn), and location (ka'iniyya).
The third attribute has a sub-debate about kawn.
3
Understand the causal chain
Notice how the attributes are related: jawhariyya is foundational, existence comes from the agent, tahayyuz follows existence, and ka'iniyya is caused by tahayyuz.
This hierarchy is important for kalam cosmology.
4
Consider the debate on kawn
Some scholars say kawn is distinct from motion, rest, etc.; others say it is one of those. The passage gives an example: a single created substance would have kawn without those four.
This shows the nuance in defining attributes.
5
Recognize the boundaries of substance attributes
The passage concludes that a substance has no attribute beyond these four. Qualities like blackness are accidents inhering, not attributes of the substance.
This corrects the misconception that a substance's color is a substance-attribute.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration and explanation of the four attributes specific to substances (jawahir). The first is jawhariyya (substantiality), an attribute persisting in both existence and non-existence. The Three Attributes of Accidents
Al-Jurjani enumerates the three attributes of accidents and explains their relation to existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three specific attributes that accidents possess and why they do not have a fourth attribute for the cause of inherence.

Core Idea
Accidents have three attributes: accidentiality, existence, and occupying a substrate.

After discussing the attributes of substances, al-Jurjani turns to accidents. Accidents share some attributes with substances but differ in crucial ways. This passage clearly lays out the three attributes that belong to accidents and explains the logical relationship between accidentiality and the accident's inherence in a substrate.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage aloud
Read the Arabic text slowly, letting the rhythmic flow of al-Jurjani's enumeration settle in your mind. Pay attention to the terms used.
The text is concise; each attribute is mentioned briefly.
2
Identify the three attributes
Look for the three distinct items listed: (1) ‘ardiyya (accidentiality), (2) wujud (existence), (3) al-husul fi al-mahall (occupying a substrate). Notice that the third attribute is said to 'follow existence'.
3
Understand the qualification about 'ardiyya
Al-Jurjani adds an important clarification: ‘ardiyya is not a cause for inherence unconditionally, but only when existence is present. This distinguishes accidents from substances.
The cause of inherence itself is not an additional attribute; it is the accident itself.
4
Contrast with substances
Recall from earlier that substances have a fourth attribute (a cause for inherence). Here, accidents lack that fourth attribute. This contrast helps solidify the unique nature of accidents.
