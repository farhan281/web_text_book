The Universal and Particular in External and Mental Existence
Commentary on the conditions of universal and particular in external and mental existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the differing logical relationships between the universal and particular in external existence versus mental existence, and the exception when the universal is a part of the particular.

Core Idea
The universal and particular relate differently in the mind than in the world.

This passage from Sharh al-Mawaqif examines a key distinction in Islamic logic: how the universal (al-a'am) and particular (al-akhass) relate in external reality versus in the mind. The author explains that while the universal has fewer conditions in the external world due to the relation of generality, in the mind this relation does not hold, unless the universal is a part of the particular's essence. Understanding this distinction clarifies how concepts exist in the intellect.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation to get an overview of the content.
Pay attention to the key terms like al-a'am, al-akhass, al-wujud al-dhihni.
2
Understand the principle in external existence
Note that in external existence, the particular cannot be realized without the universal, but the universal can exist without that particular.
This is due to the relation of generality and particularity.
3
Grasp the qualification for mental existence
In mental existence, there is no relation of generality between images; they are disparate, so the particular can exist without the universal in the mind.
The passage says 'no relation between the two mental forms'.
4
Examine the exception
When the universal is a part of the particular and the particular is known by essence, then the condition for the universal's realization is also a condition for the particular.
This exception bridges the two domains.
5
Review the commentary
Read the teacher’s commentary to solidify understanding of each phrase and its implications.
The commentary explains the author's reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining the relationship between the universal (al-a'am) and the particular (al-akhass) in both external and mental existence. The author begins by stating a principle: if the universal is not realized in an individual, the particular cannot be realized in that individual, but the converse does not hold because the universal can be realized in another individual. However, this is qualified regarding mental existence ('adam al-'ilaqah bayn al-surayn al-dhihniyyayn), where there is no relation of generality and particularity between mental images; they are disparate, so the particular image can exist without the universal image, and there is no opposition. Then the author introduces an exception: when the universal is a part of the particular and the particular is known by its essence (bi al-kunnih), then the condition for the universal's realization in the mind is a condition for the particular's realization. The commentary explains each phrase of the original text in sequence, emphasizing that the fewer conditions of the universal apply only in external existence due to the relation of generality, but not universally in the mind, unless the universal is a component of the particular. It also discusses an objection about relations between mental images, noting that relations like entailment and causality do exist. Finally, it suggests that the universal might be more known because it has more individuals, making sensory perception more abundant.

The First Proof: Existence as Identical to Essence or Superadded
Enumeration of the two reasons for existence being acquired.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first proof that existence is not self-evident but acquired, based on the disjunction that it is either identical to essence or superadded to it.

Core Idea
Existence is either essence itself or an accident of essence; both options make it non-self-evident.

This lesson presents the core argument from the source against the self-evidence of existence. The denier claims existence is acquired and needs a definition, providing two reasons: either existence is identical to essence (as per Ibn Sina) or superadded to it (as per others). In both cases, existence cannot be self-evident. Let's examine this foundational proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then its English translation. Notice the structure: the disjunction (either ... or) and the conclusion.
The passage is a continuous argument; try to identify the two options.
2
Understand the first option
If existence is identical to essence, then it is like the essence itself. Essences are not self-evident; only some aspects of them are self-evident. Therefore, existence is not self-evident.
This relies on the claim that essences are not known intuitively.
3
Understand the second option
If existence is superadded to essence, it becomes an accident of essence. Accidents are understood only after understanding their subject (essence). So existence is not self-evident.
This presupposes that accidents are conceptually dependent on substances.
4
Grasp the overall conclusion
In both cases, existence is not self-evident; it requires prior knowledge (of essence or its accidents). Hence existence is acquired (kasbi), not badihi.
The proof is a dilemma: whichever horn you take, existence is not primitive.

Commentary
This passage presents and comments on the first proof that existence is not self-evident (badihi) but rather acquired (kasbi). The original text states that the denier claims existence is acquired and needs a definition, for one of two reasons: either existence is identical to essence (as per Ibn Sina's doctrine) or it is superadded to essence (as per others). In the first case, it is not self-evident because essences themselves are not self-evident; only some aspects of them are. In the second case, existence is an accident of essences and is understood consequently. The commentary then addresses several issues: First, it notes that the proof only shows the need for a definition, not that it is obtained, so it requires another premise. Second, it discusses the problem that absolute existence cannot be identical to essences because that would unify all essences, which is not al-Ash'ari's view. The commentator offers two resolutions: either the disjunction ('either') is for division across different types of existence, or it is for possibilities in the mind. He favors the second interpretation. Third, it discusses the claim that only some aspects of essences are self-evident, clarifying that such aspects are privative (sulubiyya) and not truely accidents, so they are not self-evident by essence. There is a regress objection, but it is deflected by noting that the intention is to deny self-evidence of the essence of existing things, not of aspects. Finally, it addresses the claim that existence is understood following essences: if it means by essence, it is denied (since God's existence is not dependent on essence); if by aspect, it is granted but does not prove acquisition because the aspects themselves are self-evident.

Refinements: Absolute Existence and the Regress Objection
Commentary addressing issues with the first proof: absolute existence, regress, and dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the critiques and refinements of the first proof, particularly concerning absolute existence, the regress objection, and the nature of self-evident aspects.

Core Idea
The proof requires refinement to handle absolute existence and the claim that aspects are privative, not real accidents.

The first proof, while structurally sound, faces several challenges. The commentator addresses the problem that absolute existence cannot be identical to essences (as al-Ash'ari denies absolute existence), and also the regress objection regarding self-evident aspects. This lesson unpacks those refinements, showing how the proof can be defended and nuanced.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the problem of absolute existence
The commentator notes that the dispute is about absolute existence (al-wujud al-mutlaq), and that it cannot be identical to essences because that would unify all essences, which al-Ash'ari does not hold. Thus the disjunction needs reinterpretation.
The proof's disjunction appears to assume a universal existence, which is not universally accepted.
2
Two proposed resolutions
First resolution: the disjunction is for division (taqsim) across types of existence (particular vs absolute). Second resolution: the disjunction is for possibilities in the mind regarding absolute existence. The commentator favors the second.
Both resolutions preserve the proof's conclusion that existence is acquired.
3
Understand the nature of self-evident aspects
The passage clarifies that the self-evident aspects (wujuh) are privative (sulubiyya), not true accidents. They have no essence and are not understood by essence, so they do not threaten the proof.

Commentary
This passage presents and comments on the first proof that existence is not self-evident (badihi) but rather acquired (kasbi). The original text states that the denier claims existence is acquired and needs a definition, for one of two reasons: either existence is identical to essence (as per Ibn Sina's doctrine) or it is superadded to essence (as per others). In the first case, it is not self-evident because essences themselves are not self-evident; only some aspects of them are. In the second case, existence is an accident of essences and is understood consequently. The commentary then addresses several issues: First, it notes that the proof only shows the need for a definition, not that it is obtained, so it requires another premise. Second, it discusses the problem that absolute existence cannot be identical to essences because that would unify all essences, which is not al-Ash'ari's view. The commentator offers two resolutions: either the disjunction ('either') is for division across different types of existence, or it is for possibilities in the mind. He favors the second interpretation. Third, it discusses the claim that only some aspects of essences are self-evident, clarifying that such aspects are privative (sulubiyya) and not truely accidents, so they are not self-evident by essence. There is a regress objection, but it is deflected by noting that the intention is to deny self-evidence of the essence of existing things, not of aspects. Finally, it addresses the claim that existence is understood following essences: if it means by essence, it is denied (since God's existence is not dependent on essence); if by aspect, it is granted but does not prove acquisition because the aspects themselves are self-evident.

The First Proof for Mental Existence

Their first proof is striking: we deliberately conceive of things that have no external existence whatsoever, such as the impossible, the conjunction of contradictories, and absolute non-existence. Since we form a clear concept of these, they must have some mode of existence—mental existence. This lesson unpacks this proof and clarifies the key terms used.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of an argument for mental existence using Plato's theory of forms. The Trilemma on Nonexistent Things and the Reply via Huwiyya
Al-Jurjani presents a trilemma on nonexistent things and replies using the mahiyya-huwiyya distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical trilemma concerning nonexistent things and how al-Jurjani resolves it by distinguishing between quiddity (mahiyya) and individual identity (huwiyya).

Core Idea
The nonexistent is saved from sophistry by the fixed individual identities.

Al-Jurjani presents a trilemma: if nonexistent essences are essentially different, united, or neither, each leads to an absurdity or sophistry. The resolution lies in distinguishing between the quiddity (whatness) of a thing and its individual identity (huwiyya) that is fixed in nonexistence. This lesson unpacks the argument and its elegant solution.

Guided Path
1
Understand the trilemma's structure
Read the passage carefully to identify the three conditional horns and their respective consequences. Note how each horn is introduced by 'fa-in' or 'wa-in'.
The trilemma begins with 'فيلزم أن يكون كل فردين...' and presents three possibilities for nonexistent essences.
2
See the consequences of each horn
Work through each horn: (1) essential difference leads to every pair being essentially different; (2) essential unity leads to a single individual per species; (3) neither leads to the nonexistent being a locus for successive accidents and thus sophistry.
The third horn is the most problematic for the opponent.
3
Grasp the reply's central distinction
Examine the reply: 'قلنا: قولك لذواتها إن أردت به لماهيتها...' Notice how it distinguishes between mahiyya (quiddity) and huwiyya (individual identity). The quiddity is neutral to unity/difference, while huwiyyat have necessary distinguishing properties.
This distinction blocks the conclusion that the nonexistent is a locus for succession.
4
Apply the distinction to avoid sophistry
Reflect on how the reply prevents 'safsata'. Since each huwiyya has a fixed distinguishing property, no succession or alternation occurs at the level of individual identities. This preserves the rational order.
The key insight: 'كل ما تمتاز به هوية عما عداها فإنه لازم لها'.

Commentary
This passage presents a logical argument about the status of nonexistent things (ma'dumat). It begins with a conditional trilemma: (1) If nonexistent individuals were essentially different (mutabayinat bi al-dhat), then every two individuals of a species would be essentially different, which is absurd because the same quiddity would produce opposite effects. (2) If they were essentially united (muttahadat bi al-dhat), then each species would have only one individual, which is false. (3) If they are neither essentially different nor essentially united, then the nonexistent would be a locus for successive accidents (mutazayilat), meaning that attributes like motion and rest could alternate upon it in nonexistence, leading to sophistry (safsata). The author then gives the reply: the argument is directed at the quiddities (mahiyyat) of the nonexistent, but the reply chooses that quiddities in themselves are neither essentially distinguished nor essentially united. The differentiation occurs only at the level of individual identities (huwiyyat) that are fixed in nonexistence. Each huwiyya has a distinguishing property that is necessary to it, so there is no succession or alternation (tawarud wa tazayul) regarding huwiyyat. Thus, the problematic consequence is avoided.
