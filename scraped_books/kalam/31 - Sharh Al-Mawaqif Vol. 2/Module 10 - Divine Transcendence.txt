Two Proofs Against a Created Abstract Substance
Provides two arguments and their defense against objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two rational arguments from classical kalam that a created abstract substance is impossible, and how the objections to these arguments are addressed.

Core Idea
A created abstract substance would force composition in the Creator or break divine uniqueness.

This lesson presents two arguments from the commentary on al-Mawaqif against the possibility of a created abstract substance (al-jawhar al-mujarrad). The first argument shows that such a substance would share a defining attribute with the Creator, leading to composition in His essence. The second argues that the attribute of being non-spatial is the most specific attribute of the Creator, so sharing it would entail sharing in the essence itself. The commentary then refutes key objections, including the possibility of distinction by a non-existential.

Commentary
This passage presents two arguments for the impossibility of a created abstract substance (al-mujarrad al-mumkin). The first argument: if such a substance existed, it would share with the Creator the attribute of being neither spatial nor indwelling in a spatial entity. Since the Creator must be distinguished by something else, this would lead to composition (tarkīb) in the Creator—which is impossible because it would imply parts in the Necessary Being. The second argument: the attribute of being non-spatial and non-indwelling is the most specific attribute of the Creator; it defines His essence. If another being shared it, they would share in the very essence, leading to either the created being being pre-eternal (qadīm) or the Creator being originated (ḥādith)—both absurd. The commentary then addresses objections: First, the shared attribute need not lead to composition if the distinguishing factor is non-existential, but this is refuted because that non-existential would also be shared by the possible abstract, failing to distinguish. Second, the objection that sharing in essence does not necessitate sharing in temporal properties is noted, but the author defers the full response to later. The passage also mentions the Karramiyya who affirm originated attributes in God, thus accepting the existence of a non-spatial, non-indwelling entity (i.e., attributes). The commentator notes that the division of categories is according to the majority, and some mutakallimūn do accept abstract substances.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Sharing a negative attribute (like being non-spatial) with God does not imply sharing in His essence; it is merely a negation.
Correction: The passage argues that for the Creator, this negative attribute is the most specific attribute, essentially defining His nature. Therefore, sharing it would indeed force essential sharing, leading to absurd consequences.

?

?

In what area of your life do you act as though something created (a relationship, a goal, an identity) is timeless or absolute? How does that subtle elevation of the created distort your worship of the Creator?
