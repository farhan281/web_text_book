The Indivisible Atom Refutes the Denial of Human Agency
Author refutes a division that would negate human agency via an argument about the indivisible atom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the opponent's division leads to an impossibility regarding the indivisible atom and how that refutes the denial of human agency.

Core Idea
Power over an indivisible atom cannot vary—neither can one prevail without cause.

In this passage, Sanusi addresses a specific objection that would undermine human agency. He shows that if both God and the servant will opposite actions on the same indivisible atom, the only possible outcomes are all impossible—revealing a flaw in the opponent's reasoning. This sets the stage for his positive proof of human agency.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
The passage is dense, so take your time.
2
Identify the opponent's division and the problem of two conflicting wills
The passage begins by stating a division that, if valid, would negate human agency. It then explains why: if God wills a movement and the servant wills stillness on the same atom, neither both, nor none, nor one can occur without a cause.
The key is the indivisible atom (jawhar fard).
3
Understand why the indivisible atom makes variation in power impossible
Because the atom cannot be divided, its motion and stillness cannot be graded. Thus, the power over them cannot be stronger or weaker. If one will prevails, that would be a preponderance without a preponderator, which is impossible.
The impossibility is logical, not just empirical.
4
See the conclusion
Since the division leads to the absurdity that the servant has no power at all, and this is impossible (as the next lesson will show), the division itself is false.

إذا ثبت هذا فنقول : لو وقع مراد الله تعالى دون مراد العبد مع أنا بينا أن القدرة عليها لا تقبل التفاوت أصلاً ، فحينئذ يلزم رجحان الممكن لا لمرجح وهو محال . فيثبت : أن هذا التقسيم لو صح لوجب أن لا يكون العبد قادراً على الإيجاد والتكوين . وإنما قلنا : إن ذلك محال . وذلك لأن حسن المدح والذم معلوم بالضرورة ثم علمنا أن من أحسن الذم والمدح فرع على كون العبد فاهلا وموحداً، ومتى كان الفرع معلوماً بالضرورة ، كان الأصل أيضاً معلوماً بالضرورة ، فوجب أن يكون العلم بكون العبد موجداً علماً ضرورياً .

Thus it is established: He, exalted, was not at all powerful over bringing into existence and creating in pre-eternity. And if this is not impossible, then how can it be impossible that He is incapable of a single action, from a single time?
The sixth question: This division, if valid, would necessitate that the servant is not at all capable of the act, and this is impossible, indeed that is impossible. The explanation of the entailment: That if the servant were capable of bringing into existence and creating, and it happened that God willed the movement of a body, and the servant intended its stillness, then either both intended things occur together—which is impossible—or neither of them occurs—which is also impossible—for the very proof you mentioned. Or that one of them occurs without the other, and this is also impossible. That is because, even if we say that God, exalted, is capable of what has no end, and the servant is not capable except of the finite, yet the power of the Creator (al-Bārī) over moving a single atom cannot be stronger and more perfect than the power of the servant over stilling that atom, because the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard) does not accept division, so its motion and its stillness also do not accept division. And since this motion and this stillness are not susceptible to variation and division, the occurrence of variation in the power over them is impossible. So the Creator, exalted, is powerful over what has no end of potential objects of power, and the servant has no power except over the finite. However, the occurrence of that variation in the modality of power over that finite is impossible.
If this is established, then we say: If the will of God, exalted, occurs to the exclusion of the will of the servant, given that we have shown that power over them does not accept variation at all, then there would follow a preponderance of the possible without a preponderator (al-mumkin lā li-murajjiḥ), which is impossible. Thus it is established: that this division, if valid, would necessitate that the servant is not capable of bringing into existence and creating. And we say: That is impossible. That is because the goodness of praise and blame (ḥusn al-madḥ wa al-dhamm) is known by necessity (ḍarūra), then we know that whoever does good, blame and praise are dependent on the servant being a doer and a bringer into existence. And whenever the branch (farʿ) is known necessarily, the root (aṣl) is also known necessarily. So it is necessary that the knowledge that the servant is a bringer into existence is necessary knowledge.
These are premises that must be established.
As for the first premise: It is our statement that necessary knowledge is obtained of the goodness of praise and blame. Its establishment: That everyone who does good to a person and conveys to him some kind of good, his sound intellect judges the goodness of gratitude, praise, and commendation for him; and everyone who harms him and conveys to him some kind of harm, his sound intellect impels him to say: Why did you do this? How bad is what you did! And the knowledge of the goodness of this praise and blame is among the clearest of necessary sciences, and whoever disputes it is obstinate regarding the clearest of intuitive sciences.
And as for the second premise: It is in explaining that the knowledge of the goodness of praise and blame is dependent on the knowledge that he is an agent. The proof of it: That whoever throws a stone at a person's face and wounds him, he finds in himself blame for that thrower, and he does not find at all blame for that stone. And if it were said to him: You distinguished between them in this judgment? Every rational person distinguishes between them by saying: The thrower did this act by his choice, so he deserves praise and blame. As for the stone, it has no power over the act at all. Thus it is established by what we have mentioned: That sound reason judges that the goodness of praise and blame is dependent on the knowledge that the agent is an agent.
And as for the third premise: It is in explaining that since knowledge of the branch is necessary, the knowledge of the root must also be necessary. The proof of it: That if the root is not known necessarily, the occurrence of doubt about it is possible. And when doubt occurs about the root, the occurrence of doubt about the branch follows. Thus it is established: That the root being theoretical (naẓarī) permits the possibility of doubt about the branch. And since we have proven that the occurrence of doubt about this branch is impossible—I mean the knowledge of deserving praise and blame—it is necessary that the knowledge of that root, I mean the knowledge that the servant is a bringer into existence, is not susceptible to doubt. And whenever the matter is thus, that is known necessarily. Thus it is established by what we have mentioned: That the division you mentioned, if sound, would necessitate the denial of the servant being a bringer into existence of his own actions, and it is established that this is false, so that division must be false.

Commentary
This passage is a complex refutation of an opponent's division that would negate human agency, followed by a three-premise proof that human agency is known necessarily. The author begins by stating that if the opponent's division were accepted, it would lead to the absurdity that the servant has no power at all. He then explains the entailment: if both God and the servant will opposite actions on the same indivisible atom (jawhar fard), then either both wills occur (impossible), neither occurs (impossible), or only one occurs—but this last option is also impossible because the power of each over the indivisible atom admits no variation in strength, so if one prevails without a preponderator, that would be a preponderance without cause (murajjiḥ), which is impossible. Thus the division would imply the servant has no power, which is impossible because we necessarily know praise and blame are good. That knowledge is a branch (farʿ) dependent on the root (aṣl) that the servant is an agent (fāʿil) and bringer into existence (mūjid). Since the branch is known necessarily, the root must also be known necessarily. The author then elaborates three premises: (1) The goodness of praise and blame is known necessarily—everyone instinctively praises benefactors and blames harmers. (2) This knowledge depends on the knowledge that the agent is an agent—we blame the thrower, not the stone. (3) If the root were theoretical (naẓarī), doubt could arise in the branch, but since the branch is necessary and beyond doubt, the root must be necessary as well. Therefore, the servant's agency is self-evident, and any division that would deny it is false.

Misconception: Some think the argument sets human power equal to divine power.
Correction: The author explicitly states that God's power is infinite over infinite objects, but over a finite indivisible atom, strength cannot vary; it is not about equality but about the nature of the object.

Human Agency is Self-Evident Through Praise and Blame
Author presents three premises to prove human agency is known necessarily via praise and blame.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the necessarily known goodness of praise and blame proves that human agency is known necessarily, not theoretically.

Core Idea
Praise and blame are self-evident; they depend on the agent being a true agent.

After showing the logical dead end of the opponent's division, Sanusi now builds a positive argument. He argues that our immediate, instinctive judgments of praise and blame are only meaningful if humans are genuine agents. This leads him to three premises that establish human agency as necessary knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
This passage is structured into three premises, so pay attention to how each is introduced.
2
Understand the first premise: goodness of praise/blame is known necessarily
The author gives examples: we instinctively praise benefactors and blame harmers. This is clear and obvious to everyone, and anyone who denies it is obstinate.
This is the foundation of the argument.
3
Second premise: that knowledge depends on agency
The author contrasts the thrower of a stone and the stone itself. We blame the thrower because he acted by choice, but we do not blame the stone because it has no power. So judgment of praise/blame depends on recognizing the agent as an agent.
4
Third premise: if the root were theoretical, doubt would arise in the branch
If knowledge of agency (the root) were theoretical (naẓarī), doubt could occur. But since the branch (praise/blame) is known necessarily and free from doubt, the root must also be necessary.
This is a logical argument from the certainty of the branch.
5
Conclusion: human agency is self-evident
Therefore, the servant's being a bringer into existence (mūjid) is known necessarily, not theoretically. The opponent's division that would deny this is false.

The proof of it: That whoever throws a stone at a person's face and wounds him, he finds in himself blame for that thrower, and he does not find at all blame for that stone. And if it were said to him: You distinguished between them in this judgment? Every rational person distinguishes between them by saying: The thrower did this act by his choice, so he deserves praise and blame. As for the stone, it has no power over the act at all. Thus it is established by what we have mentioned: That sound reason judges that the goodness of praise and blame is dependent on the knowledge that the agent is an agent.
And as for the third premise: It is in explaining that since knowledge of the branch is necessary, the knowledge of the root must also be necessary. The proof of it: That if the root is not known necessarily, the occurrence of doubt about it is possible. And when doubt occurs about the root, the occurrence of doubt about the branch follows. Thus it is established: That the root being theoretical (naẓarī) permits the possibility of doubt about the branch. And since we have proven that the occurrence of doubt about this branch is impossible—I mean the knowledge of deserving praise and blame—it is necessary that the knowledge of that root, I mean the knowledge that the servant is a bringer into existence, is not susceptible to doubt. And whenever the matter is thus, that is known necessarily. Thus it is established by what we have mentioned: That the division you mentioned, if sound, would necessitate the denial of the servant being a bringer into existence of his own actions, and it is established that this is false, so that division must be false.
Source pages p. 139

Commentary
This passage is a complex refutation of an opponent's division that would negate human agency, followed by a three-premise proof that human agency is known necessarily. The author begins by stating that if the opponent's division were accepted, it would lead to the absurdity that the servant has no power at all. He then explains the entailment: if both God and the servant will opposite actions on the same indivisible atom (jawhar fard), then either both wills occur (impossible), neither occurs (impossible), or only one occurs—but this last option is also impossible because the power of each over the indivisible atom admits no variation in strength, so if one prevails without a preponderator, that would be a preponderance without cause (murajjiḥ), which is impossible. Thus the division would imply the servant has no power, which is impossible because we necessarily know praise and blame are good. That knowledge is a branch (farʿ) dependent on the root (aṣl) that the servant is an agent (fāʿil) and bringer into existence (mūjid). Since the branch is known necessarily, the root must also be known necessarily. The author then elaborates three premises: (1) The goodness of praise and blame is known necessarily—everyone instinctively praises benefactors and blames harmers. (2) This knowledge depends on the knowledge that the agent is an agent—we blame the thrower, not the stone. (3) If the root were theoretical (naẓarī), doubt could arise in the branch, but since the branch is necessary and beyond doubt, the root must be necessary as well. Therefore, the servant's agency is self-evident, and any division that would deny it is false.
