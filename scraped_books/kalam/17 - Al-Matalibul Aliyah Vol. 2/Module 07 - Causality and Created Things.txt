The Independent Existence of Space and Direction
Proving the independent existence of direction/space through a physical example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the ninth proof establishes the independent existence of space and direction through the example of a moving mountain and refutes the mutakallimun's view that they are mere mental constructs.

Core Idea
Space and direction are real existents, not mere mental imaginings.

Ibn al-‘Arabī now presents his ninth proof for the existence of a non-contained being. The proof first establishes the reality of space and time as existents independent of the mind. Focusing on space, he uses a vivid example of a mountain shifting its location to show that the direction from which it moved remains a distinct entity. This leads to a refutation of the mutakallimun who claim that direction is only mental.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation to get a sense of the argument. Notice the definition of space and the thought experiment about the mountain.
The passage cuts off at 'fathabata' but the core argument is complete.
2
Follow the logical steps
Identify the definition of space, the example of the mountain, and the reasoning that the direction is distinct and real. Then see how the author refutes the opposing view.
The argument uses a necessary (badihi) knowledge that the direction does not move.
3
Reflect on the refutation
Consider the mutakallimun's claim that direction is only mental. How does the author's emphasis on the reality of movement independently of mind undermine this?

The ninth proof: We say: Space and time are existent. And they are not at all contained in space and time. So the existence of that which is not encompassed by space and time is established. So we need here to explain two matters: First: That time and space are existent. And we say: As for space, what is meant is the void and empty extension in which the body occurs. The evidence for it: If we suppose that a mountain is uprooted from its place and moves entirely to another side, then what moves is the mountain. Therefore, we say: This mountain moved from that direction to another direction. This is because this transfer occurs in reality, whether the intellect and mind exist or not. And if that is so, then the direction must exist in itself whether the mind exists or not. So it is established.

Commentary
This passage presents the ninth proof, which aims to establish the reality of space (makan) and time (zaman) as existents independent of the mind, and refutes the mutakallimun who deny the independent existence of direction (jiha) and space. The argument begins by asserting that space and time exist and are not themselves contained in space or time. The author then focuses on space, defining it as the void or extension in which bodies are located. The proof uses the example of a mountain moving from one direction to another: the mountain moves, but the original direction remains. Since this direction is distinct from others and the movement is real, the direction must be a real existent. The mutakallimun claim that directions and spaces are mere mental constructs, but the author argues that if the movement is real in the external world regardless of mental consideration, then the direction must also be real. The proof cuts off at 'fathabata' (so it is established), but the argument is essentially complete in demonstrating the reality of direction. This serves to show that there are existents that are not sensible or imaginable in the ordinary sense, undermining reliance on wahm and khayal.

The Second Proof: No Dependence on a Like
Second proof that existence cannot depend on a like, due to circular self-dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof: that a thing's existence cannot depend on its like because symmetry forces self-dependence, which is impossible.

Core Idea
Dependence on a like collapses into self-dependence, which is absurd.

The second proof tackles a specific scenario: could a thing's existence depend on something similar to it? This might seem plausible, but careful reasoning shows it leads to a logical dead end. Sanusi demonstrates that if two things are alike, any dependence between them becomes circular. Let's follow the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dichotomy
The passage begins by stating that existence of a thing must either depend on the existence of its like or not. Note that these are the only two possibilities.
2
See why dependence on a like is rejected
Sanusi declares the first option false. Ask yourself: why would it be impossible? The next part explains.
3
Grasp the symmetry argument
Because the two are likes, they must be equal in all consequences. This means if one depends on the other, the other must also depend on the first.
This is the key step: symmetry forces reciprocity.
4
Follow the reductio
If A depends on B and B depends on A, then each depends on itself (indirectly). This is self-dependence, which is impossible by intuition.
The conclusion is that the first option is impossible.
5
Accept the conclusion
The only valid option is that existence does not depend on a like. Therefore, absence of a like does not threaten existence.
This supports divine transcendence: Allah has no like.

The second proof: Existence of a thing must either depend on the existence of its like or not. The first is false. Because when two things are likes, they must be equal in all consequences. So if the existence of this depended on the existence of its like, then the existence of the second would depend on the existence of the first, resulting in the existence of each depending on its own existence, and that is impossible in intuition.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof. It begins with a dichotomy: the existence of a thing either depends on the existence of a like thing or it does not. The first option is dismissed as false. The reasoning: since the two are likes (mithlān), they must be equal in all necessary implications. If existence of the first depends on the second, then by symmetry, the second's existence depends on the first. This leads to each thing's existence depending on its own existence (because if A depends on B, and B depends on A, then A depends on itself indirectly). Such self-dependence is intuitively impossible (muḥāl fī al-badīha). Therefore, the only valid option is that existence does not depend on a like, so absence of a like does not threaten existence.

The First Premise: Adjacency or Distinction
Razi presents the first premise and its proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first premise that every two existents in the witnessed world must be either adjacent or distinct by direction, and that this judgment requires a cause.

Core Idea
Every existent relation has a cause grounded in existence itself.

Before we can evaluate the adjacency argument, we need to establish its foundational premises. Razi begins with a claim about the spatial relation between any two existents in the witnessed world: they are either adjacent or distinct. But this claim, he argues, cannot be taken for granted—it must have a cause. This lesson explores that first premise and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the first premise
Read the Arabic text for the first premise and its translation. Pay attention to the structure: a claim followed by a proof.
The claim is about existents in the witnessed world, not about God.
2
Identify the key terms
Note the terms 'موجودين' (two existents), 'مجانبا' (adjacent), 'مباينا' (distinct), 'علة' (cause).
These terms are central to the argument.
3
Understand the proof
The proof contrasts existents with non-existents. Ask why non-existents cannot have this judgment and why existents can.
This establishes that the judgment depends on existence itself.
4
Reflect on the purpose
Think about why Razi starts with this general principle before addressing the specific case of God and the world.
It sets the stage for the next premises.

Commentary
This passage establishes three premises needed to complete the previous argument. The first premise asserts that every two existents in the witnessed world must be adjacent or distinct, and this judgment needs a cause. The proof: non-existents cannot have this property, so existents have it. The second premise shows the cause cannot be substantiality or accidentally because substances cannot be adjacent to each other (they are separate) and accidents cannot be distinct by direction (they inhere in substances). The third premise shows the cause is not temporality. Two proofs: (a) temporality involves non-existence, which is not a cause, so only existence remains; (b) if temporality were the cause, then ignorance of temporality would imply ignorance of the adjacency, but we see that a materialist who believes the heavens are eternal still knows that the heavens are adjacent to or distinct from other things. Therefore, existence alone is the cause. The argument forces that since God exists, He must have a spatial relation to the world.

The Second Premise: The Cause Is Not Substance or Accident
Razi eliminates substance and accident as possible causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the cause of the adjacency/distinction judgment cannot be substantiality or accidentally.

Core Idea
Neither substance nor accident can be the determinant of spatial relation.

Having established that the judgment of adjacency or distinction requires a cause, Razi now examines two possible candidates: being a substance or being an accident. He argues that neither can be the determinant (muqtada) because each would lead to impossible consequences. This lesson walks through those arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the second premise
Read the Arabic for the second premise. Notice it has two parts: one for substance and one for accident.
The structure is similar: if X were the cause, then ... but that is false.
2
Understand the argument against substance
If substantiality were the cause, then every substance would be either adjacent or distinct. But substances cannot be adjacent to each other (they are separate). So that's impossible.
Think of two separate chairs—they are distinct, not adjacent.
3
Understand the argument against accident
If accidentally were the cause, then every accident would be either adjacent or distinct. But accidents cannot be distinct by direction because they inhere in substances.
Accidents exist in substances, so they cannot be separate.
4
Reflect on the elimination process
By eliminating substance and accident, Razi narrows the possibilities for the true cause.
This prepares for the third premise that the cause is existence itself.

Refuting the Objection: Substance or Accident as Causes of Divisibility
Author refutes objection that divisibility is caused by being substance or accident.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Substance and accident cause opposite effects; they cannot be a single cause of division.

This passage presents a classic theological objection: if divisibility is a caused judgment, why not explain it by substance or accident? The author shows that the objector has conflated two different judgments—adjacency and distinctness—into one, and that no existent in the witnessed realm can exhibit both. Through careful analysis, the author reveals the sophistry and upholds the original thesis.

The Impossibility of One Wish Occurring Without a Preponderator
He answers the first objection about why only one god's wish cannot occur.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why it is impossible for one of two gods' wishes to occur without a preponderator, and how that would imply necessitation.

Core Idea
Without a preponderator, no possibility can occur necessarily—a key argument for tawhid.

When arguing for the oneness of God, theologians faced the objection: why couldn't the wish of one god occur without the other? This might seem like a simple possibility. However, a deep logical problem arises: if both gods are equally powerful and their wishes are equally possible, then the occurrence of one wish without a preponderator would imply that it occurred by necessity, which is impossible. Let's examine this argument carefully.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
Read the first sentence which presents the objection: 'why is it not permissible that the wish of one of them occurs without the other?'
This objection tries to allow for one god's wish to prevail.
2
Understand the answer
Read the answer: because it would require the occurrence of one of two possibilities without a preponderator, occurring necessarily, which is impossible.
Note the key term 'preponderator' (مرجح) and 'necessity' (وجوب).
3
Ask why necessity is problematic
Consider why a 'necessary occurrence' of a contingent event is impossible. In Islamic theology, contingent things require a cause or preponderator.
This connects to the broader principle that nothing happens without a reason.
4
Connect to tawhid
Reflect on how this argument supports the impossibility of multiple gods—if there were two, their wishes would equally compete and no wish could occur without a preponderator.
