The Dilemma of Sensory Imagination: Consistency or Rejection

Learning Objective

If we allow imagination to judge matters about God, we must apply it consistently everywhere, but that leads to absurdities. If we reject it, we must set it aside completely. The middle ground of selective use is logically incoherent. This short passage is a powerful refutation of those who would use imagination only when it suits them.

Refuting the Objection: The Subject Need Not Be Conceived
Razi refutes the objection that the subject of a proposition must be conceived.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that the rule 'the subject of a proposition must be conceived' is self-defeating.

Core Idea
A rule claiming all subjects must be conceived fails when applied to itself.

Razi now faces a logical objection: how can we know God's essence if we cannot conceive it? The objectors claim that any proposition must have a conceived subject. But Razi turns this rule against itself, showing that the objection's own statement leads to a contradiction or falsehood.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objectors assert that the subject of any proposition must be conceived. Thus to speak about God, we must conceive Him. This seems to block knowledge of the unconceived divine essence.
2
Razi's counter-argument
Razi points out that the objector's own statement 'what is not conceived cannot be judged' is itself a proposition. Its subject is 'what is not conceived.' If the rule holds, then this subject must be conceived, which is contradictory.
3
Analyze the two possibilities
The objector may try to avoid the contradiction by claiming that 'what is not conceived' is conceived insofar as it is unconceived. Razi then examines two ways the subject could be understood: as the unconceived qua unconceived, or as conceived. Both lead to problems.
4
Conclude the dilemma
If the subject is unconceived (first possibility), the rule is violated. If it is conceived (second possibility), the predicate 'cannot be judged' becomes false, because a conceived thing can be judged. Either way, the objection fails.
This shows the rule cannot be universal.

The second question: If we grant that the concept of necessary existence in itself is something fixed, and we grant that those things are distinguished by their determinations, then why do you say that the determination of every determinate thing is something fixed, such that it entails that each one of those individuals is composed in its essence? So we need here to demonstrate that determination is not a fixed thing, and then to demonstrate that if it were so, composition would not follow. As for demonstrating that determination cannot be a fixed thing, it is indicated by aspects: The first aspect: If determination were a fixed thing, then it would be necessary that the determination of this person be different from the determination of that other person, for if the determination of this were the same as the determination of that other, then this would be identical to that other, and that would prevent multiplicity. And if this is established, we say: these multiple determinations share in the essence called determination, and are distinguished by their individuations, so it would entail that the determination of determination is additional to it. And this leads to infinite regress. But the claim of infinite regress necessitates the claim of the existence of causes and effects without end. Because the determination of every determinate thing is a cause for the occurrence of that determinate thing. But the claim of the existence of causes and effects without end is impossible. So this is impossible. And here is another aspect indicating the impossibility of this and that: Every multiplicity has a unity within it. So if this multiplicity occurred, then unity would be present within it. But whatever is assumed to be one, that one is determinate, so its determination is additional to it, so that one would not be one, and we have assumed it to be so. This is a contradiction. So it is established that the claim that determination is additional to the essence leads to this impossibility, so the claim is false. And let it not be said: Why is it not permissible to say: when we say 'this blackness', this blackness is only determinate because the essence of blackness is conjoined with the essence of determination, and likewise the essence of determination is conjoined with the essence of blackness: so the essence of blackness becomes determinate by the conjunction of the essence of determination to it, and likewise the essence of determination becomes determinate by the conjunction of the essence of blackness to it, and in this way the regress is cut off? For we say: This is false. Because the essence of blackness is a universal essence, and the essence of determination is a universal essence, and the universal essence, when it is restricted by another universal essence, the result after that is universal, not particular. So if it were as you mention, this blackness would be universal, not a particular individual. And since it is not so, we know that what you have mentioned is false.

Commentary
This passage is a proof (istidlal) that determination (ta'ayyun) is not a fixed attribute (amr thabit) added to the essence. The author, Razi, presents the second question in a dialectical format. The questioner grants that necessary existence is something fixed and that things are distinguished by their determinations, but asks why the Mutakallimun claim that determination is itself a fixed thing, which would then force each necessary existent to be composite. Razi then states that we need two demonstrations: (1) that determination is not a fixed thing, and (2) that if it were, composition would not follow. He then proceeds to the first demonstration with multiple aspects (wujuh). The first aspect argues: if determination were a fixed thing, then each determination must be distinct from others, otherwise multiplicity would be impossible. Since multiple determinations share the essence 'determination', they need further differentiation, leading to an infinite regress of determinations of determinations. This infinite regress is impossible because it would entail an infinite chain of causes and effects, which is impossible. He then gives another argument within the same aspect: every multiplicity has a unity, but if determination is additional, then each unity would itself be determinate by an additional determination, leading to a contradiction. The proof concludes that determination cannot be additional. Then an objection is raised: perhaps determination and the essence determine each other mutually, thus avoiding regress. Razi replies that both essences are universal, so their conjunction yields a universal, not a particular. Therefore the objection fails. This proof is standard in kalam to refute the notion that determination is an attribute distinct from the essence, especially in discussions of necessary existence.

The First Proof: Infinite Regress of Determinations
Razi presents the first argument against determination being a fixed attribute, based on infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first aspect of Razi's proof that determination cannot be a fixed attribute because it leads to an infinite regress of determinations.

Core Idea
If determination were fixed, each determination would require a further determination, leading to an impossible infinite regress.

Razi now presents the first aspect of his demonstration. He argues that if determination were a fixed thing, then each person's determination must be distinct from others, otherwise multiplicity would collapse. But these multiple determinations share a common essence and need further individuation, leading to an infinite regress of determinations of determinations, which is impossible because it would entail an infinite causal chain.

Guided Path
1
Read the first aspect carefully
Read the passage from 'الوجه الأول' to 'فكان هذا محالا'. Follow the logical steps.
2
Trace the regress argument
Identify the steps: distinctness, shared essence, need for further individuation, regress, impossibility.
3
Understand why regress is impossible
Each determination is a cause; infinite regress means infinite causes, which is impossible.

The Second Aspect and Refutation of Mutual Determination
Razi gives a second argument and then refutes an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second aspect of the proof based on the unity-multiplicity argument and Razi's refutation of the objection that mutual determination can avoid regress.

Core Idea
If determination were additional, every unity would lose its unity, and mutual determination fails because it produces universals, not particulars.

Razi presents a second aspect of the proof: every multiplicity must contain a unity, but if determination is additional, that unity would itself need a determination, leading to contradiction. Then an objection is raised: maybe determination and essence mutually determine each other, thus avoiding regress. Razi refutes this by showing that both essences are universal, so their conjunction yields a universal, not a particular individual.

Since multiple determinations share the essence 'determination', they need further differentiation, leading to an infinite regress of determinations of determinations. This infinite regress is impossible because it would entail an infinite chain of causes and effects, which is impossible. He then gives another argument within the same aspect: every multiplicity has a unity, but if determination is additional, then each unity would itself be determinate by an additional determination, leading to a contradiction. The proof concludes that determination cannot be additional. Then an objection is raised: perhaps determination and the essence determine each other mutually, thus avoiding regress. Razi replies that both essences are universal, so their conjunction yields a universal, not a particular. Therefore the objection fails. This proof is standard in kalam to refute the notion that determination is an attribute distinct from the essence, especially in discussions of necessary existence.

The fifth argument: There is no meaning for determination except that this thing is not that other. And it is known that this concept is pure negation. And it is not said: The proof that determination is a positive concept is that we demonstrate that it is a concept additional to the original quiddity, then we demonstrate that that additional must be a fixed thing. As for what indicates that it is a concept additional to the original quiddity, there are aspects: The first: If there were no difference between blackness and this blackness, then whenever blackness occurs, this blackness would have occurred, and it is known that that is false. So it is established that our statement 'this blackness' includes a restriction that is not present in our statement 'blackness'. The second: Our statement 'blackness' is a quiddity that does not prevent sharing in its very concept, while our statement 'this blackness' prevents sharing. Were it not for a difference in reality, there would not be this difference in this judgment.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth argument (al-wajh al-khāmis) which defines determination (ta‘ayyun) as pure negation (salb maḥḍ): it merely indicates that a thing is not another. Then an objection is introduced: someone argues that determination is a positive (thubūtī) concept because it is additional to the quiddity, and any additional must be a fixed (thābit) thing. The author responds by listing two proofs (wujūh) that demonstrate determination is additional to the quiddity (although this seems to support the objection). The first proof: if there were no difference between 'blackness' and 'this blackness', then whenever blackness occurs, this blackness would occur, but that is false; thus 'this blackness' contains an extra restriction. The second proof: 'blackness' as a quiddity does not prevent sharing (shirkah) in its concept, while 'this blackness' prevents sharing, indicating an additional reality. The response continues on the next page. The purpose of these proofs within the author's overall argument is not fully clear from this excerpt alone.
