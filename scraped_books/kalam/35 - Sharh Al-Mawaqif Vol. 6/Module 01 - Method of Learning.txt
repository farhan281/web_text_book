Understanding the Distinction: Mukhtar vs Madhhab Mukhtar
The commentator clarifies three potential confusions regarding the author's use of 'mukhtar' and its implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between 'al-mukhtar' (the chosen definition) and 'al-madhhab al-mukhtar' (the chosen doctrine), and why this distinction matters for evaluating definitions of knowledge in kalam.

Core Idea
The best definition is not necessarily the school's doctrine.

This passage from the commentary on Sharh al-Mawaqif clarifies a subtle but important distinction. When an author says a definition is 'chosen', it does not automatically mean it is the official doctrine of his school. The commentator warns against conflating these two meanings and shows how the author's own statements might seem contradictory. Let's work through the passage to grasp this key methodological point.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Let the commentator's clarifications come through.
Pay attention to the phrases 'al-mukhtar' and 'al-madhhab al-mukhtar'.
2
Identify the three clarifications
The commentator makes three distinct points. Find each: the warning about 'mukhtar' vs 'madhhab mukhtar', the apparent contradiction in the author's two statements, and the interpretation of 'al-‘ālamiyya'.
These correspond to the three Arabic sentences starting with 'قوله'.
3
Understand the first clarification
Notice that 'al-mukhtar' means free from defect, not tied to any school. This is key to avoiding a common misunderstanding.
4
Analyze the second clarification
The commentator points out a potential inconsistency in the author's positions: earlier he seemed to say knowledge is an attribute with relation, later he says it is the relation itself. This shows the importance of context.
5
Grasp the third clarification
The commentator explains that 'al-‘ālamiyya' should be understood as the relation itself, not a 'state' (ḥāl), to avoid confusion with the proponents of states.

Mental Attributes as Second Intelligibles and Two Considerations of Quiddity
Defining mental attributes and explaining the two considerations of quiddity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand what mental attributes are, why they are called second intelligibles, and the two ways of considering quiddity that affect judgments about possibility.

Core Idea
Mental attributes are second-order concepts; quiddity can be considered mentally or in itself.

This passage introduces a foundational distinction in Islamic logic and theology: the concept of mental attributes as second intelligibles. The author explains that these attributes arise from reflection on first-order concepts and are key to understanding mental existence. He then distinguishes two considerations of quiddity—as existing in the mind and in itself—and clarifies when judgments about possibility can be made.

Guided Path
1
Understand 'second intelligibles'
Learn that mental attributes are called second intelligibles because they belong to the second degree of intellection, not the first. They are attributes that attach to concepts, not to external objects.
For example, 'universality' is a second intelligible applied to the concept 'human'.
2
Examine the two considerations of quiddity
Notice the distinction: quiddity as mental (impossible externally) and quiddity considered in itself (may be possible or impossible, but judgment only in the mind).
This distinction prevents confusion between mental and extra-mental existence.
3
Reflect on the commentator's added benefit
The commentary highlights that a single attribute can be considered either mental or as a quiddity attribute depending on the perspective, and its judgment occurs only in the mind.
This is a subtle logical point from the Eastern Investigations.

This distinction is foundational for later arguments about mental existence.

Objection and Answer on Knowledge of Particulars
Al-Jurjani refutes the claim that particulars can be known intellectually without sense.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the claim that particulars can be known intellectually without sense is fallacious, and the crucial distinction between universal and particular knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge of a particular as particular requires sense; intellectual knowledge is always universal.

This passage presents a philosophical objection against the necessity of sense for knowledge of particulars. The objector argues that we can have full knowledge of a particular before sensing it. Al-Jurjani responds by exposing the subtle confusion between universal and particular modes of perception. Understanding this distinction is essential for clarifying the limits of intellectual knowledge.

Guided Path
1

2
Examine Al-Jurjani's response
See how he traces the error to a failure to distinguish between perceiving the particular as particular vs. as universal. This is the core of the refutation.
The distinction is subtle but crucial.
3
Reflect on the philosophical implication
Understand that the intellect can only grasp the universal nature of things, not their concrete particularity. Sensory experience is indispensable for knowledge of particulars as such.

Division of Knowledge into Detailed and Total
Defines tafsili and ijmali knowledge with an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between detailed (tafsili) and total (ijmali) knowledge and why total knowledge is genuine knowledge, not ignorance.

Core Idea
Knowledge is either detailed (part-by-part) or total (whole at once); both are real.

This passage from Sanusi's foundational text introduces a key epistemological distinction that underlies many discussions in kalam. Before we can talk about how we know Allah, we need to clarify what it means to 'know' something. Sanusi defines two modes of knowledge with a concrete example that shows total knowledge is not mere ignorance.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Try to grasp the definitions before analyzing them.
Notice the parenthetical explanations in the Arabic.
2
Identify the two types and their definitions
Find the definitions of detailed and total knowledge. Detailed knowledge involves observing parts one after another; total knowledge involves grasping the whole all at once.
3
Analyze the example of the person who knows the answer
The example illustrates total knowledge: someone knows an answer and brings it to mind whole when asked. The author then argues this implies he knows the reality of the answer because he knows he is capable of it.
This shows that total knowledge is not vague; it includes awareness of one's ability.
4
Understand the argument about relational knowledge
The author states that knowledge of a relation (being capable of answering) requires knowledge of both endpoints: the capability and the answer itself. Therefore, total knowledge is genuine knowledge.
This is a key logical move that validates ijmali knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification. It divides knowledge into two types: detailed (tafsili) and total (ijmali). Detailed knowledge is when one observes the parts of the known thing one by one. Total knowledge is illustrated by the example of a person who knows an answer to a question; when asked, he brings the whole answer to mind all at once. The author explains that this person is certain that he is capable of answering, and this certainty implies he knows the reality of the answer, because knowledge of a relation (here, the relation of capability) requires knowledge of both endpoints (the capability and the answer itself). Thus, total knowledge is a genuine knowledge of the answer, though not yet detailed.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed investigation of the fifteenth objective concerning knowledge that has no known object (al-mustahil, the impossible). The author begins by explaining two ways to conceive the impossible: (1) by analogy (tashbih) with something possible, e.g., the combination of blackness and sweetness is actually conceivable, so we analogize to understand the impossible combination of blackness and whiteness; (2) by negation (nafy), understanding that a certain concept cannot exist at all. He then elaborates that the impossible cannot be conceived in its essence, only through relation to existent things or through a general negation. He introduces an important distinction: something like 'void' is conceived by relation, while something composite like 'Anqa' (a mythical bird) or 'flying human' is conceived by first imagining its possible parts and then their conjunction. He concludes that the impossible is not subject to positive judgments, only relative ones, and that this does not entail that the impossible has mental existence. He then defends Ibn Sina's position against misunderstandings, clarifying that the division (tardid) mentioned by the author (al-Iji) and the commentator (al-Jurjani) is not from Ibn Sina. Finally, he analyzes Abu Hashim's statement that there is knowledge with no known object, giving two interpretations: either the impossible is not known in its particular essence, or it has no fixed established reality. The passage demonstrates sophisticated epistemological analysis within Sunni kalam, showing how rational judgments about impossible objects are handled.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed investigation of the fifteenth objective concerning knowledge that has no known object (al-mustahil, the impossible). The author begins by explaining two ways to conceive the impossible: (1) by analogy (tashbih) with something possible, e.g., the combination of blackness and sweetness is actually conceivable, so we analogize to understand the impossible combination of blackness and whiteness; (2) by negation (nafy), understanding that a certain concept cannot exist at all. He then elaborates that the impossible cannot be conceived in its essence, only through relation to existent things or through a general negation. He introduces an important distinction: something like 'void' is conceived by relation, while something composite like 'Anqa' (a mythical bird) or 'flying human' is conceived by first imagining its possible parts and then their conjunction. He concludes that the impossible is not subject to positive judgments, only relative ones, and that this does not entail that the impossible has mental existence. He then defends Ibn Sina's position against misunderstandings, clarifying that the division (tardid) mentioned by the author (al-Iji) and the commentator (al-Jurjani) is not from Ibn Sina. Finally, he analyzes Abu Hashim's statement that there is knowledge with no known object, giving two interpretations: either the impossible is not known in its particular essence, or it has no fixed established reality. The passage demonstrates sophisticated epistemological analysis within Sunni kalam, showing how rational judgments about impossible objects are handled.

Mental Existence and the Impossible
Sanusi defends mental existence and interprets Abu Hashim's theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the proof of mental existence does not imply that the impossible has a specific mental existence, and how Abu Hashim's statement is interpreted.

Core Idea
The impossible has no mental existence; it is known only through relative judgments.

This lesson addresses a crucial objection: if mental existence (al-wujud al-dhihni) allows us to conceive non-existents, then why doesn't the impossible have a specific mental form? The author responds by clarifying that the impossible is known only through relative judgments (analogy or negation), not through a direct form. He then analyzes Abu Hashim's famous statement that there is 'knowledge without a known object', offering two interpretations. This shows the sophistication of kalam epistemology.

Refutation of an Intermediary between Health and Disease
Al-Jurjani refutes the existence of an intermediary between health and disease.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why there is no true intermediary state between health and disease when the conditions of logical opposition are properly observed.

Core Idea
Health and disease are true opposites; a third state exists only if definitions are changed or conditions ignored.

Al-Jurjani tackles an old medical and philosophical puzzle: is there a state between health and disease, such as convalescence? He argues that such an intermediary only appears when we neglect the conditions of logical opposition—unity of subject, time, and aspect. By grounding the discussion in Ibn Sina's framework, he shows that when these conditions are met, a single organ at a single time must be either sound or unsound. The illusion of an intermediary arises only from overly restrictive definitions, making the dispute verbal rather than real.
