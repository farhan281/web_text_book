Al-Razi on the Naming of Substance
Quotes al-Razi's explanation that 'jawhar' comes from 'jahr' (manifestness).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's etymological explanation of 'jawhar' and why manifest existence alone does not require applying the term universally.

Core Idea
A name's reason does not have to apply to all things that share that reason.

Imam al-Razi, a major figure in kalam and philosophy, offers an insight into why substance is called 'jawhar' (الجوهر). He derives it from 'jahr' (manifestness), but also addresses a subtle point: even if accidents also have manifest existence, that does not force them to be called 'jawhar'. This clarifies that naming in technical terms follows conventional usage, not strict logical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
First, read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get a sense of al-Razi's concise style. Notice how he states a derivation, then an objection, then a reply.
The passage is from the beginning of the Station on Substances.
2
Understand the derivation from 'jahr'
Al-Razi says that 'jawhar' comes from 'jahr', meaning manifestness. The term 'jawhariyya' (substantiality) is used because the existence of a substance is more manifest than that of an accident.
This etymology is not the only one, but it is the one al-Razi adopts.
3
Consider the potential objection
Al-Razi anticipates a counterargument: if manifestness is the reason for the name, and accidents also have manifest existence, why aren't they called 'jawhar'?
4
Learn al-Razi's response
He replies that naming does not require 'ittiraad' (regularity) — the reason for the name need not apply to every case that shares that property. The name 'jawhar' is fixed by usage, not by a universal rule.
This protects against overgeneralization in terminology.
5
Reflect on the lesson's importance
This short passage teaches a key principle of technical terminology: the reason for a name does not have to be exclusive or perfectly regular. It helps avoid confusion in theological debates.
Keep this principle in mind when encountering other technical terms.

The Philosophers' Division of Substance: Five Categories
Classifying substances into form, hyle, body, soul, and intellect, based on denial of atomism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Peripatetic classification of substances into five categories (form, hyle, body, soul, intellect) and why it depends on denying atomism.

Core Idea
The philosophers divide substance based on being a state, substratum, composite, or relation to body.

This lesson presents the Peripatetic division of substance (جوهر) into five types. Unlike the mutakallimun who often accept atomism, the philosophers build their classification on the denial of indivisible atoms. Understanding this division is essential for grasping later discussions in Islamic philosophy and theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic text from the source. Follow along with the English translation to grasp the logical flow of the classification.

The passage defines five substances: form (صورة), hyle (هيولى), body (جسم), soul (نفس), and intellect (عقل). For each, note the defining condition given.

3

The passage states that this entire division is built on denying the atom (الجوهر الفرد). Consider why: if atoms existed, there would be no real forms or hyle, only atoms.
This links the classification to a metaphysical stance against atomism.

The passage then notes that this entire classification depends on denying atomism (الجوهر الفرد). If atoms were real, there would be no form or hyle, only a body made of atoms.

Composition: Existing Accident or Mental Concept?
Clarifies the nature of composition: accident vs. mental.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the difference between considering composition as an existing accident versus a mental concept, and the implications for defining body.

Core Idea
The distinction between wujudi and i'tibari determines whether body is a sum of parts or an atom composed with another.

Building on the initial dispute, the commentary explains that the subsistence of composition in body is agreed upon; the real disagreement is whether it is an existing accident or a mental consideration. This affects how we define body: as a sum of parts (if composition is real) or as an atom composed with another (if composition is mental).

3
Contrast with al-Qadi's view
Al-Qadi considers composition mental, so body is defined as the atom composed with another, with composition as an external condition.
This avoids the problem of requiring an existent accident.

The Indivisible Atom Has No Shape
Proof that the atom has no shape via definition and extension to non-resemblance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why theologians unanimously agreed that the indivisible atom has no shape, based on the definition of shape as a form enclosing boundaries that requires parts.

Core Idea
Shape requires parts; the atom has no parts, so it has no shape.

Theologians agree that the indivisible atom (jawhar fard) possesses no shape. This conclusion follows directly from the definition of shape itself: shape is a form that encloses boundaries, and boundaries imply parts. Since the atom is indivisible and has no parts, it cannot have a shape. Al-Qadi extends this to argue that it cannot even resemble any shape, as resemblance in shape requires having a shape.

Guided Path
1
Read the core passage
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it begins by stating the consensus, then defines shape, then applies that definition to the atom.
The passage includes both the main argument and al-Qadi's additional point.
2
Understand the definition of shape
Shape is defined as a form (hay’a) that encloses either one boundary (sphere) or multiple boundaries (polygon). This definition is crucial because it ties shape to boundaries.
A boundary (had) is a limit, and a limit is only conceivable in relation to something that has a limit.
3
See why the atom cannot have shape
Since a boundary requires two parts—the thing that limits and the thing being limited—anything with shape must have parts. The indivisible atom has no parts, so it cannot have shape.
This is why the theologians say the atom has no shape by consensus.
4
Examine al-Qadi's extension
Al-Qadi adds that because the atom has no shape, it cannot resemble (yushakil) any shape. Resemblance in shape (mushakala) means sharing the same shape, which is impossible for something without shape.
Other scholars distinguish between mushakala (shape-resemblance) and mushabaha (general resemblance).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The atom's 'share of area' means it contributes to volume, not that it has area itself.

Definition of Body as Three Dimensions at Right Angles and the Purpose of the Condition
Defines body and explains the purpose of the right angle condition for its essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of body (jism) in terms of three dimensions intersecting at right angles and why this condition is essential for the essence of body rather than merely distinguishing it from other entities.

Core Idea
Body is defined by three dimensions at right angles—a condition that realizes its essence, not merely distinguishes it.

In this passage, the author begins by explaining the concept of a right angle geometrically, then proceeds to show how the three dimensions of length, width, and depth are assumed in a body. The key point is that the condition of right angles is not to separate body from other entities like surface, but to articulate what a body truly is. This subtle distinction is crucial for understanding the kalam definition of body.

Guided Path
1
Understand the geometric definition of a right angle
The passage opens with a clear geometric example: a line standing perpendicularly on another produces two equal right angles; if inclined, acute and obtuse angles result. Internalize this as the basis for the dimension discussion.
The author's definition here is purely geometrical, not yet theological.
2
Trace the assumption of the three dimensions
Follow how length is assumed arbitrarily, then width at a right angle, then depth which is determinate. Notice the freedom in the first two dimensions and the fixedness of the third.
This illustrates how the three dimensions fit together in a body.
3
Grasp the purpose of the right angle condition
The text explicitly states that the condition is not to distinguish body from surface but to realize its essence. Understand why the condition is essential for the definition.
The Mu'tazila objection is addressed as a concession, not the primary purpose.

Commentary
This passage is a definition of body (jism) and an explanation of the condition that its three dimensions intersect at right angles. It begins by describing a right angle geometrically: a line standing perpendicularly on another line produces two equal right angles. If the line is inclined, the angles become acute and obtuse. Then it explains how the three dimensions—length, width, and depth—are imagined in a body: first assume a length in any direction, then a width intersecting it at a right angle, then a third dimension (depth) which is determinate because only one perpendicular direction remains for the third dimension relative to the first two. The passage also notes the Mu'tazila view that surface is a substance (since they held bodies are composed of surfaces, lines, and atoms), and on that concession the condition would also distinguish from surface, but this is not the main purpose.

First Doubt: The Definition of Body and Prime Matter
Raises and responds to the doubt that the body definition applies to prime matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the doubt that the definition of body applies to prime matter is raised and refuted through the distinction between essential and possible attributes.

Core Idea
A definition must capture the essence, not accidental possibilities.

In this passage, the commentator presents a doubt that the three-dimensional definition of body might inadvertently apply to prime matter, since prime matter can acquire dimensions through corporeal form. This raises a subtle point about what 'possible' means in a definition. The response clarifies that the definition is about what is necessary to the essence, not what is possible through an intermediary. We'll unpack this argument and see how it connects to broader philosophical debates about essence and accident.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the doubt
Read the Arabic passage (first part) and translation to understand the doubt: the definition of body ('dimensions assumed intersecting at right angles') seems to apply to prime matter because dimensions can be assumed in it via the corporeal form.
Note the key term 'هيولى' (prime matter) and 'صورة جسمية' (corporeal form).
2
Examine the response from al-Shifa'
The passage cites al-Shifa' to argue that the dimension is necessary to the essence of body, so the definition is about essence, not existence. This refutes the doubt because the definition requires actual possibility, not mediated possibility.
The phrase 'لازم لماهية الجسم' (necessary to the essence of body) is key.
3
Understand the Mu'tazila context
The commentator mentions the Mu'tazila view that a substantial surface exists. The definition uses right angles as a precaution (احتراز) on the basis of that view, but this is anachronistic since the definition predates the Mu'tazila.
The phrase 'الاحتراز على التنزل' means precaution on the basis of concession.
4
Reflect on the command to ponder ('تأمل')
The commentator repeatedly says 'فتأمل' (so reflect), calling the reader to think critically about the arguments and the relationship between definition and doctrinal commitments.
This emphasizes that definitions should depict the essence as it is, not be tailored to external doctrines.

Defining Jawhar: Objection and Response
Objection and response regarding the definition of jawhar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why 'existent not in a subject' is a valid description (rasm) of substance (al-jawhar) despite including accidental conditions.

Core Idea
For simple highest genera, a description using accidents is acceptable when a true definition is impossible.

This lesson examines a classical objection to the definition of jawhar as 'the existent not in a subject.' The objection claims that both conditions are accidental and thus cannot form a proper definition. We will see how the defender responds by clarifying that this is only a description (rasm), not a definition (hadd), and that simple highest genera cannot be defined essentially.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the objection
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the initial objection that the two conditions are not essential to any reality.
Pay attention to the two specific conditions: existence and 'not in a subject'.
2
Understand the two accidental conditions
The critic argues that existence is accidental and belongs to secondary intelligibles, and that 'not in a subject' is a privation (ʿadam). Both cannot be part of a proper definition (hadd).
Consider why existence cannot be part of a definition: it is an accident of existents and is a secondary intelligible.
3
See the response: this is a description (rasm), not a definition
The defender replies that this is not a definition but a description. The simple highest genera, like substance, cannot have a true definition at all.
The response also clarifies that the conditions are external to the quiddity, so rejecting genus-hood does not negate the genus.
4
Reflect on the significance for simple highest genera
Understand why simple highest genera (like jawhar) cannot be defined by essential attributes—they are basic categories that can only be described by their accidents or relations.
This shows a key principle in logic and kalam: some concepts are known by description, not definition.

Genus and Differentia: Resolving the Regress

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a combined classification and refutation passage. It first classifies the different views on the composition of the body from parts, then presents and refutes three false definitions of the body from various theological groups. The author (al-Sharh) begins by discussing the minimal composition of a body: some say it requires at least four parts, some say six, and the truth is that four suffice. He notes that the dispute about whether a composite of two or three parts is a body is verbal. He then turns to the refutation of definitions by al-Salihiyya (self-subsistent), al-Karamiyya (existent), and Hisham (thing). Each definition is refuted by showing it applies to things that are not bodies: the first applies to God and the indivisible atom, the second to those and accidents, the third to all three. Moreover, these definitions violate linguistic usage, because the word 'jism' in Arabic implies composition and extension, as in the comparative 'ajsam' meaning bulkier or more extended. The commentator then clarifies the textual structure: noting that the refutation is a separate independent passage, and that the term 'dispute is verbal' does not undermine the meaningfulness of the debate about whether composition alone suffices. The commentator also explains the grammatical parsing of the phrase 'what is like the saying of al-Salihiyya' in relation to the previous sentence. Finally, he addresses a potential objection that the Karamiyya might accept that their definition applies to God, but he dismisses this as irrelevant because the discussion is investigative, not merely polemical.

Key Takeaway
A body cannot be defined merely as 'self-subsistent', 'existent', or 'thing', because these terms are too broad and ignore the linguistic meaning of composition and extension.
