The First Source of Dream Images: The Rational Soul and Active Intellect
Al-Iji describes the first source of dream images.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that according to al-Iji, dream images can originate from the rational soul's connection to the Active Intellect during sleep.

Core Idea
Dreams can be spiritual reflections from higher principles, not just random images.

Al-Iji now turns to explain the sources of dream images. He begins with the first type, which involves the rational soul receiving images from the Active Intellect. This establishes that some dreams have a genuine metaphysical origin, reflecting truths about past, present, or future.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the translation. Let the author's own description of the process come through.
Pay attention to the terms 'rational soul' and 'Active Intellect'.
2
Identify the mechanism
The passage says the perceived images come from the rational soul (النفس الناطقة) which takes them from the Active Intellect (العقل الفعال). Understand that the Active Intellect contains all images of beings.
3
Note the hindrance and its removal
The soul is normally absorbed in managing the body, which prevents this connection. Sleep provides leisure, allowing the soul to connect and receive images.
4
Observe the scope of images
The images correspond to the soul's concerns: personal matters (family, region) or universal matters if the soul is inclined toward intelligibles.

Commentary

It describes that all images of beings past, present, and future are impressed in the higher principles (المبادئ العالية) and in the heavenly angels (الملائكة السماوية). The rational soul has the potential to connect to these principles spiritually, but its preoccupation with managing the body hinders it. When sleep provides a little leisure, it can connect and receive images appropriate to its concerns—either personal matters or even universal matters if the soul is inclined toward the intelligible. This is the first of two ways (the second way is presumably via the imagination directly, but that may be in another part).

Imagination's Role in Dream Formation and the Need for Interpretation
Al-Iji explains how imagination forms particular dream images and the need for interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the imaginative faculty transforms universal meanings into particular dream images, and why dream interpretation requires stripping away those images to recover the universal meaning.

Core Idea
Dream images are veils, not the message itself.

Al-Iji now explains how the imaginative faculty works in dreams. A universal meaning is clothed by imagination into particular images, which may be close or distant from the original. Because of this transformation, a dream cannot be taken literally; an interpreter must reverse the process to uncover the universal truth. This passage establishes the epistemology of dream interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get a sense of its flow. Then read the English translation to understand the key terms like 'imagination', 'clothing', and 'interpretation'.
Notice that the passage describes a dynamic process, not a static statement.
2
Identify the four functions of imagination
Find where Al-Iji lists the natural dispositions of imagination: imitation, transition, differentiation, and composition. Understand how each contributes to forming dream images.
These functions explain how a universal idea can become many particular images.
3
Understand the closeness or distance of images
The passage says the images may be 'close or far' from the universal. Consider that some dreams are clearer in meaning than others, depending on how much the imagination has transformed the original.
This explains why some dreams are easy to interpret and others are not.
4
Grasp the interpreter's task of stripping
Focus on the phrase 'returns retrogressively stripping it' (رجوعاً قهقرياً مجرداً). The interpreter must work backward, removing layer after layer of images to reach the universal meaning.
The process may take 'one degree or multiple degrees' depending on the transformation.

Commentary

The images it clothes may be close or far from the original universal. Therefore, to know the universal meaning behind the dream, one needs an interpreter (معبر) who strips away the particular images by a reverse process (رجوع قهقري), step by step (بمرتبة أو بمراتب), until the universal is reached, depending on the nature of the transformation.

The Philosophers' Theory of Dreams: Sources and Interpretation
Philosophical exposition of dream origins: Active Intellect, imagination, and bodily humors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' distinction between dreams derived from the Active Intellect (which may be interpretable) and those arising from imagination or bodily humors (which are confused dreams with no valid interpretation).

Core Idea
Dreams are either true reflections from the Active Intellect or confused mixtures from imagination and humors.

This passage from Sharh al-Mawaqif lays out the philosophers' detailed account of dreams. It explains how during sleep, the soul either receives forms directly from the Active Intellect or experiences images generated by the imagination or bodily humors. Understanding this distinction is key to knowing which dreams can be interpreted and which cannot.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main sources of dreams
Read the Arabic passage carefully and note the three sources mentioned: the Active Intellect, the imagination (based on waking experiences), and bodily humors/illness.
The passage also hints at celestial influences in a marginal note, but the main text emphasizes these three.
2
Analyze the two outcomes from the Active Intellect
The soul either delivers the form unchanged (only differing in universality vs. particularity) or transforms it through a chain of associations. Consider why transformation makes interpretation difficult.
3
Examine dreams from imagination
The second source involves the common sense receiving images from the imagination, which can combine or separate images from waking life. This kind of dream is very common.
The text says 'sleep is rarely free from dreams of this kind.'
4
Examine dreams from bodily humors
Illness or overwhelming humors cause the imaginative faculty to produce images corresponding to the dominant humor. Notice the examples for each humor and how they reflect the humor's nature.
Sanguine sees red, choleric sees fire, melancholic sees mountains/smoke, phlegmatic sees water/white.
5
Understand the classification of confused dreams
Both dreams from imagination and from bodily causes are classified as 'adghath ahlam' (confused dreams) and have no interpretation. Contrast this with true dreams from the Active Intellect.
This distinction is crucial for determining which dreams can be interpreted.

Commentary
This passage is a philosophical exposition of the origins of dreams, presented in the context of the Fourth Type (Type Four) and Tenth Objective of the work. The author begins by stating the philosophers' view on sleep and the power of the soul during sleep. The main idea is that dreams arise from three possible sources: the Active Intellect, the imagination (based on waking experiences), or bodily humors/illness. The passage first explains that when the soul takes a form from the Active Intellect, it may either deliver it unchanged (only differing in universality vs. particularity) or transform it through a chain of associations, making interpretation difficult or impossible. Then the second aspect is introduced: dreams reaching the common sense (al-hiss al-mushtarak) either from the imagination (which stores images from waking life) or from bodily causes such as illness or overwhelming humors. The imaginative faculty can combine or separate images. The text then gives examples of how different humors (sanguine, choleric, melancholic, phlegmatic) cause dreamers to see corresponding colored or shaped things (red things, fire, mountains, water, etc.). Dreams from imagination or bodily humors are classified as 'adghath ahlam' (confused dreams) and have no valid interpretation. The marginal notes (indicated by 'قوله :') further clarify details: the difference when forms are taken from lower souls; the meaning of 'empty' common sense; the two meanings of 'dream' in Arabic; the coordination of causes; the influence of celestial bodies; the reason liars' and poets' dreams are unreliable; the suitability of bile humor; the definition of overwhelming humor; and a response to a potential objection about the use of 'or' between illness and overwhelming humor, explaining it as synonymous or for choice. The passage is a blend of main text and commentary, covering both the philosophical theory and its terminological clarifications.

Commentary
This passage presents Imam al-Razi's two objections to the view that separation of continuity is the essential cause of pain, along with the responses to those objections. The first objection is empirical: if one cuts a hand with a sharp knife, pain is not felt immediately but after a delay. Commentary
This passage presents Imam al-Razi's two objections to the view that separation of continuity is the essential cause of pain, along with the responses to those objections. The first objection is empirical: if one cuts a hand with a sharp knife, pain is not felt immediately but after a delay. If separation were the essential cause, pain should be simultaneous with the cut; since it is not, separation is not the cause. Rather, the cut prepares the organ for a bad temperament, which then causes pain. The second objection is metaphysical: separation (tafarruq) is the absence of continuity (adam al-ittisal), and absence is non-existent; it cannot be the essential cause of something existent like pain. The responses follow: Regarding the first objection, the commentary denies the implication that an essential cause must be a necessitating cause (illa mujiba); it may depend on conditions such as the attention of the soul. For example, unconsciousness or medication can delay pain. Moreover, the cut does not directly cause pain but prepares the organ for bad temperament. Regarding the second objection, two answers are given: (1) separation is not mere absence but a movement of parts from each other, which is existent; (2) even if it were non-existent, it could still be part of the complete cause (al-illa al-tamma) as the final part (al-juz' al-akhir) that entails the effect, even if it is not an efficient cause. The passage also clarifies that attention (iltifat al-nafs) is a condition for feeling pain; if one's mind is occupied, pain may not be perceived immediately. Experience decides such matters.

Health, Disease, and the Psychical Quality Objection
Al-Iji defines health and addresses al-Razi's objection regarding disease.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Razi argued that health cannot be a psychical quality, based on the classification of disease as non-psychical.

Core Idea
If disease is not psychical, health cannot be either.

This lesson examines a key objection in philosophical medicine: if health is a psychical quality, its opposite disease must also be psychical. But physicians classify disease into three genera that are either sensible, positional, or non-existential—none of which are psychical. We will explore the definition of health, the breakdown of disease, and the implications for the nature of health.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and objection-and-answer discussion. The author begins by stating the definition of health as a property (malaka) or state (hala) that is natural and unimpaired. He notes that the state was not mentioned either due to disagreement or disregard. Then he cites Avicenna's definition from the Canon of Medicine. He argues that the three genera of disease—bad temperament, bad composition, and discontinuity—are either sensible qualities, from the category of position, or non-existential, none of which are psychical. The author then explains that bad temperament involves an excess or deficiency in one of the four qualities, and there are three possible interpretations: the quality itself, its strangeness, or the body's being described by it. Al-Razi notes that the author of al-Mawaqif limited himself to the first interpretation, making disease a sensible quality. For bad composition, it involves quantity, number, shape, position, or obstruction, all of which belong to categories like quantity or position, not psychical qualities. Discontinuity is non-existential. Thus, if disease is not psychical, health cannot be psychical either. The objection is answered by pointing out that it is sufficient for disease not being absolutely psychical that one type be non-psychical; the author's limitation is not deficient. The commentary then elaborates on the categories and dismisses other possibilities. Finally, it discusses whether discontinuity is a passion or non-existential.

The Argument for Discontinuity of Motion in Substance
Al-Iji argues for discontinuity of motion in substance via successive forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Iji's argument that motion in the category of substance must be discontinuous, based on the nature of successive forms.

Core Idea
Motion in substance is discontinuous because each substantial form is an indivisible instant.

Al-Iji presents a precise argument for why motion in substance—the transition from one substantial form to another—cannot be continuous. He examines the nature of successive forms a moving thing acquires and shows that the only possibilities lead to either cessation of motion or discontinuity. This reasoning is foundational for understanding the Ash'ari view of atomism and the structure of change in the physical world.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Pay attention to the key terms: الصورة (form), الآن (instant), and الحركة (motion).
Al-Iji uses a conditional structure: 'if... then...'
2
Identify the two possibilities for the forms
The argument considers two cases: either a form persists for an instant, or all forms are instantaneous. Notice the conclusion for each case.
If a form persists, motion stops. If all are instantaneous, then motion is either continuous (if instants follow without gap) or discontinuous (if there is a time empty of forms).
3
Understand the final conclusion
Al-Iji concludes that motion in substance is discontinuous because any continuous succession would require overlapping instants, which is impossible.
This aligns with the Ash'ari doctrine of atomism—time is composed of discrete instants.

Learning Objective

Motion in quality does not disprove discontinuity because the subject remains even when empty of qualities.

Al-Iji responds by distinguishing motion in quality from motion in substance: the subject (body or matter) can exist without qualities, so emptying of qualities does not annihilate the moving thing. However, a deeper problem emerges: if the subject is empty of qualities, there is no motion in that interval, and only instantaneous qualities exist, which do not constitute true motion. This rebuttal preserves the discontinuity thesis.

Commentary
This passage investigates the unity of motion. First, the author states that motion's unity can be personal (شخصية), specific (نوعية), or generic (جنسية), and then focuses on personal unity. For personal unity, three conditions are necessary: (1) unity of the subject (ما له الحركة) because an accident cannot inhere in two subjects at once; (2) unity of the category (ما فيه الحركة), meaning the kind of motion (e.g., alteration, growth, locomotion), because a single body can undergo different types of motion simultaneously—each is a distinct motion; (3) unity of time, because motion in one time is different from motion in another, based on the principle that a non-existent cannot be recreated identically. The author argues that unity of the category makes unity of starting point and endpoint follow, but not vice versa: different paths between the same start and end yield different motions because the intermediate categories differ. He then addresses an objection: if time is not considered, unity of category does not necessarily imply unity of start/end (e.g., a body moving up and down in the same path at different times). The answer is that when time is considered, unity of category together with unity of subject and time ensures unity of start/end. The conclusion: personal unity of motion requires unity of subject, category, and time, and if these three are unified, start and end will also be unified.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and definition of motion (حركة) in terms of whether it is essential (بالذات) or accidental (بالعرض). Second, if the principle is in the moving thing, it either involves perception (شعور) — voluntary motion (حركة إرادية) — or not — natural motion (حركة طبيعية). The author then gives examples: plant motion and pulse motion are natural because their principle is within the moving thing without perception. Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of motion in terms of speed and time. It begins by dividing motions into six types based on the motive force: forced, simple (celestial or natural), and compound (from animal force or plant; voluntary or subservient). Then the main topic: motion compared to another motion is either fast or slow. Fast motion is defined as covering an equal distance in less time, which implies covering more distance in equal time. These two properties are necessary and equivalent for fast motion. Slow motion is the opposite: it covers an equal distance in more time or less distance in equal time. The commentary clarifies that motion in itself is not fast or slow; these are relational. It then discusses an objection about the 'non-necessary property' (خاصة قاصرة) — covering more distance in less time. The objection is that covering more distance in equal time is a comprehensive property that seems to imply covering more distance in less time, but the answer explains that if part of the excess is considered together with the slow's distance, the fast that covers all the excess is not subject to that property. The commentary also addresses why the division does not include 'equal' motion: because equality is a property of quantity, not an essential description of motion. Finally, it returns to the discussion of the non-necessary property, reiterating the same point and leaving off with a question about the indivisibility of the excess, which is cut off.

Defining Fast and Slow Motion
Al-Jurjani defines fast and slow motion and discusses their properties and an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how fast and slow motion are defined relationally, their necessary properties, and the non-necessary property with its objection.

Core Idea
Speed is relational; fast motion covers equal distance in less time, and more distance in equal time.

This lesson examines the definition of fast and slow motion as relational comparisons. Al-Jurjani outlines the necessary equivalent properties of fast motion and discusses a subtle objection regarding a non-necessary property. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for grasping the philosophical foundations of motion measurement.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of fast motion
Read the passage starting from 'الحركة إذا قيست إلى حركة أخرى...' and note the two equivalent necessary properties of fast motion.
The definition is comparative, not absolute.
2
Identify necessary vs. non-necessary properties
Notice that covering equal distance in less time and more distance in equal time are necessary and equivalent. The property of covering a longer distance in a shorter time is non-necessary (خاصة قاصرة).
3
Understand the relational nature
The passage emphasizes that motion itself is not described as fast or slow; these attributes only arise when compared to another motion.
This counters a common misconception.
4
Review the objection and response
Read the objection that the non-necessary property might be comprehensive, and al-Jurjani's response that if part of the excess is considered, the fast motion is not subject to that property.
The objection hinges on the divisibility of the excess distance.
5
Note the equality division
The passage explains why equality is not a separate division: it is a property of quantity, not an essential description of motion.
