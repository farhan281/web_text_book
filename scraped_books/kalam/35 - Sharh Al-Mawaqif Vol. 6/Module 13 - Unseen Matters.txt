Dreams as False Imagination: The Initial Answer
al-Iji explains the objection and the initial classification of dreams as false imagination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the mutakallimun classify dreams as false imagination and the differing reasons of the Mu'tazila and Ash'aris.

Core Idea
Dreams are not real perceptions but false imagination due to missing conditions or against divine custom.

When someone objects that sleep opposes perception yet we see in dreams, al-Iji and al-Jurjani respond by categorizing dreams as false imagination. They present the views of the Mu'tazila, who cite loss of perceptual conditions, and the Ash'aris, who point to God's custom and the opposition of sleep. This lesson unpacks that foundational answer.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the passage from 'ولما كان لقائل أن يقول' to 'فلا تكون الرؤيا إدراكاً حقيقة' once. Notice how the objection is phrased: if sleep opposes perception, how can we see in dreams? The answer immediately classifies dreams as false imagination.
The answer distinguishes between real perception and imagination.
2
Compare Mu'tazili and Ash'ari reasons
The passage gives two different rationales. For the Mu'tazila, dreams lack the necessary conditions for perception (e.g., facing, light, medium). For the Ash'aris, perception in sleep is against God's custom and sleep is contrary to perception. Identify these differences.
The Ash'aris do not stipulate conditions like the Mu'tazila do.
3
Understand 'against custom'
Focus on the Ash'ari explanation: 'perception in the state of sleep is against custom (khalaf al-'ada)'. This means God does not normally create perception in a sleeping person. Reflect on why this is used as a reason rather than asserting impossibility.
This leaves room for miracles, as will be seen later.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection: if sleep opposes perception, how can the sleeper see and hear in dreams? The answer is that dreams are false imagination (khayal batil). The commentator distinguishes the Mu'tazili and Ash'ari reasons: for the Mu'tazila, the conditions of perception (e.g., facing, light, medium) are absent; for the Ash'aris, perception in sleep is against God's custom and sleep is contrary to perception. The commentator then adds a note about al-Qadi's view, clarifying that it does not deny suspension of judgment. He then explains that false imagination refers to sensations lacking real or customary conditions, but this does not preclude dreams from being reports of real things, like invented stories conveying truth. An objection arises: prophetic dreams are part of prophethood, so how can they be false imagination? The response is that 'false' means the sleeper mistakenly thinks he is perceiving, but the knowledge itself may be true; for the Ash'aris, it is a miracle breaking custom. The passage ends with a note on possible circularity.

The Prophetic Objection: How Can Righteous Dreams Be Part of Prophethood?
al-Jurjani responds to the objection that prophetic dreams contradict the classification of dreams as false imagination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the mutakallimun respond to the objection that prophetic dreams cannot be false imagination, and how they distinguish between sensory illusion and truthful content.

Core Idea
Prophetic dreams are not false in content; the falsehood lies in mistaking the dream for real perception.

An objector raises a strong point: if dreams are false imagination, how can the Prophet's righteous dreams be a part of prophethood? The commentator responds by clarifying that 'false' refers to the sensory illusion, not the truth of the dream's content. This lesson explores that distinction and the possibility of dreams being miraculous.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection from prophetic dreams
Read the passage from 'قوله: وأما الرؤيا فخيال باطل عند المتكلمين الخ فيه بحث' to 'كغير المعجزات والكرامات'. Notice that the objector cites hadith that righteous dreams are part of prophethood (1/46 of prophethood) and that the Prophet acted upon them before revelation.
This objection is serious because it challenges the blanket statement that dreams are false imagination.
2
Understand the response: distinction between perception and content
The response argues that 'false' in 'false imagination' refers to the sleeper's mistaken belief that he is perceiving by senses. The content of the dream, however, can be true and even a form of revelation. The commentator gives examples like stories of Harut and Marut that convey true knowledge.
The Arabic phrase 'خيال باطل' means false fancy, but it does not automatically negate the truth of the dream's message.
3
See the Ash'ari view on miraculous dreams
The Ash'aris allow that prophetic dreams are a break in God's custom (kharq al-ada), like other miracles. Since they defined dreams as against custom, they can make an exception for prophets. This reconciles the classification with the hadith.
The phrase 'عند الأصحاب فالظاهر أن الكل بالنسبة إلى عامة الخلق' indicates the general rule is for common people, allowing miracles for prophets.
