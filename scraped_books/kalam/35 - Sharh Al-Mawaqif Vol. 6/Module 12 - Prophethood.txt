Prophetic Dreams: Objection and Response
Objection from prophetic dreams and response distinguishing meanings of 'false'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Ash'arite response to the objection from prophetic dreams reconciles the hadith with the position that dreams are not genuine sensory perceptions.

Core Idea
Prophetic dreams are true knowledge, not sensory perception—a miracle.

A common objection against the view that dreams are false imagination comes from the prophetic hadith that good dreams are part of prophethood. This lesson presents the objection and the response, which distinguishes between the sensory content of dreams (which is not real perception) and their truth value, which can be genuine through divine intervention.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to capture the flow of objection and response.
The passage includes a commentary marker 'qāla' which is part of the source.
2
Identify the objection
The objection is based on authentic hadiths that righteous dreams are a part of prophethood, and the Prophet acted upon them. How then can dreams be false imagination?
The objection seems strong if 'false imagination' means entirely false.
3
Understand the response's core distinction
The response clarifies that 'false' (bāṭil) refers to the sensory perception being unreal, not the truth of the knowledge content. For Mu'tazilites, it is false that the sleeper actually sees or hears; for Ash'arites, the knowledge from dreams is relative to ordinary people, but can be true through divine intervention.
This distinction preserves the hadith while maintaining the general rule.
4
Note the Ash'arite explanation of miracles
The Ash'arites explain that prophetic dreams are a breaking of habit (kharq al-'āda), similar to other miracles and karāmāt. This allows exceptional knowledge in sleep for prophets and saints.
This shows that the general principle (sleep contrary to perception) has exceptions.
5
Reflect on the theological significance
Consider how this response defends both the authority of hadith and the rational framework of kalam. It shows that exceptions do not invalidate general rules but are explained by divine power.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection against the claim that dreams are false imagination. The objection is based on authentic hadiths that consider righteous dreams (al-ru'ya al-saliha) as a part of prophethood, and the Prophet himself acted upon dreams before revelation. How then can dreams be mere false imagination? The answer distinguishes between two meanings of 'false' (bāṭil). According to the Mu'tazilites, 'false' absolutely means that the sleeper's imagination of sensory perception (sight, hearing, etc.) is not actual perception. However, the knowledge that occurs in sleep being false imagination and sleep being contrary to knowledge is only relative to ordinary people ('āmmat al-khalq). For the Ash'arites, the same applies: it is relative to ordinary people, and they support this by noting that God's habit is not to create perception in a sleeping person, but it is possible through breaking the habit (kharq al-'āda), just as miracles and karāmāt occur. Thus, the prophetic dreams are exceptional and not a counterexample to the general rule.

Dream Images from the Rational Soul
Al-Jurjani defines the first aspect of dream images originating from the rational soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how dream images originate from the rational soul's connection to the active intellect during sleep.

Core Idea
Dreams are real impressions from the soul's contact with higher intellects.

This passage explains the first way dream images appear, according to the Islamic philosophers. The sleeper's common sense receives images from the rational soul, which obtains them from the active intellect. This establishes that dreams can carry true knowledge of past, present, and future, as the soul connects to eternal principles during the relaxation of sleep.

Recognize the hindrance and opportunity

Note the content of dreams
The engraved images reflect the sleeper's own states, family, country, or even intellectual concerns.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of the first way dream images arise, according to the philosophers. The author, al-Jurjani, begins by stating that what the sleeper perceives are images impressed in the common sense (al-ḥiss al-mushtarak). These images come in two aspects; the first is explained here. The first aspect is that the image comes to the common sense from the rational soul (al-nafs al-nāṭiqa), which receives it from the active intellect (al-ʿaql al-faʿʿāl). He explains that all forms of beings from eternity are impressed in the active intellect and in the higher principles and celestial angels. The rational soul naturally connects to these principles spiritually and becomes engraved with some of what they contain, past, present, or future. However, its immersion in governing the body prevents it; during sleep, it gets some respite and may connect, receiving images relevant to its own state or of those close to it, even societal matters. If the soul is inclined to intelligibles, intellectual matters may appear. Then, the universal matter engraved in the soul is clothed by imagination (al-khayāl) with particular images, because imagination has a natural tendency for imitation, transition, separation, and composition. These particular images may be close or far from the original universal. When the images are far, interpretation (al-taʿbīr) is needed: the interpreter strips away the imaginative clothing in reverse steps. If the soul's original acquisition from the active intellect is not altered by imagination, the dream occurs exactly as it is in reality (except for universality/particularity) and needs no interpretation. But if imagination acts extensively, it transfers from one image to another like it, and so on, blocking easy interpretation. Al-Jurjani then includes glosses explaining key terms: 'impressed in it' means present either actually (like in intellects) or by impression (like in celestial souls); 'high principles' refer to intellects universally; 'celestial angels' are celestial souls particularly. He also clarifies 'least emptiness' means minimal freedom from sensory and motor faculties, as complete emptiness is after death. 'Imitation' is defined lexically as resemblance. He then notes that since the common sense usually receives images from outside, the estimative faculty judges accordingly, but during sleep, images might be reflected from celestial souls. Finally, he notes that if the soul takes from natural (material) souls rather than high principles, the difference between universality and particularity also disappears, but then the soul receives them through imagination.

Imagination and Interpretation in Dreams
Al-Jurjani explains the role of imagination in dressing universal images and the consequent need for interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the imaginative faculty clothes universal images and why interpretation is needed to recover the original meaning.

Core Idea
Dreams speak in symbols; interpretation strips away the symbols to reveal truth.

In the previous lesson, we saw how the rational soul receives universal images. This lesson explains how imagination (al-khayāl) dresses these universal images with particular forms, requiring interpretation (al-taʿbīr) to uncover the original message. Al-Jurjani also discusses cases where no interpretation is needed and when extensive alteration blocks interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Understand imagination's properties
Imagination naturally imitates, transitions, separates, and composes images.
2
See how universal is clothed
The universal image from the soul is dressed by imagination with particular images, close or far from the original.
3
Learn the interpretation process
Interpretation is backward stripping of the imaginative clothing to return to the universal.
4
Know when interpretation is unnecessary
If imagination does not alter the soul's acquisition, the dream matches reality directly.
5
Understand when interpretation is blocked
Extensive imaginative alteration can obscure the original meaning, blocking the interpreter's path.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of the first way dream images arise, according to the philosophers. The author, al-Jurjani, begins by stating that what the sleeper perceives are images impressed in the common sense (al-ḥiss al-mushtarak). These images come in two aspects; the first is explained here. The first aspect is that the image comes to the common sense from the rational soul (al-nafs al-nāṭiqa), which receives it from the active intellect (al-ʿaql al-faʿʿāl). He explains that all forms of beings from eternity are impressed in the active intellect and in the higher principles and celestial angels. The rational soul naturally connects to these principles spiritually and becomes engraved with some of what they contain, past, present, or future. However, its immersion in governing the body prevents it; during sleep, it gets some respite and may connect, receiving images relevant to its own state or of those close to it, even societal matters. If the soul is inclined to intelligibles, intellectual matters may appear. Then, the universal matter engraved in the soul is clothed by imagination (al-khayāl) with particular images, because imagination has a natural tendency for imitation, transition, separation, and composition. These particular images may be close or far from the original universal. When the images are far, interpretation (al-taʿbīr) is needed: the interpreter strips away the imaginative clothing in reverse steps. If the soul's original acquisition from the active intellect is not altered by imagination, the dream occurs exactly as it is in reality (except for universality/particularity) and needs no interpretation. But if imagination acts extensively, it transfers from one image to another like it, and so on, blocking easy interpretation. Al-Jurjani then includes glosses explaining key terms: 'impressed in it' means present either actually (like in intellects) or by impression (like in celestial souls); 'high principles' refer to intellects universally; 'celestial angels' are celestial souls particularly. He also clarifies 'least emptiness' means minimal freedom from sensory and motor faculties, as complete emptiness is after death. 'Imitation' is defined lexically as resemblance. He then notes that since the common sense usually receives images from outside, the estimative faculty judges accordingly, but during sleep, images might be reflected from celestial souls. Finally, he notes that if the soul takes from natural (material) souls rather than high principles, the difference between universality and particularity also disappears, but then the soul receives them through imagination.
