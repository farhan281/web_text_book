Intellect as the Basis of Obligation by Consensus
Al-Jurjani states that intellect is the basis of taklif by consensus and defines it as knowledge of some necessities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the intellect required for religious obligation is defined by al-Ash'ari as knowledge of certain necessary truths, and that this definition is a point of consensus among Muslims.

Core Idea
The basis of religious obligation is simple necessary knowledge, not complex reasoning.

This lesson begins a key section on the intellect as the foundation of religious duty. The author establishes immediately that this is a matter of consensus among Muslims, then clarifies the definition according to the founder of the Ash'ari school, Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari. You will see how this definition avoids a disputed theological issue and sets the stage for discussing the first obligation.

The eleventh topic: The intellect is the basis of obligation by consensus.
The intellect is the basis of obligation by consensus of the people of the religion. And that the word 'intellect' is applied to meanings. Therefore they differed in the interpretation of the intellect that is the basis of obligation. Al-Shaykh Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari said: It is knowledge of some of the necessities that we have named—i.e., we have named knowledge.
His saying: 'The intellect is the basis of obligation by consensus'—that is, obligation is not valid without it, because addressing someone who understands nothing from it is ignorance, from which God must be exempted. And this is not subsidiary to the impermissibility of obligating what is beyond capacity, as was imagined, for this issue is consensual, while the issue of obligation is disputed.
His saying: 'It is knowledge of some necessities'—because the first thing the servant is obligated with is knowledge of God, and it is...
His saying: 'And not' regarding its priority over the actual intellect in the second sense—it may be objected to specifying priority over the actual intellect in the second sense, for apparently it is also prior to the first sense. The answer is that not mentioning it is an indication of disregarding that sense, because it contradicts what is in well-known books.

Commentary
This passage presents the consensus that intellect is the basis of religious obligation (taklīf). The author begins by stating that the word 'intellect' is used in multiple senses, so there is disagreement on which sense is the basis. Al-Ash'ari defines it as knowledge of some necessary truths. The marginal note explains that without intellect, obligation is invalid because addressing someone who cannot understand would be ignorance inappropriate for God. It clarifies that this is not dependent on the disputed issue of obligating the impossible; this issue is consensual. The note also relates that the first obligation is knowledge of God. Another note discusses a point about the priority of this intellect over the actual intellect in the second sense, arguing that although priority seems to hold also over the first sense, the author's lack of mention implies a disregard for that interpretation as it contradicts well-known books.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The intellect needed for taklif is sophisticated acquired intellect.
Correction: Al-Ash'ari defines it simply as knowledge of some necessary truths, especially knowledge of God.
