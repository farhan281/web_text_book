Knowledge of Knowledge: The Example of Ali ibn Musa
The author recounts a story about Ali ibn Musa to illustrate the necessity of knowing one's own knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the historical story of Ali ibn Musa and al-Ma'mun illustrates the necessity of knowing one's own knowledge in Islamic theological reasoning.

Core Idea
Knowing something necessarily includes knowing that you know it.

In this passage, the author uses a historical narrative to reinforce a logical point: that knowledge of a thing entails knowledge of that knowledge. The story of Ali ibn Musa (Imam al-Ridha) being summoned by Caliph al-Ma'mun on a festival day provides a concrete case where such knowledge is demonstrated. Pay attention to how the actions and words of Ali ibn Musa reveal his awareness of his own knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the sequence of events: al-Ma'mun's invitation, Ali ibn Musa's response, and the reaction of the people.
The story is used as evidence in a broader argument.
2

Focus on Ali ibn Musa's statement: 'علمت أنه كذلك' (I knew that it would be so). What does this reveal about his knowledge?
This statement is the crux of the illustration.
3
Understand the logical point
The author uses this story to argue that one can know something and also know that one knows it. In this case, Ali ibn Musa knew the outcome and knew that he knew it.
This supports the necessity of knowledge of knowledge.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The passage ends with a rejection of the possibility that someone could be a knower without knowing their own knowledge. Why is that necessarily false?

His statement: 'They indicate that it is not complete.' And thus was the matter. For it is narrated that al-Ma'mun experienced a slight weakness on a festival day, so he sent for Ali ibn Musa inviting him to the prayer ground, his aim being to establish his deputyship before the people. When he proceeded to the prayer ground, he said: 'We shall do as the Messenger of God (peace be upon him) did,' and he walked barefoot. Then he began the takbir. Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative used as an example in the argument. The author cites a tradition about Ali ibn Musa (Imam al-Ridha) and the caliph al-Ma'mun. On a festival day, al-Ma'mun invited Ali ibn Musa to lead the prayers, intending to designate him as his successor. However, when Ali ibn Musa walked barefoot like the Prophet and the people followed him with takbir, al-Ma'mun became afraid and ordered him to return. Ali ibn Musa then said, 'I knew that it would be so,' implying that he had prior knowledge of the outcome. The author uses this to illustrate that one can know something and also know that one knows it, contrary to the possibility that one might not know one's own knowledge. The final phrase 'And al-Jami'a, but that is necessarily false' indicates that the idea that one could be a knower of al-Jafr and al-Jami'a without knowing one's knowledge is necessarily false, thus reinforcing the necessity of the implication between knowing and knowing that one knows.
