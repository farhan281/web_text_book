Can a Miracle Support a Liar?
Argues that a miracle cannot be performed to support a false claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical objection to the miracle-based proof of prophethood and how the answer relies on divine wisdom to avoid confusion.

Core Idea
A miracle only proves truth if we assume God avoids confusion.

The proof of prophethood through miracles seems straightforward: a miracle confirms the prophet's truthfulness. But a powerful objection arises: if God can make a miracle appear after a liar's claim, how can a miracle prove anything? In this passage, the author presents this objection and gives a nuanced answer grounded in divine wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that it begins with 'الأول' (The first), indicating a structured argument.
The passage is a single continuous argument; read it as a whole.
2
Identify the objection and the answer
Mark the sentence that states the objection (from 'إن العجز' to 'الكذاب') and the sentence that gives the answer (from 'بل نقول' to 'والتلبيس').
The objection: a miracle only proves truth if we assume it cannot appear after a liar. The answer: God could do so, but it would cause ignorance and confusion.
3
Reflect on the deeper principle
Ask yourself: why is 'ignorance and confusion' (الجهل والتلبيس) a problem? Because God's wisdom requires clear signs. This shows that the miracle proof is not a logical necessity but a matter of divine consistency.
This connects to the broader theme of divine attributes and wisdom.

الأول : إن العجز إنما يدل على صدق المدعى ، إذا اعتقدنا أنه لا يجوز ظهوره عقيب دعوى الكذاب . فمن اعتقد أنه لا خالق لجميع أنواع الكفر والفواحش إلا الله تعالى . فكيف يمكنه أن يقول : إنه لا يجوز من الله تعالى إظهار المعجز عقيب دعوى الكذاب ؟ بل نقول : إنه لا يفسده في إظهار المعجز عقيب دعوى الكذب ، إلا أن ذلك قد يؤدي إلى الجهل والتلبيس
The first: The incapability only indicates the truthfulness of the claimant, if we believe that its appearance after the claim of a liar is not permissible. For whoever believes that there is no creator of all types of disbelief and obscenities except God Almighty, how can he say that it is not permissible for God Almighty to show a miracle after the claim of a liar? Rather we say: It does not prevent Him from showing a miracle after the claim of a lie, except that that may lead to ignorance and confusion.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection-and-answer regarding the proof of prophethood through miracles. The objection states that a miracle (mu'jiza) only proves the truthfulness of a claimant if we assume that it cannot appear after a liar's claim. The author then presents a counter-argument: if one believes that God is the creator of all disbelief and obscenities, how can he claim that God cannot manifest a miracle after a liar's claim? The author's answer is that God is not prevented from doing so, but doing so would lead to ignorance and confusion (al-jahl wa al-talbis). In other words, the argument that miracles cannot be given to liars relies on the principle of divine wisdom—God would not allow confusion—not on any inherent impossibility.

Miracles for False Claimants: Not Evil if for Another Purpose
Author argues that miracles for false claimants are not evil if God has another purpose, rebutting the impression objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument that miracles for false claimants are not necessarily evil if God's purpose is not confirmation, and how the rebuttal uses the analogy of ambiguous verses.

Core Idea
A miracle for a liar is only evil if God intended to confirm him; otherwise it is not.

This passage presents the third of three arguments defending the possibility that miracles can accompany false prophets. The author argues that if God's purpose is not to confirm the liar, then the miracle is not evil. He then addresses an objection that even if for another purpose, the miracle gives the impression of confirmation, which is evil. By analogy with ambiguous verses in the Quran, he shows that the blame lies with the person who makes a definite judgment, not with God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the core claim
Read the Arabic and translation. Identify the author's main assertion: that a miracle for a liar is only evil if God's purpose is confirmation. Notice the conditional phrasing: 'إنما يقبح إذا...'
The key distinction is the purpose behind the miracle.
2
Follow the argument for permissibility
The author argues that God's purposes are many and unlimited, so the miracle could be for another purpose. Trace the logic: if created for another purpose, it is not evil at all ('لم يقبح البتة').
This establishes the possibility that the miracle is not evil.
3
Examine the objection and response
The objector claims that even if for another purpose, the miracle gives the impression of confirmation, which is evil. The author rejects this by comparing to ambiguous verses (mutashabihat) that cause some to fall into error. The fault lies with the one who assumes a definite meaning where there is none.
The analogy shows that blame attaches to the observer, not the actor.
4
Conclude the argument
The author concludes that the objection against the Ash'ari view is equally binding on the opponents' principles. Reflect on how this third argument strengthens the overall case.
This argument shows the consistency of the Ash'ari position.

الثالث : وهو أن إظهار المعجز على هذا الكاذب، إنما يقبح إذا كان غرض الله منه : تصديق ذلك الكاذب. أما لو خلق الله المعجز عقيب دعوى الكاذب . لا لغرض التصديق، بل لغرض آخر . لم يقبح البتة ومن المعلوم : أن أغراض الله تعالى في خلق المخلوقات كثيرة، غير مضبوطة . ولما ثبت أنه يجوز أن يخلق الله تعالى ذلك المعجز عقيب دعوى الكاذب، لغرض آخر سوى التصديق، وثبت أن على هذا التقدير لا يقبح [خلقه] علمنا : أنه لا يقبح من الله تعالى خلق المعجز، عقيب دعوى الكاذب ولا يقال : إنه يقبح من الله تعالى خلق المعجز عقيب دعوى الكاذب مطلقاً . وذلك لأنه تعالى [إن] خلقه لغرض تصديقه، فهو قبيح . وإن خلقه لغرض آخر فهو أيضاً قبيح . لأنه يوهم كونه واقعاً لغرض التصديق وكما أن فعل القبيح قبيح، فكذا فعل ما يوهم القبيح قبيح أيضاً . لأنا نقول : لا نسلم أن فعل ما يوهم القبيح : قبيح . ألا ترى أن إنزال المتشابهات قبح المكلف تقصيراً من حيث قطع لا في موضع القطع . فكذا ههنا . لما ثبت أن خلق المعجز، عقيب دعوى الكاذب، يحتمل وجهاً آخر، سوى التصديق . فلو جزم المكلف بحمله على التصديق، كان التقصير من المكلف . حيث قطع لا في موضع القطع فثبت بهذه الوجوه الثلاثة : أن الإشكال الذي ألزموه علينا، فهو أيضاً لازم على أصولهم، من هذه الوجوه الثلاثة . وبالله التوفيق
Third: Indeed, manifesting the miracle upon this liar is only evil if God's purpose from it is to confirm that liar. But if God creates the miracle following the claim of the liar, not for the purpose of confirmation but for another purpose, it is not evil at all. And it is known that the purposes of God Almighty in creating creatures are many and not limited. Since it is established that it is permissible for God Almighty to create that miracle following the claim of the liar for another purpose besides confirmation, and it is established that on this estimation it is not evil [to create it], we know that it is not evil on the part of God Almighty to create the miracle following the claim of the liar. And it is not said: Indeed, it is evil on the part of God Almighty to create the miracle following the claim of the liar absolutely. Because if He created it for the purpose of confirming him, it is evil; and if He created it for another purpose, it is also evil, because it gives the impression that it occurs for the purpose of confirmation. And just as doing evil is evil, so too doing that which gives impression of evil is also evil. We say: We do not accept that doing that which gives impression of evil is evil. Do you not see that sending down ambiguous verses (mutashabihat) made the obligated person blameworthy for making a definite judgment where there is no place for definiteness. So too here. Since it is established that creating the miracle following the claim of the liar admits another aspect besides confirmation, if the obligated person decides definitely that it is for confirmation, then the shortcoming is from the obligated person, for making a definite judgment where there is no place for definiteness. Thus it is established by these three aspects that the objection which they imposed upon us is also binding upon their principles from these three aspects. And with God is success.
Source pages p. 129

Commentary
This passage presents the third of three arguments showing that the objection raised against the Ash'ari view (that miracles can occur for false claimants) also applies to the opponents' own principles. The author begins by stating that manifesting a miracle for a liar is only evil if God's purpose is to confirm the liar. If God creates the miracle for another purpose, it is not evil. Since God's purposes are many and not limited, it is possible that He creates the miracle for a purpose other than confirmation. The author then addresses an objection: that even if for another purpose, the miracle gives the impression of confirmation, and giving that impression is itself evil. The author rejects this by analogy with ambiguous verses (mutashabihat) in the Quran: those cause some to fall into error, but the blame lies with the person who makes a definite judgment where none is warranted. Similarly, if a person assumes the miracle is for confirmation when it may not be, the fault is with that person, not with God. Therefore, the objection that miracles cannot be produced for liars is not valid, and the opponents' own principles lead to the same difficulty.

Key Takeaway
