Jabr and Qadar: Identical to Proving the Creator
Al-Isfahani argues jabr/qadar reduces to the cosmological argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the debate on jabr and qadar is not separate from the proof of the Creator's existence, and how the principle of possibility necessitates determination.

Core Idea
The issue of jabr/qadar reduces entirely to the proof of the Creator.

Many scholars have argued that the debate over jabr (predestination) and qadar (divine decree) is not an independent theological dispute but is fundamentally the same as proving the existence of the Creator. This lesson examines that argument, showing how the foundational premise of possibility requiring a determinant ties the two together.

The author uses the conditional to show that the issue of jabr is inseparable from the proof of the Creator.

Understand the conclusion

Refuting the Mu'tazila on Possibility
Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazila claim that possibility suffices without a preponderator in some cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazila claim that possibility sometimes suffices without a cause, and why this leads to a logical dilemma.

Core Idea
Consistency demands: possibility either always requires a cause or never does.

Sanusi presents a powerful refutation of the Mu'tazila position that possibility sometimes suffices without a cause. He shows that this inconsistent view leads to an unacceptable dilemma: either predestination or atheism. By examining the shared nature of possibility, he demonstrates that the Mu'tazila claim is arbitrary and false.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the logical structure: consequence, rejection, example, uniform rule.
2
Identify the initial logical consequence
The author starts by stating what follows if the Mu'tazila premise is accepted: we cannot use possibility to prove the Creator, leaving only jabr or atheism. Understand why this is a dilemma.
This sets up the refutation: even the Mu'tazila would reject these outcomes.
3
Understand the example of identical creatures
Sanusi gives an example of a chain of creatures identical in every respect. If they are truly alike, it is arbitrary to judge some as strong and others weak. This illustrates the problem of inconsistent judgment.
The example serves as a analogy for the uniform nature of possibility.
4
Grasp the principle of uniform rule
All possibles share the same quiddity of possibility. Therefore, the rule must be uniform: either possibility always requires an effecter, or it never does. The Mu'tazila claim violates this principle.
This is the core logical argument against their position.
5
Reflect on the accusation of arbitrariness
Sanusi concludes that judging some possibles as independent while others as dependent is pure false arbitrariness. Consider why this is a strong refutation.
The argument shows that the Mu'tazila position is not rationally defensible.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Muʿtazila position on the sufficiency of possibility. The author begins by stating a logical consequence: if the premise (that possibility alone does not necessitate a preponderator in all cases) is accepted, then one cannot use the possibility of contingent beings to prove the existence of a Creator. This forces a dilemma: either one must adopt predestination (al-jabr) or deny the Creator altogether — both unacceptable outcomes for the Muʿtazila. The author then explicitly rejects the Muʿtazila claim that possibility requires a preponderator (murajjih) in some cases but not in others, calling it a corrupt saying. To illustrate, he gives an example of a chain of creatures that are identical in every respect. If the estimated situation is as described, it would be arbitrary to assign strength to some and weakness to others. Similarly, all beings other than the One, the Unique, the Necessary are possible by essence. Since they all share the nature of possibility (imkan), the rule must be uniform: either possibility always necessitates an effecter, or it never does. To claim independence for some while sharing the same quiddity (mahiyya) of possibility is pure arbitrary falsehood. Thus the argument establishes that the Muʿtazila position is inconsistent.

The Difference Between Human and Divine Will

Core Idea
God's will is eternal and self-sufficient; human wills are originated and dependent.

The third objection claims that the author's reasoning makes God a necessitating cause. In response, the author first establishes a key difference: human motives and wills are created and thus need another cause, whereas God's will is eternal and requires no external preponderator. Let's examine this distinction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage contains the fifth proof for the creation of actions. This proof uses the principle that a creator must have knowledge of what he creates, and human ignorance about knowledge itself indicates that it is not self-created but divinely created.

By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi uses a Qur'anic verse and rational reasoning about motives to argue that repentance must be created by God.

Core Idea
Repentance requires a divine motive; without God creating it, we cannot truly repent.

This lesson introduces the second proof from al-Matalib al-Aliyah. It begins with a verse from the Qur'an and an objection, then provides a rational argument based on the necessity of a preponderating motive for any action. We will see how the author defends the Ash'ari position that repentance is entirely God's creation.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening argument from the Qur'an
Start by reading the verse cited and the author's inference. Notice how he argues that if repentance were human, asking God for it would be absurd.
The verse is from Surah al-Baqara 2:128.
2
Examine the objection and reconciliation
The opponent cites another verse commanding repentance. Understand the apparent contradiction and how opponents reconcile the verses. Then see al-Razi's response that rational proof preponderates.
3

Al-Razi argues that an action only occurs when a preponderating motive is present, and that motive must be created by God. Therefore, repentance is from God.
This is a key kalam principle.

The second proof: His saying, the Most High, at the end of this verse: 'And turn to us. Indeed, You are the Oft-Returning, the Merciful.' And if repentance were by the creation of the servant, then asking for it from God would be in the vein of the servant saying: 'My God, do what I do.' Then it is said: This is opposed by the fact that God, the Most High, asked for repentance from us, saying: 'O you who have believed, repent to God with a sincere repentance.' And if repentance were an act of God, the Most High, then asking for it from the servant would be impossible. And since contradiction is established, reconciliation between them is necessary. So we interpret His saying, the Most High, 'And turn to us' as referring to granting success (tawfiq) and performing kindnesses (altaf), or that God, the Most High, accepts repentance from the servant. And the answer: Preponderance is with us, because the evidence of reason strengthens our statement that repentance does not occur except by the creation of God, the Most High. And its explanation is from two aspects: The first: We have established conclusive proofs that the capable agent is prevented from bringing about an action except when the motive (da'i) is present, and we have established conclusive proofs that when the preponderating motive is present, the action must occur. When this is established, we say: When He, the Exalted, did not create for the servant a motive towards repentance, the occurrence of repentance from him is prevented; and when He created that motive in him, the occurrence of repentance from him becomes necessary. And according to this estimation, repentance has only occurred by the bringing about of God, the Exalted, either with or without mediation. The second aspect in explaining that repentance cannot occur except by the creation of God, the Exalted: We say: Repentance is an expression of a sum of three matters arranged in order: knowledge (al-'ilm), a state (al-hal), and action (al-'amal). So knowledge is first, the state is second — and it is an effect of knowledge — and action is third — and it is an effect of the state. As for knowledge, it is the recognition of the harms in sins; then from this knowledge arises pain of the heart due to the loss of benefit and the occurrence of harm; then from that pain arise practical states related to the present, past, and future. As for its effect on the present, it is that one abandons that sin which he was committing. As for its relation to the past, it is that one makes up for what was missed through reparation, if it is reparable. As for its relation to the future, it is the resolve to abandon that action until the end of life. So knowledge is the first, and it is the origin of these good things. By this knowledge I mean: the encompassing of sins with great harms, and this faith must be certain and confirmed, free from doubts and ambiguities; otherwise it would not be knowledge but rather opinion. However, when that certainty dominates the heart, it causes pain of the heart. And that knowledge necessitates these three matters according to the three times. If you understand this, we say: The firm belief that sin is a cause for the occurrence of great harms necessitates aversion to sins; and this aversion necessitates abandonment in the present and the resolve to abandon in the future. So the arrangement of each of these levels upon what precedes it is a necessary arrangement, essential and necessary, and the servant has no ability or choice in it. It remains to be said: What falls under obligation is the acquisition of that knowledge. But we have shown with conclusive proofs that the acquisition of knowledge is not within the servant's power. So it is established by what we have mentioned that the occurrence of repentance is only from God, the Exalted. And since it is established that our school (madhhab) is strengthened by rational proofs, our school is more worthy than your school. And with God is success.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof that repentance is an act created by God, not by the servant. It begins by citing the Qur'anic verse 'And turn to us' (Surah al-Baqara 2:128) as evidence: if repentance were a human act, then asking God for it would be like saying 'Do what I do,' which is absurd. An objection is then raised: God commands us to repent (Surah al-Tahrim 66:8), so if repentance were from God, commanding it would be impossible. This creates an apparent contradiction. The author notes that opponents reconcile by interpreting 'And turn to us' as God's granting tawfiq (success) or accepting repentance. The author answers that his position is preponderant because rational evidence supports it. He then provides two aspects of proof. First, he argues from the principle that no action occurs from a capable agent without a motive (da'i), and when the preponderating motive is present, the action is necessary. Since God must create the motive for repentance, repentance itself is brought about by God, directly or indirectly. Second, he analyzes repentance into three sequential components: knowledge of the harm of sins, a state of heart pain, and action (abandonment, compensation, resolve). These are causally linked: knowledge necessarily produces the state, and the state necessarily produces action. Therefore, the servant has no choice in the sequence. The only thing that could be commanded is the acquisition of knowledge, but knowledge is not within the servant's power (as proven elsewhere). Hence, repentance is entirely from God, and the author's position is superior.

Repentance as a Causal Chain: Knowledge, State, and Action
Second aspect: analysis of repentance into three sequential elements.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Repentance is a necessary causal sequence starting with divinely given knowledge.

Al-Razi argues that these stages are necessarily linked, leaving no room for human choice. He concludes that the only possible command is to acquire knowledge, but that too is beyond human power.

The Third Proof: Abraham's Prayer as Evidence
Cites Quran 14:37 and 14:40 as proof that actions are created by God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Abraham's prayer in the Quran demonstrates that God creates human inclinations and actions, supported by a rational argument against infinite regress.

Core Idea
Abraham's prayer shows that even our inner inclinations are created by God.

This lesson examines the third proof from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah, which draws on two verses from Abraham's supplication. The author argues that Abraham's request for God to make hearts incline toward Mecca demonstrates that such inclinations come from God. A rational argument about the impossibility of infinite regress in will reinforces this. By the end, you'll see how this Quranic evidence supports the doctrine that all actions are created by Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic verses
Read the two verses quoted from Abraham's prayer (Q14:37 and 14:40). Notice what Abraham asks of God: to make people's hearts incline toward his offspring and to make him an establisher of prayer.
The verses are abbreviated; focus on the request for inclination.
2
Understand the rational argument
Consider the logical argument: if a servant were to acquire will in his heart, that acquisition would require another will, leading to an infinite chain. Therefore, the will must come directly from God.
This argument is called the infinite regress argument (tasalsul).
3
Connect to the doctrine
Reflect on how the Quranic request and the rational argument together prove that all actions of servants are created by God.
The proof shows that even inner inclinations are not self-generated.

Commentary
This passage presents the eighth proof (al-ḥujja al-thāmina) based on the verse 'Indeed Allah is over all things competent' (2:20). The author argues that since human actions are 'things' (ashyā'), Allah must be capable of them. The Incapacitation Objection and Its Resolution

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Incapacitation due to an external cause is not a defect in power.

The objector concedes that human acts are within Allah's power but raises a new challenge: if both agents can perform the same act, then when the servant performs it, Allah can no longer do so—implying Allah is incapacitated. Understand the objection

The Decisive Rational Proof: All Contingent Things Are from God
Sanusi presents a rational proof for the verse 'Say: All is from God' based on contingency.
