The First Proof: Faith as a Blessing from Allah
Author presents first proof with objection and answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the first proof that faith is from Allah, including the objection that distinguishes between direct creation and causation by command.

Core Idea
Faith is a blessing; every blessing is from Allah, but attribution can be as command and assistance.

This lesson presents the first of several proofs that faith (īmān) originates from Allah. The author constructs a syllogism: faith is a blessing, and every blessing is from Allah, so faith is from Allah. However, an important objection arises: does this attribution mean Allah directly creates faith, or could it be that He commands and assists? The passage explores this nuance through the example of a father teaching his son and the story of Moses, showing how attribution can occur without direct action.

Guided Path
1

The passage is short but contains a complete argument.
2
Identify the structure of the proof
The proof is a syllogism with two premises and a conclusion. Find the premises (faith is a blessing; every blessing is from Allah) and note the evidence provided for each (consensus of the Ummah and Quran 16:53).
3
Understand the objection
The objector first denies that faith is a blessing, but then concedes for the sake of argument. The key move is the distinction between two types of attribution: direct doing vs. commanding and assisting. Grasp this distinction and the examples given (father and son, Moses and Satan).
This shows that even if faith is from Allah, it might not mean He creates it directly.
4
Analyze the answer's implications
Consider why the author includes this objection and answer. How does it affect the overall proof? Reflect on how this distinction relates to theological debates about free will and divine causation.
This lesson sets up a crucial conceptual tool used in later proofs.

Commentary
This passage presents the first proof (al-ḥujja al-ūlā) that faith (al-īmān) is from Allah, followed by an objection and its answer. The proof is a syllogism: (1) Faith is a blessing (niʿma). (2) Every blessing is from Allah. Conclusion: Faith is from Allah. The author supports the first premise by consensus (ijmāʿ) of the Ummah, who say 'Praise be to Allah for the blessing of faith.' The second premise is supported by the Quranic verse 'And whatever blessing you have is from Allah' (16:53). Then an objection is raised: The opponent first denies that faith is a blessing, rejecting the claimed consensus. But then they concede for argument's sake that faith is a blessing and that every blessing is from Allah, but they introduce a distinction: Attribution of something to an agent can be in two ways: (a) as the direct doer (fāʿil), or (b) as the one who commanded (āmir) and assisted (muʿīn). They argue that faith's attribution to Allah could be in the second sense, not necessarily that Allah directly creates it. To illustrate, they give the example of a father teaching his son: the learning is attributed to the father as the one who taught and assisted, not as the direct doer (the son himself does the learning). Then they cite the story of Moses (peace be upon him) who, after killing the Coptic, said 'This is from the work of Satan,' but meant that Satan whispered and incited, not that Satan directly killed. Thus, the objector claims that faith may be attributed to Allah as the one who commands and assists, not as the creator of the act of faith.

The Fifteenth Proof: Faith is from Allah Because it is a Good Deed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the fifteenth proof from Al-Matalib Al-Aliyah argues that faith is from Allah by defining faith as a good deed and using the Qur'anic principle that all good deeds are from Allah.

Core Idea
Faith is a good deed, and every good deed is from Allah.

This lesson presents the fifteenth argument from Al-Matalib Al-Aliyah, which uses a simple syllogism to establish that faith is from Allah. The author defines a good deed, shows that faith qualifies, and then appeals to a Qur'anic verse that grammatically implies all good deeds are from Allah. Understanding this argument requires careful attention to the definitions and the grammatical principle of الاستغراق.

Thus faith is a good deed. For the second premise, the author quotes the verse 'Whatever good befalls you is from Allah' and notes that the word 'good' is indefinite and occurs in a conditional context, which grammatically indicates universality (الاستغراق), meaning every good deed is from Allah.

Refuting Mu'tazila on 'Iyyaka Na'budu'
Sanusi refutes the Mu'tazila interpretation of 'Iyyaka na'budu' regarding human agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Mu'tazila use the verse 'Iyyaka na'budu' to argue for human independence and how Ahl al-Sunna refute this interpretation.

Core Idea
The verse does not prove human independence; worship is created by God and acquired by the servant.

In this passage, Sanusi presents a classic theological dispute. The Mu'tazila claim that the Qur'anic phrase 'Iyyaka na'budu' implies that humans are the true creators of their actions, otherwise the statement would be absurd or false. Ahl al-Sunna respond by distinguishing between the act's creation (by God) and the human's intention and acquisition. This lesson unpacks the logic of both sides.

3

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Seeking help from Allah proves our need, not our independence.

The Mu'tazila point to the verse 'You alone we seek help' as evidence that humans must have independent ability to act. They argue that seeking help implies the helper can do something; otherwise the request is meaningless. Commentary
This passage contains both the Mu'tazila objection and the comprehensive Ahl al-Sunna response. The Mu'tazila argue that the command to seek help (استعانة) logically requires that the servant have some ability to act; otherwise seeking help for something one cannot do (like creating the universe) is meaningless. They also note that the verse itself is a request, so the servant is an agent. The Ahl al-Sunna respond that the verse is actually a proof for them. They analyze the 'help' sought: it cannot be help to achieve faith itself because that is already present; rather it must be help in the act of worship. They provide two arguments: (1) Faith is already attained, so seeking it would be futile; (2) The alternative interpretation reduces help to merely supplying capability and removing obstacles, which is not the meaning of the verse. They conclude that the help sought is specifically for worship, as indicated by the pairing of worship and help in the verse.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The Mu'tazila believe that the phrase 'Iyyaka nasta'in' proves humans have independent ability to act, independent of Allah.
Correction: Ahl al-Sunna show that the help sought is for worship, not for creating the ability, and that the verse demonstrates dependence on Allah in every act of worship.

Key Takeaway
The verse 'Iyyaka nasta'in' demonstrates human dependence on Allah in worship, not independent agency, because the help sought is for worship, not for creating the capacity to believe.
