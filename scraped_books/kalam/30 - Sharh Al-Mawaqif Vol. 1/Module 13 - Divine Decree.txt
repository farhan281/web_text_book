Provision and Lifespan: Core Definitions
Al-Jurjani defines rizq and ajal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari definitions of rizq (provision) and ajal (lifespan), including the view that haram can be rizq.

Core Idea
Rizq is anything beneficial, halal or haram; ajal refers to both lifespan and its end.

Al-Jurjani begins by establishing two foundational terms in the discussion of divine decree. Before we can understand predestination, we need a precise understanding of what provision and lifespan mean in Islamic theology. This passage gives the Ash'ari definitions that differ from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the passage once to get the flow. Notice the terms rizq and ajal.
Focus on the phrases 'ma yuntafa'u bihi' and 'yutlaqu 'ala jami'i muddati ash-shay'.
2
Identify the definition of rizq
Find the sentence defining rizq. Note that it includes both halal and haram.
This is a distinctively Ash'ari position.
3
Identify the two meanings of ajal
The passage gives two usages of ajal: the full duration and the endpoint. List them.
Both are used in the Quran.
4
Reflect on the significance
Why does it matter that haram can be rizq? How does this affect the concept of divine provision?
It emphasizes that all benefit ultimately comes from Allah.

He decreed the provisions and lifespans in pre-eternity—an indication to the decree (qada) which is followed by predestination (qadar). Provision (rizq) according to us is that which one benefits from, whether it is lawful or unlawful. Lifespan (ajal) is applied to the entire duration of a thing, like life, and to its end at which it expires, like the time of death. His statement: 'whether it is lawful or unlawful.' If you say: If the unlawful were provision, then the spender of usurped property would be praiseworthy, due to His statement in the context of praise: 'and from what We have provided them, they spend' [Quran 2:3], and the consequent is false. I say: The implication is denied because 'from' (min) indicates partiality, so the praised one spends some of the provision, which is the lawful good.

Commentary
This passage defines two key terms: rizq (provision) and ajal (lifespan). The author begins by stating that God decreed provisions and lifespans in pre-eternity, which is an indication to the divine decree (qada) followed by predestination (qadar). Then he gives the Ash'ari definition of rizq: anything from which one derives benefit, whether it is halal or haram. This is a distinctive Ash'ari position, as the Mu'tazila held that haram cannot be rizq. Then he defines ajal as applying both to the full duration of a thing (like a person's entire lifetime) and to its final endpoint (the moment of death). After these definitions, the author addresses an objection: if haram is rizq, then someone who spends usurped money would be praiseworthy like those described in the Quran as spending from what God has provided them. The answer is that the Quranic verse uses 'min' which implies part of, so only the lawful portion of provision is meant as the subject of praise. Thus the objection fails.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Only halal sustenance is considered rizq from Allah.
Correction: According to Ash'ari theology, rizq includes anything beneficial, even if acquired unlawfully. Haram can be rizq, though it is sinful to acquire it.

Key Takeaway

Objection: Is Spending Haram Praiseworthy?
Al-Jurjani addresses objection about haram as rizq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that if haram is rizq, then spending it would be praiseworthy, and the Ash'ari reply using the partiality of 'min'.

Core Idea
Spending haram is not praiseworthy because the Quranic praise applies only to spending halal.

After defining rizq to include haram, a natural objection arises: the Quran praises those who spend from what Allah has provided them, but if haram is included, then spending usurped property would be praiseworthy. Al-Jurjani presents this objection and gives a precise linguistic reply based on the particle 'min' (from), which indicates partiality.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objector argues that if haram is rizq, then spending it would be commanded and praised. Understand the logical chain.
The objector uses the Quranic verse from Surah al-Baqarah.
2
Identify the answer
The answer denies the implication by noting that 'min' in the verse means 'from some of', so only halal is meant.
This is a grammatical argument.
3
Reflect on the significance
This shows how precise language is used in theological debate. The answer preserves the definition while avoiding the objection.
