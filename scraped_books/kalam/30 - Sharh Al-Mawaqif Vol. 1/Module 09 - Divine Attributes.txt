The Real Meaning of 'Nothing Occurs in His Kingdom Except What He Wills'
The commentator explains the phrase and refutes a misunderstanding about restriction and generalization.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the precise meaning of the phrase 'nothing occurs in His kingdom except what He wills' and why it does not constitute a specification after generalization.

Core Idea
God's will is absolute: nothing happens except what He wills.

The commentator builds on the earlier phrase 'nothing resists His will' to clarify a deeper point: not only does God's will prevail, but nothing occurs that He does not will. This passage addresses a potential misunderstanding and defends the comprehensiveness of divine will, particularly against Mu'tazilite views.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic source passage and its literal translation. Let the author's own wording sink in before analyzing.
2
Understand the logical progression
Notice the shift from the previous claim 'nothing resists His will' to the new phrase. Ask yourself: what does the new phrase add that the old one didn't?
The previous phrase only says God's will is effective when He wills something; it doesn't say nothing happens without His will.
3
Grasp why it's not a specification after generalization
The commentator explicitly refutes the idea that this is a specification (takhsis) after a generalization (ta'mim). Why? Because the previous statement wasn't a general statement covering all occurrences.
Misunderstanding: some might think the first phrase said 'everything that occurs does so by God's will' (general), and then the second says 'only in His kingdom' (specific). But the first didn't have that general scope.
4
Examine the different interpretations of 'malakut'
The commentator offers two possibilities: the linguistic intensive form of 'mulk' (dominion) or the unseen world. Consider why he prefers the first and how it refutes the Mu'tazilites.
The Mu'tazilites only deny God's will in the visible world, so specifying 'malakut' as the unseen wouldn't challenge them. Hence the linguistic interpretation is stronger.

His saying: 'And nothing occurs in His kingdom (malakut) except what He wills.' Since the immediate understanding from His saying: 'Nothing resists His will' is that everything that God wills is actual, but from it does not appear the meaning of restriction (hasr), which is that nothing occurs except what He wills. And this [the previous phrase] was also among the aspects of the strength of sovereignty. So he brought his saying: 'And nothing occurs in His kingdom except what He wills' to convey the mentioned meaning. So there is not in it a specification after generalization, as was thought. As for the specification of malakut by mention, if it is taken in its linguistic meaning, which is dominion (mulk), then malakut is an intensive form of mulk just as rahubut is an intensive form of rahbah, then the matter is clear. And if it is taken as the world of the inner and the unseen, then it is of the type of specifying the Throne (al-'arsh) by mention in the ruling of sovereignty, as God Almighty said: 'The Most Merciful established Himself over the Throne' (Ta Ha: 5), i.e., He took possession. The first is closer because the opponents, I mean the Mu'tazilites, only claim the occurrence of contrary to will in the world of testimony (shahadah), not in the world of the unseen. So reflect.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation and defense of the phrase 'ولا يجري في ملكوته إلا ما يشاء'. The commentator notes that the earlier phrase 'لا يستعصي على إرادته شيء' (nothing resists His will) implies that whatever God wills happens, but it does not explicitly restrict occurrence to only what He wills. The current phrase is brought to indicate that restriction (hasr): nothing occurs except what He wills. This is not a specification after a generalization, as some might think. He then addresses the specification of 'malakut' (kingdom). If taken linguistically as an intensive form of 'mulk' (dominion), it is clear. If taken as the unseen world, it is like specifying the Throne in the verse about God's establishment. The commentator prefers the linguistic interpretation because the opponents, the Mu'tazilites, only deny God's will being effective in the visible world, not the unseen. The passage thus defends divine causality and will.

Divine Attributes: Hakim, Wahid, Ahad, Fard, Samad, and Perfection
Enumeration of divine attributes with concise proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proofs for several divine attributes and how they establish God's transcendence and perfection.

Core Idea
Each divine attribute is proven by a simple rational observation.

This passage lists several divine attributes with concise arguments. Each attribute is not just a name but a concept grounded in reason and observable reality. As you read, notice how each proof is brief yet compelling.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Begin by reading the Arabic text aloud or silently. Focus on the rhythm and key terms.
The passage is a list; each item starts with a word in parentheses.
2
Identify each attribute and its proof
For each attribute, find the short proof given. For example, 'Hakim' is proved by the perfection in His works.
There are seven items in total: Hakim, Wahid, Ahad, Fard, Samad, attributes of majesty, and independence.
3
Understand the logical reasoning
Each proof follows a simple logic: if the attribute were absent, a contradiction or imperfection would arise. For instance, if God had a partner, the world order would be corrupted.
4
Reflect on the overall picture
These attributes together paint a picture of God as perfectly wise, unique, and self-sufficient.
The concluding statements tie them together under majesty and perfection.

God's All-Encompassing Knowledge
Proof for God's universal knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's knowledge must encompass all things, based on the nature of His essence and the relation of concepts to it.

Core Idea
God's essence necessitates universal knowledge of all concepts equally.

The commentator begins by proving that God knows all things. He argues that the cause of knowledge is God's essence, and all concepts are equally related to that essence, so knowledge must be universal. This establishes a fundamental attribute of God.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on knowledge
Read the Arabic passage for knowledge and its translation. Notice the logical structure: cause (essence), condition (concepts), conclusion (universality).
The passage uses the term 'عالم' (knowing) as a description of God.
2
Identify the cause and condition
Identify the cause of God's knowledge (particularity of His essence) and what makes things knowable (essences of concepts). Understand why they lead to universality.
The relation of essence to all concepts is equal, so knowledge encompasses everything.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider that nothing escapes God's knowledge, not even an atom's weight. This emphasizes God's omniscience.
The phrase 'مثقال ذرة' highlights the smallest possible thing.

Commentary
This passage provides proofs for three divine attributes: knowledge, power, and will. For knowledge, the commentator states that the cause of God's knowledge is His essence, and all concepts are equally related to Him, so His knowledge encompasses all things, including the smallest particle. For power, the cause is also His essence, and all possible things share the quality of possibility, so His power includes them all, creating without a model. For will, the commentator argues that God wills all things, good and evil, because if something occurred contrary to His will, it would imply impotence, which is incompatible with divinity. This refutes the Mu'tazila who claimed God does not will evil. A counter-objection is raised: perhaps there is no impotence if God voluntarily permits evil to test people, but the commentator notes that this also implies a kind of impotence, leaving the issue open to reflection.

God's essence and the possibility of things necessitate all-encompassing power.

Refuting Necessary Knowledge from Reflection
Al-Iji refutes Imam al-Razi's view by showing it contradicts God's absolute power and freedom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the view that knowledge necessarily follows from reflection is incompatible with the Islamic doctrines of God's absolute power, free agency, and the principle that nothing is obligatory upon God.

Core Idea
Necessity in creation contradicts God's freedom.

This lesson unpacks al-Iji's critique of Imam al-Razi's claim that knowledge is a necessary result of reflection. Al-Iji argues that such necessity would conflict with three core beliefs: that everything traces directly to God, that God is powerful and free to act or refrain, and that nothing is obligatory upon Him. We will follow his reasoning step by step and see how he refutes a counter-argument from Sharh al-Maqasid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three premises
Al-Iji begins by stating that the doctrine of necessary reflection-knowledge is invalid given three premises: (1) all things are traced directly to God, (2) God is powerful and free (qadir mukhtar), (3) nothing is obligatory on God. Notice how each premise is introduced.
These premises are foundational in Ash'ari theology.
2
Understand how each premise negates necessity
The first premise negates a causal necessity between reflection and knowledge, because direct creation means no intermediary necessity. The second premise negates necessity for knowledge from reflection: if God is free, He can create knowledge without reflection or reflection without knowledge. The third premise reinforces that no obligation forces a link.
The argument uses the concept of luzum (necessary connection).
3
Examine the refutation of the counter-argument
Al-Iji responds to an objection from Sharh al-Maqasid that inseparability of an effect from its concomitant does not contradict free agency. He clarifies that true incompatibility is when the agent cannot possibly leave the effect entirely, not merely that He does not create the concomitant.
This is a nuanced point about divine agency and causal laws.
4
Reflect on the logical conclusion
The passage ends with a note about the definition of proof and logical figures. Consider how this critique challenges any attempt to impose necessary relations in the created order.
The final remark suggests a broader implication for definitions in logic.
