Commentary
This passage is a brief definition and defense of the author's choice of the word 'quds' (holiness). The commentator explains that 'quds' means transcendence (tanzuh) above all deficiency, which reinforces the idea that God encompasses all aspects of high status. The omission of the conjunction (al-'atf) between this phrase and the preceding attributes is deliberate, to show a strong connection. He refutes a criticism that the author should have said 'pavilions of His perfection' instead, arguing that the term 'quds' already implies perfection and better suits the context.

Core Idea
God acts by His own will alone; no purpose or cause compels Him.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of Mu'tazila doctrines concerning divine actions. It begins by asserting God's absolute sovereignty: creation and command belong to Him; He does whatever He wills without any preventer or repeller. The core refutation is that God's actions are not caused by purposes or final causes (ghayat/agharad). The author then clarifies a point from an earlier statement about 'uniqueness in pre-eternity' (توجه بالقدم) to emphasize that the uniqueness is not contradicted by the pre-eternity of attributes, as they are not 'other than God' in the sense of being separate entities. The passage proceeds to explain the phrase 'since there is no ruler above Him'—the Mu'tazila argue that some actions are intrinsically ugly or obligatory based on rational judgment. The author rejects this by stating that obligation upon God can only come from an external ruler (impossible) or from reason's judgment of intrinsic good and evil, which he declares false. Instead, good and evil are determined by revelation (shar'iyan). A subtle point follows: while reason may not be a judge, it could be an apprehender of obligation derived from God's eternal names and attributes—so the author advises reflection. The final part on 'purposes and causes' indicates that when the text denies purposes and causes for God's actions, 'causes' specifically means final causes (al-ghayat), and that there is no essential difference between them. The text is incomplete, cutting off in the middle of explaining this.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of Mu'tazila doctrines concerning divine actions. It begins by asserting God's absolute sovereignty: creation and command belong to Him; He does whatever He wills without any preventer or repeller. The core refutation is that God's actions are not caused by purposes or final causes (ghayat/agharad). The author then clarifies a point from an earlier statement about 'uniqueness in pre-eternity' (توجه بالقدم) to emphasize that the uniqueness is not contradicted by the pre-eternity of attributes, as they are not 'other than God' in the sense of being separate entities. The passage proceeds to explain the phrase 'since there is no ruler above Him'—the Mu'tazila argue that some actions are intrinsically ugly or obligatory based on rational judgment. The author rejects this by stating that obligation upon God can only come from an external ruler (impossible) or from reason's judgment of intrinsic good and evil, which he declares false. Instead, good and evil are determined by revelation (shar'iyan). A subtle point follows: while reason may not be a judge, it could be an apprehender of obligation derived from God's eternal names and attributes—so the author advises reflection. The final part on 'purposes and causes' indicates that when the text denies purposes and causes for God's actions, 'causes' specifically means final causes (al-ghayat), and that there is no essential difference between them. The text is incomplete, cutting off in the middle of explaining this.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between din and millah and why the Prophet's law is the most upright and just, free from the burdens of Judaism and the excesses of Christianity.

Core Idea
The Prophet's law balances between excessive rigor and excessive leniency.

This lesson examines the first two aspects of the Prophet's superiority: religion (din) and community (millah). The author defines these terms and then demonstrates that the Prophet's law is the most upright because it avoids the extreme burdens of Judaism and the extreme laxity of Christianity. Understanding this balance is key to appreciating the excellence of Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions of din and millah
Notice that the author says din and millah are identical in essence but differ in perspective: din emphasizes obedience, millah emphasizes consensus. This is a subtle but important distinction.
The terms are often used interchangeably, but here they are differentiated for conceptual clarity.
2
Identify the faults of previous laws
The author lists specific burdens in Judaism (e.g., cutting off impurity, staying apart from menstruating wives, mandatory retaliation) and excessive leniency in Christianity (mixing with impurities, intercourse during menstruation, mandatory pardon). See how the Prophet's law avoids both extremes.
These examples illustrate the 'burdens' and 'excessive leniency' mentioned.
3
Reflect on the balanced nature of Islamic law
Consider how the Prophet's law is described as 'free from burdens and heavy obligations' and also 'free from excessive leniency'. This middle path makes it the most upright and just.

The Prophet's community is the best because it is the middle, and his qiblah is the first house of worship.

Learn about the qiblah

Fifth Claim: Contiguity or Separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the fifth claimed necessary knowledge from the corporealists and why the monotheists unanimously denied it.

Core Idea
The Creator transcends spatial categories of contact and separation.

The corporealists (mujassima) argued that intuition demands every existent be either in contact with the world or separate from it; otherwise it cannot be considered real. This claim, if accepted, would confine Allah to spatial categories. The monotheists (muwaḥḥidūn) unanimously denied it, upholding Divine transcendence. Let's analyze this claim and its rejection.

The Prophet banned obstinate debate on qadar, not all theological inquiry.
