Refuting Objections to Resurrection
Refutes three objections to bodily resurrection using divine power and analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand three major objections to bodily resurrection and how classical Islamic theology refutes them using divine power and analogy.

Core Idea
Resurrection is possible because God's power and knowledge overcome all obstacles.

This passage addresses three objections that deniers of bodily resurrection raise. The first two are based on the condition of decayed bones—mixing of parts and dryness—while the third is a philosophical argument about the impossibility of world annihilation and recreation. The author, following the tradition of kalam, refutes each by appealing to God's unlimited power and knowledge, and by drawing analogies from observable creation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
Recall that the preceding passage discussed the reality of resurrection. Now the author refutes doubts. Read the Arabic passage and its translation once.
2
Identify the three objections
The passage presents three distinct doubts: (1) mixing of body parts, (2) dryness of bones vs. need for moisture, and (3) annihilation and re-creation of the world. Find each in the text.
3
Examine the first refutation
For the objection of mixed parts, the answer is that God is All-Knowing and can distinguish every particle. Focus on the phrase 'عليم بكل شيء'.
4
Examine the second refutation
For the dryness objection, the author uses the analogy of fire from green wood. Notice the logic: if God can create fire from moisture, restoring life to dry bones is easier.
Think about the principle 'what is possible for a greater difficulty is possible for a lesser'.
5
Examine the third refutation
The third objection is philosophical. The refutation uses two points: (a) if God created the world, He can annihilate it because contingency implies possibility of non-existence at any time; (b) the one capable of a thing is capable of its like, so He can create another world.

The resurrection, then he refuted their doubts which he reported from them. For their argument that bones are decayed (ramima) is from two aspects: one, the mixing of parts of bodies and limbs with each other, so how can parts of one body be distinguished from parts of another body, and parts of a limb from parts of other limbs, such that resurrection can be conceived? And the second: that decayed parts are very dry, while life requires moisture of the body. He indicated the answer to the first by saying that He is Knower of everything, so He can distinguish parts of bodies and limbs. And to the second answer: that He made fire in green wood despite the apparent opposition between them, so it is more likely that He can bring life to dry decayed bones, because the opposition here is less than that. Then, for the deniers of resurrection there is another famous doubt: that resurrection, as brought by the laws, involves the annihilation of this world and the creation of another world, and that is false due to many principles established in the books of philosophers. So he answered this doubt by saying that since the denier acknowledges that God, exalted, is the Creator of these heavens and earth, he must also acknowledge that He is capable of annihilating them, for that which non-existence is valid for at a time is valid for it at all times, and that He is capable of creating another world because the one who is capable of something is necessarily capable of its like.

Commentary
This passage refutes three objections to bodily resurrection. The first objection is that after death, body parts mix with each other and become indistinguishable, making resurrection impossible. The author answers that God is all-knowing and can distinguish every part. The second objection is that decayed bones are too dry to sustain life, but the author points to the miracle of fire from green wood, where God creates heat and fire from moisture and cold; by analogy, restoring life to dry bones is easier since the opposition is less. The third objection, from philosophers, claims that resurrection as taught in scriptures requires annihilating and recreating the universe, which they deem impossible. The author replies: if the denier accepts God as Creator of the world, he must accept His power to annihilate it, because the world is contingent (possible) and therefore can cease to exist at any time. Moreover, the one who can create one thing can create its like, so God can create a new world. This argument uses the opponent's admission of God's creative power to prove His power over annihilation and re-creation.

Key Takeaway
Resurrection is rationally possible because God's knowledge overcomes the mixing of parts, His power overcomes the dryness of bones, and His role as Creator implies He can annihilate and recreate the world.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you think about your own death and the decay of your body, do you truly believe that God knows every particle of you? If you believed it with certainty, how would it change the way you live today?
?
The philosophers doubted resurrection because they trusted their intellect over revelation. Where in your life do you rely on your own reasoning more than on what God has promised? What step will you take this week to submit your doubts to faith?
?
If you knew that God will annihilate this world and create a new one, would your attachment to this life change? What possession, relationship, or status are you holding onto as if it will last forever? Will you let go of it today for the sake of the next life?

Prophetic Prohibition and the Defense of Kalam
Author refutes objection based on prophetic prohibition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the prophetic prohibition of debating qadar is reconciled with the obligation of kalam through the distinction between prohibited vain debate and permissible sound inquiry.

Core Idea
The Prophet forbade vain debate, not all kalam.

A common objection against kalam is that the Prophet explicitly forbade debating about predestination (qadar). This lesson examines that objection and shows how the author defends kalam by arguing that the prohibition was specific to vain argument and that kalam, as a means of defending creed, is a good innovation.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its basis
Read the Arabic passage and translation carefully. Notice the objection: the Prophet forbade debate on qadar, therefore kalam (which involves debate) is prohibited.
Focus on the hadith about the Prophet's anger.
2
Identify the author's two-part response
The author responds in two ways. First, the prohibition was for vain debate (al-jadal bi al-batil), not sound inquiry. Second, even if kalam is an innovation, it is a good innovation (bid'a hasana) like codifying sciences.
3
Reflect on the concept of 'good innovation'
Consider how the author uses the concept of 'good innovation' to argue that kalam is not inherently prohibited. Innovations are judged by their benefit or harm, not by their novelty.
This is a key principle in Islamic legal theory.

Commentary
This passage is the second objection-and-answer in a series defending kalam. The objection is based on prophetic hadiths: the Prophet forbade debating about qadar (predestination), once becoming angry when he saw Companions discussing it. The objector argues that since theoretical inquiry (nazar) is a form of debate (jadal), and debate is prohibited, kalam must be prohibited. The author's response is multi-layered. First, he notes that the prohibition applies only to vain or false debate (al-batil), not to sound investigation. The commentary explains that the Companions' discussion was about superstitious or contentious points, not legitimate theological inquiry. Second, the author distinguishes between types of innovation: a good innovation (bid'ah hasanah) like codifying religious sciences is permissible, even commendable. The prohibition on debating qadar is specifically because the subject is a divine secret beyond human grasp, and arguing about it can lead to error. However, this does not ban all kalam, which aims to defend creed using sound reasoning. The passage ends by suggesting that some of the Companions might have been hypocrites, but the safer view is that the prohibition was due to the inaccessibility of the matter, and thus it does not apply to all topics of theological inquiry.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The Prophet's prohibition of debating qadar means all kalam (theological debate) is forbidden.
Correction: The prohibition was specifically for vain, contentious debate about a matter beyond human comprehension. Legitimate kalam that uses sound arguments to defend faith is not only permissible but obligatory.

Key Takeaway
The Prophet's forbidding of debate on qadar does not ban all kalam; it bans vain argumentation on matters beyond human reason, while sound theological inquiry remains commendable.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you find yourself in a heated discussion about religious topics (like predestination), do you stop to check whether your words are 'vain debate' or sincere inquiry? What would the Prophet’s command to 'refrain' mean for you in that moment?
?
Have you ever dismissed someone else’s theological study as 'innovation' when it was actually a praiseworthy effort to defend the truth? How does this lesson’s distinction between good and bad innovation change your attitude?
?
The qadar debate was forbidden because it is a secret beyond human reason. Is there a question about Allah’s decree that you have wrestled with or argued about, when deep down you know you should simply say 'I believe and do not argue'? Will you surrender that debate to Allah today?
