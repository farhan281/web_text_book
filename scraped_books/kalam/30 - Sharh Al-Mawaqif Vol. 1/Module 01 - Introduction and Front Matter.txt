The Purpose of Traditional Islamic Book Openings
Editor opens with basmala, hamdala, and statement of purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the significance of the basmala, hamdala, and editorial preface in classical Islamic scholarly works.

Core Idea
Every Islamic book begins with gratitude and a declaration of purpose.

This opening passage is more than a formality—it grounds the reader in the religious and scholarly context of the work. The editor’s note reveals the care taken to present the text accurately and accessibly. In this lesson, we will examine each component and its function.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening formulas
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the three parts: basmala, hamdala, and blessings on the Prophet.
These are standard in Islamic texts.
2
Understand the editor's role

This shows the religious importance of preserving knowledge.
3
Connect to the learning objective
Consider how this opening sets the tone for the reader and prepares them to engage seriously with the content.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to his Sharḥ, where he explains his motivation, method, and the qualities of his book. The passage is expository and includes parenthetical glosses that define terms. The author begins by criticizing other writers: some produce excessively long books, making them seem like a vast sea, while others are like a wood-gatherer at night, unable to distinguish good from bad, or like one who brings the entire army without discernment. These writers simply transmit what they find without using reason to judge value. This situation prompted the author to compose a book out of compassion for students. He describes his book as 'moderate' (muqtaṣid)—neither too long nor too short. He includes the 'kernel of kernels' (lubb al-albāb), distinguishing the husk from the kernel. He spares no effort in refining theological topics (al-maṭālib al-kalāmiyya) and establishing doctrinal schools. He presents arguments that are clear and strutting with confidence, while doubts become small and ashamed. He also includes subtle points (nukat) that guide verification. The author then describes his careful process: he examines sources and outcomes, checks for deficiencies, and revises repeatedly as a 'preserver' (ḥāfiẓan) of proper format. He balances brevity and detail as needed. He consulted with friends and the Sultan of India, Muhammad Shah Juna, among others. Finally, he chose to present the book to a worthy patron. The passage ends with a philological note on the word 'ʿawaj' (crookedness): it is pronounced with fatḥa for intellectual matters and with kasra for physical ones, quoting philologists and the Quran. Throughout, the author emphasizes his dedication to producing a clear, balanced, and well-argued work.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to his Sharḥ, where he explains his motivation, method, and the qualities of his book. The passage is expository and includes parenthetical glosses that define terms. The author begins by criticizing other writers: some produce excessively long books, making them seem like a vast sea, while others are like a wood-gatherer at night, unable to distinguish good from bad, or like one who brings the entire army without discernment. These writers simply transmit what they find without using reason to judge value. This situation prompted the author to compose a book out of compassion for students. He describes his book as 'moderate' (muqtaṣid)—neither too long nor too short. He includes the 'kernel of kernels' (lubb al-albāb), distinguishing the husk from the kernel. He spares no effort in refining theological topics (al-maṭālib al-kalāmiyya) and establishing doctrinal schools. He presents arguments that are clear and strutting with confidence, while doubts become small and ashamed. He also includes subtle points (nukat) that guide verification. The author then describes his careful process: he examines sources and outcomes, checks for deficiencies, and revises repeatedly as a 'preserver' (ḥāfiẓan) of proper format. He balances brevity and detail as needed. He consulted with friends and the Sultan of India, Muhammad Shah Juna, among others. Finally, he chose to present the book to a worthy patron. The passage ends with a philological note on the word 'ʿawaj' (crookedness): it is pronounced with fatḥa for intellectual matters and with kasra for physical ones, quoting philologists and the Quran. Throughout, the author emphasizes his dedication to producing a clear, balanced, and well-argued work.

And among them is one who is like a gatherer of wood at night, like one who collects firewood in the night, not distinguishing between moist and dry, harmful and beneficial; and a bringer of footmen and horsemen—'al-rajl' is the plural of 'rajil' which is the opposite of a horseman, and 'al-khayl' are the horsemen—meaning like one who brings the entire army, its weak and its strong. Then he pointed to the aspect of likeness in the side of the likened in both similes by his saying: 'He collects what he finds of the people's speech, transmitting it by transmission, and does not use intellect to know whether what he took is worthless or valuable, and foolish' i.e., thin and feeble, 'what he found' what he encountered 'or firm' i.e., strong. So all that he mentioned became an incentive for him to compose the book, as he indicated by his saying: 'So compassion drove me' — 'al-ḥadb' is affection and sympathy for the people of study for this science, and for one who has a need in investigating the truth in it — 'to write this', referring to his book, 'a moderate book' balanced, neither lengthy by its prolongation nor brief prejudicing by its conciseness. And his saying: 'a virgin' as an alternative to 'speech' from the virgins of the gardens, not touched by any human or jinn before. 'And I was for a long period of time' a long duration from it, 'turning my opinion' rotating it, 'and returning my arrows' as the gambler does when thinking about gambling, 'and consulting myself' from consultation, which is deliberation, because each of the two consultants commands his companion with what he sees, 'and consulting those possessing intellect' plural of 'nuhya' which is reason because it forbids obscenity, 'from my friends, with the multiplicity of her suitors' from 'al-khuṭba', and the pronoun is for the virgin. And among her suitors is the Sultan of India, Muhammad Shah Juna, and the abundance of those desiring her. And that strengthening necessitates this support, for the cutting sword if the proof does not sharpen its edge—as it is said: a player's handkerchief, which is a cloth folded to strike with during play—'until the choice fell' as a result of turning opinion and what is coordinated with it, 'on one who is not comparable' from 'wāzantu' when I compare two things. His saying: 'No crookedness in it'—crookedness is bending from uprightness, and it is with fatḥa on the ʿayn in what is perceived by insight and thought of intellectual matters, and with kasra in what is perceived by sight of sensible matters. This I found in the handwriting of my grandfather in the margins of al-Muṭawwal. And it is supported by His saying, exalted: 'You will not see in it any crookedness or unevenness' [Ṭā Hā 20:107]. And perhaps it is said the opposite also, until some philologists said: 'al-ʿawaj' with fatḥa is like 'ḥabī dar jūb wa mānand-i ān', and with kasra is like 'ḥabī dar dardīn wa mānand-i ān'. And Ibn al-Sikkīt said: 'Whatever is upright like a wall or a stick, it is said in it ʿawaj with fatḥa; and ʿawij with kasra is what is in land, religion, or livelihood.'

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to his Sharḥ, where he explains his motivation, method, and the qualities of his book. The passage is expository and includes parenthetical glosses that define terms. The author begins by criticizing other writers: some produce excessively long books, making them seem like a vast sea, while others are like a wood-gatherer at night, unable to distinguish good from bad, or like one who brings the entire army without discernment. These writers simply transmit what they find without using reason to judge value. This situation prompted the author to compose a book out of compassion for students. He describes his book as 'moderate' (muqtaṣid)—neither too long nor too short. He includes the 'kernel of kernels' (lubb al-albāb), distinguishing the husk from the kernel. He spares no effort in refining theological topics (al-maṭālib al-kalāmiyya) and establishing doctrinal schools. He presents arguments that are clear and strutting with confidence, while doubts become small and ashamed. He also includes subtle points (nukat) that guide verification. The author then describes his careful process: he examines sources and outcomes, checks for deficiencies, and revises repeatedly as a 'preserver' (ḥāfiẓan) of proper format. He balances brevity and detail as needed. He consulted with friends and the Sultan of India, Muhammad Shah Juna, among others. Finally, he chose to present the book to a worthy patron. The passage ends with a philological note on the word 'ʿawaj' (crookedness): it is pronounced with fatḥa for intellectual matters and with kasra for physical ones, quoting philologists and the Quran. Throughout, the author emphasizes his dedication to producing a clear, balanced, and well-argued work.

Author's Concluding Prayer for the Patron
Author concludes his introduction with a supplication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the purpose and content of the author's concluding supplication for his patron, and how it reflects common practices in classical Islamic introductions.

Core Idea
The prayer links the author's work to his patron's well-being in this world and the next.

The author concludes his introduction with a heartfelt supplication for the patron who supported his work. This prayer not only asks for worldly blessings like long life and continued rule but also for spiritual rewards in the afterlife. Understanding this passage gives insight into the relationship between scholars and patrons in classical Islamic scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then the English translation. Notice the rhetorical style and the specific requests.
The Arabic uses elevated language typical of prayers.
2
Identify the three requests
The author asks for three things: perpetuation of the patron's state, long enjoyment of bestowed gifts, and success in earning good remembrance and great reward. Identify each in the text.
3
Consider the rhetorical style
Note the phrases 'أطلق لسان' (most eloquent tongue) and 'أرق جنان' (tenderest heart). These reflect the author's sincerity and humility before God.
Such phrases are common in supplications.
4
Reflect on the purpose
Think about why authors include prayers for patrons. This is a traditional way to show gratitude and invoke blessings, connecting the work to divine favor.
