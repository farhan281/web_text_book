Commentary
This passage presents evidence from the Gospels and Pauline epistles supporting Tawhid and Tanzih. It quotes Mark 12:28-32 where Jesus affirms the Shema and the scribe agrees. John 17:3-4 shows Jesus praying to God as the only true God and calling himself sent. John 9:1-4 shows Jesus referring to the one who sent him. John 1:18 states that no one has seen God. Paul in 1 Timothy 1:17 and 6:15-16 describes God as invisible and unapproachable. The author concludes that these texts prove Jesus is not God but a messenger, and God is one and invisible.

Fourth Proof: The Slap and the Need for Prophets
Fourth sub-proof: the child's need for someone to explain just retribution proves the need for prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the child's need for someone to explain just retribution leads to the conclusion that prophets are necessary.

Core Idea
The child's instinctive search for a just explainer proves prophethood is rational.

In this final sub-proof of the second path, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi uses the same slap incident to demonstrate the necessity of prophets. After establishing that retribution is necessary, the child naturally seeks an authority to define just recompense without excess or deficiency. This reasoning shows that human reason alone cannot specify the measures of reward and punishment; a divine messenger is required.

Guided Path

2

Notice that after accepting retribution, the child seeks a person to explain it. Ask yourself why this search is significant.
The child is not satisfied with his own reasoning; he looks for an external authority.

The passage argues that only a messenger can specify the exact measures of incentives and deterrents. Reflect on why reason alone cannot determine these.
This ties back to the idea that prophets convey divine commands.

Commentary
This final sub-proof completes the second path by showing that the slap also requires prophethood. Once the child acknowledges the necessity of retribution, he then needs someone to define what constitutes just recompense—neither excessive nor deficient. The child instinctively looks for a person to specify the exact measures of what is desirable (المرغبات) and what is deterring (الزواجر). The author concludes that such a person can only be a prophet sent by God. Thus, the single slap has demonstrated all four principles: God's existence, His commands, reward and punishment, and the sending of prophets. The passage ends with a prayer for divine assistance.
