Refuting the Misunderstanding of Allah's Misguidance
Author refutes a claim about Allah's misguidance and explains its true meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari perspective on the meaning of 'idhal' (misguidance) as the creation of straying, and how it refutes the objection that this interpretation leads to weakness in Quranic verses.

Core Idea
Idhal means creating straying, not unjustly leading astray.

This lesson tackles a critical theological debate: what does it mean when Allah is described as 'leading astray'? Some argue it implies injustice, but the author clarifies that 'idhal' refers to the creation of the state of straying. Through an objection-and-answer format, the passage defends the soundness of this interpretation against claims of scriptural inconsistency.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation once to get the gist of the argument. Note that the text is corrupted in places, but the main lines of the objection and reply are clear.
Pay attention to the objection that interpreting idhal as 'creating disbelief' leads to 'rakaka' (weakness).
2
Identify the objection and the author's response
The objector claims that interpreting idhal as creation of disbelief makes Quranic verses weak. The author rejects this and defines idhal as 'an action that leads to straying'—either by command/incitement or by creating the necessitating motive.
The author emphasizes that idhal is not a capricious act but has a causal context.
3
Consider the examples given
The author references Pharaoh and Iblis as cases where misguidance occurred through divine incitement or creation of motive. Reflect on how these examples support the definition.
This shows that idhal can be mediated through creatures' actions.
4
Address the specific verse about messengers
The objector raises an apparent problem: how can Allah send a messenger to misguide? The author defends the interpretation by explaining that idhal here means creating straying in those who reject, not that the messenger's mission is misguidance.
This connects to the broader argument that all actions, including misguidance, are created by Allah.
5
Understand the key term: idhal
Consolidate your understanding: idhal is not unjust leading astray but the creation of straying as a divine act within a just framework.
This aligns with the Ash'ari view that Allah creates all actions, good and bad.

Worldly and Otherworldly Interests Depend on This Knowledge
The author argues that both worldly and otherworldly interests depend on knowledge of God and the Last Day.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author argues that both worldly and otherworldly interests depend on the knowledge of God and the Last Day, establishing this science as the foundation of all goods.

Core Idea
No order in this life or the next without knowledge of God.

This lesson presents the fourth and final proof in the series, demonstrating the essential role of knowledge of God and the Last Day (al-mabda' wa al-ma'ad) for both worldly and otherworldly interests. The author contrasts the two domains and shows that without true revealed laws, social order collapses, and without knowledge of God, angels, books, messengers, and the Last Day, no otherworldly benefit can be achieved. The passage concludes with a powerful summary: this science is the origin of all goodness and the ultimate human achievement.

Third Proof: Possibility Implies Persistence of Essence During Non-Existence
Argues that possibility would require the essence to persist during non-existence, which is absurd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof against possibility as a real attribute, which shows that either possibility would require non-existence to be a thing or leads to contradiction for those who hold that non-existence is a thing.

Core Idea
Possibility as a description forces us to treat non-existence as a thing, which is absurd.

This passage presents the third proof from the author against the view that possibility (imkan) is a real attribute. The argument is twofold: first, if we describe something as possibly non-existent, its essence must persist during non-existence, making non-existence a thing. Second, even if one accepts that non-existence is a thing (as the Mu'tazila do), possibility cannot coherently be an attribute of either the essence or existence. Let's see how the author develops this reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to get the flow of the argument. Notice the two distinct parts.
The passage uses a reductio ad absurdum and a dilemma.
2
Identify the two main parts
The first part argues against those who deny non-existence is a thing. The second part argues against those who affirm it (Mu'tazila).
3
Analyze the first part
Understand why describing a thing as 'possibly non-existent' implies its essence exists during non-existence. Why does the author find this objectionable?
The author says 'And you do not say that' – this is a reductio.
4
Analyze the second part
See why, for Mu'tazila, possibility cannot be an attribute of the essence (immutable) nor of existence (prior to existence).
Existence cannot be described by something that occurs before it.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The author concludes that possibility cannot be a real attribute; it must be a mental judgment.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof against possibility (imkan) as a real attribute. It begins by stating that if we say something is possible both to exist and not to exist, this implies that the essence of that thing remains even when it becomes non-existent. The reasoning is that for a thing to be described by an attribute, the thing must be present when the attribute applies. If we describe a thing as 'possibly non-existent', the essence must be present at the time of non-existence, which would mean non-existence itself is a thing (shay'), a view the author rejects. Then the passage turns to those who do hold that non-existence is a thing (the Mu'tazila). For them, possibility must be either an attribute of the essence (mahiyya) or of existence (wujud). It cannot be an attribute of the essence because essences are immutable and do not acquire new attributes. It cannot be an attribute of existence because possibility is prior to existence, yet existence cannot be described before itself. Thus, possibility cannot be an attribute of either, so it is incoherent.
