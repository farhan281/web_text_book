The Third Proof: Universal Would Lead to Infinite Regress
Presents the third proof against the reality of universals and the resulting infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third argument against real universals, which shows that if universals were real, individuation would lead to an infinite regress.

Core Idea
If universals were real, each individual would need another individuator—forever.

This lesson presents a powerful argument against the reality of universals as extramental entities. The argument, attributed to Ibn Sina (Avicenna), shows that if a universal like 'humanity' existed as a real thing outside the mind, then the process of individuation would require an endless chain of additional individuating factors. This leads to an infinite regress, which is rationally impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the literal translation. Let the logical chain from premise to conclusion come through before you interpret.
Notice that the argument uses assumption, consequence, and objection.
2
Identify the infinite regress
The argument shows that if individuation is an addition to the universal essence, then that individuation itself, being a determination of a universal, would require another individuation. See how this leads to an endless chain.
The term 'تسلسل' means infinite regress.
3
Reflect on the implication
Consider why this proof is used to deny the reality of universals. It sets up Ibn Sina's alternative explanation that universals exist only in the mind, not externally.
The passage ends by mentioning Ibn Sina's two aspects.

The Four Types of Concepts and Their Exhaustiveness
Author classifies all human concepts into four types and proves that judgments are also limited.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four categories of human concepts according to Fakhr al-Din al-Razi and how he argues that all human knowledge (concepts and judgments) is limited to these categories.

Core Idea
All human concepts are reducible to four types: sensed, introspected, innate, and composed.

Before we dive into complex theological arguments, we need to understand the raw materials of human thought. In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi offers a comprehensive classification of every concept a human can form. This is the third proof in his chapter on the limits of human intellect, and it establishes that our minds are bounded by these four categories. Once you grasp this, you will see why even judgments are constrained.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the overall structure
The passage begins by dividing knowledge into conceptual and judgmental, then focuses on concepts. Read the Arabic and translation to get a sense of the argument before analysing details.
This is a third proof, so it builds on earlier ones.
2
Identify the four types of concepts
As you read, list the four types: (1) sensed essences, (2) internal perceptions, (3) innate intellectual concepts, (4) composed concepts (by imagination or intellect).
The fourth type is further subdivided into imaginative composition and intellectual composition.
3
Study the examples for each type
The author gives concrete examples: sensed: visual, auditory etc.; internal: pain, pleasure; innate: existence, necessity; composed: sea of mercury, partner of God. See how each example fits its category.
Notice that the composition examples show how new concepts arise from simpler ones.
4
Understand the argument for exhaustiveness
The author claims that after thorough induction and introspection, we find that all concepts fall into these four. There is no fifth type. See how the composition examples demonstrate that even apparent new concepts are derived.
This is a claim of empirical exhaustiveness.
5
Follow the conclusion about judgments
Since judgments depend on concepts, and concepts are limited to these four, judgments must also be limited. This is a key step in the overall proof of the chapter.
The author uses the example of judging 'partner of God is impossible' to show the dependency.

The third proof in this chapter: knowledge is either conceptual (tasawwuri) or judgmental (tasdiqi). As for conceptual knowledge, we find from ourselves intuitively after complete examination and thorough induction that we cannot refer with our intellects, estimation, and imagination except to one of four types of concepts. Commentary
This passage is the third proof (al-hujjah al-thalithah) in this chapter. The author classifies knowledge into two types: conceptual (tasawwuri) and judgmental (tasdiqi). He then focuses on concepts (tasawwurat), asserting through introspection and induction that all human concepts fall into exactly four categories. The first category is essences perceived by the five external senses (e.g., visible, audible). The second category is internal sensations perceived necessarily from ourselves (e.g., pain, hunger). The third category is concepts grasped by the innate disposition of the intellect (fitrat al-‘uqul), like existence, non-existence, unity, necessity, etc. The fourth category is composite concepts formed by either imagination or intellect from simpler elements. The author gives examples: imaginative composition like 'sea of mercury', intellectual composition like 'partner of God'. The latter involves combining the concept of 'partner' (understood from human experience) with the concept of 'God' to form a compound meaning, which is then judged impossible. The conclusion is that all human concepts are confined to these four types, and since judgments depend on concepts, judgments are also confined to them. This establishes the limitation of human intellection.

The Seventh Shubha: Uncertainty of Proofs
Seventh shubha on uncertainty of proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the argument of the seventh shubha that if a proof can be corrupt in form or matter, then no proof can be certain, and why it is considered a strong objection.

Core Idea
If any proof can be flawed, no proof is trustworthy.

In this lesson, we examine the seventh shubha, a skeptical challenge that strikes at the foundation of certainty. The argument claims that if a proof could be defective in its logical structure (form) or its premises (matter), then no proof can be relied upon—including the proof for God's existence. This objection is presented as the strongest of all shubahat.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the overall argument.
Notice the two conditions: form and matter.
2
Identify the two aspects of proof
The passage mentions two respects in which a proof is evaluated: 'form' (الصورة) and 'matter' (المادة). Form refers to logical structure, matter refers to the premises.
These are the two conditions for a proof to be sound.
3
Understand the conditional argument
The shubha argues: if it is possible that a proof could be corrupt in either form or matter, then one cannot be certain of the soundness of any proof. This is a conditional statement (if... then...).
The conclusion follows logically from the premise.
4
Note the sweeping conclusion
The shubha concludes that if the opponent allows this possibility, then no confidence remains in any proof, including the proof for the Necessary Being. This is a devastating blow to theistic arguments.
The shubha targets all proofs, not just some.
5
Reflect on the strength of the shubha
The author states that this is the strongest of the sophistical arguments. Consider why it is so powerful: it undermines certainty in reasoning itself.
This sets up the need for a response in subsequent discussions.

This passage is a classification of three possible views on the relationship between voluminousness (spatial extension) and the factors that differentiate bodies, followed by a proof that the first view is false. Core Idea
The impossible's external existence, not its mental quiddity, is the real subject of judgment.

In this passage, an objector challenges the fourth argument by claiming that impossibility could be described by a positive attribute if it has a distinguishing feature. The author responds with a precise analysis of the subject of the judgment, distinguishing between the mental quiddity and the external existence. This distinction is crucial for understanding how kalam handles negative attributes.

Learning Objective

This lesson presents the eighth proof from Razi's theological work, which employs the logical analysis of propositions. By examining the proposition 'God is necessarily existent,' Razi shows that existence must be a predicate added to a subject (the quiddity of God), thus proving a distinction between essence and existence. المنطق : أن الاستواء في المفهوم ، لا يوجب الاستواء في الماهية ، وعلى هذا التقدير لا يبعد استواء الذوات في هذا المفهوم السلبي ، مع أنه يكون كل واحد منهما ممتازا عن الآخر بتمام حقيقته . والذي يقوي هذا الذي ذكرناه : أن الدليل الذي ذكرتم في كون الذوات متساوية في المفهوم من كونها ذوات ، فهو أيضا بعينه يدل على أن الصفات متساوية في المفهوم من كونها صفات . وإذا كان كذلك وجب أن لا يحصل الاختلاف البتة ، لأن الاختلاف لو حصل الحصل إما في الذوات ، وإما في الصفات، وعلى التقديرين فالاختلاف مفقود (۳) ، فوجب أن لا يحصل الاختلاف البتة ، وحيث حصل ، علمنا أن ما ذكرتم من الدليل ساقط . والجواب عن الثاني : إنا بينا بالدلائل الثلاثة في مقدمة هذه المسألة : أنه لا يلزم من كون الشيء مخالفا لشيء آخر ، أن تكون تلك المخالفة واقعة لأجل صفة زائدة ، وإذا تقررت هذه المقدمة سقطت تلك الشبهة . وهذا هو الجواب بعينه من الشبهة الثالثة . واعلم أن حاصل الكلام في هذه المسألة أنا نقول : كل واحدة من الذوات تخالف غيرها، في كونها تلك الحقيقة المخصوصة ، إلا أن هذه الحقائق المختلفة ، يلزمها لازم واحد سلبي ، وهو كونها ذوات . والأشياء المختلفة لا يمتنع اشتراكها في لازم سلبي . أما الخصوم فإنهم يقولون : الذوات متساوية في كونها ذوات إلا أن كل واحدة منها يلزمها صفة معينة لأجلها ممتاز عن غيرها وذلك محال . لأن الأشياء المتساوية يمتنع أن يلزمها لوازم مختلفة . فهذا تمام هذا الكلام في هذا الباب وبالله التوفيق
The logical reasoning: that equality in concept does not necessitate equality in essence. And on this basis, it is not unlikely that essences are equal in this negative concept, while each one of them is distinguished from the other by its entire reality. And what strengthens this what we have mentioned is that the proof which you mentioned concerning the equality of essences in the concept of being essences — it also exactly indicates that attributes are equal in the concept of being attributes. And if that is the case, it would necessitate that no difference occurs at all, because if difference occurred, it would occur either in the essences or in the attributes, and on both suppositions difference is missing, so it would necessitate that no difference occurs at all. And since [difference] does occur, we know that the proof you mentioned is invalid. And the answer to the second [objection]: We have explained by the three proofs in the introduction of this issue that it is not necessary, from something being different from another thing, that that difference occurs because of an additional attribute. And when this premise is established, that doubt collapses. And this is the precise answer to the third doubt as well. And know that the summary of the discussion in this issue is that we say: Each one of the essences differs from others in being that specific reality, except that these different realities entail one negative necessary concomitant, which is their being essences. And different things are not impossible to share in a negative necessary concomitant. As for the opponents, they say: Essences are equal in being essences, except that each one of them entails a specific attribute by which it is distinguished from others, and that is impossible because things that are equal are impossible to have different necessary concomitants. So this is the complete discussion in this chapter, and with God is success.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation and summary of an argument about the equality and difference of essences. The author first states his position: equality in concept (such as the concept of 'being an essence') does not entail equality in the essential reality; different essences can share a negative concept while each has its own specific reality. He then strengthens this by showing that if the opponents' method is applied consistently — that equality in the concept of being essences implies equality in essence — then by the same logic attributes should be equal in the concept of being attributes, leading to no difference anywhere. Since difference is observed, their proof is invalid. He then answers the second objection by referencing earlier proofs that difference does not require a superadded attribute. Finally, he summarizes the dispute: his view is that essences differ in their specific realities but share a negative concomitant (the concept of being an essence); the opponents claim essences are equal in being essences but each entails a distinct attribute, which is contradictory because equal things cannot entail different necessary concomitants.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: If two things are different, there must be a superadded attribute causing the difference.
Correction: Difference can be inherent in the essences themselves; a superadded attribute is not necessary.
