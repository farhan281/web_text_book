The Second Argument: Necessity of Knowing Action Details
Second proof: conditional syllogism with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structure of the second argument: if the servant independently creates his actions, he must know their details, and why this is false using the example of the sleeper.

Core Idea
Independent creation requires full knowledge; but we often act without such awareness.

The second argument from al-Mawaqif is a conditional proof that the servant cannot be the independent creator of his actions. It consists of a conditional premise (if independent creation, then full knowledge) and an exceptional premise (the consequent is false, as shown by sleepers and forgetful actors). In this lesson, we unpack the logical structure and the key examples.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the second argument
Read the Arabic passage from 'الوجه الثاني' through the exceptional premise example. Notice the conditional and exceptional parts.
The argument is a conditional syllogism: If A then B, but not B, so not A.
2
Understand the conditional premise
The premise states that if the servant independently creates, he must know the details. Why? Because choosing a specific action requires intention and choice, which depend on knowledge.
This is based on the intuitive premise that you cannot intend what you do not know.
3
Examine the exceptional premise
The consequent is false because people (like sleepers turning over) perform voluntary actions without awareness of the details. This shows that the servant cannot be an independent creator.
The objector may claim that the sleeper does perceive but forgets; we'll address that in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage presents the second argument (al-wajh al-thānī) from the Mawaqif that the servant cannot be the independent creator of his actions. The argument is structured as a conditional syllogism: if the servant independently creates his actions, then he must know all their details (conditional premise), but the consequent is false (exceptional premise). The conditional premise is justified because the specific occurrence of an action with particular details requires intention and choice, which are conditioned by knowledge. The exceptional premise is supported by examples: a sleeper or forgetful person may perform voluntary actions like turning over without awareness of the quantity or quality. An objection is then raised: it is possible that the person perceives the details but does not perceive that perception, or the perception does not persist. The commentator (al-Jurjani) then provides a series of marginal notes. First, a response to a possible objection that this proof also negates acquisition (kasb): the response distinguishes between primary and subsidiary intention—the primary intention (e.g., walking to a place) necessitates awareness, but subsidiary intentions (e.g., muscle movements) do not. Second, a grammatical note about the word 'min'. Third, a note about the declension of 'wa al-ikhtiyār'. Fourth, a critique of the example of the sleeper: since intention requires knowledge and sleep is opposite to knowledge, how can the action be voluntary? Then a response to that critique: awareness of a thing does not entail awareness of that awareness, but it can be accessed by attention; thus the objection is repelled. Then additional supportive arguments: the claim of atomism (jawhar fard) explains why slow movements involve interpolated moments of rest that are not perceived; the Mu'tazilite view that the action is motion, an attribute not generally conceived; and the example of moving a finger where the parts move without awareness. Finally, it is noted that the second and third arguments for refuting the consequent do not apply to Abu al-Husayn due to his skepticism about the atom and motion attribute.

Defending the Argument: Objections and Refinements
Objection, response, and additional proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the main objection to the second argument—that the agent may perceive details without perceiving that perception—and the responses that refine the distinction between primary and subsidiary intention.

Core Idea
The argument survives by distinguishing primary intention from subsidiary; we may not be aware of every subsidiary detail.

The second argument faced an important objection: it is possible that the sleeper or forgetful person does perceive the details of his action but does not perceive that perception, or the perception does not persist. In this lesson, we explore the objection and the responses given by the commentator, including the crucial distinction between primary and subsidiary intention. We also look at additional proofs from atomism and the nature of motion that support the second argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and its response
Read the passage from 'واعترض عليه' through 'لا يدوم له الشعور الثاني'. Notice the objection that awareness of details does not require awareness of that awareness.
The objector tries to save the possibility that the agent knows but is not aware of knowing.
2
Understand the primary vs subsidiary intention distinction
The response to the kasb objection distinguishes between primary intention (e.g., walking to a place) which requires awareness, and subsidiary intention (e.g., muscle movements) which does not.
This distinction is key to preserving the argument against acquisition (kasb) as well.
3
Examine the additional proofs
The commentator adds proofs from atomism (sakanat) and motion (the attribute of motion) to further show that we lack awareness of action details.
These proofs are not accepted by all theologians, but they strengthen the argument for those who accept them.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that if an agent can perceive details but forgets, then the argument fails. But the response shows that even if that were possible, there are cases where no perception of details ever occurs.
Correction: The argument does not rely on permanent awareness; it only requires that the agent lacks knowledge of details at the time of action, as with sleeper and finger mover.

The Third Branch: The Claim and Two Proofs
Al-Isfahani states the claim and gives two proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the claim that theoretical knowledge is not generated from remembering reflection and the two proofs supporting it.

Core Idea
Remembering reflection is a divine act, so knowledge from it cannot be obligatory.

This lesson introduces the third branch of the discussion on generation. The author states the position and provides two key arguments: first, that remembering reflection is a necessary act of God and thus not a basis for obligation; second, that remembering cannot be directly within human power. Understanding these arguments is essential for grasping the Mu'tazili theory of generation.

Guided Path
1
Read the claim
Read the opening lines where the author states that theoretical knowledge is generated from initial reflection but not from remembering it.
Notice the contrast between 'ibtida'an' and 'tadhakkur'.
2
Identify the first proof
Find the first proof which argues that remembering is a necessary act of God, so if knowledge came from it, it would also be necessary and not subject to obligation.
The key phrase is 'daruri min fi'li Allah ta'ala'.
3

The response says the apparent meaning of confinement is invalid because there is consensus (ijma') that wine-drinkers and adulterers are punished, yet they do not explicitly deny Allah. Even Jews and Christians do not deny Allah, though their denial of prophethood may entail denial. The response distinguishes between the actual denier (al-mukadhdhib) and the one for whom denial is entailed (man yalzimuhu al-takdhib). The verse likely applies to the former, not the latter. Thus the major sinner is not necessarily an actual denier.

After his knowledge that what he commands is good and what he forbids is evil, and that it is not among the ijtihadi matters in which the beliefs of the commander and the commanded, the forbiddener and the forbidden differ. (Two conditions: First: that he thinks it will not become a cause for the eruption of fitna (tribulation); otherwise it is not obligatory. Likewise, it is not obligatory if he thinks it will not lead to the intended goal; rather, it is recommended (mustahabb) then as a manifestation of the symbol of Islam. Commentary
This passage states the two conditions for the obligation of commanding good and forbidding evil. First, the commander must know that what he commands is indeed good and what he forbids is evil, and that it is not a matter of ijtihad. Then the conditions: (1) He must not anticipate that his action will cause fitna. If he does, it is not obligatory. Similarly, if he expects no result, it is not obligatory, but becomes recommended as a display of Islam. (2) He must not spy or investigate people's private lives. The text provides proofs from the Quran (verse forbidding spying) and the Prophet's sayings and example. The Prophet would conceal faults, not expose them. The passage ends with a supplication for guidance.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: To command good, one must first find out people's private sins by spying.
Correction: The passage forbids spying and encourages concealment; commanding good applies to public matters or with knowledge from normal observation.

Key Takeaway
Spying on people's faults is forbidden; the proper approach is to conceal sins and command good only based on apparent public knowledge.
