The Logical Order of the Three Purposes
Explanation of the logical order of the three purposes in al-Mawaqif.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author placed the first purpose (proving existence) before the others, and how the second and third purposes follow logically.

Core Idea
The order of the three purposes is not arbitrary—it follows a logical dependency chain.

In this lesson, we explore the reasoning behind the arrangement of the three purposes in al-Mawaqif. The commentator explains that the order is not arbitrary but grounded in the logical priority of proving existence over attributes, and the connection between purposes. By understanding this, you will grasp the methodical structure of kalam arguments about God.

Al-Razi's Objections and Their Resolution
Describes al-Razi's objection method and the general solution of attacking weaker proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's method of raising objections in theological debates and the general strategy for resolving them by attacking weak proofs.

Core Idea
Invalidating both proofs does not disprove the positions themselves.

Imam al-Razi frequently employed a distinctive method of objection in theological debates. He would pit two opposing views against each other, using each side's proof to invalidate the other, aiming to avoid negating a shared premise. The resolution to this dialectical trick lies in recognizing that attacking a weak proof does not disprove the claim itself. This passage outlines that methodology.

Guided Path
1
Read the Passage Carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Focus on understanding the overall method described.
The passage has some OCR issues; rely on the translation for clarity.
2
Identify Al-Razi's Objection Pattern
Note how al-Razi handles two opposing schools: he oscillates between them to prevent voidness, then invalidates each using the other's proof to preserve the common subject.
The 'common subject' (al-qadr al-mushtarak) is the shared premise underlying both views.
3
Understand the General Resolution
The resolution is to impugn the proof of the weaker side, or both if possible. This works because invalidating a proof does not invalidate the position itself.
The logical principle: proof is a necessary condition (lazim) for the conclusion, but its absence does not disprove the conclusion.
4
Reflect on the Logical Principle
Notice the distinction between the proof and the position. The proof is a means to establish the claim; a flawed proof does not make the claim false.

Commentary
This passage describes the general method of Imam al-Razi's objections in his books regarding the proof of the Creator. The method is: for any disputed issue where there are two opposing schools, al-Razi presents their arguments and then invalidates each one using the proof of the other, to avoid negating the common subject (the shared premise). The general resolution to these objections is to attack the weaker proof of the two sides, or both if possible. It is possible that both proofs are weak, and invalidating them does not invalidate the positions themselves, because the proof is a necessary condition for the conclusion, but the absence of a proof does not prove the conclusion false. The passage then notes that we will list several such objections as examples. The marginal commentary adds that when we speak of the proof as a 'lazim' (necessary condition), we mean the valid proof. What is invalidated is the unsound proof. So if the negation of a condition entailed the negation of its necessary consequence, it would be problematic, but here we are only invalidating unsound proofs, not the positions themselves.

The author warns that this fallacy is the source of many theological ambiguities. He then applies this insight to four specific cases: (1) The claim that existence is shared — we are certain of existence but uncertain of its particulars. The response is that what is certain is the concept, not the reality. (2) The claim that existence is additional to quiddity — we can conceive existence without quiddity and vice versa. The answer is that what is additional is the concept, not the reality. (3) The claim that unity is non-existent to avoid regress. The answer is that the proof only shows the concept of unity is non-existent, not the reality; the reality of unity may be existential or otherwise. (4) The claim that attributes are additional to the essence, otherwise the concepts of knowledge, power, etc., would collapse into one. The answer is that what they apply to (their instances) may be one (the essence), but the concepts themselves are distinct. The commentary also clarifies that the proper formulation is 'confusing the concept of the subject with what it applies to', and it explains the metaphor of the vigilant heart. Each application demonstrates the general principle that confusing the concept with the instance leads to erroneous theological positions.

The author argues that tawatur in itself is clear and yields necessary knowledge of events distant in time and place; it does not depend on the condition of evenness (istiwā’) or knowledge of the criterion, which would lead to circularity. However, if one tries to prove something by asserting that its reports are mutawātir with specific conditions and criteria, then the objections about lack of knowledge of conditions and circularity would apply. But the knowledge derived from tawatur itself is necessary and not theoretical. A marginal note explains that the phrase 'ḍarūrī ʿindanā lā naẓarī' (necessary in our view, not theoretical) is used because al-Ka‘bī and Abū al-Ḥusayn considered it theoretical, claiming it depends on two premises: that the group cannot conspire to lie, and that every such report is truthful. The author refutes this by stating that when a report reaches tawatur, its content is known immediately and certainly, without needing to consider these premises, as even children can attain such knowledge.

Commentary
This passage presents the second chapter of the section on the proofs of Muhammad's prophethood, dealing with the objections of the critics against the Quran's inimitability. It opens with two main objections: First, the aspect of inimitability should be evident to anyone using it as a proof, but since Muslims disagree on the specific nature of the miracle, this proves that the miracle is hidden, rendering it useless as a proof. Second, the aspects proposed (extraordinary composition, eloquence, etc.) are not valid. Regarding eloquence, they give three faces. First face: comparing the most eloquent human speech or poetry to the shortest Quranic surah, if we ignore meter and specific composition, no clear difference in eloquence is found; in fact, the human composition might be considered more eloquent. The commentator then notes a response: it is said that this refutation is invalid because the inimitability of the Quran is theoretical (nazari), meaning it is intellectually apprehended and not evident to everyone; it becomes clear only to the elite (the experts). Therefore, they required testimony also to uphold the legal procedures of the Sharia. Thus, the passage includes both the objections and a preliminary answer.
