The Fifth Standing: Correcting the Number of Observatories
Correction of the number of marasid in the Fifth Standing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the structural setup of the Fifth Standing on Divine Matters and the correction of the number of its observatories from five to seven.

Core Idea
The Fifth Standing has seven observatories, not five.

This passage opens the Fifth Standing on Divine Matters (al-ilahiyyat) with an important textual correction. The author notes that some manuscript copies mistakenly report five observatories, but the correct number is seven. This introductory correction ensures that the reader has the right framework before delving into the detailed discussions on the divine attributes and proofs.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and its translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Pay attention to the correction statement: 'not five as it occurred.'
The first part 'التي هي في بعض النسخ' is a variant note.
2
Identify the key structural terms
Note the terms 'marasid' (observatories) and 'maqasid' (purposes). Understand that the Fifth Standing is divided into these categories.
The first observatory is on the Essence; the first purpose is on proving the Creator.
3
Reflect on the significance of the correction
Ask yourself why the author would bother to correct the number. This shows careful attention to the manuscript tradition and the importance of accurate structure in theological works.

The first observatory: On the Essence. The first purpose: On Proving the Creator.

Commentary
This passage begins with a note about variant readings in some copies, then the basmalah. It then introduces the Fifth Standing (الموقف الخامس) on divine matters (الإلهيات). The author corrects a common mistake: the highest intention (المقصد الأعلى) in this science actually contains seven observatories (مراصد), not five, and has three purposes (مقاصد). The observatories and purposes are then listed: the first observatory is on the essence (في الذات), and the first purpose is on proving the Creator (في إثبات الصانع). This sets the structure for the subsequent discussion.

The Consensus on Not Declaring Disbelief Among Ahl al-Qibla
States the consensus and announces detailed discussion of takfir claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that there is a consensus that no one who prays toward the Kaaba is declared a disbeliever due to doctrinal differences, and that the author will now detail specific accusations of disbelief.

Core Idea
Consensus protects Ahl al-Qibla from blanket takfir; details follow.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle in Islamic theology: the agreement (ijma') that no one from Ahl al-Qibla is to be declared a disbeliever. The author then sets the stage for a detailed examination of specific accusations of disbelief. As you read, note the clarity of the consensus and the announced plan for detailed discussion.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the original Arabic and its literal English translation. Notice the two clear parts: the consensus statement and the announcement of detailed discussion.
The Arabic uses concise terms: 'الاتفاق على أنه لا يكفر أحد من أهل القبلة'.
2
Identify the consensus principle
The passage states that it is agreed upon that none of Ahl al-Qibla are declared disbelievers. Understand that this is a broad, inclusive principle.
This consensus is a safeguard against declaring Muslims disbelievers over doctrinal disagreements.
3
Understand the purpose of the section
The author announces that he will now mention what some have considered disbelief and detail it. This sets up a refined, point-by-point analysis.
The word 'تفصيل' (detailed) signals a methodical approach.
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this principle matters: it prevents takfir from being applied broadly, but does not deny that some specific beliefs may constitute disbelief.
The tension between consensus and specific cases will be explored in upcoming lessons.

Commentary
This passage introduces a major discussion on takfir (declaring disbelief). The author first affirms the principle of consensus (ijma') that no Muslim who prays towards the Kaaba (Ahl al-Qibla) is to be declared a disbeliever merely based on doctrinal differences. Then he announces the goal of the section: to list and examine specific beliefs for which some groups have been accused of disbelief, and to do so in a detailed, point-by-point manner. This sets the stage for the subsequent arguments and refutations.

Author's Closing Supplication and Colophon
Author closes the commentary with prayers and details of completion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the content and significance of the closing section of the commentary, including prayers for steadfastness, methodological remarks, and colophon details.

Core Idea
The colophon documents the completion of the commentary and the author's humble prayers.

This lesson examines the concluding passage of the commentary, where the author offers a supplication for divine guidance and forgiveness, describes his methodological approach, and provides the date and place of completion. This colophon serves as both a spiritual seal and a historical record.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get the overall flow. Note the shift from prayer to methodological description to date.
The passage is the final part of the commentary, so it has a reflective tone.
2
Identify the two main sections
Recognize that the passage has two parts: first the prayer from the original author (al-Iji?), then the commentator's (al-Jurjani?) description of his work and the colophon.
The phrase 'وأن أقول' marks the commentator's own words.
3
Study the methodological principles
The commentator states he aimed to clarify problems, refine questions, and establish proofs, avoiding extremes of brevity and verbosity. Note the mention of objections and answers.
4
Locate the colophon details
Find the date: Saturday, early Shawwal 807 AH, in Samarqand. This is a key historical data point for dating the work.
The year 807 AH corresponds roughly to 1405 CE.

The author said: Let this be the end of the speech from the book al-Mawaqif. We ask God Almighty to establish our hearts upon His religion and not to deviate them after guidance, and to protect us from error, and to grant us success in following the Messenger of God, his Companions, and the Successors who followed them in goodness, and to pardon the transgression of the pen and what human beings are never free from of forgetfulness and slips, and to treat us with His bounty and mercy; indeed He is the Oft-Forgiving, the Most Merciful. And I say: This is what was facilitated for us with the help of God and His good success: of uncovering its problems, clarifying its difficulties, refining its questions, and establishing its proofs, while refraining from tedious verbosity and defective brevity, and indicating in some places the objections that arise against his speech and the answers that can be used to repel them. May God benefit the students with it and make it a provision for us on the Day of Judgment; indeed He is the best Granter of success and Helper. The completion of its composition occurred on Saturday near the afternoon of the early days of Shawwal 807 AH in guarded Samarqand, may it be preserved from afflictions. God is sufficient for us, and He is the best Disposer of affairs, the best Protector and Helper. May God bless our master Muhammad, the master of mankind, and his noble family and his great Companions, and grant them abundant peace. Amen.

Commentary
This passage is the author's concluding statement and colophon. It begins with a prayer from the original author of al-Mawaqif (al-Iji?) asking God for steadfastness, protection from deviation, and forgiveness for errors of the pen. Then the commentator (al-Jurjani?) adds his own remarks, describing his methodology in writing the commentary: he aimed to uncover difficult passages, clarify obscure points, refine the questions, and establish proofs, while avoiding both excessive verbosity and overly terse brevity. He also notes that he occasionally mentions objections to the original text and provides answers. He prays that the work benefits students and serves as a provision on the Day of Judgment. Finally, he gives the date of completion: Saturday, early Shawwal 807 AH (around 1405 CE) in Samarqand. The colophon ends with praises to God and blessings upon the Prophet.
