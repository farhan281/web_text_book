Six Arguments That Equality in Some Attributes Does Not Imply Complete Likeness
The author lists six arguments proving that sharing some attributes does not imply complete likeness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand four rational arguments (from the six) proving that sharing some attributes does not necessitate complete likeness in essence.

Core Idea
Equal in one attribute does not mean equal in essence.

The author presents several concise arguments that demonstrate a key logical principle: similarity in some attributes does not imply total identity. This principle is crucial for defending divine transcendence: Allah shares existence with creation, but that does not make Him like creation. The passage uses examples from accidents, opposites, numbers, and formal logic to illustrate the point.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation. Let the author's rhythm come through before interpreting.
The passage lists arguments numbered third through sixth, implying earlier arguments existed.
2

Identify each argument (accidents, opposites, numbers) and note how they show shared attributes without complete likeness.
These arguments use examples from ontology and arithmetic.
3

The sixth argument refers to the second figure of syllogism. Realize that two affirmative premises yield no conclusion because equality in one attribute (affirmative) does not imply sameness.
This argument is logical rather than empirical.
4
Synthesize the conclusion
Connect all arguments to the final statement: equality in some attributes does not indicate likeness in the complete essence.
This is the main point for defending divine transcendence.

Refutation of the Likeness Argument from Revealing Attributes
The author refutes the objection that equality in a revealing attribute requires likeness in essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the objection that an attribute revealing God's essence necessitates likeness between God and other beings.

Core Idea
Sharing a revealing attribute does not prove likeness; it only shows we lack evidence of difference.

The opponent claims that if God shares a 'revealing' attribute with others, then they must be alike in essence. This seems logical at first glance, but the author shows it is not necessarily true. He denies both premises of the argument and clarifies a crucial distinction between lacking evidence of difference and having positive evidence of likeness. This lesson unpacks that refutation.

3
Examine the denial of the second premise
Understand how the author denies the second premise: even if there were equality in that attribute, we do not affirm absolute likeness, only that we lack evidence of difference.
The author stresses the logical gap between 'not proven different' and 'proven alike'.

Refutation: Distinction Without Temporal or Spatial Separation
The author refutes the claim that lack of distinction in time, place, etc., implies lack of real distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the claim that lack of distinction in time, place, existence, or non-existence implies lack of real distinction, using the example of knowledge and power.

Core Idea
Real distinction does not require separation in time or space.

In this passage, the author tackles a common misconception: that if two things are not distinguished by time, place, existence, or non-existence, they must be identical. He refutes this by pointing to the case of knowledge and power in a single subject—they are distinct in reality despite lacking such external differentiation. This argument is crucial for defending the real distinctness of God's attributes.

This shifts the focus to intrinsic distinction.
3
Examine the evidence
Study the example of knowledge and power existing in a single subject. Despite not being distinguished by time, place, existence, or non-existence, they are distinct in reality. Conclude that the same applies to divine attributes.
This example is the core of the refutation.

Third Argument: Distinction by Reality

The argument counters the claim that if two things are not distinct in external circumstances, they must be identical. The author proposes that distinction can be by essence itself, and he offers a powerful analogy from knowledge and power inhering in a single subject.

First Objection and Answer on the Simplicity of the Body
Objection and answer on the simplicity of the body

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the philosophers' argument that a simple body is truly one and the response that its divisibility is possible, and why the impossibility arises from the attribute rather than the division itself.

Core Idea
A simple body's divisibility is possible, but impossibility stems from combining an indivisible attribute with a divisible subject.

This passage presents a first objection against the Ash'ari position on the inherence of attributes in bodies. The objecter borrows the philosophers' concept of a simple body, arguing that since it is truly one with no parts, the earlier reasoning about the accident being wholly or partially in parts does not apply. The answer clarifies that division is possible, and the real impossibility lies in the attribute's indivisibility, not the body's division.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection carefully
Read the objector's argument from 'فإن قيل' to 'لم يصح هذا الكلام'. Note the philosophers' claim about the simple body and the conclusion that the accident discussion is invalid.
The objector uses three conditions: stripping division, difference of accidents, and imagination.
2
Understand the philosophers' premise
Identify that the philosophers consider the simple body as truly one when those conditions are met, and being divisible does not mean actual division occurs.
This is analogous to a body being susceptible to blackness without being black.
3
Analyze the answer's logic
Read the answer from 'والجواب' onward. Notice the distinction between possible and impossible. The answer concedes unity but asserts that division is possible, and impossibility does not follow from a possible supposition.
The impossibility that follows (indivisible attribute in a divisible subject) shows the supposition of division is impossible, but the real cause is the attribute's nature.
4
Reflect on the overall refutation
Consider how this answer defends the original Ash'ari framework: the accident (knowledge) is indivisible, so it cannot be wholly or partially in parts precisely because the subject is divisible, not because the body has no parts.
The argument preserves the need for the body to have parts for the accident to inhere in each part, but the impossibility arises from the attribute's indivisibility.

Commentary
This passage presents a first objection and its answer. The objector borrows the philosophers' view that a simple body, without division, difference of accidents, or imagination, is truly one and has no composition. First Two Divisions: Impossibility of Attributes in Many Loci or Every Part
Razi presents and refutes the first two divisions of the body as subject of attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why Razi argues that it is impossible for human attributes to inhere in either multiple loci simultaneously or in every part of the body individually.

Core Idea
One human cannot be many; necessary knowledge proves it.

Razi begins his refutation by examining two possible ways the body might be the subject of human attributes. The first is that a single attribute inheres in multiple loci, which is impossible. The second is that each part of the body bears the attributes, which would make a single person into many persons, contradicting necessary knowledge. This lesson covers these two arguments.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Razi uses a very structured, enumerative style.
2
Identify the two divisions
The passage presents two divisions. Find the first (single accident in many loci) and the second (attributes in every part). Notice how each is introduced with 'الأول' and 'الثاني'.
3
Understand the first division's refutation
The first division is dismissed quickly: a single accident cannot inhere in multiple loci. This is an axiom in kalam.
Think of an accident like color: the same whiteness cannot be in two bodies at once.
4
Understand the second division's refutation
If every part has the attributes, then each part would be a distinct human, but necessary knowledge tells us we are one human. This contradiction proves the division false.
Necessary knowledge is immediate, certain, and universally shared.
5
Reflect on necessary knowledge
Razi asserts that the unity of a human is known by necessity. Consider why this is a strong argument in Islamic theology.
This is not empirical observation but an axiomatic truth.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the claim that the body is the subject (al-mawṣūf) of the attributes that constitute a specific human (al-insāniyya). The author, Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī, presents three possible ways the body could bear these attributes and demonstrates the impossibility of each.

First, he states the first division: that the attribute (al-ʿaraḍ) subsists in multiple parts simultaneously. This would require a single accident to inhere in multiple loci, which is impossible (muḥāl).

Second, he considers the division where the attributes subsist in each and every part of the body. If each part is described with the attributes that make a person that specific person (e.g., life, knowledge, power), then the body would not be one human but many humans. Yet necessary knowledge (al-ʿilm al-ḍarūrī) tells everyone that it is one human, not many. If this were not known necessarily, there would be no necessary knowledge at all. Thus this division is false.

Third, he considers the division where only a single indivisible part (al-juzʾ alladhī lā yatajazzā) bears the attributes. If one denies the existence of indivisible atoms (al-juzʾ alladhī lā yatajazzā), the argument falls away. But if one affirms atoms, it is also impossible. The reason is that the principle (al-mabdaʾ) is universal emanation (ʿāmm al-fayḍ), so a particular essence cannot be distinguished by a specific attribute unless it has a specific preparedness (istiʿdād khāṣṣ). For a single atom to have the preparedness to receive life, knowledge, and power is impossible. If all other parts also have that preparedness, then they would all be knowing and powerful, but we assumed only one is. If the other parts have the opposite preparedness (istiʿdād muḍādd), then the one atom is overpowered by the majority, and the stronger (al-ghālib) turns the weaker to its own state. Therefore, the atom cannot be distinguished by that preparedness, and thus it cannot alone be described with the attributes. 

Consequently, all three divisions are false. Hence, the body cannot be the subject of the attributes considered in humanity.

Third Division: Impossibility of Attributes in a Single Indivisible Part
Razi refutes the third division: attributes in a single indivisible part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's complex argument against the view that a single indivisible atom (al-juzʾ alladhī lā yatajazzā) is the subject of human attributes.

Core Idea
An atom cannot be uniquely prepared for life, knowledge, and power without contradiction.

After refuting the first two divisions, Razi tackles the third: that a single indivisible part (atom) bears the human attributes. He first notes that if one denies atoms, the argument collapses. If one affirms atoms, he shows it is impossible because an atom cannot be distinguished by a unique preparedness for life, knowledge, and power—either all parts would have it, or the one atom would be overpowered by the opposite preparedness of other parts.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
This passage is longer and more intricate; take your time.
2
Understand the conditional nature of the argument
Razi notes that if atoms are denied, the argument falls. But if atoms are affirmed, he proceeds to show impossibility.
This is a common tactic: refuting on the opponent's own terms.
3
Grasp the concept of preparedness (istiʿdād)
An attribute must be granted by the universal Giver, but a particular essence can only receive it if it has a specific preparedness. For an atom to have a unique preparedness is problematic.
Preparedness is a disposition to receive an accident.
4
Follow the two sub-arguments
Razi presents two possibilities: either all parts have the same preparedness (then all would be knowing, contradicting unity) or other parts have opposite preparedness (then the one atom is overcome by the majority, preventing its preparedness).
Both lead to impossibility.
5
Conclude the overall refutation
Razi concludes that all three divisions are false, so the body cannot be the subject of human attributes.
This paves the way for the soul (nafs) as the true subject.

The Fifth Proof: Body Cannot Bear Divine Attributes
Razi presents a trilemma to prove the body cannot bear divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's fifth proof that the body cannot be the subject of life, power, and knowledge, and why the three possible relationships between parts of the body regarding preparedness are all impossible.

Core Idea
The body fails as a subject for divine attributes because it leads to logical absurdity.

Razi now presents a sophisticated logical argument (the fifth proof) to show that the body cannot be what is living, powerful, and knowing. He considers three possible ways that parts of the body might be prepared to receive these attributes, and demonstrates that each leads to a contradiction. This argument is a key refutation of anthropomorphic views of God.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage presents a fifth proof (al-ḥujja al-khāmis) that the body cannot be the subject of the attributes of life, power, and knowledge (al-ḥayy, al-qādir, al-ʿālim). The author argues by considering the relationship between parts of the body regarding preparedness (istiʿdād) to receive these attributes.

The reasoning is somewhat compressed: if preparedness could occur in one without the other, that would violate the condition of the second division. Therefore, both sides must be dependent together, leading back to the first division's circularity, hence false.

Third division (mutual independence): The text cuts off before fully explaining this, but the overall conclusion is that all three divisions are false, so the body cannot be the subject of these attributes. The proof is a logical argument from the impossibility of explaining how multiple parts could each be prepared to receive the same attribute without unacceptable consequences.

This passage presents the second argument for the pre-eternity of rational souls and then refutes it. Learning Objective

Superiority of the First Principle over Celestial Spirits
Argues for the superiority of the First Principle over any hypothetical celestial spirits due to the former's self-sufficiency and the latter's dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the First Principle (المبدأ الأول) is more worthy of worship and reliance than any intermediate celestial spirits, even if they exist and have power.

Core Idea
The First Principle is superior because He is self-sufficient; all others depend on Him.

The author presents a second subtle point from the verse, addressing those who might consider celestial spirits as effective agents. He argues that even if such spirits exist and can help, the First Principle is still more complete and it is better to turn directly to Him. This reinforces the Islamic emphasis on tawhid and direct reliance on Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on the second subtlety
Read the Arabic passage about the second subtlety (دقيقة أخرى). Notice the hypothetical language: 'بتقدير كونها قادرة' (assuming they are capable).
2
Understand the assumption about celestial spirits
The author grants, for argument's sake, that celestial spirits (الأرواح الفلكية) have the power to repel harm and bring blessings. This is not an affirmation that they exist, but a logical exercise.
The word 'بتقدير' means 'assuming'.
3
Grasp the reason for the First Principle's superiority
The spirits are in need of the Necessary Existence (واجب الوجود) in their essences and attributes. The needed one (المحتاج إليه) is more complete, better, higher, and stronger than the needy (المحتاج). Therefore, the First Principle's power is more complete and it is superior.
This is a key metaphysical principle: dependency entails inferiority.
4
Apply the conclusion to worship
Even if the spirits manage the world, it is more appropriate to resort to the Necessary Existence directly for needs, because He is above all in perfection and mercy. This is why the verse ends with 'And You are the best of the merciful.'

Commentary
This passage analyzes the verse 'وقل رب اغفر وارحم وأنت خير الراحمين' (Say: My Lord, forgive and have mercy, and You are the best of the merciful). The author finds two subtle points. First, the order: forgiveness (غفران) is mentioned before mercy (رحمة) because the purpose of worship (المقصود بالعبادات) is twofold: repelling harm (دفع المؤذي) and attaining good (الفوز بالراحة). Repelling harm is more important, so it is prioritized. Forgiveness corresponds to repelling harm (i.e., punishment), while mercy corresponds to attaining rest. Therefore, forgiveness is requested first. Second, even if one assumes that celestial spirits (الأرواح الفلكية) have the ability to repel harm and bring blessings, the First Principle (المبدأ الأول) has a more complete and superior power. This is because the spirits are in need of the Necessary Existence (واجب الوجود لذاته) in their essences and attributes, and the one in need is inferior to the one needed. Moreover, even if these spirits exist and manage the world, it is more fitting to turn to the Necessary Existence directly for needs, because He is above all in perfection, mercy, favor, generosity, and kindness. Hence, the verse ends with 'And You are the best of the merciful', affirming His superiority.

Quranic Attributes of Angels: Tasbih, Obedience, Power, and Fear
The author lists four divine attributes of angels from Quranic verses and interprets their fear.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the four Quranic attributes of angels—tasbih, absolute obedience to Allah's command, immense power as administrators of the heavens, and fear rooted in their awareness of contingency—as presented by the author.

Core Idea
Angels glorify Allah, obey absolutely, wield cosmic power, and fear Him out of awareness of their own contingency.

This passage from Al-Matalib al-Aliyah enumerates four attributes of angels derived from Quranic verses. The author not only lists these qualities but also provides a profound philosophical interpretation of their fear as an existential recognition of their dependence on Allah. Let's examine each attribute and the insightful commentary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four attributes
Read through the passage and note each attribute: tasbih, obedience, power, and fear. Pay attention to the Quranic proof for each.
The author numbers them as second, fourth, fifth—there is no third; consider them as four distinct attributes.
2
Understand the power attribute via Throne and Kursi
The author describes the angels' power by mentioning their bearing of the Throne and the encompassing of the Kursi. Reflect on the greatness of these creations.
The Kursi encompasses the heavens and earth, yet the Throne is far greater.
3
Analyze the fear attribute and its interpretation
The author explains that angels' fear stems from their recognition of possibility (imkan) of existence and non-existence. Understand how possibility considered in itself is non-existence.
This is a key philosophical point linking contingency to existential insecurity.
4
Grasp the resolution of fear
The author suggests that when Allah effuses existence upon them, they become necessary through another and gain security from annihilation.
This shows the angels' ultimate reliance on Allah for existence.

Angels have distinct ranks and roles, yet all are utterly absorbed in awe and worship. Are you content with the role God has placed you in, or do you envy others? How can you find the same contentment that the angels have in their assigned worship?
?
The passage says angels 'do not locate Him in places' – they know God is beyond space. Yet you may act as if God is far or absent when you sin, or think He is confined to a mosque. When have you treated God as if He were limited to a location or distant from your daily life? Commit to one action today that acknowledges His omnipresence.
