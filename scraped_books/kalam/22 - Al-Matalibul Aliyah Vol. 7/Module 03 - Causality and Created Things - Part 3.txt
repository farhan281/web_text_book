The Fifth Argument: Rationality of the Celestial Sphere
Fifth argument: dilemma on sphere's rationality leading to futility of motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the dilemma that the fifth argument poses against the rationality of the celestial sphere and its implication for the observed perpetual motion.

Core Idea
A rational sphere would realize its motion is pointless and choose rest.

This argument presents a powerful dilemma: if the celestial sphere is irrational, its motion carries no meaning; but if it is rational, it would see that all possible positions are identical, making continuous motion futile. Thus, a rational sphere would rest, contradicting its observed perpetual motion. This challenges the assumption of a rational mover behind the cosmos.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation to grasp the logical flow.
Pay attention to the two conditional structures (if insane, if rational).
2
Identify the two branches of the dilemma
Find the two possibilities: the sphere is insane (idiotic) or it is rational. Notice how each branch leads to a different conclusion about its motion.
The first branch dismisses the sphere's motion as unreliable.
3
Understand the rational sphere's reasoning
The rational sphere knows it can only actualize one position at a time, and all positions are identical in essence. So there is no benefit in substituting one for another.
Key concepts: extractions are identical, no futility in change.
4
Connect reasoning to the conclusion
If there is no benefit, a rational sphere would be satisfied with one position and rest. This contradicts the observed perpetual motion.
The argument forces the opponent to accept either an irrational sphere or a non-moving sphere.
5
Reflect on the force of the argument
Consider how this dilemma undercuts the claim of a rational mover. It uses logical reasoning to challenge empirical observation.
Think about why this argument is considered a 'proof' in kalam.

Why Concentrate on One Orbit?
A logical refutation of the extraction theory using the principle of equal paths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical problem of why a rational agent would persist in one among many equally effective paths, and how this refutes the extraction theory of celestial motion.

Core Idea
Equal paths lead to impossibility of permanent preference for one.

This argument challenges the extraction theory by focusing on the sphere's unwavering commitment to one specific orbit. If the goal were simply to extract places and positions, there are infinite orbits offering the same opportunity. Why cling to just one? The reasoning exposes a fatal flaw in the theory, using a principle of rational choice.

Guided Path
1
Read the full argument
Read the Arabic passage and its translation carefully. Notice the structure: a conditional premise, a question, a general principle, and a conclusion.
The argument is short but dense; take your time.
2
Identify the key principle
Find the sentence that states the principle about equal paths. This principle is the heart of the argument.
It says that if many paths equally reach the goal, persisting in one forever is impossible.
3
Apply the principle to the case
Understand how the principle applies to the celestial sphere: if extraction is the purpose, all orbits are equal, so persisting in one orbit is impossible. Yet we observe persistence, so the extraction theory is false.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider what this argument implies about the true cause of the sphere's motion. It suggests the purpose cannot be arbitrary extraction.
The argument does not give an alternative, but it refutes one explanation.

Commentary
This passage presents an argument about the celestial souls (nufus falakiyya) and their movements. It begins by stating the second division (القسم الثاني) of the purpose of celestial motion, which is the acquisition of psychic perfections. The author then argues that the celestial soul and its perfections are contingent existents requiring an efficient cause. He enumerates four possible types of causes: accidents, bodies, an abstract substance acting through a bodily instrument, and an abstract substance acting without an instrument. He rejects the first three using a previous argument from human souls, stating that if human souls—despite their lowliness—require an abstract intellectual cause, then celestial souls (which are higher and more sacred) are even more in need of such a cause. Then, based on the principle 'from the One only one proceeds' (الواحد لا يصدر عنه إلا الواحد), he excludes God as the direct cause of the multiplicity of celestial souls and their perfections. He draws an analogy: a student who believes his teacher is perfect and that he can only gain perfection by going to him and learning from him; the student's movement to the teacher is desiderative, and his desire to learn is imitative. Commentary
This passage presents the eighth proof from a series of arguments concerning causality. The claim is that a body cannot be the cause of another body. The proof is built on the composition of body from matter (hayula) and form (sura). If it influences without participation of matter, then the form would be independent of matter in its act, which would imply independence in essence, but material forms are inherently dependent on matter—contradiction. In his fifth argument, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi builds on the premise that celestial spiritualities are principles for earthly ones. He then applies a key causal principle: the source of perfection is always superior to that which receives it. This passage shows how that principle establishes the complete dependence of human souls on higher spiritual beings.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth argument for the superiority of celestial spiritualities. It begins by restating a previously established point: the celestial spiritualities (al-rūḥāniyyāt al-falakiyya) are principles (mabādi’) for the spiritualities of this world, and are their cause (ma‘lūl). The author then applies the general principle that the principle (al-mabda’) is nobler (ashraf) than that which derives from it (dhū al-mabda’). The reasoning is that any perfection found in the effect is first and primarily in the cause; only a small portion flows (yafīḍ) to the effect. Additionally, the bliss (sa‘ādāt) of human souls after death consists in their connection (ittiṣāl) to these higher entities and their acquiring even a tiny atom of their lights. The conclusion is that every perfection of human souls—whether at the beginning (origin) or end (return)—depends on the influx of a minute particle of perfection from that higher world. This establishes the complete dependence and inferiority of human souls.

The Seventh Argument: Human Bodily Powers as Obstacles to Spiritual Perfection
Al-Razi lists 19 bodily powers opposing reason, showing human weakness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi argues that the human rational soul is weakened by nineteen bodily powers, proving human inferiority to angels.

Core Idea
Nineteen bodily powers oppose reason, making human spiritual perfection difficult.

This argument from al-Razi uses a detailed analysis of human physiology and psychology to show that the rational soul is constantly battling bodily distractions. The author counts nineteen specific powers—from senses to plant-like functions—that dominate the body and turn the soul away from spiritual matters. By interpreting a Quranic verse about 'nineteen' allegorically, he argues that the body is a 'hell' that prevents reason from shining.

And the seventh: Man, even if he possesses the rational, perceiving power, it is opposed by nineteen types of bodily powers (3), and all of them oppose the rational power in acquiring spiritual perfections. The external senses are directed to the modality of corporeal things, and are turned away from spiritualities. As for the five internal senses, they are occupied with regulating those states acquired by the external senses by disposing them. As for appetite and anger, it is evident that they are preventers of spiritual states. As for the seven plant-like powers: the attractive, repulsive, nutritive, growth, and generative—it is obvious that they prevent turning towards the world of spiritualities. For this reason, it came in the Divine Book that Hell has nineteen angels (zabāniya): He said, 'Over it are nineteen.' (4) And the intended meaning is the Hell of the body. And it is dominant over the body from the beginning of formation and origination to its end. As for the rational power, it only appears after puberty, after the bodily powers have become firmly established in the body and have taken control of it. Then it is stripped of helpers and supporters. And if the matter is so, then it becomes clear that the light of the rational power in the substances of the rational souls is weak. As for the celestial souls and abstract intellects, they are in their essences higher than to be called lights. And their knowledges are intellections.

Commentary
This passage presents the seventh argument, which highlights the weakness of the human rational soul due to the overwhelming opposition of bodily powers. The author enumerates nineteen types of bodily powers that oppose the rational power (al-quwwa al-‘āqila). He classifies them: external senses (5), internal senses (5), appetite and anger (2), and seven plant-like powers (total 5+5+2+7=19). All these powers are directed toward corporeal matters and distract from spiritual perfections. The author interprets the Quranic verse 'Over it are nineteen' (Surah al-Muddaththir 74:30) as referring to the 'Hell of the body' (jahannam al-badan), meaning these nineteen powers dominate the body from beginning to end. The rational power, in contrast, appears only after puberty when the bodily powers are already entrenched, and it has no helpers. Therefore, the light of reason in human souls is weak. In contrast, celestial souls and abstract intellects are higher in essence and their knowledges are pure intellections (al-ta‘aqqulāt). The argument serves to show that humans are far inferior to angels and cannot be considered superior to them.
