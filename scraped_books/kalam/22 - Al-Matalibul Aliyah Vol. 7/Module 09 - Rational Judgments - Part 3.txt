The Fifth Proof: Four Categories of Bodies
Divides bodies into four categories to prove celestial bodies are alive and rational.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a rational division of all bodies into four categories leads to the conclusion that celestial bodies are living and rational.

Core Idea
A rational division of bodies shows that only celestial beings can possess noble wisdom and eternal life.

The fifth proof in this series employs a powerful logical tool: a complete division of all bodies into four mutually exclusive categories. By observing which categories exist in the earthly realm, the proof argues that the remaining category must also exist and can only be occupied by the celestial spheres. This lesson unpacks this rational division and its theological implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Get a sense of the overall argument.
Notice the clear structure of division and elimination.
2
Identify the four categories
List the four categories with their defining attributes: wisdom/eternal life vs. lust/anger, etc.
The categories are exhaustive and mutually exclusive.
3
Observe the existence of three categories
Note how the author provides examples for categories 2, 3, and 4: animals, humans, plants.
This establishes that these categories are not empty.
4
Follow the argument for the first category
Read why the first category must exist and why only celestial bodies fit. Consider the divine wisdom argument.
If this category were missing, creation would lack its noblest part.
5
Conclude: celestial bodies are living and rational
Understand that the proof arrives at the necessity of celestial bodies being alive and rational. Review the key terms.

The fifth proof: The bodies of the world, according to rational division, are of four categories: either they are described with wisdom and noble eternal life and are free from lust and anger and death; or they are described with lust and anger and are free from wisdom and noble life; or they are described with both; or they are free from both together. As for the second category: it is the body described with lust, devoid of wisdom. It exists. It is the beasts, predators, birds, and insects. As for the third category: it is the one described with both together. It also exists. It is the human. As for the fourth category: it is the one free from both together. It also exists. It is plants, minerals, and inanimate objects. There remains the first category: it is the body described with noble life, perfect wisdom, and honorable knowledge. There is no body here that can be described with these attributes except the high celestial bodies. So if they were not described with these attributes, this category of bodies would be missing. And there is no doubt that this category is the noblest of categories, the highest in degree, and the greatest in rank. So if this category did not exist, the categories of Allah's creatures would be devoid of the noblest and most sublime of categories, and there would be restriction to the creation of base bodies and prohibition of creating the noblest highest category. That would necessitate corruption of the system in the governance of this world. And the wise, holy God is exalted above that. So it is necessary to affirm the existence of this category, and that cannot be achieved except by saying that the celestial spheres and planets are living, rational beings.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth proof (al-hujja al-khamisa) for the life and rationality of celestial bodies. The author begins with a rational division (al-qisma al-‘aqliyya) of all bodies into four mutually exclusive categories: (1) those characterized by wisdom (hikma) and noble eternal life, free from lust, anger, and death; (2) those characterized by lust and anger, free from wisdom and noble life; (3) those characterized by both wisdom and passions; (4) those free from both wisdom and passions. The second category exists: animals (beasts, predators, birds, insects). The third category exists: humans. The fourth category exists: plants, minerals, and inanimate objects. The first category—bodies with noble life, perfect wisdom, and honorable knowledge—remains. The author asserts that no body in this world can possess such attributes except the high celestial bodies (al-ajram al-‘aliya al-falakiyya). If they lacked these attributes, this noblest category would be missing from creation. But it would be unfitting for the all-wise God to create only base bodies and omit the noblest category, as that would imply corruption in the system of governance (tadbir hadha al-‘alam). Therefore, this category must exist, which necessitates that the celestial spheres and planets are living, rational beings (ahya’ ‘aqila). The argument is a classic kalam proof by division and elimination: all other categories are observed, and the only possible candidate for the remaining category is the heavens.

The Fifth Proof: Fourfold Division of Bodies
Razi enumerates four categories of bodies and proves the celestial bodies belong to the first category.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses a fourfold rational division of bodies to prove that celestial bodies are living, rational beings.

Core Idea
A missing noblest category in a rational division forces the conclusion that the heavens are alive.

Razi presents a logical proof based on a comprehensive categorization of all bodies. By showing that three of four categories exist among earthly things, he argues that the fourth and noblest must also exist—and only the celestial spheres fit. This proof relies on the principle of divine wisdom and order.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice that Razi uses a very structured, enumerative style here.
2
Identify the four categories
The passage divides all bodies into four rational categories based on presence or absence of wisdom/noble life and desire/anger. Find each category and note its defining features.
Category 1: wisdom and noble life, free from desire/anger/death. Category 2: desire/anger, free from wisdom. Category 3: both. Category 4: neither.
3
See which categories exist among known bodies
Razi then provides examples for categories 2, 3, and 4: animals, humans, and plants/minerals respectively. Observe how he matches each category to a known class of beings.
Category 1 is not yet assigned an example.
4
Understand why the first category must exist
Razi argues that the first category (the noblest) must exist because its absence would contradict divine wisdom and order. Only celestial bodies fit this description.
This step involves deductive reasoning from the perfection of God's creation.
5
Reflect on the conclusion
The proof concludes that the celestial spheres and planets are living, rational beings (أحياء عاقلة). Consider how this argument uses classification and the principle of wisdom.
This is a key proof in Islamic natural theology for the animated nature of the heavens.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth proof for the life and rationality of celestial bodies. It begins by dividing all bodies in the world into four rational categories: (1) those with wisdom and noble life but free from desire/anger/death; (2) those with desire/anger but free from wisdom and noble life; (3) those with both; (4) those with neither. Then it identifies the existing examples for three categories: animals (2), humans (3), plants/minerals (4). The remaining category (1) must also exist, and since no earthly body fits, it must be the celestial spheres and planets. If they were not alive, this noblest category would be missing from creation, which would be inconsistent with divine wisdom and order. Therefore, the proof concludes that the celestial spheres and planets are living, rational beings.

The Same Soul Perceives Partials and Universals: Razi's Second Argument
Razi argues the same soul must perceive both partials and universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Razi's argument that the same abstract soul must be the subject of both partial and universal perceptions, and how this refutes the claim that partial perception requires a physical force.

Core Idea
Judgment unites the particular and universal, so the soul must do both.

Razi presents a second argument, based on the structure of judgment itself. When we say 'Zaid is a human,' we must have already conceived both Zaid as a particular and 'human' as a universal. This shows that the same perceiver must be responsible for both kinds of perception, leading to the conclusion that the abstract soul perceives partials as well as universals.

Guided Path
1
Examine the judgment 'Zaid is a human'
Read the Arabic passage and notice how Razi begins with a self-evident judgment. This judgment requires two distinct perceptions: Zaid (particular) and human (universal).
The judgment is a simple sentence we use daily.
2
Identify the necessary condition
Razi argues that if the same subject didn't perceive both, the judgment would occur without conception, which is impossible. This is a key logical principle.
Implicit: judgment presupposes conception.
3
Follow the conclusion
Since universal perceptions belong to the abstract soul, and the same subject must perceive both, the soul also perceives partials. Therefore, the claim that physical force is needed for partial perception is invalid.
This refutes a specific opponent's view.

But the possessor of universal perceptions is the abstract soul, so it must be that the possessor of partial perceptions is also the soul. And if that is the case, then their claim that the possessor of partial perceptions must be a physical force becomes invalid.

Commentary
This passage presents the second argument, based on the necessity of combining universal and partial perception in the same act of judgment. When we say 'Zaid is a human,' we are making a judgment that requires conceiving both Zaid as a particular and 'human' as a universal. The perceiver must be the same subject for both. Since universal perception is attributed to the abstract soul, the same soul must also be the perceiver of partials. Therefore, it is not necessary that partial perceptions require a physical force. The argument is presented as self-evident.

Third Argument: The Dilemma of the Rational Soul's Governance
Razi presents a dilemma on the rational soul's governance to refute the opponent's claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Razi uses a dilemma about the rational soul's governance to refute the opponent's claims about universal and partial perceptions.

Core Idea
The rational soul's governance creates an inescapable dilemma for the opponent.

Razi presents a concise but powerful argument. He takes a premise accepted by both sides—that the rational soul governs the body—and shows that it leads to a contradiction in the opponent's position. This lesson walks through the dilemma step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
The rational soul is an abstract substance that governs this particular body. This is agreed upon.
2
Identify the two options
Governance must be either by universal perceptions/intention or by partial perceptions/intention.
3
Examine the first horn
If governance is by universal, then the opponent's claim that universal intention cannot produce partial actions is refuted.
4
Examine the second horn
If governance is by partial, then the opponent's claim that an abstract substance cannot have partial perceptions is refuted.
5
Conclude the dilemma
Either way, the opponent's position is invalid. The author expresses astonishment at their oversight.

Third: The rational soul is an abstract substance. It manages this particular body. Either it manages this body by universal perceptions and universal intention, or it manages it by partial perceptions and partial intention. And I say: Truly, I am greatly astonished at the heedlessness of these people regarding these clear words. This is the critique of their proof.

Commentary
This passage presents the third argument, a simple dilemma. The rational soul (al-nafs al-nāṭiqa) is an abstract substance that governs this particular body. Its governance must be either by universal perceptions/intention or by partial perceptions/intention. If by universal, then the opponent's claim that universal intention cannot produce particular actions is false (since the soul does produce particular acts). If by partial, then the opponent's claim that an abstract substance cannot have partial perceptions is false. Either way, the opponent's position is invalid. The author then expresses amazement that people overlook this clear point.

Commentary
This passage presents the opponents' final proof (the third proof) for human superiority over angels, followed by detailed refutations of all their arguments. The passage begins by concluding a prior argument about difficulty and reward. Then it introduces the third proof: God created angels with intellect only, animals with lust only, and humans with both; if a human follows lust, he is worse than animals; if he follows intellect, he must be better than angels, by analogy. The author then states that these are the opponents' proofs. The responses follow: (1) Against the first proof (angels commanded to prostrate): The command was specific and the Quran limits prostration to God alone. (2) Against the second proof (Adam as caliph): Being caliph on earth does not imply superiority over heavenly beings. (3) Against the third proof (Adam's knowledge): Adam's knowledge was only over earthly angels, not heavenly; the author promises a proof that heavenly angels are more knowledgeable. (4) Against the fourth proof (God's choice): "Worlds" is interpreted as people of the earth. (5) Against the argument that harder worship implies superiority: Refuted by analogies to bakers and rulers, and to two men with different health conditions; difficulty alone does not confer superiority. (6) Against the final inference: The verse comparing humans to animals is hyperbole for deterrence, not a literal statement of superiority. The passage thus systematically dismantles all arguments for human superiority over angels.

Core Idea
Angels know through active, constant, and perfect intellect; human knowledge is passive, imperfect, and fleeting.

Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, in his theological work, provides five compelling proofs that angelic knowledge surpasses human knowledge. These arguments highlight fundamental differences in the nature of knowledge acquisition, causality, imperfection, presence, and progression. By examining these proofs, we gain insight into the Islamic theological conception of angelic intellect.

Commentary
This passage presents five proofs that angels' knowledge is superior to humans' knowledge. 

First proof: Angels' knowledge is active (they know without needing to learn), while human knowledge is passive (acquired through senses and reasoning). 

Second proof: Angels are the effusing causes of human knowledge—they are the intermediaries through which knowledge flows to humans. A cause is more perfect than its effect. 

Third proof: Angelic knowledge is free from forgetfulness, error, and mistake, whereas human knowledge is mixed with these imperfections. 

Fourth proof: Angels' knowledge is constantly present (habitual intellect), but human knowledge is often not present; it is mutually exclusive—when focusing on one thing, other knowledge disappears. 

Fifth proof: New human souls are born daily and will eventually equal the knowledge of previous human souls, showing human knowledge is capable of being matched and surpassed. In contrast, angelic knowledge is fixed and not subject to such progression or equality from newcomers, indicating their superiority.
