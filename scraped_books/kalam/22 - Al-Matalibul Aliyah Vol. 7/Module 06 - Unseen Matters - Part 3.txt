Commentary
This proof cites Quran 6:50 and 17:90. The disbelievers demanded miracles from the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), such as causing a spring to gush forth, which are acts that angels can do. An objection is raised: this only shows that angels have greater ability, not greater virtue. The author answers with an analogy: if a man is asked to do things beyond his ability and he says he is not a sovereign, that does not mean he is inferior to a sovereign in all respects; a knowledgeable man might be better than a sovereign. However, in the case of the Prophet, the negation is absolute—he denies having any of those powers—and this implies that his state is inferior to that of angels, because if he had a virtue that outweighed the angels' powers, he would have mentioned it. The fact that he only denies powers without asserting any superior virtue shows that his general state is lower than that of angels.

The verse's qualification 'many' preserves angelic superiority.

This lesson examines a proof from the Qur'an that relies on careful attention to wording. The Twentieth Proof: Angels Are More Knowledgeable
Two-part proof that angels are more knowledgeable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two arguments that demonstrate angels' superior knowledge over humans, including the role of Jibril as teacher and the division of religious sciences.

Core Idea
Angels are more knowledgeable than humans, making them superior in rank.

This final proof in the series addresses a key aspect of prophethood: the relative knowledge of angels and prophets. The author presents two clear reasons why angels, particularly Jibril, possess greater knowledge. First, the Prophet Muhammad learned from Jibril, establishing Jibril as the teacher. Second, religious knowledge is divided into principles and branches, and in both categories, the angel's knowledge is either equal or superior due to direct witnessing of the divine realm.

Commentary
This passage presents the twentieth and final proof: angels are more knowledgeable than humans, and the more knowledgeable is better. The author provides two reasons. First, Muhammad (peace be upon him) learned from Jibril, as stated in Qur'an 53:5 ('Taught by one of great power'), and the teacher is always more knowledgeable than the student. Second, he divides religious knowledge into two categories: knowledge of principles (usul, i.e., tawhid and essential beliefs) and knowledge of branches (furu', i.e., legal rulings). Regarding principles, both Jibril and Muhammad are equal in perfection and cannot be deficient. Regarding knowledge of creation's details (the modality of things), Jibril is more perfect because he has directly witnessed the Throne, the Chair, the Tablet, the Pen, Paradise, Hell, the heavens, stars, and the elements, seas, mountains, etc. His dwelling is in the heavens, and he is obeyed by the angels overseeing those realms, so he knows them intimately. Thus his knowledge of these things is incomparable to human knowledge. Regarding branches (revealed laws), this knowledge only comes via revelation, and necessarily, Muhammad and all prophets only received it through learning from Jibril. Therefore, in all respects, Jibril is more knowledgeable and thus superior.

Moreover, Muhammad knew only his own law, not the laws of previous prophets or the laws of celestial beings, while Jibril knows all these. Therefore, there is no proportion between Jibril and Muhammad in either principles or branches, so Jibril is superior, as the Quran says: 'Are those who know and those who do not know equal?' (39:9). This establishes the superiority of Jibril over Muhammad in comprehensive knowledge.

Reconciling Adam's Knowledge of the Names with Angel Superiority
Objection and answer about Adam's knowledge of the names.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author resolves the apparent contradiction between Adam's knowledge of the names and the superiority of certain angels over humans.

Core Idea
Scriptural contradictions are resolved by interpreting terms contextually, not by discarding proofs.

In this brief passage, the author tackles a classic objection: if Adam knew all the names and the angels did not, does that not prove Adam's superiority over all angels? The answer lies in careful scriptural reconciliation. By distinguishing between different categories of angels, the author preserves the earlier proofs for the superiority of angels like Jibril without dismissing the story of Adam.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the objection
Understand what the objectors are claiming: that Adam knew the names (Quran 2:31-33) and the angels did not, implying Adam's superiority over angels.
This objection challenges the earlier scriptural proofs for angel superiority.
2
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Read the Arabic text and its literal translation carefully. Notice the direct structure: 'If they say... We say.'
3
Analyze the author's response
The author does not deny the event but interprets the term 'angels' in that context as 'earthly angels' (al-mala'ikat al-ardiyyah), not the high-ranking angels like Jibril.
This is a principle of tawfiq (reconciliation) between proofs when possible.
4
Reflect on the principle of reconciliation
Think about why this interpretation is necessary. The author is harmonizing two sets of scriptural evidence, showing that apparent contradictions can be resolved through proper contextualization.
This method is a hallmark of sound theological reasoning.

This reconciliation (tawfiq) between the various scriptural proofs is necessary when possible. Thus the objection does not undermine the earlier scriptural proofs for the superiority of Jibril over humans.
