Every existent falls into one of four categories: affecting, affected, both, or neither.

This lesson introduces a foundational rational classification used in Islamic theology to distinguish between different kinds of existents. By considering whether something can affect or be affected, we arrive at four mutually exclusive categories. This simple but powerful division helps clarify the unique nature of the Creator and the rest of creation.

Why the Self Cannot Be Proven by Demonstration
The objector gives two proofs that the self's essence cannot be demonstrated.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two arguments used by the objector to show that the self's essence cannot be demonstrated through logical proof, and the alternative possibilities left open.

Core Idea
Demonstration needs a middle term; between a thing and its own essence, no middle term can exist.

The objector raises an important challenge: how can we claim to prove the self's essence when the very structure of demonstration seems to exclude it? This lesson presents the two proofs that aim to show that the self's essence cannot be the subject of a demonstrative argument. The first proof focuses on the impossibility of a middle term between a thing and its essence, while the second shows that even if a middle term could exist, it would not be more known than the essence itself. These arguments force us to reconsider what kind of knowledge we can have of the self.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the two distinct proofs introduced by 'the first' and 'also'.
2
Understand the structure of demonstration
The passage explains that demonstration (burhan) requires a middle term (al-hadd al-awsat) that is more known than the conclusion. Familiarize yourself with the terms: major term, minor term, middle term.
This logical framework is essential for the objection.
3
Analyze the first proof
The first proof argues that no middle term can exist between a thing and its own essence. Therefore, demonstration is impossible for the self. Focus on the reasoning: 'impossible that between a thing and its specific reality there occur a middle term'.
4
Analyze the second proof
The second proof considers a hypothetical: even if a middle term could exist, it would be absurd claim it is more known than the thing's own essence. This reinforces the impossibility.
The second proof is a conditional argument, not a separate claim.
5
Consider the conclusion
The objector concludes that the self's essence cannot be proven by demonstration. Two alternatives remain: it is either impossible to prove absolutely, or it is known by necessity (intuitively). Ask yourself which alternative seems more plausible.
This sets up the next discussion in the text.

Self-Knowledge Is Intuitive: Analysis of 'I Am Existent'
The author analyzes the proposition 'I am existent' to prove self-knowledge is intuitive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why knowledge of the self is considered intuitive (badihi) and not demonstrable, based on the analysis of the proposition 'I am existent'.

Core Idea
The concept of 'I' cannot be proven because it is already known before any demonstration.

This passage from al-Matalib al-Aliyah presents a sophisticated argument that self-knowledge is immediate. The author takes the necessary knowledge that 'I exist' and examines what the word 'I' refers to. By analyzing the referent, he shows that it must be the entire essence, making self-knowledge intuitive. We will walk through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the starting point
The argument begins with the premise that knowledge of one's own existence is necessary (daruri). Understand that this is granted by the opponent.
This premise is crucial for the rest of the argument.
2
Analyze the proposition 'I am existent'
Break down the proposition into subject ('I') and predicate ('existent'). Since conception precedes assent, the concept of 'I' must already be known. The meaning of 'I' is the specific essence.
This leads to the conclusion that the essence is known intuitively.
3
Examine the possibilities for the referent of 'I'
The author considers three possibilities: it is the entire essence, a part of the essence, or something external. Each is examined to show that only the first is coherent, and the others lead to absurdities or circularity.
This logical trilemma confirms that self-knowledge is intuitive.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal argument establishing the heart as the primary organ and the first to form in the body, refuting the tripartite soul theory. The author begins by stating the chapter's topic: proving that the soul's primary attachment is the heart and that the heart is the absolute principal organ. He then presents two competing views: the view of the majority of verifiers (including Aristotle) that the heart is the principal organ and the soul first attaches to it, and Galen's view that there are three souls each attached to a different organ (liver, heart, brain). The author rejects Galen's view as false and chooses the first view. He then provides arguments, starting with the first proof: having already demonstrated that the soul is one, it follows that a single principal organ must exist, and that is the heart. This is supported by experience (testimony of experimenters that the heart is the first organ to form) and analogy. The analogy is detailed from two aspects, but here only the first is given. The author explains that semen is composed of four elements, with airy and fiery elements dominating, evidenced by the whiteness of fluids due to airy parts. And the third division: we say: sight depends on conditions. Likewise insight. The first condition: that the object of sight should not be in extreme clarity, like the sun, for the eye is bewildered by it due to its strength; nor in extreme obscurity, like atoms and the degrees of mixtures. The second condition: if the object of sight is present, when a person does not move his pupil from side to side with many movements, he does not see the object. And just as the sight of the eye is turning the pupil from one direction to another seeking vision, so the sight of the intellect is turning the pupil of the intellect from side to side seeking perception of the intelligible. The third condition: the visual faculty cannot perceive visible objects except when the air becomes illuminated due to the rising of luminous things. Likewise the intellect cannot see except when the spiritual lights rise. Then the lights of the corporeal world are four: the sun, the moon, the stars, and fire. The greatest is the sun, then the moon, then the stars, then fire. Likewise the lights of the spiritual world are four: the First Principle—exalted and holy—, after it the Great Spirit, which is the noblest of the holy spirits, after it the degrees of angels, like the ranks of the stars, and after it the human intellect, which is like fire. The first are the angels, prophets, and saints; the second are the philosophers.
