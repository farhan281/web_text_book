The Status of Kalam and Its Role
Author states the importance of kalam and categorizes its authors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the great status of the science of kalam in establishing and defending the Islamic creed, and the classification of its authors.

Core Idea
Kalam is the science that establishes and defends the creed.

In this opening passage, the author highlights the central importance of the science of tawhid, also known as kalam, in grounding and safeguarding the Islamic faith. He then notes that theologians have written works of varying depth, from moderate to profound. This sets the stage for the discussion of key texts that follows.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once, then the translation. Notice the key terms: علم التوحيد, علم الكلام, المتكلمون.
This passage is a single, dense sentence.
2
Identify the three categories
The author mentions three types of theologians: المقتصد (moderate), المتوسط (intermediate), and المتعمق (profound). Reflect on what each might entail.
3
Connect to the creed
The passage states that kalam is concerned with تأصيل هذه العقيدة والدفاع عنها (establishing and defending this creed). Ask yourself why this makes the science so important.
This connects to the learning objective.

The moderate, and among them the profound, and the difficulty therein, therefore it is eternal as the eternity of time, no alteration in it, nor distortion until the Hour is established, so it was a mercy to the worlds and general for all people, and Allah spoke the truth when He says: 'Today I have perfected your religion for you, completed My favor upon you, and have chosen for you Islam as your religion.' This, and since the science of tawhid (Oneness), which is called the science of kalam (theology), has this great status in establishing this creed and defending it, the mutakallimun (theologians) have been concerned, anciently and modernly, with authoring in it, and they competed in that, and among them is the moderate, among them the intermediate, and among them the profound. And among the most famous profound books that include the general matters needed by the one who infers the temporality of the world and from it the existence of the Maker, are the book al-Matalib al-‘Aliya by Fakhr al-Razi, and al-‘Aqa’id al-Nasafiyya by al-Nasafi, and the book al-Mawaqif by al-Iji, the teacher of Sa‘d al-Taftazani, and the book Tawali‘ al-Anwar by al-Baydawi, and the book al-Maqasid by the eminent scholar al-Sa‘d al-Taftazani, and it is a comprehensive book needed by every researcher, for what it contains of investigations and rational theories, and it made up for what was missing from the books of his predecessors, especially the book al-Mawaqif and al-Matalib al-‘Aliya. And although it is difficult of attainment due to the precision of its style and profundity in its investigations, yet it was authored in an era in which minds had matured and their perceptions had expanded, so much so that they would only accept that in which there was depth in thinking and reflection. Such was the practice of authors in this era, and the way of their students: the most capable of them in knowledge was the most intricate in writing. And in truth, if a person makes the difficult easy, reflects on it and considers, then sharpens his mind on the essence behind that to understand what is subtle of the issues, that becomes established in his mind, and fosters in him a strong faculty by which he understands everything subtle in understanding, and not so the one who runs after easy books, for he does not have this faculty, nor does a personality emerge by which he is distinguished from others in understanding and achievement. And indeed, the book al-Maqasid was among the most intricate of knots, so much so that researchers competed in understanding it, and its commentaries multiplied to bring it closer to minds. This book was taught in al-Azhar, along with other lengthy and profound books, at a time when there were scholars whose only concern was perusing such books, and at a time when there were also students who followed the method of their teachers, learning only for knowledge, seeking thereby neither status, nor wealth, nor authority. And the teaching of these books continued in al-Azhar until the new al-Azhar law was established in 1911 CE, known as the system of Shaykh Shakir, and these books were replaced with books easily acquired by any student, such as the book al-Jawhara, and al-Kharida, and Umm al-Barahin, and al-Musayara, and others. And when the collegiate faculties of al-Azhar were established, al-Azhar returned to teaching some of these intricate books, including al-Mawaqif and al-Maqasid, especially in the old specialization system and later in the subject specialization, until there graduated from it eminent scholars of whom al-Azhar is proud. However, when preoccupations multiplied and students learned for the sake of employment, understanding these books became difficult for them, so they asked their teachers to prepare notes to make it easy for them to pass the exams, until they forgot the names of these books, and it became rare to find them for those who seek them. Therefore, the Committee for the Revival of Heritage, emanating from the Islamic Research Academy, saw the renewal of printing these books, preserving the heritage of these eminent figures who authored them, so that they might be a source of pride for them and for the generations after them. And indeed, the aforementioned committee recommended that the book al-Maqasid be the first book to be printed. However, Allah willed, and His Will is blessed, that Dr. ‘Abd al-Rahman ‘Umayra, professor at the Faculty of Usul al-Din in Asyut, should precede this recommendation by editing this book and bringing it into existence. So he undertook, may he be thanked, with high resolve, expending his effort, paper, wealth, and sleeplessness in proceeding with what he intended, despite his many preoccupations. And he kindly entrusted me with reviewing this edition, so I accepted, thanking him for his loyalty and his good trust in his teacher. And I, before reviewing this edition, thought, and some thought is not good, that Dr. ‘Abd al-Rahman ‘Umayra was more of a man of letters and poet than a mutakallim (theologian), because it is the habit of men of letters and poets to concern themselves with imagination, sensation, emotion, and feeling, and to seek rhetorical embellishments, metaphors, metonymies, and similes to give their prose and poetry impact.

Commentary
This passage is a preface that explains the importance of the science of kalam (theology), lists famous works in the field, describes the difficulty of al-Maqasid by al-Taftazani, and recounts its teaching history in al-Azhar up to the modern revival led by Dr. ‘Abd al-Rahman ‘Umayra.

The author begins by affirming the eternal and immutable nature of the Islamic creed, quoting Quran 5:3. He then states that the science of tawhid, also called 'ilm al-kalam, has a great status in establishing and defending the creed. Theologians from past and present have authored works on it, ranging from moderate to profound.

He lists the most famous profound books that deal with general matters necessary for proving the temporality of the world and the existence of the Creator: al-Matalib al-‘Aliya by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, al-‘Aqa’id al-Nasafiyya by al-Nasafi, al-Mawaqif by al-Iji, Tawali‘ al-Anwar by al-Baydawi, and al-Maqasid by al-Sa‘d al-Taftazani. He describes al-Maqasid as comprehensive, making up for deficiencies in earlier works like al-Mawaqif and al-Matalib al-‘Aliya.

Despite its difficulty due to precise style and profound investigations, the book was written in an era of mature intellects. The author argues that struggling with difficult books builds a strong intellectual faculty, unlike the ease of simple books.

He notes that al-Maqasid was among the most difficult texts, leading to many commentaries. It was taught in al-Azhar at a time when scholars and students were dedicated purely to knowledge. However, after the 1911 reform (Shaykh Shakir's system), it was replaced by easier texts like al-Jawhara and al-Kharida. Later, with the establishment of Azhar's collegiate faculties, some difficult books like al-Mawaqif and al-Maqasid were reintroduced in specialization tracks. But as students became career-oriented, they struggled and relied on notes, forgetting the books.

Finally, the author explains that the Committee for the Revival of Heritage recommended reprinting these works, starting with al-Maqasid. Dr. ‘Abd al-Rahman ‘Umayra undertook the editing before the committee's recommendation. The author admits his initial skepticism that Dr. ‘Umayra was more literary than theological, but he commends his efforts and accepts the task of reviewing the edition.

Major Works of Kalam
Author enumerates five major kalam books and praises al-Maqasid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be familiar with the major profound books of kalam listed by the author, including their authors and distinctive features.

Core Idea
These five books are the foundational references for kalam argumentation.

After establishing the importance of kalam, the author proceeds to name the most renowned profound works in the field. Each book is briefly described, with special attention to al-Maqasid by al-Taftazani, which he considers comprehensive and essential for researchers.

Commentary
This passage is a preface that explains the importance of the science of kalam (theology), lists famous works in the field, describes the difficulty of al-Maqasid by al-Taftazani, and recounts its teaching history in al-Azhar up to the modern revival led by Dr. ‘Abd al-Rahman ‘Umayra.

The author begins by affirming the eternal and immutable nature of the Islamic creed, quoting Quran 5:3. He then states that the science of tawhid, also called 'ilm al-kalam, has a great status in establishing and defending the creed. Theologians from past and present have authored works on it, ranging from moderate to profound.

He lists the most famous profound books that deal with general matters necessary for proving the temporality of the world and the existence of the Creator: al-Matalib al-‘Aliya by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, al-‘Aqa’id al-Nasafiyya by al-Nasafi, al-Mawaqif by al-Iji, Tawali‘ al-Anwar by al-Baydawi, and al-Maqasid by al-Sa‘d al-Taftazani. He describes al-Maqasid as comprehensive, making up for deficiencies in earlier works like al-Mawaqif and al-Matalib al-‘Aliya.

Despite its difficulty due to precise style and profound investigations, the book was written in an era of mature intellects. The author argues that struggling with difficult books builds a strong intellectual faculty, unlike the ease of simple books.

He notes that al-Maqasid was among the most difficult texts, leading to many commentaries. It was taught in al-Azhar at a time when scholars and students were dedicated purely to knowledge. However, after the 1911 reform (Shaykh Shakir's system), it was replaced by easier texts like al-Jawhara and al-Kharida. Later, with the establishment of Azhar's collegiate faculties, some difficult books like al-Mawaqif and al-Maqasid were reintroduced in specialization tracks. But as students became career-oriented, they struggled and relied on notes, forgetting the books.

Finally, the author explains that the Committee for the Revival of Heritage recommended reprinting these works, starting with al-Maqasid. Dr. ‘Abd al-Rahman ‘Umayra undertook the editing before the committee's recommendation. The author admits his initial skepticism that Dr. ‘Umayra was more literary than theological, but he commends his efforts and accepts the task of reviewing the edition.

Key Takeaway
The author teaches that human effort alone is insufficient; true completion of righteous works depends on divine grace and success (tawfiq).

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
When you look back at your greatest achievements—whether in learning, work, or service—do you honestly attribute the success to your own cleverness and effort, or can you point to a moment where divine grace overtook you despite your weakness? The author had the humility to confess helplessness before grace. Can you recall a time your plans failed and only Allah's intervention saved the outcome? Write down one such incident and ask yourself: Did I thank Him sincerely then?
?
The author describes a state where 'the loneliness of loss and the confusion of the frightened have taken possession of me.' Is there an area of your life right now where you feel that same confusion—where your efforts seem blocked and your hopes distant? What would it look like to pause 'the pause of the contemplative' (وقفة المتأملين) instead of rushing into more striving? Are you willing to stop and wait for a flash of divine light before taking your next step?
?
The author watched 'the extinction of sciences' in his time. In our time, we witness the neglect of sacred knowledge and the rise of ignorance. Instead of lamenting, what concrete step will you take today to preserve one piece of beneficial knowledge in your heart or share it with another? Do not let this lesson pass without a specific commitment—even one verse or one hadith learned and acted upon.
