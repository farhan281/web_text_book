The Linguistic Key to God's Promise and Threat
Al-Bayhaqi narrates a debate showing how Arabic grammar affects theology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a subtle Arabic linguistic distinction between threat and promise directly affects a major theological debate about God's justice and punishment of sinners.

Core Idea
Arabic precision prevents theological error; promise is binding, threat may be rescinded.

This lesson presents a vivid anecdote from classical scholarship where a single word confusion leads to a major doctrinal mistake. A Mu'tazili debater uses a Quranic verse to argue that God must punish sinners, but a philologist corrects him: Arabic differentiates between a promise (binding) and a threat (which can be lifted). Understanding this distinction is essential for correct theology.

Guided Path
1
Read the anecdote carefully
Read the full Arabic passage and its translation without analysis. Let the story unfold as narrated by al-Bayhaqi.
2
Identify the two debaters
Notice that Amr ibn Ubayd (Mu'tazili) represents a theological position, while Amr ibn al-Ala (philologist) represents linguistic expertise. Their backgrounds matter for the debate.
3
Grasp the linguistic distinction
Focus on the correction: al-wa'id (threat) vs al-i'wad (pledge). Understand that in Arabic, a promise is binding, a threat may be rescinded. This is the core of the anecdote.
The poetry line reinforces this: 'I break my threat but fulfill my promise.'
4
Apply the lesson to theology
Consider how this distinction resolves the problem of God punishing sinners. It shows that divine threats do not necessitate punishment if God chooses mercy, preserving both justice and mercy.

Commentary
This passage is an anecdotal illustration supporting the previous argument about the danger of neglecting Arabic. It is narrated by al-Bayhaqi. Amr ibn Ubayd (a Mu'tazili) came to Amr ibn al-Ala (a philologist) to debate the necessity of punishment for the sinner (fasiq). Amr ibn Ubayd asked: Does God break His promise? Amr ibn al-Ala replied that God does not break His promise. Then Amr ibn Ubayd cited a Quranic verse of threat (wa'id). Amr ibn al-Ala rebuked him saying he came from 'ajam (non-Arabic understanding) because he confused al-wa'id (threat) with al-i'wad (pledge or alternative form). To clarify, he recited a line of poetry: 'I am one who breaks my threat (i'wadi) and fulfills my promise (mu'idi).' The point is that in Arabic, a promise (wa'd) is binding, while a threat (wa'id) may be rescinded. The Mu'tazili argued from a faulty linguistic assumption, which led to a theological error about God's justice.

Abrogation and Ellipsis in Quranic Interpretation
Defines naskh and hadhf with Quranic examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concepts of naskh and hadhf in textual interpretation and how hadhf differs from iḍmār.

Core Idea
Hadhf is an obligatory omission with no trace, unlike iḍmār which leaves a trace.

This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid defines two key terms used in interpreting Quranic texts: naskh (abrogation) and hadhf (ellipsis). It clarifies the technical meaning of hadhf and distinguishes it from a similar concept, iḍmār, using Quranic examples to illustrate the difference.

Or the times by some of that [such] that by that part is intended from the beginning of the matter, or what indicates the end of the time of the ruling is intended, and it is called abrogating (nāsikh). And on [the issue of] not allowing forward or backward shift without the intended meaning from its apparent [sense], and in some books of the Imam, and on [the issue of] not allowing ellipsis (ḥadhf). And ellipsis is explained as being that in the speech there is an addition that must be omitted to obtain the intended meaning, like His saying, Exalted is He, 'And it is forbidden upon a town which We have destroyed that they will not return' and His saying, Exalted is He, 'No, I swear by the Day of Resurrection.' For the word 'lā' in both places is omitted, i.e., its omission is obligatory. And many people understand from it that in the speech there is something omitted that must be supplied to obtain the meaning. And they distinguish between it and allusion (iḍmār) in that what is implied (muḍmar) has a trace left in the wording, like your saying 'a forward predicate by implying qadamtu.'

Commentary
This passage defines two technical terms: naskh (abrogation) and hadhf (ellipsis), with Quranic examples. The author first explains naskh as indicating the end of a ruling's time by intending a part from the beginning. Then he moves to hadhf, which he defines as a required omission of an addition in the speech to achieve the intended meaning. He cites two Quranic verses where the letter 'lā' is understood to be omitted: 'And it is forbidden upon a town which We have destroyed that they will not return' (where 'lā' negates the return), and 'No, I swear by the Day of Resurrection' (where 'lā' indicates an implied negation). The author then clarifies that many scholars think hadhf requires supplying the omitted word for meaning, but he distinguishes it from iḍmār (allusion): in iḍmār, the omitted element leaves a trace in the wording (e.g., a predicate implied by a preceding element), while in hadhf the omission is total and obligatory.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Hadhf and iḍmār are essentially the same concept.
Correction: Hadhf is an obligatory omission with no trace in the wording, while iḍmār is an ellipsis that leaves a trace, such as a predicate implied by context.
