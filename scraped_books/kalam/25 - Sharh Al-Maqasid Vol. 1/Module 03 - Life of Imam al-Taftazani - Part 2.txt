Ibn Khaldun's Praise of al-Taftazani
Ibn Khaldun praises al-Taftazani's works and mastery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Ibn Khaldun's testimony confirms al-Taftazani's mastery across multiple Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Ibn Khaldun's praise proves al-Taftazani's mastery was widely recognized.

Ibn Khaldun, the renowned historian, is not usually the first source we turn to for theological biography. Yet his testimony about al-Taftazani carries weight because it comes from a scholar who evaluated works across disciplines. This lesson presents his direct words, showing that al-Taftazani's expertise extended beyond kalam into balaghah, usul al-fiqh, and even philosophical sciences.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage

Notice the plural 'works' indicating breadth.
2
Identify the subjects mentioned
Note the three Islamic sciences explicitly listed: kalam, usul al-fiqh, balaghah. Also note the additional comment about philosophical sciences.
These are distinct disciplines requiring separate expertise.
3
Understand 'malakah rāsikhah'
Reflect on what a 'firmly rooted faculty' means in Islamic scholarship—deep, intuitive mastery, not just surface knowledge.
This phrase elevates al-Taftazani's status.
4
Connect to broader context
Consider why a historian like Ibn Khaldun would value such multidisciplinary mastery. How might this influence al-Taftazani's legacy?
Ibn Khaldun himself was a polymath.

Commentary
This passage presents the testimony of the famous historian Ibn Khaldun. It first states that Ibn Khaldun recognized al-Taftazani's merit and examined his works in balaghah (rhetoric), kalam (theology), and usul al-fiqh (principles of jurisprudence). Ibn Khaldun described al-Taftazani as 'one of the great ones of Herat' and praised his position in rational sciences. The direct quote from Ibn Khaldun confirms that he saw in Egypt multiple works by al-Taftazani in kalam, usul al-fiqh, and balaghah, and that these works demonstrate a deeply rooted mastery (malakah rāsikhah) in these sciences. Furthermore, they show his familiarity with the philosophical sciences (al-ʿulūm al-ḥikmiyyah) and his high skill in all rational arts.

Al-Taftazani as a Universal Scholar: Testimonies from A'lam al-Akhyar and Ibn Hajar
Two sources describe al-Taftazani as an encyclopedic scholar and a master of multiple disciplines.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how two major biographers described al-Taftazani as an encyclopedic scholar who mastered multiple disciplines.

Core Idea
Al-Taftazani was praised as an unparalleled master of both religious and linguistic sciences.

In this lesson, we examine two important testimonies about al-Taftazani's vast learning. The first comes from the book 'A'lam al-Akhyar', which calls him a 'scientific encyclopedia'. The second is from the great hadith scholar Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, who emphasizes his rare combination of comprehensiveness and verification. As you read, notice how these sources highlight his ability to excel across different fields.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation

Notice the vivid language: 'scientific encyclopedia', 'circle of knowledge'.
2
Identify the two sources

Each source gives a distinct perspective on his scholarship.
3
Reflect on 'comprehensiveness and verification'
Ibn Hajar says these are rarely combined. Ask: what does this tell us about al-Taftazani's rare intellectual breadth?
This phrase is key to understanding his legacy.

لقد رأى ابن حجر في التفتازاني رجلا يختلف عن كثير من الرجال في سلامة عقله، وإصابة رأية فهو لا يكتب في علم من العلوم، ولا يقتصر على فن من الفنون ولكنه يتناول العلوم الشرعية واللغوية ويتبحر في كل فرع من فروعها، وفي كل فن من الفنون .
As for the author of the book 'A'lam al-Akhyar', he describes him as a scientific encyclopedia and a circle of knowledge in the Islamic sciences. For although he was of the Shafi'i school, he wrote in the fiqh of the Hanafis, and compared between the opinions of the people of opinion (ahl al-ra'y) and those who apply the sunna and adhere to the text. He says: 'Al-Taftazani was among the great Shafi'i scholars, yet he has magnificent works in Hanafi usul. He was among the beauties of the age; eyes have not seen his like among notables and dignitaries. He is the absolute master, the one pointed to without disagreement, famous to the horizons, mentioned in the folds of papers. His works became famous on earth and spread far and wide, such that al-Sayyid al-Sharif, in the beginnings of authorship and during composition, used to plunge into the seas of his verification and refinement and pick pearls from his precision and writing, and he acknowledged his high status, exalted worth, and lofty rank.' And if these distinguished figures, who are among the great historians, describe him with these attributes and praise him with every praiseworthy characteristic and sound opinion, then there is a giant among the hadith scholars, namely the noble scholar Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, author of many works and exquisite compilations. He describes Imam al-Taftazani in his book 'al-Durar al-Kamina', saying: 'He was among the greatest scholars of Arabic and the most excellent of their profound investigators. His numerous works indicate the greatness of his intellect, the quality of his understanding, the abundance of his knowledge, the firmness of his opinion, the uprightness of his nature, the vastness of his knowledge, the excellence of his exposition, the perfection of his virtue, and that he was a sign (allama) among scholars and a verifier (muhaqqiq) in various arts, even though comprehensiveness and verification are rarely combined in one man.' Ibn Hajar saw in al-Taftazani a man who differed from many men in soundness of mind and correctness of opinion. He does not write in one science nor confine himself to one art, but he takes up the religious and linguistic sciences and becomes deeply versed in every branch of them and in every art.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical account that presents two additional testimonies to al-Taftazani's scholarly breadth. The author first quotes from 'A'lam al-Akhyar', which describes al-Taftazani as an 'encyclopedia' and a 'circle of knowledge' in Islamic sciences. It notes that although al-Taftazani was Shafi'i in madhhab, he wrote in Hanafi fiqh and compared the opinions of the ahl al-ra'y (Iraqi school) with those who strictly follow sunna and text. The quote from 'A'lam al-Akhyar' praises him as unequalled, the absolute master (al-ustadh 'ala al-itlaq), and even mentions that al-Sayyid al-Sharif (al-Jurjani) would dive into the 'seas' of his verification and acknowledge his high rank. The passage then transitions to Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, the great hadith scholar, citing his work 'al-Durar al-Kamina'. Ibn Hajar's description emphasizes al-Taftazani's mastery of Arabic language and his status as a profound investigator (muhaqqiq). Ibn Hajar highlights that his many works demonstrate his intellect, understanding, knowledge, firm opinion, upright nature, vast knowledge, excellent exposition, and perfection of virtue. He notes that it is rare for comprehensiveness (jami'iyyah) and verification (tahqiq) to be combined in one person. The final paragraph (author's own commentary) summarizes Ibn Hajar's view: al-Taftazani was exceptional in soundness of mind and correctness of opinion; he did not restrict himself to one science but covered both religious and linguistic sciences, mastering every branch and art.

The Hanafis compare the opinions of the supporters of the school of opinion with those who apply the Sunna and adhere to the text, saying: al-Taftazani was one of the great Shafi‘i scholars, yet he has magnificent works in Hanafi principles. He was one of the beauties of the age; eyes have not seen his like among the notable and eminent. He is the absolute master, the one pointed to without dispute, famous far and wide, mentioned in the folds of pages. His works became famous on earth, extending far and wide, to the extent that al-Sayyid al-Sharif, at the beginning of composition and during authorship, would plunge into the seas of his investigation and refinement and pick pearls from his precision and writing, and acknowledge the loftiness of his station, the majesty of his rank, and the highness of his standing. Ibn Hajar saw in al-Taftazani a man different from many men in soundness of mind and accuracy of opinion: he does not write in one science, nor confine himself to one art, but rather he takes up religious and linguistic sciences and becomes deeply versed in every branch of them and every art. What Ibn Hajar said is echoed by Imam al-Shawkani, the scholar, investigator, exegete, verifier, brilliant historian, who says: al-Taftazani studied under the greatest scholars of his time, excelled in many sciences, his fame spread, students traveled to him, and he began authorship at the age of sixteen. He was unique in his sciences in the eighth century Hijri, and among his people there was no equal to him in them. He had such fortune, fame, and renown among his contemporaries that none after them can match him in it. His works flew during his lifetime to all countries, and people vied to acquire them. Then if we leave Imam al-Shawkani and turn to the author of Miftah al-Sa‘ada, we find that he counts him a wise scholar and places him in the same rank as Ibn Sina, al-Suhrawardi, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, and others, saying: Among the pillars of wisdom are Abu ‘Ali Ibn Sina, Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, and those who followed their path such as Nasir al-Din al-Tusi, and among these is Shaykh Shihab al-Din al-Suhrawardi, and those who joined their ranks include ‘Allama Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi, ‘Allama Qutb al-Din al-Razi, and our master Sa‘d al-Din al-Taftazani. Then he adds when mentioning al-Taftazani that 'he is the imam of the world, the earth shone with the light of his sciences, compositions, and writings.' And in the modern age, the Encyclopedia of Islam says about him: 'Sa‘d al-Din Mas‘ud ibn ‘Umar is a noted authority in eloquence, logic, metaphysics, kalam, jurisprudence, and other sciences, and he has many books still taught in the schools of the East.' This is Sa‘d al-Din in the eyes of scholars and in the minds of literati and historians; may God have mercy on him with abundant mercy, in proportion to the good he brought to Islam and Muslims.

Commentary
This passage collects testimonials praising the scholar Sa‘d al-Din al-Taftazani from multiple authoritative sources across different periods, demonstrating his universal acclaim. It begins by noting that even the Hanafi scholars, who follow the school of opinion (ra'y), acknowledge Taftazani's contributions to Hanafi principles despite his being a Shafi‘i. The praise then becomes general: he is described as the unmatched master of his time, such that even al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani (his scholarly counterpart) would draw from his works.
