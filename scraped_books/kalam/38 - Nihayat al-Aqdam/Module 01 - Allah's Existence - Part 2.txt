The atom is an endpoint, not a tiny body.

Before we can build arguments about the nature of the universe, we must first understand what a fundamental indivisible unit — the atom (jawhar) — truly is. The author rejects the notion that atoms are tiny bodies and instead argues they are endpoints (aṭrāf) of bodies. As for their objections, they said: If we suppose an atom between two atoms, does it contact what is on its right with the very same thing it contacts what is on its left, or with something else? If you say: with the same thing, that is sophistry; if you say: with something else, then divisibility in the indivisible atom is established. Likewise, it contacted one of them with its whole or with a part. If it contacted it with its whole and entirety, there remains no contact for the second, for how can that which is occupied with its whole and entirety become occupied with something else? And if it contacted it with a part, then it is divided. Likewise, they imagined an atom on a line connecting two atoms; it contacts the two lower atoms, and it contacts each of them with a different side. And this imagining they represented as a line composed of six parts, and a line parallel to it of six parts, with an atom on the head of one line and an atom on the tail of the other line, and we suppose the two atoms move simultaneously with equal motion—there is no doubt that one surpasses the other and they become aligned and meet at a point, and they are not aligned except at the third and fourth, so the atom moving on the line of the third and fourth becomes aligned with the second atom. Likewise, they forced us [to consider] atoms surrounded by atoms: there is no doubt that each atom touches the others with something different from what it touches the rest, so it divides into six parts. Likewise, the point on the circle touches the parts of the circle; were it not that it contains potentially what is in the parts of the circle, it would not touch them. Likewise, if we suppose an atom moving on the circumference of a circle and an atom moving on the center of the circle, with a line connecting them both: if the atom on the circumference moves with one motion and traverses one space, and the line moves with its motion, then the line at the center must move less than that distance—its smallness or largeness is due to the length or shortness of the line, which is the distance between circumference and center—this leads to a part less than one part being cut from the center; that is like saying [the atom] divides. And like the motion of the sun with the shadow: the sun moves from its sphere many feet until one foot appears in the shadow; if we suppose its motion by the amount of one part, then the shadow must move by a thousandth part of a part, so the supposed part divides.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of objections (shubah) from opponents against the doctrine of the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard). The objections are based on geometrical and physical inconsistencies. The first objection: an atom between two atoms must contact both sides; if it contacts with the same part, that is impossible, and if with different parts, it is divisible. The second: an atom touching another with its whole leaves no room for a second contact; if with a part, it divides. The third: an atom on a line touching two lower atoms must contact each with a different side, implying division. The fourth: two parallel lines of six atoms each, with atoms moving; alignment and meeting force divisibility. The fifth: an atom surrounded by atoms touches each with a different side, leading to division into six parts. The sixth: the point on a circle must potentially contain the circle's parts to touch all. The seventh: an atom moving on the circumference and one at the center connected by a line; the motion requires the center to move less than a part, implying division. The eighth: the sun and shadow analogy—the sun's motion produces a shadow motion of lesser magnitude, implying the division of the supposed indivisible part. These are meant to show the absurdity of the indivisible atom.

Consider the surrounded atom

2
Analyze the point on a circle

3
Synthesize the thrust
Both examples show that contact with multiple things forces multiplicity within the atom.
This directly challenges indivisibility.

Motion on a Circle and the Sun-Shadow Analogy
Two objections based on motion and differential distances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the final two objections: the motion of an atom on a circle connected to the center, and the analogy of the sun and shadow.

Core Idea
Motion with differential distances forces parts smaller than an atom, proving divisibility.

The last objections use motion. If an atom moves along a circumference while connected to a center atom, the center must move less than a full atom's width. Similarly, the sun's motion produces a smaller shadow movement. Both imply that the atom must be divisible into smaller units.

Guided Path
1
Visualize the circle setup
An atom on the circumference and another at the center are connected by a line. When the outer atom moves, the line moves the center atom a shorter distance.
2
Reason about the part
The distance moved by the center is less than one atom's width, so the atom at the center must move a fraction of itself, implying it has parts.
3
Consider the sun-shadow analogy
The sun moves many feet but the shadow moves only one foot. If the sun moves by one part, the shadow moves by a fraction of a part, dividing the part.

As for their objections, they said: If we suppose an atom between two atoms, does it contact what is on its right with the very same thing it contacts what is on its left, or with something else? If you say: with the same thing, that is sophistry; if you say: with something else, then divisibility in the indivisible atom is established. Likewise, it contacted one of them with its whole or with a part. If it contacted it with its whole and entirety, there remains no contact for the second, for how can that which is occupied with its whole and entirety become occupied with something else? And if it contacted it with a part, then it is divided. Likewise, they imagined an atom on a line connecting two atoms; it contacts the two lower atoms, and it contacts each of them with a different side. And this imagining they represented as a line composed of six parts, and a line parallel to it of six parts, with an atom on the head of one line and an atom on the tail of the other line, and we suppose the two atoms move simultaneously with equal motion—there is no doubt that one surpasses the other and they become aligned and meet at a point, and they are not aligned except at the third and fourth, so the atom moving on the line of the third and fourth becomes aligned with the second atom. Likewise, they forced us [to consider] atoms surrounded by atoms: there is no doubt that each atom touches the others with something different from what it touches the rest, so it divides into six parts. Likewise, the point on the circle touches the parts of the circle; were it not that it contains potentially what is in the parts of the circle, it would not touch them. Likewise, if we suppose an atom moving on the circumference of a circle and an atom moving on the center of the circle, with a line connecting them both: if the atom on the circumference moves with one motion and traverses one space, and the line moves with its motion, then the line at the center must move less than that distance—its smallness or largeness is due to the length or shortness of the line, which is the distance between circumference and center—this leads to a part less than one part being cut from the center; that is like saying [the atom] divides. And like the motion of the sun with the shadow: the sun moves from its sphere many feet until one foot appears in the shadow; if we suppose its motion by the amount of one part, then the shadow must move by a thousandth part of a part, so the supposed part divides.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of objections (shubah) from opponents against the doctrine of the indivisible atom (al-jawhar al-fard). The objections are based on geometrical and physical inconsistencies. The first objection: an atom between two atoms must contact both sides; if it contacts with the same part, that is impossible, and if with different parts, it is divisible. The second: an atom touching another with its whole leaves no room for a second contact; if with a part, it divides. The third: an atom on a line touching two lower atoms must contact each with a different side, implying division. The fourth: two parallel lines of six atoms each, with atoms moving; alignment and meeting force divisibility. The fifth: an atom surrounded by atoms touches each with a different side, leading to division into six parts. The sixth: the point on a circle must potentially contain the circle's parts to touch all. The seventh: an atom moving on the circumference and one at the center connected by a line; the motion requires the center to move less than a part, implying division. The eighth: the sun and shadow analogy—the sun's motion produces a shadow motion of lesser magnitude, implying the division of the supposed indivisible part. These are meant to show the absurdity of the indivisible atom.

The Finite Potency of Matter and the Limits of Estimation
Proving matter cannot sustain infinite division or extension, and estimation is constrained by reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why matter cannot undergo infinite division or extension and why estimation is not a reliable guide when it contradicts rational proof.

Core Idea
Matter's potency is finite; estimation does not prove infinite divisibility.

This passage from al-Ash'ari's school tackles a fundamental question: can bodies be divided infinitely? The author establishes that division occurs through matter (hayula), not form, and then examines whether matter's potency is limitless. By reasoning through two possibilities, he shows that infinite division or extension leads to impossibility, and that our imagination (wahm) must be constrained by reason. This lesson unpacks that proof and clarifies the limits of both matter and estimation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the distinction between hayula and surah
Read the opening sentence: division is accepted through matter, not form. Hayula is the receptive principle that underlies change.
Hayula is akin to prime matter, surah is the form that makes it a specific body.
2
Follow the dilemma about infinite potency
The author asks: can hayula receive an infinite series of connections? Two possible answers are considered, each leading to a conclusion.
This is a classic argument by exhaustion: either it can or it cannot.
3
Examine the first horn: infinite body
If hayula could receive infinite connection, an infinite body would result—which is impossible (muhaal). So this option is rejected.
Infinity in actual bodies is deemed rationally impossible in kalam.
4
Examine the second horn: limited potency
If hayula's potency is limited, it stops at the indivisible part (al-juz' al-fard). This is the atom of kalam.
This supports the atomic theory of bodies.
5
Address the role of estimation (wahm)
Estimation might imagine further divisions or extensions without end, but the author argues that wahm is only valid when it does not lead to an infinite body or infinite parts—contradicting reason.
Wahm is a cognitive faculty that can produce false imaginings.

ونقول قد بينا أن الجسم إنما يقبل التجزي بهيولاه لا بصورته، فإن قابل الاتصال والانفصال يجب أن يكون غير الاتصال في الانفصال؛ لأن الاتصال يزول بالانفصال ولا يزول القابل بوجود المقبول، فنقول: أفتقوى تلك الهيولي على قبول صورة الاتصال إلى ما لا يتناهى؟ فإن قويت حصل جسم لا يتناهى أو بعد لا يتناهى واتصال لا يتناهى وذلك محال، وإن لم تقو على ذلك، بل قوتها إلى حد ما إليه هو الجزء الفرد الذي لا يتجزى بقي هاهنا في هذه الصورة وهو الانفصال في جانب الوهم أنه لا يقف حتى يقدر عالما متصلا بعالم آخر إلى ما لا يتناهى، فذلك عمل الوهم المخالف لبرهان العقل، فلا نقبل ذلك بل نقول: ثم إن الوهم إنما يصدق بشرط أن لا يؤدي إلى جسم غير متناه، وذلك خلاف العقل. ونقول هاهنا: أن الوهم إنما يصدق بشرط أن لا يؤدي إلى أجزاء غير متناهية وذلك خلاف العقل، وهذا آخر ما ينتهي إليه نظر الناظر في هذه المسألة، فإن الخصم ربما يساعد على أن الأجزاء التي هي غير متناهية بالفعل في الجسم غير متصور، وإنما يخالف في القوة والوهم، وقد ثبت في القوة أن هيولي الجسم بقوته لا تقوى على قبول انفصالات بلا نهاية، كما لا تقوى على قبول اتصالات بلا نهاية، وقد بينا في الوهم أن الوهم لا يقف إلى حد لا يتوهم زيادة على الجسم المحدود كزيادة عالم آخر، وزيادة فضاء وخلاء وراء العالم، كذلك لا يقف إلى حد لا يتوهم نقصانا على الجسم إلى نقصان آخر حتى يقدر ملاقي الجسم لا يتناهى، وبعض الفلاسفة يفطن فيما وراء العالم فلم يثبت خلاء وفضاء بتناهي الأجسام، وما يفطن فيما هو داخل العالم، فأثبت املا وفيضا بلا تناهي الأجزاء، ولا خارج من المحيط، ولا داخل في المركز، والله أعلم.
And we say: We have explained that the body only accepts division through its hyhulic matter (hayula), not through its form. For if it is receptive to connection and separation, it must be other than both connection and separation, because connection ceases by separation, and the receptive subject does not cease by the existence of what it receives. So we say: Does that hayula have the potency to receive the form of connection to infinity? If it is potent, then a body infinite or an extension infinite or a connection infinite would result, and that is impossible. If it is not potent for that, but its potency reaches a limit which is the indivisible part (al-juz' al-fard) that does not divide, then it remains here in this form, which is separation in the aspect of estimation (wahm) that it does not stop until it imagines a world connected to another world to infinity. That is the work of estimation opposed to the proof of reason. So we do not accept that. Rather we say: Then estimation is only true on condition that it does not lead to an infinite body, and that is contrary to reason. And we say here: Estimation is only true on condition that it does not lead to infinite parts, and that is contrary to reason. This is the final end of the investigator's inquiry in this matter. For the opponent may agree that parts which are actually infinite in the body are inconceivable, but he disagrees concerning potency and estimation. And it has been established concerning potency that the hayula of the body by its potency is not capable of receiving separations without end, just as it is not capable of receiving connections without end. And we have shown concerning estimation that estimation does not stop at a limit beyond which it cannot imagine an increase upon the limited body, like an increase of another world, and an increase of void and space beyond the world. Likewise, it does not stop at a limit beyond which it cannot imagine a decrease upon the body to another decrease until it estimates the opposite of the body without end. And some philosophers paid attention to what is beyond the world and so did not affirm void and space due to the finitude of bodies, but they did not pay attention to what is inside the world, so they affirmed [infinite] filling and extension without end of parts, neither outside the circumference nor inside the center. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage constitutes a proof demonstrating that the body cannot be divided infinitely. The author begins by stating that the body accepts division through its hyhulic matter (hayula), not through its form (surah). The hayula is the receptive principle. He then poses a question: Can this hayula receive an infinite series of connections (i.e., be part of an infinite body) or separations (i.e., divisions)? If it could, an infinite body or infinite extension would result, which is impossible (muhaal). If it cannot, its potency is limited to the indivisible part. The author then addresses the role of estimation (wahm), which might suggest that one can always imagine further divisions or extensions. He argues that estimation is only valid when it does not lead to an infinite body or infinite parts, as that would contradict reason. He concludes that the opponent may concede that actual infinite parts are impossible, but may argue about potential or imagined infinity. The author counters that the hayula's potency is finite: it cannot support an infinite number of separations or connections. As for estimation, it does not have a limit in imagining increase or decrease, but this does not imply that such infinite extension or division is possible in reality. Some philosophers recognized that beyond the world there is no void or space because bodies are finite, but they failed to apply the same reasoning internally, thus erroneously positing infinite parts within. The author refutes this, affirming that both external and internal infinite extension are impossible.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Since we can imagine infinite divisions (e.g., cutting a body in half forever), infinite division must be physically possible.
Correction: The author argues that estimation (wahm) is not a reliable guide; it is only valid when it does not lead to an infinite body or infinite parts. Reason shows that actual infinite division is impossible because it would require an infinite body, which is impossible.
