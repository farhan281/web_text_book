The Emanation of Forms and Elements from Abstract Hyle
Author describes the first faction's view of an abstract hyle and the emanation of forms and elements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first faction's doctrine of an abstract hyle and the hierarchical process by which forms, qualities, and elements emanate from it.

Core Idea
Abstract hyle is not ordinary matter but a metaphysical principle that actualizes through three stages.

The first faction among the supporters of hyle posits a completely abstract hyle as one of the first principles. They describe a process of emanation where successive layers of form and quality transform this pure potentiality into the physical world we observe. The passage traces this hierarchy from the first form (the three dimensions) through the four elements to the composites that undergo generation and corruption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three stages of hyle
Read the passage and locate the three distinct stages of hyle: the abstract first hyle, the composite body as second hyle, and the four elements as third hyle. Note the role of forms and qualities in each transition.
The first hyle is purely potential; the second is actually existent; the third are the elemental bodies.
2
Understand the sequence of emanation
Trace the order: abstract hyle → three dimensions (first form) → composite body (second hyle) → four qualities → four elements (third hyle) → composites subject to generation and corruption. Notice that some composites become hyle for others.
This sequence is not temporal but logical or ontological.
3
Distinguish hyle from ordinary matter
Reflect on the common misunderstanding that hyle is the matter we see. The passage defines hyle as a metaphysical principle that underlies all physical reality, not as a particular substance.
Abstract hyle has only a disposition for receptivity before receiving form.
4
Review the key terms and concepts
Use the key terms list and the commentary to solidify your understanding of terms like 'ab'ad thalatha', 'kayfiyyat arba'', and 'arkan'. Then proceed to the checkpoint.

Commentary
This passage classifies the supporters of hyle (matter) into two groups. The first group affirms an abstract hyle (al-hayula al-mujarrada) that is one of the first principles. There is a dispute whether the principles are three (intellect, soul, hyle) or four (adding the Creator). They hold that this hyle initially had no forms at all, but only a pure potentiality (isti'dad li-mujarrad al-qabul). Then the first form—the three dimensions (length, width, depth)—appears, making it an actually existing body (this composite body is called the second hyle). Then the four primary qualities (heat, cold, moisture, dryness) attach to it, resulting in the four elements (fire, water, air, earth), which constitute the third hyle. Finally, from these elements composites are formed, which undergo generation and corruption, and some of these composites serve as hyle for others (i.e., as matter for further composition). The passage outlines a hierarchical emanation or composition process from pure potentiality to actual bodies.

Author describes the second faction's view that hyle is not abstracted and ordering is mental.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hyle and form are never separate in reality—only in the mind.

The second faction of the supporters of hyle offers a different perspective: they argue that hyle is never actually abstracted from form in external reality. The sequence of hyle receiving dimensions and forms is only a mental analysis. In this lesson, we will examine their reasoning and how it contrasts with the first faction's view.

Proof for the Existence of Abstract Hyle
The first faction presents a rational proof that every body requires an underlying substance beyond its form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational proof for the existence of an underlying abstract substance (hyle) in all bodies, as presented by the first faction.

Core Idea
Every body has a substrate that remains when forms change; that substrate must be non-composite.

In this passage, the 'supporters of the abstract hyle' present a logical argument that every body requires an underlying substance beyond its visible form. They reason that since bodies can change in shape, size, and composition while remaining the same subject, there must be a stable substrate. This proof is foundational for understanding the philosophical concept of prime matter.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the use of technical terms like الهيولي and البرهان.
2
Identify the key claim
The proof asserts that there is a substance (jawhar) other than the corporeal form that receives conjunction and disjunction. Find where this claim is stated.
The phrase 'here there is a substance other than the corporeal form' is central.
3
Follow the logical steps
Trace the argument: (1) Bodies are receptive of changes, (2) The receptive must be distinct from what is received, (3) Any specific form can be removed, (4) Therefore the substrate can be stripped of all forms, (5) To avoid infinite regress, the principle must be non-composite.
Pay attention to the reasoning about infinite regress (التسلسل).
4
Consider the conclusion
Understand why the principle must be non-composite (to avoid infinite regress) and what this implies about the nature of matter. Reflect on how this differs from atomism.
This proof argues for a continuous substrate, not indivisible parts.

The supporters of the abstract hyle said: As for the affirmation of hyle for everything, it is a matter rational and verified by demonstration. That is because every body is receptive of conjunction and disjunction and of taking on form and shapes. And it is known that the receptive of conjunction and disjunction is something beyond conjunction and disjunction, for the receptive remains when one of them occurs, whereas conjunction does not remain after the occurrence of disjunction and vice versa. So it is apparent that here there is a substance other than the corporeal form to which conjunction and disjunction both occur. Then they said: There is no specific conjunction or disjunction except that its removal is possible, and no shape, form, location, or direction except that it is an accident to that substance. So it is possible for it to divest itself of all forms just as it was possible for it to divest itself of every form. Commentary
This passage is a rational proof offered by the first faction (ashab al-hayula al-mujarrada) for the existence of an abstract hyle underlying all bodies. The proof proceeds as follows: (1) Every body is capable of receiving conjunction (ittisal) and disjunction (infisal), and also of assuming forms and shapes. (2) The thing that receives conjunction and disjunction must be something distinct from the conjunction and disjunction themselves, because the recipient remains the same when one or the other occurs, but the conjunction itself does not remain when disjunction replaces it. Therefore, there exists a substance (jawhar) other than the corporeal form (al-sura al-jismiyya) that undergoes these accidents. (3) Any specific conjunction, disjunction, shape, form, location, or direction can be removed without destroying the underlying substance; thus it is possible for that substance to be stripped of all forms. (4) Consequently, the principle of composite bodies must be a non-composite substance (jawhar ghayr murakkab); otherwise, if every composite were composed of another composite, there would be an infinite regress (tasalsul). This proof establishes the necessity of a prime matter that is simple and underlies all physical composition.

The Inseparable Union of Hyle and Form
Shahrastani argues that hyle never separates from form using a thought experiment with two atoms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Shahrastani's proof that prime matter (hyle) is never without form, and why the thought experiment with two atoms demonstrates their inseparability.

Core Idea
Hyle and form are always together; they are only distinct in the mind, not in reality.

In this passage, Shahrastani uses a logical thought experiment to prove a key metaphysical point: prime matter cannot exist separately from form. By examining what happens when two atoms become one, he shows that a common substrate is required, and that form is needed for actual existence. This argument also reveals a surprising connection between the Mu'tazila doctrine of the non-existent and certain philosophical views.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the literal translation. Notice the logical structure: a hypothesis, three possibilities, and a conclusion.
The passage is dense; take your time.
2
Identify the three possibilities
Shahrastani considers three scenarios when two atoms become one: both exist, one exists while the other is non-existent, or both are non-existent. Identify each in the text.
These are the core of the thought experiment.
3
Understand why each possibility fails
For each scenario, Shahrastani shows an absurdity: two existent things remain two, a non-existent cannot unite with an existent, and two non-existents cannot unite to produce a third. Ask why each fails.
This leads to the need for a common substrate (matter).
4
Grasp the conclusion about hyle and form
The thought experiment proves that a common matter (hyle) is necessary both when form is removed and when it is added. Hyle never exists without form; form gives it actuality.
Note that hyle is receptive, form is constitutive.
5
Consider the connection to Mu'tazila
Shahrastani notes that the Mu'tazila view of the non-existent as a 'thing' parallels the philosophical idea that hyle exists before form. Reflect on this comparison.
This connects theology with philosophy.

Defending the Soul as a Spiritual Substance
Author defends his view on the soul as spiritual substance against criticism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Shahrastani defends his view of the soul as a spiritual substance by appealing to the self-evidence of rational meanings.

Core Idea
Rational truths, once evident, must be accepted regardless of theological schools.

This passage captures al-Shahrastani's response to a specific criticism of his position on the soul's nature. Rather than engaging in a detailed rebuttal, he dismisses the objection as unworthy and asserts a powerful epistemological principle: when rational meanings become as evident as sensory perceptions, they command belief irrespective of one's doctrinal affiliation. This approach shifts the debate from theological consequences to the self-evidence of rational insight.

Identify the criticism

Understand the purpose of the prayer
Reflect on why a theologian would end a work of kalam with a devotional prayer. Note the prayer's focus on God's transcendence and ineffability.
The prayer is not a proof but a humble admission of human limitation.

أليس الخلق الأول في مادة الإنسان يتصرف في تربيته من سلالة إلى نطفة إلى علقة إلى مضغة إلى عظام إلى كسوة العظام لحما إلى خلق آخر فتبارك الله أحسن الخالقين أنشأ من طور إلى طور أبدا من كور إلى كورة وكل مرتبة ناسخة للصورة الأولى لابسة صورة أخرى ثم خالفت تلك الصورة إلى أخرى كذلك الأمر الأول في كسوة صورة لنوع الإنسان يتصرف بتربيتها من شريعة إلى شريعة وكل شريعة ناسخة صورة ولابسة أخرى حتى ينتهي إلى كمال الشرائع كلها وتختم بخاتم النبيين كلهم وليس بعد الكمال والاستقامة إلا المعاد والقيامة بعثت أنا والساعة كهاتين فيتم الخلق هنالك ويتم الأمر وتختم الخلقة بكمال حال النطفة إنسانا تاما وتختم الشريعة بكمال حال الشرع الأول دينا تاما كاملا اليوم أكملت لكم دينكم وأتممت عليكم نعمتي ورضيت لكم الإسلام دينا رضينا بالله ربا وبالإسلام دينا وبمحمد المصطفى صلى الله عليه وسلم نبيا وبالقرآن إماما وبالكعبة قبلة وبالمؤمنين إخوانا وقد نجز غرضنا من عشرين قاعدة في بيان نهايات أقدام أهل الكلام وإن تنفس الأجل وأمهل العمر شرعنا في عشرين أخرى في بيان نهايات أوهام الحكماء الإلهيين وعند ذلك يحق لنا أن نقول وحقا وصدقا نقول متبركين بألفاظ الصالحين وهي كلمات ملتقطة من دعوات زين العابدين رضوان الله عليه يا من لا يبلغ أدنى ما استأثرت به من جلالك وعزتك أقصى نعت الناعتين يا من قصرت عن رؤيته أبصار الناظرين وعجزت عن نعته أوهام الواصفين يا من لا تراه العيون ولا تخالطه الظنون ولا يصفه الواصفون أنت كما وصفت به نفسك وأنت كما أثنيت على نفسك ضلت فيك الصفات وتقسمت دونك النعوت وحارت في كبريائك لطائف الأوهام والعقول أنت الأول في أزليتك وعلى ذلك أنت دائم لا تزول وأنت الآخر في أبديتك وكذلك أنت قائم لا تحول وأنت الظاهر فما احتجبت عن شيء وأنت الباطن فما اختفيت في شيء
Is not the first creation in the substance of man disposed in its development from a sperm-drop to a clot to a lump to bones to clothing the bones with flesh to another creation? So blessed be Allah, the best of creators. He brought forth from stage to stage always, from sphere to sphere. And every rank abrogates the first image, putting on another image, then that image changes to another. Likewise the first command in clothing the image for the human species disposes its development from one law to another law. And every law abrogates an image and puts on another until it reaches the perfection of all laws and is sealed with the seal of all prophets. And after perfection and uprightness there is only the return and the resurrection. 'I and the Hour are like these two.' So creation is completed there, and the command is completed, and the created form is sealed with the perfect state of the sperm-drop as a complete human, and the law is sealed with the perfect state of the first law as a perfect complete religion: 'Today I have perfected for you your religion and completed My favor upon you and have approved for you Islam as a religion.' We are pleased with Allah as Lord, with Islam as religion, with Muhammad the Chosen, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, as Prophet, with the Quran as Imam, with the Kaaba as Qibla, and with the believers as brothers. And we have completed our purpose from twenty bases in clarifying the ends of the footsteps of the people of kalam. And if the term should breathe and life grant respite, we will begin another twenty in clarifying the ends of the fancies of the divine philosophers. And at that point it is right for us to say: Truly and truthfully we say, blessing ourselves with the words of the righteous, which are words gathered from the supplications of Zayn al-Abidin, may Allah be pleased with him: 'O He whose utmost description of describers does not reach the least of what You have appropriated of Your majesty and might; O He from whose vision the sights of beholders fall short and the fancies of describers are incapable of describing Him; O He whom eyes do not see, whom conjectures do not mix with, and whom describers do not describe. You are as You have described Yourself, and You are as You have praised Yourself. Attributes are lost concerning You, and descriptions are divided below You. The subtleties of fancies and intellects are bewildered in Your greatness. You are the First in Your pre-eternity, and upon that You are everlasting, not ceasing. You are the Last in Your post-eternity, and likewise You are standing, not changing. You are the Manifest, so You are not veiled from anything. You are the Hidden, so You are not hidden in anything.'

Commentary
This passage is the conclusion of the book, combining a theological reflection, a statement of the work's completion, and a supplication. The author begins by drawing an analogy between the physical development of the human being (from sperm-drop to perfect human) and the evolution of divine laws (sharia) from one stage to another, culminating in the final religion of Islam and the final Prophet. This parallels the creation and the end of time. He then states that he has completed his purpose: twenty bases (qawa'id) clarifying the 'ends of the footsteps of the people of kalam' (i.e., the ultimate positions of theological reasoning). If time permits, he will add another twenty bases on the 'ends of the fancies of the divine philosophers' (the ultimate opinions of metaphysicians). Finally, he concludes with a prayer attributed to Zayn al-Abidin (Ali ibn al-Husayn), praising God's transcendence and ineffability. This prayer serves as a fitting spiritual seal to a work of kalam, emphasizing God's unknowability except as He describes Himself.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: The prayer is a theological argument for God's attributes.
Correction: The prayer is a devotional conclusion, not an argument. It expresses submission and acknowledges God's unknowability, consistent with the author's approach.

Key Takeaway
The book of kalam ends not with a triumphant proof but with a humble prayer acknowledging that only God can describe Himself.

?

?
