Mu'tazila Scriptural Proofs on Divine Will
Enumeration of Mu'tazila scriptural proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the key Quranic verses the Mu'tazila used to argue that Allah does not will or approve of sin.

Core Idea
Mu'tazila used verses of will and approval to limit divine decree.

The Mu'tazila argued from scripture that Allah cannot will or approve of evil. They cited eight Quranic verses that mention Allah's will (irada), approval (rida), and love (mahabbah) in contexts seemingly excluding sin. This lesson presents those prooftexts and sets the stage for the Ash'ari response.

Guided Path
1
Read the opening dismissal
Note how the author dismisses opponents' excuses and asserts the sufficiency of earlier verification.
This frames the debate.
2
Identify the eight verses
Go through each verse listed, noting which attribute (will, approval, love) is mentioned.
There are verses about irada, rida, and mahabbah.
3
Reflect on Mu'tazila reasoning
Consider why the Mu'tazila thought these verses prove that Allah does not will sin.
They took the apparent meanings at face value.

وللخصوم عن هذه الأمثلة اعتذارات، ولنا عنها غنية، وبما سبق من التحقيق كفاية، ومن غرق في بحر الحقيقة لم يطمع في شط، ومن تعالى إلى ذروة الكمال لم يخف من حط. وقد تمسكت المعتزلة بكلمات من ظواهر الكتاب؛ منها قوله تعالى: {وَلَا يَرْضَى لِعِبَادِهِ الْكُفْرَ} [الزمر:7]، وقوله: {وَاللهُ يُرِيدُ أن يَتُوبَ عَلَيْكُمْ وَيُرِيدُ الَّذِينَ يَتَّبِعُونَ الشَّهَوَاتِ أَن يَمِيلُوا مَيْلاً عَظِيمًا} [النساء:27]، وقوله: {يُرِيدُ اللهُ بِكُمُ الْيُسْرَ وَلَا يُرِيدُ بِكُمُ الْعُسْرَ} [البقرة:185]، وقوله: {تُرِيدُونَ عَرَضَ الدُّنْيَا وَاللَّهُ يُرِيدُ الآخِرَةَ} [الأنفال:67]، وقوله: {وَمَا اللَّهُ يُرِيدُ ظُلْمًا لِلْعِبَادِ} [غافر:31]، وقوله: {لَا يُحِبُّ اللَّهُ الْجَهْرَ بِالسُّوءِ مِنَ الْقَوْلِ إِلَّا مَن ظُلِمَ} [النساء:148]، وقوله: {سَيَقُولُ الَّذِينَ أَشْرَكُوا} [الأنعام:148]، وقوله: {وَمَا خَلَقْتُ الْجِنَّ وَالْإِنسَ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُونِ} [الذاريات:56]، وأجابت الأشعرية بتأويلات وتخصيصات. وأحسن الأجوبة أن نقول: إرادة الله ومشيئته أو رضاه ومحبته لا تتعلق بالمعاصي قط من حيث إنها معاص، كما لا تتعلق قدرته تعالى بأفعال العباد من أكسابهم فيرتفع النزاع ويندفع التشنيع، وخرج ما قدرناه من التحقيق، وإن شئت حملت كل آية على معنى يشعر به اللفظ ويدل عليه الفحوى. أما قوله تعالى: {وَلَا يَرْضَى لِعِبَادِهِ الْكُفْرَ} أي: لا يرضى لهم دينا وشرعاً فإنه وخيم العاقبة كثير المضرة، كمن يشتري عبداً معيبًا في يقول له: لا أرضى هذا لك عبدا، ومما يتقوى به هذا المعنى أن الرضا والسخط يتقابلان تقابل التضاد، ثم
The opponents have excuses for these examples, but we have no need of them, and what has preceded of verification is sufficient. Whoever drowns in the sea of reality does not hope for a shore, and whoever ascends to the summit of perfection does not fear a fall. And the Mu'tazila have clung to phrases from the apparent meanings of the Book: among them His saying: {And He does not approve for His servants disbelief} [Az-Zumar:7]; and His saying: {And Allah wants to forgive you, and those who follow lusts want you to deviate greatly} [An-Nisa:27]; and His saying: {Allah wants ease for you and does not want hardship for you} [Al-Baqarah:185]; and His saying: {You desire the goods of this world, while Allah desires the Hereafter} [Al-Anfal:67]; and His saying: {And Allah does not want injustice for the servants} [Ghafir:31]; and His saying: {Allah does not like the public mention of evil except by one who has been wronged} [An-Nisa:148]; and His saying: {Those who associate partners will say} [Al-An'am:148]; and His saying: {And I did not create jinn and mankind except to worship Me} [Adh-Dhariyat:56]. And the Ash'ariyya answered with interpretations and specifications. The best answer is that we say: the will (إرادة) of Allah and His mashi'a (مشيئته) or His approval (رضاه) and love (محبته) do not pertain to acts of disobedience (معاصي) at all insofar as they are acts of disobedience, just as His power (قدرته) does not pertain to the acts of the servants from their acquisitions. Thus the dispute is removed and the reproach is repelled, and what we have determined from verification emerges. If you wish, you may carry every verse to a meaning indicated by the wording and suggested by the context. As for His saying: {And He does not approve for His servants disbelief}, meaning: He does not approve for them as a religion and a law, for it is evil in consequence and great in harm, like one who buys a defective slave and says to him: 'I do not approve of this as a slave for you.' And what strengthens this meaning is that approval and displeasure are opposites in a contradictory opposition. Then [incomplete].

Commentary
This passage begins by dismissing the opponents' objections to the previous examples, asserting that the earlier verification is sufficient. Then it presents the scriptural proofs used by the Mu'tazila, citing eight Quranic verses that mention 'will' (irada), 'approval' (rida), and 'love' (mahabbah) in contexts that seem to indicate that Allah does not will or approve of disbelief and sin. The Ash'ari response is that these terms do not refer to the eternal will that is identical with decree, but rather to legislative approval or religious love. The author gives a concise answer: Allah's will, mashi'a, approval, and love do not pertain to acts of disobedience as such, just as His power does not directly cause human acquisitions. This resolves the apparent conflict. The passage then begins to interpret the first verse {And He does not approve for His servants disbelief} as meaning He does not approve it as a religion (i.e., He does not legislate it or find it good), giving the analogy of a defective slave. The discussion is cut off at the point where it mentions that approval and displeasure are opposites.

The Ash'ari Response: Distinguishing Will from Approval
Ash'ari hermeneutical response to Mu'tazila proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari distinction between Allah's cosmological will and His legislative approval.

Core Idea
Allah's will is universal; His approval is only for good.

The Ash'ari response is not to deny the verses but to reinterpret them. The key is to distinguish between Allah's will (irada) as the decree that brings everything into existence, and His approval (rida) and love (mahabbah) which pertain only to what He commands and finds good. This lesson presents that answer.

Guided Path
1
Read the Ash'ari answer
Read the passage carefully, noting the key thesis: Allah's will and approval do not pertain to acts of disobedience as such.
2
Note the distinction
Understand the difference between will/mashi'a (decree) and approval/love (legislative pleasure).
This is the core of the Ash'ari hermeneutic.
3
Reflect on the analogy with power
The author compares this to divine power not directly causing human acquisitions.
This reinforces the distinction between what Allah does and what He approves.

Interpreting 'He Does Not Approve Disbelief'
Ash'ari exegesis of Quran 39:7.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Ash'ari interpretation of the verse 'And He does not approve for His servants disbelief'.

Core Idea
The verse means legislative disapproval, not final decree.

The first Mu'tazila prooftext is 'He does not approve for His servants disbelief.' The Ash'ari response interprets 'approval' here as religious and legal approval, not the eternal will. The author gives an analogy of a defective slave and notes that approval and displeasure are opposites.

Guided Path
1
Read the interpretation of the verse
Read how the author explains the meaning of 'does not approve' as 'does not approve as a religion and law'.
2
Understand the slave analogy
The analogy illustrates that something can be present but not approved of as a good instance.
The defective slave is still a slave, but not approved as a slave.
3
Reflect on the logical relation
Approval and displeasure are opposites; Allah's approval is only for good.
This reinforces the meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the view that major sinners among Muslims may be forgiven and that the Prophet's intercession is valid. The author begins by stating that there are two conflicting scenarios: either the sinner is rewarded first and then punished eternally, or the opposite. He rules out the first scenario on grounds of divine bounty and justice: God's mercy is wider than sins, faith and knowledge deserve eternal reward more than a temporary sin deserves eternal punishment, and there is no report of anyone leaving Paradise for Hell. Thus only the second scenario remains, and the Prophet's intercession is established by the hadith 'My intercession is for the people of major sins from my community.' Then he addresses objections from Quranic verses that seem to threaten eternal punishment for sinners. He argues that the general threats in those verses are specified (makhsusa) and are to be understood as applying to those who deem sins permissible (istihlal). He cites verses of hope, such as 'God forgives what is less than shirk for whom He wills' and 'God forgives all sins' (interpreted as conditional on repentance or not). He then interprets specific verses: 'Whoever disobeys God and His Messenger' (4:14) means disbelief because it is coupled with transgression of all limits, which only a disbeliever can do. A believer who has obeyed for seventy years cannot be said to transgress all limits by one sin. 'Whoever earns evil' (2:81) means shirk, because the evil encompasses him, which is impossible for a believer whose faith prevents full encompassment. 'Whoever kills a believer intentionally' (4:93) means he deems it permissible, because absolute intentionality is only from a disbeliever; the severe threat at the end of the verse is not applied absolutely to the killer who does not deem blood permissible. Finally, the mention of the blessed and the wretched refers to their final outcome, and the evidence is the exception at the end of the verse.

Refutation of the Shi'a Argument for Explicit Appointment of an Imam
Shahrastani presents and refutes the Shi'a argument for an explicitly appointed Imam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical dilemma Shahrastani uses to refute the Shi'a argument for an explicitly appointed Imam.

Core Idea
Silence on Imamah proves delegation, not neglect.

In this passage, Shahrastani engages with the Shi'a claim that the Prophet must have explicitly named a successor. He presents their argument sympathetically, then deconstructs it using a powerful dilemma. The key move is that both possible interpretations—either the Prophet appointed and the Companions ignored it, or he did not appoint—lead to unacceptable consequences, leaving the Sunni position as the only consistent one.

Guided Path
1
Read the Shi'a argument
Notice how they analogize good opinion about the Companions to good opinion about the Prophet. They argue that just as we must assume the Companions followed the text, we must assume the Prophet would not neglect the most important matter.
2
Identify the first dilemma
The passage forces the Shi'a into a choice: either the Prophet knew the need but did not appoint (bad opinion of Prophet) or he appointed but the Companions ignored it (bad opinion of Companions).
This is the central logical trap.
3
Examine the second dilemma
Shahrastani then presents two options: either the matter was left as a free-for-all (fawda) to ijtihad, or a specific text (nass) designates an Imam. Both lead to absurdities if applied consistently.
The analogy with sending prophets is used to undercut the fawda option.
4
Understand the conclusion
The only logical outcome is that the Prophet deliberately left the matter to consultation (shura) and the community's ijtihad, as demonstrated by the Companions' actions after his death.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the Shi'a argument that the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) must have explicitly appointed a specific person as Imam. The author (Shahrastani) adopts an adversary's argument and then undermines it through a dilemma. 

The passage begins by establishing an analogy: just as we must have good opinion (husn al-zann) of the Companions that they do not abandon an explicit text for personal choice, we must also have good opinion of the Prophet. If the Prophet knew the community would need a leader to unite them, enforce the Sharia, and protect from disunity, he would have appointed one. Since he gave detailed rulings on minor matters (e.g., purification), he would not neglect the most important matter (Imamah) without any text or indication. The consequence of this silence is that the nation splits into factions, each claiming truth, with no arbiter.

Then the author introduces a parallel: just as it was legitimate for people to ask God why He did not send a messenger (to remove excuse), it is legitimate for the nation to ask the Prophet why he did not appoint an Imam. 

After stating the Shi'a position, the author presents a dilemma (فلئن قلتم...): If you say the Prophet knew the need but did not appoint, then you have bad opinion of him. If you say he did appoint but the Companions did not follow, then you have bad opinion of the Companions. Thus the adversary must choose between two unpalatable options.

The adversary is then forced into a second dilemma between two possible explanations: either the matter was left as a free-for-all (fawda) to the ijtihad of the scholars, or there was a specific text (nass) designating an Imam. 

- If the matter was left to the people's reasoning (fawda), then why did God send prophets? The analogy would imply that sending prophets was unnecessary, which is absurd. Also, why did the Companions not follow this method? They busied themselves with appointing an Imam instead of focusing on the Prophet's burial. If fawda were correct, they would have delayed the appointment.
- If the matter was not left to consultation (shura) and required a text, then the text must exist. But the text is only claimed by one party (the Shi'a), and for those who do not claim it, how can they be bound by it? This leaves the Sunni position that the Prophet deliberately left the matter to the umma's ijtihad through consultation (shura) and consensus (ijma'), without a specific text, as the only coherent option.

The author's purpose is to show that the only logical conclusion is that the Prophet did not explicitly appoint a successor, and that the Companions' method of consultation was correct.

And likewise, those severe obligations upon the Children of Israel were not obligatory upon those before them. The secret in all this is that prohibition and obligation are rulings that do not revert to the actions so as to be attributes of them; nor were the actions in a state of goodness or evil that the Law came to affirm; nor did the statement of the Lawgiver acquire for them attributes that do not accept removal and placement. Rather, rulings revert to the statements of the Lawgiver, and actions are described by them in speech, not in action, legally not rationally. So it is permissible for some of them to be lifted by others. This is like the prohibition regarding unrelated women being lifted by a valid contract, and the permissibility in a married woman being lifted by a clear divorce; and like the rulings of a resident differing from the rulings of a traveler, and the rulings of men in some circumstances differing from the rulings of women of the curtains. And if rulings are subject to removal, placement, change, and alteration, then what is impossible in placing rulings upon some peoples in a time and then removing them from other peoples in another time? Then obligation is compared with actions, and rational changes are compared with death, life, health, sickness, comfort, hardship, blessing, and affliction; the growth of crops and their corruption; the creation of animals and their destruction; the creation of humans and their annihilation, sometimes for a period and sometimes instantly without delay, His disposal—they must, wherever He wills, as He wills, 'He is not questioned about what He does' [al-Anbiya 23]—with the obligatory changes of permission, prohibition, obligation, release, walking sometimes to a place and standing sometimes in a place, standing sometimes and sitting at other times, as a disposal of their intellects and souls, as He wills wherever He wills, 'He is not questioned about what He does' [al-Anbiya 23]. So glory be to Him in whose hand is the dominion of all things, and to Him you shall be returned.

Commentary
This passage is an argument defending the possibility of abrogation (naskh) in divine law. The author begins by stating that if a new ruling appears that contradicts an earlier one, then either the first ruling is lifted or its term has ended, and abrogation is thus proven. He then provides scriptural examples: marriage of sisters in Adam's time, calf-worshippers' repentance through self-killing, circumcision on the seventh day for Abraham but not earlier, the Sabbath being obligatory only from Moses onward, and the appointment of forty nights for Moses' encounter. The author asserts that these changes are not impossible; rather, they are consistent with the nature of rulings. He then explains the underlying principle: prohibitions and obligations are not inherent attributes of actions, nor do actions have intrinsic goodness or evil that the Law merely affirms. Instead, rulings are statements of the Lawgiver (ash-Shari'), and actions are described by them only in a legal, not rational, sense. Therefore, rulings can be changed and replaced. He supports this with analogies from daily life: prohibition in marriage is lifted by contract, marriage permissibility is lifted by divorce, and rulings differ for residents vs. travelers, or men vs. women in certain cases. Since rulings are changeable, there is no impossibility in God prescribing different laws for different communities at different times. The author then draws a parallel between these legal changes and the changes God brings about in the natural world (life, death, health, etc.) and in human obligations, concluding that God acts as He wills without being questioned, as per Quran 21:23. The passage ends with a doxology glorifying God's sovereignty.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: That abrogation implies change in God's knowledge or will, or that actions are inherently good or evil independent of divine command.
Correction: The passage clarifies that rulings are not intrinsic to actions; they are statements of the Lawgiver. Changing rulings does not imply change in God's essence, and actions have no inherent moral quality apart from divine command.

Key Takeaway
Abrogation is possible because rulings are divine commands that can be changed, as shown by numerous prophetic examples and the principle that rulings are not intrinsic to actions.
