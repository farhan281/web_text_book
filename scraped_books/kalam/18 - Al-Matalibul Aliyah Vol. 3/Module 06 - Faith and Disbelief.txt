Assumption and Belief as Motives
Explaining that assumption or belief takes the place of knowledge as a motive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that in Islamic theology, both certain knowledge and mere belief or conjecture can serve as motives for action, as demonstrated through introspection and example.

Core Idea
Belief moves us even when certainty is absent.

In this passage, the author argues that the mental states of conjecture (zann) and belief (i'tiqad) function as motives just like certain knowledge ('ilm). He invites us to introspect and provides a vivid example: whether we know with certainty or merely believe that a lion is on the road, we will guard against it. This establishes a key principle for understanding human action and moral responsibility.

Guided Path
1

Read the Arabic passage once, then the literal translation. Notice the author's claim that assumption replaces knowledge as a motive.
2

The author says 'if we consult ourselves, we find the matter to be so.' Why does he appeal to introspection? Consider how this method grounds the argument.

This connects to broader discussions in theology about the sufficiency of belief for accountability.

Commentary
This passage is an argument that even uncertain knowledge can serve as a motive. The author states that when a person is merely conjecturing (zann) or believing (i'tiqad), that mental state functions as a motive just like certain knowledge ('ilm). He appeals to introspection: if we look within ourselves, we find this to be the case. He gives an example: if we know with certainty that the road contains a lion, we will avoid it. Similarly, if we only believe or assume that it contains a lion, we will also avoid it. Thus, the motive does not require certainty; belief or conjecture suffices. The passage is cut off due to the page break, but the main point is clear.
