Core Idea
Motives are either from our own action or from Allah—both are proven.

This passage from al-Matalib al-Aliyah begins a crucial analysis of human motives. The author sets out a clear classification: some motives we produce through our own actions, while others come directly from Allah's creation. Commentary
This passage compares divine punishment with human punishment to clarify the difference. In the human realm, when a master punishes his slave for disobedience, it is considered good because the master acts for his own benefit—the slave's disobedience deprived him of that benefit. But this rationale cannot apply to God because God cannot benefit from His servants' actions. Therefore, the only possible explanation for God's commands and prohibitions is that they are for the slaves' own benefit. Since that is the case, it is not ugly (لا يقبح) for God to punish them for failing to do obligatory acts or for doing forbidden acts, because that punishment serves to motivate them to attain benefit. Thus, the difference between the two cases is that human punishment is for the master's benefit, while divine punishment is for the slave's benefit, and hence it is not morally wrong.

The tenth proof: The actions of servants are either compelled or accidental. On both suppositions, the claim of rational good and evil is false. Explanation of the first position: If the power (qudra) that is fit for motion is fit for rest, then when power over motion is obtained, the motion is compelled. And if it is fit for both opposites, then the preponderance of one side over the other either depends on a preponderator (murajjih) or does not depend on a preponderator. If it depends on a preponderator, then when that preponderator is obtained, that first side must occur, because when what preponderates that side exists, the preponderator of the second side does not exist; and when its preponderator does not exist, the second side cannot occur. Otherwise, it would occur without a preponderator. In this section we hold that the occurrence of preponderance without a preponderator is impossible. And if the occurrence of the second side is impossible, then the first side must occur, necessarily because exiting from two contradictories is impossible. Thus it is established that on this supposition the act is also compelled. Then we consider the division concerning how that preponderator occurs: its effecter (mu'aththir) is either God, or the servant, or an event without an effecter. If its effecter is God, then when it occurs the act is necessary, and when it does not occur the act is impossible, so the act is compelled. And if its effecter is the servant, then the first division returns, so the servant in his act needs another preponderator, and an infinite regress follows, which is impossible. And if we say that this preponderator occurred without any effecter or originator, or if we say that the power is fit for both opposites and then from the agent one side emerges rather than the other, then the occurrence of the act on both of these suppositions is accidental, because when the power's relation to both sides is equal and then one side occurs without a specifier or preponderator, it is accidental. Thus this overpowering proof establishes that the occurrence of the act from the servant is either compelled or accidental. And when this is established, we say: it follows that rational approval and disapproval do not remain, because the opponent agrees with us that each of these two suppositions contradicts the affirmation of rational good and evil.

Commentary
This passage presents the tenth proof (al-hujja al-ashira). The author aims to negate the theory of rational good and evil (al-husn wa al-qubh al-'aqliyyan) by analyzing human action. He states that human actions are either compelled (idtirari) or accidental (ittifaqi), and in either case rational moral judgment is invalid. He first explains the compelled case: if a power is only fit for one action (like motion) and not its opposite, then when that power is present the action is necessitated—it is compelled. If the power is fit for both opposites (like motion and rest), then a preponderator (murajjih) is needed to tip the balance. If a preponderator exists, its presence necessitates the action, making it compelled. If no preponderator exists, then preponderance without a cause is impossible; thus the action is impossible, and by necessity the opposite occurs—so again the act is compelled. He then considers the source of the preponderator: if God, then the act is compelled; if the servant, then an infinite regress occurs (since the servant would need another preponderator), which is impossible; if the preponderator is uncaused or if the power acts without a preponderator, then the act is accidental (purely chance). Thus all possibilities lead to either compulsion or accident. Both negate rational moral judgment because moral responsibility requires free choice with a meaningful relation to both alternatives. Therefore, rational good and evil are false.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think this argument denies all moral judgment, including moral categories based on revelation.
Correction: The argument only rejects rational good/evil (al-ḥusn wa al-qubh al-ʿaqliyyān) which are independent of divine command. It does not deny that actions can be good or bad according to Sharia.

Key Takeaway
The tenth proof shows that all human actions are either compelled or accidental, leaving no room for rational moral judgment independent of divine revelation.
