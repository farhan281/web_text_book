Proof that God Created the World for Beneficence
The author presents a logical proof by elimination that God created the world for the motive of beneficence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof based on elimination that God created the world for the motive of beneficence, not for any selfish need or without purpose.

Core Idea
God's creation is purposeful and motivated by beneficence, not by need.

This lesson presents a concise proof from classical Islamic theology that God did not create the world without purpose, nor for His own benefit, but solely for the benefit of His creatures. The argument uses a method of division (taqsīm) to eliminate impossible alternatives, leaving beneficence as the only acceptable motive.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the English translation. Pay attention to the logical flow.
Notice the structure: it poses two main alternatives and then sub-alternatives.
2
Identify the alternatives
The passage first divides possibilities: either God created the world without a motive or with a motive. Then the second alternative is divided into benefit for Himself or benefit for others.
Write down the two main branches and see how each is handled.
3
Understand the elimination logic
The 'no motive' option is rejected because it implies imperfection, which is impossible for God. The 'benefit for Himself' option is rejected because God has no needs.
Why does a purposeless act imply imperfection? Why is neediness impossible for God? Think about divine self-sufficiency.
4
Recognize the conclusion
The only remaining possibility is that the motive is to bring benefit to others, which is exactly the motive of beneficence. The author calls this a decisive proof.
The proof is decisive because it eliminates all other logical possibilities.
5
Reflect on the proof's strength
Consider why the author calls it a 'decisive proof' (dalīl qāṭiʿ). The elimination argument leaves no room for doubt if the premises are accepted.
This proof is foundational in kalām for establishing that God acts with wisdom and beneficence.

Therefore, the motive must be to bring benefit to others, which is precisely what is meant by the motive of beneficence. Third Argument: From Knowledge to a Necessary Agent
Logical argument dividing knowledge into eternal/temporal to prove a necessitator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the nature of knowledge—whether eternal or temporal—leads to the conclusion that the ultimate cause must be a necessitator (mūjib) rather than a free agent (qādir).

Core Idea
Knowledge's own nature compels the cause to be a necessitator, not a free agent.

This passage presents the third proof for the existence of a necessitating cause. By examining the two possible states of knowledge—eternal or temporal—the argument systematically eliminates alternatives to show that only a necessitator can be the origin of knowledge. As you read, follow the logical divisions carefully to see how each path leads to the same conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Don't worry about every detail yet; just get the overall flow of the argument.
Notice that the passage begins with a clear disjunction: knowledge is either eternal or temporal.
2
Understand the two main cases
The argument splits into two major branches: eternal knowledge and temporal knowledge. Each branch is analyzed to see what kind of cause it requires.
Keep track of how each branch concludes that the cause must be a necessitator.
3
Analyze the eternal knowledge case
Eternal knowledge is further divided into necessary in itself and possible in itself. Trace why possible in itself leads to a necessitator, while necessary in itself is rejected.
The key point is that if knowledge is possible in itself, it needs an effect, and that effect cannot be a free agent.
4
Analyze the temporal knowledge case
If knowledge is temporal, its cause is either a necessitator (done) or a free agent. If a free agent, its power must be either temporal (infinite regress) or eternal (necessitates effect). Both subcases lead back to necessitation.
The argument uses the principle that whatever is eternal cannot cease, so an eternal power relation forces the effect.
5
Synthesize the conclusion
Both branches of the argument converge: the cause of knowledge must be a necessitator, not a free agent. This establishes the third proof for a necessary agent.
Reflect on how this argument fits into broader theological discussions about divine attributes.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof, which uses a logical division of knowledge to demonstrate that the ultimate cause must be a necessitator (mu’jib) rather than a free agent (qādir). The argument considers two cases for knowledge: eternal or temporal. 

Case 1: Knowledge is eternal. Eternal knowledge can be necessary in itself or possible in itself. If necessary in itself, it requires no effect, eliminating both free agent and necessitator, which is not the desired conclusion. If possible in itself, it requires an effect, and that effect cannot be a free agent because that would involve creating what already exists (impossible). So the effect must be a necessitator.

Case 2: Knowledge is temporal. The effect (cause of knowledge) is either a necessitator (which proves the point) or a capable agent. If a capable agent, its power is either temporal (leading to infinite regress) or eternal. If eternal, that power is eternally related to the effect, necessitating its occurrence at a specific time. The theologians agree that whatever is eternal cannot cease to be. Therefore the eternal relation of God's power to the effect is itself eternal, and thus the existence of the effect is necessary through that eternal relation. The argument ends abruptly, but the conclusion is that the agent must be a necessitator, not a free chooser.

The Necessity of a Preponderator for Divine Power
Razi presents a dilemma and refutes an objection about divine power's attachment to creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Razi argues that divine power's attachment to creation cannot be merely permissible but must involve a preponderator (the will) to avoid infinite regress or necessitarianism.

Core Idea
Divine power alone cannot create without a preponderator—that role belongs to the will.

Razi presents a tight logical argument to demonstrate that the world's existence cannot be explained solely by God's power acting on a basis of mere permissibility (siha). He shows that without a preponderator (murajjih), the argument for a Creator collapses. This passage is a masterclass in kalam reasoning: it builds a dilemma, anticipates an objection, and refutes it by showing the objection undermines the very foundation of theistic proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without pausing, then read the translation. Get a sense of the overall flow.
Notice the structure: dilemma → objection → refutation.
2
Identify the two options for divine power's attachment
Find where Razi says the attachment is either on the basis of permissibility (siha) or necessity (wujub). Understand the consequences of each.
3
Trace the regress argument
Follow the reasoning: if permissibility, then a preponderator (will) is needed. Then the same division applies to the will, leading to either infinite regress or necessitarianism.
Infinite regress is impossible in kalam.
4
Analyze the opponent's objection and Razi's refutation
The opponent says possibility (jawaz) itself might not need a preponderator. See how Razi shows this closes the door to proving God's existence.
This is a crucial move: the objection destroys the original cosmological argument.
5
Synthesize the conclusion
Understand that for Razi, possibility either requires a determinant or it does not. Either way, the opponent's claim fails.

The fifth proof: The attachment of the power of God Almighty to the bringing into existence of the world in the future is either an attachment on the basis of permissibility (siha) or on the basis of necessity (wujub). If it is on the basis of permissibility, then the preponderance of its existence over its non-existence needs a preponderator (murajjih); let that preponderator be the will (irada). Then the division mentioned concerning the will returns, namely that the attachment of this will to the occurrence of the desired thing in the future is either on the basis of permissibility or on the basis of necessity. If it is on the basis of permissibility, it needs another preponderator, leading to infinite regress, which is impossible; or it ends at a preponderator that occurs on the basis of necessity, in which case the effect becomes necessitating (mujib). That contradicts their statement that it is on the basis of permissibility and not on the basis of necessity. If they say: Why is it not permissible to say that His power attaches to requiring the existence of the world as an attachment on the basis of possibility (jawaz) and not on the basis of necessity, and yet it does not need a preponderator? And since you allowed that, then the door of inference from the possibility and temporality of the world to its need for a Maker is closed for you. And every branch that leads to the invalidity of the root is invalid. The conclusion is that possibility either requires a determinant (muqtada) or does not require it. If it requires a determinant, then wherever it occurs, the requirement of the determinant to the determined must obtain. And if we say that possibility as such does not require anything, then it does not follow from the world being possible in existence that it needs an effect. That closes the door of inference from the temporality of the world, or from its possibility, to its need for an effect. This completes the discussion in establishing this investigation.

Commentary
This passage is the fifth proof (al-hujja al-khamisa) which argues that God's power (qudra) in relation to creating the world cannot be solely on the basis of permissibility (siha) without a preponderator. The author structures it as a dilemma: if the attachment is on the basis of permissibility, then the preponderance of existence over non-existence requires a preponderator (murajjih), which he identifies as the will (irada). Then the same division applies to the will itself: either it attaches to the creation on the basis of permissibility or necessity. If permissibility, infinite regress; if necessity, then God becomes a necessitating cause (mujib), contradicting the opponent's claim that it is by choice. The opponent objects that possibility itself might not need a preponderator. The author refutes this by showing that if possibility needs no preponderator, then the argument from the world's possibility to a Creator collapses. Thus the opponent's position leads to the invalidity of the original proof of God's existence. The argument ends by concluding that possibility either requires a determinant (muqtada) or not; in either case, the opponent's claim is false.

Fifth Proof: Necessary Causes Imply Eternal Effects and Impossibility
Sanusi presents the fifth proof that God is a free agent, not a necessary cause.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A necessary cause makes the effect eternal, leading to the absurdity of causing what already exists.

Sanusi now presents the fifth argument against the idea that God is a necessitating cause. This proof is particularly sharp: if God were a necessary cause, the world would have to be eternal, and then its continued existence would depend on God—an impossibility. Let's trace the logic step by step.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that multiple eternal beings (qudama') are impossible. It begins by establishing that preeternity (al-qidam) is a positive attribute (ṣifa thubūtiyya) because it is the negation of the negation of priority by non-existence. Then the proof considers two cases for multiple hypothetical eternal beings: either they are identical in all constituents of essence, or they differ in some. If they are completely identical, then each would be cause and effect of the others, which is absurd. If they differ, then they share in preeternity but differ in some other aspect, so each is composed of two 'restrictions' (qaydayn): one for the shared preeternity and one for the specific difference. Each of those restrictions must itself be eternal, so the same reasoning applies recursively, leading to an infinite regress of parts. The author then gives the strongest reason against infinite parts: in any multiplicity, the 'one' exists, but that one would itself be composed of two parts if it shares preeternity with another, so it is not truly one. Without a true one, multiplicity collapses. Thus the hypothesis of multiple eternal beings leads to contradiction, so it is impossible. Since God is eternal, the world cannot be eternal (i.e., it must be originated).

Core Idea
Self-knowledge is the necessary consequence of self-presence in an immaterial being.

Razi's argument for divine self-knowledge is foundational for understanding God's omniscience in Islamic theology. He builds on two premises: God is self-subsistent and abstract, and such a being must know itself. The proof for the second premise is unpacked through two clear arguments. This lesson walks through both proofs, showing how the concept of knowledge as 'presence' leads to the conclusion that God is knowing of Himself.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that the Necessary Existent (God) must be knowing of Himself (ʿālim bi-dhātihi). The author, Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī, presents two proofs. He first states the overall argument: God is self-subsistent and abstract from corporeality, and such a being must know Himself. Therefore, our self-knowledge is the presence of our very self to ourselves. By analogy, any self-subsistent being whose self is present to itself is knowing, and God is such, so God knows Himself. The argument is rigorous, grounding self-knowledge in the ontology of presence.

The Impossibility of Change in God Entails Necessary Omniscience
al-Razi proves that possibility of knowledge implies actuality via impossibility of change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why al-Razi argues that any attribute possible for God must be actual, based on the impossibility of change in the Necessary Being.

Core Idea
For God, possibility and actuality cannot differ — change is impossible.

This lesson unpacks al-Razi’s sophisticated argument that God’s knowledge of all things is not merely possible but necessary. Starting with the nature of intellection, al-Razi shows that knowledge is possible for God, then proves that every possible attribute of God must be actual because otherwise change would occur—which is impossible for the Necessary Being.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on possibility of knowledge
Read the Arabic passage from 'سائر المعقولات' to 'وجب القطع بأنه يصح على ذات الله تعالى أن يكون عالماً بالأشياء'. Notice how al-Razi defines intellection as accompaniment (muqarana) and concludes that knowledge is possible for God.
This sets the stage for the main argument.
2
Understand the principle: possibility implies actuality
Al-Razi states: 'كل ما صح على ذات الله تعالى وجب حصوله له' – whatever is possible for God must be actual. Absorb why he adds 'because otherwise change would be possible'.
The key intuition: if it were merely possible, God could acquire it later, implying change.
3
Follow the three-case classification of attributes
Al-Razi divides attributes relative to the essence: essence necessitates, essence negates, or neither. The first two prevent change; the third leads to dependence on an external cause — which makes God contingent. This proves change is impossible.
This is the logical core of the argument.
4
Conclude that God's knowledge is necessary
Since change is impossible and knowledge is possible, knowledge must be actual. Thus God is necessarily omniscient.
This completes the first principle: God knows everything other than Himself.

First Argument: Mental Accident vs External Substance

The first argument of the seventh objection challenges this by highlighting a fundamental ontological difference: the mental form is an accident dependent on a subject, while the external object is a substance subsisting by itself. First Branch: Negation of Child (Lam Yalid)
Author explains the first branch of tawhid: denial of a child with two interpretations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first branch of tawhid—the negation of God having a child—and its two interpretations.

Core Idea
God cannot have a child because having a child implies composition, and God's causation is not by generation.

He provides two interpretations: a literal physical meaning (separation of a part) and a deeper theological meaning related to necessitarian causation (al-ijab wa al-tawlid). Both interpretations emphasize that God is free from such processes.

The First Implication: The Necessary Being is One
Proof that the Necessary of Existence is one.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical proof that the Necessary of Existence must be one, and why any multiplicity would contradict absolute oneness.

Core Idea
If there were two necessary beings, composition would arise in each.

The first implication of tawhid is that the Necessary Being must be absolutely one. The argument is subtle: if there were two, they would share necessity but differ in identity, introducing multiplicity into each. This lesson unpacks that proof.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage for the first implication once. Then read the translation to grasp the argument.
Focus on the logical flow of the proof.
2
Understand the proof structure
The proof assumes two necessary beings, shows they share necessity and differ in particularization, leading to multiplicity in each. Conclude that absolute oneness is compromised.
3
Reflect on the concept of 'fard 'ala al-itlaq'
The phrase 'absolutely one' (fard 'ala al-itlaq) means no multiplicity in any sense. The proof shows that two necessary beings would each have multiplicity.
This is a key technical term in kalam.

The second: that since He is one (fard), it is impossible for Him to be a body (mutahayyiz).

Commentary
This lesson presents two logical implications (lawazim) derived from the principle of tawhid (oneness). The first implication: the Necessary of Existence must be one. The proof: if there were two necessary beings, they would share in the attribute of necessity (wujub) but differ in their individual identity (ta'ayyun). This would introduce multiplicity (kathrah) into each one, meaning each would have both shared and distinct aspects, so neither would be absolutely one (fard 'ala al-itlaq). The second implication: since He is one (fard) in the absolute sense, He cannot be a body (mutahayyiz), because bodily existence involves spatial occupation and thus potential division and multiplicity.

All creation glorifies God by their very existence, not just by speech.

This passage from al-Matalib al-Aliyah addresses a common misunderstanding about the universal tasbih mentioned in the Quran. Some argued that glorification must be verbal, but the verifiers explain that the entire creation inherently points to God's transcendence. We will explore their fourfold argument for why this essential indication is far superior.

And know that the tasbihat (glorifications) mentioned in the Qur'an are many. Commentary
This passage addresses a theological objection. Some people argued that the verse 'there is not a thing except that it glorifies with His praise' refers to verbal glorification, because if it meant the indication of creation pointing to the Creator, then everyone would understand it (since they already acknowledge God as Creator when asked). The verifiers (muhaqqiqun) respond that the indication of created things regarding God's transcendence is actually very subtle and not grasped by everyone. They further argue that this intrinsic (dhati) indication is superior to verbal indication for four reasons: (1) speech can be false, but the essential indication is always true; (2) speech is conventional and changeable, while the essential indication is inherent; (3) speech is only heard by the external ear, but the essential indication is understood by the intellect, which is nobler; (4) speech is transient, while the rational indication is lasting. Thus, the glorification of all things is not necessarily verbal but is the very fact that they point to God's perfection.
