Commentary
This passage presents the third proof. The author cites a logical principle: if one thing is conditioned by a condition and another is not conditioned by that same condition, they are different. Moreover, a thing existing now is different from a thing that does not exist now but will exist later. Applying this: Knowledge of future existence is not conditioned on the thing's present existence; in fact, it is incompatible with it (since if the thing existed now, the knowledge would be of present existence, not future). Knowledge of present existence, however, is conditioned on the thing's present existence. Therefore, the two knowledges are different. Further, knowledge of future existence is present now but ceases when the thing exists, whereas knowledge of present existence is absent now but arises when the thing exists. They are like opposites, so they cannot be identical.

The Six Restrictions of Injustice Explained
Abu al-Husayn explains each of the six restrictions in the definition of injustice with examples.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Each restriction clarifies a circumstance where harm is not unjust.

After defining injustice, Abu al-Husayn unpacks the six restrictions (قيود) individually, providing examples and reasoning for each. This lesson examines those explanations, from deserved punishment to actions that follow customary causation, revealing the depth of the Mu'tazili moral framework.

[Injustice] – Abu al-Husayn said in the book 'Al-Qadar' in the chapter on compensation: Our scholars said: Pains are good when they are characterized by conditions. Among them: that they be deserved; among them: that there be in them a benefit that outweighs the pain; among them: that there be in them repelling a greater harm; among them: that benefit and repelling harm be presumed; among them: that it be done in the manner of defense; among them: that it be done in the customary way or follow the action of another. So when pain is characterized by any of these conditions, it is good and not unjust, and when it is stripped of them, it is unjust. Therefore, we have defined injustice as: harm that is not deserved, and there is no benefit outweighing it, and no repulsion of a greater harm, and that is not presumed, and is not done in the manner of defense, and does not follow the way of another. And I say: This definition that he mentioned for injustice includes six restrictions: First: that it not be deserved; if it is deserved, it is not ugly, like killing in retaliation. The second restriction: that there not be in it a benefit that outweighs it; if that occurs, it is not injustice, as when God permits slaughtering animals for the compensations therein. The third restriction: his saying: 'and no repulsion of a greater harm' – for when the harm is such that it repels another greater harm, bearing it becomes good. The fourth restriction: that it not be presumed of it; for when this presumption occurs, slaughtering becomes good. The fifth restriction: that it not be done in the manner of defense; if it is done in the manner of defense, it is good, as when Zayd intended to kill Amr and Amr defended himself, and Zayd became killed in that defense. Abu Hashim said: Rational people deem that good when they know it is done in defense. The sixth restriction: our saying: 'and it does not follow the way of another.' This is a caution for when a person throws a child into snow; the pain occurring in it is from God's act, either initially or generated from the cold of the snow, and it is good because it follows the way of the thrower's act, because it is ugly for God to do that according to our scholars in non-temporal existence of the thing, because it is some habit. And we said: It is good not because of compensation, because the compensation due in that is incumbent on the thrower, and it is a compensation that is not sufficient, because if it were sufficient, the thrower's throwing would not be injustice, because he has attained sufficient benefit. This is the interpretation of injustice according to what Abu al-Husayn al-Basri mentioned.

Commentary
This passage defines injustice (الظلم) as per Abu al-Husayn al-Basri, citing the views of his teachers (شيوخنا). It begins by discussing when pains (الآلام) are considered good (حسن) – i.e., not unjust. The passage lists six conditions that render pain justifiable: (1) being deserved (مستحقة), (2) containing a greater benefit, (3) repelling a greater harm, (4) a reasonable presumption of benefit or harm-repulsion, (5) being done in self-defense, and (6) following customary practice or another's action. Then it extracts a definition of injustice from these: harm that lacks all six conditions. After stating the definition, the author notes that it contains six restrictions (قيود ستة) and explains each one. The first restriction excludes deserved punishment like retaliation. The second excludes cases where benefit outweighs harm, e.g., God's permission to slaughter animals for compensation. The third excludes actions that prevent greater harm. The fourth excludes cases where benefit is reasonably expected, again using slaughter as example. The fifth excludes self-defense, citing Abu Hashim's view that rational people approve defensive acts. The sixth excludes actions that follow a pattern set by a human agent, even if the immediate pain is from God, as in the example of throwing a child into snow: the pain is from God but follows the thrower's act, so it is not unjust of God. The author then clarifies that such actions are good not because of compensation owed by the thrower, but because they follow customary causation. Finally, it concludes that this is the interpretation of injustice according to Abu al-Husayn.
