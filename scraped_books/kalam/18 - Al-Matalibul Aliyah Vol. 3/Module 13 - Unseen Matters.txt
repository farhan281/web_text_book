The First Scriptural Proof: Quranic Verses Denying a Preventer of Faith
Mu'tazila present first scriptural proof against foreknowledge causing impossibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Mu'tazilite argument that Quranic rhetorical questions imply there is no preventer of faith, and thus foreknowledge does not make belief impossible.

Core Idea
Quranic rhetorical questions rebuke disbelief, implying that no divine prevention exists.

The Mu'tazila present their first scriptural proof that God's foreknowledge of disbelief does not make belief impossible. They argue that the Quran is filled with verses that ask, 'What prevented you from believing?' — rhetorical questions that would be absurd if God Himself had prevented faith. This lesson unpacks that argument and its supporting analogy.

Commentary
This passage begins a new section where al-Razi presents the opponents' (Mu'tazilite) arguments. He states there are two stations (maqams); this is the first. The Mu'tazila claim that both scriptural and rational evidence agree that God's knowledge of disbelief and His announcement of it do not make belief impossible. Then they present the first scriptural proof: the Quran is full of verses that imply there is no preventer (mani') of faith. They argue that verses like 'What prevented people from believing when guidance came to them?' are rhetorical questions that rebuke disbelief, implying that nothing actually prevents belief. They illustrate: if a man imprisons someone and asks 'What prevents you from helping me?' it is absurd—similarly, God's question implies there is no real prevention. They then list several Quranic verses: 'What harm would it do them if they believed?', God's question to Iblis, Moses' question to Aaron, and others. All these verses, they claim, show that belief is possible and that people are culpable for not believing. Finally, they quote al-Sahib ibn Abbad, a Mu'tazilite scholar, who poses more rhetorical questions: How can God command faith while He prevents it? How can He forbid disbelief while He has created them upon it? These questions are designed to highlight the apparent contradiction in the Ash'arite position. The passage cuts off at 'وخلق' (and He created), which likely continues the list of attributes.

The Fourth Scriptural Proof: Rebuke Shows No Prevention
Sanusi uses Quranic verses as evidence against determinism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses the Quranic rebuke of disbelief as a proof that humans are not prevented from choosing faith.

Core Idea
If disbelievers were prevented from faith, rebuke would be unjust—like blaming the blind for not seeing.

Sanusi continues his refutation of determinism with a fourth argument drawn from the very purpose of Quranic verses. These verses rebuke and condemn disbelief, but such rebuke only makes sense if the disbelievers had the ability to believe. This passage shows that determinism collapses divine justice.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the Quranic quotations
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Identify the two Quranic verses Sanusi cites. They express that disbelievers will not believe.
The verses are from Surah Al-Baqarah and Surah Ya-Sin.
2
Identify the argument's structure
Notice how Sanusi first states the purpose of revealing those verses: rebuke (dhamm), deterrence (zajr), and condemnation (taqbih). Then he presents the conditional: if they were prevented, they would not deserve blame–they would be excused.
This is a conditional argument with a consequence.
3
Understand the analogy
Sanusi uses an analogy of the blind and the paralyzed. Just as these individuals are naturally excused for their inability, so too would disbelievers be excused if they were truly prevented by God.
The analogy highlights the absurdity of blaming someone for an unavoidable condition.
4
Reflect on the refutation of determinism
Consider how this argument undermines the Jabriyya position: if God's knowledge and decree cause disbelief, then rebuke would be unjust. Since the Quran clearly rebukes, determinism must be false.

The Fifth Scriptural Proof: Qur'an as Proof Against Disbelievers
Sanusi argues that if knowledge prevented faith, the Qur'an becomes proof for disbelievers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses the Qur'an's role as proof against disbelievers to refute the claim that divine knowledge prevents faith.

Core Idea
Divine knowledge cannot be a preventive because that would make the Qur'an a proof for disbelievers.

This passage presents the fifth scriptural argument in Sanusi's refutation of fatalism. He argues that if God's foreknowledge were a barrier to faith, then the Qur'an itself would become ammunition for disbelievers to reject prophethood and moral obligation. Let's see how Sanusi constructs this reductio.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic and translation
Read the Arabic passage once to get the flow, then the translation. Notice the conditional structure: 'If knowledge were preventive...'
The passage uses a hypothetical speech of disbelievers.
2
Identify the key premise
The Qur'an is sent as God's proof against disbelievers, not for them. This is the starting point.
3
Understand the hypothetical
If knowledge prevented faith, disbelievers could argue: 'You knew we would disbelieve, so why command us?' Understand why this would be a strong objection.
The phrase 'أقررت بأن الله علم الكفر' implies God admits His knowledge.
4
Grasp the conclusion
Sanusi concludes that such a speech would be unanswerable by God or the Prophet. Therefore, knowledge cannot be a preventive.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

The Sixth Scriptural Proof: Divine Praise Contradicts Determinism
Sanusi presents the sixth scriptural proof using a Qur'anic verse to argue against determinism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Sanusi uses the Qur'anic praise of God as 'Excellent Protector' to argue that determinism (commanding the impossible) is incompatible with God's praiseworthy attributes.

Core Idea
If God commanded the impossible, He would deserve blame, not praise.

This lesson explores the sixth scriptural argument against determinism. Sanusi cites the Qur'anic verse 'Excellent is the Protector and Excellent is the Helper' to establish that God is deserving of praise. He then argues that if God were to obligate faith which is impossible and then punish for its abandonment, He would not be praiseworthy but blameworthy. This logical inference shows that determinism contradicts the very praise God gives Himself in the Qur'an.

3

This shows determinism leads to a contradiction with divine praise.

Apply the lesson

Determinism would make God unjust and unworthy of praise.

The Sixteen Absurd Answers: Determinism Refuted by the Qur'an
Sanusi enumerates sixteen ways the determinist's own principles answer the Qur'anic rebuke.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the verse 'How can you disbelieve?' yields sixteen rational responses on deterministic premises, exposing the incoherence of Jabriyya.

Core Idea
Determinism gives the disbeliever sixteen valid excuses, making the Qur'anic rebuke meaningless.

In this final scriptural proof, Sanusi takes the determinist position to its logical conclusion. If God truly causes everything—including disbelief—then the Qur'anic question 'How can you disbelieve?' can be answered in multiple ways. By examining the four sets of four factors, we see a powerful reductio that leaves determinism with no escape.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation. Let the structure of the argument emerge before analyzing it.
Notice the repeated phrases 'كل واحدة من هذه الأربعة موجب تام'.
2
Identify the four categories
The passage lists four groups of factors: two affirming deterministic causes for disbelief, and two denying causes for faith. Find each group and note the four items within each.
The first group: creation, will, knowledge, foretelling of disbelief. The second: creation of power+motive combination, will, knowledge, foretelling of that combination.
3
Count the sixteen ways
Each group has four items, each item is a complete cause. Since all groups apply simultaneously, there are 4+4+4+4 = 16 distinct ways to answer the verse.
Sanusi himself summarizes 'من ستة عشر وجهاً'.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
If the determinist interpretation were true, the Qur'anic rebuke would be answerable—and thus false. This is a reductio ad absurdum that refutes determinism.
The ultimate point: the verse proves human free will.

This passage presents the seventh scriptural argument (al-hujja al-sabi'a), the most detailed. It focuses on the Qur'anic rebuke 'How can you disbelieve in God while you were dead and He gave you life?' The author analyzes the Jabriyya position into four layers: God's creation of disbelief, His willing it, His knowing it, and His foretelling it. Each layer is a complete cause for disbelief. Similarly, regarding faith, the absence of these four is a complete cause. Then, by combining the affirmations and negations, the author derives sixteen ways in which a disbeliever could validly answer the rebuke. Thus, on determinist assumptions, the Qur'anic question becomes invalid. This reductio ad absurdum powerfully refutes determinism.
