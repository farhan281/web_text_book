Author enumerates five groups attacking prophethood via miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the five distinct arguments used to reject prophethood by attacking miracles, from outright denial to historical skepticism.

Core Idea
Five groups undermine prophethood by attacking miracles, each conceding more than the last.

The author presents a systematic classification of objections against prophethood, all centered on miracles. These five groups represent escalating concessions: some deny miracles outright, others question divine agency, evidentiary force, moral implications, or historical certainty. Understanding these arguments is essential for defending the proofs of prophethood.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of five groups that reject prophethood through attacking miracles. The author lists them in order of escalating concession: The first group denies the very existence of miracles, asserting that breaking customs is rationally impossible. The second group concedes the possibility but denies divine agency – they claim there is no proof that God performs these acts. The third group concedes divine agency but denies that miracles prove the claimant's truthfulness. The fourth group goes further: even if the miracle shows God's corroboration, they argue that with the doctrine of predetermination (jabr), God's act of affirming does not imply the claimant is truthful, because God can affirm a liar without evil. The fifth group concedes the logical force of miracles but denies historical certainty – we only have reports which yield opinion (ẓann), not certain knowledge (yaqīn), so the foundational matter of prophethood cannot rest on mere probability. The author concludes these five factions together attack prophethood indirectly by attacking miracles.

Classification of Deniers of Taklif and First Premise on Compulsion
Classification and beginning of argument for jabr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two groups of deniers of taklif and the first argument for the truth of jabr based on the need for a preponderator.

Core Idea
Deniers of taklif are two: those who base it on jabr and those who don't; the first group argues that choice needs a preponderator.

The author begins by classifying the opponents of prophethood into those who deny religious obligation (taklif) based on compulsion (jabr) and those who use other arguments. He then focuses on the first group, who construct a syllogism: if jabr is true, taklif is false, and if taklif is false, prophethood is false. The first premise of this syllogism is that jabr is true, and the author starts with an argument about capacity and the need for a preponderating factor.

The Mu'tazila argued that obligation (taklif) is good because it allows the servant to earn honor from God, as gratuitous honor would be ugly. However, the author counters that this reasoning is weak from several angles. In this lesson, we examine their claim and the author's statement.
