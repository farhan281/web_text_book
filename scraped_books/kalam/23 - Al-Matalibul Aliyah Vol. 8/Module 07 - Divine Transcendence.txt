Second and Third Arguments: God's Perfection Precludes Need for Worship
Two rational arguments proving God's independence from worship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand two rational arguments demonstrating that God cannot be in need of human worship because need implies imperfection and weakness, which is impossible for God.

Core Idea
Need implies imperfection; God's absolute power and self-sufficiency make worship unnecessary for Him.

This passage presents two concise but powerful arguments that build on the previous discussion. The second argument links neediness to a lack of perfect power, which would undermine trust in God's promises. The third argument turns the tables: if God benefited from our weak acts of worship, He would be weak Himself—an impossibility. Together, they establish God's utter independence from creation.

Fourth Argument: God's Power Refutes Obligation for Benefits
Fourth argument using God's power to create in paradise to deny benefit purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the fourth argument uses God's power to create everyone in paradise to refute the claim that divine obligation is for benefits.

Core Idea
If God wanted good for us, He would have placed us directly in paradise.

The fourth argument presents a logical challenge to the claim that obligation is for benefits. It begins with an indisputable premise: God is all-powerful and could have created all people directly in paradise. From there, it deduces that if God intended good for them, He would have done so. But since He did not—instead subjecting them to desires, doubts, and devils—the argument concludes that obligation cannot be for the sake of exposing them to benefits. This forces a deeper understanding of God's wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage and translation
Carefully read the Arabic passage and its literal translation. Notice the logical connectors: 'فلو', 'ولما لم', 'علمنا'.
The passage is a single line of reasoning.
2
Identify the key premise
What is the starting point? God's ability to create everyone in paradise, give them goods, and protect them from harm. This premise is not denied.
The argument does not question God's power.
3
Understand the conditional reasoning
The argument uses a counterfactual conditional: if God intended good, He would have created them thus. Since He did not, His intention was not good (in the sense of giving direct benefit).
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Draw the conclusion
The final conclusion: it is impossible that obligation is for the sake of benefits. Why? Because the means (obligation with tests) contradicts the end (direct benefit).
The argument targets the 'exposure to interests' (ta'rid li'l-masalih) view.
5
Reflect on the significance
This argument does not deny God's wisdom or mercy; it merely shows that the purpose of obligation is not simply to give benefits. Think about alternative purposes (e.g., worship, testing).
