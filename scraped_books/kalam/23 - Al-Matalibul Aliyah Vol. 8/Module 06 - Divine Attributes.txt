The Eleventh Proof: God's Will Cannot Contradict His Knowledge
Logical proof that God's will aligns with His knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's will must be consistent with His foreknowledge, and how this proof supports the doctrine that God does not will faith from those He knows will disbelieve.

Core Idea
God's will and knowledge are perfectly consistent; willing a contradiction is impossible.

This proof, the eleventh in a series, addresses a key question about divine will and foreknowledge. If God knows that a person will not believe, can He still will faith from that person? The argument shows that such a will would be contradictory and therefore impossible. Let us examine the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof in Arabic and English
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation to get a sense of the logical flow.
Notice the conditional structure: 'If... then...'
2
Identify the conditional premise
The proof sets up a conditional: if God wills faith from a servant while knowing he will not believe, then something follows. Identify both parts of the condition.
3
Understand the impossible consequence
The consequence is that God would have willed a combination of opposites (belief and disbelief) from the same person. Why is this impossible? Reflect on the nature of contradiction.
A contradiction cannot exist in reality.
4
Grasp the conclusion
The proof concludes that the original statement (God wills faith from one He knows will disbelieve) is impossible. This aligns with the principle that divine will must be consistent with divine knowledge.
5
Reflect on the theological implication
Consider how this proof supports determinism (qadar) while affirming the coherence of God's attributes. Does it limit God's will? Why or why not?
See the misconception section for a common error.

Commentary
This proof argues that God's will and His foreknowledge cannot conflict. If God wills faith, but knows the servant will not believe, then the will would be asking for something that cannot happen (since knowledge is infallible), which is a contradiction. Therefore, God's will must be aligned with His knowledge, so He does not will faith from those He knows will disbelieve. This supports determinism.

The Thirteenth Proof: Determinism from Divine Knowledge
Proof that God's knowledge of particulars necessitates determinism, with alternative formulation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the thirteenth proof for determinism (jabr) based on God's exhaustive knowledge of particulars, including its main argument and alternative disjunctive formulation.

Core Idea
If God knows everything, then nothing can happen other than what He knows.

This lesson presents a powerful argument that ties divine omniscience directly to determinism. The proof proceeds in two stages: first, a straightforward deduction from God's knowledge to the necessity of events; second, a disjunctive argument that forces the opponent to accept either determinism or a worse consequence for taklif and prophethood. Pay close attention to the logic and the implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the main proof carefully
Focus on the first part: God knows all particulars; what He knows to occur must occur; what He knows not to occur cannot occur.
Notice the key terms: واجب الوقوع (necessary) and ممتنع الوقوع (impossible).
2
Understand the logical basis
The proof relies on the impossibility of combining opposites: if God knows X will happen, then X not happening would contradict His knowledge.
3
Examine the alternative formulation
The second part presents a dilemma: either God knows particulars (leading to determinism) or He does not (undermining taklif and prophethood).
Consider why not knowing would be even worse for the opponent.
4
Reflect on the consequences
The proof concludes that determinism is necessary, and rejecting it leads to more serious problems in Islamic theology.
This is a typical kalam argument showing the opponent must accept determinism or face a greater difficulty.

If not, then the basis for taklif and prophethood (which require God's knowledge) is undermined. This is a strong argument for jabr.

The Fourteenth Proof: Divine News Necessitates Disbelief
Proof that divine information necessitates the occurrence of reported disbelief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that God's truthful report about future disbelief necessitates its occurrence, with implications for determinism.

Core Idea
God's truthful news cannot be false, so what He reports must happen.

This lesson examines a key argument from the Qadariyya debates: if God has informed us that certain people will disbelieve, then their disbelief must actually occur. Otherwise, God's report would be false, which is impossible. The argument supports a deterministic view where human actions are necessitated by divine foreknowledge and report. Read the original Arabic passage and see how this concise proof unfolds.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic source and its literal translation. Notice the logical flow: premise (God informed), condition (if not occur), consequence (news false), impossibility, and conclusion (occurrence necessary).
2
Identify the key concepts
The argument uses four key terms: خبر (news), كذب (falsehood), محال (impossible), جبر (determinism). Understand how each functions in the proof.
The term محال (impossible) is central: both the falsehood of God's news and anything leading to it are impossible.
3
Analyze the logical structure
The argument is a conditional syllogism: If disbelief does not occur, then God's news would be false (which is impossible). Therefore, disbelief must occur. Trace the chain: premise → conditional → impossibility → necessity.
4
Reflect on the theological implication
Consider why the argument concludes that determinism (الجبر) is necessary. How does God's foreknowledge and truthful report relate to human free will? This is a classic point of debate.
The common misunderstanding warns: this argument does not claim God causes disbelief, but that His foreknowledge guarantees it.

Commentary
This passage is a proof that God is powerful over all possible things. So He said: 'And with Him are the keys of the unseen; none knows them except Him' (1) and He said: 'Allah knows what every female carries and what the wombs decrease and increase' (2). Then He did not explain whether He is knowing by His essence or by knowledge. Also He made clear that the servant is an agent, intelligent, maker, creator, and originator in many verses. Commentary
This passage discusses the method of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) in calling people to God. It begins by noting that the Quran affirms God's comprehensive knowledge of all things, citing verses that God possesses the keys of the unseen and knows every pregnancy. The author points out that the Quran does not specify whether God's knowledge is essential or acquired through a separate attribute. Similarly, the Quran affirms that human beings are agents and creators of their actions in many verses, yet it also affirms that all good and evil are from God, without explaining how these two statements are reconciled. The author further notes that the Quran affirms that nothing escapes God's will and decree, yet it also states that God does not will injustice or falsehood. The conclusion is that the Prophet's method is to magnify God from all rational perspectives and to forbid delving into whether these perspectives are contradictory. The author illustrates with two Quranic verses: 'Say: Everything is from Allah' (4:78) and 'Whatever good befalls you is from Allah, and whatever evil befalls you is from yourself' (4:79). The first magnifies God's power, the second His wisdom. The laity are required to have absolute faith in both aspects without seeking to resolve the apparent tension. The author affirms that this approach is the only correct one for a general call.

The Necessity of Higher Spiritual Beings as Causes of Events
Explanation of the two possibilities for causal order and proof of spiritual beings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the chain of causes and effects must be sequential and why the existence of higher spiritual beings (other than God) is necessary to explain changing perceptions and events in the world.

Core Idea
Changing perceptions in the world prove the existence of higher spirits besides the unchanging God.

This passage from al-Matalib al-Aliyah presents a profound argument about the structure of causality. The author first establishes that the causal chain cannot be instantaneous but must be sequential. He then argues that the changing events in this world cannot be directly caused by the immutable God; instead, they are mediated by higher spiritual beings with changing perceptions. Understanding this argument is crucial for grasping the Islamic philosophical perspective on the role of spiritual intermediaries.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Pay attention to the two main parts: the preliminary discussion on causal order and the proof of spiritual beings.
The author uses a very logical and compact style.
2
Understand the two possibilities for causal order
The author presents two possibilities: all causes and effects occurring at once, or each being preceded by another without a first. Identify why the first is rejected and the second is accepted as truth.
The rejection of simultaneous causation relies on a prior proof of the Necessary Existence.
3
Analyze the division of events
After accepting sequential causation, the author divides events into two types. Focus on the first type: the existence of a spiritual being with changing perceptions that cause events.
The second type is not elaborated in this passage.
4
Grasp the attributes of the spiritual being
Notice the necessary attributes: the spiritual being must be eternal and something other than God. Understand why God cannot be the source of changing perceptions due to His absolute necessity.
This is the key argument distinguishing the spiritual being from God.
5
Understand the conclusion
The author concludes that the existence of higher spirits (arwah 'aliya) is inevitable and that extraordinary events require their aid. Reflect on how this connects to the overall argument.
This conclusion has practical implications for the pursuit of extraordinary acts.

Commentary
This passage is a combined statement of a premise and a proof. It begins by establishing the truth that the chain of causes and effects must be sequential without a first cause (i.e., an infinite regress is accepted as the correct view). Then it divides the events into two types. The first type is the existence of a spiritual being that is constantly moving from one concept to another, and these changing perceptions are the causes of events in the world. The author argues that this spiritual being must be eternal and other than God, because God is absolutely necessary and unchanging. Therefore, the existence of such higher spirits is necessary, and all extraordinary events occur only with their assistance.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: One might think that this argument posits multiple eternal beings (polytheism), but the author specifically distinguishes them from God and affirms only one Necessary Being.
Correction: The spiritual being is eternal but not necessary in itself; it is created and dependent on God. The argument does not violate tawhid.
