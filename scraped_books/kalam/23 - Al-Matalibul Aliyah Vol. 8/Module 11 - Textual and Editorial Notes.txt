The Meaning of 'Abad' in the Torah: A Limited Duration
Explanation of the limited meaning of 'abad' in the Torah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the term 'abad' in the Torah refers to a long but finite duration, not endless eternity, based on scriptural examples and the Jubilee law.

Core Idea
In the Torah, 'forever' does not mean endless; it means until the next Jubilee.

This lesson examines a key term from the Torah, 'abad' (eternity). The author uses scriptural examples to show that 'abad' is always limited, especially by the Jubilee year. This understanding is crucial for discussions about the abrogation of previous laws.

3

The author explains that the slave's 'forever' is limited to the Year of Jubilee (every 50 years), proving that 'abad' has a finite duration.

Reflect on the conclusion
The passage concludes that when the Torah law is called 'forever', it means until the coming of the awaited prophet from Deuteronomy 18.
This implies the law is temporary.

The copyist preserved two versions of the zodiac division for scholarly benefit.

The alternate version presents a detailed division of the zodiac into twelve parts based on seasons, astronomical points, and philosophical qualities like heat and dryness.

Commentary
This passage is a copyist's note (mulahiza) inserted into the manuscript. The copyist, indicated by sigla '(la lahu li)' and '(la)', explains that he found the 'third section on knowing the nature of the zodiac' in his source manuscript. After copying it, he discovered another manuscript where the same third section differed. He therefore included both versions for benefit. The text then presents the alternative version. It begins by discussing the division of the zodiac into twelve sections, giving the reason that the year is divided into four seasons due to the sun's position, and each season has a beginning, middle, end, thus twelve parts. The division is considered in two ways: first, regarding the signs (buruj). Due to the intersection of the great sphere's band (mintaqat al-falak al-a'zam) and the zodiac band (mintaqat al-buruj), two intersection points (Aries and Libra) and two points of divergence (Cancer and Capricorn) arise. Equinoxes (i'tidal) are nobler than solstices (ifrat), so the intersection points are hotter and nobler. Dryness (yubusa) is better than moisture (rutuba) because dryness prevents passivity (infi'al) while moisture allows it. The passage then relates seasonal transitions: when the sun enters Aries, spring brings life and joy; when it enters Libra, autumn brings transition from perfection to deficiency. Thus Aries is hot and dry, Libra hot and moist. The note ends mid-sentence with 'Capricorn is nobler than Cancer because...' indicating the original text likely continued.

The Colophon and Correct Order of Volumes in al-Matalib al-Aliyah
Scribal colophon with correction of volume order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the role of colophons in determining the intended order of a multi-volume work and learn the correct sequence of al-Matalib al-Aliyah's volumes.

Core Idea
Colophons reveal the author's intended order, not the copyist's assumption.

The passage we are about to read is a scribal note at the end of a manuscript of al-Matalib al-Aliyah. It tells us who copied it and when, and corrects a common misunderstanding about which volume was meant to be last. This lesson will help you see how such notes are invaluable for reconstructing the intended structure of classical works.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyze it. Then read the translation. Notice the colophon's formal opening and the details provided.
Pay attention to the dates and names.
2
Identify the copyist and date
The passage names the copyist (Abu al-Fath Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr al-Dabiwirdi) and gives two dates: 18 Safar 640 AH and 5 Shawwal 640 AH. These are important for manuscript dating.
3
Understand the correction about volume order
The scribe states that the copyist believed 'Prophethoods' (an-Nubuwat) was the last volume, but the truth is that 'Predestination' (al-Jabr wa al-Qadar) is last. The author died before completing it.
This correction is crucial for understanding the intended scope of the work.
