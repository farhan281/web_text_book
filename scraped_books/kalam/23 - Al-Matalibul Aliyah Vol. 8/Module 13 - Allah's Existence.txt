Refutation of Multiple Independent Creators
Sanusi presents a disjunctive argument against multiple creators.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical disjunctive argument used to refute the possibility of multiple independent creators, and why two independent agents cannot both cause the same effect.

Core Idea
No effect can be caused by two completely independent agents without contradiction.

This passage presents a rigorous logical argument against the possibility of any effective agent other than Allah. The author uses a disjunctive argument that exhausts the possibilities for how an effect could be produced by two supposed independent agents. By showing that the first two possibilities are impossible, the argument clears the way for the conclusion that only one agent can truly bring things into existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the argument's structure
Read the Arabic and translation to understand the overall flow: conclusion, supposition, division into three cases.
Notice the concise logical style.
2
Examine the refutation of joint causation
Focus on the first division: why two agents cannot cause the same effect together. Note the reasoning about necessity and dependence.
The key concept is 'necessary of existence in itself'.
3
Examine the refutation of no causation
See the second division: why neither agent causing the effect leads to absurdity (no effects or uncaused effects).
The first wajh is given; the second is missing but the conclusion is clear.
4
Consider the missing third division
The author only refutes two; think about why the third (by one alone) leads to a single creator.
This division is implicitly accepted.

So we say: There does not exist in existence any effective agent (mu'aththir) nor any bringer-into-existence (mujid) except God Almighty. Commentary
This passage is a proof that no effective agent (mu'aththir) exists except God, i.e., the refutation of shirk in creating. The author first states the conclusion: there is no creator besides God. Then he provides a proof via a disjunctive argument. Suppose there were another being with the capacity for influence and bringing into existence. Then for a given effect, two independent agents (mu'aththiran mustaqillan) would be present, each requiring the effect. The author then lists three possibilities for how the effect occurs: (1) by both together, (2) by neither, (3) by one alone. He refutes the first two. The first possibility is impossible because if an effect is with a complete independent agent, it becomes necessary by that agent. If it is necessary by both, it would be necessary by each, but a necessary thing cannot be necessary by another. Thus the effect would have to be dependent on each yet independent of each, a contradiction. The second possibility is that neither agent brings about the effect. This would mean the effect either does not occur at all (which is false because there are observable effects) or occurs without any agent (which is impossible as it violates causality). Both scenarios are absurd. The third possibility (the effect by one of them) is not refuted explicitly here, but the implication is that only one agent can be truly effective, and that must be God. The text as given only refutes the first two; the full argument likely continues to show that the third leads to the exclusive existence of one creator.

Commentary
This passage is a proof for the exclusive creative power of God. The author first states the conclusion: only God is a true effective agent and bringer into existence. Then he presents a detailed argument by assuming a hypothetical second creator and analyzing three exhaustive possibilities for how an effect could relate to two independent agents. The first possibility is that the effect is produced by both agents together. This is shown impossible because a specific effect with a complete independent cause is necessary in itself, and therefore cannot be dependent on another. If it were dependent on both, it would be simultaneously dependent and independent, a contradiction. The second possibility is that the effect is produced by neither. This is refuted in two ways: first, if neither produces it, either it never occurs or it occurs without any cause, both absurd; second, a more subtle argument: each cause alone would be sufficient, but the presence of the other prevents it. The prevention is not due to the essence of the other but due to the fact that if one caused it, that would prevent the other. Then if both are prevented, they must both cause it to be preventers, leading to the same contradiction as the first case. The third possibility is that one causes it alone. This is also refuted in two ways: first, reason dictates that the weaker is overcome by the stronger, and the contingent is weaker than the Necessary Being, so God alone is the cause; second, a thing coming into existence is either simple or composite, and a simple thing does not admit of degrees, so one agent cannot be stronger than another regarding it. Thus all alternatives are impossible, establishing that only God is the creator.

Refuting Shared Causality: First Two Possibilities
The author refutes the first two of the three possibilities for shared causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why an effect cannot be produced by two independent agents together or by neither of them.

Core Idea
Two creators cannot both cause one effect, and no effect can exist without a cause.

Having outlined the three possibilities, the author now refutes the first two: that the effect is caused by both agents, or by neither. These refutations rely on logical contradictions and the principle that every effect needs a cause.

Guided Path
1
Read the refutation of the first possibility
The author argues that if two agents together cause an effect, the effect would be simultaneously dependent on both and independent of each, which is impossible.
This uses the concept of necessity and contingency.
2
Understand the two arguments against the second possibility
First, if neither causes the effect, then either it doesn't exist or it exists without a cause — both absurd. Second, if each alone is sufficient but the other prevents it, we get a contradiction.
The second argument is subtle; follow the chain of prevention carefully.
3
Notice the pattern
Both refutations show that assuming two independent creators leads to logical impossibilities. This supports the claim of monotheism.

Commentary
This passage classifies the prophet's religious instruction into three temporal dimensions: past, present, and future. It then elaborates on the past: teaching that the universe is created, has an eternal God, who is unlike contingent beings, possesses attributes of power and knowledge, is absolutely one without parts, and is unique without partners. Additionally, all events occur by God's decree, and He is free from injustice and futility. The passage ends with a poetic citation that reinforces God's transcendence and perfection.

Proof of Muhammad's Superiority through the Spread of Tawhid
Comparative argument for Muhammad's superiority via da'wa effectiveness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the comparative impact of prophetic calls demonstrates Muhammad's superiority over all prophets.

Core Idea
The success of Muhammad's call is the clearest proof of his superiority.

This lesson examines a unique argument that proves Prophet Muhammad's superiority not through miracles but through the observable effect of his da'wa. By contrasting the hidden call of an earlier prophet with the global spread of Islam, the author establishes a rational proof based on the very purpose of prophethood: guiding humanity to tawhid.

Commentary
This passage is a comparative argument for the superiority of Prophet Muhammad over all other prophets. It begins by contrasting the unsuccessful call of an earlier prophet (possibly Jesus or another) whose message did not spread, with the universally impactful call of Muhammad. The author states that the earlier prophet's call never appeared, whereas Muhammad's call reached most of the inhabited world. He then describes the state of humanity before Muhammad: idolators, Jews, Magians, Christians, and Sabians were all engaged in false beliefs and practices. When Allah sent Muhammad, these false religions were abolished and tawhid spread everywhere. The author concludes that the effect of Muhammad's call in healing diseased hearts and darkened souls is more complete, therefore Muhammad is superior. This is presented as a clear proof based on investigation of the reality of prophethood.
