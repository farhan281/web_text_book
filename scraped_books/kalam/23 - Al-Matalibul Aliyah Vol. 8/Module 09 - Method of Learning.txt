Three Differences Between King and God, and the Weakness of Analogy
Author lists three differences and dismisses analogy method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three differences between a king and God that undermine analogical reasoning, and why the author ultimately dismisses the need for them based on the weakness of qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid.

Core Idea
Analogy from the witnessed to the unseen fails because God is fundamentally different, and the method itself is weak.

In this passage, the author tackles a common theological argument that uses analogy from human kings to God. He concedes the premise but then presents three key differences that break the analogy. However, he goes further, arguing that even these differences are unnecessary because the entire method of analogical reasoning in rational matters is unreliable. This lesson walks through his critique.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice the author's structure: he concedes the premise, then lists differences, then dismisses the need.
2
Identify the three differences
The passage presents three distinct ways in which a king differs from God. Find each one and note how each difference undermines the analogy.
The first difference concerns the king's care for his kingdom; the second, self-interest; the third, multiple observations.
3
Understand the dismissal
After listing the differences, the author argues they are unnecessary because the method of analogy itself is weak. Notice how he references logic to support this.
This is a meta-critique: not only does the analogy fail on its own terms, but the method is fundamentally flawed.
4
Reflect on the overall argument
Consider why the author would present differences only to dismiss them. What does this reveal about his approach to theological reasoning?
He shows that even if the opponent's method were accepted, the analogy fails; but he also rejects the method entirely.

Commentary
This passage presents the second assumption of the opponent (that the action in the witnessed world indicates truthfulness) and then provides three differences between the king and God to show that the analogy fails. First difference: our judgment about the king depends on our knowledge of his care for his kingdom; if the king were careless or his wisdom inscrutable (like God's), we would not judge. Second difference: if the king supported a liar, he would harm himself, but God cannot be harmed. Third difference: we have multiple observations of the king giving cumulative certainty, but with God only one act is seen. However, the author then argues that these differences are actually unnecessary because the method of analogical reasoning from the witnessed to the unseen (qiyās al-ghā'ib 'alā al-shāhid) is itself weak, as proven in logic. Therefore, the entire argument is moot. This passage guards against over-reliance on analogy in theological matters.

Prophethood Depends on Belief in a Free Agent
Al-Razi states the first principle and rejects other effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the assertion of prophethood is built upon the belief in a free agent (الفاعل المختار), and that only God brings things into existence.

Core Idea
Prophethood is a branch of tawheed: no prophecy without a sovereign Creator.

Al-Razi begins by stating that three principles, when firmly grasped, dispel all doubts about prophethood. The first principle is that belief in prophethood is a branch of belief in a free agent. He then asserts that no effect other than God brings anything from non-existence into existence, rejecting all alternative agents such as planets, spheres, or spirits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three principles
Read the opening to see that al-Razi mentions three principles that eliminate doubts. Though only the first is discussed here, note their importance.
The other two principles are covered later in the work.
2
Understand the logical dependence
Notice the claim: establishing prophethood is a branch (فرع) of establishing the free agent. This means one must first affirm that God is a free agent before discussing prophethood.
3
Analyze the rejection of other agents
Al-Razi categorically denies that any other being—planet, sphere, intellect, soul, or spirit—has any effect in bringing things into existence. Only the One God does.
This directly challenges astrological and philosophical causal claims.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of an introduction and a proof. It begins with the author stating that there are three principles which, when firmly understood, dispel all doubts about prophethood. He then presents the first principle: that belief in prophethood is built upon belief in a free agent (الفاعل المختار). Therefore, anyone who denies the free agent should not discuss prophethood until that issue is settled. For those who accept a free agent, the author asserts that no effect (مؤثر) brings anything from non-existence into existence except the One God. This nullifies any other supposed agents like planets, spheres, intellects, etc. He then provides a proof for this principle. The proof is in multiple steps: (1) God is powerful over some things, and those things are qualified by possibility (الإمكان). If possibility is removed, only necessity or impossibility remain, both of which preclude being within power. (2) The concept of possibility is a single, univocal concept across all possible things. (3) Since the ground for being within power (i.e., possibility) is the same for all possibles, they are all equally valid as objects of power. (4) God's power is a relation between His essence and possible things. This relation is itself possible and requires an effect, which is God's essence. (5) If God's essence were to necessitate power over some possibles but not others, this would be an unjustified preference which is impossible. Therefore, the only valid conclusion is that God is powerful over all possibles. The argument uses the principle of no preference without a preponderator (ترجيح بلا مرجح).

The Proof: God is Powerful Over All Possibles
Al-Razi proves God's power over all possibles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand al-Razi's argument that God is powerful over all possible things, using the unity of possibility and the principle of no preference without a preponderator.

Core Idea
All possibles are equally valid objects of God's power because they share the same ground: possibility.

After stating the first principle, al-Razi provides a multi-step proof that God's power extends to all possible things. He argues that since God is powerful over some things due to their possibility, and possibility is a single concept, all possibles are equally valid objects of power. Any limitation would require an unjustified preference, which is impossible. Thus, God must be powerful over all.

Guided Path
1
Understand the role of possibility (الإمكان)
The argument begins by establishing that the ground for something being within God's power is its possibility. If possibility is removed, only necessity or impossibility remain, both preclude being within power.
Possibility here means logical possibility: not necessary and not impossible.
2
Recognize the unity of possibility
Al-Razi asserts that the concept of possibility is a single, univocal concept applicable to all possible things. This means all possibles share the same attribute.
3
Trace the equality argument
Since the ground (possibility) is the same for all possibles, they are all equally valid as objects of God's power. God's power is a relation that does not cause unjustified preference.
Any preference would violate the principle of no preference without a preponderator.
4
Consider the elimination of alternatives
After ruling out selective power due to impossibility of unjustified preference, only two options remain: God is powerless over all (false, since prophethood assumes power) or powerful over all (true).
The argument uses a disjunctive syllogism to reach the conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of an introduction and a proof. It begins with the author stating that there are three principles which, when firmly understood, dispel all doubts about prophethood. He then presents the first principle: that belief in prophethood is built upon belief in a free agent (الفاعل المختار). Therefore, anyone who denies the free agent should not discuss prophethood until that issue is settled. For those who accept a free agent, the author asserts that no effect (مؤثر) brings anything from non-existence into existence except the One God. This nullifies any other supposed agents like planets, spheres, intellects, etc. He then provides a proof for this principle. The proof is in multiple steps: (1) God is powerful over some things, and those things are qualified by possibility (الإمكان). If possibility is removed, only necessity or impossibility remain, both of which preclude being within power. (2) The concept of possibility is a single, univocal concept across all possible things. (3) Since the ground for being within power (i.e., possibility) is the same for all possibles, they are all equally valid as objects of power. (4) God's power is a relation between His essence and possible things. This relation is itself possible and requires an effect, which is God's essence. (5) If God's essence were to necessitate power over some possibles but not others, this would be an unjustified preference which is impossible. Therefore, the only valid conclusion is that God is powerful over all possibles. The argument uses the principle of no preference without a preponderator (ترجيح بلا مرجح).

Commentary
This passage presents Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's explanation of the prophetic method of da'wah (invitation to God). It begins by noting that the Qur'an mentions divine attributes such as hearing, sight, supremacy, acceptance of good speech, and istiwa' on the Throne. The prophetic method prevents most people from delving into theological subtleties, allowing only the exceptionally intelligent to investigate. The method emphasizes both divine transcendence (tanzih) and affirmation (ithbat) of attributes, but does so in a general, summary fashion without detailed reconciliation of apparent contradictions. Al-Razi illustrates this with examples: for transcendence, verses like 'God is Self-Sufficient' and 'Nothing is like Him' are cited to deny composition, spatiality, and corporeality. For affirmation, many verses affirm God's existence, knowledge, and control. The prophetic way obliges belief in both divine power and wisdom without explaining how they coexist. The passage contrasts two extreme positions: attributing all evil to God's creation magnifies power but diminishes wisdom, while denying any evil from God magnifies wisdom but diminishes power. The Qur'an, however, affirms both by verses like 'Everything is from God' and 'Evil is from yourself'. The conclusion is that the common people must have absolute faith in both aspects, and this is the only way for public da'wah to succeed.

Balancing Tanzih and Ithbat in Da'wah
Al-Razi details the prophetic method's use of tanzih and ithbat verses and its approach to qadar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the prophetic method balances divine transcendence and affirmation without resolving apparent contradictions.

Core Idea
The Qur'an affirms both divine power and wisdom generally, avoiding detailed reconciliation.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the three methods by which one can arrive at knowledge of the natures (طبائع) of celestial bodies (الأجرام الفلكية). The author presents them as three: analogy (القياس), experience (التجربة), and revelation/inspiration (الوحي والإلهام). He first discusses analogy, giving examples: from the dim color of Saturn they judged it cold and dry (like black bile), and from the red color of Mars they judged it hot and dry (like fire). However, the author rejects reliance on analogy alone, stating that in medicine such inference from colors is the weakest kind of evidence. The truth is that colors give only a weak indication. But when experience is added, and the results of experience match the analogy, then knowledge is obtained from the combination. So analogy serves as the initial stimulus for inquiry, while experience provides completion and perfection. Next, the author explains experience in detail: a practitioner observes celestial configurations when a particular event occurs; if the same event recurs with the same configuration multiple times, a strong conjecture (ظن قوي) arises that the configuration causes that event. Experience is thus inference from events to configurations, and then from configurations to events. The author notes that this method has been used since ancient times, and mastery requires persistent observation and comparison with predecessors. Finally, the author mentions the third path: revelation and inspiration, which is also accepted by practitioners. He cites a story of Tankalusha and Dhuwani (possibly ancient sages) who, through spiritual purification, perceived celestial forms beyond description. The author concludes by noting that just as religions and schools rely on transmission from predecessors, so too does this science, making revelation a valid source.

As for the fixed stars: there is no doubt that knowing their natures is difficult, and they have mentioned ways to attain this knowledge. The first way: inference by their colors for their natures. So every star whose color is equal to the color of some planet, they attribute its nature and temperament to it. And what they found its color composite of the colors of two planets, they attribute its nature to them. Zoroaster said: 'The bayabaniyya (stars) that are red, yellow, dark, and speckled are all definitely dismal.' The second way: inference by their magnitudes. 'Umar ibn al-Farrukhan al-Tabari said: 'Only the stars of the first, second, and third magnitude indicate honor and elevation. As for the stars of the first magnitude, they indicate kingship and caliphate. Those of the second magnitude indicate what is below that, and likewise those of the third magnitude.' And Zoroaster said: 'The greatest star in the sky among the bayabaniyya is Sirius the Yemeni, which is the crosser. If it happens to be on the degree of the ascendant, it gives kingship and wealth. If it happens to be on the degree of the midheaven, it gives victory and authority. If it happens to be on the ninth degree, it gives prophethood. It is a pure lucky star, worshipped by the Arabs in ancient times. Beasts rejoice at seeing it. Its nature is the nature of Jupiter and Venus, because it resembles each of them in color and size.' And he said in the book Tiqatuna: 'Looking at Sirius calms the fever mixed of yellow and black bile.' And know: what can be considered in the attributes of fixed stars is a collection of things: one: color; second: orbit; third: proximity to the zenith and distance from it. So that which is close to the zenith has a stronger effect. Therefore it is said: 'The star Suhayl (Canopus), its power in the land of Yemen is greater than its power in these lands.'

Commentary
This passage explains the methods for recognizing the natures of fixed stars. Al-Razi first notes the difficulty and then lists two methods. The first method is inference by color: if a star's color matches a single planet, its nature is assigned accordingly; if it is a composite of two colors, it shares the natures of both. He quotes Zoroaster stating that the 'bayabaniyya' stars of certain colors are all definitely unlucky. The second method is inference by magnitude: 'Umar ibn al-Farrukhan al-Tabari says stars of first magnitude indicate kingship and caliphate; second magnitude indicates lower; third magnitude indicates still lower. Then al-Razi presents a detailed example from Zoroaster about the star Sirius (the Yemeni Sirius), describing its effects when placed on different astrological houses: ascendant gives kingship and wealth, midheaven gives victory and authority, ninth gives prophethood. Finally, al-Razi states that three attributes of fixed stars are considered: color, orbit, and distance from zenith. The Necessity of Experimentation in Astrology
The author concludes that only experimentation can distinguish true from false astrological claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author insists that only experiential testing can validate astrological claims, and how this sets astrology apart from demonstrative sciences.

Core Idea
Astrology is not proven by reason; only experiment can separate true from false.

In this concluding passage, the author drives home a crucial methodological point: no amount of theoretical knowledge about celestial causes can guarantee the truth of an astrological prediction. The only reliable judge is repeated experimentation. This passage serves as a reminder that even after mastering all the conditions and principles, the practitioner must remain humble and empirical.
