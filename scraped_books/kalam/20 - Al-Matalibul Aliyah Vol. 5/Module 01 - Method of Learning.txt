The answer to the second proof: We say: This proof is based on premises. The first of them: That if void occurred, that void would be infinite. But one could say: Did you not say that outside the world there is neither void nor plenum? So why is it not permissible to also say: The existing void is a finite magnitude, and it is the magnitude in which the body of the world occurs, and as for the outside of it, there is neither void nor plenum? On this estimate, you cannot say: Why did the world occur in this part of void rather than other parts? For if they say: Everyone who affirms void says it is infinite. Then we say: On this estimate, this statement ceases to be a demonstrative proof, but rather its utmost is that it is an objection against the opponent's statement.

Commentary
This passage is the response (jawab) to the second proof (not included in this window). The second proof argued that void must be infinite. The author challenges the first premise: that void must be infinite. He proposes an alternative: void could be finite, exactly the volume occupied by the world, with no void or plenum outside. In this case, the question 'why is the world in this part of void?' is meaningless because there is no other part. Even if opponents claim all proponents of void say it is infinite, the argument then ceases to be a demonstrative proof (burhan) and becomes merely a refutation of a specific opinion (ilzam). Thus, the proof is not universally valid.
