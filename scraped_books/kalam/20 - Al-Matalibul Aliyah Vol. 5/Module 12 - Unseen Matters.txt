Refuting the Objection: Dimension in Matter and Motion
Refutation of the objection that dimension-in-matter is a condition for motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the author argues that if a dimension were inherent in matter, it would prevent motion, and thus cannot be a condition for motion.

Core Idea
What accompanies something immovable cannot enable motion.

The author clears away an objection before presenting the fourth proof. The objection claims that a dimension being inherent in matter is a condition for its motion. The author argues that entification in matter actually attaches dimension to something immovable, which would block motion, not enable it. This refutation sets the stage for the main argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the objection
Read the Arabic passage carefully. Notice the initial statement and the objection that is being refuted.
The objection is the claim that dimension being inherent in matter is a condition for motion.
2
Understand the author's reasoning
The author shows why the objection is impossible: entification in matter means the dimension accompanies something that does not accept motion, which would prevent motion rather than enable it.
This is a direct refutation of the objection.
3
Note the concluding remark
The author notes that this argument supports Aristotle against Plato, but also points out it relies on the assumption that dimensions are equal in quiddity.
This sets up the fourth proof that follows.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument and a refutation. The author begins by continuing an earlier point: if a dimension (al-bu'd) were inherent in matter, it would be accompanied by something that does not accept motion, so it cannot be a condition for motion. This removes the objection. Then he notes that this argument supports Aristotle against Plato, but it depends on the assumption that all dimensions are equal in quiddity (tamām al-māhiyya) and that whatever applies to one applies to its like. The core of the fourth proof (al-ḥujja al-rābiʿa) starts: the dimension called space (al-faḍāʾ), place (al-makān), and locale (al-ḥayyiz) is divisible by supposition and indication. We can say 'from here to there is a cubit' etc. This division yields parts that are either equal in quiddity or not. If equal, then what applies to one applies to the other, so a part above can be below, which allows motion, and then everything that has motion has a place, leading to a regress of places (al-makān yaḥtāju ilā makān). This is absurd, so the assumption of equality leads to impossibility. If the parts are not equal, then the space is composed of heterogeneous parts, so it is composite, and every composite resolves into simple parts. But even a simple part has a right and left, so it is divisible into two halves that are equal in quiddity, and again motion is possible, leading to the same regress. Thus the assumption of divisibility leads to impossibility either way. The author then notes that if this argument holds, it implies that the celestial spheres (al-aflāk) can be broken apart (al-kharq wa al-tafarruq wa al-tamazzuq). This would require a place outside the convex sphere, implying a void (khalāʾ) outside the world, and thus that place is a dimension. He then sets out to establish this consequence with premises. The first premise (al-muqaddima al-ūlā) is that the argument necessitates the possibility of tearing the celestial spheres. He proves this by a case split: if the sphere that determines directions is simple, then all its pieces are equal, so they can interchange positions, leading to separation. If it is composite, it resolves into simple parts, and each simple part has two halves that can switch places, again allowing tearing. Thus the first premise is established, but the rest of the premises are missing from the text due to a lacuna.

The Fourth Proof: Divisibility of Space Leads to Regress
Proof that divisibility of space leads to regress of places.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proof that space cannot be divisible, because assuming divisibility leads to the absurdity of place requiring another place.

Core Idea
Assuming space is divisible forces place to require an infinite chain of places.

The author now presents the fourth proof, which argues that the dimension we call space is divisible only by supposition, but this assumption leads to a contradiction. Whether the parts are equal or not, it ends in an infinite regress. This argument is central to the discussion of the nature of space and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of divisibility by supposition
The passage defines divisibility by supposition as the ability to posit parts, like saying 'from here to there is a cubit'.
This is a mental division, not physical.
2
Follow the dichotomy: parts equal or not equal
The passage splits into two cases: the supposed parts are either equal in quiddity or not. Both are considered.
Pay attention to the logical structure.
3
See how both branches lead to regress
In the equal case, motion between parts leads to place needing a place. In the unequal case, composition leads to simple parts that are still divisible, again causing regress.
The conclusion is that assuming divisibility is absurd.

Commentary
This passage is a complex argument and a refutation. The author begins by continuing an earlier point: if a dimension (al-bu'd) were inherent in matter, it would be accompanied by something that does not accept motion, so it cannot be a condition for motion. This removes the objection. Then he notes that this argument supports Aristotle against Plato, but it depends on the assumption that all dimensions are equal in quiddity (tamām al-māhiyya) and that whatever applies to one applies to its like. The core of the fourth proof (al-ḥujja al-rābiʿa) starts: the dimension called space (al-faḍāʾ), place (al-makān), and locale (al-ḥayyiz) is divisible by supposition and indication. We can say 'from here to there is a cubit' etc. This division yields parts that are either equal in quiddity or not. If equal, then what applies to one applies to the other, so a part above can be below, which allows motion, and then everything that has motion has a place, leading to a regress of places (al-makān yaḥtāju ilā makān). This is absurd, so the assumption of equality leads to impossibility. If the parts are not equal, then the space is composed of heterogeneous parts, so it is composite, and every composite resolves into simple parts. But even a simple part has a right and left, so it is divisible into two halves that are equal in quiddity, and again motion is possible, leading to the same regress. Thus the assumption of divisibility leads to impossibility either way. The author then notes that if this argument holds, it implies that the celestial spheres (al-aflāk) can be broken apart (al-kharq wa al-tafarruq wa al-tamazzuq). This would require a place outside the convex sphere, implying a void (khalāʾ) outside the world, and thus that place is a dimension. He then sets out to establish this consequence with premises. The first premise (al-muqaddima al-ūlā) is that the argument necessitates the possibility of tearing the celestial spheres. He proves this by a case split: if the sphere that determines directions is simple, then all its pieces are equal, so they can interchange positions, leading to separation. If it is composite, it resolves into simple parts, and each simple part has two halves that can switch places, again allowing tearing. Thus the first premise is established, but the rest of the premises are missing from the text due to a lacuna.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This second proof draws on a legal ruling familiar to every Muslim: the direction of prayer (qibla). By imagining the complete removal of the Ka'ba, the jurists concluded that the qibla becomes the empty space itself. This teaches us something profound about the nature of space and its independence from material bodies.
