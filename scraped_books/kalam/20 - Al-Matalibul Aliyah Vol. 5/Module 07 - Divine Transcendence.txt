The Impossibility of Time as a Container for the Eternal
Sanusi argues that time cannot be a container for the eternal, using a parity argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the notion of time as an independent container for the eternal leads to logical impossibility, and why Islamic theologians negate time as a real entity.

Core Idea
Time cannot contain the eternal; the very idea leads to infinite regress.

In this passage, the author completes an argument that demolishes the common-sense assumption that time is a container for events. By analyzing the implications of eternity and temporality, he shows that positing time as a receptacle leads to absurdity. This argument is crucial for understanding the Islamic theological stance on time.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then the translation. Notice the dense logical structure.
2
Identify the core principle
The passage starts by stating that the eternal cannot be preceded by another. This is key to the argument.
The eternal is defined as having no beginning.
3
Understand the parity analogy
The author draws a parallel between temporal events (which suggest two times) and the eternal (which suggests one continuous time). If time is a container, both would require time, leading to infinite regress.
4
Grasp the conclusion
The author concludes that the suggestion of time as a container is false, and the intellect necessitates negating time entirely.
The phrase 'وهو المطلوب' marks the conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a complete argument (the tail end of a previous proof) that aims to negate the existence of time as an independent container. The author begins by stating a principle: something that requires being preceded by another is incompatible with eternality, because the eternal by definition is not preceded. Therefore, combining the reality of time (which is essentially sequential) with the reality of eternity (which is without beginning) is impossible. He then examines the common notion that time is a container for events. Just as temporal events imply two successive times (one for non-existence, one for existence), the eternal would imply a single continuous time from eternity to eternity in which it exists. But if we then judge that time itself is eternal, that would imply that time is contained in another time, leading to an infinite regress. Since we reject that suggestion as false for time, we must also reject the analogous suggestion for temporal events. The author concludes that any suggestion that time is a real container is false and that the intellect necessitates the outright denial of time. The final phrase 'وهو المطلوب' marks the conclusion.

Tenth Argument: God's Timelessness
Al-Razi argues that God's eternal existence shows time is unrelated to motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how al-Razi uses God's eternal existence to demonstrate that temporal priority, posteriority, and simultaneity are independent of motion, and how this leads to Ibn Sina's distinction between time, duration, and eternity.

Core Idea
God's existence gives priority and simultaneity without any motion.

Al-Razi presents his tenth argument for God's transcendence over motion and change. He starts from our necessary knowledge that God existed before this day, exists with it, and will exist after it, concluding that the concepts of priority, posteriority, and withness have no connection with motion. He then extends this reasoning to intellectual substances, which exist with God perpetually without motion. This argument prepares the ground for understanding time as a relation among changing things, as articulated in Ibn Sina's famous threefold distinction between time, duration, and eternity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and identify the three temporal predicates
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice that al-Razi applies 'before', 'with', and 'after' to God himself. Identify these three concepts in the text.
These are priority (al-qabliyya), posteriority (al-ba'diyya), and withness (al-ma'iyya).
2
Trace the logical inference
Al-Razi says: since these predicates apply to God, and God is transcendent above motion and change, it follows that these concepts have no connection with motion. Follow this simple but powerful inference.
3
Consider the supporting evidence from intellectual substances
The passage also mentions intellectual substances (jawahir 'aqliyya) that are abstract from motion. They also exist with God perpetually. This reinforces the independence of temporal concepts from motion.
The philosophers' proof that cause must exist with effect also demonstrates withness without motion.
4
Understand Ibn Sina's threefold distinction
Al-Razi quotes Ibn Sina: time (zaman) is the relation of changing to changing, duration (dahr) is changing to fixed, and eternity (sarmad) is fixed to fixed. This distinction arises from the independence of temporal concepts from motion.

5
Reflect on the significance for divine transcendence
Consider how this argument safeguards God's transcendence by showing that time is not a necessary accompaniment of existence, and that God and the intellects exist in a timeless realm.

Some claimed God is time itself, citing a hadith and a supplication.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument concerning the relationship between God and time (al-dahr). It begins by presenting a view attributed to some people: that God, in relation to His creative action and His states, is called by various temporal names (Time, Eternity, etc.). These people also rely on a prophetic hadith, 'Do not revile Time, for God is Time,' and a supplication containing divine names including 'O Time.' This is presented as the first opinion. The author then states that the majority holds that God is exalted above being time or duration. Within this majority view, he reports the position of the ancient philosophers, who assert that there are five eternal necessary existents: an unaffected cause (God), an affected non-causal entity (hyle), a cause-and-effect entity (the soul), and two non-causal, non-affected entities (time and space). These philosophers argue that the attributes of majesty are found in time, and their presence in time indicates that they are more perfect in God. The author then gives his own preferred opinion ('al-aqrab'). He argues that proofs show the Necessary Existent is one and necessary in all aspects, which precludes any change. Since time and duration are subject to change (by the succession of before and after), they cannot be the Necessary Existent; otherwise, they would be contingent. God, by contrast, is the being sanctified from change and exalted above potentiality. This is the author's conclusion, affirming God's transcendence over time.

The Majority View and the Five Eternal Existents

Core Idea

This lesson presents the contrary position: the majority of scholars affirm that God is exalted above being time or duration. Within that, the ancient philosophers proposed a system with five eternal necessary existents: God, hyle, soul, time, and space. They argued that the attributes of majesty belong to time in a limited way, but more perfectly to God.

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument concerning the relationship between God and time (al-dahr). It begins by presenting a view attributed to some people: that God, in relation to His creative action and His states, is called by various temporal names (Time, Eternity, etc.). The author then states that the majority holds that God is exalted above being time or duration. The fourth branch: It became clear from the aforementioned investigations concerning the matter of place, space, and direction that the God of the world cannot be particularized to any place. Its establishment is from aspects: The first: that if God's existence in a space and direction were necessary, then God's existence would be dependent on that space, but we have shown that the void is homogeneous in parts, and obtaining a space empty of body is conceivable. Thus, God's essence would be in need of that space, and that space would be independent of Him and of all contingent beings, so God would be contingent in essence, dependent on another, and that space would be necessary in essence, independent of others—and all of that is impossible. The second: that if He were to obtain in this expanse, and in this void, then either it is said that He obtains in all parts of this void, or in a specific part of it. The first is impossible because it would require Him to be divisible and composite, and also His essence would mix with all originated and contingent things—which is false. The second is impossible because we have shown that this void is homogeneous in parts, and there was not in pre-eternity one side of it described as above and another side described as below. Then, obtaining His essence in some parts of this void without others would be a preponderance of one of the two sides of the possible without a preponderator—and that is impossible. The third aspect: that we say: place, direction, and space either refer to dimension or to surface. If space refers to dimension, then it would necessitate the dependence of God's essence on that dimension, with that dimension being independent of God's essence, and the aforementioned impossibilities would follow. And if space and direction refer to the surrounding surface, then outside the world there is no void, no plenum, no space, and no direction. Then, it would be impossible for God's essence to obtain in it; so it remains that if He were in a space and direction, He would be obtaining inside the world, and would be an extended finite body, surrounded by the sphere of the world—and that is impossible. And God knows best.

Commentary

Second Proof: Homogeneity of Void Prevents Arbitrary Location
Razi's second proof that God cannot be located in the void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second proof that God cannot occupy a part of the void without implying composition or arbitrary preference.

Core Idea
If God were in the void, He would either fill all parts (making Him composite) or a specific part (requiring an unjustified choice)—both impossible.

Razi's second proof addresses the scenario where God exists within the void. He considers two possibilities: occupying the entire void or a specific part. Each leads to contradictions with God's simplicity and aseity.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Notice the two explicit alternatives.
2
Analyze the first alternative
If God occupies all parts of the void, He would be divisible and composite, and His essence would mix with all contingent things.
Both implications are impossible for God.
3
Analyze the second alternative
If God occupies a specific part, then because the void is homogeneous, there would be no reason to choose that part—requiring an arbitrary preference (tarjīḥ bilā murajjiḥ).
This violates the principle of sufficient reason.

Third Proof: The Nature of Place Precludes Divine Location
Razi's third proof analyzing definitions of place.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the third proof that analyzes the definition of place to show that God cannot be contained by any spatial category.

Core Idea
If place means dimension, God would depend on it; if place means surface, God would be inside the world as a finite body—both impossible.

Razi's third proof examines the very concept of place (makān), direction (jiha), and space (ḥayyiz). He argues that these must refer either to a dimension (bu‘d) or a surrounding surface (saṭḥ). Both alternatives lead to contradictions with God's transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage
Read the Arabic passage, then the translation. Notice the two definitions of space.
2
Understand the first definition
If space is a dimension, then God would depend on that dimension, repeating the first proof's impossibility.
Here, space would be independent of God.
3
Understand the second definition
If space is a surrounding surface, then there is no such surface outside the world. Therefore, God would have to be inside the world, which would make Him a finite extended body—impossible.
This shows that any spatial category leads to contradiction.
