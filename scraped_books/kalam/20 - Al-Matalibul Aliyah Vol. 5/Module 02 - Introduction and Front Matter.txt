Opening of the Fifth Book: Introduction to Time and Space
Al-Razi introduces the fifth book on time and space.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how classical Islamic theological works introduce their topics and what this particular introduction reveals about al-Razi's Al-Matalib al-Aliyah.

Core Idea
Every great book begins with a blessing and a boast.

Al-Razi's Al-Matalib al-Aliyah is a monumental work of Islamic theology. The fifth book focuses on the nature of time and space, a topic that bridges physics and metaphysics. This short opening not only invokes God's name and praises the author but also sets the stage for the detailed investigations to follow. Understanding how classical authors frame their work helps us appreciate the scholarly tradition they belong to.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the meaning.
2
Observe the formal structure
Notice the three parts: basmalah, author eulogy, and book announcement. This is a standard opening for classical Islamic works.
3
Reflect on the author's self-praise
Al-Razi describes himself with grand titles. Consider why authors might include such praise: establishing authority, following tradition, or both.
4
Identify the book's subject
The passage clearly states that the fifth book is on time and space (al-zaman wa al-makan). Note that this is part of the Ilahiyat (divine matters) section.
5
Prepare for the two treatises
The passage ends by saying the book contains two treatises. Though their titles are not given here, this signals the structure of the volume.
We will learn about these treatises in subsequent lessons.

Commentary
This passage opens the fifth book (volume) of al-Razi's Al-Matalib al-Aliyah (The Sublime Pursuits). It begins with the basmalah and an elaborate eulogy of the author himself—a common feature in classical Islamic works, where the author praises himself or his teacher. Here al-Razi is described as the best of the world in his time, the seal of the investigators, and the completer of the sciences of the ancients and moderns. He is styled as the imam who calls to God. Then the subject of the book is stated: it is the fifth book of the Ilahiyat (divine matters) section of the overall work, and it deals with the investigation of time and space (al-zaman wa al-makan). The passage concludes by noting that this book contains two treatises (maqalatani). This is a straightforward introduction that sets the topic and scope of the volume.
