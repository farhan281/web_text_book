Al-Razi's Third Proof Against the Void: Motion and Time
Al-Razi presents a two-part reductio argument against the existence of void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's reductio ad absurdum argument that assuming the existence of void leads to contradictory conclusions about motion and time, thus void cannot exist.

Core Idea
Motion in the void would be either timeless or yield absurd speed comparisons, disproving the void.

Al-Razi presents a sophisticated proof that the existence of void (al-khalāʼ) is impossible. He argues that if void existed, motion in it would have to be either instantaneous or occur in time. Both possibilities lead to contradictions. In this lesson, we examine his detailed reasoning, which relies on observed correlations between medium density and speed of motion.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two branches
Al-Razi begins by dividing the possibilities: motion in void either occurs in time or not. Note that instantaneous motion is dismissed quickly, while motion in time is analyzed in depth.
2
Grasp the premise on medium density
Learn the principle: the thinner the medium, the faster the motion. Al-Razi supports this with empirical examples (stone in water vs. oil) and analogical reasoning (tearing thin vs. thick materials).
3
Follow the thought experiment
Work through the example comparing motion in void, water, and hypothetical thinner media. Notice how times are calculated proportionally.
Assume 10 cubits in void takes 1 hour, in water 10 hours.
4
Identify the absurd consequences
The example shows that a medium thinner than water by a factor of 10 yields a time equal to void (1 hour), meaning motion with resistance is as fast as without. An even thinner medium yields even less time, meaning resistance makes motion faster—both absurd.
5
Conclude the impossibility of void
Since both branches (timeless motion and motion in time) are impossible, the initial assumption of void existence is false. Reflect on the strength of this reductio argument.

The third proof: If we suppose the existence of void, then the motion of a body in it would either take place in time or not in time. Both divisions are false, so the statement of the existence of void is false. We only say: it is impossible for it to occur in time, because when a body moves in a distance, the thinner the body present in that distance, the faster the motion in it. Experience and analogy indicate this. As for experience, it is obvious, because the sinking of a stone in water is easier and faster than its sinking in oil and molasses. As for analogy, because tearing the thin is easier than tearing the thick. And when this premise is established, we say: Let us suppose that the moving object traverses ten cubits in void in one hour, and traverses ten cubits of the distance filled with water in ten hours. According to this estimation, the time of motion in void would be one-tenth of the time of motion in water. Then let us suppose another medium, thinner than water, such that its thinness exceeds the thinness of water ten times. Since the smallness of motion time corresponds to the increase in thinness of the medium present in the distance, it would be necessary that in this thin medium the time be one-tenth of the time of motion in water. But the time of motion in void was one-tenth of the time of motion in water. Consequently, the time of motion in this thin medium would be equal to the time of motion in void, so motion with an obstacle would be like motion without an obstacle. Moreover, if we suppose another medium even thinner than that, the time of motion in it would be less than the time of motion in void, so motion with an obstacle would be faster than motion in the absence of an obstacle. This is contradictory. Thus, it is established by what we have mentioned: if the statement of the existence of void were correct, it would be impossible for motion to be without time, and impossible for it to be in time, so the statement of the existence of void is impossible.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof against the existence of void (al-khalāʼ). The argument is a reductio ad absurdum: assuming void exists, motion in void must be either in time or not in time; both are impossible, therefore void cannot exist. To show that motion in time is impossible, al-Razi establishes a principle: the thinner (more rarefied) the medium, the faster the motion. He supports this with empirical examples (stone sinking faster in water than in oil) and analogical reasoning (tearing thin material easier than thick). Applying this, suppose a body moves 10 cubits in void in 1 hour, and 10 cubits in water in 10 hours. Then time in void = 1/10 time in water. Now consider a medium thinner than water by a factor of 10. According to the principle, time in that medium should also be 1/10 time in water, equaling the time in void. But this would mean motion with a resisting medium (the thin medium) is as fast as motion without any medium (void), which is absurd. Furthermore, if we take an even thinner medium, motion time would be less than in void, implying that having an obstacle makes motion faster than having none—another contradiction. Hence motion in void cannot occur in time. The alternative—motion not in time (instantaneous)—is also impossible because motion inherently involves succession. Thus both options lead to absurdity, so the existence of void is impossible.

Third Proof Against Void: Motion and Time
Al-Razi presents the third proof against void using motion and time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's third proof against the existence of void, which uses the relationship between motion, time, and medium density to derive a contradiction.

Core Idea
Motion in void would be both in time and not in time — a logical impossibility.

Al-Razi now employs a more intricate argument, drawing on empirical observations and proportional reasoning. He shows that if void existed, motion in it would have to be both in time and not in time, which is impossible. Follow carefully the reductio ad absurdum he constructs using different densities of media.

Guided Path
1
Read the disjunctive premise
Notice the initial division: motion in void is either in time or not in time. Al-Razi will show both are false.
This is a classic kalam technique of dividing possibilities.
2
Follow the reductio for 'in time'
Al-Razi uses a premise about speed and medium density, then constructs a scenario where motion with an impediment (medium) becomes faster than without (void). Identify the contradiction.
He relies on two sources: experience (stone in water vs. oil) and analogy (tearing).
3
Understand the argument for 'not in time'
Motion over a divisible distance requires time for its parts. So motion cannot be timeless.
4
Conclude the proof
Since both options lead to impossibility, void cannot exist. Notice how the proof closes the loop.

Commentary
This passage presents the third proof against the existence of void (khala'), employing a detailed argument about motion and time. The author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, structures the proof as follows: First, he states the disjunctive premise: if void existed, motion in it would either be in time or not in time. Both are impossible. He then focuses on proving the impossibility of motion in time within void. He establishes that motion is faster in thinner media, citing both experience (stone in water vs. oil) and analogy (tearing). Using this, he constructs a reductio ad absurdum: suppose motion in void takes a certain time. Then imagine a medium thinner than water but not void; the time would equal that of void. Then an even thinner medium would yield a time less than that in void, implying motion with an impediment is faster than without — a contradiction. Therefore, motion in void cannot be in time. Next, he argues motion in void must be in time because any motion over a divisible distance requires temporal succession of halves. Hence, motion in void cannot be outside time either. Since both alternatives are impossible, void cannot exist.

Fourth Proof Against Void: Stone Thrown Upward
Al-Razi presents the fourth proof against void using a stone thrown upward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's fourth proof against the existence of void space, which uses the example of a stone thrown upward to demonstrate that motion requires a resistive medium.

Core Idea
A stone's fall proves that void cannot exist — only air can stop its upward flight.

This lesson presents Al-Razi's clever argument from everyday experience: a stone thrown upward eventually falls back. According to Aristotelian physics, the stone's motion is sustained by a motive force that is gradually neutralized by collisions with air particles. If the space were empty, the stone would never stop. Al-Razi uses this to conclude that void does not exist.

Guided Path
1
Understand the physical premises
Identify the two premises that Al-Razi takes as established by demonstration: first, when a stone is thrown upward, the mover imparts a motive force; second, this force is nullified only by air collisions.
These premises are based on Aristotelian physics, which you may take as given for this argument.
2
Follow the conditional reasoning
Consider the hypothetical: if the distance were void (no air), there would be no collisions, so the force would not weaken. Therefore the stone would continue rising until it hit the celestial sphere.
This is a counterfactual that leads to an absurd conclusion.
3
Connect the absurd conclusion to the rejection of void
Since we observe that the stone does fall back to earth, the premise that the distance is void must be false. Thus, void does not exist.
This is a modus tollens argument: if void then absurd consequence, absurd consequence is false, so void is false.

The fourth proof: It is established by demonstration that when a stone is thrown forcefully upward, it moves because the forcible mover produced in it a motive force upward. And it is established that that force is nullified only by collisions with the air in that distance. When this is established, we say: If that distance were void, the collisions would not occur, so that force would not weaken nor be nullified, and the thrown stone would be required not to descend until it reached the surface of the celestial sphere. And since that is false, we know that this distance is not void.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth proof against void. Al-Razi begins with a premise established by demonstration: when a stone is thrown upward, the mover imparts an upward motive force. This force is gradually nullified by collisions with the air particles in the path. Based on this, he argues: if the distance through which the stone travels were void (i.e., no air), there would be no collisions, so the motive force would not diminish. Consequently, the stone would never fall back; it would continue until it hit the celestial sphere. Since we observe that a thrown stone does fall back, the distance cannot be void. The proof relies on the causal connection between air resistance and the dissipation of motion.

Al-Razi presents a tangible experiment to argue against the existence of void. By observing water behavior in a sealed narrow-necked vessel, he demonstrates that nature abhors a vacuum. This proof is a clear example of empirical reasoning in kalam.

Empirical argument against void using bubbles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how an empirical observation (bubbles in water) is used to argue that void does not exist within the world.

Core Idea
Bubbles prove that void is impossible because air must escape from a submerged bottle.

This lesson presents the eighth of several arguments against the possibility of void. The author uses a simple, observable experiment: if a bottle could be truly empty (void), then water would fill it without displacing air. But we see bubbles, so air must be present, proving void is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the source passage and translation
Read the Arabic argument once, then read the literal translation. Notice the conditional structure: 'if... then...', 'since the matter is not so...'
The passage is a concise logical proof with an empirical base.
2
Understand the logical structure
Identify the premise (if void were possible), the expected consequence (no bubbles), the observed reality (bubbles appear), and the conclusion (void does not occur inside the world).
This is a modus tollens argument: if P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.
3
Reflect on the scope of the argument
Ask yourself: does this argument prove void is impossible everywhere? The author only claims it proves void does not occur 'inside the world' (dākhil al-ʻālam).
The argument leaves open the possibility of void outside the world, which is debated in other contexts.

First Premise: Is Void Necessarily Infinite?
Refutes the first premise of the second argument for void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the claim that void must be infinite, showing that it can be finite and that the argument loses its demonstrative force.

Core Idea
The opponent's assumption of infinite void is not necessary; finite void suffices.

The second argument for void assumes that if void exists, it must be infinite. The author begins by challenging this premise. He points out that the opponent themselves deny void outside the world, so they cannot consistently argue that void is infinite. Alternatively, if they insist on infinite void, the argument loses its demonstrative force and becomes merely a dialectical claim. This lesson examines the first premise and its refutation.

Guided Path
1
Read the first premise
The opponent states: if void exists, it is infinite. Pay attention to how the author presents this.
2
Identify the two responses
The author offers two lines of attack: either void is finite (based on the opponent's own view) or, if infinite, the argument becomes non-demonstrative.
The key is to see that the opponent cannot have it both ways.
3
Consider the dialectical outcome
If the opponent insists on infinite void, their argument becomes a mere claim (ilzām) not a proof.
This shows that the first premise is not necessary.

Learning Objective

Similarity in properties does not prove identity of essence.

The author questions this assumption, arguing that parts of void could differ in essence, making one part more suitable. This challenges the assumption of homogeneity.
