The Fourth Proof: The Premise and the Dilemma
The initial premise and the dilemma from the fourth proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the initial premise of the fourth argument and the dilemma it poses: either time-containers lead to infinite regress or the notion of a container is unnecessary.

Core Idea
Prior times cannot coexist; either containers regress infinitely or are irrelevant.

The fourth proof begins with a simple observation: we know with certainty that yesterday's event is prior to today's, and that yesterday itself is prior to today. This priority cannot allow the prior and posterior to coexist. In this lesson, we examine the two possibilities this priority presents and the logical consequences of each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core premise
The author states that we know by necessity that yesterday's event is prior to today's. This is the foundation of the argument.
2
Understand the dilemma
If this priority requires a container (time) to hold prior and posterior, then that container itself would need another container, leading to an infinite chain. If no container is needed, then the concept of container can be abandoned altogether.
3
Reflect on the two horns
Consider which horn seems more plausible. The author will later build on this dilemma to show the impossibility of infinite times.

[ ولكونه (۳) ] أحد الأيام الأمسية يجب أن يكون داخلا في مجموع الأيام الأمسية وحينئذ يلزم كون ذلك الظرف خارجاً عن ذلك المجموع ، لكونه ظرفاً له ، وكونه داخلا فيه ، لكونه أحد تلك الأزمنة ، فيكون ذلك الواحد داخلاً في مجموع تلك الأزمنة ، وخارجاً عنها ، وكون الشيء الواحد بالنسبة إلى الشيء الواحد خارجاً عنه ، وداخلاً فيه محال . فكان هذا القول محالاً
The fourth proof: We know by necessity that yesterday's event is prior to today's event; likewise we know by necessity that yesterday is prior to today, a priority. And it is impossible for the prior to exist with the posterior. If this kind of priority requires the existence of a container and receptacle by which this priority and posteriority occur, then let this container have another container, and this receptacle another receptacle, ad infinitum. And if it does not require it at all, then let the matter be the same for all temporal events. It cannot be said: Suppose that it necessitates for us the establishment of infinite times, some surrounding others; why do you say that is impossible? Because we say: On this supposition, the present day would not be a single day, but infinite days surrounding one another, and their sum would be present in this day; and yesterday would not be a single yesterday, but infinite days surrounding one another, while their sum was present yesterday. If you have understood this, we say: The sum of those infinite yesterdays is prior to all the infinite todays, and that priority must be by another time that is a container and receptacle for them; but to say that is impossible. That is because that time which is a container for the sum of yesterdays—since it is a container for them—must be external to them, due to the necessity of the container being distinct from the contained. And [because it is] one of the yesterdays, it must be internal to the sum of yesterdays. Then it follows that this container is external to that sum, because it is its container, and internal to it, because it is one of those times. So that single thing is internal to the sum of those times and external to it, and a single thing being both external and internal with respect to a single thing is impossible. So this statement is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a complete argument—the fourth proof—that establishes the impossibility of positing infinite nested times. The author begins with a clear premise: we know by necessity that yesterday's event is prior to today's event, and likewise that yesterday itself is prior to today. The prior cannot coexist with the posterior. He then presents a dilemma: if this priority requires a time-container, then that container itself would need another container, leading to an infinite regress. If it does not require a container at all, then we can simply drop the idea of a container for all temporal events. An objector then suggests that maybe infinite nesting of times is acceptable. The author responds by showing the absurdity: if infinite times could surround one another, then today would not be a single day but an infinite collection of days, all present simultaneously; likewise yesterday would be an infinite collection. Then the sum of all yesterdays is prior to the sum of all todays. This priority must be accounted for by another time-container. But this container, being a container for the yesterdays, must be external to them; yet being one of the yesterdays, it must also be internal. This contradiction (a single thing being both inside and outside the same set) proves the impossibility. The argument is rigorous and hinges on the logical contradiction of a container being both distinct from and a member of the contained set.

The Objection: Why Can't Times Be Nested Infinitely?
The objection and the initial absurdity from the fourth proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection against the impossibility of infinite times and the author's initial response showing the absurdity of infinite nesting.

Core Idea
Infinite nesting of times would make today an infinite collection of days.

Having presented the dilemma, the author now entertains an objection: what if infinite times can be nested, with some surrounding others? In this lesson, we explore the objector's suggestion and the author's immediate counterargument: if times can be infinitely nested, then today would not be a single day but an infinite set of days all present together, and similarly yesterday would be an infinite set.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objector says: suppose infinite times surrounding each other are possible. Why is that impossible?
2
Understand the author's response
The author says: if that were the case, today would be infinite days nested and simultaneously present; yesterday would also be infinite days nested and present yesterday.
3
Grasp the absurdity
This leads to a situation where the sum of yesterdays is prior to the sum of todays, and this priority would itself require another time-container, which we will see leads to contradiction.

The Final Contradiction: A Time-Container That Both Surrounds and Is Inside
The final deduction of contradiction from the fourth proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the final step of the fourth proof: the contradiction that arises when a time-container must be both external to and a member of the set of yesterdays.

Core Idea
A container cannot be both outside and inside the same set.

In this final part of the fourth proof, the author deduces the logical contradiction. Starting from the absurd scenario of infinite nesting, he argues that the sum of all yesterdays is prior to the sum of all todays, and this priority requires a time-container. But this container, being a container for the yesterdays, must be external to them, yet as one of the yesterdays it must also be internal. This impossibility proves that infinite nested times cannot exist.

Guided Path
1
Understand the new premise
The sum of infinite yesterdays is prior to the sum of infinite todays. This priority must be accounted for by a time-container.
2
Identify the container's dual role
The container for the yesterdays must be external to them (as container) but also one of the yesterdays (internal).
3
Recognize the contradiction
A single thing cannot be both outside and inside the same set. This contradiction shows the initial supposition is impossible.

The Fifth Argument: Why God's Priority Cannot Depend on Time
The fifth proof begins: premise, dichotomy, first branch leads to contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first part of the fifth argument: if God's priority over events were time-dependent, it would entail contingency, contradicting God's necessity.

Core Idea
Dependence on time would make the Necessary Existent contingent.

The fifth proof tackles a subtle question: Is God's priority over temporal events itself dependent on time? The text begins by affirming that God must be prior to events and that His essence is inseparable from this priority. It then sets up a critical dichotomy and explores the consequences of one branch—leading to a contradiction. Although the passage is incomplete, the logical structure up to this point is clear and instructive for understanding kalam arguments about divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the literal translation to grasp the flow. Notice the argument is incomplete.
Pay attention to the key terms: الباري, الحوادث, واجب الوجود.
2
Identify the premise and dichotomy
The argument starts with an agreed premise: God must be prior to events, and that priority is essential. Then it presents a dichotomy: either this priority depends on time or not.
This is a standard kalam tactic to reduce options.
3
Follow the first branch to the contradiction
Trace the reasoning: if priority depends on time, then God's essence (which is inseparable from priority) depends on time. Dependence on another is a mark of contingency (imkan). Therefore the Necessary Existent would be contingent—a logical contradiction.
The argument cuts off here; the second branch is not given.
4
Reflect on the incompleteness
Consider why the text ends mid-sentence. What would the conclusion likely be? The missing part probably completes the reductio and affirms the other branch (priority does not depend on time).
This is a good exercise in reconstructing logical arguments.

The paradox of infinite days requiring a time container
The author proves that positing a temporal container for infinite past days leads to contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why God's priority over creation cannot require a temporal container, based on a logical contradiction.

Core Idea
A temporal container for infinite days leads to self-contradiction.

The author now presents a crucial argument against the need for a time container to explain God's priority. By assuming such a container, a logical impossibility emerges: the container would have to be both inside and outside the set of past days. This passage walks through that reductio proof.

Guided Path

Read the passage carefully

The Dilemma: God's Priority and Time
The first half of the fifth proof: dilemma and first reductio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the first part of the fifth proof: the dilemma that if God's priority depends on time, then God would be contingent, which is contradictory.

Core Idea
God's priority cannot depend on time, or He would be contingent.

The fifth proof begins by stating what is indisputable: God must be prior to events, and that priority is essential to Him. Then it presents a dilemma: either this priority depends on time or not. The first horn leads to a contradiction, which is what we will examine in this lesson.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the necessary premise
Read the opening lines where the author asserts that God's priority over daily events is necessary and inseparable from His essence. This is the starting point.
2
Identify the dilemma
The author presents a choice: either this priority depends on time or it does not. Notice that both horns will be examined.
The first horn is developed in this lesson.
3
Trace the first reductio
If priority depends on time, then because God's essence is inseparable from priority, God's essence depends on time. But anything that depends on another for existence is possible (mumkin), not necessary (wajib). This contradicts God's necessity.
The argument uses the principle: 'that which depends on something that depends on something is dependent on that something.'

Al-Razi's Proof That Space Is Not a Dimension

Core Idea
If space were a dimension, it would lead to logical contradictions.

In this passage, Al-Razi presents a crisp proof by contradiction against the view that space (al-makan) is a dimension (al-bu'd). He shows that this view leads to two impossible results, thereby establishing that space must be something else. Let's walk through his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythms come through before you interpret.
Notice that Al-Razi uses a conditional structure here.

The passage presents a dilemma: if place is a dimension, then it is both included in and excluded from the set of dimensions—a contradiction. If place is not a dimension, then place is something other than dimension, which is exactly the desired conclusion.

Both branches lead to false or unwanted outcomes, therefore the premise that space is a dimension must be rejected.

قرارها ، وأذهنت له رواصن الأسباب في منتهى شواهق أقطارها . يستشهد بكلية الأجناس على ربوبيته ، وبعجزها على قدرته ، وبفطورها على قدمه ، ويزوالها على بقائه . فلا إليه حد منسوب ، ولا له مثل مضروب ، ولا شيء عنه محجوب . تعالى عن ضرب الأمثال ، وصفات المخلوقات ، علواً كبيراً ، قال مولانا الداعي إلى الله ، رحمة الله عليه : يا إله العالمين . إنك ذكرت في كتابك الكريم : قل : لمن ما في السموات والأرض ؟ قل : لله فيثبت بهذا البرهان الباهر ، والدليل القاهر : أن المكان والمكانيات منقادة لك بالخضوع والخنوع ، معترفة لك بالفقر والخشوع ، منادية على أنفسها بالذل والصغار والعجز والافتقار . ثم قلت : « وله ما سكن في الليل والنهار ، فأظهرت بهذا السر المكتوم، والأصل المعلوم : أن الزمان والزمانيات تحت خلقك وتقديرك وإبداعك وتدبيرك . فالمكان والمكانيات والزمان والزمانيات كلها ناطقة بشواهد مجدك وعظمتك ، معترفة بوجوب وجودك وقدمك . فالزمان بحركته دل على أنك منزه عن الحركات، والمكان بسكونه اعترف بأنك متعالي عن السكنات ، فتعاليت في كنه أزلك عن لواحق الأضداد ، وعلائق الأنداد. لأنك فرد لا كالأفراد، وواحد لا كالأحاد، ومتعالي عن علائق عالم الكون والفساد. يا سابق الفوت، وياسامع كل صوت ، ويا محيي العظام الرميمة بعد الموت . أسألك بحق وجوب وجودك ، وكمال جودك : أن لا تجعلني من جنات رحمتك من المحرومين والمردودين، وأن تفيض على جسدي وروحي قطرة من قطرات فيض فضلك . يا أرحم الراحمين ، ويا أكرم الأكرمين والحمد لله رب العالمين وصلوات الله على سيدنا محمد النبي ، وعلى آله الطيبين الطاهرين أجمعين . قال مصنف الكتاب - قدس الله روحه - في آخر هذا المكتوب : تم هذا الكتاب ليلة السبت السابع عشر من جمادي الأولى سنة خمس وستمائة . والحمد الله كما يستحقه . تم الجز الخامس من كتاب: المطالب العالية من العلم الإلهي ، ويليه الجزء السادس وهو ( في الهيولى ) .
Its stability, and the causes of its peaks in the highest of its regions have amazed him. He seeks witness from all species for His lordship, from their incapacity for His power, from their originatedness for His eternality, and from their passing away for His permanence. So no limit is attributed to Him, no likeness is struck for Him, and nothing is veiled from Him. Exalted is He above striking similitudes and the attributes of created things, a great exaltation. Our master, the caller to God, God's mercy be upon him, said: O God of the worlds. Indeed You mentioned in Your noble Book: 'Say: To whom belongs what is in the heavens and the earth? Say: To God.' So by this brilliant proof and overwhelming evidence it is established that space and spatial things are submissive to You with humility and lowliness, acknowledging to You poverty and abasement, proclaiming against themselves lowliness, insignificance, incapacity, and neediness. Then You said: 'And to Him belongs what rests in the night and the day.' So You manifested by this hidden secret and known principle that time and temporal things are under Your creation, determination, invention, and management. So space and spatial things, and time and temporal things, all speak with the evidences of Your glory and greatness, acknowledging the necessity of Your existence and Your eternality. For time, by its motion, indicated that You are free from motions, and space, by its stillness, acknowledged that You are exalted above stillnesses. So You are exalted in the essence of Your eternity from the contingencies of opposites and the attachments of rivals. Because You are singular not like individuals, and one not like units, and exalted above the attachments of the world of generation and corruption. O Outstripper of passing, O Hearer of every voice, O Reviver of decayed bones after death. I ask You by the right of the necessity of Your existence and the perfection of Your generosity: not to make me among those deprived and rejected from the gardens of Your mercy, and to pour upon my body and soul a drop from the drops of the flood of Your favor. O Most Merciful of the merciful, O Most Generous of the generous. And praise be to God, Lord of the worlds, and blessings of God be upon our master Muhammad the Prophet, and upon his pure and good family altogether. The author of the book, may God sanctify his soul, said at the end of this writing: This book was completed on the night of Saturday, the seventeenth of Jumada al-Ula of the year six hundred and five. And praise be to God as He deserves. The fifth part of the book Al-Matalib al-Aliya from the Divine Science is completed, and the sixth part follows it, which is (On Hyle).

Commentary
This passage is a concluding supplication and colophon. It begins with a description of how creation testifies to God: by the totality of species to His lordship, their incapacity to His power, their originatedness to His eternality, and their passing away to His permanence. Then the author quotes a prayer addressing God, drawing on Qur'anic verses (6:12-13) to argue that space and spatial things, time and temporal things, all confess God's necessity and eternality. The prayer highlights that time's motion proves God is free from motion, and space's stillness proves God is exalted above stillness. It emphasizes God's uniqueness and transcendence above contingencies and rivals. The prayer ends with a request for mercy and blessings on the Prophet. Finally, the colophon states that the book was completed on the 17th of Jumada al-Ula, 605 AH.
