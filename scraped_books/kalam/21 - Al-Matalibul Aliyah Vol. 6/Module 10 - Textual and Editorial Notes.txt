Author's Colophon on Completion
Author records the completion time of a problem.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the purpose and typical contents of a scholarly colophon in Islamic manuscripts.

Core Idea
Colophons anchor a text in time and authorial care.

Before diving into the next problem, the author pauses to record when and how this one was completed. Such colophons are small but significant features of classical texts: they connect the reader to the human act of writing and often provide precious dating clues. This short note does exactly that.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic colophon once, then its translation. Notice the specific details recorded: day, time, astronomical sign, and year.
These details are not random—they reflect the author's precision.
2
Identify the elements of a colophon
List the pieces of information included: day of week, time of day, astronomical reference, Hijri year, and a closing praise.
This is a standard but compact colophon format.
3
Reflect on the function
Consider why authors add such notes. They mark completion, express gratitude, and help later scholars date the work.
In this case, the author also invokes a blessing ('may God sanctify his secret').

The author of the book – may God sanctify his secret – said: This problem was completed on the morning of Sunday, at the time of the sun's transition into Capricorn, in the year six hundred and five. And praise be to God in all circumstances.
Source pages p. 167

Commentary
This is a colophon where the author of the book records the completion of this particular problem (mas'ala). It includes the day of the week (Sunday), the time of day (morning, bukra), the astrological sign (the sun entering Capricorn), and the Hijri year (605 AH). Such notes are valuable for establishing the date of composition and the author's personal dedication.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?

?
