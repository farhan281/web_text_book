Argument from Numbers to Infinite Divisibility
Syllogistic argument from numbers to divisibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the infinite increase of numbers serves as a proof for the infinite divisibility of magnitudes.

Core Idea
If numbers can increase without limit, then magnitudes can be divided without limit.

This short passage presents a powerful analogy: the infinity of numerical increase mirrors the potential infinity of division in physical magnitudes. Building on a prior premise, the author draws a necessary conclusion about the nature of spatial quantities.

Guided Path
1
Position the argument
Note the opening phrase 'If you know this' – the argument depends on a previous premise. Identify what that premise might be: the infinite increase of numbers is already established.
This is a conditional proof: if the premise holds, the conclusion is necessary.
2
Trace the logical chain
Follow the reasoning: infinite numerical increase → infinite diminution of that acute thing → magnitudes are divisible ad infinitum. Ensure you see how each step follows.
The middle step connects quantity to spatial divisibility.
3
Understand the modal necessity
The author uses 'فوجب القطع' (it is necessary to conclude). Reflect on why this conclusion is deemed necessary and what kind of necessity (logical, metaphysical) is implied.
The necessity stems from the analogy and the impossibility of a limit in the cause implying a limit in the effect.

The Fourth Geometric Argument Against Atoms
Geometric proof against indivisible atoms via right triangle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a geometric argument using a right triangle disproves the existence of indivisible parts (atoms).

Core Idea
Geometry proves that atoms cannot form a triangle without requiring division.

This lesson presents a sophisticated geometric argument from the Islamic theological tradition against the concept of indivisible atoms (جوهر فرد). By constructing an equilateral triangle with sides composed of three atomic parts and analyzing the lengths of its internal lines, the argument forces a contradiction unless the parts are divisible. Prepare to follow a precise geometric reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysing, then the translation. Let the argument's flow come through before interpreting.
2
Identify the construction
Notice that the equilateral triangle is built on a line of three parts, and a perpendicular is dropped from one angle to the opposite side.
This creates two right triangles.
3
Follow the analysis of hypotenuses
The argument identifies three hypotenuses: the right triangle's (3 parts), the half-acute's (2 parts), and the acute's (the perpendicular, unknown).
4
Understand the contradiction
If the perpendicular is 3 or 2 parts, it creates impossible equalities. Both options lead to contradictions.
This is the key step.
5
Draw the conclusion
The only remaining possibility is that the perpendicular is between 2 and 3, meaning the parts are divisible, thus atoms cannot exist.
This refutes the indivisible atom.

The Pythagorean Argument Against Indivisible Atoms
Using the Pythagorean theorem to refute indivisible atoms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Pythagorean theorem is used to argue that indivisible atoms cannot compose geometric lengths because most right triangles have irrational hypotenuses.

Core Idea
If space is made of atoms, then every hypotenuse is irrational — impossible.

This argument from classical Islamic kalam uses geometry to challenge the atomic theory. It shows that if lines are composed of a finite number of indivisible parts, then the Pythagorean theorem forces many hypotenuses to be irrational, contradicting the assumption that all lengths are expressible in integer parts. This passage presents several examples to make the point.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and the theorem statement
Read the Arabic passage once. Notice the opening statement of the Pythagorean theorem (Shape of the Bride). Then read the translation.
The phrase 'شكل العروس' refers to the Pythagorean theorem.
2
Understand the argument setup
The author assumes lines are made of a finite number of indivisible parts. He constructs right triangles with integer sides and computes the hypotenuse.
If sides are integer multiples of the atom, the hypotenuse should also be expressible in atoms.
3
Examine the list of examples
Go through each example: sides (2,2) gives √8; (2,3) √13; (2,4) √20; (2,5) √29; (3,3) √18; (3,4) √25=5; (3,5) √34. Note which are irrational.
The only rational case is 3-4-5, but it doesn't disprove the general claim.
4
Understand the conclusion
The author concludes that most hypotenuses are irrational, which forces the breaking of the atom (انكسار الأجزاء). This refutes atomism.
Irrational means it cannot be expressed as a ratio of two integers, thus cannot be composed of indivisible atoms.

The Incoherence of Atomism: The Right Triangle Argument
Presents a right triangle argument that forces the hypotenuse to be between two integers, requiring division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the geometry of a right triangle composed of indivisible atoms forces the atoms to be divisible, thus refuting atomism.

Core Idea
A right triangle's hypotenuse cannot be a whole number of atoms, so atoms must be divisible.

This lesson presents a clever geometrical argument from Mulla Sadra against the atomist theory of indivisible parts. By constructing a simple right triangle from indivisible atoms, the author shows that the hypotenuse cannot be an integer number of atoms, which contradicts the atomist claim. The argument proceeds by exhaustion: the hypotenuse must be either equal to one side, equal to the sum of sides, or between them. The first two are impossible, and the third implies division.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation

The author examines three possible lengths for the hypotenuse: 4 parts, 5 parts, or a number between 4 and 5. Understand why each is considered.

Understand why the first two are impossible
If the hypotenuse equals 4 parts, it equals one leg (contradiction). If it equals 5 parts, it equals the sum of the legs (impossible in a right triangle).
In a right triangle, the hypotenuse is longer than either leg but shorter than the sum.
4
Realize the consequence of the third possibility
If the hypotenuse is between 4 and 5, then it is not an integer number of atoms, so atoms must be divisible.
This directly refutes the atomist claim that lengths are integer multiples of indivisibles.
5

This is a limitation of the source, but the pattern is clear.

The Third Objection: Past Events Must Have a Beginning
Applies the argument to past events, concluding they must have a first event.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the argument of increase and decrease is applied to past events to show that they must have a beginning, and why this challenges the philosophers' view of an eternal universe.

Core Idea
Comparing past event sets forces a beginning even for an infinite past.

In this passage, the author extends a previous argument to the realm of past events. By comparing the number of events from different starting points back to eternity, he forces the conclusion that events must have a beginning. This directly contradicts the philosophers' claim that the universe has no temporal beginning.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation. Notice the key terms: حوادث (events), أزل (eternity), أول (beginning).
The passage is a single objection in a series.
2
Identify the comparison being made
The author compares two sets of past events: from the time of the Flood to azal, and from our present time to azal. The second set is larger by the period from Flood to now.
This is a finite difference between two infinite sets.
3
Apply the argument of increase and decrease
The argument states that if one infinite set is smaller than another by a finite amount, the smaller must actually be finite. Therefore, the events from Flood to azal must be finite, meaning events began at some point before the Flood.
This forces a beginning (أول) for all past events.
4
Understand the conclusion and its implication
The conclusion is that events must have a first event. This contradicts the philosophers' belief that the universe has existed eternally with an infinite past of events.
The objection is presented as a problem for the opposing view.

The Objection of Rational Souls and Initial Response
Author presents the objection, answer, and a criticism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the objection that rational souls pose to the proof against infinite regress and the philosophers' initial answer based on the condition of order.

Core Idea
The proof requires order; rational souls lack it, so the proof does not apply.

The proof against infinite regress relies on the idea that an incomplete set must be finite when compared to a larger set. However, rational souls seem to form an infinite multiplicity without order. In this lesson, we read the objection and the philosophers' immediate response, as well as a theologian's criticism of that response.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
Read the Arabic passage from 'وأما السؤال الثاني' to 'هذا غاية ما وصل إلينا في هذا المقام'. Identify the objection (rational souls) and the philosophers' answer (requires order).
Notice that the philosophers claim that infinite regress is only impossible for ordered sets.
2
Understand the condition of order
The philosophers specify two types of order: natural (like celestial cycles) and positional (like spatial dimensions). Rational souls have neither.
This distinction is key to the rest of the argument.
3
Consider the theologian's criticism
Some theologians argue that the proof's core logic (incomplete set terminates while additional set remains) works regardless of order. Reflect on why this seems plausible.
The theologian's objection sets up the need for a deeper defense.

But one may say: This criterion which the philosophers mentioned is very reliable. And if we mean by its being finite that it lacks what exists in another, then it becomes as if we said: And every set that lacks what exists in another lacks what exists in another. It is known that this speech is nonsense. And if we mean by its being finite the necessity of the deficient coming to a rank beyond which there is nothing else, then this only occurs in that which has order by position or by nature. What is not like that does not have this meaning. So it is established that this mentioned proof is only valid for number that has order by nature or by position. As for that which is not like that, this speech does not apply to it.

Commentary
This passage handles the second objection to the proof against infinite regress, raised by those who appeal to rational souls. The author first states the philosophers' initial answer: the proof requires order (tartīb) in the set, either natural (as in celestial cycles) or positional (as in spatial dimensions). Rational souls lack both orders, so the proof does not apply. Some theologians object that this distinction is weak because the core of the proof is simply that an incomplete set (al-jumla al-nāqiṣa) terminates while the additional set continues, implying finitude regardless of order. The author then provides a counter-defence: the criterion of order is essential. He explains that when two ordered sets are superimposed, correspondence by rank forces the deficient set to end. For ordered sets, each element corresponds to a unique element in the larger set, leading to a necessary termination. For unordered sets, the meaning of 'deficient' becomes either trivial (a set that lacks something another has) or circular (a set that lacks something is a set that lacks something), neither proving finitude. Only when deficiency implies a last element beyond which there is nothing—which requires order—does the proof work. Therefore, the proof is valid only for ordered multiplicities.

Defending the Order Criterion: Correspondence and Finitude
Author defends the order criterion through correspondence and analysis of unordered sets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the philosophers insist on the condition of order and how the proof of finitude through correspondence works for ordered sets, while failing for unordered ones.

Core Idea
Ordered sets allow precise correspondence that forces finitude; unordered sets do not.

The theologians argued that the proof's logic works regardless of order. The philosophers now respond with a detailed defense, explaining the mechanism of correspondence in ordered sets and showing that the concept of deficiency in unordered sets is either trivial or circular. This lesson unpacks that defense.

Guided Path
1
Understand the correspondence argument for positional order
Read the explanation starting with 'ولقائل أن يقول'. Note how when two ordered sets are superimposed, each element corresponds uniquely, forcing the deficient set to end.
The example uses spans (ashbār) to illustrate the point.
2
Apply the same logic to natural order
The argument extends to sets with natural order (like effects), where the last effect corresponds to the last, and so on, leading to termination.
The key is the one-to-one correspondence by rank.
3
See why unordered sets fail
The author analyzes the meaning of 'deficient' for unordered sets, showing it either reduces to a tautology or a circular statement, thus proving nothing about finitude.
This shows that the proof only works when deficiency implies a last element beyond which there is nothing, which requires order.

Third Objection: Past Motions and the Sum of Events
Author refutes the objection based on past motions by distinguishing individual events from their sum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the sum of past events cannot be considered a valid subject of quantitative judgment, and how this refutes the objection of infinite regress.

Core Idea
The sum of past events has no existence, so it cannot be measured.

This passage addresses a key objection to the finitude of past events. The opponent argues that past motions are infinite, but the author shows that the sum of past events is not a real entity and thus cannot be described as more or less.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Start by reading the Arabic passage and its literal translation to get a sense of the author's argument.
Pay attention to the distinction between 'each individual event' and 'their sum'.
2
Identify the two possible subjects
Notice that the author considers two possibilities for what is judged to be more or less: each individual past event, or their sum.
The first is conceded, the second is rejected.
3
Understand why the sum fails to exist
The author argues that the sum has no existence, externally because past events are gone, and mentally because the mind cannot grasp an infinite series.
Without existence, it cannot be described as increasing or decreasing.
4
See how this refutes the objection
The objection assumes the sum of past motions is an infinite quantity, but the author shows it is not a real entity, so the objection collapses.
This blocks the argument for an infinite regress.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth argument for the composition of body from hyle and form. It is a proof by considering the different properties of celestial spheres (al-aflāk) versus sublunary bodies. The author sets up a conditional: if body were simple (not composite), then the celestial spheres would be capable of being torn apart and recombined, like terrestrial bodies. But philosophers agree that celestial spheres are not susceptible to such changes. Therefore, the conditional must be false, i.e., bodies are composite. The author then systematically eliminates all possible reasons for the special property of the spheres. Bodies are equal in their essence (body-ness, jismiyya). Therefore, only (3) remains: the impossibility is due to the specific substrate (matter) that receives that particular body-ness. This substrate is hyle, which accepts only a specific form (that of the celestial sphere). Thus body must be composite of hyle and form. The conclusion: each body has a specific matter that determines its form and shape, explaining its unique properties. This is a sophisticated argument from differentiation of properties to the necessity of a material substrate with specific capacities.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the opponents who posit matter (hyele) as a component of bodies, based on the principle that 'the one does not issue from the one.' The Fourth Objection: The Bodily Form and Accidents
Fourth objection: bodily form would need to be composite to receive accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the bodily form itself would have to be composite by the opponents' logic.

Core Idea
The bodily form, which is supposed to actualize hyele, would itself need to be composite to receive accidents.

The fourth objection targets the bodily form. The opponents claim that the body, not hyele alone, is receptive of motion, rest, color, and shape. But the bodily form is what actualizes hyele into a body. If the bodily form itself makes the body receptive, then the bodily form must be able to receive accidents. The Third Argument: 'Long' and the Indivisible Atom
Refutation of the argument that 'long' implies an attribute, using the indivisible atom theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes the argument that the term 'long' implies an attribute necessitating composition, using the indivisible atom theory.

Core Idea
The term 'long' does not prove composition; it only denotes a sum of atoms.
