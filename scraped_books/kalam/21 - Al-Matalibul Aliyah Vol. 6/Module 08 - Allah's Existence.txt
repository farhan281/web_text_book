First Proof of the Indivisible Atom: Introduction and First Argument
Author introduces the first geometrical proof for the indivisible atom and gives the first argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a geometric argument involving a sphere and a plane establishes the indivisibility of the contact point, thereby proving the existence of the indivisible atom.

Core Idea
A sphere's point of contact with a plane must be indivisible, proving the atom.

This passage begins the first geometrical proof for the indivisible atom. The author argues that when a true sphere meets a flat surface, the exact point of contact cannot be divided. If it were divisible, absurd consequences would follow. The first of four arguments is presented here, using a reductio ad absurdum: if the contact point were divisible, the sphere would contain a flat surface, which is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the Arabic passage
Read the Arabic text once to get the flow of the argument. Note the technical terms: الكرة الحقيقية (real sphere), موضع الملاقاة (place of contact), غير منقسم (indivisible).
2
Understand the proof structure
The author states the proof: a sphere touching a plane has an indivisible contact point, implying the existence of the indivisible atom. Then he begins the first of four ways to explain why the contact point is indivisible.
The first argument uses two absurd consequences to prove indivisibility.
3
Analyze the first argument
Focus on the reductio: if the contact point were divisible, it would coincide with the plane, making the sphere have a flat surface (impossible). Also, if the sphere rolls, the new contact would be with another indivisible part, leading to further absurdities.
The rolling argument shows the sphere would extend in a straight line, which is impossible for a sphere.
4
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this geometric proof is used to establish the indivisible atom. The contact point must be a simple, indivisible entity, supporting atomic theory.
This is a classical kalam argument that does not rely on physics but on pure geometry.

Indivisibility via Geometric Construction
Geometric proof of indivisibility via triangle and perpendicular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the geometric argument that proves the indivisibility of the contact point through a contradiction from unequal radii.

Core Idea
Geometric equality of radii forces the contact point to be indivisible.

In this lesson, we examine the second argument from al-Matalib al-Aliyah, which uses a geometric construction with a sphere and triangle to demonstrate that the point of contact must be indivisible. The argument relies on a contradiction: if it were divisible, three radii would become unequal, impossible for a sphere.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its translation once without analyzing. Notice the conditional structure: 'if it were divisible...'.
The argument moves from assumption to construction, to deduction, to contradiction.
2
Visualize the geometric setup
Imagine a sphere with center C. The contact point is an arc (or line segment) on the surface. Assume it is divisible, so it has two endpoints. Draw two lines from C to the endpoints. Together with the arc (now a chord), they form a triangle.
The 'place of contact' is treated as a chord for the construction.
3
Understand the perpendicular drop
From the center, drop a perpendicular to the chord. This divides the triangle into two right triangles. Note the properties: the two angles at the perpendicular are right angles, making the perpendicular the longest side? Actually, the argument uses a known theorem: the hypotenuse of a right triangle is longer than the side adjacent to an acute angle.
In a right triangle, the hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle.
4
Identify the contradiction
The perpendicular line (from center to chord) is a radius. The two lines from center to endpoints are also radii. All radii must be equal, but the argument shows the perpendicular is shorter than the two side lines. This contradiction proves the initial assumption wrong.
The conclusion is that the contact point cannot be divisible; it is indivisible.

The two lines at the ends become the hypotenuses of those two right angles. And that perpendicular becomes the hypotenuse of the two acute angles at the ends. And it is known that the hypotenuse of a right angle is greater than the hypotenuse of an acute angle. So this perpendicular line is shorter than the two lines at the ends. Commentary
This passage presents the second argument (al-wajh al-thānī) for the indivisibility of the contact point. This contradiction proves the contact point is indivisible.

Geometric Lemma and Initial Absurdity of Infinite Divisibility
Presents the geometric lemma and its immediate application to show absurdity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how a geometric theorem from Euclid is used to show that infinite divisibility leads to an absurdity regarding angles that never become equal.

Core Idea
Euclid's horn angles prove that infinite division leads to an eternal gap.

This lesson introduces the second proof against infinite divisibility of magnitudes. The author draws on Euclid's geometry to demonstrate a contradiction: if magnitudes could be divided infinitely, then certain angles would simultaneously increase and decrease without ever meeting, which is rationally impossible. The proof uses the concept of horn angles and their relation to rectilinear angles.

Guided Path

Identify the initial claim and contradiction

The First Proof: Finitude of an Infinite Line
First proof for finitude of dimensions via thought experiment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the first proof for the finitude of spatial dimensions using the thought experiment of an infinite line.

Core Idea
An infinite line cannot exist; adding a finite part leads to contradiction.

This lesson examines the first proof from Al-Matalibul Aliyah for the finitude of dimensions. Through a thought experiment with an infinite line, the author demonstrates that assuming infinite extension leads to absurd consequences. Understanding this proof is crucial for kalam arguments about the contingency of the world.

Guided Path
1
Set up the thought experiment
Imagine a line starting from a fixed point and extending infinitely. Then add a finite cubit to it from the side nearest to you. Align the ends of the added part and the original part.
The line is assumed to be infinite in one direction, and we add a finite length.
2
Consider the first possibility
If after adding, the line still goes to infinity without showing any deficiency, then the whole would equal its part, which is absurd. Also, a thing with another would be the same as without it.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Consider the second possibility
If deficiency appears, it must appear on the far side, not the near side, because we aligned the near ends. So the original line must be finite on that far side.
The added part is finite, so the total is finite.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since both possibilities lead to the conclusion that the whole is finite, the original assumption of an infinite line is impossible.
This proves dimensions are finite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the first proof for the finitude of bodies is accused of circularity due to presupposing finitude in the act of applying ends.

Core Idea
Applying ends of lines requires finitude, so using it to prove finitude is circular.

The first proof for the finitude of bodies relies on applying the end of an increasing line to a decreasing one. But an objector argues that this very act of application already assumes the line is finite. In this lesson, we examine this objection step by step.
