Bodyness Is Distinct from Dimensions: Refuting the Composite View
Argument that bodyness is distinct from dimensions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the body's essence (jismiyya) is distinct from its dimensions (length, width, depth) and how the author refutes the definition of body as an extended substance.

Core Idea
Dimensions are accidental to body, not part of its essence.

In this passage, the author responds to those who deny atomism and argue that body is simple. He presents their view and then refutes the notion that length, width, and depth are essential to body. Through three powerful arguments, he shows that bodyness (jismiyya) is distinct from magnitude (miqdar), and thus the definition of body as 'long, wide, deep' is invalid. This lesson unpacks that crucial distinction.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Notice the overall flow: first the claim of simplicity, then discussion of lines and surface, then three proofs about magnitude.
2
Identify the three proofs
The passage lists three distinct proofs that magnitude is distinct from bodyness. Find each one: wax example, equality/difference in bodies, rarefaction and condensation.
Each proof shows that bodyness remains while magnitude changes.
3
Understand the surface argument
Note how surface is necessary in external reality but not in mental existence. This shows surface is not part of the essence of body.
Contrast with magnitude which is always present both externally and mentally.
4
Reflect on the conclusion
The conclusion is that 'being a body is distinct from being long, wide, deep'. Ask yourself why this matters for defining a body.
This refutes the common definition of body as an extended substance.

The conclusion: being a body is other than being long, wide, deep, so the Mu'tazilite definition fails.

First Two Proofs: Dimensions Exist Before Supposition
Al-Razi gives two proofs that the extension exists before supposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Al-Razi's first two proofs that dimensions exist prior to any mental supposition, and why the idea they are created by supposition leads to a vicious circle.

Core Idea
Indication presupposes existence; dimensions cannot be caused by our pointing.

In this section, Al-Razi presents the first two proofs against the claim that dimensions only come into existence when we suppose them. The first proof argues that the extension must have existed before any supposition, otherwise the body itself would not exist. The second proof shows that if the extension were caused by the act of indication, it would lead to a logical circle.

Guided Path
1
Read the first proof
Read the passage beginning with 'First: This line is an expression...' and grasp the argument: if the extension did not exist before supposition, then the body (which is that extension) would not have existed before.
The key point: body = extended thing; if extension is new, body is new.
2
Read the second proof
Read the second proof about the vicious circle. Understand that indication of something requires its prior existence, so the extension cannot be caused by the indication without circularity.
The term 'dawr' (vicious circle) is a standard refutation in kalam.
3
Compare the two proofs
Notice how the first proof is direct (logical entailment) while the second is indirect (reductio ad absurdum). Both aim to prove the pre-existence of dimensions.

First Proof Against the Indivisible Part: Slow Motion
Reductio ad absurdum proof that slowness is not due to interspersed rests.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why slow motion cannot be caused by interspersed rests, and how this argument refutes the existence of the indivisible part.

Core Idea
Slow motion is not a mix of motion and rest—it is continuous, slower movement.

This lesson presents a powerful reductio argument from classical Islamic theology. By comparing a fast horse and the celestial sphere, the author demonstrates that slow motion cannot involve rests, thereby negating the indivisible part (al-juz' alladhi la yatajazza').

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, then its literal translation. Let the author's own rhythm and structure sink in.
Pay attention to the key terms: بطء (slowness), تخلل السكنات (interpolation of rests).
2
Identify the claim and the assumption
The author states the conclusion first: if slowness is not due to rests, then the indivisible part is false. Then he sets up the first proof: comparing a horse and the celestial sphere.
3
Follow the ratio argument
The argument establishes that if slowness were due to rests, the ratio of rests to motions in the horse would equal the ratio of the sphere's speed to the horse's speed, leading to an absurdity.
The sphere is over a million times faster, so the horse would have over a million times more rests than motions.
4
See the absurd consequence
If the horse had that many rests, the sphere's motion would be imperceptible because it would be interrupted. Yet we perceive the sphere's motion continuously.
The consequent is false; we sense only successive motions, not rests.
5
Conclude the proof
The impossibility of the prior assumption proves that slowness is not from rests, yielding certainty (qat') that the indivisible part does not exist.
