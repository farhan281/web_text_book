Answering the Rational Objections about Angelic Nature and Deeds
Two rational objections and their answers regarding angelic nature and deeds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the author refutes rational arguments that angels are superior to prophets due to their spiritual nature and abundant good deeds.

Core Idea
Angels may have certain advantages, but prophets have unique merits that outweigh them.

This lesson addresses the strongest rational objections: that angels are purely spiritual, free from material defects, and have more enduring deeds and knowledge. The author counters by pointing out that these arguments are based on philosophical assumptions, not religious teachings, and that prophets have distinct qualities like overcoming opposition and bearing hardships.

Guided Path
1
Read the first rational objection and answer

The objection lists many attributes: spiritual, free from matter, no lust/anger, powerful, knowledgeable of the unseen.
2
Understand the author's reply
The author dismisses this objection by stating it is based on philosophical principles, not on the divine law (الملة).
This is a powerful refutation: the objection uses non-Islamic premises.
3
Read the second rational objection and answer
Continue with 'ومنها أن أعمالهم المستوجبة للمثوبات أكثر' to the end. Note how the author acknowledges the points but counters with the unique challenges prophets face.
The answer highlights that prophets endure hardships and opposition, which angels do not.

Commentary
This passage is a series of three objections and answers defending the superiority of prophets over angels. The first objection arises from the verse 'And never will the Jews nor the Christians be pleased with you' and the mention of angels as 'the close ones'. The author explains that the context is refuting Christian claims about Jesus, and the mention of angels does not indicate superiority in reward or perfection, but rather in the occasion of disdain. The second objection is the consistent pattern of mentioning angels before prophets in the Quran. The answer is that this may be due to their precedence in existence or the strength of faith in them since they are hidden. The third objection presents rational arguments: angels are spiritual, free from matter, lust, and anger, powerful, and know the unseen. The author responds that these are philosophical principles, not religious ones. A further rational argument is that angels' good deeds and knowledge are more abundant and enduring. The answer is that prophets have other aspects like overcoming opposition and bearing hardships that make their deeds superior.

The Shii Argument: The Imam Must Be Infallible to Avoid Infinite Regress
Al-Taftazani presents the fourth Shii objection regarding the imam's infallibility and the threat of infinite regress.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the imam can err, an infinite chain of imams is required.

The Shia argue that the community needs an imam because they are fallible. For the imam to truly be a grace (luṭf), he must be infallible—otherwise, you would need another imam to correct him, leading to an infinite regress. They compare this to the cosmological argument that ends with a necessary being.
