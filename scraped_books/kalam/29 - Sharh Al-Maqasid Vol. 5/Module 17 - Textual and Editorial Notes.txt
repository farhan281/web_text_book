A Biographical Note on a Scholar from Isfahan
A brief biography of a scholar from Isfahan with death year and references.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how to read and interpret a brief biographical footnote from a classical Islamic text, including its key components and the significance of the referenced sources.

Core Idea
Biographical footnotes anchor scholarly debates in historical personalities.

In classical Islamic works, authors often insert brief biographical notices to identify figures mentioned in the text. This note from a commentary provides a glimpse into the life of a scholar from Isfahan, though his name is missing from the manuscript. Understanding such footnotes helps us reconstruct the intellectual history of the period.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and translation
Read the Arabic passage once, then read the English translation. Note that the scholar's name is omitted in the text—imagine how this might affect interpretation.
The passage begins abruptly, suggesting it is a marginal note.
2
Identify key biographical details
From the text, extract the scholar's origin (villages of Isfahan), his activities (sessions and debates with theologians), and his death year (298 AH).
The death year places him in the early 4th century AH / 10th century CE.
3
Examine the list of references
The author cites multiple works: Wafayat al-A'yan, Tarikh Ibn al-Wardi, Muruj al-Dhahab, etc. Notice that these are standard biographical and historical sources.
The references show the scholarly network and the importance of cross-referencing.
4
Reflect on the purpose of the footnote
Consider why the commentator included this note. It likely identifies a figure mentioned earlier in the main text, providing context for his debates.
Even without the name, the note indicates the scholar's significance.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical footnote about a scholar from the villages of Isfahan, as related by Ibn Khallikan. It notes that he held scholarly sessions and debates with theologians, and that he died in 298 AH. The footnote provides a list of references where more information can be found: Wafayat al-A'yan, Tarikh Ibn al-Wardi, Muruj al-Dhahab, al-Bidaya wa al-Nihaya, al-Milal wa al-Nihal, Sharh Nahj al-Balagha, and Ma'ahid al-Tansis. The identity of the scholar is not explicitly given in this snippet, but it appears to be a reference note in the margin of the original text.
