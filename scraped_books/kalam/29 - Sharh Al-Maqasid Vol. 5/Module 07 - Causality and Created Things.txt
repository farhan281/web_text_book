Refuting the Objection That Parts Are Easily Imitable
Author refutes the first objection to Quranic inimitability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the inimitability of the Quran as a whole does not apply to its individual parts, and how the author refutes the claim that Arabs could produce similar short phrases.

Core Idea
The whole is greater than the sum of its parts – Quranic inimitability applies to composition, not isolated words.

Some skeptics argue that since the Quran is composed of common Arabic words and short phrases like 'al-hamdu lillah', any Arab could produce similar expressions. This objection misunderstands how inimitability works. In this lesson, we examine the author's response, which uses the principle that the ruling of the whole differs from the ruling of its parts.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection and answer
First, read the objection that focuses on individual words and short compounds. Then read the author's response, which distinguishes the whole from its parts.
Notice how the objection assumes that ability to produce parts implies ability to produce the whole.
2
Understand the analogy with poetry
The author compares the Quran to the odes of Imru' al-Qais: even though the words are common, no one can reproduce the whole poem. This shows the fallacy of the objection.
The analogy highlights that inimitability is about the entire composition.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes two objections against the inimitability of the Quran. The first objection claims that because the Quran consists of short words and phrases like 'al-hamdu lillah' and 'Rabb al-'alamin', an Arab could produce similar phrases. The author answers that the whole has a ruling different from its parts; analogously, an individual Arab cannot produce an entire ode like those of Imru' al-Qais, even though its parts are common words. The second objection notes that the Companions required testimony from reliable witnesses when collecting the Quran, and Ibn Mas'ud hesitated about al-Fatihah and al-Mu'awwidhatain, implying that the Quran's composition alone was not sufficient proof. The author answers that the collection was after the Prophet's death, and the precaution was to prevent even trivial changes that do not affect inimitability. Commentary
This passage is a defense of the reality and possibility of sorcery (siḥr) according to Ahl al-Ḥaqq (the People of Truth), refuting the Mu'tazila who deny its reality. The author first lists characteristics of sorcery that distinguish it from miracles (mu'jizāt), then states that it is intellectually possible and established by revelation. The Mu'tazila claim it is mere illusion, like sleight of hand. The proof for its possibility is the same as for miracles: the thing itself is possible and God's power encompasses it; the sorcerer is only an agent and acquirer (kāsib) in the Ash'ari sense. Additionally, there is consensus of jurists on its occurrence. The author provides three textual proofs: (1) the verse about Harut and Marut (Q 2:102) which teaches sorcery and mentions separating spouses, indicating it is real and that only God is the true effecter; (2) Surah al-Falaq, revealed because of the Jewish sorcerer Labid who bewitched the Prophet; (3) reports that Aisha and Ibn Umar were bewitched. Then the author addresses two objections. First objection: If sorcery were real, sorcerers would harm all prophets and gain power, and the Prophet was protected by God's promise (Q 5:67) and the verse 'the sorcerer never succeeds' (Q 20:69), yet disbelievers called him bewitched. The answer: Sorcery is limited in time and place; the Prophet's protection is from being killed or from flaw in prophethood, not from bodily harm; the disbelievers meant he was insane. Second objection: The verse about Moses' staff (Q 20:66) says 'it seemed to him from their sorcery that they were moving', implying illusion. The answer: Their sorcery may have caused actual illusion; even if in that case it was mere illusion, that does not prove sorcery has no reality at all.

Philosophical objections collapse if their premises are not granted.

The author's answer challenges the philosophical underpinnings of this argument, reminding us that kalam does not accept all premises of Greek philosophy. Second Objection: Simultaneous Cessation and Circularity
Taftazani records the second objection and its answer regarding simultaneous cessation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the second objection to the causal argument and Taftazani's response clarifying why simultaneous cessation does not avoid circularity when causation is assumed.

Core Idea
Simultaneous cessation cannot avoid circularity when one event causes the other.

The objection claims that if the cessation between a new event and the removal of a prior one is simultaneous, circularity is avoided. Taftazani answers by clarifying that on the assumption of causation, priority in essence is necessary, so simultaneous cessation is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing it. Then read the translation. Let the author's rhythm come through before interpreting.
Notice that Taftazani uses a very compressed argumentative style.
2
Identify the objection
The objector claims that the cessation (tawaqquf) between the occurrence of a new accident and the removal of a prior one could be a cessation of simultaneity (tawaqquf ma'iyya) without temporal priority, thus avoiding circularity (al-dawr al-muhal).
This is a potential loophole to the earlier argument.
3
Understand the answer
Taftazani responds that the discussion is based on the assumption that the new event is the cause (sabab) of the removal of the prior one. Therefore, the new event must precede the removal in essence (bi al-dhat), which contradicts the idea that the new event is conditional upon the prior removal, as that would require it to be later in essence.
The key is the concept of essential priority (taqaddum bi al-dhat).
4
Reflect on the logic
Consider why simultaneous cessation would contradict the causality assumption. If A causes B, then A must be prior to B in essence, not simultaneous. Thus the objection fails.
This shows the necessity of essential priority in causal relations.
