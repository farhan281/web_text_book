The Two Meanings of Nazm in Qur'anic Composition
Classification of nazm into two meanings: structural and contextual eloquence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the two distinct meanings of nazm (composition) as they relate to the Qur'an's inimitability, and how each meaning contributes to the argument for i'jāz.

Core Idea
Nazm is both a structural break from Arab norms and a matchless eloquence suited to every context.

The concept of nazm (composition) is central to understanding the Qur'an's linguistic miracle. Scholars have distinguished two ways in which the Qur'an's composition sets it apart. This passage from Sharh al-Maqasid clarifies these two meanings, one focusing on the unusual patterns of its verses and the other on its perfect eloquence and contextual appropriateness. Let's explore both.

Guided Path

2

The first meaning focuses on structural difference. Look for examples of unusual verse endings and openings that the author provides.
Examples include 'ya'lamūn', 'yaf'alūn', 'yā ayyuhā al-nās', 'al-ḥāqqah mā al-ḥāqqah', etc.
3
Identify the second meaning of nazm
The second meaning contrasts with the first: it is about eloquence (faṣāḥah) and conformity to context (muqtaḍā al-ḥāl) beyond human capacity.
This meaning is linked to ʿAbd al-Qāhir's definition of nazm.
4
Compare the two definitions of nazm itself
The author distinguishes between nazm as mere word arrangement (first meaning) and nazm as ordered meanings with consistent indication (second meaning).
This distinction is crucial for understanding the depth of i'jāz.
5
Connect to the broader theory of i'jāz
Reflect on how both meanings together support the doctrine of Qur'anic inimitability. The first shows uniqueness, the second shows perfection.

Commentary
This passage explains the two meanings of 'nazm' (composition) mentioned earlier in the definition of i'jāz. The author first clarifies the first meaning: the Qur'an's composition differs from ordinary Arab speech in its unusual verse endings (e.g., ya'lamūn, yaf'alūn) and openings (e.g., 'yā ayyuhā al-nās', 'al-ḥāqqah mā al-ḥāqqah', etc.). These patterns are not found in Arabic poetry or prose. The second meaning is that the Qur'an's composition is supremely eloquent and perfectly suited to the context (muqtaḍā al-ḥāl), a quality beyond human capacity. The author then distinguishes between the two senses of nazm: in the first sense, it is merely the arrangement of words; in the second, it is the assembly of words with ordered meanings and consistent indications according to rational requirements. This second sense is what ʿAbd al-Qāhir al-Jurjānī defined as the pursuit of grammatical meanings to achieve the purposes of speech, a topic elaborated in the author's works on bayān.

The Logical Force of 'Law' in the Quranic Challenge
Author refutes objection about logical fallacy using 'law'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Arabic particle 'law' functions in Quranic arguments to indicate negation of the result due to negation of the condition, and how this forms a valid logical inference.

Core Idea
'Law' in Arabic logical argument negates the result when the condition is negated.

In this passage, the author addresses a common objection to the Quranic argument from the absence of discrepancy. Critics claim the inference is fallacious because it affirms the consequent. The author clarifies that the particle 'law' follows a different logical pattern: it negates the result when the condition is negated. By understanding this, we see the argument is not only valid but also consistent with standard Arabic usage.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic text and translation once to get the overall flow. Notice the objection and the author's response.
Pay attention to the logical terms like 'lazim' and 'malzum'.
2
Identify the objection
The objector claims the inference affirms the consequent. Understand why this is considered a fallacy in formal logic.
Affirming the consequent: If P then Q, Q therefore P. That is invalid.
3
Understand the function of 'law'
The author explains that 'law' in Arabic indicates that the result is negated because the condition is negated. This is different from standard conditionals.
This turns the inference into a valid modus tollens: If P then Q, not Q therefore not P.
4
See the example from Surah al-Anbiya'
The author cites 21:22 to illustrate the same pattern. Notice how 'law' is used there.
Both verses use the same logical structure.
5
Reflect on the argument's validity

The proper linguistic understanding shows the inference is valid.

For we say: No, rather it is based on the fact that the word 'law' in the language indicates the negation of the result due to the negation of the condition, meaning the non-finding of discrepancy in it because it is not from other than God. Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer regarding the logical structure of the Quranic challenge. The objector claims that the inference from the absence of discrepancy to the divine origin is flawed because it affirms the consequent. The author responds by clarifying that the particle 'law' in Arabic functions to negate the result due to the negation of the condition. In the Quranic verse (4:82), 'law' indicates that if it were from other than God, they would have found much discrepancy. Since no discrepancy is found, it follows that it is not from other than God. This is a valid inference by contrapositive: negating the consequent negates the antecedent. The author then gives another example from Surah al-Anbiya' (21:22) to illustrate the same logical pattern. He refers to his own commentary on Talkhis al-Miftah for further detail.

Classification of Rulings and Objection to Abrogation
Classification of rulings into three types and objection against abrogation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the threefold classification of rulings (temporary, perpetual, unrestricted) and why the objector argues that abrogation is impossible for perpetual and unrestricted rulings.

Core Idea
The objector divides rulings into three to argue only temporary ones can be abrogated.

Before we can resolve the issue of abrogation, we need a precise classification of rulings. The objector presents a tripartite division to argue that abrogation is either unnecessary or contradictory. This lesson sets out that classification and the objection.

Guided Path
1
Read the classification of rulings
Read the Arabic passage listing the three types: temporary, perpetual, unrestricted. Notice the examples given for each.
2
Understand the objection for each type
For temporary rulings, negating after the time is not abrogation. For perpetual, abrogation is a contradiction. For unrestricted, either it is eternal (cannot be lifted) or has an end (so no abrogation).
The objector uses this to show abrogation is impossible.
3
Grasp the underlying assumption about God's knowledge
The objector assumes that if God knows a ruling continues forever, lifting it would imply ignorance, which is impossible.
This sets up the need for a nuanced answer.

Source Text

وثانيهما: أن الحكم إما مؤقت مثل : صم غداً، فنفيه بعد ذلك لا يكون نسخاً، وإما مؤبد مثل صم أبداً. فنسخه تناقض بمنزلة قولك: الصوم واجب أبداً وليس بواجب . وإما مرسل لا توقيت فيه ولا تأبيد، وحينئذ فإما أن يعلم الله تعالى استمراره ابداً، فلا يرتفع للزوم الجهل، أو إلى غاية ما، فلا رفع بعدها ولا نسخ. والجواب : أنه مرسل عن توقيت الوجوب مثلاً، وتأبيده ، والمعلوم عند الله استمرار الوجوب إلى غاية هي وقت نسخه ورفعه . ولا تناقض من ذلك، سواء كان الواجب مؤقتاً أو مؤبداً بمنزلة قولك: صوم الغد أو الأبد واجب حيناً دون حين . وإنما التناقض في رفع الوجوب بعد تأبيده ، كما إذا قبل: الوجوب ثابت ابداً، ثم نسخ فيكون زمان لا وجوب فيه، وهذا لا نزاع في امتناعه، وهو المراد بقولهم: إن النسخ ينافي التأبيد، وعليه يبتنى امتناع نسخ شريعتنا. والفرق بين كون التأبيد راجعاً إلى الواجب، أو إلى الوجوب مما يتضح بالرجوع إلى الأصل الذي مهدنا في بحث الرؤية في قوله تعالى : (لَا تُدْرِكُهُ الأبْصَارُ). على ان التحقيق أن لا رفع ههنا وإنما النسخ بيان لانتهاء حكم شرعي سبق على الإطلاق.
And the second: that a ruling is either temporary like 'Fast tomorrow,' then negating it after that is not abrogation; or perpetual like 'Fast forever,' so abrogating it is a contradiction like saying 'fasting is obligatory forever and not obligatory'; or unrestricted with no temporality or perpetuity, and then either God knows its continuation forever, so it cannot be lifted due to necessary ignorance, or to a certain end, so no lifting after it and no abrogation. And the difference between perpetuity referring to the obligated act or to the obligation is clarified by referring to the principle we established in the discussion of vision regarding His statement (لَا تُدْرِكُهُ الأبْصَارُ). Commentary
This passage presents a second objection against the possibility of abrogation and its answer. The objection classifies rulings into three types: (1) temporary (mu'ajjal), like 'fast tomorrow'—negating it later is not abrogation because its time is limited; (2) perpetual (mu'abbad), like 'fast forever'—abrogating it would be a contradiction; (3) unrestricted (mursal), with no specified time or perpetuity. For the unrestricted, either God knows it will continue forever—so lifting it would imply ignorance, or He knows it will end at a certain point—so no lifting after that. The answer clarifies that the unrestricted ruling is free from both temporal specification and perpetuity; God knows that its obligation continues until the moment of abrogation. Commentary
This passage presents the first and strongest proof for Abu Bakr's caliphate: consensus (ijmāʿ) of the people of authority (ahl al-ḥall wa al-ʿaqd). The author acknowledges that some had initial hesitation, citing narrations about the Anṣār's proposal of a leader from each party and Abū Sufyān's objection. He then relates a detailed story from Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, noting a blank in the manuscript, about Abū Bakr and ʿUmar sending Abū ʿUbayda to ʿAlī. The story includes some harshness from ʿUmar and ʿAlī, but ultimately ʿAlī came and agreed, saying 'May God bless what has displeased me and pleased you.' The narration describes that after ʿAlī and others initially held back, Abū Bakr gave them the choice to pledge or not, and ʿAlī declared he saw no one else. The author then remarks that this narration is subject to scrutiny (maḥall naẓar). He concludes by affirming that the consensus on Abu Bakr's caliphate and his qualification is so clear that it does not require the second proof.

Second Proof: Agreement on Abu Bakr's Imamate
Author presents proof from consensus and bay'ah with objections and responses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the logical structure of the second proof for Abu Bakr's caliphate, including how the author responds to objections about Ali's silence and alleged disqualification.

Core Idea
Silence from qualified candidates proves consent, not disqualification.

This passage presents a key argument from the early community's consensus. The author uses the fact that Ali and al-Abbas, the only other candidates, pledged allegiance to Abu Bakr, to argue that they recognized his right. He then anticipates and refutes objections based on the possibility of coercion or taqiyya. The discussion reveals the interplay between political authority and theological reasoning about infallibility.

Guided Path
1
Read the proof
Read from "أن المهاجرين" to "فتعين أبو بكر". Understand the premise: limited candidates, both gave bay'ah, so they must have considered him rightful.
Notice the premise that if Abu Bakr were wrong, they would have disputed like Ali did with Muawiya.
2
Analyze the first objection and response
After "فإن قيل", read the objection about contradiction. Then the first response: "قلنا: عدم كونه على الحق...". This is a logical argument showing absurdity.
The response uses a reductio ad absurdum: if not-being-right entails being-right, then not-being-right is impossible.
3
Examine the second response
Read "وقد يجاب بأنه يجوز..." This alternative response says Ali's superiority was forfeited by his failure to dispute.
This is a different line of reasoning: Ali's silence invalidated his own claim.
4
Consider the sub-objection and reply
Read "فإن قيل: يجوز أن يكون ترك المنازعة لمانع..." and the terse reply.
The author dismisses the idea that taqiyya or fear of fitna excused Ali's silence, saying the previous answer suffices.

أن المهاجرين والأنصار اتفقوا على أن الإمامة لا تعدو أبا بكر وعلياً والعباس ، ثم إن علياً والعباس بايعا أبا بكر وسلما له الأمر . فلو لم يكن على الحق، لنازعاه كما نازع علي معاوية لأنه لا يليق لهما السكوت عن الحق، ولأن ترك المنازعة يكون مخلاً بالعصمة الواجبة عندكم فيخرجان عن أهلية الإمامة . فتعين أبو بكر للاتفاق على أنها ليست لغيرهم . فإن قيل : إذا لم يكن على الحق، كيف يتعين إماماً على الحق؟ وهل هذا إلا تهافت؟ قلنا: عدم كونه على الحق إذا استلزم كواء على الحق، كان باطلاً، لأن ما يفضي ثبوته إلى انتفائه كان منتفياً قطعاً وفيه المطلوب. وقد يجاب بأنه يجوز أن لا يكون على الحق بفضل علي عليه واستحقاقه الإمامة دونه ، ثم يبطل ذلك الفضل والاستحقاق بترك ما وجب من المنازعة، فيصير أبو بكر هو الإمام بالحق . فإن قيل : يجوز أن يكون ترك المنازعة لمانع ،التقية، وخوف الفتنة . قلنا: قد سبق الجواب، والله أعلم.
That the Muhājirūn and the Anṣār agreed that the imamate does not go beyond Abū Bakr, ʿAlī, and al-ʿAbbās. Then ʿAlī and al-ʿAbbās gave the pledge to Abū Bakr and submitted the matter to him. So if he were not upon the truth, they would have disputed with him just as ʿAlī disputed with Muʿāwiya, because it is not befitting for them to remain silent about the truth, and because abandoning dispute would violate the infallibility (ʿiṣma) that is obligatory in your view, and they would thereby exit from the qualification for imamate. Thus Abū Bakr is determined [as the imam] due to agreement that it is not for others. If it is said: If he is not upon the truth, how can he be determined as an imam upon the truth? Is this not a contradiction? We say: If his not being upon the truth entails his being upon the truth, it is false, because that whose existence leads to its non-existence is definitely non-existent, and thereby the desired conclusion is obtained. And it may be answered that it is possible that he is not upon the truth due to ʿAlī's superiority and his entitlement to the imamate over him, but then that superiority and entitlement are invalidated by the abandonment of what was obligatory in disputing, so Abū Bakr becomes the imam by right. If it is said: It is possible that the abandonment of disputing was due to an impediment, such as taqiyya and fear of fitna. We say: The previous answer already suffices, and God knows best.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof for Abu Bakr's caliphate. The author argues that the Muhājirūn and Anṣār agreed that the imamate is limited to three candidates: Abū Bakr, ʿAlī, and al-ʿAbbās. Since ʿAlī and al-ʿAbbās both gave bayʿa to Abū Bakr and submitted the matter to him, if Abu Bakr were not rightful, they would have contested him, just as ʿAlī later contested Muʿāwiya. Their silence would violate the required infallibility (ʿiṣma) according to the opponent's own view, disqualifying them from imamate. Hence Abu Bakr is established. An objection is raised: how can someone not on the truth be determined as the rightful imam? This seems contradictory. The author gives two responses. First, if his not being on the truth entails his being on the truth, it is a logical absurdity, so the premise is false. Second, it is possible that ʿAlī was more deserving, but by failing to dispute, he forfeited his right, making Abu Bakr the rightful imam. A further objection suggests ʿAlī may have refrained due to taqiyya or fear of fitna. The author replies that the previous answer already addresses this (presumably because if taqiyya were a valid excuse, it would not invalidate his right, but the earlier argument assumed that silence indicates consent; the author implies the earlier reasoning stands).
