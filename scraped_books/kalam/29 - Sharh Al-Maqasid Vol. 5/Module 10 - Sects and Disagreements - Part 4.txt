The Sunni Consensus on Ali's Correctness
Consensus on Ali's correctness and status of opponents as rebels with interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Sunni consensus that Ali (ra) was the rightful imam and correct in the Battle of Siffin, and that his opponents are classified as rebels (bughat) due to their doubtful interpretation, not as disbelievers.

Core Idea
Rebellion based on interpretation does not make one a disbeliever.

The Battle of Siffin was a major fitna among the Companions. Sunni scholars reached a clear consensus: Ali (ra) was in the right, and his opponents, though they rebelled, are not to be declared disbelievers or sinners because they acted upon an interpretation (ta'wil). This passage lays out that consensus and its evidence.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analysis, noting key terms like بغاة and تأويل. Then read the translation.
Pay attention to the flow: consensus statement → evidence for Ali → classification of opponents.
2
Identify the evidence for Ali's correctness
The passage gives three proofs: his legitimate imamate (بيعة أهل الحل والعقد), his superiority over opponents, and prophetic hadith. List them.
The hadith 'تقتلك الفئة الباغية' is directly linked to Ali's opponents.
3
Understand why opponents are not kuffar
Ali himself called them 'brothers who rebelled' and explicitly said they are not disbelievers or sinners because they have ta'wil. Reflect on how ta'wil prevents takfir.
This is the core doctrinal point: error in ijtihad does not remove one from Islam.

And what the people of truth agreed upon is that the correct one in all of that is Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) due to the establishment of his imamate by the pledge of the people of loosing and binding, and the appearance of distinction between him and the opponents, especially Muawiya and his parties, and the multitude of reports that the truth is with him, and what was agreed upon even by the enemies that he is the best of his time and that no one is more entitled to the imamate than him. And the opponents are rebels (bughat) for their rebellion against the rightful imam due to a doubt (shubha) which is his leaving of retaliation for Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him), and due to his saying (peace be upon him): 'The rebellious party will kill you' and he was killed on the day of Siffin at the hands of the people of Syria. And due to the saying of Ali (may Allah be pleased with him): 'Our brothers have rebelled against us, and they are not disbelievers nor sinners nor wrongdoers because they have interpretation (ta'wil). And if it is false, the utmost is that they erred in ijtihad. And that does not necessitate declaring them sinful (tafsiq), let alone declaring them disbelievers (takfir). For this, Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) forbade his companions from cursing the people of Syria, and said: 'Our brothers have rebelled against us.' How? While the regret of Talha and al-Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with them) is established, and al-Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with him) withdrew from the war, and the regret of Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) is well-known. And the investigators (muhaqqiqun) among our companions hold that the Battle of the Camel was a sudden event without intent from either party, but rather instigation by the killers of Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) when they became two factions, mixed with the two armies, and incited the war out of fear of retaliation, and the intention of Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) was only to reconcile the two groups and calm the fitna, so she fell into war. And what the Shia went to, that those who fought Ali are disbelievers and his opponents are sinners, clinging to his saying (peace be upon him): 'Your war, O Ali, is my war,' and that obedience is obligatory and leaving the obligatory is sin, is among their audacities and ignorances because they did not distinguish between what is based on interpretation and ijtihad and what is not. Yes: if we said that the Khawarij are disbelievers based on their declaring Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) a disbeliever, it would not be far-fetched, but that is another discussion.

Commentary
This passage is a statement of the Sunni consensus regarding the Battle of Siffin and the ruling on Ali's opponents. The author begins by affirming that the correct position is that Ali (r) was the rightful imam and that he was correct in all his actions during the conflict, citing his legitimate appointment by the people of loosing and binding and the overwhelming evidence that the truth was with him. The opponents, including Muawiya, are classified as rebels (bughat) because they rebelled against the rightful imam based on a doubt (shubha): that Ali delayed retaliation for Uthman's murder. The author then quotes the hadith that Ali would be killed by a rebellious party, which occurred at Siffin. Ali himself stated that they were his brothers who rebelled, but he did not label them as disbelievers (kuffar) or sinners (fasiqun) because they had an interpretation (ta'wil). Even if that interpretation was false, they erred in ijtihad, which does not entail tafsīq (declaring them sinful) or takfīr (declaring them disbelievers). In fact, Ali forbade cursing them. The author then addresses the Battle of the Camel, noting that the investigators (muhaqqiqun) hold that it was a sudden event without intentional aggression from either side, instigated by the actual killers of Uthman who infiltrated both armies out of fear of retaliation; Aisha's intent was only reconciliation. The author refutes the Shia position that those who fought Ali are kuffar and fasiqun, arguing that the Shia fail to distinguish between actions based on valid ijtihad/ta'wil and those without. Finally, the author notes that declaring the Khawarij as disbelievers (due to their takfir of Ali) is a separate discussion, implying it is more plausible but not the same as the case of Muawiya's party.

Misconception & Correction
Misconception: Some think that anyone who rebels against a rightful imam automatically becomes a disbeliever.
Correction: This passage shows that if the rebellion is based on a doubtful interpretation (shubha/ta'wil), the rebels are not considered disbelievers or even sinners; they are only considered in error.

?

?

?
How often do you rush to label others as sinful or unjust in your heart? This lesson shows that even rebellion against the rightful imam does not justify such labels if there is interpretation. What step will you take this week to withhold judgment about someone you disagree with?

Ijtihad Error and the Battle of the Camel
Clarification of ijtihad error and the unintended nature of the Battle of the Camel.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that even if an interpretation is wrong, it does not call for tafsir; and you will learn the Sunni account of the Battle of the Camel as an unintended conflict caused by the instigation of Uthman's killers.

Core Idea
Error in ijtihad does not make one a sinner, and fitna can arise from the machinations of others.

After establishing that the opponents had a valid interpretation, the author goes further to clarify that even if that interpretation was false, it only means they made an error in ijtihad—not a sin. He then relates the Sunni understanding of the Battle of the Camel, emphasizing that it was not premeditated by the parties involved but instigated by the actual murderers of Uthman.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage on ijtihad error
Focus on the phrase 'even if it is false, the utmost is that they erred in ijtihad.' Understand that this reduces the severity of the opponents' action.
This is a key principle in usul al-fiqh and kalam: erring in ijtihad does not make one a fasiq.
2
Trace the evidence from Ali's actions
Ali forbade cursing his opponents and pointed to the regret of Talha, Zubayr, and Aisha. Why does this matter? It shows that even the opposition leaders later regretted their involvement.
3
Analyze the account of the Battle of the Camel
The muhaqqiqun say it was a sudden event (فلتة) instigated by the killers of Uthman. Note how the passage distinguishes the intention of Aisha (reconciliation) from the outcome (war).
This explanation absolves the Companions of intentional aggression.

Refutation of Shia Extremism and Khawarij Exception
Refutation of Shia position on the status of Ali's opponents and a note on the Khawarij.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why the Shia position that Ali's opponents are disbelievers is rejected, and why the Khawarij case is different due to their takfir of Ali.

Core Idea
The Shia wrongly conflate political opposition with disbelief, but ta'wil matters; Khawarij are a separate discussion.

The author now addresses a more extreme view: that of the Shia who declare Ali's opponents as outright disbelievers and his opposition as sinful. He dismisses this as audacity and ignorance because they fail to distinguish between actions based on interpretation and those without. He then notes a possible exception: the Khawarij, who explicitly declared Ali a disbeliever, making their own takfir more plausible—but that is a different topic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Shia claim
The passage states the Shia view: those who fought Ali are kuffar, and his opponents (who did not fight?) are fasiqun. Their evidence: the hadith 'Your war, O Ali, is my war' and the principle that obedience is obligatory.
2
Understand the author's refutation
The author calls it 'audacities and ignorances' because they ignore the distinction between actions with ta'wil/ijtihad and those without. The key is that the opponents had a valid interpretation, so their action is not bare defiance.
This echoes the earlier principle: ta'wil prevents tafsir and takfir.
3
Differentiate the Khawarij case
The author notes that if we were to declare the Khawarij disbelievers based on their takfir of Ali, it would not be far-fetched—but that is a separate discussion. Why? Because the Khawarij made takfir of Ali the basis of their rebellion, unlike the Siffin opponents who had a different doubt.
This shows that the ruling can vary depending on the nature of the rebellion.

Why Some Scholars Refrained from Cursing Yazid
Objection and answer on why scholars avoided cursing Yazid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rationale behind some scholars' decision to refrain from cursing Yazid despite his deserving blame.

Core Idea
Preventing escalation to cursing higher figures is a greater good.

This passage presents an objection and answer regarding the permissibility of cursing Yazid. While some scholars acknowledged Yazid's guilt, they avoided explicit curses out of concern for the common people's faith. The author explains that this caution was a strategy to prevent a slippery slope towards extremism.

Guided Path
1
Read the objection
The objection states that some scholars did not permit cursing Yazid even though they knew he deserved it. Notice the phrasing 'فإن قيل'.
2
Understand the answer
The answer explains the precautionary measure: to prevent rising to cursing higher figures, as the Rafidah do. The focus is on protecting the common people.
3
Reflect on the underlying principle
Consider the principle of blocking the means to greater evil. The scholars prioritised long-term stability over expressing righteousness.

If it is said: 'Some scholars of the school did not permit cursing Yazid, despite knowing that he deserves what is more than that and more.'
We say: 'As a precaution against rising to the higher and higher, as is the slogan of the Rafidah according to what is narrated in their supplications and practiced in their assemblies. So those concerned with the affairs of religion saw the complete restraint of the common people as a path to moderation in belief. So that feet do not slip from the right path, and intellects are not led astray by whims. Otherwise, who is unaware of the permissibility and deservingness? And how can agreement not occur upon them? This is the secret behind what has been transmitted from the Salaf of extreme caution in avoiding the people of misguidance, and blocking the path that is not safe from leading to error in the end, despite their knowledge of the reality of the matter and the clarity of the statement. And that has become clear to us when conditions were disturbed and terrors loomed, and there was no scope nor room, and the complaint is to the Knower of the unseen and the seen, the Great, the Exalted.'

Commentary
This passage is an objection-and-answer. The objection states that some scholars did not permit cursing Yazid even though he deserved it. The answer explains that this was a deliberate strategy to prevent the common people from escalating to cursing figures of higher status, following the pattern of the Rafidah. The scholars chose to restrain the masses to maintain correct belief, not because they doubted Yazid's guilt. The author notes that this precaution was taken despite full knowledge of the truth, and the situation became clearer over time.
