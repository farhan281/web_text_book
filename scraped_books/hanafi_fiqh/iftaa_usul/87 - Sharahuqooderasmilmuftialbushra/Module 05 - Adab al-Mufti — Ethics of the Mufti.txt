Caution in Fatwa: Avoiding Unreliable Books and Following the Rajih
Warning against unreliable fatwa sources and emphasis on following rajih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which later Hanafi books are considered unreliable for fatwa and state the ethical and methodological obligation to follow the preponderant opinion (rajih).

Core Idea
A mufti must only use verified works and follow the rajih; reckless fatwa is a slander against Allah.

In this passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, the author delivers a stern warning against carelessness in issuing fatwas. He emphasizes that the mufti must know the preponderant opinion (rajih) and avoid books that are not properly verified (ghayr muharrara). The passage lists specific later works — such as Sharh al-Niqaya, al-Durr al-Mukhtar, and al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir — that contain errors and sometimes even present non-Hanafi positions. Understanding this critique is essential for responsible fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the obligation of tathabbut (thoroughness)
The passage states it is 'obligatory upon the mufti and the judge to be thorough in the answer.' This is a core ethical duty to avoid declaring halal as haram or vice versa.
2
Identify the books criticized and the reasons for criticism
The author lists Sharh al-Niqaya (al-Qahistani), al-Durr al-Mukhtar, and al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir as unreliable due to extreme conciseness, omissions, and occasional preference for weak or even non-Hanafi views. He also cites a second source criticizing Mulla Miskin's Sharh al-Kanz and al-Qahistani.
The critique does not apply to the original matn (e.g., al-Durr al-Mukhtar is a commentary, but the base text al-Tanwir may be reliable).
3
Apply the principle: rely on verified sources and the rajih
Knowing that later books may contain errors, the mufti must refer to the core Hanafi works (zahir al-riwayah) and the preponderant opinion as established by the scholars of tarjih. The goal is to avoid the 'great affliction' of following desire or wealth.
The passage ends abruptly but reinforces that one must not trust the fatwa of contemporaries based on unverified books.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti addresses the ethical and methodological obligations of the mufti. It quotes al-Fatawa al-Khairiyya emphasizing that the mufti must know the rajih (preponderant) opinion among differing views. Fatwa requires thoroughness (tathabbut) and prohibits reckless answers, following desire, or being influenced by wealth. Then the author adds his own comment, warning against relying on later books that are not properly verified (ghayr muharrara). He specifically mentions Sharh al-Niqaya by al-Qahistani, al-Durr al-Mukhtar, and al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, saying they are too concise and contain errors, sometimes preferring weak or even non-Hanafi statements. He also cites a statement from Sharh al-Ashbah by Muhammad Hibatullah, who lists Mulla Miskin's Sharh al-Kanz and al-Qahistani as 'strange books' (ghariba) due to unreliable authors or weak narrations. The text cuts off at 'ka-sahib' (like the author of...), likely referring to another unreliable book. The overall message is that a mufti must be discerning and not rely on any book without knowing the reliability of its author and the strength of its rulings.

Key Terms
الراجح (al-rajih): The preponderant opinion in the madhhab, which must be followed in fatwa.
الكتب غير المحررة (al-kutub ghayr al-muharrara): Books that are not properly verified or refined; unreliable for fatwa due to errors or weak narrations.

Books Unfit for Issuing Fatwa: The Duty to Follow the Rajih
Warning against unreliable fatwa sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the types of books that are unfit for issuing fatwa and explain why following the rajih is obligatory.

Core Idea
Mufti must rely only on verified, authoritative sources and never follow desire or unreliable compilations.

A mufti carries the heavy responsibility of speaking on behalf of Allah. This passage warns against relying on concise, poorly-edited later works that contain errors and incorrect preponderations, and emphasizes that the mufti must diligently follow the preponderant opinion (rajih) from original sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited practice
Spot the analogy: collecting fatwas indiscriminately 'like a night wood-gatherer' — mixing good and bad without discernment.
This is an adab (ethical) warning, not a legal ruling.
2
Recognize the obligation to know the rajih
The passage quotes Al-Fatawa al-Khayriyya: the mufti must know the preponderant opinion and its levels of strength, and must be thorough and not reckless.
This is a fard (obligation) upon the mufti.
3
List the disqualified books
Identify the books named: Sharh al-Nuqaya by al-Qahistani, Al-Durr al-Mukhtar, Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, Mulla Miskeen Sharh al-Kanz, etc. Understand why they are unreliable: extreme conciseness, errors, and preferring non-Hanafi opinions.
Memorize these examples for future reference.
4
Apply the principle of returning to original sources
The passage insists on referring back to the original authoritative source (al-ma'khadh al-asli), such as Al-Qunya, rather than relying on flawed summaries.
This is a key methodological rule in Hanafi iftaa.
5
Conclude with the mufti's responsibility
The teacher's statement: it is not permissible to issue fatwa from such books unless one knows the transmitted content and is aware of their sources.
The burden of verification lies with the mufti.

Key Terms
الراجح (al-rajih): The preponderant opinion among differing views within the Hanafi school, determined by strength of evidence or authority.
المأخذ الأصلي (al-ma'khadh al-asli): The original authoritative source from which a ruling is derived, as opposed to later commentaries or abridgements.
الكتب المحررة (al-muharrara): Books that are carefully edited and reliable, free from errors and weak opinions.

The Necessity of Tracing Fatwas to Original Sources
Emphasizing the obligation to trace fatwas back to original reliable works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the risk of relying solely on later compilations and apply the principle of tracing a fatwa to its ma'khadh asli, using the example of ijarah on Quran recitation.

Core Idea
A fatwa repeated in twenty books may still be wrong if the original source says otherwise.

A mufti's authority rests on the accuracy of his sources. This passage by the author of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' reveals a dangerous blind spot: a single erroneous opinion can propagate through dozens of later works, gaining false credibility. The lesson is not just about one ruling—it is about how a mufti must interrogate his sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the commonly transmitted ruling
In the example, the widespread fatwa in later books claimed that hiring for mere Quran recitation is valid.
2
Locate the earliest authority for that ruling
Trace back to the first book that stated it—here, Al-Siraj al-Wahhaj and Al-Jawhara Sharh al-Quduri.
3
Compare with the statements of the three founding Imams
The unanimous position of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad is that hiring for acts of worship (including recitation) is invalid.
4
Detect the discrepancy
The original source (ma'khadh asli) shows that only hiring for teaching is valid, not recitation. The first transmitter confused the two.
5
Apply the lesson to your own practice
Before issuing a fatwa, verify the ruling in a primary source (e.g., Al-Quduri, Al-Hidayah, or the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah) and confirm the consensus of the early Imams.

Commentary
This passage discusses the necessity of returning to original sources (al-ma'khadh al-asli) for fatwa. The author observes that an erroneous opinion can propagate through many later books if the first transmitter made a mistake. He gives two examples: (1) a problem in the chapter of 'what is correct to suspend and what is not' (from 'Al-Bahr al-Raiq'), and (2) the more detailed example of hiring (ijarah) for Quran recitation. The author emphasizes that the consensus of the three founding Imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) is that hiring for acts of worship is invalid. The lesson is a critical refinement of the principle: one must trace the ruling back to the original, authoritative texts and not rely on later compilations that may contain propagated errors.

The Necessity of a Qualified Teacher for Ifta
Warning against self-study and emphasizing the need for a qualified teacher

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a mufti's qualification and state why studying under a master is obligatory in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
A mufti must be trained under a qualified teacher; self-study from books does not qualify one to give fatwa.

The passage you are about to read is a sharp warning against the common misconception that reading books alone qualifies one to issue fatwa. The author narrates a real incident where a respected later scholar made an error, and some muftis blindly followed him, leading to incorrect rulings. He then cites a fatwa from Ibn Hajar al-Haytami affirming that a person who studies without a teacher is an ignorant layperson and cannot give fatwa at all.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the anecdote about al-Haskafi's error
Notice that even the leading later scholar al-Haskafi made a mistake in his text al-Durr al-Mukhtar. This shows that later works are not infallible.
The author points out that a gloss by Ibrahim al-Halabi corrected the error, so consultation of commentaries is essential.
2
Identify the criticism of blind following
Some muftis rejected the correct fatwa because it contradicted al-Haskafi, showing blind reliance on a single source. The author calls this 'great ignorance'.
Blind following (taqlid) of a text without verification is not acceptable.
3
Examine Ibn Hajar's fatwa
Ibn Hajar rules that a person who reads books without a teacher is not permitted to give fatwa at all. Such a person is an ignorant layman.
This includes even those who study thoroughly; they lack the training to apply principles correctly.
4
Derive the condition of studying under a master
From the anecdote and fatwa, we learn that proper tafaqquh requires a skilled teacher (ustadh mahir) who can train the student in the nuances of the madhhab.
Al-tafaqquh implies deep understanding, not mere reading.
5
Apply to modern contexts
Consider how this principle applies today: online resources, self-study courses, and fatwa websites do not replace the teacher-student relationship.
The Hanafi tradition emphasizes ijazah and direct transmission.

When a Mufti Must Say 'I Do Not Know'
Explains the obligation of a mufti to say 'I do not know' when lacking evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchy of sources a mufti must consult and apply the rule that a muqallid mufti must say 'I do not know' when no text is found.

Core Idea
A muqallid mufti must never issue a fatwa from his own opinion; he must refer to a higher authority or say 'I do not know'.

This passage from Rasm al-Mufti establishes a fundamental ethical obligation: the mufti must know his limits. It presents a clear hierarchy for issuing fatwas, from the well-known transmitted rulings (zahir al-riwaya) to the opinions of later scholars, and finally ijtihad for those qualified. Crucially, it warns the muqallid mufti—one who is not a mujtahid—that he may not rely on his own reasoning. Instead, he must attribute his answer to a more learned scholar, or if he cannot find any transmitted ruling, he must simply say 'I do not know'.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the overarching principle
The passage begins with 'لا بد للعالم من لا أدري'—the scholar must inevitably say 'I do not know'. This is a foundational adab for all scholars, from the Sahabah to the Prophet ﷺ.
This principle appears in many early texts; it is a sign of taqwa, not ignorance.
2
Identify the hierarchy of sources
The text from al-Khaniyya outlines a stepwise procedure: first check if the case is in zahir al-riwaya; if not, see if it agrees with the principles (usul) of the Hanafi school; then check if the later scholars (mutakhkhirun) agreed; if they differed, a mujtahid may do ijtihad.
Each step represents a fallback; the mufti must not skip a level.
3
Distinguish the role of the muqallid
If the mufti is a muqallid (not a mujtahid), he cannot perform ijtihad. He must take the statement of the most learned scholar he knows and attribute the answer to that scholar. He must not issue a fatwa based on his own opinion.
The term 'muqallid' here means a scholar without ijtihad capacity; even a well-read mufti can be a muqallid.
4

The commentary clarifies: even if a scholar has read many books and can locate cases, if he cannot find the specific incident in any reliable book, he must say 'I do not know'. This follows the practice of the greatest scholars, including the Prophet ﷺ.
This is the ultimate fallback; it protects the religion from false attributions.

His statement: 'If he knows etc.' is evidence that whoever does not know that, but rather read a book or more, understood it, and has the ability to review and locate an incident in a famous relied-upon book, if he does not find that incident in a book, he may not issue a fatwa regarding it with his opinion. Rather, he must say: 'I do not know', as those greater than him in rank from the mujtahid Companions and those after them said, indeed, he who was supported by revelation, may Allah bless him and grant him peace.

Commentary
This passage opens with a crucial principle for muftis: 'It is necessary for the scholar to say I do not know.' The author then quotes al-Khaniyah detailing the hierarchy of authority for a mufti. If a matter is not from Zahir al-Riwayah (the core transmitted texts), the mufti first checks whether it conforms to Hanafi principles. If not found in any narration from the founding Imams, he follows the agreed opinion of later scholars (mutakkhirin). If they differ, a mujtahid mufti may exercise ijtihad. But if the mufti is a simple muqallid (not a mujtahid), he must rely on the most knowledgeable scholar known to him, even if in another city, and attribute the fatwa to that scholar. He must not give his own opinion, lest he lie against Allah. Then the commentator (the author of the Sharh) explains that this indicates the muqallid may not issue fatwa when no text exists. He supports this with a quote from al-Bahr from al-Tatarkhaniyyah: if later scholars differ, he takes one opinion; if no opinion from later scholars, he can only do ijtihad if he knows the aspects of fiqh and consults experts. The commentator stresses that the condition 'if he knows' shows that one who merely reads books without deep understanding must not use personal reasoning but must say 'I do not know', emulating the greater scholars.

Key Terms
مقلد (muqallid): A mufti who is not a mujtahid and must follow the opinions of a higher authority.
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The well-known transmitted rulings from Imam Muhammad's six books.
لا أدري (la adri): The phrase 'I do not know' which a mufti must utter when lacking knowledge.

Prohibition of Acting on Weak Opinions in Fatwa and Judgment
Sets the rule that weak opinions are not to be used in fatwa or judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that weak (marjuh) opinions are prohibited in fatwa and judgment, and identify the limited exception for personal necessity.

Core Idea
A weak opinion cannot be used in fatwa or judgment; only the preponderant (rajih) governs official rulings.

The following passage from the Rasm al-Mufti tradition lays down a foundational rule for muftis and judges. It states in verse that acting on a weak (marjuh) opinion is prohibited, with only a narrow exception for personal necessity. The commentary reinforces this with quotes from later Hanafi and Shafi'i authorities, emphasizing that any deviation from the rajih violates scholarly consensus (ijmah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the verse and commentary to determine the core prohibition: acting on weak opinions in fatwa and judgment is not allowed.
2
Recognize the exceptions
Note the exception for a person acting for himself under necessity (darurah). Determine that this does not extend to fatwa or judgment.
3
Understand the basis
Analyze the commentary's reference to ijmah (consensus) and the principle that marjuh is like non-existent in official contexts.
4
Apply the rule
Distinguish between private action (allowed under necessity) and official capacity (mufti/judge) where only rajih is used.
5
Evaluate the ikhtilaf
Consider the Shafi'i position (al-Subki) that allows personal action but not fatwa, and how this aligns with the Hanafi view in practice.

Nor is it used to answer one who asks, or one who knows its well-known (weak) opinion. But the judge does not judge by it, and if he judges, his judgment is not executed, especially our judges since they are bound by the preponderant (rajih) of the school when they assumed the role. Commentary
This passage states the rule that acting on weak (marjuh) opinions is strictly prohibited in fatwa and judgment. The author presents it in verse form as a mnemonic. The verse clarifies: (1) it is not allowed for a mufti or judge to act on weak opinions; (2) an exception is made for an individual who has a necessity (e.g., personal pressure) to act on a weak opinion for his own practice; (3) the weak opinion cannot be used to answer a questioner; (4) the judge cannot base a judgment on it, and if he does, his judgment is void and not executed—especially Hanafi judges who are bound by the preponderant (rajih) view of the school. The commentary quotes al-Allama Qasim emphasizing that issuing fatwas or judgments based on marjuh is against ijmah. Even the authority to follow a weak opinion for personal action is disputed; al-Subki (Shafi'i) allowed it only for oneself, not for fatwa or judgment, while Ibn al-Salah transmitted ijmah against it entirely. Al-Shurunbulali (Hanafi) states that the Shafi'i position as per al-Subki is prohibition of acting on the weak view. Thus the Hanafi position leans toward prohibition in official capacity, with limited personal allowance under necessity.

المرجوح (al-marjuh): The outweighed opinion in comparison to the preponderant (rajih)
الراجح (al-rajih): The preponderant or stronger opinion after weighting evidence
