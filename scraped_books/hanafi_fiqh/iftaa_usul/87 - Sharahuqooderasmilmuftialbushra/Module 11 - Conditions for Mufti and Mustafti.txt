Change of Rulings with Changing Customs: Eight Examples from Hanafi Fatwa Practice
Listing examples where later Hanafi jurists modified rulings due to changing times and corruption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle of taghayyur al-ahkam and apply it to identify when a ruling may be modified due to change of custom or corruption.

Core Idea
Rulings based on custom change when custom changes; later jurists may override earlier fatwas for necessity.

This passage from a Hanafi iftaa manual lists eight instances where later jurists modified the original rulings of Abu Hanifah due to changing times and corruption. It illustrates a critical principle: that fiqh rulings are not static but respond to societal conditions, within the framework of the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
The passage states that many rulings change with changing times due to corruption or general necessity. This establishes the principle of taghayyur al-ahkam.
The principle applies only to rulings based on custom, not to core obligations like prayer.
2
List the Eight Examples
Go through each of the eight modifications: hiring for Qur'an teaching, coercion from non-ruler, liability of tax collector, liability of common hireling, executor's prohibition on mudarabah, liability of usurper of orphan/waqf property, judge not judging by own knowledge, husband's travel restriction with wife.
Each example explicitly contradicts an earlier ruling of the school.
3
Analyze the Reasoning for Each
For each modification, identify the changed custom or necessity that justified the new fatwa. For instance, the tax collector's liability was changed as a deterrent for corruption.
The reasoning often involves istihsan (juristic preference) or darurah (necessity).
4
Recognize the Authority Dynamic
The later jurists (mutakhkhirin) override the earlier Imam's view based on changed circumstances. This shows that fatwa within the school can evolve.
The authority of the later jurists is not absolute; they must have sound evidence.
5
Apply to New Scenarios
Consider a modern scenario, such as digital transactions, and ask whether the ruling on liability of agents should change due to widespread fraud.
This step requires understanding the underlying custom and necessity.

Commentary
This passage is a critical part of Hanafi iftaa methodology: it explains that many rulings initially formulated by Abu Hanifah and the early masters were later modified by later jurists (mutakhkhirin) because of changes in society, corruption, and general necessity. The principle is that rulings based on custom and time do not remain static. Eight examples are given: (1) hiring for teaching Qur'an was not allowed by Abu Hanifah because it was considered detestable, but later scholars allowed it due to necessity. (2) Coercion (ikrah) was originally only recognized from the ruler, but later due to widespread oppression, it was recognized from anyone, based on Imam Muhammad's opinion. (3) The tax collector (sa'i) was made liable for losses even though normally liability is on the direct agent; this was a deterrent. (4) The common hireling (ajir mushtarak) was also made liable. (5) The executor (wasi) was forbidden from doing mudarabah with orphan's property in later times. (6) The usurper (ghasib) of orphan and waqf property was made liable and leasing restrictions imposed. (7) Judges were prevented from judging based on their own knowledge. (8) Husbands were prevented from traveling with wives due to moral corruption. These changes are explicitly against the original rulings of the school (mukhalif li asl al-madhhab), but were justified by the principle of necessity and change of custom.

Key Terms
تغير الأحكام (taghayyur al-ahkam): The change of legal rulings due to change in custom and time.
المتأخرون (al-muta'akhkhirun): Later Hanafi jurists who modified earlier rulings based on changed circumstances.
الضرورة (darurah): Necessity that permits otherwise prohibited acts or changes rulings.

Conditions for Acting on Weak Reports in Fatwa
Explaining when it is permissible to use weak opinions for oneself and for issuing fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules governing when a mufti or a layperson may act on weak reports, distinguishing between personal action and issuing fatwa, and identifying the role of necessity and juristic insight (ra'y).

Core Idea
Weak reports are only for necessity or for scholars with insight for themselves; muftis must follow the authentic madhhab for others.

This passage from the Rasm al-Mufti literature clarifies the delicate conditions under which a Hanafi jurist may deviate from the established madhhab position and act on or issue fatwa based on weaker reports. It distinguishes between personal practice and public fatwa, and between the scholar with insight (dhū ra'y) and the common person ('āmmī). The discussion includes a real example from the author's life and an important exception for necessity (ḍarūra).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contexts
The passage distinguishes between acting on a weak report for oneself and issuing a fatwa to someone else. Note that different rules apply.
The anecdote shows personal action; the later part discusses fatwa.
2
Apply the necessity exception
Necessity (ḍarūra) allows both personal action and fatwa with weak reports. This is derived from Fakhr al-Islam's statement in al-Mi'raj.
Necessity is a legal cause (sabab) that relaxes the rule.
3
Classify the actor
For personal action, only a person with juristic insight (dhū ra'y) may act on weak reports; a common person ('āmmī) must follow the authentic madhhab.
Al-Biri's statement in Sharḥ al-Ashbāh.
4
Understand the mufti's limitation
When giving fatwa to others, even a mujtahid mufti must adhere to the established madhhab because the questioner relies on that madhhab.
The objection and response from al-Khāniyya.
5
Recognize the takfir principle
The passage also notes that in cases of doubt about apostasy, even weak reports are considered to avoid wrongful takfir.
This is another circumstance where weak opinions are acted upon.

Issuing fatwa and acting on the weak (report). Explanation of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: for a continuous flow at every prayer, as I used to do, but there was great hardship and difficulty, so I was compelled to follow this opinion. Then when Allah cured me from it, I repeated the prayers of that period. Praise be to Allah. The author of al-Bahr mentioned in the chapter on menstruation, in the discussion of the colors of blood, weak opinions. Then he said: In al-Mi'raj from Fakhr al-A'imma: 'If a mufti gives a fatwa with some of these opinions in cases of necessity seeking ease, it would be good.' From this it is known that the one compelled may act upon that for himself as we said, and that the mufti may give fatwa with it to the compelled one. So what has passed—that one may not act on the weak nor give fatwa with it—is applied to other than a case of necessity, as you have understood from the totality of what we have established. And Allah knows best. One should also include under necessity what we have stated earlier: that one does not give a fatwa of apostasy against a Muslim if there is a difference of opinion concerning his apostasy, even if it is a weak report, for they turned away from giving fatwa with the sound (report) because apostasy is a grave matter.
Conditions for acting on the weak report:
In Sharh al-Ashbah by al-Biri: 'Is it permissible for a person to act on the weak (report) from the narrations with regard to himself? Yes, if he has an opinion (i.e., juristic insight). As for if he is a common person, I have not seen it (permitted). But the implication of restricting it to one with opinion is that it is not permissible for the common person.'
He said in Khazanat al-Riwayat: The scholar who knows the meanings of the texts and reports and is among the people of insight—it is permissible for him to act upon them, even if it contradicts his madhhab. His restricting it to 'one with opinion'—meaning the mujtahid within the madhhab—excludes the common person as he said, for the common person must follow what they (the scholars) have deemed authentic, except in a case of necessity as you have learned earlier. If you say: This contradicts what we presented earlier—that the mujtahid mufti may not deviate from what Abu Hanifa and his companions have agreed upon, so he may not give fatwa with (a weak opinion) even if he is a thorough mujtahid, because they knew the evidences and distinguished between what is authentic and established and what is not, and his ijtihad does not reach their ijtihad as we have presented from al-Khaniyya and others. I say: That applies to the one who gives fatwa to others. Perhaps the reason is that since he knows that their ijtihad is stronger, he may not base the cases of the common people on his weaker ijtihad, or because the questioner only came to ask him about the madhhab of the Imam whom that mufti follows, so he must give fatwa according to the madhhab about which the questioner came to seek a fatwa. For this reason, 'Allama Qasim mentioned in his Fatawa that he was asked about a waqif who stipulated for himself the right to change and alter, and then made the waqf for his wife. He answered: I have not come across the consideration of this in any of the books of our scholars, and the mufti has nothing but to transmit what is authentic according to the people of his madhhab whose words are relied upon for fatwa; and because the questioner only asks about what the imams held.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the conditions under which a mufti or a layperson may act upon weak (da'if) reports or issue fatwa based on them. The discussion arises from a personal anecdote of the author (or the commentator) who, due to a medical condition (continuous flow), was compelled to follow a weak opinion that allowed him to pray without repeating ablution for every prayer. He later made up those prayers after being cured, highlighting that acting on weak opinions in necessity is a concession, not a license for permanent departure from the stronger position.

The passage then cites the author of al-Bahr and Fakhr al-Islam (Fakhr al-A'imma) from al-Mi'raj, who state that a mufti may issue a fatwa based on weak opinions in cases of necessity to provide ease. This establishes two principles: (1) a person compelled by necessity may act on a weak opinion for himself, and (2) a mufti may give such a fatwa to someone who is compelled. The earlier strict prohibition against acting on weak opinions is thus restricted to non-necessity situations.

An additional point: the principle of not excommunicating a Muslim (takfir) over a disputed matter, even if the opinion of apostasy is based on a weak report, is also considered a case of necessity, because the gravity of takfir demands caution; thus the stronger (sound) opinion is set aside.

The remainder of the passage (from 'Conditions for acting on the weak report') delves into a further nuance: who may act on weak reports for himself? According to al-Biri in Sharh al-Ashbah, it is permissible only for a person who has 'ra'y'—meaning juristic insight and the ability to weigh evidence. A common person ('ammi) who cannot evaluate the strengths of reports must follow what the scholars of his madhhab have deemed authentic (i.e., the mu'tamad). This is affirmed by Khazanat al-Riwayat, which allows a scholar who understands the texts and is a person of insight (min ahl al-diraya) to act on a weak report even if it contradicts his own madhhab, but this is only for himself, not for giving fatwa to others.

The passage then addresses a potential objection: if a mujtahid mufti (one capable of ijtihad within the madhhab) is allowed to act on weak reports for himself, does that contradict the established rule that a mufti cannot deviate from the agreed-upon positions of Abu Hanifa and his companions? The response distinguishes between acting for oneself and giving fatwa to others. When giving fatwa, the mufti must adhere to the well-known positions of his madhhab because the questioner is asking about the madhhab of the Imam he follows, and the mufti is a transmitter of that madhhab. The mufti's personal ijtihad, even if sound, is weaker than the collective ijtihad of the founding imams, so he cannot impose his weaker view on the public. An example from 'Allama Qasim's Fatawa illustrates this: regarding a waqf condition, if nothing authentic is found in the madhhab's books, the mufti cannot invent a ruling; he only transmits what is established.

Key Terms
الضعيف (al-ḍa'īf): A legal report or opinion that is not from the core authoritative books (Zahir al-Riwayah) or is considered less sound than the primary position, but may still be acted upon under certain conditions.
ذو رأي (dhū ra'y): A person who has juristic insight and the ability to discern legal proofs; a mujtahid within the madhhab or one capable of weighing evidence.
