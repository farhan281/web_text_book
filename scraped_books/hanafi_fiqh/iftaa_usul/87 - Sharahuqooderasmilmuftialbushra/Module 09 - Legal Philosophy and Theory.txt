Divisions of Mafhum and the Hanafi Ruling
Defines two types of textual implication and the Hanafi position on each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mafhum muwafaqah and mafhum mukhalafah, identify the five subtypes of the latter, and state the Hanafi ruling that mafhum mukhalafah is invalid in the speech of the Lawgiver.

Core Idea
Mafhum muwafaqah is agreed upon; mafhum mukhalafah is rejected by Hanafis in divine speech.

This passage introduces the two fundamental types of textual implication (mafhum) used in legal derivation. You will learn how a ruling may extend to unmentioned cases by a stronger reason (muwafaqah) or be restricted to the mentioned case, implying the opposite for others (mukhalafah). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the Hanafi method of istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions
The passage divides mafhum into two categories: mafhum muwafaqah (implication of agreement) and mafhum mukhalafah (implication of opposition). Note that the first is unanimously accepted, while the second is disputed.
Mafhum is the implied meaning beyond the literal text.
2
Define mafhum muwafaqah with example
Mafhum muwafaqah applies when the unspoken case is of higher priority than the spoken case. The classic example is the verse 'do not say uff' implying prohibition of hitting, since hitting is a greater offense.
This is also called 'fahwa al-khitab'.
3
List the five subtypes of mafhum mukhalafah
The passage mentions five subtypes: attribute (sifa), condition (shart), end (ghaya), number (adad), and title (laqab). Each restricts the ruling to the described case, implying the opposite for cases without that description.
Example: 'In pasturing livestock is zakat' implies no zakat on fodder-fed livestock.
4
Compare Hanafi and Shafi'i positions
Shafi'is accept all subtypes of mafhum mukhalafah except mafhum al-laqab. Hanafis reject all subtypes in the speech of the Lawgiver (Qur'an and Sunnah), though they may use it in other contexts like the statements of jurists.
The Hanafi position is based on the principle that divine speech has its own patterns.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: e.g., regarding zakat on fodder-fed livestock, Hanafis judge based on trade value (since the hadith is restricted to pasturing), while Shafi'is rely on mafhum al-sifa to negate zakat entirely.
This illustrates how the disagreement affects real fatwa.

Divisions of the Implication and Their Ruling
Know that the implication is of two types: Implication of Agreement (mafhum muwafaqah) – it is the indication of the word on the establishment of the ruling of the spoken (mantuq) for the unspoken (maskut) by mere understanding of language, i.e., without depending on opinion and ijtihad, like the indication of 'Do not say to them 'uff'' on the prohibition of hitting. (al-Isra: 23)
And Implication of Opposition (mafhum mukhalafah) – it is the indication of the word on the establishment of the opposite of the ruling of the spoken for the unspoken. It has divisions: Implication of the attribute (mafhum al-sifa), like 'In pasturing livestock there is zakat.' Implication of the condition (mafhum al-shart), like 'If they are pregnant, then spend on them' (al-Talaq: 6). Implication of the end (mafhum al-ghaya), like 'Until she marries another husband' (al-Baqara: 230). Implication of the number (mafhum al-adad), like 'Eighty lashes' (al-Nur: 4). And Implication of the title (mafhum al-laqab), which is the attachment of the ruling to a proper noun, like 'In sheep is zakat.' Acceptance of the first of the two types is agreed upon, and there is disagreement on the second with its divisions. According to the Shafi'is, it is considered – except for the last one – so it indicates the negation of zakat from fodder-fed livestock, and that there is no maintenance for a divorced woman who is not pregnant, and permissibility if she marries another, and negation of the excess over eighty. According to the Hanafis, it is not considered in its divisions in the speech of the Lawgiver only. And its full investigation is in the books of Usul.

Commentary
This passage, a qawida definition from the commentary, explains the concept of 'mafhum' (implication) in legal texts. It states that mafhum is of two types: (1) mafhum muwafaqah (implication of agreement), where the text's ruling for a mentioned case applies to an unmentioned case by a higher priority, e.g., the verse 'do not say uff' implies prohibition of hitting, since hitting is worse. This is accepted by all scholars. (2) mafhum mukhalafah (implication of opposition), where the text's ruling implies the opposite for an unmentioned case. It has subtypes: attribute (e.g., 'in pasturing livestock zakat' implies no zakat on fodder-fed), condition (e.g., 'if pregnant' implies no spending if not pregnant), end (e.g., 'until she marries' implies permissibility after marrying), number (e.g., 'eighty lashes' implies no more), and title (e.g., 'in sheep zakat' implies no zakat on other animals). The Shafi'is accept all mafhum mukhalafah except the last (title), while the Hanafis reject them all in the speech of the Lawgiver (i.e., Qur'an and Sunnah), though they may use it in other contexts. The couplet advises acting on the mafhum of narrations as long as it doesn't contradict the clear text.

Key Terms
مفهوم موافقة (mafhum muwafaqah): The implication that a ruling mentioned for one case applies to an unmentioned case by a stronger or equal reason, based on pure linguistic understanding.
مفهوم مخالفة (mafhum mukhalafah): The implication that a ruling mentioned for a case is restricted to that case, so the opposite ruling applies to unmentioned cases.
مفهوم اللقب (mafhum al-laqab): Implication of the title: attaching a ruling to a proper noun (e.g., 'in sheep zakat') implying the ruling does not apply to other animals.
