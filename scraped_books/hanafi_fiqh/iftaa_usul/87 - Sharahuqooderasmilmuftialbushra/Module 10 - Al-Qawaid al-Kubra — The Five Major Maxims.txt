The Maxim: Custom is Authoritative
Defines urf, states maxim, provides condition and example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-ada muhakkama', identify its proof, apply its condition (consistency/predominance), and evaluate its use in a sales contract scenario.

Core Idea
Custom can override literal meaning if it is consistent or predominant in practice.

Custom (urf) and habit (ada) are central to Hanafi fatwa methodology. This passage introduces the second major maxim: 'Habit is authoritative' (al-ada muhakkama). It provides the definition, the textual basis, the key condition, and a concrete example from sales contracts. Understanding this maxim helps you see how Hanafis integrate community practice into legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the definition of urf and ada
Read the two definitions: from al-Mustasfa (custom settles in minds by intellect and is accepted by sound nature) and from Sharh al-Tahrir (habit is recurring without intellectual connection). Note that they are used interchangeably.
The difference is subtle: urf has a rational basis, ada is purely empirical recurrence.
2
Identify the maxim and its proof
Locate the maxim 'al-ada muhakkama' (العادة محكمة). Identify its proof: the hadith 'What the Muslims see as good is good with Allah.' Understand that this is a dalil naqli (transmitted evidence).
This hadith is recorded by Ahmad and is considered mursal by some, but Hanafis accept it as a basis for istihsan.
3
Apply the condition for considering custom
State the dabt (restrictive condition): custom is only considered if it is consistent (mutarid) or predominant (ghalib). This filters out irregular or rare practices.
This condition prevents arbitrary or temporary customs from changing established rulings.
4
Work through the furu example
Read the example: a sale mentions dirhams or dinars in a city with multiple currencies. The payment is referred to the predominant currency. Identify the ruling and why the maxim applies.
The absolute term (dirham) is specified by custom because custom is predominant.
5
Connect to the related maxims
Note the related maxims: 'al-maruf ka al-mashrut' (what is known by custom is like a condition) and 'al-thabit bil urf ka al-thabit bil nass' (established by custom is like established by text). See how they reinforce the authority of custom.

Commentary
This passage is a qawida (maxim) with its applications. The author begins by affirming the authority of custom (urf) in Islamic law. He provides two definitions: from al-Mustasfa (custom is what settles in minds by reason and is accepted by sound natures) and from Sharh al-Tahrir (habit is something recurring without an intellectual connection). The core maxim is from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: 'Habit is authoritative' (العادة محكمة), which is one of the five major maxims of Hanafi fiqh. Its basis is the hadith: 'What the Muslims see as good is good with Allah.' The author then states that custom leads to abandoning literal meanings in favor of usage. He adds a crucial condition: habit is only considered if it is consistent (mutarid) or predominant (ghalib). He gives an example from sales: if a sale contract mentions 'dirhams' or 'dinars' in a city with multiple currencies, the payment is referred to the predominant currency, as it is the customary understanding. This is supported by al-Hidayah and al-Mabsut. The authority weight: These are from well-known Hanafi sources (al-Ashbah, al-Hidayah, al-Mabsut).

Key Terms
العرف (al-urf): What has settled in the minds by intellect and accepted by sound nature; also defined as recurrent practice without intellectual analogy.
العادة (al-ada): A matter that recurs without an intellectual connection; used interchangeably with urf.
اطردت (ittaradat): Consistent; occurs regularly without exception.
غلبت (ghalabat): Predominant; occurs more often than not.

Classification of Custom: General vs Specific
Defines general and specific custom and their roles in legal reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general and specific custom and state the legal effect of each according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
General custom may specify qiyas and texts; specific custom only governs its own context.

Custom plays a vital role in Hanafi legal reasoning. This passage defines two types: general custom ('urf 'amm) and specific custom ('urf khass). Understanding their distinct legal effects is essential for a mufti when applying custom as a source of law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage and pinpoint where the author divides custom into general and specific. Note the Arabic terms.
2
State the legal effect of each
For general custom, note that it establishes a general ruling and can specify (mukhassis) qiyas and textual evidence. For specific custom, it only establishes a ruling in its own context and cannot specify if it contradicts qiyas or athar.
The passage says 'bikhilaf al-khass' – specific custom does not qualify as a specifier.
3
Apply to the kharaj example
Consider why scholars refrained from giving fatwa on the obligatory kharaj despite the ruling being known. How does ignoring custom lead to oppression?
This illustrates the danger of rigidity and the importance of considering custom.
4
Extract the qawida
Formulate the legal maxim: 'General custom is a specifier for qiyas and texts; specific custom is valid only within its sphere and does not overrule established proofs.'

Commentary
This passage defines the two categories of custom ('urf): general ('amm) and specific (khass). It begins with an example illustrating the danger of ignoring custom: if a landowner cultivates an inferior crop when he could cultivate a superior one, the kharaj (land tax) of the superior crop is obligatory. However, scholars said this ruling is known but not given as a fatwa, lest oppressors seize people's money. The commentary from al-'Inayah questions how concealment is permissible if the ruling is correct. The response is that if such a fatwa were given, every oppressor would claim that the land used to be planted with saffron or similar high-value crops, leading to unjust exactions. This shows that sticking rigidly to transmitted texts while ignoring custom and clear circumstances leads to loss of rights and oppression. The passage then states the qawida: custom is divided into general and specific. General custom establishes a general ruling and can serve as a makhsis (specifier) for qiyas (analogy) and athar (textual evidence). In contrast, specific custom can only establish a specific ruling for its context, and it cannot be used to specify general textual evidence if it contradicts qiyas or athar. The distinction is crucial for muftis: general custom is a legitimate source for juristic reasoning across all cases, while specific custom is limited to its particular domain and cannot override established legal principles.

Key Terms
العرف العام (al-'urf al-'amm): Custom that is widespread among all people or in most regions; it can specify general texts and analogies.
العرف الخاص (al-'urf al-khass): Custom specific to a particular group, region, or industry; it applies only in that context and cannot override qiyas or textual evidence.
مخصص (mukhassis): A specifier that restricts the application of a general ruling to certain cases.

The Rasm Rule for General and Specific Custom
Rasm rule distinguishing general and specific custom in fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm rule distinguishing when general and specific custom are considered in issuing a fatwa.

Core Idea
General custom specifies texts; specific custom only applies locally if it neither abandons nor specifies texts.

Custom (urf) is a crucial tool in Hanafi fatwa, but not all customs override texts. The rule distinguishes between general custom, widespread across all lands, and specific custom, limited to a locality. Understanding this distinction prevents issuing fatwas that either nullify divine texts or ignore legitimate community practices. This lesson unpacks the maxim from the source and shows how to apply it in oaths, contracts, and other speech acts.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Begin by reciting the qa'ida as given in the text: general custom is never considered if it abandons the text, but is considered if it specifies it; specific custom is not considered in either case, only for its people if it neither abandons nor specifies.
Memorize this maxim verbatim; it is a wusta (intermediate) qa'ida.
2
Identify the scope of the maxim
The maxim governs all speech acts: oaths, contracts (sale, hire), waqf, wills, vows, etc. It applies wherever customary usage affects the meaning of words.
Scope is broad but excludes acts that are purely devotional without speech.
3
Apply the maxim to a furu' (branch) case
Take an example from oaths: someone swears 'by my father's grave' in a region where this is customary. The oath is valid and interpreted per custom, even if the literal Arabic is different.
This is because the speaker intends the customary meaning.
4
Identify the istithna' (exception)
If a specific custom leads to abandonment of a clear text (e.g., a custom that violates a Quranic prohibition), it is not considered even for its people. The overriding principle is 'certainty is not removed by doubt'.
This exception prevents custom from nullifying nusus entirely.
5
Formulate a fatwa using the maxim
When a mufti receives a case involving customary terminology, he must research the local custom. If the custom only specifies a general text (e.g., defining the word 'sale' locally), it is valid; if it abandons the text (e.g., declaring a prohibited transaction valid), it is rejected.
The mufti's duty is to apply the custom within the limits of the maxim.

[Consideration of general and specific custom] The summary is that general custom is not considered if it necessitates abandoning the text, but it is considered if it necessitates specifying the text. Specific custom is not considered in either case; it is only considered with respect to its people only if it does not necessitate abandoning the text nor specifying it, even if it contradicts the apparent ruling. Commentary
This passage presents the rasm rule regarding the consideration of general and specific custom (urf) in issuing fatwas. The summary: General custom (urf 'amm) is not considered if it leads to complete abandonment of a clear text. However, it is considered if it merely specifies the text without nullifying it. Specific custom (urf khas) is not considered in either situation; it can only be considered within the community that practices it, provided it neither abandons nor specifies the text. This applies even if the custom contradicts the apparent meaning of the text (zahir al-riwaya). The text gives examples: customary expressions used in oaths and contracts (sale, hire) are interpreted according to the local custom. Each locality follows its own custom, and people are dealt with accordingly regarding validity, invalidity, etc. The rationale: a speaker speaks according to his own custom and intends that meaning, not the technical meaning used by jurists. Customary terms become 'terminological realities' (haqa'iq istilahiyya), making the original linguistic meaning metaphorical. The passage quotes two sources: Jami' al-Fusulayn (all speech is interpreted by custom) and Fatawa al-Allama Qasim (the words of a waqf maker, testator, swearer, etc., are construed by their custom, regardless of linguistic or Sharia usage). The author concludes by noting the complexity of this topic and his decision to keep the discussion brief.

Key Terms
عرف عام (urf 'amm): A custom widely prevalent across all lands; considered only if it specifies the text.
عرف خاص (urf khas): A custom particular to a locality or group; considered only for its people if it does not conflict with text.
تخصيص النص (takhsis al-nass): Specifying a general text to exclude a particular case based on custom.
لا يعتبر (la yu'tabar): Not considered; not given legal weight in fatwa.
