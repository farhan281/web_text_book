Introducing Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti and Its Author
Front matter: title page and author introduction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author, title, and significance of Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti and explain its role in Hanafi fatwa methodology.

Core Idea
Ibn 'Abidin's commentaries are the backbone of later Hanafi fatwa practice.

Before diving into the rules of issuing fatwas, we must know the book and its author. This title page reveals key facts: the author is the esteemed Ibn 'Abidin, called the 'Seal of the Verifiers', and his work is a commentary on his own earlier text on mufti methodology. Understanding who Ibn 'Abidin is—his rank and authority—is essential for using this text correctly in fatwa work.

Guided Path
1
Read the title and subtitle
Start by reading the full title: 'Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'. 'Sharh' means commentary, 'Uqud' means contracts or knots, 'Rasm al-Mufti' means the drawing/outline of the mufti. So it is a commentary on the methodology of the mufti.
Note that the same phrase appears twice with slight variation—once in Arabic script and once in Urdu script.
2
Identify the author's full name and epithets
The author is al-Sayyid Muhammad Amin ibn 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz, famous as Ibn 'Abidin al-Shami. He is given the epithet 'Khatimat al-Muhaqqiqin' (Seal of the Verifiers), indicating his high rank among jurists.
His nisba 'al-Shami' indicates he is from Damascus (Sham).
3
Note the birth and death dates
The dates 1198 AH - 1252 AH are given. This places Ibn 'Abidin in the 12th-13th centuries AH, making him a later Hanafi authority.
These dates help contextualize his work within the later Hanafi tradition.
4
Observe the publication information
The text mentions a publisher location (Karachi, Pakistan) and a publication year (1197 AH or 1252 AH). This shows the book was printed in the modern era.
The date 1197 AH is likely a typo or refers to another event; the author died in 1252 AH.
5
Reflect on the significance
The title page tells us that this is a commentary by a major later authority on his own work about mufti methodology. This means the text carries great weight in Hanafi fatwa practice.
In later Hanafi fatwa, Ibn 'Abidin's views (especially in Radd al-Muhtar) are often the relied-upon (mufta bihi) positions.

Ibn Abidin is described as 'Khatimat al-Muhaqqiqin' (Seal of the Investigators), signifying his status as the last major authority whose works are heavily relied upon in Hanafi fatwa.
This work is part of the 'mutakhkhirin' (later scholars) layer of Hanafi authority, often used to determine the mufta bihi (operative opinion).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the full title and author of this foundational commentary and explain its purpose in the context of Hanafi Usul al-Iftaa.

Core Idea

The publisher's introduction to this new edition reminds us that the science of iftaa is a living tradition, constantly being revisited to serve new generations. This lesson introduces the text and the discipline it represents, anchoring your study of Hanafi legal methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Genre and Relationship
Recognize that this is a sharh (commentary) on a matn (Uqud Rasm al-Mufti). Understand that the matn lays down foundational principles, and the sharh expands and clarifies them.

2

The author is Muhammad Amin ibn Abidin, known as 'Khatimat al-Muhaqqiqin' (seal of the verifiers). His position in the later Hanafi school is pivotal; his works, including this commentary, are highly authoritative.

3
Extract the Rationale for Renewal
The publisher cites two reasons: changed mindsets of the new generation and difficulties with old printing. This demonstrates that the tradition is maintained by adapting its presentation, not its content.
This reflects the principle of 'taghayyur al-fatwa bi taghayyur al-zaman' applicable to form, not substance.
4
Define Usul al-Iftaa
From the text, define Usul al-Iftaa as the science that governs how a mufti derives fatwas, including qualifications, methodology, and hierarchy of evidence.
This is distinct from Usul al-Fiqh, which focuses on deriving rulings from primary sources.

This lesson introduces the seminal work 'Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' by Ibn 'Abidin, a cornerstone of Hanafi Usul al-Iftaa. We explore why a modern edition was necessary and how the editorial committee aimed to make this text accessible to contemporary students while preserving its authenticity.

Identify Key Editorial Modifications

These changes improve readability while maintaining textual integrity.
4
Appreciate the Role of Usul al-Iftaa
Reflect on how works like 'Rasm al-Mufti' systematize the fatwa process. The book is not just a manual but a methodology that ensures consistency and adherence to Hanafi principles.

Authority of Al-Kafi and Al-Mabsut in Hanafi Fatwa
Introducing authoritative Hanafi texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Al-Kafi and Al-Mabsut, explain their relationship, and state why Al-Mabsut is the ultimate authority for Hanafi fatwa.

Core Idea
Al-Mabsut of al-Sarakhsi is the supreme authority for Hanafi fatwa; no fatwa is given against it.

This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti introduces two cornerstone texts of the Hanafi madhhab: Al-Kafi by al-Hakim al-Shahid and its commentary Al-Mabsut by al-Sarakhsi. Understanding their relationship and authoritative status is essential for any mufti or student of Hanafi fiqh. The passage clarifies that Al-Kafi is a compilation of the six books of Zahir al-Riwaya, and Al-Mabsut is the relied-upon commentary, such that all fatwa decisions are based upon it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text: Al-Kafi
Al-Kafi by al-Hakim al-Shahid is a compilation of Imam Muhammad's six books of Zahir al-Riwaya. It is a relied-upon text for transmitting the madhhab.
Al-Kafi itself is a mukhtasar (compendium) of the Zahir al-Riwaya books.
2
Identify the commentary: Al-Mabsut
Al-Mabsut by Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi is a commentary on Al-Kafi. It is the most famous and authoritative commentary, often referred to simply as 'Al-Mabsut'.
Al-Sarakhsi dictated Al-Mabsut while imprisoned in Uzjand.
3
Assess the authority of Al-Mabsut
The passage quotes al-Tarsusi: 'Mabsut al-Sarakhsi is not acted upon in opposition to it, nor is reliance placed except on it; no fatwa is given except based on it.' This establishes its supreme authority.
All later Hanafi fatwa work is based on Al-Mabsut.
4
Resolve the confusion about Al-Mukhtasar
Khayr al-Ramli mistakenly thought Al-Mabsut was a commentary on another work called Al-Mukhtasar. The passage clarifies that Al-Kafi itself is a mukhtasar, so Al-Mabsut is indeed a commentary on Al-Kafi, not a different work.
This confusion arises from the term 'mukhtasar' being used generically.

The Prohibition of Acting on al-Marjuh
Ruling statement: prohibition of marjuh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acting and issuing fatwa based on a marjuh opinion and explain the rationale behind the prohibition.

Core Idea
Acting on a marjuh opinion is prohibited; the mufti must only rely on the rajih (preponderant) view.

The table of contents highlights 'عدم جواز العمل والإفتاء بالمرجوح' as a key principle. This lesson delves into that ruling, explaining why the Hanafi school forbids adopting weakened opinions for action or fatwa, and how this protects the integrity of the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Define marjuh and rajih
Understand the terms: marjuh is the outweighed opinion, rajih is the preponderant one. The prohibition is about acting on the marjuh when a rajih exists.
2
Identify the ruling
The table states it is impermissible (la yajuz) to act and issue fatwa by the marjuh. Recognize this as a firm prohibition (tahrim).
3
Understand the rationale
The prohibition ensures that fatwas are based on the strongest evidence within the school, preserving consistency and authority.
4
Apply to a scenario
If a mufti finds two opinions on a matter, one rajih from Zahir al-Riwayah and one marjuh from a rare transmission, he must choose the rajih and cannot use the marjuh.

The Hierarchy of Sources for Fatwa
Qawida: priority of sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchy of sources: start with primary texts (mutun), then commentaries (shuruh), then fatwa collections (fatawa) when deriving a fatwa.

Core Idea
Sources must be consulted in order: first mutun, then shuruh, then fatawa.

The table of contents concludes with the prioritization: 'يقدم المتون ثم الشروح ثم الفتوى'. This lesson breaks down that hierarchy, explaining why each layer is used and how to move from one to the next when the earlier source is silent or insufficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary texts (mutun)
The mutun are concise foundational works like al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri and al-Asl. These are the first authority.
2
Move to commentaries (shuruh)
If the matn is unclear or silent, consult commentaries like al-Hidayah and al-Kafi, which explain and elaborate.
3
Finally, fatwa collections (fatawa)
Fatwa collections like al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya compile past rulings and are used when earlier sources are exhausted.
4
Understand the rationale
This hierarchy ensures that the closest source to the original texts is used first, preserving authenticity.
