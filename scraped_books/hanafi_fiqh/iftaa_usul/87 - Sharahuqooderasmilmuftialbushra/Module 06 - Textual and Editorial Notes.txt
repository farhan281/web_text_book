The Manuscripts of al-Mabsut: Original vs. Commentary
Describing the multiplicity of al-Mabsut manuscripts and the nature of later commentaries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the original text of al-Mabsut and its later commentaries, and explain why citations from commentaries may be ambiguous.

Core Idea
Not every 'Mabsut' is Muhammad's Mabsut — later commentaries mixed their own explanations with the original.

When studying Hanafi fiqh, you will often encounter references to 'al-Mabsut.' But this passage reveals a critical nuance: the name 'al-Mabsut' can refer to the original work of Imam Muhammad or to later commentaries that blended their own explanations with his text. Understanding which version is being cited is essential for accurate transmission and fatwa derivation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text
Recognize that al-Mabsut is part of the Zahir al-Riwaya corpus by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. It is a foundational work but exists in multiple recensions.
The original al-Mabsut is one of the six books of Zahir al-Riwaya.
2
List the known manuscript recensions
The most prominent recension is by Abu Sulayman al-Juzjani. Later scholars like Khawahar Zadeh (al-Mabsut al-Kabir) and Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani wrote commentaries that are also called 'Mabsut'.
Al-Mabsut al-Kabir is a commentary, not the original.
3
Distinguish original from commentary
Later commentaries often mix the original text of Muhammad with the commentator's own explanations, making it difficult to separate them. This was also done for al-Jami' al-Saghir.
When a later text says 'Qadi Khan mentioned in al-Jami' al-Saghir,' it often refers to his commentary, not the original.
4
Apply the rule to citations
When encountering a reference to 'al-Mabsut' in later works, verify whether it is citing the original Zahir al-Riwaya text or a later commentary, as the authority level differs.
Citing a commentary may introduce the commentator's own ijtihad.
5
Determine the authority level
Use the Hanafi textual hierarchy: original Zahir al-Riwaya works are of highest authority; recensions like al-Juzjani's are also reliable but may have slight variations; later commentaries are of lower authority and should be used with caution.
The mufta bihi is taken from Zahir al-Riwaya, but later commentaries may clarify or be used for tarjih.

Commentary
This passage discusses the textual history of al-Mabsut, one of the core works of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (Zahir al-Riwaya). The author, Ibn Abi al-Izz, notes that there are multiple recensions (nuskh) of al-Mabsut transmitted from Muhammad. The most prominent is that of Abu Sulayman al-Juzjani. Later scholars wrote commentaries on al-Mabsut, such as Bakr 'Khawahar Zadeh' (whose work is called al-Mabsut al-Kabir) and Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani. However, these later commentaries often mix their own explanations with the original text of Muhammad's Mabsut, so that it becomes difficult to distinguish the original from the commentary. This same practice occurred with the commentaries on al-Jami' al-Saghir by Fakhr al-Islam and Qadi Khan: when one says 'Qadi Khan mentioned it in al-Jami' al-Saghir,' what is meant is his commentary, not the original text. The passage also references other works: al-Bayri's commentary on al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, and Isma'il al-Nabulusi's commentary on Sharh al-Durar. The passage ends with a note that this was summarized.

Key Terms
المبسوط (al-Mabsut): A foundational text of Hanafi fiqh by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, part of the Zahir al-Riwaya corpus. Here, it refers to the original work and its multiple transmitted versions.
شرح المبسوط (sharh al-Mabsut): Later commentaries on al-Mabsut that often incorporate the original text mixed with the commentator's explanations, making it hard to separate them.

Meaning and Correct Pronunciation of 'al-Siyar al-Kabir'
Defining the term 'al-Siyar' and its correct form and rank among Hanafi texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct pronunciation and definition of 'al-Siyar al-Kabir' and state its rank among the six Usul books.

Core Idea
'Al-Siyar al-Kabir' is the plural, not singular, and is the last of the six Zahir al-Riwayah works, relied upon for siyar rulings.

This lesson clarifies a common terminological error among students: the correct name and pronunciation of 'al-Siyar al-Kabir', one of the six foundational books in the Hanafi madhhab. Understanding this ensures that when you reference this text in fatwa or study, you do so with scholarly precision. We will also examine its place among the other Usul works.

Guided Path
1
Define 'al-Siyar' linguistically
Identify the plural form 'Siyar' from singular 'Sira' meaning 'manner' or 'path'. In Islamic law, it specifically refers to the Prophet's military expeditions.
2
Correct the pronunciation
Apply the rule from al-Maghrib: The adjective 'kabir' is masculine because it stands for 'kitab' (book). The correct pronunciation is 'al-Siyar al-Kabir' with kasra on sin and fatha on ya, not 'al-Sir al-Kabir' (singular).
3
Rank the six Usul books
Recall that al-Mabsut is known as al-Asl (the foundation), compiled first. Then al-Jami al-Saghir, based on al-Asl. Al-Siyar al-Kabir was the last to be composed, making it the relied-upon (mu'tamad) for matters of siyar.
The order is not chronological importance but composition sequence.
4
Synthesize the authority hierarchy
Sum up: The earliest text (al-Asl) holds foundational authority, but later works like al-Siyar al-Kabir become the mu'tamad for their specific chapters. A mufti must know which text to rely upon.

Meaning of al-Siyar. Supplement: al-Siyar is the plural of Sira, which means the manner in matters. In the Shari'ah, it is specific to his military expeditions. Commentary
This passage is a lexical and terminological clarification about the book 'al-Siyar al-Kabir' by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. The author begins by defining 'al-Siyar' linguistically: it is the plural of Sira, meaning a manner or way of doing things. In Islamic law, Sira specifically refers to the military expeditions (maghazi) of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The author quotes from al-Maghrib to correct a common mispronunciation: the correct name is 'al-Siyar al-Kabir' with a kasra under the sin (siyar) and fatha on the ya, in the plural form, not 'al-Sir al-Kabir' (singular) which is a mistake. The masculine adjective 'kabir' is because the term stands in place of 'kitab' (book). This is similar to saying 'Jami' al-Saghir' (not 'Jam' al-Saghir') and 'Jami' al-Kabir'. He then notes the relative status of the books: al-Mabsut is famously known as 'al-Asl' (the foundation) because it was composed first among the six Usul books. Al-Jami' al-Saghir came after it, and its contents are based on al-Asl. The last of the six to be composed was al-Siyar al-Kabir, so it is the 'mu'tamad' (relied upon) for matters of siyar. This last part may indicate that later works rely on it. The passage is important for muftis to properly identify the authoritative texts and their correct names.

Key Terms
السير (al-Siyar): Plural of Sira: means paths or ways; in Hanafi fiqh, specifically refers to the book on the Prophet's expeditions and Islamic warfare.
المبسوط (al-Mabsūṭ): The first of the six Usul books, known as 'al-Asl' (the foundation), authored by Imam Muhammad.
الجامع الصغير (al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaghīr): Second of the six Usul books, also by Imam Muhammad, summarizing issues from al-Mabsut.
