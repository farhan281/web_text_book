The Seven Layers of Fuqaha: Understanding Mufti Hierarchy
Hierarchical classification of mujtahid authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three highest layers of Hanafi mujtahids and state the scope of each layer's authority.

Core Idea
Knowing the rank of each mujtahid determines the weight of his opinion in tarjih.

In Hanafi usul al-ifta, not all jurists are equal. The mufti must know which opinions carry more authority when deriving a fatwa. This lesson introduces the hierarchical classification (tabaqat) of fuqaha, focusing on the first three layers: absolute mujtahids, mujtahids within the madhhab, and mujtahids for unresolved cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first layer
Find the description of the highest layer: mujtahid fi al-shar'. Note that they do not follow any imam in usul or furu'.
Examples: the four Imams.
2
Identify the second layer
Find the description of mujtahid fi al-madhhab. They follow the Imam's usul but may differ in furu'.
Examples: Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
Identify the third layer
Find the description of mujtahids for issues with no narration from the Imam. They cannot contradict the Imam in usul or furu' but deduce rulings for new cases.
Examples: al-Khassaf, al-Tahawi, al-Karkhi.
4
Relate layers to fatwa authority
Understand that the higher the layer, the more weight their opinion carries in tarjih. The first layer's opinions are foundational, the second layer's are within the madhhab, and the third layer's are limited to unresolved cases.

Layers of the Fuqaha
We say: The fuqaha are of seven layers:
First layer: The layer of the mujtahids in the Shari'ah, like the four Imams and those who follow their path in establishing the principles of usul and deriving the rulings of the branches from the four evidences without taqlid of anyone, neither in the branches nor in usul.
Second: The layer of the mujtahids in the madhhab, like Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and the rest of the companions of Abu Hanifa who are capable of extracting rulings from the mentioned [evidences] according to the principles laid down by their teacher, for although they may disagree with him on some rulings of the branches, they follow him in the principles of usul.
Third layer: The layer of the mujtahids in matters for which there is no narration from the founder of the madhhab, like al-Khassaf, Abu Ja'far al-Tahawi, Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi, Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani, Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi, Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi, Fakhr al-Din Qadi Khan, and others. They are not able to oppose the Imam, neither in usul nor in furu', but they deduce rulings for cases where there is no text from him, according to the principles he established and the rules he laid out.
Year: I say: al-Khassaf died in 261, al-Tahawi in 321, al-Karkhi in 340, al-Halwani in 456, al-Sarakhsi around 500, al-Bazdawi in 482, Qadi Khan in 593, al-Razi in 370, al-Quduri in 428, and the author of al-Hidayah in 593.

Commentary
This passage is a classification (tabaqat) of the fuqaha into seven layers. The author provides the first three layers in detail. The first layer is the absolute mujtahid (mujtahid fi al-shar'), who derives rulings directly from the four sources without any taqlid. The four Imams and those following their methodology are cited as examples. The second layer is the mujtahid within the madhhab (mujtahid fi al-madhhab), such as Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and other companions of Abu Hanifa. They follow Abu Hanifa's usul but may differ in some branch rulings. The third layer is the mujtahid in issues where no narration exists from the Imam (mujtahid fi al-masa'il allati la riwayata fiha 'an sahib al-madhhab); scholars like al-Khassaf, al-Tahawi, al-Karkhi, al-Halwani, al-Sarakhsi, al-Bazdawi, Qadi Khan, etc. They cannot contradict the Imam in usul or furu' but derive rulings based on his principles for new cases. The text then adds a list of death dates (tawaffi) of these scholars, likely as a reference note. This classification helps the muqallid mufti know whom to follow and when to prefer one opinion over another.

Key Terms
مجتهد في الشرع (mujtahid fi al-shar'): An absolute mujtahid who can deduce rulings from the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas without following any specific imam.
مجتهد في المذهب (mujtahid fi al-madhhab): A mujtahid who derives rulings within the framework of a specific madhhab (here Hanafi) using its principles, but may disagree in furu'.
طبقات الفقهاء (tabaqat al-fuqaha): Hierarchical classification of jurists based on their level of ijtihad and authority.

The Highest Tabaqat: Absolute and Madhhab-Level Mujtahids
Classification of top-tier Hanafi jurists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the first three classes of Hanafi jurists (absolute mujtahid, mujtahid fi al-madhhab, mujtahid fi al-masail) and state their scope of ijtihad and limitations.

Core Idea
Only the top three classes perform ijtihad; below them, scholars apply and refine established rulings.

The Hanafi school classifies its jurists into seven hierarchical levels, each with a specific role in deriving and transmitting legal rulings. This lesson focuses on the highest three classes: those who can perform independent ijtihad or work within the madhhab's principles to derive new rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first class (absolute mujtahids)
Recognize that the first class consists of jurists who derive rulings directly from the four proofs without taqlid. They establish usul and furu independently. The four Imams are the prime examples.
These are the founders of the madhahib.
2
Identify the second class (mujtahidun fi al-madhhab)
These jurists follow the usul of their Imam (e.g., Abu Hanifa) but can differ in furu. Examples include Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
They are bound by the Imam's principles but can exercise ijtihad within that framework.
3
Identify the third class (mujtahidun fi al-masail)
These jurists cannot disagree with the Imam in usul or furu, but they derive rulings for unprecedented cases using the Imam's principles. They populate the madhhab for new scenarios.
Examples: al-Khassaf, al-Tahawi, al-Karkhi, al-Sarakhsi, al-Bazdawi, Qadi Khan.
4
Compare the degree of ijtihad
The first class has absolute ijtihad; the second has ijtihad within the madhhab; the third has ijtihad only for cases without explicit text.
Only the first two classes can potentially contradict the Imam in furu.

Commentary
This passage presents the foundational classification of Hanafi jurists into seven hierarchical classes, a key principle in the discipline of ifta (issuing legal verdicts). The author begins by enumerating the classes: First are the absolute mujtahids (mujtahidun fi al-shar), exemplified by the four imams, who derive rulings directly from the Quran, Sunnah, ijma, and qiyas without taqlid. Second are the mujtahids within the madhhab (mujtahidun fi al-madhhab), like Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, who follow Abu Hanifa's usul but can disagree in furu. Third are the mujtahids in specific cases (mujtahidun fi al-masail), who cannot contradict the imam but derive rulings for unprecedented issues using his principles; examples include al-Khassaf and al-Tahawi. The marginal note provides death dates for these scholars. Fourth are the ashab al-takhrij, who can clarify ambiguous rulings by extending principles to analogous cases but cannot perform independent ijtihad. Fifth are the ashab al-tarjih, who weigh and prefer one narration over another using criteria like soundness or benefit. Sixth are the compilers of matn works who distinguish between strong and weak narrations and omit the latter. Seventh are the lowest muqallids who lack even this discerning ability. This classification is crucial for muftis to know the authority of different sources and the weight of opinions within the madhhab. It guides which level of scholar can overrule or choose between positions, especially when conflicting narrations exist.

Key Terms
مجتهد في الشرع (mujtahid fi al-shar): An absolute mujtahid who can derive rulings directly from the four proofs without following any imam, in both usul and furu.
مجتهد في المذهب (mujtahid fi al-madhhab): A jurist who extracts rulings based on the principles of the madhhab's founder, able to disagree in furu but bound by the imam's usul.
مجتهد في المسائل (mujtahid fi al-masail): A jurist who derives rulings for cases where no explicit narration exists from the founder, using the founder's principles, but cannot disagree with him.

The Lower Tabaqat: From Takhrij to Taqlid
Classification of lower-tier Hanafi jurists (takhrij, tarjih, matn compilers, muqallids).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate the lower four classes of Hanafi jurists (ashab al-takhrij, ashab al-tarjih, matn compilers, and plain muqallids) and explain their roles in the madhhab's development and fatwa process.

Core Idea
Below the mujtahidun, scholars clarify, prefer, and transmit the madhhab's rulings; the lowest level simply follows without discernment.

While the top three classes engage in ijtihad, the remaining four classes are muqallids—they follow the established rulings but contribute in essential ways. This lesson covers the practitioners of takhrij (extension of principles), tarjih (preference of narrations), compilers of authoritative matns, and the common muqallids.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fourth class (ashab al-takhrij)
These muqallids cannot perform ijtihad but can resolve ambiguous rulings by applying principles and analogy. They detail how a general statement from the founder applies to specific cases.
Example: al-Razi (al-Jassas). His takhrij appears in al-Hidayah.
2
Identify the fifth class (ashab al-tarjih)
These muqallids prefer some narrations over others using criteria like stronger transmission, consistency with qiyas, or benefit to people. They shape the relied-upon fatwa.
Examples: al-Quduri, author of al-Hidayah.
3
Identify the sixth class (matn compilers)
These scholars distinguish between strong and weak narrations and compile matns (concise texts) that transmit only the sound positions, omitting weak or rejected opinions.
Examples: author of al-Kanz, al-Mukhtar, al-Wiqaya.
4
Identify the seventh class (plain muqallids)
These muqallids cannot distinguish between strong and weak narrations or even between different levels; they simply follow without discernment.
The text abruptly ends, but this class is the lowest.

This passage presents the foundational classification of Hanafi jurists into seven hierarchical classes, a key principle in the discipline of ifta (issuing legal verdicts). Commentary
This passage from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti provides a foundational classification of Hanafi legal sources, essential for any mufti or advanced student. The author, likely Ibn 'Abidin or a similar commentator, systematically divides all Hanafi legal issues into three hierarchical categories. The first and most authoritative is 'Masa'il al-Usul' or 'Zahir al-Riwayah'—the issues reported in the six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. These are the primary texts of the madhhab, transmitted through reliable chains, and constitute the default position for fatwa. The second category is 'Nawadir'—issues narrated from the same early authorities but not in the six books. They may appear in Muhammad's other works, in the Amali (dictations) of Abu Yusuf, or in isolated narrations from individuals like Ibn Sama'a. These have lower evidentiary weight but are still authoritative within the madhhab. The third category is 'Fatawa and Waqi'at'—later jurists' independent deductions when no direct transmission exists from the founders. These later mujtahids, such as those listed, may even depart from the early masters when evidence supports it. The author notes that many later fatwa collections, like Fatawa Qadi Khan, mix these categories indiscriminately, while some, like al-Muhit by Radi al-Din al-Sarakhsi, properly distinguish them. This classification is crucial for a mufti to know which rulings take precedence in case of conflict.

Key Terms
مسائل الأصول (masa'il al-usul): The fundamental issues of the Hanafi madhhab, identical to Zahir al-Riwayah, which are the rulings found in the six core books of Imam Muhammad.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The manifestly transmitted reports: the rulings narrated from Muhammad al-Shaybani via reliable chains, either mutawatir or mashhur.
النوادر (al-nawadir): Rare transmissions: rulings narrated from the early Hanafi authorities but not found in the six core books, including isolated narrations and dictation works.
الفتاوى والواقعات (al-fatawa wa al-waqi'at): Later juristic opinions and event-based rulings deduced by later mujtahids when no direct transmission existed from the early scholars.

The Hanafi Rule on Multiple Opinions of a Mujtahid
Explains Hanafi stance on multiple opinions and narrations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school denies a mujtahid can hold two contradictory opinions simultaneously and apply the rule of taharrin to resolve conflicting narrations.

Core Idea
A mujtahid cannot have two valid opinions at once; multiple narrations are temporal, not simultaneous.

This lesson examines a foundational principle in Hanafi usul al-iftaa: whether a mujtahid can have two contradictory opinions. The passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti clarifies that the Hanafi school rejects simultaneous multiple opinions, instead viewing them as chronological developments. It also explains the mechanism of taharrin (investigation) for resolving conflicts and affirms that the statements of Abu Hanifa's disciples are effectively his own rulings. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for a mufti when faced with multiple narrations.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify that the Hanafi school denies the possibility of a mujtahid having two valid contradictory opinions simultaneously. Multiple opinions are only temporal.
This is a key difference from the Shafi'i school.
2
Understand taharrin
When evidences conflict without clear preponderance, the mujtahid performs taharrin (investigation) and acts by the testimony of his heart. After acting, the other evidence becomes invalid without stronger proof.
Taharrin is unique to Hanafi usul.
3
Contrast with Shafi'i view
Al-Shafi'i allows a mujtahid to act by either evidence without taharrin, leading to multiple valid opinions on one issue.
This explains why Shafi'i school has multiple well-known positions.
4
Apply to narrations from Imam Abu Hanifa
Two narrations from the Imam on one issue were given at different times; only the later one is correct. If unknown, later scholars resolved using tarjih or other indicators.
The passage admits this is problematic but offers solutions.
5
Recognize disciples' statements as Imam's
Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar, and others stated that every opinion they expressed was a narration from Abu Hanifa. Thus, taking their view is effectively taking the Imam's view.
This reinforces the hierarchical authority in fatwa.

Hierarchy of Fatwa in the Hanafi Madhhab
Establishes the ranking of opinions for fatwa within the Hanafi school

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the standard Hanafi hierarchy for preferring narrations when giving fatwa and apply it to determine which opinion a non-mujtahid mufti must follow.

Core Idea
A non-mujtahid mufti follows: Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar and al-Hasan, but a mujtahid may choose based on evidence.

When the imams of the Hanafi school differ on a ruling, how does a mufti know which opinion to give? The school has a clear hierarchy that prioritises the founder Abu Hanifa and then his senior students. However, this hierarchy is not absolute: a mufti who is qualified to weigh evidence (dalil) is permitted to depart from it. This lesson unpacks the key rules and exceptions from a foundational rasm al-mufti passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
The passage begins by stating that when the imams agree, no mujtahid within the madhhab may deviate. This is the starting point: collective agreement binds everyone.
This principle is often abbreviated as 'لا يجوز لمجتهد أن يعدل عن اتفاق أئمتنا'.
2
Trace the hierarchy when they differ
If they differ, the order is: Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar and al-Hasan (same rank, though some give al-Hasan precedence). This hierarchy is the default for giving fatwa.
The rule is from al-Fatawa al-Sirajiyya: 'الفتوى على الإطلاق على قول أبي حنيفة ثم قول أبي يوسف ثم قول محمد ثم قول زفر والحسن.'
3
Understand the special case: imam alone vs. companions
When Abu Hanifa stands alone against his two companions who agree, there is disagreement. The preferred view (for a non-mujtahid) is to follow Abu Hanifa. For a mujtahid, he may choose the stronger evidence.
Ibn al-Mubarak held that Abu Hanifa is always preferred; others allow choice. The passage settles: the first is correct if the mufti is not a mujtahid.
4
Apply the exception for the mujtahid mufti
A mufti who is mujtahid (can weigh evidence) is not bound by the hierarchy. He looks at the strength of the evidence (العبرة لقوة الدليل). This is the mufta bihi position according to al-Hawi.
This exception is crucial: the hierarchy is a tool for those who cannot perform ijtihad, not a straitjacket for the qualified.

This is a rasm al-mufti passage establishing the hierarchy of narrations (tarjih) within the Hanafi madhhab. The author begins by affirming a foundational principle: when the founding imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, etc.) agree on a ruling, no mujtahid within the madhhab may deviate from it with his own opinion, because the collective view of the imams is more sound. Ibn al-Humam is an authoritative mufti within the Hanafi school, capable of tarjih, but his opinions outside the madhhab are not followed.

The author emphasizes that Ibn al-Humam's preferences can be followed only as long as they do not leave the madhhab. He has choices that contradict the madhhab, and those are not to be followed.
This preserves the school's integrity while allowing ijtihad within it.

Tenth Rule of Tarjih: Preferring Stronger Evidence
Rasm rule with illustrative examples of tarjih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the tenth rule of tarjih to resolve conflicts between corrected opinions (tashih) by identifying the stronger evidence and using other murajjihaat.

Core Idea
When two tashih conflict, precedence goes to the one with stronger evidence, as determined by a qualified examiner.

When two corrected opinions (tashih) appear to conflict, a mufti must have a systematic method to decide which to follow. The tenth rule of tarjih provides this method: preference is given to the opinion whose evidence is clearer and stronger. This principle is illustrated by historical examples where later scholars departed from earlier rulings due to changed circumstances, such as requiring explicit witness verification instead of presuming probity, and permitting hiring for teaching despite earlier prohibitions. Understanding this rule is essential for consistent fatwa practice in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Recognize that you have two corrected opinions (tashih) on the same issue from authoritative sources within the madhhab. Note that both are considered valid transmissions but appear to lead to different rulings.
This conflict may arise between earlier and later scholars or between different lines of transmission.
2
Examine the evidence for each
Evaluate the strength of the proof (dalil) behind each tashih. This requires the capacity for legal reasoning (ahl al-nazar). Consider the clarity, authenticity, and applicability of the evidence to the current context.
The text emphasizes that preference is by strength of evidence (al-tarjih bi-quwwat al-dalil).
3
Apply the rule of stronger evidence
If one tashih has demonstrably stronger evidence, act upon it. This overrides any other considerations, as the strength of evidence is the primary murajjih.
Example: For hiring teaching, the later tashih citing necessity (darura) had stronger evidence than the earlier blanket prohibition.
4
Consider secondary murajjihaat if evidence is equal
If the evidence of both tashih is equally strong, look to other factors: being recorded in the core texts (mutun), being the view of Imam Abu Hanifa, or being part of the manifest transmission (zahir al-riwaya). These secondary preferents can tip the balance.
