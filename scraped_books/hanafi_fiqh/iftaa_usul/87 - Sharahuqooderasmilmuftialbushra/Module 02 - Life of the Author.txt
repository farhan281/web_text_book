The Life and Legacy of Ibn Abidin – Author of Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti
Biographical account of the author's life and works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key biographical details of Ibn Abidin and explain how his scholarly career established his authority as a Hanafi mufti.

Core Idea
Ibn Abidin's life: a tireless Hanafi jurist whose works, especially Radd al-Muhtar, became foundational for fatwa.

This lesson introduces you to Muhammad Amin Ibn Abidin, the author of Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. His biography is not merely historical — it explains why his work carries tremendous weight in Hanafi fatwa practice. From his conversion to the Hanafi school to his meticulous correction of texts and his vast output, Ibn Abidin’s life exemplifies the ideal mufti.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and his full name
Note his full name: Muhammad Amin ibn Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz al-Dimashqi, famously known as Ibn Abidin. He is a Sayyid, connecting to Imam Zayn al-Abidin.
This lineage establishes his noble ancestry, which contributed to his respect in society.
2
Trace his educational journey
He studied Shafi'i fiqh, Arabic, and Qur'an with Shaykh al-Hamawi, then converted to Hanafi fiqh under Shaykh Shakir al-Aqqad, under whom he also studied tafsir, hadith, usul, tasawwuf.
His conversion to Hanafi madhhab is a key moment — he became a leading Hanafi mufti.
3
Notice his scholarly habits
He devoted nights to authoring and correcting books, and days to teaching and issuing fatwas. He was keen on correcting errors in texts.
This habit underscores his commitment to accuracy — a critical trait for a mufti.
4
List his major works
His most famous work is Radd al-Muhtar ala al-Durr al-Mukhtar, a supercommentary. Also Minhah al-Khaliq, Sharh Manzumah Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, a fatwa collection, and numerous glosses.
Radd al-Muhtar is considered the primary reference for later Hanafi fatwa.
5
Note his death and burial
He died in 1252 AH and was buried near Shaykh Ala al-Din al-Haskafi, the author of al-Durr al-Mukhtar.
His burial near al-Haskafi symbolizes his connection to the Hanafi tradition.

In Ghayat al-Bayan from Fakhr al-Islam: When al-Jami' al-Saghir was presented to Abu Yusuf, he admired it and said, 'Abu Abd Allah memorized it except for issues in which I consider him mistaken in his narration from me.' When that reached Muhammad, he said: 'I memorized them, but he forgot.' They are six issues, mentioned in al-Bahr in the chapter of Witr and Nawafil.
Source pages p. 25

Commentary
This passage gives the historical background of al-Jami' al-Saghir, one of the most important books in the Hanafi madhhab. Abu Yusuf, the great disciple of Abu Hanifa, asked Imam Muhammad to compile a collection of rulings that he could narrate from Muhammad on the authority of Abu Hanifa. Muhammad complied, and the book contained 1,532 issues. The book was so highly regarded that it was used to test candidates for judgeship. The key incident: upon review, Abu Yusuf claimed that Muhammad had preserved the book except for six issues in which he had misnarrated from Abu Yusuf. Muhammad responded that he had actually memorized those issues correctly but Abu Yusuf had forgotten them. This shows the high level of scholarly honesty and the importance of oral transmission. The six issues are not detailed here but are referenced in al-Bahr. This lesson underscores that the Jami' al-Saghir is not only authoritative but also subject to verification between the imams.

المسائل الستة (al-Masa'il al-Sitta): Six specific issues in al-Jami' al-Saghir where Abu Yusuf claimed Muhammad erred in narrating from him, but Muhammad asserted his own memory was correct.

Reason for Compiling Al-Siyar al-Kabir
Narrating the impetus from al-Awza'i's criticism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to narrate the historical impetus for Imam Muhammad's authorship of al-Siyar al-Kabir and identify its significance within the Hanafi transmission hierarchy.

Core Idea
A Syrian scholar's challenge drove Imam Muhammad to produce the Hanafi magnum opus on Islamic international law.

This brief passage from Al-Sarakhsi’s introduction to al-Siyar al-Kabir reveals the motivating context behind Imam Muhammad’s final fiqh work. It illustrates how external critique catalyzed the expansion of the Hanafi corpus, and underscores the competitive scholarly environment that shaped the madhhab’s classical texts.

Commentary
This passage recounts the motivation for Imam Muhammad's compilation of al-Siyar al-Kabir. Al-Sarakhsi identifies it as Muhammad's last fiqh work. The trigger was a challenge from Imam al-Awza'i, a prominent Syrian scholar who questioned the competence of Iraqis (meaning the Hanafi school) in the field of Siyar (Islamic international law and military expeditions). Al-Awza'i argued that since the prophetic campaigns occurred in Syria and Hijaz, Iraqis lacked authentic knowledge. This critique prompted Imam Muhammad to produce a comprehensive work on the subject, demonstrating that the Hanafi school had full command of the material. Al-Siyar al-Kabir thus became a major text in Hanafi fiqh, covering rulings related to jihad, treaties, and interactions with non-Muslims. The incident highlights the competitive scholarly environment that spurred the development of the madhhab.
