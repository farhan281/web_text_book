The Prohibition of Fatwa from Qawaid Alone
Ruling against fatwa from legal maxims without explicit transmission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a mufti must base fatwa on explicit transmitted rulings (naql sarih) and explain why legal maxims (qawaid) alone are insufficient, applying the principle that qawaid are akthari (probabilistic) not kulli (universal).

Core Idea
A mufti cannot issue fatwa from legal maxims alone; he must transmit an explicit ruling because qawaid are probabilistic, not universal.

This passage from the Uqud Rasm al-Mufti warns against the dangerous practice of deriving fatwas solely from legal maxims. While qawaid are powerful tools for organizing fiqh, they are not exhaustive; every maxim has exceptions. The mufti, especially if not a mujtahid, must rely on explicit transmitted rulings (naql sarih) or seek guidance from scholars elsewhere. This lesson explains the reasoning behind this prohibition and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Determine what the mufti is prohibited from doing. The passage explicitly forbids issuing a fatwa from legal maxims and canons (qawaid wa dawabit) alone.
The prohibition applies even if a similar case (nazir) exists.
2
Understand the reasoning
Examine why qawaid are insufficient. The passage states that qawaid are 'akthari' (apply to most cases) not 'kulli' (universal). There can always be subtle differences between cases that escape the mufti's understanding.
Scholars have compiled books on furuq (distinctions) to highlight these differences.
3
Locate the authorities
Identify the Hanafi authorities cited: Ibn Nujaym (from al-Fawa'id al-Zayniyyah), al-Khaniya, and al-Zahiriyya. They all emphasize that the mufti must transmit (hikaya) explicit rulings.
Ibn Nujaym's statement is quoted as saying 'it is not permissible to issue a fatwa from the maxims and canons; rather the mufti must transmit the transmission.'
4
Recognize the exception
Note that customary incidents (urfiyyat) that do not contradict Shariah texts are an exception, but this is deferred to later in the poem.
This exception is not fully developed here; it is mentioned for completeness.
5
Apply the rule
If a mufti has no explicit text, he must pause (tawaqquf) or ask a more knowledgeable scholar even in another town.
This is derived from the citation of al-Khaniya.

Commentary
This passage addresses a critical methodological point: a mufti cannot rely on legal maxims (qawaid) alone to issue fatwa. The author explains that finding a similar case (nazir) is not sufficient because there may be subtle differences that escape the untrained eye. Scholars have written entire books on distinctions (furuq) to highlight such differences. Imam Ibn Nujaym is quoted explicitly forbidding fatwa from qawaid; the mufti must transmit an explicit ruling. He adds that fiqh maxims are probabilistic (akthari), not universal (kulli), meaning they have exceptions. Thus, without an explicit transmitted text, the mufti must withhold judgment or consult a more knowledgeable scholar. The passage then cites al-Zahiriyyah: a non-mujtahid may only transmit rulings. The exception is customary incidents that do not contradict Shariah, but the author leaves that for later.

Key Terms
قواعد الفقه (qawa'id al-fiqh): Legal maxims that encapsulate many fiqh rulings but are not universally applicable.
نقل صريح (naql sarih): An explicit transmitted ruling from an authoritative source.
حكاية (hikaya): The act of transmitting or quoting the sayings of jurists without personal ijtihad.

The Principle of Custom Changing Rulings
Listing examples where later fatwas depart from original Hanafi rulings due to changing custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'Rulings change with the change of custom' to identify when a later fatwa rightly departs from an earlier ruling, and distinguish legitimate custom-based changes from arbitrary deviations.

Core Idea
A Hanafi ruling based on custom can change when the custom changes – but only for non-ibadat matters and only to prevent harm.

This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti presents a critical tool in the mufti's toolkit: the recognition that rulings are not frozen in time. When social circumstances – especially widespread corruption (fasād al-zamān) or overwhelming necessity – alter the very fabric of people's practices, the mufti must sometimes set aside the original ruling and adopt a new one that better serves the Sharia's objectives. The author lists seventeen concrete examples from Hanafi fatwa literature where later scholars (mutāakhkhirūn) departed from the clear statements of Abu Hanifa or even Imam Muhammad, justified by the change in custom. This lesson walks you through the principle, its limits, and how to apply it.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the principle and its source
Understand that custom ('urf) can override earlier rulings when times change, but only under specific conditions such as corruption of the time (fasād al-zamān) or general necessity (ḍarūra).
The principle is a sub-maxim (dabt) of the major qawida 'al-āda muḥakkama'.
2
Identify the original ruling and the change
For each example in the text, note what the original Hanafi position was (often from Abu Hanifa or Muhammad) and what the later muftis ruled.
The text lists 17 examples; focus on understanding the pattern.
3
Locate the justification
Determine whether the change was due to corrupted times, necessity, or a shift in customary practice. The text often explicitly mentions 'li-fasād al-zamān' or 'li-l-ḍarūra'.
The justification is critical; without it, the change would be arbitrary.
4
Recognize the limits
Note that this principle does not apply to ibadat (matters of worship) or issues fixed by definitive texts. The qawida panel lists this as an istithnā'.
Ibadat are based on revelation (tawqīf) and not subject to custom.
5
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Given a contemporary case (e.g., electronic contracts, medical fees), decide whether a change based on custom is justified, citing the conditions from this lesson.
Practice using the template: Identify original ruling → Identify custom change → Justify via necessity/corruption → Conclude new ruling.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti presents a crucial principle of Hanafi ifta: rulings are not static but can change when customs ('urf) and circumstances (zaman) change, particularly due to corruption of the people (fasād al-zamān) or widespread necessity (umūm al-ḍarūra). The author begins by stating that many rulings originally laid down by Abu Hanifa (and the early mujtahids) based on their own customs and times were later revised by later muftis (mutāakhkhirūn) because the social context had changed. He then lists seventeen examples to illustrate this principle. 

Example 1: Hiring for teaching Quran – Abu Hanifa initially forbade taking wages for teaching Quran (as it is an act of worship), but later muftis permitted it due to practical necessity. 
Example 2: Duress (ikrāh) – In Abu Hanifa's time, only the ruler could coerce; later, due to widespread corruption, non-rulers could also force someone, so Imam Muhammad recognized that, and later fatwas followed suit. 
Example 3: Holding the tax collector (sā'ī) liable – The original rule is that the direct perpetrator (mubāshir) is liable, not the indirect cause (mutasabbib). But to deter corruption, they made the collector liable (even executing him in chaotic times). 
Example 4: Common hired worker (ajīr mushtarak) made liable – extended from the same reasoning. 
Example 5: Guardian (waṣī) not allowed to do mudāraba with orphan's wealth in our time – to protect the orphan. 
Example 6: Usurper (ghāṣib) of orphan's or waqf property is liable and cannot lease for more than one/number years – contrary to the original non-liability. 
Example 7: Judge cannot rule by his own knowledge – to prevent misuse. 
Example 8: Husband banned from traveling with wife even after giving immediate dower – due to corruption. 
Example 9: A husband's claim of an exception after an oath of divorce is not accepted without evidence – against the preferable narration. 
Example 10: Wife not believed if she claims non-receipt of immediate dower after consummation – against the rule that the denier's word is accepted, but customarily she does not submit before payment. 
Example 11: The phrase 'every halal is haram' – In some regions, it caused divorce by custom, whereas Muhammad said it requires intention. Later fatwas followed custom. 
Example 12: Father's claim of not giving trousseau – resolved based on custom contrary to the rule. 
Example 13: Wife's word accepted for deferred dower. 
Examples 14-15: In sharecropping and waqf cases, the word of both parties is accepted due to necessity. 
Example 16: Preemption lost if delayed one month – to prevent harm to buyer. 
Example 17 (from separate narration): A free woman who marries herself to a non-compatible man – the marriage is invalid. 

These examples demonstrate the dynamic nature of fatwa and the importance of considering custom and necessity even when they appear to contradict original texts.

Key Terms
العرف (al-'urf): Custom; prevailing practice of the people that is not opposed to sharia
فساد الزمان (fasād al-zamān): Corruption of the time; moral decay in society that justifies changing earlier rulings
تضمین (taḍmīn): Holding liable for damages or compensation

The Qawida of 'Urf: Acting Upon Custom Within Shariah Boundaries
Establishes the legal maxim of custom and its application in fatwa with examples from Hanafi texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'Act upon custom as long as it does not contradict Shariah' to new nawazil cases by distinguishing valid custom from prohibited practice and selecting the appropriate opinion from the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Custom is authoritative unless it directly violates a text of Shariah.

This lesson explores the Hanafi maxim that a mufti must consider the customs of his time when issuing fatwas, as long as those customs do not conflict with Shariah. The passage from Rasm al-Mufti demonstrates this through concrete examples: the acceptance of witness testimony in murder cases and the effect of personal habit on fasting. These cases show how the mufti navigates between the literal madhhab and the need to avoid public harm. Understanding this maxim is essential for modern fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Qawida
Recognize the maxim: 'Act upon custom as long as it does not contradict Shariah.' Note its rank (sughra) and type (dabt).
This is a restriction on the major maxim 'Custom is authoritative.'
2
Understand the Condition
The maxim applies only to customs that do not directly violate Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma. Customs like usury or unlawful taxes are excluded.
The passage explicitly excludes practices like riba and maks.
3
Analyze the Testimony Example
The mufti must choose between Abu Hanifa's view (rejecting testimony) and the Sahibayn's view (accepting) based on custom and public harm. Note thamarah al-khilaf.
Al-Maqdisi preferred the Sahibayn's view to prevent criminals from exploiting the rule.
4
Analyze the Fasting Example
The ruling on meat stuck in teeth depends on individual custom: does the person’s nature abhor it? The mufti applies Abu Yusuf or Zufar accordingly.
This shows that fatwa can vary by personal habit, not a single rule.
5
Apply the Istithna'
Confirm that customs directly violating Shariah (e.g., riba) are not binding.
The mufti must be able to distinguish valid custom from prohibited innovation.

Types of Custom and Its Use in Specifying Legal Texts
Defines general and specific custom and shows how it can specify a text but not abandon it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the types of custom (urf) and apply the Hanafi qawida that general custom can specify textual rulings while specific custom cannot.

Core Idea
General custom specifies texts; local custom cannot override nass.

In Hanafi legal theory, custom ('urf) plays a vital role in shaping rulings, especially in transactions. However, not all customs carry the same weight. This passage from al-Dhakhira distinguishes between general custom (urf 'amm) and specific custom (urf khas), and establishes a key maxim: general custom can specify texts and qiyas, but specific custom cannot. Understanding this distinction is crucial for a mufti when evaluating prevalent practices against established texts.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of 'urf
Identify the definitions and scopes of 'urf 'amm (widespread) and 'urf khas (localized). General custom applies across regions, while specific is confined to one town.
2
Understand the legal effect of each type
General custom can specify (takhsis) a general text or qiyas. Specific custom can only establish a ruling if it does not contradict nass or qiyas; it cannot specify.
The key principle: specification is permissible, abandonment is not.
3
Examine the ijarah example
The scholars of Balkh permitted weaving cloth for a share (ijarah bi al-thulth) due to widespread custom, even though it resembles the prohibited qafiz al-tahan. This is a specification of the text, not abandonment.
The custom was general in Balkh, so it could specify.
4
Contrast with the istisna' example
Istisna' (manufacturing contract) is universally practiced, thus it specifies the prohibition of selling what one does not possess. The assumption is that istisna' was not the intended meaning of the prohibition.
The universality of the custom is the decisive factor.
5
Identify the boundary (istithna')
If only one town practices qafiz al-tahan, the custom is local and cannot specify the text because it would amount to abandonment of the nass.
Specification requires widespread practice; doubt prevents specification.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This passage demonstrates a practical application of the maxim 'harm must be removed' (al-darar yuzal). The well-known opinion is expressed by 'qil' (it was said).

The necessity (darurah) is established by the inability to pray without difficulty.

Two conditions are met: (1) the source is a qualified imam (al-Marghinani), (2) there is genuine necessity that makes the well-known ruling unduly burdensome. The mufti or layperson should not generalize this exception but use it only for the excused individual.

Nevertheless, because al-Marghinani is a great imam from the class of those capable of independent reasoning and correction (as established earlier), his opinion can be followed by someone who has an excuse (ma'dhur) when necessity arises. The author gives a personal example: he suffered from a cauterized pimple and found that only al-Marghinani's opinion enabled him to pray without hardship. The well-known opinion would have required him to wash and wrap the area with a waterproof covering (like leather) to maintain purity, which was difficult. Thus, the necessity exception applies, allowing him to follow the weak opinion for his own practice.

Key Terms
قول شاذ (qawl shadhdh): An isolated opinion that is not widely accepted and has no known supporter besides the transmitter.
معذور (ma'dhur): A person who has a legitimate excuse (like chronic illness) that causes hardship in following the preferred ruling.

Key Takeaway
A weak opinion (qawl shadhdh) from a qualified Hanafi imam can be followed in cases of necessity (darurah) to remove hardship, based on the maxim 'al-darar yuzal'.
