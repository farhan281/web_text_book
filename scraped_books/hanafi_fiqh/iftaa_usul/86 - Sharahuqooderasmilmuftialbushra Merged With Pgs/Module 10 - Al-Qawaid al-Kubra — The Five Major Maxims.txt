Preferring Istihsan over Qiyas Except in Certain Issues
Rule that istihsan is preferred over qiyas except specific exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that istihsan is preferred over qiyas in fatwa, identify the exceptions, and evaluate the intra-madhhab disagreement on whether the preference is definitive or merely a priority.

Core Idea
Istihsan trumps qiyas in Hanafi fatwa, barring specific exceptions where qiyas prevails.

When a case involves both qiyas (analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference), the Hanafi school generally gives precedence to istihsan. This rule is foundational for muftis, but it comes with a caveat: there are specific exceptions where qiyas must be followed. Moreover, there is an internal debate on whether this preference is binding or merely a recommendation. This lesson dissects the rule, its exceptions, and the practical implications of the scholarly disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify presence of both qiyas and istihsan
Determine whether the case at hand has a conflict between a clear analogy (qiyas) and a juristic preference (istihsan). This is the trigger for applying the rule.
2
Apply the general rule of preference
By default, adopt the istihsan ruling over the qiyas. This is the standard Hanafi position and the first step in fatwa.
3
Check for exceptions (mustathnayat)
Consult the list of specific issues where qiyas is preferred despite the general rule. The number of exceptions varies (11 or 22 depending on the source). The mufti must know these exceptions to avoid error.
Common exceptions include certain matters in purity, prayer, and transactions.
4
Consider the binding nature of preference
Recognize the two views within the school: al-Talwih holds that preference is definitive (must act on istihsan), while Fakhr al-Islam holds it is only priority (may act on qiyas). Your fatwa may depend on which view you follow.
The passage does not settle which view is mufta bihi, so a mufti should be aware of both.
5
Issue the fatwa
Based on the above steps, deliver the ruling. If the case is not an exception, istihsan governs. If it is an exception, qiyas governs. If the preference is only priority, you may choose according to necessity.

Commentary
This passage presents a rasm rule from general Hanafi books: when a case involves both qiyas (deduction by analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference), istihsan takes precedence except in a specific set of cases. The number of exceptions is debated: eleven according to Ajnas al-Natifi, expanded to twenty-two by al-Nasafi. The passage also discusses the nature of this preference (tarjih): the correct view according to al-Talwih is that it is a definitive preference, meaning one must act on the preponderant (istihsan) and abandon the preponderated (qiyas). However, Fakhr al-Islam's view (as understood) is that it is only a priority, meaning it is permissible to act on qiyas if necessary.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference: setting aside a clear analogy (qiyas) in favor of a different ruling due to a stronger evidence or necessity.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical deduction: extending a ruling from an original case (asl) to a new case (far') based on a shared effective cause (illah).
ترجيح (tarjih): Preference: determining which of two conflicting evidences or opinions is stronger and should be acted upon.
راجح (rajih): Preponderant: the stronger evidence or opinion that is given precedence.
مرجوح (marjuh): Preponderated: the weaker evidence or opinion that is set aside.

The Rule: Mafhum is Considered in Non-Divine Speech
Rationale for considering mafhum in non-divine speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule regarding mafhum in non-divine speech and apply the maxim 'al-ma'ruf ka al-mashrut' to interpret utterances in people's speech.

Core Idea
Mafhum is binding in people's speech because custom treats it as stipulated, but not in divine speech because of its eloquence.

This lesson explains the rationale behind the Hanafi rule that mafhum (implied meaning) is considered authoritative in the speech of people but not in the speech of the Lawgiver. The key to this rule is the distinction between ordinary speech, which relies on customary understanding, and divine speech, which is eloquent and may have purposes beyond mere negation. We will explore the maxim 'al-ma'ruf ka al-mashrut' and its application in fiqh texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
State the operative rule: mafhum is considered in non-Shari' speech (people's speech and scholarly texts) but not in divine speech (Quran, hadith).
The passage says 'al-'amal al-ana 'ala i'tibar al-mafhum fi ghayr kalam al-Shari'.' This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Locate the Rationale for Divine Speech
Explain why mafhum is not considered in divine speech: because divine speech is 'ma'dan al-balaghah' (the mine of eloquence). A qualifier may not be intended to exclude but rather to describe the common case, as in Quran 4:23 'step-daughters in your laps.'
The verse mentions 'in your laps' but the ruling applies to all step-daughters, not just those in the lap.
3
Apply the Maxim to People's Speech
People's speech is interpreted by custom. The maxim 'al-ma'ruf ka al-mashrut' means that what is customary is treated as if it were explicitly stipulated. Therefore, a qualifier in people's speech creates a mafhum that excludes other cases.
The passage cites Sharh al-Siyar al-Kabir: 'al-thabit bil-'urf ka al-thabit bil-nass.'
4
Examine Application to Scholarly Texts
Scholars in their fiqh works habitually mention qualifiers to hint at exclusion. This widespread practice is accepted, so the mafhum from these texts is generally authoritative.
The passage states 'hadha mimma sha'a wa dha'a baynahum bi-la nakir.'
5
Recognize Exceptions
Not every qualifier is intended for exclusion. Some are incidental (ittifaqi), as in al-Hidayah's wudu example: 'when one wakes from sleep.' The majority hold that the sunna applies to both waking and non-waking persons; the mention is just following the hadith wording.
Shams al-A'imma al-Kardari considered it precautionary (ihtirazi), but the majority view is that it is incidental.

The Authority of Custom (Urf) and its Conditions in Hanafi Iftaa
Establishes urf as a legal source and defines its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-adah muhakkamah' by identifying which customs are authoritative and which are excluded, and construct a fatwa based on custom using the conditions of consistency or predominance.

Core Idea
Custom is authoritative, but only when consistent or predominant — not every practice qualifies.

Custom (urf) is a recognized secondary source in Hanafi fiqh, especially in mu'amalat. However, not every habitual practice carries legal weight. This lesson establishes the maxim 'custom is authoritative' (العادة محكمة) and lays out its essential conditions: consistency or predominance. Understanding this distinction is crucial for a mufti when interpreting absolute statements in contracts and transactions.

Guided Path
1
Define custom and its legal basis
Understand the definitions from al-Ghazali and Sharh al-Tahrir. Note the hadith basis from al-Ashbah.
Al-Ghazali defines urf as what settles in souls and is accepted by sound nature; Sharh al-Tahrir defines it as a recurring matter without intellectual connection.
2
State the maxim and its authority
Identify the maxim 'العادة محكمة' (Custom is authoritative) and its proof: the hadith 'ما رآه المسلمون حسناً فهو عند الله حسن'.
This hadith is used by Hanafis to legitimize custom as a source.
3
Identify the condition for validity
Note that custom is only considered if it is consistent (iṭradat) or predominant (ghalabat). Rare or sporadic customs are not authoritative.
This condition is explicit in al-Ashbah.
4
Apply the condition to an example
Work through the currency sale example: why the absolute term is referred to the most common currency (al-aghlab).
From al-Hidayah: 'because that is what is known, so the absolute is referred to it'.
5
Recognize exceptions
Note that custom cannot override a clear explicit text (sarih) from Quran or Sunnah. This is an istithna' of the maxim.
From the qawida panel: 'When custom contradicts a clear explicit text, the text takes precedence.'

Custom and Its Authority with Explicit Text Overriding
Defining custom and establishing its authority with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of custom (urf) in Hanafi usul, explain the maxim 'al-adah muhakkamah', and identify when custom is overridden by explicit text.

Core Idea
Custom is authoritative unless explicit text contradicts it.

Custom (urf) plays a central role in Hanafi jurisprudence, especially in mu'amalat. This passage from a later Hanafi rasm al-mufti text defines custom, establishes its authority via the maxim 'al-adah muhakkamah', and clarifies a critical limitation: custom cannot override explicit textual evidence. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for a mufti navigating between revealed sources and community practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key definitions
Read the definitions of custom/urf from al-Mustasfa and habit/ada from Sharh al-Tahrir. Note that urf is defined as 'what settles in minds through intellect and accepted by sound nature' while ada is 'a repeated matter without rational connection.'
In Hanafi usage, urf and ada are often interchangeable.
2
State the maxim and its proof
The passage cites the maxim 'al-adah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative) from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, and its basis in the hadith 'What the Muslims see as good is good with Allah.' This hadith serves as the primary textual proof for employing custom.
The hadith is a well-known principle (qawl al-Sahabi or mursal) accepted in Hanafi usul.
3
Recognize the condition for applying custom
Custom is only considered if it is consistent (mustaqirr) or predominant (ghalib). The passage gives the example of selling unspecified dirhams in a place with multiple coin types: the sale refers to the most common coin, because the absolute term is understood according to custom.
This condition prevents irregular or rare practices from being treated as authoritative.
4
Apply the limitation: explicit text overrides
The passage explicitly states that if an explicit text (sarih) contradicts the implicit meaning derived from custom or mafhum, the explicit text takes precedence. This is a universal usul principle.
The condition is that the text must be explicit and established (thabit).
5
Work through the furu' example
Consider a sale of unspecified dirhams in a city where multiple coin types circulate. According to custom, the obligation defaults to the most common coin. If an explicit contract term specified a different coin, that term would override the custom.
This shows how custom operates within the bounds of nass.

Rulings Change with Changing Customs: A Hanafi Fatwa Principle
Lists 17 examples of changed rulings due to custom and time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle 'تغير الأحكام بتغير العرف' to identify when a fatwa may shift due to changed custom, and explain why later Hanafi jurists modified selected rulings.

Core Idea
Rulings built on custom change when custom changes, even if it contradicts the founding imam.

The Hanafi school is not static. Its later jurists (mutakhkhirun) issued fatwas that departed from earlier positions when custom, necessity, or corruption of the age demanded it. This lesson explores the maxim 'Rulings change with the change of custom' through 17 concrete examples from a classical text on fatwa methodology. You will see how the school adapts without abandoning its foundations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its scope
Locate the statement 'تغير الأحكام بتغير العرف' in the passage. Understand that it is a minor maxim (sughra) under the major maxim 'العادة محكمة'.
The passage opens by explaining that many rulings changed due to 'فساد أهل الزمان أو عموم الضرورة' (corruption of the people or general necessity).
2
Analyze a representative example: ikrah (duress) from non-rulers
Read the second example: Abu Hanifa held that only the ruler can exercise effective duress, but later Muhammad recognized duress from others because of increased corruption. Later fatwa followed Muhammad.
This shows a shift in the ruling (hukm) because the custom/condition changed.
3
Examine the reasoning (ta'lil) behind the change
For each example, note the justification: 'زجراً لفساد الزمان' (to deter due to corruption of the time), 'دفعاً للضرر' (to remove harm), or 'لغلبة الاستعمال بالعرف' (due to predominant custom).
The jurists are not changing divine law but adjusting its application to new contexts.
4
List the furu' (branches) that illustrate the maxim
From the passage, extract at least five distinct cases where the ruling changed. These become your furu' for the qawida panel.
Examples include: hiring for Quran teaching, liability on tax collector, pre-emption delay, wife's dower denial, and phrases of divorce.
5
Apply the maxim to your own context
Consider a contemporary scenario, such as online contracts or digital evidence. Does the custom justify a change? Remember the istithna': custom cannot override a definitive text (nass).
The maxim is powerful but bounded – it operates where there is room for ijtihad.

Commentary

The author ends by noting the complexity of the issue and his brief treatment.
