Issuing Fatwa by Qawaid is Not Allowed
Prohibition of relying on qawaid for fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that qawaid are not sufficient for fatwa and identify the requirement of hikayat al-naql.

Core Idea
A mufti cannot derive fatwa from legal maxims alone; he must narrate explicit transmitted rulings.

This passage from Sharah 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti addresses a critical principle in Hanafi ifta: the prohibition of issuing fatwa based solely on qawaid (legal maxims). It explains why even finding a similar case (nazir) is insufficient, as subtle differences (furuq) may exist. Instead, the mufti must rely on explicit transmitted texts (hikayat al-naql) or refrain from answering unless an exception for customary incidents applies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Rule
State the main prohibition: it is not permissible to issue fatwa from qawaid and dawabit alone. The mufti must narrate transmitted rulings.
This is a rasm al-mufti rule, not a fiqh ruling.
2
Understand the Insufficiency of Similar Cases
Explain why finding a nazir (similar case) is not enough: there may be a hidden difference (farq) that the mufti cannot perceive. This is why scholars authored works on furuq.
Furuq are critical for accurate taqlid.
3
Recognize the Requirement of Hikayat al-Naql
The mufti, if not a mujtahid, must only transmit what he memorizes from the statements of the jurists. He cannot derive from principles.
This restricts the role of a muqallid mufti.
4
Note the Exception for Customary Incidents
If an incident is purely customary (urfi) and does not contradict sharia texts, the mufti may give fatwa based on custom, as will be explained later.
Custom operates as a source only when explicit text is lacking and custom is sound.

Commentary
This passage explains the strict limitation on using qawaid (legal maxims) for issuing fatwa. It first notes that a mufti's failure to find an explicit text is usually due to his own lack of knowledge or failure to locate the issue, because most incidents are covered in the madhhab's books either directly or via a comprehensive maxim. However, even if he finds a similar case, he cannot rely on analogy (qiyas) between them because there may be subtle distinctions that only trained scholars can detect. The author then quotes Ibn Nujaym declaring it impermissible to issue fatwa from qawaid alone; the mufti must narrate explicit transmissions. He adds that qawaid are 'akthariyyah' (apply to the majority of cases) not 'kulliyyah' (universal), so they cannot be used to derive rulings for new cases without supporting text. The passage concludes by stating that if a mufti lacks an explicit text, he must refrain from answering or consult a more knowledgeable scholar, even by correspondence. An exception is made for customary (urfi) incidents that do not contradict sharia, where the mufti may give fatwa by custom, to be discussed later.

Key Terms
قواعد (qawaid): Legal maxims that summarize fiqh principles but are not universally applicable.
حكاية النقل (hikayat al-naql): Narrating the transmitted text or ruling verbatim from the school.
فروق (furuq): Differences between seemingly similar cases; jurists authored works on distinctions.

Mafhum Muwafaqa and Mukhalafa: Definitions and Hanafi Position
Defines mafhum muwafaqa and mafhum mukhalafa, with examples and inter-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of mafhum, distinguish their subtypes, and apply the Hanafi rule that mafhum mukhalafah is not valid evidence in the words of the Lawgiver.

Core Idea
Mafhum muwafaqa is universally accepted; mafhum mukhalafah is rejected by Hanafis in the words of the Lawgiver.

In usul al-fiqh, understanding implied meanings (mafhum) is essential for deriving rulings beyond the explicit text. This lesson distinguishes between mafhum muwafaqa, where the unspoken case shares the same ruling, and mafhum mukhalafah, where the opposite is inferred. The Hanafi position restricts the latter's validity, leading to significant practical differences in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Define mafhum and its two primary divisions
Identify mafhum muwafaqa and mafhum mukhalafah from the text. Mafhum muwafaqa agrees with the explicit ruling; mafhum mukhalafah opposes it.
This is a linguistic operation — the meaning is derived from language alone, not from reasoning.
2
Analyze mafhum muwafaqa using the Quranic example
The verse 'Do not say to them uff' (17:23) explicitly prohibits a mild expression of annoyance. The implied prohibition of hitting is a mafhum muwafaqa because hitting is more severe.
This type is agreed upon by all schools.
3
List the five subtypes of mafhum mukhalafah
From the passage: mafhum al-ṣifah (description), al-sharṭ (condition), al-ghāyah (destination), al-ʿadad (number), and al-laqab (proper noun). Each is an implication from a specific qualification.
Mafhum al-laqab is rejected even by the Shafiʿis.
4
Examine the inter-madhhab dispute
The Shafiʿi school accepts all subtypes except laqab; the Hanafi school rejects all in the words of the Lawgiver. Understand their respective proofs: Shafiʿis rely on linguistic custom, Hanafis on the principle that the Lawgiver's speech is not constrained by it.
This is a fundamental difference in usul al-fiqh.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
For example, the hadith 'On grazing livestock there is zakāh' — Shafiʿis infer no zakāh on hay-fed animals via mafhum al-ṣifah; Hanafis do not and require explicit text for the opposite ruling.
This directly affects fatwa in zakāh chapter.

Types of Mafhum and Their Rulings
Know that al-mafhūm is of two types: mafhūm muwāfaqah, which is the indication of a word to establish the ruling of the spoken word for the unspoken, merely by understanding the language, i.e., without depending on opinion or ijtihād, like the indication of "Do not say to them 'uff'" to the prohibition of hitting. (al-Isrā' 23)
And mafhūm mukhalafah: which is the indication of a word to establish the opposite of the ruling of the spoken word for the unspoken. And it has types: mafhūm al-ṣifah (concept of description) like "on grazing livestock there is zakāh"; mafhūm al-sharṭ (concept of condition) like "if they are pregnant, then spend on them" (al-Ṭalāq 6); mafhūm al-ghāyah (concept of destination/end) like "until she marries another husband" (al-Baqarah 230); mafhūm al-ʿadad (concept of number) like "eighty lashes" (al-Nūr 4); and mafhūm al-laqab (concept of appellation/name) which is the attachment of a ruling to a proper noun, like "on sheep there is zakāh". Considering the first type is agreed upon; the second type and its subdivisions are disputed. According to the Shāfiʿī school, all except the last are considered, so they imply negation of zakāh on hay-fed animals, and that there is no maintenance for a divorced non-pregnant woman, and permissibility when she marries another, and negation of more than eighty. According to the Ḥanafī school, they are not considered in all their subdivisions in the words of the Lawgiver only. Its full verification is in the books of uṣūl.

Commentary
This passage defines two categories of mafhūm (implied meaning) in uṣūl al-fiqh. The author first defines mafhūm muwāfaqah: when the explicit ruling logically entails the same ruling for an unmentioned case through linguistic understanding alone, as in the analogy from 'uff' to hitting (more severe). This type is agreed upon by all schools. Then he defines mafhūm mukhalafah: when the explicit ruling implies the opposite for the unmentioned case (e.g., the mention of 'grazing' implies that hay-fed animals are not subject to zakāh). He lists five subtypes: mafhūm al-ṣifah, al-sharṭ, al-ghāyah, al-ʿadad, and al-laqab. The key dispute: Shāfiʿīs accept mafhūm mukhalafah (except laqab) as valid evidence; Ḥanafīs do not consider it in the words of the Lawgiver (i.e., in Quran and Hadith). This has important implications: for example, a Ḥanafī muftī would not use mafhūm al-sharṭ from the verse on pregnant women to deny maintenance to a divorced non-pregnant woman. The passage notes that the full discussion is in uṣūl books.

Key Terms
مفهوم الموافقة (mafhūm al-muwāfaqah): Implicit meaning that agrees with the explicit ruling: when the unmentioned case has the same ruling as the mentioned case due to a stronger reason.
مفهوم المخالفة (mafhūm al-mukhālafah): Implicit meaning that opposes the explicit ruling: the unmentioned case takes the opposite ruling.
مفهوم الصفة (mafhūm al-ṣifah): Implication from a descriptive adjective; e.g., 'grazing livestock' implies the opposite for non-grazing.
مفهوم اللقب (mafhūm al-laqab): Implication from a proper noun or static term; e.g., 'in sheep there is zakāh' does not imply anything about other animals.

Key Takeaway

The Two Hanafi Positions on Mafhum
Explaining the two Hanafi positions on using mafhum as proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two Hanafi positions on the validity of mafhum (implied meaning) and explain which one is the operative view in fatwa.

Core Idea
Mafhum is valid in narratives, not divine speech—but later scholars allowed it even in divine speech.

The Hanafi school has an internal debate on whether mafhum al-mukhalafah (the opposite implication of a restricted phrase) can be used as legal evidence. This passage from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti presents two opinions and resolves them, showing that the preferred view accepts mafhum in rational and narrative contexts but not absolutely in divine speech. Understanding this distinction is crucial for a mufti who must interpret restricted texts correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
What is the contested question? The passage asks whether mafhum (especially mafhum al-mukhalafah) is a valid form of evidence in Hanafi fiqh.
Mafhum is the implied meaning derived from a restricted expression; e.g., 'if X, then Y' implies that if not X, then not Y.
2
State the first position
The apparent madhhab (zahir al-madhhab) holds that mafhum is not valid as evidence. This is reported in al-Zahiriyya.
This position relies on the principle that specification (takhsis) does not imply negation of what is not mentioned.
3
State the second position
Imam Muhammad, al-Khassaf, and many later scholars accept mafhum as evidence. This view is found in al-Siyar al-Kabir and other works.
They argue that mafhum is valid in narrations and rational matters, and some extend it even to divine speech.
4
Identify the key distinction
The passage resolves the conflict by distinguishing between divine speech (khitab shar'i) and rational/narrative contexts. In divine speech, specification does not automatically negate others; in narrations and rational arguments, it does.
This distinction allows both positions to coexist: the rejection applies to divine speech, while acceptance applies elsewhere.
5
Determine the operative view
The later scholars (muta'akhkhirun) and al-Biri conclude that the operative opinion (al-'amal) is the permissibility of using mafhum, but not absolutely—it is applied in other than the speech of the Lawgiver. However, Imam Muhammad and al-Khassaf used it even in divine speech, and the author has not seen opposition to this.
For fatwa, the mufti should know that the school has two valid positions, and the preferred view leans towards acceptance.

Commentary
This passage addresses a key usul al-fiqh issue: the validity of mafhum al-mukhalafah (implied meaning from a restricted phrase) as legal evidence. The author opens by noting that the Hanafi school has two positions on this matter. The discussion begins with a concrete example from Hajj: if a predatory animal attacks a muhrim and he kills it in self-defense, no penalty is due. This is deduced from the action of 'Umar (ra), who killed a lion and sacrificed a ram because he initiated the attack. The rationale is that the cause (initiation) implies that without initiation (i.e., self-defense), no penalty applies. This is an inference from mafhum. The author then addresses an objection: why do you use mafhum when you Hanafis generally do not accept that specification implies negation? The answer distinguishes between divine speech (khitab shar'i) and rational narrations or logical arguments. In divine speech, the Hanafis do not automatically accept mafhum; but in rational matters and narrative reports, mafhum is valid. The objection from al-Ashbah claims that mafhum is not valid even in people's speech, but the author clarifies that the later scholars uphold the differentiation. The commentary of al-Biri is then cited, showing that in al-Zahiriyya, mafhum is rejected as the apparent madhhab, and Imam Muhammad's acceptance in al-Siyar al-Kabir is considered against the apparent narration (khilaf al-riwaya). However, al-Sarakhsi and others found that Imam Muhammad built many rulings on mafhum, and al-Khassaf used it in hiyal. The author concludes that the operative opinion (al-'amal) is the permissibility of using mafhum, but not absolutely: it applies to other than divine speech. Yet, interestingly, the author notes that in al-Siyar al-Kabir, Imam Muhammad actually used mafhum even in interpreting divine speech, citing the hadith about the Magians of Hajar to prove that marrying Christian women from the people of war is not forbidden (because the hadith mentions Magians, implying others are not included). This passage is essential for a mufti to understand the internal Hanafi difference on a fundamental interpretive tool.

Key Terms
المفهوم (al-mafhum): The implied meaning derived from a restricted textual expression, especially the opposite implication (mafhum al-mukhalafah), e.g., 'If the animal attacks, kill it' implies that if it does not attack, you should not kill it.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwaya): The apparent or well-known narrations from Imam Abu Hanifa and his students that form the basis of the madhhab, as opposed to rare or anomalous transmissions.
التخصيص بالذكر (al-takhsis bi al-dhikr): Specifying something by mentioning it in a phrase, which may or may not imply the negation of the ruling for unmentioned items.

أقسام العرف وأحكامها] ثم اعلم أن العرف قسمان عام و خاص فالعام يثبت به الحكم العام، ويصلح مخصصاً للقياس والأثر، بخلاف الخاص فإنه يثبت به الخاص ما لم يخالف القياس أو الأثر؛ فإنه لا يصلح مخصصاً. جواز تخصيص الأثر بالتعامل لا تركه] قال في "الذخيرة" في الفصل الثامن من الإجارات في مسألة ما لو دفع إلى حائك غزلا لينسجه بالثلث ومشايخ بلخ كنصير بن يحيى، ومحمد بن سلمة، وغيرهما، كانوا يُجيزون هذه الإجارة في الثياب؛ لتعامل أهل بلدهم في الثياب، والتعامل حجة يترك به القياس، ويخص به الأثر. وتجويز هذه الإجارة في الثياب للتعامل، بمعنى تخصيص النص الذي وَرَدَ في قفيز الطحان؛ لأن النص ورد في قفيز الطحان لا في الحائك، إلا أن الحائك نظيره فيكون وارداً فيه دلالةً، فمتى تركنا العمل بدلالة هذا النص في الحائك، وعملنا بالنص في قفيز الطحان كان تخصيصا لا تركا أصلا، وتخصيص النص بالتعامل جائز. ألا ترى أنا جوّزنا الاستصناع للتعامل، والاستصناع بيع ما ليس عنده، وإنه منهي عنه وتجويز الاستصناع بالتعامل تخصيص منا للنص الذي ورد في النهي عن بيع ما ليس عند الإنسان، لا ترك للنص أصلا؛ لأنا عملنا بالنص في غير الاستصناع. قالوا: وهذا بخلاف ما لو تعامل أهل بلدة قفيز الطحان؛ فإنه لا يجوز، ولا تكون معاملتهم معتبرة؛ لأنا لو اعتبرنا معاملتهم كان تركاً للنص أصلا، وبالتعامل لا يجوز ترك النصّ أصلا، وإنما يجوز تخصيصه، ولكن مشايخنا لم يجوزوا هذا التخصيص؛ لأن ذلك تعامل أهل بلدة واحدة، وتعامل أهل بلدة واحدة لا يخص الأثر؛ لأن تعامل أهل بلدة إن أن يجوز التخصيص، فترك التعامل من أهل بلدة أخرى يمنع التخصيص، فلا يثبت التخصيص بالشك، بخلاف التعامل في الاستصناع، فإنه وجد في البلاد كلها. انتهى كلام "الذخيرة".
[Divisions of custom and their rulings] Then know that custom is of two types: general and specific. The general establishes a general ruling and is suitable as a specifier of analogy and transmitted text, unlike the specific, for it establishes a specific ruling as long as it does not contradict analogy or transmitted text; then it is not suitable as a specifier. [Permissibility of specifying transmitted text by practice, not abandoning it] He said in al-Dhakhirah, in the eighth chapter of al-Ijarat, concerning the case where one gives yarn to a weaver to weave it for a third share, and the scholars of Balkh like Nasir ibn Yahya, Muhammad ibn Salama, and others, they permitted this contract for cloth due to the practice of the people of their town in cloth, and practice is a proof by which analogy is abandoned and transmitted text is specified. And permitting this contract for cloth due to practice is in the sense of specifying the text that came concerning the qafiz of the miller; because the text came concerning the qafiz of the miller, not the weaver, except that the weaver is its analogue, so it applies to him by indication. When we abandon acting upon the indication of this text regarding the weaver, and act upon the text regarding the qafiz of the miller, it is specification, not abandonment at all, and specification of the text by practice is permissible. Do you not see that we permitted istisna' due to practice, and istisna' is selling what is not in one's possession, and it is prohibited, and permitting istisna' by practice is a specification by us of the text that came prohibiting selling what one does not possess, not abandonment of the text at all; because we act upon the text in other than istisna'. They said: This is unlike the case where the people of one town practice the qafiz of the miller; then it is not permissible, and their practice is not considered; because if we considered their practice, it would be abandonment of the text entirely, and by practice it is not permissible to abandon the text at all, only to specify it. But our scholars did not permit this specification because that is the practice of people of one town, and the practice of people of one town does not specify a transmitted text; because if the practice of one town permits specification, then the abandonment of practice by people of another town prevents specification, so specification is not established by doubt, unlike the practice in istisna', for it existed in all towns. End of the words of al-Dhakhirah.

Commentary
This passage establishes a key qawida (maxim): custom can specify a general text but cannot entirely abrogate it. The author first divides custom into two: general (common across all or many regions) and specific (local). General custom can specify both qiyas and nass (text), while specific custom only specifies if it does not contradict qiyas or nass; otherwise it cannot. The author then quotes al-Dhakhirah to illustrate with two cases. First, the weaver's contract: Scholars of Balkh allowed a weaver to weave yarn for a share of the cloth, based on their local custom. This is a specification of the general prohibition of such contracts (qafiz al-tahan) which is a type of gharar. They argued that this is specification, not abandonment, because the prohibition text is maintained for other cases. Second, the istisna' (manufacturing contract): It is prohibited to sell what one does not possess, but istisna' is widely practiced and thus permitted as a specification of that text. The key condition: local custom (one town) cannot specify a text because it is not universally established; only widespread custom can. The istisna' custom was prevalent in all towns, so it could specify. Thus the maxim is: 'Custom specifies a text but does not abandon it; only widespread (general) custom is effective for specification.'

Key Terms
العرف (al-'urf): Customary practice; divided into general (common) and specific (local).
التعامل (al-ta'āmul): Mutual customary practice of people in transactions; considered a proof.
تخصيص الأثر (takhṣīṣ al-athar): Specifying a transmitted textual rule by applying it to a subset of cases based on custom, while maintaining the general rule for other cases.

The Maxim: Practice Specifies Texts (Takhsis al-Athar bi al-Ta'amul)
Explanation and application of the maxim with furuat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'practice is a proof' to specify a textual ruling, and identify when a local custom is insufficient for specification.

Core Idea
Practice can specify a text but not abandon it; only widespread practice suffices.

The passage now delves into a practical application of the classification. It quotes al-Dhakhirah to illustrate the maxim: mutual practice (ta'amul) is a proof that can set aside analogy and specify transmitted texts. Two famous cases are discussed: the weaver contract of Balkh (local custom) and the istisna' contract (widespread custom). The critical limit is that practice confined to one town cannot specify a text because that would amount to doubt; only universal practice can specify without risking abandonment of the text.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
The maxim is directly quoted: 'التعامل حجة يترك به القياس، ويخص به الأثر'. Memorize it.
Practice (ta'amul) has two effects: abandoning analogy and specifying texts.
2
Analyze the first furu': weaver contract
The scholars of Balkh permitted giving yarn to a weaver for a share of the cloth based on their local practice. This is a specification of the text regarding qafiz al-tahan (a prohibition of similar uncertainty). Note that it is specification, not abandonment.
The prohibition text remains operative for other cases.
3
Analyze the second furu': istisna'
Istisna' (manufacturing contract) involves selling what one does not possess, which is prohibited by a text. Yet it is permitted due to widespread practice across all towns. This is specification of the prohibition text.
Widespread practice ensures certainty.
4
Identify the istithna' (exception)
If people of a single town practice the very same qafiz al-tahan contract, it is not permissible. Their practice cannot specify because it would be abandonment of the text, not specification. The absence of practice elsewhere creates doubt.
The exception is governed by the sub-maxim: 'تعامل أهل بلدة واحدة لا يخص الأثر'.
5
Apply the maxim to a new case
Given a new transaction practiced in multiple cities, determine if the practice is widespread enough to specify a conflicting text. If not, the text remains operative.
Always distinguish between specification and abandonment.
