Seven Layers of Fuqaha (Tabaqat al-Fuqaha)
Classification of fuqaha into seven layers for proper tarjih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the first three layers of fuqaha in the Hanafi hierarchy and explain how each layer's authority affects tarjih.

Core Idea
A mufti must know the ranks of scholars to correctly weigh conflicting opinions.

In this passage, the author classifies jurists into seven layers to guide a mufti in tarjih. The first three layers are detailed: absolute mujtahids, mujtahids within the madhhab, and mujtahids for un-narrated cases. Understanding these ranks is essential for determining whose opinion takes precedence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first layer
What qualifies someone as an absolute mujtahid? Who are examples? The text says they establish usul and derive furu without taqlid. Examples: the four imams.
This layer includes Imam Abu Hanifa and others like Imam al-Shafi'i, Imam Malik, Imam Ahmad.
2
Identify the second layer
What distinguishes mujtahids within the madhhab? They follow the imam's usul but may differ in furu. Examples: Abu Yusuf, Muhammad.
These are the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad).
3
Identify the third layer
Who are the mujtahids for cases with no transmission? They derive rulings using the madhhab's principles but cannot oppose the imam. Examples: al-Khassaf, al-Tahawi, al-Karkhi, al-Halwani, al-Sarakhsi, al-Bazdawi, Qadi Khan.
They are limited to cases with no explicit text from the founder.
4
Note the death dates
The passage provides death dates; how does this help in tarjih? Older scholars often have higher authority, but not always. The dates assist in chronological ordering.
Death dates are given for al-Khassaf (261), al-Tahawi (321), al-Karkhi (340), al-Halwani (456), al-Sarakhsi (~500), al-Bazdawi (482), Qadi Khan (593), al-Razi (370), al-Quduri (428), and author of al-Hidayah (593).

Layers of the Jurists
We say: The jurists are of seven layers:
First layer: The layer of mujtahids in the Sacred Law, like the four imams and those who follow their path in establishing the principles of usul and deriving rulings of furu from the four proofs without taqlid of anyone, neither in furu nor in usul.
Second: The layer of mujtahids in the madhhab, like Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and the rest of Abu Hanifa's companions who are able to extract rulings from the aforementioned [proofs] according to the principles established by their teacher; even though they may have opposed him in some furu rulings, they follow him in the principles of usul.

They are unable to oppose the imam, neither in usul nor in furu, but they derive rulings for matters for which there is no text from him according to the principles he established and the dictates of the rules he laid down.

Commentary
This passage is a mufti adab lesson that classifies fuqaha into seven layers (though only three are detailed here; the remaining four are omitted in this excerpt). The purpose is to equip the mufti with the knowledge of scholarly ranks so he can properly weigh conflicting opinions. The first layer consists of absolute mujtahids like the four imams who derived rulings directly from the primary sources. The second layer is mujtahids within the madhhab, such as Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, who followed Abu Hanifa's usul but could differ in furu. The third layer is mujtahids in issues without explicit narration from the founder; they derive rulings using the established principles of the madhhab. The death dates provided help in assessing the chronological order. Understanding these layers is crucial for a mufti to give correct tarjih, as emphasized by Ibn Kamal Pasha in the previous lesson.

Key Terms
المجتهد في الشرع (al-mujtahid fī al-sharʿ): An absolute mujtahid who derives rulings from Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas without taqlid of any imam.
المجتهد في المذهب (al-mujtahid fī al-madhhab): A mujtahid who follows the usul of the imam of the madhhab but may differ in furu rulings.
المجتهد في المسائل التي لا رواية فيها (al-mujtahid fī al-masā'il allatī lā riwāyata fīhā): A mujtahid who derives rulings for cases not explicitly transmitted from the founder, using the madhhab's principles.

The Seven Tabaqat of Hanafi Jurists
Classification of Hanafi jurists into seven hierarchical levels for fatwa authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify each of the seven tabaqat of Hanafi jurists and explain their authority hierarchy for issuing fatwas.

Core Idea
Hanafi fatwa relies on a hierarchy: highest class is absolute mujtahid, lowest is non-discriminating muqallid — each level has defined authority.

The Hanafi school developed a precise classification of its jurists to regulate the authority of opinions. A mufti must know from which level a ruling originates. This lesson maps the seven tabaqat from the highest mujtahid to the ordinary muqallid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the seven tabaqat
List the seven classes from the text in order from highest to lowest: absolute mujtahid, mujtahid within the madhhab, mujtahid for novel issues, practitioners of takhrij, practitioners of tarjih, discriminating muqallids, non-discriminating muqallids.
The first three are mujtahids; the last four are muqallids.
2
Differentiate ijtihad levels
Note which classes can oppose the Imam (first and second), which cannot (third), and which are purely muqallids (fourth to seventh). Each level has a distinct scope of authority.
Even the third tabaqa (mujtahid fi al-masa'il) cannot oppose the Imam in usul or furu.
3
Understand the scope of authority
For each tabaqa, define what they can do: absolute ijtihad (1st), ijtihad within madhhab usul (2nd), ijtihad for novel issues (3rd), takhrij of ambiguous statements (4th), tarjih among transmissions (5th), distinguishing strong/weak (6th), following without distinction (7th).
4
Recognize key names
Associate notable jurists with their tabaqa: e.g., Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (2nd), al-Tahawi and al-Sarakhsi (3rd), al-Karkhi (4th), al-Quduri and al-Marghinani (5th), authors of al-Kanz and al-Mukhtar (6th).
These names appear frequently in Hanafi texts.
5
Apply the hierarchy in ifta
Explain how a mufti uses this classification: the ruling from a higher tabaqa takes precedence over a lower one. The mufti must check if there is a ruling from the highest available level before resorting to lower levels.
This is a core principle of Hanafi fatwa methodology.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti presents the seven hierarchical classes (tabaqat) of Hanafi jurists, a foundational concept in the methodology of ifta'. The author enumerates them from highest to lowest, defining each class's scope of authority and capability. The first class consists of absolute mujtahids (al-mujtahidun fi al-shar'), who establish usul and derive ahkam directly from the four proofs without taqlid. Examples are the four Imams. The second class comprises mujtahids within the madhhab (al-mujtahidun fi al-madhhab), like Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, who can derive rulings using the usul of Abu Hanifa but may differ in furu. The third class includes those who exert ijtihad on matters not explicitly addressed by the madhhab founder (al-mujtahidun fi al-masa'il), such as al-Khassaf and al-Tahawi; they cannot oppose the Imam but can deduce rulings via his principles. The fourth class are the practitioners of takhrij (as'hab al-takhrij), who cannot perform ijtihad but, due to their knowledge of usul, can elaborate ambiguous statements from the madhhab by explicating which of two possible meanings is intended, using analogy. The fifth class are those who weigh and prefer (as'hab al-tarjih) among transmissions, like al-Quduri and the author of al-Hidayah; they select which riwaya is most authentic, most appropriate, etc. The sixth class are muqallids who can distinguish between strong and weak transmissions and know the apparent madhhab versus rare narrations; they compile concise texts excluding weak material. The seventh class are ordinary muqallids who lack these abilities. This classification is crucial for a mufti: the fatwa must be based on the highest available authority. For example, a ruling from the first class takes precedence over later classes.

Key Terms
طبقة (tabaqa): A hierarchical level or class of jurists based on their capacity for ijtihad and authority in the madhhab.
مجتهد (mujtahid): A jurist who exerts effort to derive rulings from the primary sources. The text distinguishes three levels: absolute (fi al-shar'), within the madhhab (fi al-madhhab), and for novel issues (fi al-masa'il).
مقلد (muqallid): A jurist who follows an Imam without being able to derive rulings independently. The text divides them into four subclasses (takhrij, tarjih, discriminating, and non-discriminating).

The Seven Classes of Muqallids
Classification of muqallid levels and their roles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize Hanafi muqallids into the seven levels and identify the role and limitation of each level for issuing fatwa.

Core Idea
Only muqallids of the sixth level and above may properly rely upon their own discernment; lower muqallids cannot distinguish strong from weak.

Understanding the hierarchy of muqallids is essential for any mufti. Not every follower of the school is qualified to issue a fatwa. This passage from the Usul al-Ifta tradition outlines seven distinct levels, from those who can derive new rulings (takhrij) down to those who cannot differentiate between sound and unsound reports. Knowing where a jurist stands on this ladder determines which books he may use and how much weight his opinion carries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the highest level described
The passage starts with the fourth level (ashab al-takhrij). Note their ability: they cannot do ijtihad but can elaborate on ambiguous statements using usul and qiyas.
This level includes al-Razi (al-Jassas).
2
Contrast the fourth and fifth levels
The fifth level (ashab al-tarjih) does not derive new rulings but weighs existing narrations. Understand the difference between takhrij and tarjih.
Tarjih uses criteria like 'this is more authentic' or 'this is more beneficial for the people'.
3
Recognize the sixth level's function
The sixth level compilers (authors of reliable matns) already filtered out weak and rejected narrations. Their books are safe to rely on.
Examples: al-Kanz, al-Mukhtar, al-Wiqaya, al-Majma'.
4
Understand the seventh level's incapacity
The seventh level cannot distinguish between strong and weak at all. The passage issues a severe warning against following them.
They are compared to 'a night gatherer' who collects any wood indiscriminately.
5
Relate hierarchy to mufti's obligation
The final quote from al-Fatawa al-Khairiyya stresses the mufti's duty to be precise and avoid whims. Knowing the hierarchy helps fulfill this duty.
The mufti must use only well-screened sources.

This passage defines the hierarchy of muqallids (those who follow a school) within the Hanafi tradition. Hierarchy for Fatwa When No Narration Exists
Method of fatwa when no explicit narration from early imams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the hierarchical order of authorities for issuing a fatwa when no explicit riwaya exists, and apply it to a given scenario.

Core Idea
When no riwaya exists, fatwa follows a strict seniority chain: early imams, then later scholars' majority, then mufti's ijtihad.

This lesson presents a foundational rasm al-mufti principle: the proper procedure when no explicit narration (riwaya) from the founding Hanafi imams is available. Drawing from al-Hawi al-Qudsi, the passage establishes a clear hierarchy that prevents arbitrary ruling and ensures adherence to the madhhab. The mufti must follow this order carefully, always conscious of his responsibility before Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the absence of riwaya
Confirm that there is no explicit narration from Imam Abu Hanifa on the issue.
This is the starting point; if a riwaya exists, it takes precedence.
2
Check the early companions in order of seniority
Look for an apparent statement from Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar and Hasan ibn Ziyad, proceeding through the major ashab in order of seniority ('al-akbar fa al-akbar').
The apparent statement (zāhir qawl) is taken, not an implicit one.
3
Consult the later scholars (muta'akhkhirin)
If no early companion has an explicit answer, turn to the later authoritative scholars such as Abu Hafs, Abu Ja'far, Abu al-Layth, and al-Tahawi.
These scholars compiled and weighed opinions but their rulings are not riwaya.
4
Apply the majority rule
If the later scholars agree on an opinion, adopt it. If they differ, adopt the opinion of the majority among the reliable ones.
The majority here refers to the preponderance of the acknowledged great scholars.
5
Resort to personal ijtihad with taqwa
If there is absolutely no answer from any of the above, the mufti must exert his own ijtihad with careful reflection, avoiding speculation, and always fearing Allah.
This is a last resort; the mufti is warned of the severity of speaking without knowledge.

Establishing the hierarchy of sources for issuing fatwas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hierarchy of sources for issuing fatwas (matn, sharh, fatawa) and apply the rule when sources conflict, distinguishing implicit from explicit correction.

Core Idea
Matn texts are the primary authority; they trump commentaries and fatwa collections.

When a mufti encounters conflicting rulings, which source should he rely upon? This lesson presents a foundational rule from Hanafi usul al-iftā: matn texts (mutun) carry implicit correction and are given precedence over commentaries (shurūḥ) and fatwa collections. Explicit corrections in later works override the implicit presumption. Understanding this hierarchy ensures that fatwas are rooted in the most authoritative transmissions of the madhhab.

Guided Path

2
Distinguish Implicit vs Explicit Correction
Implicit correction (tas-hih iltizami) is the presumption that matn authors only state the correct view. Explicit correction (tas-hih sarih) is a clear statement in another work that a particular opinion is correct. Explicit overrides implicit.
This distinction is key to resolving conflicts.
3
Understand the Hierarchy Order
The hierarchy is: (1) matn texts, (2) commentaries (shuruh), (3) fatwa collections. When sources contradict, the higher-ranking source is relied upon.
Quotes from al-Khayriyya and al-Bahr confirm this.

The passage gives the example of the blind person's testimony.

Recognize the Limitation
Fatwa collections are only used for assistance (isti'nas) when no contradictory matn or usul text exists. They are not primary sources.
