Attribution of Disciples' Opinions After the Imam's Retraction
Question and answer about attribution of disciples' opinions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the opinions of Abu Hanifa's disciples, based on his principles, remain attributed to his madhhab even after his retraction.

Core Idea
A disciple's ruling, based on the imam's principles, remains part of the madhhab.

How can a ruling be attributed to Abu Hanifa if he himself retracted it and his disciples held a different view? This question strikes at the coherence of the madhhab. Ibn Abidin resolves it by showing that disciples' opinions built on the imam's foundational principles are not entirely retracted; they become an extension of his madhhab. This lesson unpacks his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
State the objector's claim: if a mujtahid retracts an opinion, it no longer exists as his; disciples' contradicting opinions become their own madhhab, not his. Hence, how can they be attributed to Abu Hanifa?
The objector uses the analogy of abrogation (naskh).
2
Locate the Core Issue
The core issue is the nature of madhhab attribution: is the madhhab only the imam's direct statements, or does it include rulings derived from his methodology?
The Hanafi madhhab includes both.
3
Analyze Ibn Abidin's Solution
Ibn Abidin states that the Imam ordered his disciples to adopt what is supported by evidence from his statements. Thus, their rulings are based on his principles (qawa'id), making them his rulings in effect. They are not retracted 'from every respect' (min kulli wajh).
The principle: the disciples' ijtihad operates within the imam's framework.
4
Apply to Modern Fatwa Practice
A mufti must recognize that the madhhab includes not only Abu Hanifa's direct views but also those of his disciples if they follow his methodology. This affects which opinion is relied upon (mufta bihi).
For example, many fatwa preferences follow Abu Yusuf or Muhammad, not Abu Hanifa directly.

For if you say: When a mujtahid retracts a statement, it no longer remains a statement of his because it becomes like an abrogated ruling, as will come. Then what his companions said in opposition to him in it is not his madhhab; rather their statements become madhhabs for themselves, so how is it attributed to him? Yet the Hanafi follows Abu Hanifa, therefore it is attributed to him and not others. I say: I indeed found that problematic, and I answered in my gloss Radd al-Muhtar that since the Imam ordered his companions to take from his statements what is supported by evidence for them, their statement became a statement of his because it is based on the principles he established for them, so it was not retracted from in every respect.

Commentary
This is a question-answer passage (فإن قلت... قلت) addressing a methodological problem. The objection states: if a mujtahid (Abu Hanifa) retracts an opinion, it no longer exists as his opinion; thus, when his companions contradict that retracted opinion, their positions are not his madhhab but their own. The author, Ibn Abidin, resolves this by explaining that Abu Hanifa authorized his disciples to derive rulings using his foundational principles. Therefore, even if he retracted a specific ruling, the disciples' opinions based on his principles are still attributed to him because they originate from his methodology. They are not retracted in every aspect.

Key Terms
رجوع (ruju'): Retraction; a mujtahid abandoning his earlier legal opinion.
قول (qawl): A legal statement or ruling issued by a mujtahid.
مذهب (madhhab): The school of law; here the collection of rulings attributed to Abu Hanifa, including those derived by his disciples via his principles.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi madhhab includes disciples' opinions derived from the imam's principles, even if the imam later retracted his own direct statement.
