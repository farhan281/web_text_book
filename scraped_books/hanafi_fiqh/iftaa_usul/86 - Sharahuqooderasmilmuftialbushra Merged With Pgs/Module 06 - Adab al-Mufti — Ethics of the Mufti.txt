Books Unfit for Fatwa: The Mufti's Duty to Verify Sources
Prohibiting fatwa from unreliable later Hanafi books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which later Hanafi books are unreliable for fatwa and apply the principle that fatwa must be based on verified original sources.

Core Idea
A mufti must not rely on later abridged books without verification; fatwa requires tracing the ruling back to its original source.

This lesson addresses a critical adab for muftis: the sources from which fatwa is permissible. The author warns against the prevalent practice of using later compilations that contain errors, omissions, and weak tarjih. You will learn which books are problematic and the conditions under which a fatwa can be issued from them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling
The author explicitly states that it is not permissible to issue fatwa from certain later books. Recognize the prohibition as a general rule.

3
Understand the principle of referring to original sources
The author quotes a commentary stating that one must refer to the original source (al-ma'khadh al-asli) like al-Qunya. This is the safeguard against errors in later books.

Shaykh Salih al-Jinini states that fatwa from these books is only permissible if the mufti knows the original transmission and has verified the sources. This is the operative rule.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from a Hanafi mufti on the prohibition of issuing fatwa from certain later books. The author (probably Ibn 'Abidin or a commentator on Uqud Rasm al-Mufti) explains that most contemporary muftis cannot be trusted because they merely refer to later abridged books without verifying the preponderant positions. This underscores the Hanafi principle that a mufti must rely on authoritative, well-edited works and trace rulings back to the foundational texts of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.

Key Terms
الكتب المتأخرة (al-kutub al-muta'akhirah): Later Hanafi works (after the 6th-7th centuries) that often abridge earlier texts and may contain errors.

المأخذ الأصلي (al-ma'khadh al-asli): The original source text from which a ruling is derived, typically a foundational Hanafi work like al-Qunya.

The Danger of Blind Transmission: Verifying Fatwa Sources
Warning against relying on later transmissions without verification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of tashih al-naql (source verification) to identify erroneous rulings in later Hanafi fatwa collections.

Core Idea
A ruling repeated in twenty later books may still be wrong; the mufti must trace it to the original Imams.

Later Hanafi works sometimes contain errors that are copied from one author to the next without verification. The great Hanafi jurists warned against this. This lesson examines a concrete example: the issue of hiring for Quran recitation. You will learn the correct mufta bihi position and why source verification is essential for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The author warns that errors can propagate through later books. What is the core principle being emphasized about verifying rulings?
Focus on the idea that widespread transmission does not guarantee correctness.
2

The issue of hiring for Quran recitation. Who made the mistake and what was the correct position according to the three Imams?
The author of al-Siraj al-Wahhaj and al-Jawhara claimed the fatwa permits hiring for recitation, but the correct position permits only for teaching.
3
Trace the propagation
How did later authors follow the error and even extend it to other acts of worship?
Many later authors took the mistaken ruling and expanded it to all acts of obedience (ta'at) and even to Hajj.
4
Return to the original sources
What did the three Imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) say about hiring for acts of obedience?
The consensus from the three Imams is that such hiring is invalid (batil).
5

How should a mufti verify a ruling today when encountering a claim in later books?
Check the earliest sources: al-Mabsut, al-Hidayah, or reliable early fatwa collections, not just later compilations.

The passage serves as a crucial lesson for muftis: never issue a fatwa based solely on secondary compilations without checking the foundational texts of the madhhab.

Commentary
This passage illustrates the principle that rulings based on custom can change with the times. It lists several examples: 1) Permitting hiring for Quran teaching (Abu Hanifa had forbidden it), and not accepting apparent justice. 2) Realizing coercion from non-rulers due to widespread corruption. 3) Holding tax collector liable (against the usual principle of direct liability). 4) Liability of common worker. 5) Guardian cannot do mudarabah with orphan's property nowadays. 6) Holding usurper of orphan/waqf property liable and limiting lease durations. 7) Judge cannot decide based on personal knowledge. 8) Prohibiting husband from traveling with wife even if prompt dower paid. These changes are justified by the corruption of the age (fasad al-zaman) and general necessity. Note that these are not arbitrary changes but based on the need to preserve order and justice. The authority weight is from the later fatawa (mutakhkhirin), not the founding texts.

Key Terms
تغيّر الأحكام بتغير العرف (taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-urf): The principle that legal rulings may change when the underlying custom changes
فساد الزمان (fasad al-zaman): Corruption of the time; moral decay in society
المتأخرون (al-muta'akhkhirun): Later Hanafi jurists who issued fatwas different from the early imams

The Prohibition of Acting Upon Weak Opinions in Fatwa
Prohibition of fatwa and judgment based on weak opinions, with exception for personal practice in necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ruling on acting upon weak opinions in fatwa and apply the exception for personal practice with necessity.

Core Idea
A mufti must only give fatwa based on the rajih opinion; weak opinions are prohibited for fatwa and judgment, except for personal practice in necessity.

This passage from the matn and its commentary establishes a foundational rule of mufti adab: the obligation to rely on the preponderant (rajih) opinion in the madhhab. It prohibits the mufti and judge from using weak (marjuh) opinions, except in a narrow case of personal necessity. The commentary cites consensus on this principle, while also presenting an allowance from Imam al-Subki for personal practice. Understanding this rule is essential for anyone who aspires to give fatwa or issue judgments in the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Distinguish between rajih (preponderant), marjuh/da'if (weak), and mufta bihi (operative opinion). The passage treats marjuh as legally non-existent when a rajih is available.
The terms are not synonyms for 'unreliable' but indicate position in the madhhab hierarchy.
2
Understand the general rule
The matn states: 'It is not permissible to act upon the weak except for one who has a necessity.' The commentary clarifies that this applies to fatwa and judgment, not personal practice with necessity.
The rule is stated as a prohibition for the mufti and judge.
3
Examine the evidence
The commentary reports consensus (ijma) from 'Allama Qasim and Ibn al-Salah that using marjuh for fatwa or judgment is impermissible. This gives the rule its binding force.
The argument type is ijma proof.
4
Note the exception
Imam al-Subki permits following a weak opinion for one's own action (fi nafs al-amr) if there is a necessity. This exception does not extend to fatwa or judgment.
The exception is restricted to personal practice and genuine necessity (darura).
5
Apply the hierarchy
In fatwa, the mufta bihi is always the rajih opinion. Weak opinions are only for personal necessity and cannot be used to answer a questioner or in a judge's verdict.
Judges are specifically bound by the rajih of the madhhab, as emphasized in the matn.

Commentary
This passage addresses the critical rule that a mufti and judge must base their rulings on the preponderant (rajih) opinion, not on weak (marjuh) ones. The poetic matn states that acting upon a weak opinion is prohibited except for one who has a necessity (darura), and it cannot be used to answer a questioner or for a judge’s verdict; if a judge does so, his verdict is not enforceable. This is especially emphasized for Hanafi judges who are appointed upon the condition of following the preponderant view of the madhhab. The commentary reinforces this by citing Qasim’s statement that using marjuh is against consensus and that a marjuh opinion is considered legally non-existent when a rajih is available. A person who picks any opinion without tarjih is ignorant and violates consensus. However, an exception from Imam Subki is presented: following a weak opinion is permissible for one’s own personal practice (in ‘nfs al-amr’) but not for giving fatwa or judgment. Ibn al-Salah reported consensus on the impermissibility. Al-Shurunbulali’s treatise is also cited, confirming that in the Shafi'i school, acting upon marjuh is prohibited. The overall ruling for the Hanafi mufti is clear: fatwa must be on the rajih opinion, and weak opinions are only allowed for personal practice in genuine necessity.

Key Terms
ضعيف (da'if): A weak opinion; one that is outweighed (marjuh) by stronger evidence in the madhhab.
مرجوح (marjuh): The opinion that is outweighed; not preponderant.
راجح (rajih): The preponderant opinion; the stronger view in the madhhab.
تقليد (taqlid): Following a mujtahid’s opinion without knowing the evidence.
