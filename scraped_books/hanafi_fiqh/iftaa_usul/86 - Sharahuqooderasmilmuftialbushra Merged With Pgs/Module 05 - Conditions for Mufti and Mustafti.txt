Books Not Permissible for Fatwa
Warns against using certain later books for fatwa due to errors and weak rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify books that are not permissible for fatwa according to the Hanafi school and explain the reasons for their exclusion.

Core Idea
Not all later Hanafi books are reliable for fatwa; some contain errors and weak opinions and must be avoided.

You have learned that a mufti must follow the preponderant (rajih) opinion. Now, a critical question arises: which books can a mufti rely on? This passage warns that many later (muta'akhkhirin) books, especially those that are poorly edited, contain transmission errors and even non-Hanafi opinions. As a future mufti, you must know which sources are trustworthy and which are not.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Principle
Start from the overarching rule: fatwa must be based on the preponderant (rajih) opinion of the Hanafi school. This is the standard against which all books are measured.
The author assumes you have already learned the obligation of following the rajih.
2
Identify the Problematic Books
The author lists specific books: Sharh al-Nuqaya by al-Qahistani, al-Durr al-Mukhtar, al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, and others. These are classified as 'later books' (al-muta'akhkhira) that are 'unedited' (ghayr muharrara).
Note that al-Durr al-Mukhtar later became a core text, but the author here criticizes its earlier form or usage.
3
Understand the Criticisms
The criticisms are: (1) extreme brevity makes them nearly like riddles, (2) they contain transmission errors (saqt fi al-naql), (3) they prefer non-preponderant opinions, and (4) they sometimes prefer opinions from outside the Hanafi school.
These are serious defects that undermine the reliability of fatwa derived from them.
4
Review the Quoted Authority
The author cites Muhammad Hibatullah's commentary on al-Ashbah, which labels Mulla Miskin's Sharh al-Kanz and al-Qahistani's works as 'outlandish books' (ghariba) due to unknown authors or weak transmissions.
This reinforces the warning with a recognized scholar's opinion.
5
Apply the Rule
When encountering a fatwa from these books, a mufti must reject it and refer to reliable sources like the six books of Imam Muhammad or well-edited commentaries.
The practical takeaway: always verify the source before issuing a fatwa.

The Muqallid Mufti Must Say 'I Don't Know'
States the rule that a muqallid mufti cannot give fatwa from his own opinion without a text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a muqallid mufti must say 'I don't know' when no transmitted ruling exists, and distinguish it from the authority of a mujtahid mufti.

Core Idea
A muqallid mufti must say 'I don't know' when no transmitted text exists.

This lesson examines a critical principle from Hanafi usul al-iftaa: a mufti who is a muqallid (one who follows a mujtahid) cannot issue a fatwa based on his own opinion when he lacks a textual basis. The passage from the commentary on 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti explains this rule, warning against reckless fatwa-giving and emphasizing the need to either refer to a more learned scholar or admit ignorance. This principle upholds the integrity of the Shari'ah and protects against slandering Allah by declaring something halal or haram without divine warrant.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the mufti's capacity
Identify whether the mufti is a mujtahid (capable of ijtihad) or a muqallid (a follower who cannot derive new rulings). The ruling on how to proceed depends on this distinction.
The text explicitly mentions both types and treats them differently.
2
Search for a textual foundation
The muqallid mufti must first look for the ruling in Zahir al-Riwayah, then in the principles of the school, and then from later scholars. If a clear ruling is found, it is followed.
The passage quotes al-Khaniyya on this sequence.
3
Handle disagreement among later scholars
If the later scholars differ on a matter, a mujtahid mufti may give his own ijtihad. A muqallid mufti, however, cannot; he must take the opinion of the most learned scholar.
The text stresses that a muqallid is not allowed to exercise ijtihad.
4
Apply the rule of 'la adri'
If no text exists from any recognized source, the muqallid mufti must state 'I don't know' (la adri) and refer the questioner to someone more knowledgeable.
This follows the example of the Companions and the Prophet himself.
5
Reflect on the wisdom behind the rule
Restricting the muqallid mufti prevents unauthorized legislation and protects the sacred law from personal bias. It reinforces the principle that fatwa is a serious responsibility.
The passage mentions fear of 'slandering Allah' as a deterrent.

This is supported by references from al-Bahr and al-Tatarkhaniyya, and by the practice of the Companions and even the Prophet (peace be upon him). The passage underscores the adab of the mufti: knowing one's limitations and avoiding self-issuance of fatwas without basis.

The Rule of Caution in Takfir: Even a Weak Narration Suffices
Stating the rule to avoid takfir when a weak narration exists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a Muslim is not declared a kafir if there exists any plausible good interpretation or any scholarly disagreement, even if based on a weak narration.

Core Idea
Takfir is grave: avoid it whenever any narration suggests a Muslim's statement can be interpreted well.

This passage from a classical Hanafi work on mufti conduct (rasm al-mufti) addresses the most sensitive ruling in Islamic law: declaring a Muslim an apostate. The text establishes a firm principle of caution: as long as there is any possibility of interpreting the person's words in a good light, or any scholarly difference — even from a weak transmission — takfir must be avoided. This reflects the Hanafi school's deep reluctance to expel someone from the fold of Islam.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the gravity of takfir
The passage opens by stating that kufr (disbelief) is a grave matter. This sets the tone: takfir is not to be taken lightly.
This principle is rooted in the Quranic verse warning against wrongful takfir.
2
Identify the two conditions that prevent takfir
The text gives two conditions that block a fatwa of kufr: (1) if the person's statement can be given a plausible good interpretation (maḥmil ḥasan); (2) if there exists any scholarly disagreement (ikhtilāf) about whether the statement constitutes kufr.
The disjunction 'or' indicates that either condition suffices.
3
Note the evidentiary threshold for the ikhtilāf
The text explicitly states that even a weak narration (riwāya ḍaʿīfa) is sufficient to establish ikhtilāf and thus prevent a fatwa of kufr.
Normally, weak narrations are not used for rulings, but here the gravity of takfir warrants a lower threshold.

Action upon Weak Opinions: The Mufti's Personal Practice
Ruling on acting upon weak opinions for oneself and exceptions for necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on acting upon weak opinions for oneself and identify the necessity exception, applying it to a real case.

Core Idea
Acting upon a weak opinion for oneself is prohibited unless necessity demands it.

This lesson examines a critical principle for muftis: can a Hanafi scholar follow a weak (marjuh) opinion in his own personal practice, even when the school's strong (rajih) opinion is known? The source passage clarifies the strict Hanafi stance—prohibition except for genuine necessity—and contrasts it with the Shafi'i position. Understanding this rule preserves the integrity of the madhhab while allowing flexibility for excused individuals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi rule
State the general rule: the Hanafi school prohibits acting upon a weak (marjuh) opinion for oneself. Understand the rationale: the weak opinion is considered like non-existence (bimanzilat al-'adam) or abrogated (mansukh) relative to the strong.
This applies to personal practice, not to issuing fatwa or judgment, which have their own stricter rules.
2
Recognize the exception for necessity
The prohibition is lifted when genuine necessity (darurah) exists. Necessity means extreme hardship that would otherwise prevent the fulfillment of a religious obligation. The example given is a traveler or guest who fears suspicion and follows Abu Yusuf's weak opinion on ghusl for wet dreams.
Darurah is narrowly defined; mere convenience or preference does not qualify.
3
Analyze the dispute with Shafi'i
Al-Subki reports that Shafi'i permits acting upon weak opinions for oneself, unlike the Hanafi stricter view. Hanafis argue this contradicts a transmitted consensus (Ibn Hajar) and weakens the school's methodology.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf): a Hanafi mufti cannot freely choose weak opinions; a Shafi'i mufti can.
4
Examine the case study: blood from wounds
Imam al-Marghinani (author of al-Hidayah) chose a weak opinion in Mukhtarat al-Nawazil: non-flowing blood from wounds does not nullify wudu nor prevent prayer, even if left to flow. This contradicts the well-known strong opinion. However, because al-Marghinani is a great imam of takhrij and tas-hih, his opinion can be followed by someone with an excuse (ma'dhur) due to necessity.
The author himself used this weak opinion when afflicted with a cauterized pimple, finding it the only easy way to pray validly.
5
Apply the rule to the mufti's practice
As a mufti, you must distinguish between issuing fatwa (always follow the strong relied-upon opinion) and personal practice. For personal acts, you may adopt a weak opinion only if a genuine necessity arises, and you should ensure that the opinion comes from a credible Hanafi authority.
This principle upholds the school's hierarchy and prevents arbitrary selection of opinions.

The Hanafi school: prohibition of the weak opinion for oneself because the weak has become abrogated. Until I said: The reasoning that it has become abrogated only appears if there were two statements in the issue, the mujtahid retracted one, or it is known that one is later than the other; otherwise no, as if there is a statement of Abu Yusuf and a statement of Muhammad, then abrogation does not appear. But his intent is that when one is corrected, the other becomes like abrogated. That is the meaning of what passed from 'Allamah Qasim: "The weak in comparison to the strong is like non-existence." Then what al-Subki mentioned of the permissibility of acting upon the weak for oneself according to al-Shafi'i contradicts what passed from 'Allamah Qasim, and we presented its like at the beginning of the commentary from the fatawa of Ibn Hajar regarding the transmission of consensus on not issuing fatwa and acting upon any opinion one wishes, unless it is said: meant by acting is adjudication and judgment, which is far-fetched. Upon that is carried what preceded from al-Shurunbulali: that the Hanafi school is prohibition, evidenced by their permission for the traveler and the guest who fears suspicion to take the statement of Abu Yusuf that there is no obligatory ghusl on the one who had a wet dream who held his penis when he felt the wet dream until his desire subsided then released it, even though this statement contradicts the strong opinion in the school; but they permitted taking it due to necessity. And it was said: If it were such that if left it would flow, it prevents." Then he repeated the issue in the nullifiers of wudu, and said: "If something little came out of it and he wiped it with a cloth, such that if left it would flow, it does not nullify. And it was said: etc." I have checked another copy and saw the phrase likewise. It is not hidden that the well-known opinion in all books of the school is the second statement expressed by "qil". As for what he chose of the first statement, I have not seen anyone before him nor anyone after him following it after extensive review; it is a shadh (isolated) opinion. But the author of al-Hidayah is a great imam from the greatest shaykhs of the school, from the class of scholars of takhrij and tas-hih, as passed. Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi ruling on acting upon weak (marjuh) opinions in one's personal practice, as opposed to issuing fatwa or judging. The author begins by stating the Hanafi position: it is prohibited to act upon a weak opinion for oneself because the weak opinion is considered abrogated (mansukh) in the face of the strong. However, the author refines this: the abrogation reasoning only applies when there is a clear chronological order or retraction; otherwise, as in cases where Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ, the weak is merely like non-existence relative to the strong, not truly abrogated. The author then contrasts the Hanafi position with the Shafi'i position as reported by al-Subki, who permitted acting upon weak opinions for oneself. The author leans toward the stricter view, aligning with a transmitted consensus (from Ibn Hajar) that one cannot pick and choose opinions at will. However, an exception is made for necessity (darurah): if a person acts upon a weak opinion due to genuine need, it is allowed. The author supports this with an example from al-Shurunbulali: a traveler or guest who fears suspicion may follow Abu Yusuf's weak opinion on ghusl for wet dreams. The text then gives a detailed example from Imam al-Marghinani's Mukhtarat al-Nawazil regarding blood from wounds. Al-Marghinani chose a weak opinion (that non-flowing blood does not nullify wudu or prevent prayer) over the strong opinion. The author notes that this weak opinion is shadh (isolated), but because al-Marghinani is a highly authoritative scholar of the school, a person with an excuse (like the author himself who suffered from a cauterized pimple) may follow it in necessity. The author concludes that this weak opinion provides great facility for those with chronic excuses, as it allows prayer without the hardship of constant washing and bandaging.

Key Terms
المرجوح (al-marjuh): The weaker opinion in a given legal issue, as opposed to the preponderant (rajih) opinion
الضرورة (al-darurah): A situation of extreme need or hardship that permits deviation from the strong opinion
قيل (qil): A weak or secondary opinion cited in Hanafi texts, often not part of the relied-upon position
