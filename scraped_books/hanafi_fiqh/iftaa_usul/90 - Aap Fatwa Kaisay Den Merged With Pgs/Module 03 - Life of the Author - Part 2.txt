Early Hanafi Fatwa Authorities and Their Works
Listing Hanafi scholars and their works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key early Hanafi scholars mentioned in the passage, state their teachers and works, and evaluate the weight of their fatwa collections in the Hanafi hierarchy.

Core Idea
A mufti must know the scholarly chain and collection type to prioritize reliable fatwa sources.

This passage presents biographical notes on several influential Hanafi jurists of the early centuries whose works became foundational for fatwa methodology. Understanding who these scholars were, whom they studied under, and the scope of their compilations is essential for a mufti to evaluate the strength of a reported opinion. As we move through the entries, focus on the scholarly connections and the types of works—whether comprehensive nawazil collections, abridgments, or specialized compilations.

Guided Path
1
Identify each scholar and his period
Scan the passage for names, death dates, and birth dates if given. Note the century and region (e.g., Balkh, Bukhara, Ray) to place the scholar in historical context.
Death dates help gauge the era of compilation and the chain of transmission.
2
Trace the teacher-student chain
For each scholar, identify their teachers and students mentioned. This reveals the chain of knowledge transmission (sanad) that adds to the authority of their works.
For example, Sadr al-Shahid studied under earlier authorities; his work Waqi'at Husamiyyah collects from Abu al-Layth and Natafi.
3
Catalogue the works
List all books mentioned for each scholar, noting which are extant (in manuscript) and which are lost. This affects a mufti's ability to consult them.
For instance, Al-Natafi's Majmu' al-Nawazil is lost, while his Ahkam al-Ajnas survives.
4
Classify the work type
Determine whether the work is a nawazil collection (like Waqi'at Husamiyyah), a comprehensive manual (like Jami'), an abridgment (like Khulasat al-Fatawa), or a specialized text.
Nawazil collections compile actual fatwas and cases; they are primary sources for later muftis.
5
Evaluate authority level
Assess the author's rank in the Hanafi hierarchy (e.g., 'mujtahid fi al-masa'il' for Iftikhar al-Din) and the traditional acceptance of their works (e.g., Zahir al-Riwayah status).
Ibn Kamal Pasha classified Iftikhar al-Din as a mujtahid fi al-masa'il, elevating his Khulasat al-Fatawa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify issues of uncertain authorship in Hanafi legal texts and apply the principle of verifying the chain of authority before citing a work in fatwa.

Core Idea
A mufti must verify the author of a cited work or its authority within the madhhab before relying on it.

In Hanafi iftaa, every reference must be traceable to a recognized authority. Al-Hawi al-Qudsi is a Hanafi work, but its authorship is disputed among several candidates. This lesson explores how to navigate such cases using the tools of textual criticism and the hierarchy of Hanafi sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the multiple claimants to authorship
List the different individuals proposed as the author of al-Hawi al-Qudsi: a judge of Jerusalem (unknown), Qadi Jamal al-Din Ahmad al-Ghaznawi, Muhammad al-Ghaznawi, and someone from Farghana. Note the sources for each claim.

For each proposed author, examine the evidence: the name, the date, the location (Jerusalem for Qudsi). The reason for 'Qudsi' is that it was written in Jerusalem, not that the author's name is Qudsi.
The name al-Qudsi is a nisbah to al-Quds (Jerusalem), indicating the place of composition.
3
Determine the practical consequence for a mufti
If a mufti encounters a citation from al-Hawi al-Qudsi, he cannot assume a specific author's reliability. He must assess the work's authority within the madhhab: Does it gain acceptance from later Hanafi scholars? Is it frequently cited? The uncertainty may reduce its weight.
The work itself may still be used if it aligns with established Hanafi positions, but direct attribution is problematic.
4
Apply the principle of verifying authority in a modern context
In contemporary fatwa research, a mufti should trace the chain of transmission for any text. For al-Hawi al-Qudsi, he might consult reliable secondary sources that evaluate its status, or he may avoid relying on it except for corroboration.
The mufti's methodology includes checking the authority hierarchy: Zahir al-Riwayah, Nawadir, Fatawa Mutakhkhirin.
