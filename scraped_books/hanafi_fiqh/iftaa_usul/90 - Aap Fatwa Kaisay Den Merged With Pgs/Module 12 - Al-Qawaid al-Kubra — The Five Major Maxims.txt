Exception for 'Urf-Based Nazir and Hierarchy of Imam's Qawl
Provides the exception where nazir is allowed and introduces a rasm rule for preferring Imam Abu Hanifa in ibadat and Abu Yusuf in qada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a mufti may use nazir in 'urf-based matters and apply the Hanafi hierarchy for preferring Imam Abu Hanifa in ibadat and Abu Yusuf in qada.

Core Idea
In 'urf-based matters nazir is allowed; in ibadat prefer Abu Hanifa, in qada prefer Abu Yusuf.

This passage establishes two critical principles for the mufti: an exception to the general prohibition of using nazir when the ruling depends on 'urf that does not contradict the texts, and a clear hierarchy for preferring the sayings of the Imams in different domains. These are foundational for reliable and consistent fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the exception for 'urf-based nazir
Identify that when a case's ruling is based on custom ('urf) that does not contradict shariah texts, the mufti may give fatwa using similar cases (nazair) because such rulings are not rigidly textual.
This exception will be elaborated later in the poem's commentary (line 69).
2
Memorize the hierarchy: ibadat → Imam Abu Hanifa; qada → Abu Yusuf
The poetic lines state: in all chapters of worship, the saying of Imam Abu Hanifa is preferred absolutely, unless a stronger narration from him emerges; in every branch related to judicial matters, the saying of Abu Yusuf is relied upon.
This hierarchy is a settled rasm al-mufti rule in the Hanafi school.
3
Apply the hierarchy in practical fatwa scenarios
When a mufti faces a question, first categorize the issue: if it pertains to ibadat (e.g., prayer, fasting, zakat), prefer Abu Hanifa's view; if it pertains to qada (e.g., contracts, disputes, court procedures), prefer Abu Yusuf's view.
Do not generalize this hierarchy to all matters; it applies specifically to these two domains.

The Legal Status of Custom and Habit in Hanafi Ifta'
Definitions of Urf and Adah for legal application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ʿurf and ʿādah according to Hanafi usul, distinguish between them, and apply the maxim that custom has consideration in legal rulings.

Core Idea
Custom (ʿurf) carries legal weight due to rational acceptance; habit (ʿādah) is mere repetition — only custom shapes fatwa.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational concepts of custom (ʿurf) and habit (ʿādah) as defined in classical usul works. Understanding the difference is crucial for muftis because many fatwas rest on prevailing customs. We'll analyze the qawida 'Custom in the Shari'ah has consideration' and see how it operates in practice.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its meaning
Read the Arabic couplet: 'وَالْعُرف في الشَّرع لَهُ اعْتِبَارُ لِذَا عَلَيْهِ الحكمُ قَدْيْدَارُ'. Understand that it declares custom as a legally recognised source upon which rulings may be based.
This is a sughra (minor) maxim, often cited in fatwa manuals.
2
Define ʿurf from al-Mustasfa
Locate the definition: ʿurf is a habit that settles in hearts by reason and is accepted by sound natures. Note the two key conditions: rational acceptance and alignment with sound disposition.
This definition distinguishes ʿurf from mere societal practice.
3
Define ʿādah from Sharh al-Tahrir
Read the definition: ʿādah is something that occurs repeatedly without any rational connection. Contrast with ʿurf – repetition alone is insufficient for legal weight.
ʿĀdah lacks the rational and ethical dimension of ʿurf.
4
Distinguish between ʿurf and ʿādah
Create a mental table: ʿurf requires rational acceptance and sound nature, while ʿādah is mere recurrence. In Hanafi usul, ʿurf can be a source of law, but ʿādah is not authoritative on its own.
The common error is conflating them; this step clarifies the legal hierarchy.
5
Identify the legal implications for a mufti
Consider a scenario where a local custom meets the definition – the mufti may base a fatwa on it, provided it does not contradict a clear text. If only a habit (ʿādah) exists, it carries no binding force.
This is the practical takeaway for ifta' work.

The Legal Maxim: Custom is Decisive (العادة محكمة)
Defining 'urf and 'adah with maxim, basis, condition, and furu'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'urf and 'adah, state the maxim al-'adatu muhakkamah, explain its basis and condition of generality/predominance, and apply it to the furu' of currency determination.

Core Idea
Custom is decisive, but only when it is general or predominant.

In Hanafi fiqh, custom ('urf) is a recognized source of law, often ruling in the absence of explicit text. This lesson unpacks the famous maxim 'al-'adatu muhakkamah' from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, tracing its definitions, its evidential basis (a hadith from Ibn Mas'ud), and its key condition: only general or predominant custom carries weight. Through a practical furu' from Hidayah on currency, you will see how this maxim operates in actual legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Define the terms
Read the definitions of 'urf (from al-Mustasfa) and 'adah (from Sharh al-Tahrir). Note the subtle difference: 'urf is rooted in reason and accepted by sound natures, while 'adah is repeated occurrence without rational connection. In legal usage, they are often synonymous.
2
Identify the maxim and its basis
Locate the maxim 'al-'adatu muhakkamah' and the supporting hadith 'What Muslims consider good is good with Allah'. Note the authentication note: 'Ala'i found it mawquf (saying of Ibn Mas'ud), not marfu' (prophetic). This illustrates Hanafi care in source criticism.
The maxim is from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, one of the five major maxims.
3
Understand the condition
The passage explicitly states that custom is only considered when it is general ('aam) or predominant (ghalib). A local or rare custom does not qualify.
This restrictions prevents arbitrary or idiosyncratic practices from becoming law.
4
Examine the furu' (application)
Analyze the currency example from Hidayah: when multiple currencies circulate, a sale mentioning unspecified dirhams and daniqs is referred to the commonly used currency. This shows how the maxim resolves ambiguity in favor of general custom.
The ruling is based on 'urf being the prevailing indicator.
5
Note the supplementary principle
The passage ends with the rule 'al-thabit bi al-'urf ka al-thabit bi al-nass' (what is established by custom is like what is established by text). This elevates custom almost to the level of textual proof in Hanafi fiqh.

Custom is Decisive: The Maxim and Its Applications
Explaining the maxim 'al-ʿāda muḥakkama' with evidence and furuat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'al-ʿāda muḥakkama' to identify when custom changes a ruling and state the mufta bihi position in cases of intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Core Idea
Custom is decisive — what is established by custom carries the weight of textual proof in Hanafi law.

This lesson explores one of the five major legal maxims of the Hanafi school: 'Custom is decisive' (العادة محكمة). The maxim is not merely a theoretical principle; it has been applied by later Hanafi scholars (mutā'akhkhirūn) to update rulings when customs and times change, even when those rulings originally contradicted the explicit statements of Imam Abu Hanifa. We will examine its textual basis, its scope, and eight concrete furuat (applications) that demonstrate its power in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its evidence
Begin by identifying the maxim 'al-ʿāda muḥakkama' and the hadith that serves as its basis (even if mawquf). Recognize that the Hanafi school considers this a major qawida.
The hadith is reported by Ibn Mas'ud (mawquf), but scholars treat it as a valid basis.
2
Identify the scope and condition
The maxim applies only when custom is general (ʿāmm) or dominant (ghālib). Note the explicit restriction: custom that is not widespread is not considered.
This is a self-limiting condition within the maxim itself.
3
Work through each furu' case
For each of the eight applications in the passage, identify the original ruling (Imam's view), the change due to custom, and the later fatwa. Pay attention to how the principle of 'al-thābit bil-ʿurfi ka al-thābit bil-naṣṣi' operates.
Examples: sale currencies, ijarah for Quran, witnesses, ikrah, informer, hired worker, executor's mudarabah, usurper.
4
Note the istithna' and exceptions
Recognize that the maxim does not apply when custom is not general or dominant. No other exception is mentioned in the passage.
Other maxims may restrict custom if it contradicts a definitive text.
5

Custom ('Urf) Permissible Unless Contrary to Shariah
Affirming that custom is followed in fatwa as long as it does not contradict Shariah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim governing custom ('urf) and identify its exception when custom contradicts explicit nass.

Core Idea
Custom rules in fatwa until it clashes with revelation – then it falls silent.

The authority of custom is a cornerstone of Hanafi fatwa methodology. Yet not every practice is legitimised simply by being widespread. In this passage, the author clarifies the decisive boundary: custom is authoritative only when it does not contradict Shariah. Understanding this balance is essential for any mufti who must navigate between timeless texts and changing social realities.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Identify the qawida being defined: 'al-ada muhakkama' (custom is authoritative). This is one of the five major legal maxims of Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the condition
Custom is authoritative only when it does not contradict Shariah. The passage explicitly states 'bishart an la yakun khilaf shariah'.
3
Note the exception
When custom contradicts an explicit text (nass) or a definitive principle, the text takes precedence. This is the istithna' to the maxim.
4
Apply to examples
Even widespread practices like tax (chungi) and interest (sood) are rejected because they violate clear prohibitions. The maxim does not legitimize them.
5
Reflect on the mufti's duty
The mufti must know the customs of his time ('ahl al-zaman'). Ignorance of contemporary conditions is ignorance itself. Additionally, in judicial matters, Imam Abu Yusuf's opinion is preferred due to his practical experience.

Custom ('urf) is a recognised subsidiary source in Hanafi fiqh, but not all custom carries the same weight. This passage introduces two fundamental types: general custom ('urf 'amm) and specific custom ('urf khass). Commentary
This passage defines two types of 'urf: 'urf 'amm (general custom) and 'urf khass (specific custom). It states that general custom can serve as a mukhassis (specifier) for qiyas and hadith, meaning it can restrict the application of a general ruling. Specific custom, however, cannot be a mukhassis; it only establishes a specific ruling as long as it does not contradict primary sources. The text then provides two furuat (applications) from al-Zakhirah. First, the ijarah (contract for wage) for weaving cloth: the Hanafi jurists of Balkh allowed it based on prevalent practice (ta'amul), even though qiyas might disallow it. They argue that ta'amul justifies specifying the hadith that prohibits measuring grain in such contracts; the hadith applies to milling but not to weaving because of different customs. Second, the contract of istisna' (manufacturing sale) is allowed due to ta'amul, despite the general prohibition of selling what is not in possession. Thus, ta'amul serves as a basis for specifying the relevant hadith, not abandoning it entirely.

Key Terms
عرف عام (urf 'amm): Custom that is widespread and prevalent among all people in general.
عرف خاص (urf khass): Custom that is limited to a particular region, group, or profession.
تعامل (ta'amul): Prevalent practice among people in a locality or trade.
مخصص (mukhassis): Something that specifies the application of a general ruling to exclude certain cases.

General and Specific Custom in Hanafi Iftaa: 'Urf 'Amm and 'Urf Khaas
Defining general and specific custom and their legal authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'urf 'amm and 'urf khaas, apply the Hanafi rule that only general custom can specify texts without abandoning them, and construct a fatwa that respects custom while maintaining textual authority.

Core Idea
Only general custom can specify texts; specific custom applies locally but cannot override hadith or qiyas.

In this lesson, we explore a critical distinction in Hanafi iftaa: the classification of custom ('urf) into general ('amm) and specific (khaas). A mufti who ignores custom risks injustice, but custom must not undermine textual proofs. The passage from 'Sharh Rasm al-Mufti' establishes the principle that only general custom can serve as a specifier (mukhassis) for hadith and analogy; specific custom is valid only for its people and only if it does not oppose texts. Through the example of cloth-weaving contracts in Balkh and the universally accepted istisna', we see how this principle is applied.

Guided Path
1
Identify the custom and its scope
Determine whether the custom in question is general (widespread across regions) or specific (limited to a region or group). This determines its legal weight.
The passage defines 'urf 'amm as establishing a general ruling and being a potential mukhassis, while 'urf khaas only establishes a specific ruling.
2
Apply the rule on specifying texts
For 'urf 'amm, check if relying on it leads to takhsis (specification) of a text or complete abandonment. Only takhsis is allowed; abandonment is prohibited. For 'urf khaas, check if it contradicts a text or requires takhsis — if so, it is rejected.
The key distinction: 'urf 'amm can specify, 'urf khaas cannot. Even if 'urf khaas is contrary to zahir al-riwayah, it is valid for that locality as long as no text opposition.
3
Analyze the example: Balkh cloth-weaving
The Balkh scholars considered the custom of weavers taking one-third of the cloth as wage as a proof (ta'amul) that specifies the hadith on the miller's measure. Later scholars rejected this because the custom was specific to Balkh, not universal.
The later position holds that a specific custom cannot create takhsis because non-practice elsewhere creates doubt.
4
Compare with istisna'
Istisna' (manufacturing sale) is permissible despite the hadith prohibiting selling what is not possessed, because it is a universal custom ('urf 'amm). This takhsis is valid as it does not abandon the hadith completely.
Istisna' is a clear example of 'urf 'amm specifying a text.
5
Formulate the fatwa
When issuing a fatwa involving custom, first classify the custom. If it is general and leads to takhsis, accept it. If it is specific, ensure it does not contradict texts. If it does, reject it. In contracts and oaths, interpret according to local custom even if it contradicts technical juristic definitions.
The mufti must be aware of the scope of the custom and its compatibility with textual evidence.

How to Give a Fatwa? 127. Sharh Rasm al-Mufti. In short, it is clear for you that a mufti or qadi sticking to the apparent meaning of transmitted narrations, abandoning custom ('urf) and clear indications (qara'in), and being unaware of people's circumstances is tantamount to wasting many rights and oppressing a very large creation. 'Urf 'Amm, 'Urf Khaas, and Their Rulings: Then know that custom is of two types: general custom ('urf 'amm) and specific custom ('urf khaas). From general custom, a general ruling is established, and it can be a specifier (mukhassis) for qiyas and hadith. The matter of specific custom is different from it because from it a specific ruling is established, provided that it does not oppose qiyas or hadith, because specific custom cannot become a specifier. In Al-Dhakhira, Kitab al-Ijarah, eighth chapter, where this issue is mentioned: if someone gave spun yarn to a weaver with the condition that he would take one-third of the finished cloth and weave the cloth, there the author of Al-Dhakhira wrote: 'The fuqaha of Balkh, such as Nasir bin Kayji, Muhammad bin Salama, and others, consider this ijarah permissible in cloth-making, due to the prevalent practice (ta'amul) in their region in cloth-weaving, and ta'amul is a proof by which qiyas is abandoned and specification (takhsis) is made in the narration.' And the meaning of deeming this ijarah permissible due to ta'amul in cloth-weaving is to make takhsis in that hadith which is reported about qafiz al-tahhan (the measure of the miller), because that hadith is reported about the measure of the one who grinds flour; it is not reported about the weaver, but the weaver is analogous to it, so that hadith would also apply to it by indication (dalalah). Then, when we did not act upon that hadith regarding the weaver, and we acted upon that hadith regarding the measure of the miller, this constitutes takhsis of the hadith, not complete abandonment of the hadith, and takhsis of the hadith due to ta'amul is permissible. See, we consider istisna' permissible due to ta'amul, even though it involves selling something that is not in the seller's possession, and there is a prohibition in the hadith about such a thing, and deeming istisna' permissible due to ta'amul is a takhsis of the hadith that came prohibiting selling what is not in one's possession; it is not completely abandoning the hadith, because we act upon that hadith in other cases besides istisna'. The scholars also said that this matter is completely different from when in a region only qafiz al-tahhan becomes prevalent, then it would not be permissible, and the practice of those people would not be considered valid, because if we consider their practice valid, it would require completely abandoning the hadith, and abandoning the hadith due to ta'amul is absolutely impermissible; only takhsis in it is permissible. But our scholars (Ulama) did not deem this takhsis (which the scholars of Balkh made) permissible, because this matter of cloth-weaving is the practice of people of a specific region, and the ta'amul of people of one region does not create takhsis in the hadith, because if the ta'amul of one region causes takhsis, then the lack of ta'amul in another region would prevent the takhsis. Thus, due to doubt, takhsis would not be established, and the matter of istisna' is different from it because it is the ta'amul of all regions (the text of Al-Dhakhira ends). And the summary of Al-Dhakhira's text is that in a situation where relying on general custom necessitates abandoning a text (mansus), it will not be relied upon. General custom will only be relied upon if relying on it necessitates takhsis in the text. And specific custom will not be relied upon in either situation; it will be valid only for the people of that custom, provided that relying on it does not necessitate abandoning the text nor requires making takhsis in it, even if that specific custom contradicts the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) (still it will be considered valid). And specific custom is considered valid, such as in the conventional words concerning oaths, and in contracts like sale and ijarah, etc., in prevalent custom and habit. Accordingly, those words and transactions in each region will be implemented according to the custom of that region, and from them the meaning that is conventional among the people will be taken, and the same validity and invalidity, permissibility and impermissibility will be understood as required by the custom of those people, even if the jurists have explicitly stated that the requirement of the words and contracts is contrary to the people's custom. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Speech is construed according to custom, not literal definitions, in all legal declarations.

This lesson introduces a cornerstone of Hanafi fatwa methodology: the principle that words in legal declarations (waqf, will, oath, vow, contract) are interpreted according to the speaker's custom, not by strict linguistic or Shar'i definitions. The passage from Rasm al-Mufti, citing Jami' al-Fusulayn and Allamah Qasim, establishes this rule and notes its breadth. Understanding this principle is essential for a mufti to issue rulings that align with the intent of the parties, especially when the literal meaning would contradict common usage.
