Using Maliki Scholars' Works in Hanafi Fatwa
Biography with bibliography of Ibn Abd al-Barr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify reputable non-Hanafi sources (like Ibn Abd al-Barr's works) that can be used for cross-referencing in Hanafi fatwa and explain their relevance.

Core Idea
A mufti can cite Maliki scholars when they support the Hanafi position or clarify points of ikhtilaf.

This lesson introduces the works of the great Maliki muhaddith and jurist Ibn Abd al-Barr. Though he is not of our madhhab, his encyclopedic works on hadith, ikhtilaf, and the virtues of the imams are valuable tools for a Hanafi mufti. Knowing when and how to cite a non-Hanafi authority requires understanding their scholarly standing and the purpose of the citation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scholar's Credentials
Read the biographical data: name, birth/death, affiliations, and scholarly reputation. This establishes reliability.
Ibn Abd al-Barr (378-463 AH) was a Hafiz of the Maghrib, a Maliki jurist and foremost muhaddith.
2
List the Works and Their Subjects
Extract the titles and brief subjects. Each work covers hadith, fiqh, or ikhtilaf.
e.g., al-Tamhid – commentary on Muwatta; al-Intiqad – virtues of Malik, Abu Hanifa, al-Shafi'i.
3
Assess Cross-Madhhab Utility
Determine which works are useful for a Hanafi mufti: those that document scholarly differences or support a common position.
al-Insaf fi ma bayn al-Ulama min al-Ikhtilaf is directly about ikhtilaf.
4
Apply in Fatwa Context
Learn when citing a non-Hanafi authority is permissible: when the Hanafi school itself acknowledges the other view or when the other view supports the mufta bihi by corroborating the evidence.
Citing Ibn Abd al-Barr on a point of hadith authentication can strengthen a fatwa.
5
Ensure Epistemological Weight
Always rank the authority: hanafi zahir riwayah comes first; non-Hanafi sources are secondary and should be used as corroboration, not primary proof.
A non-Hanafi source alone does not establish a ruling unless the Hanafi school adopts that view through tarjih.

Al-Sharani's comparative fiqh works bridge madhahib for muftis.

Verifying the Author of Fatawa Sirajiyya
Resolving the authorship of Fatawa Sirajiyya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct author of Fatawa Sirajiyya and explain the reason for the common misattribution.

Core Idea
A fatwa collection is only as reliable as its author — verify attribution by checking the author's known works.

When citing a fatwa collection, a mufti must know its author to assess its authority. The Fatawa Sirajiyya has often been incorrectly attributed to Siraj al-Din al-Qari al-Hidayah. This lesson resolves the confusion by examining the evidence, showing why the correct attribution is to Siraj al-Din al-Hindi al-Ghaznawi.

The first attribution is from Allama Shami and Brockelmann; the second is presented as the correct view.

The passage states that Allama Shami in Radd al-Mukhtar and Brockelmann attributed the work to al-Qari al-Hidayah.

The passage rejects the first attribution because al-Qari al-Hidayah has no known works. This is a strong reason: an author with no other writings is unlikely to have authored a major fatwa collection.

Confirm the correct author
The passage states that the correct author is Allama Siraj al-Din Abu Hafs Umar ibn Ishaq ibn Ahmad al-Hindi al-Ghaznawi al-Misri, an 8th-century Hanafi scholar.
His death date is unclear in the text, but he is identified as a kabar (great) scholar.

Understand the significance for fatwa practice

Serial biographies of Shafi'i scholars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify seven prominent Shafi'i jurists referenced in Rasm al-Mufti and state their key contributions to usul al-fiqh and hadith.

Core Idea
Shafi'i scholars like al-Zarkashi and al-Juwayni authored foundational usul works used across madhahib.

This lesson introduces you to several Shafi'i scholars whose works are cited in the commentary on Rasm al-Mufti. Although they are not Hanafi, their contributions to usul al-fiqh and hadith are essential for a mufti to know. Familiarity with these scholars enriches your understanding of the inter-madhhab dialogue within fatwa methodology.

Guided Path
1
Scan the biography
For each scholar, note the full name, death date (Hijri and Gregorian), and madhhab. Pay attention to teachers and students, if mentioned.
2
Identify major works
List the key works, especially those in usul al-fiqh, hadith, or qawaid. Note which are printed and which remain in manuscript.
3
Assess cross-madhhab relevance
Consider why a Hanafi fatwa manual would reference a Shafi'i scholar. Often it is because their works on usul or qawaid are used by later Hanafis.
Example: al-Zarkashi's Qawaid al-Zarkashi is a standard reference in Hanafi legal maxims.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical listing of prominent jurists and usul scholars whose works are referenced in the commentary on Rasm al-Mufti (the manual on the ethics and methodology of issuing fatwas). The entries cover a range of scholars from the Shafi'i and Hanafi schools, providing their names, death dates (in both Hijri and Common Era), teachers, and notable works. The purpose of such a listing is to identify the authorities behind the legal and methodological positions discussed in the text. The author (likely Ibn Abidin or a later commentator) includes both well-known figures like Badr al-Din al-Zarkashi (author of al-Bahr al-Muhit in Usul al-Fiqh) and lesser-known ones like Ali ibn Ghanim al-Maqdisi. The passage also notes the status of some works as manuscripts or printed. Students of Usul al-Iftaa should familiarise themselves with these scholars as they represent the chain of transmission and authority in the Hanafi tradition, although many listed here are Shafi'i, indicating the cross-madhhab references typical in later Hanafi fatwa works.

علامہ بدر الدین زرکشی (Allamah Badr al-Din al-Zarkashi): A Shafi'i jurist and muhaddith (d. 794 AH), author of al-Bahr al-Muhit in Usul al-Fiqh and Qawaid al-Zarkashi.
ابن الملقن (Ibn al-Mulaqqin): A Shafi'i muhaddith and faqih (d. 804 CE), known for al-Tawdih (Sharh al-Bukhari) and works on hadith.
ابن برہان (Ibn Burhan): A Shafi'i jurist and usuli (d. 518 AH), author of al-Awsat in Usul al-Fiqh.
امام مزنی (Imam al-Muzani): A Shafi'i jurist (d. 262 AH), author of Mukhtasar al-Muzani, a key Shafi'i fiqh text.
ابن سریج (Ibn Suraij): A Shafi'i faqih (d. 306 AH), prolific author and qadi of Shiraz.
امام الحرمین (Imam al-Haramayn): Title of Abu al-Ma'ali al-Juwayni (d. 478 AH), a Shafi'i usuli and theologian.
امام رافضی (Imam al-Rafi'i): Abd al-Karim al-Qazwini (d. 623 AH), a Shafi'i jurist, author of Fath al-Aziz.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical listing of prominent jurists and usul scholars whose works are referenced in the commentary on Rasm al-Mufti (the manual on the ethics and methodology of issuing fatwas). The entries cover a range of scholars from the Shafi'i and Hanafi schools, providing their names, death dates (in both Hijri and Common Era), teachers, and notable works. The purpose of such a listing is to identify the authorities behind the legal and methodological positions discussed in the text. The author (likely Ibn Abidin or a later commentator) includes both well-known figures like Badr al-Din al-Zarkashi (author of al-Bahr al-Muhit in Usul al-Fiqh) and lesser-known ones like Ali ibn Ghanim al-Maqdisi. The passage also notes the status of some works as manuscripts or printed. Students of Usul al-Iftaa should familiarise themselves with these scholars as they represent the chain of transmission and authority in the Hanafi tradition, although many listed here are Shafi'i, indicating the cross-madhhab references typical in later Hanafi fatwa works.

Navigating the Scholars of Rasm al-Mufti
Biographical index of scholars cited in Rasm al-Mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major Hanafi and other madhhab scholars listed in the biographical index of Rasm al-Mufti and explain their significance in Hanafi fatwa methodology.

Core Idea
Knowing the scholars is key to understanding the authority chain of a fatwa.

This lesson introduces the biographical index found in the commentary on Rasm al-Mufti. The index lists scholars from various Islamic legal schools, with a focus on Hanafi authorities. Understanding who these scholars are and their place in the Hanafi tradition is crucial for correctly weighing their opinions in fatwa work.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the index's purpose
The index provides a quick reference to scholars cited in the main text. Note that it includes both Hanafi and non-Hanafi scholars, reflecting the comparative nature of fatwa methodology.
2
Identify core Hanafi authorities
Focus on frequently mentioned Hanafi names like Sahib al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani), al-Quduri, and al-Kasani. These are central to Hanafi fatwa practice.
3
Note non-Hanafi inclusions
Scholars like Ibn Hajar al-Haytami (Shafi'i) and al-Baji (Maliki) appear for reference or rebuttal. Their inclusion does not imply equal authority in Hanafi fatwa.
4
Trace the chain of authority
The index helps you understand the hierarchy: Zahir al-Riwayah authorities (e.g., al-Marghinani) have higher weight than later commentators. This is key for tarjih (preference) in fatwa.
5
Apply when reading the text
Whenever a ruling is cited, check the index to determine the scholar's school and authority level. This will guide you in applying the correct fatwa hierarchy.

Understanding the Biographical Index of Hanafi Scholars
Biographical index of scholars and works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify key Hanafi scholars from the biographical index and state their major works, using the list to trace the authority of rulings.

Core Idea
Knowing the scholars and their works is essential to evaluate the strength of a fatwa.

This passage is a reference tool: a list of Hanafi (and some non-Hanafi) scholars whose names appear in the book, along with their key texts. In the Hanafi tradition, the chain of scholarship (riwayah) matters—knowing who authored a foundational text like al-Hidayah or who commented on it helps you understand the weight of a ruling. This lesson will help you navigate such biographical indexes so you can quickly locate the authority behind a legal opinion.

Guided Path
1
Parse the entry structure
Each entry typically begins with a scholar's name, followed by a title like 'sahib' (author of), 'sharih' (commentator), or 'talmidh' (student). Page numbers in the book are also given. For example, 'Ali ibn Abi Bakr al-Farghani al-Marghinani (sahib al-Hidayah) 142' tells you that he wrote al-Hidayah and is referenced on page 142.
These page numbers refer to where in the book the scholar is discussed.
2
Identify the scholar's madhhab and role
The list includes Hanafis (most) and occasionally other madhhabs (e.g., Ibn Hajar al-Haytami al-Shafi'i). Note the titles: 'Imam', 'Allama', 'Qadi', etc. A scholar like 'Shams al-A'imma' indicates a high rank. This helps you gauge the authority of the source.
For example, 'Fakhr al-Islam' means 'Pride of Islam'—a distinguished title.
3
Link the scholar to his major work
Focus on the book title after 'sahib'. For instance, 'sahib al-Hidayah' points to al-Hidayah, a core text of Hanafi fiqh. 'sahib al-Kanz' refers to Kanz al-Daqaiq. Memorizing these associations helps you quickly recall which text a ruling comes from.
Some scholars author multiple works; the list mentions only one key text.
4
Understand the biographical context
The introduction says these persons are mentioned in the book with introductions in footnotes. So this index is a quick way to find background info. In your own studies, you can use such indexes to trace the chain of teachers and students (e.g., 'talmidh al-Imam Muhammad' shows a direct student link).
Chain continuity is important in Hanafi fiqh to verify transmission.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical and bibliographical listing of Hanafi scholars and their works, with particular emphasis on those whose writings are central to the fatwa process. The first section lists notable figures, including Abu al-Walid ibn al-Shihnah al-Kabir, a commentator of al-Hidayah, and Imam Ali Razi, a student of Hasan ibn Ziyad. It also mentions scholars from other madhahib, like the Shafi'i al-Dhahabi and the Maliki Ibn Abd al-Barr, indicating the breadth of reference. The second section corrects common errors in book titles: for example, 'al-Durr al-Mukhtar' (an Arabic descriptive compound) and 'Dar Mukhtar' (Persian descriptive) are correct, while 'Dar al-Mukhtar' (idafa compound) is wrong. The author also notes that 'Radd al-Mukhtar' (with a mu'jam kha') is incorrect, whereas 'Radd al-Muhtar' is proper. This attention to nomenclature is crucial for muftis to cite texts accurately in their fatwas. The list serves as a catalogue of authoritative sources in the Hanafi tradition, guiding scholars toward reliable references.

Key Terms
الدر المختار (al-Durr al-Mukhtar): A Hanafi fiqh text by Haskafi; the correct title is an Arabic descriptive compound, not an idafa.
رد المحتار (Radd al-Muhtar): The famous gloss by Ibn Abidin on al-Durr al-Mukhtar; the correct spelling uses a 'ha' not a 'kha'.
مركب توصيفي (murakkab tawseefi): An Arabic descriptive compound consisting of two nouns in apposition (e.g., al-Durr al-Mukhtar).

Always verify the Arabic compound structure of Hanafi reference works before citing them; the correct forms are descriptive (al-Durr al-Mukhtar) not possessive (Dar al-Mukhtar).

Commentary
This passage is a biographical and bibliographical listing of Hanafi scholars and their works, with particular emphasis on those whose writings are central to the fatwa process. The first section lists notable figures, including Abu al-Walid ibn al-Shihnah al-Kabir, a commentator of al-Hidayah, and Imam Ali Razi, a student of Hasan ibn Ziyad. It also mentions scholars from other madhahib, like the Shafi'i al-Dhahabi and the Maliki Ibn Abd al-Barr, indicating the breadth of reference. The second section corrects common errors in book titles: for example, 'al-Durr al-Mukhtar' (an Arabic descriptive compound) and 'Dar Mukhtar' (Persian descriptive) are correct, while 'Dar al-Mukhtar' (idafa compound) is wrong. The author also notes that 'Radd al-Mukhtar' (with a mu'jam kha') is incorrect, whereas 'Radd al-Muhtar' is proper. This attention to nomenclature is crucial for muftis to cite texts accurately in their fatwas. The list serves as a catalogue of authoritative sources in the Hanafi tradition, guiding scholars toward reliable references.

Key Terms
تلميذ إمام أعظم (tilmidh al-Imam al-A'zam): Student of the Great Imam (Abu Hanifa), indicating direct transmission of the school.
صاحبين (al-sahibayn): The two companions: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the main disciples of Abu Hanifa.
الهداية (al-Hidayah): A foundational Hanafi fiqh text by Al-Marghinani, widely commented upon.
