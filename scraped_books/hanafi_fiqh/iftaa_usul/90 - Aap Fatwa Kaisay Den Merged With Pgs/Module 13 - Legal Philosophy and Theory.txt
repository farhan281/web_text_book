Custom and Change in Fatwa Rulings
Explaining when and how custom overrides explicit rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that custom (urf) can override explicit rulings (musarrah ahkam) from Zahir al-Riwayah when the underlying custom has changed.

Core Idea
A fatwa ruling built on custom changes when the custom changes, even if the earlier ruling was explicit.

In the Hanafi school, many rulings from the early imams were based on the customs of their time. When those customs change, the rulings may change too. This lesson explores the basis for such change and the conditions under which a mufti may depart from explicit texts in Zahir al-Riwayah. The passage from 'Aap Fatwa Kaisay Den' provides the foundation for this dynamic and context-sensitive approach to fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
Read the passage and extract the central idea: rulings that were based on custom (urf) can change when the custom changes. The mufti may then issue a new ruling that aligns with the new custom.
Look for the sentence: 'احکام کی تبدیلی کی بنیاد یا ضرورت ہے یا عرف و عادت'.
2
Examine the examples
The passage gives two examples: (21) bathing in a public bath without specifying duration or water amount, and (22) borrowing dough or bread without weighing. These rulings were originally based on custom. How does the principle apply to them?
These are practical cases where the original explicit ruling was relaxed due to a change in custom.
3
Understand the justification
The author argues that if Imam Abu Hanifa or his immediate students were alive in the new time, they would have given the same changed rulings. This shows that the change is not an abandonment of the madhhab but a faithful application of its methodology.
The principle is rooted in the idea that the founder's intention was tied to the circumstances of his time.
4
Note the conditions for the mufti
The passage stresses that the mufti must be insightful (dhi ra'y), know Shari'ah principles, and distinguish valid from invalid custom. He must also be aware of the custom of his time and have trained under an expert scholar.
This prevents arbitrary departure from texts; only a qualified mufti can apply this principle.
5
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Think of a modern example where a custom has changed (e.g., types of currency, business practices). Would a mufti be justified in issuing a fatwa that differs from an explicit ruling in Zahir al-Riwayah? Why or why not?
Use the conditions from step 4 to evaluate.

Commentary
This passage from 'Aap Fatwa Kaisay Den' discusses the foundational principle that legal rulings may change with changing customs and circumstances, and it explains the mufti's role in applying this principle. The author first gives examples: (21) permitting bathing in a public bath without specifying duration or water quantity—this is based on the custom that such details are left open. (22) permitting borrowing of dough or bread without weighing—again based on custom. These issues and many others are mentioned in al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. The author then makes a critical point: all these changed rulings are within Hanafi fiqh because if Imam Abu Hanifa or his immediate students were living in the new era, they would have given the same rulings. The change in ruling is based either on necessity (darurah), custom and habit (urf wa adah), or circumstantial evidence (qara'in al-ahwal). This is not an abandonment of the madhhab but a proper application of its methodology. The author then states that this understanding gave later Hanafi mujtahids (mujtahidin fi al-madhhab) and insightful later scholars (mutakhkhirin) the courage to oppose explicit rulings (musarrah ahkam) found in Zahir al-Riwayah texts. Why? Because the founder (sahib al-madhhab) based those rulings on the conditions of his own time. For example, Imam Muhammad's ruling on 'every isthmus [؟]' was based on the custom of his era. Similarly, the permissibility of ijarah for Quran teaching was based on custom. Then follows a question: if custom changes repeatedly, can a mufti oppose explicit texts and follow new custom? The answer is yes—because the later scholars only opposed explicit rulings when a new custom arose after the imam's time. The author then clarifies the scope: the mufti must follow custom in customary wording (alfaz urfiyyah) and in rulings based on the mujtahid's custom. Moreover, the mufti must be insightful (dhi ra'y), have correct thinking, and be familiar with Shari'ah principles to distinguish between valid and invalid custom. Since full ijtihad is absent, the mufti must at least know the issues with their conditions and restrictions. He must also know the custom of his time and have trained under an expert teacher. The passage cites Madinat al-Mufti stating that even memorizing all imam's books requires apprenticeship for fatwa, because many answers are based on the lawful habits of the time. Finally, al-Qunyah states that it is not permissible for a mufti or judge to decide according to Zahir al-Riwayah while ignoring custom. This shows the authority of custom within the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
عرف (urf): Custom or prevailing practice of the people that is not opposed to Shari'ah and can form the basis of legal rulings.
مصرح احکام (musarrah ahkam): Explicit rulings found in the core Hanafi texts (Zahir al-Riwayah) that were based on the customs of the early imams' time.
ظاہر روایت (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six primary books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani that form the authoritative core of the Hanafi school.
مفتی (mufti): A qualified jurist who issues fatwas (legal opinions) based on the Hanafi school.
