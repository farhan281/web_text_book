Prohibition of Following Desires and the Obligation of Tarjih
Ruling on following desires in fatwa and the duty of tarjih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why following desires in fatwa is prohibited by consensus and state the obligations of a mufti to perform tarjih.

Core Idea
Following desire in fatwa is haram by consensus; a mufti must weigh evidence and cannot pick any opinion arbitrarily.

This lesson establishes the foundational ethical principle for muftis: the absolute prohibition of following personal desire when issuing fatwas. The text draws on multiple authorities, including Qasim bin Qutlubugha and Ibn al-Salah, to emphasize that tarjih (preference) is not optional but obligatory. Even when evidence seems contradictory, a mufti must seek a preponderant factor; if none exists, consensus allows choosing any valid opinion without following desire.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Identify what is being prohibited: giving a fatwa following personal desire. The text states it is haram by consensus.
This is the key ruling of the passage.
2
Locate the evidence (dalil)
The text cites ijma (consensus) as the evidence for the prohibition. No specific Quranic verse or hadith is mentioned; the consensus is the proof.
Ijma is a primary source of law.
3
Understand the scope of the prohibition
The prohibition applies to all muftis, including mujtahid fil-madhhab. For a mujtahid muqallid, if tarjih is impossible, consensus allows picking any opinion without desire.
There is an exception when tarjih is not possible.
4
Analyze the warnings
Ibn al-Salah says that a person who does not perform tarjih and acts on any opinion is ignorant and violates consensus.
This underscores the gravity of the duty.
5
Apply the principle
If a mufti has two contradictory views, he must perform tarjih using accepted criteria. If no preponderance is found, he may choose any view but without desire.
Tarjih criteria include strength of evidence, reliability of narrator, etc.

How do you give a fatwa? Explanation of Rasm al-Mufti. Either act or give a fatwa. Because doing so follows desire, which is unanimously forbidden. And regarding the mujtahid fil-madhhab, the statement of Allamah Qurani applies to the case where there is no contradiction in the texts, or there is contradiction but preference is possible. And if in a matter the evidences are so contradictory that preference is not possible in any way, then for the mujtahid muqallid, it is correct to decide and give a fatwa on any one of the two opinions, by consensus. (The text from Fatawa Kubra ends). And Imam Muhaqqiq Allamah Qasim bin Qutlubugha (10) writes at the beginning of his book Sahih al-Quduri: 'I have seen with my own eyes among the followers of my three Imams people who followed their desires. So much so that I heard from the mouths of some judges: “What harm is there in this?” i.e., taking any opinion. I replied: Yes! Following desire is haram, and in comparison to the preponderant, the outweighed is non-existent, and preference among contradictory sayings without a preponderant factor is impermissible.' And Ibn Sayyid al-Nas al-Muammari (11) wrote in Kitab al-Usul: 'One who does not know which of the two sayings and which of the two reasons is famous, it is not permissible for him to follow his desire and to decide on any saying or reason without reflecting on preference.' And Imam Abu Amr ibn al-Salah (7) writes in Adab al-Mufti: 'Know that whoever does not reflect on preference and is satisfied with just that his fatwa or action conforms to any saying or any reason, and he acts on whichever of the different sayings and reasons he wants, then he is ignorant and violates consensus.' And Allamah al-Baji (8) narrated a story: an incident occurred with him, and the muftis gave a fatwa that was against al-Baji. Then when al-Baji himself asked about the issue, the muftis apologized saying they did not know it was his case. And they gave fatwa on the other narration that agreed with al-Baji. Al-Baji said that doing this is not permissible by the consensus of all respectable Muslims. And in Usul al-Aqdiya it says: The difference between a mufti and a judge is only that the mufti informs of the ruling and the judge enforces it. (Here the text of Allamah Qasim bin Qutlubugha ends.) After that, Allamah Qasim (10) narrated: 'Judging or giving a fatwa according to a outweighed narration is against consensus. Note: If there is no preference among the different sayings, its ruling will come later in the explanation of verse no. 66.' Tabaqat al-Fuqaha: I have added the condition of 'qualified for preference' in the second hemistich of the ninth verse. This indicates that the preference of any scholar is not reliable (rather, only those jurists who have importance in preference, their preference is reliable). Allamah Shams al-Din Ahmad bin Sulayman, famous as Ibn Kamal Pasha (12), wrote in his treatise: 'For the muqallid mufti, it is necessary to know the condition of the person whose saying he is issuing fatwa upon. And knowing the condition does not mean merely knowing the name, lineage, and national affiliation, for that is completely useless; rather, it is necessary to know what his status is in narrating issues and what his rank is in understanding the evidences of issues. And which class of the jurists he belongs to. By knowing these things, the mufti will have complete insight and will be able to distinguish among jurists with different views, and will have sufficient ability to give preference to one saying among contradictory sayings. Therefore, below we mention the classes of jurists.' About Usul al-Aqdiya, it could not be known whose book it is. Students often confuse the titles of three jurists: Kamal, Ibn al-Kamal, and Akmal. By Kamal, Allamah Ibn al-Humam, author of Fath al-Qadir, is meant. By Ibn al-Kamal, Allamah Ibn Kamal Pasha (14) is meant. By Akmal, Allamah Akmal al-Din al-Babarti, author of al-Inayah (2), is meant.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ethical and legal obligations of a mufti, particularly concerning the prohibition of following desires and the necessity of proper tarjih (preference) in contradictory rulings. The text opens with a statement that giving fatwa based on desire is unanimously forbidden, as it leads to following whims. It then clarifies that a mujtahid fil-madhhab (a jurist capable of preference within the madhhab) may only give fatwa if either there is no conflict in the texts or if a preponderant factor exists. If the evidences are so conflicting that no preference is possible, then it is permissible to choose any opinion, a point established by consensus. The author then cites Qasim bin Qutlubugha, who reports observing people in the Hanafi madhhab following desires; he strongly rebukes that, stating that a marjuh (outweighed) opinion is legally non-existent. In the absence of a preponderant factor, preference is invalid. Ibn al-Salah is quoted to emphasize that those who choose any opinion without reflection are ignorant and violate scholarly consensus. The anecdote about al-Baji illustrates that muftis must avoid partisanship; switching to a different narration to favor a person is not permissible. The passage then introduces the distinction between mufti and qadi: both convey rulings but the qadi enforces them. It also highlights the importance of knowing the tabaqat (classes) of jurists. A muqallid mufti must know the rank and reliability of the jurist he is quoting to correctly assess preference. The author explains that he added the condition of 'qualified for preference' in his poetic text. Finally, he clarifies common confusion among three titles: Kamal (Ibn al-Humam), Ibn al-Kamal (Ibn Kamal Pasha), and Akmal (Akmal al-Din al-Babarti).

Key Terms
اتباع الهوى (itba‘ al-hawa): Following one's personal desire in issuing a fatwa, which is prohibited by consensus.
ترجیح (tarjih): The process of weighing conflicting evidences or sayings to determine which is preponderant (rajih) and thus binding for fatwa.
مرجوح (marjuh): The outweighed opinion that is not acted upon; it is considered legally non-existent (ka-l-'adam) when a rajih exists.
مجتهد مقلد (mujtahid muqallid): A jurist who does not reach the level of absolute ijtihad but is capable of tarjih within his madhhab (mujtahid fil-madhhab).
طبقات فقهاء (tabaqat al-fuqaha'): The levels or classes of jurists according to their knowledge, ability in narration, and understanding of evidence.

The Mufti's Ethical Obligations: Impartiality and Knowledge of Tabaqat
Ethical obligations of mufti: impartiality, knowledge of tabaqat, and the mufti-qadi distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a mufti must be impartial and knowledgeable about the tabaqat of jurists, and state the distinction between a mufti and a qadi.

Core Idea
A mufti must be impartial and know the scholarly hierarchy to correctly weigh opinions and avoid favoritism.

This lesson continues the adab al-mufti by highlighting two additional ethical duties: impartiality (exemplified by the al-Baji story) and the necessity of knowing the scholarly hierarchy (tabaqat). The text also clarifies the difference between a mufti and a qadi, and stresses that acting upon a marjuh (outweighed) opinion violates consensus. Finally, the author explains his own addition to the poetic text regarding the condition of 'qualified for preference'.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the al-Baji story
Read the story: muftis initially gave a fatwa against al-Baji. When they discovered it was his case, they changed to a different narration that favored him. al-Baji said this is not permissible by consensus.
The ethical violation is favoritism based on personal connection.
2
Distinguish mufti and qadi
The text from Usul al-Aqdiya states: a mufti informs of the ruling, a qadi enforces it. Both must avoid desire, but the mufti's role is advisory.
The distinction affects responsibility but both are bound by ethical rules.
3
Apply the marjuh rule
Judging or giving fatwa according to a marjuh narration is against consensus. The outweighed opinion is not to be acted upon.
This reinforces the need for tarjih.
4
Understand the requirement of tabaqat
A muqallid mufti must know the class of the jurist he quotes – not just name and lineage, but his rank in narration and understanding of evidence.
Tabaqat knowledge enables proper tarjih.
5
Note the author's expansion
The author added a condition in the ninth verse: 'qualified for preference'. This means only those jurists who are qualified to perform tarjih have their preference considered.
This restricts who can do tarjih.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ethical and legal obligations of a mufti, particularly concerning the prohibition of following desires and the necessity of proper tarjih (preference) in contradictory rulings. The text opens with a statement that giving fatwa based on desire is unanimously forbidden, as it leads to following whims. It then clarifies that a mujtahid fil-madhhab (a jurist capable of preference within the madhhab) may only give fatwa if either there is no conflict in the texts or if a preponderant factor exists. If the evidences are so conflicting that no preference is possible, then it is permissible to choose any opinion, a point established by consensus. The author then cites Qasim bin Qutlubugha, who reports observing people in the Hanafi madhhab following desires; he strongly rebukes that, stating that a marjuh (outweighed) opinion is legally non-existent. In the absence of a preponderant factor, preference is invalid. Ibn al-Salah is quoted to emphasize that those who choose any opinion without reflection are ignorant and violate scholarly consensus. The anecdote about al-Baji illustrates that muftis must avoid partisanship; switching to a different narration to favor a person is not permissible. The passage then introduces the distinction between mufti and qadi: both convey rulings but the qadi enforces them. It also highlights the importance of knowing the tabaqat (classes) of jurists. A muqallid mufti must know the rank and reliability of the jurist he is quoting to correctly assess preference. The author explains that he added the condition of 'qualified for preference' in his poetic text. Finally, he clarifies common confusion among three titles: Kamal (Ibn al-Humam), Ibn al-Kamal (Ibn Kamal Pasha), and Akmal (Akmal al-Din al-Babarti).

Key Terms
اتباع الهوى (itba‘ al-hawa): Following one's personal desire in issuing a fatwa, which is prohibited by consensus.
ترجیح (tarjih): The process of weighing conflicting evidences or sayings to determine which is preponderant (rajih) and thus binding for fatwa.
مرجوح (marjuh): The outweighed opinion that is not acted upon; it is considered legally non-existent (ka-l-'adam) when a rajih exists.
مجتهد مقلد (mujtahid muqallid): A jurist who does not reach the level of absolute ijtihad but is capable of tarjih within his madhhab (mujtahid fil-madhhab).
طبقات فقهاء (tabaqat al-fuqaha'): The levels or classes of jurists according to their knowledge, ability in narration, and understanding of evidence.

It is not permissible to give fatwa by looking at a single book or from unclear books: I (Allamah Shami) say—when it became known that following the rajih qawl among disputed statements is obligatory and the condition of those who give tarjih is known, then it is necessary to know that those fatwas have no validity which many muftis of our time give by looking at one of the books written in later times. Especially, giving fatwa from unclear books is not correct. Examples: Qahistani's Sharh al-Nuqayah (29), Allamah Haskafi's al-Durr al-Mukhtar (30), Ibn Nujaym al-Misri's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir (31), and other similar books. Because these books, due to excessive brevity, have become like riddles. Also, in these books, in many places, some words have been omitted when quoting from other books, and they have given preference to non-prevalent (ghayr ra'ij) statements. Rather, they have even given preference to statements from other madhhabs for which there is no proponent in the madhhab.
Weak books:
Allamah Muhammad [Hastah Allah?] writes in the beginning of his commentary on al-Ashbah (31): 'The weak books regarding fatwa are: Sharh al-Kanz by Mulla Sikin (32), Sharh al-Nuqayah by Qahistani (29), because the condition of the authors of these two books is unknown. All the books of the author of al-Qunyah (33), because he transmits weak statements in his books. Allamah Haskafi's al-Durr al-Mukhtar (30), Allamah Umar ibn Nujaym's Sharh al-Kanz al-Nabr al-Fa'iq (34), Allamah Ayni's Sharh al-Kanz Ramz al-Haqa'iq (35). The last three books, due to their brevity, are not worthy of being included among mufta bih books.' Shaykh Salih al-Jinini (36) says: 'It is not permissible to give fatwa from the mentioned books, unless one knows the source (manqul anhu), i.e., knows their references.' Ba'li (37) says that he heard similarly from him, and he was a famous scholar of fiqh, and he bears responsibility for what he said. (The text of Sharh al-Ashbah ends.)
Benefit:

And Mawla Barkali (37) also counted Sharh al-Siraj al-Wahhaj on al-Quduri (38) among the weak and unreliable books. (Kashf al-Zunun vol. 2 p. 1631)

Commentary
This passage is a methodological instruction for muftis regarding the sources they should rely on. It begins with a quote from Allamah Khayr al-Din al-Ramli emphasizing that distinguishing between rajih and marjuh, and between qawi and da'if, is the highest goal of a fiqh student. It is obligatory (fard) for a mufti or judge to investigate thoroughly before giving a response. They must not give casual or uninformed answers. Declaring halal as haram or vice versa is a grave sin. Then Ibn Abidin (Allamah Shami) states that it is not permissible to give fatwa by looking at a single book, especially unclear ones written in later times. He lists examples: Qahistani's commentary on al-Nuqayah, al-Durr al-Mukhtar by Haskafi, and al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir by Ibn Nujaym. These books are extremely brief, have omissions in quotations, and sometimes give preference to non-prevalent or even inter-madhhab statements that have no supporter in the Hanafi madhhab. Next, a list of 'weak books' is cited from a commentary on al-Ashbah: Sharh al-Kanz by Mulla Sikin and Sharh al-Nuqayah by Qahistani (authors unknown), all books of the author of al-Qunyah (who transmits weak statements), al-Durr al-Mukhtar, al-Nabr al-Fa'iq, and Ramz al-Haqa'iq (the last three due to brevity). Shaykh Salih al-Jinini says that fatwa from these books is impermissible unless one verifies their references. Ba'li confirms. Ibn Abidin then adds that Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir is also unsuitable because of its extreme brevity leading to misunderstanding. Mawla Barkali includes Sharh al-Siraj al-Wahhaj on al-Quduri as weak. Finally, the passage stresses the necessity of verifying references, because errors can be transmitted through successive books. Examples follow on later pages. This passage teaches that a mufti must not take shortcuts; he must know the hierarchy of sources, use well-known reliable books, and always check the original references.

Key Terms
قوی (qawi): Strong or well-supported opinion within the madhhab, opposite of da'if.
ضعیف (da'if): Weak opinion, either due to chain of transmission, textual problems, or being outweighed by stronger evidence.
غیر واضح کتابیں (ghayr wadih kutub): Books that are unclear due to excessive brevity, omitted words, or inclusion of non-rajih statements, making them unreliable for fatwa without cross-referencing.
فتوی (fatwa): A non-binding legal opinion issued by a qualified mufti in response to a specific question.

Unreliable Sources for Fatwa: Weak and Unclear Books
Catalogue of weak and unclear books for fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify specific books considered weak or unclear for fatwa and explain why they are unsuitable without verification.

Core Idea
Even widely used mukhtasar works may be weak or unclear; a mufti must know their status and verify references.

This lesson continues Ibn Abidin's warning by listing specific books that later Hanafi scholars deemed weak or unclear for fatwa. We will examine why these books are problematic—whether due to the author's unknown status, transmission of weak opinions, extreme brevity, or inclusion of non-madhhab views. The goal is to equip you with practical source criticism skills.

Guided Path
1
List the books explicitly called 'weak'
From the commentary on al-Ashbah, identify the books considered weak and note the reason given for each.
Reasons include unknown author condition, weak statements, brevity.
2
Understand the 'unclear books' category
Ibn Abidin mentions Qahistani, al-Durr al-Mukhtar, and al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir as unclear. What common problems do they share?
Excessive brevity, omitted words, frequency of non-rajih opinions.
3
Note the condition for using such books
Shaykh Salih al-Jinini says fatwa from these books is impermissible unless one knows their sources. What does this imply for a mufti's methodology?
He must trace the statement back to its original source.
4
Recognise the danger of transmitted errors
Ibn Abidin warns that errors can propagate through later books if the original mistake is repeated. Why does this require verifying references?
Even a widely quoted statement may have originated from a mistake.
5
Apply the lesson: evaluate a hypothetical source
Given a list of books, determine which are reliable for fatwa and which require caution based on the criteria in this passage.
Use the categories: weak, unclear, reliable.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ijarah for acts of worship like Quran recitation is invalid unless necessity (darura) genuinely requires it.

In this passage, the author tackles a common practice in many Muslim communities: hiring someone to recite Quran for the deceased. He argues that such ijarah is generally invalid because it turns worship into a transaction and nullifies reward, except when necessary to preserve religious practice. Understanding this ruling is crucial for a mufti advising on contemporary practices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
What is the practice being evaluated? It is paying someone to recite Quran for the purpose of isal-e-thawab (sending reward to the deceased).
2
State the General Rule
What is the default Hanafi ruling on ijarah for acts of worship? It is invalid (batil) because acts of worship require sincerity (ikhlas) and compensation corrupts it.
This is the position of early authorities like Qadi Khan and Ibn al-Humam.
3

Under what condition did the later Hanafi scholars (muta'akhkhirin) permit ijarah? They permitted it for necessity (darura), specifically to prevent loss of religious practice (e.g., teaching Quran).
The exception is conditional and not automatic.
4
Analyze the Misapplication
Why does the author argue that mere tilawah (recitation without teaching) does not qualify? Because there is no genuine necessity—if no one hires for recitation, no harm occurs. The practice leads to riya and other evils.
The author cites that the muta'akhkhirin themselves restricted the exception to cases of necessity.
5
Apply the Fatwa
What should a mufti rule about the common practice? It is impermissible (haram) and does not convey reward. The mufti must warn against the associated evils.
The mufta bihi position follows the restricted view, not the loose interpretation of muta'akhkhirin.

Correcting the Fatwa on Rahn Guarantee: Hanafi vs Maliki
Inter-madhhab dispute with fatwa correction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct Hanafi ruling on mortgagee liability for destroyed pawned property and distinguish it from the Maliki view, applying the principle of tarjih when conflicting fatwas exist.

Core Idea
Mortgagee liability in rahn is only for the lesser of value and debt — not full value, and witnesses are irrelevant.

This passage presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute over a specific fatwa rule: the liability (daman) of a pawned property (rahn) when it is destroyed. The author, Allamah al-Shami (Ibn Abidin), corrects a common error among some later Hanafi scholars who inadvertently adopted the Maliki position. As a mufti, you must be able to navigate conflicting fatwas and identify the correct transmitted ruling of the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
First, extract the Maliki position and the corrected Hanafi position from the text. The Maliki view restricts liability to hidden wealth and only when no witnesses are present. The Hanafi view imposes liability for the lesser of the pledge's value and the debt, regardless of witnesses.
Note that the Hanafi ruling is unconditional on the type of wealth or presence of witnesses.
2
Recognize the mistaken attribution
The author points out that some later Hanafi scholars (like Tamartashi and Khayr al-Din al-Ramli) gave fatwa following the Maliki view. This is a fatwa error (ghalat al-fatwa) that needs correction.
This highlights the importance of verifying madhhab from original sources.
3
Understand the correction
Ibn Abidin clarifies the correct Hanafi ruling from reliable books like Haqa'iq al-Manzuma and his own Radd al-Muhtar. The key point is that the mortgagee's liability is limited to the amount necessary to protect the debtor's interest, not a full guarantee.
The correction relies on the principle that the pledge is a trust (amanah) in the mortgagee's hands but with a security interest.
4
Apply the ruling
As a mufti, when asked about a destroyed pledge without witnesses, you must rule that the mortgagee owes only the difference between the pledge's value and the debt (if any). This is the mufta bihi position.
The practical consequence saves the mortgagee from undue burden.

Commentary
This passage presents a specific fatwa rule: the liability (daman) of a pawned property (rahn) when it is destroyed. The author first states the Maliki position: liability only for hidden wealth (like dirhams, dinars) and only when the mortgagee cannot produce witnesses. According to Maliki, the property is considered in trust, so no liability if witnesses are present. Then he presents the mistaken attribution: some later Hanafi works (like Tamartashi and some fatwas) adopted the Maliki view, implying guarantee for the full value regardless of witnesses. But al-Shami corrects this: the true Hanafi position is that the mortgagee is only liable for the amount by which the value of the pledge exceeds the debt (or vice versa, i.e., the lesser), and this holds irrespective of witnesses because the pledge is subject to the debt as a trust becomes a guarantee when the mortgagee has exclusive possession. The correct ruling is from the Hanafi school as transmitted in the reliable books, not the Maliki view. This serves as a methodological lesson for the mufti: to verify the madhhab from original sources, not rely on occasional fatwas.

On Carelessness in Quoting and the Need for Verification
Critical discussion on scholarly errors and proper methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the causes of scholarly error in later Hanafi works, apply Ibn Abidin's verification method to assess a fatwa's reliability, and construct a fatwa that accounts for the need to cross-check sources.

Core Idea
Later Hanafi works are not infallible; a mufti must verify every quote against the original sources of earlier scholars.

In this passage, Imam Ibn Abidin (al-Shami) exposes a critical oversight: even the most respected later scholars sometimes made mistakes when quoting earlier authorities. He blames this on tasamuh (carelessness) and sahw (inadvertence) in copying texts. To safeguard the integrity of fatwa, he describes his own rigorous method of verification—a model every mufti must emulate. This lesson teaches you to recognize the limits of later works and to apply a critical eye to every reference you encounter.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the prevalence of errors in later works
Ibn Abidin states that even major later scholars like the authors of al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Fatwa Khayriyya have committed errors (tasamuhat). Understand that these are not isolated incidents but occur repeatedly across many texts.
Key term: tasamuh – a slip in copying or quoting that changes the meaning.
2
Identify the cause of carelessness
The cause is often a simple slip of the eye when copying (sahw al-nazar), resulting in omitted or misplaced phrases that distort the intended ruling. This is not intentional distortion but a human error in transmission.
OCRd text: 'سبقت نظر' – the eye moving ahead or jumping.
3
Apply the verification method of Ibn Abidin
Ibn Abidin, in his Hashiya Radd al-Muhtar, did not rely solely on later texts. He consulted the original books of the earlier scholars (mutaqaddimin) to which those later authors attributed their rulings. He would quote the original text, highlight the error, and add supporting references.
This method ensures that the fatwa is based on the authentic source, not on a mistaken copy.
4
Understand the akthari qa'ida principle
Some muftis assume that later scholars, being more comprehensive, only wrote correct and predominant opinions (qa'ida akthariyya). Ibn Abidin refutes this: it is not a universal principle (qa'ida kulliyya). Counterexamples exist, as he demonstrates with his own waqf case where even the great al-Haskafi was confused.
A mufti must never presume infallibility; verification is an ongoing duty.
5
Integrate this lesson into fatwa practice
When giving fatwa, always trace a ruling back to its earliest authoritative source. If you find a discrepancy between a later text and an earlier one, investigate. Ibn Abidin's hashya is a prime tool for this, but the principle applies to any source.
This builds a culture of scholarly accountability and precision.

The Adab of Titles for the Mufti: Avoiding Exaggerated Honorifics
Discussion of exaggerated titles among later Hanafi jurists and their prohibition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify exaggerated titles (e.g., Muhyi al-Din) and explain why they are considered prohibited self-praise (tazkiyah) in Hanafi mufti adab.

Core Idea
Using exaggerated titles like 'Muhyi al-Din' constitutes prohibited self-praise (tazkiyah).

This lesson examines the historical trend of honorific titles among Hanafi jurists, contrasting the simple attributions of early Iraqi scholars with the grandiose titles adopted later in Khurasan and Transoxiana. We will explore the legal basis for prohibiting such titles, rooted in the Quranic and Prophetic prohibition of self-purification (tazkiyah), and understand the practical implications for a mufti's conduct.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the historical contrast
Read the passage's account of early Iraqi jurists (al-Jassas, al-Quduri) who used simple occupational or locational titles, versus later Eastern jurists who adopted exalted titles like Shams al-Aimmah and Muhyi al-Din. Note the temporal and geographical shift.
The author al-Lakhnawi praises the simplicity of earlier scholars.
2
Identify the legal basis
Consider the quotation from al-Qurtubi, who states that using self-praising titles falls under the prohibition of tazkiyah (self-purification) established from Quran and Hadith. This is the core proof (dalil) for the ruling.
The prohibition is general; applying it to titles is an elaboration by later scholars.
3
Apply the ruling to specific titles
From the list of titles like Zaki al-Din, Muhyi al-Din, and Alam al-Din, note that they include attributes that belong properly to Allah alone (e.g., al-Muhyi is a divine name). Such titles imply self-praise and are therefore problematic.
Muhyi al-Din literally means 'Reviver of the Faith', which mimics a divine attribute.
4
Consider the mufti's role
As a mufti, one should avoid using such titles for oneself or for others in fatwas and public discourse. The mufti's conduct must reflect humility and adherence to the Shari'ah, including prohibitions against self-praise.
This falls under adab al-mufti (etiquette of the mufti) and is part of upholding scholarly integrity.

The Ruling on Exaggerated Titles for Scholars
Evaluating the permissibility of grandiose titles in Islamic scholarship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on exaggerated titles for scholars and apply the qualification based on worthiness and intent.

Exaggerated titles are permissible as customary names but blameworthy if used for self-praise or by unworthy bearers.

In Islamic scholarship, the use of grandiose titles became common in later centuries. This lesson examines the ethical and legal ruling on such titles, drawing from Hanafi and other sources to provide a nuanced position. We will explore the historical context, the basis of prohibition, and the crucial qualification that determines permissibility.

Rule for muftis on resolving conflicting narrations by preferring the stronger dalil.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When imams' narrations conflict, the mufti weighs the underlying evidence, not the number of narrations.

In Hanafi fatwa methodology, a mufti often encounters multiple narrations from the founding imams on a single issue. The sixth rule of fatwa teaches that one should not simply count narrations but examine the strength of the proofs behind each. This lesson illustrates the rule with the example of whether tuma'ninah (composure) in prayer is sunnah or wajib.

Terms for Tas-hih and Their Hierarchy
Lists terms used to indicate correction of an opinion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchy of tas-hih terms in Hanafi texts and apply the rule that the corrected opinion is mu'tamad.

Core Idea
When two opinions exist, the one marked with a stronger tas-hih term is the mu'tamad for fatwa.

The Hanafi madhhab is vast, and early texts sometimes record multiple opinions. Later scholars developed a system of 'tas-hih'—correcting and identifying the relied-upon view. This passage introduces the key terms used to indicate that an opinion has been selected as authoritative, and ranks them by strength. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for a mufti navigating the Hanafi corpus.

Guided Path
1
State the qawida
The passage opens with a rule: wherever two opinions exist and one is corrected (suhhiha), that corrected opinion is the mu'tamad (relied-upon one). Memorize this maxim.
This qawida is the foundation; the rest of the passage defines what 'corrected' means.
2
List the tas-hih terms
Identify the terms listed: al-fatwa 'alayhi, bi hadha al-ashbah, hadha al-azhar, hadha al-mukhtar, hadha wa la wujah, sahih, ashah, bihi yufya. Write them down in Arabic.
Some terms may have variant readings; focus on the standard list.
3
Understand the hierarchy
The passage explicitly states that 'bihi yufya' and 'al-fatwa 'alayhi' are the strongest. Among the others, there is a dispute whether 'ashah' is stronger than 'sahih' or vice versa. Note the relative weakness of terms like 'ashbah' and 'azhar'.
For practical fatwa, the strongest terms always override weaker ones.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given two opinions in a Hanafi text, one described with 'al-fatwa 'alayhi' and the other with 'hadha al-mukhtar', which is mu'tamad? Answer: the one with the stronger term ('al-fatwa 'alayhi').
This is the direct application of the hierarchy.

Commentary

It then lists the various phrases used in Hanafi works to indicate such correction, along with their hierarchy. The Role of Custom and Necessity in Changing Hanafi Rulings
Listing examples of rulings changed by custom and necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the basis for later Hanafi jurists' deviation from explicit rulings (musarrah ahkam) and list at least three examples where custom or necessity caused a change in the operative ruling.

Core Idea
Custom and necessity allow later Hanafi jurists to change explicit rulings while remaining within the madhhab.

In the Hanafi school, explicit rulings in Zahir al-Riwayah carry great weight. However, later jurists recognized that the founders based many rulings on the custom and circumstances of their time. When custom changed or necessity arose, later scholars had the courage to adopt different positions—while still operating within the Hanafi tradition. This passage from Sharh Rasm al-Mufti lists numerous examples and defends this practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the tension between explicit rulings and later practice
Note that the passage lists cases where later Hanafi jurists (mutakhkhirin) departed from the explicit rulings (musarrah ahkam) of the founders (mujtahidun fi al-madhhab). Understand that this is not a rejection of the madhhab but an application of its principles to new circumstances.
The founders themselves would have ruled similarly if they were in the later context.
2
Categorize the justifications: custom, necessity, and circumstantial evidence
The passage explicitly states that the basis for change is either necessity (darurah), custom and habit ('urf wa 'adah), or circumstantial evidence (qara'in al-ahwal). For each example in the list, determine which justification applies.
For instance, 'eating without weighing bread' is based on widespread custom (ta'mul).
3
Analyze a specific example: deferred mahr
In example (13), the woman's claim about deferred dower is accepted, contrary to the normal rule that the denier's word is taken. The justification is custom that the woman often knows the amount better. Trace how custom overrides a procedural general principle.
This shows that 'urf can even affect evidentiary rules.
4
Examine the ruling on pre-emption (shuf'ah)
Imam Muhammad limited the pre-emptor's right to one month to protect the buyer (example 15). This is a change based on necessity (removing harm). Note that this was a new ruling not found in earlier texts, yet it became part of the madhhab.
The justification is a specific necessity (daf' al-darar).
5
Synthesize the principle: permissibility of following a new custom
The passage concludes with a clear qa'idah: custom and necessity justify opposing explicit earlier rulings. In the Q&A, the author affirms that a mufti may follow a new custom after the founders' time. This becomes a fundamental tool for fatwa in changing circumstances.
This is not arbitrary; the new custom must be genuine and not conflict with a clear nass.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh Rasm al-Mufti discusses the legitimate role of custom ('urf) and necessity (darurah) in the Hanafi school for modifying or even overriding explicit rulings (musarrah ahkam) found in the foundational texts of Zahir al-Riwayah. The author lists a series of twenty-two examples (items 3-22) from various chapters of fiqh where later Hanafi jurists (mutakhkhirin) deviated from the apparent general rules or from the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa or Imam Muhammad in favor of the opinions of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) or other rulings, justified by changing customs or widespread need. For instance, in matters of deferred dower, the woman's word is accepted contrary to the normal rule that the denier's word is taken; in muzara'ah and waqf, the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad is chosen due to necessity; Imam Muhammad limited the pre-emption right to one month to protect the buyer; Hasan ibn Ziyad reported that a woman's marriage without a proper guardian or to a non-equivalent is invalid; later scholars disregarded the obligatory removal of mud from the road out of necessity; bay' al-wafa (a sale with an agreement to repurchase) was permitted as a legal stratagem (hilah) despite its resemblance to riba; istisna' (manufacturing contract) was permitted even though it involves selling a non-existent item; uncharted amounts of water and unspecified duration in hammams were permitted; borrowing unweighed dough and bread was permitted. The author then explains that all these changes are based on either necessity, custom, or circumstantial evidence (qara'in al-ahwal), and they remain within Hanafi fiqh because if the founders were alive in the present time, they would issue the same rulings given the changed circumstances. The passage concludes with a direct question and answer: it is permissible for a mufti to follow a new custom and oppose explicit earlier rulings, as evidenced by the practice of the later scholars.

Concealing a Ruling to Prevent Oppression: The Case of Land Tax
Dispute about concealing a land tax ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on withholding a ruling when its disclosure would lead to harm, and evaluate the dispute over concealing the land tax ruling.

Core Idea
Concealing a ruling is permitted if its disclosure enables oppression, even if the ruling itself is wajib.

The source passage presents a controversial case: a landowner who could grow a valuable crop but grows a cheap one is liable for higher tax. The Hanafi scholars advised not to give this ruling as a fatwa, fearing it would be exploited by oppressive rulers. This lesson examines the dispute that arose over concealing a wajib ruling, and the principled reasoning that led to the position of concealment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in question
Locate the specific ruling: 'If the owner of land, despite being able to cultivate a superior crop, grows an inferior crop, then the tax for the superior cultivation will be obligatory upon him.' Note that this ruling is considered wajib.
2
Understand the Hanafi recommendation
The scholars said this issue should only be known, not given as a fatwa. Why? To prevent oppressive rulers from demanding higher taxes unjustly.
3
Analyze the objection
Some scholars objected: 'How is it permissible to conceal a ruling? The rulers are justified because they collect what is wajib.' This challenges the concealment on the grounds that hiding a wajib is impermissible.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
The response: if we give this fatwa, every oppressor will claim that any land is capable of superior cultivation (e.g., saffron) and demand the higher tax, causing widespread oppression. The harm outweighs the benefit.
The response relies on the principle of preventing harm (dar' al-mafasid muqaddam).
5
Conclude the tarjih
The preferred position is to not give fatwa on this issue. Ibn al-Humam and others support this, as the danger of oppression is real and the remedy difficult.

A Qadi Cannot Judge by a Weak Opinion or Another Madhhab
Ruling restricting a qadi's authority to specific opinions within his madhhab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the binding rule for a qadi in issuing a judgment (hukm) regarding which opinion he must follow, distinguishing between the obligations of a mujtahid and a muqallid.

Core Idea
A judge must follow only the preponderant opinion in his own ijtihad or the well-known opinion of his madhhab — personal desire is prohibited.

The role of a qadi (judge) is to deliver justice based on Islamic law. However, within a madhhab, not all opinions carry equal weight. This passage lays down a foundational rule from rasm al-mufti: a judge cannot pick a weak opinion (ḍaʿīf qawl) or switch to another madhhab's ruling arbitrarily. Whether he is a mujtahid or a muqallid determines his obligation. Let us explore the precise legal criterion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of judges
Recognize that the passage distinguishes between a mujtahid (capable of independent reasoning) and a muqallid (one who follows an imam). Each has a different binding rule.
2
State the rule for a mujtahid qadi
The passage says a mujtahid must judge only according to the opinion that is preponderant (rajih) in his own view. He cannot follow a weaker opinion of his own ijtihad or another imam's opinion.
3
State the rule for a muqallid qadi
A muqallid must judge according to the well-known (mashhur) opinion of his madhhab, even if it is not preponderant in his personal view. He follows his imam's preference.
4
Recognize the prohibition
Judging by a weak opinion (marjuh) — whether within the madhhab or from another madhhab — is prohibited by consensus (ijma). Following personal desire in judgment is also haram.
This applies to both mujtahid and muqallid; the weak opinion is defined relative to the judge's authority level.
