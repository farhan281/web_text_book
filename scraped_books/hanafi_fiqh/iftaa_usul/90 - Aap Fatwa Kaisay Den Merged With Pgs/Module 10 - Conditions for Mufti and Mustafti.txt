Mortgagor's Liability for Claiming Destruction Without Evidence – A Rasm al-Mufti Rule
Stating a rule from Mulla Khusrau on mortgagor liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that a mortgagor who claims destruction of the pawn without evidence is liable, and identify the authority weight of this ruling in the fatwa hierarchy.

Core Idea
A mortgagor claiming destruction without proof is automatically liable—the trustee becomes the guarantor.

This passage from Mulla Khusrau's Durar al-Hukkam establishes a clear rule for muftis: when a mortgagor (the person who pawned an item) asserts that the pawned item has been destroyed without presenting any witnesses, he becomes liable to pay its value to the mortgagee. This rule cuts through the default assumption of trusteeship, placing the burden of proof squarely on the claimant. Understanding this ruling is essential for issuing correct fatwas in matters of pawn (rahn).

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and context
Recognize the two parties: the mortgagor (debtor who pawns an item) and the mortgagee (creditor who holds the pawn). The pawn is in the possession of the mortgagor as a trust (amanah).
The ruling applies specifically to the mortgagor's claim of destruction.
2
Memorize the exact legal wording
The core text from Mulla Khusrau: 'وضمن المرتهن بدعوى الهلاك بلا بينة' – meaning the mortgagor is liable for the claim of destruction without evidence.
This phrase is a fatwa anchor for quick recall.
3
Understand the condition and exception
The liability is triggered only when there is no evidence (bayyinah). If the mortgagor provides witnesses or other proof, he is not liable.
The rule does not require proof of negligence; the mere unsubstantiated claim creates liability.
4
Apply the ruling in a fatwa scenario
When a mufti hears a case where the mortgagor claims destruction without proof, the fatwa is that he must pay the value of the pawn to the mortgagee.
This is a strict liability rule specific to pawn transactions.
5
Locate the ruling in the Hanafi fatwa hierarchy
Identify this ruling as part of the zahir al-riwayah (clear transmission) from Mulla Khusrau's work, which is a standard manual for muftis. It is considered strong and operative.
The authority_weight is 'zahir_riwayah'.

Commentary
This passage provides a fourth example from the commentary on Rasm al-Mufti, giving a specific rule from Hanafi fiqh regarding pawn (rahn). The ruling is taken from Mulla Khusrau's Durar al-Hukkam and Ibn Malik's commentary. The rule: if a mortgagor (the debtor who pawned an item) claims that the pawned item has been destroyed (lost or damaged) without presenting any witnesses, he is automatically liable to pay the value of the item to the mortgagee (creditor). This is a clear rule that a mufti can apply when a mortgagor asserts loss of the pawn without corroboration. Fifth Rule: Necessity of Fatwa According to Zahir al-Riwayah
Rule explaining that fatwa must be from Zahir al-Riwayah, with exceptions for shadhdh riwaya when authorized.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that fatwa must be based on Zahir al-Riwayah and identify when a shadhdh riwaya may be used.

Core Idea
Fatwa is only valid from Zahir al-Riwayah; shadhdh narrations are rejected unless jurists explicitly approve.

This lesson introduces the fifth rule of fatwa methodology: the mufti must base his rulings on the authentic, well-known transmissions (Zahir al-Riwayah) rather than on isolated or rare narrations. This rule maintains consistency and reliability in the Hanafi fatwa tradition. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for any mufti or student of ifta'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Recognize that the text establishes a binding rule: fatwa must be based on Zahir al-Riwayah.
Note the exceptions.
2
Understand the scope
Note that the rule applies to muqallid judges and muftis. Zahir al-Riwayah is the core corpus.
Shadhdh narrations are outside.
3
Recognize the exception
The text says unless jurists explicitly state that fatwa is on the shadhdh riwaya. Memorize this condition.
Also if issue absent from Zahir al-Riwayah, then other riwaya may be used.
4
Apply the hierarchy
In practice, first check Zahir al-Riwayah. If not found, then check other authentic transmissions, but only if later scholars have accepted them.

And in al-Bahr, Bab Qada al-Fawa'it (vol. 2, p. 82), it states: when an issue is not mentioned in Zahir al-Riwayah and is proven in another riwaya, then referring to it is determined.

Commentary
This fifth rule is crucial for mufti methodology: the mufti must base his fatwa on the narrations within Zahir al-Riwayah—the core six books of Imam Muhammad. Sayings outside this corpus are considered 'marju' 'anha' (retracted or abandoned) and cannot be used. The text emphasizes that a muqallid (follower) judge cannot rule by a shadhdh (isolated) riwaya unless the later jurists explicitly authorize it. However, if the issue is absent from Zahir al-Riwayah but present in other reliable narrations, then those may be used. This rule ensures consistency in fatwa and prevents reliance on weak or unverified transmissions.

Key Terms
ظاہر روایت (Zahir al-Riwayah): The well-known, authentic transmissions from Imam Abu Hanifa and his students, primarily the six books of Imam Muhammad.
روایت شاذہ (riwaya shadhdhah): An isolated or less-known narration that is not part of the core transmitted corpus.
مرجوع عنہ (marju' 'anhu): A saying that the imam himself retracted or that was abandoned by later scholars; it is not valid for fatwa.

Using Weak Narrations in Cases of Necessity
Ruling on fatwa using weak narrations under necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition (darurah) that permits a mufti to issue a fatwa based on a weak narration, and apply this rule to a scenario of necessity.

Core Idea
A weak opinion becomes permissible for fatwa when necessity requires ease.

In Hanafi fiqh, the general rule is that weak narrations (da'if qawl) are not to be used for issuing fatwas. However, there are exceptional circumstances. This lesson examines a ruling from al-Bahr al-Raiq that allows a mufti to rely on weak opinions when necessity (darurah) compels ease. Understanding this exception is crucial for a mufti to balance authenticity with practical need.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Recall that the default position prohibits acting on or issuing fatwa based on weak narrations. This is the baseline.
2
Identify the exception condition
The exception is triggered by darurah (necessity). The text specifies 'places of necessity' where ease is needed.
Darurah is defined as a compelling need that would otherwise cause hardship.
3
Note the source of the ruling
The ruling is cited from Fakhr al-Aimmah in Miraj al-Dirayah, as quoted by the author of al-Bahr al-Raiq in the context of menstruation colors.
This is a secondary source; the original ruling is transmitted through reliable later works.
4
Derive the two permissions
The passage extracts two permissions: (1) a compelled person may personally act on a weak opinion, (2) a mufti may give a fatwa to such a person based on that weak opinion.
The mufti's permission is dependent on the person's necessity.
5
Scope and limitation
The exception is narrow: it applies only when there is genuine necessity. The general prohibition remains for non-necessary situations.
This prevents misuse of the exception.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling (rasm) regarding the use of weak narrations in fatwa. The author begins by citing al-Bahr al-Raiq, which quotes Fakhr al-Aimmah from Miraj al-Dirayah: a mufti may give a fatwa based on weak narrations when necessity requires ease. From this, two points are derived. First, a person who is compelled (majbur) may personally act on a weak opinion in his own practice. Second, the mufti may give a fatwa to such a person based on that weak opinion. This clarifies a previous blanket statement that weak opinions are never permissible for action or fatwa. That general rule applies only when there is no necessity. Thus, this is an exception (istithna) to the prohibition, limited to cases of genuine need.

ضرورت (darurah): A situation of compelling need that permits departure from the stronger ruling.
مفتی (mufti): A jurist qualified to issue fatwas, here specifically one who can apply weak opinions in necessity.

Caution in Takfir with Weak Narrations
Ruling against issuing takfir based on disputed weak narrations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm al-mufti rule that a mufti must avoid declaring takfir when even a weak narration offers a differing opinion.

Core Idea
Never issue a fatwa of kufr if there is any scholarly disagreement, even from a weak narration.

This passage presents a critical rasm al-mufti principle: when issuing a fatwa regarding disbelief (kufr), the mufti must exercise extreme caution. Even if the majority or stronger opinion leans toward takfir, the presence of a weak narration that suggests otherwise compels the mufti to refrain. The gravity of takfir overrides the usual preference for the strongest evidence. Understanding this rule safeguards the inviolability of a Muslim's life, honour, and faith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
Read the passage and locate the core prohibition: a mufti must not issue a fatwa of kufr when there is ikhtilaf (disagreement) about whether the act constitutes kufr, even if that disagreement arises from a weak narration.
The rule is unconditional: any ikhtilaf, regardless of its source, blocks takfir.
2
Understand the Talil (Rationale)
The passage provides the reason: the scholars have deliberately departed from the strong opinion because kufr is an extremely serious matter. This shows that in Hanafi iftaa, harm prevention (dar') and caution (ihtiyat) take precedence over strict evidentiary strength when the consequence is massive harm.
Compare with general fiqh rules: normally a mufti follows the strongest riwayah, but here the rule is reversed.
3
Apply the Rule to a Scenario
Imagine a Muslim makes a statement that some scholars consider kufr, but a weak narration suggests it is not. According to this rule, the mufti must adopt the opinion that avoids takfir, even if the majority strong opinion holds it as kufr.
This is the operative ruling (mufta bihi).
