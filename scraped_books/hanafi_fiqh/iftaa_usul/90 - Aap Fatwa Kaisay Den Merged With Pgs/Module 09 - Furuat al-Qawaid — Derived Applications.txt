Applying 'Al-Ma'rufu Ka al-Mashroot' to Quran Recitation Gifts
Explaining the ruling on wages for Quran recitation and invalidating a common stratagem

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the legal maxim 'al-ma'rufu ka al-mashroot' to determine the ruling on customary payments for Quran recitation and evaluate the validity of a hila to circumvent the prohibition.

Core Idea
Customary gifts for Quran recitation are treated as impermissible wages.

In Hanafi fiqh, custom (urf) can elevate a voluntary gift to the status of a wage, especially in transactions. This lesson examines a common scenario: Huffaz receiving customary gifts for reciting Quran in Tarawih. The author applies the maxim 'al-ma'rufu ka al-mashroot' to rule that such gifts are effectively wages and impermissible. He also analyzes a hila (stratagem) people use to circumvent this ruling and explains why it fails unless specific conditions are met.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal maxim
Locate the maxim 'al-ma'rufu ka al-mashroot' in the source. Understand its meaning: customary practice in transactions is equivalent to an explicit condition.
This is a sughra (minor) qawida from the Hanafi qawaid system.
2
Apply the maxim to the concrete case
The author applies the maxim to the case of Huffaz receiving customary gifts for reciting Quran in Tarawih. Because giving and receiving is customary, the gift is legally classified as a wage (ujrah), making it impermissible due to the prohibition of taking wages for Quran recitation.
3
Analyze the hila and its conditions
Some people attempt a hila by assigning one or two prayers to the Hafiz so the payment appears as a wage for prayer. The author states this hila is only valid if: (1) the salary is fixed, and (2) the recitation of Quran is not a condition. Show why these conditions are usually absent in practice.
If the hila fails, the payment remains a wage for Quran recitation and is impermissible.
4
Evaluate the objection of harming the system
The author refutes the argument that disallowing these gifts would harm Tarawih or Quran memorization. He argues that Tarawih can be done with short surahs and that children memorize well without payment. The gift itself can corrupt the purpose of memorization.
This step requires distinguishing between the ruling and its potential consequences.
5
Identify the fatwa hierarchy and note the retraction
The passage mentions that Hazrat Thanawi (quddisa sirruhu) retracted a previous fatwa of permissibility. Note that the mufta bihi position is impermissibility, and later fatawa from Darul Uloom Deoband confirm this. Understand the importance of tarjih in iftaa.
The retraction (ruju') is a sign of scholarly integrity and shows that fatwa positions can evolve.

The Fourth Rule of Mufti Methodology: Preference of Istihsan over Qiyas
Explanation of the fourth rule preferring istihsan over qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that istihsan is preferred over qiyas, identify its exceptions as noted by classical Hanafi scholars, and apply the correct understanding of tarjih when issuing fatwas.

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides qiyas—but with specific exceptions where qiyas prevails.

This lesson explains a foundational rule for muftis: when istihsan (juristic preference) and qiyas (analogy) conflict, istihsan is given preference. However, the rule is not absolute; there are exceptions. Additionally, scholars differed on what 'tarjih' (preference) means—does it exclude acting on qiyas entirely, or simply indicate that istihsan is better? Understanding this rule and its nuances is essential for correct fatwa issuance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
State the fourth rule clearly: when both qiyas and istihsan apply to a matter, preference is given to istihsan over qiyas.
This is the default principle in Hanafi methodology.
2
Recognize the exceptions
Note that the rule has exceptions. Classical scholars differed on the count: al-Natafi mentioned 11 cases, while al-Nasafi expanded it to 22. These exceptional cases are where qiyas is followed instead of istihsan.
The exact list is detailed in works like al-Natafi's Ajnas.
3
Analyze the meaning of tarjih
Understand that 'tarjih' in this context has two interpretations. Ibn Nujaim holds that tarjih means only the preferred evidence (istihsan) may be acted upon; the marjuh (qiyas) is not permissible. Al-Bazdawi, however, suggests that tarjih simply means preference, and acting on the marjuh remains permissible, though inferior.
The practical difference is significant for fatwa: can a mufti ever issue a ruling based on qiyas when istihsan is present, even outside the exceptions?
4
Apply in fatwa practice
When issuing a fatwa, first determine if the case falls under the exceptional cases. If yes, qiyas may be applied. If not, apply istihsan. Additionally, be aware of the tarjih dispute: the majority view (including later Hanafis) leans toward Ibn Nujaim's position that the marjuh is not to be acted upon.
Always check authoritative fatwa collections for the exceptional cases.

The Maxim: Middle Opinion Not Preferred and Its Refinement
Defines and refines a maxim for opinion selection in fatwā texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'the middle opinion is not preferred' correctly by identifying the two exceptions: when the author's habit is known and when proofs are mentioned.

Core Idea
The middle opinion is not preferred unless the author's custom or clear proofs guide otherwise.

In Hanafi iftāʾ literature, you will often encounter multiple opinions on a single issue. A well-known maxim from Imam al-Nasafī advises that when three opinions are listed, the middle one holds no preference. However, this rule is not absolute. The great later jurist Ibn ʿĀbidīn (al-Shāmī) added crucial conditions that prevent mechanical application. This lesson unpacks that maxim and its nuance, giving you a precise tool for navigating fatwā references.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim statement
Read the original maxim from Imam al-Nasafī: when three opinions are cited, the preferred is either first or last; the middle is not preferred. Understand that this is a default rule.
The maxim appears in the book 'Al-Istasfī' and is cited by Ibn ʿĀbidīn.
2
Recognize the refinement by Ibn ʿĀbidīn
Ibn ʿĀbidīn adds a key condition: the default applies only when (a) the author's customary method (ʿādah) is unknown, and (b) no proofs (adillah) are given. Otherwise, the rule is overridden.
3
Apply the first exception: known author habit
If the author's habit is known—for example, in Fatawā al-Khāniyyah or Fatawā al-Muttaqī—follow that habit. Do not apply the default rule.
This aligns with the maxim 'al-ʿādah muḥakkamah' (custom is authoritative).
4
Apply the second exception: proofs are mentioned
If the author provides proofs alongside the opinions, prefer the last opinion mentioned. This is because proofs are considered, and the last opinion is typically the final verdict.
Related maxim: 'al-qawl al-mudallal muqaddam' (the supported opinion is preferred).
5
Summarize the operative rule
When reading a fatwā collection: first check if the author's habit is known; if yes, follow that. If not, check if proofs are given; if yes, take the last opinion. Only when both are absent do you fall back on the default (first or last, not middle).

Rule: Opinion with Proof is Preferred
Defines a rule for preferring evidenced opinions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida that a supported opinion is preferred when weighing multiple opinions in fatwa, and explain the role of mentioning the illah in tarjih.

Core Idea
An opinion with explicit proof outweighs one without.

When a mufti encounters multiple views on a matter, how does he decide? This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi rule: the opinion that comes with its proof (dalil) is given preference. It also shows that mentioning the legal cause (illah) similarly strengthens a ruling. This principle helps muftis navigate conflicting opinions systematically.

Guided Path
1
State the qawida
Read the Arabic rule: 'جو قول مدلل کیا گیا ہو وہی راج ہے' – the opinion accompanied by proof is the preferred one.
This is the core maxim.
2
Identify the source
Locate the source: Attributed to Allamah Khayr al-Din al-Ramli in Fatawa Khayriyya, and similar principle from al-Tahrir and al-Taqrir.
Understanding the chain of authority helps assess its weight in Hanafi iftaa.
3
Understand the expansion
Analyze the parallel principle: a ruling whose illah (cause) is mentioned is more prevalent than one without, because the illah indicates importance.
Mentioning the illah is a form of implicit proof.
4
Apply to a scenario
Take two opinions on a sale contract: one cites a hadith, the other does not. Apply the rule: the one with proof is preferred.
This demonstrates the practical use.
5
Recognize limits
Note the dabt: if both opinions have proofs, further tarjih methods are needed. The rule only applies when one side lacks evidence.
Avoid overgeneralization.

The reasoning is that mentioning the cause (illah) signals that the ruling is important and worthy of adoption. This is a common method in Hanafi fiqh: providing the rationale (ta'lil) strengthens the authority of a ruling. This rule helps muftis determine which view to follow in the absence of explicit preference.

مدلل (mudallal): An opinion that is accompanied by its proof (dalil) in the text.
علت (illah): The legal cause or rationale behind a ruling, mentioned to indicate its strength.

Rulings Change with Changing Customs: Examples from Later Hanafi Fatwas
Multi-example expansion of the maxim on custom-based ruling change

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-hukm yataghayyar bi taghayyur al-urf' to identify when later Hanafi fatwas depart from earlier rulings due to changing custom and justify the shift using the concept of fasad al-zaman.

Core Idea
Hanafi fatwas evolve with custom - later scholars changed rulings when society changed.

This passage from Rasm al-Mufti demonstrates a core principle of Hanafi ifta: rulings are not static but adapt to changing circumstances, especially when custom ('urf) or the moral condition of the age (fasad al-zaman) shifts. The author lists over a dozen examples where later Hanafi scholars gave fatwas contrary to the explicit statements of Imam Abu Hanifa, showing the dynamic nature of the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its justification
Recognise the core principle that rulings can change when custom or circumstances change, either due to fasad al-zaman (corruption of the age) or general necessity.
This maxim is a wusta qa'ida governing many fiqh chapters.
2
Identify the original ruling and the changed ruling for each example
For each of the 11 examples, note what Imam Abu Hanifa (or the zahir al-riwayah) originally held and what the later scholars (mutakhkhirin) held as the operative fatwa.
The examples span ikrah, jinayat, ijarah, ghasb, qada, nikah, talaq, and mahr.
3
Analyse the reason for change
Determine whether the change was due to a shift in custom ('urf) or due to fasad al-zaman. Note the textual clues: some examples explicitly say 'due to corruption of the time'.
The underlying basis is that the earlier ruling was based on the custom of that era.
4
Distinguish between legitimate change and overstepping
Note that custom cannot override a definitive text (nass). The istithna is that rulings based on nass remain unchanged. This keeps the change within the usul framework.
The related qa'ida is 'al-nusus la tunsakh bi al-'urf'.

Explanation of Rasm al-Mufti
Rulings change with changing customs: After this, it should be known that many of those rulings which the mujtahid of the school clearly expressed based on his own custom and the conditions of his time have changed due to the change of time. The change of time occurs either due to the appearance of corruption among people or due to a general need arising, as we have previously mentioned that the later scholars gave fatwa on the permissibility of ijarah for teaching the Quran, and the later scholars gave fatwa that apparent probity in witnesses is not sufficient. Although these two matters are contrary to the explicit statement of Imam Abu Hanifa. And from the same category of issues are: (1) The verification of coercion from other than the king (government). Although this is contrary to the statement of Imam Sahib. Imam Sahib's statement was based on the conditions of his time, that coercion from other than the king was not possible. Then corruption increased and coercion began to occur from other than the sultan, so Imam Muhammad accepted it, and the later scholars gave fatwa on Imam Muhammad's statement. (2) Making an informer to the government liable for damages, although this is contrary to the school's maxim: 'Liability is on the direct doer, not on the one who is a cause, but...' The later scholars, due to the corruption of the time, gave a fatwa on the liability of the informer. Rather, in times of civil strife, they even gave a fatwa on his killing. No. (3) Making a common hireling (like a washerman, tailor, etc.) liable for loss. (4) The jurists' statement that now in this time, it is permissible for a guardian (wasi) to engage in mudarabah with the orphan's wealth. (5) The later scholars' fatwa that the usurper of an orphan's and waqf property will be held liable. (6) And from this category is the fatwa that if the property of an orphan and waqf consists of houses, then renting them out for more than one year, and if it is rural property, then for more than three years, is not permissible. Although these two matters are contrary to the original school: in number 5, the original school is that liability is not obligatory, and in number 4, non-specification of a duration for ijarah. (7) The later scholars' prevention of the judge from adjudicating according to his own knowledge. (8) The later scholars' fatwa that a husband cannot move his wife to a distant place, even if he has given her the entire prompt dower (mu'ajjal); this fatwa is due to the corruption of the time. (9) The later scholars' fatwa that without testimony, the husband's statement that he said 'In sha Allah' after swearing an oath of divorce for his wife will not be accepted, although this fatwa is contrary to the zahir al-riwayah, and the later scholars attributed the reason for their fatwa to the corruption of the time. (10) The later scholars' fatwa that after love has occurred, the woman's statement that she did not receive that dower which was conditioned in the marriage contract will not be accepted, although the woman is the trustee of receiving the dower. And the school's principle is: 'The trustee's statement is accepted.' But custom is that a woman does not surrender herself without receiving the prompt dower. (11) And from this category is the later scholars' statement that if the husband says 'Everything permissible is forbidden to me' (kullu hillin 'alayya haram), then based on custom, his wife will be divorced. The jurists of Balkh said that Imam Muhammad's statement that 'divorce does not occur without intention' was based on the custom of their region, and in our custom, people intend by this statement to make their wife forbidden. Therefore, it will be interpreted accordingly. This statement of the Mashayikh of Balkh was narrated by Allamah Qasim, and he narrated from Mukhtarat al-Nawazil that fatwa is given on this because in custom this is the common usage. Then Allamah Qasim said that in our region, among the words used to make a wife forbidden, a few are: (1) al-talaq yalzamuni (divorce is binding on me), (2) al-haram yalzamuni (the forbidden is binding on me).

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the maxim 'Rulings change with changing customs and times' with numerous examples from later Hanafi fatwas. The author begins by restating the principle that many rulings pronounced by the founding mujtahid (Imam Abu Hanifa) were based on the custom and circumstances of his era. When those circumstances change (due to moral corruption or general necessity), later scholars (mutakhkhirin) gave fatwas that depart from the original explicit ruling. The author then lists eleven examples, showing how each deviates from the original Hanafi position. For instance, regarding coercion (ikrah), Imam Abu Hanifa held that coercion could only be effective from a ruler, but when corruption spread, Imam Muhammad recognized coercion from others, and later scholars followed him. Similarly, making informers liable violates the maxim that only the direct doer is liable, but due to corruption, later scholars imposed liability. Other examples include making common hirelings liable, allowing mudarabah with orphan's property, restricting leases of waqf property, preventing judges from using personal knowledge, restricting a husband's right to move his wife, and interpreting ambiguous divorce oaths according to prevalent custom. The passage concludes with the specific case of the phrase 'kullu hillin 'alayya haram' where the scholars of Balkh argued that Imam Muhammad's earlier ruling (that divorce does not occur without intention) was based on their custom, but in their own time, custom indicates an intent to divorce, so the fatwa changes accordingly. This passage illustrates the dynamic nature of Hanafi fiqh and the importance of considering 'urf in issuing fatwas.

Key Terms
عرف ('urf): Custom, prevalent practice of the people that is not opposed to Islamic law; a basis for rulings in the absence of nass
فساد الزمان (fasad al-zaman): Corruption of the age; moral decay leading to changes in legal rulings to prevent harm or abuse
مشاخ بلخ (Mashayikh Balkh): The scholars of Balkh (region in Central Asia) known for ihterpreting divorce oaths according to local custom
اجیر مشترک (ajir mushtarak): Common hireling, such as a washerman, tailor, or courier, who works for multiple people and is not dedicated to one employer
ضمان بدلنے کا قاعدہ (qa'idat al-daman): The maxim that 'liability is on the direct doer, not the cause' (al-daman 'ala al-mubashir la 'ala al-mutasabbib)
