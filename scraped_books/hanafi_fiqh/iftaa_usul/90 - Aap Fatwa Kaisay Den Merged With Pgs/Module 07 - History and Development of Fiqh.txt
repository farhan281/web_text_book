The Original Rule: Ijarah for Acts of Worship is Invalid
Historical statement of the original Hanafi principle on ijarah for worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the original Hanafi ruling on ijarah for acts of worship and identify the authority (the three imams) on which it is based.

Core Idea
Hiring for worship is invalid in the Hanafi school – worship is purely for Allah, not for wages.

This passage begins by establishing the foundational ruling of the Hanafi school: ijarah (hiring) for acts of worship is invalid. This ruling is attributed unanimously to the three founding imams – Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this principle is essential before exploring the exceptions that later scholars introduced due to necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is ijarah for acts of worship (ta'at). The ruling is bāṭil (invalid).
2
Recognize the source of authority
The passage states it is 'unanimously narrated from our three Imams', indicating a strong consensus (ijma) among the early Hanafi masters.
The three imams are Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
Understand the underlying rationale
Acts of worship are for Allah alone; they cannot be bought or sold. This is a core principle in Hanafi fiqh that prevents commodification of religious duties.
This rationale is not explicit in the passage but is the basis for the ruling.

The impermissibility of ijarah for acts of worship and for hiring Qur'an recitation for conveying reward: It is unanimously narrated from our three Imams (may Allah have mercy on them) that ijarah for acts of worship is invalid. But later mujtahids, who were among the people of takhrij and tas'hih (extraction and correction), gave a fatwa for the permissibility of ijarah for teaching Qur'an due to necessity. Because teachers of the Qur'an used to receive stipends from the government, which were later discontinued. So if ijarah and salary for teaching the Qur'an were declared impermissible, the Qur'an would be lost. For teachers of the Qur'an would be preoccupied with earning a livelihood, and the loss of the Qur'an is the loss of the religion. Similarly, later scholars, who were equivalent to the people of tarjih and tashih, gave fatwa on the permissibility of ijarah for adhan and imamah, because these two are also religious symbols, so based on necessity, taking a salary was declared permissible. The view of the absolute permissibility of ijarah for acts of worship is that of 'Allamah Ibn Nujaym and 'Alaqi. See Rasa'il Ibn 'Abidin.

Commentary
This passage explains the development of the Hanafi ruling on ijarah for acts of worship. The original position of the three founding imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) is that ijarah for acts of worship (طاعات) is invalid. However, later mujtahids issued fatwas permitting ijarah for teaching the Qur'an due to necessity (ضرورت), because the government stipends for teachers had stopped, and without payment, Qur'anic education would collapse. This was extended to adhan and imamah as religious symbols. The author notes that the view of absolute permissibility (for all acts of worship) is held by Ibn Nujaym and 'Alaqi, but this is not the relied-upon view. The passage illustrates how necessity (darurah) can lead to a change in fatwa while the original ruling remains in principle.

ضرورت (darurah): Necessity that justifies an exception to the general rule, here preserving Qur'anic education.
اجارہ (ijarah): Hiring for wages; here hiring someone to perform a religious act.

Necessity and Change: Permissibility for Teaching Quran, Adhan, and Imamah
Explanation of the exception to the original ruling due to necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why later Hanafi scholars permitted ijarah for teaching Quran, adhan, and imamah, and apply the concept of darurah (necessity) to evaluate whether a new scenario qualifies for an exception.

Core Idea
Necessity (darurah) justifies exceptions: the original ban on ijarah for worship was relaxed to preserve the Quran and religious symbols.

While the original Hanafi ruling prohibits ijarah for acts of worship, later mujtahids issued fatwas permitting it for teaching Quran, adhan, and imamah due to necessity. This lesson examines the reasoning behind these exceptions and the principles that govern when necessity can override a general ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the nature of the exception
The passage lists three specific acts: teaching Quran, adhan (call to prayer), and imamah (leading prayer). These are acts that were originally under the general prohibition of ijarah for ta'at.
2
Understand the cause (sabab) for the exception
The cause is necessity (darurah): the discontinuation of government stipends for Quran teachers threatened the preservation of Quranic knowledge.
Darurah is a recognized principle in Hanafi law that permits otherwise prohibited actions to prevent greater harm.
3
Identify the governing principle
The preservation of the Quran is considered the preservation of the religion itself (dīn). Therefore, any means necessary to protect it become permissible.
This ties into the concept of maqāṣid al-sharīʿah (higher objectives of the law).
4
Note the limits of the exception
The passage limits the exception to these three acts due to necessity. The view of absolute permissibility (Ibn Nujaym) is mentioned but not the relied-upon position.

The Zahir al-Riwayah: Core Corpus of Hanafi Fatwa
Describing the major compilations of Imam Muhammad and their status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah and explain their authoritative status for issuing fatwas in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Zahir al-Riwayah books are the most authoritative Hanafi sources; fatwas are primarily based on them.

The Hanafi school rests on a hierarchy of texts. At the top are the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah compiled by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding which texts belong to this category is essential for any mufti, because these are the primary sources for issuing fatwas. This lesson introduces you to the core corpus and its significance.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the term 'Zahir al-Riwayah'
Zahir al-Riwayah refers to the six books of Imam Muhammad that were transmitted by his students with reliable chains and contain the most authoritative rulings of the school.
The term distinguishes these from the Nawadir (rare transmissions).
2
List the six books
From the passage, identify the books mentioned that belong to this category: al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat, al-Ziyadat al-Ziyadat, Kitab al-Asl (al-Mabsut), and al-Jami' al-Saghir (implied). Also recall the others: al-Siyar al-Saghir and al-Ziyadat are part of the six.
The six are: al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, and al-Ziyadat.
3
Understand their authority
These books are considered the most reliable because their transmission is mutawatir and their rulings are well-supported by evidence. They take precedence over later works in fatwa.
Later commentaries and abridgments may reflect the madhhab but cannot override a clear ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Saghir vs. Kabir: Navigating the Difficulty and Authority of Hanafi Texts
Describing the difference between Saghir and Kabir works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'Saghir' and 'Kabir' works by Imam Muhammad and explain how their difficulty relates to their authority in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
In Imam Muhammad's works, 'Saghir' means easier but lesser authority; 'Kabir' means profound and highest authority.

Imam Muhammad wrote multiple books, some with 'Saghir' (small) or 'Kabir' (great) in their titles. The passage gives a telling comparison: al-Siyar al-Saghir was so simple that Imam al-Awza'i dismissed it, while al-Siyar al-Kabir earned his respect. Understanding this distinction helps you gauge the depth and reliability of each text when consulting sources for fatwa.

1

Al-Jami' al-Saghir is not detailed here but is referenced in distinction.
2
Analyze the difficulty indicator
The passage contrasts the ease of the Saghir books with the extreme precision of the Kabir books. It cites Imam al-Awza'i's reaction: he criticized al-Siyar al-Saghir but praised al-Siyar al-Kabir.
This shows that difficulty correlates with depth and legal substance.
3
Relate difficulty to authority
In the Hanafi hierarchy, the Kabir books are part of Zahir al-Riwayah and carry more weight in fatwa. The Saghir books are also part of the corpus but are sometimes simpler summaries, though still authoritative for basic rulings.
The mufta bihi is usually derived from the Kabir versions when available.

Al-Ziyadat and Kitab al-Asl: Supplements and the Encyclopedia of Hanafi Fiqh
Describing al-Ziyadat, al-Ziyadat al-Ziyadat, and Kitab al-Asl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the purpose of al-Ziyadat and al-Ziyadat al-Ziyadat as supplements, and explain the significance of Kitab al-Asl (al-Mabsut) as the most comprehensive Hanafi text.

Core Idea
Al-Ziyadat supplements al-Jami' al-Kabir; Kitab al-Asl is the encyclopedia of Hanafi fiqh from Zahir al-Riwayah.

After completing al-Jami' al-Kabir, Imam Muhammad continued to encounter new issues. He compiled these additions into separate works: al-Ziyadat and further al-Ziyadat al-Ziyadat. Additionally, his magnum opus, Kitab al-Asl (also known as al-Mabsut), stands as the largest compilation of Hanafi fiqh. This lesson helps you understand where these texts fit in the hierarchy and their role in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Ziyadat as a supplement
The passage explains that after revising al-Jami' al-Kabir (second version), further issues led to a separate book called al-Ziyadat. Later, even more details were added in al-Ziyadat al-Ziyadat.
These are considered part of the same chain of transmission.
2
Recognize their transmission status
The text notes that al-Ziyadat are transmitted with a solid chain (tawatur). However, the physical manuscripts are lost, but commentaries exist.
This shows they are from Zahir al-Riwayah despite being supplements.
3
Understand Kitab al-Asl's role
Kitab al-Asl (al-Mabsut) is described as the most important and largest work, an encyclopedia of fiqh. It is the foundation of later Hanafi texts.
It is also part of Zahir al-Riwayah and is referred to as al-Mabsut.

Commentary
This passage is a descriptive introduction (front matter) from the book 'Aap Fatwa Kaisay Den?' It presents a bibliographic overview of the major works of Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani (d. 189 AH), the foremost student of Imam Abu Hanifah and the primary codifier of the Hanafi school. The author explains that al-Jami' al-Kabir is a profound collection of Hanafi rulings with strong proofs, considered miraculous by some. It notes that many great Hanafi scholars wrote commentaries on it, but none have been published except the edited version by Mawlana Abu al-Wafa al-Afghani. An important distinction is made between 'Saghir' (small) and 'Kabir' (great) works: the 'Saghir' books are relatively easy, while the 'Kabir' books are so intricate that even accomplished scholars find them challenging. This is exemplified by Imam al-Awza'i's differing reactions to al-Siyar al-Saghir (which he dismissed) and al-Siyar al-Kabir (which he praised). The passage also discusses al-Ziyadat and al-Ziyadat al-Ziyadat—supplements to al-Jami' al-Kabir—and Kitab al-Asl (also known as al-Mabsut), which is the largest and most comprehensive Hanafi compendium. The treatment of the books' transmission and publication status is also provided: some are printed, some exist only in commentaries, and some are lost. This material is essential for students to understand the authority weight of different Hanafi texts: the 'Zahir al-Riwayah' (the six core books) include al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat, and others, while later commentaries and appendices belong to the Nawadir or Fatawa categories.

Key Terms
زیادات (al-Ziyadat): A supplement to al-Jami' al-Kabir containing additional rulings not included in the original revision.
زیادات الزیادات (al-Ziyadat al-Ziyadat): A further supplement to al-Ziyadat, containing seven chapters of additional rulings. It is a small book and is considered an appendix to al-Ziyadat.
کتاب الاصل (Kitab al-Asl): The largest and most comprehensive work of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, often called al-Mabsut. It is an encyclopedia of Hanafi fiqh.

The Authority Hierarchy: Zahir al-Riwayah vs Nawadir
Defining Nawadir as less widespread transmissions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify Hanafi fiqh sources as Zahir al-Riwayah or Nawadir based on transmission fame, and apply the hierarchy in fatwa.

Core Idea
Zahir al-Riwayah is the primary source; Nawadir are secondary, used only when no ruling exists in the former.

In the Hanafi school, not all transmissions from the early imams carry equal weight. The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani are known as Zahir al-Riwayah because they were transmitted by multiple students, giving their rulings widespread acceptance. In contrast, Nawadir are lesser-known works transmitted by a single student, and they occupy a lower position in the hierarchy. This lesson clarifies the distinction and its practical implications for issuing fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two source categories
From the passage, note that there are two categories: Zahir al-Riwayah (the six famous books) and Nawadir (other books with single transmission).
Focus on the reason for classification: number of transmitters.
2
Understand the basis for hierarchy
The criterion is tawatur (mass transmission). Zahir al-Riwayah is considered mutawatir; Nawadir are ahad (single chain). This affects the level of certainty.
This is a key usul al-fiqh principle applied to riwayah.
3
Apply the hierarchy in fatwa
A mufti must first search Zahir al-Riwayah. Only if no clear ruling is found there should they turn to Nawadir, and even then with caution.
This principle is known as 'al-‘amal bi al-ashhar qabla al-mashhur'.

Commentary
This passage introduces the concept of Nawadir in the Hanafi tradition. The six core books (Zahir al-Riwayah) are widely transmitted by multiple students of Imam Muhammad. In contrast, Nawadir are works that were transmitted by only a single student, hence less widespread and less authoritative. The term also includes compilations from other students of Imam Abu Hanifa aside from Imam Muhammad. The passage clarifies that the classification is based on the number of transmitters and the fame of the rulings. This is a key point in usul al-iftaa: fatwa is primarily based on Zahir al-Riwayah, and Nawadir are used with caution when no clear ruling exists in the main corpus.

Key Terms
نوادر (Nawadir): Fiqh compilations from Imam Muhammad or other early Hanafi scholars transmitted by a single student, thus less widely known.
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six core books transmitted from Imam Muhammad by multiple students, forming the primary source of Hanafi law.

Defining Nawazil as post-mujtahid issues derived by later jurists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Nawazil, distinguish them from Nawadir, and explain their role in Hanafi fatwa methodology.

Core Idea
Nawazil are new issues derived by later Hanafi scholars (Mashayikh) using proofs when no direct ruling exists from the founding imams.

In the previous lesson, we learned about Nawadir—transmissions that are less widely known but still from the early period. Now we turn to a distinct category: Nawazil. These are entirely new issues that arose after the time of the founding mujtahids. Understanding this tier is crucial for a mufti because it defines where to look when a ruling is not found in the core texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify linguistic meaning
First, note that 'Nawazil' is the plural of 'Nazilah', which literally means an incident that occurs. This gives the sense of something new arising.
2
Define terminological meaning
In Hanafi terminology, a Nazilah is a new legal issue that arose after the era of the founding mujtahids (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) and for which no direct ruling is transmitted from them.
This distinguishes Nawazil from issues that were addressed in the early period.
3
Recognize the authority of Nawazil
Nawazil rulings were derived by later scholars (Mashayikh) using proofs, i.e., through ijtihad based on the school's usul. Therefore, they carry authority as the school's position for novel issues.
The authority is lower than Zahir al-Riwayah and Nawadir.
4
Distinguish from Nawadir
Nawadir are also less transmitted but from the same early period; Nawazil are from a later period. A mufti must consult Nawadir before Nawazil.
Common confusion: mixing these two categories.
5
Recall an example
The book 'al-Nawazil' by Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi is a well-known collection. This helps anchor the concept in a concrete source.

Why Imam Muhammad Wrote Al-Siyar al-Kabir
Narrates the story behind writing Al-Siyar al-Kabir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the scholarly motivation behind Imam Muhammad's composition of Al-Siyar al-Kabir and identify the adab (etiquette) demonstrated by Imam Muhammad in responding to criticism.

Core Idea
Imam Muhammad wrote Al-Siyar al-Kabir to answer a Syrian scholar's critique, showing a mufti must respond to valid criticism with thorough research.

This passage recounts a pivotal moment in Hanafi fiqh history: the catalyst for Imam Muhammad's final magnum opus on siyar. Understanding this context reveals the scholarly conduct expected of a great jurist—humility, responsiveness, and depth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key figures and their scholarly contexts
Recognize Imam Muhammad (Iraqi) and Imam al-Awza'i (Syrian). Note their academic reputations and the geographical distance.
Imam al-Awza'i was a respected mujtahid whose school later became extinct.
2
Understand the criticism leveled
Imam al-Awza'i questioned the right of Iraqis to write on military campaigns, claiming they lacked knowledge of battles in Syria and Hijaz.
This criticism targets the scope of Imam Muhammad's earlier work, al-Siyar al-Saghir.
3
Analyze Imam Muhammad's response
Instead of dismissing the criticism or defending himself, he composed a far more comprehensive work, al-Siyar al-Kabir, to fill the knowledge gap.
This response reflects scholarly humility and a commitment to completeness.
4
Extract the adab lesson for a mufti
A mufti should not ignore or retaliate against valid criticism but use it as motivation to deepen his knowledge and produce more rigorous work.
This story exemplifies the ethical principle that a scholar's goal is truth, not victory.
