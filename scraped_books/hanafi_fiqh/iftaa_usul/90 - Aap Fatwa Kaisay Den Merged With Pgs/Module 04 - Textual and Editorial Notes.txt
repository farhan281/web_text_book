Decoding Hanafi Abbreviations: Wiqayah, Ghayah, Nihayah, Riwayah, Dirayah
Explains the meaning of abbreviations Wiqayah, Ghayah, Nihayah, Riwayah, Dirayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the full titles, authors, and significance of the abbreviations Wiqayah, Ghayah, Nihayah, Riwayah, and Dirayah as used in Hanafi fiqh literature.

Core Idea
Know your abbreviations: Wiqayah, Ghayah, Nihayah, Riwayah, Dirayah — each keys to a treasure of Hanafi rulings.

When reading advanced Hanafi texts, you will encounter abbreviations that point to key commentaries and technical terms. This lesson decodes the common ones: Wiqayah, Ghayah, Nihayah, Riwayah, Dirayah. Mastering them will unlock the cross-references in works like Rasm al-Mufti and help you navigate the Hanafi tradition with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the abbreviation and its full title
For each abbreviation (Wiqayah, Ghayah, Nihayah, Riwayah, Dirayah), state the full Arabic title and its English translation as given in the source.
Note that Wiqayah refers to Wiqayat al-Riwayah fi Masa’il al-Hidayah, Ghayah to Ghayat al-Bayan wa Nadirat al-Aqran, Nihayah to al-Nihayah fi Sharh al-Hidayah.
2
Note the author and significance of each work
Identify the author of each work and its role in the Hanafi scholarly tradition. For example, Wiqayah is a matn by Taj al-Shari’ah Mahmud, Ghayah is a commentary on al-Hidayah by Amir Katib, and Nihayah is a commentary by Husam al-Din al-Sighnaqi.
The source also mentions teacher-student relationships to illustrate scholarly chains.
3
Distinguish Riwayah from Dirayah
Understand that Riwayah in this context refers to transmitted issues (masa’il manqulah) from the Imams, while Dirayah means rational proof or reasoning. This distinction is crucial in Hanafi usul.
Do not confuse Riwayah here with hadith transmission; it specifically means transmitted fiqh rulings.

Parsing al-Siyar al-Kabir and Textual Hierarchy in Hanafi Sources
Linguistic parsing and chronological notes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct pronunciation and meaning of 'sir' in 'al-Siyar al-Kabir' and explain the chronological order of Imam Muhammad's major works in terms of authority for fatwa.

Core Idea
In Hanafi scholarship, al-Siyar al-Kabir is the last and most authoritative text on siyar; its title is pronounced 'sir' (plural of sirah), not 'sayr'.

Understanding the exact wording and pronunciation of key Hanafi texts is not pedantry — it directly affects how a mufti cites authority. This passage clarifies a common mispronunciation of al-Siyar al-Kabir and establishes the foundational hierarchy among Imam Muhammad's six works, with al-Mabsut as al-Asl and al-Siyar al-Kabir as the mu'tamad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word 'sir'
Determine the root, form, and meaning. 'Sir' (with kasra on sin and fatha on ya) is the plural of 'sirah' (biography/conduct), not the singular 'sayr' (with fatha on sin and sukun on ya).
The mispronunciation 'al-Sayr al-Kabir' is common but incorrect.
2
Explain the masculine adjective
Although 'sirah' is feminine, the adjective 'kabir' is masculine because 'al-siyar' stands in place of the implied masculine noun 'kitab' (book). The full phrase is 'kitab al-siyar al-kabir'.
Similar to 'salat al-zuhr' where 'al-zuhr' replaces the implied 'waqt'.
3
Recognize the chronological order
Imam Muhammad composed his works in a specific order: al-Mabsut (first, called al-Asl), then al-Jami' al-Saghir, and finally al-Siyar al-Kabir (last).
This order is crucial for determining textual authority.
4
Apply the authority principle
The later work (al-Siyar al-Kabir) is considered the mu'tamad (relied-upon authority) in the Hanafi madhhab, as it benefits from earlier works and points of revision.
However, al-Mabsut remains the foundational text (al-Asl).

Commentary
This passage is a parsing exercise that clarifies the correct grammatical form and meaning of 'sir' in the title 'al-Siyar al-Kabir'. The author explains that 'sir' (with kasra on sin and fatha on ya) is the plural of 'sirah' (biography, way of conduct), not the singular 'sayr' (with fatha and sukun). The phrase 'al-Siyar al-Kabir' is a genitive construction where 'al-siyar' stands in for 'kitab' (book), so the masculine adjective is justified despite 'sirah' being a non-rational plural. The passage then notes that in common usage, the idafa form 'Siyar al-Kabir' is incorrect; the correct form is 'al-Siyar al-Kabir'. Finally, it mentions that al-Mabsut is famously known as 'al-Asl' because it was the first composition among the six books, followed by al-Jami' al-Saghir, and then al-Siyar al-Kabir being the last, hence it is the relied-upon authority. This helps the mufti understand the chronological and authoritative order of Hanafi texts.

Key Terms
السیر الکبیر (al-Siyar al-Kabir): The Great Book of Siyar, one of Imam Muhammad's six core works, dealing with the conduct of war and peace. The word 'sir' is the plural of 'sirah'.
مبسوط (al-Mabsut): Al-Mabsut by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, also known as 'al-Asl' because it is the first composed book of Zahir al-Riwayah.
