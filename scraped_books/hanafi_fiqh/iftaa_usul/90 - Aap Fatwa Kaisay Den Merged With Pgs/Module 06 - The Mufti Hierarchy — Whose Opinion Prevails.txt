Tabaqat al-Fuqaha: First Four Ranks of Hanafi Jurists
Explanation of the necessity of knowing jurists' ranks and enumeration of first four tabaqat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the first four ranks of Hanafi jurists and explain why a mufti must know these ranks to prefer one opinion over another.

Core Idea
Knowing the tabaqa gives the mufti the insight to distinguish and prefer between conflicting opinions.

A mufti cannot simply quote any Hanafi scholar; he must know that scholar's rank in the hierarchy. This lesson from Ibn Kamal Pasha explains why superficial knowledge is useless and enumerates the first four tabaqat, from absolute mujtahids to the ashab al-takhrij. Understanding these ranks equips the mufti with the tools to choose the strongest opinion when views conflict.

Guided Path
1
Understand why rank knowledge is essential
Ibn Kamal Pasha states that knowing a jurist's name and lineage is useless; the mufti must know his standing in narrating and understanding proofs, and his exact tabaqa. This enables him to distinguish among different views and prefer one over another.
The mufti is a muqallid, but he must still have insight into the authority of the opinions he quotes.
2
Define the first rank: Mujtahid Mutlaq
The highest rank: absolute mujtahids who derived law directly from the four proofs without following any other. Examples: the four Imams (Abu Hanifa, Malik, Shafi'i, Ahmad). They established usul al-fiqh and can issue rulings independent of any school.
For the Hanafi mufti, the founding Imam is Abu Hanifa; later absolute mujtahids are not part of the Hanafi school.
3
Define the second and third ranks: Mujtahid Fil-Madhhab and Mujtahid Fil-Masa'il
Second rank: mujtahids within the school (e.g., Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani) who follow the founding Imam's principles but may differ in particulars. Third rank: mujtahids in specific issues (e.g., Tahawi, Karakhi) who cannot change principles or established rulings but can derive rulings for new cases not narrated from the founding Imam.
The difference: Mujtahid Fil-Madhhab can oppose the Imam in minor issues; Mujtahid Fil-Masa'il cannot oppose in principles or branches.
4
Define the fourth rank: Ashab al-Takhrij
These are muqallids who have mastered the school's principles and can derive rulings for new cases using takhrij (extraction) from the school's sources. Example: Bassas Razi. They lack absolute ijtihad but are highly competent in applying the school's usul.
The source mentions non-Hanafi scholars at the end of this section, which may be a textual interpolation; focus on the Hanafi ranks.

And knowing the condition does not mean merely knowing the name, lineage, and geographical affiliation, for that is completely useless. Rather, it is necessary to know his standing in narrating issues and his rank in understanding their proofs, and which class of jurists he belongs to. Knowing these things will give the mufti complete insight, enabling him to distinguish among jurists holding different views and have sufficient ability to prefer one statement among conflicting ones. Those who performed ijtihad in the Shari'ah, like the four Imams (13) and those mujtahids who followed their way. They established the principles of Usul al-Fiqh and derived legal rulings from the four proofs (Qur'an, Hadith, Ijma, and Qiyas) without taqlid of anyone in principles and branches. The second class: ...... that of the mujtahids within the school, like Imam Abu Yusuf (14), Imam Muhammad (15), and other students of Imam A'zam. They are fully capable of deriving rulings from the four proofs in light of the principles and rules set by their teacher. Although they opposed their teacher in some particulars, they follow him in principles. The third class: mujtahids in specific issues. For particulars for which no narration is transmitted from Imam A'zam and his students, they derive rulings by their own ijtihad, such as Khasaf (16), Tahawi (17), Karakhi (18), Tawani (19), Sarkhasi (20), Bazdawi (21), and Qadi Khan (22), etc. They cannot oppose Imam A'zam in principles nor in branches, but they can derive rulings for those particulars about which nothing is narrated from Imam A'zam, keeping in view the principles and rules set by him. They do not have absolute ijtihad, but since they have well-preserved the principles and from their sources, like Sufyan Thawri, Imam Awza'i, Ishaq ibn Rahawayh, Ibn Abi Layla, etc.

Commentary
This passage is a key section on the adab of a mufti, explaining why it is essential for a mufti to know the rank (tabaqa) of the jurist whose opinion he is quoting. Ibn Kamal Pasha emphasizes that superficial knowledge of a scholar's name or birthplace is useless; the mufti must know his competence in narrating and understanding proofs and his exact place in the hierarchy of Hanafi jurists. This enables the mufti to differentiate between conflicting opinions and gives him the ability to choose the correct one. The author then proceeds to enumerate the first four of the seven tabaqat of Hanafi jurists. 1) The first tabaqa: Mujtahid Mutlaq (absolute mujtahids) like the four Imams, who derived law directly from the primary sources without following any other. 2) The second tabaqa: Mujtahid fil-Madhhab (mujtahids within the school) like Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad. They followed the principles of Imam Abu Hanifa but could derive rulings and sometimes differed in particulars. 3) The third tabaqa: Mujtahid fil-Masa'il (mujtahids in specific issues) like Khasaf, Tahawi, etc. They cannot change the principles or established rulings, but they can derive rulings for new cases not narrated from Imam Abu Hanifa using his principles. 4) The fourth tabaqa: Ashab al-Takhrij (jurists capable of deriving rulings from the school's principles) like Bassas Razi. They are muqallids but have mastered the principles and can derive rulings for new cases. The mention of non-Hanafi scholars (Sufyan Thawri, etc.) at the end seems to be a digression or perhaps a misplacement in the text.

Key Terms
طبقہ (tabaqa): A class or rank of jurists based on their level of ijtihad and authority.
مجتہد مطلق (mujtahid mutlaq): One who performs ijtihad independently without following any other mujtahid.
مجتہد فی المذہب (mujtahid fi al-madhhab): One who follows the principles of a specific imam but can derive rulings within that framework.
مجتہد فی المسائل (mujtahid fi al-masa'il): One who can derive rulings for new cases not explicitly covered by the founding imam, using his principles.
اصحاب تخریج (ashab al-takhrij): Jurists who are muqallids but can derive rulings based on the school's principles.

Core Idea
The Sahibayn were absolute mujtahids who voluntarily adhered to Abu Hanifa's school, proving that rank is determined by capability, not choice.

In this passage, the author critiques Ibn Kamal Pasha's classification that placed the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) in the second tier. Maulana Abdul Hayy Lucknawi's response clarifies a key rasm al-mufti principle: a jurist's capacity must be distinguished from the scope of his work. Understanding this distinction is vital for a mufti when weighing opinions from different generations of the school.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of Ibn Kamal Pasha's classification of Hanafi jurists. The author notes that while ulema generally accepted the framework, they disputed the placement of specific scholars. Regarding the Sahibayn (Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani), Ibn Kamal Pasha assigned them to the second tier, but the response by Maulana Abdul Hayy Lucknawi clarifies that they were actually absolute mujtahids (mujtahid mutlaq) who voluntarily adhered to Imam Abu Hanifa's methodology out of respect. Thus they are classified as mujtahid fi al-madhhab. Next, the passage criticizes the downgrading of three early Hanafi jurists (Imams Khassaf, Tahawi, Karkhi) to the third tier, arguing they frequently differed with Imam Abu Hanifa, which indicates a higher rank. Similarly, Imam Abu Bakr al-Jassas al-Razi was placed in the fourth tier, but he was actually superior to those in the third tier; some even compared him to Imam al-Shafi'i. Lastly, the authors of al-Hidayah and al-Quduri were placed below Qadi Khan, but they are at least his equal. The overall message is that the classification of jurists is not straightforward, and a mufti must know the true rank of scholars to properly weigh their opinions. This is a rasm al-mufti issue because it affects which opinions are given precedence in fatwa.

مجتہد فی المذہب (mujtahid fi al-madhhab): A jurist who derives rulings within the principles of the Hanafi school, often resolving new cases by extending existing rules.

The Three-Tier Hierarchy of Masail in the Hanafi Madhhab
Defining and distinguishing the first two levels of masail.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Masail al-Usul, Masail al-Nawadir, and Masail al-Nawazil, and apply the mufti's priority order in deriving fatwas.

Core Idea
A mufti must issue fatwa first from Zahir al-Riwayah, then Nawadir, then Nawazil.

In the Hanafi school, not all legal rulings carry the same weight. The classical jurists classified masail into three levels based on their source and reliability. This classification guides the mufti in prioritizing which ruling to adopt when conflicting opinions exist. In this lesson, we explore the first two levels — Usul (also called Zahir al-Riwayah) and Nawadir — and understand their place in the hierarchy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three levels
Learn the names of the three tiers: Masail al-Usul, Masail al-Nawadir, and Masail al-Nawazil.
The third level (Nawazil) is covered separately; focus here on the first two.
2
Define Masail al-Usul (Zahir al-Riwayah)
Understand that these are the foundational rulings transmitted from the three Imams (Abu Hanifah, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) with tawatur or shuhra, compiled in Imam Muhammad's six books.
These are the most authoritative source for fatwa.
3
Define Masail al-Nawadir
Recognize that these are also from the same Imams but not included in the six core books; they are found in other works by Imam Muhammad or other early scholars.
They are of secondary authority compared to Zahir al-Riwayah.
4
Apply the hierarchy in fatwa
When issuing a fatwa, a mufti must first search for the ruling in Zahir al-Riwayah. If not found, then in Nawadir. If still not found, then in Nawazil (issues from later scholars).
This order ensures reliance on the strongest transmitted evidence.

Layers of Hanafi Rulings: Al-Usul, Al-Nawadir, Al-Fatawa
Classifying Hanafi interpretive rulings into three authority levels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify Hanafi rulings into three authority levels (Zahir al-Riwayah, Nawadir, Fatawa) and apply the correct hierarchy when issuing a fatwa.

Core Idea
The Hanafi fatwa hierarchy: Zahir al-Riwayah first, then Nawadir, then later Fatawa.

A mufti must know which rulings carry the strongest authority. The Hanafi school has a graded system: the core transmissions (Zahir al-Riwayah) are the primary reference, followed by less widely transmitted rulings (Nawadir), and finally rulings of later scholars (Fatawa). This lesson unpacks these three layers.

Beginning of Rasm al-Mufti. The second statement is that the early scholars (mutaqaddimin) are those who encountered the era of Imam A'zam and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and benefited from them, and those who did not encounter the era of the three Imams are the later scholars (muta'akhkhirin). The third statement is that the early scholars extend up to Imam Muhammad, and after him up to Hafiz al-Din Bukhari (77) are the later scholars (from Mabadi' al-Fiqh, p.73). Salaf and Khalaf: In terminology, Salaf is from Imam A'zam to Imam Muhammad, and Khalaf is from after Imam Muhammad to Shams al-A'imma al-Halawani (19) (Mabadi' al-Fiqh, p.74). Now the translation of Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti begins; until now, the explanations and benefits that came within the verses were additions. Categories of Rulings: It is necessary to know that the rulings expressed by our imams have three levels: First Level: The rulings of al-Usul, also called Zahir al-Riwayah. These are the rulings transmitted from the imams of the madhhab, i.e., Imam A'zam and the two companions (may Allah have mercy on them). These three are called 'the marked imams'. Sometimes Imam Zufar (78) and Imam Hasan ibn Ziyad (79) and others who studied under Imam A'zam are also included. But generally, the term Zahir al-Riwayah is used for the statements of the three imams or some of them. And the rulings of Zahir al-Riwayah and Usul are those mentioned in the six books of Imam Muhammad, namely: (1) al-Mabsut, (2) al-Ziyadat, (3) al-Jami' al-Saghir, (4) al-Siyar al-Saghir, (5) al-Jami' al-Kabir, and (6) al-Siyar al-Kabir. And they are called Zahir al-Riwayah because they are transmitted from Imam Muhammad through reliable narrators, i.e., these rulings are transmitted from Imam Muhammad with tawatur or shuhra. Second Level: The rulings of al-Nawadir. These are the rulings transmitted from the aforementioned imams of the madhhab, but they are not mentioned in the aforementioned books; rather: (1) either they are mentioned in other fiqh books besides those six books of Imam Muhammad, such as al-Kayyisaniyat, al-Haruniyat, al-Jurjaniyat, and al-Raqqiyat, and these rulings are called Ghayr Zahir al-Riwayah because they are not transmitted from Imam Muhammad with sound and famous transmission like the rulings of the aforementioned books. (2) Or those rulings are mentioned in the books of other students of Imam A'zam besides Imam Muhammad, such as the book al-Hujja of Imam Hasan ibn Ziyad (79) and other books; also the rulings mentioned in the Amali of Imam Abu Yusuf are included in this category. Benefit: Amali is the plural of Imla', meaning dictation. Its method was that a scholar would sit, and around him his students would sit with pen, inkpot, and paper. Whatever Allah placed in the scholar's heart, he would dictate orally. Then those papers would be collected to form an independent book. This method was called Imla' or Amali. Among the Salaf, muhaddithun, fuqaha', and scholars of literature all adopted this method and dictated the sciences of their respective fields. Now, neither those scholars remain nor that knowledge, so this method of Imla' has become a thing of the past. In the terminology of the Shafi'is, it is called Ta'liqah. (3) Or these rulings are narrated by only one student of Imam Muhammad, such as the specific rulings narrated by Ibn Samma' (58) and Mu'alla ibn Mansur (80), etc. Third Level: The rulings of Fatawa and Waqi'at. These are the rulings deduced by later mujtahids at that time. Al-Kayyisaniyat are rulings narrated by Shu'ayb ibn Sulayman al-Kayyisani (may Allah have mercy on him) from Imam Muhammad; they are also called Amali; a part of it has been printed in Hyderabad (Muqaddima al-Ziyadat, p.9). Al-Haruniyat are rulings made for or during the time of Harun al-Rashid. Al-Jurjaniyat are rulings narrated by 'Ali ibn Salih al-Jurjani from Imam Muhammad (Muqaddima al-Ziyadat and Kashf al-Zunun). Al-Raqqiyat are rulings narrated by Ibn Samma' from Imam Muhammad; Imam Muhammad deduced these rulings during his stay in the city of Raqqa (Muqaddima al-Ziyadat and Kashf al-Zunun, vol.1, p.911). Qanadi and Waqi'at are two words for the same meaning.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti serves as an introduction to the hierarchical classification of Hanafi rulings, which is essential for a mufti to understand the relative authority of different positions. The author first clarifies terminology regarding early and later scholars (mutaqaddimin/muta'akhkhirin) and Salaf/Khalaf, noting different opinions. Then he presents the three tiers of masa'il: (1) al-Usul (Zahir al-Riwayah), (2) al-Nawadir, and (3) al-Fatawa/waqi'at. The first tier consists of rulings from Imam A'zam, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani as recorded in Imam Muhammad's six core books, transmitted with tawatur or shuhra. These form the highest authority. The second tier includes rulings from the same imams but found in other compilations (e.g., Kayyisaniyat, Haruniyat) or from other direct students of Abu Hanifa, transmitted with less than tawatur. The third tier comprises rulings deduced by later mujtahids for new cases. The author also inserts a benefit on the practice of imla' (dictation) and clarifies the subcategories of Nawadir. A mufti must weigh these layers when issuing fatwas: after the Qur'an and Sunnah, the first recourse is Zahir al-Riwayah; if no clear ruling, then Nawadir; then Fatawa.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zāhir al-Riwāyah): Rulings transmitted from Imam Muhammad through reliable, widely-known chains, recorded in his six core books.
النوادر (al-Nawādir): Rulings from the same imams but not in the six books; transmitted via less famous paths or in other collections.
الفتاوى والواقعات (al-Fatāwā wa al-Wāqi‘āt): Rulings derived by later mujtahids (mutakhkhirin) for new incidents not explicitly covered in earlier texts.

Hierarchy of Hanafi Imams in Fatwa and Rules for Preference
Hierarchical ranking and deviation rules for Hanafi fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi fatwa hierarchy and identify when a mufti may deviate from Imam Abu Hanifa's opinion, distinguishing between a mujtahid and non-mujtahid mufti.

Core Idea
Fatwa follows Imam Abu Hanifa first, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar and Hasan — but a mujtahid may choose based on evidence.

Issuing a fatwa in the Hanafi school is not a free personal opinion. The mufti must follow a well-defined hierarchy of imams. This passage lays out the ranking and also the nuanced conditions under which a mufti may depart from the primary view of Imam Abu Hanifa, especially when the Sahibayn agree against him. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for correct fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hierarchy order
From Fatawa Sirajiyyah and other sources, the fixed order is: Imam Abu Hanifa → Imam Abu Yusuf → Imam Muhammad → Imam Zufar and Imam Hasan ibn Ziyad (with some variation on their internal order). This is the default ranking for a non-mujtahid mufti.
This is the qawl al-mutlaq (absolute statement) for fatwa.
2
Understand when deviation is permissible
Deviation from the agreement of the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) is allowed only if later circumstances would have led Imam Abu Hanifa to change his ruling. Similarly, if one of the Sahibayn agrees with Abu Hanifa, deviation without necessity is not permitted.
This is from al-Hawi al-Qudsi.
3
Analyse the dispute when Abu Hanifa alone opposes both Sahibayn
There are two positions: (1) Follow Abu Hanifa (Ibn al-Mubarak and some shaykhs). (2) The mufti has choice (especially if a mujtahid). Fatawa Sirajiyyah indicates the first is more correct when the mufti is not a mujtahid, implying a mujtahid may choose.
The thamarah al-khilaf is practical: a non-mujtahid must stick to Abu Hanifa; a mujtahid weighs evidence.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider a disputed issue where Imam Abu Hanifa rules one way and both Sahibayn agree on the opposite. If the mufti is a muqallid, he must follow Abu Hanifa. If he is a mujtahid, he may study the evidence and choose whichever he finds stronger.
The text states 'the most correct is to consider the strength of evidence'.
5
Summarise the hierarchy with exceptions
The default: follow Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar and Hasan. Exceptions: (a) if all three imams agree, deviation only with necessity; (b) if Sahibayn agree together against Abu Hanifa, non-mujtahid follows Abu Hanifa, mujtahid may choose.
This summary is given at the end of the passage.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive ruling on the hierarchy of the Hanafi imams for issuing fatwas. It is taken from a commentary on Rasm al-Mufti, which sets out the methodology for muftis. The passage opens by establishing that among the students of Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Zufar and Imam Hasan ibn Ziyad hold a special rank above other students, though there is a difference of opinion on their relative order. It then moves to a general principle: when there is a dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa alone and his students (including the Sahibayn), there are two views: one gives the mufti absolute choice, and the other restricts that choice to a mujtahid mufti who must weigh evidence. The text then offers confirmations (ta'yidat) from three authoritative sources: Fatawa Sirajiyyah, Tanwir al-Absar, and al-Hawi al-Qudsi. Fatawa Sirajiyyah lays down the classic order: absolute priority to Imam Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar and Hasan; it also notes that if Imam Abu Hanifa is opposed by both Sahibayn, a non-mujtahid mufti must follow the first (Imam Abu Hanifa), implying a mujtahid may have choice. Al-Hawi al-Qudsi adds the condition that when Imam Abu Hanifa's narration is missing, one moves down the hierarchy. It also permits deviation from the agreement of the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) if later circumstances would have led Abu Hanifa to change his ruling. The passage concludes with a summary (makhsal) in three numbered points: (1) when Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree, no deviation; (2) same when one Sahib agrees; (3) when both Sahibayn disagree with Abu Hanifa, there are subcases: if they disagree among themselves, Abu Hanifa's view prevails; if they agree against him, there is a disagreement among later scholars — some follow Abu Hanifa (Ibn al-Mubarak), some give the mufti choice (especially if he is a mujtahid). The benefit (faa'idah) clarifies that 'choice' for a mujtahid means he must study the evidence and adopt the stronger proof. Who May Act on Weak Narrations? Birri's Rule for Muftis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify who may act on a weak narration personally, distinguish between personal practice and fatwa-giving, and apply the rule that even a mujtahid mufti cannot depart from the agreed-upon madhhab in issuing fatwas.

Core Idea
A mujtahid can follow a weak narration for himself but must give fatwa by the madhhab's stronger opinion.

This passage from Al-Birri's commentary on al-Ashbah addresses a critical distinction: the permissibility of acting on weak narrations in personal matters versus the obligation to adhere to the established madhhab position when issuing fatwas. It resolves an apparent contradiction that arises when a qualified mufti knows of a weak hadith but the school's founding Imams have a stronger consensus. Understanding this rule is essential for any mufti navigating between personal ijtihad and institutional authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question and Birri's answer
Read the question: Is it permissible for a person to act on a weak narration in his own affairs? Birri answers: yes, if he is dhi ra'y (a mujtahid fil madhhab). For a common person (including a non-mujtahid mufti), it is not permissible. Note the condition (قيد) of being dhi ra'y.
The term 'dhi ra'y' is key – it means a person of opinion, i.e., capable of independent reasoning within the madhhab.
2
Define the scope of 'dhi ra'y'
Birri specifies that 'dhi ra'y' means a mujtahid fil madhhab – one who can weigh evidences and understand texts. This excludes the common person and also non-mujtahid muftis (who are included in 'common person' for this rule).
In Hanafi terminology, a mujtahid fil madhhab is above a mere muqallid but below the founding Imams.
3
Distinguish personal practice from fatwa-giving
The passage then raises an objection: earlier it was stated that even a mujtahid mufti cannot depart from the agreed-upon opinion of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad when giving fatwa. How can he then act on a weak narration for himself? The author resolves: the earlier rule applies to fatwa (for others), not personal practice. Even if the mufti is a mujtahid, he is bound by the stronger ijtihad of the Imams in his public role.
The ruling for personal practice is based on his own responsibility; fatwa binds others and must follow the madhhab's stronger evidence.
4
Understand the epistemological basis
The reason for forbidding departure in fatwa is that the Imams knew all the evidences and distinguished authentic from unauthentic. The mufti's ijtihad cannot reach their level. Therefore, he must follow their consensus.
This is a key principle of 'rasm al-mufti': the mufti's ijtihad is subordinate to the ijtihad of the school's founders.
5
Apply the rule to a concrete scenario
Consider a mufti who finds a weak hadith suggesting a slightly different ruling in a minor matter. In his personal worship, he may follow that weak hadith if he is a mujtahid fil madhhab. But when someone asks him for a fatwa, he must give the agreed-upon Hanafi position. This maintains consistency and prevents confusion.
The distinction is practical: the mufti's personal practice is his own, but his fatwa represents the madhhab.

Al-Birri's statement and its interpretation: In Allamah Al-Birri's Sharh al-Ashbah, it says: (Question) Is it permissible for a person to act on a weak narration in matters concerning himself? (Answer) It is permissible, provided that the person is dhi ra'y, i.e., a mujtahid fil madhhab. And if he is a common person (which includes a non-mujtahid mufti), I have not seen a ruling on him, but the requirement of restricting permissibility to dhi ra'y implies that it is not permissible for a common person. In Khizanat al-Riwayat, it is written: A scholar who knows the meanings of texts and hadith and is among those of understanding and insight, it is permissible for him to act on a weak narration, even if that narration is against his madhhab. (Birri's statement ends.) And restricting permissibility to dhi ra'y excludes the common person, as Birri stated, because he is obligated to follow the opinion that the scholars have deemed correct. But this is apart from cases of necessity, as you have just learned. A severe contradiction: If someone says that this is contrary to what you stated earlier: that even a mujtahid mufti is not permitted to depart from the opinion on which Imam Abu Hanifa and his students agreed. Commentary
This passage is a ruling (rasm) on who may act on weak narrations personally, and a resolution of an apparent contradiction with the rule that a mujtahid mufti cannot depart from the agreed-upon madhhab. The author presents Al-Birri's question: may a person act on a weak narration for himself? Birri answers: yes, if he is 'dhi ra'y' meaning a mujtahid within the madhhab. For a common person (including a non-mujtahid mufti), it is not permissible because he must follow what the scholars have deemed correct. This is for non-necessity cases. Then an objection arises: this seems to contradict the earlier rule that even a mujtahid mufti cannot depart from the agreed position of the three Imams when giving fatwa. The resolution is that the earlier rule applies to the mufti when issuing fatwa to others; his personal practice may be different if he is dhi ra'y, but even then, for fatwa he is bound by the stronger consensus. The author begins giving two reasons but the text cuts off.

Key Terms
ذی رائے (dhi ra'y): A person who is a mujtahid within the madhhab, capable of weighing evidences and understanding texts.
عام آدمی (aam aadmi): A layperson who is not a scholar; also includes non-mujtahid muftis.
مجتہد فی المذہب (mujtahid fil madhhab): A jurist who can derive rulings within the Hanafi school, though not an absolute mujtahid.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fatwa practice, a qualified mujtahid may follow a weak narration personally, but when issuing a fatwa to others, he must adhere to the agreed-upon position of the three Imams, as their ijtihad is stronger.
