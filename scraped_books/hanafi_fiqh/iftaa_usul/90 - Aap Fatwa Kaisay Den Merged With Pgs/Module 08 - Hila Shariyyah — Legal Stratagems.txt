Payment to Huffaz in Tarawih and the Invalid Hila
Clarifies payment to Huffaz is ijarah not hiba, and discusses a hila with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-ma'ruf kal-mashrut' to classify payments to Huffaz and evaluate the validity of the common hila used to justify such payments.

Core Idea
Payment to Huffaz in Tarawih is ijarah, not hiba, due to custom; the common hila is invalid because its conditions are unmet.

This passage addresses a common practice: giving gifts (nazrana) to Huffaz who recite in Tarawih. It clarifies that due to custom, such payments are legally considered wages (ujrat), not pure gifts, and thus fall under the prohibition of taking payment for acts of worship. The passage also examines a legal stratagem (hila) that attempts to circumvent this ruling and explains why it fails, reinforcing the original prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the PaymentType
First, determine whether the payment given to a Hafiz for reciting in Tarawih is a gift (hiba) or a wage (ujrat). Consider the custom in the locality.
The maxim 'al-ma'ruf kal-mashrut' treats customary practice as if it were a stipulated condition.
2
Apply the Maxim
Apply the maxim 'al-ma'ruf kal-mashrut' to the payment. In a place where giving payment for recitation is customary, that payment is legally a wage, even if called a gift.
3
State the Ruling
Since the payment is a wage, and the service (Quran recitation) is an act of worship, the ijarah is invalid. Taking payment for worship is impermissible unless necessitated by necessity, which is not the case here.
Hanafi school prohibits ijarah on acts of worship in principle.
4
Analyze the Hila
Examine the proposed hila: assign two rak'ahs of prayer to the Hafiz to make the payment appear for prayer rather than recitation. Note the two required conditions: (1) salary must be fixed, (2) recitation cannot be a condition for payment.
The hila is valid only if both conditions are met; in practice they are not.
5
Evaluate the Tarjih
Consider the earlier fatwa of permissibility from Imdad al-Fatawa and its retraction by Hazrat Thanwi. The relied-upon position is impermissibility, as affirmed by later Hanafi authorities.
The tarjih is based on the consistency with Hanafi principles and the absence of genuine necessity.

Benefit: The ruling mentioned above regarding Quran recitation for isal-e-thawab is the same for the wage or gift (nazrana) of Huffaz. That is, in the month of Ramadan, the Huffaz who recite the Quran in Tarawih and take a gift (nazrana) for it, this is in reality a wage (ujrat), because the juristic principle is: 'What is customarily known is like a stipulated condition' (al-ma'ruf kal-mashrut). That is, in a locality or mosque where giving and taking is customary, even without a formal agreement, taking a gift is considered a wage, which is impermissible. And the stratagem (hila) that one or two prayers be assigned to the hafiz can be valid with two conditions: (1) The salary is fixed; otherwise the ijarah is invalid. (2) Reciting the Quran in Tarawih is not stipulated or customary; i.e., even if he does not recite the Quran in Tarawih, he still receives the fixed salary. It is obvious that these two conditions are not present in the mentioned hila, so that hila is also invalid. And the thought that if the wage or gift is not permissible, the system of Tarawih or the system of memorizing the Quran will break down is incorrect. Tarawih can be performed with short surahs, and in areas where Huffaz are not given anything, children still memorize well. Rather, this gift (nazrana) itself harms Quran memorization. Such Huffaz remain only 'Ramadan Huffaz' and the real purpose of memorizing the Quran is lost. Similarly, taking payment for listening (to recitation) is also not permissible. And regarding the fatwa of permissibility in Imdad al-Fatawa, vol. 1, p. 496, question 424, Hazrat Thanwi (may his secret be sanctified) retracted from it in al-Tadhkir, part 3, al-Tahdhib, no. 3, p. 86. Also, Fatawa Dar al-Ulum Deoband (new edition), vol. 4, p. 295, gives a fatwa of impermissibility for taking payment for listening. (End of benefit)

Commentary
This passage is a 'faidah' (benefit) section that extends the earlier ruling on ijarah for Quran recitation to the specific case of Huffaz in Tarawih prayers. The author clarifies that the payment (nazrana) given to Huffaz is in fact ijarah (wage) due to the legal maxim 'al-ma'ruf kal-mashrut' (custom is like a stipulated condition). Since the custom is to pay for recitation, without any express condition, the payment is considered a wage, not a gift. Consequently, the ijarah is invalid because it involves payment for an act of worship. The passage then discusses a common legal stratagem (hila) where two rak'ahs of prayer are assigned to the hafiz to make the payment appear a gift. The author states that this hila is only valid with two conditions: (1) the salary must be fixed (otherwise the ijarah is fasid), and (2) the recitation in Tarawih must not be a condition for payment. In practice, these conditions are not met, so the hila is invalid. The author also refutes the argument that disallowing payment would harm the system of Tarawih or Quran memorization, noting that Tarawih can be done with short surahs and that memorization continues in places where no payment is given. He further mentions that even payment for listening (by the audience) is impermissible, and that earlier permissive fatwas (e.g., from Imdad al-Fatawa and specially from Hazrat Thanwi) have been retracted. Thus, the final Hanafi position is that payment to Huffaz and listeners is not permissible.

Key Terms
نذرانہ (nazrana): A gift or offering given to a hafiz for recitation, but due to custom is considered a wage (ujrat)
حیلہ (hila): A legal stratagem to make a transaction permissible; here, assigning prayers to the hafiz to avoid the impermissibility of paying for recitation
رمضانی حافظ (ramdhani hafiz): A hafiz who only recites during Ramadan for pay, without sincere memorization for the sake of Allah
معروف (ma'ruf): What is customarily established in a society; here, the customary practice of giving payment to Huffaz
