When Evidences Are Equal: The Rule for the Mujtahid
Rule for equal evidences in fatwa methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for a mujtahid when two opinions are equally strong (musawi) and apply it to correctly attribute opinions to the Imam.

Core Idea
When evidences are equal, the mujtahid may choose either opinion — and both remain his.

This lesson addresses a nuanced scenario in Hanafi fatwa methodology: what happens when a mujtahid (like Imam Abu Hanifa) finds two conflicting evidences that he cannot weigh as stronger or weaker? Normally, only the preponderant (raajih) opinion may be used for fatwa. But when the evidences are genuinely equal (musawi), a different rule applies. The passage corrects two misunderstandings about attribution of opinions in such cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Rule
Recall that generally a non-preponderant (marjuh) opinion cannot be used for fatwa by the mujtahid or muqallid. This is the baseline principle.
The passage begins by reminding us of this rule via Imam Qurani.
2
Recognize the Exception
When the mujtahid cannot prefer one evidence over another (adam al-tarjih), the ruling changes: he may choose either opinion.
Key condition: the evidences are genuinely equal in strength.
3
Understand Correct Attribution
In this stalemate, both opinions are attributed to the mujtahid, not just one, because there is no indication of retraction (ruju') from either.
The text rejects two wrong views: that neither is attributed, or that only one is attributed.
4
Refute the Incorrect Views
The first incorrect view is that no saying can be attributed; the second is that only one saying can be attributed. Both are wrong because the Imam's ruju' from the other saying is not established.
This is a critical refinement (critical_refinement tag).
5
Apply the Rule in Practice
A mufti today, when encountering two equally strong narrations from the early school, may choose either for fatwa, and both are considered valid opinions of the Imam.
This is part of the fatwa methodology (rasm al-mufti).

In the case of no preference (adam al-tarjih), both are sayings:

And we have earlier mentioned, by reference to Imam Qurani, may Allah have mercy on him, that deciding or giving fatwa based on a non-preponderant (ghayr raajih) saying is not permissible for either the mujtahid or the muqallid. However, if in the view of the mujtahid the evidences are conflicting and he cannot prefer one over the other, then it is permissible for him to decide on whichever of the two sayings he wishes, because in this case both sayings are equal in his view. And on this basis (i.e., when it is correct to decide on both sayings), both sayings can be attributed to that mujtahid. And the view of some usulis – that in such a case no saying should be attributed to the mujtahid – is incorrect. Also, the view of some people – that only one saying can be attributed to the mujtahid – is also incorrect, because the Imam's return (ruju') from the other saying is not established.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling when a mujtahid (like Imam Abu Hanifa) faces conflicting evidences and cannot determine which is stronger. Normally, acting on a non-preponderant (marjuh) opinion is not allowed for either the mujtahid or the muqallid. However, when there is a genuine stalemate – the evidences are equally strong and no tarjih is possible – then the mujtahid may choose either opinion for his judgment or fatwa. In such a scenario, both opinions are considered valid and can be attributed to the mujtahid. The author refutes two incorrect views: (1) that neither can be attributed, (2) that only one can be attributed. The correct Hanafi position is that both are his opinions, as there is no evidence that he withdrew from one. This principle is crucial for a mufti: when encountering two equally strong narrations from the Imam, he may choose either, but if one is clearly preferred, the muqallid must follow the preferred one. The passage is from the later Hanafi fatwa tradition (mutakhkhirin) and reflects the developed usul al-iftaa.

Key Terms
راجح (raajih): Preponderant opinion or evidence that outweighs others.
مرجوح (marjuh): Non-preponderant opinion or weaker evidence.
رجوع (ruju'): The act of a mujtahid retracting a previous opinion.
مساوی (musawi): Equal in weight; no preference between two opinions or evidences.

Mafhum: Implied Meaning and Its Types in Hanafi Usul
Defining mafhum muwafiq and mafhum mukhalif with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mafhum muwafiq from mafhum mukhalif, apply the qawida about acting on mafhum, and identify when it is overridden by an explicit text.

Core Idea
Mafhum derives rulings from text by linguistic implication, not personal opinion.

This lesson introduces a foundational concept in Hanafi usul: the implied meaning (mafhum) of texts. The mufti must know when a text's implication is binding and when it must yield to an explicit statement. We will dissect the qawida that governs this area and examine the two types of mafhum.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Qawida
The couplet commands: 'Act upon the implied meaning of narrations, as long as they do not contradict an established explicit text.' Commit this rule to memory – it is your gatekeeper for using mafhum.
This is a minor (sughra) qawida but essential for daily fatwa work.
2
Distinguish Mafhum Muwafiq and Mafhum Mukhalif
Mafhum muwafiq grants the same ruling to an unmentioned case because it is more deserving (e.g., hitting parents from 'do not say uff'). Mafhum mukhalif grants the opposite ruling based on a condition or attribute.
Mafhum muwafiq is also called fahwa al-khitab; mafhum mukhalif is called dalil al-khitab.
3
Apply the 'Uff' Example
The verse 'Do not say uff to them' (Quran 17:23) textually prohibits a minor annoyance. By mafhum muwafiq, the prohibition extends to any form of harm, especially hitting, because it is worse.
This is not an analogy (qiyas) but a direct linguistic implication – it does not require ijtihad.
4
Identify the Istithna' (Exception)
The qawida includes a crucial exception: if a clear explicit text (nass sarih) contradicts the mafhum, the explicit text prevails. The implied meaning is only valid when it does not conflict.
This protects against arbitrary interpretation overriding clear injunctions.
5
Understand the Scope in Iftaa
A mufti may rely on mafhum in deriving rulings, but must always check for an explicit contradictory text. When in doubt, the explicit rule takes precedence (al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk).
This is a standard Hanafi methodology – it balances textual fidelity with linguistic reasoning.

And act upon the implied meaning of the traditions that have come, as long as they do not contradict an established explicit text. Translation (68): And you should act upon the implied meaning of the narrations that have occurred, as long as they are not contrary to any established explicit text. The implied meaning (mafhum) and its types: Know that mafhum has two types: (1) Mafhum muwafiq (congruent meaning): It is that the words indicate the ruling of the spoken (mantooq/mentioned) for the unspoken (maskoot/unmentioned) by merely understanding the speech according to the idioms of language, i.e., that indication does not depend on reflection and opinion and ijtihad, like the indication of 'Do not say Uff to them' (parents) upon the prohibition of hitting them (its other name is fahwa al-khitab). (2) Mafhum mukhalif (opposite meaning): It is that the words indicate the opposite of the ruling of the spoken for the unspoken (its many examples will come within its types, and this mafhum is also called dalil al-khitab). Terms: Mafhum: that which is understood from the speech. Maskoot: that which is not expressed. Mantooq: that which is spoken, i.e., expressed. Mahwi?: subject of discourse. Dalil: guidance.

Commentary
This passage begins with a verse (couplet) from the text instructing the mufti to act on the implied meaning (mafhum) of narrations as long as they do not contradict an explicit established text. Then it defines mafhum and its two types. Mafhum muwafiq (also called fahwa al-khitab) derives the same ruling for an unmentioned case by language alone, e.g., from 'do not say uff' we deduce hitting parents is also prohibited because it is worse. Mafhum mukhalif (also called dalil al-khitab) derives the opposite ruling for the unmentioned case, e.g., if a text says 'if it is sheep, then zakat is one goat', the unspoken (cows) would have a different ruling. The passage also clarifies key terms: mantooq (explicitly stated), maskoot (unstated), mafhum (understood). This is a foundational usul al-fiqh concept that a mufti must understand to correctly apply hadith and Quranic verses.

Key Terms
مفہوم (mafhum): That which is understood from speech, either congruent or opposite to the explicit ruling.
مفہوم موافق (mafhum muwafiq): Indication that the ruling of the spoken applies to the unspoken because it is more deserving of the ruling.
مفہوم مخالف (mafhum mukhalif): Indication that the unspoken has the opposite ruling of the spoken, based on a condition or attribute.
منطوق (mantooq): That which is explicitly stated in the text.
مسکوت (maskoot): That which is not expressed but understood by implication.
