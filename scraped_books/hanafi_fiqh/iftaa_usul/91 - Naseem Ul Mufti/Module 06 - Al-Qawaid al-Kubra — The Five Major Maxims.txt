The Authority of Custom in Hanafi Fatwa
States the principle that custom is considered as long as it does not contradict an explicit text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition for using 'urf in fatwa and apply the maxim to a new case, distinguishing between custom that is acceptable and custom that is rejected due to conflict with an explicit text.

Core Idea
Custom is valid for fatwa only if it does not contradict an explicit established text.

This lesson introduces a foundational rule for the mufti: the role of 'urf (custom) in issuing fatwas. In the Hanafi school, custom carries significant weight, especially in transactions. However, its application is bounded by the condition that it must not oppose a clear textual proof. Understanding this limit is crucial for avoiding illegitimate changes to the Shari'ah.

Guided Path
1
Recite the Arabic verse and its translation
Familiarize yourself with the exact wording of the principle, noting both the positive command ('act upon the understood meaning') and the crucial condition ('as long as it does not contradict an explicit established statement').
Memorization anchor: the verse is often quoted in rasm al-mufti works.
2
Identify the key condition
Parse the phrase 'mal-am yukhalif li-sarih thabata'. Understand that 'sarih thabata' means an explicit and authentically established text from the Quran, mutawatir sunnah, or definitive ijma. The condition is a restriction: custom is only considered when such a text is absent.
The opposite of sarih is muhtamil (probable), where custom may specify the general.
3
Understand the rationale for considering custom
The verse affirms that custom has consideration in Shari'ah. Reflect on why: many rulings are derived from community practice, especially in mu'amalat, where the primary texts are limited and custom fills the details.
The Hanafi maxim 'al-urf muhakkam' (custom is authoritative) is based on this principle.
4
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Given a custom or practice, first determine if there is an explicit text (sarih) that directly addresses the issue. If yes, the custom is rejected. If no, the custom may be used to derive the ruling.
If the custom conflicts with a general principle but not an explicit text, it may still be accepted (e.g., overruling qiyas).
5
Distinguish from other sources
Understand that custom can override qiyas (analogy) but not nass (text). This hierarchy is essential for the mufti's methodology.
This distinguishes Hanafi approach from other schools that may give less weight to custom.

Introduces the principle of 'urf as a source for fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that 'urf is a legitimate source in Hanafi fatwa and explain when it takes precedence over the zahir al-riwaya.

Core Idea
Custom can override the explicit madhhab position in muamalat when it does not contradict a clear nass.

This lesson introduces the thirteenth foundational principle for muftis: the necessity of considering local custom ('urf) when issuing fatwas. In Hanafi fiqh, 'urf is not merely a sociological factor but a recognized legal source that can influence rulings, especially in transactions, contracts, and social dealings. The passage cites Allama Shami to stress that a mufti or judge cannot ignore prevailing custom in favor of a literal reading of the madhhab's transmitted positions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the status of 'urf
Recognize that 'urf is a major source of Islamic law, especially in muamalat. The passage counts it among the fountains of fiqh, alongside Quran and Hadith.
This is not merely a recommendation but a binding principle for fatwa.
2
Memorize the definition
Learn al-Nasafi's definition: 'urf is what settles in souls through intellects and is accepted by sound nature. This definition gives 'urf an objective basis.
The definition emphasizes rational acceptance, not mere habit.
3
Apply the hierarchy
Understand the rule: 'urf can override the zahir al-riwaya in muamalat, but cannot contradict a clear nass (Quran or Sunnah) or ijma. This is the limit of its authority.
Always check for a contrary nass before applying 'urf.

Proofs for the Validity of Custom (Urf)
Presents three categories of proof for custom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Quranic and hadith proofs for the authority of custom and apply the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' to determine a ruling in a new case.

Core Idea
Custom is authoritative in Islamic law, supported by Quran, hadith, and consensus – but it must never override clear textual proofs.

Custom (urf) plays a crucial role in Hanafi fiqh, especially in areas like family law and commerce. This lesson presents the scriptural and consensual foundations for the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah', showing how the term 'ma'ruf' in the Quran and hadith is interpreted as local custom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its textual basis
Recognize that the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative) is derived from scriptural sources: the Quran (2:233), hadith (Hind bint Utbah), and ijma of the scholars.
The maxim itself is not found verbatim in primary texts, but its meaning is extracted from these proofs.
2
Analyze the Quranic and hadith proofs
Examine how the term 'bil-ma'ruf' (according to what is reasonable/customary) in both verse and hadith indicates that the Shari'ah defers to local custom to determine obligations like maintenance (nafaqah).
The hadith allows the wife to take from her husband's wealth 'bil-ma'ruf', linking the amount to societal norms.
3
Apply the maxim to the furuat provided
Work through the two furuat from the toolbox: (1) determining maintenance amount based on custom, and (2) the wife's permission to take without consent when the husband is miserly. Confirm how custom sets the standard.
These applications illustrate that custom operationalizes general shariah commands.
4
Recognize the limitation (istithna')
Note that the maxim is not absolute: when custom contradicts an explicit ruling from Quran or sunnah, custom is disregarded. This is the major exception.
The governing maxim for the exception is 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' (certainty is not removed by doubt).

Classification of Custom: Verbal, Practical, General, Specific
Defining and classifying custom (urf) into types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define verbal custom (urf qawli) and practical custom (urf amali) and distinguish general from specific custom according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Custom (urf) is authoritative in Hanafi fiqh when it is either a prevalent word meaning or a consistent practice.

The Hanafi school relies on custom as a subsidiary source of law in matters not explicitly covered by nass. This lesson introduces the fourfold classification of custom: verbal/practical by subject, and general/specific by scope. Understanding these categories is essential for applying the legal maxim 'al-urf muhakkam' (custom is authoritative).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two bases of classification
Custom is classified by subject/locus (verbal vs practical) and by scope/prevalence (general vs specific). Note these two axes.
These categories are interdependent; a custom can be both verbal and general, etc.
2
Define verbal custom (urf qawli)
A word that acquires a specific meaning in common usage, understood without context or evidence. Provide examples like 'bay' for sale.
The condition is that the meaning be prevalent and not contradicting Shariah.
3
Define practical custom (urf amali)
A repeated action or practice among most people in a community. E.g., methods of contracting or customary weights.
Practical custom is given weight in mu'amalat (transactions).
4
Distinguish general from specific custom
General custom (urf amm) prevails over a wide area or among all people; specific custom (urf khass) is limited to a region, trade, or group.
The passage mentions this classification but detail is cut off; note that specific custom may be subordinate to general custom in case of conflict.
5
Recognize authority and conditions
Both verbal and practical custom are authoritative (mu'tabar) in Hanafi fiqh as long as they do not contradict explicit nass (Quran and Sunnah).
The authority of custom is supported by texts and consensus of the fuqaha.

Core Idea
The validity of custom in Hanafi fiqh depends on its generality or specificity; definitions determine authority.

This lesson introduces the two categories of custom ('urf) in Hanafi legal theory: general custom ('urf 'amm) and specific custom ('urf khas). Understanding these definitions is the first step to applying the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative) correctly. The passage also presents a refined definition of 'urf 'amm by a contemporary scholar, which highlights the contextual nature of 'general' custom.

The Authority of Custom When Conflicting with Texts
Determining when custom is authoritative despite textual conflict

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the three scenarios of conflict between custom and text to determine when custom is valid or invalid, and explain the Hanafi position on 'urf khas and takhsis.

Core Idea
A custom that completely contradicts a text is void; a general custom partially conflicting may specify the text; a specific custom does not specify.

This lesson builds on the definitions of 'urf 'amm and 'urf khas to explore their legal authority when they clash with primary texts. Drawing from Allamah Shami's works, the passage outlines three scenarios and clarifies the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position regarding the specification (takhsis) of general texts by specific custom. This is a core principle in the mufti's methodology.

Guided Path
1
Recall the two types of custom
Remember that custom is either 'urf 'amm (general, widespread across a country or all Islamic lands) or 'urf khas (limited to a city, class, or profession).
2
Learn the first scenario: no conflict
If custom does not contradict any Shari'ah text, both 'urf 'amm and 'urf khas are valid by consensus. No further analysis needed.
This is the starting point: custom is authoritative (al-'adah muhakkamah) when it does not clash with a text.
3
Learn the second scenario: complete contradiction
If acting on the custom would require completely abandoning the text, both types are invalid by consensus. The text prevails absolutely.
Example: a custom that permits riba would completely contradict the Quranic prohibition, so it is rejected.
4
Learn the third scenario: partial conflict
If the custom only partially conflicts (i.e., the text is general and the custom specifies some of its applications, or the evidence is analogy), then there is a difference between 'urf 'amm and 'urf khas.
5
Understand the ruling for 'urf 'amm in partial conflict
'Urf 'amm is valid and can specify the general text; analogy is abandoned in its favor. This is by consensus.
Example: the custom of istisna' specifies the general prohibition of selling what you do not possess.
6
Understand the ruling for 'urf khas in partial conflict
According to the relied-upon position (al-madhhab), 'urf khas is not valid for specification, and analogy is not abandoned for it. This is the mufta bihi.
The reason is that custom varies by region, causing uncertainty and inconsistency in applying the text.
7
Study the dispute over 'urf khas and takhsis
Some scholars argue that 'urf khas can specify a general text if it is widespread in that region. But the Hanafi madhhab rejects this due to the lack of universality.
A mufti may consider the opposing view in local fatwa if needed, but the default position is that 'urf khas does not specify.

Commentary
This passage from Naseem Ul Mufti explains the classification of custom ('urf) into 'urf 'amm (general custom) and 'urf khas (specific custom) and discusses their legal authority when they conflict with primary textual evidence (nusus). The author begins by presenting the standard definition of 'urf 'amm as a practice prevalent among all or most people across all Islamic lands, giving examples such as the istisna' contract, bathing in public baths for a fee, and using the word 'haram' for divorce. He notes that most jurists accept this definition, but the contemporary scholar Maulana Khalid Saifullah Rahmani refines it: 'urf 'amm is the common practice across different cities, regions, and classes of a single country. 'Urf khas is defined as a practice limited to a particular city or a specific group, such as a profession. The passage then turns to the core issue: the validity of these customs when they conflict with Shari'ah texts. Drawing from Allamah Ibn 'Abidin's works, three scenarios are outlined: (1) If there is no conflict, both 'urf 'amm and 'urf khas are valid by consensus. (2) If there is complete contradiction such that acting on the custom would require abandoning the text entirely, both types are invalid by consensus. (3) If the custom only partially conflicts—i.e., the text is general and the custom specifies some of its applications, or the evidence is based on analogy—then 'urf 'amm is valid and can be used to specify the text, and analogy is abandoned in its favor. However, 'urf khas in this situation is not accepted according to the relied-upon position of the madhhab. The passage ends with the disagreement regarding 'urf khas: some scholars argue that because custom varies from region to region, allowing specification by a specific custom would create uncertainty, so it is impermissible. This discussion is fundamental for a mufti, as it provides the framework for when customs can override or modify rulings from textual sources, a key principle in Hanafi fatwa methodology.

General and Specific Custom: Their Authority in Hanafi Fatwa
Explaining the Hanafi rule on general vs. specific custom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general custom (`urf 'aam`) and specific custom (`urf khaas`) and apply the Hanafi rule that general custom establishes general rulings while specific custom establishes specific rulings.

Core Idea
General custom binds universally; specific custom binds only its people.

In Hanafi fatwa methodology, custom (`urf`) is a recognized source, but not all customs carry the same weight. This lesson unpacks the critical distinction between general custom, which is widespread across regions, and specific custom, which is limited to a locality or community. Understanding this distinction is essential for a mufti when ruling on cases where custom may override a textual ruling, as seen in the example of istisna' (manufacturing contract) versus local weaving practices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Qawida (Legal Maxim)
Extract the central rule from the passage: general custom establishes a general ruling, specific custom establishes a specific ruling. This is stated explicitly by Ibn Abidin.
This is the core principle for fatwa in cases of custom.
2
Analyze the Dispute: Balkh Scholars vs. the Madhhab
Understand the disagreement: the mashayikh of Balkh used a local custom (cloth-weaving) to specify a hadith. The Hanafi madhhab rejects this because local custom cannot override a text universally.
The key reasoning: if local custom could specify, doubt would arise from lack of practice elsewhere.
3
Distinguish the Scope of Application
Apply the qawida: general custom (e.g., istisna') applies everywhere; specific custom applies only to its people. The cloth-weaving custom cannot establish a general ruling, only a ruling for that locality.
This explains why istisna' is valid despite textual prohibition, while local weaving practices do not override the hadith.
4
Synthesize the Fatwa Rule
For a mufti: when custom is cited, first determine if it is general or specific. If general, it can establish a universal ruling and may specify texts. If specific, it can only establish a ruling for that community and only in matters directly related to them.
This is a key rule of rasm al-mufti (mufti's methodology).

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi rule regarding the authority of custom (`urf`) in issuing fatwas, specifically the distinction between general custom (`urf 'aam`) and specific or local custom (`urf khaas`). The passage begins by critiquing the practice of the mashayikh of Balkh who used a local custom (weaving cloth) to specify a hadith. The author argues that local custom cannot specify a text because if one region's practice could do so, another region's lack of practice would prevent it, causing doubt. Unlike istisna' (manufacturing contract), which is universally practiced, local custom lacks the universality needed to override textual evidence. The passage then cites Imam Ibn Nujaym stating that the madhhab officially does not consider specific custom authoritative, but many scholars have given fatwa by it. The apparent contradiction is resolved: the difference is that general custom can establish a general ruling and can specify texts, while specific custom can only establish a specific ruling for its own people and cannot generate a general rule. Imam Ibn Abidin confirms this by quoting al-Hamawi's converse implication: 'If general ruling is not established by specific custom, then specific ruling is established by it.' The practical takeaway for a mufti: when following custom, one must distinguish between the scope of the custom. A custom limited to a specific region or community affects only those people and only in matters directly related to them. This is a rule of fatwa methodology.
