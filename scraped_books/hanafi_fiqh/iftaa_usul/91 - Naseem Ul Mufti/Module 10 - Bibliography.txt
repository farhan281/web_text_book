Key Sources in Hanafi Fatwa Methodology
List of referenced books and authors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major reference works in Hanafi fatwa methodology and classify them by subject (fatwa, fiqh, usul, biography).

Core Idea
A mufti's library is his arsenal—knowing the hierarchy of sources is essential for sound fatwa.

A mufti does not issue fatwa from memory alone. He relies on a carefully curated set of authoritative texts. The bibliography in Naseem Ul Mufti lists the essential works that every Hanafi mufti should know. This lesson will help you navigate these sources and understand their role in the fatwa process.

Guided Path
1
Scan the list of titles
Read through the Arabic titles and their authors. Note the variety: some are classical (Ibn Abidin, al-Khatib al-Baghdadi), others are contemporary (Wahba al-Zuhayli, Sa'id Bakdash).
Pay attention to recurring themes: 'Rasm al-Mufti', 'al-Ifta', 'al-Madhhab al-Hanafi'.
2
Categorize by subject
Group the works into four categories: (1) Fatwa methodology and manuals (e.g., Radd al-Muhtar, Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti), (2) General Hanafi fiqh (e.g., Al-Fiqh al-Islami wa Adillatuhu, Al-Madkhal al-Mufassal), (3) Usul al-fiqh (e.g., Al-Wajiz fi Usul al-Fiqh), (4) Biographies and history (e.g., Bulugh al-Amani, Al-Faqih wa al-Mutafaqqih).
Some works may fit multiple categories; choose the primary focus.
3
Identify the most authoritative texts
Among the classical texts, note that Radd al-Muhtar by Ibn Abidin is a cornerstone of fatwa in the late Hanafi school. Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti (also by Ibn Abidin) is the commentary on the foundational manual of fatwa procedure.
These two works are often considered the final word in Hanafi fatwa.
4
Note the contemporary scholars
Modern authors like Wahba al-Zuhayli, Muhammad Mustafa al-Zuhayli, and Abd al-Karim Zaydan have written comprehensive works that are widely used today. Their books summarize classical positions and cite evidence.
These are valuable for understanding the contemporary application of Hanafi rulings.
5
Reflect on the purpose of the list
This bibliography shows that a mufti must be grounded in usul, fiqh, and the history of the madhhab. It also demonstrates the continuity of Hanafi scholarship from early times to the present.
The list is not exhaustive but represents the core references for a mufti's training.

Navigating the Bibliography of Naseem Ul Mufti: Key Arabic and Urdu Sources for Hanafi Fatwa
List of Arabic and Urdu references and bibliographic entries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major classical and modern sources listed in Naseem Ul Mufti and explain their role in the Hanafi fatwa tradition.

Core Idea
A mufti's library is his arsenal: knowing which source to cite earns authority.

This passage lists the Arabic and Urdu sources that the compiler of Naseem Ul Mufti relied upon. For a student of iftaa, this is more than a bibliography—it's a roadmap of authoritative texts. Understanding the hierarchy and focus of each text is essential for constructing a sound fatwa and tracing the lineage of rulings.

Guided Path
1
Categorize the sources
Note that the list is divided into Arabic works (mainly classical) and Urdu works (modern, from the Indian subcontinent). This reflects the two major streams of Hanafi scholarship: the classical Arab tradition and the later Indian tradition.
2
Identify the classical core
Look for Ibn Abidin's works: Radd al-Muhtar (a supercommentary on al-Durr al-Mukhtar) and Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. These are foundational for Hanafi fatwa methodology.
Radd al-Muhtar is often considered the final authority in the Hanafi school for later fatwas.
3
Recognize modern expansions
Notice works like Dawabit al-Fatwa and Manhaj al-Ifta'—they systematize the rules of fatwa-giving and derive principles from the classical tradition.
These are 20th-century works that adapt classical methodology to contemporary needs.
4
Understand the Urdu tradition
The Urdu sources include practical guides for muftis (Adab al-Ifta, Fatawa Nawisi ke Rahnuma Usool) and studies on fiqh principles (Qamus al-Fiqh). They reflect the living tradition of fatwa in South Asia.
Many of these are by prominent scholars like Mufti Muhammad Taqi Uthmani and Mufti Khalid Sayf Allah al-Rahmani.
