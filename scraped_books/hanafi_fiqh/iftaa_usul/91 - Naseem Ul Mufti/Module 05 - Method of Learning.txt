Hierarchy of Imam Muhammad's Six Books and the Authority of Sarakhsi's Mabsut
Establishes the order of priority among Imam Muhammad's six books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the order of priority among the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah and apply the rule that the mu'tamad is the latest composition, and that Sarakhsi's Mabsut is the reliable commentary whose contrary is not acted upon.

Core Idea
The later the composition, the higher the authority: al-Siyar al-Kabir is mu'tamad; Sarakhsi's Mabsut is the ultimate reference.

This lesson introduces a foundational rule for Hanafi muftis: how to prioritize sources when texts conflict. The passage from Naseem al-Mufti outlines the hierarchy among Imam Muhammad's six canonical works and then establishes Sarakhsi's Mabsut as the most authoritative commentary on the collection al-Kafi. Understanding this order prevents incorrect fatwa and ensures the mufti's ruling aligns with the school's final positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six books and their composition order
List the six books: al-Mabsut (al-Asl), al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat, al-Siyar al-Kabir. Note that al-Mabsut was composed first, then al-Jami' al-Saghir, and al-Siyar al-Kabir last.
Al-Mabsut is also called al-Asl, but it is the earliest, not the most authoritative.
2
Apply the rule: later composition takes precedence
The passage explicitly says: al-Jami' al-Saghir (composed after al-Mabsut) takes precedence over al-Asl. The last composed, al-Siyar al-Kabir, is the mu'tamad (relied-upon).
This is contrary to natural assumption that the foundational text is strongest.
3
Recognize al-Kafi as a collection and its commentary
Al-Kafi by al-Hakim al-Shahid collects the six books. Its strongest commentary is al-Mabsut by Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi, which is likened to the sun.
Sarakhsi's Mabsut is a commentary on al-Kafi, not to be confused with Imam Muhammad's al-Mabsut.
4
Understand the absolute reliability of Sarakhsi's Mabsut
The passage states that opinions contrary to Sarakhsi's Mabsut are not acted upon, and one does not deviate from it. This establishes it as the ultimate reference for transmission of the school's positions.
This applies specifically to conflicts in transmitted rulings from the six books.
5
Apply the hierarchy in fatwa practice
When conflicts arise: first check al-Siyar al-Kabir (mu'tamad). If not, then al-Jami' al-Saghir, then al-Mabsut. For transmission accuracy, rely on Sarakhsi's Mabsut over other commentaries.
Always give weight to later formulations of Imam Muhammad.

The Four Bases of Tarjeeh in Hanafi Fatwa Methodology
Listing the four bases of tarjeeh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four bases of tarjeeh in Hanafi usul al-iftaa, rank them in order of priority, and apply them to determine the mufta bihi ruling when conflicting narrations exist.

Core Idea
Tarjeeh follows a hierarchy: personality, transmission strength, book type, and evidence.

The Hanafi madhhab is rich with multiple narrations on numerous issues. When a mufti faces conflicting reports, he must employ the science of tarjeeh—giving preference to one ruling over another based on established principles. This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti outlines the four fundamental bases that guide preference, ensuring consistency and authority in fatwa issuance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Recognize that multiple narrations exist on the same issue. The mufti must determine which one to adopt for fatwa.
The passage assumes a scenario where two or more sayings are in conflict.
2
Apply the personality base
First, check the ranks of the jurists transmitting the rulings. The saying of a higher-ranking jurist is preferred over a lower-ranking one.
Hanafi jurists are stratified into tabaqat (classes); higher tabaqa means greater authority.
3
Apply the transmission base
Next, compare the strength of transmission. A ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Imam Muhammad) takes precedence over one from Nawadir (rare transmissions).
Zahir al-Riwayah is the backbone of the madhhab; its narrations are considered most reliable.
4
Apply the book type base
Then, consider the type of source. Rulings found in matn (core texts like Al-Quduri) are preferred over those in sharh (commentaries like Al-Hidayah) because matn authors are more selective in transmitting only the most trusted positions.
This base operates when the personality and transmission are otherwise comparable.
5
Apply the evidence base
Finally, evaluate the underlying evidence. A ruling based on istihsan (juristic preference) is preferred over one based on qiyas (analogy). Also, specific words of tarjeeh (e.g., 'asahh' vs 'sahih') indicate preference.
This is the lowest base; earlier bases take priority if they yield a decisive preference.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the four bases upon which preference (tarjeeh) is given among conflicting narrations in the Hanafi madhhab. The first basis is the personality of the jurist: higher-ranking jurists' sayings are preferred over lower-ranking ones. The second basis is the strength of transmission: narrations from Zahir al-Riwayah (the core six books of Imam Muhammad) are preferred over Nawadir (rare transmissions). The third basis is the type of book: matn (core texts) are preferred over sharh (commentaries) because the authors of matn are more selective. The fourth basis is evidence: sayings based on istihsan (juristic preference) are preferred over those based on qiyas (analogy), and also the specific words of tarjeeh used by jurists (like 'sahih' vs 'asahh') indicate preference.

Key Terms
ترجیح (tarjeeh): The process of giving preference to one legal ruling over another based on established principles.
ظاہر الروایہ (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat).
نوادر (Nawadir): Rare or infrequent transmissions, often collected by later scholars or from less authoritative sources.
متن (matn): A core text of the madhhab, such as al-Quduri or al-Mukhtar, which typically contains the relied-upon rulings.
شرح (sharh): A commentary on a matn, often including multiple views and explanations.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference, a method of giving priority to a ruling that is more equitable or fitting.
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning, extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a shared effective cause.
اصح (asahh): A term used by late Hanafi jurists to indicate that a particular narration is the most sound (sahih) among conflicting ones.

Introduction to the Six Principles of Book-Based Preference
Introduction to book-based preference principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the role of book authority in preferring among Hanafi opinions and apply the rule to identify reliable sources for fatwa.

Core Idea
The authority of the source book governs the preference of opinions in Hanafi fatwa.

This passage introduces a foundational principle: not all books in the Hanafi school carry equal weight. When a mufti faces conflicting opinions, the reliability of the book transmitting each opinion becomes a key factor. The author explains that some books are authoritative and systematically present the correct opinion, while others lack this rigor. Therefore, before issuing a fatwa, the mufti must evaluate the source book's authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem
What is the central issue addressed? The passage discusses how to prefer one Hanafi opinion over another when they conflict.
2
Understand the basis for preference
The preference is sometimes based on the books that transmit the opinions. Why are some books preferred? Because they are 'well-known in terms of authority' and their authors committed to the preponderant opinion.
3
Analyze the variation among books
Not all books include evidences and discussions. Some books are comprehensive, while others are concise. Recognize that the presence of legal reasoning (istidlal) and preference determination enhances a book's authority.

Hanafi vs. Shafi'i Classification of Textual Indications
Introducing the classification of textual indications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi four-fold classification of textual indication from the Shafi'i two-fold classification and explain the practical difference.

Core Idea
Hanafis analyze textual indication more finely into four categories, whereas Shafi'is merge them into two: explicit and implicit.

This lesson introduces a key usul taxonomy. The mufti must know which classification system he is working with. Hanafi usul distinguishes four types of textual indication: explicit text, textual implication, inferred meaning, and required meaning. Shafi'is and others recognize only two: explicit speech (manṭūq) and implicit meaning (mafhūm). This divergence affects how rulings are derived from primary texts.

Guided Path
1
List the four Hanafi types

2
Define each type briefly
Use the key terms: ʿibāra is explicit wording; ishāra is implication; dalāla is inferred via ʿilla; iqtiḍāʾ is presupposed meaning.
3
Note the Shafi'i alternative
Shafi'is have only two: manṭūq (explicit) and mafhūm (implicit).
4
Map the correspondence
Hanafi ʿibāra corresponds to Shafi'i manṭūq; the other three types correspond to Shafi'i mafhūm.
This mapping is general; some nuances exist.
5
Understand the practical impact
The Hanafi classification allows finer distinction, affecting how strong a derivation is considered (e.g., ʿibāra is strongest, iqtiḍāʾ is weakest).

Definition of Isharat al-Nass
Defining isharat al-nass with a Quranic example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify isharat al-nass from a textual ruling and explain how it necessarily follows from the intended ruling.

Core Idea
Isharat al-nass: a necessary implication of a ruling, not intended but inseparable.

In Hanafi usul, texts convey meanings at multiple levels. Isharat al-nass is a meaning that necessarily follows from the intended ruling, even if not intended. Understanding this helps the mufti derive rulings that are logically inseparable from the text.

Guided Path
1
State the formal definition
Read the definition: a meaning that is neither independently nor implicitly intended, but necessarily follows the intended ruling.
Focus on 'non-intent' and 'necessity'.
2
Identify the two components
The meaning is (a) not intended, and (b) necessary for the ruling. Without necessity, it is not isharah.
3
Analyze the Quranic example
From the verse (2:233), 'ibarat al-nass yields two intended meanings: father's maintenance and child's attribution to father. From the attribution, two necessary implications arise via isharah: exclusivity of maintenance and father's right over child's wealth.
Trace the logical necessity.
4
Contrast with 'ibarat al-nass
'Ibarat al-nass is intended; isharat al-nass is not intended but necessarily follows.
This distinction is crucial in fatwa derivation.
5
Recognize levels of reflection
Sometimes isharah is immediately clear; sometimes it requires light reflection.
Both are valid unless contradicted by stronger evidence.

Definition of isharat al-nass with example: The text indicates a meaning that is neither independently nor implicitly intended by the speech, but that meaning is necessary for the ruling for which the speech was brought; this is called isharat al-nass. Sometimes that necessary meaning is understood without reflection, sometimes after slight reflection. Its example is Allah's statement: 'And upon the father of the child is their provision and clothing in the customary manner' (Surah al-Baqarah, 233). By 'ibarat al-nass, two meanings are understood: that the maintenance of nursing mothers is the responsibility of the father, and that the child is attributed to the father, not the mother. Both are intended: the first independently, the second implicitly. From this second meaning, two additional meanings are understood by isharat al-nass: first, just as no one else shares in the child's attribution to the father, similarly no one else shares in the maintenance; second, the father has a right in the child's wealth and may spend from it according to his need.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-nass extracts a higher-intensity ruling from a lower-intensity one without reflection.

This passage introduces dalalat al-nass, a key Hanafi concept in textual interpretation. It explains how a text can imply a meaning beyond its literal words through evident priority reasoning. Iqtida' al-Nass: Definition and Application
Defining iqtida' al-nass as a missing necessary element for textual validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define iqtida' al-nass, state its condition (necessity for textual validity), and apply it to a Quranic verse by identifying the implied suppressed word.

Core Idea
Iqtida' al-nass supplies a suppressed word without which the text's meaning or legal effect fails.

One of the key tools in Hanafi usul is iqtida' al-nass, where the text implies a missing word crucial for its validity. This lesson unpacks its definition and shows how it operates, using the common example of the prohibition of dead animals (Quran 5:3). Understanding this concept prevents misinterpretation of texts and ensures accurate legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the definition
Begin by stating the definition of iqtida' al-nass exactly as given: the text's indication of a suppressed word upon which the validity of the text depends, legally or rationally.
Memorize the phrase: 'نص کا ایسے محذوف لفظ پر دلالت کرنا جس پر شرعا یا عقلا اس نص کی صحت و درستی کا مدار ہو'.
2
Identify the key condition
Note that the suppressed word must be necessary for the text's validity—either shar'an (legally) or 'aqlan (rationally). Without this word, the text would be meaningless, contradictory, or legally unsound.
Distinguish from other types of implication: here the need is strict necessity.
3
Recognize the consequence of omission
Understand that without assuming the suppressed word, the meaning of the text is 'not correctly understood' (معنی درست طور پر سمجھ میں نہ آتا ہو). This underscores the indispensability of the implied word.
The text would remain valid in form but its intended legal effect would be lost.
4
Apply to the given example
Take the Quranic verse from the source: 'حُرمَت عَلَيْكُمُ الْمَيْتَةُ وَالدَّمُ وَلَحْمُ...' (5:3). The typical completion is 'لحم الخنزير'. Identify the suppressed word: 'أكل' (eating). Argue that the prohibition is on consumption, not on the mere existence of these items.
Without 'أكل', the verse could be misread as prohibiting dead animals themselves in any context, which is not intended.
5
Confirm the necessity
Check that without 'أكل', the ruling would be legally invalid (e.g., prohibiting possessing dead animals for non-food purposes). Since the text's validity depends on this word, iqtida' al-nass is correctly applied.
The necessity can be rational ('aqlan) because the natural subject of prohibition related to dead animals is consumption, or legal (shar'an) because other texts indicate permissibility of uses like leather.
