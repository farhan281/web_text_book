Nine Causes of Unreliability of Books for Fatwa
Lists nine reasons for a book's unreliability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply each of the nine causes to evaluate a book's reliability for issuing fatwa.

Core Idea
A mufti must only use books from a known, trustworthy scholar, verified by muhaqqiqun, and free from errors or omissions.

Before issuing a fatwa, a mufti must ensure the source book is reliable. This lesson presents nine causes that render a book unreliable, as compiled by Hanafi jurists. Memorize these causes to build a foundational filter for selecting authoritative texts.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the nine causes
Learn the nine causes by heart: unknown author, weak opinions, extreme brevity, muhaqqiq declares book weak, muhaqqiq declares author weak, doubt in attribution, non-fiqh subject, rarity, many printing errors.
Use mnemonic: A, W, E, M, A, D, N, R, P.
2
Understand each cause
For each cause, ask: Why does this make a book unreliable? For example, an unknown author (majhul) cannot be trusted; weak opinions require cross-checking; extreme brevity omits necessary details.
The third cause (extremely brief) does not mean all short books are unreliable—only those that omit essential details.
3
Apply to a real scenario
Given a book, check its author, content, and history. If any cause applies, avoid using it for fatwa.
Multiple causes can combine; e.g., a rare book with many printing errors is doubly unreliable.
4
Prioritize the causes
Some causes are more serious than others. An unknown author or weak opinions are fundamental; printing errors may be less severe if corrected. A mufti must weigh them.
The judgment of a muhaqqiq (causes 4 and 5) is particularly decisive.

Necessity Allows Acting on Weak Opinion
Explains exception to the rule that weak opinions cannot be followed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a mufti may apply a weak opinion and distinguish between the general prohibition and the necessity exception.

Core Idea
Necessity (darurah) overrides the general prohibition against using weak opinions in fatwa and personal practice.

In Hanafi ifta, weak opinions are generally not to be acted upon or used for fatwa. However, the school recognizes an exception: in cases of genuine necessity, both the individual and the mufti may resort to a weak opinion to avoid hardship. This lesson examines the scope of this exception through classical texts.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Recall the established Hanafi principle: weak opinions are not permissible to follow or to issue as fatwa.
This rule preserves the authority of the rajih (preponderant) position.
2
Identify the exception
Locate where the text introduces the exception: 'Nevertheless, in a situation of constraint, permission is given to act upon it.'
The exception is explicitly tied to constraint (majburi) and necessity (darurah).
3
Analyze the supporting evidence
The passage cites Ibn Nujaym and Fakhr al-Islam, and Allamah Shami, confirming that a mufti may give fatwa based on weak opinions to facilitate ease.
These authorities are from later Hanafi scholarship, showing the development of the ruling.
4
Understand the scope
The exception applies to both personal action (the mudtarr) and giving fatwa for such a person. It does not apply in normal circumstances.
The general rule remains operative outside necessity.
5
Apply to the example
Work through the given case: a person holds his private part to avoid ghusl, which is against the rajih view but permitted under necessity.
This illustrates a practical scenario where the exception is invoked.

Commentary
This passage clarifies a key principle in the usul al-ifta: the general prohibition against acting on or issuing fatwas based on weak opinions has an exception in cases of necessity (darurah). The text provides a concrete example from Hanafi fiqh: a person who feels a strong sexual desire may hold his private part to prevent semen emission, thereby avoiding obligation of ghusl. Although this contradicts the preponderant (rajih) Hanafi view, it is permitted under constraint. The passage further supports this exception by citing Imam Fakhr al-Islam via Ibn Nujaym, indicating that a mufti may give a fatwa based on a weak opinion to facilitate ease in necessity. Finally, Allamah Shami concludes that both the individual in need and the mufti giving him a fatwa may act on the weak opinion, and that the general rule applies only in non-necessitous circumstances.

Key Terms
ضعیف قول (da'if qawl): An opinion within the Hanafi school that is not the preponderant (rajih) view; often based on weaker evidence or less authoritative transmission.
مجبوری / ضرورت (majburi / darurah): A state of constraint or necessity that relaxes normal prohibitions; in this context, it allows acting on a weak opinion.
مضطر (mudtarr): A person in a state of compulsion or distress, who is permitted to take a weak opinion.

Caution in Rulings of Kufr
Sets a methodological rule for muftis to avoid declaring kufr when possible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of caution in rulings of kufr by interpreting ambiguous statements in a non-kufr manner and by preferring the opinion that avoids declaring kufr when scholars differ.

Core Idea
A mufti must exhaust all possible good interpretations before declaring a Muslim a disbeliever.

This passage establishes a critical methodological rule for muftis: in matters of kufr, caution overrides even stronger evidence. As a mufti, you will encounter statements that appear to be kufr, but the Hanafi tradition requires you to first seek a valid interpretation that preserves the person's Islamic status. This lesson unpacks the two sub-rules laid out by Faqih al-Asr Maulana Khalid Saifullah Rahmani and the supporting authority of Allamah Shami.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Statement
Read the exact words uttered by the person. Note the context and any ambiguities.
Context includes tone, situation, and the person's background.
2
Search for Good Interpretations
Brainstorm all plausible meanings that do not entail kufr. These could be based on linguistic possibility, excuse (joking, anger, ignorance), or intention.
If even a weak interpretation exists, it must be adopted per the rule.
3
Adopt the Non-Kufr Interpretation
If a good interpretation is found, the mufti must rule that the statement is not kufr and the person remains Muslim.
This applies even if the literal meaning or common understanding suggests kufr.
4
Handle Scholarly Disagreement
If scholars differ on whether the statement constitutes kufr, the mufti must give preference to the opinion that avoids declaring kufr, even if the opposing opinion is stronger in evidence.
Ihtiyat (caution) takes precedence over qiyas or majority.
5
Document the Authority
Cite the sources: Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, Muqaddimah Kitab al-Fatawa, and the quote from Allamah Shami via Ibn Nujaym.
This roots the fatwa in the Hanafi tradition.

(Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, 180) Faqih al-Asr Maulana Khalid Saifullah Rahmani writes in response to a question: The matter of kufr is very delicate, therefore great caution must be exercised in ruling something as kufr. This passage articulates a foundational rule for muftis: in matters as grave as declaring someone an apostate, caution is paramount. The author reinforces this by quoting Allamah Shami from Ibn Nujaym, emphasizing that as long as a good interpretation is possible or there is scholarly disagreement, a fatwa of kufr must not be issued, even if the supporting narration is weak. This reflects the Hanafi principle of preserving the Muslim status of a person unless absolutely certain.

احتیاط (ihtiyat): Legal caution; principle of avoiding harm or error by choosing the safer path.

Expert Mufti May Follow Weak Opinion Based on Ijtihad
Rule on mufti's authority to follow weak opinion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a Hanafi mufti may follow a weak opinion within the madhhab, and distinguish between a dhi ra'y mufti and a common person in this regard.

Core Idea
A mufti with ra'y must follow a weak opinion if its evidence is stronger; a common person cannot.

In Hanafi iftaa, not all opinions carry equal weight. This lesson clarifies a crucial rule from Rasm al-Mufti: a mufti who is dhi ra'y – capable of evaluating evidence – may prefer a weak opinion if he finds its proof stronger. For a common person, however, this is not allowed. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for proper fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify who is dhi ra'y
Determine the mufti's level: he must be expert in the madhhab and able to evaluate the strength of evidence (dalil). This is not a full mujtahid but someone capable of partial ijtihad (tajzi' ijtihad).
The term 'dhi ra'y' literally means 'possessor of opinion' – here technical nuance.
2
Apply the condition for following a weak opinion
The mufti must find the weak opinion (da'if qawl) to have stronger evidence than the normally preferred view. Then it becomes necessary (lazim) for him to act on it in his own practice.
It is not optional once he is convinced of its strength.
3
Distinguish the common person's restriction
The passage restricts this permission to dhi ra'y only. A common layperson (ammi) cannot follow a weak opinion because they lack the ability to weigh evidence. Their duty is to follow the mufti's fatwa or the prevalent opinion of the madhhab.
This is based on the commentary of Allamah Biri.

Commentary
This passage presents a third scenario—when a mufti is sufficiently expert (dhi ra'y) that he can evaluate the strength of evidence underlying different opinions within the Hanafi school. Such a mufti may follow a weak opinion if, in his judgment, its proof is stronger than the one normally considered rajih. This is based on the concept of tajzi' ijtihad (partial ijtihad), meaning that even a non-absolute mujtahid can exercise ijtihad within his madhhab. The passage then cites Allamah Biri's commentary on al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, which clarifies that this permission is for the mufti himself in his personal practice, and it is not extended to the common layperson who cannot weigh evidence. The necessity of acting according to his own ijtihad is emphasized: once he determines the stronger view, he must follow it.

Key Terms
ذی رائے (dhi ra'y): A mufti who has the intellectual capacity to evaluate evidence and prefer one opinion over another within the madhhab; not a fully independent mujtahid but capable of partial ijtihad (tajzi').
اجتہاد میں تجزی (ijtihad mein tajzi): Partial ijtihad; the ability to exercise ijtihad in specific issues without being an absolute mujtahid.
ضعیف قول (da'if qawl): A weaker opinion in the madhhab, here considered valid for a dhi ra'y mufti if he finds its evidence stronger.
