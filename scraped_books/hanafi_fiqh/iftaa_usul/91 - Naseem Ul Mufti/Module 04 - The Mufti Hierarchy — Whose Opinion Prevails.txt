Levels of Jurists and Masail in Hanafi Fatwa
Classifying jurists into seven levels and Hanafi masail into three levels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate the seven levels of Hanafi jurists and the three levels of Hanafi masail, and apply this hierarchy to determine the authority of a legal opinion.

Core Idea
The hierarchy of jurists and masail determines which rulings carry greater weight in Hanafi fatwa.

A mufti must know not only the rulings but also their pedigree. This lesson presents two fundamental classifications: the seven levels of jurists (from absolute mujtahid to pure muqallid) and the three levels of masail (from the most authoritative Zahir al-Riwayah to later fatwas and takhrijat). Understanding these hierarchies is essential for weighing evidence and issuing sound fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two classifications
The passage presents the levels of jurists and the levels of masail. Note that these are distinct but related. The mufti must be familiar with both.
2
Trace the levels of jurists
List the seven levels from highest to lowest: Mujtahidun fi al-shar' (absolute), Mujtahidun fi al-madhhab, Mujtahidun fi al-masa'il, Ashab al-takhrij, Ashab al-tarjih, Ashab al-tamyiz, Muqallid mahd. Understand the role and scope of each.
Ibn Kamal Pasha's classification is standard.
3
Trace the levels of masail
List the three levels in descending authority: Zahir al-Riwayah (usul), Ghayr Zahir al-Riwayah (Nawadir), and Fatawa waqi'at takhrijat mashayikh.
Zahir al-Riwayah comes from Imam Muhammad's six books.
4
Relate the two hierarchies
Understand that a fatwa should rely on the highest available source: prefer rulings from higher-level jurists and from more authoritative levels of masail.
For example, a ruling from a mujtahid mutlaq in Zahir al-Riwayah takes precedence over a later fatwa.
5
Apply the hierarchy in a hypothetical case
Given a query, determine which sources to prioritize. If a ruling is found in a higher level, it should be followed unless overridden by an equally or more authoritative source.
This exercise develops practical fatwa methodology.

Hierarchy of Zahir al-Riwayah and the Authority of al-Mabsut
Hierarchy rule and supreme authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchy principle 'later composition is relied upon', apply it to rank the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah, and explain why al-Mabsut is the supreme authority in Hanafi iftaa.

Core Idea
In Hanafi iftaa, later compositions by the Imam supersede earlier ones; al-Mabsut is the ultimate authority.

A mufti must know which source to prioritize when different books offer conflicting rulings. This lesson unpacks a key principle from Rasm al-Mufti: among the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah, the later composition takes precedence. We also learn that al-Mabsut by al-Sarakhsi is the most reliable commentary of al-Kafi, and no fatwa may contradict it. Mastering this hierarchy ensures consistency and authority in fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six books and their order
List the books mentioned: al-Asl, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, and the compendium al-Kafi which gathers the six. Note that al-Siyar al-Kabir is the last composition.
The passage only names al-Asl, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, and al-Kafi. The other three are part of the six but not named here.
2
Understand the precedence rule
The text states: 'the earlier in composition is later in precedence' meaning the latest book (al-Siyar al-Kabir) is the most authoritative. Al-Jami' al-Saghir takes precedence over al-Asl.
This is encapsulated in the qa'ida: 'al-muta'akhkhir min tasnif al-imam huwa al-mu'tamad'.
3
Grasp the special status of al-Mabsut
Al-Kafi by al-Hakim al-Shahid compiles the six books. Its strongest commentary is al-Mabsut by al-Sarakhsi, which is 'reliable in transmissions' and no action is taken contrary to it.
Al-Mabsut is considered the ultimate reference for fatwa derivation in the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the hierarchy in a fatwa scenario
If a ruling in al-Asl differs from one in al-Jami' al-Saghir, the mufti follows al-Jami' al-Saghir. If al-Siyar al-Kabir differs from both, it takes precedence. If any source contradicts al-Mabsut, al-Mabsut prevails.
This step internalizes the practical consequence for iftaa.

Hierarchy of Hanafi Imams in Takhrij
Establishing the hierarchy of companions for fatwa selection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the hierarchical order of Hanafi imams (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar, Ibn Ziyad) to determine the mufta bihi opinion when no direct statement from Abu Hanifa exists.

Core Idea
When Abu Hanifa's own choice is unknown, follow Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar and Ibn Ziyad.

This passage from Naseem al-Mufti lays out the foundational rule for a Hanafi mufti when the direct preferred opinion (mukhtar) of Imam Abu Hanifa is not recorded. It teaches us that all valid opinions originate from the Imam himself, and his senior disciples simply transmitted and selected from his vast corpus. The hierarchy is strict: Abu Yusuf takes precedence, then Muhammad al-Shaybani, then Zufar and Hasan ibn Ziyad. Understanding this chain is essential for correct fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of all opinions
Note that the passage emphasizes all narrations (riwayat) come from Abu Hanifa. His companions (rifaq) only select from among these narrations; they do not introduce independent rulings.
This establishes that the madhhab is unified under the Imam's authority.
2
Determine when the hierarchy applies
The hierarchy applies when no explicit choice (ikhtiyar) from Abu Hanifa is found for a particular issue.
If the Imam's own mukhtar is known, that is followed directly.
3
Apply the first level: Abu Yusuf
If no direct statement from Abu Hanifa, the opinion of Abu Yusuf (Ya'qub al-Ansari) is the preferred one (mukhtar) for fatwa.
4
Apply subsequent levels
If neither Abu Hanifa's nor Abu Yusuf's choice is known, then follow Muhammad al-Shaybani, then Zufar, then Ibn Ziyad in that order.
This is a strict sequence: each lower level is only consulted when the higher is unavailable.
5
Recognize the binding nature
The passage states that no one outside these disciples has a say; the companions swore that all statements belong to Abu Hanifa. This makes the hierarchy binding for the mufti.
This prevents reliance on later scholars' personal ijtihad beyond transmission.

Ninth Principle: Following Later Scholars
Procedure when no opinion exists from Imam A'zam or his students

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ninth rasm al-mufti principle to determine the operative opinion when no transmission exists from the earliest Hanafi authorities.

Core Idea
When no opinion from Imam A'zam or his students exists, follow later scholars—unanimous if possible, otherwise the most senior.

This principle governs what a mufti should do when a matter has no direct transmission from the earliest authorities of the school. It ensures continuity and authority by directing the mufti first to later scholars' consensus, and if not, to the most eminent among them. By mastering this rule, you will know how to navigate cases beyond the explicit corpus of the early school.

Guided Path
1
Identify absence of early transmission
The mufti must first ascertain whether Imam Abu Hanifa or his direct students (e.g., Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani) have any narrated opinion on the matter. This requires thorough knowledge of the major works of the school.
Absence includes both explicit silence and lack of reliable transmission.
2
Check for later scholars' consensus
Look to the later scholars (mashayikh muta'akhkhirin) for a unanimous ruling. If they all agree, that ruling becomes binding (mufta bihi).
Consensus here refers to the agreed position among the well-known later jurists.
3
Identify senior most scholars if disputed
If the later scholars differ, the mufti must defer to the opinion of the most senior and famous among them, such as Imam Tahawi, Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi, or Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi.
Seniority is determined by scholarly rank, not mere chronological order; these figures are the pillars of the later school.
4
Apply the selected opinion for fatwa
The mufti issues the fatwa based on the chosen opinion, whether unanimous or from a senior scholar. This ensures the ruling is rooted in the school's authoritative tradition.
This principle avoids arbitrary selection and maintains the school's internal consistency.
5
Document the tarjih reasoning
In the written fatwa, the mufti should briefly note the basis for following the later scholars, especially in case of dispute, to show the tarjih mechanism.
This practice, though not always required, is recommended for transparency and future reference.

If they differ, the mufti follows the opinion of the most eminent among them, such as Imam Tahawi, Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi, or al-Karkhi. This principle ensures continuity and authority as the school develops over time.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ghair Zahir al-Riwayah rulings are authoritative but secondary; a mufti must know their place in the hierarchy.

In the previous lesson, you learned about Zahir al-Riwayah—the six core books of Imam Muhammad. But what happens when a ruling is not found in those books? The Hanafi school has a second tier of transmitted rulings called Ghair Zahir al-Riwayah. This lesson will help you classify these transmissions and understand their relative authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Categories

These collections are named after the place or person associated with their compilation.
3

Understand that Ghair Zahir al-Riwayah is less authoritative than Zahir al-Riwayah. A mufti should first look for a ruling in Zahir al-Riwayah; if none exists, then turn to Ghair Zahir al-Riwayah, and within that, prefer the stronger transmissions over the nawadir.
The nawadir are the weakest and used only when no other source is available.

The Third Class: Fatawa, Waqa'i, and Takhrijat
Defines the third tier of Hanafi rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the third class of Hanafi rulings and explain how later jurists' fatwas remain within the madhhab despite differing from earlier authorities.

Core Idea
Later Hanafi jurists' ijtihad-derived fatwas are valid and integral to the madhhab, even when they differ from the founding imams.

In the previous classes, we covered the rulings from the founding imams and the Sahibayn. But what happens when a new case arises that is not explicitly covered in their works? The Hanafi school recognizes a third tier: the fatwas and rulings of later jurists (mujtahidun muta'akhkhirun). This lesson introduces that tier, its sources, and its authority within the school.

Guided Path
1
Define the Third Class
Identify the three components that constitute this class: Fatawa, Waqa'i, and Takhrijat of the Mashayikh. Understand that these are rulings derived by later scholars when they did not find an explicit answer in the books of the earlier imams.
Fatawa refers to answers given to specific questions; Waqa'i are actual cases; Takhrijat is the process of deriving rulings from the school's principles.
2
Identify the Jurists
Note that the third-class jurists are the direct or indirect students of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). The passage lists key figures: 'Isam ibn Yusuf, Ibrahim ibn Rustam, Muhammad ibn Sam'ah, Abu Sulayman al-Juzjani, Abu Hafs al-Bukhari, and later students like Muhammad ibn Muqatil.
Their large number shows the school's living tradition of ijtihad.
3
Understand the Principle of Disagreement
The passage states that these later scholars sometimes oppose the foundational scholars based on evidence (istidlal), yet their statements are not considered outside the madhhab. This is a key principle: as long as they derive their rulings from the school's principles, their differences are part of the school.
This distinguishes the Hanafi approach from rigid adherence to only the earliest opinions.
4
Apply the Fatwa Hierarchy
Now that you know the third class exists, learn how it fits into the fatwa hierarchy. For a mufti today, these later fatwas can be the operative opinion (mufta bihi) if they are sound and derived correctly.
The source_authority is fatawa_mutakhkhirin; the tarjih mechanism is that later ijthad within the school is valid if it follows the school's usul.

Dr. Sa'id Bakdash, the son-in-law of Shaykh 'Awwamah (who has carried out research and annotations on over a dozen books of Hanafi fiqh), holds the opinion that the term 'Hanafi fiqh' applies only to the statements of Imam Abu Hanifa, because in the expressions of the early Hanafi jurists there is no such explicit indication that the statements of the Sahibayn are included in the application of 'Hanafi fiqh'. On the contrary, in the books and style of composition of the early scholars, there are many indications that the statements of the Sahibayn are not included in Hanafi fiqh. For example, in al-Zahir al-Riwayah, Imam Muhammad's style is that he uses the word 'madhhab' separately for the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf?) and for his own statement, and he presents the statements of each in a comparative manner. Also, in al-Mukhtar of 'Allamah al-Mawsili and Kanz al-Daqa'iq of Imam al-Nasafi, only the statements of Imam (Abu Hanifa) are included in Hanafi fiqh. Besides, in other texts as well, they adhered to declaring the statement of the Imam as the preponderant (rajih), although in some issues they deviated to the statement of the Sahibayn on the basis of custom and necessity. But such deviation occurs in every school, it does not necessitate that the statements of the Sahibayn are part of Hanafi fiqh. (Taken from 'Takwin al-Madhhab al-Hanafi ma'a Ta'mmulat fi Dawabit al-Mufta bihi' by Sa'id Bakdash). According to researcher Sa'id Bakdash, the seed of including the Sahibayn's statements in Hanafi fiqh and thereby correcting and preponderating between the statements of the Sahibayn and Imam Sahib was sown by the author of al-Hidayah. He adopted the style of mixing the statements of the Sahibayn and Imam Sahib in various issues and then correcting and preponderating among the three imams. After the author of al-Hidayah, Qadi Khan in Fatawa Qadi Khan, 'Allamah Ibn al-Humam in Fath al-Qadir, Ibn Nujaym in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, and Ibn 'Abidin in his Hashiyah Radd al-Muhtar adopted the same style. Thus, instead of the style of the early scholars, the style of these late scholars became the actual basis, and today the situation is that the statements of the Sahibayn are considered a part of Hanafi fiqh, and issuing fatwa according to their statements is not considered a departure from the madhhab, whereas this is completely contrary to the style of the early scholars and the authors of matn and mukhtasar works. (Taken from 'Takwin al-Madhhab al-Hanafi...' by Sa'id Bakdash).

Commentary
This passage presents a significant challenge to the common understanding of the Hanafi madhhab. Dr. Sa'id Bakdash argues that the early Hanafi authorities did not consider the opinions of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) as part of the Hanafi school proper. Rather, they treated them as separate madhhabs or opinions. He cites examples from works like al-Mukhtar and Kanz al-Daqa'iq that only mention Imam Abu Hanifa's view. He then critiques later scholars, starting with the author of al-Hidayah, for mixing the three imams' views and thereby implicitly including them in the school. This led to the current practice where fatwa can be given according to any of the three imams without being considered leaving the madhhab. Bakdash views this as a deviation from the early method. This perspective is important for students of Hanafi fiqh to understand the historical development of the school's boundaries.

Key Terms
متقدمین (mutqaddimin): Early Hanafi scholars of the first few centuries, whose works are considered foundational.
متاخرین (muta'akhkhirin): Later Hanafi scholars beginning from the author of al-Hidayah and onwards.
صاحبین (sahibayn): The two companions of Imam Abu Hanifa: Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.
راجح (rajih): Preponderant or stronger opinion within the school.
عدول (udul): Deviation from the normal practice, here from the Imam's statement to the Sahibayn's statement due to custom or necessity.
