Definition of Mantuq and Its Components
Defines mantuq and its components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of mantuq according to Hanafis and distinguish mantuq from mafhum.

Core Idea
Mantuq is direct textual indication; Hanafis uniquely include 'ibarah, isharah, and iqtida' under it.

This lesson introduces the foundational concept of mantuq in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. Understanding mantuq is critical for a mufti because it determines how a ruling is extracted directly from the text. The Hanafi school classifies textual indications differently from other schools, which directly affects fatwa derivation.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the definition of mantuq
Mantuq is the meaning directly indicated by the words of a text. Read the Arabic definition carefully.
2
List the three components of mantuq
The three types are: 'ibarah an-nass (explicit meaning), isharah an-nass (implied meaning), and iqtida' an-nass (required meaning). Memorise them.
These are all considered mantuq by Hanafis.
3
Distinguish mantuq from mafhum
Hanafis call implied meaning (mafhum) as 'dalalah an-nass', which is separate from mantuq. Understand the terminology difference.
Other schools may classify mafhum differently.
4
Apply to a Quranic verse
Take the verse 'وَأَحَلَّ اللَّهُ الْبَيْعَ' and identify which type of mantuq it represents. It is 'ibarah an-nass because the ruling is explicitly stated.

Understanding Mafhum and Mafhum Muwafiq in Hanafi Usul
Definition and classification of mafhum with Hanafi terminology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mafhum, distinguish between mafhum muwafiq and mafhum mukhalif, and apply fahwa al-khitab and lahn al-khitab to derive rulings from Quranic texts.

Core Idea
Mafhum muwafiq is a direct textual implication, not analogy, and is binding in Hanafi usul.

This lesson unpacks the concept of mafhum (implied meaning) in Hanafi legal theory. You will learn how the Qur'an and Sunnah convey rulings not only explicitly (mantuq) but also implicitly (mafhum). The focus is on mafhum muwafiq, where the unmentioned case shares the same ruling, either with greater reason (fahwa al-khitab) or equal reason (lahn al-khitab). Understanding this tool is essential for a mufti to extract rulings correctly without resorting to arbitrary reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Define mafhum and its scope
Read the definition: mafhum is the meaning that the text indicates without being the direct object of the speech. Hanafis call this dalalah an-nass. Note that it is not explicit (mantuq) but is nonetheless binding.
Remember: mafhum is not qiyas; it's a direct implication from the text.
2
Distinguish mafhum muwafiq from mafhum mukhalif
Identify the two categories: mafhum muwafiq (same ruling carried to unmentioned case) and mafhum mukhalif (opposite ruling applies). The lesson covers only muwafiq; mukhalif is mentioned for contrast.
Mafhum muwafiq is also called tanbih al-khitab.
3
Analyze the two subdivisions of mafhum muwafiq
Break down fahwa al-khitab (a fortiori: greater reason) and lahn al-khitab (equal degree). Use the Quranic examples: fahwa – prohibition of striking parents from 'uff'; lahn – threat for burning orphans' wealth from consuming it.
Fahwa is like qiyas awla; lahn is like qiyas musawi.
4
Apply the concept to a new scenario
Given a new text, determine if it yields mafhum muwafiq and which type. For instance, from 'Do not withhold testimony' (Q 2:283), derive that false testimony is also prohibited, likely as lahn al-khitab (equal degree).
Check if the unmentioned case shares the same 'illah.

Definition of Mafhum: The text indicating a meaning for which the speech was not intended, but that meaning is understood from the speech. In other words, mafhum is the meaning that the words of the text do not directly indicate, but that meaning is derived from the speech. According to the Hanafis, mafhum is what is called dalalah an-nass. Types of Mafhum: Mafhum has two types: mafhum muwafiq and mafhum mukhalif. Holding that the same ruling expressed in mantuq also applies to the unmentioned case is called mafhum muwafiq. Holding that the opposite of the ruling expressed in mantuq applies to the unmentioned case is called mafhum mukhalif. Mafhum muwafiq is also called tanbih al-khitab, and mafhum mukhalif is called dalil al-khitab. Categories of Mafhum Muwafiq: Then mafhum muwafiq also has two types: fahwa al-khitab and lahn al-khitab. Fahwa al-khitab is that the ruling of mantuq applies to the unmentioned case with greater reason. For example, Allah's saying 'Do not say 'uff' to them' implies the prohibition of striking parents with greater reason. Lahn al-khitab is that the ruling applies equally. For example, Allah's saying 'Indeed, those who consume the wealth of orphans unjustly...' (4:10) threatens consuming orphans' wealth, but from this verse the threat for burning orphans' wealth is also understood, because the purpose is prohibition of destroying orphans' wealth, and destruction in both cases is of the same degree. So both rulings are established equally.

Commentary
This passage defines mafhum (implied meaning) as understood by Hanafis. It clarifies that mafhum is not the direct meaning but is derived from the text. Hanafis use the term 'dalalah an-nass' for mafhum. The passage then divides mafhum into two categories: mafhum muwafiq (consonant implication) and mafhum mukhalif (dissonant implication). In mafhum muwafiq, the ruling of the mentioned case applies to the unmentioned case either with greater reason (fahwa al-khitab) or equally (lahn al-khitab). Two Quranic examples illustrate these: the prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents implies prohibition of striking them (greater reason), and the threat for consuming orphans' wealth implies the same threat for burning it (equal degree).

لحن الخطاب (lahn al-khitab): Implication where the unmentioned case is equal in reason for the ruling

Mafhum Mukhalif and Mafhum al-Sifah
Lists and defines the well-known types of mafhum mukhalif

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mafhum mukhalif, identify its well-known types, and apply mafhum al-sifah to derive the opposite ruling.

Core Idea
When a ruling is tied to an attribute, the opposite ruling applies when that attribute is absent.

In usul al-fiqh, texts often imply more than they say. Mafhum mukhalif (dissonant implication) allows us to derive a ruling for an unmentioned case by considering the restriction in the expressed text. This lesson introduces the major types of mafhum mukhalif and focuses on the first type: mafhum al-sifah (implication from an attribute). You will learn how to identify when an attribute is legally effective and apply the opposite ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept
Understand that mafhum mukhalif is the opposite implication of the mantuq. The mantuq is what is explicitly stated; the mafhum is what is understood for the unmentioned case.
2
List the five types
From the passage, enumerate: mafhum al-sifah (attribute), mafhum al-shart (condition), mafhum al-ghayah (limit), mafhum al-adad (number), and mafhum al-laqab (proper noun).
These are the well-known types in Hanafi usul.
3
Define mafhum al-sifah
The ruling is restricted to an attribute. When that attribute is absent, the opposite ruling applies. The definition requires that the attribute be meaningful and not incidental.
The legal_definition_dissector block breaks this down into hukm, mahall, sabab, shart, mani, athar.
4
Work through the example
Consider the hadith: 'In grazing sheep, zakat is obligatory.' Mantuq: zakat wajib on grazing sheep. Attribute: grazing (sa'imah). Mafhum: non-grazing (stall-fed) implies zakat is not wajib.
This is a classic Hanafi example for zakat.
5
Note the condition for hujjiyyah
Mafhum al-sifah is hujjah only if the attribute is legally effective (not merely for praise or blame). If the attribute is incidental, the mafhum does not hold.
This distinguishes Hanafi acceptance from broader acceptance in other schools.

Types of Mafhum Mukhalif: Mafhum mukhalif has several types, among which the following are well-known: mafhum al-sifah (attribute), mafhum al-shart (condition), mafhum al-ghayah (limit), mafhum al-adad (number), and mafhum al-laqab (proper noun). Definition of Mafhum al-Shart with Example
Defines mafhum al-shart with a Quranic example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of mafhum al-shart in a given text and apply it to derive the opposite ruling for the silent case.

Core Idea
When a ruling is tied to a condition, the condition's absence implies the opposite ruling—this is mafhum al-shart.

In Hanafi usul, understanding implied meanings (mafahim) is essential for deriving rulings from texts. Mafhum al-shart is one type where the explicit condition governs not only the stated case but also the unstated opposite. This lesson defines it and illustrates with a Quranic example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition structure
Break down the definition: 'Restricting the ruling of the explicit text with a condition leads to understanding that when that condition is not found, the opposite ruling applies for the silent case.' Identify the mantiq (explicit ruling), the condition, and the maskut anhu (silent case).
The opposite ruling is the logical contrary of the explicit ruling.
2
Analyze the Quranic example
Look at the verse: 'And if they are pregnant, then spend on them.' Identify the condition (pregnancy) and the explicit ruling (maintenance). Determine the silent case (when pregnancy is absent).
The text is silent on non-pregnant divorced women.
3
Apply the definition to the example
From the condition, derive the opposite ruling: since maintenance is tied to pregnancy, the absence of pregnancy implies no maintenance. This is the mafhum al-shart.
Verify that the derived ruling is the opposite of the mantiq.
4
Consider limitations
Mafhum al-shart is only valid when the condition is truly restrictive and not merely descriptive or emphatic. If the condition is for description, the opposite may not hold.
This is a key point of dispute between schools.

Definition of Mafhum al-Shart with example: Restricting the ruling of the explicit text (mantiq) with a condition leads to understanding that when that condition is not found, the opposite of the ruling of the explicit text will be established for the silent case. This is called mafhum al-shart. For example, Allah's saying: "And if they are pregnant, then spend on them" (At-Talaq: 2). The explicit text of this verse is that maintenance of a divorced pregnant woman is obligatory upon the husband. And as for a divorced woman who is not pregnant, whether maintenance is obligatory upon the husband or not, the text is silent about it. But by restricting maintenance with the condition of pregnancy, we understand that maintenance of a non-pregnant divorced woman is not obligatory upon the husband. This implied meaning is called mafhum al-shart.

This passage defines mafhum al-shart, one of the types of mafhum mukhalif in Hanafi usul. The condition is pregnancy. Therefore, the mafhum al-shart tells us that if she is not pregnant, no maintenance is due. This reasoning is used by Hanafi jurists in deriving rulings from texts.

Defines mafhum al-laqab and illustrates with hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mafhum al-laqab, distinguish it from other types of mafhum, and apply it to a hadith to identify the implied ruling.

Core Idea
Mafhum al-laqab infers the opposite from a non-derived noun, but Hanafis reject it as a valid proof.

In this lesson, we break down the definition of mafhum al-laqab—a type of implied meaning based on a concrete, non-derived noun. Using the hadith 'Fi al-ghanam zakah,' we will see how some scholars would infer that zakah is not due on other animals, and why the Hanafi school does not accept this inference as authoritative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the manṭūq (explicit ruling)
Look at the explicit text: the hadith states that zakah is obligatory on sheep/goats. This is the clear, spoken ruling.
2
Locate the laqab (non-derived noun)
The word 'ghanam' (sheep/goats) is a concrete noun, not derived from a verb. It is a name for a specific category.
A laqab is a fixed label, not a description like 'al-muslim.'
3
Understand the mafhum al-laqab claim
Mafhum al-laqab asserts that because the ruling is attached to this particular noun, the opposite ruling applies to all other categories. Thus, no zakah on cows, camels, etc.
4
Apply to the hadith example
Take the hadith 'Fi al-ghanam zakah.' If one accepts mafhum al-laqab, they deduce that zakah is NOT wajib on any animal besides sheep/goats.
5
Note the Hanafi position
Hanafis do not accept mafhum al-laqab as a valid proof in shari'ah, because a specific name alone does not restrict the ruling unless evidence shows it was meant restrictively.
This is a point of ikhtilaf with other madhāhib.

Valid and Invalid Custom in Shari'ah

Core Idea
Only custom that does not contradict shari'ah is authoritative; invalid custom has no legal weight.

Custom (urf) plays a significant role in Islamic jurisprudence, but not every custom is legally binding. The Hanafi school distinguishes between valid custom that aligns with shari'ah and invalid custom that opposes it. In this lesson, we will unpack this classification, understand the underlying maxim 'al-aadah muhakkamah', and learn to identify which customs hold authority in Islamic law.

This is the shart (condition) in the legal definition.
3
State the Condition for Invalid Custom

Commentary
This passage defines the two fundamental types of 'urf (custom) in Islamic law: valid custom ('urf sahih) and invalid custom ('urf fasid). The classification is based on whether the custom contradicts the texts of shari'ah and its established principles. Valid custom is that which does not oppose any shari'ah text or principle, and therefore can be considered authoritative. Invalid custom is that which goes against the revealed law, such as customs that make permissible what is clearly forbidden (e.g., drinking alcohol) or make forbidden what is permissible. The author emphasizes that shari'ah only gives weight to valid custom; invalid custom has no legal force. This distinction is foundational for the maxim 'al-aadah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative), which applies only to valid custom.
