The Tenth Principle: Imam A'zam's Opinion in Ibadat
Preference for Imam A'zam's famous opinion in acts of worship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a fatwa using the tenth principle, identifying when to apply Imam A'zam's famous opinion and when to follow the tarjih of ashab al-tarjih in acts of worship.

Core Idea
In ibadat, Imam A'zam's famous opinion is default, but ashab al-tarjih can override if they prefer another of his sayings adopted by his students.

This lesson introduces a key principle for muftis when issuing fatwas on matters of worship (ibadat). While the school generally gives precedence to Imam A'zam's widely transmitted (mashhur) opinion, there is a carefully defined exception. Understanding this principle ensures your fatwas align with the school's established methodology and the authority of later tarjih scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Rule
First, recognize that the default in ibadat is to prefer Imam A'zam's famous (mashhur) opinion over those of his companions or other transmissions.
This applies across salah, sawm, zakat, hajj, and similar acts.
2
Recognize the Exception
Next, note the exception: if the ashab al-tarjih (scholars of preferment) deem another opinion of Imam A'zam to be preponderant, and that opinion was adopted by his students, then it becomes the mufta bih.
The alternative must still be a saying of Imam A'zam, not of another mujtahid.
3
Understand the Role of Ashab al-Tarjih
Ashab al-tarjih are scholars specialized in weighing transmissions and evidence within the school. Their tarjih is binding on the mufti in such cases.
They are not independent mujtahids but have deep knowledge of the school's principles.
4
Apply to a Hypothetical Scenario
Given a real case (e.g., a specific salah ruling), determine whether the famous opinion stands or the tarjih overrides it based on the available transmissions and the decision of ashab al-tarjih.
Always check the historical practice of scholars as reported by Halabi.

Tenth Principle: In acts of worship, Imam A'zam's saying is the mufta bih. In all chapters of ibadat, for example salah, sawm, zakat, and hajj, when there is ikhtilaf between Imam A'zam and his companions, the famous saying of Imam A'zam will be preponderant and mufta bih. However, if the ashab al-tarjih deem preponderant a saying of Imam A'zam that is not his famous saying, but his students adopted that saying, then in that case it is necessary for the mufti to act on the tarjih of the ashab al-tarjih, and it would not be correct to adopt Imam A'zam's famous saying. Allama Ibrahim al-Halabi says: By induction and following, it has become clear that the scholars have adopted Imam Abu Hanifa's famous saying in ibadat. If ever there was a need to leave the famous saying in some matter, they adopted a saying that also has a narration from Imam A'zam. (Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: 126; al-Madhhab al-Hanafi: 1/251)

Commentary
This tenth principle establishes a default rule: in matters of worship (ibadat), the famous (mashhur) opinion of Imam A'zam is to be followed. The reason is that ibadat are more directly based on textual evidence and less open to analogical reasoning. However, the principle has an exception: if the ashab al-tarjih (scholars of preferment) have chosen a different opinion of Imam A'zam that is not his famous one, but which his students adopted, then the mufti must follow that preferred view. Allama Halabi confirms that scholars rarely depart from the famous view, and when they do, they ensure it is still supported by a transmission from Imam A'zam. This preserves the authority of Imam A'zam while allowing flexibility in specific cases.

Key Terms
مشہور قول (mashhur qawl): The widely transmitted and well-known opinion of Imam A'zam on a particular issue.
اصحاب الترجیح (ashab al-tarjih): Scholars qualified to assess and prefer one transmitted opinion over another within the school.

Types of Textual Indication: 'Ibarah al-Nass and Isharah al-Nass
Defining the first two types of textual indication with Quranic examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ibarah al-nass and isharah al-nass, identify each in a Quranic text, and explain the Hanafi classification of textual indication.

Core Idea
Direct meaning is 'ibarah; necessary implication is isharah. Both derive rulings, but differently.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the text (nass) can indicate rulings in multiple ways. The first two levels are 'ibarah al-nass and isharah al-nass. Understanding this classification is essential for proper istinbat. In this lesson, you will learn how to identify each type and appreciate the difference between intended meanings and necessary implications.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the definition of 'ibarah al-nass
The meaning that is understood directly from the text without reflection, and is intended by the Lawgiver, either primarily or secondarily.
Example from Quran 2:275 shows two intended meanings: the primary (distinctness of trade and usury) and the subordinate (halal/haram ruling).
2
Apply 'ibarah to Quran 2:275
Identify the two meanings that come directly: that trade and usury are distinct, and that trade is halal while usury is haram. Note which is primary and which is subordinate.
3
Recognize the definition of isharah al-nass
The meaning that is not directly intended but is a necessary concomitant (lazim) of the ruling; it may require some reflection.
Example from Quran 2:233: the father's maintenance obligation necessarily implies child's attribution to him.
4
Apply isharah to Quran 2:233
From the ruling that the father must provide maintenance, the necessary implication is that the child is attributed to the father, because the obligation is based on paternity.
5
Distinguish between the two
Compare: 'ibarah is intended and direct; isharah is necessary but not intended. Both are binding in Hanafi usul, but different in evidentiary character.

Definition of 'Ibarah al-Nass with Example
Indication of the text upon a meaning that is understood from the text without thought and reflection, and that meaning is also intended from the text, is called 'ibarah al-nass, whether that meaning is intended independently or implicitly. For example, Allah's saying: 'Allah has permitted trade and forbidden usury' (Surah al-Baqarah: 275). From this verse, as 'ibarah, two meanings are understood: the first meaning is that trade and usury are two separate things, not one, as the polytheists thought, and this meaning is the primary intent of the verse, because this verse was revealed to refute those who considered trade and usury as one thing. The second meaning understood is that trade is lawful and usury is unlawful, and this meaning is the intended meaning of the verse as well.

Definition of Isharah al-Nass with Example
Indication of the text upon a meaning which is neither independently intended nor implicitly intended from the speech, but that meaning is necessary for the ruling for which the speech was brought. This is called isharah al-nass. Sometimes that necessary meaning is understood without reflection, and sometimes a little reflection is needed. Its example is Allah's saying: 'And upon the father (of the child) is their (the mothers') provision and clothing according to what is reasonable' (Surah al-Baqarah: 233). From this verse, by 'ibarah al-nass, it is understood that the maintenance of the breastfeeding mothers is upon the father, and that the child is attributed to the father, not the mother. Commentary
This passage defines two of the four types of textual indication in Hanafi usul: 'ibarah al-nass and isharah al-nass. 'Ibarah al-nass refers to the direct meaning that comes to mind immediately without reflection, and it is the intended meaning, whether primary or subordinate. The example from Quran 2:275 shows that the verse directly indicates both that trade and usury are distinct and that trade is permissible while usury is forbidden. Isharah al-nass refers to a meaning that is not directly intended but is a necessary implication of the ruling. The example from Quran 2:233 shows that the verse directly states the father must provide maintenance for nursing mothers, but it also necessarily implies that the child is attributed to the father, because the obligation is on the father. The passage notes that isharah meanings may require some reflection. The text cuts off as it was about to elaborate further.

Key Terms
عبارة النص ('ibarah al-nass): The explicit meaning of the text that is understood directly and is intended by the Lawgiver, either as the primary or subordinate meaning.
اشارة النص (isharah al-nass): A meaning that is not directly intended but is a necessary concomitant (lazim) of the explicit ruling, such that the ruling would be incomplete without it.
منطوق (mantiq): Explicit statement, the apparent meaning (as used by non-Hanafi schools).
مفہوم (mafhum): Implicit understanding, as used by Shafi'is to cover what Hanafis call dalalah and iqtida'.

Dalalat al-Nass and Iqtida' al-Nass: Two Key Types of Textual Indication
Defining and exemplifying dalalat al-nass and iqtida' al-nass with Quranic evidence and hierarchy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate between dalalat al-nass and iqtida' al-nass, provide Quranic examples for each, and apply the Hanafi hierarchy of textual indications to resolve conflicts.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-nass conveys a meaning understood without reflection; iqtida' al-nass supplies a missing word for the text's validity.

The Hanafi school uniquely classifies textual indications into four categories, each with distinct strength. This lesson unpacks two of them—dalalat al-nass and iqtida' al-nass—with classic Quranic examples. Mastering these distinctions is essential for any mufti who must extract rulings from the primary texts with precision.

Guided Path
1
Define the two terms
Provide clear definitions from the text: dalalat al-nass indicates a meaning understood effortlessly, not the primary intent; iqtida' al-nass indicates a deleted word upon which the text's validity depends.
2
Examine the Quranic examples
For dalalat al-nass: the verse on parents (al-Isra:23) deriving prohibition of beating (a fortiori). For iqtida' al-nass: the verse on carrion (al-Ma'idah:3) supplying the word 'eating'.
3
Understand the condition and effect
Dalalat requires no reflection; iqtida' requires assuming an implied word for the text to be legally or rationally valid.
4
Place them in the Hanafi hierarchy
Recognize the order: ibarat > isharat > dalalat > iqtida'. All are qat'i unless contrary explicit evidence exists.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i classification
Note that the Shafi'is and Mutakallimin divide textual indication into mantuq and mafhum, whereas Hanafis include all four under mantuq.

Specification in Fiqh Narrations: Mafhum Mukhalif in Women's Ghusl
Illustrating mafhum mukhalif with the ruling on ghusl for women

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a specification (qaid) in riwayat fiqhiyyah indicates mafhum mukhalif, and distinguish this from the treatment of specifications in nusus.

Core Idea
A specification in riwayat fiqhiyyah negates the ruling for what is unspecified, unlike in nusus.

In the previous lesson, we learned the principle that in riwayat fiqhiyyah, a specification (qaid) creates mafhum mukhalif by consensus. This lesson shows its application: the Hidayah ruling that a woman need not undo her braids in ghusl. By specifying 'woman', the ruling implies that men are not excused, so they must undo braids. This contrasts with nusus, where such specification does not automatically negate the opposite according to Hanafis. Understanding this distinction is crucial for muftis to correctly apply rulings derived from fiqh narrations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ruling
State the ruling from al-Hidayah: In ghusl janabah, a woman is not obliged to undo her braided hair.
This is the matn being expanded.
2
Note the specification (qaid)
The ruling specifies 'woman' (al-mar'a). This is a qaid – a restriction or specification.
The term 'woman' is not superfluous; it carries legal effect.
3
Apply the principle of mafhum mukhalif in riwayat
According to the principle from the previous lesson, in riwayat fiqhiyyah, specifying something negates the ruling for what is other than it by consensus. Thus, the ruling that undoing braids is not obligatory applies only to women; for men, the opposite ruling holds – they must undo braids (if applicable).
This is bil-ittifaq – agreed upon by Hanafi scholars.
4
Distinguish from nusus
The commentator notes that this is contrary to nusus (Quran and Sunnah), where according to Hanafi usul, specification does not indicate negation of the opposite. Thus, the principle is specific to fiqh narrations (riwayat fiqhiyyah).
Other schools may differ on nusus, but within riwayat, there is consensus.
5
Derive the mufta bihi ruling
The operative fatwa: Women are not required to undo braids; men are required to undo any similar obstruction (if they have braids, which is rare, but the principle stands that the ruling is exclusive to women).
The practical effect: a mufti must check whether a ruling comes from riwayat or nusus to apply mafhum mukhalif correctly.

Defining 'Urf and 'Adah: Terms and Legal Significance
Lexical and terminological definitions of 'urf and 'adah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'urf and 'adah, distinguish their lexical and terminological meanings, and identify the conditions for 'urf as a legal source.

Core Idea
'Urf is custom accepted by sound nature; 'adah is repeated practice without reason—legally they are identical.

Understanding the precise definitions of 'urf (custom) and 'adah (habit) is essential for applying them as subsidiary legal sources. This lesson unpacks the lexicographical and terminological definitions provided by Hanafi jurists, clarifying the conditions under which a practice qualifies as 'urf and thus can influence fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of 'urf
Read the definition from Shaykh Mahmud Fahmi Abusunna: 'urf is a matter that reassures souls, is approved by sound intellects, and is not rejected by people of sound taste. Note that it must be prevalent among a community.
This definition emphasizes general acceptance and rational approval.
2
Identify the definition of 'adah
Read the definition from Ibn Amir Haj: 'adah is a matter that occurs repeatedly without a rational connection ('ilaqah 'aqliyyah). This repetition makes the act easy to perform, like natural matters.
The absence of rational cause is key; if a cause exists, it is not 'adah but a practice based on reason.
3
Compare lexical and terminological meanings
Lexically, 'urf means 'that which is known' and 'adah means 'returning habit'. Terminologically, the Hanafi view treats them as synonyms for a custom or practice that meets the conditions for legal consideration.
This equivalence is crucial for fatwa: what matters is the prevalence and non-contradiction to texts, not the label.
4

From the definition, extract conditions: (1) prevalence among people, (2) acceptance by sound intellects, (3) no opposition to a definitive text or principle. These ensure that custom does not override shari'ah.
These conditions are universally recognized in Hanafi usul.

(al-'Urf wa al-'Adah fi Ra'y al-Fuqaha': 8) 'Adah is derived from 'awd, which lexically means the repeated occurrence of something and its repeated occurrence without any cause. Allamah Ibn Amir Haj defines 'adah terminologically: 'al-'adah hiya al-amr al-mutakarrar min ghayr 'ilaqah 'aqliyyah' ('adah is that which occurs repeatedly without a rational connection). (al-Taqrir wa al-Tahbir: 1/282, Rasm al-Mufti: 158) That is, the repeated occurrence of something without a rational connection, such that its performance becomes easy like natural matters, is called 'adah. If a cause or reason is involved in its repeated occurrence, it is not called 'adah. Difference between 'urf and 'adah: Is there any difference between 'urf and 'adah, and if there is, what is it? In this regard, there are three opinions from scholars: 1. The first opinion is that there is a relationship of absolute generality and specificity between 'urf and 'adah; 'adah is more general relative to 'urf, in that 'adah can relate to the action of one person and also to the action of a group, whereas 'urf applies only to those statements and actions that are prevalent among common people; it does not apply to the statement or action of a single individual. According to this opinion, every 'urf must necessarily be an 'adah, but not every 'adah must be an 'urf. Professor Mustafa al-Zarqa has adopted this opinion. 2. The second opinion is that both have an absolute generality-specificity relationship, but 'urf is more general relative to 'adah, because 'urf applies both to statements prevalent among people and to actions that are known among them, whereas 'adah applies only to actions prevalent among people; it does not apply to prevalent statements. As Allamah Ibn al-Humam says: 'al-'adah: al-'urf al-'amali' (adah is the practical 'urf). According to this opinion, 'adah is a type of 'urf, and it is correct to apply 'urf to every 'adah, but it is not correct to apply 'adah to every 'urf. 3. Most fuqaha hold that 'urf and 'adah are two distinct things in terms of concept, but there is no difference between them in terms of reference and reality. Allamah Ibn 'Abidin al-Shami, Hafiz al-Din Abu al-Barakat 'Abdullah ibn Ahmad al-Nasafi, Shaykh 'Abd al-Wahhab Khallaf, Dr. 'Abd al-Karim Zaydan, etc., hold this view, and Faqih al-'Asr Maulana Khalid Saifullah al-Rahmani has declared this correct, writing that the term 'adah is used lexically for personal habits like menstruation, etc., but terminologically 'urf and 'adah are one, and there is apparently no proof or reason to restrict 'adah to actions. (Muhadarat Usul al-Fiqh: 153, al-'Urf wa al-'Adah fi Ra'y al-Fuqaha': 13)

The Relationship between Urf and Adah
Three scholarly opinions on the relationship between urf and adah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three scholarly views on the relationship between 'urf and 'ādah and apply the majority view in fatwa issuance.

Core Idea
The majority Hanafi view: 'urf and 'ādah are conceptually distinct but identical in practical reality.

This passage examines the nuanced relationship between two key sources of subsidiary law: 'urf (custom) and 'ādah (habit). While they are often used interchangeably, some scholars have drawn careful distinctions. Understanding these differences is crucial for a mufti when determining which practices can be considered legally binding sources of law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three views
List the three scholarly positions on the relationship between 'urf and 'ādah: (1) 'urf is more general than 'ādah (every 'urf is an 'ādah but not vice versa); (2) 'urf is more general than 'ādah because 'urf includes sayings while 'ādah is only actions; (3) they are conceptually distinct but identical in reality.
Pay attention to the proponents of each view: al-Zarqa for the first, Ibn al-Humann for the second, and Ibn Abidin and others for the third.
2
Analyze the differences
For each view, explain the practical implication. View 1 implies some 'urf may not be 'ādah. View 2 excludes habitual sayings from 'ādah. View 3 treats them as identical in application, with 'ādah being a lexical term for personal habits like menstruation.
Consider how each view affects the use of 'urf as a source in the absence of text.
3
Determine the operative view
Identify the majority (third) view as the mufta bihi (operative) position, as adopted by later Hanafi masters. Justify why it is preferred: it harmonizes the term 'ādah used in classical texts with the modern understanding of 'urf.
This view is supported by Ibn Abidin, al-Nasafi, and contemporary scholars like Dr. Abd al-Karim Zaidan.

The Authority of Custom vs. Sacred Texts
Explains rules for custom conflicting with Shariah texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida on general vs. specific custom to determine when custom can specify a text or override qiyas.

Core Idea
General custom can specify texts and override qiyas; specific custom cannot—unless it is widespread practice (ta'mul).

This passage from Naseem Ul Mufti lays out the Hanafi doctrine on the interaction between custom ('urf) and sacred texts. It distinguishes between general custom, which can specify general texts and set aside analogy, and specific custom, which is only valid if it does not contradict texts or analogy. You will learn the precise conditions and the debate over whether specific custom can ever serve as a mukhassis (specifier).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios of conflict
First, note that if custom completely contradicts a text (necessitating its abandonment), both general and specific customs are unanimously invalid. Second, if the conflict is partial (custom specifies the text's application or contradicts only analogy), the type of custom matters.
Complete contradiction leads to rejection; partial conflict opens the door for custom to be considered.
2
Distinguish general custom from specific custom
General custom ('urf 'amm) is prevalent across regions or among the majority. Specific custom ('urf khas) is limited to a locality, group, or profession. The ruling differs based on this distinction.
The Hanafi school generally gives weight to general custom but not to specific custom.
3
Understand the role of general custom
General custom can specify a general text (takhsis) and can cause analogy to be abandoned. This is agreed upon.
Example: allowing istisna' (manufacturing contract) despite general prohibition of selling non-existent goods.
4
Analyze the dispute over specific custom
Some Hanafi jurists (e.g., Balkh scholars) allowed specific custom to specify texts based on ta'mul (widespread local practice). However, the relied-upon view is that specific custom cannot be a mukhassis due to lack of general applicability.
Ibn 'Abidin clarifies that ta'mul is a stronger proof than mere 'urf khas.
5
Apply the furuat and istithna'
Apply the maxim to concrete cases: ijarah for weaving (allowed due to ta'mul) and istisna' (allowed due to ta'mul). Note the exception: if a specific custom completely contradicts a text, it is rejected.
The furuat illustrate how general principle yields specific rulings.

If there is a conflict between the texts of the Shariah and custom such that acting upon custom would necessitate completely abandoning the text, then both types of custom—general custom and specific custom—are unanimously invalid. As for when it contradicts a legal evidence or what is explicitly stated in the madhhab... if it contradicts it completely such that abandoning the text necessarily follows, there is no doubt in rejecting it. (Nashr al-'Urf fi Bina' Ba'd al-Ahkam 'ala al-'Urf: 64-65) If there is a conflict between the texts of the Shariah and custom such that acting upon custom does not necessitate completely abandoning the text, but rather specifies the text, then general custom is unanimously valid, and specifying the text by it is permissible. And if it does not contradict it completely, such that the evidence is general and custom contradicts it in some of its individuals, or the evidence is analogical, then custom is valid if it is general, for general custom is suitable as a mukhassis... and analogy is abandoned due to it... and if the custom is specific, then it is not considered, and that is the relied-upon view. (Nashr al-'Urf: 65-66) If there is a conflict between the texts of the Shariah and custom such that acting upon custom does not necessitate completely abandoning the text, but rather specifies the text, then is specifying the text by specific custom permissible? Scholars differ on this. Some scholars opine that specifying the text by specific custom is not permissible, because if a specific custom of one region necessitates specifying the text, then the absence of that custom in another region demands non-specification of that text. Thus, due to doubt, specifying the general ruling of the text is not permissible. Allamah Ibn 'Abidin al-Shami states: You should know that custom is of two types: general custom and specific custom. By general custom, a general ruling is proven, and it can be a mukhassis for analogy and hadith. The case of specific custom is different from this, because it proves a specific ruling, provided it is not contrary to analogy or hadith, because specific custom cannot be a mukhassis. In al-Dhakhirah, in the eighth section of the Book of Ijarah, where this issue is discussed that if someone gives spun yarn to a weaver with the condition that he takes one-third of the finished cloth as his wage, the author of al-Dhakhirah wrote: The jurists of Balkh, such as Nasir bin Muhammad bin Salamah and others, deem this ijarah permissible for cloth weaving due to the prevalence of this practice (ta'mul) in their region. And ta'mul is such a proof that analogy is abandoned and the narration is specified due to it. And deeming this ijarah permissible due to the ta'mul in cloth weaving means specifying the hadith that came regarding a non-miller (ghayr tahan). Because that hadith came regarding the measure of a miller (tahan), it did not come regarding a weaver, but the weaver is its analogue, so the hadith would also indicate regarding him. Then when we did not act upon this hadith for the weaver and acted upon it for the miller, this is specification of the hadith, not complete abandonment. And specification of the hadith due to ta'mul is permissible. See, we deem istisna' permissible due to ta'mul, even though it involves selling something not in possession, and there is a prohibition in the hadith against selling something not in possession. And deeming istisna' permissible due to ta'mul is specifying that hadith which prohibits selling what is not in possession, not completely abandoning it, because we act upon that hadith in other details besides istisna'. The scholars have also stated that this case is completely different from when in a region the qafiz of a miller becomes prevalent—then it is not permissible, and the practice of those people is valid...

Commentary
This passage from Naseem Ul Mufti discusses the interaction between custom (urf) and the texts of Shariah (nusus) within the Hanafi methodology of ifta. It begins by stating a fundamental rule: if a custom completely contradicts a text such that following the custom would require abandoning the text entirely, then both general and specific customs are unanimously rejected. This is a clear limitation: custom cannot override a definitive text. The text then cites from Nashr al-Urf, explaining that if the conflict is only partial—i.e., the text is general and the custom specifies some of its applications, or the proof is analogical (qiyas)—then general custom is valid and can serve as a mukhassis (specifier) of the text, and analogy can be abandoned in favor of the custom. However, specific custom is not considered a mukhassis according to the relied-upon view of the madhhab. The passage then presents a scholarly dispute regarding whether specific custom can specify a text. Those who reject it argue that because custom varies by region, allowing specification by a local custom would create uncertainty about the text's applicability elsewhere. Ibn Abidin then clarifies the classification: general custom establishes a general ruling and can specify texts and abandon qiyas; specific custom establishes only a specific ruling, but only if it does not contradict texts or qiyas—it cannot serve as a mukhassis. The passage then provides an extended example from al-Dhakhirah concerning a contract of ijarah (hire) for weaving cloth, where the weaver is given yarn and takes a third of the cloth as his wage. The jurists of Balkh allowed this contract based on the prevalent local practice (ta'mul), even though analogy would forbid it. The author of al-Dhakhirah explains that this is not a complete rejection of the relevant hadith (which prohibits the measure of a miller), but rather a specification of it, since the hadith was about millers, not weavers. Similarly, the permissibility of istisna' (manufacturing contract) is based on ta'mul, which specifies the hadith prohibiting the sale of non-existent goods. This shows that custom and practice, when widespread, can authorize exceptions to general prohibitions as long as the core principle remains intact. The passage concludes by distinguishing this from a case where a specific custom (like the qafiz of a miller) becomes prevalent—such a custom would not justify departing from the text.

Key Terms
عرف عام ('urf 'amm): Custom prevalent among most people across regions or among the majority of a community
عرف خاص ('urf khas): Custom limited to a specific place, group, or profession
تعامل (ta'mul): Widespread customary practice among people in transactions, often equivalent to custom

Four Conditions for Custom's Legal Validity
Lists four conditions for 'urf to be authoritative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for a custom to be legally authoritative in the Hanafi school and apply each condition to a real scenario.

Core Idea
Custom is authoritative only if it is universal/prevalent, concurrent, uncontradicted by explicit stipulation, and not conflicting with shari'ah.

This lesson presents the four conditions that a custom must satisfy to be considered legally binding in Islamic law. These conditions are derived from the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-ʿāda muḥakkama' (custom is authoritative), and they ensure that custom does not undermine clear textual evidence or contractual intent. Understanding these conditions is essential for a mufti when evaluating customary practices in contracts and transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching maxim and its scope
The maxim 'al-ʿāda muḥakkama' (custom is authoritative) governs many areas of fiqh, especially mu'amalat. The four conditions serve as a dabt (restrictive sub-maxim) that specify when custom can be applied.
The maxim is a major legal principle (al-qāʽida al-kubrā).
2
List the four conditions in order
Recall the four conditions: 1) custom must be general (kullī) or prevalent (aktharī); 2) it must exist at the time of the transaction (ʻurf qārin); 3) no explicit statement (ṣarāḥa) contradicts it; 4) it must not contradict a shari'ah text or definitive principle.
Memorize them as a checklist for fatwa.
3
Understand the nuance of each condition
For condition 1: kullī means 100% practice, aktharī means majority. For condition 2: only concurrent custom counts; prior (sābiq) or subsequent (ṭāriʼ) are ignored. For condition 3: an explicit agreement overrides custom unless it violates contract essentials. For condition 4: custom yields to nass; it cannot render a textual ruling inoperative.
These nuances prevent misapplication.
4
Apply the conditions to a sample case
Take a common market custom: customers can return goods within a week without stating it explicitly in the sale. Check each condition: it is prevalent (aktharī), exists at the time of sale, no explicit contrary agreement, and does not contradict any text. Thus, it is enforceable.
Practice with multiple scenarios.
5
Identify the exception (istithnāʼ)
If custom conflicts with a clear nass or a definitive principle, it is abandoned. This is the boundary of the maxim, governed by the sub-maxim 'al-nuṣūṣ muqaddama ʻalā al-ʻurf' (texts are given priority over custom).
The fourth condition itself defines the exception.

Conditions for custom being considered authoritative. In shari'ah, there are four conditions for custom being considered authoritative: 1. That custom be general (kulli) or prevalent (akthari). General means that in the society, 100% of people act upon that custom. Prevalent means that in the society, in most cases, they act upon that custom. 2. The custom must have existed at the time of the transaction. So if the custom did not exist at the time of the transaction, but existed in some earlier time, or when the matter reached the judge such custom had arisen, then both of these are not considered (i.e., concurrent custom is considered; prior custom and subsequent custom are not considered). 3. There must be no explicit statement from the parties contrary to the custom. Therefore, if at the time of the transaction or contract the parties made an explicit statement contrary to the custom and it does not oppose the requirements of the contract, then in such case the custom is not considered; rather, action will be according to the explicit statement. 4. The fourth condition for custom being considered is that adopting this custom must not render any shari'ah text or any definitive principle of shari'ah inoperative. Otherwise, we will abandon the custom and act upon the text, because compared to the text of shari'ah, custom has no authority. (See al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: 271-292, al-Urf li Abi Sinnah: 56-65).

Commentary
This passage sets out the four conditions required for a custom to be considered legally authoritative in the Hanafi school. The first condition is that the custom must be either universal (kulli) or prevalent (akthari). Universal means it is practiced by the entire community, while prevalent means it is the norm in the majority of cases. The second condition is temporal: the custom must exist at the time the transaction takes place (concurrent custom, 'urf qarin). Customs that existed in the past or that arise later are not given weight. The third condition is that the parties did not explicitly stipulate something contrary to the custom; if they did, that explicit condition takes precedence as long as it does not conflict with the contract's requirements. The fourth condition is that the custom must not contradict any text of shari'ah or a definitive principle; if it does, the text prevails because custom cannot override revelation. These conditions ensure that custom is applied properly within the framework of Islamic law.

Key Terms
عرف کلی (‘urf kullī): A custom practiced by the entire society (100% of people).
عرف اکثری (‘urf aktharī): A custom practiced in the majority of cases within a society.
عرف قارن (‘urf qārin): A custom that exists at the time of the transaction.
صراحت (ṣarāḥat): An explicit statement made by the parties that contradicts the custom.
