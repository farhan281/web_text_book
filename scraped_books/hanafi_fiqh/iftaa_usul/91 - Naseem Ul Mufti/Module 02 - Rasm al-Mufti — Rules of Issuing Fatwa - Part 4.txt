The Mufti's Consideration of 'Urf
Stating the rule and defining 'urf and 'adah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'urf and 'adah, distinguish between them, and apply the rule that a mufti must consider prevailing custom when issuing fatwas.

Core Idea
A mufti cannot ignore the customs of his time — ‘urf can override the literal text of the madhhab.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle in Hanafi iftaa: the mufti's duty to consider 'urf. The source passage explains why custom is a binding source and defines the key terms. You will learn how to differentiate ‘urf from ‘adah and understand the juristic rule that prohibits a mufti from blindly applying zāhir al-riwāyah when it contradicts established custom.

1
Identify the rule

This is a direct quote from 'Allamah Shami.

Contrast the two definitions to understand their distinct emphases.
3
Distinguish between 'urf and 'adah
Apply the first opinion: 'adah is more general than 'urf because 'adah can apply to individuals, while 'urf is collective.
The text mentions three opinions but only details the first.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider a situation where local custom conflicts with a ruling in zāhir al-riwāyah. Decide whether the mufti should follow the custom or the text.
Refer to the principle that 'urf overrides unless it contradicts a definitive text or consensus.

The mufti's consideration of 'urf in giving fatwa. Thirteenth principle: The mufti's consideration of 'urf in giving fatwa. Explanation of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. 'Urf and custom are important sources and springs of Islamic jurisprudence. Countless issues from the Quran, Hadith, and fiqh issues derived from both have been based on 'urf and custom. Especially those chapters of Islamic jurisprudence that deal with transactions, contracts, social dealings, divorce, and oaths, 'urf has great influence. Considering this importance of 'urf and custom, it is extremely necessary for a faqih and mufti to be aware of the 'urf of his time and the custom of the people of his time. 'Allamah Shami says that it is not permissible for a mufti and judge to give fatwa and decide by leaving 'urf and following the apparent madhhab. (Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: 169) Below, several discussions related to 'urf and custom are recorded. Definition of 'urf and custom: Linguistically, 'urf applies to several meanings. Thus, knowledge, patience, gift, goodness, generosity, and high and prominent things are expressed as 'urf. Similarly, that with which the soul becomes familiar and satisfied is also called 'urf. However, in language, it is mostly used for known things. ('Urf wa 'Adah, Islamic Fiqh Academy: 16-19) Abu al-Barakat Hafiz al-Din 'Abdullah ibn Ahmad al-Nasafi, while stating the terminological definition of 'urf, says: 'Al-'urf wa al-'adah: what becomes established in the souls by means of intellects and accepted by sound natures. (That is, 'urf is something that becomes entrenched in human souls through intellects and sound natures accept it.) (Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: 158) Shaykh Mahmud Fahmi Abu Sanah, while further clarifying this definition, defines 'urf in this way: 'Urf is that matter by which souls are satisfied and aware, and it has become rooted in human souls, souls become familiar with it due to the intellect's liking, and individuals of the group possessing sound taste did not express displeasure at it. (Al-'Urf wa al-'Adah fi Ra'y al-Fuqaha': 8) 'Adah is derived from 'awd, which linguistically means the repeated return of something and the repeated occurrence of a thing without any cause. 'Allamah Ibn Amir Hajj has defined 'adah terminologically: Al-'adah hiya al-amr al-mutakarrar min ghayr 'ilaqah 'aqliyyah. ('Adah is that which occurs repeatedly without an intellectual connection.) (Al-Taqrir wa al-Tahbir: 1/282, Rasm al-Mufti: 158) That is, something occurring repeatedly without an intellectual connection in such a way that its performance becomes easy like natural matters is called 'adah. And if some cause or reason is involved in that thing's repeated occurrence, it will not be called 'adah. Difference between 'urf and 'adah: Is there any difference between 'urf and 'adah? The first opinion is that between 'urf and 'adah there is a relationship of absolute generality and specificity. Compared to 'urf, 'adah has more generality. Commentary
This passage introduces a crucial principle for the mufti: the necessity of considering prevailing custom ('urf) when issuing fatwas. The author begins by emphasizing that 'urf is a fundamental source in Islamic law, alongside the Quran and Hadith, particularly in areas like transactions, contracts, and family law. He quotes 'Allamah Ibn 'Abidin (al-Shami) that a mufti or judge may not abandon 'urf in favor of the literal ruling of the madhhab (zāhir al-riwāyah) when 'urf contradicts it. This is a significant qualification: the madhhab's explicit text is not to be applied rigidly if it conflicts with established custom. The passage then defines 'urf linguistically and terminologically, drawing from al-Nasafi and Abu Sanah. 'Urf is what is established in people's minds and accepted by sound nature. 'Adah is defined as repeated occurrence without a rational cause, making actions easy. The distinction between 'urf and 'adah is then introduced, with the first opinion stating that 'adah is more general than 'urf, as 'adah can apply to an individual's action while 'urf is collective. This lesson is foundational for understanding how a Hanafi mufti navigates between textual rulings and social reality.

Six Key Differences Between Custom and Consensus
Six-point comparison between custom and consensus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six differences between 'urf and ijma and apply them to evaluate whether a given practice can serve as a basis for fatwa.

Core Idea
Custom is probabilistic and changeable; consensus is definitive and irreversible.

This passage contrasts two closely related legal sources: 'urf (custom) and ijma' (consensus). Although both involve agreement, their legal authority and conditions differ radically. Understanding these distinctions is essential for a mufti who must know when a custom can justify a ruling and when only consensus suffices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the shared element
Note that both 'urf and ijma involve agreement (ittifaq) and generality (umum). This is the basis for their comparison.
The passage says 'both involve agreement and generality'.
2
Distinguish the participants
Determine who is involved: 'urf arises from the sayings and actions of the general public, while ijma requires the agreement of mujtahids only.
Point 1: 'urf = public; ijma = mujtahids.
3
Contrast the degree of agreement needed
Compare the threshold: 'urf requires majority agreement; ijma requires unanimity of all mujtahids.
Point 2: majority vs. unanimity.
4
Compare epistemological binding force
Consider the legal force: rulings from ijma are definitive (qat'i) and unchangeable; rulings from 'urf are probabilistic (zanni) and can change, even obligatorily.
Point 3: ijma = fixed; 'urf = mutable.
5
Examine hierarchy with primary texts
Assess authority when conflicting with nass: ijma is authoritative even against a text; 'urf must yield to the text.
Point 4: ijma > nass; 'urf < nass.
6
Consider temporal stability
Observe how each is established: ijma is convened by a single occurrence; 'urf requires repetition and continuity.
Points 5 and 6: one-time vs. repeated, and ijtihad remains open for 'urf but closed for ijma.

Proportion and Difference between Custom and Consensus
Another term close to custom is called consensus. The proportion between custom and consensus is that both involve agreement and generality; especially common custom and consensus are highly similar. Despite this, there are differences between custom and consensus due to the following reasons:
1. Custom arises from the sayings and actions of the general public, while in consensus the opinion of the general public has no role; it arises only from the agreement of the mujtahids.
2. For consensus, it is necessary that all mujtahids agree on it, and not even one differs, while in custom, the agreement of the majority and most people is sufficient.
3. The ruling established by consensus is definitive, with no room for change; conversely, a ruling established by custom can change, and sometimes it is obligatory to change the ruling due to the change of custom.
4. Consensus is authoritative even against a text because it itself is a definitive proof, but custom is not authoritative against a text; that is, if custom conflicts with a text such that only one can be acted upon, by unanimous agreement we abandon custom and act on the text.
5. Consensus is established by agreeing once, while for custom to be authoritative, repetition and continuity are necessary.
6. Once consensus has been convened on a matter, there remains no room for ijtihad in that matter, but the case of custom is different, because even when custom exists in a matter, the door of ijtihad remains open.
Benefited from Muhadarat Usul al-Fiqh, the jurist of the age Maulana Khalid Saifullah al-Rahmani: 154.

Commentary
This passage compares 'urf (custom) and ijma' (consensus), two sources of Islamic law. While both involve agreement, they differ fundamentally: ijma' is the definitive agreement of all mujtahids and is binding forever; 'urf is the common practice of the general public, is probabilistic, and can change. The six differences are crucial for a mufti: a fatwa must never contradict ijma', but it may change with evolving 'urf. The text emphasizes that 'urf is subordinate to the primary texts (Qur'an and Sunnah), whereas ijma' is an independent definitive proof.

Key Terms
عرف (‘urf): A prevalent practice among the general public (sayings and actions) that is accepted as a legal source in the absence of a contrary text.
اجماع (ijmā‘): The unanimous agreement of all mujtahids of an era on a legal ruling, which is a definitive proof.

Five Distinctions Between Custom (Urf) and Consensus (Ijma)
Lists five key differences between urf and ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the five key differences between 'urf and ijma when weighing evidence in fatwa construction.

Core Idea
Ijma is definitive and unchangeable; 'urf is presumptive and changeable, and must yield to nass.

Custom ('urf) and consensus (ijma) are both sources of law in the Hanafi school, but they differ fundamentally in authority, scope, and flexibility. This lesson lays out five critical distinctions every mufti must know to correctly evaluate evidence. Understanding these ensures that custom is never given the weight of definitive consensus, and that the door of ijtihad remains open where appropriate.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the source
State the first difference: 'urf arises from the sayings and actions of common people, whereas ijma requires the agreement of qualified mujtahids. Lay opinion has no role in ijma.
2
Compare the level of agreement
State the second difference: ijma demands unanimous agreement of all mujtahids; 'urf can be established by majority practice. One dissenter breaks ijma but not 'urf.
3
Analyze definitiveness and changeability
State the third difference: the ruling derived from ijma is definitive (qat'i) and cannot change; 'urf-based rulings are presumptive and may change when the custom changes, sometimes obligating a change.
4
Assess authority relative to nass
State the fourth difference: ijma is authoritative over explicit textual evidence (nass) because it is itself a definitive proof; however, 'urf must yield to nass if conflict arises. By consensus, nass prevails.
5
Examine temporal nature and effect on ijtihad
State the fifth difference: ijma is established by a single occurrence, and once formed, ijtihad is closed on that issue; 'urf requires repetition and continuity, and its existence does not close the door of ijtihad.

Urf has more resemblance to ijma, but despite this, there are the following differences between urf and ijma: 1. Urf comes into existence from the sayings and actions of common people, whereas in ijma the opinion of common people has no role; it comes into existence only from the agreement of mujtahids. 2. Commentary
This passage presents a comparative analysis of two fundamental sources of Islamic law: 'urf (custom) and ijma' (consensus). The author begins by noting that although 'urf resembles ijma' in providing legal rulings, there are crucial differences. The first difference: 'urf arises from the common practice of people, whereas ijma' requires the unanimous agreement of qualified mujtahids, excluding lay opinions. Second: ijma' demands complete agreement of all mujtahids; 'urf can be established by the overwhelming majority. Third: the ruling derived from ijma' is definitive and unchangeable, while 'urf-based rulings remain flexible and may even obligate change when 'urf changes. Fourth: ijma' is authoritative even against explicit texts because it is itself a definitive proof, but 'urf must yield to nass if they conflict. Fifth: ijma' is established by a single occurrence, while 'urf requires repetition and continuity. Moreover, once ijma' is formed, ijtihad is closed on that issue, but 'urf does not close the door of ijtihad. These distinctions are crucial for a mufti to correctly weigh legal evidence.

Key Terms
عرف (urf): Common practice of people, whether in sayings or actions, that becomes a recognized norm.
اجماع (ijma): Consensus of all mujtahids of an era on a legal ruling.
نص (nass): Explicit textual evidence from Quran or hadith.

A mufti must interpret words by custom and adopt the opinion that is lenient, customary, and strongly evidenced.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological exposition on the authority of custom ('urf) in Hanafi jurisprudence, specifically for the mufti. It begins by establishing the legal maxim "Al-'Adatu Muhakkamah" (Custom is authoritative) through three primary proofs: Quranic verse 2:233, where 'ma'ruf' is defined by societal practice; the hadith of Hind bint Utbah, where the Prophet directs taking maintenance 'bi al-ma'ruf' according to custom; and the consensus of jurists that custom is a valid source in ancillary matters. The passage then moves to practical applications drawn from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. Qasim ibn Qutlubugha rules that in contracts, wills, oaths, vows, and endowments, words are interpreted according to the custom of the speaker, even if contrary to literal Arabic or legal language. He further instructs the mufti, when faced with conflicting scholarly opinions, to adopt the view that is lenient, customary for the people, and has strong evidence, relying on experts if needed. Finally, Ibn Abidin (al-Shami) stresses that a mufti must know the custom of his time and be trained under a proficient teacher. The passage reinforces that custom is not a peripheral consideration but a central tool for interpreting texts and issuing fatwas in accordance with changing societal norms, while remaining rooted in the Shari'ah's objectives.

Authority of Custom ('Urf) in Issuing Fatwas
Statements of Hanafi jurists on the authority of custom in fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' to interpret contracts, oaths, and other legal acts according to custom, and identify the limits of custom's authority when contrary to Shariah.

Core Idea
Custom ('urf) overrides literal meanings in contracts, oaths, and waqf, but not when it contradicts an explicit text.

This lesson explores the foundational role of custom ('urf) in Hanafi fatwa practice. Drawing from the works of Qasim ibn Qutlubugha, Ibn Nujaym, and others, we examine the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative) and its application across various chapters of fiqh. The passage also provides essential guidance for muftis: they must know the custom of their time, undergo apprenticeship (shagirdi), and avoid mechanically applying apparent rulings (zahir al-riwayah) when custom has changed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central maxim and its rank
Recognize the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' as one of the five major qawaid (kubra) and note its universal scope.
This maxim is foundational and appears in Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir.
2
Note the five key applications
List the specific chapters where custom is applied: waqf, wills, oaths, vows, and contracts. Understand that in each case, the speaker's intent is interpreted by custom.
These are the furuat provided in the qawida panel.
3
Understand the exception
Custom is authoritative only as long as it does not contradict an explicit text of Quran or Sunnah. This istithna' limits the maxim.
The governing qawida for exceptions is 'النصوص مقدمة على العرف'.
4
Recognize the mufti's duty
The mufti must know the custom of his time, be trained through apprenticeship (shagirdi), and choose the opinion that brings ease and aligns with custom, even if it differs from zahir al-riwayah.
This is emphasized by Allamah Shami and Qasim ibn Qutlubugha.
5
Grasp the rational argument
Custom is validated by reason and analogy because ignoring it would cause hardship (haraj), contrary to the Quranic principle of removing hardship (Quran 22:78).
This is the dalil aqli mentioned in the passage.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of statements from several Hanafi jurists, all emphasizing the authoritative role of custom (urf) in fatwa. The author begins by quoting Qasim ibn Qutlubugha, who states that in matters like waqf, wills, oaths, vows, and contracts, the intention of the speaker is interpreted according to the custom of his time and place, even if that differs from the literal Arabic meaning or the technical meaning used by the Lawgiver. This is because the Shariah often takes custom into account. Next, the author addresses a practical difficulty: when earlier scholars merely transmitted multiple opinions without preferring one, the mufti must exercise ijtihad by considering the current custom and the condition of the people, choosing the opinion that provides ease, is supported by strong proof, and aligns with prevailing custom. The mufti should not rely solely on his own knowledge but should consult those who are able to discern. Allamah Shami emphasizes that a mufti must know the custom of his time and should have acquired this knowledge through apprenticeship with a skilled teacher. Ibn Nujaym reinforces that custom is such a fundamental principle that it can override the literal meanings of words. The author then cites the famous maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative) from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. He further warns that even complete mastery of the texts of the Imams is insufficient without training in the actual practice of fatwa, because many answers depend on the custom of the people. A key point is that it is impermissible for a mufti or judge to ignore custom and mechanically apply the apparent ruling of the madhhab (zahir al-riwayah) if custom differs. Finally, the author provides a rational argument: if custom were not considered, people would face undue hardship because all their speech and contracts are based on their custom, and the Shariah aims to remove hardship (as per Quran 22:78). Thus, disregarding custom would contradict the spirit of the law. This passage is from later fatwa literature, not the foundational Zahir al-Riwayah, and reflects the developed Hanafi methodology that gives serious weight to custom.

Key Terms
عرف ('urf): The customary practice of a community that is not contrary to Shariah.

Specific Custom ('Urf Khas) and Its Legal Effect: Can It Specify a General Text?
Discussion of 'urf khas and its legal effect on text specification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on 'urf khas, identify the dispute between early and later scholars, and apply the reconciled rule in fatwa.

Core Idea
'Urf khas cannot establish a general ruling, but it can specify a text for the practicing community.

This lesson explores the delicate balance between custom and textual evidence. The Hanafi school acknowledges that universal custom can specify a general text, but what about a custom limited to a particular region? The passage presents a real case from al-Dhakhirah and reconciles apparently conflicting statements from Ibn Nujaym and later Hanafi scholars.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish 'urf 'amm and 'urf khas
Identify the two categories of custom: 'urf 'amm (general) can specify qiyas and hadith; 'urf khas (specific) is disputed. Note the different legal effects.
'Urf 'amm is like istisna' across all regions.
2
Analyze the Balkh case from al-Dhakhirah
The scholars of Balkh allowed a weaving ijarah based on local custom, arguing that custom specifies the prohibition hadith. Understand their reasoning: custom is a proof that can lead to takhsis.
They compared it to istisna'.
3
Examine the counterargument
The author of al-Dhakhirah rejects their specification because the custom is local. Conflicting regional customs create doubt, preventing definitive takhsis. Only universal custom like istisna' is acceptable.
This is based on the principle that takhsis requires certainty.
4

Ibn Nujaym states the madhhab does not consider 'urf khas, but many later scholars gave fatwa considering it. This is not a contradiction: 'not considering' means it cannot establish a general ruling.
The later scholars allow it for specific cases.
5

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of a rasm al-mufti rule: the treatment of 'urf khas (specific custom) in the Hanafi school. It begins by distinguishing 'urf 'amm (general custom) from 'urf khas. 'Urf 'amm can specify qiyas and hadith, whereas 'urf khas cannot, unless it does not contradict them. The text then presents a case from al-Dhakhirah: the scholars of Balkh permitted an ijarah contract where a weaver receives one-third of the cloth in return for weaving, based on their local custom. They argued that custom justifies specifying the hadith that prohibits such contracts by analogy to the prohibition of juzaf sales (like the miller's measure). The author of al-Dhakhirah counters that this specification is not valid because the custom is local, not universal. A local custom cannot specify a general text because there would be conflict between regions, leading to doubt. In contrast, istisna' (manufacturing contract) is universally practiced, so its specification of the hadith is accepted. Then the passage quotes Ibn Nujaym from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir stating that the relied-upon madhhab (madhhab) does not consider 'urf khas, but many later shuyukh gave fatwa considering it. Finally, the contemporary scholar Maulana Khalid Saifullah Rahmani clarifies the point: those who consider 'urf khas allow specification of texts by it, and those who deny it mean that a general ruling cannot be established by it, but a specific ruling for that community can be established. Thus, the difference is not as stark as it seems. The practical takeaway for a mufti is: 'urf khas may be used to specify a general text, but only for the people who practice it, and it cannot create a universal ruling.

عرف خاص ('urf khas): A custom that is specific to a particular region, group, or community, as opposed to a universal custom.
تخصيص (takhsis): The act of specifying a general text to apply to a subset of its apparent meaning, based on a specific evidence.
تعامل (ta'amul): Common practice or custom of the people; considered a proof in the Hanafi school.

The Scope of Custom: General vs Specific in Hanafi Fatwa Methodology
Distinguishing effects of general and specific custom on ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that general custom establishes a general ruling and specific custom establishes a specific ruling, and identify when custom can override Zahir al-Riwayah.

Core Idea
A custom's effect is limited to its scope: general for all, specific for one locality.

When a mufti encounters a local practice that differs from the texts of the madhhab, how should he decide? The Hanafi school distinguishes between general customs that affect everyone and local customs that only bind their practitioners. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its consequences for issuing fatwas, drawing from the authoritative statements of Ibn Abidin and al-Hamawi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the custom's scope
Determine whether the custom is general ('urf 'amm) – widely practiced across regions – or specific ('urf khas) – limited to one town, profession, or group.
General custom includes practices like using a particular currency standard; specific custom might be a local trade convention.
2
Apply the rule of scope
If the custom is general, it establishes a general ruling (hukm 'amm) that applies to all people of that custom. If specific, it establishes a specific ruling (hukm khas) only for its practitioners.
This is derived from the maxim 'العرف الخاص لا يثبت الحكم العام' (specific custom does not establish a general ruling).
3
Check for conflict with sacred text
If the custom contradicts a clear nass (Quran or Mutawatir Sunnah), it is invalid regardless of scope. Custom can only override Zahir al-Riwayah, not a nass.
This is a fundamental principle across all madhahib.
4
Determine the effect on Zahir al-Riwayah
If the custom (general or specific) opposes a ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah and no nass exists, the mufti follows the custom. The custom displaces the established madhhab text.
Ibn Abidin explicitly supports this, and it was endorsed by the Islamic Fiqh Academy.
5
Apply territorial limitation
The ruling from the custom applies only to its people. General custom binds all regions; specific custom binds only the locality. The mufti must not extend a specific custom's ruling beyond its place.
For example, a local trade practice in Cairo does not automatically apply in Damascus.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Custom is valid only if parties do not stipulate otherwise and if it does not contradict a clear text.

In Hanafi fiqh, custom (urf) is recognized as a subsidiary source for deriving rulings, but its application is strictly conditioned. This lesson presents two pivotal conditions from the Rasm al-Mufti tradition: the precedence of explicit contractual stipulations over custom, and the inviolability of definitive texts. A mufti must verify these conditions before relying on custom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the custom in question
Determine what the alleged custom is: is it consistent, widespread, and contemporaneous with the transaction? The source distinguishes between concurrent custom (urf qarin) and prior or transient customs.
Only urf qarin is considered; earlier or passing customs are ignored.
2
Check for explicit stipulation (sarahat)
Examine whether the parties have explicitly stipulated something that contradicts the custom. If such a stipulation exists and does not conflict with the inherent requirements of the contract, the stipulation prevails, and the custom is disregarded.

Rule on avoiding takfir in fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of avoiding takfir by interpreting a Muslim's statement in the best possible light and identify when a fatwa of kufr must be withheld.

Core Idea
Avoid takfir whenever a Muslim's words can bear a non-kufr interpretation—caution prevails over evidence.

This lesson introduces a fundamental principle in Hanafi fatwa methodology: the presumption of a Muslim's good faith. When a statement could be interpreted as disbelief or otherwise, the mufti must exhaust all possible meanings that avoid kufr. Even if the evidence for kufr appears stronger, the safer opinion is adopted. This rule safeguards the Muslim community from hasty excommunication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the statement and its apparent meaning
Recognize the literal or obvious understanding of the Muslim's words.
Do not assume bad intention.
2
Search for alternative interpretations
Look for plausible meanings that do not imply kufr, even if far-fetched.
The alternative must be linguistically or contextually possible.
3
Adopt the non-kufr interpretation
If such an interpretation exists, it must be taken as the intended meaning, and a fatwa of kufr is avoided.
This is an obligation, not a recommendation.
4
Handle ikhtilaf among fuqaha
If scholars disagree on whether the statement constitutes kufr, give precedence to the view that avoids takfir out of caution.
Even if the takfir view is stronger in evidence, caution wins.

If a meaning of a statement can be found that is not a cause of disbelief, then that meaning is intended and issuing a ruling of kufr is avoided. Similarly, if a statement is uttered which some scholars consider kufr and others do not, then even if the opinion of those who consider it kufr is stronger in terms of evidence, out of caution the opinion of those who do not give a fatwa of kufr is given precedence. Commentary
This passage addresses two main issues: the principle of avoiding takfir, and the permissibility for certain muftis to act on weak opinions. The passage first establishes a key rule in Hanafi fatwa methodology: when a Muslim's statement can bear a meaning that does not constitute disbelief, that interpretation must be adopted to avoid charges of kufr. This applies even if the evidence for kufr seems stronger, given that the default for Muslims is soundness. The rule applies also when there is ikhtilaf among fuqaha. The passage then transitions to rules regarding weak (da'if) opinions. It distinguishes between types of muftis: a mufti who possesses analytical insight (dhi ra'y) and can evaluate evidence may act on a weak opinion in his own practice, because he is engaged in partial ijtihad. This is affirmed by Allama Biri and the Khizanat al-Riwayat. However, a qadi is strictly bound to judge by the rajih (preponderant) opinion. According to Qasim ibn Qutlubugha, a muqallid qadi cannot judge by weak opinion. Even if he does, the judgment is void. The notion that a weak opinion becomes strong by virtue of a court judgment only applies to mujtahids, not to muqallids. This distinction preserves the integrity of judicial rulings and prevents judicial discretion from overriding established madhhab positions.

Key Terms
ضعیف قول (da'if qawl): A legal opinion within the madhhab that is less authoritative, with weaker evidence or fewer supporters.
ذی رائے (dhi ra'y): A mufti who possesses analytical insight and the ability to weigh evidence, capable of preferring one opinion over another.
قاضی (qadi): A judge who is bound to rule according to the preponderant (rajih) view of the madhhab and cannot use weak opinions.

The Use of Weak Opinions by Muftis and Qadis
Rules on weak opinions and the distinction between mufti and qadi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a mufti (who may act on weak opinions if he is dhi ra'y) and a qadi (who must follow the rajih opinion), and apply the relevant rules.

Core Idea
Only a mufti with insight (dhi ra'y) may act on a weak opinion; a qadi must always judge by the rajih view.

This lesson explores the nuanced rules surrounding weak (da'if) opinions within the Hanafi madhhab. While a mufti who possesses analytical insight (dhi ra'y) may rely on a weak opinion for himself and his fatwa, a qadi is strictly bound to the preponderant opinion. This distinction preserves the integrity of judicial rulings while allowing scholarly flexibility in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Determine the role: mufti or qadi
Identify whether the person is issuing a fatwa (mufti) or a judicial ruling (qadi).
The rules differ fundamentally.
2
Assess the mufti's qualification
Is the mufti dhi ra'y (insightful, capable of tarjih) or a muqallid (pure follower)?
This determines permissibility.
3
For a dhi ra'y mufti: may prefer weak opinion
He may act on a weak opinion if he considers it stronger in evidence, and it becomes necessary for him to act accordingly.
This is based on partial ijtihad.
4
For a muqallid mufti: must follow rajih
A mufti without tarjih ability is bound to the rajih opinion of the madhhab.
He cannot pick weak opinions arbitrarily.
5
For a qadi: always use rajih opinion
A qadi, especially a muqallid one, must judge by the well-known (mashhur) opinion. A judgment based on weak opinion is void.
The maxim 'a weak opinion becomes strong by judgment' applies only to mujtahids.

دوسری صورت مفتی کا اپنے مذہب میں ماہر ہونا دوسری حالت جس میں اپنے مذہب کو چھوڑ کر دوسرے مذہب پر عمل کرنا جائز ہوتا ہے وہ یہ ہے کہ مفتی اپنے مذہب کا ماہر ہو، دلائل کی خوب معرفت رکھتا ہو، قرآن وسنت پر اس کی گہری نظر ہو اگر چہ وہ درجہ اجتہاد پر فائز نہ ہو لیکن وہ اگر کسی صحیح حدیث کی واقفیت حاصل کرلے جس کی اپنے معنی پر دلالت بھی بالکل واضح ہو اور اسے اس حدیث کے مقابلہ میں اپنے امام کے قول کی کوئی مضبوط دلیل نہ ملے تو اس کے لئے جائز ہے کہ وہ اس مجتہد کے قول کو اختیار کرلے جس کا عمل اس حدیث پاک کے مطابق ہے لیکن اس کے لیے بلا ضرورت اس قول پر فتوی دینا جائز نہیں ہوگا۔
(مستفاد از اصول الافتاء و آدا به ۲۵۹، محاضرات اصول فقه : ۳۴۹)
تیسری صورت: مجتہد قاضی کا مذہب غیر کے مطابق فیصلہ کرنا شیخ الاسلام مفتی تقی عثمانی فرماتے ہیں: مذہب غیر کے مطابق افتاء کے جواز کی تیسری صورت یہ ہے کہ حاکم کسی ایسے شخص کو عہدہ قضاء سونپ دے جو مجتہد ہو اور عہدہ قضاء سونپتے وقت اس قاضی کو کسی متعین مذہب کی پیروی کا پابند نہ بنائے ، پھر وہ قاضی کسی مسئلہ کی بابت ایسا فیصلہ کرے جو اس کے مذہب کے خلاف ہو تو اس کا یہ فیصلہ نافذ ہو جائے گا، بشرطیکہ وہ مسئلہ مجتہد فیہ ہو، لہذا اگر کسی مفتی سے قاضی کے ایسے فیصلوں کی بابت سوال کیا جائے تو مفتی ایسے فیصلوں کے نفاذ کا فتوی دے گا، اگر چہ قاضی کا وہ فیصلہ اس مفتی کے مذہب کے خلاف ہو، اور اس قول کی دلیل یہ ہے کہ فقہاء کرام کا اس بات پر اتفاق ہے کہ حاکم کا حکم اور قاضی کا فیصلہ سابقہ اختلاف کو ختم کر دیتا ہے ( یعنی جب مسئلہ مجتہد فیہ ہو اور قاضی کوئی فیصلہ کر دے تو فریقین کے لیے اس پر عمل کرنا لازم ہو جاتا ہے ) ۔ (اصول الافتاء وآدابه للعثمانی:٢٦٣)
It is obligatory upon a muqallid mufti that he give fatwa only according to the preponderant opinion of his own madhhab. It is not permissible for him to abandon the madhhab of his imam and give fatwa according to the madhhab of another imam. The reason is that the mustafti comes to this mufti so that he may learn the madhhab of his imam regarding the relevant issue and act accordingly. Therefore, it becomes obligatory upon the mufti to give fatwa in accordance with his own and the mustafti's madhhab. However, there are three scenarios where it is permissible to abandon the madhhab of one's imam and act, give fatwa, and judge according to the saying of an imam of another madhhab. These three scenarios are as follows:
First scenario: Giving fatwa on the basis of necessity (ḍarūrah). Among the specific circumstances in which it is permissible to abandon the madhhab of one's imam and give fatwa according to another madhhab is the first scenario: necessity, severe difficulty, hardship, constriction, and widespread affliction. That is, when there is such constriction in a particular issue of one's own madhhab that acting upon it leads to encountering unbearable hardship, then to remove that constriction and fulfill the need of the mustafti, it is permissible to give fatwa according to a weak opinion. (Uṣūl al-Iftā' wa Ādābuh 244) It should be clear that the meaning of 'necessity' here is not the well-known situation which the jurists term 'idtirār' (dire necessity), but rather it is a state lower than idtirār, on account of which a person falls into severe hardship, even if there is no risk of loss of life or limb. (Muḥāḍarāt Uṣūl al-Fiqh 365, Jadīd Fiqhī Mubāḥith, ma'rūf bi Maqālāt As'adī 3/232)
Second scenario: The mufti being an expert in his own madhhab. The second state in which it is permissible to abandon one's madhhab and act according to another madhhab is this: that the mufti is an expert in his own madhhab, has good knowledge of the proofs, has deep insight into the Quran and Sunnah, even if he does not attain the rank of ijtihad. But if he gains acquaintance with a sound hadith whose indication of its meaning is absolutely clear, and he finds no strong evidence for the saying of his own imam in light of that hadith, then it is permissible for him to adopt the saying of the mujtahid whose practice is in accordance with that noble hadith. However, without necessity, it is not permissible for him to give fatwa according to that saying. (Derived from Uṣūl al-Iftā' wa Ādābuh 259, Muḥāḍarāt Uṣūl al-Fiqh 349)
Third scenario: A mujtahid judge's verdict according to a different madhhab. Shaykh al-Islām Muftī Taqī 'Uthmānī says: The third scenario for the permissibility of giving fatwa according to a different madhhab is that the ruler appoints a person who is a mujtahid to the office of judiciary and does not bind that judge to follow a specific madhhab at the time of appointment. Then that judge renders a verdict regarding an issue that is contrary to his own madhhab. That verdict will be enforceable, provided that the issue is one of mujtahad fīh (i.e., open to ijtihad). Therefore, if a mufti is asked regarding such verdicts of the judge, he will give a fatwa that these verdicts are enforceable, even if that verdict of the judge is contrary to the mufti's madhhab. The evidence for this statement is that the noble jurists are in agreement that the ruler's decree and the judge's verdict eliminate prior disagreement (i.e., when an issue is mujtahad fīh and a judge renders a verdict, it becomes obligatory for the parties to act upon it). (Uṣūl al-Iftā' wa Ādābuh al-'Uthmānī 263)
Source pages p. 132

Commentary
This passage from Naseem Ul Mufti presents a crucial rasm al-mufti rule: a mufti bound to a madhhab (muqallid) is generally required to give fatwa only according to the preponderant view of his own imam. The underlying rationale is that the seeker (mustafti) comes to learn the ruling of his own madhhab, not a different one. However, the text outlines three exceptional scenarios where deviation is permitted. 

First, when there is genuine need (hājah) short of dire necessity (idtirār). This hājah is defined as a situation where following one's madhhab leads to unbearable hardship, even if life or limb is not threatened. In such cases, the mufti may resort to a weak (ḍa'īf) opinion from another madhhab to ease the difficulty. The text explicitly distinguishes this from idtirār, which is a higher level of compulsion. 

Second, when the mufti is deeply knowledgeable in his own madhhab and its proofs, and he encounters a clear, unambiguous hadith that contradicts his imam's position, while finding no strong evidence supporting his imam's view. In this situation, he may adopt the opinion of another mujtahid that aligns with the hadith. However, he may not give fatwa based on that adopted view without a pressing need (i.e., the hadith alone does not make it generally permissible; need is still required). 

Third, regarding a mujtahid judge (qāḍī) who is not bound to any madhhab. If such a judge issues a verdict contrary to the mufti's madhhab on a matter open to ijtihad (mujtahad fīh), the mufti must confirm the enforceability of that verdict, even if it conflicts with his own madhhab. This is based on the established principle that a judge's ruling resolves prior disagreement.

The passage emphasizes that these are exceptions; the default rule is adherence to one's own madhhab. The distinctions between hājah and idtirār, and the requirement for expertise in one's own madhhab before looking elsewhere, are important safeguards.

Key Terms
ضرورت (ḍarūrah): Here it means severe hardship (ḥaraj) short of dire necessity (iḍṭirār), causing constriction and difficulty but not threatening life or limb.
مقلد مفتی (muqallid muftī): A mufti who is not a mujtahid but follows a particular madhhab, bound to its preponderant views.
مجتہد فیہ (mujtahad fīh): An issue open to ijtihad, where scholars may legitimately differ, as opposed to matters settled by definitive texts or consensus.

Key Takeaway
A muqallid mufti may depart from his madhhab only in three specific, justified circumstances: severe need, clear hadith evidence for the knowledgeable, or a mujtahid judge's verdict on a disputable matter.

Second Scenario: Following a Clear Hadith Outside the Madhhab
Rasm rule for mufti departing from madhhab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a Hanafi mufti may act upon a clear hadith contrary to his madhhab and identify when giving fatwa on such a view is permissible.

Core Idea
A non-mujtahid mufti may follow a clear hadith over his imam only if he lacks strong evidence for the imam and does not issue fatwa without necessity.

This lesson presents the second scenario from the usul al-ifta: when a mufti—though not a mujtahid—may depart from his madhhab. The passage sets strict conditions: the mufti must be an expert in his own madhhab and proofs, and the contrary hadith must be sahih and clear. Even then, giving fatwa on the alternative view is restricted to cases of necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
What situation is being described? A mufti who is an expert in his own madhhab but finds a sahih hadith that contradicts his imam's opinion.
This is the second of two scenarios; the first (not covered here) deals with a mujtahid mufti.
2
List the prerequisites
What qualifications must the mufti have? He must be an expert in his madhhab, have good knowledge of evidences (dalail), and deep insight into Quran and Sunnah.
Even though he is not a mujtahid, these qualifications allow him to evaluate the hadith.
3
Identify the trigger
What must the mufti encounter? A sahih hadith whose indication (dalalah) is completely clear (wadih) and which contradicts his imam's statement.
The hadith must be authentic and unambiguous in its meaning.
4
Identify the condition
When can he adopt another madhhab's view? If he finds no strong evidence (dalil qawi) for his imam's opinion in comparison to that hadith.
The absence of strong evidence for the imam's view is essential.
5
Identify the restriction
What is prohibited? He cannot give fatwa on the alternative view without necessity (darurah). He may adopt that view for himself but issuing a fatwa requires a genuine need.
This prevents frivolous departure from the madhhab.

Third Scenario: Mujtahid Qadi's Ruling Contrary to His Madhhab
Explains when a mujtahid qadi's ruling contrary to his madhhab is enforceable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a mujtahid qadi's ruling contrary to his madhhab is enforceable and apply the rule that a mufti must uphold such a ruling.

Core Idea
A mujtahid qadi's ruling, when not bound to a madhhab, ends ikhtilaf and is binding even if contrary to his own school.

This lesson examines the third scenario where a mufti may give a fatwa according to a different madhhab: when a mujtahid qadi, appointed without madhhab restriction, issues a ruling that contradicts his own school. The passage clarifies the conditions for enforceability and the mufti's duty to affirm such rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the qadi is a mujtahid and the ruler did not bind him to a specific madhhab.
2
Check the condition
Verify that the matter is mujtahid fih (open to ijtihad), meaning there is no definitive text or consensus on the specific ruling.
3
Determine enforceability
The qadi's ruling is enforceable and ends the previous disagreement among scholars.
4
Apply the mufti's role
If a mufti is asked about such a ruling, he must issue a fatwa confirming its enforceability, even if it contradicts his own madhhab.
5
Recall the proof
The fuqaha are unanimous (ijma) that a qadi's ruling on a mujtahid fih matter resolves the ikhtilaf and becomes binding.
