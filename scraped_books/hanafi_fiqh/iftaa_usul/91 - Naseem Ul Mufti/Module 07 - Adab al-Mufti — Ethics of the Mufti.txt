The Weighty Responsibility of the Mufti
Introduction on the high office and duties of the mufti.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the primary responsibilities of a mufti as outlined in the Hanafi tradition and identify the key source texts for ifta principles.

Core Idea
The mufti is the Prophet's deputy, bound to truth and the preponderant (raajih) opinion, not personal desire.

Before diving into specific rules of fatwa-writing, this lesson establishes the moral and spiritual foundation of the mufti's office. The mufti is not merely a jurist answering questions; he is a deputy of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and will be questioned by Allah. This lesson clarifies the mufti's ultimate accountability and the need for truthfulness and adherence to the preponderant opinion.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the mufti's high office
Identify the description of the mufti's status in the passage (jaleel al-qadr, azeem al-shaan). Consider why this status demands special conduct.
The mufti's office is not ordinary; it carries great honor and responsibility.
2
Identify the mufti's duty
From the passage, extract what the mufti must avoid (cheap fame, corrupt motives) and what he must uphold (truthfulness, safeguarding the office).
The mufti must only give fatwa based on the correct and preponderant (raajih) opinion.
3
Understand the mufti's accountability
Read Ibn al-Qayyim's quote: the mufti is a deputy of the Prophet and will be questioned by Allah. Reflect on how this accountability shapes his approach.
This is a critical reminder that the mufti's role has eternal consequences.
4
Locate the authoritative sources
List the Hanafi works mentioned as sources for the principles of ifta in this book. Note the authors: Ibn Abidin, Mufti Taqi Uthmani, etc.
These are the foundational references for Hanafi usul al-ifta.

The Prohibition of Fatwa Based on a Weak Opinion
Multiple scholars affirm consensus on prohibiting fatwa by marjuh opinion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that a mufti must give fatwa based on the rajih opinion, explain the consensus (ijma) behind it, and identify the exception for mujtahids facing ta'āruḍ.

Core Idea
Giving fatwa based on a weak opinion is following desire, unanimously forbidden.

This lesson compiles statements from Hanafi, Maliki, and Shafi'i scholars affirming a core principle of fatwa: it is impermissible to issue a fatwa or judicial ruling based on a marjuh (weak) opinion. The evidence is that doing so amounts to following one's desire (hawa), which is prohibited by consensus (ijma). An exception exists when a mujtahid within the madhhab faces genuine conflict of evidences (ta'āruḍ) and is unable to give preference; then either opinion may be chosen. The lesson also warns against the common abuse of picking opinions based on personal interest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The ruling is that it is impermissible to give fatwa based on a marjuh (weak) opinion. This applies to both mujtahid and muqallid, as stated by al-Qarafi.
The word 'marjuh' means outweighed; the opposite is 'rajih'.
2
Recognize the primary evidence
The scholars argue that following a weak opinion amounts to following one's desire (hawa), which is unanimously prohibited (bil-ijma). Understanding this connection is crucial.
Ibn al-Salah emphasizes that acting without tarjih is acting against ijma.
3
Examine the exception
A mujtahid within the madhhab may choose between conflicting opinions when the evidences are truly in ta'āruḍ and he is unable to perform tarjih. This exception is limited to mujtahids.
Al-Qarafi explicitly states this exception.
4
Understand the implications for the muqallid
A muqallid (lay follower) must follow the mashhur (well-known) opinion. He cannot pick an obscure opinion that suits his desire.
Ibn Farhun stresses this point.
5
Apply the rule to prevent abuse
The incident from Qadi al-Baji shows how switching opinions based on personal interest is condemned. The lesson is that fatwa must be objective.
The scholars cited so that the rule is not manipulated for personal gain.

Commentary
This passage is a collection of authoritative statements from scholars of different madhhabs (Maliki, Hanafi, Shafi'i) all affirming a single rule: it is prohibited for both a mujtahid (within a madhhab) and a muqallid (lay follower) to give a fatwa or judicial ruling based on a weak (marjuh) opinion merely because it suits one's desire, without proper tarjih (weighing of evidence). The core evidence is that this constitutes following one's desire, which is unanimously (bil-ijma') forbidden. The passage clarifies an exception: if a mujtahid finds the evidences truly conflicting (ta'āruḍ) and he is unable to give preference, then it is permissible (by consensus) to choose either opinion. However, for a muqallid who does not even know which opinion is stronger (i.e., the mashhur), it is not allowed to pick and choose arbitrarily. Imam Ibn al-Salah emphasizes that acting without tarjih is acting against ijma'. The narration from Qadi al-Baji illustrates the practical abuse: jurists gave a fatwa causing harm, but when they realized it was for al-Baji himself, they switched to a more favorable opinion. This confirms the prohibition is not based on the person but on the principle. The Hanafi school fully endorses this rule, as seen in Ibn Qutlubugha's statement, and it is a foundational principle in Rasm al-Mufti literature.

Key Terms
مرجوح قول (marjuh qawl): The weaker opinion that has been outweighed by the stronger (rajih) opinion after proper tarjih.
راجح قول (rajih qawl): The stronger opinion that is preferred based on evidence.
مجتہد (mujtahid): A scholar who can derive rulings directly from evidence; here, specifically a mujtahid within the madhhab who can weigh conflicting opinions.
مقلد (muqallid): A layperson who follows a mujtahid; in this passage, a muqallid who cannot perform tarjih.
ترجیح (tarjih): The process of giving preference to one opinion over another based on the strength of evidence.
تعارض (ta'āruḍ): Apparent contradiction between two pieces of evidence that cannot be reconciled.

The Mufti's Judgment When Tarjih Principles Conflict
Critical refinement: limits of tarjih rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why conflicting tarjih principles require the mufti's sound taste and identify the role of sadd al-dhara'i' and ibtilā' 'āmm in choosing a ruling.

Core Idea
Tarjih principles guide but do not dictate: when they conflict, the mufti's developed juristic taste decides.

The passage from Mufti Taqi Uthmani warns that the many grounds of preference (wujuh al-tarjih) sometimes conflict with each other. There is no universal rule to resolve such conflicts. Instead, the mufti must rely on his sound taste and juristic faculty. Moreover, the mufti may consider broader objections like blocking evil means (sadd al-dhara'i') or providing ease in widespread affliction (ibtilā' 'āmm). This lesson explores the limitations of tarjih rules and the essential role of the mufti's ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim
The passage states that tarjih principles are not universal rules applicable in all circumstances. The mufti cannot blindly follow them.
The claim is based on the reality of conflict between different grounds of preference.
2
Recognize the conflict example
The text gives an example: one principle favors a saying because it is in Zahir al-Riwayah (most authoritative transmission), while another principle favors a different saying because it is the direct view of Imam Abu Hanifa. No inherent hierarchy exists.
This illustrates that multiple valid grounds can point to different outcomes.
3
Understand the solution: mufti's dhawq and malakah
In such cases, the matter is entrusted to the mufti's sound taste (dhawq ṣaḥīḥ) and juristic faculty (malakah fiqhiyyah). These are developed through rigorous study and practice.
This is not arbitrary preference but informed intuition.
4
Note the two additional considerations
The mufti may also consider broader maqasid: sometimes he chooses a cautious ruling to block evil means (sadd al-dhara'i'), and sometimes a facilitative ruling when the issue is widespread (ibtilā' 'āmm) to prevent hardship.
These considerations reflect the mufti's responsibility to the community.
5
Conclude the mufti's role
The mufti's judgment is not mechanical; it requires ijtihad and awareness of objectives. The tarjih principles are tools, not rigid formulas.
This underscores the importance of developing a strong malakah fiqhiyyah.

The Rule of Declaring a Book Weak for Fatwa
Rule about declaring a book unreliable for fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a book declared weak by a muhaqqiq is unreliable unless a higher-ranking scholar affirms it.

Core Idea
A book declared weak by a muhaqqiq is unreliable unless a higher-ranking scholar affirms it.

In Hanafi iftaa practice, not every book carries equal weight. A mufti must know which sources are reliable. This lesson explains the fourth cause: when a qualified jurist declares a book weak, it becomes unreliable for fatwa — unless a higher-ranking jurist overrides that judgment. This rule is part of rasm al-mufti, the discipline that trains muftis in proper source selection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
What is the basic rule? When a muhaqqiq faqih or great scholar declares a book weak, it becomes unreliable (ghayr mu'tabar) for fatwa. No reason is required from the scholar.
The declaration itself is sufficient.
2
Identify the exception
The exception is when another jurist, of higher rank (daraja), explicitly affirms the book's reliability. Then the book is considered reliable.
Higher rank means greater scholarly authority (a'lamiyyah, awsaqiyyah).
3
Apply the exception condition
The affirming jurist must be higher in rank than the one who weakened the book. If equal or lower, the weakening stands.
4
Study the example
The book 'Mushtamil al-Ahkam' by al-Rumi was declared weak by Barkali and Lakhnawi. Since these scholars were senior, the book is unreliable.
This shows how the rule is applied in practice.

Fifth Cause: Author Being Weak — Evaluating Source Reliability
Rule about unreliability due to weak author

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of author weakness to determine whether a book is reliable for fatwa, and identify when a higher-ranking jurist's endorsement overrides a declaration of weakness.

Core Idea
A mufti must not rely on books by an author declared weak by a jurist, unless a higher-ranking jurist affirms his reliability.

When evaluating sources for fatwa, a mufti must consider the author's reliability. The fifth cause of unreliability is the author being weak (da'if). However, this is not absolute: a higher-ranking jurist can restore the author's credibility. This lesson unpacks the rule and its exception, using the example of al-Zahidi's Quniyat al-Munya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause and its effect
Read the first statement: if a jurist declares an author weak, then all his books become unreliable. This is the default rule.
The cause is 'author being weak' and the effect is 'all books unreliable'.
2
Identify the exception
Notice the 'but' (lekin). If another jurist, higher in knowledge, declares the same author reliable, then the books become reliable. This is the exception.
The exception requires the second jurist to be superior in scholarly rank ('almi lihaz se pahle se buland tar').
3
Analyze the example
Study the example of al-Zahidi. Ibn Abidin al-Shami, a great Hanafi jurist, declared al-Zahidi weak because he narrated weak traditions. Consequently, al-Zahidi's book Quniyat al-Munya is unreliable. No higher jurist refuted Ibn Abidin, so the default rule stands.
The example shows the application: a single jurist's declaration suffices unless overridden by a higher authority.
4
Understand the hierarchy of jurists
Recognize that this rule depends on the hierarchy of jurists. Not every jurist's opinion carries equal weight; the higher-ranking jurist's endorsement can override a lower one's declaration of weakness.
This reflects the Hanafi principle that later scholars ('mutajaddidun') can overturn earlier rulings if they are more knowledgeable.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Consider a case: Jurist A declares Author X weak. Jurist B, whom the community considers less knowledgeable than A, declares X reliable. Which opinion prevails? Answer: Jurist A's, because the second jurist is not higher. The book remains unreliable.
Practice applying the condition: the second jurist must be higher, not just any endorser.

Fatwa with Weak Opinion in Cases of Compulsion
States the first scenario permitting fatwa with a weak opinion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a Hanafi mufti may depart from the rajih opinion and apply this principle to a case of necessity, using the example of ghusl after a wet dream.

Core Idea
A mufti may issue a fatwa with a weak opinion when following the preponderant view causes extreme hardship.

You already know that a mufti must normally follow the preponderant (rajih) view of the madhhab. But what if strict adherence leads to severe difficulty? The Hanafi tradition recognizes a principle of necessity (darura) that allows reliance on a weaker (marjuh) opinion in exceptional circumstances. This lesson explores the first such scenario: compulsion (idhtirar), illustrated with the classic case of ghusl after a wet dream in a sensitive social situation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Note the default rule: a mufti must give fatwa according to the rajih opinion as determined by the ashab al-tarjih. The weak (marjuh) opinion is normally not to be acted upon.
2
Recognize the exception for compulsion
The exception arises when a person is in a state of 'idhtirar' – extreme hardship. If following the rajih becomes extremely difficult, the weak opinion may be used to provide relief.
The term 'majboor' (compelled) is key.
3
Understand the condition
The condition is that the hardship must be genuine and severe. The weak opinion must offer measurable ease. This is not a blanket permission for any preference.
The mufti must assess the degree of difficulty.
4
Examine the example
The text provides the example of ghusl after a wet dream. The rajih view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) obligates ghusl whenever mani exits with shahwa from its source. Imam Abu Yusuf requires shahwa at the moment of exit.
This intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is the basis for applying the weak opinion.
5
Determine the relief
A guest who has a wet dream and fears suspicion (because performing ghusl would be noticed) may follow Imam Abu Yusuf's opinion. The mufti may issue a fatwa accordingly, allowing the guest to avoid ghusl.
The relief is immediate: no need to perform ghusl in that situation.

Fourteenth Principle: Mufti giving fatwa on a weak opinion.

At the beginning of the book, under the first principle, it has been transmitted from Allamah Qasim bin Qutlubugha and other jurists that in issues where there are different opinions among jurists, the mufti will give fatwa according to the opinion that the people of tarjih have declared preponderant. It is not permissible for the mufti to give fatwa according to the weak and marjuh opinion. However, there are several situations where it is permissible for both the mufti and the acting person to give fatwa and act according to the weak and marjuh opinion. These situations are as follows:

First Situation: A person being compelled. Whoever becomes so compelled that acting on the preponderant opinion is extremely difficult for him, and acting on the weak opinion gives him some sort of ease and comfort, then such a distressed person is permitted to act on the weak and marjuh opinion, and mufti scholars may also give him fatwa according to the weak opinion.
(Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: 180)

Its example is: When mani exits from its place with shahwa, according to the preponderant opinion of the Hanafis, ghusl becomes obligatory, whether there is shahwa at the time of exiting from the private part or not. In contrast, Imam Abu Yusuf's opinion is that the presence of shahwa at the time of exiting from the member is also necessary. Therefore, if a person is a guest at someone's place and he experiences a wet dream, and performing ghusl would cause suspicion, then such a person is permitted to act on Imam Abu Yusuf's opinion—that is, he is permitted that as soon as he has a wet dream...

Commentary
This passage introduces the Fourteenth Principle regarding a mufti issuing a fatwa based on a weak (marjuh) opinion. Normally, a mufti must adhere to the preponderant (rajih) view. However, the first exceptional scenario is when a person is in a state of duress (idhtirar): if following the rajih opinion causes extreme hardship, and the weak opinion provides relief, then it becomes permissible to act upon and issue fatwa according to that weak opinion. The example given is from the issue of ghusl after a wet dream. The rajih Hanafi position, attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa (and the majority), holds that ghusl is obligatory whenever mani exits with shahwa from its source, regardless of shahwa at the point of exit. Imam Abu Yusuf, however, requires shahwa at the actual exit from the private part. If a guest has a wet dream and performing ghusl would arouse suspicion (perhaps because he would need to bathe in a shared facility), he may follow Imam Abu Yusuf's opinion to avoid embarrassment or accusation. This illustrates how dire necessity (haja or darura) can justify departing from the dominant view within the madhhab.

Key Terms
مجبور (majboor): A person in a state of compulsion or dire need, where following the rajih view is extremely difficult.
ضعیف قول (da'if qawl): A weak or marjuh opinion—one that is not preponderant according to the scholars of tarjih.
اصحاب الترجیح (ashab al-tarjih): Scholars who weigh and determine which opinion is preponderant (rajih).

The Mufti's Exception: Fatwa on Weak Opinion Under Necessity
Explaining the first exceptional scenario where a mufti may give fatwa on a weak opinion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a Hanafi mufti may issue a fatwa based on a weak opinion and apply the rule to a case of necessity.

Core Idea
A mufti may follow a weak opinion only when necessity makes the preponderant opinion unbearable and the weak one brings ease.

The default rule in the Hanafi school is that a mufti must issue fatwa according to the preponderant (rajih) opinion. However, the law recognizes that blind adherence can lead to hardship. This lesson introduces the first exception: when a person is so compelled (mudtarr) that following the rajih opinion is extremely difficult, and a weak (marjuh) opinion provides relief, both the mufti and the questioner may act on that weak opinion. The classical example involves the obligation of ghusl after a wet dream when social harm is feared.

Guided Path
1
Recall the default rule
The mufti normally follows the preponderant opinion (rajih) determined by ashab al-tarjih; weak (marjuh) opinions are not permitted in fatwa.
This is the baseline from which the exception deviates.
2
Identify the exception condition
The exception requires the questioner to be mudtarr (compelled) such that following the rajih opinion would cause extreme hardship, and the marjuh opinion offers ease.
Mudtarr is a key term; it implies genuine compulsion, not mere preference.
3
Analyse the example: ghusl dispute
The rajih Hanafi view (Abu Hanifa & Muhammad) makes ghusl wajib when semen exits with desire, regardless of desire at exit. Abu Yusuf’s weak view requires desire at exit. A guest who fears suspicion if he performs ghusl may follow Abu Yusuf's view to avoid ghusl.
Note the hila (legal stratagem) of grasping the private part to suppress desire.
4
Understand the mufti's role
The mufti may give fatwa on the weak opinion for such a compelled person, as confirmed by Fakhr al-A'imma and Allama Shami.
The general prohibition on weak opinions remains for non-necessity cases.

Commentary
This passage is a rasm al-mufti rule explaining when a mufti may deviate from the preponderant opinion and give fatwa on a weak opinion. The author begins by restating the default principle: the mufti must follow the opinion deemed preponderant by the scholars of tarjih. He then introduces exceptions, focusing on the first exception—necessity (idrār). The ruling is that if a person is in such a state of compulsion that following the preponderant opinion would cause extreme hardship, and the weak opinion provides ease, then both acting on that weak opinion and giving fatwa on it are permitted. The example given is the Hanafi dispute on ghusl for seminal emission. The preponderant Hanafi position (from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is that ghusl is wajib whenever semen exits with desire, regardless of desire at the point of exit. Imam Abu Yusuf considered desire at the point of exit necessary. A guest who has a wet dream may fear suspicion if he performs ghusl, so he is allowed to follow Abu Yusuf's weak opinion by grasping his private part after the dream to suppress desire, avoiding the obligation of ghusl. This is a classic hila (legal stratagem) applied under necessity. The passage further supports this with a transmission from Fakhr al-A'imma via Ibn Nujaym, stating that a mufti may give fatwa on weaker opinions in places of hardship. Allama Shami confirms that the general prohibition on weak opinions applies only in non-necessity cases. The second scenario (caution in declaring kufr) is introduced but not completed in this window.

Key Terms
راجح (rajih): The preponderant opinion within the Hanafi madhhab, determined by the scholars of tarjih.
مرجوح (marjuh): The weaker opinion in the madhhab, which is not normally acted upon.
ضعیف (da'if): A weak opinion, synonymous with marjuh in this context, meaning the less authoritative view.
مضطر (mudtarr): A person in a state of duress or necessity (darura), facing extreme difficulty in following the preponderant opinion.
