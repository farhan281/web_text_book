Fatwa Hierarchy in Later Times: The Ruling of Allamah Shami
Provides a practical fatwa rule for later times based on Shami's observation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Allamah Shami's rule that in later times, non-mujtahid muftis must follow Imam A'zam's opinion unless ashab al-tarjih have preponderated another view.

Core Idea
In the absence of mujtahid muftis, the default preponderant opinion is always Imam A'zam's.

This lesson builds on the previous principle by adding a crucial practical note from Allamah Shami (Ibn 'Abidin). He observes that in his time (and by extension in later centuries), finding a mujtahid mufti is rare, and most muftis are non-mujtahids. Therefore, the default rule for choosing between Imam A'zam and the Sahibayn is heavily tilted: non-mujtahid muftis must follow Imam A'zam unless later scholars (ashab al-tarjih) have explicitly preferred another view. This solidifies the fatwa hierarchy for post-classical Hanafis.

Guided Path
1
Context: Allamah Shami's Observation
Allamah Shami notes that in his era, mujtahid muftis are virtually nonexistent. Most muftis are muqallids (non-mujtahids) who cannot weigh evidence or prefer one opinion over another.
This reflects the later Hanafi view that the door of absolute ijtihad is closed.
2
Implication for Non-Mujtahid Muftis
Since non-mujtahid muftis lack the ability to perform tarjih (preferring one evidence over another), they cannot choose between Imam A'zam and the Sahibayn based on evidence.
Their duty is to follow a pre-established ruling.
3
Default Ruling: Imam A'zam's View
Therefore, in any non-custom difference, the default ruling for the mufti is to adopt Imam A'zam's opinion. This is because Imam A'zam is the founder of the school and his view carries foundational authority.
This matches the first view of Abdullah ibn Mubarak, but now applied universally for non-mujtahids.
4
Exception: Intervention of Ashab al-Tarjih
If later scholars (ashab al-tarjih, such as the authors of the relied-upon texts) have explicitly declared the Sahibayn's view preponderant (rajih) in a particular issue, then the mufti may follow that instead.
This exception preserves the possibility of following the Sahibayn when the school's later consensus supports it.
5
Application to a Modern Scenario
Consider a mufti today faced with a difference on a contract. He checks the major Hanafi references. If the later jurists have not explicitly preponderated the Sahibayn's view, he follows Imam A'zam.
This ensures consistency and prevents individual preference from overriding school authority.

Core Idea

This lesson introduces the foundational principle of Hanafi fatwa: the six core books of Imam Muhammad (Zahir al-Riwayah) are the primary source for rulings. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for any mufti to ensure consistency and authority in issuing fatwas.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In Hanafi fatwa, the concise authoritative texts (mutun) prevail over later commentaries and fatwa collections.

As a mufti, you will inevitably encounter situations where the ruling in a classical commentary or a later fatwa collection seems to contradict a ruling in a concise primary text. This principle clarifies the hierarchy: the matn (plural mutun) carries more weight because it was composed to preserve the most preponderant opinion in a distilled form. Understanding this rule safeguards the integrity of fatwa by anchoring it to the core texts of the madhhab.

Verify the matn is mu'tabar
Ensure the matn in question is among the reliable (mu'tabar) mutun of the Hanafi school, such as al-Quduri, al-Mukhtar, Kanz al-Daqa'iq, etc. If the text is doubted, it loses precedence.
Mu'tabar mutun are those universally accepted as authoritative by the later Fuqaha.

Apply the precedence rule
Give preference to the ruling from the matn over the sharh or fatwa. The matn's wording is considered more concise, preservative, and reflective of the preponderant opinion.
This rule is based on the nature of the mutun's composition, not on chronological order.
4
Confirm no other overriding factor
Check if there is any later consensus or explicit tarjih that overrides the mutun. In the absence of such, the matn prevails.
If a later sharh clearly states the mufta bihi is from a different matn, that is also valid.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi fatwa methodology (rasm al-mufti): when a ruling can be derived by both qiyas and istihsan, the mufti must prefer the istihsan-based opinion. The reason is that istihsan is grounded in stronger evidence. Understanding this nuance is essential for correct fatwa practice.

Fifth Principle: Following the style of authors is necessary. Most authors sometimes use explicit words to prefer one of the conflicting statements, but generally they adopt a unique style and specific manner according to their temperament to determine the preferred statement, and they adhere to this methodology throughout the book. Therefore, for determining the preferred statement in disputed issues, it is necessary to follow this specific style of theirs, and those specific styles are as follows:

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the third style of evidence presentation in Hanafi works and explain how the presence of evidence signals the preponderant (mufta bihi) opinion.

Core Idea
When evidence is cited for only one opinion, that opinion is the mufta bihi.

A Hanafi author may present multiple opinions but only provide supporting evidence for the one they consider strongest. This stylistic choice is a subtle yet effective way to indicate which opinion is relied upon for fatwa. Understanding this convention helps you read such texts accurately and avoid the misconception that all opinions carry equal weight.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the style
Look for passages where multiple opinions are stated but only one is accompanied by a proof (dalil). This is the third style described by the author.

Apply the principle
When studying a Hanafi text, if you see an opinion without any evidence, you should treat it as marjuh (weak). The mufta bihi is the opinion for which the author actually provides a proof.
Always cross-check with other sources if needed, but this convention is a reliable shortcut.

Fourth Style: Refusing the Non-Preponderant to Indicate the Preponderant
Describes the method of refuting the weak opinion to indicate the preponderant one.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the preponderant opinion in a text where the author refutes the evidence of one opinion and leaves another unchallenged.

Core Idea
If an author refutes one opinion's evidence, the unchallenged opinion is the preponderant one.

In Hanafi mufti literature, authors sometimes indicate the preponderant opinion (rajih) not by explicitly stating it, but by refuting the evidence of the weak opinion. This lesson explains how to read such texts correctly. Understanding this style is essential for identifying the mufta bihi position when studying classical fatwa works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting opinions
First, determine which two (or more) opinions are being discussed. The passage will mention at least two positions on a fiqh issue.
2
Determine which opinion's evidence is cited
The author may cite the evidence (dalil) for one of the opinions. If no evidence is cited for a particular opinion, it may be the preponderant one — the author feels no need to defend it.
3
See if the cited evidence is refuted
If the author, after citing an opinion's evidence, proceeds to refute it (tardid), then that opinion is non-preponderant. The refutation signals weakness.
The refutation may be explicit or implicit, e.g., pointing out a flaw in the evidence.
4
Conclude the preponderant opinion
The opinion that remains unchallenged — whose evidence is either not mentioned or not refuted — is the preponderant (rajih) opinion according to that author.
This conclusion is drawn from the author's silence on the preferred opinion's evidence.

Principle for resolving sahih/asahh conflict

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the 'asahh' opinion takes precedence over the 'sahih' opinion when they come from different scholars and books.

Core Idea
The madhhab prefers 'asahh' over 'sahih' because the counterpart of 'asahh' may be invalid.

When two contradictory opinions are labeled 'sahih' and 'asahh' by different scholars in different books, a mufti must know which to prefer. Some argue that 'sahih' wins because it implies the other is invalid. However, the well-known Hanafi view is that 'asahh' is stronger. This principle refines your ability to navigate the Hanafi fatwa hierarchy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
You have two sayings from different scholars and books: one described as 'sahih', the other as 'asahh'. Note the source of each label.
The disparity in sources (different scholars, different books) is crucial to this principle.
2
Examine the opposing argument
Some scholars argue that 'sahih' should prevail because it asserts the other saying is invalid, while 'asahh' allows for both to be correct.
They reason that the opinion agreed upon as correct (the 'sahih' one) should be acted upon.
3
Apply the madhhab's relied-upon view
Despite the initial reasoning, the mas'hur (well-known) and mu'tamad (reliable) view of the Hanafi madhhab is that the 'asahh' saying is stronger.
This is because the counterpart of 'asahh' may be invalid, so 'asahh' indicates a higher degree of correctness.
4

The reason: 'asahh' is a comparative which does not necessarily imply the other is correct; it could be invalid. Thus, the label 'asahh' itself signals that the opinion is the one to follow.
The term 'sahih' is absolute, but its counterpart is explicitly negated; 'asahh' leaves room for the other to be wrong.
5
Conclude with mufta bihi
Therefore, when you encounter this conflict in practice, the opinion labeled 'asahh' is mufta bihi (the operative ruling).
This principle overrides the apparent logic that 'sahih' is more definitive.

Seventh Principle: Preference of Asahh over Sahih in Conflicting Qawls
Ruling on preferring asahh over sahih in conflicting qawls from same Imam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that when a single Imam's conflicting qawls are corrected as 'sahih' and 'asahh', the asahh is given preference, and identify the well-known opinion when a third qawl exists.

Core Idea
Asahh dominates sahih in same-Imam qawls by consensus.

This principle addresses a common scenario in Hanafi fatwa: when two rulings from the same Imam exist, one labeled 'sahih' and the other 'asahh'. The rule is clear-cut: the 'asahh' is preferred. But careful—'sahih' here does not mean 'invalid'; it means correct, only less so. This lesson will walk you through the rule, its rationale, and the nuanced exception when a third qawl appears.

Guided Path
1
Identify the labels
Recognize that the two qawls are both corrected—one as sahih, the other as asahh. The superlative 'asahh' inherently indicates a higher degree of correctness.
These labels come from mujtahids within the madhhab (as-hab al-tarjih).
2
Apply the consensus rule
By consensus (bil-ittifaq), the qawl labelled asahh is given preference. No further weighing is needed.
This is because the asahh is more correct according to the mujtahid who made the correction.
3
Clarify the opposite of sahih
Understand that here 'sahih' is not opposed to 'fasid' (invalid); its opposite is 'asahh'. Both are valid, but one is stronger.
This avoids confusion: a sahih qawl is not weak; it is simply not the strongest.
4
Address the exception
If a third qawl exists in the same issue, some scholars differ on whether the asahh still prevails. The well-known (mashhur) opinion is that it does.
The ikhtilaf is resolved by the mashhur position, maintaining clarity.
5
Apply in fatwa
When giving fatwa, a mufti should adopt the asahh qawl, even if a third qawl exists, according to the well-known view.
This ensures consistency and reliance on the most authenticated position.

Eighth Principle: Preference Based on a Later Author's Declaration of Asahh
Rule on preference when a later author declares asahh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the eighth principle of Rasm al-Mufti to determine which qawl is preferred when a later author declares one correction as asahh.

Core Idea
A later author's declaration of asahh overrides earlier labels, even if that qawl was only sahih before.

This lesson presents a key rule of mufti hierarchy: the tarjih of a later authoritative compiler (musannif) takes precedence over earlier jurists' labels of sahih or asahh. This principle ensures consistency in fatwa when later scholars review and refine earlier work. The rule is unanimous (bil-ittifaq) among Hanafi jurists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Determine that there are two conflicting qawls, each previously corrected: one labeled 'sahih' and the other 'asahh' by an earlier jurist.
2
Note the earlier jurist's labeling
Observe which qawl was marked sahih and which was marked asahh by the earlier faqih.
This sets the baseline but does not determine the final preference.
3
Observe the later author's intervention
The musannif (later author) mentions both corrections in his book and then declares one of them as asahh relative to the other.
The musannif may declare as asahh the qawl that was previously sahih or the one that was already asahh.
4
Apply the principle
By consensus, the qawl which the musannif declared as asahh is automatically preferred (rajih), irrespective of the earlier labels.
This is a rule of tarjih based on the authority of the later compiler.
5
Conclude the mufta bihi
The mufti must adopt the qawl declared as asahh by the later author as the relied-upon position for fatwa.
This resolves the apparent contradiction between sahih and asahh labels.

Preference via Comparative Words (Ism Tafdil) in Tarjih
Ruling on preferring the qawl described with comparative adjectives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the use of ism tafdil in a tashih and apply the rule that such a qawl is given preference over one without such a description.

Core Idea
A qawl corrected with an ism tafdil (comparative) word is automatically preferred over a qawl without it.

In Hanafi fatwa methodology, muftis often encounter two conflicting qawls within the school. This lesson introduces the ninth principle of tarjih: when one qawl is praised using comparative or superlative adjectives (ism tafdil) like 'al-ashbah' or 'al-arfaq', it indicates a higher degree of correctness, and acting upon it becomes necessary. This is a subtle but powerful tool for resolving apparent contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting qawls
Determine the two positions that are in apparent contradiction. They must both be from reliable Hanafi sources.
The principle applies only when the conflict is real, not imagined.
2
Examine the correction terminology
Look at how each qawl is described in the tashih (correction report). Specifically, check if any word follows the ism tafdil pattern (af‘al) such as ashbah, arfaq, alyaq, aslah.
These are not limited to 'asahh' alone.
3
Check if only one qawl has ism tafdil
If one qawl is described with an ism tafdil word and the other is not, the principle triggers: the qawl with ism tafdil is given preference.
If both have ism tafdil words, further principles may be needed.
4
Apply the preference rule
Conclude that the qawl with ism tafdil is the mufta bihi (operative opinion) and must be acted upon. No further tarjih is necessary based on this principle alone.
This principle operates at the level of tashih, not at the level of original strength of evidence.
5
Document the fatwa
In writing the fatwa, cite this principle and indicate that the preferred qawl is the one described with ism tafdil, referencing the source (e.g., Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti).
Always include the authority level: from Zahir al-Riwayah or later fatwa works.

Tenth Principle: Ikhtiyar When Only One Qawl is Corrected
Ruling on preference when only one of two conflicting qawls is corrected

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a mufti has ikhtiyar between two conflicting qawls and apply the preference for the corrected qawl in a fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
When only one qawl is corrected, the mufti has choice, but the corrected qawl is recommended as the better path.

This lesson introduces the tenth principle from 'Uqūd Rasm al-Muftī, a key text in Hanafi iftā' methodology. It addresses a common scenario: when a mufti finds two conflicting opinions (qawls) but only one is explicitly described as 'more correct' (al-asahh), 'more appropriate' (al-arfaq), or 'more precautionary' (al-ahwat). Understanding this principle helps the mufti navigate such cases with both discretion and proper guidance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The principle applies when there are two conflicting qawls (opinions), and only one of them is explicitly corrected (tashih) using terms like 'huwa al-asahh', 'huwa al-arfaq', or 'al-ahwat'. The other qawl is not described as either sahih or fasid.
The absence of any description on the other qawl is key; if it were described as sahih or fasid, different principles apply.
2
Recognize the corrective phrases
Common phrases used for tashih include 'huwa al-asahh' (it is more correct), 'huwa al-arfaq' (it is more appropriate), and 'al-ahwat' (the more precautionary). These indicate a preference but not an absolute obligation.
These phrases are signs of the author's assessment, not binding commands.
3
Determine the ruling on choice
The mufti has ikhtiyar (choice) between the two qawls because there is no explicit conflict in tarjih and tashih. The other qawl is not declared fasid, so it remains a valid option.
Ikhtiyar means the mufti may select either, but the principle provides guidance.
4
Identify the preferred action
Despite the choice, acting on the qawl that has been corrected is better (behtar). The presence of corrective words indicates that this qawl is more important (aham) and more correct (ziyada sahih).
This preference is not mandatory but is recommended for sound fatwa practice.
5
Apply the principle
In a fatwa, the mufti should first check if only one qawl is corrected. If so, he may choose either but is advised to adopt the corrected qawl. This balances flexibility with reliability.
The principle helps avoid hesitation when the other qawl has no explicit status.

Ninth Principle: Tafdhil Words Indicate Preference
Rule for when tafdhil words indicate preference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ninth principle of rasm al-mufti by identifying when a tafdhil word (ism tafdhil) is used and ruling that the corresponding opinion is preferred.

Core Idea
A tafdhil word in a scholar's correction signals preference – act on that opinion.

When a Hanafi mufti encounters two conflicting opinions recorded in the madhhab, he must determine which one to follow. Scholars often indicate their preferred opinion using specific terms of the superlative pattern. This lesson explains the binding tarjih value of such terms, a key tool in the mufti's hierarchy of preference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting opinions (qawlayn mutaʿāriḍayn)
The mufti must first recognize that there are two reported rulings that conflict in the same issue. The source text begins by stating the condition of two mutaʿāriḍ aqwāl.
The conflict must be real, not apparent.
2
Check if one opinion is corrected using a tafdhil word (ism tafdhīl)
Examine the language used by the authorities who corrected (ṣaḥḥaḥa) each opinion. If one opinion is described with a word on the pattern afʿal (e.g., al-ashbah, al-arfaq, al-aliyaq, al-aslah li zamanihi), it falls under this principle.
Simple positive adjectives like ṣaḥīḥ or al-mukhtār do not trigger this rule.
3
Prefer the opinion with the tafdhil word
If one opinion has a tafdhil word and the other does not, the former is given preference. The text says 'acting upon the first saying is necessary' (lāzim).
This is a binding tarjīḥ, not merely recommended.
4
Apply to examples
The source provides examples: if Opinion A is corrected with 'al-ashbah' and Opinion B is not, then Opinion A is preferred. The mufti must follow Opinion A.
The same applies for al-arfaq, al-aliyaq, al-aslah li zamanihi, etc.

Commentary
This passage presents a rule from the Hanafi usul al-iftaa concerning how a mufti should choose between conflicting opinions when scholars have used specific terminology. The rule states that if one opinion is described with a word of the pattern 'af‘al' (superlative/comparative, e.g., al-ashbah, al-arfaq), and the other opinion is not described with such a word, then the opinion with the superlative description is to be preferred. These terms indicate that the opinion is considered stronger by the scholar who used them. The author emphasizes that acting upon the opinion so described is obligatory (lazim). This is a straightforward tarjih rule: the presence of a tafdhil word creates a clear preference.

Tenth Principle: Correction of One Opinion Only
Rule when only one conflicting opinion is corrected without comment on the other

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that when only one conflicting opinion is corrected without rejecting the other, the mufti has choice, and determine when acting on the corrected opinion is better (awla).

Core Idea
Correction of one opinion without rejecting the other gives the mufti choice, but the corrected one is better.

This lesson covers the tenth principle of the Rasm al-Mufti. When a Hanafi jurist corrects only one of two conflicting opinions using terms like al-asahh, al-arfaq, or al-ahwat, but does not comment on the other, the mufti is not bound to follow that corrected opinion exclusively. Instead, he may choose either, though following the corrected one is recommended. Understanding this nuance prevents the mistake of assuming silence implies rejection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that there are two conflicting opinions (qawlayn muta'aridayn) and only one is explicitly corrected with a tafdhil word.
The correction word may be al-asahh, al-arfaq, or al-ahwat.
2
Understand the mufti's ikhtiyar
Because the other opinion is not declared correct or incorrect, there is no absolute preference; the mufti has a choice between the two.
The text says 'the mufti has a choice' (مفتی کو اختیار ہوگا).
3
Recognize the 'awla' recommendation
Despite the choice, acting on the corrected opinion is better (awla) because the correction word indicates it is more important and more correct.
The reason: the presence of such words is a sign of that opinion's strength.
4
Distinguish from other principles
This principle differs from cases where one opinion is explicitly rejected (e.g., 'fasid') or where both are corrected; in those, the mufti's authority is different.
Silence does not equal rejection; it leaves the opinion valid.
5
Apply to a concrete example
Practice with a scenario: opinion A is marked 'al-ahwat', opinion B is unmarked. Apply the rule: mufti may choose either, but following A is better.
Verify that the condition 'only one corrected' holds.

This principle teaches that not all corrections create a binding preference; when only one side is explicitly corrected, the mufti is not forced to follow it, but doing so is recommended.

The Need for Principles of Tarjeeh in the Hanafi Madhhab
Introduces the need for principles to reconcile conflicting tarjeehat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a Hanafi mufti requires principles (usul) to reconcile conflicting tarjeehat and tasheeh statements, and identify the key terms (sahih, asahh, fatwa) that indicate different levels of endorsement.

Core Idea
A mufti cannot rely on personal preference; predefined principles guide the choice among conflicting tasheeh and tarjeeh statements.

This passage introduces the foundational problem that the fourteen principles of tarjeeh are designed to solve. In the Hanafi madhhab, later scholars often declared conflicting opinions as 'sahih' or 'asahh' or used 'fatwa' for different positions. Without objective criteria, a mufti would be lost. This lesson establishes why systematic principles are essential before exploring their details.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the scenario of conflicting tasheeh
Understand that it is common in Hanafi fiqh for two scholars to each declare a different opinion as sahih or asahh. This creates a dilemma for the mufti.
2
Distinguish between the terms sahih, asahh, and fatwa
Each term indicates a different level of endorsement. 'Sahih' means correct, 'asahh' means more correct (stronger), and 'fatwa' indicates the opinion relied upon for fatwa. A mufti must understand these nuances.
3
Identify the mufti's responsibility
The mufti cannot choose arbitrarily; he must follow established principles. The passage emphasizes 'utmost deliberation and subtlety'.
4
Appreciate the role of the fourteen principles
These principles provide a systematic method for weighing conflicting claims. Even if some were detailed earlier, their summary is crucial for quick reference.

Third Principle: Imam's Opinion Precedes Sahibayn When Tasheeh Terms Are Equal
Defines precedence between Imam Abu Hanifa and his disciples when tasheeh wording is equal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the third principle of Rasm al-Mufti to determine which opinion takes precedence when two corrected opinions conflict, given equal tasheeh wording and corrector rank.

Core Idea
When correctors agree on wording, the Imam's word is chosen.

This lesson unpacks a foundational rule in Hanafi fatwa hierarchy. It clarifies that Imam Abu Hanifa's opinion is not automatically always dominant; rather, it takes precedence only when later scholars have used identical language to correct conflicting opinions and are of equal scholarly standing. This rule preserves the school's core while accounting for later refinements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting opinions
Determine which opinion originates from Imam Abu Hanifa and which from the Sahibayn (or other companions). This is the source distinction.
2
Examine the tasheeh wording
Check whether both opinions have been corrected using the same term (e.g., 'sahih', 'asahh', 'mufta bihi'). If the terms differ, this principle does not apply.
Equal wording means e.g., both are called 'sahih' or both 'asahh'.
3
Evaluate the correctors' rank
Assess whether the scholars who performed the tasheeh are of equal rank. If one corrector is higher, their designation may override the default preference for Imam.
Rank can refer to chronology or recognized authority within the school.
4
Apply the rule
If both conditions (equal wording, equal corrector rank) are met, give precedence to Imam Abu Hanifa's opinion over the Sahibayn.
This is a default rule; exceptions exist.
5
Recognize exceptions
Note that in many areas of mu'amalat, the Sahibayn's opinion is adopted as fatwa due to later juristic preference (istihsan). This principle is for the narrow case of equal tasheeh.
Always check later fatwa compilations for actual practice.

The Fourth Principle: Zahir al-Riwayah Prevails
Defines precedence between Zahir al-Riwayah and non-Zahir al-Riwayah opinions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the fourth principle of Rasm al-Mufti to determine which of two conflicting opinions should be preferred when one is from Zahir al-Riwayah and conditions of correction are equal.

Core Idea
When two conflicting opinions are equally corrected, the one from Zahir al-Riwayah prevails.

In Hanafi fatwa, not all opinions carry equal weight. The school has developed a hierarchy to help muftis choose between conflicting rulings. This lesson introduces a key rule: when two opinions have been confirmed as 'sahih' (correct) by scholars of equal standing and using the same terminology, the opinion found in the core Zahir al-Riwayah texts takes precedence over one from less authoritative sources. Understanding this principle ensures that the mufti relies on the most reliable transmission of the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting opinions
Confirm that both opinions have been declared 'sahih' (correct) by recognized scholars. If not, this rule does not apply.
The term 'tasheeh' refers to the act of certifying a ruling as correct within the madhhab.
2
Verify equality of conditions
Check that the wording for tasheeh is identical and the scholars who performed the tasheeh are of equal rank. If there is any inequality, further evaluation is needed.
Equality of rank is judged by scholarly consensus (e.g., both are major muhaqqiqun).
3
Determine source classification
Identify which opinion belongs to Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Imam Muhammad) and which to non-Zahir al-Riwayah. The Zahir al-Riwayah opinion is preferred.
Zahir al-Riwayah includes: al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat.
4
Apply the rule
If conditions are equal, give fatwa according to the Zahir al-Riwayah opinion. This ensures adherence to the most widely transmitted and authoritative corpus of the school.
This principle is mufta bihi and must be followed unless a stronger evidence compels otherwise.

Agreement of Senior Jurists Preferred
States the sixth principle of Rasm al-Mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the sixth principle of Rasm al-Mufti to choose between conflicting corrected statements based on the rank of the scholars who performed the tas'hih.

Core Idea
When scholars disagree, the senior Hanafi jurists' consensus overrides less authoritative ones.

This lesson introduces the sixth principle of Rasm al-Mufti, which provides a clear hierarchy for resolving conflicts among corrected statements within the Hanafi school. It's not enough that a statement is 'corrected'; we must also consider who corrected it. The principle states that the agreement of senior jurists—like Imam al-Tahawi, al-Sarakhsi, and al-Samarqandi—is given precedence over the agreement of lower-ranking jurists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Recognize that there are two conflicting corrected statements from authoritative Hanafi sources. Each statement has been 'corrected' (musahhah) by some scholars.
The conflict exists only between statements that have undergone tas'hih, not between raw opinions.
2
Identify the ranking of the scholars on each side
Determine which group of scholars agreed upon each statement. The senior jurists (akabir fuqaha) are well-known, towering figures like Imam al-Tahawi, Imam al-Sarakhsi, and Imam Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi. The other group consists of jurists of a lower rank.
The akabir are not necessarily the earliest; they are the most authoritative in the school.
3
Apply the principle
Prefer the statement agreed upon by the senior jurists. This is the mufta bihi position.
If both groups include senior jurists, the principle may not resolve the conflict; other tarjih principles apply.
4
Understand the rationale
The senior jurists possess deeper knowledge, more comprehensive ijtihad, and their consensus is more reliable due to their stature and expertise in the school's methodology.
This principle is a form of tarjih by jama'ah (collective authority).

Principle 7: Istihsan Preferred over Qiyas in Mufti's Practice
Introduces a rule of preference in fatwa methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that istihsan is preferred over qiyas, identify exceptions where qiyas prevails, and apply this rule in fatwa drafting.

Core Idea
Istihsan outweighs qiyas, but exceptions exist where qiyas governs

In the Hanafi school, when two valid proofs (istihsan and qiyas) contradict, which one takes precedence? The seventh principle of Rasm al-Mufti lays down a clear hierarchy: istihsan (juristic preference) is stronger and generally prevails. However, the principle is not absolute; there are specific cases where qiyas is followed instead. Understanding this rule is crucial for muftis who must weigh evidence and issue sound fatwas. This lesson explores the rule, its rationale, and the exceptions catalogued by later scholars.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Write out the principle: Istihsan is preferred over qiyas when they conflict. The stronger proof (istihsan) is chosen.
Memorize this as a key maxim in rasm al-mufti.
2
Understand the rationale
Istihsan has greater legal force because it departs from strict analogy for a stronger evidence or necessity. It is considered more comprehensive.
Istihsan is a recognized source in Hanafi usul.
3
Acknowledge the exceptions
The rule is not absolute. There are a few issues where qiyas is followed instead of istihsan. The exact number has been inventoried by Hanafi scholars.
e.g., al-Natifi, al-Nasafi, and Mufti Mansurpuri provide differing counts.
4
Apply the rule in practice
When encountering conflicting proofs, first check if the case belongs to the exceptional issues (where qiyas rules). If not, adopt the istihsan-based ruling.
Knowledge of the exceptions is vital for correct fatwa.

Principle 8: Beneficial for Waqf Preferred
States a tarjih principle for waqf disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of 'anfa' lil-waqf' to select the preferred statement among conflicting corrected statements in waqf cases.

Core Idea
Among conflicting corrected statements, prefer the one most beneficial for the waqf.

This lesson introduces the eighth principle from Rasm al-Mufti, which provides a specific tarjih mechanism for waqf-related disputes. When two corrected statements conflict, the mufti must prioritize the one that brings greater benefit to the endowment, its founder, and its beneficiaries. Understanding this principle ensures that fatwa-giving in waqf matters aligns with the maqasid of perpetuity and social welfare.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting corrected statements
Determine the two positions that have been authenticated (musahhah) by Hanafi jurists regarding the same waqf issue.
These are not any statements, but those that have been formally validated within the school.
2
Assess benefit for the waqf
Evaluate which statement is more beneficial (anfa') for the waqf as a whole: the endower (waqif), the beneficiaries (mawquf 'alayhim), and the continuity of the property.
Benefit here means preserving the waqf's purpose and assets.
3
Prefer the beneficial statement
Choose the statement that is more beneficial as the rajih (preferred) and mufta bihi (operative for fatwa).
This overrides other general principles of tarjih in waqf matters.
4
Confirm the scope of application
Remember that this principle applies only to waqf disputes, not to all areas of fiqh.
Other areas may use different tarjih principles.
