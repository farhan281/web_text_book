Hila for Tahlil Using a Slave
Hila for secret tahlil using a slave intermediary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi hila for secret tahlil using a slave and identify its conditions and the resolution of the kuf' issue.

Core Idea
A woman can secretly perform tahlil by gifting a slave via a trustee, leveraging the principle that ownership dissolves marriage.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents a hila for a woman who wishes to keep her tahlil marriage confidential. Instead of a known muhallil, she uses a slave purchased by a trusted friend. After consummation, the slave is gifted to her, automatically dissolving the marriage. The passage also addresses a potential objection regarding the slave's suitability (kafa'ah) and its resolution. This lesson dissects the mechanics and legal reasoning behind this hila.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objective
Determine why the woman needs a hila: to conceal her tahlil from others. The hila must achieve the legal effect (marriage and dissolution) without public knowledge.
Secrecy is the key driver; the hila avoids social stigma or family opposition.
2
Break Down the Steps of the Hila
List sequentially: (1) Woman gives money to a trustworthy person. (2) That person buys a pubescent male slave capable of intercourse. (3) The trustee marries the slave to the woman. (4) The slave consummates the marriage. (5) The trustee gifts the slave to the woman. (6) She takes possession, dissolving the marriage (owner cannot be married to her slave). (7) She sends the slave to another town to be sold.
Each step is a condition for validity.
3
Identify the Key Legal Principle
The dissolution occurs through the woman's ownership of the slave. In Hanafi law, if a woman owns her husband (through purchase, gift, etc.), the marriage is automatically annulled. This is the underlying mechanism that makes the hila work.
This principle is unique to Hanafi fiqh and is the pivot of the hila.
4
Analyze the Kuf' (Suitability) Objection
The passage notes that the slave may not be a suitable match (kuf') for the woman. It offers two solutions: either the guardian consents to the marriage, or the woman has no guardian (thus the requirement is waived). This ensures the marriage is not invalid due to lack of kafa'ah.
Kafa'ah is a condition in marriage, but it can be waived by the guardian's permission or if the woman is independent (no guardian).
5
Summarize Conditions for the Hila's Validity
Conditions: trustworthy agent, slave of legal age and capable of intercourse, marriage contract, consummation, gifting, woman's possession, and sale afterwards. Also, the kuf' issue must be resolved as above.
If any step fails, the hila does not produce the intended effect.

Hila for Oath to Divorce Today
Hila to avoid divorce when sworn to divorce today

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid legal stratagem (hila) to avoid divorce when a husband has sworn to divorce his wife today, applying Hanafi conditions for ta'liq and khul'.

Core Idea
Suspending divorce with 'in sha Allah' or offering khul' not accepted prevents divorce.

A man swears he will divorce his wife today, but seeks to avoid the consequence. Hanafi law provides two permissible ways to fulfill the letter of the oath while avoiding the divorce: suspending the pronouncement upon an uncertain condition (Allah's will) or making a khul' offer that the wife rejects. This lesson dissects each method and its conditions so you can apply them correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath scenario
The husband swore an oath that he would divorce his wife today. This creates a binding obligation to divorce, but he wishes to avoid it without breaking the oath.
The oath is taken seriously; the hila provides a lawful exit.
2
Method 1: Suspending divorce with 'in sha Allah'
If he says 'You are divorced, if Allah wills', the divorce is suspended upon the occurrence of Allah's will, which is uncertain. According to Hanafi fiqh, this prevents the divorce from taking effect immediately.
The condition 'in sha Allah' renders the talaq mu'allaq (suspended).
3
Method 2: Offering khul' without acceptance
He offers to divorce her in exchange for a sum of money (e.g., a thousand dirhams). If she does not accept, no divorce occurs. This is based on the principle that khul' is a contract requiring mutual consent.
The wife's non-acceptance means the offer lapses.
4
Analyse conditions for validity
For Method 1, the pronouncement must be articulated clearly; the condition must be genuine and not a mere pretense. For Method 2, the offer must be explicit and the wife's refusal genuine. Both methods require sincere intent to avoid divorce, not deceive.
These are shar'i hilal (permitted stratagems) within Hanafi law.
5
Apply the mufta bihi position
Both methods are operative (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school. The mufti should advise the husband to use either, ensuring conditions are met.
No dispute among the Hanafi imams on these basic hilal.

Alternatively, he can offer to divorce her in exchange for a sum of money (khul') and if she does not accept, no divorce takes place.

Core Idea
A husband does not break his oath if a third party initiates khul', since he did not divorce.

A man swears he will not divorce his wife, but circumstances change and separation becomes desirable. How can he proceed without breaking his oath? The Hanafi school provides a hila: a third party (ajnabi) may negotiate a khul' with the wife, and the husband accepts the compensation. Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the prohibited act
The husband swore 'I will not divorce her'. Divorce (talaq) is the act he vowed to avoid. Note that the oath is personal: he must not be the one who issues the talaq.
The oath is not broken by divorce initiated by another party.
2
Recognise the hila mechanism
A stranger (ajnabi) performs khul' with the wife. Khul' is a divorce in exchange for compensation paid by the wife (or a third party) to the husband. Here, the stranger pays the husband and the husband accepts.
The husband receives compensation but does not pronounce talaq himself.
3
Determine the legal effect on the oath
The text explicitly states: 'lam yahnath' – he does not break his oath. The divorce took place via khul', but it is attributed to the third party's action, not the husband's.
Key: the husband's consent does not make him the initiator of the divorce for oath purposes.
4
Extract the conditions for validity
From the ruling, infer the conditions: (1) the husband swore a valid oath not to divorce; (2) a third party (non-spouse) arranges khul'; (3) the husband accepts the compensation and agrees; (4) the divorce occurs via that khul'.
If the husband himself suggests or pressures the third party, the hila may fail.

Core Idea
A conditional divorce statement can trick a denying husband into unintentionally confirming the divorce.

When a husband denies pronouncing a divorce, the wife may struggle to prove it. Hanafi jurists have sanctioned a stratagem that uses the husband's own words against him. This lesson unpacks that stratagem step by step, analyzing its mechanism and the conditions for its validity.

The first answer establishes his ignorance, the second creates a conditional divorce.
3
Analyze the legal mechanism
The husband's first statement is based on ignorance, so it is not a lie. The second is a conditional divorce (ta'liq) that is triggered when the wife is found inside. The witnesses record both statements and later confirm the connection.
This works because the husband's second statement is a general conditional that applies to any woman of his in the house.
4
Evaluate the conditions for validity
The husband must be genuinely unaware of the wife's presence. Witnesses must be present from the start. The husband must answer freely without coercion. The questions must be asked in the correct order.
If the husband suspects or knows, the stratagem fails.
5
Derive the ruling
The divorce is proven and takes effect as a bā'in divorce (irrevocable) because the husband used the word 'bā'in' in his statement. The wife is separated, and the marriage cannot be resumed without a new contract.

The hila transforms the factual subject of the oath, preventing the condition from being fulfilled.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a clever legal stratagem (hila) used to avoid a conditional divorce. The wife's oath is tied to cooking a specific pot with mixed halal and haram ingredients. The hila involves adding wine to change the pot's entire contents, thereby altering the subject of the oath. Understanding such hilas is crucial for a mufti to provide effective and permissible solutions.

Identify the conditional clause

The consequence is divorce (فهي طالق). The wife wants to avoid this.

The text says: 'the stratagem is that she puts wine in the pot and then cooks the eggs in it.'

The hila changes the pot's legal identity, not the act of cooking.

Extract the underlying principle

Here, using wine is controversial; the hila is accepted because avoiding divorce is prioritized.

The Hila for Conflicting Oaths on Khul'
Hila for conflicting oaths on khul'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Abu Hanifa's hila to resolve conflicting oaths about khul', identifying the conditions for its validity and the steps involved.

Core Idea
A hila can reconcile conflicting oaths by legally redefining the act, preventing oath-breaking.

In this passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, Ibn Nujaym presents a practical legal stratagem devised by Abu Hanifa for a case where a husband and wife have sworn conflicting oaths regarding khul'. The husband swore his wife would be thrice divorced if she asked for khul' and he did not grant it; the wife swore to ask for khul' before night. Abu Hanifa's solution allows both to fulfill their oaths through a carefully structured sequence of actions. Understanding this hila requires analyzing the legal definitions of khul' and oath fulfillment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting oaths
Read the scenario carefully. The husband's oath triggers divorce if the wife asks for khul' and he does not divorce her. The wife's oath obliges her to ask for khul' before nightfall. Both oaths must be fulfilled without violation.
Note that the husband's oath is conditional ('if you ask and I do not divorce') and the wife's is unconditional ('I will ask').
2
Understand the legal nature of khul'
Khul' is a divorce initiated by the wife in return for compensation. In Hanafi law, it requires an offer from the husband and acceptance from the wife. If the wife does not accept, no divorce occurs.
This definition is key to the hila: the wife's refusal prevents the khul' from taking effect.
3
Apply Abu Hanifa's hila step by step
The wife asks for khul'. The husband responds by offering khul' for a specified amount (e.g., a thousand). He instructs the wife to say 'I do not accept'. She does so. Since she did not accept, no khul' occurs. Thus, she asked (fulfilling her oath) and he did not divorce her (fulfilling his oath).
The key is that the husband's oath required that he 'not divorce' her; offering khul' is not divorce unless accepted.
4
Verify fulfillment of oaths
After the wife refuses, both oaths are satisfied: the wife asked, and the husband did not divorce. Abu Hanifa declares that both have 'fulfilled their oaths'.
This demonstrates the principle that a hila must achieve its intended effect without violating any legal prohibition.
5
Consider the alternative hila
Abu Hanifa also provides another stratagem: the wife can sell all her slaves to a trusted person before the day ends, then repossess them later. This avoids the oath deadline by postponing her ability to ask (since she no longer owns property to offer as consideration).
This alternative applies when the wife's oath requires her to ask 'before night' – selling slaves removes means to offer compensation, thus she cannot ask until after the deadline.

Legal Stratagems in Gift: Three Hila Cases
Three hila examples for conditional gifts and oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a hila for conditional gifts using sale and inspection option, and explain the legal mechanism for each of the three scenarios.

Core Idea
A hila uses a genuine sale with inspection option to achieve conditional outcomes without violating oath or gift conditions.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents three practical stratagems (hiyal) where direct conditional gifts or oaths would be invalid or problematic. Instead, the Hanafi method uses a sale combined with khiyar al-ru'yah (option of inspection) to achieve the desired result lawfully. We will examine each scenario, understand why the direct approach fails, and see how the sale-and-return mechanism provides a valid alternative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problematic objective
For each scenario, determine the underlying goal that cannot be achieved directly: (1) wife wants to gift dowry but reclaim if she survives childbirth; (2) creditor wants to discharge debt only upon his death; (3) husband swears wife must gift dowry today else divorce—wife wants to avoid gifting while not causing divorce.
Direct conditional gifts are invalid in Hanafi fiqh because they create uncertainty (gharar).
2
Understand the direct prohibition
Recognize why a direct conditional gift or conditional remission of debt is not permitted: the condition makes the contract suspended or null; also an oath requiring a gift could be seen as coercion.
The hila circumvents these prohibitions by using a separate valid contract (sale).
3
Analyze the hila structure
The common pattern: the would-be donor sells a wrapped item of equivalent value to the recipient. The recipient has the option to inspect and return the item (khiyar al-ru'yah). If the desired condition occurs, the recipient returns the item, effectively reclaiming the value. If the condition does not occur, the sale stands and the value is kept.
This is a genuine sale with a valid option; it is not a sham transaction.
4
Apply to each scenario

(2) creditor sells debtor a wrapped item worth the debt; if creditor dies, the debtor inspects and returns it (or keeps it, depending on the condition). (3) wife buys a wrapped cloth from husband worth the dowry; after the day, she returns it, so the dowry remains hers and the oath is not broken.
The timing of the inspection and return matters to match the condition.
5
Identify the underlying maxim
The hila operate on the principle that legal effects are based on intentions (al-umur bi-maqasidiha). The outward contract is a sale, but the intention is to achieve a conditional gift or discharge. Hanafi fiqh permits this as long as the contract is sound externally.
This maxim is a key tool for muftis in constructing lawful stratagems.

Hiyal are legal descriptions of outcomes, not commands to lie.

This passage presents a hila for selling a house on condition that the seller can deliver or return the price, and addresses a common criticism that such teaching amounts to teaching falsehood. As a mufti, understanding the Hanafi response is essential for instructing others in permissible legal stratagems without moral compromise.

Guided Path

Identify the desired outcome
The seller wants to sell his house but only if he can deliver it; otherwise the price is returned. The problem is that normally the buyer has the right to detain the seller for delivery, which the seller wants to avoid.
2
State the hila
The hila is that the buyer acknowledges that the house was sold while in the hands of a wrongdoer (ghasib), not the seller. This acknowledgment limits the seller's liability.
This is an iqrar talji'a (shielding acknowledgment).
3

Critics argue that Khassaf and Abu Hanifa are teaching the buyer to falsely acknowledge something (that the house was in a wrongdoer's hand) to absolve the seller. They claim this is teaching lying.
4

The response is that the statement is not an order to lie; it is a hypothetical: 'if he does thus, the ruling is thus.' The hila describes a legal outcome, not a command to actually make a false statement.

5

This defense applies to all hiyal that involve hypothetical acknowledgments. The mufti must distinguish between teaching legal options and commanding lies.

Hiyal for Avoiding Liability and Recourse in Sales
Hiyal preventing litigation, recourse, and intermediaries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct hiyal that prevent the buyer's recourse for defects or reclaim, and explain the use of an intermediary to bypass Shafi'i rules.

Core Idea
Hiyal can restructure transactions to shift liability and avoid legal consequences within Hanafi rules.

This passage presents several hiyal where the seller wants to avoid being sued for defects (by having the buyer waive litigation via sadaqa) or avoid recourse if the item is reclaimed (by the buyer acknowledging a prior sale to the seller). It also includes selling with disclaimer via an intermediary to avoid Shafi'i inheritance rules. Understanding these mechanisms allows a mufti to advise clients on lawful risk management.

Guided Path
1
Identify the seller's goal
The seller wants to prevent the buyer from litigating over a defect, or prevent the buyer from seeking recourse if the item is claimed.
Each scenario has its own goal.
2
State the hila for preventing litigation
The seller orders the buyer to say: 'If I litigate with you over a defect, it is sadaqa.' This creates a conditional sadaqa that deters litigation.
The statement is a threat of sadaqa, not an actual donation.
3
State the hila for preventing recourse
The buyer acknowledges that he sold the item to the seller, essentially reversing the transaction in acknowledgment, so the buyer cannot later claim.
This is an iqrar talji'a that makes the buyer admit the item was already sold back.
4
Understand the intermediary hila
To avoid Shafi'i rules on inheritance of a sold item, the seller sells to a third person who then sells to the original buyer. This breaks the chain of liability.
The Shafi'i rule likely involves the item returning to the seller via inheritance.
5
Note the common principle
All these hiyal use contractual mechanisms (sale, acknowledgment, conditional oath) to achieve desired legal outcomes without violating fiqh rules.
They rely on the Hanafi recognition of such stratagems.

Miscellaneous Hiyal: Manumission, Tadbir, Loan, and Litigation
Hiyal for manumission, tadbir, loan with profit, and partial payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct hiyal for freeing a slave upon purchase, creating tadbir, avoiding riba in loans, and preventing litigation over defects.

Core Idea
Hiyal use conditional declarations and multiple contracts to achieve specific legal outcomes within Hanafi boundaries.

This passage covers several distinct hiyal: (1) selling a slave-girl with a condition that she is free upon purchase; (2) adding a condition that she becomes mudabbara after the buyer's death; (3) buying a gold vessel with partial payment and loan to avoid tafarrud; (4) avoiding riba by first making a small purchase then borrowing; (5) the already-covered sadaqa hila. Each demonstrates how conditional statements and contractual sequences can structure transactions lawfully.

Guided Path
1
Identify the goal of the slave-girl hila
The buyer wants to free the slave-girl immediately upon purchase, and possibly also have her serve him until death.
The goal is to combine purchase with emancipation.
2
State the hila for immediate freedom
The buyer says before purchase: 'If I buy her, she is free.' This is a conditional ta'liq. Upon purchase, the condition is met and she is freed immediately.
The buyer does not need a separate deed of manumission.
3
State the hila for tadbir (service until death)
If the buyer wants her to serve him, he adds: 'after my death she will be a mudabbara.' This defers the freedom until his death.
This uses two conditions: immediate purchase + freedom, and additional tadbir.
4
Explain the hila for buying a gold vessel with insufficient funds
The buyer pays half the price immediately, then borrows the remaining half from the seller and pays it, so the sale is not invalidated by separation before full payment.
This avoids the problem of tafarrud (separation before payment).
5
Explain the hila to avoid riba in the loan
If the seller only lends with profit, the buyer makes a small purchase from the seller for the amount of the desired profit, then borrows. This makes the profit appear as a sale rather than riba.
This is a classic hila to avoid interest while achieving the same financial outcome.

Hiyal to Avoid Istibra': Stratagems and Scholarly Disagreement
Legal stratagems to circumvent the wajib of istibra'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Hanafi hiyal to avoid istibra' and evaluate the ikhtilaf on whether such stratagems are disliked.

Core Idea
Hiyal to drop istibra' are structurally valid but their moral permissibility is disputed among Hanafis.

Istibra' is a wajib waiting period to confirm a slave-girl is not pregnant before sale. Hanafi jurists devised several stratagems to avoid this requirement under specific circumstances. This passage from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Nadha'ir outlines these hiyal and highlights an internal Hanafi dispute on whether using them is disliked (makruh). Understanding both the mechanics and the ikhtilaf is essential for a mufti who encounters such issues.

Guided Path
1
Understand the wajib of istibra'
Recall that istibra' is a wajib waiting period (one menstrual cycle) before selling a slave-girl, to ensure she is not pregnant. It is a requirement for the validity of the sale? Actually it is wajib but not a condition of sale; if omitted, the sale is still valid but the seller commits a sin. This background is necessary to appreciate why hiyal are sought.
Istibra' is not a pillar or condition of the contract, but an obligation on the seller.
2
Analyze the first hila: seller marries before sale
The text describes: the seller marries the slave-girl to a man who has no free wife (so the marriage is valid), then sells her and takes delivery, then divorces her before consummation. Because divorce before consummation does not impose an 'idda, no istibra' is required after sale. However, if the seller divorces before delivery, istibra' becomes necessary (according to the soundest view).
The key is that divorce occurs before consummation, so no 'idda period arises.
3

Alternatively, the buyer can marry the slave-girl before taking delivery, then take delivery and divorce her before consummation. If the buyer fears he may not divorce, he can give her the power of divorce (tamlik) at any time (not limited to the session). Another variant: the buyer marries her before the seller (i.e., before the sale contract), then buys her and takes delivery.
Tamlik gives the woman the right to divorce herself whenever she wishes.
4

The passage concludes: 'And they differed on the dislike of the stratagem to drop istibra'.' This indicates an internal Hanafi dispute. Some scholars consider such hiyal makruh because they circumvent a wajib; others permit them as valid legal stratagems. The mufti must be aware of both views and apply the tarjih according to the context.
The text does not specify which view is stronger; it remains a point of ijtihad.

Invalid Discharge Through Judicial Interdiction – The First Hila and the Hanafi Dispute
Legal stratagem for invalid discharge using judicial interdiction and the ensuing intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the steps of the hila for invalidating a creditor's discharge, postponement, or compromise, and evaluate Abu Hanifa's objection to judicial interdiction in such cases.

Core Idea
Using judicial interdiction, a creditor's own discharge can be rendered void – but Abu Hanifa rejects interdiction itself as impermissible.

This lesson unpacks the first debt hila from Al-Ashbah, where a clever mechanism uses a trustee and a judge's order to prevent the creditor from effectively discharging or postponing a debt. After explaining the stratagem in detail, we examine Abu Hanifa's direct critique, highlighting a key intra-Hanafi conflict over the permissibility of hajr (judicial interdiction). Understanding this dispute is essential for a mufti when advising on such stratagems.

Guided Path
1
Identify the goal of the hila
The creditor wants to make his own discharge, postponement, or compromise void. He achieves this by transferring control to a trustee.
2
Trace the steps of the stratagem
Step 1: Creditor acknowledges the debt to a trusted man. Step 2: The trusted man claims the debt was his in the creditor's name. Step 3: The trusted man asks the judge to interdict the creditor from taking possession. Step 4: After the judge issues the interdiction, any subsequent action by the creditor (discharge, etc.) is void.
The key is the judge's order; without it, the hila fails.
3
Locate the key legal element
The judge's interdiction (hajr) is crucial. It effectively removes the creditor's authority to act, making his later actions void. The text warns that this step is often overlooked.
The hila requires court involvement.
4
Note Abu Hanifa's objection
Abu Hanifa (ra) states that the creditor's actions after acknowledgment and interdiction remain valid because he does not consider hajr permissible in this context. Thus, the hila is ineffective according to him.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if a mufti follows Khassaf (or the general view presented), the hila works; if he follows Abu Hanifa, the hila does not achieve its goal.
The mufti must be aware of this disagreement when issuing a fatwa.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of legal stratagems (hiyal) related to debts, collected under the sixteenth heading of a larger work on such stratagems. The author begins by addressing a scenario where a debtor wishes to make an invalid discharge, postponement, or compromise. The stratagem involves the creditor temporarily transferring ownership of the debt to a trusted third party through a formal acknowledgment, then having that third party seek a court order to interdict the original creditor from collecting or disposing of the debt. The author notes that this trick requires a judge's interdiction because the original creditor still retains the right to collect the debt; without it, the stratagem fails. The text then quotes al-Khassaf reporting that Abu Hanifa disagreed and did not consider such interdiction valid, allowing the creditor's subsequent actions to stand. Additional stratagems include the creditor acknowledging receipt of the debt on an earlier date, then the debtor acknowledging a new postponed debt the next day. Finally, the passage covers postponing a debt after the debtor's death, which is normally impossible. The stratagem is for the heir to acknowledge that he guaranteed the debt during the deceased's life on a postponed basis, and for the claimant to confirm that and also acknowledge that the deceased left no estate. This approach relies on the apparent position that while death makes a debt due immediately against the deceased's estate, it does not accelerate the debt against a guarantor.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Convert invalid tenant repair obligation into rent increase with agency.

Hire contracts become invalid if the tenant is burdened with the lessor's responsibilities like repairs, land tax, or servant food. This lesson shows how to lawfully incorporate these costs by increasing the rent and authorizing the tenant as an agent to spend on those items. The stratagem preserves the contract's validity while achieving the intended financial arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the invalid condition

These conditions violate the principle that the lessor bears ownership-related expenses.
2
Estimate the cost and add to rent
Calculate the expected repair cost and increase the rent by that amount. This changes the forbidden condition into a permissible rent adjustment.
The added amount must be approximately equal to the future expense.
3
Make the tenant an agent
The lessor then orders the tenant to spend the added amount on the repairs (or tax/food). The tenant becomes an agent (wakīl) by mutual agreement, not an obligor of the condition.
Agency is key: the tenant spends on behalf of the lessor, not as a contractual duty.

If the tenant claims to have spent the money, his word is not accepted without proof. A robust stratagem is for the tenant to advance the amount to the lessor, who then returns it with the order to spend, making the tenant's claim credible without further evidence.
This avoids disputes and protects both parties.

Commentary
This passage, the seventeenth chapter on stratagems in hire contracts, addresses common invalid stipulations that would otherwise void the contract. The first stratagem handles the invalid condition that the tenant bear the cost of repairs. The solution is to incorporate the estimated repair cost into the rent, then for the lessor to order the tenant to spend that amount on repairs, making the tenant an agent. If the tenant claims he spent the money, his word is not accepted without proof—even if the lessor testifies that his word should be taken, it is not accepted. Another stratagem is for the tenant to advance the repair amount to the lessor, who then gives it back to the tenant with an order to spend; this makes the tenant's claim more credible. Next, if a lessor grants permission for the tenant to build on a vacant lot, the building costs offset the rent, and the building belongs to the lessor. There is disagreement if the lessor only ordered building without specifying the origin of funds. Then, a stratagem for leasing land that already has crops: sell the crop to the tenant first, then lease the land. Some restrict this to a nominal sale to avoid the prohibition of selling crops before they are ready. Stipulating land tax (kharaj) on the tenant is treated like repairs: add the tax amount to the rent and authorize the tenant to pay it. Similarly for fodder or servant's food. The next set of stratagems addresses the termination of a hire contract upon the death of either party. If the tenant wants the lease to continue after the lessor's death, the lessor can acknowledge a long-term lease (e.g., ten years) or that the lease was for a Muslim man (implying perpetuity?), which prevents dissolution. Analogous stratagems are given for oil/bitumen rights and palm trees: the lessor acknowledges a long-term right to use or enters a sharecropping arrangement for dates. All these methods achieve lawful ends without violating the rules of hire.

Stratagem for Leasing Land Already Planted with Crops
Stratagem for renting land with existing crops

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid stratagem for leasing land that already has standing crops, avoiding the prohibition of selling future crops.

Core Idea
Sell the crop first, then lease the land – but only as a genuine sale, not a ruse.

Leasing land that has standing crops is problematic because the crop belongs to the owner and cannot be included in the lease without being sold separately. The stratagem is straightforward: first sell the crop to the tenant, then lease the land. However, the scholars caution that the sale must be genuine – if it is merely a nominal transaction (talji'ah), the crop effectively remains with the owner, and the stratagem fails. A sign of genuine desire is that the sale price is at fair market value or only slightly discounted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibition
Directly leasing land with existing crops is problematic because the crop is not part of the lease (it belongs to owner). The contract would be void if the tenant is allowed to harvest the owner's crop without a proper sale.
The issue is ownership of the crop, not the land itself.
2
Sell the crop first
The owner sells the standing crop to the tenant at an agreed price. This transfer of ownership makes the crop the tenant's property.
The sale must be valid: price must be certain, delivery possible (the crop is still in ground but can be harvested later).
3
Then lease the land
After the sale, the owner leases the land to the same tenant. Now the tenant owns both the crop and the right to use the land, so he can harvest his own crop.
This avoids any confusion about who owns the crop during the lease.
4
Verify genuine intent
Some Hanafi jurists restrict the stratagem to cases where the sale is genuine (raghbah), not a cover-up (talji'ah). A genuine sale is indicated by a price at or near market value.
If the sale is a sham, the crop remains the owner's, and the lease may be invalid.

Stratagems for Lease Continuation After Death and Resource Rights
Stratagems to prevent lease termination upon death and to secure resource rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct stratagems to prevent lease termination upon death and to allocate oil/bitumen rights and palm dates to the tenant.

Core Idea
Acknowledge long-term rights or use sharecropping to circumvent lease termination and ownership issues.

Hire contracts normally dissolve upon the death of either party. To avoid this, the lessor can make a binding acknowledgment of a long-term lease (e.g., ten years) or declare that the lease was for a Muslim man (implying a term not tied to the lessor's life). Similarly, to allow the tenant to benefit from oil/bitumen or palm dates, the lessor can acknowledge a long-term right of use or enter a sharecropping contract (mu'amalah) for the dates.

Guided Path
1
Understand the default rule
A hire contract (ijarah) is dissolved by the death of either the lessor or lessee. This can cause problems if the tenant wants to continue using the property.
This default rule protects heirs but can be inconvenient.
2
Use acknowledgment to extend the term
The lessor can acknowledge (iqrār) that the lease is for a fixed long term (e.g., ten years) or that he rented to a Muslim man (which implies a binding term regardless of his death). Such an acknowledgment prevents dissolution upon death.
The acknowledgment must be explicit and admissible in court.
3
Apply the same logic to land with oil/bitumen
If the land has a mineral resource (oil, bitumen) and the tenant wants to exploit it, the lessor can acknowledge that the land is let for ten years with full use rights, including the resource.
The resource is treated as part of the land's usufruct.
4
For palm dates: use sharecropping (mu'amalah)
If the lessor wants the dates to belong to the tenant, he should not include them in the lease but instead give the palm trees to the tenant as a sharecropping contract (mu'amalah), where the owner takes a share of the dates.
This avoids the problem of leasing trees with fruit.

Recognize the hila structure
The lessor makes an independent iqrar (acknowledgment) that the land is for the tenant for a fixed term (e.g., ten years), or that he leased it to a Muslim man. This iqrar is a separate obligation that does not dissolve upon death.
The iqrar is not merely a restatement of the lease but a new, independent declaration.
3
Understand the conditions
The iqrar must be for a fixed term, specify the tenant's rights, and not be contingent on the original lease. It should be a clear, binding declaration that survives the death of the declarant.
The iqrar creates a debt-like obligation on the estate.
4
Apply the fatwa hierarchy
This hila is found in later Hanafi works (fatawa mutakhkhirin). It is considered valid (mufta bihi) because it avoids the dissolution rule without contradicting a clear text. The source authority is the nawadir or fatawa literature, not the zahir al-riwayah.
The hila is a legitimate application of iqrar as a binding declaration.

Hiyal for Preventing a False Claim: Defensive Stratagems in Hanafi Jurisprudence
Hila to avoid oath when faced with a false lawsuit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four Hanafi hiyal for preventing a false claim, apply their conditions, and evaluate the disagreement regarding the second stratagem.

Core Idea
Hiyal are permissible defenses against false claims, not tools to evade legitimate rights.

When a defendant faces a false lawsuit (da'wa batila), the Hanafi school permits certain stratagems (hiyal) to avoid having to take an oath, as oaths in such cases can be oppressive. This lesson examines four such stratagems as outlined in al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, their conditions, and a point of internal disagreement. Understanding these hiyal requires careful parsing of the text and awareness of the ethical boundaries of legal procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
Recognize that the passage addresses a scenario where someone makes a false claim (da'wa batila) against a defendant. The defendant wants to avoid a potentially false oath.
The oath (yamin) is the core concern; the hiyal aim to eliminate the need for it.
2
Enumerate the four hiyal
List each stratagem from the text: (1) acknowledging the claimed item for a minor son; (2) acknowledging for a stranger; (3) secretly lending to someone who then offers it for sale, prompting the claimant to bargain; (4) selling to a trusted person who then gifts it to the claimant and proves ownership.
The second hila has disagreement noted in the text.
3
Identify conditions and invalidators
For each hila, determine what must be true for it to work and what would invalidate it. For example, if the claimant bargains for the lent item, the claim becomes invalid.
The text provides specific invalidators like bargaining or saying 'I did not know' after dyeing.
4
Analyze the internal disagreement
Note that the text says 'there is disagreement in the second' (referring to acknowledging for a stranger). Identify the divergent views and the preferred position (mufta bihi) if possible.
The source does not elaborate the disagreement; further research may be needed, but the fatwa hierarchy block will address it.
5
Apply the hiyal to a modern case
Consider a contemporary scenario, such as a false property claim, and determine which hila might apply and its conditions.
Ensure the hila is used defensively, not to defraud.

This section provides hiyal for a defendant facing a false claim (da'wa batila) to avoid having to take an oath (yamin). The stratagems include: (1) Acknowledge the claimed item for his minor son or a stranger (though the latter has disagreement). Hila for Agent to Purchase Specified Item for Himself
Hila for agent to purchase specified item for himself

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four valid stratagems (hiyal) for a Hanafi agent to purchase a specified item for himself and apply them to analogous agency scenarios.

Core Idea
An agent cannot buy the principal's specified item for himself without using a valid hila.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazahir addresses a common conflict of interest in agency: the agent wants to buy the very item he was sent to purchase for his principal. The solution lies in using one of four specific stratagems that restructure the transaction to avoid breach of trust. Understanding these hiyal is essential for a mufti advising on agency contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
Recognize the conflict: the agent is ordered to buy a specific item but desires it for himself. Direct purchase would be a breach of trust (khayanah) because the agent's duty is to act in the principal's interest.
The prohibition is based on the general rule that an agent cannot benefit from the agency without the principal's consent.
2
Parse the first two hilas
Understand that buying a different type (jins) or at a higher price (thaman) than authorized changes the subject matter or price, thereby creating a new contract distinct from the principal's order.
If the agent buys a different type, it is a different commodity; if at a higher price, the principal is not bound by the excess.
3
Parse the third hila
If the agent explicitly declares 'I am buying this for myself' in the presence of the principal, the principal is informed and can object; if silent, it is consent.
This is a form of tacit permission (idhn bil-sukut).
4
Parse the fourth hila
The agent appoints a sub-agent (wakil akhar) to purchase the item for himself, thereby removing himself from the direct conflict.
The sub-agent buys for the agent, not for the principal, so no breach.
5
Practical note and mufta bihi
All four stratagems are valid according to the muta'akhkhirun. The mufta bihi position accepts them as lawful hiyal to avoid khyanah.
The agent must not resort to subterfuge (hilah batilah); these are shariyyah stratagems.
