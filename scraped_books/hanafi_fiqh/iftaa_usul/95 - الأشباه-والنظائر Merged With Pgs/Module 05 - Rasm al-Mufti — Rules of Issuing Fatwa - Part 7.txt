Concurrent reading by witness and another invalidates testimony because the speaker is unclear.

The testimony is ruled invalid because it is unclear who is reading and who is the witness. The ambiguity vitiates the evidence.

Hanafi law requires that testimony be directly and unmistakably attributable to the witness. Any ambiguity in attribution makes the testimony unacceptable.
This is a procedural safeguard to maintain credibility.

The problem is only with simultaneous reading.

Reliance on Mufti's Handwriting and Testimony from Documents
Rulings on relying on mufti's handwriting and testifying from books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on reliance on a mufti's handwriting and identify the conditions for valid testimony from a written copy.

Core Idea
A mufti's handwriting is as reliable as his gesture; but testimony from a copy requires distinct recitation and pointing.

This lesson explores a set of practical rulings from Hanafi fatwa and judicial practice concerning written documents. The passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents several cases on when a judge may accept claims or testimony based on a written copy, establishing important distinctions between reliance on the mufti's handwriting and testimony from a document in court.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Analogy
The passage begins by analogizing handwriting to gesture: if a deaf-mute's gesture is reliable, then writing, being clearer, is even more reliable. This establishes the permissibility of relying on the mufti's handwriting.
This analogy (qiyas) is the foundation for the subsequent rulings.
2
Examine Claim from a Book
A plaintiff claiming from a book must point to the specific location in the text. A deputy (wakil) must have learned the content directly from the client's recitation, not just from reading the copy.
Pointing ensures the claim is specific and not based on general knowledge.
3
Analyze Testimony from a Copy
If two witnesses read from the same copy simultaneously, the testimony is invalid because it is unclear who is the reader and who is the witness. Testifying by writing alone (without oral recitation) is sufficient according to custom.
Distinct roles are required: the witness must actively testify, not just read along.
4
Evaluate the Witness's Use of a Document
If a witness needs to look at a document to describe boundaries, the testimony is accepted only if the witness uses the document as an aid (like reading Quran from mushaf), not if he merely memorizes from looking and then recites.
The distinction is between dependence (not acceptable) and assistance (acceptable).
5
Synthesize the Conditions
Overall, the passage teaches that written documents are a valid basis for claims and testimony, but specific conditions (pointing, distinct recitation, learning from the source) must be met to prevent ambiguity and ensure authenticity.
These rulings balance practicality with the need for certainty in judicial proceedings.

Testimony by Writing: Conditions for Valid Witnessing
Fatwa condition for accepting written testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three permissible ways for a witness to bear witness to a written will according to the correct Hanafi position, and apply this ruling in a fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
A written document alone is insufficient; testimony requires explicit reading or witnessed writing with verbal request.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair addresses a common pitfall in legal practice: relying solely on written documents as evidence. While earlier sources such as Shahadat al-Mujtaba seemed to permit witnessing based on handwriting alone, the later authoritative Hanafi position, as recorded in al-Khaniyya, restricts it to three specific scenarios. As a mufti, you must distinguish between these positions and apply the correct rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal transactions
The passage begins with hawala bi al-kitab (transfer by letter) and then focuses on wasiyya bi al-kitaba (will by writing). Your task is to understand the specific ruling for each. The author directs the reader elsewhere for hawala, so the detailed ruling here is about wills.
The term 'suftaja' is also introduced as a type of letter of credit.
2
Extract the initial permissive ruling from al-Mujtaba
From Shahadat al-Mujtaba: a man writes in his own hand an acknowledgment or will, then tells a person 'Bear witness to me' without reading it. The ruling is that it is permissible for that person to bear witness.
This seems to allow testimony based solely on handwriting.
3
Identify the restrictive ruling from al-Khaniyya
In al-Khaniyya, the scenario is: a man writes a will and tells witnesses 'Bear witness to what is in it' without reciting it. Our scholars (علماؤنا) say it is not permissible. The correct view is that it is not permissible except in three specific ways.
Note the shift from permissibility to restriction.
4
List the three permissible scenarios
The three scenarios are: (1) the document is read to the witnesses; (2) someone else writes it, reads it in front of the witnesses, and the testator says 'Bear witness for me'; (3) the testator writes it in front of the witness while the witness knows its contents and the testator verbally says 'Bear witness for me.'
The common thread is that the witness must directly hear or see the writing process accompanied by a verbal request.
5
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is that written testimony without direct oral or visual witnessing is unreliable. The Hanafi school requires a direct link between the testator's statement and the witness's knowledge, avoiding potential forgery or misinterpretation.
This is a fatwa condition (rasm al-mufti) that muftis must apply when accepting written evidence.

Core Idea
In Hanafi contracts, the object pointed to determines the ruling over the verbal name.

This lesson unpacks a foundational maxim of Hanafi mu'amalat: the indicated object (al-mushār ilayhi) governs over the verbal name (al-tasmiya). The principle applies across marriage, sale, lease, and even prayer. We will trace how Abu Hanifa's classification of genera (e.g., wine/vinegar as one genus) leads to specific outcomes, and navigate the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether same‑genus differences render a contract void or irregular.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conflict
Recognize when the verbal description (tasmiya) differs from the physical indication (mushār ilayhi).
This is the trigger for applying the qawida.
2
Determine the Genus
Classify both items into Hanafi genera (e.g., wine and vinegar are one genus; free and slave are one genus; ruby and glass are different genera).

Apply the Qawida
If different genus → contract is batil (void). If same genus → contract is valid but may be fasid (irregular) if the description is specific and the indicated item differs in type.

Check Special Cases
In marriage, if the name is wrong but the indication is correct (e.g., pointing to the correct woman), the marriage is valid. In iqtida, intention is based on indication of the specific imam.
These cases reinforce the primacy of indication.
5
Issue the Fatwa
State the ruling with the appropriate hukm (batil, fasid, sahih) and any options (khiyar) for the affected party.
For same‑genus disputes, the mufta bihi is fasid with an option for the buyer.

For example, if a man marries a woman promising a container of vinegar but points to wine, the wife is entitled to the dowry of the like (mahr al-mithl) because the indication overrides the name. In sales, if the named item and the pointed item are of different genera (e.g., ruby vs. glass), the sale is void because it amounts to selling a non-existent item. If they are of the same genus but different types (e.g., Herawi vs. Marwi cloth), there is disagreement on whether the sale is void (batil) or irregular (fasid). The passage also covers analogous situations in following the imam in prayer (iqtida) and marriage contracts, emphasizing that the soundness of the contract depends on the intended object indicated, not the verbal name, except when the mistake is in the name alone while the object is correctly pointed to.

The Hanafi Definition of Debt and Its Repayment by Muqassa
Defining dayn and explaining its repayment via muqassa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of dayn, explain why repayment is exclusively via muqassa, and identify when kafala and rahn are valid.

Core Idea
Debt in Hanafi law is a legal liability (mal hukmi) in the dhimmah, repaid only through set-off (muqassa), not by returning the same object.

The concept of debt (dayn) is central to Hanafi muamalat. This passage from al-Hawi al-Qudsi defines dayn as a 'legal property' (mal hukmi) that arises in the dhimmah through transactions like sale or consumption. Its repayment, according to Imam Abu Hanifa, occurs exclusively through set-off (muqassa), not by physical return of the same coins. Understanding this foundation is key to correctly applying rulings on suretyship (kafala) and pledge (rahn).

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of dayn
Identify that dayn is a legal property (mal hukmi) that arises in the dhimmah (legal liability) through causes like sale or consumption. It is not a physical 'ayn.
The term 'mal hukmi' distinguishes it from tangible property.
2
Analyze the mechanism of muqassa
Work through the example: A buys a garment for 10 dirhams. The seller has a claim against A. When A pays, the 10 dirhams become a debt in the seller's dhimmah, and the two claims set off. No physical coin is transferred; it is a legal cancellation.
Muqassa is automatic; it is not a separate contract.
3
Distinguish sound debts for kafala and rahn
A sound debt (dayn sahih) is one that is extinguished only by payment or discharge. Kafala and rahn are valid for such debts, but not for debts that can be extinguished without payment (e.g., badal kitaba via disability).
Badal kitaba is extinguished by disability ('ajz) without payment.
4
Identify properties 'guaranteed by themselves'
Properties like usurped goods, mahr, khul' compensation, etc., are treated as debts for kafala and rahn because they are guaranteed by themselves, not by another party.
Trust properties (amana) or property guaranteed by others (e.g., sold item before delivery) are not valid.

Obligations of the Insolvent: Categories and Rules
Rules for when obligations become binding on an insolvent person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify obligations (zakat, fitr, hajj, kaffarat, fidyah) into three categories and apply the Hanafi rule for each when the obligated person becomes insolvent.

Core Idea
Obligations that become due while wealthy remain despite insolvency; those conditional on wealth at obligation drop if insolvent then.

The Hanafi school provides detailed rules for when financial obligations remain binding on an insolvent person. This passage from Imam al-Suyuti's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir distills these rules into three categories: obligations that drop if the wealth perishes, obligations that persist, and expiations where the person's financial status at the time of expiation matters. Understanding these distinctions is essential for a mufti issuing fatwas on missed obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage divides obligations into three groups based on how insolvency affects them. First: zakat (wealth perishes after obligation). Second: sadaqat al-fitr and hajj (obligated while wealthy, then wealth perishes). Third: expiations and ransoms (choice or conditional). List each category and its distinguishing feature.
This grouping is a core memorization anchor for Hanafi muftis.
2
Analyze the zakat rule
For zakat: if the zakatable wealth perishes after zakat becomes obligatory (e.g., after the year ends), the zakat debt drops even if the person later acquires wealth. Exception: if the person consumed the wealth (ate it), the zakat remains. Contrast this with the second category.
The key is the perishing vs. consumption.
3
Analyze fitr and hajj rule
For sadaqat al-fitr and hajj: if these become obligatory while the person is wealthy (e.g., possesses nisab at the end of Ramadan for fitr, or has ability for hajj), then even if the wealth perishes later, the obligation does not drop. But if the person was insolvent at the time of obligation (e.g., no nisab at end of Ramadan), the obligation never arose, and later wealth does not make it due.
This is a strict condition: obligation is fixed at the moment it becomes due.
4
Analyze expiations with choice
For expiations where the person has a choice between fasting, feeding, or other options (e.g., kaffarat al-yamin, fidyah for shaving due to excuse), the ruling is the same for rich and poor. The person may choose fasting regardless of wealth. This contrasts with expiations where fasting is conditional on insolvency.
Examples: part of fidyah for hunting, shaving, etc.
5
Analyze expiations conditional on insolvency
For expiations where fasting is only allowed if the person is insolvent (e.g., kaffarat al-fitr in Ramadan, kaffarat al-zihar, kaffarat al-qatl, dam al-tamattu' wal-qiran), the determining factor is the person's wealth status at the time he chooses to expiate with fasting. If he is wealthy at that time, he must use the other options (freeing a slave, feeding, etc.). Also for fidyah of an elderly person unable to fast: if poor, no obligation; if later wealthy, no repayment.
Insolvency at the time of expiation, not at the time of the sin.

Priority Among Debts and Bequests
Priority rules for debts and bequests in estate settlement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi priority rules for settling debts and bequests and apply them to an estate distribution scenario.

Core Idea
Secured debts and obligatory bequests take precedence over unsecured debts and voluntary bequests.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir outlines the Hanafi hierarchy for settling competing financial claims after death. A mufti must understand which claims drop (rights of Allah), which are prioritized by attachment to assets, and how to handle multiple bequests. These rules guide equitable distribution of the estate.

Guided Path
1
Classify the type of claim
Determine whether the claim is a right of Allah (e.g., unpaid zakat, sadaqat al-fitr) or a right of people (debt, bequest). Rights of Allah drop upon death, so they are not paid from the estate unless specifically bequeathed.
If the testator made a bequest for a right of Allah, it is treated as a bequest.
2
Prioritize among debts of people
Among debts, those attached to a specific asset (e.g., mortgage, pawn) take precedence over unsecured debts. If the estate is consumed by secured debts, unsecured creditors get nothing.
Secured debts are paid first from the encumbered asset.
3
Apply priority among bequests
If multiple bequests are made, bequests for obligatory acts (e.g., unpaid hajj, zakat) are given precedence over voluntary ones. Among voluntary bequests, manumission and muhabah (selling below market price) are given priority. The order of mention in the will is irrelevant unless specified.
Manumission and muhabah are singled out for special priority in Hanafi law.
4
Handle ties in strength
If two bequests are equal in strength (both obligatory or both voluntary), the testator's own order of mention is followed, but if not specified, none is given precedence.
The passage says 'no consideration is given to earlier or later' unless specified.

Explaining when equivalent dower is obligatory and when it multiplies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the cases where mahr al-mithl is obligatory and determine whether the dower multiplies with multiple acts of intercourse, using the Hanafi rules.

Core Idea
Mahr al-mithl is the default dower when specified is invalid, but it does not multiply in valid marriage — only in void or dubious intercourse.

This lesson examines the rules of mahr al-mithl (equivalent dower) as laid out in Hanafi fiqh. Building on the hadith of Barwa' bint Washiq, scholars have delineated when this default dower becomes due and how it behaves across different types of marital and quasi-marital relations. Understanding these distinctions is critical for a mufti dealing with nikah cases involving invalid or missing specifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering event
Determine if the case is a valid marriage, void marriage, or dubious intercourse (wat' shubha). Each has different rules.
Valid marriage with proper mahr specification does not require mahr al-mithl.
2
Check for dower specification
If the marriage is valid but no mahr is named, or an invalid mahr is named (e.g., wine, pig, free person, Quran, service, another marriage), then mahr al-mithl becomes due.
Also cases where specification is dangerous or benefits are lost.
3
Apply the condition of consummation or death
In valid marriage, mahr al-mithl is only due if consummation or death of either spouse occurs. Otherwise, mut'ah (consolatory gift) is given without half.
In void marriage and dubious intercourse, consummation is required for mahr to be due.
4
Assess multiplicity of dower
In a valid marriage, mahr does not multiply with multiple acts of intercourse (Abu Hanifa's view). In void marriage and certain dubious cases, it can multiply.
The passage cites examples: son with father's slave-girl, husband with wife's slave-girl.
5
Handle special scenarios
Note the zina-then-marriage case (two mahr) and the conditional divorce hypothetical (multiple mahr). These are detailed in Fatawa Qadikhan.

The mudabbar and umm walad share slave status but diverge in liability, estate share, idda, lineage, and sale permissions.

The umm walad is not liable for damages arising from usurpation (ghasb), emancipation (i'taq), or void sale (bay' fasid). The mudabbar is liable in these cases.
The umm walad's immunity protects her from financial claims that could affect her eventual freedom.
4
Examine Emancipation and Valuation
The umm walad is emancipated from all (meaning from the entire estate). Her value as a slave is recorded in multiple narrations: one-third, half, two-thirds, or all. These variations reflect different transmission chains.
The mudabbar is also freed from the third, but the umm walad's freedom is not constrained to the third.
5
Observe Idda
The umm walad must observe an 'idda (waiting period) after being emancipated or upon the master's death. The mudabbara (female mudabbar) does not have to observe 'idda upon emancipation or master's death.
This is because the umm walad has a marital-type relationship with the master; the mudabbara does not.
6
Analyze Joint Ownership
If a man fathers a child on a jointly-owned umm walad (i.e., he owns a share and another owns the other share), he does not automatically own the partner's share by guarantee (i.e., he cannot take possession by paying compensation). In the case of a mudabbara, he does.
The wording: 'لا يملك نصيب صاحبه بالضمان' for umm walad, but for mudabbara he does.
7
Establish Lineage
The parentage of a child born to an umm walad is established by the master's silence (i.e., if he does not deny it, the child is attributed to him). For a mudabbara's child, silence is insufficient; the master must affirmatively acknowledge the child.
This reflects the stronger presumption of paternity for the umm walad's child due to the master's ongoing relationship.
8
Check Post-Death Rules
After the master's death, the umm walad cannot be sacrificed (i.e., sold or used) to pay the master's debts. The mudabbar can be sold to satisfy debts.
This protection of the umm walad prevents her from being separated from her child.
9
Understand Capacity
A master cannot declare an umm walad as mudabbar (i.e., tadeer is invalid on her). However, a master can father a child (istilad) on a mudabbara, making her an umm walad and thus changing her status.
Tadbir on an umm walad is redundant because she is already free upon death; istilad on a mudabbara elevates her.
10
Note Special Rules
A harbi (non-Muslim from enemy territory) can sell a mudabbar but cannot sell an umm walad. Also, if a man fathers a child on his son's slave-girl, it is valid even if the son is a minor. If a man declares his slave mudabbar, the declaration is not valid (implying specific conditions).
The umm walad cannot be sold by a harbi because she is considered part of the Muslim community.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir enumerates key practical differences between a void (fasid) and a valid (sahih) sale. These differences arise from the core principle that in a void sale, ownership of the sold item remains with the seller, so any disposition or benefit from the item still belongs to the seller. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for a mufti when advising on sales that are defective but not entirely void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its classification
First, recognize that each case involves a void sale (bay' fasid) in contrast to a valid sale (bay' sahih). In Hanafi fiqh, a void sale is one that is valid in essence but has a defect in the consideration or a condition.

The text states that if the seller emancipates the slave after the buyer's repeated wording, it is valid in a void sale but not in a valid one. Understand that in a void sale, the slave still belongs to the seller, so his emancipation is effective.
3
Parse the second scenario: buyer ordering emancipation
If the buyer orders the seller to emancipate the slave on his behalf, the emancipation still takes effect on the seller in a void sale, unlike a valid sale where it would take effect on the buyer.

Parse the third and fourth scenarios: grinding and slaughtering
Similarly, if the buyer orders the seller to grind wheat or slaughter a sheep, the resulting product (flour or meat) belongs to the seller in a void sale, but to the buyer in a valid sale.

If after rescinding the void sale, the buyer discharges the seller from the price, but then the item perishes, the seller still owes the price. In a valid sale, such a discharge would mean nothing is owed.

The text concludes that pre-emption (shuf'a) does not apply in a void sale, whereas it does in a valid sale of real property.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In fiqh, a mufti states his school is correct but may err; in creed, he states it is the only truth.

This short passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair gives a crucial protocol for Hanafi muftis. When asked about differences, the answer depends on whether the issue is a subsidiary fiqh ruling or a foundational creedal belief. Understanding this distinction prevents the mufti from falling into either false modesty in creed or unjustified dogmatism in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of issue: furu or aqidah

Fisq, Safah, and Ghafalah: Legal Effects on Eligibility and Testimony
Ruling on eligibility for testimony and guardianship based on moral and mental faults

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between fisq, safah, and ghafalah, and apply the Hanafi ruling on eligibility for testimony, guardianship, and oversight (nazar) based on these defects.

Core Idea
Fisq prohibits appointment but eligibility remains; safah and ghafalah independently affect guardianship and testimony.

This passage from the Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir critically refines the mufti's understanding of three moral-legal defects: fisq (sinfulness), safah (foolishness/extravagance), and ghafalah (heedlessness). While fisq does not nullify one's capacity for office, it makes appointment impermissible and requires removal. Safah and ghafalah have distinct definitions and consequences for guardianship over wards and endowments, as well as for the admissibility of testimony. A mufti must grasp these nuances to correctly evaluate a person's legal eligibility in both ibadat and muamalat contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for fisq
State that fisq does not prevent eligibility (ahliyyah) for positions like testimony, judgeship, imamate, and guardianship. Note that appointment to such positions is prohibited (la tahillu tawliyatuhu). If a person already in office becomes fasiq, he is not automatically removed; rather removal is either obligatory or recommended.
This rule has an exception: the safih father loses guardianship over his child's wealth.
2
Define safah and distinguish it from fisq
Safah is habitual extravagance and wasteful spending, whether in sinful activities (e.g., entertaining drunks) or seemingly good ones (e.g., excessive building of mosques). It does not necessarily entail fisq. The judge may impose hijr (restriction) on a safih to protect his wealth.
A safih may be fasiq if his waste is in evil; if in good, he remains non-fasiq but is still subject to hajr.
3
Apply the ruling on guardianship and oversight
A safih father has no guardianship (wilayah) over his child's property; analogously, a safih nazir (overseer) of a waqf is unfit even if named by the endower, because his own transactions are invalid. The judge may remove such a nazir.
If fisq appears in a nazir (e.g., drinking wine), he is also removed by the judge.
4
Understand ghafalah and its two types
Ghafalah (heedlessness) is a cause for hijr according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Two distinct meanings: (1) in hijr – inability to make profitable transactions; (2) in testimony – inability to remember what one saw or heard. The latter leads to rejection of testimony.
The maghfal in testimony is not necessarily the same as the maghfal in hijr.
5
Derive the ruling on testimony of a safih or maghfal
If a safih wastes in evil, he is fasiq and testimony rejected. If he wastes in good, testimony accepted. If he is maghfal (cannot recall), testimony rejected regardless of moral state.
This is deduced by the author; he states he did not find an explicit ruling in the sources.

A ruler's appointment is void if the appointee is unqualified — qualification is an implicit condition.

This passage from Ibn al-Kamal's work (via al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair) establishes a critical rule in Hanafi administrative law: the ruler's discretion is not absolute. When appointing a teacher (mudarris), the ruler's action is bound by maslahah, and if the appointee lacks qualification, the appointment is invalid. The reasoning is supported by analogy to judges and by the principle that the ruler's authority must align with Sharia.

2

Determine the underlying principle: the ruler's actions are bound by public interest (maslahah). There is no maslahah in appointing an unqualified person, so the appointment fails.
This principle is derived from the ruler's role as guardian of public welfare.
3
Trace the analogy (qiyas) to judiciary
The text draws an analogy to the case of a judge: if a ruler appoints a just judge who later becomes corrupt, the judge is removed because justice was an implicit condition. Similarly, qualification is an implicit condition for the teacher.
This qiyas strengthens the ruling by showing consistency across rulings.
4
Consider the waqf condition
The passage distinguishes between the general ruling and the condition set by the endower (waqf founder). If the endower stipulates a specific qualification, that condition must be followed even if the appointee is generally qualified.
This highlights the binding nature of waqf conditions.
5
Define the qualifications of a teacher
The text lists qualifications: knowledge of Quranic meanings, ability to extract rulings, proficiency in grammar, and ability to correct errors. These are the criteria for ahliyyah.
This provides a practical checklist for evaluating eligibility.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Intention determines the validity of takbirat al-ihram; astonishment's 'Allahu Akbar' does not count.

This brief passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a seemingly paradoxical riddle: there is a takbir that is permissible in itself but does not serve to enter prayer. The answer lies in the role of intention (niyyah). The key takeaway for the mufti: the takbirat al-ihram requires the specific intent to begin salah; otherwise, the utterance is legally inert.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is uttering 'Allahu Akbar' with the intention of astonishment. The ruling: it does not count as takbirat al-ihram.
2
Locate the underlying principle
The principle is 'al-umur bi maqasidiha' (actions are judged by intentions). In Hanafi usul, intention is a condition (shart) for the validity of certain acts, including the opening takbir.
3
Distinguish the two types of takbir
Takbir al-ta'ajjub (astonishment) vs. takbir al-ta'zim (glorification for prayer). Only the latter, when accompanied by niyyah, initiates prayer.
4
Apply the rasm al-mufti rule
As a mufti, you must clarify that the mere utterance is insufficient; the questioner must understand that intention is decisive.
This is a straightforward case of the maxim 'al-umur bi maqasidiha'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Reciting Quran while exiting prayer due to hadath invalidates the prayer because the exit is not a legitimate pause.

This lesson examines a specific fatwa guideline from the Hanafi tradition: if a person is overtaken by an invalidating event during prayer (such as passing wind) and, upon leaving the prayer space, recites Quran, the prayer becomes invalid. This rule clarifies the boundary between permissible and impermissible recitation during a prayer interruption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
The text presents the case of a praying person who is overtaken by a hadath (ritual impurity) mid-prayer. This forces the person to leave the prayer space to renew wudu.
This is a common occurrence; the ruling depends on the manner of leaving.
2
Identify the action that triggers invalidation
The invalidating action is reciting Quran while walking away from the prayer space (in dhahabihi). The ruling is that such recitation nullifies the prayer.
Recitation here is not part of the prayer, nor is it a permitted speech.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The exit for hadath is a necessary interruption, but recitation during that exit is not part of the prayer and shows intentional engagement outside the prayer, thus breaking the continuity (muwalah) required for validity.
If the person had remained silent or only made dhikr for the need, the prayer would be preserved.
4
Apply the rule to a similar scenario
Consider a person who sneezes during prayer, goes to the bathroom, and while walking recites Quran. The ruling is the same: prayer invalid.
The key is that the recitation is deliberate and not linked to the prayer.

Zakat Dropping After Hawl on a Gift
Ruling on zakat obligation on a returned gift

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that zakat obligation drops when ownership is transferred after the hawl, and identify the correct fatwa for the returned gift scenario.

Core Idea
Zakat is tied to ownership at the end of the hawl; if ownership changes after, the obligation drops and the new owner does not inherit it.

This lesson addresses a subtle scenario in zakat: when a gift is given and then returned after a full lunar year. This riddle from the Hanafi tradition tests the mufti's understanding of the condition that zakat is owed only by the owner at the end of the hawl. We will unpack the ruling, its rationale, and how to apply it correctly in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Read the riddle: a gift is given at the start of the hawl. The recipient holds it for a full lunar year, so zakat becomes wajib on the recipient at the end of that year. Then the gift is returned to the giver after the year.
The phrase 'after the year' means after the hawl has completed, not during.
2
Apply the ownership rule
The recipient owned the item for the full hawl, so zakat was due on him. However, since he no longer owns it (it is returned), the obligation drops because the wealth is no longer in his possession.
Benefits and burdens follow ownership.
3
Consider the giver's position
The giver now owns the item again, but because he did not own it for the entire hawl (only after the hawl ended), he does not owe zakat for that year.
The giver's year restarts from the moment he repossesses it.
4
State the final fatwa
Conclude that no one owes zakat for that item for the past year: the recipient's obligation dropped upon return, and the giver never completed a hawl.
This is the mufta bihi ruling according to the Hanafi school.

Kaffarah Waived Due to Rejected Testimony
Ruling on invalid fasting and no kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that kaffarah is lifted when the witness's testimony is rejected, and state the correct mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Kaffarah requires certainty; rejected testimony creates doubt.

This short rule from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair illustrates a crucial exception to the general rule that intentionally breaking a Ramadan fast requires kaffarah. As a mufti, you must recognize when doubt (shubha) removes the expiation, even if the fasting is invalid and requires qada.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
Who is the subject? A man who sees the crescent of Ramadan alone (i.e., only he saw it) and the judge (qadi) rejects his testimony about the sighting.
The rejection could be due to insufficient reliability of the witness or legal procedural issues.
2
State the ruling
He breaks his fast without an excuse (i.e., intentionally), yet no kaffarah is due upon him. The text says 'no kaffarah upon him'.
The absence of kaffarah is the exception; normally intentional breaking requires kaffarah.
3
Identify the underlying principle
Kaffarah is lifted because of doubt (shubha). Since the judge's rejection creates uncertainty about whether the day is really Ramadan from the perspective of the public ruling, the certainty required for kaffarah is not present.
Hanafi usul: kaffarah is only imposed when the act is certain (qat'i) as a violation; shubha prevents it.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon ruling is that only qada (make-up) is required, not kaffarah. The man must fast one day later to compensate.
This rule is consistently upheld in later Hanafi fatwa collections.

When a Qarin Is Exempt from Sacrifice
Ruling on qarin who avoids sacrifice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition under which a qarin is exempt from the obligatory sacrifice and apply this rule in fatwa-giving.

Core Idea
A qarin owes no sacrifice if ihram for both was entered before their proper times.

This brief but precise rule from Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir addresses a subtle exception in the law of qiran Hajj. Normally, the qarin must offer a sacrifice (hady). However, if he enters ihram for both hajj and umrah before the designated times, then delays all actions until the proper season, his ihram is not fully valid as qiran from the start—thus the sacrifice is waived. Understanding such exceptions is crucial for a mufti to give accurate rulings.

Guided Path
1
Define the qarin and default obligation
Identify that a qarin (one who combines hajj and umrah with one ihram) is generally obliged to offer a blood sacrifice (hady). This is the baseline rule.
The default is based on the Qur'an and Sunnah.
2
Parse the Arabic phrase for the exception
Break down the condition: 'أحرم بهما قبل وقته' (entered ihram for both before their time) and 'ثم أتى بأفعالهما في وقته' (then performed their actions in their time). Note that 'وقته' refers to the fixed months of hajj (Shawwal, Dhul-Qa'dah, Dhul-Hijjah).
'قبل وقته' means before the hajj months begin (i.e., before Shawwal).
3
Understand the legal reasoning
Explain that ihram for hajj before its months is invalid; thus the pilgrim effectively never made a valid qiran combination. His actions during the proper time are considered as separate umrah and hajj, not a combined pilgrimage.
Umrah ihram is valid at any time, but hajj ihram has a specific time window.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Test a case: someone enters ihram in Rajab (pre-hajj months) and performs all rituals in Dhul-Hijjah. According to the rule, no sacrifice is required because the ihram was premature.
Contrast with a case where ihram is entered in Shawwal—then sacrifice is obligatory.
5
Formulate the fatwa response
When a mufti receives a question about a qarin's sacrifice, he must ask about the timing of ihram. If it was before the hajj months, he can give the ruling of no sacrifice.
This is a narrow exception; the mufti must verify the exact timing.

Father's Marriage That Is Not Executed
Ruling on father's marriage with improper mahr from Rasm al-Mufti.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions that render a father's marriage of his daughter non-executable according to Imam Abu Hanifa, and apply this rule to similar cases of incapacitated guardians.

Core Idea
A father's marriage is void if he is intoxicated and the mahr is insufficient.

This lesson examines a specific rule from the Rasm al-Mufti tradition: when a father's marriage of his daughter is not executed despite his general authority. The key factors are the father's state of intoxication and the deficiency of the dower. Understanding this helps muftis evaluate guardianship contracts where capacity is compromised.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that the marriage is not executed (la yanfudh) according to Imam Abu Hanifa. The ruling is non-execution, meaning it is legally ineffective.
Non-execution is a form of invalidity (batil) in this context.
2
Identify the actor and his state
The actor is the father (al-ab), but specifically described as 'al-sukrān' (intoxicated). This indicates that the father's legal capacity is compromised at the time of the contract.
Intoxication vitiates the father's ahliyya (legal capacity) for such actions.
3
Identify the object and the defect
The object is the daughter (ibnatahu) and the marriage is to a suitable match (min kuf'). The defect is that the mahr is less than her proper mahr (aqall min mahr mithliha).
Both the father's incapacity and the deficient mahr are required for the ruling.
4
Recognize the conjunctive condition
The passage combines both conditions: intoxicated father AND deficient mahr. Neither alone is sufficient for non-execution under this specific rule.
This is a parsing exercise: note the word 'if' (إذا) indicating the two conditions together.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that such a marriage is not executed. Later Hanafi jurists affirm this as the established ruling.
The ruling is consistent across zahir al-riwayah.

When Divorce Does Not Occur: The Role of False News
Ruling on verbal divorce intended as false news

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a divorce does not occur if the husband intended only to give false news, and identify the conditions under which intention overrides apparent meaning.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a divorce does not occur if the husband intended only to report a false divorce — intention overrides apparent meaning in ambiguous statements.

This brief passage from Ibn Nujaym's *al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir* addresses a critical nuance in divorce law: when does a verbal divorce not take effect? The ruling hinges on the speaker's intent, specifically when he claims he meant to give false news. Understanding this helps muftis evaluate claims of false reporting and avoid unintended consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage begins with the question: 'Which man divorced and it did not occur?' The answer: when he says 'I meant to give false news (of divorce).' Recognize that the words of divorce were uttered but the husband claims a different intention.
The scenario assumes the words are explicit (e.g., 'You are divorced') but the husband reinterprets them as news.
2
State the Hanafi rule
According to Hanafi fiqh, intention can override the apparent meaning when the statement is ambiguous or can be construed as something other than a divorce pronouncement. Here, the phrase 'I meant to give false news' shows the speaker's true intent was not to effect divorce.
This rule is based on the principle that verbal expressions are interpreted by the speaker's intent in cases of ambiguity.
3
Distinguish from clear divorce
If the statement is completely unambiguous (e.g., 'I divorce you' in a clear context of ending marriage), intention is irrelevant. The divorce occurs. The key is the statement's capacity to be interpreted as news.
For example, saying 'I divorce you' during a quarrel is usually clear; claiming false news would not be credible.
4
Apply the fatwa hierarchy
This ruling is from the *fatawa mutakhkhirin* (later jurists) and is the mufta bihi (operative) position. The mufti must consider the context and credibility of the husband's claim.
The authority comes from works like *al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir* which compile later Hanafi positions.

Paradoxical Cases in Hudud: A Rasm al-Mufti Lesson
Three paradoxical hudud cases with Hanafi resolutions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the mufti's principle of resolving apparent contradictions in hudud rulings by identifying the missing legal condition.

Core Idea
The mufti resolves paradoxes by pinpointing the condition that fails, not by abandoning the rule.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents three puzzling cases where an act apparently meets the criteria for a hadd penalty but the penalty is not applied, or vice versa. These are not exceptions but applications of the underlying legal conditions. Understanding these paradoxes trains the mufti to think in terms of conditions (shurut) rather than surface appearances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent paradox
Read each case carefully: what seems contradictory? For example, stealing a large total but no cutting.
2
State the general rule underlying each hadd
Recall the basic conditions for each hadd (theft, drinking). For theft: nisab, hirz, no shubha, etc.
For theft: nisab = 10 dirhams per act.
3
Identify the missing or non-standard condition
In each case, which condition is lacking? In theft by installments: each theft is below nisab. In theft from father by fosterage: no real filiation so no shubha. In drinking with slave testimony: evidence insufficient for hadd.
4
Resolve the paradox by applying the condition
The ruling follows the condition: no hadd because condition fails, or hadd applies because condition is present.

Source Text

كتاب الحدود
أتي رجل سرق مائة من حرز ولا قطع ؟ فقل : إذا سرقها على دفعات؛ كل مرة أقل من عشرة أي رجل سرق من مال أبيه وقطع؟ فقل : إذا كان من الرضاعة. أي رجل قال إن شربت الخمر طائعا فعبدي حرّ ؛ فشربها طائعا بالبينة وعتق العبد ولم يحد ؟ فقل : إذا كانت رجلاً وامرأتين .
Book of Hudud. A man stole one hundred from a safe and no cutting (of hand)? Say: If he stole it in installments, each time less than ten. What man stole from his father's wealth and (still) cutting? Say: If by fosterage. What man said: 'If I drink wine willingly, my slave is free'; then he drank it willingly with evidence, the slave was freed, and he was not flogged? Say: If (the witnesses were) one man and two women.
Source pages p. 345

Commentary
This passage presents three rulings from the Book of Hudud. Each ruling resolves an apparent contradiction. First: A thief who steals a total of one hundred dirhams from a secure place (hirz) but does not have his hand cut off. The answer: He stole in multiple instances, each less than the nisab (ten dirhams in the Hanafi school). Since each theft individually did not reach the threshold, the hadd penalty is not applied. Second: A son steals from his father's property and still has his hand cut off. Normally, a son is not subject to hand-cutting for stealing from his father's property due to shared ownership or suspicion. But if there is no real filial relationship (e.g., fosterage only), the penalty applies. Third: A man vows to free his slave if he drinks wine, drinks with evidence, the slave is freed, but he is not flogged for drinking. The reason: The evidence required for flogging is two just male witnesses or one man and two women; if only one man and two women testify, the hadd for drinking is not established, but the man's own confession (or the condition) still necessitates the freeing of the slave.

Key Terms
حرز (hirz): A secure place where property is stored, necessary for the application of the hadd of theft.
رضاعة (rada'ah): Fosterage; a relationship established by breastfeeding that creates certain prohibitions but does not create actual lineage for inheritance or hadd exceptions.

Four Paradoxical Rulings in Siyar
Four paradoxical siyar cases: aman, apostasy, siege, and foundling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the four siyar rules to novel scenarios and explain the reasoning behind each exception.

Core Idea
Each case flips a default rule based on a hidden premise that overrides the default.

This lesson presents four intriguing cases from the Hanafi Book of Siyar. Each appears paradoxical at first glance, but the underlying rationale reveals the consistency of Hanafi legal reasoning. A mufti must grasp these 'rasm' rules to correctly answer unusual questions that challenge default assumptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
For each case, note the expected default rule. In case 1, aman should protect the grantor; instead he is killed. In case 2, apostates are normally killed; here one is not. In case 3, a fort under siege can normally be attacked; here attack and aman are both barred. In case 4, a child normally follows parents; here the foundling is Muslim without following.
State the default before looking at the answer.
2
Find the hidden premise
Determine the specific fact that overrides the default. In case 1, the Harbi asked aman for a thousand but did not count himself. In case 2, the apostate's initial Islam was as a follower or with doubt. In case 3, an unknown dhimmi is present. In case 4, the foundling has no known parents and is found in Dar al-Islam.
The hidden premise is always a condition that makes the default inapplicable.
3
Articulate the operative rule
Write the corrected rule for each case. Case 1: A Harbi who gives aman to others but excludes himself is not protected. Case 2: An apostate whose Islam was not fully independent is not killed. Case 3: A fort with an unknown dhimmi cannot be attacked or given aman; if some exit, the rest can be killed. Case 4: A foundling in Dar al-Islam is Muslim without taba'iyyah.
Memorize these precise formulations for fatwa.
4
Generalize the principle
Observe a pattern: each exception arises because the default presumption (aman protects all, apostates die, forts are attacked, children follow parents) is overridden by a specific condition that removes the basis for the default. This teaches a mufti to always check for hidden conditions.
This skill applies across all fiqh.

أي رجل أمن ألفًا؛ فقتل هو ولم يقتلوا ؟ فقل : حربي طلب الأمان لألف فعدَّها ولم يعد نفسه أي مرتد لا يقتل؟ فقل : مَن كان إسلامه تبعًا أو فيه شبهة. أي حصن لا يجوز قتل أهله ولا أمان لهم؟ فقل : إذا كان فيهم ذمّيّ لا يعرف فلو خرج البعض حل قتل الباقي . أي رضيع يحكم بإسلامه فلا تبعيَّة؟ فقل: لقيط في دار الإسلام.
Book of Siyar. Which man gave aman to a thousand, then he was killed and they were not killed? Say: A Harbi asked for aman for a thousand, counted them but did not count himself. Say: If among them there is a dhimmi who is not known; if some come out, it becomes permissible to kill the rest. Which infant is ruled to be Muslim without (the law of) following? Source pages p. 345

Commentary
This passage presents four rulings from the Book of Siyar. First: A person gives aman (safe conduct) to a thousand people, but he himself is killed while the others are not. The answer: The person was a Harbi (enemy combatant) who requested aman for a thousand of his people but did not include himself; thus his own aman is not valid, and he can be killed. The others remain protected. Second: An apostate who is not killed. Normally apostasy warrants death, but if the person converted to Islam as a minor or under coercion (with doubt), he is not executed. Third: A fort whose inhabitants can neither be killed nor granted aman. The reason is that a dhimmi (protected non-Muslim) is present but unknown. If some emerge, the rest can be killed because the dhimmi might be among those who came out. Fourth: An infant ruled to be Muslim without the law of tawalli (allegiance to non-Muslims). A foundling in Dar al-Islam is deemed Muslim because the default is Islam in Muslim lands, and he is not considered a follower of his parents.

Key Terms
أمان (aman): Safe conduct granted to an enemy combatant, making his person and property inviolable.
تبعية (taba'iyyah): The law of following: a child follows the religion of his parents in matters of status.

The Missing Person: Living but Legally Dead
Ruling on missing person paradox

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi paradox regarding the mafqud and distinguish the two domains where he is considered dead vs alive.

Core Idea
The mafqud is legally dead for inheritance but alive for his own marriage — a paradox in Hanafi law.

This lesson explores a classic puzzle in Hanafi fiqh: a missing person (mafqud) is treated as dead for the purposes of inheritance and divorce of his wife, yet he is considered alive for the management of his own property and his own marriage. This duality is a deliberate juristic construction to balance competing interests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the paradox
Read the question: 'What man is considered dead while he is alive and well?' The answer is the mafqud. Notice the contradiction: legally dead, biologically alive.
2
Distinguish the two domains
The mafqud is considered dead for the rights of others (inheritance, divorce) but alive for his own rights (property, marriage). Understand the reason: to prevent harm to his dependents while preserving his own interests.
This is based on the judge's ijtihad after a missing period.
3
Apply the rule
In a practical scenario: A man disappears. After a period, the judge declares him dead for inheritance. His wife remarries. Later, he returns. The marriage to the new husband is valid, and the first marriage is dissolved. But his property from before remains his.
Application of the paradox.

Paradoxes of Waqf: Possession and Apostasy
Rulings on waqf possession and apostasy paradoxes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a waqif cannot take possession of his own waqf but an agent can, and under what condition a waqf lease is annulled due to apostasy.

Core Idea
Waqf cannot be possessed by the donor, and apostasy of the waqif can break the perpetuity of endowment.

This lesson tackles two counterintuitive rulings in Hanafi waqf law. The first: the founder (waqif) taking possession of the waqf property invalidates the waqf, but if his agent does so, it is valid. The second: if the waqif leases the waqf property, then apostatizes and dies, the waqf is nullified and the lease ends. These rulings highlight the importance of legal personality and the effect of apostasy on property.

Guided Path
1
First paradox: Who can take possession?
The question: What action is invalid when done by oneself but valid when delegated to an agent? Answer: Taking possession (qabd) of a waqf. The waqif cannot take qabd of his own waqf because it would be like taking possession of his own property in trust, which is contradictory. His agent, being a third party, can validly take possession.
This is based on the Hanafi rule that waqf is not binding until qabd is done by the beneficiaries or a representative.
2
Second paradox: Can a waqf lease be annulled?
Normally, waqf is perpetual and leases continue. But consider: if the waqif himself leased the waqf property, then apostatized (left Islam) and died as an apostate, the waqf becomes invalid because an apostate's property is inherited by his heirs. Thus the lease ends with the change of ownership.
This applies only if the waqif dies as an apostate; if he returns to Islam, the waqf remains.
3
Extract the principle
The first paradox shows that legal acts require proper capacity and avoidance of conflict of interest. The second shows that apostasy can sever the perpetuity of waqf because the property reverts to being part of the estate.
These are memorization anchors for muftis.

Paradoxical Cases in Sales (Buyu') – Rasm al-Mufti Applications
Four paradoxical rulings in the Book of Sales

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi ruling for each paradoxical sale scenario and explain the underlying principle that resolves the apparent contradiction.

Core Idea
A sale may be invalid when done by the owner but valid when done by a proxy – the key is the change in legal personality.

The mufti occasionally encounters cases where the apparent ruling seems contradictory. This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents four such paradoxes from the Book of Sales. The mufti must distinguish between the owner's capacity and the proxy's capacity, and consider the effects of illness, kinship, religion, and impurity classifications. This lesson trains you to identify the governing principle behind each paradoxical ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the paradoxical element
For each case, state what seems contradictory: e.g., owner cannot sell but proxy can; a man selling his own father; buying a slave who remains unlawful; bread that cannot be sold to non-Muslims but can be sold to Shafi'is.
The paradox is resolved by a change in legal capacity or a difference in the ruling of impurity.
2
State the underlying principle
Identify the Hanafi rule or maxim that resolves the paradox: e.g., muhabah by a marid is treated as a bequest; permission can transfer the right to sell; prohibition due to prior ownership or relationship; impurity ruling differs for Shafi'is.
These principles are well-established in Hanafi fiqh.

Guarantor's Non-Recourse: The Freed Slave Case
A ruling from Kitab al-Kafala on the non-recourse of a slave guarantor after freedom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a slave guarantor's lack of recourse after freedom and apply it to analogous cases.

Core Idea
A slave's guarantee of his master's debt loses recourse upon freedom — the master's liability ends.

This lesson examines a paradoxical ruling from the Book of Guarantee: a slave guarantees his master's debt with permission, then is freed and pays. Normally a guarantor can recover from the debtor, but here he cannot. Why? Because the guarantee was executed during slavery, and after freedom the master-servant relationship—and its attendant obligations—dissolves. This ruling is a cornerstone for understanding how legal capacity affects rights of recourse.

The right of recourse (ruju') arises from the guarantee itself, but the guarantor's status as a slave and the master's authority over him prevent the normal right of recourse.
4

The freed slave cannot claim reimbursement. The master is not liable. This is an exception to the general rule that a guarantor can recover.

Judge's Power to Compel Sales and Oath Procedures
Rulings on judicial compulsion of sales and discharge of oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a judge can compel a sale and explain the effect of an oath taken by one of multiple defendants.

Core Idea
A judge can compel the sale of a Muslim slave to a disbeliever and the Mus-haf of a disbeliever; if one of multiple oath-takers swears, the rest are excused because the purpose of nukul is defeated.

This lesson tackles two paradoxical scenarios from the Book of Judgment. First, a judge has the authority to compel certain sales that would otherwise be voluntary: selling a Muslim slave to a disbeliever and selling a Mus-haf (Qur'an) owned by a disbeliever. These are exceptions to the rule that sales are voluntary, justified by the need to prevent prohibited ownership. Second, a procedural rule: when multiple defendants are required to take an oath, if one swears, the others are released from that obligation. Why? Because the purpose of demanding the oath is to elicit a refusal (nukul) that would result in judgment against the defendants. Once one swears, the possibility of nukul disappears, so the others' oaths become superfluous.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two forced sales
First, a Muslim slave must be sold if owned by a disbeliever because a disbeliever cannot own a Muslim. Second, a Mus-haf owned by a disbeliever must be sold because a disbeliever cannot own the Qur'an. These are cases of judicial compulsion (jabr).
2
Understand the rationale
The judge compels these sales to remove a prohibited state of ownership. It is a socially beneficial act that overrides the normal freedom of contract.
3
Analyze the oath scenario
In the case of a disputed right-of-way, neighbors are required to swear that the house had no such right. If they refuse (nukul), the judge rules for the plaintiff. If one swears, the others' oaths become pointless because the refusal condition is no longer present.
4
State the legal consequence
When one oath-taker swears, the rest are discharged. This is based on the principle that the benefit (nukul) is lost.

Paradoxical Rulings in Testimony (Shahadat)
Four exceptional rulings on testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi exceptions for accepting non-Muslim testimony, secondary testimony, concealment of testimony, and contradictory testimonies in fatwa cases.

Core Idea
Non-Muslim testimony is accepted against non-Muslims; concealment is permitted for valid reasons.

The Book of Testimonies contains rulings that appear paradoxical at first glance. However, each exception is grounded in the underlying legal reasoning (ta'lil) that balances justice and practical realities. This lesson examines four such cases from the Hanafi tradition that every mufti must know to issue sound fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first case: Christian witnesses vs. Muslim and Christian partners
Note that the testimony of Christian witnesses is accepted against the Christian partner but not against the Muslim partner. The key principle is that non-Muslims cannot testify against Muslims, but they can testify against fellow non-Muslims.
This exception applies only when the non-Muslim party is the defendant.
2
Examine the second case: Testimony upon testimony (shahada 'ala shahada)
Secondary witnesses need not know the original party; they only need to know the primary witnesses. This facilitates proof when original witnesses are unavailable.
Conditions include number and reliability of secondary witnesses.
3
Analyze the third case: Concealment of testimony (kitman al-shahada)
Concealment is permissible if the right can be established through other evidence, if the judge is corrupt, or if the judge is known to reject the testimony. This protects the witness from futile risk.
In general, concealing testimony is sinful; these are exceptions.
4
Study the fourth case: Conflicting testimonies between Muslim sons and Christian witnesses
Two Muslim sons testify their father died a Christian; two Christians testify he died a Muslim. The Christians' testimony is accepted because the sons are interested parties (they would inherit if the father died Muslim) and Muslims cannot testify against their father in matters of faith.
The presumption of Islam is not strong enough to overcome the conflict of interest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fiqh chapter
Each ruling begins with the chapter name (e.g., كتاب الصلح). Determine the transaction type and its general legal context.
This step anchors the ruling in its proper domain.
2
Locate the conditional phrase
Look for the word 'إذا' or 'إذا كان' or a similar conditional marker. The condition is the precise circumstance that activates the ruling.
Often the condition is nested inside a longer sentence.
3
Extract the legal consequence
Identify the result that follows the condition. This is often a change in rights or obligations (e.g., invalidation of a right, requirement to pay, permission to revoke).
The consequence is the answer to the 'which' question.
4
Analyze the rationale of the hila
Consider why this particular condition triggers that consequence. The hila (legal stratagem) is designed to achieve a lawful outcome while avoiding a prohibited or undesirable result.
Understanding the rationale helps in applying the principle to new cases.
5
Test with a counter-condition
Ask: What would happen if the condition were absent or reversed? This clarifies the boundary of the ruling and reveals the hila's limits.
This step trains the mind of the mufti to see the spectrum of possibilities.

Chapter of Settlement
Which settlement, if it occurs, invalidates the right of the one making settlement and returns the substitute to the opponent? Say: The settlement regarding pre-emption.
Chapter of Mudarabah
Which mudarib must pay back what he spent from his own pocket? Say: If nothing remains in his hand from its capital.
Chapter of Gift
Which father gifted to his son and has the right to revoke? Say: If the son is owned by a stranger. Which gifted item requires paying its price to the pawnbroker? Say: The item sold for deferred delivery; if the seller of salam gifted it to the one receiving it, he must return the capital.
Chapter of Hire
A dispute of the lessee to rescind the hire by the lessor's acknowledgment of a debt, what is the stratagem? Say: That he makes the first year's rent a little and the last year's more.
Chapter of Deposit
Which man claimed a deposit, the defendant confirmed it, but the judge did not order delivery to him? Say: If the heir acknowledges that the deceased left a deposit and the deceased had a debt, his acknowledgment is invalid; and if the creditors confirm it, the judge pays the deceased's debt and the claimant recovers from the creditors due to their confirmation. Similarly in hire, mudarabah, loan, and pawn.
Chapter of Loan
Which borrower acquires the right to refuse after demand? Say: If he demands a ship in the middle of the sea, or a sword to kill unjustly, or a wet nurse after the child takes only her breast, or the horseman's horse in enemy territory, or a loan of pledged property before debt repayment. Which place is liable for destruction? Say: If it appears to be owned by someone else. Which depositor is liable without transgression? Say: If he orders its delivery to some of his heirs and he delivers it after his death.

Commentary
This passage lists conditional rulings from various chapters of fiqh, each answering a specific 'which' question. The pattern is: identify the case where a particular ruling applies. For example, in Sulh, the only settlement that invalidates the right and returns the substitute is the one concerning pre-emption (shuf'a). In Mudarabah, the mudarib must repay expenses only if no funds remain. The section on Ijarah explicitly asks for a hila (legal stratagem): to vary rent over years to escape rescission. The Wadi'a entry involves a complex scenario with acknowledgment of a deposit and debt. The 'Aariyah entries cover refusal to return (e.g., ship at sea, sword for unjust killing) and liability conditions. All rulings are strictly conditional and illustrate how precise circumstances change the hukum.

Key Terms
صلح عن الشفعة (sulh 'an al-shuf'a): A settlement where one party gives up their right of pre-emption in exchange for compensation, which can invalidate the right of the one making sulh.
مضارب (mudarib): The managing partner in a mudarabah arrangement, who may be liable for expenses if no capital remains.
مسلم فيه (muslam fih): The item sold on deferred delivery in a salam contract. If gifted by the seller, he must return the capital.
ظئر (dha'r): A wet nurse; the child may refuse to take milk from anyone else, creating a right to retain the wet nurse.

Patterns of Hiyal in Hanafi Fiqh: Conditional Rulings from Ashbah wa-Nazair
List of conditional rulings across fiqh chapters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the pattern of conditional rulings (hiyal) by parsing the precise condition and ruling in each case from this passage.

Core Idea
A hila is a conditional ruling that depends on a specific fact pattern.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's Ashbah wa-Nazair presents a series of sharply conditional rulings from various fiqh chapters. Each entry follows a pattern: 'Which [case]? Say: [ruling under specific condition].' These are not general rules but precise hiyal — legal stratagems that identify exactly when an unusual outcome applies. Understanding this pattern is crucial for the mufti who must discern the operative condition before issuing a fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chapter heading and subject
Look at the chapter title (e.g., Kitab al-Mukatab) to know the general fiqh area. Then identify the specific subject of the question (e.g., 'Which kitabah is annulled?').
2
Parse the question: 'Which X?'
The question asks for an exceptional case within the chapter. Identify the category (e.g., mukatab, mudabbar, etc.) and what is unusual about it.
3
Identify the condition introduced by 'إذا' (if)
The condition is the key to the ruling. It specifies the exact scenario that triggers the exceptional outcome. For example, 'if the mukatab is indebted to creditors.'
4
State the ruling (after 'فقل')
The answer after 'Say:' is the ruling that applies under the given condition. It may deviate from the default rule in that chapter.
5
Note the exceptional nature
Consider what the default ruling would be and how the condition changes it. This highlights the function of hiyal as precise legal stratagems.

Chapter of the Mukatab
Which kitabah (contract of emancipation) is annulled by non-contractants? Say: If the mukatab is indebted to creditors, they annul it. Which mukatab or mudabbar is allowed to be sold? Which slave's permission is not established by silence even if his master sees him buying and selling? Say: The slave of the judge.

Commentary
This page continues the pattern of conditional rulings. In Makatib: creditors can annul a mukatab's contract if he is in debt; a mukatab or mudabbar can be sold only under specific conditions like enemy action. For Ma'dhun: silence of the master does not establish the slave's permission if the slave is the judge's slave (since judges typically don't own slaves? Actually, it's a specific case). In Ghasb: consuming part of a set (like one door leaf or a shoe) requires compensating for the whole; a usurper is not acquitted if the owner is insane. The depositor of a usurper is liable. In Shuf'a: the pre-emptor only succeeds against an agent buyer. In Qismah: a non-through lane cannot be divided even by consent. In Udhiyah: tasmiyah must be intended for the animal; a butcher who ties the animal for slaughter is not liable if it dies. In Karahiyah: vessels from human parts are prohibited; vessels set aside for oneself are disliked for wudu. The rulings are precise and conditional.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has a contract of emancipation (kitabah) with his master, paying installments.
سكة غير نافذة (sikkah ghayr nafidhah): A dead-end alley or lane that is not a thoroughfare, jointly owned by residents.
