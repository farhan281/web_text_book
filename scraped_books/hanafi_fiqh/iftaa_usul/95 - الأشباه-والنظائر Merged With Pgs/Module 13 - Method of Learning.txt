Author explains compilation method and distinguishes qa'idah from dhabit

Core Idea
A qa'idah spans multiple fiqh chapters; a dhabit is confined to one chapter.

He then clarifies a crucial terminological distinction: the difference between a dhabit (a 'control' or 'precept') and a qa'idah (a 'maxim'). This distinction is foundational for understanding how Hanafi jurists categorize legal principles and organize knowledge in usul al-iftaa.

The Finality of a Judge's Judgment and Its Exceptions
Ruling that a judge cannot retract his judgment with three exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the default ruling on a judge's retraction, apply the three exceptions, and explain the underlying rationale using Hanafi usul al-iftaa.

Core Idea
A judge cannot retract a valid judgment; only when based on personal knowledge, error, or ruling against his madhhab may it be reversed.

In Hanafi legal theory, a judge's judgment is generally final to preserve legal stability. However, certain circumstances allow retraction. This lesson explores the default rule and its three articulated exceptions, essential for understanding the limits of judicial authority.

Guided Path
1
State the default rule
The judge cannot retract his judgment once issued, provided it met conditions of validity (proper claim and evidence). The statement 'I retract' is ineffective.
Sources: al-Khaniya, al-Khilasa, al-Kanz.
2
Identify the restrictive conditions
The rule applies only when the judgment was with conditions of validity (shurut al-sihha) and after a valid claim and proper testimony. This ensures the default non-retractability is tied to procedural correctness.
The restrictions from al-Khilasa and al-Kanz narrow the scope of the rule.
3
Exception 1: Judgment based on personal knowledge
If the judge relied solely on his own knowledge (qada' bi-'ilmihi) without evidence, he may retract. This is derived from requiring evidence in the valid judgment case.
Derived by Ibn Wahban from the restriction in al-Khilasa.
4
Exception 2: Error becomes apparent
If the judge discovers he made an error in the judgment, he must reverse it. This is distinct from a mere change of opinion, which does not allow retraction.
The text explicitly contrasts error with a change of view.
5
Exception 3: Ruling against his own madhhab in ijtihadi matters
If the judge issued a judgment on a debatable issue (mujtahad fih) that contradicts his own school, he alone may reverse it; no other judge may do so.
Source: Sharh al-Manzumah. This preserves the judge's prerogative to correct a ruling that goes against his ijtihad.

Miscellaneous Rules Regarding the Judge
Collection of various rules about the judge's authority and procedures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply key procedural rules for a Hanafi judge, including rules on testimony, oaths, jurisdiction, and removal, and differentiate between agreed and disputed positions within the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A judge's authority is strictly territorial; he is not removed by apostasy or sin.

This lesson compiles a set of practical rules for a Hanafi judge and mufti, drawn from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. The text covers when a hearer of an acknowledgment may testify, the swearing of creditors and minors, the writing of discharge documents, and the critical principle that a judge's jurisdiction is limited to a specific time and place. Understanding these rules is essential for issuing correct fatwas and ensuring judicial procedures align with Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify rules on testimony after prohibition
Read the first two conditions: if the acknowledger says 'do not testify against me' generally, the hearer may still testify; if he says 'do not testify regarding what I acknowledged,' the hearer cannot testify.
The distinction is between a general prohibition and a specific one.
2
Analyze the dispute on retraction
Note the difference of opinion when the acknowledgee later asks for testimony: some say the hearer testifies, others say he does not.
The text does not name the scholars or give a tarjih.
3
State rules on swearing and evidence
Review the rules: the judge does not swear the creditor of a deceased person; evidence against a coerced person is valid only if the judge is unaware of coercion; and agency can be established without opponent if the judge knows the principal.
These are procedural safeguards.
4
Explain removal and document writing
Understand that the judge is not removed by apostasy or sin; only a decree removes him. Also, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ on writing a discharge document in absentia.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute; the text does not resolve it.
5
Apply the principle of jurisdiction
The judge's authority is specialized by time and place; a ruling outside his jurisdiction is invalid, with a difference of opinion regarding real estate outside his territory.
This is a foundational principle of judicial authority.

Reliability of Authentic Fiqh Books for Fatwa
Permissibility of relying on books without sanad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on relying on fiqh books for fatwa and identify the two methods of transmission (sanad or well-known book).

Core Idea
A mufti can rely on a well-known fiqh book without needing a live teacher.

In the early centuries, Hanafi scholars insisted on oral transmission (samāʿ) for reliable fatwa. However, as the madhhab matured and authoritative texts became widely circulated, later jurists relaxed this requirement. This lesson examines the rule from Fath al-Qadir and the consensus reported by al-Asyuti, showing that a mufti in our time can base his fatwa on well-known books like those of Muhammad ibn al-Ḥasan even without a continuous chain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two transmission methods
The passage from Fath al-Qadir states that a mufti can transmit from a mujtahid either through a chain (sanad) or by relying on a well-known book. Note that these are alternatives.
The absence of sanad is compensated by the book's fame and acceptance.
2
Understand the consensus
Al-Asyuti narrates from Abu Ishaq al-Isfara'ini that there is consensus (ijma) on the permissibility of transmission from authoritative books without a continuous chain to the authors.
This consensus removes the earlier strict requirement of oral ijazah.
3
Apply to everyday fatwa practice
A modern mufti can open a standard Hanafi text like al-Hidayah or al-Mukhtar and issue a fatwa from it without needing to have heard it directly from a teacher.
This is the operative rule (muftā bihī) in the late Hanafi school.

Mufti's Handwriting and Court Claims from Books
Application of book reliance to mufti's writing and court claims

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a mufti's handwriting is accepted as a transmission method and state the conditions for claims from a book in court.

Core Idea
A mufti's handwriting is even stronger than his gesture, and a claim from a book requires pointing to the specific text.

After establishing the permissibility of relying on books, the passage extends the principle to the mufti's own writing (khatt al-mufti) and to using written copies in court claims. 1
Understand the basis for accepting mufti's handwriting
The passage argues that if gesture (ishāra) is accepted as a valid transmission from a mute person, then writing is even more reliable (awlā) because it is clearer and more permanent.

This prevents the agent from fabricating claims from documents.

Three Categories of Sciences: A Hanafi Pedagogical Hierarchy
A brief classification of sciences using a cooking metaphor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the three categories of Islamic sciences according to the Hanafi view and explain their hierarchical relationship in the context of iftaa methodology.

Core Idea
Fiqh and hadith are the most refined sciences — the 'ripened and burned' — because they represent the final, applied product of divine guidance.

This short benefit from the Ashbah wa al-Nazair uses a vivid cooking metaphor to lay out a hierarchy of Islamic sciences. Understanding this classification is essential for a mufti: it reminds us that tools like grammar and usul are foundational, explanatory sciences like tafsir are intermediate, and the ultimate goal is mastery of fiqh and hadith. This shapes how we approach learning and issuing fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Understand the metaphor
The author uses 'ripening' (nuduj) for development and 'burning' (ihtiraq) for completion. A science that 'ripened but did not burn' is still in development; one that 'ripened and burned' is fully mature and ready for use.
Think of cooking: once food is burnt, it cannot be cooked further.
2
Identify the three categories
List each category with its sciences: 1) Ripened but not burned: nahw (grammar) and usul (foundations of fiqh). 2) Neither ripened nor burned: bayan (rhetoric) and tafsir (exegesis). 3) Ripened and burned: fiqh (jurisprudence) and hadith.
These are the exact sciences mentioned in the text.
3
Explain the pedagogical rationale
This classification reflects a study sequence: first learn the linguistic and methodological tools (nahw, usul), then the interpretive sciences (bayan, tafsir), and finally the applied law (fiqh) and its primary source (hadith). The 'burning' indicates that fiqh and hadith are the most authoritative and directly applicable.
In Hanafi education, students begin with nahw and usul before tackling advanced fiqh.
4
Apply to iftaa methodology
When issuing a fatwa, a mufti draws on all three levels: he uses nahw/usul to understand the text correctly, tafsir/bayan to interpret Qur'an and hadith rhetorically, but the final ruling is grounded in fiqh and hadith — the 'ripened and burned' sciences.
This hierarchy prevents the mufti from relying on incomplete or intermediary knowledge.

Commentary
This short benefit classifies Islamic sciences using a cooking metaphor. 'Nadaj' (ripening) and 'ih'taraq' (burning) represent stages of refinement and completion. Fiqh (jurisprudence) and hadith are at the highest level of refinement (ripened and burnt), implying they are the most advanced and practical. This reflects a pedagogical hierarchy: tools first, then explanatory sciences, then applied law. The Hanafi tradition emphasizes fiqh as the crown of religious sciences.
