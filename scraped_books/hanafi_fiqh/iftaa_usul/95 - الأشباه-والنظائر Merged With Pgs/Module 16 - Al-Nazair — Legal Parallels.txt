The Fetus's Legal Dependency on the Mother
Enumeration of rules of fetus dependency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'the fetus follows its mother in specific rulings' by identifying which rulings are included and which are exceptions.

Core Idea
The fetus is not an independent legal person but follows its mother in twelve rulings and is independent in eleven; six exceptions define where it does not follow.

This passage from the Book of Sales presents a comprehensive enumeration of legal positions where the fetus is considered a dependent of its mother (tabi') and where it is treated independently. Understanding this sughra qa'ida is essential for muftis when handling cases involving pregnant women, inheritance, and transactions. The passage lists twelve matters of dependency, six of non-dependency, and eleven of independent treatment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The text first states that the fetus follows its mother in twelve specific rulings (e.g., emancipation, pledge). Then it lists six where it does not follow (e.g., tort, hadd). Finally, it mentions eleven rulings where the fetus is treated independently while still attached (e.g., bequest, inheritance).
2
Parse the twelve following rulings
Read the initial list: emancipation (itq), tadbir (absolute, not restricted), istila', kitaba, original freedom, slavery and ownership by all causes, right of old owner, right of reclaim in corrupt sale and debt, right of sacrifice, pledge. Count them and note they are from Jami' al-Fusulain.
The text says 'these are twelve issues' – verify by counting.
3
Identify the six non-following rulings
Look for the phrase 'la yatba'uha fi sittati mas'ala' – six matters: tort (jinaaya), right to revoke gift, zakat on grazing animals, qisas on mother, hadd on mother (execution postponed), and fetus not made halal by mother's slaughter.
These are exceptions where the fetus is separate.
4
Note the eleven independent rulings
The text says 'la yufradu bihukm ma dam muttasilan biha... illa fi ihda 'ashrata mas'ala' – eleven matters where the fetus is treated independently: emancipation, tadbir, bequest, acknowledgment for it and by it, lineage, maintenance, inheritance, ghurra, khul', paternity if born within six months.
These apply while the fetus is still attached.
5
Apply to a modern fatwa scenario
A mufti is asked: Can a pregnant woman be sold for her debt? Apply the maxim: the fetus follows the mother in debt, so yes, the fetus is sold with her. But if the issue is qisas on the mother, she cannot be executed until delivery because the fetus does not follow in that ruling.
Distinguish dependency vs. independence.

And it does not follow the mother in any ruling after birth except in one issue: if the mother is proven to belong to someone else by evidence, her child follows her; and by acknowledgment, not as in al-Kanz. And it can be said again: the offspring of an animal follows its mother in sale if it is with her at the time, according to the mufta bihi opinion.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Sales is a systematic enumeration of rulings concerning the fetus (haml) in the womb, particularly in relation to its mother. The author begins by noting that the fetus cannot be sold independently because it is unknown (majhul). It follows the mother in twelve specific rulings: emancipation (itq), tadbir (slavery upon owner's death) — but only absolute tadbir, not restricted; istila' (possession by conquest?); kitaba (contract of manumission); original freedom (hurriyyah asliyyah); slavery and ownership by all causes; the right of the old owner (haqq al-malik al-qadim) extends to it; the right of reclaim (istirdad) in a corrupt sale and in debt, so it is sold with the mother for debt; the right of sacrifice (udhiyya); and pledge (rahn). These are twelve issues, with additional ones from Jami' al-Fusulain. Then details: in pledge, if a pledged female gives birth, the child is also pledged, unlike a hired female, etc. The author then discusses missing rulings: selling a pregnant slave-girl with her fetus, etc., and suggests it is corrupt as combining known and unknown. From Fath al-Qadir: after the fetus is freed, the mother cannot be sold but can be gifted; but not gifted after the fetus's tadbir. Then a case about a non-believer slave-girl pregnant by a non-believer whose owner converts to Islam. Then hiring the fetus is valid because hiring non-existent is allowed. Then waqf for the fetus is valid. Then it states that the offspring of animals belongs to the owner of the female, not the male. Then lists six matters where the fetus does NOT follow the mother: torts (jinaaya), right to revoke a gift, zakat on grazing animals, qisas on the mother, hadd on the mother (she is not executed until delivery), and the fetus is not rendered halal by the mother's slaughter. Then it lists eleven matters where the fetus is treated independently while still attached to the mother: emancipation, tadbir, bequest, acknowledgment (for it and by it), lineage, maintenance, inheritance, ghurra, khul', paternity if born within six months. After birth, the child does not follow the mother except in one case: if the mother is reclaimed by evidence, the child follows her; but not by acknowledgment. Finally, the offspring of an animal follows the mother in sale if present at the time, according to the mufta bihi opinion.

Key Terms
الحمل (al-ḥaml): The fetus in the womb, considered legally as part of the mother until birth, with specific rules of dependency and independence.
التبعية (al-tabiʿiyyah): The legal principle that the fetus follows its mother in certain rulings (e.g., sale, pledge, emancipation).
الغرة (al-ghurrah): Compensation (blood money) paid for causing a miscarriage; valued at one-twentieth of diyah for a free woman.

Three People Whose Supplication Is Not Answered
Lists three types of people whose dua is not answered.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of people whose dua is not answered according to this Hanafi maxim, and apply the underlying principle to similar cases.

Core Idea
Supplication is blocked when the supplicant persists in a sin or neglects a duty.

This passage from Hajar al-Muhit lists three situations where a person's dua is not accepted. It serves as a moral and legal maxim (qa'ida) highlighting that certain actions negate the efficacy of supplication. Understanding this helps a mufti advise individuals to rectify their affairs before expecting their prayers to be answered.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the three categories
Commit to memory the three types of people: a man with a bad wife who does not divorce, a man who gives wealth to a fool, and a man who lends without witnesses.
This is a memorization anchor – you will encounter this maxim in Hanafi fatwa literature.
2
Identify the common element
Notice that each category involves a sin or negligence: enduring a bad marriage without action, misusing wealth, and failing to document a loan.
The common principle is that persisting in a sin blocks dua.
3
Apply the maxim
Extend the reasoning: any similar sin or negligence can block dua. For example, giving money to a known spendthrift or lending without documentation.
The maxim is illustrative, not exhaustive.
4
Note the source
The source is Hajar al-Muhit, a Hanafi fiqh work by Ibn al-Shihnah. Its authority level is fatawa mutakhkhirin (later fatwa literature).
This gives the maxim weight in Hanafi ifta.

Exceptions to Kaffarah in Fasting
First three riddles of the fasting section

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the specific scenarios in which a fasting person avoids kaffarah despite intentionally breaking the fast, and explain the underlying legal rationale.

Core Idea
Kaffarah is not automatic—it is waived when the obligation was uncertain or the act's nullifying effect is conditional.

The first three riddles in this passage probe the boundaries of kaffarah liability. You might assume that any intentional break of a Ramadan fast requires expiation, but the Hanafi school carves out exceptions based on doubt in the obligation and the nature of the nullifying act. This lesson unpacks those exceptions, training you to spot when kaffarah is and is not due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question pattern
Each riddle presents a seemingly contradictory ruling (e.g., broke fast but no kaffarah). Your task is to find the missing condition that resolves the paradox.
The mufti must think of cases where a general rule does not apply due to a hidden condition.
2
Extract the legal cause (illah)
For riddle 1, the key is that the fast was not originally obligatory because the testimony was rejected. For riddle 2, the intention was valid but the obligation changed. For riddle 3, the nullification depends on pleasure.
3
Apply to new variations
Test your understanding by constructing a similar scenario where kaffarah is due despite a doubt, and one where it is not.
Common mistake: thinking that any doubt voids kaffarah—only certain types of doubt (shubhah) matter.

Book of Fasting. A man broke his fast without excuse and has no expiation? Say: One who saw the crescent alone and the judge rejected his testimony. And you may say: One concerning the validity of whose fasting there is disagreement. A man intended Ramadan at the time of intention and it occurred as supererogatory? Commentary
This passage presents five fasting riddles typical of the 'ashbah wal-naza'ir' genre. Each riddle requires identifying the specific scenario that matches the given ruling. First: a man breaks his fast without excuse but has no kaffarah—this is someone whose testimony about seeing the new moon was rejected, so his fast was not originally obligatory. Second: a man intends Ramadan but his fast becomes nafl—this occurs if he intended Ramadan before dawn but only reached puberty after dawn, making the obligation begin later. Third: a fasting person who swallows another's saliva incurs kaffarah only if the saliva is from a beloved (due to likelihood of pleasure, nullifying the fast). Fourth: a fasting person who breaks his fast has no qada' if he began fasting doubtfully (e.g., intending qada' but later discovering no qada' was due). Fifth: a man's nafl fast is invalid if he was a disbeliever at dawn and only embraced Islam after noon, then intended nafl—since the time for intention for nafl is before noon for a Muslim.

Key Terms
كفارة (kaffārah): Expiation due for invalidating a fast intentionally without valid excuse
شبهة (shubhah): Legal doubt that clouds the certainty of obligation or prohibition
نية (niyyah): Intention, required for fasting to be valid

No Qada' and Invalid Nafl: Further Fasting Riddles
Riddles 4 and 5 of the fasting section

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the conditions under which a fasting person has no qada' despite breaking the fast, and when a nafl fast is invalid due to timing of conversion.

Core Idea
Qada' is waived when the fast was originally uncertain; nafl after noon is invalid for a convert.

The final two riddles deal with exceptions to qada' and the validity of nafl fasting for a new Muslim. These cases further illustrate that fasting rules are not absolute—they depend on the precise circumstances. This lesson trains you to identify when the usual consequences of breaking a fast are suspended.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contradiction
Riddle 4: broke fast but no qada'. Riddle 5: intended nafl but invalid. Find the hidden condition that makes the ruling non-obvious.
Both riddles hinge on the timing of intention or obligation.
2
Analyze the doubt (shakk) in riddle 4
The person began fasting with the intention of qada', but later discovered that no qada' was due. His fast was based on a mistaken assumption—hence the obligation was uncertain from the start.
This is similar to the first riddle: uncertainty removes liability.
3
Analyze the timing in riddle 5
A disbeliever embraced Islam after noon and then intended a nafl fast. But for nafl, intention must be made before noon (for a Muslim). Since he was not Muslim before noon, his intention after noon is invalid.
Contrast: if he embraced before noon, he could intend nafl then.

When Does a Qarin Not Require Dam?
Riddle about qarin without dam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the exception to the dam requirement for a qarin and apply the underlying principle to similar cases.

Core Idea
Entering ihram before its proper time can waive the dam for qiran.

This riddle tests the mufti's knowledge of the fine points of Hajj. Normally, a qarin must offer a dam. But the riddle presents a scenario where the qarin has no dam. How is this possible? The answer lies in the timing of ihram.

Guided Path
1
Identify the norm
Recall that a qarin (one who combines Hajj and Umrah in one ihram) is generally required to offer a dam (sacrificial animal). This is the default ruling.

This lesson presents several riddles from the Book of Oaths where a mufti can advise on hiyal. These are not tricks to deceive but lawful interpretations that keep the oath technically unbroken. Understanding them is essential for issuing fatwas that help believers avoid sin without lying.

Identify the oath condition

Analyze the words: what is the precise referent? 'Leave this water' – which water? 'Untie' – does dissolving count? 'Wear silk' – does having it put on count? List the key terms.

Commentary
This section from the Book of Oaths presents legal stratagems (hiyal) to avoid violating oaths without lying or sin. The first riddle: a man swears 'if you leave this water' and the woman can leave without breaking the oath if the water has flowed away (so she is not leaving the same water). Second: a bag with sugar or salt can have its contents removed by dissolving, thus not untying or cutting. Third: a woman who removes silk clothes can avoid the husband's oath if he puts them on her and then has intercourse. Fourth: a face veil can be avoided by having intercourse without it. Fifth: a man who swore not to have intercourse with anyone else can intend intercourse with his foot (i.e., trivial contact) and be truthful. Sixth: three wives and two garments can wear them in rotation to fulfill the condition. These stratagems are designed to keep the oath technically unbroken while avoiding the original intent.

Key Terms
حيلة (ḥīlah): A permissible legal stratagem to achieve a licit result without violating a ruling
حنث (ḥinth): Breaking an oath, incurring expiation
تعليق (ta'liq): The condition on which the oath is suspended (e.g., 'if you do X, then Y')
