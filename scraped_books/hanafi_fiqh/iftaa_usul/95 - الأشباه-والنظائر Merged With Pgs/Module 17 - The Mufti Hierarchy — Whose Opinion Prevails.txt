The Effect of a Judge's Death or Dismissal on His Deputy: A Study in Fatwa Evolution
Discussion of whether deputies are removed when the judge dies or is dismissed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the factors that caused the Hanafi fatwa on the deputy's removal to change and apply the principle of changing custom (taghayyur al-'urf) to similar fatwa cases.

Core Idea
The deputy's status shifted from being the sultan's man to the judge's man, changing the ruling on his removal by the judge's death or dismissal.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair examines a dispute among Hanafi scholars: when a judge dies or is dismissed, does his deputy automatically lose his position? The answer changed over time because the understanding of the deputy's role changed. This lesson analyzes the dispute and the rationale behind the evolving fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
Multiple Hanafi authorities differ on whether the deputy (na'ib) is removed (in'azal) by the judge's death or dismissal. The passage surveys opinions from Khizanat al-Fatawa, al-Muhit, al-Bazzaziyya, and others.
The dispute hinges on whether the deputy is considered a deputy of the sultan or of the judge himself.
2
Analyze the earlier position
Many early Hanafi scholars (e.g., in al-Muhit, al-Bazzaziyya) held that the deputy is not removed by the judge's dismissal because he is a deputy of the sultan. Therefore, only the sultan's death or dismissal can remove him.
This reflects a time when judges simply administered the sultan's courts, and deputies were seen as state appointees.
3
Analyze the later position
Later authorities, like Ibn al-Ghars, noted that custom had changed. By the author's time, deputies were regarded as personal deputies of the judge. Hence, the deputy is removed both by the judge's death and dismissal, analogous to an agent (wakil) with his principal (muwakkil).
This shift is a classic example of 'taghayyur al-'urf' (change of custom) altering a fatwa.
4
Determine the operative fatwa
The passage concludes that the fatwa is on the later position: the deputy is removed by the judge's death or dismissal. This is the mufta bihi ruling in the author's time.
Al-Bazzazi's statement that 'the fatwa is that he is not removed by dismissal' is explained as referring to the old custom, but it is no longer applicable.
5
Apply the methodology to contemporary issues
This case teaches muftis to assess whether the underlying custom or legal framework has changed before applying a ruling. The deputy's status depends on the actual practice of appointments.
This aligns with the Hanafi maxim al-‘ādah muḥakkamah (custom is authoritative).

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed discussion among Hanafi scholars about whether a judge's deputy (na'ib) is automatically removed when the judge dies or is dismissed. There are multiple opinions: some say the deputy is removed upon the judge's death but not upon dismissal, others say the opposite, and some differentiate between the caliph and lesser officials. The relied-upon position (fatwa) evolved: in earlier times, deputies were considered deputies of the sultan, so they were not removed by the judge's dismissal. However, in the author's time, deputies are considered deputies of the judge in every respect, so they are removed by the judge's dismissal and death. The passage also contrasts this with the Shafi'i and Hanbali views. The final note from Waqf al-Quniya states that the deputy's appointment remains despite the judge's death or dismissal, but the context suggests this is a different scenario (endowment custodian).

النائب (al-na'ib): The deputy of a judge, appointed to carry out judicial functions.

A deputy judge is dismissed upon the qadi's dismissal or death in modern Hanafi fatwa due to custom.

This lesson examines a nuanced issue in judicial appointments: whether a deputy judge (na'ib) loses his position when the appointing qadi is removed or dies. The Hanafi school contains multiple views, and the operative ruling shifts as custom changes. You will learn to navigate these views and understand how the fatwa hierarchy operates in a living legal tradition.

Identify the Initial Ruling

Commentary

The text then cites multiple authorities: The view in al-Muhit aligns with the initial ruling. Al-Bazzaziyya discusses the caliph's death versus the qadi's death, emphasizing that the fatwa is that the deputy is not dismissed by the qadi's dismissal because he is the sultan's deputy. Al-Imadiyya and other works note that if the caliph dies, judges and workers are not dismissed, and similarly, a successor appointed by the qadi remains even after the qadi's death or dismissal. The author then synthesizes (tahrir) the differences: although the fatwa is non-dismissal upon dismissal, the reasoning (being deputy of sultan) implies that in the author's time, deputies are considered deputies of the qadi in every respect, like an agent (wakil) with the principal. The passage also notes that the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools treat the deputy as a deputy of the chief qadi, while the Hanafi school views him as a deputy of the sultan. The Tatarkhaniyya confirms that the qadi is merely a messenger of the sultan in appointing deputies, reinforcing that the deputy's authority stems from the sultan, not the qadi.

This passage discusses which confessions (iqrar) require repetition in Hanafi law. The question asks for confessions that must be repeated. The answer states: confession of zina (unlawful sexual intercourse) and confession of debt. However, the author notes that this ruling is not from Zahir al-Riwaya (the authoritative transmitted core of the school) but is mentioned by Ibn al-Shihnah. The latter part (confession of debt) is considered 'among the strangest' and apparently there is no reliable transmission for it. Thus, the ruling is weakened. This highlights the hierarchy of transmissions within the Hanafi school: Zahir al-Riwaya takes precedence over nawadir (rare reports).

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwaya): The six authoritative compilations of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani
نوادر (Nawadir): Rare transmissions not part of Zahir al-Riwaya
الإقرار (Iqrar): Legal confession or admission
