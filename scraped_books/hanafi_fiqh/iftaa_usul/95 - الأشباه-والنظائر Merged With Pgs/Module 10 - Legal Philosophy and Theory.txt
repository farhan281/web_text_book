The Five Stages of Intention and Their Legal Effects
Taxonomy of intention stages and their legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five stages of intention (qasd) and determine which stages incur moral responsibility (sin/reward) according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Only firm resolve (azm) incurs sin for a sinful intention; passing thoughts are forgiven.

The Hanafi legal tradition carefully distinguishes between fleeting thoughts and deliberate resolve. This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents a five-stage taxonomy of intention, clarifying when a thought becomes actionable in Islamic law. Understanding these stages is essential for muftis to correctly apply rulings on intention, particularly in matters of sin, reward, and apostasy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five stages
Read the passage and list the five stages in order: hajis, khatir, hadith al-nafs, hamm, azm. Note the brief definitions provided.
These stages progress from involuntary thought to firm determination.
2
Analyze the ruling for each stage in sinful intentions
For each stage, determine whether the person is held accountable (sin) or not. The passage states that hajis, khatir, and hadith al-nafs are not sinful; hamm is not written as a sin; azm incurs sin for the resolve itself.
The authentic hadith ('thoughts of the heart are forgiven') is the basis for the first three.
3
Examine the special case of hamm
Note the nuance: hamm for a good deed is written as a good deed; hamm for a sin is not written, but if abandoned for Allah, it becomes a good deed. The most correct view is that only the action is recorded.
This encourages good intentions and forgiveness for not acting on sin.
4
Consider the dispute over azm
Identify that some scholars consider azm as part of the 'lifted' hamm, but the verifiers (muhaqqiqun) hold that azm is actionable. The passage cites Al-Bazzaziyya to clarify that resolve on sin incurs sin of resolve, except for apostasy where resolve completes the act.
The practical consequence: a mufti must differentiate between mere hamm and firm azm.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed taxonomy of the stages of thought (khatirat) and their legal and moral consequences. The five stages are: (1) Hajis – initial thought cast into the mind; (2) Khatir – the thought passes through; (3) Hadith al-nafs – hesitation; (4) Hamm – intention to act; (5) Azm – firm resolve. The first three (hajis, khatir, hadith al-nafs) are not punishable because they are involuntary or weak; the authentic hadith states that Allah forgives the thoughts of the heart until one acts or speaks. For good deeds, no reward is written for these early stages due to lack of deliberate intent. Hamm is treated specially: for good deeds, it is recorded as a good deed; for sins, it is not recorded as a sin, but if one abandons the sin for Allah, it becomes a good deed. Azm (firm resolve) is generally actionable: if one resolves to commit a sin, he incurs sin for the resolve itself, not the act unless executed. An exception is apostasy, where resolve alone completes the act.

Key Terms
الهاجس (al-hajis): The first fleeting thought that enters the mind without choice.
العزم (al-azm): Firm resolve and determination to act; incurs accountability for the resolve itself.
الخاطر (al-khatir): The thought flowing within the mind after the initial hajis.
حديث النفس (hadith al-nafs): The hesitation or discussion with oneself whether to act on a thought.
الهم (al-hamm): The intention or preponderance of the will to act; one is inclined to do it.

The Basic Rule in Sexual Matters is Prohibition (al-Asl fi al-Bud' al-Hazr)
A maxim clarifying the default prohibition of sexual intercourse and the acceptance of a single trustworthy report, with a note distinguishing legal ruling from piety.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the default for sexual intercourse is prohibition, and apply the exception when a trustworthy report (khabar al-wahid) permits it, while distinguishing between binding rulings and recommended piety (wara').

Core Idea
The default for any sexual relationship is prohibition; only a trustworthy report can permit it, and caution beyond that is piety, not law.

In this lesson, we study a foundational maxim of Hanafi fatwa that governs the permissibility of sexual relations. The passage from Ibn Nujaym’s al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir clarifies that the original state of intercourse is prohibition, and that a single trustworthy report can lift that prohibition. However, the passage also distinguishes between a binding legal ruling and the recommended piety (wara') of refraining when doubts remain. This distinction is crucial for the mufti who issues fatwas on such sensitive matters.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Core Maxim
State the rule clearly in Arabic: الأصل في البضع الحظر (al-asl fi al-bud' al-hazr). This is the starting point for any fatwa on sexual matters.
This is a universal qawida in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the Exception
Recognize that a khabar al-wahid (report of one trustworthy person) can overturn the prohibition. The passage explicitly says 'يقبل في حله خبر الواحد'.
The reporter must be 'adl thiqah (just and trustworthy).
3
Recognize the Limits of the Exception
The exception is not open-ended; it applies only when the report is credible. The one receiving it must think her truthful, as in the example of the slave-girl sent as a gift.
Subjective satisfaction of the hearer is relevant.
4
Distinguish Hukm from Wara'
Understand that additional caution (like avoiding all slave-girls from non-Muslim lands) is a matter of piety (ورع), not a binding ruling. The passage states 'ورع لا حكم لازم'.
A mufti must not present wara' as an obligation.
5
Apply to the Dispute
Compare the Hanafi position (permissibility based on possession or trustworthy report) with the Shafi'i view (prohibition unless proper division). The Hanafi response is that the Shafi'i view is wara', not a binding fatwa.
The thamarah al-khilaf is that Hanafi muftis permit, Shafi'i muftis prohibit.

Commentary
This passage addresses the fundamental Hanafi principle regarding sexual intercourse: the basic rule is prohibition, meaning one cannot assume permissibility. However, an exception is made for the report of a single trustworthy person: if a trustworthy individual informs that a woman is permissible, his report is accepted. Examples include buying a slave-girl where a witness says the seller authorized the sale, or a slave-girl sent as a gift. The author then discusses a case where an agent buys a slave-girl with a description and dies before delivery, stating that the original rule of prohibition applies, and one should refer to the heir. Shakk, Zann, Wahm, and Ghalabat al-Zann in Hanafi Fiqh
Terminological clarification of doubt, presumption, and preponderant belief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shakk, zann, wahm, and ghalabat al-zann, and apply the Hanafi principle that ghalabat al-zann is treated as certainty in legal rulings.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, ghalabat al-zann is legally equivalent to certainty, while mere zann is treated as doubt.

This passage clarifies the technical meanings of key terms: shakk (doubt), zann (presumption), wahm (misgiving), and ghalabat al-zann (overwhelming presumption). Understanding these distinctions is crucial for a mufti when issuing fatwas based on predominant belief.

Guided Path
1
Define the four terms
Read the definitions: shakk is equal probability, zann is preponderant side (usually correctness), wahm is preponderant error, ghalabat al-zann is the preponderant side accepted by the heart.
Note that in Hanafi terminology, zann often includes shakk.
2
Identify the Hanafi peculiarity
Understand that Hanafi jurists use zann to mean hesitation (taraddud) between existence and non-existence, regardless of equality or preponderance. This differs from the linguists' definition.
This is why saying 'in my zann' does not create liability.
3
Distinguish ghalabat al-zann
Ghalabat al-zann (also called akbar al-ra'y) is a strong presumption that the heart accepts and is treated as certainty (mulhaq bi al-yaqin) for building legal rulings.
It is the operative level of presumption for fatwa.
4
Apply the principle to examples
Work through the examples: (a) nullifiers of wudu: predominant belief of passing wind breaks wudu; (b) divorce: mere supposition does not effect divorce, but predominant belief does.
These illustrate the legal consequence of ghalabat al-zann.
5
Summarize the mufti's approach
A mufti must assess whether doubt is equal (shakk) or one side preponderates. Only when it becomes ghalabat al-zann does he act upon it as if certain.
This is the practical takeaway for ifta.

Commentary
This passage is a terminological clarification (fā'ida) of the key concepts shakk, zann, wahm, and ghalabat al-zann. The author explains that shakk is doubt with equal probability, zann is when one side is preponderant (usually of correctness), and wahm is when the side of error is preponderant. The terms akbar al-ra'y and ghalabat al-zann refer to the preponderant side that is accepted by the heart, and this is what the Hanafi jurists consider legally effective. Importantly, the Hanafi usage of zann is closer to shakk because it includes hesitation; thus, a statement made 'in my zann' is not binding. However, ghalabat al-zann (overwhelming presumption) is treated as certainty for legal purposes. Examples: in wudu, doubt about passing wind: if his predominant belief is that he did pass wind, wudu is broken; in divorce, if he only supposes divorce occurred, it does not take effect, but if he has strong presumption, it does.

Abu Hanifa's Expansion in Worship
Summary of Abu Hanifa's lenient rulings in worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key rulings where Abu Hanifa adopted leniency, explain the Quranic and principle-based evidence, and apply the maxim 'al-mashaqqah tajlib al-taysir' to new cases.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa's expansion in worship is rooted in the principle of removing hardship, not disregard for obligation.

This lesson explores a classic passage from Ibn Nujaym's 'Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir' that encapsulates Imam Abu Hanifa's approach to worship: prioritizing ease and removing hardship (raf' al-haraj). The passage lists several rulings—from wudu to prayer—where Abu Hanifa adopted the easier position, each based on a deep understanding of Quranic evidence and the broader objectives of Shari'ah. Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, a quarter of a garment is legally equivalent to the whole — it triggers the same ruling.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir illustrates a key legal principle: the quarter (rub') has the ruling of the whole (kul). Distinction Between Ahl al-Waqf and Mawquf Alayh in Beneficiary Succession

A named beneficiary (mawquf alayh) is not necessarily ahl al-waqf; only those with vested entitlement can transmit their share.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed Hanafi fatwa on waqf share distribution when multiple beneficiaries die. The author first states the general rule: if a beneficiary dies leaving children, the share goes to them according to prescribed shares (for the male twice the female); if no descendants, it goes to the nearest at the same level. He then applies this to a specific case: Abd al-Qadir dies, leaving children Ali, Amr, Latifa, and also grandchildren from a predeceased son Muhammad. The author's ruling is that only the direct children (Ali, Amr, Latifa) inherit the share, with Ali taking 2/5, Amr 2/5, Latifa 1/5. Then later deaths: Amr dies without children, his share goes to siblings; Latifa dies leaving a daughter Fatima; Ali dies leaving a daughter Zaynab; Fatima dies without children. The author calculates final shares: Abd al-Rahman (son of predeceased Muhammad) gets 22/60, Malaka (daughter) 11/60, Zaynab 27/60. He discusses three possible legal bases for including the grandchildren: (1) the waqif's general intent not to deprive anyone – rejected because intent must be indicated by wording; (2) treating each level separately – possible but contrary to apparent meaning; (3) the waqif's explicit statement that children of deceased beneficiaries take their place – strong but requires the deceased to have been a beneficiary. The author then analyzes the distinction between 'ahl al-waqf' (eligible to receive) and 'mawquf alayh' (specifically named beneficiary). A person can be a named beneficiary without being 'ahl al-waqf' until conditions are met. Thus, Muhammad (who died before his father Abd al-Qadir) was never 'ahl al-waqf' because he never received entitlement; therefore his children do not automatically substitute. The author refutes the counterargument by parsing the waqif's wording: 'before his entitlement to something' – the deceased might have been entitled to something previously, so the child only substitutes for that pending portion, not the entire share. The passage illustrates the Hanafi methodology of strict textual interpretation in waqf conditions.

أهل الوقف (ahl al-waqf): Those eligible to receive from the waqf and whose share can pass to their children upon death before receipt
الموقوف عليه (al-mawquf alayh): A specifically named beneficiary who is entitled to receive the waqf at a given time

Only faith and marriage continue from Adam to Paradise; all other acts cease.

This lesson presents a concise observation from the Hanafi legal tradition that highlights the unique status of marriage. Unlike salah, fasting, and other rituals, marriage (nikah) is an act of worship that persists even in Paradise. Understanding this maxim helps appreciate marriage's elevated position in Islam.

The Qa'ida: Endowments from Treasury Retain Treasury Rulings
Detailed rules distinguishing waqf from treasury vs. private ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida 'ما كان أصله من بيت المال فهو على حكم بيت المال' to distinguish between endowments from treasury and private endowments, and identify the mufta bihi ruling for each.

Core Idea
Endowments from treasury retain treasury rules; conditions of the ruler are not binding.

This passage from al-Asyuti's al-Yanbu' presents a key distinction in Hanafi waqf law: endowments funded by the public treasury (bayt al-mal) follow treasury legal rules, not the conditions of the ruler who established them. This principle has wide implications for fatwa, especially regarding eligibility of beneficiaries and the non-binding nature of stipulations. Understanding this qawida prevents common errors in contemporary practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the origin of the endowment
Determine whether the endowment's funds originated from the public treasury (bayt al-mal) or from private property of the waqif. This is the first question in any fatwa about a ruler's endowment.
If the land was purchased by the ruler from the treasury and then endowed, it may still be considered treasury origin unless a formal transfer occurred.
2
Apply the governing qawida
If the origin is from bayt al-mal, apply the qawida: 'ما كان أصله من بيت المال فهو على حكم بيت المال' (What has its origin from the treasury remains subject to the ruling of the treasury). If private, apply 'الوقوف على حسب ما يشرطه الواقف'.
The qawida is a dabt (restrictive sub-maxim) of the kubra qawida 'الأصل بقاء ما كان على ما كان'.
3
Determine entitlement for treasury endowments
Only those possessing sifat al-istihqaq (quality of entitlement) from the treasury—such as scholars, students, and Ahl al-Sunnah Sufis—may benefit from the endowment, regardless of what the ruler stipulated. Conditions are not binding.
Even if the nazir appoints a non-eligible person, that person cannot lawfully take from the endowment.
4
Handle scarcity and prioritization
If funds are insufficient for all entitled, first give preference to those with sifat al-istihqaq, then the more needy, then the older. In private endowments, follow waqif's order; if none, divide equally.
The order: teacher (mudarris) > mu'adhdhin > imam > custodian (qayyim).
5
Recognize the exception: transferred property
If the sultan sold treasury land to a private person and that person then endowed it, the endowment is treated as private. The conditions of the waqif (the buyer) must be observed because a valid transfer (nāqil) occurred.
This exception prevents misapplication of the qawida to lands that have left treasury ownership.

The Rule of Descriptor Return to the Last Antecedent in Waqf (Hanafi vs Shafi'i)
Fatwa interpreting whether a description applies to all beneficiaries or only the last

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of descriptor return (al-wasf ba'd al-jumal) to interpret a waqf clause and distinguish the Shafi'i position for 'waw' vs 'thumma'.

Core Idea
When a descriptor follows a series with 'thumma', Hanafis return it to the last; Shafi'is agree for 'thumma' but differ for 'waw'.

A waqf deed specified beneficiaries in a sequence, ending with 'from males specifically'. The crucial interpretative question: does this restriction apply only to the last category, or to all previous ones? The Hanafi rule, grounded in Quranic usage and linguistic principle, returns the descriptor to the last antecedent. This lesson unpacks that rule, its proof, and the Shafi'i distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the waqf sequence and the disputed phrase
List the hierarchical beneficiaries in the waqf: (1) a named person, (2) his children, (3) their children, (4) their children's children, (5) offspring, descendants, progeny. The disputed phrase 'min al-dhukuri khassatan' follows the last category.
The sequence uses 'thumma' (then) between each category.
2
Parse the grammatical structure
The antecedents (categories) are coordinated with 'thumma', which indicates order and prevents the last from being part of a single series with the others. The descriptor is a separate clause after the series.
In Arabic grammar, 'thumma' creates a sequence; the descriptor after a sequence typically refers to the nearest antecedent.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
The Hanafi principle: 'al-wasf ba'd al-jumal yarji'u ila al-akhir' – a descriptor after coordinated terms returns to the last one. Apply this to the waqf: the restriction 'from males' applies only to the last category (offspring, etc.), not to the earlier ones.
This rule is well-established in Hanafi usul, especially in waqf and marriage prohibitions.
4
Examine the Quranic parallel (dalil)
Quran 4:23 lists women prohibited for marriage, then says 'except those you have entered with them'. Hanafis interpret this descriptor as returning to 'your stepdaughters' (the last category) only, not to all previously mentioned women. This is a key proof.
The verse is: 'And your wives' mothers and your stepdaughters... from your women whom you have entered with'. The Hanafi interpretation restricts the exception to stepdaughters.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i view and note the consensus on 'thumma'
Imam al-Isnawi in al-Tamhid reports that Shafi'is rule: when the conjunction is 'waw', the descriptor returns to all antecedents. But when it is 'thumma', there is consensus that it returns to the last. Thus, both schools agree on this case, though some Shafi'is and Hanafis differed.
The practical difference arises only when the conjunction is 'waw', not in this waqf.

The Three Methods of Establishing Legal Rulings (al-Iqtisar, al-Inqilab, al-Istinad)
Defines three methods for establishing legal rulings with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish among the three methods of establishing legal rulings (iqtisar, inqilab, istinad) and apply them correctly to Hanafi fiqh scenarios.

Core Idea
Legal rulings are established through three temporal structures: direct creation, conditional transformation, and retrospective assignment.

Understanding how a ruling is temporally anchored is crucial for accurate fatwa. The Hanafi school recognizes that not all rulings arise at the moment of the act. This lesson, drawn from al-Mustasfa via al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair, explains three key methods: iqtisar (direct), inqilab (transformative upon condition), and istinad (retrospective). Each method has distinct applications in ibadat and muamalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Iqtisar
Al-Iqtisar is the creation of a ruling at the moment the act is performed. The cause is the act itself. For example, uttering 'You are divorced' or 'You are free' immediately creates the ruling of divorce or emancipation. This is the default method.
Iqtisar applies to most non-suspended legal acts.
2
Identify al-Inqilab
Al-Inqilab occurs when a condition transforms a non-cause into a cause. In suspended (mu'allaq) acts like conditional divorce, the condition itself becomes the cause upon fulfillment, and the ruling takes effect at that moment.
The condition is not the cause initially; it becomes the cause after occurrence.
3
Identify al-Istinad
Al-Istinad assigns a ruling established in the present back to the time of the cause. Examples: a guarantor owns the debt from the time of the guarantee (not payment), zakat becomes wajib at year's end but is owed from when nisab was possessed, and the purity of a mustahada/mutayammim is nullified retroactively when the impediment ceases.
Istinad is said to oscillate between tabiyin (clarification) and iqtisar.
4
Apply to New Cases
Given a new Hanafi scenario, determine which method is operative. Ask: Is the ruling created at the moment of the act? Does it depend on a condition that transforms a non-cause? Or is it a present ruling that refers back to an earlier cause?
The correct classification affects legal consequences such as ownership, obligation timing, and purity status.
5
Note the Fourth Method
The passage states there are four ways but only describes three. The fourth, often identified as al-Tabyin (clarification), is not elaborated here but is part of the full taxonomy.
This omission is acceptable for the purpose of this lesson.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of universal rules; 'ilm is the skill of applying them to specific cases.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair clarifies a critical distinction for any qadi or mufti. While fiqh gives you the principles, 'ilm al-qada or 'ilm al-fatwa is the practical ability to apply them correctly to real-life scenarios. The story of Asad ibn al-Furat illustrates the danger of missing this application.

Extend the distinction to fatwa
The same difference exists between fiqh al-fatwa and 'ilm al-fatwa. A mufti must move from knowing the general rulings to applying them to actual queries.

Knowledge Will Not Be Questioned on Judgment Day
Legal benefit (fa'idah) establishing the exception of knowledge from Judgment Day questioning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal maxim that seeking knowledge is not questioned on Judgment Day, cite its Quranic proof, and apply it to scenarios in mufti training.

Core Idea
Knowledge is exempt from questioning because Allah commanded seeking more — what He commands He does not punish.

This brief but profound fa'idah from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir highlights the unique status of knowledge in Islam. It asserts that on the Day of Judgment, Allah will question human beings about everything except knowledge. The reason is that Allah Himself commanded His Prophet to seek an increase in knowledge (Quran 20:114). If He commanded seeking it, He would not then hold one accountable for it. For the mufti, this principle underscores the mercy and reward inherent in the pursuit of religious knowledge, and it serves as a comforting reminder that sincere efforts to learn are not a source of liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The passage claims that everything will be questioned on Judgment Day except knowledge. Pinpoint this as the core legal maxim.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
The author cites Quran 20:114: 'And say, my Lord, increase me in knowledge.' This verse is the proof that knowledge is a commanded good, and thus not subject to accountability.
The verse is a direct command from Allah to the Prophet, indicating that seeking knowledge is praiseworthy.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (wajh al-dalalah)
The reasoning is that if Allah commands seeking knowledge, it would be contradictory to punish for having it. Hence, knowledge is an exception.
This is a logical inference from the text: the command implies a reward, not a punishment.
4
Note the scope of the maxim
This maxim applies to religious knowledge ('ilm) sought for Allah's sake. It may not extend to knowledge pursued with evil intent or worldly knowledge used for harm.
The Hanafi tradition emphasizes that knowledge for Allah is a fard 'ayn to the extent needed.
5
Apply to a mufti's context
A mufti who seeks knowledge to issue fatwas correctly incurs no sin for the knowledge itself. However, he is accountable for how he applies it (e.g., negligence).
This maxim protects the act of learning, not necessarily the application.

Preference of Slave's Right over Allah's Right: The Hanafi Qawida and Its Exception
Explaining a key Hanafi qawida with one exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'preference of the slave's right' to conflicting rights cases and identify the exception in ihram.

Core Idea
The slave's right is preferred over Allah's right due to his need, except in ihram with a hunted animal.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi maxim that guides muftis when the rights of individuals (ḥuqūq al-ʻibād) conflict with the rights of Allah (ḥuqūq Allāh). Understanding this rule is essential for issuing balanced fatwas that respect both the servant's need and divine commands. The source text from al-Ashbāh wa al-Naẓāʼir states the rule and its one exception, along with a subtle alternative interpretation.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim clearly
First, internalize the Arabic text of the qawida: 'إذا اجتمع حق العبد وحق الله قدّم حق العبد'. Understand its literal meaning and the rationale: because the slave is in need, while Allah is self-sufficient.
Memorize this maxim; it is a key tool for fatwa.
2
Identify the scope of application
Consider which areas of fiqh involve conflicts between huquq al-'ibad and huquq Allah. For example, financial debts (slave's right) vs. missed prayers (Allah's right), or qisas vs. hadd punishments.
The rule applies broadly across ibadat, mu'amalat, and jinayat.
3
Analyze the exception (istithna)
The text gives one exception: a person in ihram who owns a hunted animal must release it as a right of Allah, even though he owns it (slave's right). Understand why ihram elevates Allah's right in this case.
This exception is based on specific Quranic and sunnah prohibitions during ihram.
4
Examine the alternative view
Some scholars say this case is not preference but combining both rights: the animal is released in a way that it is not lost. Consider how this might affect fatwa in marginal cases.
The majority view is the clear preference rule; the alternative is a minority position.

Imam Abu Hanifa's Three Analogies: A Lesson in Rational Theology
Anecdote of Abu Hanifa answering three theological questions as a youth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how Imam Abu Hanifa used analogical reasoning from common experience to defend the transcendence of Allah (tanzih).

Core Idea
Everyday analogies prove God is beyond time, space, and anthropomorphic activity.

This anecdote from Imam Abu Hanifa’s youth is not merely a colourful story; it is a masterclass in using rational argumentation within the boundaries of Islamic creed. The young Imam, despite his age, employed analogies from common experience to answer sophisticated theological questions that had stumped other scholars. His method—known as qiyas in the broader sense—is a fundamental tool of Hanafi usul, and this lesson will dissect each of his three arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three questions
The Byzantine messenger asked: (1) What existed before Allah? (2) In which direction is Allah's face? (3) With what does Allah occupy Himself? Each question implies an anthropomorphic or temporal limitation.
These questions are traps for those who attribute literal human-like qualities to Allah.
2
For each question, find the everyday analogy
Abu Hanifa uses: (1) the concept of 'one' in numbers—nothing before one; (2) the light of a lamp—equally in all directions; (3) the difference between a person on a pulpit and on the ground—status depends on position, but Allah is above all.
The analogies are drawn from basic arithmetic, physics, and social hierarchy.
3
Explain how each analogy proves tanzih
(1) Just as 'one' has no predecessor, the Real One (Allah) is before all; (2) light from a lamp has no single direction, so Allah's 'face' (essence) transcends direction; (3) a person's elevation is relative, but Allah is exalted above all comparisons—He is ever engaged in creation, not limited.
Each analogy refutes anthropomorphism (tashbih) and affirms transcendence (tanzih).
4
Note the underlying rational principle
Abu Hanifa uses qiyas (analogical reasoning) from the known (mundane) to the unknown (divine). This is a legitimate tool in Hanafi usul for defending creedal points, provided the analogy does not lead to error.
This method is distinct from qiyas in fiqh; here it is used for istidlal (rational inference).
5
Summarise the overall approach
Imam Abu Hanifa engages the questioner on his own terms, using universally accepted truths (like arithmetic and physics) to lead him to acknowledge Allah's uniqueness. No sectarian bias is needed—sound reason suffices.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on using ‘aql (reason) in harmony with naql (revelation).
