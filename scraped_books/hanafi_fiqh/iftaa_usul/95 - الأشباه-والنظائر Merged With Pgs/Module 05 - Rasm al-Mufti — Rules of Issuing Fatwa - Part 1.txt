الاجتهاد لا ينقض بمثله: The Rule of Finality of Ijtihad
Introducing a rule on finality of ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that ijtihad is not overturned by similar ijtihad and apply it to distinguish between binding judicial rulings and revisable fatwas.

Core Idea
A judge's valid ijtihad is final and cannot be undone by another similar ijtihad.

This lesson introduces a foundational rule from the Hanafi legal maxims: 'الاجتهاد لا ينقض بمثله' (Ijtihad is not overturned by similar ijtihad). This rule ensures stability in judicial rulings. We will unpack the rule, its scope, its exceptions (four instances), and its implications for judges, muftis, and notaries.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the maxim and its literal meaning: ijtihad cannot be overturned by similar ijtihad.
2
Identify the legal scope
The rule applies to a judge's ruling based on his ijtihad. It does not apply to fatwas or personal opinions.
3
List the exceptions
The text mentions four exceptions regarding testimony rejection (if a judge rejects a witness, another cannot accept except in four cases). Also consider exceptions like when a judge rules contrary to his madhhab or a waqf condition.
4
Distinguish between judge and mufti
A judge's verdict binds the parties; a mufti can change his ijtihad for future cases. This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
5
Apply to cases
Use the anecdote of Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani and the examples of weak opinions or forgotten rulings to see how the rule operates.

This passage introduces a rule from the second type of rules: 'الاجتهاد لا ينقض بمثله' (Ijtihad is not overturned by similar ijtihad). It means that a ruling given by a qualified judge based on his ijtihad cannot be nullified by another judge's ijtihad that differs. The text details several applications: if a judge rejects a witness's testimony, another judge cannot accept it except in four exceptions. If a judge's ijtihad changes after a ruling, the previous ruling remains. It also covers the difference between 'judging with validity' and 'judging according to its requirement', the anecdote of Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani, rulings based on weak opinions or abandoned narrations, and intentional or forgetful contravention of one's madhhab. Judging contrary to a waqf condition is equated to judging contrary to nass. Finally, a judge's actions and orders are valid only if they conform to Sharia; otherwise they are rejected.

Intention in Zakat and Forcible Collection
Ruling on the necessity of intention in zakat and the weakness of forcible collection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that zakat requires intention and evaluate the minority view that forcible taking suffices, citing the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Core Idea
Zakat without intention is invalid; forcible taking by the imam does not discharge the obligation.

The Hanafi school affirms that intention (niyyah) is a condition for the validity of zakat, whether paid voluntarily or under compulsion. A minority view within the school, attributed to al-Qadi al-Asbijabi, held that the imam's forcible taking could substitute for the payer's intent. This lesson examines the dispute, the Hanafi response, and the operative fatwa position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Base Rule
State the fundamental ruling: zakat payment is not valid except with intention.
This is the starting point of the passage.
2
Recognize the Contested Scenario
What happens if a person refuses to pay zakat? The passage considers the imam forcibly taking the wealth.
This is the factual scenario disputed by al-Asbijabi.
3
Examine the Opposing View
Al-Qadi al-Asbijabi held that forcible taking suffices because the imam's authority stands in place of the owner's choice.
Understand his reasoning to see why it is rejected.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The school dismisses al-Asbijabi's view as weak. Instead, the imam should imprison the refuser to compel voluntary payment, not take the wealth by force.
The response reinforces the centrality of intention.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon position is that forcible taking does not fulfill the zakat obligation; the person must still pay with intention.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa.

Niyyah in Prayer: Condition or Not?
Ruling on intention as a condition for prayer validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that niyyah is not a condition for prayer's validity and distinguish the contextual exception for determining qibla.

Core Idea
Intention is not required for a valid prayer, but it determines qibla in the desert and guarantees reward.

In Hanafi fiqh, intention (niyyah) is essential for acts of worship, but its role can be nuanced. This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair clarifies that niyyah is not a condition for the validity of prayer itself, contrary to a view attributed to al-Jurjani. Instead, the need for intention arises contextually—to identify the qibla in open spaces—while in a mosque the mihrab suffices. Moreover, reward is linked to the sincerity of intention, not the formal correctness of the act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling
What does the passage say about niyyah as a condition for prayer's validity? The correct view is it is not a condition.
2
Understand the contextual exception
When is intention still needed? When praying in the desert without a mihrab, intention helps determine the qibla.
In a mosque with a mihrab, intention is not needed for validity.
3
Note the ruling on covering awrah
Covering the awrah is not a condition for validity, with no disagreement.
4
Derive the principle of reward
Reward is for intention, even if the act is invalid due to an excusable mistake.
Example: praying without ablution thinking one is pure.

This passage is a ruling on the role of intention (niyyah) in prayer. It begins by noting that al-Jurjani required intention for the prayer's validity, but the established Hanafi position (as in al-Mabsut and al-Binayah) is that intention is not a condition for validity. The difference is reconciled by context: for someone praying in the open desert where the qibla is unclear, intention is needed to determine the direction; for someone praying in a mosque facing a mihrab, intention is not required because the mihrab already indicates qibla. The passage then states that covering the 'awrah is not a condition for prayer's validity, with no disagreement recorded. Finally, it clarifies that reward depends on intention, not on the formal validity of the act. Even if a prayer is invalid due to an excuse, like forgetting one's lack of ablution, one is still rewarded for the sincere intention.

Enumeration of Sunan Rawatib and Mustahabb Prayers
Enumerates the numbers of confirmed and recommended prayers in the Hanafi school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the twelve rak'ats of sunan rawatib and distinguish them from mustahabb prayers, and state the number of rak'ats for Taraweeh and additional prayers.

Core Idea
The confirmed sunnah prayers are exactly twelve rak'ats daily; other voluntary prayers are mustahabb, not rawatib.

The Hanafi school precisely enumerates the confirmed sunnah prayers (sunan rawatib) that accompany the five daily prayers. This list is essential for a mufti to know when asked about the number of recommended prayers or to clarify the difference between emphasized and optional acts. The passage also clarifies the status of other prayers like Taraweeh, witr, and Eid, which have varying rulings.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the daily rawatib list
Read the Arabic text and note the twelve rak'ats: two before Fajr, four before Zuhr, two after Zuhr, two after Maghrib, two after Isha. Commit these to memory.
The total is exactly twelve – no more, no less for daily rawatib.
2
Identify the Jumu'a extension
For Jumu'a (Friday prayer), the rawatib are increased: four before and four after. That is separate from the daily count.
Jumu'a replaces Zuhr, so its rawatib are additional.
3
Note the special prayers and their numbers
Taraweeh: twenty rak'ats (ten salams). Witr: considered sunnah according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Eid and eclipse prayers: status varies (sunnah or wajib).
These are not part of the daily rawatib but are mentioned for completeness.
4
Distinguish mustahabb from rawatib
The mustahabb list includes four before Asr, four before Isha, two after the second rak'at of Zuhr, two after the second of Isha, six after Maghrib, plus Duha (4-12 rak'ats), and others. These are not confirmed rawatib but are recommended.
Mustahabb prayers are less emphasized; missing them is not sinful.
5
Understand the function of this list for fatwa
A mufti uses this enumeration to answer questions about recommended prayers, to correct misconceptions, and to distinguish between rawatib and other voluntary acts.
This is a practical reference (rasm al-mufti) for daily fatwa work.

Error in Specification: When It Harms and When It Does Not
Defining a rule about error in specification with numerous applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'Error in what specification is not required for does not harm' to new cases in worship and testimony, distinguishing between required and non-required specification.

Core Idea
Error in specification harms only when specification is a legal condition.

When a person specifies a detail in their intention and gets it wrong, the validity of the act depends on whether that detail was legally required. This lesson unpacks a core maxim from the Hanafi tradition that helps muftis and judges decide when a mistake invalidates an act and when it can be ignored. Through numerous cases from prayer, testimony, and funeral, you will learn to distinguish harmless from harmful specification errors.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its two sides
Identify the general rule: error in non-required specification does not harm; error in required specification does. Memorize the Arabic maxim and its English translation.
The maxim is a dabt (regulatory sub-maxim) that restricts the broader principle of intention.
2
Identify which specifications are required
From the furuat, list cases where specification is a condition: type of act (prayer vs fasting), specific prayer (Zuhr vs Asr), gender of deceased. Recognize that number of rak'ahs, day of the week, and color of animal are not required.
The key is the legal requirement, not the presence of specification.
3
Apply the rule to the furuat
Work through each case in the text (number of rak'ahs, imam by name, testimony color, etc.) and state the ruling. Confirm that the outcome matches the maxim.
Some cases may seem contradictory; the textual details (e.g., pointing vs. naming) resolve them.
4
Handle the istithna' (exception)
Identify the exception: when specification IS required (e.g., confusing fasting and prayer). Note that the same maxim applies in reverse: error here DOES harm.
The exception is actually the second half of the same rule, not a true exception.

The Hanafi Principle: Riya' Does Not Invalidate Obligatory Acts
Defines sincerity and rulings on riya' in worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that riya' does not invalidate obligatory acts in terms of fulfilling the obligation, and apply it to a case of prayer performed with mixed intention.

Core Idea
Riya' affects reward, not validity of obligatory acts.

This lesson clarifies a key Hanafi principle: in obligatory acts, the presence of ostentation (riya') does not nullify the act or prevent it from fulfilling the obligation. However, it does affect one's reward. This distinction between validity (sihha) and acceptance (qubul) is essential for a mufti to understand when issuing fatwas regarding worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Determine whether the act is obligatory (fard) or supererogatory (nafl). The text specifies that riya' does not affect the obligation in fara'id.
Hanafi classification: fard acts are those established by decisive proof.
2
Extract the principle from the text
From al-Bazzaziyya: 'There is no riya' in fara'id concerning the dropping of the obligation.' This is the relied-upon Hanafi position.
The principle is stated in the passage: 'لا رياء في الفرائض في حق سقوط الواجب'
3
Distinguish validity from reward
Riya' does not make the act invalid (batil) for fard; the obligation is discharged. However, it may nullify reward (thawab) or reduce it.
The text mentions: 'له ثواب أصل الصلاة دون الإحسان' - reward for essence without excellence.
4
Apply to a concrete scenario

This ruling is mufta bihi according to later Hanafi authorities.

Precedence of Obligations in Combined Intentions
Rule for mufti: which obligation prevails when two are intended

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of precedence when two obligatory acts are combined in a single intention, identifying which obligation prevails and justifying with the underlying qa'ida.

Core Idea
When two obligations are intended together, the stronger prevails; if equal, choice (fasting) or time priority (prayer) applies.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a key rule for muftis: how to adjudicate when a person intends two obligatory acts simultaneously. The rule is grounded in a hierarchy of obligation strength—some obligations are 'debt-like' (qada) and thus stronger than expiatory fasts, while zakat outweighs oath expiation. In prayer, time and order further determine which is fulfilled. Understanding this rule prevents erroneous fatwas and clarifies the mufti's method in resolving intentional ambiguity.

Rulings on intending an obligatory and a supererogatory act together

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi rulings on combining fard and nafl intentions and apply the relevant maxims to determine the mufta bihi position in intra-madhhab disputes.

Core Idea
When fard and nafl are intended together, the fard prevails but not always; varies by act and scholar.

The Hanafi school addresses the common scenario where a worshipper intends both an obligatory and a supererogatory act simultaneously. This lesson parses the key cases from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair, focusing on prayer, zakat, and hajj, and highlights the ikhtilaf between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding these rulings is essential for a mufti when issuing fatwas on combined intentions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cases
List the different scenarios mentioned: prayer (Zuhr + nafl), zakat + nafl, funeral prayer + nafl, two nafls, multiple ihrams.
Note the variation in rulings across acts.
2
Note the ikhtilaf
For each case, record the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and where relevant, Abu Hanifa.
The key dispute is whether the intention is divisible.
3
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: for prayer, whether the fard is valid or must be repeated; for hajj, number of blood penalties.
The thamarah affects both validity and compensation.
4
Identify the authority weight
Note that the text cites al-Mabsut as stating the opinion as zahir al-riwaya.
Zahir al-riwaya is the highest authority in Hanafi fatwa.

Rulings on changing intention mid-act and when intention must be made

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for changing intention mid-worship and determine the validity of a prior intention for prayer and fasting.

Core Idea
Intention can precede the act if no interrupting act occurs; a transfer of intention requires a new initiating action.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir deals with two critical aspects of intention (niyyah) in worship: the effect of changing one's intention during an act, and the permissible timing for making intention. Understanding these rules is essential for a mufti when asked about the validity of a prayer or fast when the worshipper's intention was formed earlier or altered mid-act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two distinct rulings
The passage first discusses transferring intention during worship, then the timing of intention. Distinguish these two.
The 'Benefit' section bridges the two by showing application outside worship.
2
Extract the condition for valid intention transfer
Note that mere inner intention to transfer is insufficient; a new takbir (or corresponding initiation) is required to leave the first act.
This is analogous to the need for a new takbir to start a new prayer.
3
Analyze the timing rule for prayer
Prior intention is valid as long as no act of a different genus (like speech, eating, sleeping) occurs between the intention and the start. Walking to the mosque is not considered interrupting.
The passage cites multiple authorities agreeing.
4
Distinguish real vs. legal beginning
The 'beginning' of worship can be legal (prior intention persisting) or real (concurrent intention). The best is concurrent, but prior is valid.
This distinction is essential for understanding Hanafi flexibility.
5
Apply to a modern fatwa scenario
Consider a person who intends to pray in the car, then talks on the phone before entering the mosque. The talk breaks prior intention; prayer invalid without renewal.
This shows the practical significance for muftis.

Intention and Ambiguity in Talaq: The Hanafi Ruling When an Expression Applies to Multiple Entities
Fiqh rulings on talaq with ambiguous expressions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that in ambiguous talaq expressions, the wife is not divorced unless the utterance exclusively refers to her, either by context or intention, and identify when intention overrides apparent meaning.

Core Idea
In ambiguous talaq, the wife is not divorced unless the utterance can only apply to her.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents a series of cases that illustrate a key principle of Hanafi fiqh: when a husband uses an ambiguous expression that could refer to his wife and something else not capable of divorce, his intention (niyyah) determines the outcome, and in the absence of intention, the wife is not divorced if the expression could also apply to another entity. The mufti must carefully analyze the utterance and the legal capacity of the other referent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous utterance
Determine whether the husband's words can apply to the wife and another person or thing. Look for phrases like 'one of you' or ambiguous timing terms like 'according to Sunnah'.
2
Assess the legal capacity of the other entity
Is the other entity capable of being divorced? For example, a man, an animal, a stone, or a dead person is not capable of divorce. A living woman who is not his wife (ajnabiyya) is capable of divorce in the sense that she could be his wife, but she is not his wife at the moment.
The key distinction: mahall (locus) of talaq must be a living wife.
3
Apply the rule based on capacity and intention
If the other entity is incapable of divorce (e.g., animal, stone), then the utterance necessarily refers to the wife, and divorce occurs regardless of intention. If the other entity is capable of divorce (e.g., another woman, a man who could be a wife?), then the wife is not divorced unless the husband intended her. If he had no intention, Abu Hanifa holds she is not divorced; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad sometimes hold she is divorced based on apparent meaning.
The Hanafi mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view in doubtful cases.
4
Consider the timing ambiguity
If the husband specifies a timing like 'according to Sunnah' but intends something else (e.g., all three immediately), his intention is followed, contrary to the author of Al-Hidayah in some views.
This shows that intention can override the default meaning of the phrase.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for the validity of intention in Hanafi fiqh and apply them to determine the validity of worship for a disbeliever.

Core Idea
Intention requires Islam, discernment, knowledge of the intended act, and absence of contradiction.

Intention (niyyah) is a fundamental element in acts of worship, but its validity depends on precise conditions. This lesson unpacks the four prerequisites taught in the Hanafi tradition, with special attention to the first condition—Islam—and its practical implications for purification and worship.

Guided Path
1

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm al-mufti principle of resolving doubt using akbar al-ra'y (preponderant opinion) and presumption of correctness to cases of doubt in prayer.

Core Idea
When a follower doubts preceding the imam, his preponderant opinion governs; if equal, validity is presumed.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair illustrates a crucial rasm al-mufti principle: how the Hanafi school resolves doubt (shakk) in worship using the mufti's tools of preponderant opinion (akbar al-ra'y) and presumption of correctness. The Tatarkhaniyya quote provides a clear fatwa on a common prayer scenario. Understanding this will equip you to give fatwa in similar doubtful situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of doubt
Determine whether the doubt is about a specific act (e.g., preceding imam in takbir) or about an obligation (e.g., missed prayers). Note that the passage deals with two distinct scenarios.
2
Check for preponderant opinion
Ask: Does the follower have a stronger belief (akbar al-ra'y) one way or the other? If yes, follow that opinion.
Preponderant opinion is more than 50% certainty based on indications.
3
Apply presumption of correctness
If the two possibilities are equal (no preponderant opinion), apply the presumption that the act was done correctly (haml al-amr ala al-sadad). This yields validity.
This is based on the maxim that certainty is not removed by doubt, and default is correctness.
4
Apply to analogous cases
Note that the same rule applies to doubt about precedence and delay, and also to doubt about missed prayers (fawa'it). In missed prayers, if doubt about qada, the prayer is considered sunnah unless preponderant opinion indicates obligation.
5
Conclude with mufti’s toolkit
The mufti must always ask about the inquirer's preponderant opinion. If unavailable, rule based on default validity. This is part of the rasm al-mufti: giving weight to the follower's subjective certainty.
This principle prevents undue invalidation of worship.

Core Idea

This lesson examines the Hanafi legal maxim that events are attributed to their closest possible time (al-hadith yudafu ila aqrab awqatihi). Work through the oil-pouring and meat-destruction cases. Note how the defendant's claim about past najasa or carrion is rejected because the nearest time (pouring/destruction) is the operative event, and the defendant denies liability.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A stipend assigned by the imam cannot be reassigned by private settlement; doing so is a double injustice.

This passage from al-Bazāziyyah presents a case where two sons attempt to settle the father's stipend from the diwan, with one taking the stipend and compensating the other. The ruling declares such a ṣulḥ invalid, grounding the reasoning in the imam's exclusive authority to designate the recipient. Understanding this principle helps a mufti navigate cases where private agreements conflict with state-administered entitlements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the subject matter
The case involves two sons of a deceased man who had a stipend (ʿaṭāʾ) in the dīwān. They reach a settlement: one son's name is entered, he takes the stipend, and gives the other a known sum of money. Identify what is being exchanged: the stipend vs. compensation.
2
Determine the ruling on the settlement
The text explicitly says 'al-ṣulḥu bāṭil' (the settlement is invalid). Note the consequence: the compensation paid (badal al-ṣulḥ) is returned, and the stipend goes to the one whom the imam had originally designated.
3
Extract the reasoning for invalidity
The reason given: 'because entitlement to the stipend is by the imam's designation; the consent of others has no influence.' Thus, private parties cannot override a right fixed by the ruler. Dissect the phrase: al-istihqāq (entitlement) is tied to the imam's action, not to rida (consent) of others.
This is a key principle in rasm al-mufti: certain rights are public or sovereign and cannot be traded.
4
Analyze the double injustice
The passage warns that if the sultan (authority) allows the wrong person to receive the stipend, he commits two wrongs: depriving the rightful recipient (ẓulm al-ḥirmān) and installing an undeserving person (ithbāt ghayr al-mustaḥiq). Understand that this is a critique of the ruler's complicity, not just the parties' agreement.
This shows the mufti's ethical concern: the ruling protects the integrity of the public trust.
5
Generalize the rule for similar cases
The principle applies beyond stipends: any right established by the imam (or state) that is not transferable by private will cannot be reassigned by ṣulḥ. A mufti must check whether the right in question is purely personal or has a public dimension.
E.g., pensions, government benefits, or waqf rights assigned by the ruler.

Al-Farisi, his commentator, said: As for his manumission, it is void because its execution is impossible by virtue of general guardianship, since the qadi's guardianship is restricted to what is beneficial, and no benefit was found; thus it is void. (End).
In Qada' al-Walwaljiyah: A man appointed an executor and ordered him to give in charity from his wealth one hundred dinars to the poor of such-and-such town. The executor was far from that town, and he had a debtor in that town who owed him dirhams, and the executor had no means to reach that town. So the qadi ordered the debtor to pay what he owed to the poor. The debt remains upon him (the debtor), and he is doing that voluntarily, and the testator's will stands. (End).
By this it is known that the qadi's order is not executed unless it agrees with the law. And it is explicitly stated in al-Dhakhirah, al-Walwaljiyah, and others that if the qadi appoints a caretaker for the mosque without a condition from the founder, it is not permissible for the qadi to do so, nor is it permissible for the caretaker to receive the salary. (End).
Also by this is known the prohibition of creating positions (lit. functions) in endowments a fortiori; because the mosque's need for a caretaker—its appointment was not permissible due to the possibility of hiring a caretaker without appointment. So appointment of other (non-necessary) positions is not permissible a fortiori. By this it is also known the prohibition of creating salaries in endowments a fortiori. I was asked about the qadi creating salaries from endowments, and I answered that if it is from an endowment conditioned for the poor, the appointment is valid but not binding, and the supervisor may spend on others and cut off the first—unless the qadi has ruled that no other appointment be made; then it becomes binding. This is in Awqaf al-Khassaf and others. But if it is not from an endowment for the poor, it is not valid and not permissible. Likewise if it is from an endowment for the poor and he appoints (a person) who owns enough to be self-sufficient. Then I was asked: If he appointed (someone) from the surplus of an endowment whose founder remained silent about how to spend its surplus, is it valid? I answered that it is not valid either; as in al-Tatarkhaniyah: The surplus of an endowment is not to be spent on the poor; rather, the administrator buys real estate with it. And it is explicitly stated in al-Bazzaziyah, followed by al-Durar wa al-Ghurar, that the surplus of one endowment is not to be used for another endowment, whether the founders are the same or different. (End).
We wrote in Sharh al-Kanz from the Book of Judicature that among the invalid judgments is judgment contrary to the condition of the founder, because contradicting it is like contradicting a textual proof. In al-Multaqat: If the qadi marries off a minor girl to someone who is not her equal, it is not permissible. (End). Thus it is known that his action is restricted to public interest. For this reason they explicitly stated that if a wall leans toward the road, and they have one witness testify against its owner, and then the qadi discharges the owner, it is not valid—as in al-Tahdhib. Likewise, the qadi's deferment (of a debt) is not valid because the right is not his—as in Jami' al-Fuslayn.

Commentary
This passage is a rule (rasm) regarding the qadi's executive authority. It establishes that any action taken by a qadi regarding the property of orphans, estates, and endowments is inherently constrained by the requirement of maslahah (public interest). If the action is not founded upon maslahah, it is legally invalid. The commentator then illustrates this rule with several cases from the Hanafi tradition: (1) A will to buy and free a slave with one-third of the estate; a debt emerges that consumes two-thirds; the qadi's purchase is valid to avoid conflict of interest, but his manumission is void because no maslahah exists since the slave would still be partly owned by the estate. (2) A testator orders charity to a distant town; the qadi orders the debtor to pay directly; the debtor's act is voluntary, the will remains. (3) The qadi appointing a mosque caretaker without the founder's condition is prohibited, and the caretaker may not take the salary. (4) Creating new positions or salaries from waqf funds is impermissible, with detailed conditions—appointments from poor endowments are valid but revocable, except when the qadi makes a binding ruling. Surplus waqf funds cannot be used for another waqf. Judgment contrary to waqf conditions is invalid. These examples all confirm that the qadi's action must be grounded in maslahah and shari'ah compliance.

Reconciling Conflicting Waqf Stipulations
Analysis of a mufti's reasoning in a waqf case with conflicting stipulations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method for reconciling conflicting stipulations (ta'arud) in a waqf deed using chronological priority and the distinction between asl and far'.

Core Idea
When waqf conditions conflict, prioritize the earlier condition and the generational framework over a later specific transfer rule.

This lesson examines a classic waqf case where the endower's stipulations appear to contradict each other. You will learn how the Hanafi jurist navigates such conflicts by distinguishing between the collective class of beneficiaries (ahl al-waqf) and individually named beneficiaries (mawquf 'alayh), and by applying principles of textual priority. The passage presents the mufti's own analytical process, including his initial opinion and subsequent recantation.

Commentary
This passage demonstrates a mufti's method in resolving a difficult waqf case with conflicting stipulations. It begins with a historical example from Sham around 690 AH, where the jurists could not find a transmitted ruling. The author later encounters a discussion among Hanafi scholars about a similar waqf: a person endows on his children, with the condition that if a child dies, his share goes to his own children, and if he dies without children, his share goes to the remaining beneficiaries. The author then defines two key terms: 'ahl al-waqf' (the collective body of beneficiaries) and 'mawquf 'alayh' (a specific named beneficiary). He explains that a person named as a subsequent beneficiary (like Amr) is considered 'mawquf 'alayh' during the life of the preceding beneficiary, but not yet from the 'ahl al-waqf' until entitlement reaches him. The author applies this to the case: the son of Abd al-Qadir who died during his father's lifetime (Muhammad) is neither from the ahl al-waqf nor mawquf 'alayh because the endower did not name him. Initially, the author thought that such a person could still be considered from ahl al-waqf and his children could inherit his potential share, but he later recants. He then presents an objection: the endower's phrase 'whoever dies from the beneficiaries before his entitlement to something' includes Muhammad, because he died before receiving, so he should be considered a beneficiary. The author refutes this by parsing the phrase: it says 'before his entitlement to something,' not 'before his entitlement' absolutely—meaning he may have had some entitlement (like the right to receive after his father) but died before receiving the actual produce. He also argues that even if we accept 'before his entitlement,' the entitlement may be delayed due to conditions. After this theoretical discussion, the author proceeds to the actual distribution after Abd al-Qadir's death. He lays out the facts: Amr (a brother of Abd al-Qadir?) dies without issue, so his share goes to his siblings (Ali and Latifa). Then Latifa dies, her share goes to her daughter Fatima. Then Ali dies, leaving a daughter Zaynab. At this point, two conflicting interpretations emerge: (1) Following the literal stipulation 'whoever dies leaving a child, his share transfers to his child,' Ali's share goes entirely to Zaynab, and Latifa's share remains with Fatima. Thus Zaynab gets two-thirds of the grandfather's share, Fatima one-third. (2) Following the overall structure of generations—'then to his children, then to the children of his children'—after the death of the last child (Ali and Latifa are the last children? Actually they are the 'children' generation, and now they are gone), the estate should be divided among all grandchildren (including Zaynab, Fatima, and also Abd al-Rahman and Malka, who were previously excluded because the children blocked them). The author notes that the second interpretation contradicts the explicit transfer rule, but the first interpretation contradicts the generational priority. This is a strong conflict. The author proposes two methods for reconciliation: (a) Since the condition for grandchildren's entitlement is earlier in the waqf document, and the specific transfer rule is later, we should prioritize the earlier condition, as this is not abrogation. (b) The arrangement of generations is the original principle (asl), while the specific transfer rule is a branch (far') and detail, so we should stick to the original. Both methods favor the second interpretation, leading to a division among all grandchildren. The passage highlights the mufti's careful reasoning and awareness of conflicting texts.

Key Terms
أهل الوقف (ahl al-waqf): The collective group of beneficiaries entitled to the waqf revenue, not necessarily individually named; becoming an 'ahl' requires that entitlement (istihqaq) reaches them upon the occurrence of a condition.
الموقوف عليه (al-mawquf 'alayh): A specific named individual or group of individuals explicitly designated by the endower as the recipient of the waqf revenue; such a person is a 'mawquf 'alayh' even before entitlement.
استحقاق (istihqaq): The legal entitlement to receive the waqf revenue; it may be immediate or conditional upon an event.
طبقة (tabaqa): A generation or layer of beneficiaries in a multi-generational waqf; e.g., children, then grandchildren.

Types of Guardianship: Binding and Non-Binding
Classifying four levels of guardianship in Islamic law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the four types of guardianship (wilayah) in Hanafi law and identify the binding nature of each type for fatwa purposes.

Core Idea
Guardianship varies in bindingness: the father's is inherent, the agent's is revocable, the executor's is intermediate, and the endowment supervisor's dismissal is debated.

In Hanafi legal theory, guardianship (wilayah) is not uniform—its binding nature depends on the source of authority. Conditions for a Valid Vow (Nathr) in Hanafi Fiqh
Rules and examples on binding vows

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for a binding vow (nathr) in Hanafi fiqh and apply them to determine the validity of specific vows.

Core Idea
A vow binds only if it is an act of obedience, not already wajib, and its type has a wajib counterpart.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair distills the Hanafi school's core conditions for a valid, binding vow (nathr). Many people think any verbal promise is binding, but the school narrows it to acts of worship that are not already obligatory. The text also provides concrete examples that a mufti can use to guide questioners.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
Read the sentence: 'لا يلزم النذر إلا إذا كان طاعة وليس بواجب وكان من جنسه واجب على التعيين'. Extract the three conditions: (1) the vowed act must be an act of obedience (طاعة), (2) it must not be already obligatory (ليس بواجب), (3) there must exist a wajib act of the same type specified on a specific occasion (من جنسه واجب على التعيين).
Condition (3) means, for example, fasting has a wajib form (Ramadan), so a voluntary fast can be vowed; visiting the sick has no wajib counterpart, so it cannot be vowed.
2
Understand the 'obedience' condition
Acts of disobedience (like drinking alcohol) or even neutral acts (like walking) are not binding if vowed. Only acts that are inherently good and rewarded by Allah qualify.
This excludes sins and mubah acts.
3
Understand the 'not wajib' condition
If someone vows to perform an act that is already wajib upon him (e.g., the five daily prayers, Ramadan fasts), the vow does not create an extra obligation. He was already required to do it.
Thus, vowing to pray Fajr tomorrow is not binding because it is already fard.
4
Check the 'type has a wajib' condition
The type of act (e.g., prayer, fasting, Hajj, charity) must have a wajib form in Shariah. Vowing to visit the sick fails because visiting the sick is not a wajib act by itself.
This condition ensures the vow is a form of worship that can be obligatory.
5
Apply to the examples
Go through each example in the text: (a) vowing to fast forever: binding because fasting is obedience, not already wajib (only Ramadan is wajib), and fasting has a wajib counterpart (Ramadan). (b) Vowing to fast on arrival day: valid but must intend for vow if already fasting voluntarily. (c) Vowing Hajj al-Islam: not binding as extra because Hajj is already wajib once. (d) Vowing prayer for a year: if meaning obligatory prayers, not binding; if meaning voluntary prayers, binding (prayer has wajib form). (e) Vowing to visit sick: not binding (no wajib counterpart). (f) Vowing tasbihat after prayer: not binding.
The ruling on 'complete the maghrib' is obscure; the main point is the conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the three Hanafi rules regarding marriage validity disputes, acknowledgment of marriage/dower, and exceptions to the permissibility of marriage without dower.

Core Idea
Presumption favors validity; acknowledging a child acknowledges the marriage but not the dower.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents three distinct legal rules that a mufti must know when handling marriage disputes and acknowledgments. The first rule deals with who is believed when spouses disagree on validity. The second set of rules clarifies what certain statements imply about marriage, dower, and divorce. The third rule outlines when marriage without a proper dower is permissible and when it is not. Understanding these rules will help you parse similar cases in fatwa work.

1
Identify the three distinct rulings
Read the passage and separate the three legal issues: validity dispute, acknowledgment implications, and dower permissibility exceptions.
The rulings are separated by the mention of sources: al-Khaniya and Iqrar al-Yatima.
2
Analyze the validity dispute rule
Understand that when the spouses differ on whether the marriage is valid or invalid, the one who claims validity is given credence. This is a procedural rule for the mufti.
This is based on the principle that validity is the default state (al-asl al-sihhah).
3
Examine the acknowledgment rules
Work through each scenario: acknowledging a child from a free woman implies marriage but not the dower amount. Saying 'take this for iddah maintenance' does not acknowledge divorce. Saying 'give me my dower' acknowledges marriage.
Note the nuance: the word 'mahr' is used but the acknowledgment is of the marriage, not the specific mahr.
4
Study the exceptions to marriage without dower
Marriage without dower or with less than mahr al-mithl is generally valid, but there are three exceptions: a minor given in marriage by someone other than father/grandfather, an interdicted woman (mahjurah), and an agent specifically authorized for a fixed dower (mukallah 'aynatihi).
For these exceptions, the marriage is valid but the dower must be the proper mahr al-mithl.

Apply the rules to a mufti scenario
Imagine a case: a husband acknowledges a child from his wife but denies he owes her any dower. According to the rule, his acknowledgment of the child is acknowledgment of the marriage, but not of the dower. The mufti would rule that the marriage is established, but the dower is not automatically acknowledged.
The mufti must not assume the dower amount from the child acknowledgment.
