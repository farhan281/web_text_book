Rule of Non-Interference (Tadakhul) in Legal Consequences
Explaining conditions for non-interference of legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi dabt of tadakhul to distinguish between merging and non-merging legal consequences in cases of multiple crimes and iddah, and construct a fatwa using this rule.

Core Idea
Multiple legal consequences merge only when they are of the same type, same objective, and involve accidental acts without full recovery.

The principle of tadakhul determines whether multiple causes yield a single consequence or remain separate. This lesson unpacks a key dabt from Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, focusing on its application in jinayat (crimes) and iddah (waiting period). Understanding this dabt is essential for any mufti dealing with consecutive acts causing harm or interruption of waiting periods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the nature of the acts
First, determine whether each act is intentional (amd) or accidental (khata'). The dabt only allows merging when both acts are mistakes.
Intentional acts never merge, regardless of other conditions.
2
Check the identity of the victim
Merging requires that both acts affect the same person. If the victims differ, consequences do not merge.
This applies to both jinayat and iddah.
3
Assess the healing condition (for jinayat)
If the first act caused injury and that injury healed (bur') before the second act, merging is blocked. Only if healing did not intervene do the consequences merge.
Healing restores the victim to a state equivalent to a new person for the purpose of liability.
4
Apply to iddah cases
For a woman in iddah who is cohabited with by mistake (shubhah), a second iddah becomes obligatory and merges with the first, regardless of who the cohabitor is.
Here, the objective (waiting for clarification of pregnancy) is the same, so merging occurs.
5
Construct the fatwa using the dabt
State the legal question, apply the dabt, and issue the ruling. Ensure to reference the conditions (same type, same objective, accidental, no healing).
When in doubt, the default is non-merger; merging is the exception.

Liability of the Minor Under Interdiction: A Hanafi Dabt
General rule and exceptions for a minor's liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'الصبي المحجور عليه مؤاخذ بأفعاله' to identify when a minor is liable for property damage and when an exception applies, and construct a fatwa using the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A minor under interdiction is liable for torts, but not for property without guardian permission — with one exception.

This lesson unpacks a concise yet crucial dabt (sub-maxim) from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: the minor under interdiction is held accountable for his actions. While this sounds absolute, the Hanafi school carves out specific exceptions where liability is lifted. Understanding these nuances is essential for a mufti when dealing with cases involving minors and property damage.

Guided Path
1
State the general qawida
Read the first line: 'الصبي المحجور عليه مؤاخذ بأفعاله'. This is the dabt. Understand its core: the minor is liable for his acts, especially torts like destroying property or causing death.
The liability for death falls on the 'aqila, not the minor personally.
2
Identify the exceptions
The text lists exceptions: cases where the minor destroys property that came to him without guardian permission (borrowed, deposited) or his own property without permission. In these, he is not liable.
The phrase 'ما اقترضه وما أودع عنده بلا إذن وليه وما له وما منه بلا إذن' covers four scenarios.
3
Examine the istithna' (exception to the exception)
The text adds a further scenario: if a minor deposits an item owned by two others with another minor, the owner can choose to hold either minor liable. This overrides the general exception.
The commentator resolves the apparent problem by noting the owner's permission is absent, so liability is by the owner's choice, not by the minor's act.
4
Understand the sharh chain
The passage cites Jami' al-Fusulayn, which called this case problematic, then the commentator (likely Ibn Nujaym) clarifies it. Recognize the expansion at each level.
The 'qultu' response shows how later scholars refined the rule.
5
Apply the qawida in a fatwa
Given a real scenario, use the qawida to determine liability. The mufta bihi follows the general rule with these exceptions. Remember the istithna' for joint ownership deposits.
This is a dabt, so it governs both torts and deposits chapters.

This passage presents a general ruling and its exceptions. The general rule is that a minor under interdiction (sabi mahjur) is liable for his torts: if he destroys property, he must compensate; if he kills, the blood money is on his 'aqila (paternal male relatives). Then the author lists exceptions: if the minor destroys property that came to him through borrowing or deposit without guardian permission, or if he destroys his own property without permission, he is not liable in those cases? Wait—the text says 'لو أتلف ما اقترضه وما أودع عنده بلا إذن وليه وما له وما منه بلا إذن' — this seems to be part of the 'illa (except in cases) — meaning he is NOT liable in these four scenarios. However, the exact parsing is tricky: the exception is for when he destroys what he borrowed, what was deposited with him without guardian permission, and his own property and from his property without permission. Permission in Lease Implies Permission in Trade
A maxim equating permission in hire with permission in trade

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that permission in hire implies permission in trade to scenarios involving slaves or minors, and vice versa.

Core Idea
Permission in hire = permission in trade: one implies the other unless restricted.

This lesson introduces a concise legal maxim (qawida) from the Hanafi school: if a person gives permission to another to enter into a hire contract, that permission extends to engaging in trade as well, and the reverse also holds. This rule is particularly relevant in cases of slaves and minors where the master or guardian grants limited commercial authority. Understanding this dabt helps muftis resolve questions of implied permissions in contractual agency.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Memorize the Arabic and English of the maxim: 'الإذن في الإجارة إذن في التجارة وعكسه' – 'Permission in hire is permission in trade, and vice versa.'
The maxim is from the Hanafi work al-Sirajiyya.
2
Identify the scope
The maxim governs the field of permission (idhn) granted to slaves or minors for commercial activities. It is a dabt (sub-maxim) within the broader Hanafi qawida framework.
It does not apply to all contracts, only to the relationship between hire and trade.
3
Apply to furu‘ (branches)
Given a scenario where a master tells his slave 'I permit you to hire yourself out,' the slave is also implicitly permitted to buy and sell goods in the course of that hire. Conversely, permission to trade includes permission to hire workers.
This prevents narrow readings that would invalidate contracts entered by the agent.
4
Note limitations and exceptions
If the permission explicitly restricts the scope (e.g., 'hire only, no trading'), then the general implication is overridden. Also, the maxim applies only when the permission is general (mujmal); specific instructions take precedence.
Istithna' (exception) arises from explicit restriction.

The Qāʿida of Permission for Trade: General vs. Specific
Conditions for valid permission and implied permission by silence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that permission for trade is general unless the permitter is a mudārib in one type, and identify when a master's silence constitutes permission.

Core Idea
Permission for trade is general; specification only when the permitter is a mudārib in one type. A master's silence implies permission, unless he is a judge.

This lesson explores a key legal maxim (dabt) from the Hanafi school regarding the permission given to a slave to engage in trade. The passage clarifies when such permission is valid, whether it can be restricted to specific types of trade, and what constitutes implied permission through a master's silence. Understanding these nuances is essential for a mufti issuing fatwas on commercial transactions involving slaves or analogous modern cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Qāʿida
Locate the central maxim: 'Permission for trade does not accept specification unless the permitter is a mudārib in one type and permits the slave for mudāraba.' This is the rule that governs the scope of permission.
The maxim is a dabt (sub-rule) within the chapter of idhn.
2
Understand the General Rule
The default rule is that permission is absolute and allows the slave to trade in all types of goods. Commands like 'Hire yourself out' or 'Buy a cloth for me' are deemed general permission.
The passage also states that if the master merely sees the slave trading and remains silent, that silence is considered permission, unless the master is a judge.
3
Note the Exception
The only exception is when the permitter is himself a mudārib (investor) in one specific type of trade and grants permission for mudāraba specifically. In that case, the permission is restricted to that type.
Even this exception is disputed within the school.
4
Examine the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilāf
Al-Sarakhsi disagreed and held that even in the mudāraba case, permission should be general. This is a legitimate ikhtilāf within the Hanafi school.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is whether the slave can trade in other types or not.
5
Apply the Rulings on Silence
A master's silence upon seeing his slave trade constitutes permission, but with an important exception: if the master is a judge, his silence does not imply consent because he is duty-bound to object to wrong.
This is a separate qāʿida: 'The silence of a judge is not permission.'

Commentary
This passage collects several interrelated rulings about the validity and scope of permission (idhn) for trade, especially concerning slaves. The Hanafi Maxim: Direct vs Indirect Causation (al-Mubashir wa al-Mutasabbib)
Stating the Hanafi rule for direct vs indirect causation in tort liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that a direct actor (mubashir) is liable even if unintentional, while an indirect causer (mutasabbib) is liable only if intentional, and distinguish the two categories using the examples from the text.

Core Idea
Direct actors always pay; indirect causers pay only if they intend harm.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a foundational Hanafi principle of tort liability. The distinction between direct and indirect causation determines who bears responsibility for harm. While a direct actor is strictly liable, the liability of an indirect causer hinges on intentional wrongdoing. Understanding this maxim is essential for muftis when evaluating cases of accidental harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
State the rule for mubashir (direct actor) and mutasabbib (indirect causer) as given: the direct actor is liable even if unintentional; the indirect causer is not liable unless intentional.
Memorize the Arabic phrasing: 'al-mubashir damin wa in lam yata'ammad wa al-mutasabbib la yadman illa idha kana muta'ammidan.'
2
Apply to the arrow example
The shooter is a direct actor because his physical act caused the harm. According to the rule, he is liable even though the hitting was accidental.
This is a clear case of direct causation (mubasharah).
3
Apply to the well example
Digging a well on one's own property is an indirect act; if someone falls in, the digger is not liable unless he intended harm. However, digging on another's property changes the ruling: the digger becomes liable because the act is wrongful (trespass), effectively turning the indirect causer into a direct actor or establishing intent.
Property ownership affects the classification: on own land, digger is mutasabbib; on another's land, he is treated as mubashir.
4
Analyse the breastfeeding case
An older woman breastfeeding a young girl who is married may invalidate the marriage. The woman is an indirect causer; she is not liable for half the dower unless she intentionally causes corruption. The conditions for intent include: knowledge of the marriage, awareness that breastfeeding would invalidate it, and no necessity.
Ignorance (jahl) prevents liability, as confirmed by the Hidayah.
5
Note the role of ignorance
The maxim explicitly states that ignorance is considered to prevent corruption (al-jahl mu'tabar li-daf' al-fasad). This reinforces that indirect causation only leads to liability when combined with knowledge and intent.
This principle is illustrated in the breastfeeding case: if she was ignorant of the marriage, she is not liable.

The Three Exceptions to Non-Liability of Usufruct of Usurpation
Qawida with furuat: the three exceptions to non-liability of usufruct of usurpation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories where usufruct of usurpation is compensable and apply the exceptions to cases of joint ownership, waqf, and orphan property.

Core Idea
Usufruct of usurpation is not guaranteed except in three cases: orphan property, waqf, and property prepared for exploitation.

The Hanafi school generally holds that the usufruct (manafi') of a usurped property is not compensable because usufruct is not considered property in itself. However, this rule has three important exceptions. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for muftis dealing with property disputes involving orphans, endowments, and income-generating assets.

Identify the property type
Determine whether the usurped property is an orphan's property, waqf property, or property prepared for exploitation (mu'add li al-istighlal). Only these three categories trigger liability for usufruct.
Orphan property refers to property belonging to a minor orphan, not just any minor.
2
Check for exceptions within the 'prepared for exploitation' category
Even if the property is prepared for exploitation, the usufruct is not guaranteed if the usurper occupied it under a claim of ownership or under a valid contract (e.g., co-owner residing).
Also, property prepared for exploitation does not include mere renting; it requires building or buying for that purpose.
3
Apply rulings for renting out usurped property
If the usurper rents out a property whose usufruct is guaranteed (waqf, orphan, prepared), the tenant only pays the named rent (not market value), and the usurper must return that rent to the rightful owner or waqf.
The usurper is not liable for the equivalent rent (ujrat al-mithl) beyond what was received.
4
Analyze residence under a contractual claim
For cases like a mortgagee residing, determine if the property is prepared for exploitation. If not, the mortgagee may recover rent paid mistakenly, unless it was a gift.
This highlights the interplay between the 'prepared' status and contractual rights.
