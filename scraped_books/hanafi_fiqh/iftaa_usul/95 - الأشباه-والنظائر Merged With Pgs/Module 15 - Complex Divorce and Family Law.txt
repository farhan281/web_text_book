Thirteen Types of Separation and the Rule Distinguishing Talaq and Faskh
Classification of spousal separation and a rule for talaq vs faskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the thirteen types of spousal separation, classify them as requiring a judicial decree or not, and apply the rule distinguishing talaq (divorce) from faskh (annulment).

Core Idea
Not every separation is talaq; faskh annuls without reducing divorce counts.

The Hanafi school classifies spousal separation into thirteen types. Seven require a judicial decree, six do not. Beyond the classification, a crucial distinction separates divorce (talaq) from annulment (faskh). This lesson presents the list and the underlying rule that governs the legal outcome.

Guided Path
1
Memorise the two groups
First learn the seven types that require a court ruling: jabb, 'unnah, option of puberty, lack of compatibility, deficiency of mahr, husband's refusal to accept Islam, and li'an. Then the six that do not: option of emancipation, Ila', apostasy, difference of realms, one spouse owning the other, and nikah fasid.
For fatwa, remember which cases the qadi must be involved in.
2
Identify the source of each separation
For each type, determine whether the separation originates from the husband or from the wife (or from a third cause). This is key to applying the rule in the footnote.
E.g., option of puberty originates from the wife; 'unnah from the husband.
3
Apply the rule distinguishing talaq from faskh
The footnote gives a rule: separations from the wife (not due to the husband) are faskh; separations from the husband are talaq, except apostasy (from husband) is faskh because it negates the marital bond entirely.
Apostasy is the exception: though from husband, it is faskh due to incompatibility.
4
Understand the practical consequences
Talaq reduces the number of remaining divorces (three in a non-menstruating woman), while faskh does not. Also, talaq can be revocable (raj'i) or irrevocable; faskh is always irrevocable without counting as talaq.
This distinction affects fatwa significantly.

Divorce and Manumission Based on Internal States and Conditions Known Only to One Party
Rule on ta'liq based on internal states and exclusive knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when a condition (ta'liq) depends on an internal state or fact exclusively known to one party, that party's statement is taken, and identify the exception and the distinction between verifiable and non-verifiable facts.

Core Idea
If a condition depends on what only one party knows, that party's word suffices—unless the other's action contradicts.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a key maxim: when divorce or manumission is suspended on an internal state or a fact exclusively known to one party, that party's own statement is accepted, even if they might lie. However, an exception exists when the husband's own action contradicts the claimed state. The passage also distinguishes between observable and non-observable facts, explaining why menstrual blood is treated differently from semen emission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition type
Determine whether the condition is based on an internal state (fi'l qalbi) or a fact known only to one party (e.g., menses known only to wife, wet dream known only to husband).
Internal states are not externally provable, so the word of the one experiencing them is taken.
2
Apply the general rule
If the condition is on an internal state or fact known only to the wife (like her menses or happiness), her statement that it occurred triggers the divorce, even if she is lying. Similarly, if the condition is on a fact known only to the husband (like a wet dream), his statement triggers manumission.
The rule is based on necessity: no one else can know these facts.
3
Recognize the exception
When the husband's own action contradicts the claimed internal state, the wife's statement is not accepted. Example: husband says 'If I please you, you are divorced' and then beats her; her claim of being pleased is invalid because the beating negates pleasure.
The exception applies only when the condition is linked to the husband's action (e.g., 'I please you'), not to a purely internal state of the wife.
4
Analyze the distinction between observable and non-observable facts
For manumission conditioned on a wet dream, the slave's statement is accepted? Actually, the passage says it is accepted (as in al-Muhit). But then it contrasts with menstrual blood: the wife's statement about menses is accepted because blood from the womb cannot be externally observed. However, for the slave, emission of semen can potentially be observed externally (by seeing the semen), so there is a possibility of verification. The distinction is that for menses, there is no external way to confirm, so the wife's word is taken; for wet dreams, the slave's word is also taken but with a caveat: the author of al-Khaniyya distinguishes by the possibility of external observation.
This is a subtle analogy (qiyas khafi) based on the ability to verify externally.

Commentary
This passage presents rulings for a mufti on divorce and manumission conditioned on internal states or facts exclusively known to one party. The Suspended Follows the Condition: Qawida in Complex Talaq
Rulings on testimony and complex formulations in conditional talaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'al-taliq yatba' al-shart' to determine the validity of conditional talaq in cases involving testimony for zina and sequential conditions.

Core Idea
The suspended talaq occurs only when the condition is proven by the legally required evidence.

This passage presents several disti, nct rulings under the overarching principle that the validity of a conditional talaq depends on the precise fulfillment of its condition—both in substance and in proof. We examine how testimony for zina, complex formulations with multiple wives, and the order of condition and consequence all impact the outcome. By mastering these applications, you will sharpen your ability to parse intricate talaq formulations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition and consequence
In each scenario, first locate the 'if' clause (the condition, shart) and the 'then' clause (the consequence, jaza). For the testimony case, the condition is the husband's zina; for the four-wife case, it is the failure to have intercourse; for the ordering rules, it is the sequence of elements.
Remember: In Hanafi talaq, the condition must be a possible future event.
2
Examine the proof required for the condition
The condition of zina can be proven either by confession (iqrar) or by direct observation (mu‘ayana). Confession is accepted from any two witnesses, but direct observation requires four upright witnesses. If only two witnesses testify to direct observation, the condition is not proven and talaq does not occur.
This distinction mirrors the rule for hadd al-zina: four witnesses or confession.
3
Apply the ordering rules for multiple conditions
When the husband mentions more than one condition or consequence, the qawida 'al-taliq yatba' al-shart' determines which consequence is attached to which condition. The passage gives three scenarios: (a) consequence first then condition with a time – the suspension is valid; (b) condition first then consequence – straightforward; (c) condition, consequence, waw, another consequence – the first two consequences attach to the first condition, the third to the second.
The order of mention matters: if the consequence is placed before the condition, it can still be valid if a time is inserted (to avoid ambiguity).

Writing Without Intention: Divorce vs Oaths by Allah
Ruling on writing without intention for divorce and oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the legal effect of writing without intention for divorce and for oaths by Allah, and apply the Hanafi rule that intent is required for divorce but not for oaths by Allah.

Core Idea
Writing without intent does not effect divorce but does effect oaths by Allah.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir examines a critical distinction in Hanafi law: the role of intention (niyyah) in different legal acts. While divorce requires a direct intent to address, oaths by Allah are binding even without full intent. Understanding this distinction is essential for fatwa-giving, as a mufti must know when intent is a condition and when it is not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage deals with two distinct cases: writing a divorce statement without intending to address the spouse, and writing an oath by Allah without intending to swear.
2
Determine the condition for divorce
For divorce, intent to address (qasd al-khitab) is required. Without it, the writing does not effect divorce.
The text cites al-Qunyah: a woman writes 'You are divorced' and asks her husband to read it – no divorce if he did not intend to address her.
3
Determine the condition for oaths by Allah
For oaths by Allah, no intent is required. The forgetful, mistaken, and heedless are treated like the intentional.
This principle prevents trivializing oaths by Allah.
4
Apply the distinction
If a man writes oaths and asks another to read them, divorce oaths do not bind without intent, but oaths by Allah bind regardless.
The mufti must ask about intent in divorce cases but not for oaths by Allah.

Husband Ordering Writing of Divorce Document: Agency or Divorce?
Dispute over legal effect of ordering a divorce document

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of agency (tawkil) to distinguish between ordering a divorce document and the divorce itself, and identify the mufta bihi position on this ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
Ordering a divorce document is agency, not divorce — divorce requires execution with intention.

A common scenario arises when a husband, perhaps in anger or haste, orders a scribe to write a divorce document. Does the divorce take effect immediately? Hanafi scholars differed, and this lesson unpacks the two views and the relied-upon ruling. Understanding this distinction is crucial for muftis advising on such cases, as the consequences for the marriage are significant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Dispute
State the act: a husband orders a scribe to write a divorce document. The key question: does this order itself effect a divorce, or is it merely a preparatory step? Identify the two opinions mentioned in the text.
This is a typical parsing exercise: isolate the different rulings from the same factual scenario.
2
Classify the Legal Nature of the Order
The first view classifies the order as iqrar (acknowledgment) — implying the husband admits divorce. The second classifies it as tawkil (agency) — the husband merely authorizes writing, not the divorce itself. Compare the legal consequences of each classification.
Agency requires execution; acknowledgment is immediate.
3
Identify the Mufta Bihi Position
The text states 'and by this fatwa is given' referring to the second view (tawkil). Also notes 'this is correct in our time'. Extract the operative ruling for fatwa: divorce does not occur until the document is written.
The phrase 'wa bihi yufata' signals the relied-upon position in Hanafi literature.
4
Note the Additional Nuance
The passage from al-Quniya adds a further layer: even after writing, divorce does not occur unless the husband intended divorce. This emphasizes the role of niyya (intention). Consider how this refines the ruling.
Intention is a central element in Hanafi talaq; mere writing may be ambiguous.
5
Apply the Ruling to a Practical Scenario
Given a husband who orders writing but dies before the document is written, determine the outcome based on the mufta bihi: no divorce occurs, inheritance rights unchanged. Contrast with the first view where divorce would have occurred.

And they differed concerning a husband ordering the writing of a document for her divorce: some said it occurs and it is an acknowledgment by him; others said it is an agency so it does not occur until he writes it, and according to this fatwa is given. And this is correct in our time; likewise in al-Quniya. And after it in that book: and some said it does not occur even if he writes unless he intended divorce.

Commentary
This passage presents an ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school regarding the legal effect of a husband ordering a scribe to write a divorce document. Two views are recorded: (1) The order itself is considered an acknowledgment of divorce, so divorce takes effect immediately. (2) The order is merely an authorization (tawkil) to write, so divorce does not take effect until the document is actually written. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the second view, and it is considered correct in the author's time. A further view from al-Quniya states that even after writing, divorce does not occur unless the husband specifically intended divorce at the time of writing. This highlights the importance of intention (niyya) and the distinction between an expression of divorce and preparatory acts.

Key Terms
التوكيل (al-tawkil): Agency; the husband authorizes the scribe to act on his behalf.
الإقرار (al-iqrar): Acknowledgment; a statement of a fact that is binding.
النية (al-niyya): Intention; a necessary element for divorce when using ambiguous wording (kinayah).
