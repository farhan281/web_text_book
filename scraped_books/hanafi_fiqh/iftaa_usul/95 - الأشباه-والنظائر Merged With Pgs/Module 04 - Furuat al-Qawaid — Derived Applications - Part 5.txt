Vows (Nathr) and Fasting
Opening rulings on vowed fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for vowed fasting (perpetual and day-specific) and apply the rule of converting intention from voluntary to vow.

Core Idea
A vow to fast can be converted from a voluntary intention if the vowed condition occurs after the intention.

This lesson explores two specific cases of vowed fasting from the collection. The first deals with a perpetual vow interrupted by an excuse, requiring expiation and makeup. The second addresses a vow to fast on a specific day when the intended event occurs after you have already begun a voluntary fast. Understanding how intention (niyyah) interacts with vows is key to correct fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the vow types
Note that there are two distinct vows: perpetual fast and fast on a specific day when a person arrives. The rulings differ.
2
Analyze the perpetual vow case
If one vows to fast forever but eats due to an excuse (e.g., illness), he must expiate (kaffarah) for what he ate and then make up the fast.
Expiation is required because breaking a voluntary fast with food/drink triggers kaffarah in the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the day-specific vow case
If one vows to fast on the day a certain person arrives, but that person arrives after the person had already intended a voluntary fast for that day, he may change his intention to the vow. This is allowed because the vow becomes due and the intention can be redirected.
This shows flexibility in niyyah for overlapping obligations.
4
Extract the fatwa principle
The key principle: a voluntary intention can be repurposed for a wajib (vowed) fast if the obligation arises after the intention. This is a practical ruling for muftis.

Commentary
This passage contains a series of rulings (rasm) on fasting from the Hanafi madhhab. It begins with vows (nathr): if someone vows to fast perpetually and eats for an excuse, he must expiate and later make up the fast. If he vows to fast on the day a specific person arrives, but that person arrives after he had already intended a voluntary fast on that day, he may convert that intention to the vow. A husband can prevent his wife from performing any fast that she herself made obligatory (like a voluntary vowed fast), but not a fast made obligatory by Allah (like Ramadan). However, scholars hesitated about preventing her from Ramadan if she breaks it without excuse. The issue of relying on astrologers for moon sighting is discussed: some Hanafis allowed it if multiple astrologers agree (from Muhammad ibn Muqatil), but Imam al-Sarakhsi strongly rejected it, citing the hadith of kufr. The footnote explains that the prohibition may only apply to believing their claims about hidden events, not to astronomical calculations. Intention for fasting made during prayer is valid and does not break the prayer. Eating or drinking something nutritious or medicinal in a voluntary fast requires expiation (kaffara); blood drinking also requires expiation because it is considered food for some. Fasting while traveling is generally better unless there is fear of harm or the travel companions who share provisions choose to break it. Fasting on the day of doubt (30th of Sha'ban when the moon is uncertain) is disliked unless one intends a voluntary fast or a missed obligatory fast; the preferred view is to break it unless it aligns with a habitual fast or the person is a mufti (who might need to confirm the moon). Finally, slaves (male/female), mudabbars (slaves whose freedom is conditioned on master's death), and umm walad (slave mother of master's child) may not perform voluntary fasts without the master's permission.

Husband's Authority over Wife's Fasting
Ruling on husband's right to prevent wife's fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on when a husband may prevent his wife from fasting and distinguish between obligations from Allah and self-imposed obligations.

Core Idea
A husband can prevent his wife from any fast she voluntarily obliged herself to, but not from Ramadan or other Allah-obligated fasts.

This lesson covers the delicate balance between a husband's authority and a wife's religious duties. The text gives a clear rule: the husband may prevent his wife from fasts that she made obligatory by her own action (e.g., a vowed fast), but cannot prevent her from fasts made obligatory by Allah (e.g., Ramadan, missed mandatory fasts). The scholars also note hesitation about preventing her from Ramadan if she breaks it without excuse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of fasts
The passage divides fasts into those that become obligatory by the wife's own action (e.g., nathr, qada of voluntary fasts) and those obligatory by Allah's command (e.g., Ramadan, qada of Ramadan).
2
State the husband's right
The husband may prevent the first category (self-imposed) but not the second (Allah-imposed). This is a clear ruling.
3
Note the hesitation about Ramadan
The scholars hesitated ('tawaquf') about preventing her from Ramadan if she breaks it without excuse. This indicates a grey area: if she is already breaking Ramadan without excuse, can he prevent her? The hesitation suggests it is not absolute.
This hesitation implies that if she is already committing a sin, preventing her might be permissible to stop further sin, but it is not explicitly stated.
4
Extract the fatwa principle
The key principle: a husband's authority over his wife's voluntary acts of worship is limited; he cannot override obligations imposed by Allah.

Relying on Astrologers for Moon Sighting
Dispute on reliance on astrologers for moon sighting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two scholarly opinions on using astrologers for moon sighting and apply the hadith-based refutation by Imam al-Sarakhsi.

Core Idea
Relying on astrologers for determining the start of Ramadan is rejected by strong hadith, though some earlier Hanafis allowed it if consensus of astrologers.

This lesson addresses a fatwa dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the use of astrologers to determine the moon's appearance. Some Hanafis, including Muhammad ibn Muqatil, permitted relying on astrologers if a group agreed. However, Imam al-Sarakhsi refuted this using the hadith: 'Whoever believes a soothsayer or astrologer has disbelieved in what was revealed to Muhammad.' The footnote clarifies that astronomical calculations for the moon may be exempt if they are based on calculation, not divination.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
First position: Some Hanafis (e.g., Muhammad ibn Muqatil) allowed relying on astrologers if multiple astrologers agree. Second position: Imam al-Sarakhsi rejected this completely based on the hadith.
2
Understand the basis of permissibility
The permissibility was based on the idea that astrologers' calculations about the moon could be accurate and reliable if they agree.
This is a practical concession for determining moon sighting when actual sighting is difficult.
3
Understand al-Sarakhsi's refutation
He cited the hadith that equates believing an astrologer/soothsayer to kufr. This strong textual evidence overrides the practical concession.
Al-Sarakhsi is a major Hanafi jurist, so his view carries weight.
4
Note the footnote's qualification
The footnote (probably from the commentator) says the hadith prohibition may apply only to believing their predictions about unseen events, not to astronomical calculations about natural phenomena like the moon. This suggests a possible distinction.
This qualification allows for the use of scientific astronomy in moon sighting, which is accepted by many later Hanafis.
5
Extract the fatwa principle
The stronger view (mufta bihi) is to avoid reliance on astrologers due to the hadith, but astronomical calculations may be permissible if purely based on science, not magic.
Modern practice often uses calculation for moon sighting, but the classical ruling remains cautionary.

Expiation for Breaking a Voluntary Fast
Rulings on expiation and intention for voluntary fasts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules of expiation (kaffarah) for breaking a voluntary fast, including the special case of drinking blood.

Core Idea
Eating or drinking anything nutritious in a voluntary fast requires kaffarah; blood also counts as nutritious.

This lesson covers the rulings on expiation (kaffarah) for intentionally breaking a voluntary fast. The text states that if one eats or drinks something nutritious or medicinal, expiation is due; otherwise not. An interesting exception: drinking blood requires expiation because it is considered food for some people. Also, the intention for fasting can be made during prayer and remains valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for kaffarah
Kaffarah is required if one eats or drinks something that is nutritious (yaghadhdha bih) or medicinal (yutadawa bih).
2
Apply the condition to non-nutritive items
If one eats or drinks something not nutritious or medicinal (e.g., a pebble, water? – water is nutritious?, etc.), no kaffarah according to the text.
The text says 'or else not' (wa illa fala).
3
Analyze the blood exception
Blood is considered nutritious because it is food for some people (e.g., certain tribes). So drinking blood triggers kaffarah.
This is specific to the Hanafi school.
4
Understand the intention ruling
Making intention for fasting while in prayer is valid and does not invalidate the prayer.
This shows the flexibility of niyyah in Hanafi fiqh.

Slaves and Voluntary Fasting: Master's Permission
Ruling on slaves' voluntary fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that a slave (including mudabbar and umm walad) may not observe a voluntary fast without the master's permission.

Core Idea
A slave cannot perform voluntary fasts without the master's permission because the master has rights over their time and labor.

This lesson covers the ruling that slaves, both male and female, as well as mudabbars (slaves whose freedom is conditional on the master's death) and umm walad (female slave who bore her master's child), may not perform voluntary (nafl) fasts without the permission of their master. This is because the master has the right to their service, and a voluntary act of worship could interfere with that.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of slaves mentioned
Four types: 'abd (male slave), amah (female slave), mudabbar, and umm walad.
2
State the ruling
None of these may observe a voluntary fast without the master's permission.
3
Understand the reason
The master's rights over the slave's time take precedence over voluntary worship, as the slave's labor is owned by the master.
This is a fiqh principle: the rights of humans (huquq al-ibad) can limit acts of worship.
4
Differentiate obligatory fasts
For obligatory fasts (e.g., Ramadan, qada, vows), the master cannot prevent; but voluntary fasts require permission.
This aligns with the earlier lesson on husband's authority: only self-imposed obligations are subject to prevention.

Fasting and the Principle of Harm (La Darara)
Harm-based furuat on voluntary fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'La Darara' and apply it to the rulings on a wife's voluntary fast, a hired worker's fast, a baker's work limit, and informing a forgetful eater.

Core Idea
A wife's voluntary fast is prohibited without husband's consent only if it harms him; the same applies to a hired worker.

The Hanafi school uses the maxim 'لا ضرر ولا ضرار' (no harm and no reciprocating harm) to regulate voluntary acts when they conflict with others' rights. This lesson presents several furuat from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir where this maxim determines whether a voluntary fast is permitted or restricted. You will learn to identify the harm condition and apply it consistently.

3
Apply the 'La Darara' maxim

Note the exceptions

Furuat on vows, permissions, and intention-based fasting rulings

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A vow binds only if it is obedience of a type with an obligatory counterpart; intentions determine whether breaking a fast is allowed.

The second set of furuat from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir revolves around the maxim 'الأمور بمقاصدها' (matters are judged by intentions). You will learn to analyse how the same maxim produces different outcomes based on the person's intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
Each furu' presents a scenario: is a vow binding? Is breaking a fast disliked? Can a husband revoke i'tikaf permission?
2
Determine the underlying intent

Apply the maxim of intentions

The Maxim: Liability Multiplies with Multiplicity of Agents
Analysis of a qawida and its furuat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim of multiplied liability to Hajj scenarios involving joint actions and repeated acts.

Core Idea
Liability for an act multiplies with agents; but for sanctuary violations, it's singular.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi qawida from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: the liability for an action multiplies when multiple agents commit it, but the sanctity of the Haram imposes a different rule. We will explore its application to two pilgrims jointly hunting game and to repeated intercourse during Hajj.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its scope
Read the founding maxim: ضمان الفعل يتعدد بتعدد الفاعل وضمان الحلّ لا. Explain that the first part applies to actions (like killing game) and the second to sanctuary violations.
2
Apply to joint hunting by pilgrims
Two muhrim jointly kill game: the penalty is multiplied (each pays a penalty). Why? Because the act is attributed to each agent.
3
Contrast with non-pilgrims in the Haram
If they are not in ihram (halal) in the Haram, the liability is not multiplied; it's singular. Explain the rationale: it's not like liability for rights of servants (huquq al-'ibad).
4
Apply to repeated intercourse
If a pilgrim has intercourse multiple times during Hajj, each act requires a dam (sacrifice), unless done in one sitting (majlis wahid), then one dam suffices.
5
Identify the thamarah
The practical consequence: in fiqh, when issuing fatwa, one must distinguish between multiple agents vs. repeated acts by one agent.

Deputized Hajj: Mixing Funds, Trading, and Authority of the Deputy
Furuat on deputized Hajj with intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a valid deputized Hajj, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on trading with Hajj funds, and state the rulings on surplus, delays, and expenses.

Core Idea
A deputy mixing funds is permissible, but trading with funds invalidates the Hajj according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

This lesson deals with the intricacies of performing Hajj on behalf of another (al-hajj bi al-niyaba). The passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir covers the deputy's handling of money, the validity of mixing funds, the disputed ruling on trading for profit, the treatment of surplus expenses, and the authority of the executor. The intra-Hanafi disagreement between the two imams and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the trading issue highlights a key point of differentiation in Hanafi fatawa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the deputy's permissible actions
The deputy may mix the Hajj funds with his own money (خلط) without invalidating the Hajj.
2
Analyze the trading dispute
If the deputy takes the money, trades, profits, and then performs Hajj: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say the Hajj does not suffice (لا يجزيه); Muhammad says it is valid.
3
Understand the rationale
Why do Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf invalidate? Likely because the deputy acted beyond his agency (trading not authorized), so the funds are not properly applied. Muhammad may consider the intention and eventual performance.
4
Determine the ruling on surplus
After Hajj, any surplus (fadil) belongs to the commissioner or his heirs, unless the deputy was authorized to gift it to himself.
5
Examine the deputy's authority to delay
The deputy may delay Hajj beyond the first year; specifying a year is for expediting, not restriction.
6
Outline the executor's authority
An executor (wasi) may perform Hajj personally if given absolute authority; otherwise must hire.
7
Apply the rules on expenses
Deputy's expenses are from the commissioner's money; staying 15 days or intending prolonged stay invalidates expense coverage.

Inherited Retaliation (Qisas) – Complete Right for Each Heir
Ruling on inherited qisas among multiple heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of inherited qisas to cases with multiple heirs of different ages and presence, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Each heir inherits a complete right to qisas; execution depends on age and consent of all.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a key rule of inherited retaliation (qisas). According to the Hanafi school, the right to demand or execute qisas passes to each heir as a complete, not fractional, entitlement. The rule then specifies two distinct scenarios: when an adult heir is present alongside a minor, and when all heirs are adults. Understanding this distinction is essential for a mufti dealing with retaliation claims.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the core principle: inherited qisas is established for each heir completely (ʿalā al-kamāl). This means the right is not divided proportionally but granted in full to every heir.
This is the foundational rule—memorize it as a qāʿida.
2
Distinguish the two scenarios
The text contrasts two cases: (1) adult heir and minor heir(s), (2) all heirs are adults. For each, note the different ruling on execution.
The key factor is the possibility of pardon (ʿafw).
3
Analyze the adult-minor scenario
The adult heir may execute qisas before the minor reaches puberty, with the ruler's permission. The minor's right can be forfeited by the guardian (ruler) who may pardon.
The minor's incapacity to pardon is the basis.
4
Analyze the all-adults scenario
If all heirs are adults, the one present cannot unilaterally execute in the absence of the others. This is agreed upon (ittifāqan) because the absent heir might choose to pardon.
Consent of all adult heirs is required for execution.
5
Determine the operative ruling for fatwa
The rule is agreed upon among the major Hanafi authorities (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad), so it is mufta bihi without internal dispute.
No tarjih needed; it is sound transmission.

Core Idea
Intercourse in Dar al-Islam always triggers either hadd or mahr – except in two cases of shubha or ownership.

This lesson examines a concise Hanafi legal maxim that governs the consequences of sexual intercourse within the Abode of Islam. The maxim establishes a default rule, then carves out two specific exceptions where neither the prescribed punishment (hadd) nor the bridal gift (mahr) applies. Understanding these exceptions requires applying the principle that hadd is averted by doubt (shubha) and that an invalid contract does not entitle mahr. The exceptions are drawn from later Hanafi fatwa works, illustrating how qawaid operate in practice.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Maxim
State the Arabic maxim and its literal meaning: 'Intercourse in Dar al-Islam is not free from hadd or mahr.' This is the default rule – any such intercourse either incurs a fixed punishment (if unlawful) or obligates a mahr (if lawful).
The maxim is a nathr (miscellaneous) qa'ida, sughra rank, covering criminal and marriage law.
2
Identify the Scope
The rule applies to all instances of sexual intercourse occurring within a territory where Islamic law is enforced (Dar al-Islam). It does not cover Dar al-Harb or ambiguous domains.
The term 'Dar al-Islam' is defined by the Hanafis as lands where Muslim rule and Islamic law prevail.
3

Exception 1: A man marries a mature woman without her guardian's permission (an invalid contract), and she consents to intercourse. No hadd, no mahr. The principle 'al-hudud tudra'u bi al-shubahat' (hadd penalties are averted by doubts) underpins both exceptions.

Consider a case analogous to the exceptions: if a man has intercourse with a woman under a mistaken belief of marriage (e.g., she claims to be his wife and he believes her), the Hanafi view may apply shubha to avert hadd. However, the mahr would be due as she is not under his ownership.

The Qa'ida of al-Qawl Lahu in Conditional Talaq and Its Exceptions

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Husband's word is presumed in conditional divorce, except when the wife alone knows the condition's occurrence.

In disputes surrounding conditional divorce (talaq) or manumission (itq), the Hanafi school applies a general rule: the statement of the one who imposed the condition is accepted. However, this rule has clear exceptions where the wife's personal knowledge overrides the presumption. This lesson unpacks the rule and its four exceptions, grounded in practical concerns of evidence and knowledge.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its scope
Articulate the general rule: 'al-qawl lahu' — the word of the husband (or the one who made the condition) is accepted in disputes about the occurrence of a condition for divorce or manumission, so long as the condition is not something uniquely knowable to the wife.
This rule relies on the fact that the condition was initiated by the husband and he is usually the one who knows its fulfilment.
2
Identify the first exception: maintenance
When the husband conditions divorce on non-receipt of maintenance for a month, and they dispute whether he sent it, the wife's statement is accepted regarding both the financial right and the divorce. Reason: she knows best whether she received maintenance.

If the husband divorces her according to the Sunnah (i.e., one revocable divorce in a pure state), and then claims he had intercourse during her menses (making the divorce irregular), the wife's denial of that claim is accepted.
She is the authority on her own menstruation.

Two exceptions involve a female slave: (a) the master claims she approached him after the 'iddah period (she denies, her word accepted); (b) the master suspends manumission on her divorce, gives her a choice, and disputes the timing of her choice. Repetition of Condition and Result in Conditional Talaq: Hanafi Qawa'id
Rulings on multiple conditions and multiple results in conditional divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on repetition of conditions and results to determine the number of divorces falling in various conditional talaq formulations.

Core Idea
Repeating the condition does not multiply the divorce if the result is stated once; repeating the result multiplies the divorce.

In Hanafi law, the wording of a conditional divorce can be complex. This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir clarifies how repetition of conditions and results, as well as conjunctions, affect the number of divorces. Understanding these maxims is essential for a mufti to correctly determine the outcome of such pronouncements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrasing pattern
Examine the statement to determine whether the condition (shart) or the result (jaza) is repeated. Count the instances of each.
Use the Arabic text to see repetition of words like 'if' or 'you are divorced'.
2
Apply the basic maxim
If the condition is repeated multiple times but the result is stated only once, then only one divorce falls when the condition occurs once. If the result is repeated, each occurrence triggers a divorce.
Example: 'If you enter, if you enter, you are divorced' -> one divorce. 'If you enter, you are divorced, you are divorced' -> two divorces.
3
Handle conjunctions (ʿaṭf)
When two wives are connected by waw, thumma, or fa, the first wife receives two divorces (one from the first utterance and one from the conjunction linking her to the second) and the second receives one.
This applies only when the first wife is explicitly divorced and then connected to another.
4
Distinguish iḍrāb (disjunction)
If the husband says 'you are divorced, then I add and confirm it', no extra divorce occurs without intention. Similarly, including the first wife with another in an idrab construction multiplies for the first only if intended.

The Rule of Continuous Attributes in Conditional Divorce

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A condition on a continuous attribute looks to the future; on a non-continuous one, it is void.

In Hanafi law, when a husband suspends divorce on an attribute of his wife, the validity depends on whether that attribute is inherently continuous. This lesson unpacks the governing maxim from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazā’ir and its practical applications in ta‘līq al-ṭalāq.

1
Identify the attribute and classify its continuity
Determine whether the attribute (waṣf) is inherently continuous and permanent (qā’im) in the wife, such as menstruation or sickness, or whether it is not continuous (e.g., health in a healthy woman).
A continuous attribute is one that, once present, remains present by its nature (e.g., sickness lasts until recovery).
2
Apply the maxim to the condition
If the attribute is continuous, the condition is valid and looks to its future existence. If the attribute is not continuous, the condition is considered invalid (void) because there is no meaningful future state to attach to.
The maxim: 'ما يمتد فلدوامه حكم الابتداء وإلا لا'.

The word 'in' (إن) implies postponement unless there is a contextual indicator of immediacy (qarīna al-fawr). This affects timing but not validity.
4

5
Identify exceptions
Past-tense formulations like 'you are divorced yesterday' are exceptions because they do not depend on future existence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A partially freed slave is like a mukatab except in three specific rulings.

In Hanafi law, a slave who has been partially freed (e.g., one of two owners frees his share) occupies an intermediate status. This lesson presents the sub-maxim that he is treated like a mukatab (a slave under a contract of manumission) except in three matters. The maxim is a dabt (restrictive sub-maxim) within I'tq.

The first exception: if the mu‘taq al-ba‘d becomes unable to earn (e.g., due to disability), he is not returned to slavery. Contrast: a mukatab who cannot pay his kitaba can be returned to slavery.
3
Identify the second exception: combined sale
Second exception: if a mu‘taq al-ba‘d is sold together with a full slave (qinn), the invalidity of the sale (due to the partial freedom) extends to the full slave. For a mukatab, the invalidity does not extend.
This applies in Buyu' contracts.

Identify the third exception: homicide without property

Twins: The Second Follows the First (Qawida)
Sub-maxim on twins with furuat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim regarding twins, apply it to manumission, torts, and postpartum purity, and identify the exceptions.

Core Idea
For legal rulings, twins are considered like one child; the second twin follows the first.

When a woman gives birth to twins, Hanafi law treats them as a single legal unit in certain contexts. This lesson presents the sub-maxim that the second twin follows the first in its rulings, with applications to manumission of a fetus, tort liability, and postpartum purity. Understanding this maxim helps muftis resolve cases involving multiple births.

Guided Path
1
Learn the maxim
Memorize: 'Al-taw’amān ka al-walad al-wāḥid fa al-thānī yattabi‘ al-ūlā fī aḥkāmih' (Twins are like one child; the second follows the first in its rulings).
2
Apply to manumission of a fetus
Case: A man frees what is in his slave-woman's womb. She gives birth to twins. If the first is born prematurely (less than 6 months) and the second at full term, the second is also freed because it follows the first. If the first is full-term, neither is freed (since the first was not a fetus at the time of manumission).
3
Apply to torts (ghurra)
Case: A pregnant woman is struck and miscarries twins. If the first twin is born before the mother's death and the second after, only one ghurra (compensation) is due for the first twin, not the second.
4
Apply to postpartum bleeding (nifas)
Case: A woman gives birth to twins. Nifas (postpartum bleeding) begins with the delivery of the first twin. Any bleeding after the second twin is not considered nifas.

Partner's Manumission During Terminal Illness

Core Idea

In Hanafi law, if one co-owner frees his share of a jointly owned slave, the other owner can demand payment of the value of his share, provided the freeing partner is wealthy. 3
Understand the opposing view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that liability applies regardless of illness; the exception is not valid.
4
Analyze the basis of difference
Abu Hanifa considers terminal illness transactions as akin to bequests; freeing is like a gift that does not create a debt against the freeer. The Sahibayn analogize to the healthy case.

The text states 'at the Imam contrary to the two', indicating that Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
If the number is a slip, it's ignored; the exception applies to actual slaves.

In the Hanafi school, the wording of manumission is taken seriously. However, when a person makes a mistake in counting, the law looks to the speaker's true intent. This lesson examines two scenarios where the stated number exceeds owned slaves, and how the exception particle 'illā' interacts with that error.

3

If the computed number exceeds actual slaves, the exception still operates: all actual slaves are freed because the speaker intended to free that many.

If the number is presented as an explanation (tafsir) of 'my slaves' and it contradicts reality, the number is a slip and is ignored.

Appraisers' Disagreement and the Kitabah Exception
Ruling on resolving appraisal disagreement with a kitabah exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the general rule of taking the middle value when appraisers disagree and identify the kitabah exception where the highest value is paid.

Core Idea
When appraisers disagree, take the middle; in kitabah, the slave pays the highest.

When a person is required to pay a value (e.g., for a lost or damaged item, or for a slave's freedom), appraisers may give different estimates. The Hanafi jurists have established a general rule: the middle value is taken. However, there is an important exception in the case of a kitabah contract, where a slave is buying his own freedom. Here, the slave must pay the highest appraisal. This lesson unpacks both the rule and its exception, grounding you in a practical maxim for valuation disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize when a value is due from a person and appraisers are called. This often occurs in cases of liability for property or in manumission contracts.
The rule applies to any valuation dispute, not only slaves.
2
State the General Rule
When appraisers disagree, the Qadi (judge) rules by the middle value. This is based on justice and avoiding extremes.
Middle value means the median, not the average.
3
Identify the Exception
If the valuation is for a kitabah contract (where a slave is valued to determine the price of freedom), the slave must pay the highest appraised value to be freed.
Kitabah is a contract of manumission by payment.
4
Understand the Rationale
The exception exists because the slave is eager for freedom, so imposing the highest value protects the master's right and prevents the slave from undervaluing himself.
This is a Hanafi specific ruling; other schools may differ.
5

When a person makes an oath, the words used determine its scope. Learning Objective

Core Idea
The later condition overrides the earlier when it intervenes.

In Hanafi legal theory, when a condition is inserted after another condition in a statement, the later condition takes precedence. This maxim, known as 'al-shart mata i'tarada 'ala al-shart, yuqaddam al-mu'akhkhar', is crucial for interpreting oaths, contracts, and other conditional statements. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Insulting Abu Bakr and Umar is kufr; preferring Ali is bid'ah unless hatred or denial of caliphate makes it kufr.

Acts of Worship and Imitation: Idolatry, Mockery, and the Zunnar
Lists concrete examples of kufr and their conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify acts that constitute kufr through outward mockery or imitation of disbelief, such as idol worship, disrespecting the Quran, and wearing the zunnar, and understand that inner intention does not mitigate when the act is clearly kufr.

Core Idea
Outward acts of worshiping idols, mocking Islamic symbols, or imitating disbelievers are kufr regardless of inner belief.

This concluding section of the passage focuses on actions that are clearly kufr by their very nature. It covers idol worship, mockery of the Prophet, disrespect to the Quran and mosque, and wearing the zunnar (girdle of non-Muslims). The key principle here is that such acts are kufr even if the person claims he was only mocking or did not actually believe in that religion. The mufti must recognize that in these cases, the outward act overrides any inner disclaimer.

1
List all acts mentioned
The passage mentions: worship of an idol, mocking a Prophetic saying, uncovering private parts disrespectfully, making an image of Jesus for prostration, taking an idol for that purpose, disrespecting the Quran or mosque, using filth with intention of disrespect, and wearing the zunnar (girdle of Jews/Christians).
2
Identify the common element
All these acts involve either clear shirk (idol worship), mockery of Islamic symbols, or imitation of disbelievers in their distinctive religious practices.
3
Apply the principle: outward act overrides inner state
The text explicitly states 'ولا اعتبار بما في قلبه' for idol worship, and for wearing the zunnar, even if the person says 'I was mocking and I do not believe in their religion', it is still kufr.
4
Distinguish from earlier lessons
Unlike the previous lesson where intention could affect the ruling (e.g., saying 'I won't pray' only kufr if juhud), here no such inquiry is needed. The acts are inherently kufr.
5
Understand the mufti's role for such cases
When a mufti encounters such acts, he should rule kufr immediately without needing to ask for the person's inner belief, because the act itself is a clear violation of Islamic sanctity.

Mudaraba vs Bida'a: Profit Share Determines Contract Nature
Profit sharing rules in partnership from Ashbah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mudaraba and bida'a based on the profit share condition and state the resulting contract type.

Core Idea
Profit share determines contract nature: worker's share > capital = mudaraba; investor's share > capital = bida'a.

This passage from Ashbah defines a critical boundary in Islamic partnership law. The allocation of profit beyond capital proportion triggers different legal consequences. The mufti must distinguish mudaraba (investment partnership) from bida'a (bilateral trade) to advise correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: worker's profit share is more than his capital contribution, and investor's profit share is more than his capital contribution.
These are the only two possibilities considered.
2
For worker excess: mudaraba
If the worker is stipulated to get more profit than his capital proportion, the condition is valid and the contract is mudaraba.
This is permitted because the worker provides labor.
3
For investor excess: bida'a
If the investor is stipulated to get more profit than his capital proportion, the condition is invalid and the contract becomes bida'a, where each party takes back his capital.
The investor cannot take extra without labor risk.
4
Understand the rationale
Mudaraba allows the worker to earn from his labor; bida'a simply returns capital to both, as the excess profit claim fails.
The worker in bida'a may get a fixed wage or nothing, but not share.

Partner Actions, Restrictions, and Dispute Resolution
Practical rules and dispute resolution from Ashbah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on partner liability, permission to travel, and dispute resolution between capital owner and mudarib.

Core Idea
Partners must agree on major actions; in disputes, the mudarib's word is taken over capital owner, but principal's word in agency.

This segment of Kitab al-Sharika addresses practical issues arising in partnerships: work division, conditional purchases, travel restrictions, and disputes. A mufti must know these rules to resolve conflicts fairly and advise on permissible actions.

Guided Path
1
Analyze work division
If one partner works without the other, profit is shared regardless of excuse. However, if three accept a job without a partnership contract and only one works, he gets only his share of the wage.
Formal partnership ensures profit sharing even with unequal work.
2
Conditional purchase agreements
A statement like 'what I purchase today is between us' followed by acceptance validates a partnership. Similarly, including someone in a purchase after the fact is valid if accepted before taking possession.
This creates a partnership retroactively.
3
Restrictions on partners
A partner can prohibit his co-partner from going out or selling on credit. Neither may travel without the other's permission; if travel without permission leads to loss, there is no liability for goods that have no carrying cost or maintenance.
Restrictions protect the partnership.
4
Partnership with dhimmi
Partnership with a dhimmi (non-Muslim subject) is makruh (disliked), not invalid.
It is permissible but discouraged.
5
Dispute resolution
In disputes between capital owner (rabb al-mal) and mudarib about the terms, the mudarib's word is taken. In agency (wakala), the principal's word is taken. In disputes between master and slave's creditors, the creditors' word is taken.
This favors the agent in mudaraba and the principal in agency.

The Maxim: Conditions of the Waqif Are Like the Text of the Lawgiver
States a legal maxim followed by five exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim 'shart al-waqif ka nass al-shari'' and identify at least three exceptions where the condition is not followed along with the legal reasoning.

Core Idea
The waqif's condition is as binding as divine law — but five exceptions show when overriding it serves a higher legal purpose.

This lesson examines a foundational maxim for waqf management and fatwa. While the condition of the waqf founder is generally inviolable, Hanafi jurists have carved out specific exceptions where public interest or legal principle takes precedence. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for muftis and judges when applying the maxim.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim and its authority
Learn the Arabic text 'شرط الواقف كنص الشارع' and its meaning: the condition of the waqif is like the text of the Lawgiver in binding force. Recognize that this maxim is well-established in Hanafi fiqh and found in later works like al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair.
The maxim establishes a strong default: conditions must be followed unless they conflict with higher principles.
2
Identify the general rule and the five exceptions
The maxim is general, followed by the word 'except' (إلا). List the five cases: (1) stipulation preventing dismissal of nazir; (2) restriction on lease term; (3) stipulation of recitation at grave; (4) stipulation of charity to a specific asker at a specific mosque; (5) stipulation of specific food items. Note that in each case, the condition is not followed as stipulated.
The exceptions are contained in 'إلا في مسائل' — so the maxim remains the rule, and the exceptions are limited.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning behind each exception
For each exception, understand why the condition is overridden: (1) the judge has authority to remove an unqualified nazir; (2) public interest (people unwilling or benefit to poor) gives the judge the power; (3) recitation at graves is not a valid act in waqf; (4) the caretaker must give charity to any deserving person; (5) cash value is permissible, with a differing view. Recognize that overriding is allowed only by the judge or caretaker as specified.
Each exception involves a conflict with a higher legal principle: necessity, public interest, invalidity of the act, or flexibility in charity administration.
