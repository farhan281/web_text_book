The Mufti's Humility in Fiqh vs Certainty in Creed
Ruling on responding about madhhab in fiqh vs. creed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rasm rule that distinguishes fiqh answers (with epistemic humility) from creed answers (with definitive truth claims).

Core Idea
In fiqh, acknowledge fallibility; in creed, affirm absolute truth — never confuse the two.

A mufti's words carry weight. When asked about differences with other madhhabs in subsidiary fiqh matters, the Hanafi tradition commands a humble formulation: 'our view is correct but may contain error; theirs is erroneous but may contain truth.' But in matters of creed, this humility is replaced by firm affirmation of one's own position. This lesson unpacks that rule from al-Musaffa, clarifying why the distinction matters for proper fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Domains
Recognise that the rule divides questions into two categories: subsidiary matters of fiqh (furu') and matters of creed ('aqidah). The mufti's response must differ based on the domain.
Furu' includes all rulings derived by ijtihad; 'aqidah includes foundational beliefs like tawhid.
2
State the Fiqh Response Formula
For fiqh: 'Our madhhab is correct but may contain error; the opponent's madhhab is erroneous but may contain correct.' This reflects the principle that a mujtahid may err or be correct (al-mujtahid yukhti' wa yusib).
The phrase 'sawab yahtamil al-khata' is the key.
3
State the Creed Response Formula
For creed: 'The truth is what we are upon; falsehood is what our opponents are upon.' This is absolute, because there is only one correct belief in foundational matters.
No room for 'possibly correct' in 'aqidah.
4
Understand the Rationale
The reason for the difference: categorical statements in fiqh would contradict the principle that ijtihad can yield multiple valid results. In 'aqidah, truth is unitary and ijtihad does not apply.
This preserves both scholarly humility and doctrinal integrity.
5
Apply to a Real Fatwa Scenario
When a layperson asks about a fiqh difference, use the humble formula. When asked about a creedal issue (e.g., attributes of Allah), use the definitive formula. Practice with examples.
The rule applies to the mufti's own madhhab compared to others.

Commentary
This is a rasm rule (methodological rule for muftis) from al-Musaffa. It distinguishes between fiqh (furu') and 'aqidah (usul). In fiqh, multiple valid opinions exist because of legitimate ijtihad; thus a mufti should not be dogmatic. He says 'our madhhab is correct (for us) but possibly wrong, and theirs is wrong (for us) but possibly correct' — this reflects the principle that a mujtahid may err or be correct. In 'aqidah, there is only one truth; so one must affirm one's own position as truth and the opponent's as falsehood. This rule prevents arrogance in fiqh and maintains principle in creed.

Key Terms
صواب يحتمل الخطأ (sawāb yaḥtamil al-khaṭa): A statement acknowledging that a ruling may be correct but still open to the possibility of error.
خطأ يحتمل الصواب (khaṭa yaḥtamil al-ṣawāb): A statement acknowledging that an opposing ruling may be erroneous but could be correct from their ijtihad.
