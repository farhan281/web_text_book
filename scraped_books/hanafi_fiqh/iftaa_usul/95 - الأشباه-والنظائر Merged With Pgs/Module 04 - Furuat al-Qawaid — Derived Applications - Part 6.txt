Core Idea
A nazir may cancel a waqf lease, except when the lessee was a prior nazir or when rent was prepaid.

This lesson presents a concise legal maxim on waqf lease management: the nazir (administrator) generally has the authority to cancel a lease, but this power is restricted in two specific situations. Understanding these exceptions is essential for issuing correct fatwas on waqf property disputes.

Cancellation is not allowed if the contracting party (the lessee) was a nazir before the current nazir. The reasoning is implied from the jurists' comments.

The exceptions protect rights: the prior nazir's status or prepayment creates a vested interest that should not be undone by cancellation.

This shows the limits of the nazir's authority.

Four Exceptions to the Prohibition of Istibdal (Substitution of Waqf)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule regarding substitution of a productive waqf and identify the four exceptions as per the Hanafi school, including the intra-madhhab difference on the fourth exception.

Core Idea
Istibdal of a productive waqf is prohibited unless one of four specific exceptions applies.

This lesson examines a key sub-maxim (dabt) in Hanafi waqf law: substitution of a productive waqf is generally not allowed. However, the fuqaha have identified four exceptional cases where istibdal becomes permissible. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for a mufti when advising on waqf management and disputes.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Begin by stating the baseline: 'استبدال الوقف العامر لا يجوز' – substitution of a productive waqf is not permissible. This rule protects the perpetuity and purpose of the waqf.
This rule is from the Hanafi school and is the starting point for any fatwa on waqf substitution.
2
List the four exceptions
Memorize the four exceptions as mentioned in the text: (1) condition by the waqif, (2) irreversible damage by a usurper making the land unusable, (3) the usurper denies the seizure and there is no evidence, (4) someone offers a better substitute with higher revenue and quality (Abu Yusuf's view).
The third exception is referenced from al-Khaniyyah, a Hanafi fatwa collection.
3
Identify the authority for each exception
For the first three exceptions, there is general agreement among the Hanafi imams. The fourth exception is specifically attributed to Imam Abu Yusuf, indicating an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
The source cites Fatawa Qari al-Hidayah for the fourth exception.
4
Analyze the intra-madhhab difference
Note that Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani do not permit substitution merely for a better offer. Thus, when issuing a fatwa, the mufti must identify which position is relied upon (mufta bihi).
The majority position is that substitution for betterment is not allowed.
5
Apply the exceptions in a hypothetical case
Consider a waqf land that has been flooded by a usurper and is now a swamp. The mutawalli (administrator) may substitute it with another land after compensating the value. This matches the second exception.
Ensure the conditions of the exception are fully met before issuing a permissive fatwa.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A waqf must be leased at market rent; exceptions only when vacancy threatens or the shortfall is trivial.

This lesson focuses on a specific legal maxim (qa'idah) from the Hanafi school regarding waqf management. The rule prohibits leasing a waqf property for less than its fair market rent. However, two narrow exceptions are recognized: when there is no demand at the market rate, or when the shortfall is insignificant. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for a mufti to protect waqf assets while avoiding harmful vacancy.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the General Rule
State the prohibition: 'Leasing a waqf for less than ujrat al-mithl is not permissible.' This protects the benefit of the waqf.
Ujrat al-mithl is the prevailing market rent for similar properties.
2
Learn the First Exception
If no one is willing to lease the property except at a lower rate, then leasing below market is permissible to prevent total loss (vacancy).
This exception is based on necessity (darurah) to avoid loss of the waqf's benefit.
3
Learn the Second Exception
If the deficiency compared to market rent is minor (nuqsan yaseer), the lease is permissible. What constitutes 'yaseer' is determined by custom ('urf).
This exception prevents impractical strictness – small differences are overlooked.

The answer depends on whether the stipend is categorized as a gift (silah) — which lapses — or as rent (ujrah) — which is earned and thus inheritable. The silah view analogizes the stipend to a gift: it is not a right of the employee until delivery. The ujrah view analogizes it to rent: it is earned day by day. The passage does not elaborate further, but the analogy is implicit.

Al-Bughyah affirms that the stipend for waqf functions is inherited (i.e., does not lapse), except for the judge's salary. This is the tarjih within the text.

Priority in Endowment Distribution and Nature of Stipend
Rules for priority among endowment recipients and classification of stipends

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida for prioritizing endowment income to identify which functionaries receive priority and classify the legal nature of stipends (jāmiqiyya) as resembling wages, gift, or charity.

Core Idea
When endowment funds are tight, maintenance comes first; then the most essential personnel — imam and school teacher — take priority over others.

This passage presents a key Hanafi maxim derived from al-Hawi al-Qudsi, guiding muftis on how to prioritize distribution of limited endowment income. It establishes a clear hierarchy: physical maintenance first, then personnel essential to the endowment's function. The text also dissects the legal nature of stipends, determining entitlements and whether payments are revocable.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its scope
Identify the core qawida: priority is given to maintenance (ʿimāra) of the endowment from its income, then to what is closest to maintenance and more general in benefit. This applies when funds are insufficient to cover all needs.
The maxim is from al-Hawi al-Qudsi, a key Hanafi reference.
2
Identify the first tier of priority
The first priority is physical maintenance (repairs, construction), regardless of the endower's conditions. This includes all that is necessary to keep the endowment operational.
Even if the endower specified equal distribution, maintenance still comes first.
3
Apply the maxim to personnel
The second tier includes personnel closest to maintenance: imam, school teacher (mudarris), stoker, servant. They receive sufficient stipends. Then muezzins and time-keepers are included under the imam. The text clarifies that a school teacher is more essential than a mosque teacher because absence disrupts the school.
The expression 'then' (ثم) indicates a hierarchy.
4
Distinguish included vs excluded roles
Roles like overseer (shadd) during maintenance, scribe, tax-collector, water-carrier are included. Roles like witness, direct overseer outside maintenance, police, absentee clerk, librarian are excluded as non-essential.
The mufti must assess each role's connection to the endowment's core purpose.
5
Classify the legal nature of stipends
Stipends (jāmiqiyya) have three legal aspects: resembling wages (ujrah) – earned by work, permissible for the rich; resembling a gift (ṣilah) – not reclaimable after receipt; resembling charity (ṣadaqah) – permissible only for the needy. The mufti must determine which aspect governs based on context.
For example, if the stipend is for a specific time and the recipient dies mid-period, the portion for the remaining time is not reclaimed (gift aspect).

This passage is a qawida (maxim) with its furuat (applications). Priority Order in Waqf Expenditure
Explains the priority order for waqf expenditure and applies it to various roles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida for waqf disbursement priority to determine which beneficiaries receive first when revenue is limited.

Core Idea
Maintenance of the waqf property comes first, then those closest to that maintenance, then general utilities.

When waqf revenue is insufficient to cover all expenses, a clear priority order is needed. The Hanafi school relies on the maxim that spending begins with what is closest to the physical maintenance ('imāra) and most general in benefit. This lesson unpacks that maxim through the detailed furu' (applications) provided in the text, showing how different personnel and items are ranked.

The Triple Nature of Waqf Stipends and Apportionment upon Death
Analyzes the triple legal nature of waqf stipends and the rule for apportionment upon death before harvest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the triple legal nature of a waqf stipend (jāmakiyya) and apply the rule for apportioning it when a beneficiary dies mid-year before the crop is harvested.

Core Idea
A waqf stipend is not pure wages; it combines rent, gift, and charity to address refundability and permissibility for rich recipients.

The stipend (jāmakiyya) paid to waqf functionaries has a complex legal nature. It resembles rent (ujra) because it is compensation for service, but it also resembles a gift (sila) because if the recipient dies after receiving it, the remainder of the year is not reclaimed. Additionally, it resembles charity (sadaqa) because the waqf is valid even if the recipient is rich, which would not be the case for a pure rental. This lesson unpacks this triple analysis and shows how it affects the distribution of stipends when a beneficiary dies mid-year before the agricultural crop is harvested—a common scenario in agrarian waqfs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three shibh (resemblances)
From the text: jāmakiyya has shibh al-ujra (rent), shibh al-sila (gift), and shibh al-sadaqa (charity). Understand that each resemblance addresses a different legal issue.
The text says 'فاعتبرنا شبه الأجرة باعتبار زمن المباشرة وما يقابله من المعلوم' for rent; 'شبه الصلة لعدم الاسترداد' for gift; 'شبه الصدقة لتصحيح أصل الوقف' for charity.
2
Explain the ruling on death after receiving the full stipend
Because of the gift aspect, if the recipient receives the salary and then dies or is dismissed, the portion for the remaining period is not taken back. This is unlike rent, which would require refund for unperformed service.
The text: 'إذا قبض المستحق المعلوم ثم مات أو عزل فإنه لا يسترد منه حصة ما بقي من السنة'.
3
Explain the ruling on the waqf being valid for rich recipients
Because of the charity aspect, the waqf can legitimately pay rich functionaries; otherwise, if it were pure rent, paying a rich person would be invalid as rent requires a valid contract, but waqf is a charity.
The text: 'شبه الصدقة لتصحيح أصل الوقف فإنه لا يصح على الأغنياء ابتداء'.
4
Apply the hybrid nature to death before harvest
When a teacher dies mid-year before the crop comes, the stipend is apportioned based on the period of service actually performed, not on when the crop is harvested. This differs from the rule for children in a family waqf, where entitlement depends on being alive at harvest time.
The text: 'ينبغي أن ينظر وقت قسمة الغلة إلى مدة مباشرته... ويعطى بحساب مدته'.
5
Formulate a fatwa for a similar scenario
Given a modern waqf that pays salaries from investment returns, if an employee dies mid-year, apply the same principle: apportion based on service period, not on when the returns are distributed.
The triple nature ensures fairness: the estate gets the earned portion, and the remaining goes to the successor.

Distributing Waqf Proceeds: Employees vs Children
Ruling on how to distribute stipends from waqf produce based on time served

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida that waqf employees are paid by service duration and distinguish it from the rule for children, and construct a fatwa for a waqf distribution scenario.

Core Idea
Pay employees by time served, children by produce arrival — a crucial distinction of justice.

Waqf income distribution can be tricky when employees serve for part of a year or die before the harvest. This passage clarifies a key Hanafi rule: for employees (teachers, jurists, etc.), stipends are prorated based on the actual months of service, ignoring when the produce is actually collected. For children, however, entitlement depends on being alive when the produce arrives. Understanding this distinction is essential for muftis advising on waqf administration.

Guided Path

The passage contrasts employees (teacher, jurist, functionary) with children of beneficiaries. Employees are paid for work; children are recipients akin to inheritors.
Children here means offspring of the waqf's beneficiaries, not workers.

Contrast with a child born after 5 months: if installments exist, he misses the first installment.

Employees are compensated for labor already rendered, so their entitlement vests as they work. Children's entitlement is based on existence at the time of benefit receipt, following inheritance-like principles.
This distinction aligns with justice (الأعدل) and fiqh appropriateness (الأشبه بالفقه).

The Rule of Trustee Delegation: Health vs. Illness
Qawida with furu' application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida 'التفويض في الصحة لا يصح إلا بالشرط، وفي المرض يصح' to waqf delegation scenarios and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
A trustee’s delegation is only valid if the waqif allowed it, or if it happens during terminal illness.

This lesson examines a pivotal qawida governing waqf trusteeship: when can a trustee (nāẓir) delegate his authority to another? The principle distinguishes sharply between the trustee’s state of health and terminal illness, with an critical exception when the waqif explicitly grants the power to delegate. We will walk through the qawida, its exceptions, and a series of fatwa applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qawida and its scope
The central maxim is: 'التفويض في الصحة لا يصح إلا بالشرط، وفي المرض يصح' — delegation in health is invalid without a condition, but in terminal illness it is valid. Note that this applies to the trustee of a waqf, not the waqif himself.
This is a minor (sughra) maxim that restricts the trustee's default authority.
2
Understand the exception (waqif's condition)
The waqif can stipulate that the trustee has the power to delegate. If such a condition exists, delegation is valid absolutely, even in health. This shows the waqif's stipulation overrides the default restriction.
The trustee's authority is derivative; the waqif's condition expands it.
3
Apply the rule to health vs. illness
Without a condition, delegation in health is invalid — the delegate does not take over. In terminal illness (marad al-mawt), delegation is valid, analogous to a will (wasiyya). The trustee's deathbed act is given effect.
Terminal illness cases are treated like wasiyya because the trustee's concern for the waqf is assumed genuine.
4
Consider the power of dismissal after valid delegation
If the delegation is valid (e.g., by condition or illness), does the trustee retain the power to dismiss the delegate? The author extends the analogy to wasiyya: the trustee should have that power unless the waqif also granted dismissal power explicitly.
Al-Tarsusi notes that if the waqif gave both delegation and dismissal authority, the trustee can dismiss.
5
Review the furu' (applied cases)
Three furu' are given: (1) delegation in health without condition -> invalid, authority returns to ruler upon trustee's death; (2) delegation in illness without condition -> valid, delegate remains; (3) trustee vacates for another without valid delegation -> upon death authority goes to the next stipulated beneficiary.
Each fur' demonstrates the practical consequence of the qawida.

Beneficiaries' Rights During Waqf Repair
Maxim and its application to furuat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'لا حق للمستحقين في الغلة زمن التعمير' to determine whether cut stipends become debts, how surplus in later years is distributed, and when the trustee becomes personally liable.

Core Idea
A beneficiary's right to endowment revenue is subordinate to the necessity of repair; cuts do not create future debts.

Endowment management often requires balancing the needs of the physical asset against the entitlements of beneficiaries. This lesson examines a key maxim from the Hanafi school that governs this tension: during a year when repairs are necessary, beneficiaries have no claim to the revenue—their stipends may be cut without creating a future liability. We will explore the reasoning behind this rule, its application in surplus distribution, and the important exception where the trustee may be held personally liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim and Its Scope
State the maxim: 'لا حق للمستحقين في الغلة زمن التعمير'. Recognize that it applies specifically to the period when the endowment is under repair (ta'mir) and the entire revenue is consumed by that need.
The maxim is a sub-maxim (dabt) that limits the general rule that beneficiaries are entitled to revenue.
2
Apply to the First Fur: Stipend Cuts
When the trustee cuts stipends to fund repairs, those cut amounts do not become a debt owed to the beneficiaries. The act of cutting is valid because the beneficiaries had no right to that revenue during the repair period.
This holds even if repairs are not actually carried out; the right is tied to the period of need.
3
Apply to the Second Fur: Surplus in Later Years
If in the second year there is a surplus after paying that year's stipends, that surplus cannot be used to compensate the earlier cut. The cut was not a debt, so no compensation is owed. The surplus must go to the next stipulated category (e.g., freeing slaves) as per the waqif's condition.
The distinction: a debt would need repayment; a cut during repair is simply a loss of entitlement.
4
Recognize the Istithna (Exception)
If the trustee pays out stipends despite knowing that repairs are needed, he becomes personally liable for the amounts paid. This is because he acted against the waqf's interest by depleting funds needed for preservation.
This exception is derived from al-Dhakhira and invokes the maxim 'الضرر يزال' (harm must be removed).
5
Analyze the Fatwa Given
In the case where the waqif stipulated surplus for freeing slaves, and beneficiaries were cut in a repair year, the surplus in the second year goes to the slaves, not to compensate the beneficiaries. This directly follows from the maxim: the cut created no debt, so the surplus is simply the revenue of the second year, which goes to the next category.
This illustrates how the maxim resolves real fatwa scenarios.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Liability retroactively transfers ownership to the liable party, barring recourse unless the payer was not a transgressor.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi maxim governing cases where a person pays money without authority and is later held liable. The maxim states that items subject to liability become the property of the liable party retroactively to the time of transgression. This has profound implications for whether the liable party can recover the payment from the recipient. Through several furuat and exceptions from waqf, amanat, ghasb, and qadi authorization, we will see how the concept of 'transgression' (ta'addi) determines the outcome.

List the three furuat from the text: trustee paying during repair need, depositary spending on parents, usurper hiding item.
Each case results in liability and retroactive ownership.
3

For each case, explain why the maxim bars recourse: the payment becomes the liable party's own property.
The liable party cannot recover because he owns the payment at the time of transgression.
4

Two exceptions: trustee repays debt that later appeared (non-transgression) and payer under qadi authorization.
In these cases, the payer was not a transgressor, so he can recover.

The trustee in the waqf case is a transgressor, so cannot recover; the exceptions confirm the rule.

Core Idea
Waqf surplus must be preserved for future maintenance if the waqif stipulated saving; otherwise, distribute immediately.

Managing a waqf's income requires balancing current maintenance needs, future risks, and the beneficiaries' rights. This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir provides detailed guidance: what happens when income is cut for maintenance in one year, and a surplus appears later. If the nazir pays full stipends despite needing maintenance, he is liable (yadman). This is analogous to other fiduciary cases: a depositee spending on the depositor's parents without permission cannot recover because the payment retroactively becomes his own property.

4

The nazir can recover payments if he was not transgressing (e.g., when a debt later appears and he had paid in good faith). This contrasts with cases where he knowingly violated the maintenance priority.
Refer to the al-Qunyah and al-Atabiyya examples.

The mu'tamad (relied-upon) position is that of Abu Ja'far: if enough is saved for future maintenance, distribute the rest. This is the preferred choice (al-ikhtiyar) and should guide contemporary fatwa.

Judicial return is rescission, but hawala stands and buyer's sale of movable fails.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents a crucial qa'ida (maxim) regarding the legal effect of a judicial decree for the return of a defective item. While such a decree is treated as a rescission (faskh) annulling the sale retroactively, the Hanafi jurists identified two specific situations where this general ruling does not apply in full. Understanding these exceptions deepens your grasp of how contractual obligations interact and how judicial intervention shapes them.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Identify the default effect of returning a defective item by court order: it is a rescission (faskh) for all purposes.
The passage opens with this rule.
2
First Exception – Hawala
If the seller had already transferred the price via hawala before the return, the hawala remains valid despite the rescission.
The hawala is a separate contract; the rescission does not void it.
3

Learning Objective

This lesson explores a key maxim from the Hanafi tradition: when a court orders the return of a defective item, the legal effect is a rescission (faskh) of the sale as if it never occurred. However, two important exceptions limit this principle, one involving hawala (assignment of debt) and another involving resale by the seller. Understanding these exceptions is critical for accurate fatwa issuance, as they prevent unintended nullification of separate contracts and protect buyer rights.

Guided Path

Possession in a fasid sale gives ownership, but with critical restrictions on use and specific exceptions.

When a sale is fasid (defective) but not batil, Hanafi law treats the transaction as capable of transferring ownership through possession (qabd). However, this rule is not absolute. The Maxim: Repeating a Contract Invalidates the Original
Maxim with furuat and istithna'at

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'every contract that is repeated and renewed, the second is void' and identify its exceptions (purchase, suretyship, lease) when issuing a fatwa.

Core Idea
Repeating a contract with the same parties voids the first – except purchase, suretyship, and lease.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi maxim about contract renewal. Understanding when a second contract invalidates the first is crucial for muftis dealing with repeated transactions. The maxim applies broadly to sulh, nikah, and hawala, but has important exceptions that every mufti must recognize.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim and Its Scope
State the maxim: 'Every contract that is repeated and renewed, the second is void.' Note that it applies to contracts between the same parties concerning the same subject matter.
The maxim is a wusta (intermediate) legal principle governing contracts.
2
Apply to Void Repetitions
Examine the three clear examples given: sulh (peace settlement) after sulh, nikah (marriage) after nikah, and hawala (debt transfer) after hawala. In each case, the second contract is void because it implies an intention to replace the first.
The sources cited are Jami' al-Fusulayn, al-Quniyya, and al-Talqih – all from the Zahir al-Riwayah corpus.
3
Analyze the Exceptions
Identify the exceptions: purchase after purchase (shira ba'd shira), suretyship after suretyship (kafala ba'd kafala), and lease after lease (ijarah ba'd ijarah). For each, understand why the second contract does not void the first or instead rescinds it.
The exception for shira has an internal ikhtilaf: some scholars allow it unconditionally, others only if the price differs.
4
Understand the Rationale Behind Exceptions
For shira, a second purchase is a new independent contract, not a mere repetition. For kafala, additional suretyship increases security. For ijarah, a second lease from the same lessee is treated as a rescission of the first, leaving only the second valid.
Hawala is different because it is a transfer; two transfers cannot coexist.
5

When a mufti encounters a case of repeated contracts, first determine the contract type. Apply the general rule: the second is void unless it falls under one of the exceptions. If it is purchase, verify the price condition. If suretyship or lease, the second is valid.
Always cite the relevant source (e.g., al-Quniyya for the restricted shira view) and state the mufta bihi.

Gift rules are stricter; once the donee is given possession, the gift is complete despite invalidity.

Understanding this boundary is essential for writing correct contracts and fatwas.

The condition for a valid exception is that the item must be capable of being the sole subject of a contract of the same type. For example, a room can be sold alone, so it can be excepted from the sale of a house.

Core Idea
A condition attached to a sale does not automatically invalidate it; Hanafi fiqh permits many beneficial conditions.

In Hanafi contract law, not every condition renders a sale invalid. This lesson examines a legal maxim from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir that lists thirty-two conditions that are permissible in a sale contract. Understanding this maxim allows a mufti to correctly assess contracts with stipulations and distinguish between valid and invalid conditions.

2
Compare with the 32 Permissible Categories
Check if the condition falls under one of the thirty-two types listed in the maxim. If it does, the sale remains valid; if not, proceed to the next step.
The list is not exhaustive but provides a strong indication of permissible conditions.
3
Evaluate the Condition's Effect
If the condition is not in the list, assess whether it contradicts the contract's essence (e.g., adds gharar, changes the object's identity) or serves a legitimate interest of the parties.
Conditions that are beneficial and do not corrupt the contract may still be permissible by extension.
4
Issue the Ruling
Conclude whether the sale is valid with the condition or if the condition is void. If the condition is invalid, the sale may still be valid if the condition can be severed; otherwise, the sale may be fasid.
In Hanafi fiqh, an invalid condition does not necessarily invalidate the entire sale; the contract may stand without the condition.

The Option of Sight for Unseen Purchases
Ruling on khiyar al-ru'ya for buyer who hasn't seen the item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim of khiyar al-ru'ya to determine when a buyer has the option to reject an unseen purchase and identify the exception when the seller delivers the item to the buyer's home.

Core Idea
The buyer of an unseen item retains the option of sight until he sees it, but delivery to his home restricts rejection to physical return.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi maxim governing khiyar al-ru'ya, the option of sight for a buyer who purchases an item without seeing it. The maxim provides a clear default ruling and an important exception that reflects how possession affects the option. Understanding this rule is essential for muftis evaluating modern sales disputes, especially in e-commerce and remote transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the buyer's situation
Determine whether the buyer ever saw the item at the time of contract, before it, or at the time of taking possession. If not, the default rule applies.
The three times are specified to cover all possibilities.
2
Apply the default rule
If the buyer never saw the item, the maxim grants him the option (khiyar) upon sight. He may accept or reject the sale.
This option exists even if not stipulated.
3
Check for the exception
Verify whether the seller physically carried the item to the buyer's house. This changes the ruling.
Delivery to the house is a specific condition that triggers the exception.
4
Determine the consequence of the exception
If the seller delivered to the buyer's house, the buyer cannot simply reject upon seeing; he must physically return the item to the seller to exercise the option.
This preserves the seller's right to possession after delivery.
5
Conclude the ruling
Based on the facts, state whether the buyer has the option and how it must be exercised.
If delivery occurred, the option is conditioned on physical return.

Sale by Non-Owner (Fuduli): Default Suspended, Three Exceptions Void
Qa'idah (maxim) with three exceptions (furu')

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three exceptions where a fuduli sale becomes void and apply the maxim to new cases involving unauthorized sales.

Core Idea
A sale by a non-owner is normally suspended, not void — except in three specific cases.

The Hanafi school holds that a sale by a non-owner (fuduli) is not automatically void; it is suspended (mawquf) pending ratification by the owner. However, this general rule has three exceptions where the sale becomes void outright (batil). This lesson unpacks those exceptions and their rationale, equipping you to distinguish between validly suspended and void fuduli sales.

Guided Path
1
State the default ruling
Recognize that the maxim begins with the general rule: 'بيع الفضولي موقوف' – sale by a non-owner is suspended. Understand that suspension means the contract's validity depends on the owner's subsequent ratification.
The default is not void; it is merely suspended.
2
Identify the three exceptions
The maxim lists three cases where the sale is void (فباطل). Extract each case from the text: (1) fuduli stipulates an option for the owner, (2) fuduli sells for himself (as if it were his own), (3) a usurper sells one of the owner's goods in exchange for another of the owner's goods.
Each exception has a specific reason that overrides the suspension.
3
Analyze the first exception
If the fuduli stipulates a khiyar (option) for the owner, the sale becomes void. Explain: The option contradicts the nature of suspension because it implies the fuduli had authority to grant an option, which he lacks.
Referenced source: al-Talqih.
4
Analyze the second exception
If the fuduli sells for himself (i.e., without disclosing that the property belongs to another), the sale is void. This is because he is acting as if he owns the property, which is a form of deception and usurpation.
Referenced source: al-Bada'i.
5
Analyze the third exception
If a usurper (ghasib) sells one of the owner's goods in exchange for another of the owner's goods, the sale is void. This involves a corruption of the exchange because the usurper is trading the owner's property without any real transfer of ownership.
Referenced source: Fath al-Qadir.

Commentary
This maxim deals with the sale of another's property without permission (fuduli sale). Normally such a sale is suspended (mawquf) pending ratification by the owner. However, in three cases the sale is void outright (batil): (1) if the fuduli stipulates an option (khiyar) for the owner – this makes it void because the owner's option is tied to his potential ratification, but if the fuduli stipulates it, it invalidates the suspension; (2) if the fuduli sells as if for himself (meaning he does not disclose that it belongs to another) – then it is void; (3) if a usurper (ghasib) sells one of the owner's commodities in exchange for another commodity belonging to the owner – this is also void. The author cites sources: al-Talqih, al-Bada'i, and Fath al-Qadir.

Key Terms
فضل (fuduli): A person who transacts in property without ownership or authorization
موقوف (mawquf): Suspended; a contract whose validity depends on subsequent ratification
خيار (khiyar): Option or right to cancel a contract within a specified period
