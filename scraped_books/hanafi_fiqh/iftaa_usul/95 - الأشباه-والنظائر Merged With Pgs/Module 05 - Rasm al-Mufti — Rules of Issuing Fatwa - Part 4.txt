Territorial and Temporal Jurisdiction of the Qadi
Principle of territorial jurisdiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the judiciary is specialized by time and place, and identify the disputed cases regarding real estate outside the qadi's jurisdiction.

Core Idea
A judge's authority is limited to his appointed territory; judgments outside are invalid.

This lesson focuses on the final part of the passage, which establishes the principle that judicial authority is strictly territorial and temporal. A judge appointed for one location cannot render valid judgments elsewhere, especially for real estate. The passage also notes a dispute about real estate outside his jurisdiction and addresses the validity of attestations outside the territory.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the principle
Read: 'القضاء يتخصص بالزمان والمكان'. What does this mean?
Judicial authority is limited to the time and place of appointment.
2
Apply to a general case
If a judge is appointed for city A and issues a ruling in city B, what is the general ruling?
The ruling is invalid because it is outside his place of jurisdiction.
3
Examine the exception for real estate
The text mentions a dispute when the real estate is located outside the judge's jurisdiction. What are the positions?
Al-Kanz says invalid; al-Khulasa says valid; Qadikhan confined to the dispute.
4
Understand the scope of the dispute
The passage clarifies that the dispute is only about real estate, not about movables or debts. Why might real estate be special?
Real estate is fixed in a location, so it is more closely tied to the local jurisdiction.
5
Apply the rule for attestation
If a judge gives a ruling in his jurisdiction but attests to it outside, is the attestation valid?
No, the attestation is invalid per al-Quniyya.

Commentary
This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir compiles several rulings concerning the office of the qadi (judge) and related procedures, typical of the Rasm al-Mufti genre. It begins by stating that appointment of a representative (tawkil) before the judge is permissible even without an opponent, provided the judge knows the principal's name. Then it addresses removal: a judge is not removed by apostasy or sin; similarly, the one responsible for Friday prayer (wali al-jum'a) is not removed upon mere knowledge of removal but only upon the successor's arrival. There is scholarly dispute about whether a judge is removed by a letter of removal; the text implies that unless the letter explicitly states 'when my book reaches you, I have removed you,' removal does not take effect without the actual receipt. Regarding writing documents: Abu Yusuf holds that the judge should not write a document of acquittal (ibra') without the opponent present, while Muhammad allows it—but they agree on writing documents of receipt (istifa') and divorce. The judge may send a representative to a secluded woman (mukhaddara) for her lawsuit and oath. Minors are not required to take oaths in lawsuits, and even if interdicted (mahjur), they are not brought before the judge. However, a slave may be sworn in even if interdicted, and if he refuses (nukul), judgment is given against him, and it is enforceable after emancipation. The judge should not administer an oath regarding a deferred debt before maturity. The statement of the judge's trustee (amin) that he swore the woman is not accepted unless two witnesses corroborate. Finally, if a judge issues a ruling within his jurisdiction but then attests to it outside, the attestation is invalid.

Acceptance of Testimony Without a Claim and Identification Rules
Lists cases where testimony is accepted without a claim (hisan) and rules on defining the person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the cases where testimony is accepted voluntarily (ḥisba) in Hanafi fatwa and apply the identification requirements for witnesses.

Core Idea
Testimony without a claim is accepted in divine-right cases like divorce and waqf, but not for personal claims like manumission of a male slave.

When can a witness testify without being summoned by a party? The Hanafi school distinguishes between cases involving divine rights, where testimony is accepted voluntarily (ḥisba), and personal rights, where a claim is required. This lesson explores the specific rulings from al-Ashbāh wa al-Naẓā'ir and also covers how to properly identify a person when testimony is given, especially when the person is absent. Understanding these rules is essential for a mufti issuing fatwas on marriage, divorce, and other sensitive matters.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish cases where testimony is accepted without a claim
List the categories mentioned: divorce of a woman, manumission of a female slave, waqf, crescent of Ramadan (except Eid crescents), ḥudūd (except qadhf and theft), and disputed cases like lineage. Understand the underlying principle: divine rights (ḥuqūq Allāh) allow testimony even without a formal lawsuit.
The crescent of Eid al-Fiṭr and al-Aḍḥā are excluded because they involve a specific right of the community that requires a claim.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on manumission of a male slave
Abu Hanifa holds that manumission of a male slave requires the slave's own claim, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad accept testimony without claim, reasoning that the right of Allah may be involved. Note the practical consequence (thamarah): under the Sahibayn's view, the slave can be freed without his request.
The text does not give a final tarjih; both views remain within the school.
3
Apply identification rules for a person against whom testimony is given
If the person is present, pointing suffices. If absent, he must be identified by his father's and grandfather's names; clan or profession alone is insufficient. For a woman, her description (ḥilya) must be stated, and the judge (not the witness) writes it after looking at her face. For a slave, the master's name suffices.
The fatwa is according to the Sahibayn that two just witnesses suffice to inform the witness's name and lineage.
4
Understand the role of the judge vs. witness in identification
The judge is responsible for looking at the woman's face and recording her description; the witness should not do this. This preserves modesty and ensures accurate identification by the authority.
This rule is from al-Bazzāziyya.

Commentary

حلية (hilya): Physical description of a person, especially a woman, for identification.

The Maxim: Testimony of a Careless Person Rejected, Confession Accepted
A concise Hanafi legal maxim on the maghfal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the testimony and confession of a careless person (maghfal) and apply it to distinguish between admissible and inadmissible statements in legal proceedings.

Core Idea
Carelessness destroys testimony but not confession: a maxim protecting others' rights while respecting self-incrimination.

This lesson presents a concise Hanafi legal maxim from al-Walwaljiyyah, a key fatwa source. The distinction between testimony and confession hinges on who the statement affects: testimony involves others' rights and requires reliability, while confession is an admission against oneself and is thus accepted even from a careless person. Understanding this maxim is essential for a mufti weighing evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The passage refers to a 'maghfal' – a person habitually careless or lacking precision in observation and memory. Recognize that this is a specific legal category distinct from a liar or a sinner.
In Hanafi fiqh, maghfal is defined by ghafla (carelessness), not by moral character.
2
Separate the two rulings
The maxim gives two distinct rulings: (1) testimony (shahada) is not accepted; (2) confession (iqrar) is accepted. Note the contrast – one is rejected, the other upheld.
3
Understand the rationale
Testimony affects the rights of third parties; it requires high reliability ('adalah). Carelessness undermines this. Confession is against the person's own interest, so it is presumed truthful and does not require strict 'adalah.
This is a standard Hanafi principle: confession is accepted even from a fasiq.
4
Note the source authority
The ruling is cited 'as in al-Walwaljiyyah', a Hanafi fatwa collection by al-Walwalji (6th century AH). This places it in the category of fatawa mutakhkhirin (later fatwas), which are operative.
Al-Walwaljiyyah is a respected text in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This is a concise ruling from a Hanafi source. A 'maghfal' (غفل) is a person known for carelessness or lack of precision, making his testimony unreliable. Therefore, his testimony in legal matters is not accepted. However, his confession (iqrar) against himself is accepted because it is against his own interest and he is considered competent regarding his own affairs. This distinction is standard in Hanafi fiqh: testimony requires trustworthiness ('adalah), while confession does not require such strict conditions as it is an admission of a right against oneself.

Key Terms
المغفل (al-maghfal): A person who is habitually careless or negligent, lacking precision in observation and memory.
شهادة (shahada): Testimony given in court about the rights of others.
إقرار (iqrar): Confession or admission against one's own interest.

Rules for Conflicting Testimonies in Death, Divorce, and Clientage
Rules for conflicting testimonies in death, divorce, and clientage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving conflicting testimonies in death, divorce, and clientage disputes, and identify when testimony is accepted or rejected based on the witnesses' knowledge.

Core Idea
When testimonies conflict, the first presented evidence prevails; ignorance of some details may not bar testimony.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents a collection of rulings on shahada (testimony) in specific disputes. It covers scenarios where testimonies conflict, the role of istishab (presumption of continuity), and the conditions for accepting testimony. We will parse these rules to understand the underlying maxims and their practical applications for a mufti.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting testimonies
Read each scenario and identify what is being testified (death, divorce, clientage, sale, marriage) and who the witnesses are. Note the specific conflict or uncertainty.
The passage uses shorthand; unpack each case fully.
2
Determine the basis for precedence
For the first case, the rule 'the first testimony takes precedence' is stated. Understand why the first is given priority: it is the first evidence presented to the court, not necessarily the chronologically first event.
This prioritizes procedural order over substantive truth in case of equal proof.
3
Analyze the effect of witness ignorance
Differentiate between ignorance of the price in a sale (fatal) and ignorance of the dower in marriage (acceptable). Understand the reason: the dower is not part of the contract's essential validity, while price is.
This shows that some details are integral, others are not.
4
Apply istishab to present state
In the case of 'he is the husband now' or 'he owns now', the ruling uses istishab: if a valid marriage or ownership existed before, it is presumed to continue until proven otherwise. Understand that this applies even if witnesses cannot confirm the present state directly.
Istishab bridges the gap between past certainty and present uncertainty.

Commentary
This is a compilation of various rules regarding testimonies (shahadat). First, a conflict: one pair witnesses death of a man and his wife (implying she died after him?), another pair witnesses divorce. The rule: the first evidence presented is given priority. Second, a dispute over clientage (wala) after death: each party brings proof that they freed the deceased while owning him. The ruling: inheritance is split equally, analogous to a case where two claim lineage of a child with equal proof. Third, once a judge accepts and rules based on one evidence, the other is not accepted. Fourth, when witnesses to a sale do not know the price, their testimony is rejected; but for marriage, ignorance of the dower does not invalidate the testimony. Fifth, it is not permissible to testify about a woman wearing a niqab (veil) because identity is uncertain, nor to testify from behind a wall. Sixth, if witnesses testify to divorce or manumission and say it happened in sickness or health, it is considered in sickness (the less favorable for the testator). The Finality of Judgments: Two Exceptions
Rule and exceptions from Hanafi procedural law.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that a valid judgment is not invalidated except in two specific cases, and apply it to a given scenario.

Core Idea
A final judgment stands until the winner admits it's wrong or the witnesses were disqualified.

In Hanafi procedural law, judgments are presumed valid after issuance to maintain stability and finality. However, the school recognizes two exceptional situations where the judgment collapses automatically, protecting fundamental justice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Default Rule
The default rule is that once a judgment is issued soundly, it cannot be invalidated by any third party's claim. This preserves the authority of judicial decisions.
2
Identify the First Exception: Confession of the Beneficiary
If the party in whose favor the judgment was given (al-maqḍī lahu) confesses that the judgment was invalid, then the judgment is overturned. However, this does not apply in cases where the judgment declared someone free (ḥurriyyah).
The exception for freedom cases ensures that personal status rights are not undone by a single party's admission.
3
Identify the Second Exception: Disqualified Witnesses
If it later becomes known through evidence that the witnesses upon whom the judgment was based were slaves or had received the ḥadd penalty for false accusation of fornication (qadhf), the judgment is invalidated because it was not sound from the start.

Two Claimants and the Possessor's Admission
Rule on oath of denied claimant when possessor admits to one of two

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three claims (ghasb, wadi'a, i'ara) where the denier is sworn after the possessor admits to one claimant.

Core Idea
When a possessor admits to one of two claimants, the other is not sworn unless the claim is ghasb, wadi'a, or i'ara.

The default is no oath.
3
List the three exceptions
The exceptions are: ghasb (trespass/unauthorized possession), wadi'a (deposit), and i'ara (loan for use). In these cases, the denied claimant must be sworn.
These are property claims where the possessor's admission creates a conflict.
4
Understand the reason
In these three contracts, the possessor holds the property on behalf of the true owner, so his admission to one creates a need for the other to swear to protect his claim.

Admissibility of Debt Claims Against the Deceased
Ruling on admissibility of debt claims against a deceased person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the lawful defendants for a debt claim against a deceased estate and apply the exception when the deceased gifted all property to a stranger.

Core Idea
A creditor cannot sue the deceased's estate directly; only heirs, executors, or legatees are proper defendants.

In Hanafi fatwa practice, a mufti must know against whom a claim of debt against the deceased can be heard. This lesson explains the general rule of inadmissibility and the specific exceptions, clarifying who can be sued and under what circumstances. The ruling is drawn from two authoritative later works: Jami' al-Fusulayn and Khizanat al-Muftin.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule of inadmissibility
The text opens with 'A claim of debt against the deceased is not heard' — this is the baseline: no direct suit against the deceased himself.
The deceased is not a proper defendant; the claim must target someone representing or possessing the estate.
2
Identify the three excepted persons
The claim is heard against three categories: the heir (warith), the executor (wasi), and the legatee (musa lahu). These individuals either inherit, manage, or receive part of the estate.
These are the only proper defendants by default.
3
Recognise the non-defendant: the creditor (gharim)
The text explicitly excludes a creditor of the deceased as a defendant — the claim is not heard against him.
A gharim is a creditor, not an owner or representative.
4
Apply the exception for a donee of all property
If the deceased gifted all his property to a stranger and delivered it, that stranger becomes a possessor (dhu yad) and the claim is heard against him.
Possession (yad) is the key factor; the exception is from Khizanat al-Muftin.
5
Note the authority of the sources
The ruling is supported by Jami' al-Fusulayn and Khizanat al-Muftin, both later Hanafi works of fatwa and rasm al-mufti.
These sources are part of the fatawa mutakhkhirin tradition.

Defendant's Defense of Deposit from Third Party
Ruling on defendant's defense of deposit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a defendant's claim of deposit from a third party dismisses the plaintiff's claim without evidence, and identify the two exceptions where the rule does not apply.

Core Idea
A simple claim of deposit from a third party repels the claim, unless inheritance or a purchase order is alleged.

In Hanafi judicial practice, a defendant can often dismiss a claim by asserting a different ownership chain. However, not all such claims are treated equally. This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a key rule with two critical exceptions. Understanding these nuances is essential for muftis and judges when evaluating defenses in property disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The defendant's assertion that the property belongs to a third party who deposited it with him is sufficient to dismiss the claim without evidence.
This rule is a specific Hanafi position that eases the burden on the defendant.
2
Note the two exceptions
The text explicitly lists two exceptions: (a) if the defendant claims inheritance from the depositor, and (b) if the defendant claims purchase and that the seller ordered him to take possession from the plaintiff.
These exceptions are not exhaustive; they are the only ones mentioned in this passage.
3
Analyze why these exceptions differ
Inheritance implies actual ownership by the defendant, so the claim is no longer a simple denial of ownership. Similarly, a purchase order directly involves the plaintiff in the transaction, making the defendant's story inconsistent with a mere deposit.
The underlying rationale is that these exceptions indicate a stronger connection to the property.
4
Apply the rule to hypothetical scenarios
Test your understanding by considering cases: e.g., defendant says 'Zaid deposited it with me' – claim dismissed. Defendant says 'I inherited it from Zaid' – claim not dismissed.
Practice helps solidify the distinction.
5
Consider the reference to Furua al-Karabisi
The passage points to Furua al-Karabisi for further details. This indicates that the reasoning is elaborated elsewhere in Hanafi literature.
For advanced study, consult that work to understand the differences (furua) between the cases.

Identify the general rule

The exceptions are based on necessity and practicality, not on arbitrary selection.

Preferring Possessor's Evidence: The Rule and Its Exceptions
A procedural rule with two exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedural rule for competing evidence on a slave's birth and identify the two exceptions where the external claimant's evidence prevails over the possessor's.

Core Idea
Possessor's evidence generally wins, but two exceptions shift the balance.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a key procedural rule for muftis: when both the possessor and an external claimant bring evidence that a slave was born in their ownership, the possessor's evidence is normally preferred. However, the author notes two important exceptions where the external claimant's evidence takes precedence. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correct fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Locate the statement: 'If the external presents evidence of birth in his ownership and the possessor likewise, the possessor's evidence is given precedence.' This is the baseline.
The possessor's yad (possession) gives procedural advantage.
2
Understand the author's comment
Note the phrase 'I say: except in two issues.' This signals that the general rule is not absolute. The author (Ibn Nujaym) is adding exceptions from another source (Khizanat al-Akmal).
This is a classic istithna' (exception) in Hanafi legal writing.
3
Parse the first exception
Read: 'if the external says he was born in my ownership and I emancipated him' and provides evidence, while the possessor only says 'born in my ownership', then the external's evidence is not preferred—i.e., the exception applies.
Contrast with mudabbar or mukatib: those do not trigger the exception.
4
Parse the second exception
Read: 'if the external says he was born in my ownership from this my slave woman, and he is my son' then his evidence is preferred over the possessor's.
This invokes lineage (nasab) which overrides possession.
5
Synthesize the operative rule
Combine: Generally, prefer possessor's evidence. But if external proves both birth and either (a) emancipation or (b) paternity, then external's evidence prevails. For tadbir and kitaba, the general rule holds.
This is the mufta bihi position from this text.

Core Idea
Muslims and non-Muslims are equal in court, except when lineage is at stake.

As a mufti, you will encounter claims involving Muslims and non-Muslims. The default rule is equal treatment of testimony, but a critical exception is lineage claims. This lesson from Khizanat al-Akmal clarifies the rule and its furuat for inheritance and relationship testimony.

State the general rule

The exception is 'إلا في دعوى النسب' — except in lineage claims.
Lineage (nasab) is given special weight in Islamic law.

The text states that testimony to inheritance without specifying cause is not accepted unless it refers to a judge's ruling.
This is a separate rule from the lineage exception.
4
Understand relationship specification
Testimony to relationships like brotherhood must specify maternal/paternal, except for very close relatives like son, daughter, grandson, father, mother.
This specification is needed for precise lineage determination.
5
Connect to mufti practice
In practice, when a lineage claim is made, the mufti recognizes the precedence of the Muslim's witness.
This is a rare case where faith affects procedural weight.

A judge cannot rely on what he knows personally; only formal evidence (hujja) matters.

In this passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair, we find a concise definition of hujja (proof) in Hanafi legal procedure. Qasama is specific to murder cases.

This protects the judge from allegations of bias and maintains procedural fairness.

Testimony of Heirship and Kinship
Rules for accepting testimony of heirship and kinship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for accepting testimony of heirship and kinship, and apply them to determine the validity of testimony.

Core Idea
Heirship testimony requires stating the cause; kinship testimony must specify lineage branch except for direct ascendants/descendants.

This lesson focuses on the rules about testimony concerning lineage relationships. The court demands specificity to avoid vague claims that may prejudice inheritance. General statements like 'he is an heir' are insufficient unless supported by a judicial ruling or detailed kinship.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish heirship from kinship testimony
Heirship testimony (shahada bi al-irth) must include the cause (sabab) of inheritance, e.g., 'he is his son'. Pure statement 'he is his heir' without specification is invalid. Kinship testimony (shahada bi al-qaraba) must clarify whether the relation is paternal (li abih) or maternal (li abihi wa ummihi) unless the relation is immediate family.
The exception for immediate family: son, daughter, grandson, father, mother – no need for specification.
2
Identify the exception for heirship
If witnesses testify that a judge has ruled the person is an heir, that is accepted because the judgment itself is a basis. This is a shortcut to avoid re-litigating the cause.
The judgment serves as a proof (hujja) in itself.
3
Apply the kinship specification rule
For relationships like brother, uncle, cousin, witnesses must state whether it is from the father's side or mother's side. The text provides a clear list of exempted relatives: son, daughter, son's son, father, mother.
Why these? They are unambiguous and the relationship is determined by the fact of birth.
4
Practice classification
Given a scenario, decide whether the testimony is acceptable and what is missing.
Example: Witnesses say 'he is his cousin' – fails because no specification.

The Comprehensive Proof (Hujja) and the Judge's Knowledge
Definition of proof (hujja) and the ruling on judge's knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven types of proof recognized by the Hanafi school and explain the mufta bihi ruling that the judge's personal knowledge is not a valid basis for judgment.

Core Idea
Proof includes seven forms, but the judge's personal knowledge is excluded in fatwa to ensure impartiality and evidentiary consistency.

This lesson focuses on the most important definition in the chapter: what constitutes legal proof (hujja) according to the Hanafi school. While Imam Muhammad's original list includes the judge's personal knowledge, the later relied-upon position excludes it. This shift illustrates how the madhhab prioritizes procedural justice over individual judicial convenience.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the seven proofs
The text lists: just testimony (bayyina), acknowledgment (iqrar), refusal to take oath (nukul), oath (yamin), qasama (oath for blood money), judge's knowledge after appointment, and conclusive circumstantial evidence (qarina qati'a). Commit these to memory.
These are the only recognized means of proof in court.
2
Understand the dispute
Imam Muhammad originally included the judge's personal knowledge as a proof, but the consensus of later scholars (as in Jami' al-Fusulayn and al-Bazzaziyya) is that it is not considered. This is the mufta bihi position.
Why the shift? To prevent partiality and ensure judgments are based on evidence presented.
3
Trace the tarjih mechanism
The tarjih is based on the principle that the judge should act only on what is presented in court. The later texts state 'عليه الفتوى' (upon this is the fatwa).
This is a classic case of a ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah being overridden by later practice.
4
Apply in fatwa
When asked if a judge can use his private knowledge, the mufti must answer: no, according to the relied-upon position.
Example: Judge sees someone steal outside court but the case is brought later; he cannot convict based on his personal observation.

Spending Claims, Tasdiq, Qarina, and Register Exceptions
Rules on spending claims, tasdiq, qarina, and register exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules for spending claims, distinguish tasdiq from iqrar, identify when qarina is accepted, and list the five types of judgments not proven by court registers.

Core Idea
Father's spending claim is accepted with oath; confirmation equals acknowledgment; qarina is rarely used; five sensitive judgments cannot be enforced by register alone.

This lesson covers several practical rules that complete the chapter on evidence. The father's claim of spending on his child is given special weight to protect the child's welfare. The concept of tasdiq (confirmation) is aligned with iqrar for efficiency. The use of circumstantial evidence is strictly limited, and certain judgments require direct proof despite being recorded in registers.

Guided Path
1
Understand spending claims
The father's statement that he spent on his minor child is accepted with an oath, even if the mother denies it, and even if the amount was fixed by court or by the father himself. This differs from claims for a wife where the husband's word is not accepted if she denies.
Reason: father's duty to support child is a religious obligation; trust is placed in him.
2
Define tasdiq and its effect
Confirmation (tasdiq) of a claim has the same legal effect as an acknowledgment (iqrar), except in hudud (fixed punishments). So if someone confirms a debt claim, it is as if he admitted it.
This simplifies proceedings but protects the accused in hudud.
3
Identify qarina limitations
Judgment cannot be based on circumstantial evidence (qarina) except in specific cases listed in the chapter of tahaluf (mutual oaths). These cases are limited and not generally applicable.
Example: in a sale dispute where both parties swear, qarina may tip the scale.
4
List the five register exceptions
Five types of judgments cannot be enforced solely based on the court register: lineage (nasab), judgment based on a midwife's testimony, annulment of marriage due to impotence, annulment of sale due to a slave's escape, and declaring a witness corrupt (tafsiq). These require direct testimony.
Reason: these involve serious personal rights that demand the highest level of proof.
5
Apply to scenarios
Given a case, determine which rule applies and what the outcome is.
Practice: father claims he spent 100 on child, mother denies. Ruling: father's claim accepted with oath.

Agent's Credibility: Discharge vs. Reimbursement
Ruling on agent's testimony regarding discharge and claim of additional payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that an agent is believed in discharge but not in reimbursement, and identify the resolution method (tahlif and division) when both parties swear.

Core Idea
Agent believed for discharge, not for reimbursement; dispute resolved by mutual oaths and division.

This passage from the Hanafi usul al-fiqh literature sets down a clear evidentiary rule for agency disputes. The agent's statement is believed when it discharges his duty, because the principal cannot easily verify. But when the agent claims reimbursement, he is not believed because it would impose liability. The example of a purchase with an authorized addition illustrates how the dispute is resolved when both parties swear.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of claims
Recognize that the agent's statements fall into two categories: claims of discharge (i.e., 'I have performed my duty') and claims of reimbursement (i.e., 'I spent more and am owed money').
Discharge benefits the agent by relieving him; reimbursement imposes a cost on the principal.
2
Apply the default rule
The Hanafi rule: the agent is believed regarding discharge but not regarding reimbursement. This is because the principal is not in a position to confirm discharge, while reimbursement would be a claim against the principal.
The rule is based on the principle that the agent is a trustee (amin) and is trusted for actions, but not for claims that harm the principal.
3
Analyze the example given
The principal gives 1000 dirhams and authorizes the agent to add up to 500 from his own funds to buy a slave. The agent claims he added 500 (so total price is 1500), but the principal denies. The agent's claim is a reimbursement claim, so he is not believed.
The dispute is about whether the addition actually occurred.
4
Determine the resolution method
Since neither party has proof, both take oaths (tahluf): the principal swears that the agent did not add, and the agent swears that he did add. After both oaths, the price is divided into thirds: the principal pays a third of the total (500?) and the agent bears a third? Actually the text says 'the price is divided into thirds' meaning the value of the slave is split proportionally.
The division is due to impossibility of determining the exact amount of addition.
5
Note the exception
The rule differs if the purchase is of a specific, existing item (like a particular slave). In that case, the item can be appraised and the addition can be determined by the market value difference, so no need for division.
The full details are referred to al-Jami'.
