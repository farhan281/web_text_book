Ruling on Intention of Obligatory Nature and Interchangeability of Performance and Make-up
Explains the Hanafi rules for intention of obligation and interchangeability of ada/qada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when niyyat al-fardiyyah is not required in Hanafi fiqh and apply the rule that niyyat al-ada' and niyyat al-qada' are interchangeable for time-bound acts.

Core Idea
Intention of obligation is not needed for means or inherently obligatory acts; ada and qada intentions are interchangeable if the essence of intention is present.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir addresses two common fatwa questions: when must a mufti require the client to specify 'fard' in their intention, and when can performance and make-up intentions be swapped. The answers are based on Hanafi principles about the nature of acts and excusable error. We will explore the key cases and the operative rule for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act category
Determine if the act is a means (wasila), inherently obligatory (fard), or time-bound with both ada/qada.
Acts like tayammum are means; funeral prayer is inherently obligatory; daily prayers are time-bound with both descriptions.
2
Apply the rule for niyyat al-fardiyyah
For means (e.g., tayammum) and inherently obligatory acts (e.g., funeral prayer), intention of obligation is not required.
The rule is based on the principle that only the attainment of the act is considered, not the achievement of obligation.
3
Check interchangeability of ada/qada
For acts described by both ada and qada (like the five daily prayers), intention of one suffices if there is doubt or error about the time.
The Hanafi school holds that one is used in place of the other, as error in estimation is excused.
4
Note exceptions
Friday prayer and acts not described by time (zakat, kaffarah) are not subject to interchangeability.
Friday prayer is unique; if missed, it is prayed as Zuhr with a separate intention.
5
Recognize the fatwa hierarchy
The chosen view (mukhtar) is that interchangeability is valid, per later authorities like Ibn Nujaym.
The text cites various sources (Fath al-Qadir, al-Tataraniyya, Kashf al-Asrar) and concludes with the chosen view.

Commentary
This passage discusses the conditions for intention (niyyah) in acts of worship, focusing on whether one needs to specifically intend the obligatory nature (niyyat al-fardiyyah) and the distinction between intention for performance (ada') and make-up (qada'). The author follows the Hanafi position that in actions that are merely means (wasā'il) like tayammum, or acts that are inherently obligatory like the funeral prayer (janāzah), it is not necessary to intend the obligatory nature. Similarly, for prayers that are repeated due to a disliked action (makrūh) or omission of a wājib, the intention should be for a supererogatory (nafl) act that compensates the deficiency. Regarding intention for adā' and qadā', the Hanafī school holds that they are interchangeable in most cases; if one intends performance but the time has ended, it suffices for make-up, and vice versa, based on the principle that the essence of intention (aṣl al-niyyah) is present, and the error in estimation is excusable. The passage also addresses the prayer of a child (who is not obligated) and the intention for fard al-‘ayn vs fard al-kifāyah.

Key Terms
نية الفرضية (niyyat al-fardiyyah): Intent of the obligatory nature of an act, i.e., consciously intending that the act is fard (obligatory) as opposed to nafl.
نية الأداء (niyyat al-ada'): Intent to perform an act within its prescribed time.
نية القضاء (niyyat al-qada'): Intent to make up an act after its time has passed.

Rules Concerning Non-Muslims: Teaching, Oaths, and Intention
Two rulings: teaching a Christian fiqh/Quran and validity of non-Muslim oaths/intentions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a non-Muslim's intention is valid for worldly matters (like travel) but not for acts of worship, and identify the Quranic evidence for the invalidity of a non-Muslim's religious oath.

Core Idea
A non-Muslim's intention counts for worldly actions but not worship; oaths are invalid as religious acts.

This lesson examines two interrelated rulings from the Hanafi tradition concerning non-Muslims: the permissibility of teaching them Islamic knowledge, and the legal weight of their intentions and oaths. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for a mufti when issuing fatwas involving non-Muslims, especially in matters of travel, contracts, and religious obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two distinct rulings
The passage presents two separate benefits: (1) teaching a Christian fiqh and Quran with conditions, and (2) the invalidity of a non-Muslim's oath and the general rule about their intention.
2
Examine the dalil for oath invalidity
The author cites Quran 9:12 to argue that a non-Muslim's 'yamin' (oath) is not a religious oath but a pact. Understand how the Hanafi school interprets 'ayman' in this verse.
3
Analyze the exception to the rule of intention
The rule 'the intention of a non-Muslim is not considered' has one exception: when the intention relates to a non-worship matter like travel. The case of a Christian and a boy traveling illustrates this.
4
Formulate the governing maxim
Derive the qawida: لا تعتبر نية الكافر إلا في غير العبادات (The intention of a non-Muslim is not considered except in non-worship matters).
This maxim helps in fatwa cases involving non-Muslims.

Commentary
This passage contains two distinct rulings. First, a benefit (fa'ida) from al-Multaqat quoting Abu Hanifa: it is permissible to teach a Christian the Quran and Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) in the hope of guidance. However, he should not touch the physical copy of the Quran (mushaf) unless he performs ghusl. This restriction is based on the impurity of a non-Muslim, but once he performs a full bath (ghusl), touching is allowed. Second, the passage addresses the invalidity of a non-Muslim's acts of worship: atonement (kaffarah) and oaths (yamin) are not valid for a non-Muslim, citing Quran 9:12 where 'oaths' are interpreted as pacts, not religious oaths. Then, a rule about the intention (niyyah) of a non-Muslim: generally, their intention is not considered (for acts of worship), except in one case mentioned in al-Bazzaziyya and al-Khulasa. The case: a boy (who is a minor) and a Christian travel together for a distance of three days' travel. The boy attains puberty mid-journey, and the Christian converts to Islam. In this scenario, the Christian may shorten his prayer (qasr) because his intention to travel is considered (he had the capacity for intention), while the boy's intention is not considered because he was non-pubescent at the start. The footnote by Shaykh Muhammad Ali clarifies that the exception is understood when intention is required for matters that are not acts of worship by nature (like travel), not for worship per se.

نية الكافر (niyyat al-kafir): Intention of a non-Muslim, generally not considered for acts of worship but considered for non-worship actions like travel.
يمين (yamin): Oath taken by a non-Muslim, which is not valid because they are not bound by religious oaths.

Conditions for Sickness Dispensations: Breaking Fast and Tayammum
Criteria for sickness dispensations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the criterion for sickness to permit breaking fast or tayammum according to Hanafi law, and apply the rule that dispensation requires harm, not just hardship.

Core Idea
A mere sickness does not permit dispensation; only when harm is feared.

Not every sickness excuses a Muslim from fasting or performing wudu with water. The Hanafi school carefully defines which conditions justify the rukhsah (dispensation). This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir explains the key distinction between absolute sickness (mutlaq al-marad) and sickness that poses a genuine risk of harm. We will parse the criteria and see how they apply to breaking fast, tayammum, and even Hajj provisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle
Locate the statement that what does not harm is not a license for breaking fast. This establishes the rule: only harm triggers dispensation.
2
Distinguish Types of Sickness
Find the reference to three categories. The second type (intermediate) is the one that permits breaking fast: fear of increased illness or delayed recovery.
The first and third types are not fully detailed here but are delineated in other Hanafi texts.
3
Apply the Rule to Tayammum
Note that the same harm requirement applies: use of water must cause loss of limb, injury, new illness, or delay. Absolute sickness does not justify tayammum.
4
Consider the Hajj Analogy
See how the principle extends to Hajj: provisions must be appropriate to the person so as not to cause harm. This reinforces the idea that hardship alone is insufficient.
5
Analyze the Water Purchase Ruling
Note that one is not required to buy water at an exorbitant price. This illustrates that while harm is a condition, it is balanced with practicality.

Commentary
This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir discusses the conditions under which a sick person may break the fast in Ramadan. The author begins by stating that a sickness which does not cause harm is not a valid license to break the fast. The discussion then turns to three categories of sickness (two mentioned here: the second type is intermediate where one fears increased illness or slow recovery – breaking fast is allowed). Rulings on 'Na'am' in Oaths and Acknowledgments
Rulings on the word 'na'am' in oaths and acknowledgments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the legal effect of the word 'na'am' when used in response to an oath-demand or a demand for acknowledgment, and determine whether it creates an oath, an acknowledgment, or no effect based on Hanafi custom and context.

Core Idea
Context and custom determine the legal force of 'na'am' — it can create an oath or an acknowledgment even if uttered mockingly.

In Hanafi fatwa, everyday words can carry significant legal weight. The word 'na'am' (yes) may seem simple, but when used in response to an oath or a demand for acknowledgment, it can bind the speaker. This lesson extracts from classical Hanafi sources the rules for interpreting 'na'am' in such contexts, helping the mufti parse the speaker's intent and the custom (urf) of the community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of precedent statement
Determine whether the prior statement was an oath (e.g., 'By Allah, you did X?') or a demand for acknowledgment (e.g., 'You owe me money, pay it.'). The effect of 'na'am' differs based on this.
2
Assess the custom (urf) of 'na'am'
In the local custom, does 'na'am' affirm the entire prior statement or just part? The Hanafi school considers urf to interpret the scope of affirmation.
3
Apply the default Hanafi principle
Unless otherwise proven, 'na'am' is an affirmation of the entire prior statement, making the speaker either a swearer (for oath) or an acknowledger (for iqrar).
4
Consider mockery or jest
Even if said mockingly, in acknowledgment it is binding (as per Iqrar al-Qunyah). For oaths, mockery is not excused; the literal affirmation stands.

Land that has left Bayt al-Mal by valid sale is no longer subject to the rule of ignoring endower's conditions.

Many contemporary jurists had been applying a general rule from al-Asyuti to all endowments originating from Bayt al-Mal, even after the property had been sold and re-endowed by a private individual. Ibn Nujaym clarifies that the rule only applies to endowments that never left Bayt al-Mal. Ibn al-Humam's fatwa in Fath al-Qadir concerning Sultan al-Ashraf Barsbay's purchase and endowment establishes that after a valid sale, the buyer's conditions must be observed.

The mufta bihi position: for endowments that have left Bayt al-Mal through a valid sale, the endower's conditions are to be followed. For those remaining attached, the general rule of ignoring conditions applies.

General Acquittal and Its Effect on Lawsuits
Ruling on general acquittal preventing lawsuits unless subsequent right established

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of general acquittal to determine whether a subsequent lawsuit is admissible, distinguishing between rights existing before and after the acquittal.

Core Idea
A general acquittal voids only existing claims; it does not bar lawsuits for rights that arise afterward.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, a foundational text in Hanafi legal maxims, clarifies a critical rule for muftis: a general acquittal (الإبراء العام) is an isqāt (dropping) of all rights that exist at the time of the statement. However, it does not prevent claims for rights that accrue subsequently. The author provides multiple furu' illustrating when a lawsuit is heard despite a prior general acquittal, and explains the nuance regarding contradiction (تناقض) in a party's statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
The passage begins by stating that a general acquittal within an invalid contract does not prevent a lawsuit. Later, it explicitly says: 'لا تسمع الدعوى بعد الإبراء العام إلا بحق حادث بعده' — a lawsuit is not heard after a general acquittal except for a right that arises afterward. Establish this as the default rule.
The word 'عام' (general) is key; it covers all rights at the time.
2
Recognize exceptions where the acquittal is ineffective
The author mentions that an acquittal from usury is invalid, so a claim based on usury can still be heard. Similarly, if the acquittal is given in an invalid contract, it does not bar a lawsuit. Note these exceptions.
These exceptions prevent the acquittal from operating on rights that are legally non-existent or unenforceable.

Work through the examples: saying 'I have no right in this estate' and then claiming the seed (بذر) is his — the seed was owned before the acquittal, but the right to claim it existed at the time? Actually, the seed claim is about ownership that existed before, but the acquittal covers 'rights' not 'ownership'. The text says it is heard. This shows that 'rights' here refers to claims, not proprietary interests. Then, the claim that the estate is a waqf upon him and his children: this is a pre-existing right? There is disagreement. Then, after division of inheritance, a claim of a debt against the deceased: the debt is a pre-existing right, but it is heard because the acquittal was between heirs, not between the heir and the creditor? Actually, the text: 'أبرأ كل واحد منهم الدعاوى' among heirs, then one claims a debt against the deceased. That claim is against the estate, not against the other heirs, so it is a new right? More analysis is needed. The key is to apply the rule: if the right claimed existed at the time of acquittal, the claim is barred unless it is a subsequent right. The seed claim might be allowed because the acquittal only covers 'claims' (دعاوى) not the underlying ownership.
The nuance is that a general acquittal is an isqāt of claims, not of substantive rights. So proprietary rights can still be proven.
4
Understand the effect of acknowledgment after acquittal
The passage distinguishes between saying 'I have no right' (which is a general acquittal) and acknowledging that the property belongs to the claimant. If after a general acquittal, the defendant acknowledges ownership, the property is surrendered; the acquittal does not prevent that. Similarly, if the defendant claims purchase without a date after acknowledging the claimant's ownership, it is accepted. But if he says 'I have no right' first and then claims purchase, he must prove the purchase happened after the acquittal.
The difference lies in the nature of the statement: 'I have no right' is an isqāt of claims, while 'it is his' is an acknowledgment of ownership that can be contradicted by a later purchase claim only if proven new.
5

The passage ends with a discussion of contradiction. A surety who proves that the creditor acknowledged the debt was invalid (gambling, wine) does not have his evidence accepted because his own guarantee contradicts that. However, the original party (الأصيل) is forgiven for contradiction; he can still be told to 'seek your opponent and litigate.' This means a mufti should not automatically reject a case based on contradictory statements by the principal, but should allow the case to proceed.
This is a key adab principle: a mufti should not overrule the leniency given to the asil in matters of contradiction.

Core Idea
A defense is valid at any stage except in cases of contradiction or lack of explanation.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir lays out a foundational qawida governing the acceptance of a defendant's defense in court. Guided Path

Identify the General Rule
The text states that a defense is valid before and after presenting evidence, before and after judgment, before the first judge and before another, and before and after giving testimony.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Release from riba is valid after consumption — the Shariah right is to return the actual item, not its substitute.

This passage presents a real fatwa case where a man bought defective gold at an unfair rate. After the gold was consumed, the sellers released him from the remaining debt. Scholars differed on the validity of this release. The author consulted multiple authorities and concluded that release is valid after consumption, relying on rational proof from al-Bazdawi. This lesson examines the reasoning and the fatwa hierarchy, highlighting how a mufti refines his position through istifta' (seeking fatwa from others) and rational argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario and legal issue
Read the passage to understand the transaction: a man bought defective gold at an unequal rate (dinar for five daniqs). The gold was consumed, and the sellers forgave the remaining debt. The legal issue is whether such release is valid in a riba transaction.
The transaction is a clear case of riba al-fadl (excess in exchange of same commodity) and riba al-nasi'ah (deferred exchange).
2
Recognize the scholarly disagreement
Some scholars (Rukn al-Din al-Zanjabi, Najm al-Hulmi, and initially Zahir al-Din al-Marghinani) held that release does not work in riba because the Shariah right to return the excess cannot be superseded. Al-Bazdawi's argument: In riba contracts, ownership transfers upon possession. If the item is consumed, the buyer owes its equivalent (mithl). Release of that equivalent is not a return of the actual riba; it only cancels the guarantee. The Shariah right is to the actual riba if it still exists, not to the substitute. Therefore, release after consumption does not violate Shariah.
This proof distinguishes between the obligation to return the actual item (if extant) and the obligation to pay the equivalent (if consumed).
4

The author's final position (mufta bihi) is that release is valid after consumption. This is supported by the rational proof and the opinion of 'Ala' al-A'imma al-Hanati. The tarjih mechanism is the preference for the rational argument over the general maxim that release in riba is invalid.
This case illustrates how a mufti may rely on a secondary evidence (dalil aqli) over a general rule when the context changes.
5

Consider a person who took an interest-based loan and later the lender forgives the interest after the principal is consumed. According to this fatwa, the forgiveness is valid because the original riba (the interest) is no longer in existence; it has become a debt guarantee. This provides a lawful exit for parties in defective riba transactions.
This is not a justification to enter riba contracts but a remedy for past errors.

Trustee's Claim of Delivery: The Rule of Acceptance
Rule for accepting a trustee's claim of delivering trust property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi procedural rule that a trustee's claim of delivery is accepted with an oath, except when contradicted by the apparent facts or in the specific case of a debt agent after the principal's death.

Core Idea
A trustee's word is trusted unless reality shouts otherwise.

In Hanafi procedural law, trustees such as depositaries, agents, and executors are generally believed when they claim to have returned or disbursed the entrusted property. This rule balances trust in the trustee with the need to prevent fraud. However, exceptions exist: the claim of a debt agent after the principal's death requires evidence, and any claim that contradicts the apparent (i.e., what is normally expected) is rejected outright. This lesson unpacks the rule and its boundaries, which are essential for a mufti when evaluating such claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trustee and his claim
Determine whether the person making the claim is an amin (trustee), such as a depositary (mudi'), agent (wakil), or executor (wasi). His claim must be about returning or disbursing the trust property to its rightful owner.
The rule applies regardless of whether the owner is alive or dead, except for one case.
2
Check for the special exception: debt agent after death
If the claimant is an agent appointed to collect a debt (wakil bi-qabd al-dayn) and he claims after the principal's death that he collected and paid the debt during the principal's life, his statement is NOT accepted without evidence. Contrast this with an agent for a tangible asset (wakil bi-qabd al-'ayn), whose claim is accepted.
The difference is explained in the Hanafi work al-Walwalijiyya.
3
Check if the claim contradicts the apparent (al-zahir)
Even if the claimant is a trustee, his claim is rejected if it contradicts what is normally expected or evident. For example, an executor's claim of an excessive expense that goes against common practice is not accepted.
This is a general qualification: the claim must be plausible and consistent with the outward facts.
4
Apply the rule: accept with oath or reject
If no exception applies, the trustee's claim is accepted upon his taking an oath (yamin). If an exception applies (debt agent after death or contradiction with the apparent), the claim is rejected unless he provides evidence (bayyinah).
The default is acceptance with oath; the burden shifts only when the claim is implausible or the specific excepted case arises.

Core Idea
An executor may never discount a sale except when fulfilling a specific bequest to a named person who refuses at market price.

This rasm al-mufti rule is concise but powerful: the executor (wasi) is bound to sell estate assets at fair market value (thaman al-mithl). Any discount would normally harm the estate, especially if orphans are beneficiaries. However, the Hanafi school carves out one exception: if the testator explicitly instructs that a particular item (here a slave) be sold to a named person, and that legatee refuses to buy at the market price, the executor may reduce the price to effectuate the bequest. This exception balances the testator's explicit wish against the general protection of the estate.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The executor is not permitted to sell any item of the estate for less than its fair market value. This is the default position to safeguard the interests of heirs and creditors.
Thaman al-mithl is determined by the prevailing market price at the time of sale.
2
Identify the exception
The only case where discount is allowed is when the testator has specifically bequeathed that a particular item be sold to a named person (the legatee), and that legatee is not willing to pay the market price.
The item in the source is a slave (abd), but the rule applies to any specific bequest of sale to a named individual.
3
Apply the condition of refusal
The legatee must have refused to purchase at thaman al-mithl. If the legatee is willing to pay market price, no discount is permitted.
Refusal is a clear indication that the bequest would otherwise be defeated.
4
Understand the rationale
The exception exists because the testator's explicit directive takes precedence over the general principle of not discounting. Without this exception, the bequest could become impossible to execute.

The fourth ruling concerns inheritance: Muslims and non-Muslims do not inherit from each other, but non-Muslims inherit among themselves even if they belong to different sects (e.g., Jew from Christian), provided they are in the same dar (abode). The apostate (murtadd) is a special case: he inherits from his Muslim relatives (as he was Muslim when earning wealth) and his Muslim heirs inherit from him, despite the lack of unity of religion.

Key Terms
الذمي (al-dhimmi): A non-Muslim living under Muslim protection, bound by a pact (dhimma).
