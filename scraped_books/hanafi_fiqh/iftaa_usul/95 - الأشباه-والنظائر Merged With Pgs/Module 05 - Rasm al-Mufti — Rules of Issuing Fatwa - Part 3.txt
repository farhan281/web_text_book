Judge's Ruling in Matters of Ikhtilaf vs. Khilaf
Distinction between judicial rulings in areas of ikhtilaf and khilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ikhtilaf and khilaf and apply the rule to determine the validity of a judge's ruling.

Core Idea
A judge's ruling is valid only in matters of ikhtilaf, not in khilaf.

The early Hanafi masters recognized two types of contested legal issues: those genuinely debated among the Salaf (ikhtilaf) and those that are novel disputes with no precedent (khilaf). This distinction directly affects whether a judge's decision is enforceable. A mufti must identify which category a case falls into before issuing a fatwa on the ruling's validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage and isolate the two terms: ikhtilaf (disagreement among Salaf) and khilaf (novel dispute without Salaf basis). Note the Arabic: الاختلاف and الخلاف.
The difference is one letter (khā' vs. ḥā'), but legally significant.
2
Determine the ruling for each
The passage states: in ikhtilaf, the judge's ruling is permissible (jā'iz); in khilaf, it is not permissible. This is a fixed rule (rasm al-mufti).
3
Understand the distinguishing factor
The distinguishing factor (al-fariq) is the presence of a proof (dalīl) underlying ikhtilaf. Khilaf lacks such a proof. The passage quotes a differentiation: 'the first has a dalīl, the second does not.'
The dalīl can be from Qur'an, Sunnah, or Qiyas accepted by Salaf.

Ruling on exceptions to the bar on hearing claims after a final judgment

Learning Objective

Core Idea
After a final judgment, only three grounds let a new claim be heard: new ownership, void judgment, or nukul.

The finality of judgments is essential for legal stability, but Hanafi law recognizes that rigid finality can cause injustice. This rasm rule from the works of al-‘Imadi and al-Khaniyyah delineates the precise exceptions when a post-judgment claim becomes admissible. Understanding these narrow grounds is critical for a mufti to guide litigants seeking justice after a judgment.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
Begin by acknowledging the default rule: after a final judgment, no claim or evidence from the losing party is heard. This finality protects the authority of the judiciary.

2

From the text, extract the three grounds that break the bar: (1) claiming ownership from the original plaintiff or through birth (new ownership), (2) providing proof that invalidates the original judgment, and (3) the case where the judgment was based on the defendant's refusal to swear (nukul).
These are the only ways to reopen a case after judgment.
3

The text mentions specific scenarios—authorized agent (ma'dhun), father buying for minor son, trustee (mutawalli). These are illustrations, not limits; the exceptions apply universally.
Focus on the rule, not the examples.
4
Define nukul and its effect
Nukul is the refusal to take a required oath, treated in Hanafi law as an admission. If judgment was on that basis, the defendant can later bring a claim because the earlier 'admission' was procedural, not substantive.
This exception is specific to Hanafi procedure.

Rule on Contradiction in Legal Claims
Rules on contradictory claims and partial nullification of testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a contradictory claim is accepted in Hanafi law and apply the exception for obscure matters.

Core Idea
Contradiction is rejected unless the matter is inherently obscure.

In Hanafi ifta, a mufti often faces contradictory statements from litigants. The general rule is that such contradiction invalidates the claim. However, the law excuses certain parties—like executors, overseers, and heirs—whose roles involve complex and obscure circumstances. This lesson unpacks the first rasm rule from the passage, helping you distinguish when contradiction is fatal and when it is permitted.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The rule: 'al-tanāquḍ ghayr maqbūl' — contradiction is not accepted in legal proceedings.
This is a default principle.
2
Identify the exception
The exception: 'illā fīmā kāna maḥall al-khafā'' — except where the matter is obscure.
Obscurity (khafā') means the facts are inherently unclear or difficult to ascertain.
3
Recognise the examples given
The source gives three examples: the executor (waṣī), overseer (nāẓir), and heir (wārith). These parties often deal with complex estate matters where contradictions may arise from initial confusion.
These roles are considered 'ahl al-khafā'.
4
Understand the rationale
Because their statements pertain to obscure matters, a single contradiction does not automatically discredit them. The mufti must look into the nature of the claim.
The rule balances the need for consistency with the reality of complicated situations.
5
Apply to a fatwa scenario
If an executor first reports a debt of 500 dirhams, then later 300 dirhams, explaining a calculation error, the later statement may be accepted under this rule.
The mufti should assess whether the matter qualifies as mahall al-khafā'.

Testimony fails fully when part invalid, except when faith differs among slaves.

The second rasm rule in the passage deals with the indivisibility of testimony. If testimony is invalid for one part, it is invalid for all—unless the case involves two slaves of different faiths. Testimony is considered a single whole; partial invalidity destroys the whole.

The witnesses' testimony for the Muslim slave is invalid because Christians cannot testify for Muslims, but it remains valid for the Christian slave.

Testimony of non-Muslims is generally not accepted for Muslims, but may be accepted for fellow non-Muslims. Here, the testimony is separable because the slaves are distinct individuals of different faiths.
Indivisibility rule yields to the rule of faith-based competency.
4
Examine the practical outcome
The Christian witnesses' testimony leads to the manumission of the Christian slave only; the Muslim slave remains enslaved. This is the only case where partial invalidity does not invalidate the entire testimony.
The mufti must recognize this rare exception.

Presumption of Freedom and Its Exceptions
First rule: presumption of freedom with four exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the presumption of freedom (asl al-hurriyyah) and identify its four exceptions in Hanafi law.

Core Idea
People are presumed free except in testimony, qisas, hadd, and diyah—capacity matters.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle in Hanafi legal practice: everyone is assumed free unless proven otherwise. However, this presumption is not applied universally; certain legal areas require full legal capacity, so the presumption is inverted. Understanding this rule helps muftis correctly evaluate evidence and capacity-based claims.

Guided Path
1
State the presumption
The student recites the rule: people are free unless stated otherwise. This is the default in all transactions and claims.
2
Identify the four exceptions
The student lists the exceptions: testimony (shahada), retribution (qisas), prescribed punishments (hudud), and blood money (diyah). In these matters, a person is presumed to be a slave until proven free.
3
Explain the rationale (illa)
The student explains that these matters require full legal capacity (slavery diminishes capacity). Therefore, the presumption shifts to protect the integrity of these serious legal consequences.
4
Apply in a fatwa scenario
The student considers a case: someone claims to be a slave to avoid a hadd punishment. The presumption of freedom is applied, so the claim is rejected unless proven.

The Rule of Khasm: Evidence from One Whose Admission is Binding
Rasm al-mufti rule on evidence acceptance based on khasm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule 'man 'amila iqraruhu qubilat bayyinatu' to determine whether a claimant's evidence is admissible, by identifying whether the claim is one in which his own admission is binding.

Core Idea
Your right to bring evidence depends on your ability to admit the claim yourself - if you can't admit it, you can't prove it.

In Hanafi litigation, not everyone can simply bring evidence. The law requires that the person offering evidence be a valid opponent (khasm) – someone whose own admission regarding the claim would be legally binding. This lesson examines a foundational rule from Rasm al-Mufti: 'Whose admission is acted upon, his evidence is accepted.' We will parse the rule, its applications, and its exceptions, including the special treatment of non-Muslim testimony.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule and its wording.
Read the maxim 'man 'amila iqraruhu qubilat bayyinatu' and understand that it sets a condition for accepting evidence: the claimant must be one whose admission is legally effective in the matter claimed.
This establishes the concept of 'khasm' (valid opponent).
2
Define 'al-khasm' using the legal definition from the text.
Use the footnote from Sahib al-Taqyidat: 'Evidence is only established from an opponent against an opponent. For the plaintiff to be an opponent, the claim must be something he may validly admit and be bound by.'
The definition emphasizes that the ability to admit binds the person, making him a proper litigant.
3
Apply the rule to examples of accepted and rejected relationships.
List cases from the text: father, son, husband, patron (wala) – accepted; brother, grandfather, son's son – rejected. Explain that the former involve personal rights while the latter involve rights of others.
The pattern is that admission is binding only for direct lineal or marital ties that affect one's own status.
4
Identify the exception for inheritance, maintenance, and custody.
Note that even if the admission is not normally binding, evidence is accepted when the claim is for inheritance, maintenance, or custody, because these directly affect the claimant's own rights.
This exception shows that the rule is not absolute but tied to the nature of the claim.

Non-Muslim Testimony Against Muslims: The Two Exceptions
Rule: non-Muslim testimony against Muslims with two exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi rule on non-Muslim testimony against Muslims and apply the two exceptions (taba'an and darura) to given scenarios.

Core Idea
Non-Muslims cannot testify against Muslims unless it is incidental or necessary.

In Hanafi jurisprudence, the testimony of non-Muslims against Muslims is generally inadmissible. However, this rule has two well-defined exceptions: when the testimony is incidental (taba'an) and when it is necessitated by darura (necessity). This lesson unpacks these exceptions with concrete examples from the classical texts, enabling the mufti to correctly apply the rule in contemporary fatwa situations.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Begin with the core prohibition: 'la tuqbal shahadat kafir 'ala muslim' – testimony of a non-Muslim is not accepted against a Muslim. Understand that this is the default position in the Hanafi school.
This rule is rooted in the principle of justice and the higher status of the Muslim witness in Islamic law.
2
Identify the first exception: taba'an
When the testimony is primarily against a non-Muslim and only incidentally affects a Muslim, it is accepted. Analyze the examples: agency case (tawkeel), slave-debt case, agent-case. The key is that the Muslim's interest is secondary.
Taba'an means 'incidentally' – the testimony targets a non-Muslim, but its application extends to a Muslim.
3
Identify the second exception: darura
When no other witnesses exist and the right would be lost, testimony of non-Muslims is accepted out of necessity. Only two areas are mentioned: bequest (wasiyya) and lineage (nasab). Understand the rationale: without such testimony, essential rights would be lost.
Darura is a limited, case-specific exception and is not to be expanded analogically.
4
Distinguish direct vs indirect testimony
Note the distinction: in the taba'an examples, testimony is against the non-Muslim directly and the Muslim is affected indirectly (dimnan). In the reverse (e.g., testimony directed at the Muslim), it is not accepted because it is qasdan (directly against the Muslim).
This qasdan/dimnan distinction is crucial for correct application.
5
Apply to fatwa scenarios
Use the rule and exceptions to evaluate modern cases: e.g., non-Muslims testifying in a financial dispute where a Muslim is a party. Determine whether the testimony is incidental or necessary.
Remember that necessity is strictly limited to the two mentioned cases; no qiyas is allowed.

Admission and Evidence: Exceptions to the Rule
Exceptional cases where evidence is admitted despite admission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the exceptional cases where evidence is accepted even when a party has made an admission, and derive the underlying principle from Hanafi texts.

Core Idea
Evidence is generally not heard against an admitter, except where harm is feared from the non-admitter, forming a general maxim.

This lesson addresses a procedural rule: if a person admits a claim (iqrar), evidence is usually unnecessary and not heard. However, there are specific exceptions to protect against harm. The passage outlines three (or four) exceptions and then generalizes the principle: evidence with admission is allowed wherever harm would otherwise befall the non-admitting party. This is a classic example of istihsan overriding qiyas in Hanafi procedure.

This is from the author's own work (Sharh).

Jami' al-Fusulayn states: evidence with admission is permissible in every case where harm would be feared from the non-admitter if evidence were not allowed. This is a governing maxim.

Father's Acknowledgment in a Lawsuit for a Minor
A ruling on the effect of a father's acknowledgment in a minor's lawsuit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a father's acknowledgment in a lawsuit concerning a minor does not remove him from the lawsuit, and distinguish it from the rule for an executor or court trustee.

Core Idea
A guardian's acknowledgment does not end the lawsuit when the guardian is acting for another; evidence still stands.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from al-Qunyah: when a father is sued on behalf of his minor child, his acknowledgment does not exempt him from the lawsuit. Unlike an executor or court trustee, the father remains a party and evidence against him is still accepted. Understanding this distinction is crucial for the mufti handling guardianship and litigation.

1
Identify the parties and capacities
The lawsuit is against the father, but the right concerns the minor. The father is sued as a guardian (not personally).
The father's capacity is key: he is not a trustee but a natural guardian.
2
Analyze the effect of acknowledgment
The father acknowledges the claim. In normal cases, acknowledgment ends the lawsuit. But here, the text says he does not exit the lawsuit. Why? Because the acknowledgment is on behalf of the minor, not himself.
The ruling prioritizes the minor's right over the father's admission.
3
Compare with executor and court trustee
For an executor (wasi) or court trustee (amin al-qadi), acknowledgment removes them from the lawsuit. Distinguish the capacities: executor is a fiduciary with limited authority, while father has broader guardianship.
The distinction arises from different levels of authority and representation.
4
Determine the practical outcome
The mufta bihi: evidence (bayyinah) is accepted against the father despite his acknowledgment. The case proceeds to prove or disprove the claim against the minor.
This protects the minor's interest from a potentially prejudicial admission.

Conflicting Leases and Evidence with Presence or Absence

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In transactional disputes, coerced evidence outweighs voluntary evidence; when the nature is unknown, the claim of voluntariness prevails.

This lesson examines the second part of the passage, addressing a conflict between evidence given voluntarily (tatawwu') and evidence given under coercion (ikrah). If there is actual coerced evidence (e.g., a witness testifies under duress), that evidence is preferred. If no such evidence, the default presumption is voluntariness and validity.
This prevents abuse of claiming coercion without evidence.

A qadi's order is binding, except when distributing waqf to a relative — that order is like a fatwa, not fixed.

Rulings on Qadi Appointment and Removal
Rules on qadi appointment and removal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid appointment of a representative before the judge and identify the rules regarding a judge's removal by apostasy, sin, or written decree.

Core Idea
A judge is not removed by apostasy or sin; removal requires formal notification.

This lesson examines the first set of rules from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir concerning the initiation and termination of a qadi's authority. Understanding these principles is crucial for muftis and judges to know when a judge's decisions are valid and when a replacement takes effect. The passage clarifies that mere knowledge of removal does not suffice; the judge remains in office until the successor arrives or a written decree is received.
