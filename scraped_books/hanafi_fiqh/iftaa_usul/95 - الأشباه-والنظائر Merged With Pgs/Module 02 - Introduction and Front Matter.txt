Introduction to the Book and the First Two Legal Maxims
Author introduces book structure and outlines first two qawaid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two major legal maxims introduced in the book and explain their scope in applying Hanafi rulings.

Core Idea
Intention (niyya) conditions reward; purpose (maqsad) determines permissibility.

This lesson introduces the structure of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, a foundational Hanafi work on legal maxims. The author presents two pivotal rules: 'No reward without intention' and 'Matters are judged by their objectives'. Understanding these principles is essential for a mufti when deriving rulings, as they determine when intention affects reward, validity, and permissibility of actions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the book's structure
The author states the book has seven arts (فنون). The first art is on qawaid (legal maxims). This helps you understand how the work is organized and that these maxims are foundational.
The indexing is placed at the beginning for easy reference.
2
Identify the first maxim and its coverage
The first maxim is 'لا ثواب إلا بالنية' (No reward except with intention). It applies to acts of worship, transactions, litigation, permissible acts, prohibitions, and renunciations. Note where intention is a condition and where it is not.
Intention is required for reward, not necessarily for validity.
3
Identify the second maxim and its subtopics
The second maxim is 'الأمور بمقاصدها' (Matters are determined by their objectives). It explains that a single act can be permissible or prohibited based on intent. The author lists ten subtopics covering the reality, purpose, specification, time, continuity, etc., of intention.
This maxim is universally accepted but applied differently.
4
Understand the oath sub-rule
A sub-rule on oaths states that oaths are based on the intention of the swearer or the one demanding the oath, and that oaths are based on wording not purposes. However, intention can specify the general. This shows how intention interacts with oaths.
This sub-rule contains branches on divorce.
5
Relate to fatwa practice
As a mufti, these maxims guide you in evaluating cases where intention is disputed. For example, a person who eats with the intention to fast is rewarded, while one who eats casually is not. The second maxim helps determine whether an action is permissible based on the doer's purpose.
These maxims are applied in everyday fatwas.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction and table of contents for the first art of the book—the art of qawaid (legal maxims). The book is structured into seven arts, and he provides an index at the beginning for ease of reference. He outlines the first two base rules (qawaid). The first rule is 'لا ثواب إلا بالنية' (No reward except with intention), which covers when intention is a condition and its role in various categories of actions. The second rule is 'الأمور بمقاصدها' (Matters are judged by their objectives), which is the famous maxim that determines permissibility based on intent. He details ten sub-topics under this rule, covering the reality of intention, its purpose, specification, time, continuity, etc., and also includes a sub-rule regarding oaths that modifies the general with intention. This shows the analytical structure of the work, which proceeds from general maxims to detailed applications.

The Three Great Hanafi Maxims: An Overview
Index of the first art's legal maxims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three major legal maxims of the first art and list the seven sub-rules under the third maxim.

Core Idea
Three maxims govern Hanafi fiqh: intention, purpose, and certainty — the pillars of legal reasoning.

This lesson introduces the table of contents of the first art of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, which organizes Hanafi legal maxims. Understanding this structure is essential for navigating the detailed discussions that follow. We will map out the three qawaid kubra and their subdivisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three major maxims
From the fihrist, extract the three maxims: (1) لا ثواب إلا بالنية, (2) الأمور بمقاصدها, (3) اليقين لا يزول بالشك. Memorize them in Arabic and English.
These are the qawaid kubra that form the foundation.
2
Note the scope of each maxim
The first maxim concerns intention as a condition for reward; the second links rulings to intentions; the third establishes that certainty prevails over doubt.
Each maxim applies across multiple fiqh chapters.
3
List the seven sub-rules under the third maxim
Under 'certainty is not removed by doubt', the text mentions seven sub-rules: (1) بقاء ما كان على ما كان, (2) براءة الذمة, (3) من شك هل فعل..., (4) الأصل العدم, (5) الأصل إضافة الحادث إلى أقرب أوقاته, (6) الأصل في الأشياء الإباحة/الحظر/التوقف, (7) الأصل في الأبضاع التحريم.
These sub-rules are also called 'usul' and are detailed later.
4
Recognize the fihrist as a roadmap
This index is not a substitute for the full discussion. It serves to orient the reader and facilitate lookup.
When studying later chapters, refer back to this fihrist to see where you are in the structure.
5
Understand the function of a fihrist
In classical Hanafi texts, a fihrist provides a bird's-eye view of the author's methodology. It helps students connect specific rulings to general principles.
Always start a new book by reading its fihrist if available.

Introduction to Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: Structure and Scope
Table of contents outlining the three arts and the 25 qawa'id of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three arts (funun) of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir and explain the purpose of the qawa'id in organizing Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir organizes Hanafi fiqh into 25 qawa'id and three arts to train the jurist in analogical reasoning.

This lesson provides a roadmap for Ibn Nujaym's seminal work Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, a cornerstone of Hanafi qawa'id literature. The passage outlines the book's structure: the first art contains 25 legal maxims (qawa'id), the second art covers benefits (fawa'id) arranged by fiqh topics, and the third art deals with combining and differentiating similar cases (ashbah wa naza'ir). Understanding this structure is essential for navigating the work and grasping the Hanafi method of deriving rulings from maxims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three arts
The text divides the book into three arts (funun): qawa'id, fawa'id, and ashbah wa naza'ir. Note their sequence and purpose.
The first art is the largest, with 25 maxims.
2
List the maxims and their names
The passage names maxims 12-19. Infer that the complete set is 25. Understand that each maxim is followed by branches (furu') and exceptions.
The earlier maxims are not shown in this excerpt.
3
Examine the second art's arrangement
The second art follows the order of al-Kanz (a popular fiqh text), from purification to inheritance. Recognize that it collects benefits that are useful for a jurist.
This art provides practical applications of the maxims.
4
Understand the third art's focus
The third art deals with combining and differentiating similar and analogous cases. This is the core of ashbah wa naza'ir methodology.
It trains the jurist to distinguish rulings that appear similar.
5
Appreciate the introduction's contents
The introduction lists numerous rulings on specific persons and concepts, and the conclusion adds further maxims and benefits. This shows the comprehensive nature of the work.
The table of contents itself is a teaching tool.

This applies when a primary act or contract is invalid, all dependent obligations or components are also invalid. For example, if a sale is void due to lack of mutual consent, the obligation to deliver and pay are also void.

Qawa'id: The Foundation of Ijtihad
Explanation of qawa'id as the essence of usul al-fiqh and key to ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qawa'id in the Hanafi context and explain how mastery of them leads to ijtihad.

Core Idea
Qawa'id are the real usul al-fiqh; mastering them elevates a faqih to ijtihad, even in fatwa.

The author asserts that the branch of qawa'id he has compiled is in reality the true usul al-fiqh. By mastering these principles, a scholar can attain ijtihad, at least in the realm of fatwa. He illustrates the significance of qawa'id with the story of Imam Abu Tahir al-Dabbas, who collected the Hanafi principles into seventeen maxims, and the Shafi'i scholar al-Harawi's attempt to learn them secretly, showing their immense value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim about qawa'id
The author states that the qawa'id are in fact the true usul al-fiqh. They are not merely subsidiary rules but the foundational principles from which rulings are derived.
This elevates the status of qawa'id beyond mere branches.
2
Connect qawa'id to ijtihad
He explicitly says: 'وبها يرتقي الفقيه إلى درجة الاجتهاد ولو في الفتوى' – by these principles, a faqih rises to the level of ijtihad, even if only in fatwa. This means that mastery of qawa'id is sufficient for a scholar to derive new rulings.
'Ijtihad fi al-fatwa' is a lower level than absolute ijtihad but still requires independent reasoning.
3
Examine the story of Abu Tahir al-Dabbas
Imam Abu Tahir al-Dabbas collected 17 principles of the Hanafi madhhab. The Shafi'i scholar al-Harawi traveled to learn them secretly, indicating the high value placed on these qawa'id. Abu Tahir guarded them, showing they were considered precious knowledge.
The story underscores that qawa'id are not widely known and must be carefully preserved.
4
Understand the author's methodology
The author promises to only transmit what is sound and relied upon in the madhhab. If he includes a position based on a weak statement or transmission, he will note it. This ensures the work's reliability.
This methodology is crucial for a work intended to guide ijtihad.

The Seven Disciplines of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir
Author's introduction outlining the book's seven disciplines

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven sections of Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir and explain the purpose and significance of the first two sections (qawa'id and dawabit) in Hanafi iftaa.

Core Idea
A faqih's ijtihad is built on foundational qawa'id and precise dawabit.

In this introduction, Ibn Nujaym explains why he wrote this unique Hanafi work. He had noticed that no Hanafi book matched the comprehensive structure of al-Subki's Shafi'i work, so he compiled a volume covering seven essential disciplines. The first two—qawa'id (principles) and dawabit (controls)—are the most critical for a mufti, as they allow rulings to be derived consistently and exceptions to be identified accurately.

Guided Path
1
Understand the author's motivation
Ibn Nujaym states that he was inspired to write a book on the model of al-Subki's Shafi'i work because he found no Hanafi equivalent. His earlier work on dawabit, al-Fawa'id al-Zayniyya, served as a foundation.
Notice the emphasis on the gap in Hanafi literature.
2
List the seven sections
The author enumerates them: (1) qawa'id, (2) dawabit, (3) al-jam' wa al-farq, (4) al-alghaz, (5) al-hiyal, (6) al-ashbah wa al-naza'ir, (7) transmitted oddities and correspondences.
Memorize the order; the title of the book corresponds to the sixth section.
3
Analyze the purpose of the first section (qawa'id)
Qawa'id are the fundamental principles to which subsidiary rulings are traced. They are the 'real foundations of fiqh' and enable a faqih to reach the level of ijtihad in fatwa. The author only transmits sound, relied-upon positions.
The anecdote of Abu Tahir al-Dabbas illustrates the high value placed on these principles.
4
Analyze the purpose of the second section (dawabit)
Dawabit are specific controls that define the scope of a rule, including what is part of the rule and what is excluded. The author warns that some authors exclude certain cases, and he adds additional exclusions to prevent misunderstanding.
This section is called 'the most useful for the teacher, mufti, and judge.'
5
Recognize the significance of the remaining sections
The remaining five sections cover methods for reconciling conflicting rulings (jam' wa farq), legal riddles (alghaz), stratagems (hiyal), analogous cases (ashbah wa naza'ir), and historical oddities. They collectively equip a mufti with comprehensive tools.
The book aims to be a complete reference for investigators, judges, and muftis.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to his book, explaining his purpose and the structure of the work. He begins by praising Abu Bakr al-Siddiq and acknowledging the efforts of earlier scholars in codifying Hanafi fiqh. However, he notes that he has not come across a Hanafi work that mirrors the comprehensive approach of al-Subki's Shafi'i book. Having previously authored a short work on controls (dawabit) called al-Fawa'id al-Zayniyya, he was inspired to write a more extensive book covering seven disciplines. The first discipline is the study of qawa'id (fundamental principles), which he describes as the true principles of fiqh, enabling a faqih to reach ijtihad. He emphasizes that he only transmits what is sound and reliable, and if a ruling is based on a weak opinion, he alerts the reader. He then narrates an anecdote about Imam Abu Tahir al-Dabbas, who compiled seventeen principles of Abu Hanifa's madhhab. The story of al-Dabbas and al-Harawi illustrates the high value placed on these principles. The second discipline, dawabit (controls), is particularly useful for teachers, muftis, and judges because it clarifies what is included and excluded from a rule. The remaining five disciplines are: (3) al-jam' wa al-farq (union and difference), (4) al-alghaz (enigmas), (5) al-hiyal (legal stratagems), (6) al-ashbah wa al-naza'ir (similitudes and parallels), and (7) transmitted reports and oddities from the great imams. The author expresses hope that his work will be a valuable resource for scholars and judges, and he recounts his extensive efforts in studying fiqh and usul. The footnote from al-Hamawi clarifies that the anecdote about Abu Tahir is meant to honor the status of the qawa'id, and it discusses the permissibility of the actions narrated.

Key Terms
القواعد (al-qawa'id): The fundamental principles and rules of Hanafi fiqh to which subsidiary rulings are traced back, considered the real foundations of fiqh.
الضوابط (al-dawabit): Specific controls or regulations that define the scope of a rule, including what falls under and what is excluded from it.
الأشباه والنظائر (al-ashbah wa al-naza'ir): A genre of fiqh literature dealing with similar and analogous cases, comparing rulings across different chapters.

The Seven Pillars of Hanafi Qawa'id: Ibn Nujaym's Introduction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the seven divisions of Ibn Nujaym's 'Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir' and explain why the second division (al-dawabit with inclusions/exclusions) is the most beneficial for muftis and judges.

Core Idea
The second division of dawabit with inclusions/exclusions is the most beneficial for muftis and judges.

Ibn Nujaym opens his seminal work on Hanafi legal maxims with a preface that does far more than list contents. He recounts a story about a Shafi'i scholar who learned forty rules from a Hanafi jurist, signaling the trans-madhhab value of qawa'id. Then he lays out seven divisions, ranking the second – dawabit with inclusions and exclusions – as the most useful for teachers, muftis, and judges. This lesson unpacks that claim and orients you to the book's overall methodology and sources, which remain a fundamental reference for Hanafi fatwa today.

Guided Path
1
Trace the opening anecdote
Identify the story of Abu Sa'id al-Harawi learning from Abu Tahir al-Dabbas. Why does Ibn Nujaym include this? What does it say about the value of qawa'id across madhhabs?
The anecdote shows that even a Shafi'i scholar sought Hanafi rules, establishing the universal applicability of well-formed qawa'id.
2
List the seven divisions
From the Arabic text, extract all seven parts of the book: 1) (implied from story) the rules themselves, 2) dawabit with inclusions/exclusions, 3) jama' wa farq, 4) alghaz, 5) hiyal, 6) ashbah wa naza'ir, 7) narrated correspondences.
The second division is explicitly called out as 'anfa' al-aqsam' (most beneficial).
3
Focus on the second division's utility
Note the reason: previous authors mentioned a dabit but excluded some cases; Ibn Nujaym adds more exclusions, so a reader unaware of the additions might mistakenly include cases. This makes the section critical for accurate fatwa and judgment.
This practical refinement is why judges and muftis rely on it.
4
Analyze the author's scholarly pedigree
Ibn Nujaym describes his lifelong dedication to fiqh and usul, listing commentaries on Hidayah, Kanz, Quduri, etc., and fatwa collections. This establishes his authority and the depth of sources behind his qawa'id.
The list is a Hanafi bibliography: it shows the raw material from which the dawabit were distilled.
5
Connect to mufti practice
Consider how a mufti would use this book: faced with a new case, he first checks the relevant dabit in division 2, then sees its exceptions, then cross-references other divisions for puzzles, stratagems, or analogies.
The structure mirrors the mufti's workflow: rule → exception → analogy → resolution.

Definition of Nisyan and Its Legal Effect as a Musqit
Definition and legal effect of forgetfulness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of nisyan according to al-Tahrir and explain why Hanafi scholars consider it musqit (nullifying) in certain contexts.

Core Idea
Forgetfulness (nisyan) is failure to recall at need; it is a legal excuse that can nullify obligations.

This lesson introduces a key legal maxim from the third art of al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: the definition and effect of forgetfulness. Understanding nisyan is essential for applying exemptions in worship and transactions. We will examine the relied-upon definition, the dispute about sahw, and the consensus that it is musqit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
The passage defines nisyan as 'failure to recall a thing at the time of need' (عدم تذكر الشيء وقت حاجته إليه). This definition comes from al-Tahrir, a Hanafi usul work.
Memorize this definition precisely.
2
Understand the synonymy of sahw and nisyan
Scholars differed on whether sahw and nisyan are distinct; the relied-upon (mu'tamad) is that they are synonymous (مترادفان). This means the legal treatment of both is identical in Hanafi fiqh.
In other contexts, sahw may imply a slip during an act, but here they are equated.
3
Grasp the legal effect: musqit
The scholars agreed that nisyan is musqit—it nullifies the original obligation. For example, forgetting a wajib in prayer does not invalidate the prayer but requires sajdah al-sahw instead of repeating the prayer.
Musqit does not mean the act is invalid; it means the obligation to repeat or the penalty is dropped.
4
Consider the broader scope
The passage lists many categories (forgetful, ignorant, coerced, etc.) that will be covered. The principle of musqit applies to many situations where the person is not at fault.
This qawida (maxim) is foundational for exemptions in ibadat and muamalat.

Legal Puzzles (Alghaz) in Hanafi Pedagogy
Introduction to the art of legal puzzles with examples from purification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define alghaz, explain its purpose in Hanafi legal training, and analyze the fiqh reasoning behind one sample puzzle from purification.

Core Idea
Legal puzzles (alghaz) train the mufti to uncover hidden rulings by mastering subtle distinctions in fiqh.

This lesson introduces the fourth art of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: the art of alghaz (legal puzzles). These are not mere riddles but carefully crafted questions that conceal the face of a ruling, testing the student's depth of fiqh knowledge. The author traces the term to the burrow of a jerboa, illustrating how the intended meaning is hidden yet accessible through careful reasoning. We will explore a few examples from the chapter on purification to see how these puzzles work.

Guided Path
1
Understand the etymology of lughz
Read the definition from al-Sihah: lughz originally means the burrow of a jerboa, which has hidden twists. Analogously, a legal puzzle hides the basis of the ruling.
The concealment is meant to challenge the student, not to obscure truth.
2
Recognize the pedagogical role
The author mentions earlier works (Ḥīrat al-Fuqahāʾ, al-ʿUmdah) and his selection from al-Dhakhāʾir al-Ashrafiyyah. Puzzles are used for advanced training, weeding out superficial understanding.
Only strong opinions are included; weak or apparent rulings are omitted.
3
Analyze a sample puzzle
Consider the puzzle: 'What small basin does not become impure by falling impurity?' Answer: 'The basin of a bathhouse if scooping is continuous.' Identify the underlying principle: continuous flow prevents impurity from settling (istihlak).
The puzzle tests knowledge of istihlak (consumption) in water purity.
4
Identify the fiqh distinction
Another puzzle: 'Which well requires only one bucket to be removed?' Answer: 'A well into which the last bucket from a contaminated well was poured.' This tests the rule of minor contamination when the source is already diluted.
The distinction depends on the amount and type of impurity.
5
Reflect on the skill
Solving alghaz requires mastery of usul al-fiqh and detailed knowledge of furu' rulings. The mufti must recall the precise condition that triggers the ruling.
These puzzles sharpen the mufti's ability to think through edge cases.

Key Terms
فروق (furūq): Legal distinctions between two similar concepts or rulings, showing how they differ in application.
فوائد (fawā'id): Benefits or useful points, covering a wide range from rulings to ethical advice, often included to enrich the reader's understanding.
قاعدة (qā'ida): A legal maxim or rule that applies across multiple cases.

Navigating Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: The Six Arts
Table of contents listing fawa'id and six arts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the organizational structure of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir and explain the role of each of the six arts in Hanafi legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir is organized into six arts: Fawa'id, Riddles, Legal Stratagems, and Differences—each serving a unique purpose in Hanafi iftaa.

This lesson introduces the table of contents of Ibn Nujaym's monumental work Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. Understanding its structure is essential for navigating the text and grasping how Hanafi jurists organized legal maxims, riddles, stratagems, and differences. You will learn the purpose of each 'art' and how they contribute to the mufti's toolkit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial fawa'id
The text begins with several 'fawa'id' (beneficial rules). These are standalone legal maxims or observations that will be expanded later. Note the first one: 'Everything the slave will be asked about on the Day of Resurrection except knowledge.' Reflect on the significance of such maxims in Hanafi legal methodology.
Fawa'id are less universal than qawa'id but still serve as guiding principles.
2
Recognize the transition to the Fourth Art
After listing several fawa'id, the text announces 'The Fourth Art: Riddles' (الفن الرابع: الألغاز). This indicates a major division. Riddles (alghaz) are subtle legal problems that test analytical skills.
Note that the first three arts are not explicitly listed here; the table of contents starts from the fourth.
3
Examine the list of fiqh chapters under the Fourth Art
Under the Fourth Art, the text enumerates chapters from various books of fiqh (e.g., Kitab al-Tahara, Kitab al-Salat) with page numbers. These are likely the topics covered under the Riddles art.
The page numbers refer to the original pagination of the work.
4
Understand the Fifth Art: Legal Stratagems
The text then lists 'The Fifth Art: Legal Stratagems' (الفن الخامس: الحِيَل) with subcategories like 'Regarding Prayer, regarding Fasting, regarding Zakat.' This art deals with permissible legal devices to achieve legitimate ends.
Hiyal are a distinctive feature of Hanafi fiqh, often used to avoid unlawful outcomes within the bounds of the law.
5
Understand the Sixth Art: Differences
Finally, 'The Sixth Art: Differences' (الفن السادس: الفروق) is mentioned with a reference to 'Book of Prayer, and in it some issues of Purity.' This art likely contrasts similar rulings to clarify distinctions.
Furuq are essential for a mufti to distinguish between apparently similar cases.

Key Terms

الفن (al-fann): A major division or 'art' of the book; here the book is organized into six arts.
الألغاز (al-alghāz): Riddles; likely a set of subtle legal questions or puzzles.
الحِيَل (al-ḥiyal): Legal stratagems; permissible devices to achieve a legitimate end without violating the law.
الفروق (al-furūq): Differences; a genre that contrasts two similar rulings to clarify the legal distinction.
