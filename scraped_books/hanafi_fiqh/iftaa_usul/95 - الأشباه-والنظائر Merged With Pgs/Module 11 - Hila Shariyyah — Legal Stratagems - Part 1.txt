Rulings on Doubt in Vows and Oaths: Default to the Minimum
Rasm al-Mufti: Rules for doubt in vows and oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim of interpreting doubt in vows and oaths to the least burdensome option, and identify the exceptions.

Core Idea
When doubt arises about the specific type of vow or oath, the law defaults to the least burdensome ruling.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents two classic cases of doubt (shakk) in vows and oaths, along with a supplementary ruling on uncertain number of oaths. The underlying principle is that doubt triggers default rules that minimize legal burden. In fatwa practice, these rules guide the mufti when the questioner cannot specify the exact nature of their vow or oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main types of doubt
The passage lists two scenarios: doubt about the type of vowed act (prayer, fasting, etc.) and doubt about the type of oath (by Allah, divorce, or manumission). Recognize that each has a distinct default ruling.
The first doubt concerns the vow's content; the second concerns the oath's formula.
2
Apply the default for doubtful vows
If someone is unsure whether their vow was prayer, fasting, freeing a slave, or charity, the ruling is that they owe kaffarat yamin (oath expiation). This is analogized from the case of a completely unspecified vow.
The qiyas is based on the shared illa: both involve uncertainty about the specific obligation.
3
Apply the default for doubtful oaths
If someone forgets whether they swore by Allah, by divorce, or by manumission, the oath is invalid (batil). However, there is an exception: if they remember that the oath was conditional and recall the condition, then the oath is treated as an oath by Allah (if the swearer is Muslim).
The exception is based on the assumption that a Muslim's default oath is by Allah.
4
Handle doubt about the number of oaths
If someone knows many oaths are upon them but not the exact count, the legal ruling (hukman) is to observe the minimum number. Precaution (ihtiyat) would require more, but the law does not impose endless obligation.
This applies the maxim 'haml 'ala al-aqall' (interpret based on the minimum).
5
Synthesize the underlying qawida
The common principle is: when doubt prevents certainty about the specific obligation in vows/oaths, the ruling defaults to the least burdensome consequence (kaffarat yamin, invalidity with exception, or minimum number). This is a branch of the broader Hanafi maxim 'al-ashadd la yakun min al-shakk' (severity does not arise from doubt).
This qawida is captured in the text's use of 'yamila 'ala al-aqall' and the analogy to unspecified vow.

Commentary
This passage presents two specific issues of doubt (shakk) in vows and oaths, followed by a supplementary case from al-Yatimah. The author opens by stating the fifth issue: doubt about the specific type of vowed act—whether it is prayer, fasting, manumission, or charity. The ruling here is that such doubt leads to the obligation of kaffarat yamin (oath expiation), analogously to the case where someone says 'upon me is a vow' without specifying; in both scenarios, the doubt prevents a specific obligation, so the fallback is the expiation of an oath. The sixth issue deals with doubt about the type of oath—whether it was by Allah, by divorce, or by manumission. The primary ruling is that the oath is void (batil) due to uncertainty about its subject. The author cites al-Bazzaziyyah for the case where the swearer forgot the type; the oath is invalid. Then from al-Yatimah, a refinement: if the swearer knows that the oath was conditional and knows the condition (e.g., entering the house), but does not remember the formula (by Allah or by divorce), then upon occurrence of the condition, the oath is treated as an oath by Allah (if the swearer is Muslim) because that is the default for a Muslim. Further, if someone knows he has many oaths but not the exact count, he should act on the minimum number legally, though precaution would be endless. These rulings exemplify the Hanafi principle that doubt leads to the least burdensome obligation in matters of vows and oaths.

Key Terms
شك (shakk): Doubt or uncertainty, specifically as a legal impediment that prevents determination of the specific obligation.
كفارة يمين (kaffarat yamin): Expiation for an oath, consisting of feeding/clothing ten poor people, freeing a slave, or fasting three days.
حمل على الأقل (haml 'ala al-aqall): Interpretation based on the minimum possible, to avoid imposing excessive obligation.

The Legal Stratagem (Hila) for Leasing Waqf Land for Grazing
Explains the hila to validly lease waqf land for grazing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a hila to permit the leasing of waqf land for incidental grazing, and apply the conditions of a valid hila.

Core Idea
Leasing waqf land solely for pasture is invalid; the hila is to lease for a valid purpose and then use the grass incidentally.

The text addresses a common problem in our time: the desire to lease waqf land for grazing, which is impermissible if done directly. However, the Hanafi school provides a valid legal stratagem (hila) that allows the same benefit through a permissible primary purpose. This lesson unpacks that hila, its conditions, and the evidence that supports it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
The text states that leasing waqf land for pasture (grazing) alone is invalid (min al-bay‘ al-fasid). Recognize this as the prohibition that necessitates a hila.
Pasture here means leasing the land specifically for the grass.
2
State the Hila Mechanism
The lessee leases the land for a valid purpose, such as setting up a tent (for midday rest) or making a pen for sheep (for shelter), and then incidentally allows animals to graze.
The hila is found in the texts of al-Hidayah, al-Nihayah, and al-Zayla‘i.
3
Extract the Conditions
The lease must be genuinely for the valid primary purpose; the grazing must be incidental and not the main object. The land should not be leased specifically for pasture.
If the primary purpose is a sham, the hila fails.
4
Provide the Evidence
The text uses qiyas: leasing for a tent (nusub al-fustat) is valid for midday rest, so the same applies to maqeel. Similarly, leasing for a pen (hazirah) is valid for maraah.
This is a clear qiyas jaliy (obvious analogy).
5
Apply to Modern Scenarios
Think of a mufti advising someone who wants to use waqf land for grazing: they should lease it for a permissible purpose like parking livestock vehicles or setting up a temporary shed, then let the animals graze.
The incidental benefit must not be the primary intention in the contract.

The legal stratagem (hila) for that is that the lessee leases the land to set up a tent or to make it a pen for his sheep, then he enjoys the pasture. Commentary
This passage addresses a common practice in its time: leasing waqf land for the purposes of midday rest (qaylulah) and evening shelter for cattle. The author confirms the validity of such leases because the land is not leased for agriculture, which is the main purpose, but for other permissible uses. However, leasing land for pasture exclusively is considered invalid (from defective sales). To circumvent this, the text presents a legal stratagem (hila): the lessee should lease the land for a valid purpose like setting up a tent or making a pen for sheep, and then he can legitimately use the grass for grazing as an incidental benefit. Al-Zayla'i adds the alternative of stopping animals. The passage then provides extensive lexicographical definitions of 'maqeel' and 'maraah' to clarify their meanings, confirming that they are places (location for noon rest and cattle shelter). The rulings from al-Hidayah and other sources demonstrate that these purposes are valid grounds for leasing waqf land, and the hila ensures the lease is not for a prohibited purpose (i.e., pasture alone).

Leasing Waqf Land for Rest and Shelter: A Legal Stratagem (Hila)
Ruling on leasing waqf land for qaylulah and marah with a legal stratagem

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a hila to permit grazing on waqf land by reframing the lease purpose as a permissible benefit.

Core Idea
A hila allows an otherwise prohibited action by restructuring the legal basis of the contract.

Leasing waqf land for grazing is generally prohibited, but leasing for permissible purposes like midday rest (qaylulah) or animal shelter (marah) is valid. This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir provides a legal stratagem: lease the land for pitching a tent or making an enclosure, then benefit from the pasture indirectly. It illustrates how Hanafi jurists use detailed lexicography and precedent to craft permissible solutions.

Guided Path
1
State the prohibition
Identify that leasing waqf land for grazing (al-mar'a) is not permissible because it is a direct benefit from the land without a valid contract.
The text says 'ولا تجوز إجارة المراعي'.
2
Identify the hila
The stratagem is to lease the land for a permissible purpose such as pitching a tent (fasit) or making an enclosure (hazira) for animals, then the lessee can use the pasture incidentally.
The hila is explicitly stated: 'أن يستأجر الأرض ليضرب فيها فسطاطا أو ليجعلها حظيرة لغنمه ثم يستبيح المرعى'.
3
Analyze the lexicographical basis
The passage defines 'maqil' and 'marah' precisely to show they are valid, non-agricultural benefits. This justifies the original lease for these purposes.
The author cites dictionaries to confirm that maqil is a place of rest and marah is an evening shelter.
4
Apply the hila to a new scenario
Simulate how a mufti would apply this: a person wants to graze animals on waqf land; the mufti advises leasing it for a different permissible purpose (e.g., parking cars) and then allowing grazing as ancillary.
The underlying principle is that the primary purpose must be permissible and the prohibited benefit is secondary.

Hila to Deny Paternity via Slave Acknowledgment
Legal stratagem for blocking paternity claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the hila (legal stratagem) for denying a paternity claim by using the ruling that a paternity decision is transitive, and identify its conditions.

Core Idea
A paternity ruling binds all; a slave's acknowledgment of a son blocks later claims.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a clever legal stratagem (hila) that uses the transitivity of paternity rulings to block spurious lineage claims. The key is that once a judge rules on paternity based on a slave's acknowledgment, no one—including the claimed son—can later assert a different paternity. Understanding this requires grasping the difference between rulings on slavery (non-transitive) and paternity (transitive).

Guided Path
1
Understand the status of majhool al-nasab
A person of unknown lineage is presumed free. He can, however, validly acknowledge slavery to someone if the master confirms, but only before a court confirms his freedom. After a hadd or qisas judgment, such acknowledgment is void.
This is the baseline: the person is free until he acknowledges otherwise, but the acknowledgment must be timely.
2
Distinguish transitive vs. non-transitive rulings
A judge's ruling on slavery is non-transitive—it only affects the parties and can be challenged later with proof of freedom. A ruling on paternity is transitive—it binds everyone, including third parties, and cannot be overturned by a later claim or evidence.
This difference is the foundation of the hila.
3
Identify the hila's mechanism
If a slave acknowledges a person of unknown lineage as his son, and the son confirms, and the judge rules on the paternity, then afterwards the son cannot claim to be the son of someone else (e.g., to inherit). The slave's master must consent.
The hila works by 'locking in' the paternity to the slave, so no one can later assert a different father.
4
Apply the hila to a modern scenario
A mufti may be asked about a case where someone claims inheritance from a deceased wealthy man, but earlier a judge had ruled him the son of a slave. The hila blocks the claim because the paternity ruling is transitive.
This shows the practical utility of the hila in family law disputes.

Reversibility of Rulings and the Hila to Repel Lineage Claims
Ruling on claiming freedom after iqrar of riqq and a hila to repel lineage claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between reversible (ownership) and irreversible (lineage) court rulings, and apply the hila of a slave's acknowledgment of paternity to block a future lineage claim.

Core Idea
A lineage ruling is transitive and irreversible; a properly structured hila can preempt false claims.

This passage from the Hanafi tradition teaches two key principles: first, that a court ruling on ownership can be reversed with new evidence, while a ruling on lineage is final and binding on all relatives; second, a clever legal stratagem (hila) that uses a slave's acknowledgment of paternity, confirmed by a judge, to permanently bar the 'son' from later claiming a different father. Identify the principle after acknowledging slavery

Ownership rulings are not transitive—they only affect the parties directly.
2
Distinguish ownership rulings from lineage rulings
Lineage rulings are transitive (they affect all descendants and ancestors) and therefore cannot be reversed after final judgment. This is the basis for the hila: once a judge establishes a paternity link, it binds everyone.
The key difference is transitivity (ta'addi).
3
Describe the hila mechanism
A slave of an unknown master acknowledges a particular person (the 'son') as his own, and the 'son' confirms. If the judge rules accordingly, the 'son' cannot later claim a different father. This serves as a preemptive strike against a potential lineage lawsuit.
The slave must be of an age to plausibly be the father.
4
Note the condition from al-Tahdhib
A condition for the hila is the consent of the master (mawla) because the slave's acknowledgment affects the master's rights over the slave's property and lineage.
The master's consent ensures the hila does not harm his interests.
5

Fatwa on Leasing Iqṭā' Land: Validity and Dissolution
Fatwa on validity of leasing iqṭā' land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on the validity of leasing iqṭā' land and identify when such a lease dissolves due to ownership transfer.

Core Idea
Leasing granted land is valid despite possible revocation by the imam; dissolution occurs only if ownership actually transfers.

This passage presents a pivotal fatwa by the Hanafi jurist Qasim ibn Qutlubugha on the permissibility of leasing land granted by the imam (iqṭā'). It explains why the lease is valid even though the grantee does not own the corpus and the imam may revoke the grant. The fatwa hinges on an analogy to the lessee and a set of analogous cases from classical Hanafi law. Understanding this ruling helps muftis navigate contemporary questions about state-allocated land and contracts involving limited ownership rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal issue
Recognize that the question is whether a muqṭa' (grantee) can validly lease the granted land to a third party, given that the muqṭa' only owns the usufruct and the imam retains the right to revoke the grant.
The issue arises from the muqṭa''s limited ownership: he does not own the corpus (raqaba) unless he revived dead land.
2
Understand the key analogy (qiyas)
The muqṭa' is compared to a lessee (musta'jir) rather than a borrower (musta'ir). The muqṭa' owns the usufruct in exchange for his readiness (isti'dād) to fulfill the purpose of the grant, just as a lessee pays rent for usufruct. This analogy justifies the validity of subleasing by the muqṭa'.
The analogy rejects an alternative analogy to borrowing, which would not permit leasing.
3
Apply the qawida on dissolution
The qawida states: if the lessor dies or the imam removes the land itself from the muqṭa', the lease dissolves because ownership transfers to someone other than the original lessor. This is consistent with other Hanafi cases where the lessor loses ownership of the subject matter.
The dissolution is due to transfer of ownership (intiqāl al-milk), not merely the possibility of revocation.
4
Distinguish types of iqṭā'
The imam's right to revoke applies only to grants of developed land (ʿāmira) from the public treasury. If the grantee revives dead land (mawāt), he becomes owner of the corpus, and the imam cannot revoke. This distinction is based on Abu Yusuf's ruling in Kitāb al-Kharāj.
This distinction determines whether the imam's potential revocation can affect the lease.
5
Map the analogous cases (nazā'ir)
The fatwa cites five analogous scenarios where a lease dissolves upon ownership transfer: sublease by lessee, lease of a slave compromised for service, lease by endowment beneficiary, lease by authorized slave of trade capital, and lease of umm walad. These confirm that the dissolution rule is well-established.
These nazā'ir strengthen the fatwa's reasoning by showing consistent application of the same principle.

He clarifies that the imam's right to revoke applies only when he granted developed land from the public treasury; if he granted dead land (mawāt) and the grantee revived it, the imam cannot revoke because the grantee becomes owner of the corpus (raqaba). This is based on Abu Yusuf's ruling in Kitāb al-Kharāj.

Interpreting 'Rahn' in Waqf Conditions: A Hila via Linguistic Interpretation
Fatwa analysing a condition in waqf requiring a pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of haml al-kalam ala al-sihha to construct a valid condition for borrowing waqf books using a linguistic interpretation of 'rahn'.

Core Idea
When the shar'i meaning invalidates a condition, adopt the linguistic meaning to uphold it — haml al-kalam ala al-sihha.

This fatwa addresses a practical dilemma: an endower of books stipulates that they not be borrowed without a pledge (rahn). However, waqf property is a trust, not owned by the beneficiary, so a valid legal pledge is impossible. The Hanafi solution is a legal stratagem (hila): interpret the word 'rahn' in its linguistic sense as a token (tadhkira) to ensure return. This allows the condition to be upheld without violating the nature of trust assets. By studying this case, you will learn how the principle of interpreting words as valid (haml al-kalam ala al-sihha) operates in iftaa, and how to construct a hila when a literal reading leads to invalidity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
The endower's condition requires a 'rahn' (pledge) for borrowing endowed books. Recognize that in Hanafi law, a valid shar'i rahn requires the pledged item to be guaranteed, but waqf property is a trust (amana) in the hands of the beneficiary, so it cannot be pledged. Therefore, the condition appears invalid if 'rahn' is taken in its legal sense.
2
Apply the principle of haml al-kalam ala al-sihha
The principle states: interpret speech as valid as much as possible. When one meaning renders the condition invalid (shar'i rahn) and another plausible meaning renders it valid (linguistic token), the valid meaning is preferred to salvage the endower's intent.
This principle is a key tool for muftis to uphold contracts and conditions.
3
Adopt the linguistic meaning of rahn
The word 'rahn' in Arabic can mean a pledge or a token left as a reminder (tadhkira). Since the shar'i meaning leads to invalidity, the linguistic meaning is adopted: the borrower leaves a token to remember to return the book. This is a valid condition fulfilling the endower's legitimate purpose of ensuring return.
The token does not have the legal rulings of a pledge (e.g., it cannot be sold).
4
Determine the legal effects of the valid condition
Under this interpretation, the condition is valid: the borrower must leave a token. If the book is lost without negligence, the borrower is not liable; if lost due to negligence, he guarantees the book. The token itself remains the property of the borrower and can be taken back when the book is returned.
This differs from a full rahn where the pledge remains with the lender as security.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and its justification
The preferred position (mufta bihi) is to interpret 'rahn' linguistically, upholding the condition. The tarjih is based on the principle of haml al-kalam ala al-sihha and the valid purpose of protecting waqf property. This approach is adopted by later Hanafi authorities like al-Subki and al-Asyuti.
The opposing view (taking rahn in its shar'i sense) leads to invalidity, but is acknowledged as a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Hila on Fosterage and Marriage Prohibition
A hila case resolving marriage prohibition due to fosterage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions of a valid hila in Hanafi law and apply a specific hila concerning fosterage and marriage prohibition.

Core Idea
A hila must be built on genuine legal steps; here, fosterage creates a real kinship prohibition through a valid chain of events.

The Hanafi school permits hiyal (legal stratagems) to achieve a lawful outcome while adhering to the letter of the law. This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a complex hila involving fosterage and marriage prohibition. By tracing the steps of the hila, you will learn how Hanafi jurists constructed permissible stratagems that respect the underlying legal principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
Determine the marriage prohibition at stake. In the passage, the goal is to make a co-wife forbidden to her husband through fosterage. Understand that direct prohibitions may not apply, but a hila can create a new prohibition.
The straightforward rule: if a wife breastfeeds a child, the child becomes her foster-child, but the husband's relationship is unaffected unless the milk is his.
2
Parse the hila's chain of events
List each step: (1) A man marries his young son to a slave-girl. (2) She is freed and exercises the option to annul the marriage (takhayyur). (3) She marries another man. (4) That man's wife (the first man's co-wife) breastfeeds the boy (the first man's son) with the milk of her current husband.
Each step is a valid legal act, not a simulated transaction.
3
Verify the fosterage relationship
Confirm that the milk belongs to the man (the current husband) and that the child is breastfed sufficiently (e.g., five full feedings within two years). This creates a foster-father relationship between the man and the boy.
Hanafi conditions for fosterage: milk from a woman, with the husband as the cause, and sufficient quantity.
4
Determine the resulting prohibition
The man becomes the foster-father of the boy. Consequently, the man's marriage to his wife (the co-wife) becomes invalid because she is now married to his foster-son. The prohibition arises from the fosterage kinship, not from deception.
This prohibition is real and permanent, as the marriage becomes void.
5
Extract the legal principle
The hila is valid because it consists of genuine legal transactions (marriage, freedom, remarriage, breastfeeding). The outcome is not a trick but a natural consequence of valid acts. This illustrates the Hanafi maxim that hiyal are permissible when based on sound legal constructions.
The hila does not involve fraud or evasion of an existing obligation; it creates a new legal relationship.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents three legal stratagems (hiyal) used to avoid the application of hadd punishments. As a mufti, you must understand these stratagems to correctly advise in cases where punishments may be averted due to technicalities.

1
Analyze the theft hila
The condition for amputation is theft of property worth 10 dirhams or more from a secure place in a single act. Stealing in installments of less than 10 each avoids the nisab threshold, so no amputation applies.
The total accumulated value is irrelevant; each theft is considered separately.
2
Analyze the father's property hila
The hadd for theft is not applied to a biological son who steals from his father. However, if the thief is a foster son (from breastfeeding), the relationship does not prevent punishment, so amputation is possible.
Fosterage creates a legal impediment only in marriage, not in theft.
3
Analyze the wine drinking hila
For hadd of flogging for wine drinking, valid testimony requires two male witnesses or one male and two females only in financial matters. Using one man and two women proves the freeing of the slave but not the hadd, as their testimony is insufficient for criminal punishment.
Hanafi law requires two male witnesses for hudud; one man and two women are not accepted.

For waqf, the founder's own possession invalidates it; an agent's possession perfects it.

This lesson explores a key hila in Hanafi waqf law: the act of taking possession (qabd). The founder cannot take possession himself, but his agent can. This distinction preserves the charitable nature of waqf by severing the founder's ownership.

The founder appoints an agent to take possession. The agent's qabd makes the waqf valid while the founder's own qabd would not.
The agent must be a separate legal entity.

If the founder takes possession, it implies he still considers the property his own, contradicting the transfer of ownership required for waqf. An agent's possession severs that link.
This reasoning is based on the maxim: 'What is done by the founder himself may imply retention of ownership.'
4
Conditions for the hila
The agent must be appointed explicitly, and the qabd must be performed by the agent with the intention of perfecting the waqf.
No special формаlity is required; any clear appointment suffices.
5
Practical application
In practice, the founder declares the waqf and then instructs the agent to take possession. The agent physically takes control of the property.
The agent should not be the founder himself masquerading as an agent.

Hila in Salah: Converting Fard to Nafl to Join Congregation
Legal stratagem to exit obligatory prayer without sin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct and apply the Hanafi legal stratagem (hila) for converting a fard prayer to nafl when congregational prayer begins, identifying the conditions and the underlying Hanafi wajib principle.

Core Idea
Omit the final sitting (wajib) intentionally to transform fard into nafl and join the congregation.

Imagine you have begun praying Zuhr alone, and during your prayer the iqama for congregational prayer is given. Breaking the fard without a valid excuse is sinful, but missing the congregation also means losing great reward. The Hanafi school provides a juristic stratagem (hila) that allows you to complete your prayer as a nafl (supererogatory) and then join the imam. This stratagem relies on the distinction between fard and wajib in Hanafi fiqh, specifically that the final sitting (qada) for a four-rak'ah prayer is wajib, not fard. By intentionally omitting it, the prayer's obligation is invalidated, but the act of worship remains as nafl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
Recognize the scenario: you have started a fard Zuhr prayer alone, and during it the iqama for congregational prayer is announced. Stopping the prayer without excuse is prohibited, and you wish to join the congregation without sin.
The problem is the conflict between the obligation to complete the fard and the desire to join the congregation for greater reward.
2
Recall the Hanafi Ruling on the Final Sitting
In the Hanafi school, the final tashahhud (sitting) after the fourth rak'ah of Zuhr is wajib, not fard. This distinction is crucial: omitting a fard invalidates the prayer entirely, while omitting a wajib intentionally invalidates the fard obligation but leaves the prayer as a valid nafl if intended as worship.
This principle is based on the Hanafi usul that the prayer's validity requires intention, and the omission of a wajib does not render the act of worship void if the intention of prayer remains.
3
Apply the Stratagem: Omit the Final Sitting
When you complete the fourth rak'ah, do not sit for the final tashahhud. Instead, simply stand up or proceed to join the congregation. By not sitting, you have intentionally omitted a wajib component of the fard prayer.
If you had already sat even for a moment, the fard would be considered complete, and the hila would no longer work.
4
Consequence: Prayer Becomes Nafl
Due to the omission of the wajib sitting, the prayer loses its fard status and becomes a nafl prayer. Your original intention of fard is overridden by the new reality: the prayer is now a supererogatory act.
The transformation happens automatically; no additional intention is needed. The original intention of prayer suffices.
5
Join the Congregational Prayer
Now that your prayer is nafl, you can exit it (by saying salam) or simply join the imam in congregational prayer for Zuhr. You may either complete the nafl as two rak'ahs (since nafl in pairs is preferred) or break it and start anew with the congregation. The preferred method is to join the congregation immediately after the hila.
Some fuqaha recommend completing the nafl as two rak'ahs of nafl before joining, but the immediate joining is also valid.

And the best is that she marries him with the condition that she has the power of divorce in her own hand, provided she begins with that and then he accepts. But if the muhallil begins and says 'I married you on condition that your affair is in your hand' and she accepts, it does not become her power, unless he says 'on condition that your affair is in your hand after I marry you' and she accepts.

Commentary
This lengthy hila deals with tahlil (making a woman lawful for her first husband after three divorces by marrying and divorcing another man). The temporary husband (muhallil) can place a conditional divorce before consummation so that the marriage ends automatically after intercourse or after a set period. The preferred method is for the woman to have the power of divorce (tamlik) from the start. However, if the muhallil declares that condition first, the woman must accept after the marriage is contracted for it to be valid. Various nuances are discussed.

Key Terms
المحلل (al-muhallil): The temporary husband who marries a thrice-divorced woman to make her halal for her first husband.
التحليل (al-tahlil): The act of making a thrice-divorced woman lawful for her first husband through marriage and divorce with another man.
الحيلة (al-hila): A legal stratagem to achieve a permissible outcome without violating the law.
تمليك الطلاق (tamlik al-talaq): Granting the woman the power to divorce herself.
