Listing numerical counts and legal status of supererogatory prayers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the twelve sunan rawatib rak'ahs and identify the mustahabb prayers with their rak'ah counts as per the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The sunan rawatib are twelve rak'ahs daily; other prayers like tarawih, witr, 'eid, eclipse, and various mustahabb add to the supererogatory worship.

This passage provides a concise inventory of the regular sunan (rawatib) and other recommended prayers. Understanding these numbers helps a mufti guide laypeople on which supererogatory prayers to prioritize and the differing views within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core list
Locate the sentence stating the total number of sunan rawatib (12 rak'ahs) and break down the five time slots with their respective rak'ah counts.
The breakdown is: 2 before Fajr, 4 before Zuhr, 2 after Zuhr, 2 after Maghrib, 2 after 'Isha.
2
Note additional prayers linked to specific days
Identify the prayers for Jumu'ah (4 before and 4 after), Tarawih (20 rak'ahs with 10 salams), and the mention of Witr (according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), 'Eid (in one narration), and eclipse prayers (on the correct view).
Some of these have variant rulings; note the qualifiers like 'on their view' or 'in one narration'.
3
Extract the mustahabb list
Find the section beginning with 'As for the mustahabb' and list each recommended prayer with its rak'ah count, including those before 'Asr, before 'Isha, additional prayers after Zuhr, Maghrib, 'Isha, and others like sunna of wudu, tahiyyat al-masjid, Duha, etc.
Note that some mustahabb prayers have minimum and maximum rak'ahs, e.g., Duha (4-12).

Understand substitution rules
Observe the note that tahiyyat al-masjid can be substituted by any prayer performed upon entry, and similarly the two rak'ahs of ihram can be substituted by any prayer, obligatory or nafl.
This is a practical fiqh point often used in fatwa.

The Rule on Multiple Iḥrāms: Cancellation and Double Penalties
Ruling on multiple iḥrāms: cancellation and double penalties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that simultaneous iḥrāms for ‘umrah require cancellation of one and double penalties for violations.

Core Idea
Entering two iḥrāms at once? Cancel one, proceed with the other, and double the penalties.

This passage from Al-Ashbāh wa al-Naẓā’ir presents a key rule for muftis: when someone enters iḥrām for two ‘umrahs (or combines ḥajj and ‘umrah in one iḥrām?), the juristic ruling demands cancellation of one and multiplication of penalties. The rule reflects the Hanafi principle that iḥrām cannot be assumed twice simultaneously – a person is either in one state of consecration or the other. This lesson unpacks the rule’s components and its practical application for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and initial ruling
The person enters iḥrām for two ‘umrahs. The ruling is that one must be cancelled and the other proceeded with.
The cancellation is of the later iḥrām or the second intention; the first stands.
2
Note the required compensation
After completing the chosen ‘umrah, the person must later perform a ḥajj and an ‘umrah in place of the cancelled one. This is a heavy compensation.
The text says 'wa ḥajja wa ‘umrah' – some scholars interpret this as a full ḥajj and an additional ‘umrah.
3
Identify penalties for violations
If game is killed during the combined iḥrām states, two blood prices are due. If the person is prevented (iḥṣār), two blood offerings are required.
This is because he is considered to be in two iḥrāms simultaneously, so each triggers its own penalty.
4
Recognize the extension to consecutive entry
The same ruling applies if the two ‘umrah iḥrāms are entered consecutively without a gap. However, the text signals a difference of opinion on this point.
The phrase 'wa ‘alā hādhā al-khilāf' indicates that the ruling for consecutive entry is disputed.

Intention to Transition During Worship
Ruling on intending to transition from one prayer to another

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that intention alone does not effect a transition in worship without the takbir, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Intention to switch prayers without takbir leaves you in the first prayer.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir clarifies a common scenario: while performing one prayer, you think of switching to another. The Hanafi position is clear: mere intention is insufficient; only the takbir (saying 'Allahu Akbar' to start the new prayer) exits you from the current one. This rule also applies to renewing the same prayer. Understanding this prevents confusion between internal resolve and actual legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The passage discusses a person who is performing a prayer and during it intends to transition to another worship.
The act in question is the intention to change the current prayer.
2
Distinguish two scenarios
The rule distinguishes whether the person says the takbir for the new worship or not.
Takbir is the key action element.
3
State the ruling for each scenario
If takbir is said with the intention to transition, the person exits the first worship. If no takbir, they remain in the first.
Intention alone is legally ineffective for exit.
4
Note the analogy to renewing the same prayer
The author states that the same rule applies if one intends to renew the current prayer: takbir is required to exit and restart.
This reinforces the principle that takbir is the operative act.
5
Locate the full discussion
The passage references the chapter on nullifiers of prayer in the commentary on al-Kanz for details.
Further examples and exceptions are found there.

Time of Intention for Purification Acts
Rulings on when to intend during purification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended and valid times of intention for wudu, ghusl, tayammum, and using already-used water according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Intention for purification acts must be made at specific points: face for wudu, earth for tayammum, scooping for used water.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir clarifies the precise moments when niyyah (intention) should be placed for various acts of purification. Understanding these timings is essential for both validity and reward in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The passage covers four acts: wudu, ghusl, tayammum, and using used water (ma' musta'mal). Identify each.
Ghusl is said to be like wudu in terms of sunan timing.
2
State the time of intention for each
For wudu, the primary time is at washing the face; but it is recommended to intend earlier at the start of sunan (washing hands) to gain reward for those sunan. For ghusl, same as wudu. For tayammum, at placing hands on earth. For used water, at scooping.
The text emphasizes that for sunan, intending earlier brings reward.
3
Distinguish between obligatory and recommended
The obligatory intention for wudu is at washing the face; but intending earlier is recommended for sunan reward. This distinction is important for fatwa.
A mufti should advise accordingly.

Time of Intention for Following an Imam
Rulings on when to intend for following imam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between intention for reward and intention for validity when following an imam, and apply the Hanafi rules for the timing of intention in congregational prayer.

Core Idea
For following an imam, the best time to intend is at the imam's opening; intending before he starts is permissible, but assuming he started when he hasn't is disputed.

This passage from the same work addresses the timing of intention for iqtida' (following the imam). It distinguishes between intention for reward (thawab) and intention for validity of the following. The author cites Fath al-Qadir for the validity conditions.

This disagreement is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
4
Apply the ruling
A mufti should advise that for validity, it is best to intend at the imam's opening. If the follower intends before the imam starts, it is valid as long as he knows the imam hasn't started. The doubtful scenario is to be avoided.

Time of Intention for Zakat and Sadaqat al-Fitr
Rules on when intention must be made for zakat and fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on intention for zakat, including advancing intention at setting aside (azl) and the condition for valid delayed intention.

Core Idea

This lesson presents a concise rule from the Hanafi legal maxims regarding the timing of intention in Hajj. Unlike prayer or fasting, where intention can be concurrent, Hajj requires intention to precede all actions, established at the moment of ihram. This passage contains two distinct rulings. First: marriage can be annulled (faskh) only before consummation (tamam, which is consummation or valid seclusion). After consummation, annulment is not possible except in two exceptional cases: apostasy of one spouse and one spouse owning the other (e.g., through purchase). In these two cases, marriage is annulled even after consummation. The term 'iqalah' refers to rescission of contract, which is invalid for marriage. 'Juhud' means denial (e.g., one spouse denies the marriage); it does not annul the marriage. In all these cases, the wife is entitled to the full mahr.

The Maxim of Apostasy Repentance and the Definition of Iman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'Inkar al-ridda tawba' to cases of alleged apostasy, distinguish between apostasy that accepts repentance and that which does not, and use the definitions of iman and kufr from the text.

Core Idea
Denial of apostasy is repentance — but reviling the Prophet or the two shaykhs is unforgivable.

This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi maxim governing apostasy cases. The text defines iman as affirming all that the Prophet brought by necessity, and kufr as denying any of that. The maxim 'إنكار الردّة توبة' (Denial of apostasy is repentance) means that if a person accused of apostasy denies it, his denial is treated as a return to Islam. However, there are exceptions for those whose repentance is not accepted, such as those who revile the Prophet or the two shaykhs. Understanding this maxim is crucial for a mufti facing allegations of apostasy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its scope
The maxim is 'إنكار الردّة توبة'. It applies to apostasy cases where the accused denies the charge.
2
Parse the legal definitions
Note the definitions of iman (tasdiq of all necessary knowledge) and kufr (takdhib of any necessary knowledge). Understand that juhud (denial) is the basis for takfir, but only regarding matters known by necessity.
The text says 'ولا يكفر أحد من أهل القبلة إلا بجحود ما أدخله فيه'.
3
Apply the maxim to the scenario
When witnesses testify to apostasy and the accused denies, the ruling is that he is not interfered with because his denial is repentance. But this applies only to those whose repentance is accepted.
The text uses Fath al-Qadir to clarify.
4

Coercion in Property and Marriage: Hanafi Rulings
Memorization anchor for coerced acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that coerced property dispositions are rescinded except for acts of piety, and distinguish between divorce and agency under coercion.

Core Idea
Coercion rescinds property deals but not acts of piety; divorce sticks, agency doesn't.

In Hanafi fiqh, coercion (ikrah) generally nullifies the legal effect of a disposition, but there are important exceptions. This lesson covers the rules for a coerced purchaser's subsequent acts, divorce under coercion, and marriage with an excessive dower. These rulings are essential for a mufti when advising on cases of duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Start with the overarching principle: a coerced person's disposal of property is rescinded (yufsakh). This applies to contracts like kitabah (manumission by contract) and ijarah (lease).
The ruling is that the act is valid but rescindable, not void ab initio.
2
Memorize the exceptions
Three acts are not rescinded despite coercion: tadbir (declaration of freedom upon master's death), istilad (giving birth to a child by the owner), and itq (immediate manumission). These are acts of piety (qurbah) and are upheld.
The exception is based on the principle that acts of worship are not nullified by coercion.
3
Apply divorce rules
If a person is coerced into pronouncing divorce (talaq), it is effective (waqaʿa). However, if he is coerced into appointing a proxy (wakil) for divorce, the proxy's subsequent divorce is not effective.
Divorce is an act of repudiation that takes effect even under duress, but agency is a disposition that is rescinded.
4
Handle marriage with coerced dower
If a person is coerced into marrying with a dower (mahr) exceeding the proper amount (mahr al-mithl), only the proper amount is wajib. The excess is batil (void), and the coercer is not liable to compensate.
The marriage itself is valid, but the excessive portion of the dower is invalid.

A) It is optional; the layperson may ignore it.
B) It is binding on the layperson, just as ijtihad is binding on the mujtahid.
C) It is only a suggestion with no legal force.
D) It is equivalent to a court judgment.

Key Takeaway
The fatwa of a qualified mufti is binding on the layperson, just as ijtihad is binding on the mujtahid, unless the layperson knows a clear text contradicting it.
