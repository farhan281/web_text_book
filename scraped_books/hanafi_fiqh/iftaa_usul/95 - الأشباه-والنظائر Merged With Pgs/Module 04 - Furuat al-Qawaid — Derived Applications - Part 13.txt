Differences Between Emancipation and Divorce: A Qawida Analysis
Distinction in word usage, religious weight, and legal claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three legal differences between 'itq and talaq according to the Hanafi school and apply the maxim that words of emancipation can effect divorce.

Core Idea
Words of emancipation can cause divorce if intended, but divorce words do not emancipate.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents a concise set of three fundamental dissimilarities between emancipation ('itq) and divorce (talaq). Understanding these distinctions is crucial for a mufti when encountered with cases where ambiguous words are used, as the outcome hinges on the interplay of word usage, intention, and legal context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three differences
Read the passage carefully and list the three points where 'itq and talaq differ according to the text.
The differences are in word usage, religious weight, and claimant status.
2
Analyze the first difference: word usage
Understand that divorce can be effected by words of emancipation if intended, but emancipation cannot be effected by words of divorce. This is a key Hanafi rule.
The term 'ant harrah' (you are free) can be kinayah for divorce.
3
Analyze the second difference: religious weight
Note that divorce is described as the most hated permissible act (abghad al-mubahat), whereas emancipation is a virtuous act. This affects the moral assessment of the act.
Despite being permissible, divorce is discouraged.
4
Analyze the third difference: claimant status
In some legal claims (e.g., when a woman claims divorce and the husband denies), the husband is considered the claimant with the burden of proof. In emancipation claims, the master is not automatically the claimant.
This difference arises from the nature of the rights involved.
5
Apply the qawida
Use the maxim 'yqa' al-talaq bi-alfaz al-'itq duna 'aksih' (divorce occurs by words of emancipation, not vice versa) to new scenarios.
Always verify the intention of the speaker.

Third, in certain legal contexts (e.g., when a woman claims divorce and the man denies), the husband is considered the claimant (with the burden of proof) in divorce cases, whereas in emancipation cases (e.g., if a slave claims freedom), the master is not automatically deemed a claimant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two differences between 'itq and waqf in Hanafi law and apply the legal maxim to evaluate the validity of suspensive conditions and revocability.

Core Idea
Emancipation tolerates conditions and is irreversible; endowment must be immediate and can be revoked if confined to specific persons.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents two key distinctions between emancipation and endowment. Understanding these differences is essential for a mufti to correctly advise on whether a particular declaration is valid and which rulings apply to revocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal acts
Recognize that 'itq (emancipation) and waqf (endowment) are distinct acts with different rulings. The passage gives two dimensions: suspension and revocability.
2
Understand the suspension rule
Read the first difference: 'al-'itq yaqbalu al-ta'liq bikhilaf al-waqf'. This means emancipation can be made conditional on a future event, but waqf cannot.
The condition must be a future, uncertain event.
3
Understand the revocability rule
Read the second difference: 'wa la yartaddu bil-radd bikhilaf al-waqf 'ala mu'ayyan'. Once emancipation is executed, it cannot be revoked. However, a waqf designated to a specific person (e.g., a family member) can be revoked by the founder.
This revocation is not allowed for general charitable waqf.
4
Note the exception for waqf on a designated person
The passage specifies 'al-waqf 'ala mu'ayyan' as the exception to irreversibility. Understand that this exception only applies when the beneficiaries are specific individuals, not the general public.

Differences Between Fasid and Sahih Sale in Hanafi Fiqh
Distinction in consequences between fasid and sahih sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that a fasid sale does not transfer ownership until possession, and apply it to deduce rulings on emancipation, processing, and pre-emption.

Core Idea
In a fasid sale, ownership does not transfer until possession; thus, the seller retains rights over the sold item.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir enumerates several practical differences between a voidable (fasid) and a valid (sahih) sale in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding these differences is crucial for a mufti when determining ownership and liability in defective contracts. The underlying principle is that a fasid sale does not transfer ownership until the buyer takes possession with mutual consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the underlying principle
Recognize that the entire passage is governed by the maxim: 'A fasid sale does not transfer ownership except by possession.' This explains why the seller retains control over the sold item until the buyer takes delivery.
This maxim is a minor (sughra) legal principle in the Hanafi school.
2
Parse each furu' (branch case)
Work through each of the six scenarios: (1) emancipation by repeating the word, (2) emancipation by buyer's order, (3) grinding wheat, (4) slaughtering sheep, (5) release from price after rescission, (6) pre-emption. For each, note the ruling in fasid and how it differs from sahih.
The first four cases show the seller's actions are effective; the fifth deals with liability; the sixth with a property right.
3
Apply the principle to explain each case
For each case, explain why ownership staying with the seller yields the stated ruling. For example, because the seller still owns the slave, his repetition of emancipation frees the slave. Because he owns the wheat, grinding it produces flour belonging to him.
Contrast with a valid sale where ownership transfers immediately, so the seller's actions would be ineffective.
4
Extract the consequence for shuf'a
Pre-emption (shuf'a) exists only when ownership is fully transferred by a valid sale. In fasid, ownership remains inchoate, so no pre-emption arises.
This case directly tests the student's grasp of the condition for shuf'a.
5
Synthesize the qawida
Formulate the maxim that ties all these cases together: 'A fasid sale does not transfer ownership except by possession (qabd).' This is the legal rule that the mufti uses to derive the rulings.
This qawida is a sub-maxim under the larger maxim 'Al-umur bi-maqasidiha' but specific to sales.

Commentary
This passage enumerates several differences in the legal consequences of a fasid (voidable) sale versus a sahih (valid) sale in Hanafi fiqh. In a fasid sale, the buyer does not become owner, so actions by the seller after the contract may still affect the seller. For example: (i) If the seller repeats a word of emancipation after the buyer (who has not taken possession), the emancipation is valid because the seller still owns the slave; in a valid sale, the buyer owns it, so the seller's emancipation would be invalid. In a valid sale, after rescission by mutual consent, no price is owed. (vi) There is no right of pre-emption (shuf'a) in a fasid sale because ownership is not fully transferred; in a valid sale, pre-emption exists.

The Hanafi school distinguishes the supreme political leader (imam/caliph) from the judge (qadi) in several key conditions. The judge hears all claims; the muhtasib only public nuisances like impurity and fraud, and needs no witnesses.

The pledgee's right of retention is stronger than the seller's: it survives lending, defective payment, and buyer's disposition.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents four contrasting scenarios between the retention of a pledge and a sold item. Differences Between Executor and Heir
Lists differences between an executor and an heir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the legal differences between an executor (wasi) and an heir (warith) in Hanafi law and apply the underlying maxim to new cases.

Core Idea
The heir's ownership overrides the executor's authority in certain dispositions.

Both the executor and the heir represent the deceased in managing the estate, but their powers differ because the heir actually owns the property. This lesson explores two key differences: the methods of emancipating a bequeathed slave and the authority to sell estate assets for debts. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for applying the correct legal maxim in estate matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the shared role
Recognize that both executor and heir represent the deceased (khilafa 'an al-mayyit) in disposing of the estate. This is the common ground from which differences arise.
The text opens by stating this shared representation.
2
Locate the key distinction
The heir is stronger because he owns the asset (milk al-ayn). This ownership is the root cause (illa) for the broader powers the heir possesses compared to the executor.
3
Analyze the first furu: emancipation
If the deceased bequeaths emancipation of a specific slave, the heir can do so in four ways: immediate (tanjiz), conditional (ta'liq), tadbir, and kitaba. The executor can only do immediate emancipation. Note that the executor is limited because he does not own the slave.
Source: al-Talkhis
4
Analyze the second furu: sale for debts
The heir cannot sell a share of the estate to pay debts or execute bequests, even in the executor's absence, unless ordered by a judge. This shows that the executor has some authority over debt payment that the heir lacks, but the heir's ownership gives him control over the asset itself.
Source: al-Khaniyyah
5
Extract the qawida
From these differences, derive the maxim: 'The one with ownership of the asset has broader powers in disposition than a mere representative.' This qawida governs the hierarchy of authority between the heir and executor.
This is a wusta (intermediate) maxim relevant to estate law.

The executor can only do immediate emancipation. Furthermore, the heir cannot sell a share of the estate to pay debts or execute bequests without a judge's order, even if the executor is absent. First, clearly state the obligatory act itself (e.g., reciting Fatiha in salah, wiping a quarter of the head in wudu) and the extra action added (e.g., reciting entire Quran, wiping the whole head).
The extra must be of the same genus as the wajib (e.g., more recitation, more wiping).
2
Check if the extra is inherently part of the wajib's nature
If the extra is of the same type and inseparable in the act's common performance (like reciting more Quran after Fatiha), the maxim tends to make the whole wajib. If the extra is a distinct category (like washing a limb more than once), it usually remains sunnah.
This step explains why reciting whole Quran becomes fard, but extra washing does not.
3
Consult the relied-upon (mu'tamad) ruling for each specific case
Even when the maxim seems applicable, later Hanafi authorities have settled some cases. For wiping the head, the mu'tamad is that only a quarter is fard, the rest sunnah, despite the maxim seemingly making all fard.
This shows the maxim is not universal; exceptions exist based on evidence (dalil).
4
Apply the same reasoning to new, similar cases
Use the pattern from established furu' to evaluate novel scenarios, e.g., standing longer at Arafah or increasing maintenance for a wife. The mufti must consider if the extra is analogous to the salat examples (where extra becomes fard) or the wudu examples (where extra remains sunnah).

Singular Noun with Definite Possessive Signals Generality
Linguistic-legal maxim with fiqh applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-mufrad al-mudaf ila ma'rifa lil-umum' to derive rulings in bequest, waqf, and conditional divorce, and identify the exception for oath statements.

Core Idea
A singular noun in construct with a definite noun signals universality — unless custom overrides.

In this passage, we explore a powerful linguistic-legal maxim that governs the interpretation of many fiqh texts: the singular noun when added to a definite noun (e.g., 'Zaid's child') indicates generality. The author demonstrates its application in bequests, waqf, and conditional divorce, and then carves out an important exception for oaths, where custom and intent override the literal generality.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Begin by memorizing the maxim: 'المفرد المضاف إلى معرفة للعموم' (A singular noun added to a definite noun indicates generality). This is the foundation.
The maxim is a dabt, a sub-maxim that clarifies how to interpret such constructs.
2
Identify the evidential basis from the Qur'an
The author cites the verse 'فَلْيَحْذَر الذينَ يُخَالِفُونَ عَنْ أمْره' (An-Nur 63) where 'amri' (his command) is a singular noun added to a definite (Allah). This is used as proof that the command indicates obligation.
This shows the maxim is not just grammatical but has usul al-fiqh implications.
3
Work through the first furu‘ (bequest/waqf)
Apply the maxim to the case: a bequest or waqf for 'Zaid's child' (singular, definite). According to the maxim, it includes all children, male and female. This ruling is from Fath al-Qadir.
The furu‘ demonstrates the practical fiqh consequence of the maxim.
4
Work through the second furu‘ (conditional divorce)
Apply the maxim to the conditional divorce case: 'If your pregnancy is a male...' The word 'al-haml' (pregnancy) is singular and definite, so it refers to the entire pregnancy. When she gives birth to twins, the condition is not met, so no divorce occurs.
This shows how the maxim prevents incorrect divorce outcomes.
5
Analyze the exception (oaths) and identify the competing maxim
The author notes that in oath contexts (e.g., 'My wife is divorced' without specification), the maxim does not apply. Instead, custom and intent are prioritized. The competing maxim is 'الأيمان تبنى على العرف' (oaths are built upon custom).
This exception is crucial: not all singular definite constructs are universal; the context (especially oaths) can restrict the generality.

Commentary
This passage presents a key linguistic-legal maxim: 'A singular noun added to a definite noun indicates generality.' The author first cites the Qur'anic verse from Surah An-Nur as evidence, interpreting 'his command' as 'every command.' He then provides two fiqh applications derived from this maxim. The first application concerns bequests and waqf: if a person makes a bequest or waqf for 'Zaid's child' (which is a singular noun, 'walad', added to the definite 'Zaid'), it includes all children, both male and female, because the structure implies generality. This ruling is from Fath al-Qadir. The second application is a conditional divorce: a man says to his pregnant wife, 'If your pregnancy is a male, you are divorced once; if it is a female, you are divorced twice.' If she gives birth to twins (one male and one female), she is not divorced at all. Why? Because 'al-haml' (the pregnancy) is a singular noun added to a definite possessive ('your'), and it refers to the whole pregnancy. Since the condition requires the entire pregnancy to be male or female, this condition is not fulfilled with twins. Az-Zayla'i reported this. The author then notes an exception: if one says 'My wife is divorced' (addressing one wife) without specifying, it only results in one divorce, not all wives. Similarly, 'My slave is free' frees one slave. This exception is because such statements belong to the chapter of oaths (al-ayman), which are interpreted according to custom or intention, not by strict linguistic generality. The maxim thus has its limits, especially in oath contexts where the speaker's intent and custom prevail.

عموم (‘umūm): Linguistic generality, covering all members of the category denoted by the noun.
فروع فقهية (furū‘ fiqhiyya): Specific applied rulings derived from a general maxim.

Legal Maxim: People in Plague-Stricken City Are Treated as Healthy

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Plague-city residents are healthy in law until actually struck; majority survival governs.

When a deadly disease sweeps a city, how does Islamic law classify its residents? The Hanafi school applies a practical maxim: as long as the majority survive, residents are treated as healthy (sahih) for legal purposes. This principle, derived from the broader rule of al-ghālib (dominant outcome), affects inheritance, divorce, and wills. In this lesson, we unpack the maxim, its applications, and its sole exception.

Guided Path
1
State the Maxim

The simile to soldiers in battle emphasizes that both face danger but are considered healthy because the majority survive.
2
Identify the Scope
Determine which fiqh chapters this maxim governs: divorce (talaq) – inheritance rights of divorced wife, will (wasiyya) – restriction to one-third, and any transaction involving status of terminal illness.
The scope is defined by the ruling applied to a terminally ill patient (marid).
3
Work through the Furuat
Apply the maxim to the two cases given: (1) a man divorces his wife while in a plague city – no inheritance for wife if he dies; (2) he makes a will giving away more than one-third – the will is valid as a healthy person's will.
The reasoning is that the dominant outcome (ghalib) is safety, so the man is considered healthy.
4
Identify the Istithna'
Note the exception: once a person actually contracts the plague (tu'ina), he becomes a true patient (marid) and the rules for terminal illness apply. The maxim only applies to those not yet infected.
This exception is based on the change in the dominant outcome from safety to destruction.

Core Maxim: Voidness of the Container Voids the Contained

The Principle that Void Contracts Void Implicit Consequences
Analyzing the invalidity of implicit consequences in void contracts with multiple furūʿ and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'إذا بطل المتضمن بطل ما في ضمنه' to identify implicit effects that are void and distinguish the exceptions where implicit effects survive.

Core Idea
When a contract is void, everything it implies is also void — but custom and intention can preserve some effects.

This lesson explores a foundational Hanafi maxim that governs countless transactions: when a primary contract (المتضمن) is invalid, any legal consequences implicitly contained within it (ما في ضمنه) are also invalid. However, as with many legal principles, there are crucial exceptions where the implicit effect remains despite the contract's invalidity. Understanding both the rule and its exceptions is essential for a mufti to issue correct fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the container and its content
For a given transaction, determine which act is the primary contract (al-mutadammin) and which legal effect is implicitly contained within it (ma fi dimnihi).
Example: In a sale that includes permission to kill, the sale is the container and the permission is the content.
2
Apply the general rule
If the primary contract is void (bāṭil) or corrupt (fāsid), then the implicit effect is also void, unless an established exception applies.
The default is that the content follows the container.
3
Check for exceptions (istithnāʾāt)
Review the list of exceptions where the implicit effect survives despite the container's invalidity. These exceptions are based on custom, intention, or independent legal reasoning.
Common exceptions include: renewal for increase of mahr, sale of fruits with lease of trees, shuf'a cases, etc.
4
Determine the mufta bihi ruling
After applying the rule and checking exceptions, decide the operative ruling for the case. The exceptions are part of the established Ḥanafī position and must be incorporated into fatwa.
The mufti must be careful not to over-apply the rule without considering the exceptions.

The source lists no exceptions (istithnā‘) to these rulings. However, note that in the first case, the father's right of revocation is specific to when the son cannot own; if the son were free, the gift would be final.

A depositor claims his property was held as a wadi'ah (deposit) by a deceased person. The heir admits the deposit, but the deceased has unpaid debts. Does the depositor get his property back? Identify the scenario and parties

Breaking Your Prayer to Join the Congregation
Distinction between breaking nafl vs fard to join congregation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the distinction between nafl and fard prayers when deciding whether to break prayer to join the congregation.

Core Idea
Break your fard prayer to join the congregation to perfect it; do not break your nafl.

A person may start praying alone and then hear the congregational prayer being established. Should he break his prayer to join? Hanafi law distinguishes between supererogatory (nafl) and obligatory (fard) prayers. This lesson explains the reasoning behind the rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage considers: (a) a person who has started a nafl prayer, (b) a person who has started a fard prayer. The congregation is established after his start.
The prayer being offered in congregation could be a different prayer (e.g., he is praying fard alone for the same time, or a nafl).
2
State the ruling for each
For nafl: he does not cut his prayer. For fard: he cuts his prayer and joins the congregation without sin.
3
Understand the reasoning
The maxim says: 'The second (breaking the fard) is to perfect it, not the first (breaking the nafl).' Breaking the fard allows him to earn the greater reward of congregation; breaking the nafl does not perfect anything because congregation is not required for nafl.
4
Identify the underlying principle
The principle is that obligatory acts should be performed in the best form possible, and joining congregation is a means to perfect the fard. For nafl, there is no such requirement, so cutting it is not justified.
This reflects the qa'ida 'ma la yatimm al-wajib illa bihi fahuwa wajib' in a sense.
5
Note any exceptions
If the person is in doubt about whether the congregation is for the same prayer, or if cutting would cause confusion, other rules may apply, but the base rule is clear.

Rami Materials: Dung Permissible, Jewels Not
Ruling with rationale on rami materials.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on rami with dung and with jewels, and explain the rationale based on humiliation vs honor of the devil.

Core Idea
Rami must humiliate the devil; using valuable materials honors him and invalidates the act.

This passage teaches that the symbolic act of rami is not merely a physical throw but a spiritual act intended to degrade the devil. The material used must reflect that intent. Using dung achieves humiliation, while using jewels elevates the devil, contradicting the purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two materials
Recognize that the two substances mentioned are dung (ba'r) and jewels (jawāhir).
The text explicitly compares these two.
2
State the ruling for each
Ruling: rami with dung is permissible (jāz), with jewels it is not (lā).
The ruling is immediate from the text.
3
Analyze the rationale
The reason given: dung humiliates the devil, jewels honor him. The act must humiliate.
This rationale is the key legal basis.
4
Derive the underlying principle
The means of worship must align with its symbolic purpose. Anything that honors the object of contempt is invalid.
This can be extended to other acts with symbolic meaning.

كتاب الحج - لو رمي الجمرة بالبعر جاز، وبالجواهر ،لا، لأن في الأول استخفافا بالشيطان وفي الثاني إعزازه. لو دلَّ المحرم على قتل صيد لزمه الجزاء، ولو دلَّ على قتل مسلم لا ؛ والفرق أن الأول محظور إحرامه والثاني محظور بكل حال. ولو غلطوا في وقت الوقوف لا إعادة وفي الصوم والأضحية أعادوا ؛ والفرق أن تداركه في الحج متعذر وفي غيره متيسر . أعتق العبد بعد حجه حج للإسلام، ولو استغنى الفقير كفاه؛ والفرق انعقاد السبب في حق الفقير دون العبد والصبي كالعبد والأعمى والزمن، والمرأة بلا محرم كالفقير .
Book of Hajj - If he throws the pebble at the Jamrah with dung, it is permissible; with jewels, not; because in the first is humiliation of the devil, in the second is honoring him. If a Muhrim guides to killing game, the compensation is required; if he guides to killing a Muslim, no; the difference is that the first is prohibited due to his Ihram, the second is prohibited in all circumstances. And if they err in the time of standing (at Arafah), no repetition; but in fasting and sacrifice, they repeat; the difference is that rectification in Hajj is difficult, in other acts it is easy. If a slave is freed after his Hajj, his Hajj is considered for Islam; if a poor person becomes independent, it suffices; the difference is that the cause was established in the case of the poor person, not for the slave. And the minor is like the slave; the blind and the chronically ill are like the poor; and a woman without a mahram is like the poor.

Commentary
This passage from the Hajj chapter presents several distinctions. First: Throwing stones at the Jamarat with dried dung (ba'r) is valid because it humiliates the devil, but using jewels is invalid because it honors him. Second: If a person in Ihram guides someone to kill game (hunting), he must pay compensation (jaza'), but if he guides to kill a Muslim, he does not. The difference is that hunting is prohibited only due to Ihram, while killing a Muslim is always prohibited; thus guiding to the latter is not a cause for compensation in Hajj. Third: If people make a mistake about the timing of standing at Arafah, they do not have to repeat the Hajj because it cannot be rectified; but if they make a mistake in fasting or sacrifice, they repeat because rectification is possible. Fourth: If a slave performs Hajj and then is freed, his Hajj counts as his obligatory Hajj (for Islam). If a poor person performs Hajj and then becomes wealthy, it suffices; the difference is that the cause of obligation (having means) was established for the poor person (he had the means at the time of Hajj), but for the slave, he was not a free adult at the time. Similarly, a minor is like a slave; a blind person or chronically ill person is like the poor; a woman without a mahram is also like the poor (her Hajj is valid if she later finds a mahram? Actually the text seems to say that their Hajj is considered valid if the obstacle is removed, but the exact ruling needs careful reading. The commentary follows the text: the cause of obligation was incumbent on the poor person (since he had means) but not on the slave or minor.
