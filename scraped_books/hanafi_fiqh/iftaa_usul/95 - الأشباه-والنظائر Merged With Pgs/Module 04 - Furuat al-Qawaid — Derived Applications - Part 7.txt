Core Idea
Sale of a non-existent item is void, but istihsan permits the grocer's running account due to custom and necessity.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi legal maxim: the general prohibition of selling something that does not exist at the time of contract, and a notable exception by istihsan. Guided Path

Find the phrase 'except in what a person draws from the grocer' (illa fima yastajirruhu al-insan min al-baqqal). This refers to the practice of istijrar, where a customer takes goods on credit and later settles for what was consumed.

Here, custom ('urf) and necessity (hajah) override the general prohibition.

Test the maxim on two cases: (1) selling a specific non-existent item – void; (2) the grocer's running account – valid by istihsan. Notice that the second case involves consumption before settlement, which is why it is allowed.
The exception only applies when goods are consumed before accounting; it does not cover speculative forward sales.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
As a mufti, you must adhere to the operative ruling: the istijrar transaction is valid by istihsan. This is the relied-upon position in later Hanafi fatwa works like al-Qunyah.
This ruling is not based on a direct text but on juristic preference and custom.

Core Idea
The right of rescission (iqala) is generally available, but not in cases involving guardians, slaves, waqf trustees, or agents where conflict of interest arises.

This lesson examines the Hanafi legal maxim on the right of iqala (mutual rescission) in contracts of sale and lease. The rule grants this right broadly, but the text lists critical exceptions where iqala is invalid to protect vulnerable parties or prevent abuse. By studying the furuat, you will learn how the exception operates in specific scenarios.

State the general rule

Note the difference between agent for purchase (invalid) and agent for sale (valid).
3
Analyze the rationale for each exception
For each case, infer the underlying principle: protecting creditors (guardian), preventing exploitation (slave), preserving waqf property, respecting the limits of agency, and distinguishing between heir and legatee.

Contrast with return for defect

This shows that iqala and return for defect are distinct rights.

Note the phrase 'إلا في الشفعة' – except in pre-emption. Pre-emption allows the pre-emptor to step into the buyer's shoes and take a portion of the property, even if the seller intended a single sale.
The exception is necessary to protect the pre-emptor's right of priority.

Abstract rights are not property; they cannot be sold or settled for compensation.

The right of pre-emption (shuf'a) is the paradigmatic example.
2
List the furu' (applications)
Identify the cases given: right of pre-emption, woman's option (mukhayyara), wife's turn (nawba), endowment positions (waza'if al-awqaf). For each, apply the maxim: the right cannot be exchanged; any sulh (settlement) is void.

The Invalidity of Buyer's Rescission in Fraudulent Sales and Its Exceptions
Rule on rescission of fraudulent sale with exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'naqd al-mushtari fasid' to identify when a fraudulent sale rescission is invalid and recognize the two exceptions from Hanafi furu'.

Core Idea
A buyer's rescission of a fraudulent sale is invalid; the seller retains the right to rescind subsequent valid acts, except where overriding maslahah permits counterfeit payments.

This lesson explores a specific legal maxim (qawida) from the Hanafi school regarding the consequences of fraud (ghish) in sales. The maxim states that if a buyer attempts to rescind a fraudulent sale, that rescission is invalid. However, the buyer's subsequent valid contracts, such as lease or marriage, are not automatically void; the seller can rescind them. The maxim also carves out two exceptions where the use of counterfeit or deficient coins is permitted, despite the general prohibition of fraud. Understanding these nuances is crucial for a mufti when analyzing cases of tainted transactions.

Guided Path
1
State the qawida and its legal context
Identify the qawida text: 'نقضه المشتري فاسدًا' (the buyer's rescission is invalid). Recognize that it governs cases where a sale is fraudulent (ghish).
This qawida is a dabt (sub-maxim) within sales law.
2
Determine the main ruling
The buyer's attempt to rescind a fraudulent sale is itself invalid (fasid). The buyer does not have the right to unilaterally cancel the contract.

If the buyer leases the item after the fraudulent sale, the lease is valid, but the seller has the right to rescind that lease. Similarly, if the buyer marries using the item as dowry, the marriage is valid but the seller can rescind it.
The seller's right to rescind subsequent acts is the remedy, not the buyer's initial rescission.

The general rule (buyer's rescission invalid) applies unless one of the two exceptions is present. The mufti must weigh the maslahah and darurah to determine applicability.
The exceptions are limited; they do not open a general permission for fraud.

Core Idea

This lesson unpacks a core Hanafi qawida on suretyship (kafalah): the discharge of the principal debtor generally discharges the surety. However, the Hanafi jurists have identified several exceptions. By examining the furuat (derived cases) from al-Khaniyya, al-Majma', and al-Hidaya, you will learn when the surety remains liable despite the principal's discharge, and when deferral to the principal also applies to the surety.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Main Qawida
State the qawida and its default application: when the principal is discharged (e.g., by payment), the surety is also discharged.
The qawida is 'براءة الأصيل موجبة لبراءة الكفيل'.
2
Analyze the First Exception (Prior Payment)
If the surety guaranteed a debt that the principal claimed from a third party, and the third party proves he paid before the guarantee, the principal is discharged but the surety remains liable. Understand the logic: the debt guaranteed never existed in reality.
Exception based on istihsan and the maxim 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك'.
3
Analyze the Second Exception (Mukatib Settlement)
If a mukatib (slave under contract of manumission) makes a settlement for murder and a surety guarantees the payment, then the mukatib becomes insolvent, the creditor must wait until the mukatib becomes free to claim from him, but can claim the surety immediately.
Exception due to the mukatib's insolvency and the surety's solvency, governed by 'الضرر يزال'.
4
Examine the Rule on Surety's Death
If the surety dies before the deferred debt matures, the debt becomes due immediately against the surety's estate. The creditor can take from the heir, but the heir has no recourse against the principal until the original term expires (if the surety was at the principal's request).
This is not an exception to the main qawida but a separate rule about the effect of death.
5
Examine the Exception by Hawala
If the surety pays the debt, both are discharged from the creditor. However, if the surety transfers the debt (hawala) to his own debtor and stipulates his own discharge only, then only the surety is discharged, not the principal.
This shows that the surety's act can sever his liability while the principal remains bound.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Handwriting alone is not legal proof; only oral testimony, confession, or refusal to swear establishes rights.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir lays down a fundamental qawida of Hanafi judiciary: handwriting is not a valid proof. The judge must base decisions on clear oral evidence. However, two exceptions are recognized: letters of safe conduct and ledgers of intermediaries. This lesson unpacks the rule, its basis, its practical applications, and the scholarly debate surrounding the ledger exception.

Guided Path

Analyze the Ramadan example
If agency is conditioned on the entry of Ramadan and there is a dispute about its sighting, proving the agency implicitly proves that Ramadan has entered.
From al-Khulasa: 'يثبت رمضان في ضمن ثبوت التوكيل'.
5
Distinguish from cases requiring independent proof
Implied judgment does not apply when the incidental matter requires independent proof (e.g., if it is not necessarily entailed). The text notes that al-Imadi distinguished between two different branches.
Review the commentary in Jami' al-Fusulayn for the distinction.

Based on this, if they testify that so-and-so is the wife of so-and-so, and she appointed her husband so-and-so as agent against a denying defendant, and judgment is given for her agency, it is a judgment of marriage between them, and it is a fatwa incident. Commentary
This passage defines 'implied judgment' (qada' dimni), a concept in Hanafi procedural law where a court ruling establishes a secondary matter without it being explicitly claimed or litigated. Example: if witnesses testify about a person's name and lineage while proving a debt, the lineage is established incidentally. Similarly, proving agency in a lawsuit may prove the underlying marriage or the entry of Ramadan. The origin is illustrated: if a guarantor is sued and admits the guarantee but denies the debt, and the creditor proves the debt, the judgment against the guarantor implicitly establishes the debt against the absent principal. This is a key principle in judicial rulings.

الأصيل الغائب (al-asil al-gha'ib): The absent principal in a guarantee contract whose debt is established by implied judgment.

Continuity of Qadi-Appointed Waqf Trustees
Ruling from Waqf al-Qunyah on continuity of qadi-appointed trustees

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a qadi's appointment of a waqf trustee survives his death or removal, and apply it to analogous appointments.

Core Idea
A qadi's appointment of a waqf trustee survives his death or removal.

This lesson examines a specific fatwa rule from Waqf al-Qunyah: the stability of court-appointed trustees. Understanding this rule is crucial for muftis advising on waqf administration continuity, as it prevents disruption when a qadi leaves office.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
A qadi appoints a trustee (qayyim) to manage a waqf.
2
Identify the potential disruption
The qadi dies or is removed from office.
3
State the ruling
According to the text, the trustee remains in his position.
The Arabic says: 'لو مات القاضي أو عزل يبقى ما نصبه على حاله'
4
Recognize the underlying principle
The trustee's authority derives from the waqf endowment and the court's order, not from the qadi's personal capacity.
5
Apply to similar scenarios
This principle may extend to other appointments by judges, such as guardians for orphans.
But caution: explicit limitations may apply.

Commentary
This is a straightforward ruling from Waqf al-Qunyah: when a qadi appoints a trustee (qayyim) for a waqf, that appointment is not affected by the qadi's later death or removal from office. The trustee remains in his position. This is because the trustee's authority is derived from the original waqf endowment and the court's order, not from the qadi's personal capacity. The unclear phrase at the end likely confirms that the trustee continues in his role.

وقف القنية (Waqf al-Qunyah): A Hanafi fiqh work (al-Qunyah) dealing with waqf rules.
