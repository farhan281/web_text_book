The Minor (Ṣabī) in Hanafi Fiqh: A Comprehensive Rasm of Rulings
Compilation of legal rulings on the minor from various Hanafi sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize the rulings concerning a minor across various chapters of fiqh and identify the underlying principle governing his legal capacity.

Core Idea
A minor's acts are valid but not obligatory; his capacity is limited in authority and potentially harmful matters.

This passage is a rasm (compilation) of rulings on the minor (ṣabī), drawn from multiple Hanafi sources. It serves as a mufti's quick reference for answering questions about a minor's capacity in worship, transactions, authority, and penal matters. In this lesson, we will parse these rulings to understand the consistent Hanafi approach: the minor's actions are evaluated for validity (ṣiḥḥa) even where obligation (wujūb) is absent, but his role as an authority figure (e.g., imām, qāḍī, wāli) is severely restricted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching principle
Recognize that the minor's acts in worship and transactions are valid but not obligatory, and his authority roles are invalid except in limited cases.
The passage repeatedly emphasizes 'ṣaḥīḥ' (valid) for acts like prayer and fasting, even though they are not obligatory.
2
Categorize the rulings by fiqh chapter
Group the rulings into: (a) acts of worship (prayer, fasting, sajda, adhan, imamah), (b) purity and nullifications, (c) family law (marriage, divorce, iddah, custody), (d) transactions (gift, sale, wakalah, zakat), (e) penal and liability (damages, ta'zir, qasamah, jizyah), (f) leadership and judiciary (sultanate, qada, testimony).
Use the passage's paragraph breaks as clues; each section shifts topic.
3
Note the points of ikhtilaf
The passage records differences, e.g., validity of minor's imamah in tarawih, karahah of adhan, reward for worship.
The passage often states 'ikhtalafū' or 'qīla' to indicate dispute.
4
Identify the mufta bihi positions
The passage frequently marks the relied-upon view with 'al-mu'tamad' or 'al-mukhtār'. Extract these for fatwa application.
For example, 'wa al-mu'tamad annahu lahu' (reward for minor) and 'wa al-mu'tamad 'adamuhā' (invalidity of imamah in tarawih).
5
Apply the rasm to a new scenario
Given a new question about a minor, use the categorized rules to derive the answer by analogy or direct application.
For instance, if asked about a minor's ability to give testimony, find the ruling under 'shahādah' in the passage.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of rulings concerning the minor (sabi) from various Hanafi sources, arranged as a ready reference for the mufti. It systematically covers the minor's legal capacity across multiple chapters of fiqh. The opening rulings address the minor's acts of worship: his prayer is valid but not obligatory; his leading in prayer is invalid except possibly in tarawih (relied-upon: invalid). Sajdah tilawah is wajib upon him as a listener. Congregational virtue is attained with one follower, but not for Jumu'ah. He lacks authority for marriage, judiciary, and testimony, but can deliver a khutbah with permission. His sultanate is valid only in name, with actual authority delegated to a guardian. He can be a guardian (wasi) and supervisor, and can be appointed by a judge. His litigation requires authorization. He is like an adult in wudu nullifiers except loud laughter. His adhan is valid, with a difference on dislike; intelligent minor's adhan has no karahah. Standing in prayer is required for validity, though not wajib upon him. His narration is acceptable, ijazah valid, his word accepted in gifts and permission. He is prevented from touching the Quran. Female minor's iddah prevents remarriage, but it's not obligatory upon her. Aman valid; treatment only with guardian's permission. Ear piercing of a baby girl is makruh qiyasan but permissible istihsanan. Gift to minor belongs to him; parents cannot eat without need. Wakalah valid if he understands contract. Rights revert to principal. Discerning minor's statement accepted in transactions. Tahill by intercourse only if near-pubescent. He owns mubah property by acquisition. Loot found by him is valid. Salam must be responded. Islam and riddah valid; no execution for apostasy as child. Slaughter valid with tasmiyah understanding. Game by his shot is halal if he says tasmiyah. He may enter upon women up to 15 years. Divorce and manumission do not take effect except in specific cases. Interdiction is in statements only, not actions; he is liable for damages. Prohibition by affinity established if he desires women, or if the female minor is desired (9 years old). He is not included in qasamah; blood money on aqila. No jizyah. Children of dhimmis not to be distinguished externally. No penalty on Banu Taghlib children. Child of harbi not killed if not fighting. If imam declares spoils for kills, minor gets spoils if he fights. Minor is among those who get stipend (radh) if he fights. If sultan appoints a minor as imam for Jumu'ah upon majority, it's permissible; but upon reaching majority, new appointment needed. Minor's oath is not binding. If buyer finds defect, minor cannot be made to swear until majority. Minor cannot be brought to court if no evidence, as his nukul (refusal to swear) doesn't lead to judgment. Ta'zir is permissible as discipline. Contracts ambiguous (between benefit/harm) require guardian's permission. Acceptance of gift is valid.

Key Terms
الصبي (al-ṣabī): An unemancipated male minor below the age of puberty, whose acts are evaluated for validity but not obligatory.
المراهق (al-murāhiq): A minor who has reached near-puberty, close to adulthood, capable of sexual intercourse and liable for some rulings.
المميز (al-mumayyiz): A discerning minor who can distinguish beneficial from harmful and understands contracts and their consequences.

Ruling on a Trustee's Self-Removal
Citation from al-Qunyah on trustee self-removal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a trustee's self-removal and identify the necessary condition (presence of a qadi) for its validity.

Core Idea
A trustee may resign unilaterally, but only before a qadi.

This lesson examines a concise but important ruling from the Hanafi text Al-Qunyah. The text states that a trustee (such as a waqf administrator) may remove himself from his position, but this self-removal must be done in the presence of a qadi. Understanding this rule is essential for muftis dealing with endowment disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act
Recognize that the act is 'self-removal' (عزل نفسه) of a trustee from his position.
2
Identify the ruling
The text indicates this is permissible for the trustee to do unilaterally.
3
Identify the condition
The self-removal must be done 'in the presence of the qadi' (بحضرة القاضي), meaning the qadi must be aware and witness the resignation.
This is a procedural requirement, not permission from the qadi.
4
Locate the source
The ruling is cited from the Hanafi fatwa work Al-Qunyah, which is a commonly referenced source for later fatwas.
Al-Qunyah is a recognized Hanafi text but not part of the Zahir al-Riwayah corpus.

Contracts via Correspondence: The Hanafi Ruling on Kitabah
Rasm al-mufti: Ruling on kitābah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a written contract is equivalent to a verbal one, and identify the conditions for a valid contract via correspondence, including the session of receipt.

Core Idea
A written contract is as valid as a spoken one, but acceptance must occur in the same session of receipt.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rules for concluding contracts through written correspondence (kitābah). In the absence of direct face-to-face interaction, the written word carries the same legal weight as speech—a principle derived from the maxim 'al-kitāb ka al-khiṭāb' (writing is like direct address). However, the school imposes precise conditions to ensure that offer and acceptance are properly connected. The passage from Ashbāh wa Naẓā’ir clarifies the required format for a written sale offer, the necessity of acceptance in the session of receipt, and a nuanced distinction between offers made to a present party versus an absent one. Mastering this topic equips the fatwa practitioner with a key rule for modern contracts concluded by mail, email, or messenger.

Guided Path
1
Identify the governing maxim
The text states: 'al-kitāb ka al-khiṭāb' — writing is like speech. This Hanafi qā‘ida places written communication on par with oral offer and acceptance. Confirm that the maxim makes a written contract valid in principle.
This maxim is found in legal method works; its scope includes all contracts and even divorce.
2
Understand the session requirement (majlis al-bulūgh)
The passage emphasizes that acceptance must occur in the same session in which the written offer is received and understood (majlis bulūgh al-kitāb). This parallels the rule for oral contracts where acceptance must follow the offer in the same sitting.
If the offeree delays acceptance beyond that session, the offer lapses.
3
Distinguish between present and absent parties
The source notes a disagreement: when parties are present, the first statement is considered isti’ām (preliminary bargaining) not a binding offer; only the second statement is a formal offer. For absent parties, the writing itself is a direct offer (ījāb).
This distinction affects whether the offeree can reject or counter without breaking negotiations.
4
Apply the rules to sale and marriage
The text confirms that both sale and marriage contracts are valid through kitābah. However, for marriage, additional conditions (witnesses, etc.) are required, as hinted by the reference to al-Mabsūṭ.
The passage cuts off, but other Hanafi sources confirm that the same principles of kitābah apply to marriage.

Oaths tied to removable attributes are broken only if the attribute remains; personal relationships make the oath stick to the person.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a subtle rule about oaths that include a relational description of the person. Say: One who has debts that he has not collected. Which man should conceal the giving of zakah from some people? Say: A sick person who fears his heirs; he gives it secretly from them. A man for whom it is recommended to conceal it? Say: One who fears the oppressors so they do not learn of his wealth. A man who is rich according to the Imam (Abu Hanifa) so it is not permissible for him, but poor according to Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on him) so it is permissible for him? Say: One who owns properties that he rents out but does not own a nisab (of cash).
Source pages p. 342

Commentary
This passage presents puzzles on Zakah according to the Hanafi madhhab. First: a wealth that had zakah due, then it dropped after the year without it perishing: a gift that is returned to the donor after a year has passed; the zakah obligation is lifted, and the donor does not have to pay it because it was not in his possession for the full year. Second: a nisab that is free of debt but has no zakah: a dower (mahr) not yet received, or mal al-dimār (debt that is unlikely to be collected); these are not subject to zakah because possession is incomplete. Third: a man who gives zakah and is allowed to take it: someone who owns nisab of grazing livestock (sā'imah) worth less than 200 dirhams; he is considered poor but must pay zakah on the animals, and he can receive zakah because his wealth is in non-cash form. Fourth: a man who has a cash nisab but is allowed to receive zakah: one who has debts owed to him that he has not collected; his net wealth is below nisab. Fifth: a man who should hide his zakah from some: a sick person who fears his heirs might think he is wasting inheritance; he gives secretly. Sixth: recommended to hide: one who fears oppressive rulers so they do not tax him. Seventh: a man considered rich by Abu Hanifa (imam) so zakah is not permissible for him, but poor by Muhammad al-Shaybani so it is permissible: one who owns rental properties that generate income but does not have a cash nisab; Abu Hanifa considers the rental income as wealth, while Muhammad considers only actual cash on hand.

مال الضمار (māl al-ḍimār): Property that is unlikely to be recovered (e.g., bad debt)
المهر قبل القبض (al-mahr qabla al-qabḍ): Dower not yet received by the wife
سائمة (sā'imah): Grazing livestock

Reciprocal Fairness in Transactions: The Imam's Lesson
Anecdote illustrating Hanafi transactional ethics

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ethical principle of lawful reciprocal treatment when wronged in a transaction.

Core Idea
When wronged, you may recover your loss through a lawful transaction, not fraud.

This short anecdote from the life of Imam Abu Hanifa is not a legal ruling but a display of wisdom and fair retribution. When a Bedouin overcharged him for water, the Imam later used a clever transaction to recover his loss. This story teaches a key ethical principle in Hanafi mu'amalah: one may lawfully recoup a loss without resorting to deception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial wrong
What was the Bedouin's unfair action? He charged five dirhams for a water skin, likely more than the market price.
The Imam paid the demanded price, showing that one may pay an inflated price if necessary.
2
Analyze the Imam's response
How did the Imam recover his loss? He offered sawīq to the Bedouin, which made him thirsty, then sold him water at the same high price.
The Imam used his own property (water) lawfully; he did not force the Bedouin to buy.
3
Extract the ethical principle
What legal maxim underlies this? It resembles 'al-mithl bi al-mithl' (like for like) but with a lawful transaction. It is permissible to use one's property to teach a lesson within the bounds of halal.
This is not formal retaliation (qisas) but a form of mudarabah or reciprocal justice.
4
Apply to similar modern cases
For a mufti: if a client is overcharged, can he later recover by a lawful deal? Yes, if he does not lie or cheat. The mufti should advise based on intention and means.
The principle is ethical, not a strict legal obligation.

Commentary
This brief anecdote shows the Imam's cleverness in dealing with a greedy person. The Bedouin overcharged him for the water skin. Later, when the Bedouin desired food, the Imam offered sawīq (a nourishing dish). After eating, the Bedouin became thirsty, but the Imam refused to give him water until he paid the same amount he had charged the Imam. This is not presented as a legal ruling but as a display of wisdom (ḥikmah) and fair retribution (acting as a judge who was dealt with). In Hanafi ethics, such reciprocal treatment is permissible when one has been wronged, though it is not a strict legal rule.

السويق (as-sawīq): A dish made of ground barley or wheat, often used as a meal for travelers.
المعاملة بالمثل (al-mu'āmalah bi al-mithl): Reciprocal treatment; the principle of responding with an equivalent action lawfully.
