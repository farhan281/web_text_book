Giving House for Repairs Without Payment is a Loan
Ruling on giving house for repairs and the validity of subsequent leases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a valid ijarah and a loan (ariyah) when a house is given for repairs without payment, and apply the ruling that adding benefits to a valid lease is permissible.

Core Idea
Giving your house to someone for repairs without payment is a loan (ariyah), not a lease, even if the occupier later leases it.

This lesson addresses a nuanced case: a person gives his house to another to repair it, with no rent. The Hanafi ruling classifies this as a loan (ariyah), not ijarah. If the occupier then leases the house to someone else while it is in a state of invalid occupancy, the lease may be valid or invalid depending on circumstances. This highlights the importance of differentiating between contractual types.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Initial Arrangement
The owner gives his house to another person to repair it, with no payment. This is a loan (ariyah) because the occupier is allowed to use the house temporarily for the purpose of repairs without rent.
Ariyah is a gratuitous loan of a benefit; the house is not leased.
2
Examine the Hypothetical of Invalid Occupancy
If the occupier is in the house invalidly (e.g., after repairs, he remains without permission), and then leases the house to a third party while in that state: some say the lease is valid, others say it is not.
The dispute centers on whether the invalid occupancy affects the lessee's right to sublease.
3
Apply the General Rule on Adding Benefits
The passage begins by stating: 'Adding the lease to the benefits of the house is permissible.' This means that if the owner validly leases the house, he can also include additional benefits (like the right to use fixtures) in the lease. This is the baseline principle.
This is a separate point from the repair case; it establishes the permissibility of comprehensive lease agreements.
4
Synthesize the Rulings
The key takeaway: The repair arrangement is a loan, not a lease. If the occupier later subleases, the validity depends on whether the sublessor has a right to lease (i.e., valid possession). The first part of the passage emphasizes that including all benefits in a lease is valid.
The 'قيل لا' indicates a difference of opinion.

Invalid Ijarah Contracts and Conditions
Rulings on invalid contracts and worker liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conditions that invalidate an ijarah contract and apply the rule that custom determines wages in ambiguous hiring arrangements.

Core Idea
A lease is invalid if the wage is unknown or if extra conditions burden the lessee — custom fills gaps only when no explicit stipulation.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents key rulings on when an ijarah (hire) contract becomes invalid due to uncertainty in wage or additional conditions. It also clarifies how custom (urf) governs situations where the wage is not fixed by agreement. Understanding these rules helps the mufti distinguish between valid and invalid hiring arrangements.

Guided Path
1
Identify invalid contracts due to wage uncertainty
Look at the first ruling: giving yarn to weave for half share is invalid because the wage (half the product) is unknown. Also hiring a book for reading is invalid because the usufruct is unclear.
In Hanafi, the wage must be known (ma'lum) and the usufruct must be defined.
2
Recognize invalidating conditions
The text lists conditions that invalidate: stipulating food for slave, fodder for animal, plastering/repair of house, locking door, inserting beam into ceiling on the lessee. These impose extra, non-standard obligations.
Only conditions that add burden on the lessee invalidate; conditions that are beneficial to the lessor or neutral may be permissible.
3
Apply the rule on hiring for hudud/qisas
Hiring someone to execute hadd punishments or qisas is invalid because these are state duties, not private contracts.
This is based on the principle that such acts are wajib on the state, not transferable to private hire.
4
Determine when custom decides wages
If a man helps in the market without agreement, custom determines the wage. Similarly, using a hired item outside the specified locality may or may not incur extra rent based on the item type (garment vs animal).
Custom (urf) is a recognized source in Hanafi iftaa.

Liability of Hired Workers: Scribe, Tailor, and Others
Rulings on invalid contracts and worker liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on partial error by a hired scribe, the consequences of denied service, and the entitlement of tailors and money changers to wages.

Core Idea
A hired scribe who makes errors has options: replace or deduct; a tailor gets no pay for cutting alone; a money changer must refund surplus.

This part of the passage focuses on the liability of hired workers (ajir) when they err or when the work is incomplete. The rulings cover scribes, tailors, dyers, weavers, and money changers, providing detailed compensations and options. Understanding these rules helps the mufti resolve disputes over wages and damages in hiring contracts.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the scribe's liability for errors
If the scribe makes errors in every sheet, the hirer has an option: either take the work and pay equivalent rent, or leave the work and take the value. If errors are in only part, proportional deduction from the named wage.
This shows the Hanafi principle that the hirer is protected but the scribe is not fully penalized.
2
Understand the ruling on denied service
If the worker denies that he was employed (juhuda), then after proof, the full rent and the value of the work are due.
Denial of contract leads to obligation of both the agreed wage and the market value as penalty.
3
Apply partnership rules for lost goods
If two carriers are hired and one load perishes, if they are partners, the surviving carrier must compensate the other for the full loss; otherwise, the carrier who lost gets half.
Partnership (shirkah) affects liability distribution.
4
Determine entitlement of tailors and money changers
The tailor does not deserve the cutting fee without sewing—only for completed work. The money changer must return the fee if a surplus appears in all transactions; if only part, proportionally.
Wages are earned only upon full performance of the contracted service.

Hiring of Callers and Brokers; Silence as Consent in Lease
Rulings on hiring callers/brokers and the maxim that silence in lease is consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring callers and brokers, and apply the maxim 'silence in lease is consent' to new scenarios within ijarah.

Core Idea
Silence in a lease context, when an offer is made, counts as acceptance — the owner is bound.

This passage presents two linked elements: the permissibility of hiring certain service providers due to public need, and the crucial maxim that silence in lease constitutes consent and acceptance. The maxim is then applied to two concrete cases: a shepherd adjusting his wage and a tenant responding to a landlord's terms. Understanding this maxim is essential for muftis dealing with ijarah contracts where explicit verbal acceptance is absent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the maxim
The passage first rules on hiring callers/brokers as permissible, then introduces the maxim. Note the two distinct parts.
The ruling on hiring is based on public need (ḥājah).
2
Understand the maxim's scope
The maxim applies only when silence occurs in response to an offer that normally calls for a response. This is the dabt (restriction).
Without a clear offer, silence does not indicate acceptance.
3
Apply the maxim to the examples
Shepherd and tenant cases: silence plus action (grazing/living) forms a binding contract for the stated wage or rent.
Action confirms implicit consent.
4
Consider the exception
The maxim does not apply if the offer is unclear or the party is unable to respond (e.g., denial). In such cases, the general principle of certainty (al-yaqīn lā yazūl bi al-shakk) governs.
Always check context before applying the maxim.

Rent for Land is Like Kharaj: Maxim and Application
Maxim with application to crop destruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-ajrah lil-ard ka al-kharaj' to determine rent liability when a leased crop is destroyed by a natural calamity.

Core Idea
Rent for land is due only for the productive period before the crop is destroyed, like kharaj.

This lesson introduces a Hanafi maxim from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazā'ir that analogizes rent (ajrah) for agricultural land to kharaj (land tax). The core idea is that both are tied to the land's productive capacity—if the crop is lost due to a natural disaster, rent ceases for the remaining term. Understanding this maxim equips a mufti with a precise rule for ijarah contracts involving agriculture, preventing unjust claims for rent after benefit is lost.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Read the maxim in Arabic and its English translation: 'الأجرة للأرض كالخراج على المعتمد' – 'The rent for land is like kharaj according to the reliable opinion.'
Commit this phrasing to memory; it is the mufta bihi version.
2
Understand the analogy
Recognize that both rent and kharaj are tied to the land's ability to produce benefit. Kharaj is due only when the land is capable of yielding; similarly, rent is due only while the land is capable of providing the intended benefit (the crop).
The analogy is based on the nature of the obligation, not on a textual proof.
3
Identify the triggering condition
The condition that reduces or drops rent is a total destruction of the crop (iṣṭilām) caused by a natural calamity (āfah). Partial damage or negligence by the lessee may have different rulings.
The term 'اصطلم' implies complete uprooting or destruction.
4
Apply to the furu' case
Given the scenario in the passage: land leased for cultivation, the crop is destroyed by a calamity. Rent is due for the period before destruction (mā qabla al-iṣṭilām) and dropped for the period after (sāqit mā ba‘dahu).
The ruling is a consequence of the maxim; the rent is apportioned according to the time the benefit existed.
5
Note the exception
The default rule can be overridden if the lease contract explicitly stipulates that rent is due regardless of crop outcome, based on the maxim 'Muslims are bound by their conditions.'
This exception is supported by the hadith 'al-Muslimūn ‘alā shurūṭihim'.

The application: if a person leases land for cultivation, and then a natural disaster (āfah) destroys the crop at some point, the rent is due for the period before the destruction (ma qabla al-iṣṭilām), and the rent for the remaining period is cancelled. This reflects the Hanafi principle that the obligation of rent corresponds to the ability to benefit from the land; once the benefit is lost, rent ceases.

اصطلم (iṣṭalama): To be completely destroyed or uprooted, here referring to crop destruction.

Invalid Conditions in Ijarah Contracts
Begin of the passage listing invalid conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conditions that invalidate an ijarah contract according to Hanafi law and explain why each condition is invalid.

Core Idea
Stipulations that contradict the essence of ijarah or shift default obligations invalidate the contract.

Not every condition agreed upon in a hire or lease contract is valid. Hanafi law defines specific stipulations that corrupt the contract, rendering it fasid (invalid). This lesson parses the opening rulings of our passage to understand which conditions cause invalidity and why, focusing on the nature of the contract and the rights of the parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type and its essential elements
Recognize that ijarah requires a known rent and known duration (or benefit). Without these, the contract is invalid. Note the examples: hire of a bathhouse for a specified food (unknown rent) and hire of a scribe with paper (uncertainty).
Also note that the condition must be explicit and not negate the purpose of the contract.
2
Distinguish valid from invalid modifications of default obligations
Default obligations: cost of return (mu'nat al-radd) is on the lessor; land tax (kharaj) is on the owner. Stipulating these on the lessee invalidates the contract as it imposes an extra burden not customary.
In waqf, a slight reduction is allowed; but an arbitrary reduction without basis is invalid.
3
Analyze the bathhouse attendant's condition
The condition 'if I hire during idle time, I discount the rent' is valid because it is a known discount for a specific period. But a condition 'I discount such-and-such' without specifying is invalid due to uncertainty.
Contrast with the invalid conditions above: certainty vs. uncertainty.
4
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Given a condition in an ijarah contract, determine if it conflicts with the known elements or shifts a default duty without compensation. For example, stipulating that the lessee pays repair costs is valid if explicitly agreed and includes compensation adjustment.
The key is whether the condition contradicts the nature of ijarah as a commutative contract.
5
Synthesize the underlying maxim
From these rulings, extract the maxim: 'Any condition that introduces uncertainty (gharar) or shifts the default allocation of risks without commensurate adjustment in rent invalidates the contract.' This guides fatwa in new cases.
This is a common pattern in Hanafi ijarah rulings.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi legal maxim from the chapter on trusts (amanat): when a trustee dies without revealing the trust property, the trust becomes a debt on the estate. Understanding this maxim helps the mufti determine liability in cases involving missing trust property after a trustee's death.

A slave's deposit perishes. The depositary is liable because the property ultimately belongs to the master, even though the slave has a valid hand for deposit. Note the tension between the slave's capacity and the master's ownership.

Return of Deposit to Third Parties: Discharge and Liability
Scenarios for returning deposits to third parties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine when a depositary is discharged by returning a deposit to a third party and when he remains liable.

Core Idea
Returning a deposit to anyone other than the owner does not automatically discharge the depositary; liability depends on the recipient's status and authority.

The depositary must exercise caution when returning the deposit to someone other than the owner. This lesson examines several scenarios: returning to a slave, to the owner's house or dependents, to an heir, or to the owner's agent. In each case, the depositary may or may not be discharged based on whether the recipient is authorized or if the estate is encumbered by debt.

Guided Path
1
Return to the Owner's Slave
The depositary is not discharged by returning the deposit to the owner's slave, whether the slave manages the property or not. This is the correct view.
Slavery renders the slave non-independent in property matters.
2
Return to the Owner's House or Dependents
There is difference in ifta regarding returning to the owner's house or to his dependents (family members). The correct view is not specified, but caution is advised.
The ikhtilaf indicates that practice may vary.
3
Payment to Heir Without Court Order
If the depositary pays the deposit to the heir of a deceased owner without a judge's order, he is liable if the estate is fully encumbered by debt and he was not a trustee for the heir. Otherwise, no liability, unless he paid to only some of the heirs.
This protects creditors.
4
Payment of Depositor's Debt by Depositary
If the depositary uses the deposit to pay a debt of the depositor, he is liable according to the correct view.
The depositary has no authority to settle debts without permission.
5
Discharge of Deceased's Debtor
A debtor of the deceased is not discharged by paying the debt to the heir while the deceased has other debts. Likewise, the depositary's payment to the heir may not be valid if debts exist.
This confirms the priority of creditors.

Miscellaneous Deposit and Agency Rulings
Rulings on mustabdi', loan dissolution, two claimants, and deposit with debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply several specific Hanafi rulings on deposits, loans, and agency, including the limits of the mustabdi' and the dissolution of loan by death.

Core Idea
Deposit, loan, and agency contracts have specific rules on authorization, dissolution, and dispute resolution that protect parties.

This lesson covers a collection of practical rulings: the limits of the mustabdi' (one who receives goods for trade), the dissolution of loan upon death, the division of a deposit when the recipient is uncertain, and the treatment of an unidentifiable deposit when the depositor dies with debts. Each ruling illustrates a distinct legal principle.

Guided Path
1
Authority of the Mustabdi'
A mustabdi' (one who receives goods for bida'ah) does not have the right to further entrust the goods to another (no sub-bida'ah or sub-deposit). This is like absolute agency with discretion, but unlike a mudarib, the mustabdi' cannot sell.
Bida'ah is a limited trust.
2
Loan Dissolves on Death
A loan (i'arah) is like a hire (ijarah): it dissolves with the death of either party. The borrower must return the item.
This is due to the personal nature of the contract.
3
Two Claimants, Depositary Unsure
If two people claim a deposit and the depositary does not know who the true owner is, he must give each half and is liable for the like (misl) of the deposit because his ignorance caused the loss.
He should have taken precautions.
4
Deposit with Debt of Deceased
If a deceased person had a deposit that is not specifically identified and also had debts, the deposit is treated as part of the estate: it is divided proportionally among creditors, and the depositor's owner shares with the creditors.
The deposit loses its specific identity.

The Depositary's Word in Claims of Return or Delivery to an Agent
Two furuat on depositary's claims and liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on when a depositary's claim of return or delivery to an agent is accepted, and distinguish between his acquittal and liability.

Core Idea
The depositary's word is accepted for acquittal; if he claims owner's order, owner's denial makes him liable.

This lesson examines two key rulings from the Hanafi school on deposits: first, the depositary's claim of delivering to the owner's agent, and second, his claim of return or loss. These rulings illustrate the qa'idah that the word of a trusted person (amin) is accepted in matters of trust unless there is an inconsistency, such as a contradiction with the owner's oath.

These appear in the passage after 'ادعى المودع' and after 'القول للمودع'.
2

The depositary says he gave the deposit to the owner's authorized agent. The owner and the agent deny receiving it. The rule: his word is accepted for his own acquittal (he is not liable) but not accepted to prove that he is free of liability (i.e., the owner's denial makes him liable).
This is a nuanced distinction: he is acquitted of the claim of wrongful delivery, but still liable for the loss because the owner's denial creates a dispute.
3

In Hanafi law, the depositary's word is accepted for his release from the obligation to return the exact item (barā'ah), but not to negate the obligation to pay compensation (damān).
This stems from the principle that the amin's word is taken for what benefits the amin (acquittal), not for what harms the owner (liability).
4
Analyze the second scenario: claimed order from owner
The depositary says the owner ordered him to deliver the deposit to a third party, and he did so. The owner denies giving such an order. Here, the owner's word is accepted, and the depositary becomes liable.
The depositary's claim is a direct contradiction of the owner's oath; the owner's denial prevails, making the depositary a guarantor.
5

Normally, the depositary's oath is accepted for return or loss. But when he claims a special order from the owner, that claim goes beyond the normal scope of trust and is not automatically accepted.
This exception is based on the idea that the depositary is asserting a specific command that the owner denies; the owner's knowledge is presumed.

Agency Limits: The Mustabdi' and Bida'ah
Rulings on the authority of a mustabdi' compared to a mudarib.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the authority of a mustabdi' and a mudarib in Hanafi law, and apply the ruling on when a mustabdi' can sell goods.

Core Idea
A mustabdi' cannot sub-delegate or deposit; he can only trade with the goods, unlike a mudarib who can sell. Exception: if profit intent is clear.

This lesson focuses on the ruling concerning the mustabdi' – a person given goods to trade with limited authority. The passage contrasts the mustabdi' with a mudarib (silent partnership) and clarifies when the mustabdi' may sell. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly structuring commercial agency contracts in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key actors
Define mustabdi' and mudarib. Mustabdi' is someone given goods (bida'ah) to trade with without authority to sell for profit unless specified; mudarib is a partner in a qirad (mudarabah) who has authority to sell.
The passage says: 'المستبضع لا يملك الإبضاع والإيداع' (the mustabdi' does not have the right to sub-delegate or deposit).
2
Analyze the mustabdi'’s limited authority
The mustabdi' cannot give the goods to another agent (sub-delegate) nor deposit them with someone else. He can only trade personally, similar to an agent with discretion (al-muqaranah bi al-mashiyyah).
The passage gives an example: if owner gives a cloth and says 'buy me a cloth with it', it's valid, as if he said 'buy any cloth you wish'.
3
Contrast with mudarib's authority
In mudarabah, the mudarib has the right to sell goods (yamlik al-bay'). The mustabdi' does not have this right unless his intent indicates profit-seeking or he explicitly specifies it.
The exception: إذا كان في قصده ما يُعلم أنه قصد الاسترباح أو نص على ذلك (if his intent shows he aimed for profit or he explicitly stated so).
4
Apply the exception
If the mustabdi' is given goods with the clear mutual understanding of profit (e.g., he is to trade and share profit), then he does have the authority to sell, like a mudarib. Otherwise, he can only buy and sell in the ordinary course of trade for the owner.
This aligns with the Hanafi maxim that the substance of the contract is considered (al-ibratu fi al-'uqūd li al-maqāṣid).
5
Distinguish between 'bida'ah' and 'mudarabah'
Bida'ah is a commodity partnership where the owner provides goods, and the mustabdi' trades without sharing in profit unless agreed. Mudarabah is a capital partnership where the investor provides money, and the mudarib provides labor and shares profit. The key difference: mudarib can sell freely; mustabdi' cannot unless profit is intended.
The passage states: 'والبضاعة كالمضاربة إلا أن المضارب يملك البيع والمستبضع لا' (bida'ah is like mudarabah except that the mudarib has the right to sell and the mustabdi' does not).

Hire and Loan of Animals: Scope and Dissolution
Two furuat: scope of hire vs. loan for a camel, and dissolution of i'arah by death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the scope of use for a hired vs. borrowed camel for a journey to Mecca, and explain why i'arah dissolves upon death of either party.

Core Idea
Hiring a camel only covers the outward trip; borrowing covers both ways. I'arah dissolves with death like ijarah.

This lesson covers two practical rulings: first, the difference between hiring and borrowing a camel for a journey to Mecca; second, the rule that a loan for use (i'arah) is dissolved by the death of either party, similar to hire (ijarah). These rulings are applications of the qāʿidah that the scope of permission is determined by custom and necessity, and that both ijarah and i'arah are non-binding contracts that terminate upon death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contracts
Distinguish between ijarah (hire) and i'arah (loan for use). In ijarah, the hirer pays rent; in i'arah, the borrower uses gratuitously.
The passage contrasts 'استأجر بعيرًا' (hired a camel) with 'استعار بعيرًا' (borrowed a camel).
2
Analyze the scope of use for hire
When a person hires a camel to go to Mecca, the hire covers only the outward journey (al-dhahāb) and not the return (al-mujīʾ). The hirer must return the camel after reaching Mecca.
Why? Because the contract of hire specifies the destination; the return is a separate journey not included.
3
Analyze the scope of use for loan
When a person borrows a camel for the same purpose, the loan covers both going and coming back. The borrower can use it for the round trip.
Reason: the loan is a gratuitous contract; the lender's permission is presumed to cover the full round trip unless specified otherwise.
4
Apply the ruling on dissolution by death
Both ijarah and i'arah are dissolved (tanfasikh) by the death of either party. This means the contract ends automatically upon death.
The passage says: 'الإعارة كالإجارة تنفسخ بموت أحدهما' (Loan is like hire: it is dissolved by the death of either party).
5
Connect to underlying qawida
The underlying principle is that both contracts are based on personal trust and are not binding on the heirs. Also, the scope of use in hire is limited by the contract's terms, while in loan it is broader by default.
This illustrates the qāʿidah: الأصل في الإعارة الإباحة، وفي الإجارة التحديد.

Debts, Deposits, and Uncertainty Among Claimants
Rulings on debt payment to heir, deposits in estate, and uncertain claimants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Hanafi rulings on how to handle debts and deposits when the debtor dies or when the depositary is uncertain about the true owner.

Core Idea
Debts must be paid to the estate, not to heirs; if a depositary is uncertain, he divides the deposit equally and guarantees it.

This lesson addresses two problems from the end of the passage: first, a debtor who dies while still owing a debt, and a deposit in his possession; second, a depositary who does not know which of two claimants deposited the item. These rulings illustrate the Hanafi principles that debts take precedence over inheritance and that uncertainty in deposit obligations leads to shared liability.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the case of the deceased debtor with a deposit
When a man dies with a debt, and he also holds a deposit that is not in its specific form (i.e., fungible goods like money), the deposit becomes part of his estate and is distributed among all creditors proportionally.
The passage says: 'مات رجل وعليه دين وعنده وديعة بغير عينها فجميع ما تركه بين الغرماء، وصاحب الوديعة بالحصص'.
2
Apply the rule that the debtor cannot pay the heir
First ruling: 'ولا يبرأ مديون الميت بدفع الدين إلى الوارث وعلى الميت دين' – a person who owes a debt to a deceased person does not discharge his debt by paying it to the heir of the deceased if the deceased himself had debts. He must pay the estate so that all creditors are satisfied.
This is to prevent preferential treatment of one heir over other creditors.
3
Analyze the case of two claimants and an uncertain depositary
If two people claim a deposit from the same depositary, and the depositary says 'I don't know which of you deposited it', and he refuses to swear for either, then he gives the deposit to both equally (half each) and guarantees the like amount (i.e., if the original is not divisible, he must compensate each for half).
The passage: 'المودع إذا قال لا أدري أيكما استودعني ... يعطيها لهما نصفين ويضمن مثلها بينهما'.
4
Understand the rationale for the ruling
The depositary is considered to have destroyed the deposit through his ignorance (أتلف ما استودع بجهله). He must compensate both claimants equally. This is similar to the case of a usurper who destroys a common property.
This ruling applies when there is no evidence and the depositary refuses to swear.
5
Connect to broader qawaid
These rulings fall under the principles of 'al-ghurūm bi al-ghunm' (liability accompanies benefit) and 'al-yaqīn lā yazūlu bi al-shakk' (certainty is not removed by doubt). The depositary's uncertainty leads to shared liability.
Also, debts take precedence over inheritance to ensure justice among creditors.

The Maxim: Evidence Supported by the Apparent is Rejected
Qawida with furuat on evidence and apparent state

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the qawida 'kullu bayyinah shahida laha al-zahir lam tuqbal', identify its furuat, and apply it when issuing a fatwa on conflicting evidence in cases of safah and mahr.

Core Idea
Evidence that merely confirms the apparent state is redundant and inadmissible.

In Hanafi jurisprudence, not every piece of evidence is accepted. When the default legal presumption (al-zahir) already supports a claim, additional evidence for that claim is rejected as superfluous. This lesson unpacks that principle through the qawida 'kullu bayyinah shahida laha al-zahir lam tuqbal' and shows its application in two classic furuat: conflicting evidence on the continuation of safah, and spouses disputing the mahr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its rationale
Read the qawida: 'كل بينة شهد لها الظاهر لم تقبل'. Understand that if the evidentiary burden is already satisfied by a legal presumption (the apparent state), then evidence that merely echoes that presumption is not needed and is therefore rejected. The rationale is that such evidence does not add new information; the ẓāhir itself provides a sufficient basis for the ruling.
The ẓāhir here is a legal presumption (e.g., sanity, customary mahr).
2
Apply the maxim to the furu' of safah
In a case where a person has been interdicted for safah, and conflicting evidence arises about whether the safah persists or has been removed, the ẓāhir is removal (since the default is sanity). Therefore, evidence of removal is supported by the ẓāhir and rejected; evidence of continuation is accepted because it contradicts the ẓāhir.
This follows Abu Yusuf's view that a safīh is only interdicted by a judge's order; the ẓāhir of sanity remains unless overridden.
3
Apply the maxim to the furu' of mahr dispute
When spouses dispute the mahr amount and both bring evidence, the ẓāhir is mahr al-mithl (the customary dower). Thus, evidence for mahr al-mithl is rejected as it is supported by the ẓāhir; evidence for a different amount is accepted.
This illustrates that the maxim operates across different chapters of fiqh.
4
Consider the mufta bihi and hierarchy
The ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah, as in al-Muhit, is to prefer evidence of continuation. This is the operative fatwa position. Although there is a reference to Abu Yusuf's proof regarding the need for a judge's interdiction, the maxim itself is well-established.
No contrary position is given, so the fatwa hierarchy is clear.
5
Identify exceptions (istithna')
The passage does not mention exceptions, but note that the maxim applies only when the ẓāhir is a legal presumption that suffices for proof. If the ẓāhir itself is weak or contested, the evidence may still be accepted.
This is implicit from the condition 'shahida laha al-zahir'.

Learning Objective

This lesson unpacks a key Ḥanafī legal maxim on the transference of prohibition (ḥurmatu tata'addā) in financial matters. The maxim states that when a person knowingly receives wealth that is ḥarām in the hands of the predecessor, the prohibition transfers to the recipient. However, an important exception is carved out for the heir: inherited wealth remains permissible for the heir even if he knows of its ḥarām origin. We will explore the scope of this qā'idah through its furū' and the restrictive condition recorded in al-Ẓāhīriyyah.

Guided Path
1

The exact nuance of this restriction is debated; it may refer to the testator's heirs or the original owners of usurped goods.
5

Summarise: for a knowing recipient of ḥarām wealth (e.g., buyer of stolen goods), the prohibition transfers and the wealth remains ḥarām. For an heir, the wealth becomes ḥalāl despite knowledge, unless the original owners are aware (per al-Ẓāhīriyyah).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Benefits dissolve at death; executor's ibra' binds only debts he created, except kitaba waivers are void.

Commentary
This passage establishes a qawida (legal maxim) that a slave manumitted during the master's death illness (mu'taq fi marad al-mawt) occupies a status analogous to a mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) during the period when he must work to pay off his value (zaman al-sa'ayah). (2) His testimony is not accepted during this period. Two different views exist: Abu Hanifa treats the mudabbar during sa'ayah like a mukatab, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad treat him like a free indebted person. The practical effect appears in accidental killing: Abu Hanifa requires the slave to work for the value, while the Sahibayn place the blood money on the 'aqilah. The passage ends with a ruling on the mudabbarah: she cannot marry herself because the mukatabah cannot, though the Sahibayn disagree (since she is free in their view). The Manumitter in Death Illness: Rulings on Accidental Killing and Testimony During Sā‘āyah
A legal maxim comparing two situations in Hanafi law.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim "المعتق في مرض الموت كالمكاتب في زمن سعايته" to derive rulings on accidental homicide and testimony during sā‘āyah.

Core Idea
The slave manumitted in death illness must earn two sums: one for manumission and the lesser of his value or diyah.

This passage from al-Ashbāh wa al-Naẓā’ir presents a powerful legal analogy (qiyās) equating two seemingly distinct situations: a slave manumitted during the master’s death illness and a mukātab during his earning period. By understanding this qā‘idah, you will be able to deduce rulings for accidental homicide and the admissibility of testimony, seeing how Hanafi law extends a single rationale across different chapters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the qā‘idah
The person who manumits a slave while terminally ill (marad al-mawt). This manumission is treated as a bequest from one-third of the estate.
The slave is called 'al-mu‘atiq fī maraḍ al-mawt'.
2
Recognize the comparand
The mukātab (a slave contracted to buy his freedom) during his period of earning (sā‘āyah). Both are in a state of partial freedom and must work to complete manumission.
The analogy is explicitly stated: 'ka al-mukātab fī zaman sa‘āyatih'.
3
Examine the first furū‘: accidental killing
If the slave accidentally kills his master, he must pay two values through sā‘āyah: one for the manumission (since it is a bequest and no bequest for the killer) and a second as the lesser of his own value or the blood money (diyah) of the victim.
This mirrors the mukātab's liability for accidental homicide.
4
Examine the second furū‘: testimony
If the slave testifies during the period of sā‘āyah, his testimony is not accepted. This is analogous to the mukātab, whose testimony is also rejected during the earning period.
The text cites Shahādāt al-Ṣughrā as authority.
5
Note the source authority
The passage comes from a later Hanafi work (al-Ashbāh wa al-Naẓā’ir) but relies on earlier established rulings. The testimony ruling is traced to Shahādāt al-Ṣughrā, indicating it is from the reliable corpus.
This reinforces the weight of the qā‘idah in fatwa.

Commentary
This passage establishes a qāʻidah (legal maxim): the person who manumits a slave while in his death illness (marad al-mawt) is treated like a mukātab (a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom) during the period of his earning (sā‘āyah) in terms of liability for accidental killing. The ruling is applied in the following scenario: if a man in death illness manumits his slave, and then that slave accidentally kills his master (the manumitter), the slave must pay two separate amounts, both of which he must earn and pay: (1) one value for the manumission itself, because it is treated as a bequest (waṣiyyah) from the estate, and a killer cannot inherit or benefit from a bequest; (2) a second amount equal to the lesser of the slave's own value or the blood money (diyah) of the victim, as compensation for the homicide. This mirrors the ruling for a mukātab who commits accidental homicide. Additionally, if the slave testifies during the period of sā‘āyah, his testimony is not accepted, as cited from Shahādāt al-Ṣughrā.
