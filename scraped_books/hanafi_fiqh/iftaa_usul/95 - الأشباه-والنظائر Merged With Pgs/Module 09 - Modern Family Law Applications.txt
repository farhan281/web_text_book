Combining Intentions in a Non-Worship Statement
Ruling on saying 'you are haram to me' intending both divorce and zihar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of combining intentions in a single non-worship statement to determine the legal effects for both divorce (talaq) and zihar.

Core Idea
A single statement can produce multiple legal effects if intentions are valid and not mutually exclusive.

In Hanafi law, the effect of a statement often depends on the intention (niyyah) behind it. While combining intentions is common in acts of worship (e.g., praying with multiple goals), the same principle extends to non-worship statements such as declarations affecting marital status. This lesson examines a key illustration: when a husband says 'you are haram to me' and intends both divorce and zihar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and statement
Identify the exact utterance: 'You are haram to me' said to a wife, or 'Both of you are haram' said to two wives. Note that it is a non-worship statement.
The phrase 'haram' here is used as a conditional declaration.
2
Recognize the two intended effects
The husband may intend divorce (talaq) and/or zihar. Zihar is a form of invalidation by comparing the wife to a female relative. Both have distinct legal consequences.
Divorce dissolves marriage; zihar creates a temporary prohibition requiring expiation.
3
Apply the principle of combining intentions
In Hanafi law, intentions can combine if the legal effects are not contradictory. Here, both effects can occur simultaneously because they are not mutually exclusive.
This is derived from the ruling in Al-Muhit and annotated in Sharh al-Kanz.
4
Determine the ruling for each effect
The result: if the husband intended both, both divorce and zihar take effect. If intended for two wives separately, each receives the intended effect.
The validity of each intention depends on the possibility of its occurrence.
5
Note the source authority
The author references his commentary on al-Kanz, quoting al-Muhit. This indicates the ruling is part of the mutakhkhirin (later) fatwa tradition.
Always check the authority weight in fatwa practice.

Waqf Distribution with 'Then' vs 'And'
Ruling on waqf distribution using 'ثم' vs 'واو' between classes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of interpreting waqf conditions to distinguish between 'thumma' and 'wa' and determine the correct distribution and whether naqd al-qismah applies.

Core Idea
In waqf, 'thumma' creates sequential blocking; 'wa' creates equal sharing requiring revisiting as members die.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir addresses a recurring fatwa scenario in Cairo: how to distribute waqf shares when the founder uses 'thumma' (then) or 'wa' (and) between beneficiary classes. The ruling hinges on whether the upper class blocks the lower class absolutely or only partially, and whether the division is final or subject to nullification (naqd al-qismah) as members die. Understanding this distinction is crucial for any mufti dealing with endowment documents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conjunction
Determine whether the founder used 'thumma' (then) or 'wa' (and) between classes. This is the key variable that determines the entire distribution scheme.
Look for the particle between class descriptions (e.g., 'awlaadi thumma awlaad awlaadi' vs 'awlaadi wa awlaad awlaadi').
2
Apply the rule for 'wa'
If 'wa' is used, the upper class does not block the lower; distribution is equal among classes at the start. When a member dies, his share goes to his siblings (same class), not his offspring, unless a transfer to offspring is stipulated. This triggers naqd al-qismah (revisiting division) as new members appear.
Al-Khassaf's example with 'wa' leads to nullification of the initial division each time a member dies.
3
Apply the rule for 'thumma'
If 'thumma' is used, the upper class blocks the lower. A deceased member's share transfers to his child and continues; the division is never nullified. Even if the upper class becomes extinct, the transferred shares remain with the descendants.
With 'thumma', the order is sequential and final; no revisiting of shares.
4
Evaluate the maxim 'al-ula tahjubu al-sufla'
Understand that this maxim is either absolute (if no transfer to child is stipulated) or limited to one's own branch (if transfer to child is stipulated). The conjunction 'thumma' inherently establishes blocking; if combined with phrases like 'tabaqah ba'da tabaqah', it is emphatic.
The passage clarifies that the maxim is not universal; its application depends on wording and stipulations.
5
Handle contradictory conditions
Recall the Hanafi principle that later conditions abrogate earlier ones (like naskh), contrary to al-Subki's view. This affects how waqf documents with conflicting stipulations are interpreted.
Al-Khassaf's example of a document first prohibiting sale and later permitting exchange illustrates this principle.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on waqf (endowment) distribution, specifically how to handle the shares when the founder uses 'thumma' (then) versus 'wa' (and) between classes. It opens by discussing a dispute between al-Subki (Shafi'i) and the Hanafis: al-Subki argued that if contradictory conditions appear, the first is acted upon, while the Hanafis say the later abrogates, because the condition of the founder is like the text of the Lawgiver where later texts abrogate earlier ones. The author then quotes al-Khassaf's example: if a document first says 'not to be sold or gifted' and later says 'on condition that so-and-so may exchange it', the latter allows exchange because it abrogates the former. The main rule: when the founder uses 'wa', the upper class does not block the lower; shares are distributed equally among classes, and if a member dies without child, his share goes to his siblings (not his offspring). This leads to 'naqd al-qismah' (nullification of division) as new members appear. When using 'thumma', the upper class blocks the lower; the share of a deceased member goes to his child and continues, never nullified even if the upper class dies out. The author clarifies that 'al-ula tahjubu al-sufla' (the upper blocks the lower) is either absolute (if no transfer to child is stipulated) or limited to one's own branch (if transfer to child is stipulated). Finally, he notes that combining 'thumma' with phrases like 'tabaqah ba'da tabaqah' emphasizes the order. The passage ends with a remark on scholarly disagreement about understanding endowers' conditions.

البطن الأعلى (al-batn al-a'la): The first/highest class of beneficiaries in a waqf (e.g., the founder's children)
تحجب الطبقة العليا السفلى (tahjubu al-tabaqah al-'ulya al-sufla): The upper class blocks the lower class from inheriting as long as any member of the upper class exists
نقض القسمة (naqd al-qismah): Nullification of the division of shares due to the appearance of new beneficiaries, requiring redistribution

Thirteen Types of Marital Separation: Talaq vs Faskh
Classification of marital separations with a guiding principle for talaq vs faskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify each of the thirteen types of marital separation as talaq or faskh by applying the Hanafi legal maxims.

Core Idea
Every separation from the husband is talaq; from the woman, faskh—except apostasy, which annuls the marriage itself.

Marital dissolution in Hanafi law is not monolithic. Some separations are revocable divorces (talaq) initiated by the husband, while others are annulments (faskh) arising from defects or circumstances. This lesson presents the thirteen recognized forms and the principle that distinguishes talaq from faskh.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the thirteen separations
Commit the list of thirteen types to memory, noting which seven require a judge's ruling and which six are automatic.
Use mnemonic devices for the seven: Jabb, 'Inna, Khiyar al-Bulugh, 'Adam Kafa'a, Nuqsan al-Mahr, Iba' al-Zawj 'an al-Islam, Li'an.
2
Divide into two groups
Group 1 (requires judicial decree): jabb, 'inna, khiyar al-bulugh, lack of kafa'a, deficiency of mahr, husband's refusal of Islam, li'an. Group 2 (automatic): khiyar al-'itq, iyla', ridda, tabayun al-darayn, milk, nikah fasid.
3
Apply the talaq/faskh principle
For each separation, determine its origin: if it originates from the husband (e.g., iyla', jabb, 'inna), it is talaq; if from the wife and not due to marriage (e.g., khiyar al-bulugh, khiyar al-'itq), it is faskh.
The footnote provides this principle.
4
Handle the exception
The husband's apostasy is faskh despite originating from him, because it negates the marriage contract entirely (incompatibility). This is not a direct divorce action.
Exception proves the rule.

Commentary
This passage enumerates thirteen forms of marital separation (furqa) in Hanafi law. They are divided into two groups: seven require a judge's ruling (qada) and six are automatic. The first group includes separations due to physical defects (castration, impotence), option of puberty (for a minor who reaches puberty), lack of suitable match (kafa'a), shortfall in mahr, husband's refusal to accept Islam, and li'an (mutual imprecation). Automatic Offset of Maintenance Without Consent
Ruling on offsetting maintenance against a wife's debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a wife's maintenance debt is automatically offset against her debt to her husband without his consent, and explain the reasoning based on the classification of maintenance as a weaker debt.

Core Idea
Maintenance debt is weaker; it automatically offsets the wife's debt without consent, unlike other debts.

This lesson addresses a specific Hanafi rule in family finance: when a husband owes maintenance to his wife and she owes him a debt, the two are automatically offset without the husband's consent. This is an exception to the general principle that offset requires mutual agreement. The ruling is based on the concept that the maintenance debt is considered 'weaker' (ad'af), akin to a difference in type or quality, which triggers automatic offset. Understanding this rule is crucial for fatwa issuance in modern cases where spousal debts and maintenance overlap.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the legal ruling: the husband's debt to the wife for maintenance is automatically offset against the wife's debt to the husband, regardless of the husband's consent.
The text states: 'offsetting occurs with the maintenance debt without the husband's consent'.
2
Identify the exception
Note that this is a departure from the default rule that offsetting mutual debts requires consent of both parties. The text explicitly contrasts it with 'other debts'.
General rule: muqassa requires rida (consent). Here, an exception is made for maintenance.
3
Analyze the reasoning (talil)
The reasoning given is that the maintenance debt is 'weaker' (ad'af). This weakness causes it to be treated as a different type of debt, similar to a case where one right is good (jayed) and the other defective (radī').
The analogy: difference in type or quality allows offset without consent. Maintenance's need-based nature renders it weaker.
4
Connect to broader principles
Consider the maxim that need (ḥājah) may be treated like necessity (ḍarūrah). The wife's dependence on maintenance justifies the special treatment.
This ruling can be seen as a furu' (branch) of the qā'idah 'al-ḍarūrah tabīḥ al-maḥẓūrāt' in a limited sense.
5
Apply in fatwa context
In practice, when a wife requests maintenance and she owes a debt to her husband, the mufti should rule that the maintenance is deemed paid by way of automatic offset, without needing the husband's agreement.
This alleviates the wife's hardship and ensures timely support.

Learning Objective

Using an orphan's labor without just compensation is ḥarām, even for relatives, except for the mother or within educational context.

This lesson examines a specific legal maxim from al-Ashbāh wa al-Naẓāʾir that protects the rights of orphans. It establishes a general prohibition against employing an orphan without fair wages, with limited exceptions based on close relationship or educational benefit. Understanding this maxim helps muftis evaluate real-world cases involving child labor and guardianship.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim

The term 'استخدام' implies employing or making use of the orphan's services.
2
Identify the general hukm
The general ruling is clear: the act is ḥarām (prohibited). This protection applies regardless of the user's relationship to the orphan, unless an exception is established.
The word 'حرام' is decisive in Hanafi fiqh, indicating a prohibition with legal consequences.
3
List the furu‘ (branches)
The text explicitly mentions two branches that fall under the prohibition: the orphan’s brother and his teacher. It then states two exceptions: the mother, and the teacher sending the orphan to bring his partner.
These branches illustrate the scope of the maxim.

The mother is excepted due to her special care and the lack of expectation of compensation in a mother-child relationship. The teacher's errand is excepted because it serves the orphan's education and is incidental to teaching.
The exception for the teacher is specifically limited: only when the orphan is sent to fetch the teacher's partner, not for general errands.

Consider a case where an orphan works in his uncle’s shop. The maxim applies: without pay, it is ḥarām. The uncle is not excepted. Only if the uncle is the mother? No. So the prohibition remains.
The 'illa (effective cause) is the orphan's vulnerability and the need to preserve his property.

Forbidden Acts for Adults Also Forbidden for Their Children
Ruling on parents doing forbidden acts to their children

Core Idea
What is haram for you is haram for you to impose on your child.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim governing the relationship between a guardian and a minor child in Islamic law. The rule extends certain prohibitions from the adult to actions done to the child, ensuring that guardians do not expose children to what is religiously impermissible. We will examine the maxim and its applications through concrete examples.

State the maxim and its rationale
Understand the wording: 'What is forbidden for the adult to do is also forbidden for him to do to his minor child.' The rationale is that the guardian is responsible for the child's upbringing and cannot involve the child in what is unlawful.
Maxim is from the Hanafi qawaid (Ashbah wa al-Nazair).
2

The maxim applies to acts that are inherently forbidden (haram) for the adult. Examples from the text: giving wine, dressing a male child in silk, applying henna (if considered prohibited for men), and making the child face or turn away from qiblah during elimination.
The list is not exhaustive; the principle is general.
3
Apply the maxim to new scenarios
Given a new case (e.g., feeding a child pork, shaving a boy's beard against his will), determine if the act is forbidden for the adult; if yes, it is also forbidden to do to the child.
Consider whether the prohibition is inherent or contingent.
4
Distinguish from permissible acts
Acts that are merely disliked (makruh) or permitted (mubah) for the adult do not automatically become forbidden for the child. The maxim only covers what is haram.
For example, eating garlic is makruh for adults attending mosque but not forbidden to feed to a child.

The invalid grandfather is a distant relative with limited rights — he cannot act as guardian or claim inheritance like the paternal grandfather.

The concept of a 'valid' versus 'invalid' grandfather is critical in Hanafi family law. While the paternal grandfather holds significant authority, the invalid grandfather through a female line is treated as a distant relative. The passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair succinctly enumerates these differences.

قل من تعرَّض لها، وقد ألف فيها من أصحابنا القاضي بدر الدين الشبلي في كتابه «آكام المرجان في أحكام الجان لكني لم أطلع عليه الآن، وما نقلته عنه فإنما هو بواسطة نقل الأسيوطي رحمه الله. ولا خلاف في أنهم مُكَلَّفون: مؤمنهم في الجنة وكافرهم في النار، وإنما اختلفوا في ثواب الطائعين. ففي البزازية معزيا إلى الأجناس عن الإمام : ليس للجن ثواب وفي التفاسير توقف الإمام في ثواب الجن لأنه جاء في القرآن فيهم ﴿يَغْفِرْ لَكُمْ مِنْ ذُنُوبِكُمْ﴾ [الأحقاف: ٣١] والمغفرة لا تستلزم الإثابة لأنه ستر، ومنه المغْفَر للبيضة، والإثابة بالوعد فضل. قالت المعتزلة : أُوعد عد ظالمهم فيستحق العقاب ويستحق الثواب صالحهم ؛ قال الله تعالى: ﴿وَأَمَّا الْقَاسِطُونَ فَكَانُوا لِجَهَنَّمَ حَطَبًا﴾ [الجن: ١٥] :قلنا الثواب فضل من الله تعالى لا بالاستحقاق، فإن قيل قوله تعالى: ﴿فَبِأَيِّ آلَاءِ رَبِّكُمَا تُكَذِّبَانِ﴾ [الرحمن: ١٣] بعد عد نعيم الجنة خطابًا للثقلين يردّ ما ذكرت قلنا ذكروا أن المراد بالتوقف : التوقف في المأكل والمشرب والملاذ لا الدخول فيه كدخول الملائكة للسلام والزيارة والخدمة ﴿وَالْمَلَائِكَةُ يَدْخُلُونَ عَلَيْهِمْ مِنْ كُلِّ بَابٍ سَلَامٌ﴾ [الرعد: ٢٣] الآية.
(انتهى). فمنها النكاح ؛ قال في السراجية : لا تجوز المناكحة بين بني آدم والجن وإنسان الماء لاختلاف الجنس. (انتهى). وتبعه في منية المفتي والفيض، وفي القنية : سُئِلَ الحسن البصري رضي الله عنه عن التزويج بجنية فقال : يجوز بلا شهود، ثم رقم آخر فقال : لا يجوز، ثم رقم آخر يصفع السائل لحماقته. (انتهى).
وفي يتيمة الدهر في فتاوى أهل العصر : سُئِلَ علي بن أحمد عن التزويج بامرأة مسلمة من الجن؛ هل يجوز إذا تصوّر ذلك أم يختص الجواز بالآدميين؟ فقال : يصفع هذا السائل لحماقته وجهله . قلت : وهذا لا يدل على حماقة السائل ولو كان لا يتصور ؛ ترى أن أبا الليث رحمه الله ذكر في فتاواه أن الكفَّار لو تترسوا بنبي من الأنبياء، هل يُرمَى؟ فقال : يسأل ذلك النبي، ولا يتصوّر ذلك بعد رسولنا ، ولكن أجاب على تقدير التصوّر كذا هذا. وسُئِلَ عنها أبو حامد رحمه الله فقال : لا يجوز.
(انتهى).

فإذا تقرر المنع من نكاح الإنسي الجنية؛ فالمنع من نكاح الجني الإنسية من باب أولى، ويدلّ عليه قوله في السراجية لا تجوز المناكحة وهو شامل لهما، لكن روى أبو عثمان بن سعيد بن العباس الرازي في كتاب الإلهام والوسوسة فقال : حدثنا مقاتل عن سعيد بن داود الزبيدي قال : كتب قوم من أهل اليمن إلى مالك يسألونه عن نكاح الجن وقالوا: إن هنا رجلاً من الجن يخطب إلينا جارية يزعم أنه يريد الحلال .
Rulings on Jinn. Few have discussed it; among our colleagues, Qadi Badr al-Din al-Shibli wrote a book 'Akam al-Marjan fi Ahkam al-Jan' but I have not seen it now; what I have quoted from him is via the transmission of al-Suyuti, may Allah have mercy on him. There is no disagreement that they are obligated (mukallaf): their believers are in Paradise and their disbelievers in Hell; the difference is about the reward of the obedient ones. In al-Bazzaziyya, citing al-Ajnas from Imam [Abu Hanifa]: Jinn have no reward. And in the tafasir, Imam [Abu Hanifa] suspends judgment on the reward of jinn because the Qur'an says about them: 'He will forgive you your sins' (46:31), and forgiveness does not necessitate reward because it is concealment; from it comes 'al-mighfar' for an egg; and reward by promise is a favor. The Mu'tazila said: He threatened the oppressor among them so they deserve punishment and the righteous deserve reward; Allah said: 'But as for the unjust, they are firewood for Hell' (72:15). We said: Reward is a favor from Allah, not by merit. If it is said: His saying: 'Then which of the favors of your Lord do you deny?' (55:13) after counting the bliss of Paradise, addressed to the two weighty ones, refutes what we mentioned; we said: They mentioned that what is meant by 'suspension' is suspension regarding food, drink, and pleasures, not entrance into it, like the entrance of angels for peace, visitation, and service: 'And the angels enter upon them from every gate, peace' (13:23) the verse. (End). Among them is marriage; it is stated in al-Sirajiyya: Marriage between the children of Adam and jinn and water-beings is not permissible due to difference of species. (End). This is followed in Munyat al-Mufti and al-Fayd. In al-Qunyah: Hasan al-Basri, may Allah be pleased with him, was asked about marrying a female jinni, and he said: It is permissible without witnesses; then another annotation said: Not permissible; then another annotation said: Slap the questioner for his foolishness. (End). In Yatimat al-Dahr fi Fatawa Ahl al-Asr: 'Ali ibn Ahmad was asked about marrying a Muslim woman from the jinn: is it permissible if that is conceivable, or is permissibility specific to humans? He said: Slap this questioner for his foolishness and ignorance. I say: This does not indicate the foolishness of the questioner even if it is not conceivable; do you not see that Abu al-Layth, may Allah have mercy on him, mentioned in his fatawa that if disbelievers shield themselves with a prophet from among the prophets, should they be shot? He said: Ask that prophet, and that is not conceivable after our Messenger, but he answered on the assumption of conceivability likewise. This one was asked about it and Abu Hamid, may Allah have mercy on him, said: It is not permissible. (End). Some have adduced for the prohibition of marrying female jinn by His saying in Surat al-Nahl: 'And Allah has made for you spouses from among yourselves' (16:72), i.e., from your kind and type and in your creation, as Allah said: 'There has come to you a Messenger from among yourselves' (9:128), i.e., from mankind. (End). And others adduced what Harb al-Karmani in his Masail and Ishaq narrated: He said: Muhammad ibn Yahya al-Qati'i narrated to us, from Bishr ibn 'Umar ibn Lahi'a, from Yunus ibn Yazid, from al-Zuhri, who said: The Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, forbade marriage with jinn. Although it is mursal, it is supported by the statements of scholars; the prohibition has been reported from Hasan al-Basri, Qatada, al-Hakam ibn Qutayba, Ishaq ibn Rahawayh, and 'Uqba ibn al-Asamm, may Allah be pleased with them. So if the prohibition of marriage of a human male to a female jinni is established, then the prohibition of marriage of a male jinni to a human female is even more evident; and this is indicated by the statement in al-Sirajiyya: Marriage is not permissible, which includes both. But Abu 'Uthman ibn Sa'id ibn al-'Abbas al-Razi narrated in the book al-Ilham wa al-Waswasa, saying: Maqatil narrated to us from Sa'id ibn Dawud al-Zubaydi, who said: A group from the people of Yemen wrote to Malik asking him about marriage with jinn, saying: There is a man from the jinn who has proposed to us for a young woman, claiming that he wants a lawful marriage.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of a section 'Rulings on Jinn' from a later Hanafi fatwa collection. It opens by noting the scarcity of literature on the subject and references a now-lost book by Qadi Badr al-Din al-Shibli. The first ruling establishes the legal capacity of jinn: they are mukallaf (accountable), with believers in Paradise and disbelievers in Hell. However, the Hanafi Imams differ on whether obedient jinn receive reward. According to al-Bazzaziyya citing al-Ajnas from Abu Hanifa, jinn have no reward; other tafasir report that Imam Abu Hanifa suspended judgment (tawaqquf), because the Quran only mentions forgiveness of sins for them, and forgiveness is concealment, not necessarily reward. The Mu'tazila argue that since the Quran threatens oppressors and promises good to the righteous, jinn must be rewardable. The Hanafi response distinguishes: reward is a pure favor from Allah, not a right. The objection that the verse 'Which of the favors of your Lord do you deny?' (55:13) is addressed to jinn and humans (al-thaqalayn) after enumerating Paradise's bliss is answered by noting that the suspension (tawaqquf) is only about food, drink, and physical pleasures, not about entering Paradise itself (which is confirmed for the believing jinn). The passage then turns to the specific issue of marriage between humans and jinn. The dominant Hanafi position from al-Sirajiyya is absolute prohibition due to difference of species (ikhtilaf al-jins). This is endorsed by Munyat al-Mufti and al-Fayd. A notable anecdote from al-Qunyah reports that Hasan al-Basri first permitted it (without witnesses), then retracted, then called for slapping the questioner for foolishness—indicating how far-fetched the question is considered. The author critiques this reaction by analogizing to a fatwa of Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi: even if a situation is inconceivable in reality (like disbelievers using a prophet as a human shield after our Prophet), a scholar may still answer theoretically. So the questioner's query is not necessarily foolish. Despite that, the final ruling is prohibition, as stated by Abu Hamid. Two proofs for prohibition are then given: (1) Qur'anic verse, 'And Allah has made for you spouses from among yourselves' (16:72), interpreted as 'from your own kind'—since jinn are a different species, they are excluded; (2) a hadith (mursal) narrated by al-Zuhri that the Prophet forbade marriage with jinn, supported by the consensus of early scholars. The prohibition is then extended by analogy: if marriage of a human male to a female jinni is prohibited, then marriage of a male jinn to a human female is even more strongly prohibited. Finally, a contrary report from Imam Malik is mentioned as a historical curiosity, but the Hanafi school disregards it.

Key Terms
الجان/الجن (al-jann/al-jinn): Spiritual beings created from fire, with accountability (taklif) and ability to marry.
الثواب (al-thawab): Reward from Allah for obedience, considered a favor (fadl) not a right (istihqaq).
المغفرة (al-maghfirah): Forgiveness, linguistically meaning concealment (sitr).

Marriage Between Humans and Jinn: Prohibition and Adab of the Mufti
Fatwa on marriage between humans and jinn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marriage between humans and jinn, identify the proofs, and explain the adab of answering far-fetched fatwa questions.

Core Idea
Marriage between humans and jinn is prohibited due to difference of species.

The passage moves from the theoretical reward of jinn to a practical family law issue: the permissibility of inter-species marriage. The Hanafi ruling is clear prohibition, supported by Quran, hadith, and early scholar consensus. The author also includes a valuable lesson in mufti adab: even seemingly absurd questions deserve a measured response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibition ruling
Find the explicit statement from al-Sirajiyya: 'لا تجوز المناكحة بين بني آدم والجن وإنسان الماء لاختلاف الجنس' (marriage is not permissible due to difference of species).
This is the core ruling for both male human x female jinni and vice versa.
2
Examine the primary proof from the Quran
Analyze verse 16:72: 'And Allah has made for you spouses from among yourselves' – meaning from your own kind (jins).
Compare with 9:128 'a messenger from among yourselves' to argue it means from humans only.
3
Examine the secondary proof: mursal hadith
Review the hadith narrated by al-Zuhri: 'The Prophet prohibited marriage with jinn.' Though mursal, it is supported by statements of early scholars.
The mursal hadith gains strength (i'tidad) from the fatwas of Hasan al-Basri (after retraction), Qatada, etc.
4
Apply qiyas (analogy) to the reverse case
The prohibition from human male x female jinni is extended by first awlawi (stronger reason) to male jinni x human female.
Al-Sirajiyya's general wording covers both directions.
5
Derive the adab lesson from the story
Analyze the anecdote where Hasan al-Basri first permitted, then prohibited, then called for slapping the questioner. The author criticizes this response, citing Abu al-Layth's approach: answer theoretically even if the situation is inconceivable.
This teaches that a mufti should not dismiss a questioner as foolish; rather, provide a scholarly answer.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of a section 'Rulings on Jinn' from a later Hanafi fatwa collection. It opens by noting the scarcity of literature on the subject and references a now-lost book by Qadi Badr al-Din al-Shibli. The first ruling establishes the legal capacity of jinn: they are mukallaf (accountable), with believers in Paradise and disbelievers in Hell. However, the Hanafi Imams differ on whether obedient jinn receive reward. According to al-Bazzaziyya citing al-Ajnas from Abu Hanifa, jinn have no reward; other tafasir report that Imam Abu Hanifa suspended judgment (tawaqquf), because the Quran only mentions forgiveness of sins for them, and forgiveness is concealment, not necessarily reward. The Mu'tazila argue that since the Quran threatens oppressors and promises good to the righteous, jinn must be rewardable. The Hanafi response distinguishes: reward is a pure favor from Allah, not a right. The objection that the verse 'Which of the favors of your Lord do you deny?' The passage then turns to the specific issue of marriage between humans and jinn. The dominant Hanafi position from al-Sirajiyya is absolute prohibition due to difference of species (ikhtilaf al-jins). This is endorsed by Munyat al-Mufti and al-Fayd. A notable anecdote from al-Qunyah reports that Hasan al-Basri first permitted it (without witnesses), then retracted, then called for slapping the questioner for foolishness—indicating how far-fetched the question is considered. The author critiques this reaction by analogizing to a fatwa of Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi: even if a situation is inconceivable in reality (like disbelievers using a prophet as a human shield after our Prophet), a scholar may still answer theoretically. So the questioner's query is not necessarily foolish. Despite that, the final ruling is prohibition, as stated by Abu Hamid. Two proofs for prohibition are then given: (1) Qur'anic verse, 'And Allah has made for you spouses from among yourselves' (16:72), interpreted as 'from your own kind'—since jinn are a different species, they are excluded; (2) a hadith (mursal) narrated by al-Zuhri that the Prophet forbade marriage with jinn, supported by the consensus of early scholars. The prohibition is then extended by analogy: if marriage of a human male to a female jinni is prohibited, then marriage of a male jinn to a human female is even more strongly prohibited. Finally, a contrary report from Imam Malik is mentioned as a historical curiosity, but the Hanafi school disregards it.

Commentary
This passage presents a second furq. The ruling: a man is not obligated to make a will (wasiyya) for his sick wife, but he is obligated to do so for his sick slave (male or female). The difference (farq) is that the slave is his property (milk), and since he is responsible for the wellbeing of his property, he must ensure the slave is provided for after his death through a will. His wife, on the other hand, is not his property, and her inheritance rights are already determined by the law of inheritance, so there is no obligation to make a separate will for her. The phrase 'في المحلب' likely refers to a specific work or commentary by Imam al-Karabisi. This illustrates how ownership creates a different legal responsibility.

Key Terms
المحلب (al-mahlab): A term referring to a specific section or work; possibly a shortened title of a book by Imam al-Karabisi.
الفرق (al-farq): The distinguishing factor that leads to different rulings.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi law, the obligation to make a will is linked to ownership: a slave must be provided for because he is property; a wife's rights are already fixed by inheritance.
