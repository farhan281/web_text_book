Effect of Riya on Obligatory Acts: Validity and Reward
Discusses the effect of riya on obligatory acts, especially prayer, and the distinction between validity and reward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that riya in obligatory acts does not invalidate the obligation but may affect reward, and distinguish between validity and reward in such cases.

Core Idea
Riya in obligatory acts fulfills the obligation but destroys the reward.

The issue of sincerity (ikhlās) and ostentation (riyā') is central to the acceptance of worship. In this passage, the author brings together several Hanafi sources to clarify how riya affects different acts. A key distinction emerges: while riya can spoil the reward of an obligatory act, it does not nullify the act's validity in terms of discharging the duty. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its implications for the mufti when assessing the validity of prayers performed with riya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling on riya
The passage discusses prayer (salah) and the effect of riya. Note the statement 'لا رياء في الفرائض في حق سقوط الواجب' (no riya in obligatory acts regarding dropping the obligation).
2
Understand the distinction between validity and reward
Riya does not prevent the obligation from being fulfilled, but it may nullify the reward. The mufti must distinguish between sihha (validity) and thawab (reward).
3
Analyze the examples of prayer with riya
The passage provides scenarios: beginning with sincerity then riya enters; praying to please opponents; the case of beautifying prayer when with people but not alone. Each illustrates the same principle.
4
Apply the ruling to a mufti's consultation
If a person asks whether his prayer is valid despite having riya, the mufti should confirm its validity but caution that the reward is diminished or lost.
This principle is specific to obligatory acts; for supererogatory acts, riya may invalidate the act entirely.

The fifth: On explaining sincerity: Al-Zayla‘i explicitly stated that the worshipper needs intention of sincerity in it (the prayer). I have not seen who clarified it, but it is stated in al-Khulasa that there is no riya in obligatory acts. In al-Bazaziyya: He began the prayer with sincerity, then riya mixed with it; then consideration is given to the first. And there is no riya in obligatory acts with regard to dropping the obligation. Then he said: Prayer to please opponents does not benefit; rather, he prays for the sake of Allah. If his opponent did not forgive, his good deeds will be taken from him on the Day of Resurrection. It came in some books that it is taken for one da'iq, and in one da'iq is one-sixth of a dirham's reward of seven hundred prayers in congregation, so there is no benefit in the intention. And if he forgave, then it is not taken, so what benefit is there then? (End). Al-Bazazi benefited by his statement regarding dropping the obligation that obligatory acts with riya are valid, dropping the obligation. But they mentioned in the book of Sacrifice that a camel suffices for seven if all intend qurbah even if their intentions differ (udhiyah, qiran, tamattu‘). They said: If one of them intended meat for his family or was a Christian, it does not suffice for any of them. They explained that because a part did not occur as qurbah, the whole left being qurbah because the bloodshed is indivisible. Hence, if he slaughters it as udhiyah for Allah and for other than Him, it does not suffice him a fortiori, and it should be forbidden. And it is stated in Alfaz al-Takfir that slaughter for a newcomer from Hajj, or battle, or an amir, or other, makes (the slaughtered animal) carrion in al-Bazaziyya, and they differed about the disbelief of the slaughterer: al-Shaykh al-Safkuri, ‘Abd al-Wahid al-Darraqi al-Hadidi, al-Nasafi, and al-Hakim held that he disbelieves; while al-Fadli and Isma‘il al-Zahid held that he does not disbelieve. (End). In al-Tatarghaniyya: If he began purely for Allah then riya entered his heart, he is upon what he began. And riya is that if he were left alone by people, he would not pray, but if with people, he prays. As for if he prays with people beautifying it, but when alone he does not beautify, then he has the reward of the origin of the prayer without the ihsan. And riya does not enter into fasting. In al-Yanabi‘: Ibrahim ibn Yusuf said: If he prayed with riya, then no reward for him and upon him is sin. Some said: he disbelieves. Some said: no reward for him and no sin upon him, and it is as if he did not pray.

Commentary
This passage focuses on sincerity (ikhlāṣ) and the effect of ostentation (riyā') on acts of worship. The author presents the view of al-Zayla‘ī that the worshipper needs to intend sincerity, but clarifies from al-Khulāṣah that in obligatory acts, riyā' does not nullify the validity with respect to fulfilling the obligation (sukūṭ al-wājib), but it may affect reward. The discussion extends to the example of a sacrificial camel (badanah), which can be shared by seven if all intend qurbah; if one partner intends only worldly benefit, the sacrifice is invalid for all because the slaughter (irāqah) is indivisible. This leads to the ruling that slaughtering for other than Allah (e.g., for a visiting dignitary) makes the animal carrion (maytah), with disagreement on whether the slaughterer becomes a disbeliever. Finally, the text presents various opinions on one who prays with riyā': some say no reward and sin, some say disbelief, some say no reward and no sin, as if he had not prayed. This underscores the importance of ikhlāṣ and the nuanced Hanafi treatment of riyā'.

Key Terms
الإخلاص (al-ikhlāṣ): Sincerity in intention, performing an act solely for Allah's sake.
الرياء (al-riyā'): Performing an act of worship to be seen or praised by people, i.e., ostentation.
القربة (al-qurbah): An act of drawing close to Allah; a devotional intention.

Indivisibility of Qurbah in Shared Sacrifice and Rulings on Slaughter for Other Than Allah
Discusses the indivisibility of qurbah in shared sacrifices and the ruling on slaughter for other than Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the intention of qurbah is indivisible in shared sacrifices, and state the ruling when one partner intends worldly benefit, as well as the controversy over slaughtering for other than Allah.

Core Idea
A shared sacrifice is invalid for all if one partner lacks qurbah intention, because the bloodshed is indivisible.

The passage extends the discussion of intention to the case of a sacrificial camel (badanah) shared by seven people. The Hanafi scholars clarify that the act of slaughter (iraqah) is a single indivisible act of devotion; thus, if one partner intends something other than qurbah (e.g., meat for family), the entire sacrifice fails. This lesson unpacks that rule and also examines the more severe case of slaughtering for other than Allah, which may entail takfir.

Guided Path
1
Recall the rule of shared sacrifice
A camel can be shared by seven if all intend qurbah (udhiyah, qiran, tamattu' are all valid).
2
Identify the condition
All partners must intend qurbah. If even one intends worldly benefit (meat) or is a non-Muslim, the sacrifice does not suffice for any.
3
Understand the rationale
The slaughter (iraqah) is a single act; it cannot be partially for Allah and partially for other than Him. Hence, indivisible.
4
Apply to the question of slaughter for other than Allah
The passage then discusses killing an animal for a visiting dignitary etc. The Bazaziyya rules it as carrion (maytah), with disagreement on whether the slaughterer becomes a disbeliever.
5
Evaluate the juristic difference
The difference among scholars (takfir vs. no takfir) shows the gravity of diverting intention from Allah.

Exception to Caution: Joint Slave Girl and Ownership Rights
Note on exception to caution in private parts for joint slave girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the exception to the principle of caution in private parts regarding joint ownership and explain the rationale of preserving ownership rights.

Core Idea
Caution yields to ownership rights in the joint slave girl case.

The Hanafi school is known for extreme caution in matters of sexual relations. However, this note from Mi'raj al-Dirayah reveals a deliberate exception. When two men jointly own a slave girl, their mutual fear does not trigger the removal of her from their control. Instead, the law respects their ownership by allowing each to keep her for alternating days. This case teaches us that even the highest caution can be overridden by a stronger legal consideration, and a mufti must be aware of such nuances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The Hanafi scholars exercise extreme caution (ihtiyat) in matters of private parts (furooj). This is the default position.
The principle is: 'al-ihtiyat fi al-furuj' – caution in private matters.
2
Locate the exception
The exception is the case of a jointly owned slave girl (jariyah bayna sharikayn). If each partner fears the other, they may not place her with a trustee.
The text explicitly says: 'except in this case'.
3
Understand the rationale
The rationale is 'hishmatan li'l-milk' – out of respect for ownership. Placing her with a trustee would interfere with the partners' right to use their property.
Ownership rights are given priority over extreme caution here.
4
Recognize the mufti's lesson
A mufti must realize that general principles have exceptions, especially when property rights are involved. He must weigh competing legal considerations.
This is a key point in mufti adab: not to apply rules mechanically.

Duress and Self-Destruction: The Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Rulings on duress (ikrah) when coerced to self-destruct

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi ikhtilaf on actively destroying oneself under duress and apply the distinction between qisas and diyah in such cases.

Core Idea
Under duress, you may not actively destroy yourself; patience is the Sahibayn's rule.

This passage from al-Zayla'i presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on the limits of duress. When a person is threatened with death unless he throws himself into fire, Abu Hanifa allows him to choose the easier death, while the Sahibayn insist he must endure and not actively cause his own demise. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to weigh competing legal principles (avoiding harm vs. preserving life).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Coercion Scenario
The coercer says: 'Throw yourself into the fire (or from the mountain) or I will kill you.' The throwing is inescapable but involves some 'weightlessness' (i.e., death is certain but the act feels less severe).
The phrase 'فيه نوع خفة' indicates the act is perceived as easier.
2
Compare Abu Hanifa's Position with the Sahibayn's
Abu Hanifa: the coerced may choose to throw himself because he is faced with two trials and picks the easier. The Sahibayn: he must endure and not actively cause his own death, because directly performing the act is striving to destroy oneself.
The Sahibayn's view aligns with the general prohibition of suicide.
3
Examine the Ship Fire Analogy
The origin (أصل) is a ship on fire: if you stay you burn, if you jump you drown. Abu Hanifa allows choosing; the Sahibayn say to endure. This analogy grounds the ikhtilaf in a concrete hypothetical.
The analogy shows that the dispute is not only about duress but about active vs. passive death.
4
Extract the Ruling on Qisas vs. Diyah
If the coerced throws himself into fire and dies, the coercer faces qisas (intentional killing). If he throws from a mountain or is killed by sword, Abu Hanifa says diyah is due (killing with a heavy object, i.e., indirect). This distinction rests on the directness of the act.
The difference hinges on whether the coercer's threat is seen as the direct cause (fire = direct? actually fire is also indirect? the text says fire leads to qisas, mountain to diyah – nuance to note).
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
In fatwa, the Sahibayn's view is generally preferred (preservation of life). Thus, a mufti would advise the coerced to endure and not jump, even under threat of death.
The text does not explicitly give tarjih, but later Hanafi practice favours the Sahibayn.

Fatwa on interpreting waqf for hadith teaching

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principles of waqf interpretation and urf (custom) to determine the permissible method of teaching in a madrasah mawqūfah.

Core Idea
A waqf founder's intent is followed; if unclear, local custom determines the method of teaching hadith.

When a waqf deed for a school is ambiguous about the method of teaching hadith, how should a mufti resolve the uncertainty? This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents a fatwa by al-Hafiz al-Iraqi, cited by al-Suyuti, that clarifies the Hanafi approach: first adhere to the waqif's explicit conditions, and if ambiguity remains, defer to the established custom (urf) of the locality. Understanding this ruling equips you to handle similar waqf-related questions in contemporary fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the waqf condition and its ambiguity
Read the passage and note that the waqf condition is simply 'for teaching hadith' without specifying whether it means mustalah (terminology) or matn (text) with commentary.

2
Recognize al-Iraqi's ruling
Al-Iraqi states: 'the apparent ruling is to follow the conditions of the waqifs, for they differ in conditions.' This means the primary obligation is to honor the explicit terms of the waqf.
If the condition is clear, no further inquiry is needed.
3
Apply custom (urf) when conditions are unclear
If the condition remains ambiguous, al-Iraqi says: 'likewise the custom of each country.' Custom fills the gap by indicating what the waqif likely intended.
The Hanafi maxim 'al-ʿādah muḥakkamah' (custom is authoritative) applies.
4
Note regional variation
The passage contrasts Syrian practice (listening and occasional commentary) with Egyptian practice (combining reading and commentary), showing that custom varies by locality.
A mufti must investigate local practice, not apply a universal rule.
5
Derive the general principle for muftis
From this case, the mufti learns that waqf interpretation prioritizes the founder's stipulation, but when terms are vague, prevailing custom becomes the decisive factor.
This principle extends to other waqf ambiguities (e.g., 'teaching Quran' includes tajweed if customary).

The Qadi Is Bound by Waqf Conditions and Maslahah
Ruling on void qadi acts when conditions are violated

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a qadi's act contrary to a waqf condition or without maslahah is void, and distinguish valid from invalid qadi acts in given scenarios.

Core Idea
The qadi is not an absolute sovereign; his acts are void if they contradict a waqf condition or public interest.

This passage from the Hanafi tradition of qada (judging) clarifies that a judge's authority is not untrammeled. It must be exercised within the boundaries set by the law and by the intentions of those whose rights are at stake. The waqf condition is likened to a legal text; violating it is like violating the Qur'an or Sunnah. Similarly, any act that fails to serve the public interest is legally ineffective.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act of the qadi
Determine exactly what the qadi did: e.g., issued a judgment contrary to a waqf condition, married a minor to a non-kuf', absolved a wall owner based on a single witness, or postponed a right without consent.
The act must be a judicial act, not a personal one.
2
Check for a controlling condition
Is there a waqf condition or a procedural rule (like requirement of two witnesses for liability) that governs this act? If the qadi acts against that condition, the act is batil.
Waqf conditions have the force of nas; procedural rules are also binding.
3
Assess whether the act serves maslahah
Even if no explicit condition is violated, the act must serve the legitimate interests of the parties. For example, marrying a minor to a non-kuf' does not serve her interest and thus is invalid.
Maslahah here means the objective legal benefit, not the qadi's personal opinion.
4
Conclude whether the act is batil
If the act violates a condition or does not serve maslahah, it is void. The examples in the passage (waqf, marriage, wall, postponement) all illustrate this principle.
Batil means no legal effect; it must be undone or corrected.

Resolving Conflicting Waqf Conditions: Al-Subki's Five Turuq
Case analysis of conflicting waqf conditions with five reconciliation methods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply al-Subki's five methodological approaches (turuq) to resolve conflicting apparent meanings (zawahir) in waqf conditions, and construct a reasoned fatwa that balances textual indications and the founder's intent.

Core Idea
When waqf conditions conflict, earlier conditions and general intent take precedence; giving is preferred over depriving when doubt remains.

This passage presents a complex waqf case where multiple deaths and generations create a conflict between two apparent meanings of the founder's conditions. Al-Subki, the renowned Shafi'i jurist, offers five distinct methods to reconcile the conflict. As a mufti, you will often face such scenarios where no single solution is obvious. This lesson trains you in the rigorous methodology of tarjih and the intellectual humility required when issuing fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting conditions
The founder stated: 'Whoever dies having a child, his share transfers to his child' and 'After the children, it is for the grandchildren.' These two conditions appear to conflict when the grandchildren layer is reached.
The first condition seems to give each grandchild only their parent's share, while the second suggests all grandchildren share equally.
2
Map the heir layers
Chart the generations: Abd al-Qadir (founder), his children (Ali, Latifa, Amr), then grandchildren (Zaynab, Fatima, Abd al-Rahman, Malaka). The conflict arises because grandchildren are both included by the second condition and apparently excluded by the first when their parent has died.
The 'prevention' (hajb) of Abd al-Rahman and Malaka by the children is key.
3
Apply the five turuq
Al-Subki lists: (a) Earlier condition takes precedence (not naskh), (b) Ordering of layers is the origin (asl), transfer rule is a branch (far'), (c) General wording covers collective transfer, harmonizing both, (d) Giving is preferred over depriving when no preponderance, (e) If doubt remains, divide equally.
Each turuq is a valid method; the mufti must weigh them case by case.
4
Determine distribution and note mufti adab
Al-Subki calculates shares based on the fourth and fifth turuq, then concludes with humility: 'I do not desire any jurist to imitate me, but rather to examine for himself.'
The mufti's role is to present reasoned analysis, not to demand blind adherence.

Commentary
This passage presents a complex case study in waqf inheritance, illustrating how a mufti must reconcile conflicting apparent meanings (zawahir) in the founder's conditions. The scenario involves multiple deaths and layers of heirs. The author, al-Subki, outlines several methodological approaches (turuq) to resolve the conflict: (1) giving precedence to the earlier condition over the later one (unless it's abrogation, which it isn't), (2) treating the ordering of generations as the foundational principle and the transfer rule as a detail, (3) interpreting the general wording to include collective transfer to all children, thus harmonizing both statements, (4) preferring the outcome that gives rather than deprives descendants when no preponderance exists, and (5) when doubt remains, dividing the shares equally among potential heirs. After Fatima's death without issue, the remaining heirs (all in the same generation) divide her share by general inheritance rules (male gets double female) but without referring back to their ancestor's shares. Al-Subki then calculates specific fractions. He ends by noting that he does not claim his view is binding — each jurist must exercise ijtihad. The commentator (al-Asyuti) adds that the first approach (including Abd al-Rahman and Malaka after Abd al-Qadir's death) seems preferable. The passage demonstrates the intellectual rigor expected of a mufti when faced with contradictory textual indications.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Every sin without a fixed hadd is subject to ta'zir, and it is not dropped by repentance.

This passage from the Hanafi tradition delivers the foundational qawida for discretionary punishment (ta'zir) and walks through a series of concrete scenarios a mufti may face. Understanding these rulings is essential for issuing correct fatwas in matters of public order and personal conduct.

Guided Path
1
State the qawida
Identify the rule: 'Every sin that has no fixed hadd is subject to ta'zir'. Recognize that this is a general principle governing all offenses outside the fixed punishments.
The qawida is a sughra (minor) maxim under the Book of Hudud.
2
Apply the qawida to the listed scenarios
Work through each case: harming by word or deed (including winking), saying 'O kafir' to a dhimmi, cold piety (giving a date), seizing a debtor's family, deceiving a woman, and a father cursing his child. In each, confirm that the act is a sin without a fixed hadd, thus subject to ta'zir.
Note that calling a dhimmi 'kafir' is sinful (yathim) but the text does not explicitly state ta'zir – this is an ambiguity to be resolved.
3
Identify the exception for murder in dar al-harb
A Muslim who commits a crime in enemy territory is generally not punished upon return, except for murder where only blood money is due. This is an exception to the default ta'zir rule.
The text says 'except in murder: the blood money is due' – not ta'zir.
4
Analyze the dispute over people of status
Al-Shafi'i exempts men of status from ta'zir, while the Hanafi position rejects any exemption. Understand the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) and the Hanafi proof from equality before the law.
The author notes 'I have not seen this from our scholars', affirming the Hanafi view.
5
Note the complementary rule on repentance
Ta'zir is not dropped by repentance, similar to hadd punishments. This means even if the offender repents, the discretionary punishment still applies.
This reinforces the seriousness of ta'zir.

The Mufti's Caution in Takfir
Opening rulings on honoring disbelievers and the cautionary principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of caution in declaring apostasy, distinguishing acts that constitute kufr from those that do not based on available narrations.

Core Idea
A mufti must not declare a Muslim a disbeliever if there exists any narration preventing takfir.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Repentance is generally accepted, but certain sins—like reviling prophets or the Companions—make repentance unacceptable in this world.

The exceptions are based on the severity of the crime and the likelihood of genuine repentance.

Revocability of Judicial Appointment vs Binding Guardianship
Note on revocability of qadi appointment vs binding nature of testamentary guardianship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between appointment to judiciary (tawliyat al-qada) and testamentary guardianship (wisayah) in terms of revocability, and apply the rule that a qadi may be dismissed without cause but a testamentary guardian cannot be dismissed except for treachery or incapacity.

Core Idea
The qadi can be dismissed without cause, but a testamentary guardian cannot — guardianship is binding after the testator's death.

In this note, the author clarifies a critical distinction in the Hanafi law of appointments: the authority to dismiss differs fundamentally between a qadi (judge) and a wasi (testamentary guardian). While the sultan may dismiss a qadi at will, a testamentary guardian appointed by the father enjoys a protected status — the qadi cannot remove him except for proven treachery or clear inability. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its underlying maxim, and the exceptions that apply when the guardian was appointed by the qadi himself.

Guided Path
1

Recognize that the passage contrasts two types of appointment: tawliyat al-qada (appointment of a judge) and al-wilayah 'ala mal al-yateem bi al-wisayah (guardianship over orphan's property via testamentary will).
These have different legal natures and consequences.
2
State the default ruling for each
For qadi: ja'iz min al-janibayn (revocable by both sides). For wasi: lazimah ba'da mawt al-musi (binding after testator's death).
The word ja'iz indicates permissibility to rescind; lazimah indicates obligation to remain.
3

Because qadi appointment is ja'iz, the sultan can dismiss even without cause, and the qadi can resign. Because guardianship is lazimah, the qadi can dismiss only for khiyanah (treachery) or 'ajz zahir (apparent inability), and the guardian can resign only in two exceptional cases mentioned in other works.
The exceptions for guardian resignation are detailed in 'Waqaya' al-Fawa'id'.

If the guardian was appointed by the qadi (not the father), the qadi retains power of dismissal as per al-Quniyah, and the guardian can resign only in the qadi's presence.
This shows the distinction between testamentary (binding) and judicial (revocable) appointment.

When Shuf'a Is Not Forfeited: The Buyer as Agent
Exception to the rule that shuf'a is forfeited upon taking from the buyer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the exceptional case where the right of shuf'a does not become void despite the shafi'i taking possession from the buyer, and apply this principle in fatwa practice.

Core Idea
Shuf'a is not forfeited when the buyer is an agent—the right persists against the true owner.

In the Hanafi school, the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) is normally exercised and extinguished when the shafi'i pays the buyer and takes the property. However, there is a critical exception: if the buyer is merely an agent (wakil) acting for a principal, the shafi'i's action against the agent does not nullify his claim. This principle is a vital tool for muftis handling pre-emption cases, ensuring the true owner cannot evade the claim through an intermediary.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Recall that normally, when the shafi'i takes the property from the buyer after paying the price, the right of shuf'a is fully exercised and becomes void.
This is the default position in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the exception
The exception occurs when the buyer is an agent (wakil bil-shira). In that case, taking from the agent does not extinguish the right.
The agent is not the true purchaser; the principal is.
3
Understand the legal rationale
The agent acts on behalf of another; the sale is attributed to the principal. Therefore, the shafi'i's claim remains against the principal, not the intermediary.
The maxim: 'The agent is a representative; the transaction belongs to the principal.'
4
Apply to fatwa practice
When a mufti encounters a case where the shafi'i has paid and taken from a buyer who was an agent, the fatwa should uphold the shafi'i's right against the principal.
The mufti must verify whether the buyer was indeed an agent, as this is a factual determination.

Recognize that the case involves an ijarah (hire) contract for laundry services. The dispute is about entitlement to wages when the worker initially denies the job.
The act: washing a garment. The ruling: wage entitlement.
2
State the binary answers given
Abu Yusuf first said 'deserves wage', then 'does not deserve'. The correct answer is conditional: it depends on timing.
Both absolute answers were wrong – a key lesson in fatwa-giving: avoid blanket statements.
3
Identify the condition and its legal effect
If the denial preceded the washing, it amounts to cancellation (faskh) of the contract, so no wage. If washing preceded denial, the contract was performed, so wage is due.
The denial is not simply a lie but a legal act affecting rights.
4
Apply the principle to similar scenarios
In Hanafi ijarah, if the worker abandons or denies the work after starting, he forfeits his wage. This case extends that: even if he later completes, the initial denial breaks the contract.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. The Seventh Art: Anecdotes and Correspondence. Praise be to Allah and peace upon His chosen servants. After this, this is the seventh art of al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, and with it its completion. It is the art of anecdotes and correspondence, a vast field. I have studied the ends of fatwa books and the Manaqib al-Kardari many times and the Tabaqat of 'Abd al-Qadir, but I have summarized in this booklet the cream of it, mostly restricting to what contains rulings. When Abu Yusuf (may Allah have mercy on him) sat to teach without informing Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him), Abu Hanifa sent a man to him, who asked him about five issues: First: A washerman denied the garment and brought it washed; does he deserve the wage or not? Abu Yusuf answered: He deserves the wage. The man said: You are wrong. He said: He does not deserve it. He said: You are wrong. Then he said to him: If the washing was before the denial, he deserves it; otherwise no. Second: Is entering into prayer by obligation or by Sunnah? He said: By obligation. He said: You are wrong. He said: By Sunnah. He said: You are wrong. Abu Yusuf was confused. The man said: By both, because the takbir is an obligation, and raising the hands is Sunnah. Third: A bird fell into a pot on the fire containing meat and broth; are they eaten or not? He said: They are eaten. He corrected him. He said: They are not eaten. He corrected him. Then he said: If the meat was cooked before the bird fell, it is washed three times and eaten, and the broth is thrown away; otherwise everything is thrown away. Fourth: A Muslim has a dhimmi wife who died while pregnant from him; in which cemetery is she buried? Abu Yusuf said: In the Muslim cemetery. He corrected him. He said: In the cemetery of the People of the Covenant. He corrected him. Abu Yusuf was confused. The man said: She is buried in the Jewish cemetery; but her face is turned away from the qibla so that the face of the child is towards the qibla, because the child in the womb faces its mother's back. Fifth: A man's umm walad married without her master's permission and then the master died; is she obligated (to inherit) from the master? He said: She is obligated. He corrected him. Then he said: She is not obligated. He corrected him. Then the man said: If the husband had consummated the marriage, she is not obligated; otherwise she is obligated. Thus Abu Yusuf realized his shortcoming and returned to Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him) and said: 'I became raisins before I became unripe grapes.' As in Ijarat al-Fayd. In Manaqib al-Kardari, the reason for his separation was that he became severely ill, and the Imam visited him and said: 'I had hoped you would be after me for the Muslims, and if you were afflicted, much knowledge would die.' When he recovered, he became self-impressed and held a dictation session. When he came, he said: 'Nothing brought you but the issue of the washerman. Subhanallah, a man who speaks about the religion of Allah and holds a session does not know Ijarah well.' Then he said: 'Whoever thinks he is independent of learning should weep for himself.' (End). And in al-Hawi al-Husayri, a weighty issue about whether the sold item is owned with the sale or after it. Abu al-Qasim al-Saffar (may Allah have mercy on him) said: A discussion took place between Sufyan and Bishr regarding contracts: when does the owner own by them—with them or after them? He said: The matter ended with Sufyan saying: 'What do you think if a glass fell and broke—did the breaking occur at the moment of hitting the ground, before it, or after it? Or if Allah created fire in cotton and it burned—did it burn with the creation, before it, or after it?' Someone other than Sufyan said—and it is the correct view according to most of our companions—that ownership in a sale occurs with it, not after it. So the sale and ownership occur together without precedence or delay, because the sale is a contract of exchange and compensation, so ownership must occur on both sides simultaneously. The same applies to all contracts like marriage, khul', and other exchange contracts. And in Manaqib al-Kardari, the Greatest Imam (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'A woman deceived me, a woman made me a faqih, and a woman made me a zahid. As for the first: I was passing by, and a woman pointed to something thrown on the road. I thought she was mute and that the thing was hers. When I lifted it to her, I said: Keep it until you give it to its owner. The second: A woman asked me about a menstruation issue, and I did not know it. She said something from which I learned fiqh. The third: I was passing a road, and a woman said: This is the one who prays Fajr with the wudu of 'Isha. So I made that my practice.'

Commentary
This passage belongs to the seventh art (section) of al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, titled 'Anecdotes and Correspondence'. The author indicates he has compiled the cream of stories from earlier works that contain legal rulings. The main narrative is about Abu Yusuf, who began teaching without informing his teacher Abu Hanifa. To test him, Abu Hanifa sent a man with five questions. In each, Abu Yusuf gave a binary answer and was corrected, then given a conditional ruling. The first question concerns a washerman who originally denied taking the garment but later returned it washed. The correct ruling distinguishes whether the denial preceded the washing or not: if the washing was done before the denial, the wage is due; if after, it is forfeited because the denial amounts to a waiver. The second question: entering prayer—the takbir (saying Allahu Akbar) is fard (obligatory), but raising the hands is sunnah; both are part of entering prayer, so the answer 'by both' is correct. The third: a bird falls into a cooking pot. If the meat was already fully cooked before the bird fell, the meat can be salvaged by washing, but the broth must be discarded due to impurity; otherwise, everything is discarded. The fourth: a Muslim's dhimmi wife dies pregnant. The child's sanctity (as a Muslim fetus) dictates that the mother be buried in a non-Muslim cemetery, but the child's face must be oriented toward the qibla by turning the mother away from it. The fifth: an umm walad (a female slave who bore her master's child) marries without the master's permission, then the master dies. If the husband consummated the marriage, she is no longer bound to the master's estate (the marriage stands, so she does not inherit from the master); otherwise, she remains his umm walad and is entitled to inheritance. After these questions, Abu Yusuf realized his deficiency and went to Abu Hanifa, expressing regret. The anecdote about the separation explains that Abu Hanifa had high hopes for Abu Yusuf but warned him against arrogance. The 'glass breaking' analogy by Sufyan illustrates that in a sale, ownership transfers simultaneously with the contract, not before or after—a key Hanafi position that ownership is an immediate consequence of the contract. Finally, the three women story from Abu Hanifa highlights lessons in honesty, seeking knowledge, and consistency in worship.
