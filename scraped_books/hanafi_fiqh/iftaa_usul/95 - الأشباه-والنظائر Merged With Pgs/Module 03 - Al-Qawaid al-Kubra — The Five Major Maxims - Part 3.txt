Woman Leaving Home: Hanafi Qa'ida and Its Applications
Qa'ida with furuat and ikhtilaf on bathhouse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qa'ida on a woman's right to leave the marital home, identifying which scenarios are permitted and which are prohibited, and evaluate the ikhtilaf on the bathhouse issue.

Core Idea
Before prompt dower is paid, she may leave; after, only for rights, duties, or essential family visits.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a concise legal rule that governs a wife's mobility within marriage. Understanding this rule and its exceptions is essential for issuing correct fatwas in family law matters, especially when balancing a wife's rights and a husband's authority. The text lists permitted scenarios and explicitly prohibits others, even with the husband's permission, highlighting the seriousness of certain acts.

Guided Path
1
State the Qa'ida

The qa'ida lists both unconditional and conditional permissions.
2
Work Through the Furuat
Examine each case: before prompt mahr (unlimited), after mahr if she has a right/duty, for midwifery/washing dead, visiting parents once a week, visiting mahrams once a year. Understand the ruling for each.
Note the limit on parental visits (once a week) and mahram visits (once a year).
3
Identify the Prohibited Cases
The text explicitly prohibits visiting non-mahrams, sick, and weddings, even with permission. Both spouses sin if she goes.

There is a difference of opinion (ikhtilaf) about going to the bathhouse. The relied-upon (mu'tamad) position allows it provided she does not adorn or perfume.

Consider a modern case: a woman wants to attend a job interview (a right/duty) or visit a non-mahram colleague. Apply the qa'ida to determine the ruling.
Ensure you distinguish between obligatory visits (e.g., for a legal claim) and voluntary ones.

Interpreting General Words and Conditional Particles in Hanafi Talaq
Maxims on interpreting 'kull', 'in', and suspension on existing attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida on 'kull' defaulting to three in ta'liq and the qawida on suspension on an existing attribute, and distinguish between continuous and non-continuous attributes.

Core Idea
Kull defaults to three when enumeration is impossible; suspension on an existing attribute refers to future existence.

This lesson explores two key maxims from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir that are essential for a mufti when interpreting conditional talaq statements. The first maxim defines how to interpret the general term 'kull' when the husband swears using it in an oath. The second maxim governs cases where talaq is suspended on an attribute already present in the wife. Understanding these rules prevents incorrect fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general word and its context
When a husband uses 'kull' in a conditional talaq statement, check whether enumerating every individual instance is possible. If not, the Hanafi rule applies that 'kull' defaults to three (the minimum plural).
This is based on the principle that the minimum plural in Arabic is three.
2
Analyze the attribute suspension rule
If the husband suspends talaq on an attribute that already exists in the wife (e.g., menstruation, sickness), the condition refers to the future occurrence of that attribute. The wife is not divorced at the moment of utterance but when the attribute recurs.
Exception: if the attribute is non-continuous like health, the suspension refers to a future change.
3
Apply the general rule on continuity
The governing rule (dabt) states: 'that which continues, its continuation has the ruling of its beginning; otherwise not.' Use this to classify attributes as continuous (e.g., menstruation is periodic) versus non-continuous (e.g., health is a state).
This distinction determines whether the condition is considered already fulfilled or pending future occurrence.
4
Recognize the default for 'in' (if)
The particle 'in' in conditional talaq implies delay (tarakhi) unless there is a contextual indication (qarina) of immediacy. Examples from the text illustrate when urgency is inferred.
Hanafi position contradicts the Shafi'i view that 'in' implies immediacy.

Excluding the Whole from the Whole is Invalid
A legal maxim with applications from various chapters of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'استثناء الكل من الكل باطل' to distinguish valid from invalid exceptions in statements of acknowledgment (iqrar) and manumission (itq).

Core Idea
An exception that negates the entire affirmation is void; an exception that clarifies a collective mention is valid.

This lesson introduces a key legal maxim from the Hanafi school: excluding the whole from the whole is invalid. While it may seem obvious, its application requires careful analysis of the structure of statements. Through examples from iqrar and itq, you will learn to spot when an exception truly negates everything and when it merely explains a part.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Begin by memorising the Arabic maxim: استثناء الكل من الكل باطل. Understand its literal meaning and its legal consequence.
2
Identify the type of statement
Determine whether the original statement mentions items collectively (جملة) or individually. Collective mention allows an exception to be interpretive; individual mention makes an exception a negation of the whole.
3
Apply the maxim to the exception
If the exception would negate the entire affirmation (e.g., exception of the very thing affirmed), it is invalid. If it merely qualifies part of a collective group, it is valid as explanation.
4
Check for the exception (istithna')
If the exception is explanatory (tafsir) of a group mentioned collectively, it is valid. Otherwise, it falls under the maxim and is void.
5
Derive the ruling for the given case
Apply the above steps to the furuat (branches) in the passage: ruling on iqrar with counterfeit dirhams, iqrar with debt, and the two itq scenarios.

Commentary
This passage presents a well-known legal maxim: 'استثناء الكل من الكل باطل' (excluding the whole from the whole is invalid). The maxim means that if someone makes a statement and then excludes everything he just affirmed, the exception is void because it negates the entire affirmation. For example, if someone says 'I owe you one hundred dirhams and a dinar except one hundred dirhams and a dinar', the exception is invalid because it contradicts the acknowledgment entirely. Similarly, if someone acknowledges receipt of ten good dirhams but immediately says 'except that they are counterfeit', the exception is invalid because it negates the quality, effectively nullifying the acknowledgment. However, an exception can be valid if it serves as an explanation or qualification. For instance, if someone says 'My two slaves are free; Salim and Buzaygh except Buzaygh', the exception clarifies that only Salim is free, because the original statement mentioned them as a group, and the exception is an interpretive clarification. In contrast, if he says 'Salim is free and Buzaygh is free except Buzaygh', each is mentioned individually, so the exception contradicts the individual statement about Buzaygh, making it invalid. This distinction highlights that the maxim applies when the exception negates the core of the statement, but not when it merely explains or qualifies a part.

Oaths Are Based on Wording, Not Purposes
States the principle that oaths are interpreted literally, not by underlying goals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'al-ayman mabniyya 'ala al-alfaz' to determine whether an oath is fulfilled or broken based on literal wording.

Core Idea
Oaths are judged by their words, not by hidden intentions.

In everyday life, we often assume that a person's intention behind an oath matters. However, Hanafi law takes a different approach for oaths: only the literal wording counts. This lesson unpacks that principle and its practical applications, showing how a simple shift in wording can change the ruling completely.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Read the opening line: الأيمان مبنية على الألفاظ لا على الأغراض. Understand that this is a universal ruling for all oaths.
Commit this to memory – it is a key qa'ida in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the scope of the maxim
The maxim applies to all oaths (ayman) in any chapter of fiqh. It does not apply to contracts, bequests, or other statements where intent may be considered.
Note the contrast with the maxim 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' which governs non-oath contexts.
3
Apply the maxim to the given examples
Work through each furu' in the text: swearing to feed with a thousand, to emancipate a slave for a thousand, swearing not to buy for ten, etc. For each, decide if the oath is fulfilled or broken based only on the words spoken.
Ignore any underlying purpose – only the literal words matter.
4
Note the exception
When the oath specifies a price limit (e.g., 'I will not buy it for ten'), buying for eleven breaks the oath because the wording explicitly implies a boundary. This is an istithna' that must be remembered.
The exception is still based on wording, not intention – it is a linguistic implication.
5
Contrast with the related maxim
Compare with 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by intentions). Understand that this maxim applies to acts of worship and contracts, but for oaths, the literal wording overrides intent.
This distinction is crucial for a mufti when issuing fatwas.

Maxim and its seven exceptions

The waqif's conditions are binding like divine law, but seven exceptions allow flexibility for shari'ah and public interest.

The Maxim of Repeated Contracts and Its Exceptions
Maxim with furuat: void repetition and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'كل عقد أُعيد وجدد فإن الثاني باطل' to distinguish between void repetitions and exceptions, and construct a fatwa for a repeated contract scenario.

Core Idea
Repeating a contract over the same subject voids the second, except where the contract type allows renewal or increased security.

A mufti often encounters cases where parties execute a second contract on the same subject without cancelling the first. The Hanafi school provides a clear maxim: the second is void. However, this rule is not absolute—certain contracts allow repetition for valid reasons. This lesson unpacks the maxim and its three key exceptions, equipping you to issue correct fatwas in these common scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract sequence
Determine whether two contracts of the same type exist on the same subject between the same parties. If the second contract is truly a repetition (not a cancelation or amendment), the maxim applies.
The maxim does not apply if the second contract is a different type or a concurrent contract.
2
Apply the default ruling
State that the second contract is void (batil) per the maxim. Cite the examples given: sulh after sulh, nikah after nikah, hawala after hawala are all void.
The voidness means the second contract has no legal effect.
3
Check for exceptions
Identify if the repeated contract is a sale (purchase), guarantee (kafala), or lease (ijara) from the original lessee. If so, the exception may apply.
For sale, the second is valid only if the price differs; for kafala, valid unconditionally; for ijara, the second cancels the first.
4
Issue the fatwa
State the ruling based on the maxim and exception, with references to the sources (Jami' al-Fusulayn, al-Qunyah, al-Talqih, al-Bazzaziyya).
Clear citation strengthens the fatwa's authority.

Ruling on sale by unauthorized person with exceptions

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A fuduli sale is suspended pending owner approval, but three defects make it instantly void.

This lesson introduces a key qa'ida from the Hanafi school concerning sales by unauthorized persons (al-fuduli). The default ruling is that such a sale is mauquf (suspended), contingent on the owner's ratification. However, the text presents three specific exceptions where the sale is batil (void) outright. Understanding this rule and its exceptions is essential for a mufti when evaluating transactions lacking proper authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
The passage states: 'Bay' al-fuduli mauquf' – the sale by an unauthorized person is suspended. This means the contract is not immediately valid; it awaits the owner's permission (ijazah).
The rule applies when the seller acts without prior authorization and without ownership.
2

The phrase 'illa fi thalath fa-batil' signals three cases where the rule does not apply. Instead of being suspended, the sale is void from inception.

If the seller includes a khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation) in favor of the true owner, the sale is void. When the fuduli acts as though he is the owner (e.g., says 'I sell this'), the sale is void. The contract shows no reference to the actual owner, so ratification becomes impossible.
Source: al-Bada'i'.
5

If a usurper (ghasib) barters one commodity belonging to the owner for another also belonging to the owner, the sale is void. This is a purely internal swap without any genuine third-party participation, rendering the transaction fundamentally flawed.

Deception Does Not Entitle Recourse: Maxim and Exceptions
Major maxim on deception not creating liability with three exceptions and furuat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-ghurur la yujibu al-ruju'' and identify its three exceptions, applying them to new legal cases.

Core Idea
Deception alone does not create liability; only when tied to a condition, commutative contract, or benefit-returning contract does recourse arise.

This lesson examines a foundational Hanafi legal maxim: mere deception (ghurur) does not give the deceived party a right to claim compensation. While this may seem harsh, the law recognizes three specific contexts where recourse is permitted. Understanding these exceptions is essential for a mufti to correctly advise in cases of misrepresentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and basic examples
The maxim 'al-ghurur la yujibu al-ruju'' is illustrated with cases of false assurances about a safe road or non-poisoned food; no guarantee arises. Also, misinformation about a woman's free status in marriage yields no recourse for the husband against the informer.
These examples show the default rule: deception alone does not create liability.

The text lists three exceptions: (1) deception by a condition in a contract, (2) deception within a commutative contract (mu'awada), and (3) deception in a contract whose benefit returns to the giver (like wadiah and ijarah).
Each exception has a distinct rationale: condition creates a contractual duty; commutative contracts involve warranty; benefit-returning contracts impose responsibility on the party enjoying the benefit.

4
Critique the earlier formulation
The author criticizes al-Zayla'i's statement that deception is limited to condition or commutative contract, calling it deficient (qasir). The third exception (benefit-returning contracts) is also recognized.
This refinement demonstrates how later Hanafi masters expand and correct earlier works.

Core Idea
A right remains valid regardless of how much time passes.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim: rights do not expire due to the mere passage of time. Unlike some other legal systems that allow 'prescription', Hanafi fiqh maintains that claims for qadhf, qisas, li'an, and debts remain enforceable indefinitely. Understanding this maxim is crucial for muftis when dealing with delayed claims.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Continuation is easier than initiation — but not in judicial integrity or slave agency.

This lesson explores a key minor maxim (sughra) from the Hanafi qawaid tradition: 'Continuation is easier than initiation.' The maxim grants leniency to maintaining a valid legal status once established. However, the source text highlights two important exceptions where continuation is actually stricter: the case of a judge who becomes fasiq, and a permitted slave who runs away. Understanding these exceptions sharpens your ability to apply the maxim correctly in fatwa.

Maxim: Whoever Seeks to Undermine His Own Action is Repelled
A legal maxim with its exceptions and additional applications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'Whoever seeks to undo what has been completed from his side is repelled' to transactional cases and identify the exceptions where the claim is accepted.

Core Idea
A person cannot validly contradict his own prior action, except when revealing a pre-existing right or protecting a vulnerable party.

This maxim is a key Hanafi principle in transactions, preventing a party from undermining a completed deal. However, justice requires exceptions when the later claim reveals a prior right unknown at the time of the transaction or when a guardian has sold at a gross undervalue. In this lesson, we explore the maxim, its two original exceptions, and several additional cases added by later Hanafi scholars.

Guided Path

State the maxim and its rationale

The maxim is a sughra (minor) legal principle.
2

The text lists two exceptions: (1) buyer of a slave later claims prior sale to an absent person with evidence; (2) donor of slave-girl later claims tadbir or istilad with evidence. In both, the claim is accepted because it reveals a right that existed before the transaction.
These exceptions are cited from Buyu' al-Khulasah and al-Bazzaziyyah.

Qadi's Discretion in Awqaf: The Maxim of Benefit
Maxim with furuat and istithna

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A qadi's actions in waqf are valid only if they serve public interest; any deviation is void.

The qadi (judge) has authority over waqf administration, but this authority is not absolute. This lesson examines a key maxim from the Hanafi tradition: the qadi's disposition in awqaf is based on benefit (maslaha). We will explore what this means in practice through several examples from classical texts.

This highlights the priority of the waqif's stipulation over the qadi's personal judgment.
3
Analyze the second furu': adjusting trustee salary
See how the successor trustee must reduce an excessive salary fixed by the qadi to the market rate. This shows the qadi's fixing must align with maslaha.
Market rate (ajr al-mithl) is the benchmark for fairness.

The qadi cannot introduce new allowances (e.g., for mosque furnishings) without the waqif's stipulation. This reinforces the binding nature of waqf conditions.

The exception is limited to cases where the qadi's order falls within his legitimate authority.

Ijarah as a Binding Contract with Exceptions
Maxim with exceptions for ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qa'ida 'al-ijarah aqd lazim' to identify cases where ijarah can be rescinded, distinguishing between rescission without excuse (istihlak al-'ayn) and with excuse (debt).

Core Idea
Ijarah is binding, but can be rescinded if the leased asset is consumed or the lessor is forced by debt.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi maxim that ijarah is a binding contract (lazim) and its exceptions. The source passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir lays out the rule and two major exceptions: consumption of the asset and the lessor's financial necessity. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for a mufti to issue correct fatwas in leasing disputes.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its general rule
Identify the qa'ida: 'al-ijarah aqd lazim' – ijarah is a binding contract that cannot be unilaterally rescinded without a valid excuse.
The word 'lazim' is key: it means binding, not revocable.
2
Identify the first exception: consumption of the asset (istihlak al-'ayn)
When the lease involves consumption of the physical asset itself (e.g., hiring paper for writing, or ink), the owner may rescind without any reason. The passage gives the example of istiktab (hiring for writing) where the paper is consumed.
This is an exception to the rule that rescission requires a reason.
3
Trace the analogy to muzara'ah
The passage traces the exception to muzara'ah (sharecropping): the owner of the seed can rescind, but the worker cannot. This analogy (qiyas) is used to derive the same ruling for ijarah when the asset is consumed.
Muzara'ah is a separate contract but shares the feature of consumption of the seed.
4
Identify the second exception: lessor's debt
If the lessor (mu'jir) has a debt and no means to pay it except from the leased property itself, he may rescind the lease in order to sell the property and satisfy the debt. However, if the advance rent received already covers the debt, rescission is not allowed.
This is an excuse-based exception: the lessor's need.
5
Synthesize the operative rule for fatwa
The mufta bihi is that ijarah is binding but with two recognized exceptions: (1) rescission without reason when the leased asset is consumed, and (2) rescission with excuse of debt when the lessor cannot pay otherwise. These exceptions are based on the maxim and its furuat.
Always verify whether the asset is consumed or whether the lessor's debt meets the condition.

A trustee may not transfer trust property except where the owner's permission is implied.

The general rule is that a depositary, borrower, or pawn holder cannot further transfer the property for deposit, loan, rental, or pawn. Shuf‘a is a sale in all rulings except the guarantee of risk – a forced acquisition.

Maxim: Known Not Delayed for Unknown
A legal maxim applied to pre-emption and injury

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-ma'lum la yu'akhkhar li al-mawhum' to new furuat and identify its scope in jinayat and shuf'a.

Core Idea
A known right is executed immediately — it is not postponed due to an uncertain future claim.

This lesson introduces a key legal maxim of the Hanafi school: al-ma'lum la yu'akhkhar li al-mawhum (what is known is not delayed for what is unknown). The maxim governs situations where multiple claimants exist but only some are present. The principle ensures that the certain rights of the present claimant are not held back on account of the absent, whose right remains conjectural. We will examine two classic applications from jinayat and shuf'a.

Commentary
This is a legal maxim stating that the mutawallī (administrator) of a waqf (endowment) holds the same legal status and powers as a waṣī (executor of a will). The comparison implies that the mutawallī can act within the scope of the waqf conditions, similar to how an executor acts within the will. The source cited is Jāmi‘ al-Fuṣūlayn, a Hanafi jurisprudential work.

Key Terms
المتولي (al-mutawallī): The administrator or superintendent of a waqf (endowment).
الوصي (al-waṣī): The executor of a will, appointed to carry out the testator's instructions.
