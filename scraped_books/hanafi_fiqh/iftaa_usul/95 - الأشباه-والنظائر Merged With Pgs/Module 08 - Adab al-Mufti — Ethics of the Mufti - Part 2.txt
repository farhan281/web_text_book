Entering Prayer: Fard and Sunnah Combined
Second question: entering prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain that entering prayer involves both a fard (takbir) and a sunnah (raising hands), and state the proper fatwa about the nature of entering.

Core Idea
Entering prayer is both fard and sunnah – takbir is fard, raising hands is sunnah.

The second test question asked: 'Is entering into prayer by obligation or by Sunnah?' Abu Yusuf's binary answers were corrected by the man who said 'by both'. This lesson clarifies the distinction between the obligatory and recommended parts of the opening of prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act is 'dakhul fi al-salah' – entering into the state of prayer.
2
Break down the components
Mention two actions: saying Allahu Akbar (takbir) and raising the hands (raf' al-yadayn).
Takbir is part of the rukn of opening, raising hands is a sunnah.
3
Classify each component
Takbir is fard (obligatory) because it is the opening essential; raising hands is sunnah mu'akkadah.
Hanafi: raising hands is sunnah, not wajib.
4
Derive the ruling
Therefore, 'entering prayer' is both: the act includes a fard element (takbir) and a sunnah element (raising hands). So the correct answer is 'by both'.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. The Seventh Art: Anecdotes and Correspondence. Praise be to Allah and peace upon His chosen servants. After this, this is the seventh art of al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, and with it its completion. It is the art of anecdotes and correspondence, a vast field. I have studied the ends of fatwa books and the Manaqib al-Kardari many times and the Tabaqat of 'Abd al-Qadir, but I have summarized in this booklet the cream of it, mostly restricting to what contains rulings. When Abu Yusuf (may Allah have mercy on him) sat to teach without informing Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him), Abu Hanifa sent a man to him, who asked him about five issues: First: A washerman denied the garment and brought it washed; does he deserve the wage or not? Abu Yusuf answered: He deserves the wage. The man said: You are wrong. He said: He does not deserve it. He said: You are wrong. Then he said to him: If the washing was before the denial, he deserves it; otherwise no. Second: Is entering into prayer by obligation or by Sunnah? He said: By obligation. He said: You are wrong. He said: By Sunnah. He said: You are wrong. Abu Yusuf was confused. The man said: By both, because the takbir is an obligation, and raising the hands is Sunnah. Third: A bird fell into a pot on the fire containing meat and broth; are they eaten or not? He said: They are eaten. He corrected him. He said: They are not eaten. He corrected him. Then he said: If the meat was cooked before the bird fell, it is washed three times and eaten, and the broth is thrown away; otherwise everything is thrown away. Fourth: A Muslim has a dhimmi wife who died while pregnant from him; in which cemetery is she buried? Abu Yusuf said: In the Muslim cemetery. He corrected him. He said: In the cemetery of the People of the Covenant. He corrected him. Abu Yusuf was confused. The man said: She is buried in the Jewish cemetery; but her face is turned away from the qibla so that the face of the child is towards the qibla, because the child in the womb faces its mother's back. Fifth: A man's umm walad married without her master's permission and then the master died; is she obligated (to inherit) from the master? He said: She is obligated. He corrected him. Then he said: She is not obligated. He corrected him. Then the man said: If the husband had consummated the marriage, she is not obligated; otherwise she is obligated. Thus Abu Yusuf realized his shortcoming and returned to Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him) and said: 'I became raisins before I became unripe grapes.' As in Ijarat al-Fayd. In Manaqib al-Kardari, the reason for his separation was that he became severely ill, and the Imam visited him and said: 'I had hoped you would be after me for the Muslims, and if you were afflicted, much knowledge would die.' When he recovered, he became self-impressed and held a dictation session. When he came, he said: 'Nothing brought you but the issue of the washerman. Subhanallah, a man who speaks about the religion of Allah and holds a session does not know Ijarah well.' Then he said: 'Whoever thinks he is independent of learning should weep for himself.' (End). And in al-Hawi al-Husayri, a weighty issue about whether the sold item is owned with the sale or after it. Abu al-Qasim al-Saffar (may Allah have mercy on him) said: A discussion took place between Sufyan and Bishr regarding contracts: when does the owner own by them—with them or after them? He said: The matter ended with Sufyan saying: 'What do you think if a glass fell and broke—did the breaking occur at the moment of hitting the ground, before it, or after it? Or if Allah created fire in cotton and it burned—did it burn with the creation, before it, or after it?' Someone other than Sufyan said—and it is the correct view according to most of our companions—that ownership in a sale occurs with it, not after it. So the sale and ownership occur together without precedence or delay, because the sale is a contract of exchange and compensation, so ownership must occur on both sides simultaneously. The same applies to all contracts like marriage, khul', and other exchange contracts. And in Manaqib al-Kardari, the Greatest Imam (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'A woman deceived me, a woman made me a faqih, and a woman made me a zahid. As for the first: I was passing by, and a woman pointed to something thrown on the road. I thought she was mute and that the thing was hers. When I lifted it to her, I said: Keep it until you give it to its owner. The second: A woman asked me about a menstruation issue, and I did not know it. She said something from which I learned fiqh. The third: I was passing a road, and a woman said: This is the one who prays Fajr with the wudu of 'Isha. So I made that my practice.'

Commentary
This passage belongs to the seventh art (section) of al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, titled 'Anecdotes and Correspondence'. The author indicates he has compiled the cream of stories from earlier works that contain legal rulings. The main narrative is about Abu Yusuf, who began teaching without informing his teacher Abu Hanifa. To test him, Abu Hanifa sent a man with five questions. In each, Abu Yusuf gave a binary answer and was corrected, then given a conditional ruling. The first question concerns a washerman who originally denied taking the garment but later returned it washed. The correct ruling distinguishes whether the denial preceded the washing or not: if the washing was done before the denial, the wage is due; if after, it is forfeited because the denial amounts to a waiver. The second question: entering prayer—the takbir (saying Allahu Akbar) is fard (obligatory), but raising the hands is sunnah; both are part of entering prayer, so the answer 'by both' is correct. The third: a bird falls into a cooking pot. If the meat was already fully cooked before the bird fell, the meat can be salvaged by washing, but the broth must be discarded due to impurity; otherwise, everything is discarded. The fourth: a Muslim's dhimmi wife dies pregnant. The child's sanctity (as a Muslim fetus) dictates that the mother be buried in a non-Muslim cemetery, but the child's face must be oriented toward the qibla by turning the mother away from it. The fifth: an umm walad (a female slave who bore her master's child) marries without the master's permission, then the master dies. If the husband consummated the marriage, she is no longer bound to the master's estate (the marriage stands, so she does not inherit from the master); otherwise, she remains his umm walad and is entitled to inheritance. After these questions, Abu Yusuf realized his deficiency and went to Abu Hanifa, expressing regret. The anecdote about the separation explains that Abu Hanifa had high hopes for Abu Yusuf but warned him against arrogance. The 'glass breaking' analogy by Sufyan illustrates that in a sale, ownership transfers simultaneously with the contract, not before or after—a key Hanafi position that ownership is an immediate consequence of the contract. Finally, the three women story from Abu Hanifa highlights lessons in honesty, seeking knowledge, and consistency in worship.

Key Terms
القصارة (al-qisara): The work of a washerman who launders clothes; a contract of hire (ijarah) in which the wage is earned upon completion.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has given birth to a child recognized as the master's offspring; she becomes free upon the master's death and is entitled to inheritance.
المبيع (al-mabi'): The sold item; ownership of the sold item transfers to the buyer at the moment of the contract, not after, according to the preferred Hanafi view.

Impure Bird in the Pot: Salvaging Food from Najasah
Third question: bird in pot.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on how to handle a pot of meat and broth when an impure bird falls into it, depending on whether the meat was cooked before the bird fell.

Core Idea
If the meat was already cooked, it can be washed and eaten; otherwise all is discarded.

The third test question presents a scenario where a bird (presumably impure) falls into a cooking pot. The answer depends on whether the meat was cooked before the bird fell. This lesson unpacks the principle of salvaging food from contact with impurity (najasah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the impurity
The bird is considered najis (impure) because it fell into the pot and died.
Unless the bird was slaughtered properly, it is carrion (maytah).
2
State the two scenarios
Scenario 1: The meat was already cooked before the bird fell. Scenario 2: The meat was not cooked or partially cooked.
Cooking here means the meat reached a state of readiness as food.
3
Apply the ruling for Scenario 1
If meat was cooked, it can be washed three times and eaten. The broth must be discarded because it absorbed impurity.
The meat is solid and can be cleaned; broth is liquid and cannot be fully purified.
4
Apply the ruling for Scenario 2
If meat was not cooked, everything (meat and broth) is thrown away because the impurity mixed throughout.

Burial of a Pregnant Dhimmi Wife: Orienting the Fetus to Qibla
Fourth question: burial of pregnant dhimmi wife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on burying a non-Muslim pregnant woman who dies carrying a Muslim fetus: she is buried in her community's cemetery but with the fetus facing Qibla.

Core Idea
The Muslim fetus must face Qibla, even if placed in a non-Muslim grave.

The fourth question involves a Muslim man whose dhimmi wife dies pregnant. Where should she be buried? The answer requires balancing the rights of the mother (dhimmi) and the fetus (Muslim). The fetus takes precedence in matters of Islamic sanctity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The mother is a dhimmi (protected non-Muslim), the fetus is Muslim (since the father is Muslim).
The child inherits Islam from the father.
2
Determine the burial place
The mother is not Muslim, so she cannot be buried in a Muslim cemetery. She is buried in the Jewish/Christian cemetery (her own).
But the fetus has a right to be buried facing Qibla.
3
Resolve the conflict
The mother is turned away from Qibla so that the fetus inside her faces Qibla. The fetus is in the womb facing the mother's back.
Thus the mother's back is toward Qibla, the fetus's face toward Qibla.
4
Legal principle
The sanctity and rights of the Muslim fetus override the mother's burial orientation.

Ownership Transfer in Sale: Simultaneous with Contract (al-Milk Ma'a al-Aqd)
Discussion on timing of ownership in sales.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain and apply the Hanafi position that ownership in a sale transfers simultaneously with the contract, using the glass analogy.

Core Idea
In a sale, ownership passes at the moment of contract, not after.

The later part of the passage discusses a disagreement between Sufyan and Bishr about when ownership transfers in a contract. Sufyan used the analogy of a glass breaking – at the moment of impact – to illustrate that ownership occurs simultaneously with the contract. This is the relied-upon Hanafi view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question
Does ownership in a sale transfer at the moment of the contract (with it) or after the contract?
This is an usul al-fiqh question about the nature of contracts.
2
Understand the glass analogy
Sufyan asks: when a glass falls and breaks, does the breaking occur at the exact moment it hits the ground, before, or after? The answer is 'at the moment of hitting'. Analogously, ownership transfers at the moment of the contract.
The analogy demonstrates simultaneity.
3
State the preferred Hanafi view
Most Hanafi scholars hold that ownership (al-milk) occurs with the contract (ma'a al-'aqd), not after it. The sale and ownership are simultaneous.
This is opposed to the view that ownership occurs after the contract (a position attributed to some others).
4
Apply to other contracts
The same principle applies to marriage, khul', and other exchange contracts.

Commentary
This passage belongs to the seventh art (section) of al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, titled 'Anecdotes and Correspondence'. The author indicates he has compiled the cream of stories from earlier works that contain legal rulings. The main narrative is about Abu Yusuf, who began teaching without informing his teacher Abu Hanifa. To test him, Abu Hanifa sent a man with five questions. In each, Abu Yusuf gave a binary answer and was corrected, then given a conditional ruling. The first question concerns a washerman who originally denied taking the garment but later returned it washed. The correct ruling distinguishes whether the denial preceded the washing or not: if the washing was done before the denial, the wage is due; if after, it is forfeited because the denial amounts to a waiver. The second question: entering prayer—the takbir (saying Allahu Akbar) is fard (obligatory), but raising the hands is sunnah; both are part of entering prayer, so the answer 'by both' is correct. The third: a bird falls into a cooking pot. If the meat was already fully cooked before the bird fell, the meat can be salvaged by washing, but the broth must be discarded due to impurity; otherwise, everything is discarded. The fourth: a Muslim's dhimmi wife dies pregnant. The child's sanctity (as a Muslim fetus) dictates that the mother be buried in a non-Muslim cemetery, but the child's face must be oriented toward the qibla by turning the mother away from it. The fifth: an umm walad (a female slave who bore her master's child) marries without the master's permission, then the master dies. If the husband consummated the marriage, she is no longer bound to the master's estate (the marriage stands, so she does not inherit from the master); otherwise, she remains his umm walad and is entitled to inheritance. After these questions, Abu Yusuf realized his deficiency and went to Abu Hanifa, expressing regret. The anecdote about the separation explains that Abu Hanifa had high hopes for Abu Yusuf but warned him against arrogance. The 'glass breaking' analogy by Sufyan illustrates that in a sale, ownership transfers simultaneously with the contract, not before or after—a key Hanafi position that ownership is an immediate consequence of the contract. Finally, the three women story from Abu Hanifa highlights lessons in honesty, seeking knowledge, and consistency in worship.

المبيع (al-mabi'): The sold item; ownership of the sold item transfers to the buyer at the moment of the contract, not after, according to the preferred Hanafi view.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive ethical guide for a mufti or scholar, covering interactions with rulers, common people, family, and students. The author begins by advising caution in approaching the sultan: frequent visits diminish one's status; one should be like fire—benefiting from a distance. Speaking too much in the ruler's presence is dangerous, as he may twist words to appear superior. The mufti must know his own worth and that of others, and avoid entering when an unknown scholar is present, to prevent awkward comparisons. Accepting judicial positions requires assurance that the ruler respects the mufti's madhhab. The mufti should keep distance from the ruler's retinue to preserve his own prestige. In public, he should speak only when asked, avoid laughing, markets, and adolescents. He must respect elders and children per the hadith. He should avoid eating in markets and mosques, and wearing silk or ornaments. At home, he should limit intimate talk, avoid discussing other women, and be cautious about marriage: avoid women with close relatives who might interfere, or those with children from previous marriage who may favor them. The ideal order is: seek knowledge first, then earn lawful wealth, then marry. Associating with people should involve teaching; arguing with commoners is undignified. The mufti must never abandon knowledge even in poverty, treat students like sons, answer only the question asked, and uphold truth fearlessly. His worship should exceed that of ordinary people to maintain credibility.

Priorities for a Mufti: Knowledge, Wealth, Marriage
Advice on life priorities for a mufti.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct order of priorities for a mufti according to Hanafi adab and explain why knowledge precedes wealth and marriage.

Core Idea
Seek knowledge first, then lawful wealth, then marry—reversing this order wastes time and distracts from learning.

The path to becoming a reliable mufti begins with personal discipline. This passage from the Hanafi tradition gives clear, practical guidance on the order in which a scholar should pursue knowledge, wealth, and marriage. The author warns that seeking wealth or marrying before mastering knowledge leads to worldly distractions and the loss of precious learning time.

Guided Path

Identify the three priorities

The phrase 'اطلب العلم أولاً ثم اجمع المال من الحلال. ثم تزوج' is the key.
2
Understand the reasoning
Why must knowledge come first? The author explains that seeking wealth during learning time makes you unable to seek knowledge; wealth then tempts you to buy slaves and engage in worldly affairs, and early marriage brings children and dependents that force you to abandon knowledge.
This is a practical caution based on human psychology and social realities.
3
Apply the priority order to a hypothetical scenario
Imagine a young man who marries and starts a business before studying fiqh. According to this passage, what is likely to happen? He will become occupied with family and wealth and fail to acquire deep knowledge.
The principle is not a hard rule but a strong recommendation (sunnah) for those aspiring to be muftis.
4
Note the exception
The qawida panel in this lesson notes an exception: if one is already married before seeking knowledge, he may continue his marriage obligations while studying. The priority is for those who are not yet married.
This shows the Hanafi flexibility—obligations already incurred are not neglected.

The Mufti's Personal Integrity and Fearless Truth
Ethical guidelines for a mufti's personal conduct and courage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ethical principles governing a mufti's personal conduct, including fearlessness before rulers, consistency between private and public piety, and the proper way to criticize religious corruption.

And when you walk on the road, do not turn right or left, but keep looking at the ground. And when you enter the bathhouse, do not make yourself equal to people in the bathhouse fee and the sitting place, but give more than what you give the common people to show your chivalry among them so they magnify you. And do not hand over belongings to the weaver and other craftsmen, but take for yourself a trustworthy person to do that. And do not haggle over grains and small coins, and do not weigh dirhams but rely on someone else. And consider the humiliated world as insignificant in the eyes of the people of knowledge; for what is with Allah is better than it. And delegate your affairs to someone else so that you can devote yourself to knowledge, for that preserves your need. And beware of speaking to the insane and those from the people of knowledge who do not know debate and proof, and those who seek status and immerse themselves in mentioning issues among people; they seek to disgrace you and do not care about you, even if they recognize you as on the truth. And when you enter upon great people, do not raise yourself above them unless they raise you, lest harm befall you. And when you are among people, do not go ahead of them in prayer unless they appoint you as a form of respect from them. And do not enter the bathhouse at midday and morning, and do not go out to spectacles, and do not attend the injustices of rulers; except if you know that if you say something, they will act according to your statement with truth. For if they do what is not permissible while you are with them, perhaps you cannot prevent them, and people will think that it is right because of your silence among them at the time of undertaking it. And beware of anger in a study circle. And do not preach to the common people; for the preacher inevitably lies. And if you wish to establish a circle for one of the people of knowledge, if it is a fiqh circle, attend yourself and mention in it what you know; otherwise, do not, lest people be deluded by your presence and think that he has a degree of knowledge when he does not. And if he is fit for giving fatwa, mention that; otherwise, do not. And do not sit for someone else to teach in your presence, but leave with him some of your companions to inform you of the quality of his speech and the quantity of his knowledge. And do not attend gatherings of dhikr or one who uses your status and your endorsement for a wa'z gathering; rather, direct the people of your neighborhood and your common people whom you trust along with one of your companions. And delegate the matter of marriages to the khatib of your area, and likewise the funeral prayer and the two 'Id prayers. And do not forget me in your righteous supplication, and accept this admonition from me; I advise you only for your benefit and the benefit of the Muslims. (End).

Commentary
This passage continues the ethical guidance for a mufti, focusing on practical daily conduct. The advice begins with walking modestly, not looking around, and entering the bathhouse with generosity by paying more than the common people to maintain prestige. The mufti should avoid haggling over small amounts and weighing coins himself, delegating such tasks to others to preserve dignity. He should consider worldly goods as insignificant, and delegate personal affairs to focus on knowledge. He must avoid speaking to insane people, incompetent debaters, and those who seek status through excessive discussion, as they aim to humiliate him. When meeting dignitaries, he should not elevate himself unless they do so, and should not lead prayer unless appointed. He should avoid the bathhouse at certain times, spectacles, and attending rulers' unjust acts unless he can effectively intervene. Anger in study circles is prohibited, and he should not preach to the public as preachers often lie. When establishing a study circle for another scholar, he should attend if it is figh, but otherwise not, to avoid misleading people. If the scholar is fit for fatwa, he should mention it; otherwise not. He should not let others teach in his presence but send a companion to evaluate them. He should avoid using his status to promote dhikr gatherings. He should delegate marriage and funeral prayers to the local imam. The passage concludes with a request for prayers and acceptance of the admonition.

Key Terms

مظالم السلاطين (mazalim al-salatin): Unjust acts or court sessions of rulers where rights are violated
الفتوى (al-fatwa): Legal responsa; the mufti’s issuance of non-binding legal opinions
