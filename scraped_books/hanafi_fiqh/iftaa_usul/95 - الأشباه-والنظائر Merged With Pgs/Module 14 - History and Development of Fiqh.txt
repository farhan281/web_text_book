Repeated Acts Require Multiple Sacrifices Unless Same Assembly
Ruling on repeated violations of Hajj requiring multiple blood sacrifices.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for repeated violations during Hajj to determine when multiple blood sacrifices are required.

Core Idea
Each repeated violation requires its own sacrifice, unless they occur in one continuous session.

This lesson presents a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim governing multiple violations during Hajj. The rule distinguishes between separate instances of violations occurring in different sessions and those occurring in a single continuous session. Understanding this distinction is essential for a mufti to correctly issue rulings on kaffarah obligations.

Guided Path
1
State the General Principle
The rule begins with 'جامع مرارًا لكل مرة دم': a person who commits a violation repeatedly must offer a sacrifice for each instance.
The term 'مرارًا' indicates repetition of the same type of violation.
2
Recognize the Exception
The exception is introduced by 'إلا أن يكون في مجلس واحد': if all violations occur in a single continuous session, only one sacrifice is required.
Continuity of time and place is key.
3
Apply the Distinction
Determine whether the acts were in one majlis or separate majalis. The key factor is continuity of time and place.
4
Memorize the Anchor
Commit the Arabic phrasing to memory as it is a precise legal rule.
Use repetition and practice.

Source Text

جامع مرارًا لكل مرة دم إلا أن يكون في مجلس واحد فيكفيه دم واحد.
One who repeatedly (commits a violation) each time requires a blood sacrifice, unless it is in one session, then one sacrifice suffices him.

Commentary
This is a legal rule regarding violations during Hajj that require a dam (sacrifice). If a person repeats the same violation multiple times (e.g., multiple acts of shaving before tahallul), a separate sacrifice is due for each instance. However, if all instances occur in a single continuous session (majlis wahid), only one sacrifice is required. This is based on the principle that repetitions in one gathering are considered a single act for the purpose of kaffarah.

Key Terms
جامع (jami'): One who gathers many violations or acts of disobedience during Hajj.
مجلس واحد (majlis wahid): A single sitting or session where the acts occur without significant interruption.

Three Types of Permissible Sacrificial Animals (Hady)
Listing three permissible hady types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three types of hady whose meat may be eaten by the offerer in the Hanafi school and distinguish them from other categories such as dam and vow offerings.

Core Idea
Only hady al-tamattu', hady al-qiran, and hady al-tatawwu' may be eaten by the pilgrim.

This brief rule from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir encapsulates a key practical distinction in Hanafi fiqh: not all sacrificial animals slaughtered during Hajj may be consumed by the offerer. Only three categories—those linked to tamattu', qiran, and voluntary devotion—allow consumption. This ruling directly affects a mufti's guidance to pilgrims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Read the passage and note that it states a limitation: 'Among sacrificial animals, none are eaten except three.' This is a restrictive rule (hasr).
2
List the three categories
The three permitted types are: (1) hady al-tamattu', (2) hady al-qiran, and (3) hady al-tatawwu'. Memorize these in Arabic and English.
3
Understand the common thread
All three are non-expiatory sacrifices. They are either wajib due to the type of Hajj (tamattu' or qiran) or voluntary. They are not penalties (dam) or vows.
Expiatory offerings (dam, kaffara) must be given in charity and cannot be eaten by the offerer.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given a case, determine whether the hady falls into one of these three categories. If yes, the pilgrim may eat; if no, it must be given entirely as charity.
This rule is a concrete example of the Hanafi principle that certain rituals are designed for the performer's benefit and consumption.

Source Text

الثانية . من لا يؤكل من الهدايا إلا ثلاثة هدي المتعة والقرآن والتطوع.
The second: Among sacrificial animals, none are eaten except three: the sacrifice of tamattu', qiran, and voluntary (tatawwu').
Source pages p. 145

Commentary
This passage states a ruling regarding the consumption of meat from sacrificial animals (hady) in Hajj. According to the Hanafi school, the meat of three types of sacrifices may be eaten: 1) Hady al-tamattu' (the sacrifice offered by a pilgrim performing tamattu'), 2) Hady al-qiran (offered by a pilgrim performing qiran), and 3) Hady al-tatawwu' (a voluntary sacrifice). Other types of hady, such as those for violations (dam) or for vows, may not be eaten by the offerer; their meat must be given as charity.

Key Terms
هدي المتعة (hady al-tamattu'): The sacrifice offered by a pilgrim performing tamattu' (combining umrah and hajj with a break).
هدي القرآن (hady al-qiran): The sacrifice offered by a pilgrim performing qiran (combining umrah and hajj with a single ihram).
هدي التطوع (hady al-tatawwu'): A voluntary sacrifice not compulsory.

Endowment for Public Utilities: Spending on Imams and Supplies
Presents the rule on how waqf revenue for public utilities is spent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the allocation of revenue from a waqf for public utilities and identify the specified beneficiaries and items.

Core Idea
Waqf revenue for public utilities is strictly allocated to the imam, khatib, caretaker, and supplies.

This lesson covers the first ruling from the Book of Waqf, which specifies how the income of a waqf dedicated to public utilities (such as a mosque) is to be spent. The text, quoting Ibn Wahban, enumerates the recipients and items, providing a clear guideline for the mutawalli.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the ruling
Recognize that the ruling concerns a waqf made 'for public utilities' (على المصالح), which typically refers to mosques and similar community facilities.
The term 'masalih' implies general benefit.
2
List the designated recipients and items
The revenue is allocated to the imam (prayer leader), khatib (preacher), and qayyim (caretaker), and for purchasing oil, mats, and fans.
These are essential for the functioning of a mosque.
3
Note the source of authority
The ruling is attributed to the versified work of Ibn Wahban, indicating it is a recognized Hanafi position from later sources.

كتاب الوقف
لو وقف على المصالح فهي للإمام والخطيب والقيم وشراء الدهن والحصر والمراوح. كذا في منظومة ابن وهبان .

Book of Waqf: If he endows (waqf) for public utilities, then it (the revenue) is for the imam, the khatib, and the caretaker, and for purchasing oil, mats, and fans. Thus in the versified work of Ibn Wahban. Whoever builds on another's land with his permission, the building belongs to the owner of the land; and if he builds for himself without his permission, it is his (the builder's), and he has the right to remove it unless it harms the land. As for building on waqf land: if the builder is the mutawalli (administrator) over it, then if he builds with waqf money, it is waqf; and if he builds from his own money with the intention (niyya) of the waqf or without specification (mutlaq), it is waqf; and if he builds for himself, it is his. And if he is not the mutawalli, then if the mutawalli authorized him to recover his expenditure (i.e., to reimburse himself), then it is waqf; otherwise, if he builds for the waqf, it is waqf; if for himself or without specification, he has the right to remove it as long as it does not harm (the land); if it harms, then he is the one who has wasted his wealth, so he should wait until it is salvaged. In some books: the nazir (administrator) may acquire it (the building) at the lower of the two values—with removal and without removal—with waqf funds.

Commentary
This passage begins the Book of Waqf with two main rulings. First, endowing for public utilities like a mosque's needs: the revenue is spent on the imam, khatib, caretaker, and supplies. This is quoted from Ibn Wahban's work. Second, the longer ruling covers building on another's land and specifically waqf land. The general principle: building with permission makes it the landowner's; building without permission allows the builder to remove it unless it damages the land. For waqf land, the ruling depends on whether the builder is the mutawalli or not, and on his intention. A mutawalli building with waqf funds or with his own money intending waqf makes it waqf; if he builds for himself, it's his. A non-mutawalli building with authorization for reimbursement makes it waqf; otherwise, if for waqf, it's waqf; if for himself or unspecified, he may remove it unless it harms; if it harms, he must wait. Some books give the nazir the option to purchase the building at the lower of two values using waqf funds.

متولي (mutawalli): The administrator or trustee of the waqf.
ناظر (nazir): Supervisor or overseer of the waqf.

Building on Waqf Land: Ownership and Removal According to Hanafi Fiqh
Lays out the detailed rulings for building on waqf land differentiated by builder status and intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the ownership and removal rights of a building erected on waqf land by applying the Hanafi rules based on the builder's status, authorization, and intention.

Core Idea
Ownership of a building on waqf land depends on the builder's role (mutawalli or not) and his intention, not merely on the land being waqf.

This lesson delves into the detailed Hanafi ruling on constructing buildings on waqf land, as presented in the source. The general principle for building on another's land is modified for waqf, introducing conditions about the builder's capacity, authorization, and purpose. Understanding this helps mutawallis and nazirs handle disputes over improvements on waqf property.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule for building on another's land
On non-waqf land, if the owner permits building, the building belongs to the landowner. Without permission, the builder owns it but must remove it if no harm to the land.
This is the baseline for comparison.
2
Identify the builder on waqf land: mutawalli vs. non-mutawalli
The ruling distinguishes whether the builder is the mutawalli (administrator) or an outsider. This is the primary categorization.
3
Apply the sub-rules for the mutawalli
If the mutawalli builds with waqf funds → building is waqf. If from his own money with waqf intention (niyya) or unspecified → also waqf. If building for himself → it belongs to him.
Intention is key for the mutawalli.
4
Apply the sub-rules for a non-mutawalli
If authorized by the mutawalli to recover costs → building is waqf. If he builds for the waqf (without authorization) → it is waqf. If for himself or unspecified → he owns it but must remove it if no harm to the land; if removal causes harm, he must wait until it is salvageable.
Authorization can be implied by permission to recoup costs.
5
Note the nazir's alternative option
Some books give the nazir the right to purchase the building at the lower of two values (with or without removal) using waqf funds.
This is a practical solution to avoid harm.

Effect of Nazir's Death on Waqf Leases
Ruling on lease continuation after nazir's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule that a waqf lease is not rescinded by the nazir's death, and identify the exception when the nazir is the sole beneficiary.

Core Idea
A waqf lease survives the nazir's death unless the nazir was the sole beneficiary and all revenue was his.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi fiqh of waqf: what happens to a lease when the nazir (administrator) dies? The general principle is continuity, but an exception exists when the nazir is the beneficiary. We will parse the rule and its evidential basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Read the opening clause: 'If the nazir leases and then dies, the lease is not rescinded.' This is the default ruling.
2
Identify the exception
Look for the word 'except' (illa). The exception occurs when the nazir is the beneficiary (al-mawquf 'alayh) and all revenue (ray') is for him.
In that case, the lease is rescinded upon his death.
3
Note the source of disagreement
Ibn Wahban confirmed this exception from several books, but the general wording of the mutun (standard texts) contradicts him.
This indicates a difference of opinion within the school.
4
Determine the operative ruling for fatwa
Given the contradiction, what is the mufta bihi? Later Hanafi jurists may have settled this. We'll explore in the Fatwa Hierarchy.
Typically, the general rule (lease continues) is relied upon, but the exception is also recognized.

Borrowing on Waqf: Conditions and Scope
Ruling on borrowing for waqf and special case of buying above value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling on borrowing for waqf, list the two conditions for its permissibility, and explain the special permission for buying goods above market value.

Core Idea
Borrowing on waqf is generally forbidden; it becomes permissible only for urgent repairs with judicial permission and if rental income cannot cover costs.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on borrowing (istidana) on behalf of a waqf. While the default is prohibition, the law recognizes necessities such as repairs. Two strict conditions must be met. Additionally, a unique ruling allows the mutawalli to buy goods at a premium and sell them for the waqf's benefit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The passage begins: 'Borrowing against the waqf is not permissible' — this is the default prohibition.
Borrowing includes loans and credit purchases (istidana).
2
Identify the exception and its conditions
The exception is for the benefit of the waqf, like repairs and buying seeds. Two conditions: (1) permission of the judge, (2) inability to lease the property and use the rent.
Spending on beneficiaries is not a valid reason for borrowing under necessity.
3
Note the special ruling on above-market purchases
The mutawalli may buy goods for more than their value, sell them, use proceeds for repairs, and the profit goes to the waqf. This is permissible.
This seems counterintuitive but is allowed as a practical measure.
4
Summarize the legal implications
The rulings show that waqf assets are protected; borrowing is only a last resort. The special purchase ruling facilitates maintenance without violating the prohibition of interest or injustice.
The profit from resale belongs to the waqf, not the mutawalli personally.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Replacing a productive waqf is generally prohibited but allowed in four specific exceptions.

This lesson examines the first part of the passage, which addresses when a productive waqf (al-waqf al-'amir) may be replaced (istibdal). The general rule is prohibition, but four exceptional cases are listed, including stipulation by the founder, destruction by usurpation, lack of evidence of usurpation, and a beneficial exchange per Abu Yusuf. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for muftis dealing with waqf management.

1

2

The founder's stipulation overrides the default prohibition because the waqif's condition is binding in Hanafi law.

The 'Aqila Does Not Bear Responsibility for Intentional Killing

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The 'aqila's liability is limited to accidental killing, except when some heirs forgive.

In Hanafi law, the 'aqila (paternal relatives) are responsible for paying diyah (blood money) in cases of accidental or quasi-intentional killing. However, if the killing is intentional ('amd), the killer himself must pay, not his 'aqila. Yet a unique exception is recorded: when some of the legal heirs (awliya' al-dam) forgive or accept a settlement, the share of the remaining heirs becomes a property debt that falls on the 'aqila. This lesson explores this qa'ida and its furu'.

Guided Path
1

Identify the overarching rule: al-'aqila la ta'qil al-'amd. The 'aqila does not pay diyah for intentional killing.
2
Recognize the exceptional scenario
The exception: when some awliya' forgive or settle, the remaining share becomes mal and is borne by al-'aqila.
This is from Sharh al-Majma'.
3
Understand the legal transformation
Forgiveness or settlement converts the obligation from a personal right (qisas or diyah) into a property debt, which triggers 'aqila liability.
4
Apply the qa'ida to related cases
Consider if all heirs forgive: then no diyah remains, 'aqila bears nothing. If all heirs demand full diyah, the killer pays personally, not 'aqila.

Learning Objective

A child before puberty bears no religious obligation; even intentional acts are treated as mistakes.

Fiqh distinguishes life stages with distinct legal consequences. This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir lays out the Hanafi terminology for human development—from fetus to old age—and then anchors a crucial ruling: a child (sabi) is not subject to taklif (religious obligation) for either commands or prohibitions. Rulings of slaves: No Friday prayer for him, nor Eid, nor Takbir al-Tashriq, nor Adhan, nor Iqama, nor Hajj, nor Umrah. And the description of her (slave woman) is like a man but with addition of belly and back. It is haram for a non-mahram to look at her nakedness only, and beyond that if he desires. It is not permissible for him to be a witness, nor a purifier (of witnesses), even openly, nor a tax collector, nor a divider, nor a valuator, nor a court scribe, nor a trustee for a judge, nor a supreme imam, nor a judge, nor a guardian in marriage or retaliation, nor does he undertake any public office except by deputyship from the supreme imam - thus he (the supreme imam) may appoint a judge on behalf of the sultan. If he himself judges, it is not valid. If he permits his slave to judge, and he judges after emancipation, it is valid without renewal of permission. Nor a guardian (wasi) except if he is the slave of the testator and the heirs are minors, according to Abu Hanifa. He does not own property if his master owns him. There is no zakat on him, nor fitra; rather it is upon his master if he is for service. There is no udhiya nor hady upon him. He only expiates by fasting. He does not fast non-obligatory fasts except with the master's permission, nor an obligatory fast that has become due. Likewise for i'tikaf, Hajj, and Umrah. His confession concerning wealth is not executed, whether he is authorized (ma'dhun) or mukatab, except with his master's permission, except if the authorized slave confesses to what is in his hand, even after interdiction. Likewise his confession concerning a crime that necessitates payment or ransom is not valid, unlike a crime involving a fixed penalty or retaliation. He does not independently contract his own marriage; he is compelled to marry, and the dower is made (by the master) and becomes like a pledge. He does not inherit nor is he inherited. His guarantee (kafala) is not valid unless with his master's permission. No blood money for killing him; his value stands in its place in whole and in part, but does not reach it. He has no 'aqila (blood-money paying group) nor is he one of them. His limit is half (of a free person's). He does not have ihsan. His crime is attached to his neck (i.e., his person) like his debt. He gets no share of the spoils; rather he is given a gratuity if he fights. He is sold for his debt and given for his crime if his master does not ransom him. He marries two, and he does not have concubinage at all. His divorce is two (pronouncements), and her idda is two menstrual cycles, and half of the assessed (maintenance?). There is no li'an by accusing her, and she is not married upon a free woman. His emancipation is valid for expiations. His accuser is not given the fixed penalty but is disciplined. Her share is half of the share of a free woman.
And her dower is like others. Her child is not attributed to her master unless he claims it from him, even if he acknowledges intercourse. The ila of a married slave woman is two months. She has no servant even if beautiful. Her maintenance is not obligatory except by tabyi'a (providing housing). She is not cohabited with except after istibra', unlike a free woman. There is no limit to the number of concubines. It is permissible to gather them in one dwelling without their consent. There is no zihar or ila from his slave woman. She has no claim if her master is impotent. There is no custody for her relatives; rather it is for her master. There is no qisas between him and a free person in limbs, unlike life. Government (compensation) is obligatory for shaving his beard and medical treatment of his illness upon his master, unlike a free man even if a spouse. If he cannot perform wudu except with help, then the master must make him pleased, unlike a free person. He does not marry except with his master's permission, and his dower is tied to his neck like debt, and he is sold for his wife's maintenance. His child's maintenance is not obligatory for him, nor her maintenance except by tabyi'a. The claim and testimony against him are not heard except in the presence of his master. He is not imprisoned for debt. Unbelievers own him by capture. The mutual acknowledgment of marriage between a slave and a slave woman is not valid except for captives before division, unlike two free persons, as in al-Tatarkhaniyya. His manumission is invalid even if suspended on what he will own after emancipation, and likewise his will, gift, sadaqa, and charity, except giving small gifts by an authorized slave and small favors from him, and permission to use coitus interruptus with his slave woman. He is the claimant against her husband if he is impotent or castrated, demanding separation. He is not a recipient of obligatory sadaqat (zakat) unless his master is poor or he is a mukatab. His master does not bear expenses for him except the blood of his ihsar (being prevented) for an authorized ihram. Rights do not revert to him if he is an interdicted agent. No jizya on him. He does not enter into qasama (oath for murder). Intercourse with one of two slave women is not a clarification of ambiguous manumission, unlike intercourse with one of two women is not a clarification of ambiguous divorce. His master's command to him to destroy something necessitates his guarantee. Commanding another's slave to destroy property not belonging to his master necessitates guarantee on the commander absolutely, unlike a free person except if he is a sultan. He is liable for usurpation, unlike a free person even if a minor. His waqf is not valid. His contract is suspended on his master's permission. The slave woman goes out (for idda) and her travel is permissible without a mahram. He has no right in the treasury. A dhimmi slave is not taken by distinction according to us. The waqf upon a slave himself or his slave woman is not valid according to Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on him, except for a mudabbar and umm walad. I have not seen the ruling on his finding a foundling or his taking possession of ownerless property; in the latter, his master should own it, according to us. From their statement: If he returns a runaway (slave), the reward is for his master. His master disciplines him correctly and does not impose the fixed penalty on him.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive enumeration of the rulings pertaining to slaves (both male and female) in various chapters of fiqh. It begins by stating that a slave is not obligated to perform Jumu'ah, Eid, Takbir al-Tashriq, Adhan, Iqama, Hajj, or Umrah. The description of a slave woman's aura is specified: only her nakedness is forbidden to look at for non-mahram, and beyond that only if one desires. The text then covers legal capacity: a slave cannot be a witness, judge, guardian, or hold public office except by deputyship. His confession is limited, he cannot marry without permission, his divorce counts as two, his idda is two cycles, etc. Many rulings apply half of what applies to a free person (e.g., share of inheritance is half). For female slaves, specific rules regarding dower, child attribution, ila, maintenance, and sexual relations are given. The text also discusses special cases like the authorized slave (ma'dhun), mukatab, mudabbar, and umm walad. It ends with some unique points: a slave can be owned by non-Muslims by capture, his waqf is invalid, and his contracts are suspended on the master's permission. The text is a classic Hanafi compendium of slave law.

Legal Capacity of Slaves: Public Office and Testimony
Second part: legal capacity for public office and testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the public offices and legal capacities that a slave cannot hold and explain the reason for each prohibition.

Core Idea
A slave cannot serve as judge, witness, guardian, or hold any public office due to his legal subordination.

The second segment of the passage deals with the slave's capacity to hold public office, act as a witness, guardian, or judge. The Hanafi rule is that a slave lacks the independence required for such roles. We will parse the specific offices and understand the exception of deputyship by the supreme imam.

Guided Path
1
Identify prohibited roles
List: witness (shahid), purifier of witnesses (muzakki), tax collector, divider, valuator, court scribe, trustee for judge, supreme imam, judge, guardian in marriage or retaliation.
The role of 'aashir (tax collector) is also mentioned.
2
Understand the exception
The text says a slave may be appointed as judge by the supreme imam on behalf of the sultan. This is a deputyship (niyaba).
This exception is limited; the slave cannot exercise judicial authority independently.
3
Note the ta'liq (suspension) of permission
If a master gives a slave permission to judge and the slave is later freed, the permission remains valid without renewal.
This shows that the impediment is slavery itself, not the lack of permission.
4
Apply the ruling to modern contexts
In contemporary fatwa, these rulings may apply to the capacity of certain individuals (e.g., employees, prisoners) to hold public office.
Analogous examples: a non-citizen may not hold certain offices.

Property and Financial Capacity of Slaves
Third part: financial capacity, zakat, fitra, confession, and property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the financial restrictions on a slave, including zakat, fitra, confession of debts, and his capacity to own property.

Core Idea
A slave cannot own property outright, his master is responsible for zakat/fitra, and his confession of debts is ineffective without the master's permission.

This lesson covers the slave's financial capacity as detailed in the passage. Key points: the slave does not pay zakat or fitra; his master pays fitra for him if he is used for service. The slave cannot own property independently; any property he acquires belongs to his master. His confession concerning wealth is not enforceable unless permitted, with an exception for the authorized slave (ma'dhun) regarding what is in his hand.

Guided Path
1
Identify the financial obligations not on the slave
List: zakat (not on the slave), fitra (on the master), udhiya (sacrifice) and hady (sacrificial animal) are not required.
These are financial acts of worship that require ownership and independence.
2
Understand the rule on ownership
The text says: 'ولا يملك إن ملكه سيده' – he does not own property if his master owns him. Property acquired goes to the master.
There is a difference between possession (yad) and ownership (milk). A slave can possess goods with permission, but legal title is with the master.
3
Analyze the rules on confession (iqrar)
A slave's confession concerning wealth is generally not effective without the master's permission. Exception: a ma'dhun slave can confess to what is in his hand, even after interdiction.
Confession in criminal matters differs (covered later).
4
Apply the ruling on zakat and fitra
A mufti should know that a slave does not pay zakat; the master pays fitra for each slave owned for service.
If the slave is used for trade (i.e., a commercial slave), the master pays zakat on the slave as part of his merchandise.

Marriage and Family Rulings for Slaves
Fourth part: marriage, divorce, concubinage, and related rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the special rules for slave marriage, including permission, number of spouses, divorce pronouncements, and idda.

Core Idea
A slave marries with his master's permission, can have two wives, his divorce counts as two, and the slave woman's idda is two menstrual cycles.

This lesson focuses on the marital relationships of slaves. The passage provides detailed rules: a slave cannot marry without his master's permission, he may marry up to two wives, his divorce is two pronouncements (not three), and a slave woman's idda is two cycles instead of three. We will also cover the prohibition on li'an, the permissibility of concubinage without limit, and the special rule that a slave woman cannot marry a free man if a free woman is already there.

Guided Path
1
Identify the number of spouses allowed
A male slave can marry up to two wives (free or slave). He is not allowed concubinage (tasarri) – that is reserved for the master.
Concubinage means having a female slave as a sexual partner without marriage.
2
Understand the rules of divorce and idda
The slave's divorce counts as two pronouncements (talaq), not three. The slave woman's idda is two menstrual cycles instead of three. Half of the assessed maintenance is given.
This halving is consistent with the general principle that the slave's civil rights are half of a free person's.
3
Note the prohibition on li'an and marrying a free woman
There is no li'an (mutual cursing) when a slave woman is accused of adultery; the accuser is merely disciplined. Additionally, a slave woman cannot be married to a free woman simultaneously.
This preserves the hierarchy: a free woman's status is higher.
4
Apply the rule on consent and dower
A slave does not marry independently; the master marries him off, and the dower is determined by the master.
The dower becomes a debt on the slave's neck (i.e., his person).

و مهرها كغيرها ولا يلحق ولدها مولاها إلا بدعوته منه ولو أقرَّ بوطئها، وإيلاء الأمة المنكوحة شهران ولا خادم لها ولو جميلة ولا تجب نفقتها إلا بالتبوئة(۱) ولا توطأ إلا بعد الاستبراء بخلاف الحرّة، ولا حصر لعدد السراري، ويجوز جمعهنَّ في مسكن واحد بدون الرضاء، ولا ظهار ولا إيلاء من أمته، ولا مطالبة لها إذا كان مولاها عنينا، ولا حضانة لأقاربه بل لسيده ولا قصاص بينه وبين الحر في الأطراف. بخلاف النفس، وتجب الحكومة بحلق لحيته ودواؤه مريضًا على مولاه، بخلاف الحر ولو زوجة، وإذا لم يقدر على الوضوء إلا بمعين فعلى السيد أن يرضيه بخلاف الحر، ولا يتزوج إلا بإذن ،مولاه ومهره متعلق برقبته كالدين ويُباع في نفقة زوجته، ولا تجب عليه نفقة ولده ولا نفقة لها إلا بالتبوئة ولا تسمع الدعوى والشهادة عليه إلا بحضور سيده ولا يحبس في دين، ويملكه الكفار بالاستيلاء، ولا يصح تصادق العبد والأمة على النكاح إلا المسبيين قبل القسمة بخلاف الحرين كما في التاتارخانية، وإعتاقه باطل ولو معلقا بما يملكه بعد عتقه وكذا وصيته وهبته وصدقته وتبرعه إلا إهداء اليسير من المأذون والمُحابات اليسيرة منه والإذن في العزل إلى مولاها وهو المطالب لزوجها العنين والمجبوب بالتفريق وليس مصرفًا للصدقات الواجبة إلا إذا كان مولاه فقيرًا أو كان مكاتبا، ولا يتحمل عنه مولاه مؤنة إلا دم إحصاره عن إحرام مأذون فيه ولا ترجع الحقوق إليه لو وكيلاً محجورا، ولا جزية عليه، ولا يدخل في القسامة، ووطء إحدى الأمتين ليس بيانًا للعتق المبهم، بخلاف وطء إحدى المرأتين لا يكون بيانًا في الطلاق المبهم وأمره عبده بإتلاف شيء موجب لضمانه وأمر عبد الغير بإتلاف مال غير مولاه موجب للضمان على الآمر مطلقا بخلاف الحرّ إلا إذا كان سلطانًا ويضمن بالغصب بخلاف الحر ولو صغيرا، ولا يصح وقفه، وعقده موقوف على إجازة مولاه .

(1) التبوئة : التمكن منها .
Rulings of slaves: No Friday prayer for him, nor Eid, nor Takbir al-Tashriq, nor Adhan, nor Iqama, nor Hajj, nor Umrah. And the description of her (slave woman) is like a man but with addition of belly and back. It is haram for a non-mahram to look at her nakedness only, and beyond that if he desires. It is not permissible for him to be a witness, nor a purifier (of witnesses), even openly, nor a tax collector, nor a divider, nor a valuator, nor a court scribe, nor a trustee for a judge, nor a supreme imam, nor a judge, nor a guardian in marriage or retaliation, nor does he undertake any public office except by deputyship from the supreme imam - thus he (the supreme imam) may appoint a judge on behalf of the sultan. If he himself judges, it is not valid. If he permits his slave to judge, and he judges after emancipation, it is valid without renewal of permission. Nor a guardian (wasi) except if he is the slave of the testator and the heirs are minors, according to Abu Hanifa. He does not own property if his master owns him. There is no zakat on him, nor fitra; rather it is upon his master if he is for service. There is no udhiya nor hady upon him. He only expiates by fasting. He does not fast non-obligatory fasts except with the master's permission, nor an obligatory fast that has become due. Likewise for i'tikaf, Hajj, and Umrah. His confession concerning wealth is not executed, whether he is authorized (ma'dhun) or mukatab, except with his master's permission, except if the authorized slave confesses to what is in his hand, even after interdiction. Likewise his confession concerning a crime that necessitates payment or ransom is not valid, unlike a crime involving a fixed penalty or retaliation. He does not independently contract his own marriage; he is compelled to marry, and the dower is made (by the master) and becomes like a pledge. He does not inherit nor is he inherited. His guarantee (kafala) is not valid unless with his master's permission. No blood money for killing him; his value stands in its place in whole and in part, but does not reach it. He has no 'aqila (blood-money paying group) nor is he one of them. His limit is half (of a free person's). He does not have ihsan. His crime is attached to his neck (i.e., his person) like his debt. He gets no share of the spoils; rather he is given a gratuity if he fights. He is sold for his debt and given for his crime if his master does not ransom him. He marries two, and he does not have concubinage at all. His divorce is two (pronouncements), and her idda is two menstrual cycles, and half of the assessed (maintenance?). There is no li'an by accusing her, and she is not married upon a free woman. His emancipation is valid for expiations. His accuser is not given the fixed penalty but is disciplined. Her share is half of the share of a free woman.
And her dower is like others. Her child is not attributed to her master unless he claims it from him, even if he acknowledges intercourse. The ila of a married slave woman is two months. She has no servant even if beautiful. Her maintenance is not obligatory except by tabyi'a (providing housing). She is not cohabited with except after istibra', unlike a free woman. There is no limit to the number of concubines. It is permissible to gather them in one dwelling without their consent. There is no zihar or ila from his slave woman. She has no claim if her master is impotent. There is no custody for her relatives; rather it is for her master. There is no qisas between him and a free person in limbs, unlike life. Government (compensation) is obligatory for shaving his beard and medical treatment of his illness upon his master, unlike a free man even if a spouse. If he cannot perform wudu except with help, then the master must make him pleased, unlike a free person. He does not marry except with his master's permission, and his dower is tied to his neck like debt, and he is sold for his wife's maintenance. His child's maintenance is not obligatory for him, nor her maintenance except by tabyi'a. The claim and testimony against him are not heard except in the presence of his master. He is not imprisoned for debt. Unbelievers own him by capture. The mutual acknowledgment of marriage between a slave and a slave woman is not valid except for captives before division, unlike two free persons, as in al-Tatarkhaniyya. His manumission is invalid even if suspended on what he will own after emancipation, and likewise his will, gift, sadaqa, and charity, except giving small gifts by an authorized slave and small favors from him, and permission to use coitus interruptus with his slave woman. He is the claimant against her husband if he is impotent or castrated, demanding separation. He is not a recipient of obligatory sadaqat (zakat) unless his master is poor or he is a mukatab. His master does not bear expenses for him except the blood of his ihsar (being prevented) for an authorized ihram. Rights do not revert to him if he is an interdicted agent. No jizya on him. He does not enter into qasama (oath for murder). Intercourse with one of two slave women is not a clarification of ambiguous manumission, unlike intercourse with one of two women is not a clarification of ambiguous divorce. His master's command to him to destroy something necessitates his guarantee. Commanding another's slave to destroy property not belonging to his master necessitates guarantee on the commander absolutely, unlike a free person except if he is a sultan. He is liable for usurpation, unlike a free person even if a minor. His waqf is not valid. His contract is suspended on his master's permission. The slave woman goes out (for idda) and her travel is permissible without a mahram. He has no right in the treasury. A dhimmi slave is not taken by distinction according to us. The waqf upon a slave himself or his slave woman is not valid according to Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on him, except for a mudabbar and umm walad. I have not seen the ruling on his finding a foundling or his taking possession of ownerless property; in the latter, his master should own it, according to us. From their statement: If he returns a runaway (slave), the reward is for his master. His master disciplines him correctly and does not impose the fixed penalty on him.
(1) al-Tabwi'a: providing housing.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive enumeration of the rulings pertaining to slaves (both male and female) in various chapters of fiqh. It begins by stating that a slave is not obligated to perform Jumu'ah, Eid, Takbir al-Tashriq, Adhan, Iqama, Hajj, or Umrah. The description of a slave woman's aura is specified: only her nakedness is forbidden to look at for non-mahram, and beyond that only if one desires. The text then covers legal capacity: a slave cannot be a witness, judge, guardian, or hold public office except by deputyship. His confession is limited, he cannot marry without permission, his divorce counts as two, his idda is two cycles, etc. Many rulings apply half of what applies to a free person (e.g., share of inheritance is half). For female slaves, specific rules regarding dower, child attribution, ila, maintenance, and sexual relations are given. The text also discusses special cases like the authorized slave (ma'dhun), mukatab, mudabbar, and umm walad. It ends with some unique points: a slave can be owned by non-Muslims by capture, his waqf is invalid, and his contracts are suspended on the master's permission. The text is a classic Hanafi compendium of slave law.

العدة (al-ʿidda): waiting period after divorce or death before remarriage

Criminal and Tort Liability of Slaves
Fifth part: criminal and tort liability, qisas, diya, hadd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the criminal and tort liability of a slave, including qisas, diya, and the role of the master.

Core Idea
A slave's criminal liability is attached to his person; he can be sold for debts and crimes, and qisas between slave and free is limited to life, not limbs.

This lesson covers the slave's liability for crimes and torts. The passage states that no blood money (diya) is paid for killing a slave; instead, his value is paid, which is less than the diya of a free person. There is no qisas (retaliation) between a slave and a free person for injuries to limbs; only for killing. The slave can be handed over for his crimes or sold to pay his debts. The master is not his 'aqila (blood money paying group).

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of no diya for a slave
When a slave is killed, the killer must pay the slave's value (not the full diya of a free person). This value cannot exceed the diya of a free person.
This is because the slave is property; his value compensates the master.
2
Distinguish qisas for life vs limbs
There is no qisas between a slave and a free person for cutting off limbs. For murder, if a slave kills a free person, the slave can be executed (qisas).
This reflects that a slave's life is not equal to a free person's in terms of legal status.
3
Identify how the slave pays for crimes
The slave's crime is attached to his neck; the master may ransom him or hand him over to the victim. Similarly, the slave can be sold to pay his debts.
The slave's body is collateral.
4
Note the absence of 'aqila and hadd for slander
A slave does not belong to any 'aqila (group that pays diya). Also, a person who falsely accuses a slave woman of adultery is not given hadd but ta'zir.
The hadd penalties for qadhf (false accusation) only apply if the accused is a free Muslim.

Miscellaneous Rulings: Inheritance, Testimony, and Procedure
Seventh part: inheritance, testimony, debt, and various procedural rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to summarize the rules on inheritance, testimony in court, and procedural matters for slaves.

Core Idea
A slave does not inherit nor is he inherited; his testimony is not heard without his master present; he is not imprisoned for debt.

This final lesson covers remaining miscellaneous rulings from the passage: the slave does not inherit or pass on inheritance, his testimony in court requires his master's presence, he is not imprisoned for debt, and he can be owned by non-Muslims through capture. Understand inheritance incapacity

2
Testimony and claims procedure
A claim or testimony against a slave is not heard unless his master is present. This ensures the master can defend his property.
This is a procedural safeguard.
3

He is not imprisoned for debt because his person is already tied to his master.
Instead, he can be sold to satisfy debts.
4

Unbelievers can own him by capture (istila') in war.
This was relevant in classical times; a Muslim can also own a slave, but the text mentions kuffaar.

If a slave returns a runaway (slave), the reward (ja'alah) goes to his master.
The slave himself cannot claim the reward.

Shafi'i Synonymous Usage of Batil and Fasid
Notes that Shafi'is equate batil and fasid except in specified contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Shafi'i general rule that batil and fasid are synonymous, list the exceptions, and apply this knowledge when comparing Hanafi and Shafi'i rulings in fatwa.

Core Idea
For Shafi'is, batil and fasid are the same—except in kitabah, khul', ariyah, wakalah, shirkah, qard, and hajj.

This brief note from al-Asyuti is a goldmine for the Hanafi mufti working across madhahib. While the Hanafi school sharply distinguishes between batil (void) and fasid (corrupt/invalid), the Shafi'i school generally treats them as synonyms. However, there are important exceptions. Understanding this prevents misreading Shafi'i texts and ensures accurate fatwa when a case crosses madhhab boundaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The passage states that for Shafi'is, 'al-batil wa al-fasid mutaradifan' – the two terms are synonymous. This means in most contracts and acts, calling something 'fasid' is equivalent to saying 'batil' (void ab initio).
2
List the Exceptions
Memorize the specific contracts and acts where the Shafi'is treat them distinctly: kitabah (contract of manumission for payment), khul' (divorce by redemption), ariyah (loan of usage), wakalah (agency), shirkah (partnership), qard (loan of fungibles), and in hajj.
These are cases where a distinction may have practical effect. The passage does not elaborate how, but being aware of the exception is key.
3
Contrast with Hanafi Usage
In the Hanafi school, batil and fasid are two distinct categories. Batil is void with no legal effect; fasid is corrupt but may be rectifiable. This fundamental difference means you cannot directly translate Shafi'i 'fasid' as Hanafi 'fasid'.
For example, a Shafi'i might invalidate a sale as 'fasid' meaning absolutely void, while a Hanafi might treat it as potentially curable.
4
Apply in a Fatwa Context
When a Hanafi mufti encounters a Shafi'i text or question calling a transaction 'fasid', he must first check if it falls into one of the exception categories. If not, he should treat it as void (batil) in Shafi'i terms, and then compare that outcome with the Hanafi ruling on the same scenario.
This prevents conflating the two schools' terminologies and issuing incorrect guidance.

The Evidentiary Weight of Commercial Registers in Hanafi Law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that in Hanafi law, the registers of sellers, brokers, and moneychangers are accepted as legal evidence, and apply this principle to analogous cases.

Core Idea
A written commercial register is legally binding evidence in Hanafi courts.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a key principle of Hanafi procedural law: the written records of merchants are given evidentiary weight. It also draws an analogy with the credentials of a foreign envoy, reinforcing the general acceptance of documentary proof in certain contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Find the explicit statement: 'one acts upon the register of the seller, broker, and moneychanger'. This is the core ruling.
2
Note the scope
The rule applies specifically to registers of sellers (bā‘i‘), brokers (samsār), and moneychangers (ṣarrāf). Not all written documents are included.

Understand the principle
The writing (al-khat) in the register is a hujjah (proof). This establishes documentary evidence as valid in court.

Apply the analogous case
The envoy's letter demonstrates the same principle: a warring enemy (harbi) claiming to be a messenger is not believed without written credentials from his king.
The analogy extends the principle to diplomatic communications.
5
Recognize the source
The ruling cites Siyar al-Khāniyyah, a foundational Hanafi text on international law, confirming its authority.

Conditions of Imamate: Agreed and Disputed
Listing agreed and disputed conditions of imamate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the eight agreed conditions of imamate and identify the four disputed conditions according to Al-Amidi.

Core Idea
The imam must possess eight agreed qualifications; lineage (Qurayshi/Hashimi) and infallibility remain disputed.

Imamate (supreme leadership) is a foundational institution in Sunni political thought. Scholars have agreed on essential qualifications for the imam, such as ijtihad and justice, while differing on whether he must be from Quraysh or infallible. This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair summarizes the consensus and points of disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the eight agreed conditions
Read the text and list the conditions that Al-Amidi says are agreed upon: ijtihad, knowledge of warfare, strength to enforce hudud, justice, piety, adulthood, maleness, freedom, enforceable command, being obeyed, ability to subdue rebels. Note that 'nāfidh al-ḥukm' and 'muṭāʿan' are separate.
Some conditions may be implied (e.g., adulthood and maleness are explicit).
2
Identify the four disputed conditions
From the passage: being Qurayshi, being Hashimi, infallibility, being the best of his time.
These are not universally required.
3
Understand the basis of agreement
The agreed conditions are based on consensus (ijma) among scholars. Al-Amidi's list reflects the standard Sunni position.
Ijtihad here means the ability to derive rulings independently.
4
Analyze the disputed conditions
Consider why these are disputed: lineage requirements (Qurayshi/Hashimi) stem from hadith, but some scholars interpret them as recommended; infallibility is a Shia doctrine; 'best of his time' is subjective.
The Hanafi school does not require Qurayshi descent as a condition of validity.
