Guiding to Kill: Game vs Human
Distinction between two types of prohibited acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between guiding to kill game and guiding to kill a human, and apply the principle that only acts prohibited due to Ihram incur compensation.

Core Idea
Guiding to kill game requires jaza because hunting is prohibited in Ihram; guiding to kill a Muslim does not because it is always prohibited.

This passage presents a critical distinction: the liability for causing harm depends on the nature of the prohibition. If the prohibition is specific to Ihram, causing that act triggers compensation. If the act is universally prohibited, causing it does not create a specific Hajj-related penalty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two actions
Recognize the two cases: guiding to kill game (sayd) vs guiding to kill a Muslim.
The text states both clearly.
2
State the ruling for each
Guiding to kill game: jaza required. Guiding to kill a Muslim: no jaza.
The ruling is explicit.
3
Analyze the difference
The first is prohibited only because of Ihram (mahzur al-ihram). The second is prohibited absolutely (mahzur mutlaq).
This is the core legal distinction.
4
Derive the principle
Only violations of Ihram-specific prohibitions trigger jaza. Universally prohibited acts do not create a separate Hajj compensation.
This principle can be applied to other similar cases.

Error in Timing: Rectification Difficulty in Hajj
Application of a specific maxim in Hajj.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim that rectification in Hajj is difficult, and distinguish when repetition is not required.

Core Idea
Rectification in Hajj is difficult, so timing errors are overlooked; in other acts, repetition is required.

The passage draws a sharp contrast between Hajj and other acts of worship regarding errors in timing. Because Hajj has a strict time window and cannot be repeated easily, a group error in the timing of standing at Arafah does not invalidate the Hajj. In contrast, errors in fasting or sacrifice timing require rectification because they can be made up.

For Hajj, due to difficulty, the error is overlooked; for others, correction is possible so required.

Hajj for Slave vs Poor: Cause Established
Rulings on Hajj for those not fully obligated and later become obligated.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a slave and a poor person who perform Hajj and later gain freedom or wealth, and apply the principle of cause establishment (in'iqad al-sabab).

Core Idea
The obligation of Hajj hinges on the cause (sabab) being present at the time of performance; for a slave, the cause of obligation was absent, so later freedom does not make it count; for a poor person, the cause was present, so later wealth does not affect validity.

This passage addresses the critical question of when Hajj counts as the obligatory one. The Hanafi school holds that the validity depends on whether the conditions of obligation were met at the time of Hajj. A slave performs Hajj without being obligated; later freedom does not retroactively oblige him. But a poor person who performs Hajj with assistance or savings that fulfill the condition of istita'ah (capacity) is considered to have performed the obligatory Hajj, even if he later becomes wealthy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Slave performs Hajj then freed; poor person performs Hajj then becomes wealthy.
The text contrasts these two.
2
State the ruling for each
Slave: Hajj is valid as a supererogatory act (not fard) but does not count as obligatory; he must repeat if freed and able. Poor: Hajj suffices as obligatory (he does not have to repeat).
The translation 'hajj li al-Islam' means he has performed the ritual, not necessarily the fard.
3
Analyze the difference
The cause (sabab) of obligation—freedom and capacity—was not established for the slave at the time of Hajj. For the poor person, the cause (having the means) was established.
This is the core distinction.
4
Apply to analogous cases
Minor is like slave: his Hajj does not count as fard. Blind, chronically ill, woman without mahram are like poor: their Hajj counts as fard if performed.
The text extends the analogy.

Wording of Divorce: Constructive vs Informative Statements
Ruling on ambiguous divorce wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a constructive (insha') and informative (ikhbar) divorce statement and apply the Hanafi ruling on intention.

Core Idea
Adding 'wallahi' to a divorce phrase changes it from constructive to informative, voiding the divorce even with intention.

The first ruling in this passage deals with a man saying to his wife 'You are not my wife' (lasti imra'ati). The Hanafi school rules that this is a constructive divorce if he intends divorce. However, if he adds an oath like 'by Allah' (wallahi), it becomes a purely informative statement and cannot effect divorce, regardless of his intention. This distinction hinges on the linguistic nature of the phrase.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two phrases
Compare 'lasti imra'ati' (You are not my wife) and 'wallahi lasti imra'ati' (By Allah, you are not my wife). Note the addition of the oath.
2
Classify each as insha' or ikhbar
In Hanafi usul, insha' creates a new legal reality, ikhbar reports a fact. The first phrase could be either; the second is purely ikhbar because the oath commits to a report.
The text says: 'li ihtimal al-awwal al-insha' wa fi al-thani tamahhada li al-ikhbar'.
3
State the ruling based on intention
For the first: divorce occurs only if he intended divorce (nawa). For the second: no divorce even if he intended, because the wording precludes constructive meaning.
4
Apply the rule
Consider a scenario: a man says 'You are not my wife' without addition. If he had no intention, no divorce. If he intended, divorce occurs.
This shows the importance of intention in Hanafi law.

Intercourse vs Travel in Revocable Divorce
Maxim on permissible actions during revocable divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-wat' ruj'ah bikhilaf al-musafarah' to distinguish permissible and prohibited actions during revocable divorce.

Core Idea
Intercourse constitutes a return to marriage; travel does not, so it is prohibited during revocable divorce.

This passage introduces a key maxim in Hanafi family law: during a revocable (raj'i) divorce, intercourse with the wife is permissible because it serves as a return (ruj'ah) to the marriage. However, traveling with her is not permissible because travel does not effect return. The maxim succinctly captures the difference between an act that inherently indicates return (intercourse) and one that does not (travel).

Guided Path
1
Recall the state of revocable divorce
In revocable divorce, the husband has the right to take back his wife during the waiting period without a new marriage contract.
2
Define 'ruj'ah'
Ruj'ah is the act of revoking the divorce and resuming marital life. It can be by word or action, but not all actions count.
3
Compare intercourse and travel
Intercourse is a definitive sign of returning to marriage; travel is not. Therefore, intercourse is permitted (and constitutes ruj'ah), while travel is not permitted (and does not constitute ruj'ah).
The text says: 'yahillu wat' al-mutallaqa raj'iyyan la al-safar biha'.
4
Apply the maxim to new scenarios
Other actions like kissing or touching with desire are debated but generally can also constitute ruj'ah if intended. Travel does not, even with intention.
This is a sughra maxim (dabt) restricting the broader rule that any action can be ruj'ah if intended.

Five Distinctions: Marriage Rulings and Divine Rights
Five furuq on marriage-related rulings and their justifications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five furuq, explain each justification based on haqq Allah, and apply the underlying qawida to distinguish marriage from other contracts.

Core Idea
Marriage's divine right makes it unique: some rulings are established without claim, and conditions uphold.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents five furuq that a mufti must know. Each contrasts marriage with another legal act to show how divine rights (haqq Allah) override ordinary contractual logic. Understanding these distinctions clarifies why nikah is not a mere exchange.

Guided Path
1
First Distinction: Claim Requirement
Identify that marriage and divorce are established without a court claim, unlike ownership by sale. The reason is that marriage involves haqq Allah (permissibility and prohibition are His rights), so they are not mere private claims.
Ownership is a right of the servant, thus requires claim.
2
Second Distinction: Father's Authority over Dower vs. Gift
A father cannot take his adult virgin daughter's dower without permission, but if he takes a gift given to her by her husband, he can be compelled to return it. The difference is that she is shy (haya) to claim her dower, so her silence is a sign of consent, but no such shyness applies to a gift.
Shyness is a sign of consent in matters involving modesty.
3
Third Distinction: Touching with Desire without Ejaculation
If a man touches a woman with desire but does not ejaculate, her ancestors and descendants become prohibited (i.e., he cannot marry them). If he ejaculates, the prohibition is not established through this touch. Reason: desire without ejaculation is a call to intercourse, so it is treated like intercourse itself; ejaculation confirms actual intercourse which would have already established prohibition.
The first is a stronger call to intercourse.
4
Fourth Distinction: Anal Touch vs. Intercourse
Touching the anus (with desire) establishes in-law prohibition (e.g., the woman's mother becomes haram), but does not make the act of anal intercourse itself permissible (it remains forbidden). Reason: the touch is a precursor to offspring, while the act itself does not produce offspring.
The focus is on the potential for reproduction.
5
Fifth Distinction: Condition in Marriage vs. Sale
If a man marries a slave woman on condition that every child she bears is free, both the marriage and the condition are valid. However, if he buys her on the same condition, the condition invalidates the sale. Reason: marriage is a contract of divine right that can tolerate such conditions; sale is a contract of exchange and the condition corrupts it.
The nature of the contract determines condition validity.

Revocable Divorce: Intercourse vs Travel and Stepson's Kissing
Two furuq on the consequences of actions after divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why intercourse with a revocably divorced woman is considered revocation but travel is not, and why kissing a stepmother during iddah from a bayin divorce does not cause prohibition.

Core Idea
Intercourse implicitly revokes a revocable divorce; travel does not. Prohibition from kissing depends on the existence of the marriage bond.

This lesson covers two furuq related to the effects of actions during and after marriage. The first explains why permissible actions can have different legal consequences: intercourse with a revocably divorced woman counts as raj'ah (returning to marriage), but traveling with her does not. The second shows why a stepson's kissing of his stepmother during her iddah from an irrevocable divorce does not make her haram for him, whereas during the marriage it would. Both distinctions rest on the presence or absence of the marital bond.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actions and their legal status
Read the first furq: intercourse with a revocably divorced woman is halal (allowed) and considered raj'ah; traveling is halal but not raj'ah. Note the difference in effect.
2
Analyze the reason (farq)
The text states: 'because intercourse is revocation, unlike travel.' Intercourse is an act that symbolizes resumption of marriage; travel is neutral.
This is based on the qawida 'al-wat' raj'ah'.
3
Apply the same logic to the second furq
Read the second furq: kissing a stepmother during iddah from a bayin divorce does not prohibit; during marriage it does. The reason: during iddah from bayin, there is no surviving marriage bond.
The prohibition of kissing a stepmother arises from the marriage bond; when that bond is irrevocably severed, the prohibition does not carry over.

Conditional Divorce and Agency: Numbers and Delegation
Two furuq on conditional divorce and agency/delegation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that numbers in conditional divorces are treated according to their suitability for divorce, and distinguish between agency (wikala) and delegation (tamlik) in divorce.

Core Idea
Numbers in conditions may be for the condition or for divorce; delegation gives ownership, agency does not.

This lesson covers two more furuq: one about conditional divorce with numbers, and another about the difference between appointing an agent for divorce and delegating the power to the wife. In the first, the number 'ten' is tied to entry, while 'three' is suitable for divorce. In the second, a principal can dismiss his agent, but a wife who has been delegated the right to divorce herself cannot dismiss herself because she now owns that right.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the conditional divorce example
Read the furq: 'You are divorced if you enter the house ten times' vs '...if you enter three times'. Determine why one entry in the second case triggers triple divorce immediately.
2
Apply the principle of suitability
The text says: 'because the number in the first is not suitable for divorce but for entry, unlike the second.' The number ten cannot be the number of divorces, so it is interpreted as the number of entries. Three is suitable for triple divorce, so the condition is fulfilled on the first entry.
3
Distinguish between wikala and tamlik
In the second furq: the principal can dismiss his agent (wakeel) at will, but if he delegates the right to divorce to his wife (tamlik), she cannot dismiss herself because she is the owner of the right.
Tamlik transfers ownership; wikala is mere authorization.

Distinctions in the Book of Manumission (Kitab al-'Itaq)
Furuq on manumission, oaths, and related acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for each of the four furuq in manumission and explain the reasoning (talil) behind why the same phrase yields different results in divorce or acknowledgment.

Core Idea
Wording and context determine legal effect: what frees a slave may not divorce a wife, and vice versa.

In Hanafi fiqh, the precise wording of an oath or declaration determines its legal effect. This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents four critical distinctions (furuq) between manumission, divorce, and acknowledgment. Understanding these distinctions hones your ability to discern the underlying maxims and apply them consistently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first furq: farj vs dhakar
State the ruling: using 'farj' (private part) effects manumission, but using 'dhakar' (penis) does not. Note the reason: 'farj' euphemistically refers to the whole person, while 'dhakar' refers to a part only.
This is a linguistic distinction; the word choice matters in legal oaths.
2
Identify the second furq: 'your freedom is due to an obligation'
State the ruling: saying 'your freedom is due to an obligation' does not free the slave. Contrast with divorce: saying 'your divorce is due to an obligation' does effect divorce. Reason: freedom is not described as an obligatory act (wajib) in the Shari'ah, but divorce can be.
The conceptual category of the act (wajib vs. non-wajib) determines the effect.
3
Identify the third furq: oath about buying slaves vs marriage
State the ruling: if a man says 'every slave I buy is free,' an invalid purchase does not trigger the oath. In contrast, an invalid marriage does trigger a divorce oath. Reason: the oath is dissolved by a fasid contract in marriage (because fasid marriage still has legal effects) but not in manumission (fasid purchase is void).
The legal consequences of a fasid contract differ between marriage and sale.
4
Identify the fourth furq: freeing one of two slaves then retracting
State the ruling: if a man frees one of two slaves and then says 'I did not mean this one,' the other slave becomes free (the statement is treated as a retraction). The same applies in divorce. However, in acknowledgment (iqrar) of a debt, such a statement does not transfer the acknowledgment to another. Reason: clarification (bayan) is obligatory in both, but in manumission/divorce it is considered an effective establishment of the new object.
The nature of the act (revocable vs. binding) governs the effect of retraction.
5
Synthesize the common thread
All four furuq emphasize that the legal effect of a declaration hinges on precise wording and the conceptual framework of the act (e.g., what 'whole' means, what is wajib, what constitutes dissolution). Memorize the four distinctions as key examples of Hanafi linguistic and contextual reasoning.
These furuq serve as memorization anchors for deeper principles.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Hanafi rules for emancipation expressions, distinguishing valid from invalid oaths, and compare with talaq and iqrar.

Core Idea
Emancipation oaths hinge on precise words and contract validity.

This passage from Kitab al-Itaq examines how specific expressions trigger emancipation and how defective contracts affect oaths. You will learn the Hanafi methodology for parsing kinayah, conditional oaths, and clarification (bayan). The rulings contrast with talaq and iqrar to highlight the school's legal reasoning.

1
Identify linguistic basis for farj vs dhakar
Parse why 'farj' as a kinayah for the whole person triggers emancipation, while 'dhakar' does not. Note the principle: a term that denotes the whole is effective.

Analyze 'Your emancipation is obligatory'
Compare the phrase in itaq and talaq. Understand why 'wajib' describes divorce but not emancipation due to linguistic convention.

Clarification is obligatory in both, but in iqrar it is considered a valid designation.

كتاب العتاق لو أضافه إلى فرجه عتق، لا إلى ذكره ; لأن الأول يعبر به عن الكل بخلاف الثاني. ولو قال عتقك على واجب لا يعتق، بخلاف طلاقك على واجب; لأن الأول يوصف به دون الثاني ولو قال كل عبد أشتريه فهو حرّ، فاشتراه فاسدًا صحيحًا لا يعتق، وفي النكاح تطلق; لانحلال اليمين في الأول بالفاسد بخلاف الثاني. أعتق أحد عبديه ثم قال : لم أعن هذا يعتق الآخر، وكذا في الطلاق، بخلافه في الإقرار فإنه لا يتعين الآخر لأن البيان واجب فيهما فكان متعيّنا إقامة له. والله أعلم بالصواب.
Chapter of emancipation: If he added [the emancipation] to his private part (faraj), the slave is emancipated; not if to his penis (dhakar), because the first is used to denote the entire person, contrary to the second. And if he says, 'Your emancipation is obligatory,' he is not emancipated, contrary to 'your divorce is obligatory'; because the first (emancipation) is described by [obligation] while the second is not. And if he says, 'Every slave I buy is free,' then he buys [a slave] with a defective (fasid) but [outwardly] valid (sahih) contract, the slave is not emancipated; but in marriage, the wife is divorced because the oath is dissolved by the defective in the first [case of talaq] contrary to the second. He emancipates one of his two slaves then says, 'I did not mean this one,' the other is emancipated; similarly in talaq; contrary to iqrar (acknowledgment), because then the other is not determined, because clarification is obligatory in both [cases] but it is deemed to have been made [in iqrar].

Commentary
This passage from the Chapter of Emancipation (Kitab al-Itaq) includes several specific rulings comparing itaq with talaq and iqrar. The author begins with the linguistic point: if one says 'You are free' while touching or referring to the private part (farj), it is effective because 'farj' is a term that denotes the entire person, whereas 'dhakar' (penis) does not. Next, if one says 'Your emancipation is obligatory,' the slave is not freed, unlike saying 'Your divorce is obligatory' because divorce is described as being 'obligatory' (in the sense of being pronounced) while emancipation is not. Then, if one swears 'Every slave I buy is free' and then makes a defective (fasid) purchase (e.g., without proper offer/acceptance but still outwardly valid), the slave is not freed; but in the case of a marriage oath (e.g., 'Every woman I marry is divorced'), a defective marriage would result in divorce because the oath is dissolved by a defective contract in the case of talaq but not in itaq. Finally, if someone emancipates one of two slaves and then says 'I did not mean this one,' the other slave becomes free; the same applies in talaq (if he divorces one wife and says he meant another). However, in iqrar (acknowledgment of debt), if one says 'I owe one of two persons' and then clarifies, the clarification is accepted and the other is not determined, because clarification is obligatory and is considered established.

Key Terms
الفرج (al-farj): The genitals, but in legal context, it is used metaphorically to denote the entire person (kinayah).
اليمين (al-yameen): An oath (to do or not do something). In this passage, it refers to a conditional oath ('if I buy... then free').
الكناية (al-kinayah): An indirect expression that implies the intended meaning through context or custom.
