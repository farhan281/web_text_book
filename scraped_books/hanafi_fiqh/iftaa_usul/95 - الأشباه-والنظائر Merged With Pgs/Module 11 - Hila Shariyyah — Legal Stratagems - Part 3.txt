The Hila for Valid Discharge of an Agent from the Price
Hila for valid discharge of agent from price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the hila for valid discharge of an agent from the price and explain why it achieves consensus validity.

Core Idea
Pay the price to the seller first, then get reimbursed—that is the consensual hila for the agent's discharge.

Discharging an agent from liability for the price can be contentious among scholars. This lesson presents a simple hila (stratagem) that secures consensus: the agent pays the seller directly, and then the buyer reimburses the agent. Understanding this maneuver clarifies how Hanafi jurists use legal devices to resolve practical agency problems while maintaining sound contractual logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
An agent is entrusted with paying the price on behalf of the buyer. If the buyer simply discharges the agent from this duty, some scholars may dispute the validity because the agent still owes the seller. Determine what must be achieved for a valid discharge.
The core issue is the agent's liability to the seller, not just to the principal.
2
State the hila's procedure
The hila consists of two steps: (1) the agent pays the seller the amount of the price, (2) then the buyer (principal) pays that amount to the agent. This creates a clear sequence that ends the agent's liability.
Note the order: payment first, then reimbursement.
3
Explain why it works
By paying the seller directly, the agent fulfills his obligation to the seller. The subsequent payment from the buyer to the agent is a separate transaction (reimbursement). Hence, the agent is validly discharged from any debt related to the price, and this is agreed upon by all scholars.
Consensus arises because the agent's liability is extinguished by actual payment, not by a mere waiver.
4
Identify the underlying legal concepts
This hila engages the concepts of ibra' (discharge), agency (wakalah), and payment (qabd). The Hanafi view that ibra' is a unilateral act does not apply here because the agent's liability to the seller is a separate debt. The hila circumvents any ikhtilaf by using actual payment.
The hila transforms a potential dispute into a clear, universally accepted transaction.
5
Apply to a variant scenario
Consider: the agent pays the seller with his own money, expecting reimbursement from the buyer. Does this hila still work if the buyer fails to reimburse? No, because then the agent has a claim against the buyer, but his liability to the seller is already discharged. The agent's risk is shifted to the buyer.
The hila does not protect the agent from buyer default; it only ensures valid discharge from the seller.

Guided Path

The agent can raise the matter to the judge (qadi), who can give permission to send the goods. The judge's order authorizes the dispatch and removes liability.

The employee must be part of the agent's household (min 'iyalihi). This condition ensures the agent retains constructive possession, thus no transfer of liability occurs.

Hila for Settlement with Iqrar in Inheritance
Hila for sulh with iqrar in inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a legal stratagem (hila) using sulh and iqrar to settle an inheritance dispute while preserving the heirs' shares.

Core Idea
A legal stratagem redirects acknowledgment to an outsider to preserve proportionate inheritance shares.

In this lesson, we examine a classical Hanafi hila for handling a settlement when an outsider claims property from an estate. The key issue is whether acknowledgment (iqrar) is part of the settlement. If the settlement includes iqrar, the settlement money is split equally between the heirs, which may not reflect their shariah-prescribed shares. The stratagem introduces an intermediary (ajnabi) to receive the acknowledgment, thereby protecting the heirs' shares.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the dispute
A man dies leaving a son, a wife, and a house. A third party claims the house. The heirs and the claimant reach a settlement (sulh) for money.
The house is the contested property.
2
Understand the two settlement scenarios
If the settlement is without acknowledgment (iqrar), the settlement money is divided between the heirs in eighths? (text ambiguous). If with iqrar, the money is split equally (halves) like the house would have been. This equal split may not correspond to the correct inheritance shares (1/8 for wife, 7/8 for son).
The text states: 'فالمال عليهما نصفان كالدار' – the money is upon them in halves like the house.
3
Recognize the problem with iqrar
When the settlement includes iqrar (acknowledgment), the money is divided equally. This distorts the heirs' shares because the wife should get 1/8 and the son 7/8 from the estate.
The problem is that iqrar binds the heirs to an equal split.
4
The hila: introduce an outsider
The stratagem is to have an outsider (ajnabi) settle with the claimant on acknowledgment that the wife gets her eighth (from the settlement money) and the son gets the rest. Alternatively, the claimant acknowledges that the wife has 1/8 and the son the rest, but this acknowledgment is directed to the outsider, not the heirs.
The outsider becomes the recipient of the iqrar, so the heirs' own shares remain unaffected.
5
Why this works
By redirecting the acknowledgment to the outsider, the heirs do not become bound by an unequal distribution. The settlement money can then be distributed according to inheritance shares (1/8 wife, 7/8 son). The hila preserves the shariah-prescribed division.
The hila is permissible because it avoids injustice while achieving the settlement.

The Hila for Specification in Wills (تخصيص) — Rule and Stratagem
Legal stratagem (hila) for specification in wills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a will cannot be specified by type, place, or time, and apply the hila of stipulating a condition to achieve specification indirectly.

Core Idea
A will is general; specification is invalid unless done via a conditional hila.

In Hanafi law, a will (wasīyah) is inherently general—it cannot be made specific to a particular type of property, a specific location, or a limited time. This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naẓā'ir explains this rule and begins to describe a legal stratagem (hīlah) to work around it. Although the text is cut off, the principle is clear: the testator may use a condition (sharṭ) to achieve the desired separation of bequests indirectly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The passage states that a will does not accept specification by type (نوع), place (مكان), or time (زمان). This is a default principle to prevent ambiguity and conflict among heirs.
The Arabic: 'الوصية لا تقبل التخصيص بنوع ومكان وزمان'
2
Understand the problem scenario
Suppose a testator wants to give Zayd a specific item in Egypt and ʿAmr a different item in Syria, and wants each to receive only his designated portion without sharing. Direct specification is invalid.
The text gives the example of Zayd in Miṣr and ʿAmr in al-Shām.
3
Introduce the hila
The stratagem (الحيلة) is to use a condition (sharṭ). The testator conditions the bequest to one heir on something that effectively separates the gifts. The text breaks off before completing the condition, but the typical Hanafi hila is to make each bequest conditional on the occurrence of an event that ensures only one gets the specified item.
The text ends with 'فالحيلة أن يشترط' (the stratagem is that he stipulates).
4
Complete the hila from other sources
From other Hanafi works (e.g., al-Ḥidāyah, al-Fatāwā al-Hindiyyah), the full hila: the testator says, 'I give to Zayd such‑and‑such if I do not give to ʿAmr such‑and‑such,' making each bequest conditional on the other's non‑occurrence, so that effectively only one takes effect.
This indirect method achieves the specification without violating the rule.
5
Apply the rule and hila
For any case where a testator wishes to specify, first check if the attempted specification is direct. If so, it is invalid and the will remains general. Then apply the hila by restructuring the bequests with conditions that separate them legally.
The condition must be valid (e.g., not impossible or contrary to Sharīʿah).

Core Idea
Agency hiyal allow the agent to buy for himself, discharge debt, or avoid liability—by formally altering the transaction's type, price, or authorization.

The Hanafi school permits legal stratagems (hiyal) to achieve legitimate goals without violating the law. For buying for himself: use different currency type, or higher price, or declare purchase before principal, or delegate. For discharge: agent gives principal the amount, then buyer pays agent. For sending goods: principal authorizes, or send via hired helper, or get judge's order.
The actions are specific and must be followed exactly.
3
Explain why each action changes the legal outcome
Each action creates a new legal fact that overrides the original agency relationship. For example, using a different currency changes the subject matter of the agency, so the purchase is no longer within the agency's scope.
The hila exploits permissible variations in contract terms.
4
Summarise the underlying principle
The principle is not to circumvent law but to achieve a valid result by altering a permissible element (type, price, authorization). The Hanafi school values practical outcomes over rigid formalism.
This is a hallmark of Hanafi fiqh.

Stratagems for Avoiding Pre-emption (Shuf'ah)
Stratagems for avoiding pre-emption rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid hila (legal stratagem) to transfer property without triggering shuf'ah, and evaluate its permissibility according to Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Pre-emption can be avoided by using gift, confession, or partial charity — as long as the transaction is not a sale.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair presents three stratagems (hiyal) that allow a property owner to transfer a house to a desired buyer without activating the pre-emptor's right. These stratagems are based on valid legal contracts—gift, confession, and charity—each of which does not constitute a sale. Understanding these hiyal is essential for a mufti who must navigate the boundaries of legal avoidance without falling into fraud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal obstacle
Recognize that shuf'ah (pre-emption) is triggered exclusively by a sale contract (bay'). Other transfers like gift (hiba), confession (iqrar), or charity (sadaqa) do not activate it.
The key is that the transaction must not be a sale.
2
Examine each stratagem's mechanism
Go through each of the three methods: (1) gift of the house + gift of the price, (2) mutual confessions of ownership and debt, (3) charity of a portion adjoining the neighbor + sale of the rest.
Each method uses a different non-sale contract to achieve the transfer.
3
Verify the absence of a sale
For each stratagem, confirm that no element of a sale (offer, acceptance, price, transfer of ownership for consideration) is present. The contracts are independent: gift, confession, charity.
If any stratagem disguises a sale, it may be invalid.
4
Assess permissibility and conditions
In Hanafi law, these stratagems are permissible as long as each underlying contract is valid and there is no fraud (tadlis) or harm (darar) to the pre-emptor beyond the loss of an opportunity.
Scholars differ on whether such hiyal are legitimate; Hanafis rely on the form of the contract.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school, especially among later scholars (mutakhkhirin), is that these hiyal are valid and can be used in fatwa.
Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair is a key reference for these rulings.

Break down Abu Hanifa's steps: (1) He asked each man if he wanted the woman currently with him (i.e., the one he consummated with). (2) After their affirmative, he commanded each to divorce the woman with his brother (the wrong one). (3) He then ordered renewal of the marriage contracts.
The divorce here is a talaq to dissolve the invalid union, not a divorce of the intended spouse.
4
Extract the conditions
Identify the conditions (shurūṭ) for this hila: the consent of the affected men, a valid divorce (talaq) of the incorrectly consummated union, and a new marriage contract (nikāḥ) with proper consent and witnesses. The hila cannot be used to circumvent a clear prohibition.
Hanafis permit ḥilah as long as it does not involve fraud or violation of a ḥarām ruling.

Abu Hanifa, may Allah have mercy on him, was at a wedding in Kufa where the host had married his two sons to two sisters. The women made a mistake: each girl went to the wrong husband and he consummated with her. Sufyan gave the ruling according to Imam Ali (may Allah be pleased with him): each must pay the mahr and each woman returns to her own husband. The Imam was asked, and he said: 'Bring me the two young men.' When they were brought, he said: 'Does each of you want the one who is with him?' They said: 'Yes.' He said to each: 'Divorce the one who is with your brother.' They did so, then he ordered them to renew the marriage. Then Sufyan stood and kissed him between his eyes.

Commentary
This passage presents a legal stratagem (hila) from Imam Abu Hanifa. The problem: In a double wedding, due to confusion, each brother consummated with the sister-in-law instead of his own wife (each girl went to the wrong groom). Sufyan al-Thawri initially ruled according to the known hadith of Ali, which required the women to be separated and return to their original husbands, but Abu Hanifa devised a more practical solution. He asked each man if he wanted the woman now with him (i.e., the one already consummated with), and they agreed. Then he commanded each to issue a divorce to the woman staying with his brother (the wrong wife), thus freeing both women. Afterwards, each brother could properly marry his actual wife (the sister of the one he divorced) through a new marriage contract. This allowed the couples to stay together without sin, a clear example of legal stratagem for rectifying a confused situation.

Key Terms
الحيلة (al-ḥīlah): A permissible stratagem to achieve a lawful outcome without violating the Shari'ah, used here to rectify an inadvertent marital mix-up.
تجديد النكاح (tajdīd an-nikāḥ): Renewing the marriage contract after the proper conditions are met, ensuring the new union is valid.
