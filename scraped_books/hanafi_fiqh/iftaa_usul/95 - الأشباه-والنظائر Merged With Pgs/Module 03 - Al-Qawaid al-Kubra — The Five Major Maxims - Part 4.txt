Core Idea
Three exceptions to universal inheritance: prophets do not inherit, apostates do not inherit from Muslims, fetuses inherit but are not inherited from.

Hanafi ikhtilaf on inheritance timing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two Hanafi positions on the time of inheritance and apply the thamarah al-khilaf to a practical case.

Core Idea
The time of inheritance transfer determines the validity of an heir's condition — at the last instant of life or at death.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a key disagreement among Hanafi scholars regarding the precise moment inheritance passes from the deceased to the heirs. While both positions are within the school, they lead to different practical outcomes in cases like an heir's statement to a slave-girl. Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for a mufti when issuing fatwas on inheritance and dependent rulings.

Reflect on how these two timings differ: the last part of life is before death, while death is an event. Consider how this affects ownership transfer.
The Iraqi view considers the heir as an owner at the final moment of life; the Balkhi view only at death.

Apply to the case
Read the example of the heir's statement to the slave-girl. Work out the ruling under each position and note the thamarah al-khilaf.
Iraqi: slave-girl free; Balkhi: not free.

Recognise the rule for muftis

Commentary
This is a Hanafi disagreement on the precise moment when inheritance passes from the deceased to the heirs. The Iraqis hold it occurs at the last instant of the deceased's life (so the heir is determined at that moment). The Balkhis hold it occurs at the moment of death. According to the Balkhi view, the inheritance occurs only at death, but the statement was made before death, so the condition is not fulfilled (since the heir did not own her at the time of the statement). This affects whether the slave-girl is freed or not.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the false grandfather, identify his status as a uterine relative (dhawil arham), and apply the Hanafi rulings regarding his guardianship, property rights, paternity claims, inheritance, and qisas.

Core Idea
The false grandfather is a uterine relative, not a paternal one, with limited rights in marriage, property, and retaliation.

The Hanafi school distinguishes the false grandfather from the true paternal grandfather. This lesson defines the false grandfather and outlines the key legal consequences that follow from his classification as a uterine relative. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying rules of guardianship, inheritance, and criminal liability.

Guided Path
1
Define the false grandfather
Identify that the false grandfather is any ancestor not connected solely through males (e.g., maternal grandfather). Note that he is classified as dhawil arham (uterine relatives).
2
List the legal effects
The passage mentions: no marriage guardianship with 'asaba; no right to dispose of another's property; paternity claim not established without confirmation; inheritance as dhawil arham; and no qisas for his grandson (son of daughter).

Understand the scope of each ruling

The exception in qisas highlights the agnatic weakness.

Apply the underlying qawida
The qawida is that the false grandfather is from dhawil arham, which governs the limited rights. Apply this principle to hypothetical cases involving maternal grandfathers.
This qawida is a dabt (sub-maxim) that restricts the general category of grandfathers.

Three Exceptions to the Maxim 'The Deceased Does Not Inherit, Own, or Incur Liability'
Three exceptions to the general maxim of posthumous incapacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three exceptions to the qawida 'al-mayyit la yarith wa la yamlik wa la yadman' and apply them when issuing a fatwa involving a deceased's inheritance, ownership, or liability.

Core Idea
Three exceptions puncture the maxim that the deceased cannot inherit, own, or owe—each rooted in an act committed while alive.

This lesson explores a crucial qawida in Hanafi fiqh: a deceased person generally has no legal capacity to inherit, acquire ownership, or incur new liability. However, the texts cite three specific exceptions: the fetus inherits the ghurrah (blood-money) for a miscarriage, the deceased may acquire ownership of game caught by a pre-death trap, and the 'aqilah bears liability for wrongful acts done while alive. Understanding these exceptions is essential for a mufti to avoid incorrect generalisations.

Guided Path
1
State the qawida
Begin by clearly articulating the general rule: 'al-mayyit la yarith wa la yamlik wa la yadman'. This is the baseline presumption.

2
Identify each exception

Each exception is tied to an act (injury, trap-setting, digging) that occurred while the person was alive.
3
Understand the rationale (illah)
For each exception, grasp why it escapes the general rule: the right to ghurrah vests at the moment of injury; the trap is an extension of the hunter; the wrongful act creates a liability that survives death.
The 'aqilah steps in because the deceased cannot bear new obligations, but the wrong remains.
4
Apply in fatwa
When a case arises involving a deceased person, check if it matches one of these three patterns. If not, the general rule applies.

The Four Methods of Establishing Rulings
Explaining four methods of establishing rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four methods by which rulings are established according to Hanafi usul, and apply the distinction between istinad and tabyin in conditional divorce cases.

Core Idea
Rulings are established by restriction, conversion, attribution, or clarification — each with distinct conditions and effects.

This passage from al-Mustasfa presents a foundational usul framework every mufti must master: the four ways a legal ruling can be established. Understanding these methods is essential for correctly analyzing conditional contracts, retroactive obligations, and the timing of legal effects. We will break down each method and focus especially on the subtle difference between attribution (istinad) and clarification (tabyin).

Guided Path
1
Identify the four methods
List them: iqtisar (restriction), inqilab (conversion), istinad (attribution), tabyin (clarification). Each represents a distinct temporal relationship between the cause and the ruling.
Memorize these terms in Arabic.
2
Understand each method's mechanism
For iqtisar, the ruling is immediate upon initiation. For inqilab, a non-cause becomes a cause upon fulfillment of a condition. For istinad, the ruling is established now but retroactively attached to a past cause. For tabyin, it becomes clear that the ruling was valid from an earlier time.
Note that istinad and tabyin both involve past events but differ in discoverability and locus requirements.
3
Analyze the distinction between istinad and tabyin
Three key differences: (1) Tabyin can be known by people (e.g., by opening the abdomen), istinad cannot. (2) Istinad requires the locus to exist at the time of the cause; tabyin does not. (3) Istinad affects only existing things, tabyin affects both existing and perished.
Focus on the example of menstruation: tabyin possible because inner bleeding can be detected, while istinad is used for hidden causes like the completion of a year.
4
Apply to divorce examples
Work through the two divorce cases: (a) 'You are divorced one month before the death of so-and-so' — divorce occurs upon death, attributed to the beginning of the month (istinad). (b) 'You are divorced one month before the arrival of so-and-so' — divorce is restricted to the arrival (iqtisar), not attributed.
The distinction changes the idda starting point and consequences for intercourse and khul'.
5
Note practical consequences
The choice between istinad and tabyin affects idda calculation, revocation of divorce, payment of dower, and return of khul' compensation. A mufti must correctly identify the method to issue a sound fatwa.
Refer to al-Karabisi's nine issues for further application.

Commentary
This passage is a conceptual explanation from usul al-fiqh, quoted from al-Mustasfa, on the four ways legal rulings can be established: (1) Restriction (al-iqtisar) – a ruling occurs immediately upon initiation, e.g., pronouncing divorce or manumission. (2) Conversion (al-inqilab) – something that is not a cause becomes a cause upon fulfillment of a condition, e.g., a suspended divorce. (3) Attribution (al-istinad) – the ruling is established in the present but retroactively attributed to a past time, like ownership of guarantee upon payment, zakat upon completion of year, and nullification of purity for mustahadah and mutayammim upon seeing water or exit of time. (4) Clarification (al-tabyin) – it becomes clear that the ruling was valid from an earlier time, e.g., a conditional divorce where the condition is found later. The text then distinguishes between tabyin and istinad: tabyin can be known by people, istinad cannot; the locus must exist at the time of the cause in istinad but not in tabyin; istinad affects only existing things, tabyin affects both existing and perished. Examples with divorce illustrate the application. The correct view in the given example is istinad. This is a key usul topic for muftis when drafting fatwas involving conditions.

Key Terms
الاستناد (al-istinād): A ruling established in the present but retroactively attributed to a past time.
التبيين (al-tabyīn): Clarification that a ruling was valid from an earlier time, discoverable by people.
الاقتصار (al-iqtisār): Restriction - a ruling takes effect immediately upon the action, without retroactivity.
الانقلاب (al-inqilāb): Conversion - something that is not a cause becomes a cause upon fulfillment of a condition.
المحلية (al-maḥalliyya): The locus or subject of the ruling; its existence at the time of the cause is required for istinad but not for tabyin.

The Maxim: The Dropped Does Not Return (al-Saqit La Ya'ud)
Explanation of the maxim with furuat and a fatwa incident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-saqit la ya'ud' to distinguish between true dropping and temporary prevention, and derive rulings for new furuat.

Core Idea
A right once truly dropped cannot return; only a preventing factor, once removed, can restore it.

The Hanafi legal tradition recognizes that certain rights or rulings, once dropped, are permanently extinguished. However, not every absence of a ruling is a true dropping; sometimes the effective cause remains but is blocked by a preventing factor. This lesson unpacks the maxim 'al-saqit la ya'ud' through vivid examples from prayer, purity, contracts, and family law. By understanding the distinction between saqit and mani', you will gain a powerful tool for analyzing cases across all fiqh chapters.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its core distinction
The maxim 'al-saqit la ya'ud' means something that has been truly dropped cannot return. A true dropping occurs when the effective cause (muqtadi) for the ruling is absent. If the cause is present but blocked by a preventing factor (mani'), the ruling returns once the prevention is removed. This distinction is the analytical key.
Keep the categories saqit and mani' separate; they produce opposite outcomes.
2
Identify the effective cause (muqtadi) in a given case
For any ruling that has been removed, ask: Is the underlying cause still existing? For example, in prayer order, the cause is the obligation to pray in sequence when multiple prayers are owed. If missed due to forgetfulness, the cause remains; if missed due to few prayers, the cause drops.
The cause may be a textual command or a rational basis.
3
Classify the case: true dropping (saqit) or prevention (mani')
If the cause is absent → saqit, the ruling does not return. If the cause is present but blocked → mani', the ruling returns upon removal of the block. Use the examples in the text as templates: order dropped by few prayers is saqit; order blocked by forgetfulness is mani'.
Maintenance dropped by recalcitrance is mani', not saqit, because the cause (marriage) continues.
4
Apply the maxim to the furuat
Work through each furu' in the text: prayer order, tanning, well water, salam dissolution, maintenance, options in sales. For each, classify as saqit or mani' and state the ruling. Notice that ikhtilaf on options arises from differing views on whether the option's cause remains.
The option in sales ikhtilaf shows that identification of the cause can be contested.
5
Analyze the fatwa incident using the framework
The fatwa: A creditor discharged the debtor generally, then later the debtor acknowledged the wealth. The maxim says the debt does not return because the discharge is a true dropping of the claim. The fatwa details how acceptance or rejection of the discharge affects the outcome, but the principle remains that the dropped claim does not return.
The fatwa relies on Jami' al-Fusulayn, a later Hanafi compilation, showing how the maxim is applied in mufti's practice.

Negation of Idrak Does Not Negate Ru'yah
Definition and application of a legal maxim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the qawida that negating idrak does not entail negating ru'yah, and apply it to evaluate arguments about seeing Allah.

Core Idea
Idrak means encompassing, not seeing; Allah is seen but not encompassed.

This passage introduces a critical qawida (legal maxim) in Hanafi theology: the Quranic verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' does not negate the possibility of seeing Allah (ru'yah), but rather negates complete encompassment (idrak). Understanding this distinction is key to refuting claims that Allah cannot be seen.

Locate the word 'idrak' in the source text and understand how it is used in the verse.
The verse is from Surah al-An'am (6:103).

Al-Qadi al-Baydawi defines 'idrak' as 'ihata' (encompassing), not mere vision. This is the Hanafi interpretation.
This distinction is crucial for the ru'yah debate.

Distinguish idrak from ru'yah
Ru'yah (vision) is possible without encompassment. The negation of one does not automatically negate the other.
The Mu'tazila conflated the two.

Use this qawida to evaluate Ibn Abd al-Salam's argument: his claim that angels cannot see Allah because the verse is general fails because the verse does not negate ru'yah at all.

Critical analysis of a legal argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the methodological flaw in Ibn Abd al-Salam's use of the verse 'La tudrikuhu al-absar' to deny angels vision of Allah, and explain why his argument is not sound.

Ibn Abd al-Salam's argument fails because the verse does not negate ru'yah for believers, so there is no exception to restrict its generality.

This passage presents a critique of Ibn Abd al-Salam's claim that angels do not see Allah. The General Rule and Its Application in Sale
Defining the categories of contracts and distinguishing fasid from batil across various contract types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between fasid and batil in Hanafi fiqh and apply the distinction to sale contracts to determine ownership by possession.

Core Idea
In Hanafi sales, fasid transfers ownership by possession; batil does not.

This lesson introduces the foundational Hanafi distinction between fasid (voidable) and batil (void) contracts. While in acts of worship and marriage these terms are synonymous, in sales and other mu'amalat they have different legal effects. Understanding this distinction is crucial for a mufti to correctly classify a contract and advise on ownership, liability, and other consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope of synonymy
The text states that in ibadat and nikah, fasid and batil are synonymous in the Hanafi school. This means they produce the same rulings. However, in sale (and other mu'amalat) they are distinct. Recognize that the distinction is not universal but limited to non-ibadat/munakahat.
In nikah al-mahrim there is a dispute within the Hanafi school itself, but the general rule is synonymy.
2
Define batil and fasid in sale
The author defines batil as a contract not legislated in its essence (asl) and description (wasf). Fasid is legislated in essence but not in its description. For example, a sale of alcohol is batil because the object is not mal mutaqawwim (essence not legislated). A sale of a car without specifying a major condition (e.g., unknown price) is fasid.
Essence refers to the fundamental elements like seller, buyer, object, price. Description refers to conditions like specification, time, etc.
3
State the ruling on possession
In a batil sale, possession does not transfer ownership (لا يملك بالقبض). In a fasid sale, possession does transfer ownership (يملك بالقبض). This is the key practical difference.
This applies to sale; other contracts have parallel effects.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi variant in nikah
Although in nikah fasid and batil are generally synonymous, the text notes that in nikah al-mahrim (marriage with prohibited relatives), Abu Hanifa considers it fasid (so no hadd punishment), while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it batil (so hadd is imposed). This shows that even within synonymy, there can be differences in rulings.
The thamarah al-khilaf here is the application of hadd.
5
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
The passage ends with a note: 'The void and voidable according to the Shafi'is are synonymous except in kitabah, khul', ariyah, wakalah, shirkah, and qard, and in worship in hajj.' So Shafi'is generally treat them as synonyms, while Hanafis make a broader distinction. This is an example of a madhhab difference in legal terminology.
A mufti must be aware of such differences when issuing fatwas for a diverse community.

This passage establishes the fundamental qa'ida that the mute person's gesture is legally effective and substitutes for verbal expression in all transactions and legal acts: sales, marriages, divorces, etc. The only exception is hudud (fixed criminal punishments), including the hadd for slander (qadhf). Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ownership is a legal capacity to dispose, but not every owner can dispose.

This lesson explores the definition of ownership (al-milk) according to Hanafi jurisprudence, based on the works of Ibn al-Humām (Fatḥ al-Qadīr) and al-Qudūrī (al-Ḥāwī al-Qudsī). You will dissect the definition, understand its conditions and exceptions, and learn the various causes through which ownership is acquired. This foundational qawida governs many furūʿ in muʿāmalāt and is essential for fatwa writing.

Guided Path

These are the furūʿ derived from the qawida.

Definition and Rulings on Stability of Ownership
Defining when ownership becomes stable (istiqrar) in sale, mahr, debts, and usurpation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istiqrar al-milk and apply it to sale, dower, debts, and usurpation, identifying the conditions for stability in each case.

Core Idea
Ownership becomes stable upon specific events; stability means immunity from rescission or reduction.

The concept of istiqrar (stability) of ownership is central to determining when a transaction becomes irreversible or when ownership rights become fixed. In this passage, the author explains that stability varies by context: for a sale, it occurs upon delivery; for a dower, upon consummation or equivalent events; for debts, generally upon bindment except salam; and for usurpation, retroactively upon payment of compensation. This principle helps muftis determine when a transaction cannot be undone.

Guided Path
1
Define istiqrar al-milk
Understand that ownership stability means the right is no longer subject to being undone by subsequent events like destruction, divorce, or option.
The passage gives specific meanings for sale and dower.
2
Identify stability in sale
In a sale without option, stability is achieved upon delivery (qabd). This protects the sale from rescission due to destruction.
Option is a preventer (mani) of stability.
3
Identify stability in dower (mahr)
The dower becomes stable upon consummation, valid seclusion (khalwa), death of either spouse, or the wife's obligation of idda before the marriage. Stability prevents halving by divorce and nullification by apostasy or pre-consummation acceptance.
Stability does not depend on possession of the dower.
4
Distinguish stable and unstable debts
All binding debts are stable except salam debt (forward sale), which can be rescinded upon failure to deliver. The price of a sold item is stable because it can be substituted.
This shows that stability is transaction-specific.
5
Apply to usurpation (ghasb)
In ghasb, if the usurper pays the value (by court order), he owns the item retroactively to the time of usurpation. However, this retroactivity is limited: he does not own a child born from a usurped slave-girl, but he does own connected increases. The ownership is conditional on adjudication of value.
The retroactive ownership is not absolute; it has specific fawa'id (benefits).

Retroactive Ownership in Ghasb: The First Principle
A legal maxim stating that removal of usurped property from the owner's ownership is retroactive to the time of usurpation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of retroactive ownership in ghasb to new cases, using the qawida and its furuat.

Core Idea
Compensation for usurped property transfers ownership retroactively to the time of usurpation.

The Hanafi school has a unique principle regarding usurped property: when the usurper compensates the owner, ownership is deemed to have passed retroactively from the moment of usurpation. This may seem straightforward, but its application to third parties—like depositaries, borrowers, and sub-usurpers—is nuanced. In this lesson, we will unpack the qawida from Sharh al-Ziyadat and trace its logic through multiple branches (furu').

Commentary

This retroactivity applies between the owner and the usurper directly. However, with respect to third parties, the transfer is not automatically retroactive unless a specific legal ruling requires it. The reason is that the retroactivity is not based on the usurpation being a cause of ownership by design, but rather it is a necessary consequence of the obligation of compensation arising from the time of usurpation. If a legal ruling (like freedom of a mahram relative, or validity of a sale) depends on this retroactivity, then it applies to all parties. The text then illustrates the principle with multiple furu' (applications): cases involving deposit (wadi'a), emancipation, agency, inheritance, sub-usurpation, etc. Each application shows how the retroactive ownership affects liabilities, rights of recourse, and validity of subsequent acts. The key is that the compensation triggers the retroactive ownership, and this ownership then governs the relationships between the usurper, the owner, and third parties.

The borrower cannot lease because he owns the benefit without compensation, so he cannot transfer it for compensation; also, if he could lease, he would own more than what he owns—he owns the benefit without compensation, so he owns it similar to ownership; also, if he owned it, one of two impermissible things would follow: permanence of the loan or lack of permanence of the lease. These two explanations cover the beneficiary and the borrower, and they are equal on the preferred view: the beneficiary of a residence owns the usufruct like the borrower. It is said that one to whom use is permitted—weak—has the right to lend. The complete discussion is in Fath al-Qadir on endowments.

This passage presents two numbered maxims (al-thālitha 'ashar and al-rābi'a 'ashar) from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. In charity, ownership stabilizes as mentioned in the original discussion of ownership. Then a 'tanbīh' (notice) follows, discussing the implications for owners of usufruct (manfa'ah) regarding leasing (ijarah) and lending (i'arah). Core Idea
Fair price is the market value at the time and place, but in life-threatening situations, the obligation overrides the standard.

In Hanafi fiqh, the concept of 'fair price' (thaman al-mithl) serves as a benchmark for valuation when parties have not agreed on a specific price or when circumstances require an objective standard. You will learn to identify when the standard applies and when it yields to the necessity of saving life.

Rulings on the Mosque and the Maxim 'Harm Must Be Removed'
A list of mosque rulings derived from the maxim al-darar yuzāl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-darar yuzāl' to derive and explain the rulings on mosque sanctity from this passage.

Core Idea
The mosque's rules hinge on preventing harm; even the janazah dispute reduces to whether the harm is real or presumed.

This passage from al-Ashbah wal-Nazair compiles numerous rulings on the mosque, illustrating how the Hanafi school uses the legal maxim 'al-darar yuzāl' (harm must be removed) as a unifying principle. By tracing each ruling back to this qawida, you will see how a single maxim can generate a coherent set of rules. The passage also highlights an internal Hanafi dispute over the prohibition of janazah prayer in the mosque, showcasing how ikhtilaf over the effective cause can change the degree of prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal maxim
Recognize that nearly all the rulings in this text aim to prevent harm to the mosque. The governing maxim is 'al-darar yuzāl' (harm must be removed), a major legal maxim in the Hanafi school.
The maxim covers both direct harm (impurity, disruption) and indirect harm (disrespect that leads to neglect).
2
Classify each ruling by type of harm
Group the prohibitions: physical impurity (junub, hayd, nifas, najasa), disruption (crafts, passages, loud acts), and disrespect (spitting, phlegm, urination). Each rule prevents a specific harm.
For example, forbidding urination even in a vessel prevents the smell and disrespect that harm the mosque's purity.
3
Analyze the janazah ikhtilaf
Two explanations for prohibiting janazah prayer: fear of soiling (tahrimi) vs. mosque not built for janazah (tanzihi). The first ties directly to 'al-darar yuzāl'; the second is weaker. Allamah Qasim preferred the first.
The ikhtilaf shows how the illa affects the hukm level: tahrimi vs tanzihi.
4
Recognize exceptions driven by hardship
The exception for rain in janazah prayer illustrates that 'mashaqqa' (hardship) overrides 'darar'. This is governed by the companion maxim 'al-mashaqqa tajlib al-taysir'.
Exceptions are limited; they do not undermine the general rule.
5
Apply the maxim to a new scenario
Consider: a broken water pipe is leaking inside the mosque. Apply 'al-darar yuzāl' – the harm must be removed, so either shut off the water or repair immediately, even if it temporarily disrupts prayer.
The ruling would be that it is obligatory to remove the harm, and if not possible, the mosque may be temporarily closed.

Conditions for Waqf Supervisor: Safah and Ghafla
Distinguishing safah and ghafla as impediments to wilayah and shahada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on safah and ghafla as impediments to the appointment and testimony of a waqf supervisor, and distinguish between safah in good versus evil causes.

Core Idea
Safah blocks the nazir but does not always make him a fasiq; ghafla blocks both interdiction and testimony but for different reasons.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir examines two conditions that disqualify a person from serving as a waqf supervisor (nazir): foolishness (safah) and heedlessness (ghafla). While both are impediments, the Hanafi school makes important distinctions between safah and fisq, and between ghafla in financial matters versus testimony. Understanding these distinctions is essential for a mufti when evaluating the eligibility of a nazir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on the father who is safih
Note that the foolish father has no guardianship over his child's wealth, and by analogy, he cannot be a nazir even if he is the son of the endower.
2
Analyze the condition of 'no known sin' for the nazir
Fath al-Qadir states that the nazir must not have known sin; if sin appears, the judge removes him.
The phrase 'yukhraj' indicates the judge's role in removal.

Distinguish safah from fisq
Safah does not necessarily imply fisq because wasteful spending in good causes does not make one a sinner. The passage quotes al-Dhakhira and al-Zayla'i to define safah as habitual wastefulness, possibly in good or evil.

Ghafla (heedlessness) prevents profitable transactions, leading to interdiction according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. The heedless person is not sinful but is vulnerable to being cheated.

An Index of Hanafi Legal Maxims (Qawaid 5–19): Structure and Application
Index of Hanafi legal maxims with branches and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a Hanafi legal maxim (qa'ida) and apply the maxim 'al-kharaj bi al-daman' to a furu' case.

Core Idea
A qa'ida is a general principle; its validity lies in its furu' and istithna'at.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi legal maxims (qawaid) as cataloged in Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. The index from rule five to nineteen provides a bird's-eye view of the school's key principles. Understanding how each maxim is accompanied by its branches (furu') and exceptions (istithna'at) is essential for the mufti to derive consistent rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim's text
Read the rule statement in Arabic and English. For example, rule 10: 'الخراج بالضمان' – 'Profit is against liability'.
Pay attention to any subtle wording that defines the scope.
2
List its branches (furu')
Scan the text for specific cases mentioned under the rule. Under rule 8, branches include 'combination of two hadd penalties' and 'multiplicity of forgetfulness in prayer'.
Branches show where the maxim is applied.
3
Identify the exceptions (istithna'at)
Look for phrases like 'ما خرج عنها' (what is excepted from it). For rule 12, exceptions are mentioned after 'وما خرج عنها'.
Exceptions prevent overgeneralization.
4
Apply to a new case
For a given maxim, test its scope by considering a hypothetical scenario. For 'al-kharaj bi al-daman', consider a sale where the buyer assumes no risk but demands profit – the maxim rules it invalid.
Use the branches as analogies.
5
Note the hierarchy
Understand the rank of the maxim (kubra/wusta/sughra) if determinable. Rule 10 is a wusta maxim, governing sales and leases.
Higher ranking maxims override lower ones.
