Sub-Agent Without Permission and Delegation of Delegation
Two rulings: sub-agent without permission and delegation of delegation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on subsequent ratification of sub-agency and identify the liability chain in delegation of delegation.

Core Idea
Ratification can cure unauthorized sub-agency except in talaq and 'itaq; delegation of delegation is valid with a specific recovery chain.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents two distinct rules on agency: the effect of principal's ratification on unauthorized sub-agency, and the validity and liability structure of delegating the power to delegate. These rules are essential for muftis dealing with agency disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and note that it contains two separate rulings: one about ratifying unauthorized sub-agency, and one about the validity of delegating the power to delegate (tawkil bi al-tawkil).
2
Analyze the first ruling: ratification of unauthorized sub-agency
The default rule is that an agent cannot appoint a sub-agent without permission. However, if the principal later ratifies the sub-agent's actions, it is effective except for divorce (talaq) and emancipation ('itaq). Understand the rationale: these are personal rights that cannot be delegated without explicit original authorization.
3
Analyze the second ruling: tawkil bi al-tawkil
Delegation of the power to delegate is valid. If the principal authorizes the agent to appoint a sub-agent for a specific purchase, and the sub-agent buys, the financial liability chain flows: the first agent recovers the price from the sub-agent, the sub-agent recovers from the principal, and the first agent does not recover from the principal.
4
Understand the liability chain rationale
The first agent is not directly liable to the principal because the principal authorized the delegation; the sub-agent acts directly for the principal. Therefore, the sub-agent's recourse is against the principal, and the first agent's recourse is against the sub-agent.
5
Apply the rulings in a fatwa scenario
Consider a case where an agent hires a sub-agent without permission, the sub-agent buys goods, and the principal later approves. The purchase is valid except if it involves divorce or emancipation. In the delegated delegation scenario, if the principal authorized the agent to appoint a sub-agent, the liability follows the chain described.

Agent's Claim After Principal's Death: The Exception
Rule with exception for agent's debt claim after principal's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule and the exception regarding the acceptance of an agent's claim after the principal's death, and apply it to determine when evidence (bayyinah) is required.

Core Idea
After the principal's death, the agent's claim of collecting a debt is not accepted without evidence.

This lesson extracts a crucial rule from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: an agent is generally trusted on his oath, but a specific exception arises when the agent claims after the principal's death to have collected a debt. This exception protects the heirs' rights and prevents fraud. Understanding this rule equips the mufti to properly evaluate claims in inheritance disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle
The text states: 'الوكيل يقبل قوله بيمينه فيما يدعيه' — the agent's word is accepted with his oath regarding what he claims. This is the default rule for an agent's claim of performance.
The oath is required because the agent is a trustee (amin) and is generally believed.
2
Locate the Exception
The exception is introduced by 'إلا' (except). It applies specifically to the agent appointed to collect a debt (الوكيل بقبض الدين) when he claims after the principal's death that he had collected and paid the debt during the principal's lifetime.
The exception hinges on the timing: the claim is made after death, not during the principal's life.
3
Understand the Rationale (Ta'lil)
The exception exists because after death, the heirs have a stake in the estate. The agent's self-interest is suspected — he might falsely claim payment to avoid liability. Hence, the rule shifts from 'oath suffices' to 'evidence (bayyinah) is required'.
Bayyinah typically means two male witnesses or one male and two females; a written document may also suffice under certain conditions.
4
Apply the Rule
Given a scenario: Zayd appoints Amr to collect a debt from Bakr. Zayd dies. Amr then claims he collected the debt before Zayd's death and gave the money to Zayd. According to the exception, Amr's word is not accepted without evidence. If Amr provides two witnesses, his claim is accepted; otherwise, he may be liable to pay again to the heirs.
If the agent claims after death that he collected the debt but did not pay it, different rules apply — that is not covered here.
5
Connect to Rasm al-Mufti
This rule is part of the mufti's toolkit — it must be memorized and applied when issuing fatwas on agency and debt disputes after death. It reflects a broader Hanafi principle that after death, claims against the estate require stronger proof.
The maxim 'البيّنة على من ادعى' (proof is on the claimant) applies here.

Source Text

In fatwa practice, when a mufti encounters a general agency contract, he must check if any of these three exceptions are involved. If they are, the agent's action is invalid without separate permission.

Ruling on liability for loss based on wording of delivery instruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the creditor's instructions 'send with' and 'pay to' and correctly assign liability for loss.

Core Idea
The creditor's wording determines agency: 'pay to' transfers risk to creditor; 'send with' keeps risk on debtor.

This lesson examines a key rule from the Hanafi responsa literature: when a debtor is ordered to deliver money to a named person, the precise wording of the creditor determines who bears the risk of loss. Understanding this distinction is crucial for muftis advising on debt repayment and agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the two instructions
Note the deliverer (the debtor or his agent), the creditor, and the recipient. The creditor may say 'send with' or 'pay to'.
2
Determine whose messenger the recipient is
If the creditor says 'pay to so-and-so', the recipient becomes the creditor's agent. If he says 'send with so-and-so', the messenger remains the debtor's agent.
3
Apply the liability rule for loss
If the money perishes, the loss is on the principal of the messenger: creditor if 'pay to', debtor if 'send with'.
The text gives the example of the debtor sending money via a messenger.
4
Note the exception for the deliverer's oath
The deliverer's word is accepted with oath to prove his innocence, unless he is a usurper or debtor (madin).
This exception is important in disputes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Denial is not acknowledgment: saying 'I have no right' is valid without heir's consent; only explicit iqrar requires authorization.

When a terminally ill person makes statements about property, muftis must carefully distinguish between denying any right to himself and acknowledging a right for an heir. The former is always effective; the latter is suspended on other heirs' consent. This lesson unpacks the key rule, its exceptions, and a common error to avoid.

Guided Path
1

If the heir denies it, the manumission is from one-third. The key is that the heir's denial is treated as valid because it is a denial of the sick man's ownership, not an acknowledgment for the heir.
This shows that denial (nafiy) from the heir is effective; similarly, denial from the sick person is valid.
2
Define nafiy and iqrar
Nafiy: saying 'It is not mine' or 'I have no right in it.' Iqrar: acknowledging that a specific item or right belongs to the heir, e.g., 'This is for my son.' The general rule: iqrar for an heir in marad al-mawt is suspended on authorization from other heirs, except for specific cases.
The distinction hinges on whether the statement negates the speaker's own right or affirms the heir's right.
3

Three cases where iqrar is valid without authorization: (a) acknowledging destruction of a known trust, (b) acknowledging receipt of a trust, (c) acknowledging receipt of a debt that the heir collected as agent. The common illa (effective cause) is that there is no favoritism (ithar) towards a particular heir.
These acknowledgments do not transfer property preferentially; they merely clarify existing obligations.
4

By analogy, all acknowledgments of trusts (e.g., partnership money, loaned items) are valid without authorization. The same illa applies: no favoritism.
The author explicitly adds 'all trusts' and mentions 'even partnership property or loaned item.'

If the sick person says, 'This item is my father's property and is a trust with me,' it is treated as nafiy because it denies his ownership. This is valid without authorization, as if he said 'I have no right in it.' The key is that he attributes the property to someone else without affirming a current claim of the heir.
Saying 'This belongs to my father' is not iqrar for the father if the father is an heir? The nuance is that it does not directly acknowledge the heir's right; it negates the speaker's right.

Deferring a Right: Non-Binding and Right to Rescind in Three Cases
Rule on deferring a right and three specific applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that deferring a right is not binding and identify the three specific cases where the right holder may rescind.

Core Idea
Deferring a right is non-binding and rescindable in preemption, impotence, and litigation respite.

The passage presents a nuanced rule: when the owner of a right defers its fulfillment by the counterparty, the deferment is not binding. The right holder may rescind the deferment and revert to the original demand. This rule is illustrated with three concrete examples from different areas of fiqh: preemption, marriage with an impotent man, and litigation.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
If a right is deferred by its owner, the deferment is not binding and the owner has the right to rescind.
2
Identify the first case
In shuf'ah (preemption), if the preemptor delays the seller after making two demands to take, the preemptor may rescind the deferment and take the property.
3
Identify the second case
If a woman married to an impotent man (anın) defers her right to seek annulment after the waiting period, she may later rescind and still claim annulment.
4
Identify the third case
If a defendant asks for a respite and the plaintiff grants it, the plaintiff may later rescind the respite and demand immediate payment.
5
Understand the principle
The common thread: the deferment is a unilateral concession that does not eliminate the original right. The right holder can change their mind before the right is executed.

Sulh as a Dispute-Resolution Contract: Validity Conditions
Conditions for sulh to be valid as a dispute-resolver

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the condition that sulh must resolve an existing dispute, and identify when sulh is invalid due to the absence of dispute or due to prior oath or evidence.

Core Idea
Sulh requires a live dispute; it is invalid with a depositary after loss claim, but valid after oath.

The Hanafi school defines sulh fundamentally as a contract that removes a dispute. Therefore, its validity depends on the existence of a genuine disagreement. This lesson examines key scenarios: where no dispute exists (e.g., depositary's claim of loss), where dispute arises after an oath, and the exceptional rule about evidence after sulh, especially for a guardian over orphan's property.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Sulh is a contract that ends dispute. Hence, if there is no dispute, sulh is void.
2
Analyze the depositary case
If a depositary claims the deposited item was lost, there is no dispute until proven otherwise. Therefore, sulh with the depositary is invalid because there is no acknowledged right.
3
Analyze sulh after oath
If the defendant takes an oath denying the claim, a dispute arises. Sulh after that is valid because it removes the dispute created by the oath.
4
Examine the evidence rule
Generally, if the plaintiff produces evidence after sulh, it is not accepted because the sulh ended the dispute. Exception: if the sulh was made by a guardian on behalf of an orphan, and he later finds evidence, the evidence is accepted.
5
Review the second scenario on debt acknowledgment
If the defendant acknowledges the debt but claims he paid or was acquitted, and the plaintiff denies, a dispute exists. Sulh on that is valid, and if the plaintiff later finds evidence, it is accepted because the sulh was not to cut off the oath.

Special Rulings on Sulh: Defective Claims, Inheritances, Benefit Contracts, and Hadd Penalties
Miscellaneous special rulings on sulh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules for sulh upon denial in defective claims, distinguish between property and benefit in sulh, and identify when sulh is invalid for hadd penalties except slander.

Core Idea
Sulh upon denial after a defective claim is itself defective; sulh for benefit is a lease; sulh for hadd is invalid except slander before litigation.

The concluding section of the passage addresses nuanced scenarios. It first tackles the issue of sulh upon denial after a defective claim, reconciling a apparent contradiction between al-Qunyah and al-Hidayah. It then provides a series of specific rulings: the validity of an heir's sulh with a legatee, the difference between requesting sulh regarding a claim versus property, the limited worldly effect of sulh upon denial, the reclassification of sulh for benefit as a lease, the effect of reclaiming the sulh subject, and the general invalidity of sulh for hadd penalties except for slander before litigation.

Guided Path
1
Rule on sulh after defective claim
Sulh upon denial after a claim that is defective (e.g., due to plaintiff's contradiction) is itself defective according to al-Qunyah. However, al-Hidayah clarifies that if the claim is merely unknown (majhula), sulh upon denial is permissible. The reconciliation: the defect is due to the plaintiff's contradiction, not the condition of the claim.
2
Sulh between heir and legatee
An heir's sulh with a legatee of a usufruct is valid and is not a sale. Similarly, sulh with a legatee of an umm walad is valid, as explained in Hiyal al-Tatarkhaniyyah.
3
Distinction between claim and property in sulh request
Requesting sulh or acquittal regarding a claim (da'wa) is not an acknowledgment of the claim; but requesting it regarding property is an acknowledgment.
4
Sulh upon denial and the hereafter
Sulh upon denial only removes the dispute in this world; in the hereafter, the truth will prevail unless the sulh includes an explicit acquittal (such as 'I acquit you of the rest').
5
Sulh for benefit is a lease
If sulh involves property in exchange for a benefit (manfa'ah), it is classified as a lease (ijarah). However, sulh for the service of a claimed slave is permitted unless it is for the produce of the slave or house, which is not allowed by analogy to fruit of palm trees.
6
Reclaiming the subject of sulh
If the object of sulh is reclaimed (e.g., by a third party), the parties return to the original claim. However, if the claim was something that cannot be invalidated, like retaliation, manumission, marriage, or khul', the party who lost the object takes its value.
7
Sulh for benefits except lease
Sulh is permissible for claims of benefits except for claims related to lease (ijarah) disputes.
8
Sulh for hadd penalties
Sulh is not valid for hadd penalties, nor does it drop them. The exception is the hadd of slander (qadhf) if the sulh is made before litigation.

The Last Debt Settles the First: A Rasm al-Mufti Rule
A ruling maxim from Rasm al-Mufti on debt settlement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule 'آخر الدينين قضاء للأول', identify its conditions, and apply it to determine creditor priority in a set-off scenario.

Core Idea
The later debt between same parties settles the earlier one; the creditor becomes like other creditors.

This lesson introduces a key rule from Hanafi Rasm al-Mufti that governs the relationship between two debts between the same parties. Understanding when the later debt discharges the earlier one is crucial for muftis dealing with insolvency and set-off cases. We will unpack the rule through a concrete example from al-Jami'.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the rule text
Start by learning the Arabic maxim verbatim: آخر الدينين قضاء للأول. This is the foundational axiom.
This is a memorization anchor; precision matters.
2
Understand the condition
The rule applies only when both debts are between the same creditor and debtor, and the later debt is created without fresh cash changing hands (e.g., deferred sale price).
If a third party is involved, the rule does not apply.
3
Apply to the given example
Work through the example: debtor owes 1000 as loan, then sells an item to the same creditor for 1000 deferred. The later debt (sale price) settles the earlier loan via muqassah.
The creditor's position becomes equal to other creditors (aswah al-ghurama).
4
Derive the ruling
The consequence: set-off occurs automatically unless otherwise agreed, and the creditor loses any priority he might have had over other creditors.
This prevents the creditor from using a later transaction to secure advantage in insolvency.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Land with crops or lessee improvements has special protections; the trustee may act only after the season or term ends.

This lesson expands on the general rule by addressing three important exceptions: (1) agricultural land that is currently occupied by crops, (2) lessees who have built structures or planted trees, and (3) situations where the fair market rent increases on its own without a new bidder. Each scenario has specific rules that protect the lessee's investment while allowing the trustee to secure the waqf's interest. The passage concludes with the mufta bihi (relied-upon) opinion for independent rent increases.

Guided Path
1
Classify the land type
First, determine if the land is empty (empty of crops) or occupied (with standing crops). If empty, the same rule as buildings applies: offer to lessee, then lease to others.
2

If the land is occupied with crops (e.g., wheat, barley), it cannot be leased to someone else while the crop is standing. The current lessee must pay the increase, but he cannot be evicted until the crop is harvested.
The increase is imposed on the lessee from that time (damm al-ziyada 'alayhi).
3

If the lessee has built a building or planted trees, the rule depends on the lease type. For a monthly lease, it can be leased to another after the month ends if the lessee refuses the increase. The built structure is valued; the trustee (nazir) may buy it at that value (subject to removal rights for the waqf) or let the lessee keep it until his investment is recovered.
If the lease term is ongoing (not monthly), the property cannot be leased to another; only the increase is imposed.
4
Handle independent market rent increase
If the fair market rent (ujr al-mithl) increases on its own without any party bidding higher, the trustee has the right to cancel the lease. This is the mufta bihi (operative for fatwa).
Until the trustee cancels, the lessee continues paying the named rent (al-musamma).

The final statement 'وعليه الفتوى' (upon this is the fatwa) indicates that the ruling on independent increase is the established position of the later authorities (fatawa muta'akhkhirin).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A general reward promise is invalid; only a specific address creates a binding contract.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents three distinct rulings within the chapter of ijarah (hire/lease): the duty to deliver keys and the consequence of physical impossibility, the case of a woman leasing to her husband and then residing with him, and the validity of a reward (ju'alah) offer. Invalid Conditions in Ijarah
Rulings on defective ijarah conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify invalid conditions in an ijarah contract and apply the Hanafi rulings to determine the validity of specific terms.

Core Idea
A condition that imposes uncertain or unequal cost on one party invalidates the ijarah.

Ijarah contracts are flexible but must avoid conditions that create uncertainty (gharar) or impose an unfair burden on one party. The Hanafi school provides detailed guidance on which conditions are valid and which render the contract fasid (invalid). This lesson covers key examples from the classical text, focusing on conditions related to rent, return cost, taxes, and repair obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Read the specific condition mentioned in the contract. Is it a condition on the rent, the use of property, or the return of the object?
2
Classify the condition
Determine whether the condition imposes a known or unknown burden. Conditions with known, fixed terms (e.g., reduced rent during idle time) are generally valid. Unknown or variable terms (e.g., 'reduce such-and-such') are invalid.
3
Check for gharar or inequality
Does the condition create uncertainty (gharar) or shift an unreasonable cost to one party? For example, making the lessee pay the return cost or taxes typically invalidates the contract.
4
Apply the ruling
State the effect: valid conditions are enforceable; invalid conditions render the entire contract fasid (corrupt) unless separated. Some invalid conditions may be ignored and the contract upheld.
Hanafi: if an invalid condition is added, the contract is fasid and must be renegotiated or cancelled.

This lesson unpacks a key principle from Hanafi iftaa: the liability of a trustee who dies without disclosing the trust. The passage, from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, also provides crucial exceptions and procedural rules for when the heir or claimant may be believed. Understanding tajhil is essential for muftis dealing with trust disputes after the trustee's death.

Guided Path
1

Debt and Bequest of the Authorized Slave
Rulings on debt attachment and disposal of the authorized slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of debt attachment for the authorized slave and determine the validity of bequests and gifts involving such a slave.

Core Idea
Debt follows the authorized slave into his person; bequest and gift are valid but subject to the creditor's claim.

This passage from the Hanafi tradition sets out key rules concerning the authorized slave (al-ma'dhun). When such a slave incurs debt, it attaches to his earnings and his person, with an exception for a wage worker. The passage also addresses the consequences of a master bequeathing or gifting the slave while indebted. Understanding these rules is essential for a mufti dealing with cases of insolvent slaves and their transfers.

The creditor's right overrides the gift.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi ruling on entering a house without permission, identify the two exceptions (ghazw and retrieving property), and apply the analogy from wadi'ah.

Core Idea
Entering without permission is prohibited unless necessity allows—as in ghazw or to retrieve fallen property.

The Hanafi school strongly upholds the inviolability of the home. This lesson presents a concise ruling from the rasm al-mufti tradition, spelling out the general prohibition and two specific exceptions. Understanding these exceptions helps a mufti navigate urgent situations without violating the law.

Guided Path
1
State the General Prohibition
Identify the baseline ruling: it is not permissible (la yajuz) to enter a person's house without permission. This is the default.
2
Identify the First Exception (Ghazw)
Military expedition (ghazw) allows entry without permission due to strategic necessity. Note that this is a collective obligation, not a personal license.
3
Identify the Second Exception (Fallen Garment)
When one's garment falls into another's house and there is a fear that the owner might seize it if informed, entry is permitted to retrieve the property.
This is analogized from the law of deposits (wadi'ah).
4
Understand the Analogy from Wadi'ah
In wadi'ah, a depositor may retrieve their deposit even without express permission if urgency demands. The same illa (effective cause) – necessity to protect one's property – applies here.
The asl is the permissibility to enter a house to retrieve a deposit; the far' is the fallen garment.
5
Apply the Rule to New Cases
As a mufti, use the two exceptions as precedents. For any new scenario, test whether the urgency matches: is it a communal need (like ghazw) or a personal property urgency (like the garment)?

This exception is analogized from the chapter on deposits (wadi'ah), where urgency permits entry to retrieve property. The reference to Muniya al-Mufti is a later Hanafi fatwa collection.

Fatwa for the Ignorant Equals Ijtihad for the Mujtahid
Maxim equating reliance on fatwa to ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim 'al-fatwa fi haqq al-jahil bi-manzilat al-ijtihad fi haqq al-mujtahid', explain its meaning, and apply it to determine the validity of a layperson's action based on a fatwa from a qualified mufti.

Core Idea
Following a valid fatwa excuses the layperson just as ijtihad excuses the mujtahid.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim in Hanafi rasm al-mufti. The maxim equates the authority of a fatwa for a non-mujtahid (jahil) with the authority of ijtihad for a mujtahid. Understanding this principle is essential for appreciating how laypeople are expected to fulfill their religious duties without being able to derive rulings themselves.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the exact wording
Commit the Arabic phrase to memory. It is a short but powerful maxim.
The maxim is: الفتوى في حق الجاهل بمنزلة الاجتهاد في حق المجتهد
2
Identify the two parties
Identify the 'jahil' (non-mujtahid) and the 'mujtahid'. The maxim compares their situations.
Jahil here means anyone incapable of ijtihad, not necessarily ignorant of all knowledge.
3
Understand the analogy
Just as a mujtahid's ijtihad makes his action valid and excuses him from sin even if he errs, so a layperson's reliance on a qualified mufti's fatwa makes his action valid and excuses him.
This analogy relieves the layperson from the burden of ijtihad.
4
Apply the maxim
Given a scenario, determine whether the layperson's action is valid based on the fatwa he followed. Consider the condition that the mufti must be qualified.
If the fatwa is from an unqualified source, the maxim does not apply.

Commentary
This is a foundational maxim (qa'idah) in usul al-ifta. It establishes that a layperson (jahil) who follows a reliable fatwa is excused and his action becomes valid, just as a mujtahid's own ijtihad makes his action valid. The source reference 'Qada' likely refers to a work on qada (judicial rulings). This principle absolves the common person from sin when acting on a sound fatwa, even if it contradicts the actual ruling in reality, similar to a mujtahid being forgiven for error.

Key Terms
الجاهل (al-jahil): A non-mujtahid who does not have the capacity to derive rulings

Key Takeaway
