Biography of Ibn Nujaym: The Hanafi Scholar
Biographical introduction to the author Ibn Nujaym

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key biographical details of Ibn Nujaym, identify his major teachers and works, and explain his significance in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
Ibn Nujaym's works, especially al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and Sharh al-Manar, are cornerstones of Hanafi fiqh and usul al-fiqh.

This biographical passage introduces us to Imam Zayn al-Din ibn Ibrahim ibn Muhammad ibn Bakr, famously known as Ibn Nujaym. Born in Cairo in 926 AH, he devoted his life to the pursuit of Islamic knowledge, particularly Hanafi fiqh. He studied under a constellation of scholars and later became a leading mufti, producing several essential works that are still relied upon today. Understanding his background helps us appreciate the authority of his writings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject's full name and epithet
Find the complete name as given in the first lines. Note that 'Ibn Nujaym' is the common name.
The text begins with 'زين الدين بن إبراهيم بن محمد بن بكر' and then 'الشهير بابن نجيم'.
2
Note the birth date and place
Locate the phrase 'وُلِدَ بالقاهرة سنة ٩٢٦ هجرية' – this gives his birthplace and year.
926 AH corresponds to 1520 CE.
3
List his teachers and the subjects they taught
Divide into fiqh teachers and Arabic/rational sciences teachers. Notice the names and their madhhabs where mentioned.
Some teachers were Hanafi, others Maliki, reflecting a broad education.
4
Identify his prominent students
The text mentions his brother Umar and others. Recognize their contributions (e.g., Umar authored al-Nahr).
Students often become scholars themselves, continuing the chain of transmission.
5
Enumerate his major works
The passage lists four works: al-Rasa'il al-Zayniyya, al-Fawa'id al-Zayniyya, al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, and Sharh al-Manar. Note their fields.
Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a fiqh commentary; Sharh al-Manar is on usul al-fiqh.

Introduction to the Author
The 'Allamah is the name of some of his ancestors.
Shaykh Zayn al-Din ibn Ibrahim ibn Muhammad ibn Bakr, famous as Ibn Nujaym, engaged in knowledge from the beginning of his life and persisted in it until Allah opened for him, so he gave fatwa and became unique. He was born in Cairo in the year 926 AH and studied under its scholars, gaining fiqh from Shaykh Amin al-Din ibn Abd al-Aal al-Hanafi, Shaykh Qasim ibn Qutlubugha, al-Burhan al-Kurki, Shaykh Abi al-Fayd al-Sulami, Shaykh Sharaf al-Din al-Bulqini, and Shaykh al-Islam Ahmad ibn Yunus, famous as Ibn al-Shalabi. As for Arabic and rational sciences, he studied them with many groups, including: Shaykh Nur al-Din al-Daylami al-Maliki and Shaykh Shuqayr al-Maghribi. Many people studied under him and benefited from him, among them his brother Umar (author of al-Nahr), the 'Allamah Muhammad al-Ghazzi (author of al-Minhaj), Shaykh Muhammad al-Ilmi, and Shaykh Abd al-Ghaffar (Mufti of Jerusalem). As for his works: he authored books, treatises, events, and occurrences in Hanafi fiqh from the beginning of his career, which are needed in our time. Among them: 1. al-Rasa'il al-Zayniyya. 2. al-Fawa'id al-Zayniyya fi Fiqh al-Hanafiyya. 3. al-Bahr al-Ra'iq Sharh Kanz al-Daqa'iq. 4. Sharh al-Manar fi al-Usul.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical introduction to the author, Ibn Nujaym. It begins by explaining that the title 'Allamah (the highly learned) is associated with his ancestors. His full name is Zayn al-Din ibn Ibrahim ibn Muhammad ibn Bakr, commonly known as Ibn Nujaym. He was born in Cairo in 926 AH (1520 CE) and studied under prominent Hanafi scholars such as Shaykh Amin al-Din ibn Abd al-Aal, Shaykh Qasim ibn Qutlugha, and Shaykh al-Islam Ahmad ibn Yunus. He also studied Arabic and rational sciences under scholars from other madhhabs. He became a leading mufti and teacher; his students included his brother Umar (author of al-Nahr al-Fa'iq) and Muhammad al-Ghazzi (author of al-Minhaj al-Sawi). His most famous works include al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, and Sharh al-Manar on usul al-fiqh. These works are central to the Hanafi tradition and remain in use today.

Caution Against Unauthorized Abridgment: The Fate of al-Hakim
Biographical anecdote with moral warning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the consequences of unauthorized abridgment of canonical Hanafi texts and apply the lesson to contemporary scholarly practice.

Core Idea
Tampering with a scholar's work without authority invites divine displeasure and worldly harm.

This lesson presents a powerful biographical anecdote from the Hanafi tradition. Al-Hakim al-Jalil, a respected scholar, compiled an abridgment of Imam Muhammad's works. Although his intentions may have been practical, he faced a tragic end after Imam Muhammad appeared to him in a dream and rebuked him. The story underscores the sanctity of scholarly transmission and the responsibility of those who handle the fiqh legacy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key action
Read what al-Hakim did: he abridged Imam Muhammad's books by removing repetitions and lengthy passages, claiming jurists are lazy.
Note that he did not seek permission from the original author or later authorities.
2
Understand the dream encounter
Imam Muhammad appears in a dream and asks why al-Hakim cut his books. Al-Hakim explains his rationale, but Imam Muhammad becomes angry and curses him.
The curse mirrors the action: 'cut' for 'cut'.
3
Connect the punishment to the action
Al-Hakim is later killed by Turks who tie him between two trees and tear him in half. This literal 'cutting' is seen as divine retribution.
The anecdote illustrates a principle: tampering with sacred knowledge can lead to appropriate punishment.
4
Extract the moral for the mufti
A scholar must not conceal knowledge nor alter canonical texts without due authority. The story warns against prioritizing worldly ease over fidelity to the tradition.
This aligns with adab al-mufti: respecting the integrity of primary sources.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the importance of preserving the integrity of early Hanafi texts and identify the consequences of unauthorized abridgment.

Core Idea
Tampering with the transmitted texts of the pious predecessors invites divine displeasure.

This concluding passage of a Hanafi work recounts a dramatic anecdote that serves as a stark warning. A scholar compiled al-Muntaqa from 300 volumes but then abridged the works of Imam Muhammad. The consequences were severe, teaching us about the sanctity of the transmitted texts.

وفي آخر تلقيح المحبوبي قال الحاكم الجليل نظرت في ثلاثمائة جزء مثل الأمالي ونوادر ابن سماعة حتى انتقيت كتاب المنتقى، وقال حين ابتلي بمحنة القتل بمرو من جهة الأتراك : هذا جزاء من آثر الدنيا على الآخرة والعالم متى أخفى علمه وترك حقه خيف عليه أن يمتحن بما يسوءه . وقيل كان سبب ذلك أنه لما رأى في كتب محمد مكررات وتطويلات خلسها وحذف مكررها فرأى محمدا رحمه الله تعالى في منامه. فقال: لم فعلت هذا بكتبي ؟ فقال : لأن في الفقهاء كسالى فحذفت المكرر وذكرت المقرر تسهيلاً فغضب وقال : قطعك الله كما قطعت كتبي فابتلي بالأتراك حتى جعلوه على رأس شجرتين فتقطع نصفين رحمه الله تعالى . (تم) الكتاب والحمد لله الذي بنعمته تتم الصالحات وله الفضل والمنة في الأولى والآخرة .
And at the end of Talqih al-Mahbubi, al-Hakim al-Jalil said: I looked into three hundred volumes like al-Amali and Nawadir Ibn Sam'ah until I selected the book al-Muntaqa. And he said when he was afflicted with the ordeal of being killed in Merv by the Turks: This is the recompense of one who preferred this world over the Hereafter. And whenever a scholar hides his knowledge and abandons his right, it is feared that he will be tested with what harms him. And it was said that the reason for that was that when he saw in the books of Muhammad repetitions and lengthy passages, he removed them and omitted the repetitions. Then he saw Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on him) in his dream. He said: Why did you do this to my books? He replied: Because the jurists are lazy, so I omitted the repetitions and mentioned the established points to facilitate. Then he became angry and said: May Allah cut you as you cut my books. So he was afflicted by the Turks until they placed him on the tops of two trees and he was cut in half. May Allah have mercy on him. (The book is completed) Praise be to Allah, by whose grace good deeds are completed, and to Him belongs grace and gratitude in the first and the last.

Commentary
This passage concludes the book with a sobering biographical anecdote. It relates the story of the compiler of al-Muntaqa, who extracted rulings from three hundred volumes including al-Amali and Nawadir of Ibn Sam'ah. He later met a tragic death at the hands of the Turks. The reason given is that he abridged the works of Imam Muhammad by removing repetitions and lengthy passages to make them easier for lazy students. Imam Muhammad appeared to him in a dream and cursed him, leading to his execution by being torn in two. This story serves as a warning about the sanctity of the transmitted texts and the dangers of tampering with them. In the Hanafi tradition, the works of Imam Muhammad are considered foundational, and any unauthorized abridgment or alteration is seen as a serious transgression.

Key Terms

المنتقى (al-Muntaqa): The selected work compiled by al-Hakim al-Jalil from 300 volumes.
