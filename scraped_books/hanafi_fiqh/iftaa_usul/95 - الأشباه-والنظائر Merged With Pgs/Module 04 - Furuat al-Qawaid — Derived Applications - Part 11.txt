Prohibition of Preliminaries When Intercourse is Forbidden
The rule that preliminaries are forbidden when intercourse is forbidden

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'إذا حرم الوطء حرمت دواعيه' to identify when preliminaries to intercourse are prohibited and when exceptions apply.

Core Idea
Preliminaries follow intercourse: if intercourse is haram, its preliminaries are haram unless an exception applies.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi legal maxim: 'When intercourse is prohibited, its preliminaries are also prohibited.' This principle governs situations where the primary act is forbidden, extending the prohibition to acts that lead to it. However, the law provides exceptions where the ruling is relaxed due to necessity or ease. Understanding this maxim is essential for a mufti issuing fatwas on fasting, hajj, and marital relations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its scope
State the maxim in Arabic and English. Note that it applies across multiple chapters of fiqh: fasting, hajj, i'tikaf, etc.
2
List the exceptions where preliminaries are permitted
From the text, three exceptions: menstruation, postpartum bleeding, and fasting for a person who feels secure (amn). In these cases, preliminaries are allowed if they do not lead to intercourse.
3
Identify absolute prohibitions
The text states that in i'tikaf, ihram (absolutely), zihar, and istibra', preliminaries are absolutely prohibited. No exception for self-control.
4
Analyze the condition of 'amn' in fasting
In fasting, the exception applies only to one who feels secure from falling into actual intercourse. If fear exists, preliminaries remain prohibited.
5
Apply the maxim to a concrete scenario
A mufti is asked: Can a fasting husband kiss his wife? Apply the maxim: Intercourse is prohibited during fasting, but the exception applies if he is secure from arousal leading to intercourse. If not secure, kissing is prohibited.

Exceptions to the Rule: No Mahr or Hadd Without Ownership
Lists exceptions to a general fiqh rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the seven exceptions to the Hanafi rule that intercourse without ownership requires mahr or hadd, and apply them to similar modern cases for fatwa purposes.

Core Idea
Intercourse outside ownership either requires mahr or triggers hadd, except in seven specific scenarios where shubha blocks both.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir lists exceptions to a general Hanafi rule. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for a mufti when assessing cases involving intercourse outside of marriage or slave ownership. The exceptions reveal that when shubha (legal doubt) is present, both hadd and mahr may be dropped, preserving the integrity of the law.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Begin by recalling the Hanafi principle: any intercourse without valid ownership (milk al-yamin) or a valid marriage contract triggers either the obligation of mahr (if shubha is present) or the punishment of hadd (if it is clear zina).
The general rule is the backdrop against which the exceptions are understood.
2

Identify each case mentioned in the passage. Some cases are incomplete due to OCR issues; rely on the commentary and known fiqh.

For each case, pinpoint the source of doubt (shubha) that prevents the application of hadd or mahr. For example, in case 1, the marriage was valid under their previous religion; in case 4, the slave mistakenly believed he had a right to intercourse.
Shubha can be of various types: shubha fi al-mahall (doubt about the object) or shubha fi al-‘aqd (doubt about the contract).

Use the underlying shubha to analogise to modern cases. For instance, case 5 (harbiyya) can be mapped to intercourse with a non-Muslim woman in a non-Muslim country where no marriage is recognised. Consider how fatwa would be issued.
Ensure the shubha is legally recognised in the Hanafi school.

The Eighth Maxim: No Mahr Upon Intercourse with Permission Under Shubha
States a specific case where no mahr is due due to shubha.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of the eighth maxim, explain why shubha negates mahr, and apply the maxim to analogous cases in family law.

Core Idea
Shubha (mistaken permissibility) drops the mahr obligation even if permission was given.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: if a mortgagor (raahin) permits the mortgagee (murtahin) to have intercourse with the mortgaged slave-girl, and the mortgagee does so believing it is lawful, then no mahr is due. The ruling illustrates how shubha prevents the financial obligation of mahr, even though the act itself remains prohibited. The author, Ibn Nujaym, notes he has not seen this explicitly in earlier texts, indicating it is a derived application of Hanafi principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Parties
The act is intercourse (wat') between the mortgagee (murtahin) and the mortgaged slave-girl. The mortgagor (raahin) grants permission. The mortgagee acts ظانًا الحلّ (thinking it is permissible).
Permission alone does not transfer ownership or make the act lawful; the mortgagee only has a possessory right.
2
Determine the Default Ruling
Intercourse with a slave-girl not owned or not in a valid marriage is zinā, which is ḥarām and incurs ḥadd (punishment) and mahr (if a free woman).
Here the mortgagor is the owner; his permission does not make the act permissible per se because the mortgagee lacks ownership.
3
Apply the Shubha Principle
The mortgagee’s mistaken belief of permissibility (ẓann al-ḥill) creates a shubha. In Hanafi fiqh, shubha prevents both the ḥadd penalty and the financial obligation of mahr. Therefore, no mahr is due.
Shubha is a 'doubt of permissibility' that averts punishments and financial penalties.
4
Analyze the Author’s Remark
Ibn Nujaym says ولم أره الآن (‘I have not seen it now’). This indicates the ruling is not directly transmitted from the early masters but deduced from principles, reflecting the ijtihād of later jurists.
Such rulings rely on qawāʿid and extension of existing rules, which is common in later Hanafi works.
5
Extrapolate to Analogous Cases
Consider a similar scenario: a man has intercourse with a slave-girl he co-owns with another, believing it permissible. The Hanafi ruling would be no mahr due to shubha. Also, if a woman mistakenly believes a marriage contract is valid and consummates, no mahr? Actually, in Hanafi, if the marriage is invalid but both parties had shubha, the mahr is still obligatory? Be careful: this case is specific to pledge.
The maxim applies strictly to the rahn arrangement; extension requires confirming the ʿilla (effective cause) of shubha.

General Rule and Two Exceptions: Impotent Husband and Ila'
General rule and two exceptions about burden of proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default Hanafi rule when spouses dispute intercourse and identify the two exceptions involving an impotent husband and a husband who made a vow of abstinence (ila').

Core Idea
In disputes about intercourse, the husband's word is accepted — unless the wife is a thayyib and he is impotent or he made ila'.

This lesson examines the foundational principle in Hanafi family law: when a husband and wife disagree about whether intercourse occurred, who is believed? The default answer is the husband, with his oath, but there are important exceptions that protect the wife's rights in specific circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The text states that when spouses differ about intercourse, the default is that the husband's statement is accepted (with his oath), except in specific cases. This is the qawida (maxim).
The phrase 'فالقول لنا' indicates the husband's word is taken.
2
Understand the first exception: the impotent husband
If the husband is impotent (innin) and claims penetration while the wife denies, and the wife is a thayyib (previously married), the husband's claim that she is a virgin is accepted. A virgin is expected to bleed, making her denial less credible.
Thayyib loses virginity by marriage, not penetration; a virgin would have physical evidence.
3
Understand the second exception: the husband who made ila'
If the husband made a vow of abstinence (ila') and claims he consummated before the period expired, his word is accepted with his oath. This prevents the vow from automatically triggering a divorce.
Ila' period is four months; if he claims consummation, the vow dissolves.
4
Apply the rule to practical scenarios
In both exceptions, the wife's denial is not taken because of specific circumstances. The general rule still applies: the husband is believed, but the exceptions clarify when the wife's denial is not given weight.
These are furuat (applications) of the maxim.

Third Exception: Divorce Timing and Mahr/Iddah Dispute
Complex exception to the general rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the wife's word is accepted regarding mahr when she claims consummation because she observes iddah, while the husband's word is accepted for other matters.

Core Idea
If the wife claims divorce after consummation (full mahr) and husband says before, the wife's word is taken for mahr due to iddah, but husband's for other matters.

This lesson delves into a complex application of the burden of proof. When a wife claims she was divorced after consummation and is entitled to full mahr, and the husband says it was before, who is believed? The answer is not uniform: the wife is believed regarding the mahr because she observes iddah, which implies consummation, but the husband is believed regarding permissibility of marrying her relatives. This nuanced approach shows the Hanafi method of balancing conflicting claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
The wife says: 'You divorced me after consummation, so I get full mahr.' The husband says: 'I divorced you before consummation, so you get half mahr.'
The key legal consequence: full mahr vs. half mahr.
2
Recognize the key factor: iddah
The wife must observe iddah only if consummation occurred. Since she is observing iddah (as required), her claim that consummation happened is supported by her action.
Iddah is a waiting period after divorce to ensure no pregnancy; it is obligatory only after consummation.
3
Apply the rule for mahr
Because her iddah implies consummation, her word is accepted regarding the mahr amount, i.e., she gets full mahr.
The text says: 'فالقول لها لوجوب العدَّة عليها'.
4
Apply the rule for other matters
For matters like nafaqah, housing during iddah, and permissibility of marrying her daughter or sister, the husband's word is accepted. Why? Because these are not directly proven by iddah.
Text: 'وله في المهر والنفقة والسكنى في العدة وفي حلّ بنتها وأربع سواها وأختها للحال'.
5
Consider the issue of lineage
If she gives birth to a child after a possible period, the child is attributed to the husband. He cannot deny paternity except by li'an (mutual cursing).
Text: 'فلو جاءت بولد لزمن تحتمله ثبت نسبه'.

Commentary
This passage provides detailed rules for when a husband and wife disagree about whether intercourse occurred, a topic with significant legal consequences for mahr, 'iddah, and lineage. The default rule (according to the Hanafi school) is that the husband is believed, presumably because the default is non-occurrence. However, there are several exceptions. The first exception involves an impotent man (inninent) who claims penetration; the wife's denial is not accepted if she is a thayyib (previously married). The second involves a husband who made a vow of abstinence (muwalli); his claim that he had intercourse before the period expires is accepted. The third is a complex case: the wife claims divorce after consummation (full mahr), the husband says before (half mahr). The wife's word is accepted for mahr because she has to observe 'iddah, which would only be required if consummation occurred. However, the husband's word is accepted for other matters like permissibility of marrying her relatives. If a child is born, it is attributed to him, and if he denies paternity, he must perform li'an. The fourth exception: a woman divorced thrice and then married another; if she claims the second husband consummated, she is believed for the purpose of remarrying the first (since three divorces require consummation of second marriage to become halal), but not for mahr. The fifth: if the husband made divorce conditional on not having intercourse today, and they differ on whether it happened, the husband is believed because he denies the condition that would trigger divorce. This ruling is supported by al-Kanz.

Key Terms
العدة (al-'iddah): The waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or death of husband, to ensure no pregnancy.
المهر (al-mahr): Mandatory bridal gift from husband to wife upon marriage.
اللعان (al-li'an): Mutual cursing between spouses when husband accuses wife of adultery or denies paternity.

Fourth and Fifth Exceptions: Thrice-Divorced Woman and Conditional Divorce
Two additional exceptions to the general rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a thrice-divorced woman's claim of consummation with a second husband is accepted for remarriage to the first but not for mahr, and that in conditional divorce the husband is believed regarding the condition.

Core Idea
For a thrice-divorced woman, her claim of consummation with a second husband is accepted for halal, not for mahr; in conditional divorce, husband's denial of condition is accepted.

This lesson covers two more exceptions to the general burden of proof rule. The first involves a woman divorced three times who then marries another man; her claim that the second husband consummated the marriage is accepted for the purpose of making her halal for the first husband, but not for claiming full mahr from the second. The second exception deals with a conditional divorce: if a husband says, 'You are divorced if I do not have intercourse today,' and they differ on whether it happened, the husband's word is accepted (he denies the condition). Both cases highlight the principle that the burden of proof shifts based on legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of the fourth exception
A woman divorced three times (talaq bain kubra) cannot remarry her first husband until she marries another man, consummates that marriage, and then gets divorced. If she claims the second husband consummated, who is believed?
The purpose: to determine if she is halal for the first husband.
2
Apply the rule: her word is accepted for halal, not for mahr
The wife's claim is accepted for the purpose of remarriage to the first husband, because the default is that she is not halal, and she is the one who can know. However, for claiming full mahr from the second husband, she is not believed because the default rule (husband's word) applies.
Text: 'فالقول لها لحلها للمطلق لا لكمال المهر'.
3
Understand the context of the fifth exception
A husband says: 'You are divorced if I do not have intercourse with you today.' Later, the wife claims he did not have intercourse (so divorce occurred), and the husband claims he did (so no divorce). Who is believed?
This is a conditional divorce (talaq mu'allaq).
4
Apply the rule: husband's word accepted
The husband denies the existence of the condition (intercourse happened), so his word is accepted. The default is that the condition did not occur, and the husband's oath supports that.
Text: 'فالقول له لإنكاره وجود الشرط' and reference to Al-Kanz.

Classification of Contracts According to Bindingness
Division of contracts according to bindingness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given Hanafi contract as lazim from both sides, ja'iz from both sides, or ja'iz from one side and lazim from the other, by applying the tripartite qawida.

Core Idea
Contracts in Hanafi fiqh are either binding on both sides, optional on both, or binding on one side only.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir lays out a fundamental classification of contracts according to their binding nature. Understanding this classification is essential for a mufti to determine the rights of parties to rescind or terminate a contract. The passage lists numerous contracts under each category and hints at exceptions that must be remembered.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three categories
State the three types: lazim min al-janibayn (binding from both sides), ja'iz min al-janibayn (permissible/optional from both sides), ja'iz min ahad al-janibayn wa lazim min al-akhar (optional from one side, binding from the other).
2
Identify the act and its ruling
For each contract listed, determine which category it belongs to. Use the passage as a reference.
E.g., sale is lazim, partnership is ja'iz.
3
Recognize the exceptions
Note that the passage mentions exceptions for certain contracts (like sale, ijarah) that are detailed elsewhere. This classification is the general rule, but specific cases may have nuance.
4
Apply to new cases
Given a contract not listed (e.g., ju'alah), use analogy (qiyas) to determine its classification based on similar contracts.

Commentary
This passage classifies contracts (uqud) in Hanafi fiqh based on their binding nature. Contracts can be: 1) Binding (lazim) from both sides – neither party can unilaterally rescind. This includes sale (bay'), currency exchange (sarf), salam, tawliyah, murabahah, wadi'ah, tashrik, sulh, hawalah, ijarah, hibah after possession (with obstacles), sadaq, khul', and marriage free from options (option of puberty and option of emancipation). Exceptions to the binding nature of some of these are noted (e.g., two exceptions in sale, one in ijarah) which are detailed in the 'al-fawa'id' section. 2) Permissible (ja'iz) from both sides – either party may rescind. This includes shirkah, wakalah, mudarabah, wasiyyah, ariyah, wadi'ah, qard, qada', and all other authorities except the supreme imamah. 3) Permissible from one side and binding from the other – e.g., rahn: permissible for mortgagee to release, binding on mortgagor; kitabah: permissible for slave to cancel, binding on master; kafalah: permissible for claimant to release, binding on guarantor; aman contract: permissible for harbi to revoke, binding on Muslim. The author adds a note regarding tawliyat al-qada' (appointment of a judge): it is ja'iz from both sides, meaning the sultan can dismiss without cause and the judge can resign. However, for guardianship over orphan's property via wasiyyah (executorship appointed by the deceased), it is binding on both sides after the testator's death, with limited exceptions for dismissal or resignation.

Key Terms
لازم (lazim): A contract that is binding and cannot be unilaterally rescinded.
جائز (ja'iz): A contract that is permissible (not binding) and can be rescinded by either party.

Classification of Contracts: Nafidh, Mawquf, Lazim, etc.
Classification of sales into six types per Hanafi contract theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six types of contracts and apply the classification to determine the legal effect of a given contract.

Core Idea
A contract's legal status depends on execution, bindingness, and validity: nafidh, mawquf, lazim, ghayr lazim, fasid, batil.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents a foundational classification of contracts (uqud) in Hanafi fiqh. The author lists six categories that determine a contract's enforceability and permanence. Understanding these terms is essential for any mufti when evaluating the legal consequences of a sale or other transaction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six categories
List them: nafidh, mawquf, lazim, ghayr lazim, fasid, batil.
2
Distinguish between validity and effect
Nafidh and mawquf relate to effectiveness; lazim/ghayr lazim to bindingness; fasid/batil to validity.
These are not mutually exclusive; e.g., a contract can be nafidh and lazim.
3
Understand mawquf as suspended
Mawquf does not mean invalid; it is valid but contingent on ratification.
Example: sale by a guardian without court approval.
4
Note the source enumeration
Al-Khulasa lists 15 mawquf sales; the author added 8, making 23 total.
This shows the evolving nature of fatwa compilations.
5
Apply the classification
Given a contract scenario, classify it using these categories.
Practice with examples from the application bridge.

تقسيم : في العقود البيع نافذ، وموقوف ولازم وغير ،لازم وفاسد، وباطل. وضبط الموقوف في الخلاصة في خمسة عشر، وزدت عليها ثمانية.

Commentary
This is a brief classification of sales (and by extension other contracts) according to their legal status. The author lists six types: nafidh (executed and effective), mawquf (suspended on consent), lazim (binding, not revocable), ghayr lazim (non-binding, revocable), fasid (defective, valid in essence but not in description), and batil (void, lacking essential elements). The author notes that the suspended (mawquf) variety is specifically enumerated in al-Khulasa as fifteen types, and he has added eight more. This serves as a definitional framework for understanding contract validity in Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
نافذ (nāfidh): A contract that is effective immediately without need for consent of another
موقوف (mawqūf): A contract whose effectiveness is suspended on the consent of someone (e.g., the guardian)
لازم (lāzim): A binding contract that cannot be unilaterally revoked
غير لازم (ghayr lāzim): A non-binding contract that may be revoked by one or both parties

Core Idea
Writing a lease without verbal utterance is invalid; acknowledgment and mahr documents are valid by writing alone.

In Hanafi fiqh, contracts generally require verbal offer and acceptance (ijab wa qabul). However, certain documents — like acknowledgment of a debt or fixing mahr — are valid through writing alone. Guided Path

Identify the key elements
The act is ordering a scribe to write a lease contract and having witnesses, but no verbal contract is uttered. The ruling is that the contract is not valid (la yan'aqid).

In Hanafi law, contracts (uqud) that create obligations require explicit verbal expression. Written documents alone do not create the contract; they are mere evidence.

5
Apply to other contracts
Analogous contracts (sale, marriage, etc.) also require verbal offer and acceptance. Writing alone is insufficient unless there is a custom or necessity (e.g., for a mute person).
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Gesture vs. Verbal Specification: The Hanafi Maxim
Maxim on precedence between gesture and verbal specification when combined

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim on gesture and verbal specification to cases in sale, marriage, and prayer, and identify where Abu Hanifa's exception on genus classification changes the ruling.

Core Idea
When gesture and speech conflict, sameness of genus decides which one controls the contract.

Contracts often combine a gesture (pointing to an object) and a verbal description (naming). Which one should the law follow? The Hanafi school provides a clear maxim from Al-Hidayah: if the named item is of the same genus as the indicated item, the contract attaches to the indicated item; if different, to the name. This lesson unpacks this maxim and its application across multiple chapters of fiqh, including Abu Hanifa's unique positions on genus classification.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Write the maxim in your own words: when gesture and verbal specification come together, if the specified item is of the same genus as the indicated item, the contract attaches to the indicated item; if different genus, to the specified item.
From Al-Hidayah, chapter of dower.
2
Understand the rationale
Naming identifies the essence (mahiyyah), while gesture identifies the individual entity (dhat). When both align in genus, gesture is followed because the essence is present in the individual; when they differ, naming is stronger because the indicated item does not contain the named essence.
Gem example illustrates: specifying 'ruby' but indicating glass fails due to genus difference.
3
Apply the maxim
Work through the ruby/glass example: same genus (both gemstones) → valid; different genus (ruby vs glass) → void. Then apply to the cloth example: Hirwi vs Marwi – same genus (cloth) but different description – dispute between void and corrupt.
The furuat show consistency across sale, marriage, and prayer.
4
Note Abu Hanifa's exception
Abu Hanifa considered wine and vinegar as one genus, and free person and slave as one genus. This means in a marriage dower specifying 'jar of vinegar' but indicating wine, the contract attaches to the indicated (wine) and she gets the like dower. Similarly for slave/free.
Other madhahib may classify differently.
5
Work through prayer and oath furuat
In congregation: intending Zayd by name but finding Amr invalid; intending the imam in the mihrab (description) and finding Amr valid. In oaths: swearing not to speak to 'this boy' – boy grows old, oath broken because gesture (individual) prevails over description (youth).
These follow the same logic: when same genus (human), gesture controls; description of youth is non-essential.

If the genus differs, the contract attaches to the specification, as the specification is merely similar to the indicated item and not a part of it. Naming (specification) is stronger in defining the essence (ماهية), while gesture defines the particular entity (ذات). The example of buying a gem illustrates: specifying 'ruby' but indicating 'glass' – different genus, so contract void; specifying 'red ruby' but getting 'green' – same genus, contract valid (only description differs). The commentators note this principle is agreed upon across all contracts (nikah, sale, ijarah, etc.), but Abu Hanifa made exceptions by considering wine and vinegar as the same genus, and free and slave as the same genus. Therefore in a marriage with a dower specified as 'this jar of vinegar' but indicating wine, or 'this slave' but indicating a free person, the contract attaches to the indicated, and the like dower (مهر المثل) is due. Many furuat are given: in sales, specifying a 'Hirwi cloth' but indicating 'Marwi' – there is a dispute within the school whether the contract is void (باطل) or corrupt (فاسد). Analogous to the gem ruling: male and female humans are different genera (so specifying 'man' but indicating 'woman' leads to nullity), but in animals male and female are one genus (so if description differs, the buyer has an option). In prayer following (اقتداء), if a person intends to follow Imam Zayd but it is 'Amr, it's invalid; but if he intended the imam standing in the mihrab (i.e., indicated by description/place) and it was actually 'Amr, it's valid. Similarly, following a 'young man' but he is old is invalid; but following an 'old man' who is young is valid because 'young' may be called 'old' due to knowledge. Analogy: funeral prayer intending a man but finding a woman is invalid. Al-‘Ayni deduced from this that consideration is by verbal specification (naming) in our school. Marriage examples: specifying my daughter Fatima but pointing to Ayesha – if the father says the name incorrectly but points to the correct woman and the groom accepts, it's valid. If the father says 'this boy' pointing to his daughter, valid due to gesture. Variance in descriptions (Arab/Ajami, old/young, white/black) etc. – validity relies on gesture. Oaths: swearing not to speak to 'this boy' – him growing up does not affect the oath, breaking it is triggered. Similarly for lamb growing into a ram. The passage ends with a hadith reference from Bukhari and Muslim.

Key Terms
تسمية (tasmiyah): Verbal specification or naming of an item in a contract
مشار إليه (mushār ilayhi): The object indicated by gesture (e.g., pointing)
جنس (jins): Genus or type of an object, determining legal effect when conflicting specifications arise

Core Idea
In Hanafi oath law, the binding force of an oath depends on whether the motivating description is an essential attribute or an accidental label.

Oaths often describe the object of the oath with a specific attribute, such as 'this boy' or 'this slave of Zayd.' When that attribute changes, does the oath still bind? The Hanafi school answers by distinguishing between descriptions that are intrinsic to the person and those that are merely extrinsic relationships. This lesson walks through four classical cases that illustrate the rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on ownership transfer during a stipulation option and identify the analogous case of the apostate.

Core Idea
Ownership during a stipulation option is suspended — increments follow ownership retroactively.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir examines the moment ownership transfers in a sale with khiyar al-shart. When does the lessor own the rent? The gradual ownership rule in ijāra
Hanafi ruling on ownership of rent in a lease contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four ways the lessor owns rent in Hanafi law and apply this qāʿida to determine the validity of manumission and subleasing.

Core Idea
Rent ownership in lease is gradual: not by contract, but by receipt, ability, prepayment, or condition.

In a lease (ijāra), the rent (ujra) is not owned instantly like the price in a sale. This seems counterintuitive, but it flows from the nature of usufruct (manāfiʿ) which comes into existence gradually. This qāʿida from al-Ashbāh wa al-Naẓāʾir clarifies exactly when ownership transfer occurs and what practical consequences follow — including why the lessor cannot free a slave given as rent before the lease begins, and why the lessee cannot sublease back to the landlord.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Read the first sentence: 'The lessor does not own the rent by the contract itself.' This is the core qāʿida. Note that it opposes the default rule in sale, where ownership of the price transfers immediately.
The qāʿida is negating immediate ownership by contract alone.
2
List the four modes of ownership
Ownership occurs through: (a) actual receipt (istiʿfāʾ), (b) ability to receive (tamakkun — when the lessor puts the lessee in possession), (c) prepayment (taʿjīl), or (d) a contractual stipulation (sharṭ). Memorise these.
Each mode is sufficient; they are alternatives.
3
Apply to the slave manumission case
If the rent is a specific item (e.g., a slave), the lessor does not fully own it until one of the four modes occurs. Therefore, manumission before that is invalid because he is not the owner.
This shows ownership is a prerequisite for effective disposition.
4
Apply to the lessee's ownership of usufruct
Similarly, the lessee does not own the usufruct immediately. Usufruct arises gradually over time. Hence the lessee cannot sublease the property back to the lessor, because that would require owning the usufruct to lease it.
Contrast with sale: the sold item exists, so ownership transfers instantly.
5
Derive the subleasing ruling
Since the lessee does not own the usufruct in full at the start, a sublease to the original lessor is invalid. This is a practical furūʿ derived from the qāʿida.
This ruling is specific to Hanafi fiqh.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ownership of price includes its natural increments — but not unowned grass.

This lesson unpacks a concise yet powerful Hanafi maxim: the price (thaman) transfers to the buyer's ownership upon contract, and all natural growth from that ownership belongs to the buyer. However, not everything that springs from the earth is owned — wild grass and herbage remain mubah. Understanding this qawida and its istithna is essential for a mufti ruling on sale contracts and property increments.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Express the qawida in Arabic and English: الثمن يدخل في ملك المشتري (The price enters the buyer's ownership). This means the price becomes the buyer's property immediately upon a valid sale.
2

The maxim applies primarily to contracts of exchange (buyu', ijarah) where a price is paid. It also governs natural increments (inma') that arise from the purchased item.
The scope covers offspring, fruits, water springing on the land, and earth's produce.

Legatee's Ownership by Acceptance (Qawida al-Musa lahu yamliku bi al-qabul)
States a qa'ida on legatee's ownership by acceptance with exceptions and parallel cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim that a legatee owns a bequest through acceptance, and distinguish the exceptions and analogies to gift and inheritance.

Core Idea
Ownership of a bequest requires acceptance, not possession — similar to inheritance, but acceptance is essential.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi legal maxim (qa'ida) governing bequests (wasiyya): the legatee's ownership is established by acceptance, not by the testator's death alone. Understanding this maxim is essential for a mufti to correctly advise on bequest scenarios, especially when the legatee is absent or when there is despair of acceptance. We will explore the maxim's scope, its exceptions, and its practical applications.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the qa'ida and its wording
Identify that the legatee owns the bequest by acceptance. Note the two analogies: like gift (needs acceptance) and like inheritance (no possession needed).
2
Understand the two analogies
Gift analogy: acceptance necessary. Inheritance analogy: no need for possession (qabd). The bequest shares features of both.
Possession is not required, unlike gift where possession is often necessary for completion.
3
Identify the exception: despair of acceptance
When acceptance is impossible (e.g., legatee unknown or refuses), the bequest is treated as inheritance and no acceptance is needed.
This exception is captured by the dabt (sub-maxim) 'If despair of acceptance, it becomes inheritance.'
4
Trace the furu' cases
Work through the examples: (1) legatee accepts and returns to heirs — if heirs accept, annulment; otherwise legatee remains owner. (2) absent legatee — maintenance from estate and retroactive claim upon acceptance.
Ownership is retroactive to the testator's death, as shown by the maintenance example.
5
Apply the qa'ida to a new scenario
Given a scenario with a bequest, determine whether acceptance is required, if the legatee is absent, or if despair applies.
Ensure the ruling is consistent with the qa'ida and its exceptions.

The Lessor Does Not Own Rent by Contract Alone
States a qawida with furu' and istithna'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim 'لا يملك المؤجر الأجرة بنفس العقد' to furu' cases, identify the four conditions that establish ownership of rent (istifa', tamakkun, ta'jeel, or condition), and distinguish the exceptions (istithna') where ownership is immediate.

Core Idea
The lessor owns rent only through actual receipt, ability to receive, advance payment, or a condition—not by contract alone.

In Hanafi fiqh, ijarah (hire/lease) fundamentally differs from sale (bay') regarding property transfer. While sale transfers ownership of the sold item immediately upon contract, ijarah does not grant the lessor ownership of the rent until certain triggers occur. This lesson unpacks the qawida that governs this principle, its practical furu', and the exceptional cases where ownership is immediate.

Guided Path
1
State the qawida and its scope
Start by reciting the qawida: 'لا يملك المؤجر الأجرة بنفس العقد'. Understand that it applies specifically to ijarah (hire/lease) contracts, distinguishing them from sale (bay').
This is a sughra (minor) qawida, but foundational for ijarah.
2
Identify the four triggers for ownership
The qawida lists four conditions under which the lessor owns the rent: actual receipt (istifa'), ability to receive (tamakkun), advance payment (ta'jeel), or a contractual condition. Without any of these, the lessor does not own the rent.
Tamakkun is constructive receipt—no physical use needed.
3
Analyze the furu' cases
Apply the qawida to the examples: (1) if rent is a slave and lessor frees it before any trigger, the emancipation is void; (2) the lessee owns benefits gradually as they arise; (3) the lessee cannot sublease to the lessor because the lessee does not fully own the benefits.
Each case illustrates the principle that ownership is not immediate.
4
Note the istithna' (exception)
If the rent is paid in advance (ta'jeel) or a condition stipulates immediate ownership, the lessor does own the rent immediately. This exception is governed by the qawida 'العادة محكمة' (custom is authoritative).
Exceptions are limited and must be clearly agreed upon.
5
Contrast with sale
In sale, the seller owns the price immediately upon contract because the subject matter is an existing object ('ayn). In ijarah, benefits are created gradually, so ownership of rent cannot be instantaneous without specific conditions.
This contrast helps solidify the distinct nature of ijarah.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The wife owns the mahr by contract, so increases are hers, but divorce before consummation returns half to the husband.

This passage from Ashbah wa al-Nazair extracts a key legal rule about the dower (mahr). It clarifies that ownership is established at the contract, not delivery, and then addresses the special case of divorce before consummation. Understanding this rule is crucial for calculating what each spouse is entitled to when a marriage ends early, especially when the dower has increased in value.

1

2
Identify the consequence for increases
The second part: 'فالزوائد لها قبل القبض' (so the increases belong to her before possession). All increases in the dower (e.g., appreciation, offspring) before possession accrue to the wife.

The benefit lies in the treatment of increases. If divorce occurs before consummation, the husband recovers half of the original dower and half of any increases that accrued before possession. This prevents the wife from keeping the entire increase when she must return half.

The Maxim of Liability Transfer in Ghasb and Wadi'a
Maxim on legal ruling's effect with branches on usurper depositing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'the legal ruling appears in the right of all, so the basis appears in the right of all' to determine ownership and liability in scenarios involving a usurper (ghāṣib) and a depositary (mūdi‘).

Core Idea
When liability vests, ownership vests in the one who bears final loss (qarār al-ḍamān).

This lesson unpacks a powerful Hanafi legal maxim from Ibn Nujaym's Ashbah wa al-Nazā'ir. The maxim governs how ownership transfers when a damaged item is compensated. Through numerous branches from the chapters of ghasb and wadi'a, we will see how the concept of 'ultimate liability' (qarār al-ḍamān) determines who owns an item after compensation.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and explain its meaning
The maxim: 'The legal ruling appears in the right of all, so the basis appears in the right of all.' This means when a ruling (e.g., liability) binds a person, the legal basis of that ruling (ownership) is also established for that person.
The 'basis' (al-istinād) refers to the ownership that underlies the liability.
2
Apply to the basic usurper deposit scenario
A usurper deposits an item; the item perishes; the owner makes the usurper liable. The usurper owns the item by liability and has no recourse against the depositary.
The depositary is a trustee; his liability is secondary.
3
Analyze the slave-girl running away scenario
If a usurper deposits a slave-girl, she runs away, and the owner makes the usurper liable for her value, the usurper owns her. If he frees her, it is valid. If the depositary makes himself liable and frees her, it is invalid.
Ownership vests in the one whose liability is ultimate (qarār al-ḍamān).
4
Extend to lending, gift, and chain of usurpers
If the usurper lends or gives the item, and the borrower/donee makes himself liable, ownership vests in them because they cannot recover from the usurper. Similarly, a second usurper who makes himself liable owns the item.
The principle is consistent: final liability determines ownership.
5
Consider the depositary's recovery and detention rights
The depositary, if made liable, can recover from the usurper and may detain the item until paid. If the item perishes after detention, it is at the value; if only quality is lost, he is not liable.
This follows the rules of an agent for purchase.

Borrower and Waqf Beneficiary Cannot Sub-Rent: The Hanafi Rule on I'arah
Qawida with furuat and Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida 'i'arah is ownership of benefit without compensation' to determine whether a borrower or waqf beneficiary can sub-rent, and explain why the Shafi'i position differs.

Core Idea
Borrower owns benefit without compensation, so cannot rent; renting would create a legal contradiction.

This lesson examines a fundamental qawida from Hanafi fiqh: the borrower of a dwelling does not have the right to rent it. The text contrasts the Shafi'i school's principle that anyone owning benefit may rent, and establishes the Hanafi ruling on the basis of i'arah's non-compensatory nature. The same ruling applies to the beneficiary of a waqf dwelling. Understanding this maxim is essential for correctly distinguishing between permissible and impermissible sub-letting in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi qawida
Explain the maxim: i'arah is tamlik al-manafi' bi-ghayr 'iwad, so the borrower cannot rent.
2
Identify the Shafi'i opposing principle
The Shafi'is hold that whoever owns benefit owns the right to rent and loan.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
Two reasons: (a) the borrower would own more than he owns (benefit without compensation plus benefit with compensation), (b) it leads to a contradiction (either loan becomes binding or rent becomes non-binding).
4
Apply to the mawquf 'alayh
The waqf beneficiary is like the borrower because he also owns benefit without compensation, so he cannot rent.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Hanafi prohibits sub-rent by borrower/mawquf 'alayh; Shafi'i allows it.

Commentary
This passage is a qawida with furuat. It begins with a ruling: a dwelling (sukna) cannot be rented or loaned. The text then contrasts the Shafi'i school, which holds that whoever owns the benefit owns the right to rent and loan. The Hanafi position, attributed to al-Karkhi, is that i'arah (loan) is permission (ibaha) of benefits, not ownership. However, the relied-upon Hanafi view (al-madhhab 'indana) is that i'arah is tamlik al-manafi' (ownership of benefits) without compensation, similar to ijarah. From this, the borrower cannot sub-rent because he owns the benefit without compensation; allowing him to rent would mean he owns more than he owns, and it would create a contradiction: either the loan becomes binding (which it is not) or the rent becomes non-binding. Two reasons are given, which also apply to the mawquf 'alayh (beneficiary of a waqf dwelling). On the preferred view, the mawquf 'alayh is like the borrower and cannot rent. The passage then extends to iqta' (land grant by the Imam). Qasim ibn Qutlubugha permits the muqta' to rent the granted land, unlike the borrower, because the muqta' owns the benefit in exchange for his readiness (i.e., a kind of consideration). The fatwa draws parallels with other cases: a lessee subletting, a slave assigned for service, a mawquf 'alayh renting produce, a ma'dhun slave renting, and an umm walad renting. If the lessor dies or the Imam removes the land, the lease is terminated. Finally, the Imam's right to revoke a grant is limited to land that was already cultivated at the time of grant; if the muqta' revives dead land, he becomes owner of the raqaba and cannot be removed.

Debt-Specific Contracts: Kafala, Rahn, and Muqassa
Statements of rulings on debt-specific contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that kafala and rahn are valid only for debts that do not lapse except by payment or release, and identify which obligations qualify.

Core Idea
Kafala and rahn are valid only for pure debts; obligations that lapse by other means are excluded.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir explores the legal characteristics that distinguish a debt (dayn) from other financial obligations. Using the example of a purchase and set-off (muqassa), the text then extracts the key rule that kafala and rahn are only valid for a 'sound debt'—one that does not fall except by payment or release. Understanding this rule is essential for a mufti when evaluating whether a particular obligation can be secured by suretyship or mortgage.

Guided Path
1
Understand the muqassa example
Read the example of buying a garment for ten dirhams. Recognize that upon payment, two reciprocal debts arise and cancel each other automatically. This illustrates that the way to fulfill such a debt is set-off.

2
Identify the defining characteristic of a sound debt
The passage states that a debt for which kafala is valid is one that 'does not fall except by payment or release'. Locate this phrase and understand its implications.

3
Apply the rule to the negative case: kitaba
The text gives the counterexample: the substitute (badal) in a kitaba contract is not eligible because it falls by the slave's inability to pay, not merely by payment or release. Confirm that this matches the rule.

The passage divides assets into trust assets (a'yan amana) and those guaranteed by themselves (mudmuna bi nafsiha). Note that kafala and rahn are invalid for the former but valid for the latter, because the latter are 'annexed to debts' (mulhiqa bi al-dayn).

The central maxim: 'A sound debt is one that does not lapse except by payment or release.' The furuat (branches) are: (a) kafala is valid for such debts; (b) rahn is valid for such debts; (c) obligations that are annexed to debts (like certain guarantees) also qualify.

The Invalidity of Pawning Trust Items and the Exception for Waqf Books
Ruling on pawning trust items and waqf books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'لا يصح الرهن بالأمانات' to distinguish between an invalid pawn of a trust item and a permissible reminder condition, and construct a fatwa for a waqf book scenario.

Core Idea
A pawn of a trust item is invalid and unguaranteed; but a waqf condition using 'rahn' may be upheld linguistically.

In Hanafi fiqh, pawning a trust item (amanah) is categorically invalid because the pawner lacks ownership or authority to pledge it. However, when an endowment (waqf) stipulates that a book placed as a reminder serves as a collateral, the condition can be honored linguistically without converting it into a pawn. In this lesson, we unpack the qawida and its application to waqf books, guided by the Hanafi principle of interpreting words in the best possible way (haml al-lafz 'ala al-sihhah).

Guided Path

State the qawida and its scope
Recall that 'لا يصح الرهن بالأمانات' (pawn of trust items is not valid). This is a sub-maxim (sughra) applying to the chapter of rahn and amanat. Identify the trust items: anything held on trust, not owned by the pawner.
The qawida is general; it covers all amanat including waqf books.
2
Identify the exception (istithna')
An exception arises when the endower (waqif) uses the word 'rahn' for a reminder condition (tadhkirah). In that case, we interpret the wording linguistically to uphold the condition, not as a valid pawn. The principle of haml al-lafz 'ala al-sihhah allows this.
This exception preserves the condition without applying pawn rules.
3
Analyze the furu' case of waqf books
In the furu' case: a waqf book is placed in the treasury as a reminder for the borrower to return it. This is valid as a condition, but not as a pawn. Therefore, no pawn rules apply: no sale, no automatic compensation if lost without negligence.
If the book is lost due to negligence, the borrower guarantees it, but not as a pawn—as a trust violation.
4
Contrast with invalid pawn (rahn fasid)
An invalid pawn (rahn fasid) is guaranteed (madhmun) like a valid pawn because the contract is defective but still intended as security. However, pawning a trust item is not a contract of pawn at all; it is void, not fasid, hence no guarantee.
This distinction is crucial for fatwa: the ruling on rahn fasid does not apply here.

Discharge from Corporeal Property (Ibra' 'an al-A'yan)
Ruling on validity of discharge from specific corporeal property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'discharge from corporeal property is invalid' to distinguish between valid discharges from claims/guarantees and invalid discharges from the 'ayn itself.

Core Idea
Discharge from a specific object is invalid, but discharge from a claim or guarantee regarding that object is valid.

Discharge (ibra') is a common transaction in Islamic law, but its application to specific physical objects (a'yan) requires precision. The Hanafi school holds that saying 'I discharge you from this house' does not transfer ownership or drop any claim; it is a nullity. However, if the discharge is worded as a renunciation of a claim or guarantee, it is effective. This lesson unpacks the maxim and its practical applications through furuat and an intra-Hanafi dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem: discharge from specific property
Recognize that ibra' from a specific 'ayn (e.g., 'this house') is a distinct legal act. Understand that the issue is whether such a declaration can transfer ownership or extinguish liability.
2
State the governing qawida
The maxim: الإبراء عن الأعيان باطل (Discharge from corporeal property is invalid). This is the core ruling.
The maxim is sughra (minor) and appears in the Ashbah wa al-Nazair under the topic of ibra'.
3
Distinguish valid and invalid wordings
Contrast 'I discharge you from the claim of this house' (valid) with 'I discharge you from this house' (invalid). Note that the former drops the ability to sue; the latter has no legal effect on ownership.
Also: 'I discharge you from my litigation' is invalid; he may still litigate.
4
Apply the qawida to the furuat
Work through the examples: general discharge covers a'yan but not standing crops; preemption is renounced judicially but not religiously if unintended; usurped property discharge is valid as guarantee discharge.
Use the examples from the text: house, claim, usurped 'ayn, consumed 'ayn.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Explore Zufar's objection: discharge from a usurped 'ayn is invalid because the 'ayn still exists. Abu Hanifa's response: it is interpreted as discharge from the guarantee, making the item a trust.
The practical difference: if the 'ayn perishes, per Abu Hanifa no liability; per Zufar, liability remains.
6
Account for exceptions
If the 'ayn is consumed (mustahlakah), discharge from its value is valid because the 'ayn no longer exists; only the value remains as a debt.
This exception ensures ease (taysir) in transactions.

Core Idea
