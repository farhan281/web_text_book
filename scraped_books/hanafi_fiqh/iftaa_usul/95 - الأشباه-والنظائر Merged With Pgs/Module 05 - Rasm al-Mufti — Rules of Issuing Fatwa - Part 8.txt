Two Exceptional Rulings in Kitaba: Annulment by Creditors and Sale of Harbi's Mukatab/Mudabbar
Exceptions to normal kitaba and tadbir rules for mufti's reference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two exceptional cases where a kitaba can be annulled by a third party and where a mukatab or mudabbar can be sold, and apply these rules as a mufti.

Core Idea
Creditors can annul a mukatab's kitaba if he is in debt; a harbi's mukatab/mudabbar can be sold if captured.

This passage presents two important exceptions that every mufti must know regarding the rulings of kitaba and tadbir. These are not general rules but specific cases where the normal protections of a mukatab's freedom contract are overridden. Understanding these exceptions prevents incorrect fatwas in disputes involving creditors or wartime situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two questions
The passage poses two questions: 1) Which kitaba is annulled by non-contracting parties? 2) Which mukatab or mudabbar can be sold?
2
Extract condition for annulment by creditors
The answer to the first question: when the mukatab is indebted to creditors (‏إذا كان المكاتب مديونا للغرماء‏). The creditors then annul the kitaba.
The condition is debts to creditors, not mere indebtedness to anyone.
3
Extract condition for saleability
The second answer: when a harbi (non-Muslim warring enemy) makes a kitaba or tadbir with the slave in dar al-harb, then either takes the slave out to dar al-islam, or both apostatize and go to dar al-harb, and the master (now Muslim or pagan) captures them.
The harbi's kitaba/tadbir is not recognized beyond dar al-harb.
4
Note the legal consequence
In the first case, creditors annul the kitaba, allowing them to claim the slave's value/debts. In the second, the slave can be sold as a regular slave; the kitaba/tadbir is void.
These are exceptions to the general rule that kitaba is a binding contract.

Commentary
This passage presents two exceptional rulings from the chapter of mukatab. The first: Normally, a contract of kitaba can only be annulled by the master or the slave, but here creditors can annul it when the mukatab owes them debts and cannot pay, to protect their rights. The second: A mukatab or mudabbar slave belonging to a harbi can be sold as a slave if captured after the harbi's kitaba or tadbir and then the master enters dar al-islam or both apostatize and return to dar al-harb.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has made a contract of kitaba with his master to purchase his freedom in installments.
مدير (mudabbar (likely intended)): A slave promised freedom after the master's death (tadbir).
حربي (harbi): A non-Muslim from a hostile territory (dar al-harb) with whom Muslims are at war.

The Distinction Between Looking at Mushaf and Private Part in Prayer
Furq on why looking at Mushaf invalidates prayer but looking at private part does not

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi ruling distinction between looking at the Mushaf and looking at a private part during prayer, and explain the underlying legal reasoning (ta'lil) focusing on the concept of 'ta'leem' (learning).

Core Idea
Looking at the Mushaf invalidates prayer because it constitutes learning; looking at a private part is sinful but does not involve such extraneous action.

This lesson examines a classic furq (distinction) from the Hanafi tradition that shows how subtle differences in the nature of an external act during prayer can radically change the ruling. While both looking at the Mushaf and looking at a private part are contrary to the spirit of prayer, only the former invalidates it. The key lies in the concept of 'ta'leem' (learning), which the Hanafi school treats as a significant extraneous activity that breaks the prayer's continuity. This furq is not merely theoretical; it demonstrates how Hanafi usul al-fiqh applies to concrete cases and informs the fatwa hierarchy.

Guided Path
1
State the two cases and their rulings
Clearly identify the two actions: (1) a praying person looks at the Mushaf and recites from it, and (2) a praying person looks at a woman's private part with desire. According to the text, the first invalidates the prayer (at least according to Abu Hanifa), while the second does not.
The contrasting rulings are the heart of the furq.
2
Extract the operative cause (illa) for the first case
The text states 'because the first is teaching (تعليم)'. This means that looking at the Mushaf and reading constitutes an act of learning, which is an extraneous cognitive activity. The worshiper is effectively taking the recitation from the Mushaf as if from a teacher, which introduces a significant external element into the prayer.
The 'illa' is not the looking itself but the learning that accompanies it.
3
Analyze the second case by comparison
Looking at a private part with desire, while sinful, does not involve such a learning activity. It is merely a glance that does not introduce a new cognitive or physical action beyond what is already sinful. Therefore, it does not invalidate the prayer according to the Hanafi school.
The sinfulness is separate from the category of actions that break the prayer.
4
Relate to the broader principle of 'amal kathir'
The Hanafi school generally holds that excessive actions (amal kathir) invalidate prayer. Here, looking at the Mushaf involves carrying, looking, and turning pages – physical actions that constitute 'amal kathir'. The learning aspect adds an extra dimension. The second case involves no such movement, so it does not reach the threshold.
The principle of 'amal kathir' is a key part of the Hanafi position.
5
Apply the fatwa hierarchy to resolve the dispute
There is a well-known intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa holds invalidation; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold validity with dislike. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position for fatwa is the view of Abu Hanifa, as noted in the text. This illustrates how the fatwa hierarchy operates within the Hanafi school.
The mufti must follow the tarjih mechanism, not simply choose the easiest opinion.

Commentary
This passage presents a fourth furq regarding actions during prayer. The ruling: if a praying person looks at a written copy of the Quran (Mushaf) and recites from it, his prayer is invalidated according to Abu Hanifa. But if he looks at a woman's private part with desire, the prayer is not invalidated. The difference is that looking at the Mushaf and reading from it constitutes learning (ta'leem), which is an extraneous action that distracts from the prayer and involves excessive movement (turning pages, holding the book). Looking at a private part, while sinful, does not involve such extraneous actions. The footnote then elaborates on the disagreement: Abu Hanifa rules invalidation, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the two companions) rule the prayer is valid but disliked, and al-Shafi'i permits it without dislike. This shows how the same action can be evaluated differently based on the nature of the act (teaching vs. mere looking). The footnote is taken from Ibn al-Humam's Fath al-Qadir.

Key Terms
المصحف (al-mushaf): The written copy of the Quran, in codex form.
تعليم (ta'leem): Teaching or learning; in the context of prayer, it refers to the act of acquiring knowledge from an external source, which is considered an extraneous cognitive action.
عمل كثير (amal kathir): Excessive action; a series of movements that disrupt the continuity of prayer.
