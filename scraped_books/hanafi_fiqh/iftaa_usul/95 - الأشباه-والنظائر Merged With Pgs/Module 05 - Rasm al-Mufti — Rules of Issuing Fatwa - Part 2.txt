Annulment and Corruption of Completed Marriage
Ruling on marriage not accepting annulment after completion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a completed marriage is not annulled except by apostasy, and differentiate between corruption (fasad) and annulment (faskh) for fosterage and affinity.

Core Idea
A completed marriage is stable—only apostasy annuls; fosterage and affinity corrupt but do not annul.

This lesson examines a key rasm al-mufti rule from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair: once a marriage is consummated, it cannot be annulled retroactively. The only exception is apostasy. However, if fosterage or affinity arises after consummation, the marriage becomes corrupted (fasid) but remains in effect until dissolution by divorce or death. Understanding this distinction is essential for issuing correct fatwas in marriage cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
Note that a marriage after completion (consummation) does not accept annulment. This is the default position.
The text says: 'النكاح لا يقبل الفسخ بعد التمام'.
2
Identify the Exception
Apostasy (riddah) by one spouse annuls the marriage, even after completion. This is the only exception mentioned.
The text says: 'في ردَّة أحدهما'.
3
Distinguish between Corruption and Annulment
For fosterage (rada') or affinity (musaharah) arising after marriage, the marriage is corrupted (fasid) but not annulled. The contract remains valid but marital relations are prohibited.
The text says: 'يفسده ولا يفسخه'.

The Drunk Person: Default Equality with Exceptions
Rulings on drunk person's actions and legal effect of calling out

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a drunk person is like the sober, and list the three exceptions where his legal capacity differs.

Core Idea
A drunk person's actions are valid except in hudud, apostasy, and self-testimony.

This lesson examines a foundational maxim in Hanafi fiqh: the drunk person is treated like the sober in most legal matters. The exceptions are strictly limited to areas requiring full intention and reliability.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The text begins: 'The drunk person is like the sober.' This means that legal acts performed while drunk are generally valid.
This includes divorce, sales, and confessions to non-hudud crimes.
2
Identify the exceptions
Three matters are excepted: confession to pure hudud (fixed punishments), apostasy, and giving testimony about oneself.
For example, a drunk person's confession to theft (hadd crime) is not accepted, but his confession to murder (not a hadd) would be valid.
3
Understand the rationale
The exceptions involve matters where absolute certainty and sober intention are required, such as admitting to crimes that carry fixed punishments.
4
Apply to a case
A drunk man divorces his wife. The divorce is valid. However, if he confesses to zina, the hadd punishment is not applied based on his confession alone.
The default equality ensures stability in transactions and family law.

Calling Out: Informational Unless Specified
Rulings on drunk person's actions and legal effect of calling out

Core Idea
Calling out does not create legal effects except in divorce, manumission, hudud, and ta'zir.

This lesson explores the legal nature of addressing someone with a vocative expression. In Hanafi law, such statements are generally non-binding, but specific wordings in divorce, manumission, hudud, and ta'zir have immediate legal consequences.

State the default rule
The text says: 'Calling out is for informing, so no ruling is established by it.' This means that simply addressing someone with a term does not create a legal effect.

2
List the exceptions
Four cases are excepted: (1) divorce by saying 'O divorce', (2) manumission by saying 'O free', (3) hudud by saying 'O adulteress', and (4) ta'zir by saying 'O thief'.
In these cases, the utterance itself establishes the ruling: divorce occurs, the slave is free, a hadd accusation is made, or a ta'zir accusation is made.
3
Analyze the slave-girl case
A master calls his slave-girl 'O thief, O adulteress, O madwoman' and then sells her. The buyer objects, but the seller's words are not considered proof of defects because they were merely for information, not confirmation.
The buyer cannot return the slave-girl based solely on the master's statements.
4
Analyze the wife case
If a man calls his wife 'O disbeliever', no separation occurs. Calling out 'O disbeliever' does not fall under the exceptions, so it has no legal effect on the marriage.
This is consistent with the default rule: calling out is for information only.

Ruling on the child of li'an and its legal implications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule regarding the child of li'an and identify the two exceptions according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Li'an severs lineage only for inheritance and maintenance; all other paternal rulings remain.

This passage from al-Bada'i presents a concise rasm al-mufti rule: the lineage of a child born under li'an is not completely negated. For most legal purposes, the child remains the father's child. However, two specific rights are withheld: inheritance and maintenance. Understanding this exception-based rule is crucial for a mufti to give correct fatwa in family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The subject is 'child of li'an' (ولد الملاعنة). Recognize that li'an is a process where a husband accuses his wife of adultery and they exchange oaths, resulting in negation of paternity.
Li'an is a form of divorce-like separation.
2
State the general rule
The general rule: the child's lineage is not negated (لا ينتفي نسبه) in all rulings. That means the child is still considered the father's child for most purposes.
3
List the exceptions
The text specifies two exceptions: inheritance (الإرث) and maintenance (النفقة). In these two areas, the child is not considered the father's child.
These are the only exceptions.
4
Apply the rule
When faced with a fatwa question involving a child of li'an, check if the issue is about inheritance or maintenance. If yes, apply the exception. For all other matters (testimony, zakat, marriage, manumission), apply the general rule of lineage preservation.
This is a typical rasm al-mufti parsing exercise.

Exceptions to the General Rule: Divorce of the Insane and Minor
Rasm al-Mufti listing exceptions for the insane and minor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule that divorce from an insane or minor is invalid and identify the specific exceptions where it is valid according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Insane and minor divorce is void, except in conditional, physical incapacity, and conversion cases.

This lesson presents a rasm al-mufti passage that every mufti must know: the general principle that divorce from an insane person or a minor does not take effect, and the precise exceptions where it does. Understanding these exceptions prevents erroneous fatwas and ensures that divorces are applied correctly in complex personal status cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle
The passage begins with the default rule: 'Al-majnun la yaqa' talaquhu' – the insane person's divorce does not occur. Similarly for the minor. This is the foundation.
The rule is based on lack of legal capacity (ahliyyah al-ada').
2
List Exceptions for the Insane
The text lists four exceptions: (1) conditional divorce made while sane, then insanity and condition occurs; (2) husband is castrated (majbub) and wife requests separation; (3) husband is impotent ('innin) and fails to consummate after a delay; (4) wife converts to Islam while husband is a disbeliever and his parents refuse Islam.
Each exception has its own rationale and condition.
3
List Exceptions for the Minor
For a discerning minor (sabi mumayyiz), divorce is valid if: (1) the wife converts to Islam and he refuses after being offered Islam; (2) he is castrated and separation occurs. The phrase 'ala al-sahih indicates these are the relied-upon positions.
Discerning (mumayyiz) is key for the minor's capacity.
4
Understand the 'ala al-sahih Indicator
The phrase 'ala al-sahih (according to the correct view) signals that there is a known ikhtilaf within the school, but the author is stating the mufta bihi (operative) position.
This is a typical rasm al-mufti technique.
5
Analyze the Rationale for Impotence Delay
The passage notes that a delay is given to the impotent husband because intercourse is obligatory (mustahaqq) upon him, similar to manumission of a relative. This is a qiyas: as manumission is an obligation that can be enforced by a delay, so too is consummation.
Shows the use of qiyas in deriving exceptions.

Ruling on Khul' Request and Disputes on Condition Fulfillment
Rulings on prohibition of khul' request and procedural rule on disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general ruling on a woman seeking khul', identify the exceptional circumstance that permits it, and apply the default rule and its exceptions regarding whose statement is accepted in disputes about condition occurrence.

Core Idea
A woman seeking khul' is haram unless a ba'in divorce condition was witnessed; in disputes, the husband's word is presumed except in four specific cases where the wife's word is taken.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair addresses two related issues: the prohibition of a wife initiating khul' without cause, and the procedural rule of al-qawl li-man (whose statement is accepted) when the couple disputes whether a condition for divorce occurred. Understanding these rules is essential for a mufti when handling khul' cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
The passage begins by stating 'طلب المرأة الخلع حرام' – a woman's request for khul' is prohibited (haram). Note that this is the default ruling.
Khul' is divorce initiated by the wife in exchange for payment.
2
Examine the exception
The exception applies when her ba'in divorce is conditioned on a condition, witnesses testify to its occurrence, but the judge has not ruled. In that case, she may cautiously seek fidyah to separate.
The exception is narrow – witnesses must confirm the condition, but no judicial ruling yet.
3
Analyze the default rule for disputes
The passage states 'القول له' (his statement is accepted) as the default for conditions not known from the wife's side. This procedural presumption places the burden on the wife to prove the condition occurred.
This rule applies to conditions whose occurrence is not within her knowledge.
4
List the exceptions to the default rule
Four cases where the wife's statement is taken: (1) divorce conditioned on non-receipt of maintenance for a month – her word on both maintenance and divorce; (2) sunna divorce where husband claims intercourse during menses – her word; (3) master claims intercourse during iddah – her word; (4) conditional divorce with choice, husband claims she chose after the session – her word.
These exceptions are cited from al-Khulasa and al-Kafi.
5
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider a couple disputing whether the wife received maintenance. The mufti must apply the exception: the wife's statement is taken regarding both the maintenance and the divorce.
Practice distinguishing which rule applies in a given situation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In Hanafi fiqh, a wife initiating khul' (divorce for compensation) is generally prohibited because it upsets the marriage bond. However, the mufti must recognize an exception: if the husband has suspended an irrevocable divorce upon a condition, and witnesses attest to its fulfillment, yet the judge has not pronounced the talaq, the wife may cautiously seek khul'. This ruling shows that the mufti looks beyond formal rulings to underlying realities.

Rasm al-mufti: lists the legal effects of apostasy as a rule

Core Idea
Apostasy nullifies deeds, dissolves marriage, invalidates waqf and narration; only Hajj is exempt if the apostate returns.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazha'ir presents the core ruling on apostasy as a foundational maxim governing multiple legal consequences. The Hanafi Definition of Iman and Kufr: A Foundational Qawida for Fatwa
Defines iman and kufr and gives a principle for takfir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi definition of iman and kufr to assess whether a given statement constitutes takfir and to identify when a fatwa of takfir should be withheld due to disagreement.

Core Idea
Iman is inner affirmation; kufr is denial of a necessarily known tenet — and takfir is never issued on disputed grounds.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir presents the core Hanafi definition of faith and disbelief, which is essential for any mufti dealing with issues of apostasy. The definition clarifies that iman is a matter of the heart, not external actions, and that kufr requires explicit denial of something necessarily known from the religion. The passage also provides a critical caution: where there is scholarly disagreement on whether an expression constitutes kufr, a fatwa of takfir should not be given. This lesson will help you internalize this definition and apply it in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Poles
Locate the definitions of iman and kufr in the text. Iman is tasdiq (affirmation) of everything the Prophet brought from Allah that is necessarily known from the religion. Kufr is takdhib (denial) of any such element.
The phrase 'ma ja'a bihi min al-din daruratan' is key – it restricts kufr to matters that are universally and necessarily known, not obscure points.
2
Understand the Criterion for Takfir
The text says: 'None of the people of the qibla becomes a disbeliever except by juhud (explicit rejection) of what has been entered into him [i.e., the religion].' This means that before declaring someone a kafir, you must confirm they have rejected a necessary element, not merely made a mistake or committed a sin.
Juhud implies a conscious and willful denial, not ignorance or misinterpretation.
3
Recognize the Fatwa Principle
The text concludes: 'The sum of what our companions mentioned in the fatwas... reverts to that, and there is some disagreement, but one does not issue a fatwa where there is disagreement.' This is a procedural rule for the mufti: avoid giving a ruling of takfir when the status of an expression is contested among reliable authorities.
This principle is part of the Hanafi adab al-mufti (etiquette of issuing fatwas).

This passage lays down the foundational definition of iman and kufr in the Hanafi madhhab, which is critical for issuing fatwas on apostasy. Importantly, the text notes that there is some disagreement among scholars on certain expressions, and the rule is: one should not give a fatwa on a matter where there is valid disagreement (la yufta bima fihi khilaf). This means a mufti must avoid issuing a ruling of takfir when the status of an expression is disputed.

Exceptions to the Reward for Returning a Runaway Slave
Lists exceptions to the rule that a finder gets a reward for returning a runaway slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule of ja'l for returning a runaway slave, list its ten exceptions, and explain the procedural requirement (witnesses) for entitlement.

Core Idea
The reward for returning a runaway slave is withheld from ten categories: dependents, parents, children, spouses, guardians, helpers, and authorities.

This passage from Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir delivers a concise list of exceptions to the well-known Hanafi rule that the finder of a runaway slave earns a reward (ja'l). For a mufti, memorizing these exceptions is essential to avoid issuing incorrect rulings in property and compensation cases.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The default ruling: any person who returns a runaway slave (al-abiq) to its owner is entitled to a reward (al-ja'l) as compensation for his effort and service.
This rule is widely applied in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Memorize the ten exceptions
The passage lists exactly ten categories of persons who do NOT receive the reward. They are: (1) someone under the master's care (fī ‘iyāl al-sayyid), (2) either parent of the master (al-abawayn), (3) the son to either parent (al-ibn ilā aḥadihimā), (4) one spouse to the other (al-zawjayn), (5) the orphan's guardian (waṣī al-yatīm), (6) the one who supports the orphan (man yu‘īluhu), (7) someone the owner sought help from (man ista‘āna bihi mālikuhu), (8) the ruler (al-sulṭān), (9) the police chief (al-shihna), (10) the watchman (al-khafīr).
These ten are extracted from the general statements of the well-known Hanafi texts (mutūn).
3
Understand the condition for entitlement
Even for those not in the exceptions, the returner must establish by witnesses (ishhād) that he took the slave with the intention of returning it to its owner. If he does so, he is not liable for any damage and gets the reward. Otherwise, he is liable for the slave's value and receives no reward.
This condition protects the owner's property rights.
4

Example 1: A father returns his son's slave. No reward (exception #2). Example 2: A police chief returns a slave. No reward (exception #9). Example 3: A stranger returns a slave but has no witnesses. He is liable and gets no reward.

Core Idea
The nazir's authority may or may not end with the waqif's death, depending on whether he is considered a deputy (wakil) or an independent administrator.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding the legal nature of the nazir's appointment. The classification of the nazir as a wakil (deputy) or an independent administrator carries significant practical consequences for the waqif's right to remove the nazir and for the continuity of the nazir's authority after the waqif's death. Understanding this dispute is essential for a mufti when advising on waqf management and succession.

Guided Path

Abu Yusuf holds that the nazir is a wakil of the waqif; therefore, the waqif can remove him at will, and the nazir's authority ends with the waqif's death. The condition overrides the default ruling by agreement.

The fatwa is on Abu Yusuf's view, as per al-Walwaljiyyah. Additionally, from al-Atabiyya: if the qadi appoints a qayyim (because the waqif did not designate one), the waqif cannot later remove that qayyim.

A dismissed executor's claim against the waqf requires evidence, even if the former appointee confirms it.

When a judge dismisses an executor (qayyim), the dismissed executor may still claim that he paid certain expenses to an appointee. This lesson examines the exact procedure and adjustment required when the claimed amount exceeds the value of work.

Guided Path

A judge dismisses an executor. The dismissed executor claims he paid a certain amount monthly or annually to an appointee. The dismissed appointee confirms the claim. What is the ruling?
2
Recognize the evidentiary requirement
The text states the claim is not accepted without bayyinah (evidence). Understand why confirmation alone is insufficient: the waqf's interests must be protected from collusion.
Bayyinah typically means two male witnesses or one male and two females in Hanafi law.
3
Analyze the two scenarios after evidence is provided
If the claimed amount is equal to or less than the value of the work performed, the new executor pays that amount. If it exceeds, the new executor deducts the excess and pays the remainder.
This ensures the waqf is not overcharged while still compensating legitimate expenses.

Bayt al-Mal Endowments Retain Their Legal Ruling
Ruling on priority when endowment originates from Bayt al-Mal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida that endowments from Bayt al-Mal are not subject to the endower's conditions and determine priority among beneficiaries when funds are insufficient.

Core Idea
Endowments from Bayt al-Mal cannot be privatized by condition; only those entitled to public funds may benefit.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair lays down a critical rasm al-mufti rule: the origin of an endowment fund determines whether the endower's conditions are binding. If the endowment originates from Bayt al-Mal (the public treasury), it retains its original legal character, and only persons who are inherently entitled to Bayt al-Mal funds may receive stipends. When resources are limited, a specific priority order applies. This distinction prevents the misappropriation of public endowments and clarifies the mufti's duty when faced with conflicting stipulations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the origin of the endowment
Determine whether the endowment fund originated from Bayt al-Mal (public treasury) or from private property. This is the decisive factor.
Endowments from Bayt al-Mal include those established from state lands, public revenues, or properties transferred from the treasury.
2
Apply the relevant qawida
If from Bayt al-Mal, apply the rule: 'Bayt al-Mal endowments retain their legal ruling and cannot be changed by anyone's decree.' If private, apply the rule that the endower's conditions are followed.
The qawida is derived directly from the statement: 'هذا من بيت المال لا يتحوّل عن حكمه الشرعي بجعل أحد'.
3
Check entitlement condition (sifa al-istihqaq)
For Bayt al-Mal endowments, only those who meet the condition of being entitled to Bayt al-Mal funds (e.g., the poor, the needy, students of knowledge who are not wealthy) may benefit. Others, even if appointed by the administrator, cannot lawfully receive stipends.
This overrides any condition set by the endower.
4
Apply priority hierarchy when funds are insufficient
Among the entitled, prioritize: first those with sifa al-istihqaq over others (including students and descendants of the Prophet); then by need (the most needy first); if equal in need, by age (older first); then by specific function order: teacher (mudarris), muezzin, imam, caretaker (qayyim).
This hierarchy is only for Bayt al-Mal endowments when the endower did not specify a different order or when funds are limited.
5
For private endowments without conditions
If the endowment is private (not from Bayt al-Mal) and the endower did not specify priority, distribute equally among all beneficiaries, including both ritual personnel (ahl al-sha'air) and others.
The endower's conditions are binding only for private endowments; for Bayt al-Mal endowments, conditions that contradict the qawida are void.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A child qualifies for a waqf installment only if born before the end of the fourth month of that installment.

This lesson presents a specific Hanafi rule from the waqf jurisprudence, crucial for muftis and waqf administrators. The rule clarifies when a child beneficiary is entitled to share in periodic rental income from a waqf property leased in installments. Understanding this timing prevents disputes in distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the waqf structure
The rule applies when a waqf property is leased with three equal installments, each covering four months. Note that the annual income is divided into three parts.
This is a specific structure; other installment patterns may require different rulings.
2
Determine the critical time point
The key moment is the completion of the fourth month of each installment. The child must be 'created' (i.e., conceived and born) before this point to be entitled to that installment.
The Arabic phrase 'قبل تمام الشهر الرابع' indicates the end of the month.
3
Apply the rule to a child
If the child was born before the end of the fourth month of that installment, the child receives that installment. If not, the child receives nothing from that installment.
This applies to each installment independently; a child may qualify for later installments if born before their respective deadlines.
4
Note the consequence
The rule ensures that the revenue is distributed only to those who were beneficiaries during the period the revenue accrued. It prevents retroactive claims.
This is based on the principle that waqf income is tied to the time it is generated.

The Rule of Non-Rescission of Ijara by Death of Waqf's Lessor
Establishing the rule of non-rescission of waqf lease with two exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule that the death of the lessor does not rescind a waqf lease, and identify the two exceptional cases as given in this passage.

Core Idea
A waqf lease survives the lessor's death; only apostasy or subsequent specific endowment breaks it.

In Hanafi waqf law, the perpetual nature of a waqf means that lease contracts remain binding despite the death of the lessor (the original waqif or the mutawalli). This is a crucial principle for muftis adjudicating waqf disputes. However, there are two specific scenarios where the lease is rescinded. This lesson anchors that rule and its exceptions, drawing directly from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the General Principle
The default rule: لا تنفسخ الإجارة بموت المؤجر للوقف — The lease is not rescinded by the death of the lessor of the waqf. This is the foundation for all subsequent applications.
Remember: waqf is perpetual, so its contracts persist beyond the lifetime of the original parties.
2
Learn the First Exception: Apostasy
When the waqif leases the property, then apostatizes from Islam (irtidad), and dies, the waqf becomes void (because the waqif's apostasy nullifies the waqf). Consequently, the property reverts to his heirs, and the lease is rescinded. This is an exception because the waqf itself ceases to exist.
Apostasy (riddah) invalidates the waqf from the moment of apostasy, so the contract cannot continue after death.
3
Learn the Second Exception: Subsequent Endowment
If a person leases his land and then later endows it (waqf) to a specific individual (e.g., his son), then dies, the lease is rescinded. The rationale: the subsequent endowment transfers the property in a way that overrides the previous lease.
Here the lessor was the original owner who later changed the status of the property, ending the lease.
4
Note the Source Authority
This rule and its exceptions are transmitted from Ibn Wahban's commentary, as cited by Ibn Nujaym in al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair. This is a reliable transmission (zahir riwayah) in the Hanafi school.
Knowing the source strengthens your fatwa: you can cite al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair and Ibn Wahban.

A nazir is not liable for tenant absconding; only negligence triggers liability.

Mu'ākhadha bi Zam'ihi: Binding Effect of Waqf Acknowledgment
Ruling on the binding force of prior acknowledgment of waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of mu'ākhadha bi zam'ihi to determine the waqf status of property following an acknowledgment and subsequent acquisition.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment of waqf becomes binding when the declarant later acquires the property.

This short but powerful rule from the chapter on iqrar establishes a key principle in Hanafi fatwa: a person's own prior statement can legally transform the status of property they later acquire. You will see how the maxim 'mu'ākhadha bi zam'ihi' operates in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case structure
Break down the scenario into its components: (1) a person makes an acknowledgment about land in another's possession, claiming it is waqf; (2) the possessor denies the claim; (3) later the declarant acquires the land through purchase or inheritance.
The possessor's denial is irrelevant – the key is the declarant's own statement.
2
State the legal consequence
According to the text, the land becomes waqf automatically upon acquisition. It does not require a separate declaration or court order. The ruling is based on holding the person responsible for his own claim.
3
Recognize the underlying principle
The principle is 'al-mu'ākhadha bi zam'ihi' (being held to one's own assertion). In Hanafi law, a person's unambiguous acknowledgment against his own interest is binding and cannot later be contradicted to avoid a legal consequence.
This is a specific application of the broader rule that iqrar is binding.
4
Distinguish the modes of acquisition
The rule explicitly mentions both purchase (shira') and inheritance (irth). The method of acquisition does not change the outcome – whether voluntary or involuntary, the waqf takes effect.
Purchase is a voluntary transaction; inheritance is involuntary, yet both trigger the rule.
5
Apply to analogous cases
The author notes that 'nazair' (analogous cases) are found in the chapter of iqrar. This invites the student to extend the principle to other acknowledgments (e.g., debts, gifts) under similar conditions.
This step requires cross-referencing with the broader category of iqrar.

Core Idea
A qualifying phrase after conjunctions binds only the last mentioned item.

In interpreting waqf documents, the expression 'from males specifically' appears after a list of beneficiaries. Does it restrict all prior categories or just the last? The Hanafi school applies a linguistic principle: when a description follows a series of terms joined by conjunctions, it refers only to the last term. This lesson walks through the fatwa that illustrates this rule and its practical consequence.

The Rule of Lease Continuity for Waqf Property
Ruling that waqf lease does not end with lessor's death except in two cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi rule regarding lease contracts on waqf property upon the lessor's death and identify the two exceptional cases where the lease terminates.

Core Idea
A waqf lease survives the lessor's death—except when the lessor apostatized or endowed his own land to a specific person.

In Hanafi fiqh, leases are binding contracts that generally do not dissolve upon death. However, when the leased property is waqf, there is a special rule: the lease continues even after the death of the waqf administrator or founder. But this rule has two critical exceptions that every mufti must know. This lesson, drawn from Ibn Wahban's commentary, clarifies these exceptions and their underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The lease of waqf property does not terminate upon the death of the lessor. This is the default Hanafi position based on the binding nature of contracts and the perpetuity of waqf.
The lessor here is typically the waqf administrator (mutawalli) or the founder if still alive.
2
Recognize the exceptions
Two cases are listed where the lease does terminate: (1) when the waqf founder leased the property, then apostatized from Islam and died, and (2) when a person leased his own land and later endowed it upon a specific beneficiary and then died.
Both exceptions involve a change in the ownership or status of the property that conflicts with the lease.
3
Understand the reasoning for exception 1
Apostasy (riddah) of the founder invalidates the waqf entirely because a waqf by a non-Muslim is not valid. Consequently, the property returns to the founder's inheritance and the lease terminates.
The key is that apostasy makes the waqf void ab initio, as if it never existed.
4
Understand the reasoning for exception 2
When a person leases his own land and then endows it to a specific person (waqf 'ala mu'ayyan), the endowment transfers ownership to the beneficiary. Since the lessor no longer owns the property, the lease cannot continue.
This differs from a general waqf where ownership is transferred to Allah, making the lease continue with the administrator.
5
Note the source and authority
These exceptions are mentioned by Ibn Wahban at the end of his commentary on Al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair. Ibn Wahban is a later Hanafi authority, and his clarification is relied upon in fatwa.
The rule itself is established in the earlier texts; the exceptions are detailed by later jurists.

Core Idea
An adjective after a compound sentence refers only to the last part, except with 'thumma' where it is agreed.

As a mufti, you will encounter legal documents—wills, endowments, contracts—where a descriptive phrase follows a list of items. The Hanafi school has a clear rule: the adjective qualifies only the last item mentioned, unless there is a contextual indicator. This rule differs from the Shafi'i position when the conjunction 'waw' is used, but both schools agree when 'thumma' appears. If 'thumma', the rule is unanimous: adjective refers to last part.

If the conjunction is 'waw', the adjective refers only to the last part. This is the relied-upon Hanafi position.

When Naming and Pointing Differ: Void or Corrupt?

And the judgment is annulled by his freedom, and he is made a slave property for 'Amr. This is indicated by what Qadikhan said in the beginning of al-Buyu' in Sharh al-Ziyadat. So the issues of the chapter became two types: First: emancipation in absolute ownership, which is like original freedom, and the judgment of it is a judgment upon all people. Second: judgment of emancipation in dated ownership, which is a judgment upon all people from the time of the date. There is no judgment before it. So let this be in your memory, for the famous books are devoid of this benefit. And here is another benefit: there is no difference in its being upon all people whether it is by evidence or by his saying 'I am free' if no prior acknowledgment of slavery has occurred from him, as stated in al-Muhit al-Burhani.
Source pages p. 185

Commentary
This passage presents two categories of judicial rulings regarding the declaration of a slave as free. The first category is emancipation in absolute ownership, meaning the slave was owned unconditionally, and the judgment of freedom is treated as if the person were originally free, binding on all people. The second category is emancipation in dated ownership, where the judgment is binding on all people only from that date onward, not before. The author emphasizes that this distinction is rarely found in famous books. Then a further benefit is added: the ruling applies equally whether proven by evidence (bayyina) or by the person simply declaring 'I am free,' as long as he had not previously admitted to being a slave. This is in accordance with the Hanafi position as stated in al-Muhit al-Burhani.

Key Terms
عتق في ملك مطلق (itq fi milk mutlaq): Emancipation of a slave owned unconditionally, treated as original freedom
بينة (bayyina): Legal evidence, typically testimony
