When Mafhum Mukhalif is Valid in Hanafi Fatwa
Defines the Hanafi stance on the validity of mafhum mukhalif for fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which mafhum mukhalif is valid in Hanafi fatwa and identify cases where it is overridden by an explicit text.

Core Idea
Mafhum mukhalif is valid in Hanafi fatwa only when derived from qiyas-permissible sources and not contradicted by an explicit text.

Mafhum mukhalif (the opposite implication) is a tool used in ijtihad but its validity depends on the source and context. This passage from Ibn Abidin's commentary on Rasm al-Mufti clarifies when a mufti may act upon mafhum mukhalif: it is accepted in fiqh narrations, rational proofs, everyday speech, and ijtihadi Companion statements that are subject to qiyas. However, it is not accepted in statements not subject to qiyas (like marfu' hadith), and any explicit ruling overrides the implied meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule in the matn
Read the opening line: 'واعمل بمفہوم روایات أتى ما لم يخالف لصريح ثبتا'. This establishes the basic principle: act on mafhum unless it contradicts an explicit established text.
The matn uses poetic form; 'ثبتا' has an alif ishba'.
2
List the sources where mafhum mukhalif is valid
From the commentary, extract the four categories: fiqh riwayat, rational proofs, everyday speech, ijtihadi statements of Companions that are mudrak bil-qiyas.
The common element: all are subject to analogical reasoning.
3
Identify the exception: non-qiyas-permissible sources
The commentary explicitly states that statements not mudrak bil-qiyas (like marfu' hadith) are excluded; mafhum mukhalif is not valid there.
This is because such statements are treated as direct divine revelation, not open to implication.
4
Understand the overriding condition
Even in valid sources, mafhum mukhalif is overridden if an explicit (sarih) established ruling exists. The explicit taketh precedence over the implied.
This is a key principle in fatwa: the clearer textual evidence prevails.
5
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
Given a statement from a Companion's ijtihad: 'If a man prays without wudu, his prayer is invalid.' The mafhum mukhalif would imply that with wudu it is valid. But if an explicit hadith says wudu is not required for a specific prayer, the explicit overrides the mafhum.

Discussion of mafhum mukhalif: 28. And act upon the mafhum (implication) of the riwayat (narrations) that come, as long as it does not contradict an explicit (sarih) established (thabata) [statement]. Its interpretation: [28] You may act upon the mafhum mukhalif derived from fiqh narrations, as long as that mafhum mukhalif does not oppose any explicit, established saying. Clarification: 'thabata' has an alif ishba'. Commentary: From here, Allama Shami instructs acting upon rulings established through mafhum mukhalif. The explanation is that according to the later Hanafis (mutakhkhirin), mafhum mukhalif is valid (mu'tabar) in: fiqh narrations, rational proofs, people's everyday speech, and even the ijtihadi statements of the noble Companions that are qiyas-permissible (mudrak bil-qiyas) (because statements not qiyas-permissible are treated like marfu' hadith, in which mafhum mukhalif is not valid). In all these, mafhum mukhalif is considered; the derived rule can be used for fatwa and practice, provided no explicit (sarih) ruling opposes it. If such an explicit ruling exists, then mafhum mukhalif is not acted upon, because what is established explicitly takes precedence over what is merely implied from speech.

Commentary
This passage defines the Hanafi position on using mafhum mukhalif (the opposite implication of a statement) as a basis for fatwa. The matn (verse 28) states: 'Act upon the mafhum of narrations that come, as long as it does not contradict an explicit established [text].' The commentary by Ibn Abidin (Allama Shami) explains that later Hanafis (mutakhkhirin) accept mafhum mukhalif in several sources: (1) fiqh narrations (riwayat fiqhiyya), (2) rational proofs, (3) everyday speech of people, and (4) ijtihadi statements of the Companions that are subject to qiyas. The exception is statements that are not subject to qiyas (ghayr mudrak bil-qiyas), because they are treated like marfu' hadith where mafhum mukhalif is not accepted. The condition is that the mafhum must not contradict an explicit (sarih) ruling; if such an explicit ruling exists, the explicit rules over the implied meaning. This is a key rasm al-mufti principle: the mufti can derive rulings from implications as long as no clear text overrides them.

Key Terms
مفہوم مخالف (mafhum mukhalif): The opposite implication of a statement, e.g., from 'if Z then A' we infer 'if not Z then not A'
مدرک بالقیاس (mudrak bil-qiyas): A statement that can be understood or derived through analogical reasoning
صریح (sarih): Explicit, clear text or ruling that is established

Definition of Urf and Adah and Their Legal Significance
Defining 'urf and 'adah, their difference, and the principle of changing rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'urf and 'adah and apply the maxim 'al-‘adah muhakkamah' to identify when a ruling changes due to change of custom.

Core Idea
Custom is authoritative—but only when rationally accepted and not contradicting revelation.

This lesson defines two related but distinct concepts: 'urf (custom with rational basis) and 'adah (habit or repeated practice). Understanding the difference is crucial for a mufti, because 'urf is a recognized source of law in the Hanafi school, and changes in 'urf can lead to changes in legal rulings. We will explore the definitions, the subtle distinction, the principle that 'custom is authoritative', and its application in real fatwa changes.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish 'urf from 'adah
Using the two definitions given, identify the key difference: 'adah is broader (any repeated action), 'urf is narrower (only rationally accepted adah).
People of fiqh often use 'adah synonymously with 'urf, but technically they differ.
2
Recognize the types of 'urf
Note that 'urf is classified into general and specific, each with different binding scope.
General 'urf binds all, specific 'urf binds only those in that locality or profession.
3
Apply the maxim 'al-‘adah muhakkamah'
Understand that this maxim means custom is authoritative in fiqh, subject to the condition that it does not contradict nass or ijma.
The restriction is crucial: custom cannot override clear texts.
4
Analyze the examples of changed rulings
Study the two examples: taking wages for teaching Quran, and sufficiency of apparent justice in witnesses. Understand why the later jurists changed the fatwa: because 'urf changed.
This demonstrates the dynamic nature of Hanafi fiqh.
5
Connect to the istithna'
Recognize that the maxim does not apply when custom contradicts a clear text or consensus.
Always check the dabt (restriction) before applying the maxim.

Awn al-Mughni fi Hill 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti
Definition of Urf and Adah
103
Allamah (Shami) in his commentary stated two definitions of 'urf and 'adah: Al-urf wa al-adah ma istiqarra fi al-nufus min jihat al-aql wa talaqathu al-taba'i al-salimah bi al-qubul. (Urf and adah apply to that saying or action which is firmly established in people's hearts by reason and which sound natures accept.)
Al-adah hiya al-amr al-mutakarrar min ghayr 'alaqah 'aqliyyah. Adah is that saying or action which is repeated without a rational connection (without affectation).
Difference between Urf and Adah
From the mention of the above two definitions, Allamah Shami's purpose is to explain a subtle difference between 'urf and 'adah, which is that adah has generality: every action that is repeated without any reason is called adah, whether reason accepts it or not. For example, a person gets accustomed to a word and during conversation he repeats it without reason.
Urf has some particularity: 'urf is that adah which becomes prevalent among people due to the demand of reason, and sound natures accept it. The people of fiqh generally use the word adah in the sense of 'urf, but the people of usul use both synonymously.
Awn al-Mughni fi Hill 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti
Change of Rulings Due to Change of Urf
104
Rulings change with the change of 'urf. There are many rulings which the mujtahid imam of the madhhab based on his own 'urf and conditions of his time, but due to change of time or necessity, they changed. For example: the later jurists giving fatwa on permissibility of taking wages for teaching Quran, leading prayer, teaching, etc., due to necessity; and due to change of time and conditions of people, the apparent justice of witnesses not being sufficient, etc. Due to change of time or necessity, the later jurists gave fatwa contrary to the statement of the imam of the madhhab in many issues. Allamah Shami in his Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti mentioned twenty-four such issues as examples in which the later jurists changed their fatwas based on the mentioned reasons. And in reality, this change in fatwa is not opposition to the imam of the madhhab but rather agreement; because the opinion he formed in his time was also based on the 'urf and custom and necessity of his time. So if this changing situation had come before him, he would have changed his opinion based on those same reasons.
Urf is of Two Types
Further clarification regarding 'urf: 'urf has two types: • 'urf 'amm (general custom) • 'urf khass (specific custom)

Commentary
This passage is a qawida definition and expansion from Awn al-Mughni fi Hill 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, explaining the concepts of 'urf and 'adah and their legal significance in the Hanafi madhhab. The author first presents two definitions from Allamah Shami: the first defines both 'urf and 'adah as what is firmly established in souls by reason and accepted by sound natures. The second defines 'adah alone as what is repeated without a rational connection. The key difference is that 'adah has generality (any repeated action, even irrational), while 'urf has particularity (only that adah which is rationally accepted and prevalent). However, fuqaha often use adah synonymously with 'urf, while usulis treat them as synonyms. The passage then introduces the critical principle that rulings change with the change of 'urf. This is a well-established Hanafi maxim. The author gives examples: later Hanafi jurists allowed taking wages for teaching Quran, leading prayer, etc., due to necessity, whereas the early imams had prohibited it. Also, the requirement for witnesses' apparent justice was relaxed when times changed. The author cites Allamah Shami who listed 24 such issues in his commentary. Importantly, this change is not a departure from the imam's methodology but rather an application of the same principle: the imam based his ruling on his time's 'urf; if the 'urf changes, the ruling follows. Finally, the passage classifies 'urf into two types: general ('amm) and specific (khass). This lesson establishes the authority of custom in Hanafi law and its dynamic application.

Key Terms
عرف (urf): That which is established in souls by reason and accepted by sound natures; a custom with rational basis.
عادت (adah): An action repeated without rational connection; a mere habit.
عرف عام (urf 'amm): Custom prevalent among the majority of people in all regions.
عرف خاص (urf khass): Custom prevalent in a particular locality or profession.
