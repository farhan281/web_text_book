The Gravity and Definition of Ifta: The Mufti as Inheritor of Prophets
Defining the mufti's role and the seriousness of issuing fatwas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the mufti according to Hanafi usul al-iftaa and identify the key conditions for a valid fatwa.

Core Idea
Issuing fatwa is declaring Allah’s ruling; the mufti must be extremely qualified because any error can lead to grave sin.

This lesson explores the foundational definitions of a mufti and the immense responsibility of ifta. You will learn why the Salaf were extremely cautious about issuing fatwas and why ifta is considered a communal obligation. The lesson dissects the key qualifications a mufti must possess according to Hanafi scholars.

Guided Path
1
Understand the gravity of ifta
Read Imam Nawawi's statement that ifta is 'great danger, high status, abundant virtue' and that the mufti is 'muwaqqi‘ an Allah' (one who affixes Allah's seal). This sets the tone for the entire lesson.
The phrase 'muwaqqi‘ an Allah' is key: the mufti speaks on behalf of Allah, so error is catastrophic.
2
Parse the three definitions of mufti from Ibn Hamdan
Identify the three definitions quoted in the text. Note the common element: knowledge of evidence (dalil) and preservation of most fiqh.
The third definition adds the ability to derive rulings for incidents (waqa'i). This shows the mufti must be a mujtahid or at least a qualified faqih.
3
Identify the conditions (shurut) for a mufti
From the definitions, extract the necessary conditions: knowledge of evidence, ability to derive rulings, and retention of most fiqh details.
The legal definition dissector in the toolbox formalizes these as shart.
4
Analyze the caution of the Salaf
Read the quote from Sufyan ibn 'Uyaynah and the anecdote of Ibn Abi Layla. Understand why the companions hesitated to issue fatwas.
This shows that even the most knowledgeable feared misrepresenting Allah's ruling.
5
Recognize ifta as fard kifaya
Imam Nawawi explicitly states that the mufti 'fulfills the communal obligation.' Note the implication: if no qualified mufti exists, the entire community is sinful.
Fard kifaya distinguishes ifta from individual obligations like prayer.

Commentary
This passage introduces the fiqh discipline of ifta (issuing fatwas) by emphasizing its immense responsibility and danger. The author explains that ifta is essentially declaring Allah's ruling on a given matter, so any error can lead to grave sin. He then cites three definitions of the mufti from Imam Ibn Hamdan: (1) one who informs of Allah's ruling with knowledge of its evidence, (2) one who informs from Allah about His ruling, (3) one who is capable of knowing the shariah rulings of incidents by evidence while retaining most fiqh details. This shows the mufti must be a well-rounded scholar. Next, Imam Nawawi's quote highlights that the mufti inherits from prophets, fulfills a communal obligation, yet is exposed to error—hence he is called 'muwaqqi‘ an Allah' (one who affixes Allah's seal). The passage then recounts the extreme caution of the Salaf: Sufyan ibn ‘Uyaynah said the bravest in fatwa is the least knowledgeable, and Ibn Abi Layla describes how the Ansar would refer questions to others rather than give direct answers, demonstrating their humility and fear of misrepresenting Allah's law.

Key Terms
المفتي (al-mufti): One who informs of Allah's ruling on a matter, with knowledge of its evidence, capable of deriving rulings for incidents.
الإفتاء (al-ifta'): The act of explaining and clarifying the divine ruling (hukm Allah) on a given situation, whether obligation, prohibition, validity, etc.
فرض كفاية (fard kifaya): A communal obligation; if some qualified individuals perform it, the rest are absolved; otherwise all are sinful.

The Grave Responsibility of the Mufti and the Danger of Internet Fatwa
Warning to muftis about responsibility and dangers of internet fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a fatwa is context-dependent and identify the required conduct for a mufti to avoid issuing irresponsible rulings.

Core Idea
A fatwa is not a generic text; it is a context-specific answer, and copying it blindly is a slander against Allah.

This passage from Aun al-Mughni issues a stern warning to anyone who issues fatwas without proper knowledge, especially the modern practice of copying internet fatwas without investigation. It reminds us that the mufti acts as Allah's deputy, and a fatwa must be tailored to the specific time, place, and circumstances of the question. Let's unpack this critical adab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mufti's Role
According to the passage, the mufti acts as a deputy (na'ib) of Allah in conveying rulings. This establishes the immense responsibility and the severe consequence of error (slander against Allah).
2
Recognize the Conditions for a Valid Fatwa
A valid fatwa requires the mufti to have mature vision (balagh al-nazar), ability to reach the correct answer through effort (ijtihad), and proper consideration of context (time, circumstances, personal/general).
3
Understand the Danger of Guesswork and Internet Fatwa
Answering by guesswork (andaze) is extremely dangerous. The passage specifically warns against copying internet fatwas without adaptation, because each fatwa is a response to a unique situation that may no longer apply.
4
Apply the Correct Procedure When Unsure
If a mufti is not fully capable, he must either refer the questioner to a senior mufti or seek the answer from a senior mufti and attribute it properly. This avoids misguidance.
5
Extract the Guiding Principle
The core principle is that a fatwa is a case-specific answer (jawab li surat mas'ulah) that depends on time and circumstances; therefore, blind copying is forbidden.

The Rule of Extreme Caution in Takfir
States the rule of utmost caution when issuing a verdict of disbelief

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of utmost caution in takfir and identify when a weak narration may be used to preserve a Muslim's Islamic status.

Core Idea
A mufti must exhaust every possibility, even weak narrations, to avoid takfir.

This passage establishes a fundamental adab for muftis: the bar for declaring someone a disbeliever is extremely high. Even if a person's words appear to be kufr, the mufti must search for any interpretation that would keep the person within Islam. The text cites Hanafi authorities who state that if there is a weak narration in the madhhab that can save a person from takfir, it should be adopted. This rule is rooted in the gravity of takfir and the principle of preserving the believer's status.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the gravity of takfir
Understand that issuing a fatwa of kufr is a serious matter. The mufti must never be hasty.
The passage calls it 'a very serious and dangerous thing.'
2
Identify the rule: dar al-takfir
The mufti must adopt every possible interpretation to avoid declaring a Muslim a disbeliever.
3
Understand the use of weak narrations
Normally, weak narrations are not followed, but in takfir cases, they may be used to save a person's Islam.
Ibn Nujaym: 'If I find any narration – even weak – I will not declare a person of faith a disbeliever.'
4
Apply to a case: ambiguous refusal to pray
If someone says 'I will not pray', the mufti must consider the possibility that it was a refusal to obey the person asking, not a denial of the obligation.
The mufti should not issue a fatwa of kufr in such ambiguous cases.

(7) Issuing a fatwa of kufr (disbelief) on a Muslim over trivial matters is a very serious and dangerous thing. Allamah Ibn Nujaym al-Misri, in his book Al-Bahr al-Raiq, in the chapter on apostates, has quoted from Fatawa al-Sughra that "kufr is a very heavy thing; if I find any narration – even if it is weak – I will not declare a person of faith a disbeliever." The example given is a person who says 'I will not pray' in response to being told to pray. This could be a denial of the obligation of prayer (kufr) or simply a refusal to obey the person's command (not kufr). The mufti must give the benefit of the doubt. This rule is rooted in the principle that maintaining a person's Islam takes precedence over declaring them a disbeliever (dar' al-afasid muqaddam).

Key Terms
الرواية الضعيفة (al-riwayat al-da'ifa): A weak or less authentic narration within the Hanafi school, which normally is not followed but can be used in cases of dire need to avoid takfir.

Retracted Opinions: The Marju' 'Anhu Rule
States the rule that a retracted opinion is like abrogated and cannot be used

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a retracted opinion (marju' 'anhu) and apply the rule that it is like mansukh and cannot be used for fatwa.

Core Idea
A retracted opinion of a mujtahid loses all legal force, just like an abrogated text.

When a mujtahid issues multiple rulings over time, later rulings may supersede earlier ones. The Hanafi school holds that an explicitly retracted opinion (marju' 'anhu) is analogous to an abrogated text (mansukh) and cannot be used for fatwa. This lesson clarifies that principle and illustrates it with Imam Abu Hanifah's retraction on wiping over leather socks.

Guided Path
1
Identify the retracted opinion
Determine if the mujtahid explicitly retracted an earlier ruling. Look for statements of retraction in later works or biographic sources.
Only explicit retraction counts; mere silence or abandoning the view is not enough.
2
Recognize the effect of retraction
The retracted opinion becomes like mansukh (abrogated) in terms of legal force. It is no longer valid for action or fatwa.
3
Apply the rule to fatwa practice
A mufti must search for the final, retracted-to opinion of the mujtahid and give fatwa accordingly. The earlier opinion is to be disregarded.
This rule prevents reliance on outdated or abandoned positions.
4
Study the classical example
Imam Abu Hanifah initially ruled that wiping over leather socks (khuffayn) is impermissible, then retracted and ruled it permissible. Today, the permissibility opinion is the only valid one.
This example shows how the rule operates in practice.

(8) In this couplet, Allamah Shami stated this rule: The saying from which a mujtahid retracts becomes like abrogated. It is not permissible for general muftis to act upon it or give fatwa according to it. Now it is necessary to search only for the saying to which the mujtahid retracted; only that will be acted upon and fatwa given according to it, because the retracted saying is like abrogated. Therefore, just as acting upon an abrogated ruling and giving fatwa are not permissible, similarly acting upon a retracted saying and giving fatwa are not correct. For example, regarding leather socks (khuffayn), the Sahibayn held the opinion of the permissibility of wiping (mash), while Imam Abu Hanifah initially held the opinion of impermissibility of wiping, then he retracted from the impermissibility and gave a fatwa on the permissibility of wiping. Therefore, now fatwa will not be given according to that retracted saying of Imam Abu Hanifah.

Commentary
This passage presents a fundamental rasm al-mufti rule: the legal status of a mujtahid's retracted opinion. The author, Allamah Shami, explains that once a mujtahid (here referring to Imam Abu Hanifah or other founding imams) explicitly retracts an earlier ruling, that earlier ruling becomes analogous to a mansukh (abrogated) ruling. Consequently, it is no longer a valid basis for action or fatwa for any mufti. The only valid position is the one the mujtahid retracted to. The example given is the well-known case of wiping over leather socks. Imam Abu Hanifah initially held that wiping over khuffayn is not permissible, but later retracted and adopted the view of permissibility, aligning with the Sahibayn. Hence, his earlier impermissibility ruling is abandoned. This reinforces the principle that a mufti must follow the final, settled position of the madhhab, not earlier or abandoned opinions.

Key Terms
مرجوع عنہ (marju' 'anhu): A legal ruling or opinion from which a mujtahid has explicitly retracted.
منسوخ (mansukh): A ruling that has been abrogated by a later revelation or by the mujtahid's explicit retraction.
