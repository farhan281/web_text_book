Exceptional Cases Where Qiyas is Preferred Over Istihsan
Methodological rule on exceptions to istihsan preference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify exceptional cases where qiyas is preferred over istihsan and apply the rule to a given scenario.

Core Idea
In a few well-known cases, the qiyasi ruling is given fatwa despite the general rule that istihsan overrides qiyas.

In Hanafi usul, the default when qiyas and istihsan conflict is that istihsan prevails. However, the school recognizes a small set of exceptions where the qiyasi ruling is actually given fatwa. This lesson examines those exceptions, their rationale, and how a mufti should treat them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
State the default hierarchy: istihsan takes precedence over qiyas in case of conflict. This is the established principle in the Hanafi school.
2
Recognize the Exceptions
Understand that there are specific, well-known cases where qiyas is given fatwa instead of istihsan. The number varies (11, 22, or more) but these cases are clearly documented.
3
Analyze the Example Given
Examine the example of a person wanting to demolish his house in a populated area. Istihsan would forbid it due to harm and waste, but qiyas based on ownership allows it. Note which ruling is given fatwa.
4
Understand the Rationale
The rationale for preferring qiyas in these exceptions is that the istihsani consideration (e.g., preventing harm) is outweighed by a stronger legal principle (e.g., absolute ownership rights).
5
Apply to a New Scenario
Given a new scenario, determine whether the general rule (istihsan preferred) or the exception (qiyas preferred) applies. If the scenario matches the characteristics of the known exceptions, rule by qiyas; otherwise, default to istihsan.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological rule (rasm al-mufti) concerning the hierarchy between qiyas and istihsan in the Hanafi madhhab. The general rule is that istihsan takes precedence over qiyas in case of conflict. However, there are a few exceptional issues (eleven or twenty-two according to different scholars) where the qiyasi ruling is actually given fatwa. The author provides an example: a person wants to demolish his house in a populated area. Istihsan would prevent it because it is wasteful and causes harm to neighbors, but qiyas (analogy based on ownership) allows it because one has full rights over one's property. In this case, the fatwa is given on qiyas. The author notes that these exceptional issues are well-known and clearly indicated in the books. The marginal note adds that the count is not fixed and can increase; a contemporary scholar counted 26. This teaches the mufti that while the default preference for istihsan is strong, there are specific exceptions that must be memorized.

Key Terms
قیاس (qiyas): Legal analogy; extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a common effective cause.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; abandoning qiyas for a stronger evidence or consideration of public interest.
راجح (rājiḥ): Preponderant, stronger; the ruling deemed more correct for fatwa.

Definition and Types of Mafhum in Usul al-Iftaa
Explains the concept of mafhum and its two primary divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mafhum and identify whether an example is mafhum muwafiq or mafhum mukhalif and its subtype.

Core Idea
Mafhum reveals the ruling for the unspoken: muwafiq gives the same ruling, mukhalif gives the opposite.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational usul al-fiqh concept of mafhum — the inferred meaning from speech. A mufti must distinguish between the spoken (mantuq) and the implied (mafhum) to derive correct rulings. We will parse the two main divisions: mafhum muwafiq (coextensive) and mafhum mukhalif (opposite), and examine the sub-types of the latter that frequently appear in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Define mafhum and mantuq
Start by identifying the core definition: mantuq is the direct spoken meaning; mafhum is the indirect inferred meaning. The passage states mafhum is 'that which is understood from speech, but speech is not directly uttered for it.'
2
Distinguish mafhum muwafiq and mukhalif
Mafhum muwafiq: the unspoken has the same ruling as the spoken, due to a clear linguistic connection (e.g., hitting parents from 'uff'). Mafhum mukhalif: the unspoken has the opposite ruling, due to a restriction (e.g., only Fajr is obligatory from 'Fajr is obligatory').
Mafhum muwafiq is also called dalalat al-nass or qiyas jali in Hanafi usul.
3
Identify sub-types of mafhum mukhalif
The passage lists three sub-types: mafhum al-sifah (attribute), mafhum al-shart (condition), and mafhum al-ghayah (limit). For each, note how the restriction in the spoken affects the unspoken.
Two additional types (mafhum al-'adad and mafhum al-laqab) are mentioned elsewhere but not here.
4
Apply to examples
Take the examples given: 'Zakat on grazing sheep' (sifah), 'Spend on pregnant divorced women' (shart), 'Until she marries another' (ghayah). Classify each as mafhum mukhalif and identify the subtype.
Also consider the example of 'Fajr prayer is obligatory' – that is mafhum mukhalif without a specific restriction, sometimes called mafhum al-laqab (of proper noun) though not discussed.

Is, a clarification of contrary meaning is presented. What is meant by 'mafhum'? Mafhum is that meaning which is understood from speech, but speech is not directly uttered for it. Types and definitions of mafhum: (1) Mafhum muwafiq (2) Mafhum mukhalif. Mafhum muwafiq is that the words indicate that the same ruling that applies to the spoken (mentioned) also applies to the unspoken (unmentioned), and this indication of the words does not depend on reflection and ijtihad; rather, understanding the literal meaning of the word itself yields this indication. Example: From the divine command {So do not say to them 'uff' nor rebuke them} [al-Isra 23] to deduce that just as it is forbidden to cause pain to parents by speech, so too is it forbidden to cause them pain by hitting. Obviously this deduction requires no reflection or ijtihad; rather, merely knowing the Arabic language yields this meaning. Mafhum mukhalif: That is, to deduce from the word that the opposite of the ruling established for the spoken is established for the unspoken. Example: From someone's statement 'Fajr prayer is obligatory' to understand that 'no prayer other than Fajr is obligatory'. There are five well-known types of mafhum mukhalif: (1) Mafhum al-sifah: That is, by restricting the ruling to a specific attribute among the attributes of the ruled object, to deduce that in the absence of that attribute the mentioned ruling will not exist. Example: From 'Zakat is due on grazing sheep' to derive the ruling that zakat is not obligatory on non-grazing sheep (those fed at home). (2) Mafhum al-shart: That is, by restricting a ruling to a condition, to understand that the opposite ruling of the conditioned applies to the unconditioned. Example: From the divine command {And if they are pregnant, then spend on them} [al-Talaq 6] to take the evidence that the divorced woman who is not pregnant does not have maintenance obligatory upon the husband. (3) Mafhum al-ghayah: That is, regarding a ruling limited to a limit, to hold the opinion that when the limit ceases, the opposite of the pre-limit ruling is established for the post-limit. Example: In {And if he divorces her, then she is not lawful for him after that until she marries another husband} [al-Baqara 230], the ruling of prohibition is limited until a second marriage; from this to deduce that upon marriage (the occurrence of the limit) the opposite of the pre-marriage ruling (prohibition) becomes established (lawfulness) and consummation is not a condition for lawfulness.

Commentary
This passage from Aun Ul Mughni, a commentary on Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, explains a fundamental usul al-fiqh concept essential for a mufti: the distinction between logical meaning (mafhum) and spoken meaning (mantuq). The author first defines mafhum as the meaning understood from speech indirectly. He then divides it into two primary types: mafhum muwafiq (coextensive meaning) and mafhum mukhalif (opposite meaning). Mafhum muwafiq is where the unspoken shares the same ruling as the spoken due to a clear, language-based connection, such as the prohibition of hitting parents being derived from the prohibition of saying 'uff'. This does not require ijtihad. Mafhum mukhalif is where the unspoken receives the opposite ruling, like deducing that only Fajr is obligatory from someone's statement 'Fajr is obligatory'. The author then describes three of the five well-known sub-types of mafhum mukhalif: mafhum al-sifah (attribute), mafhum al-shart (condition), and mafhum al-ghayah (limit). For each, he gives a fiqh example from Hanafi usul. These distinctions are critical for a mufti when deriving rulings from texts, as they determine whether the absence of a condition or attribute negates the ruling. The author does not discuss the validity conditions or exceptions here, but the passage serves as a foundational definition.

مفہوم (mafhūm): The meaning understood from speech indirectly, not the direct spoken words.
مفہوم موافق (mafhūm muwāfiq): When the unspoken situation is even more deserving of the same ruling than the spoken one, or equal to it.
مفہوم مخالف (mafhūm mukhālif): Deducing the opposite of the spoken ruling for the unspoken due to a linguistic restriction.
مفہوم صفت (mafhūm aṣ-ṣifah): Mafhum derived from restricting a ruling by an attribute.

مفہوم غایت (mafhūm al-ġāyah): Mafhum from specifying a limit for a ruling.

Validity of Mafhum al-Mukhalif According to Hanafis
Explaining the three contexts where Hanafis accept mafhum al-mukhalif

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three contexts where Hanafis accept mafhum al-mukhalif, explain its zanni nature, and apply it in a fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
Mafhum al-Mukhalif is zanni but valid in speech, fiqh, and Sahabi sayings — including mafhum al-laqab.

This passage addresses a key usul al-fiqh tool: mafhum al-mukhalif (opposite implication). While even its proponents agree it is speculative (zanni) and can be overridden by stronger texts, the Hanafi school, based on the statements of later scholars, holds that it is valid in three broad spheres: everyday language, jurisprudential reports, and the ijtihad of the Companions. This includes mafhum al-laqab, which the Shafi'is reject. Understanding where and how to apply mafhum al-mukhalif is essential for a mufti who derives rulings from texts and custom.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the speculative nature of mafhum al-mukhalif
Note that even those who accept it consider it zanni. If a clear text contradicts it, the clear text prevails. Example: mafhum al-ghayah from the Quran is overridden by the hadith on 'Usaylah requiring consummation.
2
Identify the three contexts where Hanafis accept it
The passage lists: (1) people's speech/custom, (2) fiqh reports and rulings, (3) sayings of the Companions. In all these, mafhum al-mukhalif is considered valid for deriving rulings.
Hanafis also accept mafhum al-laqab, unlike Shafi'is.
3
Analyze the examples given
First example: someone says 'I owe you no more than 100' — this implies acknowledgment of exactly 100 via mafhum al-mukhalif. Second: 'woman need not undo braids for ghusl' implies that a man must undo his braids. Third: Prophet's order to pray Asr only in Banu Qurayzah leads to differing ijtihad among Companions.
Each example illustrates a different context: speech, fiqh text, Sahabi practice.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Shafi'is reject mafhum al-laqab entirely but accept other types as zanni. The practical difference appears in cases like ghusl for men with braids.
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view for fatwa.
5
Apply the rule in fatwa
When issuing a fatwa, a mufti may use mafhum al-mukhalif as evidence, but must first check for any stronger text (Quran, hadith, ijma) that would override it. If none exists, mafhum al-mukhalif is valid and operative.
This is part of the mufti's usul training.

حنفیہ کے یہاں متاخرین علماء کی صراحت ہے کہ لوگوں کی آپسی بول چال، اُن کے عرف و معاملات، فقہی روایات صحابہ کے اجتہادی اقوال، دلائل عقلیہ وغیرہ میں مفہوم مخالف کی تمام قسمیں: (مفہوم لقب بھی، جو شوافع کے یہاں غیر معتبر ہے ) معتبر ہیں۔

لوگوں کی آپسی گفتگو ، معاملات وغیرہ میں مفہوم مخالف کے معتبر ہونے کی مثال
اگر کوئی شخص کسی دوسرے شخص سے کہے کہ میرے اوپر تیرے سو روپے سے زیادہ واجب نہیں، تو اُس کا یہ قول سوروپے کے اقرار کے مرادف ہوگا اور یہ جملہ کہنے والے پر سو روپے لازم ہوں گے ، ظاہر ہے کہ یہ اقرار کا حکم مفہوم مخالف کے اعتبار کی بنیاد پر ہے۔

فقہی روایات میں مفہوم مخالف کے معتبر ہونے کی مثال
فقہ کی کتابوں میں یہ جزئیہ ہے : ”ولیس على المرأة أن تنقض ضفائرها" (اور غسل کے وقت عورت پر اپنی گوندھی ہوئی چوٹیاں کھولنا ضروری نہیں ) ۔ اس سے معلوم ہوا کہ یہاں عورت کی قید سے مرد سے احتر از مقصود ہے (مرد نے اگر بڑے بال رکھے ہیں اور بالوں کی چوٹیاں بنارکھی ہے، تو غسل جنابت کے وقت اُس پر لازم ہے کہ چوٹیاں کھول کر غسل کرے ) ۔

صحابہ کرام کے اقوال میں مفہوم مخالف کے معتبر ہونے کی مثال
حدیث شریف میں ہے کہ ایک موقع پر آپ عالم نے حضرات صحابہ ے فرمایا: لا يُصَلبَنَّ أَحَدٌ الْعَصْرَ إِلَّا فِي بَنِي قُرَيْظَةَ " (سب کے سے سب کو عصر کی نماز بنوقیظہ میں پڑھنی ہے ) ، حضرات صحابہ صلى الله عليه وسلم کے اجتہادات اس موقع سے بدل گئے ، بعضوں نے لفظ کے ظاہری معنی کو سامنے رکھتے ہوئے یہ نتیجہ نکالا کہ بنو قریظہ میں ہی نماز پڑھنی ہے؛ اس کا مفہوم مخالف یہ ہوا کہ اس سے پہلے راستے میں نماز نہیں پڑھنی ہے؛ اگر چہ نماز کا وقت ختم ہو جائے۔
دوسرے بعض نے نماز کو وقت پر ادا کرنے سے متعلق نصوص کے پیش نظر آپ سا لن لینے کے مبارک ارشاد کا مطلب یہ سمجھا کہ تجھیل مراد ہے، یہ نہیں کے نماز کا وقت
It remains that according to those who uphold the opposite implication (mafhum al-mukhalif), this indication is speculative (zanni); therefore, if a clear evidence comes contrary to the indication of the opposite implication, the indication of the opposite implication will be abandoned and the clear evidence will be acted upon. For example, the implication of the limit (mafhum al-ghayah) indicates that a thrice-divorced woman becomes lawful for the first husband after a second marriage without intercourse, but contrary to this, the hadith of 'Usaylah is reported, in which the Prophet (peace be upon him) clarified that a divorced woman is not lawful for the first husband until the second husband has intercourse with her. Therefore, in this issue, the indication of the opposite implication is ignored and it is necessary to act upon the hadith. This is the position of the majority of scholars, including the Shafi'is. (Guiding Principles of Fatwa Writing: 288)

Where is mafhum al-mukhalif considered valid according to the Hanafis?
According to the Hanafis, there is a clear statement from the later scholars that in people's mutual conversation, their customs and transactions, jurisprudential reports, ijtihadi sayings of the Companions, rational evidence, etc., all types of mafhum al-mukhalif (including mafhum al-laqab, which is not considered valid by the Shafi'is) are valid.

Example of validity of mafhum al-mukhalif in people's mutual conversation and transactions:
If a person says to another: "I owe you nothing more than one hundred rupees," this statement will be equivalent to an acknowledgment of one hundred rupees, and the speaker will be liable for one hundred rupees. Obviously, this ruling of acknowledgment is based on the consideration of mafhum al-mukhalif.

Example of validity of mafhum al-mukhalif in jurisprudential reports:
In the books of fiqh, this issue is mentioned: "It is not necessary for a woman to undo her braids" (i.e., at the time of ghusl, it is not obligatory for a woman to loosen her plaited hair). From this, it is understood that the specification of a woman is intended to exclude a man (if a man has long hair and has braided it, then at the time of ghusl from janabah, it is obligatory for him to untie the braids and perform ghusl).

Example of validity of mafhum al-mukhalif in the sayings of the Companions:
In a noble hadith, on one occasion the Prophet (peace be upon him) said to the Companions: "None of you should pray Asr except in Banu Qurayzah" (i.e., all of you must pray Asr in Banu Qurayzah). The ijtihad of the Companions differed on this occasion. Some, considering the apparent meaning of the words, concluded that prayer must be offered in Banu Qurayzah; the opposite implication (mafhum al-mukhalif) of this is that prayer is not to be offered on the way before reaching there, even if the time of prayer expires. Others, in view of the texts concerning performing prayer on time, understood the blessed command of the Prophet (peace be upon him) to mean haste (i.e., hasten to reach Banu Qurayzah), not that the time of prayer should be missed.

Commentary
This passage explains the Hanafi position on the validity of mafhum al-mukhalif (opposite implication) in various contexts. It begins by noting that even among those who accept mafhum al-mukhalif, it is a speculative (zanni) indication and can be overridden by a clear text (nass). An example is given: the mafhum al-ghayah (implication of a limit) from the Quranic verse (2:230) suggests that a thrice-divorced woman becomes lawful for the first husband after a second marriage even without intercourse, but the hadith of 'Usaylah explicitly requires intercourse, so the hadith takes precedence. This is the view of the majority including Shafi'is.

The core of the lesson is the Hanafi view: according to later Hanafi scholars, mafhum al-mukhalif is valid in three areas: (1) people's everyday speech and transactions, (2) jurisprudential reports (fiqhi riwayat), and (3) the ijtihadi statements of the Companions. Moreover, Hanafis accept all types of mafhum al-mukhalif, including mafhum al-laqab (implication of a title/label), which the Shafi'is reject.

Three examples illustrate these:
- Speech: If someone says "I owe you no more than 100," this implies an acknowledgment of exactly 100, due to mafhum al-mukhalif of the phrase "no more than."
- Fiqh report: The ruling that a woman need not undo her braids for ghusl implies that a man must undo his braids, because the specification of a woman in the ruling restricts it to her opposite.
- Companion practice: The Prophet's order to pray Asr only in Banu Qurayzah led some Companions to understand it literally (mafhum al-mukhalif: do not pray on the way), while others understood it as an instruction to hasten, prioritizing the general obligation of praying on time.

The passage demonstrates that mafhum al-mukhalif is a valid tool for deriving rulings in Hanafi usul, but it remains subject to overriding by stronger evidence.

Key Terms
مفہوم مخالف (mafhum al-mukhalif): Opposite implication: the ruling derived from a restricted phrase, implying the opposite when the restriction is absent.
مفہوم لقب (mafhum al-laqab): Implication of a title/label: a ruling given for a specific name or title implies the opposite for others.
مفہوم غایت (mafhum al-ghayah): Implication of a limit: a ruling limited by an endpoint implies the opposite after that endpoint.
