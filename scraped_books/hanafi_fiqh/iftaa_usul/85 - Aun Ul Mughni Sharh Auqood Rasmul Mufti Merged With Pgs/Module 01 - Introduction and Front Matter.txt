The Gravity of Fatwa: Quranic and Prophetic Foundations
Title page with foundational proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Quranic verse and hadith that establish the importance of fatwa and identify the consequence of giving fatwa without knowledge.

Core Idea
Giving fatwa without knowledge brings the sin upon the mufti.

This lesson introduces the foundational texts that set the tone for the entire study of ifta. The author opens with two weighty proofs: a Quranic verse where Allah Himself is the Mufti, and a Prophetic warning that places the responsibility of fatwa squarely on the one who issues it. Understanding these texts is essential for every student of Hanafi ifta — they are the moral and legal anchors of the discipline.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Proofs
Locate the Quranic verse and the hadith that are cited in the front matter. Recognize that they are presented without commentary, serving as axiomatic foundations.
The verse is from Surah an-Nisa (4:176) and the hadith is widely narrated (e.g., Sunan Abi Dawud).
2
Analyze the Quranic Verse
The verse begins with 'They ask you for a legal ruling' (yastaftunaka), showing that the Prophet was questioned on a matter. Allah instructs him to respond directly — 'Say, Allah gives you a ruling' (quli Allahu yufteekum). This establishes that the ultimate Mufti is Allah, and the Prophet merely conveys the divine ruling.
The specific topic is al-kalalah, a classical inheritance case. The verse thus demonstrates the need for knowledge in ifta.
3
Analyze the Hadith
The hadith states: 'Whoever gives a fatwa without knowledge, his sin is upon the one who gave the fatwa.' This directly places the burden of sin on the mufti, not the questioner. It highlights the personal accountability of the mufti.
Some weak narrations attribute the sin to the questioner; the author cites the correct version. This nuance is important for accuracy.
4
Connect to the Book's Purpose
After citing these proofs, the title of the book — 'The Easy Translation and Commentary of Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' — appears. This indicates that the entire work is a practical guide to help muftis avoid the sin of ignorant fatwa by learning the proper methodology (rasm al-mufti).
The front matter thus frames the book as a tool for fulfilling the obligations of ifta.

The Foundation of Ifta: Quranic Authority and Prophetic Warning
Title page with Quranic epigraph and hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Quranic verse on istifta and the hadith on ignorant fatwa establish the ethical and legal gravity of issuing fatwas in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
Fatwa is a sacred trust: seeking Allah's guidance and avoiding ignorance are its twin pillars.

This lesson opens the book 'Aun al-Mughni' by reflecting on two foundational texts: a Quranic verse where people seek rulings (istifta) from the Prophet, and a hadith warning against issuing fatwas without knowledge. Together, they set the stage for the entire discipline of ifta — the mufti's work is a continuation of prophetic guidance, and its integrity depends on sound knowledge. As you study this front matter, you will see how the author frames the mufti's responsibility not merely as a technical role, but as a religious obligation with eternal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic context
Examine the verse 'Yastaftunaka' (They ask you for a ruling). Notice that the question is about kalalah, a specific inheritance case. This shows that people seek authoritative answers from the Prophet, implying that later, the mufti stands in a similar role of providing guidance from the sources.
The verse is Quran 4:176, which gives a detailed ruling on kalalah.
2
Understand the hadith's warning
The hadith 'Whoever gives a fatwa without knowledge, upon him is the sin of the one he gave the fatwa to' underscores the severe personal accountability of the mufti. It implies that ignorance is not excusable; a mufti must only speak from established knowledge.
This hadith is frequently cited in Hanafi works on ifta, emphasizing the duty to learn before answering.
3
Connect to the mufti's role
Combine both texts: the Quran shows that fatwa is a response to people's need for guidance, and the hadith shows that the mufti must be qualified. Together, they define ifta as a weighty responsibility that requires mastery of the madhhab's sources and ethical conduct.
The author places these two texts prominently to remind the reader of the gravity of the subject.

Navigating Aun Ul Mughni: A Roadmap to Hanafi Fatwa Methodology
Table of contents / index of topics

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the table of contents of a classical Hanafi fatwa manual and identify the key topics and their sequence.

Core Idea
The table of contents of Aun Ul Mughni reveals the logical progression from foundational principles to detailed fatwa rules.

The table of contents (fihrist) of a classical work is not merely an index; it is a conceptual map. Aun Ul Mughni, a commentary on Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, lays out the essential topics a Hanafi mufti must master. By understanding its structure, you gain insight into the hierarchy of sources and the method of issuing fatwa. Let us walk through this roadmap.

Guided Path
1
Identify the major sections
Look at the table: it begins with forewords (taqriz), impressions, words of congratulations, and an introduction. Then it presents the text of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' itself, followed by a series of numbered topics. These topics form the core content of the work.
The forewords by prominent scholars indicate the work's scholarly endorsement.
2
Note the core topics
After the poetic text, key topics include: necessity of giving fatwa on the preponderant (rajih) opinion, fatwa on Zahir al-Riwayah, fatwa on non-Zahir al-Riwayah (Nawadir), books of Zahir al-Riwayah, explanation of Nawadir and Nawazil, and the hierarchy of preferring sayings of Hanafi jurists.
These topics directly correspond to the famous hierarchy of Hanafi fatwa sources.
3
Observe the page numbers
The page numbers indicate the length and depth of each topic. For example, the topic 'Fatwa on preponderant saying' spans pages 13-26, showing detailed discussion, while 'Fatwa on non-apparent narrations' is only from page 17-18.
Use the numbers to gauge emphasis and allocate study time.
4
Recognize the logical flow
The sequence moves from general principles (necessity of rajih) to specific categories (Zahir al-Riwayah, Nawadir) and then to practical application (examples, hierarchy). This reflects the pedagogical approach: first establish the rule, then illustrate, then refine.
Understanding this flow helps internalize the methodology.

Commentary
This passage is the table of contents of the book 'Aun Ul Mughni Sharh Auqood Rasmul Mufti'. It lists the various sections and their starting page numbers. The contents include forewords by prominent scholars, an introduction, the text of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' (a poetic work on the rules of issuing fatwas), and then a series of topics central to Hanafi fatwa methodology. Key topics include: the necessity of giving fatwa according to the preponderant (rajih) opinion, the distinction between rulings from Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Imam Muhammad) and non-Zahir al-Riwayah (Nawadir), the books that constitute Zahir al-Riwayah, explanation of Nawadir and Nawazil issues, the position of Al-Kafi as a comprehensive work, and the hierarchy of preferring sayings of Hanafi jurists. The table of contents serves as a roadmap for the student, indicating the logical flow from foundational principles to detailed rules of fatwa issuance.

Key Terms
ظاہر الروایہ (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani that contain the authoritative narrations of the Hanafi school.
نوادر (Nawadir): Rare or less common narrations that are not part of Zahir al-Riwayah.
نوازل (Nawazil): Newly occurring issues that are not directly covered in the classical texts.
راجح (Rajih): The preponderant or strongest opinion among multiple narrations.

Navigating the Table of Contents of Aun Ul Mughni
Index listing topics and page numbers for Hanafi fatwa methodology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret the table of contents of a Hanafi fatwa methodology text to identify key principles, authority hierarchies, and relevant page references.

Core Idea
The table of contents is a roadmap to the mufti's hierarchy of evidence.

A table of contents may seem mundane, but in a discipline like fatwa methodology, it reveals the blueprint for how rulings are derived and prioritized. This index from Aun Ul Mughni — a commentary on Rasm al-Mufti — organizes topics ranging from Zahir al-Riwayah to the ranking of jurists' opinions. By learning to read this map, you gain a bird's-eye view of the Hanafi fatwa apparatus.

Guided Path
1
Scan for core terminology
Look for repeated terms such as 'Zahir al-Riwayah', 'Nawadir', 'Nawazil', 'Mufta bih', 'Rajih'. These are the building blocks of fatwa methodology.
2
Map the authority hierarchy
The index mentions that statements of the two students are considered those of Imam Abu Hanifa, and that in worship matters Imam's view is preferred, in judiciary Abu Yusuf's, etc. Trace these preferences.
3
Identify key books and their roles
Note references to al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Kafi, Fatawa Qadi Khan, Multaqa al-Abhar. Understand why some are foundational (Zahir al-Riwayah) and others compiled later.
4
Recognize exceptions and caveats
The index warns 'Not everyone's preference is considered' and 'It is not permissible to deviate from Zahir al-Riwayah' — note these constraints.
5
Create a mental map
Combine all information to sketch the mufti's decision tree: start with Zahir al-Riwayah, if not found then Nawadir, then consider later scholars, with specific rules for different fiqh chapters.

Introduction to mafhum mukhalif and its validity in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mafhum mukhalif, list its five types, and explain where Hanafis accept it and where they reject it.

Core Idea
Mafhum mukhalif (opposite meaning) is accepted in everyday speech and rational proofs but rejected as binding in Quran and Sunnah by Hanafis.

Mafhum mukhalif is the implied opposite meaning derived from a text's condition, attribute, limit, number, or label. Hanafis have a nuanced position: they accept it in non-legal contexts (people's conversations, fiqh narrations, sayings of Companions, rational proofs) but do not consider it a binding proof in legal texts (nusus shar'iyyah). This lesson clarifies the concept, types, and the reasoning behind the Hanafi stance.

Guided Path
1
Define mafhum and mafhum mukhalif
Know the difference between explicit meaning (mantuq) and implied meaning (mafhum). Mafhum mukhalif is the opposite of the apparent condition/attribute.
2
List the five types
Mafhum sifah (attribute), shart (condition), ghayah (limit), adad (number), laqab (label). Briefly describe each.
These are the five popular types mentioned in the passage.
3
Identify when mafhum mukhalif is valid according to Hanafis
From the passage: in people's mutual conversations, transactions, fiqh narrations, sayings of Companions, rational proofs.
Note that these are not primary revelatory texts.
4
Explain why it is not valid in legal texts
Hanafis argue that legal texts (Quran and Sunnah) are precise and definitive; taking opposite meanings can lead to contradictions and misinterpretation. They rely on the explicit unless there is strong evidence.
The passage asks this explicitly: 'Why is mafhum mukhalif not valid in legal texts according to Hanafis?'
5
Apply to a simple example
Given a statement: 'If Zaid comes, I will honor him.' (mafhum shart) The opposite 'If Zaid does not come, I will not honor him' is valid in everyday speech but not necessarily as a legal ruling from a Quranic verse.

Commentary
This passage serves as the table of contents for the section on tarjih (preference) rules and mafhum mukhalif (opposite meaning) within the Hanafi usul al-iftaa. It enumerates ten reasons for preferring one ruling over another when multiple authentic or corrected statements conflict. These reasons include linguistic indicators (e.g., 'asahh' vs 'sahih'), the appearance of the word 'fatwa', the statement being the maftun bihi, being the saying of Imam Abu Hanifa, being from zahir al-riwayah, being the view of the majority of mashayikh, being based on istihsan, being more beneficial for endowments, conforming to custom, and having stronger evidence. The passage then transitions to the discussion of mafhum mukhalif, defining it, listing its five types (sifah, shart, ghayah, adad, laqab), posing questions about its validity, and specifying where it is considered valid in Hanafi law—generally not in legal texts but in everyday speech, fiqh narrations, sayings of the Companions, and rational proofs. Key Terms
مفہوم مخالف (mafhum mukhalif): The implied opposite meaning derived from a text, such as from a condition, attribute, etc.
مفہوم صفت (mafhum sifah): The opposite meaning derived from an attribute mentioned in a text.
مفہوم شرط (mafhum shart): The opposite meaning derived from a condition mentioned in a text.
مفہوم غایت (mafhum ghayah): The opposite meaning derived from a limit or end point mentioned in a text.
مفہوم عدد (mafhum adad): The opposite meaning derived from a specific number mentioned in a text.
مفہوم لقب (mafhum laqab): The opposite meaning derived from a label or proper noun mentioned in a text.

Learning Objective

Commentaries must match student capacity—an overly difficult work fails its pedagogical purpose.

He then explains the scholarly need: although other commentaries on 'Rasm al-Mufti' exist, they are too difficult for intermediate students. The author therefore resolved to produce an easy, concise, and understandable commentary. This sets the stage for the entire work, emphasizing the importance of accessibility in Islamic legal education.

Because of all these works, I have strong hope from Allah Almighty that this writing of the dear respected one will be beneficial for the students of the Iftaa department, and it can also be said that it will be beneficial for some teachers as well. Learning Objective

Core Idea
A mufti derives Allah’s ruling from evidence; ifta is a grave responsibility that demands deep knowledge of fiqh and usul.

This passage, drawn from a contemporary Hanafi work, underscores the critical nature of the office of ifta (giving fatwa). The author stresses that a mufti does not merely transmit rulings but must be capable of deriving them from evidence while retaining a broad command of fiqh. We will examine the three definitions of a mufti provided by Imam Ibn Hamdan and consider the qualifications they imply.

Guided Path

In Hanafi tradition, the mufti must follow the tarjih of the madhhab, not his personal opinion.

Rasm al-Mufti is actually the science that discusses the principles and rules of giving fatwas, and it is considered an important part of the science of Usul al-Fiqh. Without it, no one can reach the rank of a faqih. It is the science through which a faqih develops the ability to harmonize between the chapters of fiqh and the practical conditions of human life. Learning Objective

Core Idea
A mufti's training requires accessible primers – even a great sharh can be too detailed for beginners.

This preface reveals a fundamental challenge in iftaa education: the standard commentary by Ibn 'Abidin is rich but beyond many students. The author, inspired by his teacher, sets out to create a bridge text that distills the core principles. Understanding his motive helps us appreciate the layered structure of iftaa learning, from matn to sharh to simplified works.

2

The author categorizes students into high, medium, and low levels. High-level students can manage the detailed sharh, but the majority are medium or low.

4
Grasp the proposed solution

Appreciate the author's methodology
The author focuses on the matn (verses) and only essential explanatory notes, bridging the gap between the concise matn and the detailed sharh.
This methodology is common in Hanafi iftaa education: starting with a concise text, then moving to commentaries.

Commentary
This passage is the author's preface (front matter) explaining the motivation behind writing this book. He begins with the basmalah and 'Dil Ki Baat' (Heartfelt Words), indicating a personal introduction. He narrates that about three years prior to writing, he was tasked with teaching the commentary of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' at the Islamic University in Bangalore. He then consulted his teacher, Maulana Ni'matullah al-Azami, who encouraged him to undertake a simplified translation and commentary focusing only on translating the verses idiomatically and explaining the rules. Therefore, the author decided to create this present work to explain the content of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' and its commentary in an easy manner. This sets the stage for the rest of the book.

In the name of the God, the Enactor of rulings, with His praise I begin my composition. Then perpetual salutation and peace be upon a Prophet who brought us guidance, and upon his family and noble companions, throughout the passage of time and years. Translation: [1] With the help of the name of Allah, who established the laws of Sharia, and with His praise, I am beginning my poetic composition. [2] Then eternal blessings and peace be upon the Prophet who brought us guidance. [3] Also upon his family and his honored companions, as long as time and years continue (until Judgment Day). Vocabulary: Shari': active participle of shara'a, meaning to legislate, to enact law. Nizam: verbal noun, stringing beads, in the sense of passive participle (strung order). Sarmad: eternal, never-ending, layl sarmad: long night. Sahab: plural of sahib, companion. Kiram: plural of karim, honored, praiseworthy. Aun al-Mughni fi Hall 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti 39 :hamr (possibly a misreading) verbal noun, (n) to cross, pass, go beyond. A'wam: plural of 'am, year. Commentary: Allama Shami, considering the hadiths regarding the beginning of any good work and the righteous tradition of the predecessors and elders in this regard, began his poetic work ('Uqud Rasm al-Mufti) by seeking blessing and help from the blessed name of Allah Almighty and praising Allah Almighty. Then he offered a gift of salawat and salam upon the greatest being of the universe. After that, he prayed for peace for the best people on earth, i.e., the family of the Prophet and his beloved companions. Supplication of Allama Shami: 'After this, the poor sinful servant Muhammad ibn 'Abidin seeks [5] the tawfiq of his generous, unique Lord * and success in acceptance in his goals, and in composing a well-arranged jewel and a unique radiant necklace of pearls.' Translation: [4] After praise and salawat, the needy sinful servant Muhammad ibn Abidin. [5] He seeks tawfiq (improvement and completion of work) from his unique, generous Lord. Also acceptance in his goals. [1] Also success in stringing pearls and composing a unique, remarkable (unmatched) necklace. Vocabulary: Nadid: well-arranged, meaning manzud, nadda al-shay' (v.) to arrange, (unclear).

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the didactic poem 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' by Imam Ibn Abidin (Allama Shami), along with its explanatory commentary. The author begins by invoking the name of Allah, the Legislator, and praising Him, following the Prophetic tradition of beginning every important matter with basmalah and hamd. He then sends blessings on the Prophet and his family, acknowledging the source of guidance. In the second part, he introduces himself humbly as the poor, sinful servant Muhammad ibn Abidin, seeking divine tawfiq and acceptance. This humility is characteristic of the great Hanafi scholars, who recognize that all success comes from Allah. The commentary explains key terms like 'shari'' (legislator) and 'sarmad' (eternal), and highlights the author's adherence to the practice of the salaf.

Key Terms
شارع (shāriʿ): اسم فاعل from شرع meaning 'the one who legislates, enactor of rulings'
نظام (niẓām): Verbal noun meaning 'arrangement, stringing (of pearls)', used metaphorically for the poem.
سرمد (sarmad): Eternal, perpetual, never-ending.
