The Mufti Must Follow the Rajih and Zahir al-Riwayah
Explaining two fundamental fatwa rules: rajih opinion and Zahir al-Riwayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two fundamental rules for issuing a fatwa in the Hanafi madhhab and apply them to determine which opinion is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A mufti must give fatwa according to the rajih opinion; if none, then according to Zahir al-Riwayah.

This passage from 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti by Allamah Shami lays down the foundational hierarchy that every mufti must follow when issuing fatwas. It introduces two key rules: first, the rule of following the preponderant (rajih) opinion, and second, the default reliance on the authoritative narrations of Zahir al-Riwayah. Understanding this hierarchy prevents arbitrary selection and ensures fatwas are grounded in the madhhab's established methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rules
From the passage, state the two rules: (1) follow the rajih opinion, (2) if no rajih, follow Zahir al-Riwayah.
These are procedural rules for the mufti, not substantive fiqh rulings.
2
Distinguish the scope of each rule
The first rule applies when a tarjih (preponderance) is explicitly established by scholars; the second rule serves as a default when no such tarjih exists.
The second rule ensures continuity with the core madhhab texts.
3
Analyze the witr example
Examine how the three narrations from Imam Abu Hanifah were resolved: the mashayikh chose wajib as rajih, so the mufti must adopt that.
This shows that tarjih can be determined by later scholars, not just the founding imams.
4
Apply the rules to a scenario
Given multiple opinions on a new issue, first check if any opinion is declared rajih by reliable Hanafi scholars; if not, look to Zahir al-Riwayah; if still not, refer to later fatawa hierarchy.
The mufti must verify through multiple sources, not rely on a single book.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti by Allamah Shami explains two fundamental rules for a mufti when issuing fatwas. The first rule is that a mufti must follow the rajih (preponderant) opinion within the Hanafi madhhab. This is not a casual matter; the mufti must verify through multiple reliable sources that an opinion is indeed considered rajih by the scholars of tarjih. Relying on a single book may lead to error if that book has mistakenly corrected a weak opinion. Even a mujtahid mufti must follow what is preponderant according to his own ijtihad, unless the proofs are so conflicting that no preference is possible—then he may choose. The example of witr (the odd-numbered prayer) illustrates this: although Imam Abu Hanifah has three reported positions (fard, wajib, sunnah), the later mashayikh have determined that the wajib opinion is the rajih, and so the mufti must issue fatwa accordingly. The second rule provides a default: if no explicit tarjih exists, the mufti should base his fatwa on what is recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Imam Muhammad). The passage underscores that these rules prevent talfiq and capricious selection, ensuring the fatwa process is methodical and grounded in the madhhab's established hierarchy.

Key Terms
راجح (Rājiḥ): The opinion that is considered preponderant or stronger based on evidence and the weight of scholarly authority within the madhhab.
ظاہر الروایۃ (Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah): The six foundational books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, etc.) containing the narrations from Abu Hanifah and the two companions.
مفتی (Muftī): A qualified jurist authorized to issue fatwas (legal rulings) in the Hanafi madhhab.

First Rule: In Ibadat, Follow Imam Abu Hanifa's Opinion
Commentary on the first rule for muftis from Rasm al-Mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the first rule from Rasm al-Mufti to determine when to give fatwa according to Imam Abu Hanifa's qawl in 'ibādāt, and identify the exception when mashāyikh have ruled on a marjū' 'anh narration.

Core Idea
In ibadat, the mufti defaults to Imam Abu Hanifa's qawl; only a marjū' 'anh explicitly adopted by mashāyikh can override.

This passage introduces the first of nine ḍawābiṭ (rules) for a Hanafi mufti, compiled by Imam Ibn Abidin (Allama Shami) in his Sharḥ 'Uqūd Rasm al-Mufti. The rule governs fatwa in acts of worship ('ibādāt). Understanding this hierarchy ensures consistency within the school while allowing necessary exceptions based on authentic transmissions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the domain
Determine that the issue falls under 'ibādāt (prayer, fasting, Hajj, zakat, etc.). The rule applies only to these chapters.
2
Apply the default
Fatwa must be given on the basis of Imam Abu Hanifa's statement (qawl), regardless of whether a student's opinion is present.
The text says 'mulaqqan' (absolutely).
3
Check for the exception
Determine if the mashāyikh have explicitly given a ruling based on a marjū' 'anh (retracted) narration of Imam Abu Hanifa. If yes, that ruling overrides the default.
The retraction must be known and confirmed.
4
Trace the narration
Even in the exception, the adopted ruling must be supported by some riwāya from Imam Abu Hanifa. It is never a total departure from his school.
Example: used water (mā' musta'mal) – ruling of purity attributed to Imam Muhammad but also a riwāya from Imam Abu Hanifa.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the commentary on the nine rules (ḍawābiṭ) for a mufti, extracted from the poem 'Rasm al-Mufti'. Allama Shami (Ibn Abidin) compiled these rules from various sources and presented them in his Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. The first rule is about acts of worship ('ibādāt). In all chapters of 'ibādāt—prayer, fasting, Hajj, Zakat—the mufti must give fatwa according to the opinion of Imam Abu Hanifa (Imam Sahib), regardless of whether there is a student (like Abu Yusuf or Muhammad) with him or not. This is the default. The exception is if the later mashāyikh (scholars) have explicitly ruled on a 'marjū' 'anh' narration from Imam Abu Hanifa—i.e., a narration that Imam Abu Hanifa himself withdrew from or abandoned. In that case, the mufti leaves the preferred statement (qawl mukhtār) of Imam Abu Hanifa and follows that explicit ruling of the mashāyikh. However, even in such exceptional cases, the ruling adopted is not entirely outside the school of Imam Abu Hanifa, because the mashāyikh's ruling is supported by some narration from him. The example given is the purity of used water (mā' musta'mal): the ruling that it is pure (ṭāhir) is attributed to Imam Muhammad, but there is also a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa supporting it, so it remains within his qawl. Thus, the Hanafi school maintains that in 'ibādāt, the fatwa is virtually always traceable to Imam Abu Hanifa's ijtihād.

Key Terms
مطلقاً (muṭlaqan): Absolutely, without any condition or exception
مرجوع عنہ (marjū' 'anh): A narration or opinion that Imam Abu Hanifa himself withdrew or retracted
مشائخ (mashāyikh): Later senior scholars of the Hanafi school, especially those who compiled fatwa collections (e.g., Ibn Nujaym, Haskafi)

When Is a Weak Opinion (Qawl Da'if) Permissible?
Couplet stating exceptions for acting on weak opinions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two exceptions to the prohibition of acting on a weak opinion and apply the rule to determine whether a weak opinion may be used in fatwa or judicial verdict.

Core Idea
Weak opinions are banned except for personal necessity or for a renowned scholar; judges can never use them.

This lesson unpacks a foundational rasm al-mufti rule: when, if ever, can a weak opinion (qawl da'if) be acted upon? The couplet you are about to study lays down a clear general prohibition, then carves out two narrow exceptions. It also absolutely forbids judges from basing verdicts on weak opinions. As a mufti, you must internalize this rule to maintain the integrity of fatwa and judiciary.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Identify the core prohibition: it is not permissible to act upon a weak opinion, nor to give it as an answer to a questioner.
The rule protects the madhhab's authoritative positions.
2
List the two exceptions
The couplet allows acting on a weak opinion in two cases: (1) a person facing darurah (necessity), acting for himself; (2) a person with ma'rifah mashhurah (well-known knowledge, i.e., a recognized scholar).
These exceptions are narrow and conditional.
3
Note the absolute prohibition for judges
A judge (qadi) is never permitted to base a verdict on a weak opinion; if he does, the judgment is void.
This underscores the higher standard of proof required in judiciary.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Given a hypothetical situation, decide whether acting on a weak opinion is allowed under the rule.
Practice distinguishing between personal necessity and formal fatwa/judgment.

Commentary
This passage is a rasm al-mufti rule presented in poetic form, instructing the mufti on when a weak opinion (qawl da'if) may be used. The general rule is that weak opinions are not to be acted upon nor given as answers to questioners. However, two exceptions are given: (1) a person acting for himself who faces a necessity (darurah) may follow a weak opinion, and (2) a scholar with well-known knowledge (ma'rifah mashhurah) may also act upon it, presumably in his own practice or when giving fatwa to himself. Importantly, a judge (qadi) is prohibited from basing his verdict on a weak opinion, and if he does so, his judgment is void and not enforceable. This rule ensures that the fatwa and judiciary adhere to strong, reliable positions of the madhhab, reserving weak opinions only for extraordinary circumstances.

Key Terms
قول ضعیف (qawl da'if): A weak opinion, i.e., a less authoritative view within the madhhab, not the relied-upon position.
ضرورة (darurah): Necessity, a condition that permits acting upon a weak opinion.
معرفة مشهورة (ma'rifah mashhurah): Well-known knowledge, i.e., the person is a recognized scholar of high standing.
