Tarjih Rules for Ibadat, Qada, and Dhawi al-Arham
A set of domain-specific tarjih rules for the Hanafi mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the three domain-specific tarjih rules (Imam in ibadat, Abu Yusuf in qada, Muhammad in dhawi al-arham) to determine the mufta bihi position for a given case.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fatwa, the preferred opinion depends on the domain: Imam for ibadat, Abu Yusuf for qada, Muhammad for dhawi al-arham.

A mufti must know not only the rulings but also whose opinion carries weight in different areas. This passage from a later usul al-iftaa text lays down three concise rules: in worship (ibadat), Abu Hanifa’s view is preferred; in judiciary (qada), Abu Yusuf’s view is chosen; and in inheritance of distant relatives (dhawi al-arham), Muhammad al-Shaybani’s view is taken. Understanding these rules prevents a one-size-fits-all approach to tarjih.

Guided Path
1
Identify the domain
Determine whether the case pertains to ibadat (worship), qada (judiciary), dhawi al-arham (inheritance by distant relatives), or another area. This classification triggers the specific tarjih rule.
If the case falls outside these three, other general Hanafi tarjih principles apply.
2
Apply the domain-specific rule
For ibadat, the preferred opinion is that of Abu Hanifa, unless a sound narration from him supports a different view. For qada, the opinion of Abu Yusuf is selected. For dhawi al-arham, the opinion of Muhammad al-Shaybani is taken.
These rules are exceptions to the general preference for the majority (Abu Hanifa and the two companions).
3
Check for exceptions
In the ibadat rule, there is an exception: if a sound narration (riwaya) from Abu Hanifa contradicts his own opinion and another imam took that, then that other imam's opinion may be preferred. The passage gives the example of tayammum.
The exception ensures that authentic transmissions are not ignored.
4
Consider istihsan over qiyas
The Hanafis generally give preponderance to istihsan over qiyas, except in a few clear cases. This principle underlies many tarjih decisions.
This is a broader methodological rule that complements the domain-specific rules.
5
Verify with the general principle
Finally, ensure that the chosen opinion aligns with the apparent meaning of transmitted texts (zahir al-marwi). Do not abandon a clear riwaya for mere opinion (diraya) unless a strong diraya supports it.
This guards against arbitrary departure from established texts.

Commentary
This passage provides three specific tarjih rules. First, in ibadat (worship), Abu Hanifa's opinion is given preponderance absolutely, unless there is a sound narration from him to the contrary; the example given is tayammum (though the OCR is garbled). Second, in all matters related to judiciary (qada), the opinion of Abu Yusuf is selected. Third, in the inheritance of dhawi al-arham (distant relatives by kinship), the opinion of Muhammad is taken. Additionally, the Hanafis prefer istihsan over qiyas generally, except for some clear cases. A general principle: the apparent meaning of transmitted texts (zahir al-marwi) is not to be abandoned in favor of mere opinion unless a strong diraya (knowledge) supports it.

Key Terms
ذوو الأرحام (Dhawi al-Arham): Blood relatives who are neither ashab al-furud (fixed-share heirs) nor asaba (residuary heirs); they inherit by special Hanafi rules.
الاستحسان (Istihsan): Juristic preference that overrides qiyas due to a stronger evidence or custom.

Applying the Double Share Rule to Distant Relatives: The Ikhtilaf of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Explanation of rule and intra-Hanafi dispute on applying double share rule to distant relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for inheritance among distant relatives when connecting ancestors differ in gender, and distinguish the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the application of the double share rule.

Core Idea
When distant relatives inherit through ancestors of different genders, Abu Yusuf applies the double share rule directly to the claimants, while Muhammad applies it first to the ancestors.

This passage from Aun al-Mughni presents a critical nuance in Hanafi inheritance law: the application of the double share rule (لِلذَّكَرِ مِثْلُ حَظِّ الْأُنْثَيَيْنِ) to distant relatives (dhawi al-arham) when the connecting ancestors differ in gender. The text outlines the general rules and then delves into a specific contested scenario, illustrating the methodological difference between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad. Understanding this dispute is essential for a mufti when issuing fatwas on complex inheritance cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for equal-degree distant relatives
The text states that if the connecting ancestors (usul) of the claimants are united in gender, there is consensus on applying the double share rule to the claimants directly. If the ancestors differ in gender, a dispute arises.
The ancestors are the immediate children of the deceased who have died, leaving their own children as claimants.
2
Parse the example of the disputed scenario
Work through Aisha's family tree: she had two daughters (Fatima and Ruqayya) who died before her, each having a child: Fatima had a son Zaid (male ancestor), Zaid had a daughter Hajira (female claimant); Ruqayya had a daughter Umm Kulthum (female ancestor), Umm Kulthum had a son Abdullah (male claimant). The ancestors (Zaid male, Umm Kulthum female) differ in gender.
Note the gender of each ancestor and claimant.
3
Apply Abu Yusuf's method: direct application of the double share rule
Abu Yusuf ignores the gender of the ancestors and applies the double share rule directly to the claimants. Since Abdullah is male and Hajira female, Abdullah gets two shares, Hajira gets one.
Outcome: Abdullah:2, Hajira:1.
4
Apply Muhammad's method: application to ancestors first
Muhammad first divides the estate among the ancestors (Zaid and Umm Kulthum) using the double share rule, giving Zaid two shares and Umm Kulthum one. Then those shares pass to their descendants: Zaid's share goes entirely to Hajira, Umm Kulthum's share to Abdullah.
Outcome: Hajira:2, Abdullah:1.
5
Compare outcomes and identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference (thamarah) is that in this case, the shares are reversed: Abu Yusuf gives the male descendant more, Muhammad gives the female descendant more. The mufti must be aware of this ikhtilaf when issuing fatwas.
This ikhtilaf arises only when ancestors are of opposite genders.

Commentary
This passage from Aun al-Mughni deals with a specific rasm al-mufti issue: how to distribute inheritance among distant relatives (dhawi al-arham) when the claimants are of equal degree but their connecting ancestors (the immediate descendants of the deceased) differ in gender. The text first establishes the general rule: if one claimant is a descendant of a regular heir and the other is a descendant of a distant relative, the former inherits and the latter is excluded. Then it considers the case where all claimants are either all from heirs or all from distant relatives. Within that, there is a unified scenario where the connecting ancestors (e.g., two daughters) are of the same gender, leading to consensus on applying the double share rule directly to the claimants. The disputed scenario arises when the connecting ancestors differ in gender (one male, one female). The text gives a detailed example involving Aisha and her descendants. The ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad is presented: Abu Yusuf applies the double share rule directly to the current claimants (the great-grandchildren), giving two shares to the male descendant (Abdullah) and one to the female (Hajira). Muhammad, however, first applies the double share rule to the connecting ancestors (the grandchildren), then passes those shares down to the respective descendants. The outcome can differ because Muhammad's method effectively gives more weight to the gender of the connecting link. This passage illustrates the depth of Hanafi legal reasoning within inheritance law and the importance of knowing the methodology of the major disciples for issuing fatwas.

Key Terms
ذوي الأرحام (dhawi al-arham): Distant relatives who are not regular heirs (as defined in Hanafi law), such as grandchildren through daughters, etc.

أصل (asl): The connecting ancestor or principle through whom a claimant inherits, e.g., a child of the deceased.
فرع (far'): The descendant claimant who inherits through the 'asl', e.g., a grandchild.

Types of Mafhum Mukhalif and Their Validity
Defines five types of mafhum mukhalif with examples and discusses school positions on their validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five types of mafhum mukhalif with examples and explain why the Hanafi school rejects them while the Shafi'i school accepts four types.

Core Idea
Mafhum mukhalif is not a valid proof in the Hanafi school; only mafhum muwafiq is accepted.

This passage from Aun Ul Mughni introduces the five well-known types of opposite implication (mafhum mukhalif) in usul al-fiqh. Understanding these types is crucial for a mufti to correctly derive rulings from texts. The passage then highlights the critical disagreement between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on the validity of these implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual feature for each type
For each of the five types, note which linguistic element (adjective, conditional clause, limit, number, noun) restricts the ruling.
The restriction is key to drawing the opposite implication.
2
Connect each type to its example
For each restriction, examine the example given (zakat on grazing sheep, maintenance of pregnant divorced women, etc.) and understand how the opposite implication is drawn.
The examples are from Quran and hadith.
3
Classify the implication
After understanding, label each example as mafhum sifah, shart, ghayah, 'adad, or laqab.
This classification is the basis for the dispute.
4
Distinguish mafhum muwafiq from mukhalif
Note that mafhum muwafiq (coherent implication) is unanimously accepted, while mafhum mukhalif is disputed.
Hanafis accept only muwafiq.
5
Trace the school difference and thamarah al-khilaf
Identify which types the Shafi'is accept and why Hanafis reject them. Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) for each example: e.g., zakat on non-grazing animals, maintenance of non-pregnant divorced women, and lawfulness after second marriage without intercourse.
This step shows the real-world impact of usul differences.

مفہوم کی کون سی قسم معتبر ہے؟

مفہوم کی بنیادی دو قسموں: (موافق ، مخالف) میں سے پہلی قسم : " مفہوم موافق، تمام ہی علماء وفقہاء کے نزدیک نصوص شرعیہ، غیر شرعیہ؛ ہر جگہ معتبر ہے اور دوسری قسم : " مفہوم مخالف" کی اقسام میں سے مفہوم لقب " تو کسی کے نزدیک معتبر نہیں ؛ البتہ اس کے علاوہ بقیہ چاروں قسمیں حضرات شوافع کے نزدیک معتبر ہیں ؛ اسی بنا پر شافعیہ کے نزدیک مفہوم مخالف سے استدلال کرتے ہوئے ، مفہوم صفت کے اعتبار سے گھر پر چارہ کھانے والے جانوروں پر زکاۃ فرض نہیں، مفہوم شرط کا اعتبار کرتے ہوئے بائنہ غیر حاملہ کا نفقہ شوہر پر واجب نہیں۔ مفہوم غایت" کا اعتبار کرتے ہوئے نکاح ثانی ہوتے ہی ، بغیر جماع کے مطلقہ ثلاثہ شوہر اول کے لیے حلال ہے۔ مفہوم عدد کا اعتبار کرتے ہوئے حد قذف میں اسی (۸۰) سے زائد کوڑے کی اجازت نہیں ، مگر یہاں یہ بات بھی پیش نظر
There are five famous types of opposite implication (mafhum mukhalif): 1. Implication of description (mafhum sifah): referring to the ruling being restricted to a specific attribute, deducing that in the absence of that attribute, the mentioned ruling does not exist. Example: from 'On grazing sheep is zakat' deducing that non-grazing (home-fed) sheep are not subject to zakat. 2. Implication of condition (mafhum shart): restricting a ruling to a condition, understanding that for the unconditional the opposite ruling of the conditional is established. Example: from the verse 'And if they are pregnant, then spend on them' deducing that a divorced woman (ba'in) who is not pregnant does not have maintenance obligatory on the husband. 3. Implication of limit (mafhum ghayah): regarding a ruling limited to an end, forming the opinion that when the end finishes, the opposite of the pre-end ruling is established for the post-end. Example: from 'And if he divorces her, she is not lawful for him after that until she marries another husband' the ruling of non-lawfulness is limited to the end of second marriage, deducing that upon marriage, the opposite of the pre-marriage ruling (lawfulness) is established and intercourse is not a condition for lawfulness. 4. Implication of number: a ruling that comes with a specific number, deducing that other than the mentioned number, the opposite of that ruling is established. Example: from 'Then flog them with eighty lashes' deducing that more than eighty lashes is not permitted. 5. Implication of title (mafhum laqab): attaching a ruling to a non-derived noun. Example: from 'On sheep is zakat' understanding that zakat is not obligatory on any animal other than sheep. Which type of implication is valid? Of the two basic types (muwafiq and mukhalif), the first type (muwafiq) is considered valid by all scholars and jurists in all places, both for religious and non-religious texts. As for the second type (mukhalif), among its types, mafhum laqab is not considered valid by anyone; but the remaining four types are considered valid by the Shafi'is. Consequently, according to the Shafi'is, using mafhum mukhalif, by mafhum sifah, non-grazing animals are not subject to zakat; by mafhum shart, a non-pregnant ba'in woman does not have maintenance; by mafhum ghayah, upon the second marriage, the thrice-divorced woman becomes lawful for the first husband without intercourse; by mafhum 'adad, in the hadd of qadhf, more than eighty lashes is not permitted. But here this point is also considered.

Commentary
This passage from Aun Ul Mughni defines the five well-known types of mafhum mukhalif (opposite implication) in usul al-fiqh and discusses their validity across schools. The author first enumerates each type with Quranic or prophetic examples: mafhum sifah (description), mafhum shart (condition), mafhum ghayah (limit), mafhum 'adad (number), and mafhum laqab (title). For each, a clear legal example is given from fiqh rulings such as zakat on grazing animals, maintenance of divorced pregnant women, the waiting period for remarriage after triple divorce, the fixed lashing penalty for slander, and zakat on sheep only. After listing, the author turns to the question of which of these implications are considered valid (mu'tabar). It states that mafhum muwafiq (coherent implication) is unanimously accepted by all scholars. For mafhum mukhalif, mafhum laqab is rejected by everyone. The remaining four types (sifah, shart, ghayah, 'adad) are accepted by the Shafi'i school but not by the Hanafi school (as is the established position). The passage then applies these Shafi'i acceptances to the examples. Note that Hanafis generally do not accept mafhum mukhalif as a binding proof; they rely on explicit text or qiyas. The passage is instructive for understanding the methodological difference between schools, particularly in deriving rulings from textual implications.

Key Terms
مفہوم مخالف (mafhum mukhalif): A ruling implied by the text that is opposite to the ruling of the unmentioned case, based on restriction to a specific attribute, condition, limit, number, or title.
مفہوم موافق (mafhum muwafiq): A ruling implied by the text that is consistent with the stated ruling, such as a fortiori reasoning.
مفہوم لقب (mafhum laqab): Implication derived from a non-derived noun (laqab), e.g., 'on sheep is zakat' implying no zakat on others.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school rejects all types of mafhum mukhalif as valid proof, while the Shafi'i school accepts four types, leading to practical differences in rulings on zakat, maintenance, and marriage.
