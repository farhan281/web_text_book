Hierarchy of Imams and Tarjih Rule for the Contemporary Mufti
Ranks and tarjih rule for the Hanafi mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchy of Hanafi imams and apply the rule of tarjih for a contemporary mufti when the Sahibayn disagree with Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
The contemporary mufti must follow the established hierarchy, not independent evidence, when the Sahibayn conflict with Abu Hanifa.

The Hanafi school has a clear hierarchy of imams for prioritizing rulings. This passage, from a text on the etiquette of the mufti, lays out the order and the rule for dealing with disagreements between the founder and his disciples. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for issuing correct fatwas within the school.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the hierarchy
State the order of precedence among the Hanafi imams exactly as listed: Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad al-Shaybani, then Zufar, then Hasan ibn Ziyad.
This order is the foundation for all tarjih within the madhhab.
2
Understand the two positions on Sahibayn disagreement
Note that there are two opinions when the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree with Abu Hanifa: one says following them causes 'takhsir' (loss), the other allows a mujtahid mufti to prefer the stronger evidence.
These positions are both reported within the school.
3
Identify the contemporary ruling
The text concludes that for the contemporary age, there is no independent tarjih by evidence; the mufti must follow the detailed approach (tafsil), i.e., the hierarchy of imams.
This applies to muftis who are not qualified for ijtihad.
4
Apply the rule to a conflict
Given a specific issue where Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ, determine which opinion to follow based on the hierarchy, not on personal assessment of evidence.
Even if the Sahibayn's evidence seems stronger, the Imam's view takes precedence.

Commentary
This passage establishes the hierarchy of the Hanafi imams: first Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf (Ya'qub), then Muhammad al-Shaybani, then Zufar, then Hasan ibn Ziyad. If a later mufti wants to choose between conflicting reports, he first looks for Abu Hanifa's chosen position; if not, then Abu Yusuf's, etc. Then it addresses disagreement between Imam and Sahibayn. Two opinions: one says following Sahibayn contra Imam causes 'loss' (invalid fatwa); the other says follow the strongest evidence, but this applies only to a mujtahid. The contemporary rule is that there is no independent tarjih by dalil; the mufti must follow the detailed approach (tafsil), i.e., the established tarjih of the madhhab.

Key Terms
تخسير (takhsir): A causing of loss; possibly meaning the fatwa is invalid or the mufti is sinful if he contravenes the Imam.
ترجيح (tarjih): Preferring one evidence or opinion over another; the act of weighing and choosing.
تفصيل (tafsil): Detailed approach; here it means following the established hierarchy of imams rather than independent evidence.
مفتي ذو اجتهاد (mufti dhu ijtihad): A mufti who is qualified to perform ijtihad, i.e., derive rulings directly from the sources.
صاحبان (Sahibayn): The two companions of Abu Hanifa: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Hierarchy of Hanafi Opinions for Fatwa
Stating the ranking of Hanafi imams for tarjih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hierarchy of Hanafi opinions for fatwa when Abu Hanifa's own choice is unknown, and apply it to determine the relied-upon ruling in a given case.

Core Idea
The Hanafi mufti must first seek Abu Hanifa's own selection; if absent, follow the established order: Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar, then Ibn Ziyad.

A mufti does not simply choose any opinion from the madhhab. There is a precise hierarchy of authorities that must be respected to ensure consistency and reliability. This lesson presents that hierarchy as a memorization anchor, critical for anyone issuing fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
Recognize that Abu Hanifa himself made selections among variant narrations attributed to him – those selections are the first recourse for a mufti.
This is the highest level of authority within the madhhab.
2
Memorize the default order
If no explicit choice from Abu Hanifa is found, the mufti must follow a fixed order: first Abu Yusuf (Ya'qub), then Muhammad al-Shaybani, then Zufar, then Ibn Ziyad.
This order is based on the students' proximity and reliability as per the later consensus.
3
Understand the exception for Sahibayn
The passage mentions a disagreement (qila) when Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) oppose Abu Hanifa. Some say the mufti should carefully distinguish (takhseer), others say follow the stronger proof.
This is a contested point; later practice often prefers the Sahibayn in mu'amalat but the Imam in ibadat.
4
Recognize the modern limitation
The passage concludes that in contemporary times (al-aan), there is no independent tarjih by proof; the only method is to follow the detailed hierarchy without weighing proofs.
This restricts the mufti to the hierarchical order, especially if they are not a mujtahid.

Hierarchy of Sources: Mutun, Sharh, and Fatawa
Verses establishing hierarchical weight of Hanafi texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchical rule (mutun > shuruh > old fatawa) to determine the mufta bihi position in a given scenario involving conflicting sources.

Core Idea
Mutun trump commentaries; commentaries trump old fatwas; later fatwas in trusted collections are preferred.

A mufti often faces conflicting rulings from different types of Hanafi texts. Which source should he prioritize? This lesson unpacks a classic poetic formulation of the hierarchy: core matn texts (mutun) are the most authoritative, followed by their commentaries (shuruh), and then older fatwa collections. Special status is given to two major fatwa compilations, al-Khaniyah and Multaqa al-Abhur, where earlier statements carry extra weight. Understanding this ranking is essential for sound fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source type
Determine whether the source in question is a matn (core text), a sharh (commentary), or a fatwa collection (old or new). This classification determines its initial rank.
Mutun are base texts like al-Quduri; shuruh are commentaries like Sharh al-Wiqaya; fatwa collections include al-Khaniyah, Multaqa, and others.
2
Apply the default hierarchy
If sources conflict, prefer mutun over shuruh, and shuruh over old fatawa. The poem states: 'Mutun outweigh shuruh, and shuruh outweigh old fatawa.'
This is the primary ranking unless a later source corrects a wording (suhhih).
3
Check for special status of al-Khaniyah and Multaqa
If the conflict involves these two works, their earliest statements (sabiq al-aqwal) have special merit (maziyyah). They are not automatically overridden by later statements in other works.
The poem says: 'And the earlier statements in al-Khaniyah and Multaqa al-Abhur have merit.'
4
For other fatwa collections, prefer later statements
In fatwa compilations other than those two, rely on what is later (ma akharu), because that is the finalized (muharrar) position, as is the custom in al-Hidayah and similar works.
This principle ensures that the mature, refined rulings of the madhhab are followed.
5
Consider corrected wordings
If a later source explicitly corrects a wording from a higher source, that corrected wording becomes preponderant, even if it comes from a lower-level source.
The line 'مَا لَمْ يَكُنْ سِوَاهُ لَفْظًا صُحِّحَا' indicates an exception: correction overrides rank.

Every statement established in the Mutun, that is a preponderance (tarjih) for it within us. So the Mutun outweigh the Sharh, and the Sharh outweigh the old Fatawa that have preponderance. Unless another wording is corrected, then the most preponderant is the one with which... And the earlier statements in al-Khaniyah and Multaqa al-Abhur have merit. In other than those two, rely on what they later made, because it is the finalized (muharrar), as is the custom in al-Hidayah and the like for the preponderant knowledge.

Commentary
This passage establishes a hierarchy of textual authority. The Mutun (core books like al-Quduri, al-Mukhtasar) are given greater weight than commentaries (shuruh), and commentaries take precedence over older fatwa collections. However, if a later text corrects a wording, that corrected version becomes preponderant. Among later compilations, the statements in al-Khaniyah (a fatwa collection) and Multaqa al-Abhur (by Ibn Nujaym) are specially favored. In other works not as authoritative, the later-dated rulings are preferred because they represent the finalized, muharrar position of the madhhab. This is the practice in al-Hidayah and similar works that favor the preponderant view.

Key Terms
المتون (al-mutun): Core matn texts of the Hanafi school, such as al-Quduri, al-Mukhtasar, etc. They are the base texts.
الشروح (al-shuruh): Commentaries on the mutun, like Sharh al-Wiqaya. They are secondary to the matn.
الفتاوى القديم (al-fatawa al-qadim): Old fatwa collections, lesser in weight than shuruh.
الخانية (al-Khaniyah): Al-Fatawa al-Khaniyah, a major Hanafi fatwa collection by al-Hasan ibn Mansur al-Khani.
ملتقى الأبحر (Multaqa al-Abhur): A famous Hanafi legal manual by Ibrahim al-Halabi, containing the relied-upon positions.

Preferred Terms and Preponderance Factors
Verses detailing tarjih terms and preponderance factors for corrected sayings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchy of tarjih terms (ashbah, azhar, sahih, asahh, etc.) and list the external preponderance factors when two corrected sayings conflict.

Core Idea
When two sayings are both corrected, the mufti must weigh external factors — not just the labels.

A mufti often finds rulings marked as 'corrected'. But not all corrections are equal. This poem teaches a hierarchy of terms and a set of preponderance factors that tip the scale when two sayings both claim correction. Mastering this hierarchy is essential for sound fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key tarjih terms
List the terms mentioned: al-ashbah, al-azhar, al-mukhtar, al-awjah, al-sahih, al-asahh, bihi yutta, alayhi al-fatwa. Understand their meanings and relative strength.
These are not all equal; the poem assigns different emphases.
2
Understand the sahih vs asahh dispute
Note that there is disagreement whether 'asahh' is stronger than 'sahih' or vice versa. The poem acknowledges this dispute without resolving it.
The practical takeaway: when both appear, look for external factors.
3
Recognize stronger indicators
'Bihi yutta' and 'alayhi al-fatwa' are described as stronger than all other terms.
These are direct fatwa markers.
4
List external preponderance factors
When two sayings are both corrected, look for: presence in mutun, imam's statement, apparent of marwiy, istihsan, benefit for awqaf, suitability for time, clarity of proof.
These factors apply when corrections conflict or no explicit text exists.
5
Apply the rule
When corrections conflict, take the saying with the most preponderant factors. If none, the mufti may choose.
The poem calls this 'the clearest' way.

وَكَذَا إِذَا مَا وَاحِدًا قَدْ عَلَّلُوا لَهُ، وَتَعْلِيْلَ سِوَاهُ أَهْمَلُوا. وَحَيْثُمَا وَجَدْتَ قَوْلَيْنِ، وَقَدْ صُحِّحَ وَاحِدٌ، فَذَاكَ الْمُعْتَمَدُ. بِنَحْوِ: ذَا الْفَتْوَى عَلَيْهِ الْأَشْبَهُ وَالْأَظْهَرُ الْمُخْتَارُ ذَا وَالْأَوْجَهُ. أَوِ الصَّحِيحُ وَالْأَصَحُّ أَكَّدَ مِنْهُ، وَقِيلَ: عَكْسُهُ الْمُؤَكَّد. كَذَا بِهِ يُفْتَى عَلَيْهِ الْفَتْوَى * وَذَانِ مِنْ جَمِيعِ تِلْكَ أَقْوَى. وَإِنْ تَجِدْ تَصْحِيْحَ قَوْلَيْنِ وَرَدْ * فَاخْتَرْ لِمَا شِئْتَ، فَكُلٌّ مُعْتَمَدُ. إِلَّا إِذَا كَانَا صَحِيحًا وَأَصَحُّ * أَوْ قِيلَ: ذَا يُفْتَى بِهِ فَقَدْ رَجَحُ. أَوْ كَانَ فِي الْمُتُونِ أَوْ قَوْلَ الْإِمَامُ * أَوْ ظَاهِرَ الْمَرْوِيِّ أَرْجَلُ الْعِظَامِ. قَالَ بِهِ، أَوْ كَانَ الْإِسْتِحْسَانَا * أَوْ زَادَ لِلْأَوْقَافِ نَفْعًا بَانَا. أَوْ كَانَ ذَا أَوْفَقَ لِلزَّمَانِ * أَوْ كَانَ ذَا أَوْضَحَ فِي الْبُرْهَانِ. هَذَا إِذَا تَعَارَضَ التَّصْحِيحُ * أَوْ لَمْ يَكُنْ أَصْلًا بِهِ تَصْرِيحُ. فَتَأْخُذُ الَّذِي لَهُ مُرَجَّحُ * مِمَّا عَلِمْتَهُ، فَهَذَا الْأَوْضَحُ.
And likewise, when they gave one a reason and neglected the reason of another. And wherever you find two sayings, and one is corrected, that is the relied-upon. By terms like: 'al-fatwa alayhi al-ashbah', 'al-azhar', 'al-mukhtar', 'al-awjah'. Or 'al-sahih' and 'al-asahh' are more emphatic than it, and it is said: the opposite is more emphatic. Also 'bihi yutta' and 'alayhi al-fatwa' are stronger among all those. And if you find two corrected sayings reported, then choose whichever you wish, for each is reliable. Except when one is 'sahih' and the other 'asahh', or it is said: 'this is given fatwa by' then it is weightier. Or if it is in the mutun, or the imam's statement, or the apparent of the marwiy, the most established. He said by it, or if it was istihsan, or increased benefit for awqaf was apparent. Or if it was more appropriate for the time, or clearer in proof. This is when corrections conflict, or there is no original explicit text. So take the one that has a preponderant factor from what you know, for this is clearest.
Source pages p. 37

Commentary
This lengthy section gives detailed rules for when a mufti encounters corrected sayings. If one saying is corrected (by the later school), that is the relied-upon view. Specific terms indicate hierarchy: 'al-ashbah' (most similar to principles), 'al-azhar' (most apparent), 'al-mukhtar' (chosen), 'al-awjah' (most appropriate), and 'al-sahih'/'al-asahh' are highly emphatic. However, there is a dispute whether 'asahh' is more emphatic than 'sahih' or vice versa. Also 'bihi yutta' (by it is given fatwa) and 'alayhi al-fatwa' are stronger signs. If two sayings are both explicitly corrected, the mufti may choose either unless one has additional weight: if one is 'asahh' and the other 'sahih', the 'asahh' is preferred; similarly if one is marked as 'yutta bihi'. Other preponderance factors include: the saying being in the mutun, being the imam's (Abu Hanifa's) own statement, being the apparent (zahir) of the transmitted (marwiy), being based on istihsan, bringing more benefit to awqaf, being more suitable for the time, or having clearer proof. These factors apply when corrections conflict or when no explicit text gives a clear preference. The mufti then weighs these factors to choose the clearest position.

Key Terms
الأشبه (al-ashbah): Most similar to the principles of the madhhab; a term of preponderance.
الأصح (al-asahh): More correct; comparative of sahih. Its strength relative to sahih is disputed.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juridical preference; a method of reasoning that sets aside a strict analogy for a preferred ruling.
المرجي (al-murajjih): Preponderance factor; something that makes one view outweigh another.

The Third Principle: Fatwa on Non-Zahir al-Riwayah
Stating the third fatwa principle: following non-Zahir al-Riwayah when scholars explicitly adopt it due to an illah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the third principle of fatwa regarding non-Zahir al-Riwayah and apply it when scholars explicitly adopt such a ruling due to an illah.

Core Idea
When later Hanafi scholars explicitly adopt a non-Zahir al-Riwayah due to an illah, the mufti must follow that, not the Zahir al-Riwayah.

The third principle of fatwa in the Hanafi madhhab addresses a crucial scenario: what if the senior scholars of the madhhab have deliberately abandoned the dominant Zahir al-Riwayah and instead adopted a non-Zahir al-Riwayah position? This passage explains that such a departure is not a weakening of the madhhab but a refinement based on a legal cause (illah). The mufti is bound to follow these later scholars, as their collective judgment carries authority. This ensures the madhhab remains responsive without compromising its doctrinal integrity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Read the passage and locate the third principle: if scholars of the madhhab explicitly leave the Zahir al-Riwayah for a non-Zahir al-Riwayah based on an illah, the mufti must follow that.
The Arabic phrase 'علت کی بنیاد پر' indicates the requirement of a legal cause.
2

The illah (cause) justifies the departure; it is not arbitrary. Scholars have a valid reason for the shift.
3
Recognize the mufti's obligation
The muqallid mufti is bound by the scholars' ijtihad — he does not have authority to choose between Zahir al-Riwayah and non-Zahir al-Riwayah independently.
Muqallid means the mufti follows the madhhab's authoritative positions.
4
Apply the principle
In practice, when a mufti encounters a ruling where the mashayikh have clearly stated that the fatwa is on a non-Zahir al-Riwayah due to an illah, he must give fatwa accordingly.
Example: child with incomplete limbs – Zahir al-Riwayah may rule one way, but later scholars may adopt another due to considerations of viability.
5
Distinguish from common practice
This is not about convenience or personal preference; it is about madhhab authority and explicit adoption based on a recognized illah.
The mufti must verify that the later scholars explicitly adopted the ruling, not just that a weaker opinion exists.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of a verse from the poem of Rasm al-Mufti, explaining the rules of giving fatwa in the later period when no mujtahid mufti exists. The author, Allama Shami, explains that previously, a mujtahid mufti had the authority to choose between different opinions based on evidence (tarjeeh bil-dalil). But in the current era, all muftis are muqallids, and thus the option of tarjeeh is no longer available. Instead, the mufti must follow a fixed hierarchy: first look for the saying of Imam Abu Hanifa; if not found, then Imam Abu Yusuf; then Imam Muhammad; and so on in descending order of the students. However, there is an exception: if a contrary saying (i.e., of a student like Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, or Zufar) has been 'corrected' (musahhah) by the later scholars (as-hab al-tashih), meaning they have declared it to be the preferable view, then that corrected saying is taken. An example is given: 17 sayings of Imam Zufar have been preferred over the sayings of Imam Abu Hanifa by the later scholars. Thus, the rule is that the hierarchy is default, but it is overridden when a later correction (tashih) has been made.

Key Terms
ترجیح (tarjeeh): Preferring one legal opinion over another based on strength of evidence (dalil).
تفصیل (tafsil): The detailed hierarchy of Hanafi imams for fatwa: first Imam Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, etc.
تصحیح (tashih): The act of later scholars (as-hab al-tashih) declaring a particular saying as the correct and preferred view over the default hierarchy.
اصحاب تصحیح (as-hab al-tashih): The later Hanafi scholars who undertook the task of correcting and preferring certain opinions among the early imams.

Preference for Imam Abu Yusuf in Judiciary Matters
States the rule of preferring Imam Abu Yusuf in judiciary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rasm al-mufti rule of preferring Imam Abu Yusuf in judiciary and testimonies, and state the conditions under which talqin (prompting witnesses) is permitted.

Core Idea
In judiciary matters, fatwa follows Imam Abu Yusuf due to his long judicial experience.

This lesson introduces a key rule in the Hanafi rasm al-mufti: when issuing fatwas on judiciary (qada) and testimonies (shahadat), the opinion of Imam Abu Yusuf takes precedence. The reason is his extensive practical experience as a chief judge, which gives his rulings added weight. The rule is illustrated through the example of talqin—whether a judge may prompt witnesses on how to testify.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rasm Rule
The passage states: in matters of qada and shahadat, fatwa is given according to Imam Abu Yusuf. This is a specific application of the broader Hanafi principle of preferring certain imams in specific fields.
This rule is based on the practical experience of Imam Abu Yusuf, not on general priority.
2
Understand the Rationale
The rationale given is Imam Abu Yusuf's long tenure as a qadi (chief judge), which gave him practical exposure to difficult judiciary cases. This experience makes his opinions more reliable in judicial matters.
The rationale is aqli (rational) rather than textual.
3
Examine the Illustrative Example
The author provides an example: when witnesses appear, other imams prohibit the judge from prompting them (talqin), while Imam Abu Yusuf permits it, even considering it commendable, provided it may elicit more truth and no bias is apparent. Fatwa follows Abu Yusuf.
This example clarifies how the rule applies in a specific scenario.
4
Apply the Rule
In practice, when a mufti receives a question about judiciary matters, he must check if the matter falls under qada or shahadat. If yes, he gives fatwa according to Imam Abu Yusuf's position, unless a stronger evidence overrides it.
The rule is limited to judiciary and testimonies; other matters follow the standard hierarchy (Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad).

Commentary

The reason given is Imam Abu Yusuf's long tenure as chief judge (qadi), which gave him practical experience in difficult judicial matters.
