Sixth Rule: Stronger Evidence Prevails
Correction of a misconception about weaker evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sixth major qawida and apply it to resolve conflicts between evidences, citing that the weaker is entirely dropped.

Core Idea
When evidence conflicts, the stronger prevails and the weaker is not merely set aside but invalidated.

This lesson introduces the principle that in any conflict between two proofs, the stronger must be acted upon and the weaker becomes entirely inoperative. The passage from Kashf al-Asrar corrects a common misunderstanding: weaker evidence is not permissible even as an alternative. This rule governs all areas of fiqh where evidence conflicts, and is especially relevant when a mufti must choose between qiyas and istihsan.

Guided Path
1
State the Maxim in Arabic and English
Write the qawida text and its translation. This is the foundation: 'When two evidences conflict and one appears preponderant over the other, it is obligatory to act by it and the other falls absolutely.'
The Arabic uses 'Saqata' meaning 'falls', indicating complete nullification.
2
Identify the Scope
The rule applies to any conflict of evidences across all chapters of fiqh. It is not limited to usul al-fiqh but covers all legal proofs.
3
Apply to the Key Furu: Qiyas vs Istihsan
When qiyas conflicts with istihsan, istihsan is stronger. The qiyas is not merely optional but must be abandoned; acting on it is not allowed.
This is the classic example in Hanafi usul.
4
Note the Absolute Drop of Weaker Evidence
The author refutes the notion that weaker evidence can still be acted upon. The phrasing 'But we have abandoned this qiyas' shows abandonment (tark) which precludes action.
The weaker is not just 'less preferred' but invalid.
5
Generalize to All Conflicts
The rule is universal: in every conflict of evidences, once preponderance is determined, the weaker is dropped entirely, not merely set aside.
This is a general principle for muftis: they must identify the stronger evidence and disregard the weaker.

Definition of 'Urf and the Maxim 'Al-'Adah Muhakkamah'
Defining 'urf, 'adah, and their legal authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of 'urf and 'adah, distinguish between them, and apply the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' to a social custom scenario.

Core Idea
Custom ('urf) is social habit; 'adah is broader; both are valid sources unless a clear text contradicts.

This passage clarifies the Hanafi legal concept of custom ('urf) and habit ('adah), which serve as secondary sources for deriving rulings. It distinguishes them from each other and from ijma' and 'amal, and introduces the foundational maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' (habit is authoritative). Understanding this distinction is crucial for a mufti when basing fatwas on prevailing customs.

Guided Path
1
State the definitions
Extract the definitions of 'urf and 'adah from the referenced works: al-Mustasfa and Sharh al-Tahrir. Note that both are rooted in what is accepted by sound natures or what recurs.
Focus on the key phrase: 'ما استقر في النفوس من جهة العقول' for 'urf.
2
Distinguish 'urf from 'adah
From al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, note that 'adah is more general: it includes social, individual, and natural habits, while 'urf is only social. Internalize the relationship: every 'urf is an 'adah but not vice versa.
Memorize the Arabic: 'فكل عرف عادة ولا عكس'.
3
Identify the maxim and its rank
The passage states 'العادة محكمة' as a major (kubra) maxim. Understand that this means when a customary practice is widespread and sound, it can be used to determine a ruling.
This is one of the five major maxims of Hanafi fiqh.
4
List the conditions and exceptions
Custom is authoritative only if it is accepted by sound natures and does not contradict a clear text (Quran or Sunnah). When it does, the text takes precedence.
The exception is governed by the maxim 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك'.
5
Differentiate 'urf from ijma' and 'amal
Ijma' requires agreement of all mujtahids; 'amal with no scholarly disapproval is attached to ijma'. 'Urf only requires majority practice including non-scholars, making it more like sira (established practice).
This affects how a mufti invokes custom in fatwa.

Commentary
This passage defines 'urf (custom) and its legal authority. It begins by noting that custom is a recognized source in the Hanafi school, and rulings are often based on it. The definition from al-Mustasfa of al-Ghazali states that 'urf and 'adah are what becomes established in minds through sound reason and accepted by upright nature. This emphasizes that valid custom must be reasonable and not contrary to innate morality. Then, from Sharh al-Tahrir, 'adah is defined as something that occurs repeatedly without a rational connection—like a natural recurrence (e.g., seasonal changes) that is not necessarily caused by reason. A crucial distinction from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir is given: 'adah is broader than 'urf. 'Adah includes social habits (like customary greetings), individual habits (like a person's routine), and natural habits (like climate effects). 'Urf is only social habit—what people commonly do. So every 'urf is an 'adah but not every 'adah is an 'urf. The well-known Hanafi maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' (habit is authoritative) is then presented. This maxim applies to social habits that are sound and not contradicting scripture. Finally, a benefit distinguishes 'urf from 'amal (general practice) and ijma'. Ijma' requires the agreement of all qualified jurists, while 'amal that is universally accepted without disapproval by scholars is attached to ijma'. 'Urf, however, does not require scholarly consensus; it is sufficient if the majority of people, both common and elite, follow it. Thus 'urf is nearer to established practice (sira). The passage underscores that 'urf is a valid source for deriving rulings in Hanafi fiqh, provided it is not corrupt and does not oppose a clear text.

Key Terms
العرف (al-'urf): What settles in minds from the aspect of intellects and is received with acceptance by sound natures (social custom)
العادة (al-'adah): The recurring matter without an intellectual connection (can be social, individual, or natural)
العادة محكمة (al-'adah muhakkamah): Habit is made an authoritative proof for establishing a legal ruling

Change of Rulings Due to Change of Custom: The Maxim and Its Nine Applications
Exposition of the maxim that custom alters rulings and its applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'تغير الأحكام بتغير العرف' to identify when a ruling shifts due to custom, and to evaluate the authority weight of the mufta bihi position against Zahir al-Riwaya.

Core Idea
Custom can change a ruling when widespread and relied upon, even overriding Zahir al-Riwaya.

This lesson examines the Hanafi legal maxim that rulings change with changing customs. Through nine concrete examples from various fiqh chapters, we see how later muftis gave fatwa contrary to the original transmitted position due to prevalent usage and necessity. Understanding this maxim is essential for a mufti who must balance textual authority with the lived reality of the community.

State the maxim and its rank
Begin by articulating the maxim 'تغير الأحكام بتغير العرف' and note that it is classified as a wusta (intermediate) dabt (restrictive) maxim. Its scope covers all fiqh chapters where custom is a factor.
This maxim is a restriction on the default rule that the school follows Zahir al-Riwaya.
2
Identify each furu' case and the original ruling
For each of the nine examples (12-19), state the original ruling according to Zahir al-Riwaya. For instance, in the case of ambiguous divorce words, the original ruling (from Muhammad) required intention.
The original ruling is often the position of the majority of the early scholars.
3
Identify the custom or necessity that changed the ruling
Pinpoint the specific custom or necessity that led the later scholars to adopt a different position. For example, in sharecropping, the fatwa shifted due to widespread need (بلوى).
The custom must be general, continuous, and not contradict a clear nass.
4
Articulate the mufta bihi position and its authority
State the operative ruling for fatwa. For each case, note its authority: often from the Sahibayn or later scholars, and justified by the maxim. For instance, in waqf, the fatwa follows Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

This safeguards the Shari'ah from arbitrary changes.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the Hanafi legal maxim 'Change of rulings due to change of custom' (تغير الأحكام بتغير العرف). The author, Ibn Nujaym, presents a series of cases where later scholars gave fatwa contrary to the original Zahir al-Riwaya position because of changes in custom and necessity. The passage begins with the example of divorce by ambiguous words: Imam Muhammad said such words require intention, but the scholars of Balkh and later fatwa rely on custom—since in their lands these words are used to mean divorce, they take effect without intention. The author then lists nine numbered examples (12-19) where custom overrides the default rule: (12) the father's claim regarding the daughter's trousseau—custom presumes ownership, so the father's word is not accepted unless local custom says otherwise; (13) the wife's word about deferred dower—contrary to the rule that the denier's word is taken, she is believed up to her proper dower because custom and the indication of proper dower support her; (14-15) sharecropping, mu'amala, and waqf—the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad is chosen for fatwa because people rely on these contracts out of necessity; (16) preemption—Muhammad's ruling that delay of one month voids preemption is adopted to protect buyers; (17) marriage without guardian—the narration of Hasan ibn Ziyad that it is invalid if the husband is non-compatible is preferred due to social corruption; (18) forgiveness of mud from the street—prayer is allowed even if the garment is soiled due to unavoidable necessity; (19) bay' al-wafa (sale with repurchase option)—despite being a disguised pledge, it is permitted for need. Each example illustrates the principle that when custom becomes widespread and people rely on it, the fatwa shifts to accommodate practical reality, even if it contravenes the primary ruling of the school.

Key Terms
العرف (al-'urf): Custom: a recurring practice among people that is acceptable in Shari'ah.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwaya): The transmitted rulings from Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad in the six authoritative books.
الضرورة (al-darurah): Necessity that permits departure from the normal ruling to avoid hardship.

The Rule of Emergent Custom (al-Urf al-Hadith) and Its Conditions
Explains the permissibility and conditions for a mufti to follow emergent custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions for a mufti to follow emergent custom and apply them to evaluate the permissibility of relying on a new custom in fatwa.

Core Idea
A mufti may follow emergent custom only if he has ijtihad, knowledge of his era, and training under a master.

This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti addresses a critical question: can a later mufti depart from explicit rulings of the founding imams when customs change? The answer is yes, but only under strict conditions. This lesson unpacks those conditions and the rationale behind them, showing that following custom is not a shortcut but requires deep scholarly qualification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
Locate the statement that it is permissible for the mufti to oppose the explicit text (al-mansus) and follow emergent custom. Understand that this is based on the principle that the founder would have ruled similarly if he were alive.
The rule is qualified by three conditions that follow.
2
Extract condition one: knowledge and reasoning
Find the first condition: the mufti must possess a degree of ijtihad and knowledge of Shariah principles and subsidiary issues with their conditions and restrictions, including those the early jurists omitted relying on the reader's understanding.
This implies not merely memorization but deep comprehension.
3
Extract condition two: awareness of contemporary custom
Find the second condition: the mufti must be fully aware of the customs and conditions of the people of his time, observing their transactions and habits.
Ignorance of one's society disqualifies the mufti from applying emergent custom.
4
Extract condition three: training under a master
Find the third condition: the mufti must have trained under a skilled teacher, received authorization (ijazah) for fatwa, and developed a mental faculty that makes his correct assessments outweigh errors.
Self-study of books is insufficient without apprenticeship.
5
Understand the rationale and precedent
Recognize that later Hanafi scholars (mutakhkhirin) have already applied this rule, using examples like the issue of 'every halal is haram' and hiring for teaching. The mufti follows their precedent.

The Divisions of 'Urf: General and Specific Custom and Their Legal Authority
Definitional and regulatory discussion of 'urf as a legal source.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general and specific custom in Hanafi fiqh and apply the rule that general custom establishes a ruling, with its exceptions.

Core Idea
General custom can specify texts and override qiyas, but not the Quran and Sunnah; specific custom is not binding in the madhhab.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi classification of 'urf (custom) into general and specific. You will learn when custom can serve as a legal source and when it must be set aside. The passage provides the foundational qawida and its practical applications, along with important exceptions and internal scholarly differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of 'urf
The passage divides 'urf into 'amm (general) and khass (specific). Note the definitions: general custom is widespread and not restricted to a region or group; specific custom is particular to a locality, trade, or group.
General custom is the one that can establish a ruling.
2
State the qawida
Extract the rule: 'العرف العام يثبت به الحكم' (general custom establishes a ruling). This is the key maxim.
This qawida is of wusta rank, governing all fiqh chapters.
3
Understand the scope of authority
General custom can specify (takhsis) general texts and override qiyas, but it cannot contradict a clear text (nass) from Quran or Sunnah. If it does, it is rejected entirely.
Examples from passage: customs of riba, wine, silk are rejected for contradicting nass.
4
Distinguish specific custom
Specific custom is not accepted as a binding source in the madhhab. However, many later scholars (mashayikh) gave fatwa considering it, creating a practical concession.
This internal ikhtilaf is important for muftis to know.
5
Apply to examples
Work through the furuat: general custom that mahr is paid in installments is accepted; tenant paying for minor repairs is accepted. But if a specific custom contradicts Zahir al-Riwaya, the default madhhab position is to follow the transmitted texts.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh Uqood Rasm al-Mufti addresses the role of custom (urf) in Hanafi fatwa. The passage type is a methodological rule (rasm al-mufti). The author first states that the mufti must use general custom as a basis for ijtihad, and it can specify (takhsis) general rules derived from qiyas and athar. Specific custom, however, only establishes a specific ruling provided it does not contradict qiyas or athar. The discussion then explains that many fiqh rulings are based on ijtihad and therefore subject to change with time due to changing customs, necessity, or corruption of the people. This aligns with the Shariah's principle of easing hardship. Consequently, later scholars of the Hanafi madhhab sometimes contravened earlier explicit rulings because they knew the earlier imam would have done the same in their context. The author then addresses a potential objection: if custom can override even rulings from Zahir al-Riwayah, is there a difference between general and specific custom? The answer is that both can override, but general custom applies universally while specific custom is limited to its locality. Importantly, this applies only when custom contradicts the madhhab's transmitted texts (nass madhhabi), not the primary Shar'i texts (nass shar'i). The author then defines both types of custom with examples: general custom includes istisna' (manufacturing contract), using the word 'haram' for divorce, and unspecified duration in bathhouse entry; specific custom includes regional usages like calling a horse 'dabbah' in Iraq. A separate heading on page 194 clarifies that general customary practice (ta'amul aam) can specify a textual ruling but cannot abandon it entirely. Evidence from al-Dhakhirah supports this: the scholars of Balkh permitted a weaver contract based on custom, which involved specifying the implied ruling of the miller's qafiz case. Similarly, istisna' is permitted despite the general prohibition on selling what one does not possess. The commentary from Minhat al-Ghaffar further illustrates that custom overrides qiyas, not the hadith itself, because custom eliminates the dispute that the hadith aims to prevent. Thus, custom is a powerful tool in Hanafi iftaa, enabling adaptation while maintaining fidelity to primary texts.

العرف الخاص (al-urf al-khaas): Custom specific to a locality, region, or group, e.g., using 'dabbah' for horse in Iraq.
التعامل (al-ta'amul): Prevailing practice of people in transactions, equivalent to 'urf in a commercial sense.
تخصيص النص (takhsis al-nass): Limiting the scope of a textual ruling to certain cases, not abandoning it entirely.

Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir: It is by the virtuous jurist Zayn al-Abidin ibn Ibrahim ibn Muhammad ibn Nujaym, known as Ibn Nujaym, the Egyptian Hanafi, who died in Egypt in 970 AH (nine hundred and seventy). It is a famous concise work, beginning with 'Alhamdulillah 'ala ma an'am... etc.' It is mentioned in the book 'al-Taj al-Subki' for the Shafi'is: 'I have not seen its like for the Hanafis.' Abd al-Wahhab al-Sha'rani said: 'I accompanied him for ten years and never saw anything that would disgrace him, and I performed Hajj with him in 652 AH and saw him with great character towards his neighbors and servants, indeed travel reveals the character of men.' His death was in 969 AH. This is as some have transmitted from 'al-Kawakib al-Sa'irah fi A'yan al-Mi'ah al-Ashirah'.

Commentary
This entry introduces Ibn Nujaym's masterpiece 'Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir', a foundational work in Hanafi qawaid (legal maxims). The passage provides the author's full name, his death date (970 AH), and the book's opening words. It also quotes a commendation by the Shafi'i scholar Taj al-Subki, highlighting the book's excellence across madhhabs. The character testimony by Abd al-Wahhab al-Sha'rani further elevates the author's reputation. The death date discrepancy (969 vs 970) is noted.
