The Three Levels of Sincerity in Worship
Commentary on the phrase 'khalisan' explaining three levels of sincerity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three levels of ikhlas (sincerity) in worship as outlined in the Hanafi tradition, and explain how the highest level applies to the mufti's intention in issuing fatwa.

Core Idea
The highest sincerity is worshipping Allah because He is worthy, not for reward or honor.

This lesson unpacks the commentary on the word 'khalisan' (pure) from the khutbah. The author explains that sincerity means freedom from worldly motives, and then presents three ascending levels of worship. As a mufti, understanding these levels is crucial for purifying one's intention when delivering fatwa, as the ruling's acceptance depends on sincere intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key phrase
Locate the phrase 'أن يجعله خالصاً' in the source. The commentary explains what 'khalisan' means in the context of worship.
2
List the three levels
Find the three levels of worship mentioned: (1) worship for reward and fear of punishment, (2) worship for the honor of being a servant, (3) worship because Allah is your God and you are His servant. Write them down in ascending order.
3
Understand the progression
Reflect on why the third level is the highest: it is purely based on Allah's worthiness, not on self-interest. The commentary also notes that being pure from obstacles of perfection coincides with the third level.
4
Apply to the mufti's intention
Consider how a mufti can aim for the highest level of sincerity when issuing fatwa. Why is it problematic if a mufti seeks fame or praise?
This connects to the discipline of iftaa: the mufti's niyyah affects the blessing and acceptance of the fatwa.

Interpreting Divine Attributes: The Salaf and Khalaf Approaches
Commentary on interpreting divine attributes with two methodologies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between the Salafi (tafwid) and Khalafi (ta'wil) methods in handling anthropomorphic expressions in the Quran and Hadith, and apply this to avoid misinterpretation when issuing fatwa.

Core Idea
Both Salaf and Khalaf agree that Allah has no physical attributes; the dispute is only in articulating the interpretation.

This lesson examines the commentary on the phrase 'li wajhihi al-karim' (for His noble face). The commentator explains that such expressions must be interpreted in a way that befits Allah, and then delineates two approaches: the Salaf delegate the meaning to Allah (tafwid), while the Khalaf specify a figurative interpretation (ta'wil). For a mufti, this knowledge is foundational to avoid anthropomorphism (tashbih) in creed and to correctly explain such texts to laypeople.

Guided Path
1
Locate the phrase and its interpretation
Find the phrase 'لوجهه الكريم' in the source. The commentary interprets it as 'for His essence' (li dhatihi).
This is a ta'wil by the Khalaf.
2
Identify the two approaches
The commentary contrasts the method of the Salaf (tafwid: delegating meaning to Allah without specifying) and the Khalaf (ta'wil: specifying a figurative meaning). Understand that both groups agree that Allah is not like creation.
3
Understand the common ground
Both Salaf and Khalaf affirm tanzih (transcendence). The disagreement is only about whether it is permissible to assign a specific interpretation or not.
4
Apply to the mufti's role
Consider how a mufti should handle questions about verses like 'Yad Allah' (Hand of Allah). Should he give a specific interpretation or simply say 'Allah knows best'? The Hanafi tradition often follows a middle path that respects both approaches.

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: Attached Mujtahids, Not Independent
Classifying Sahibayn as mujtahid muntasib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'mujtahid muntasib' and justify the classification of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad as attached mujtahids using the evidence from this passage.

Core Idea
Frequent disagreement with the imam does not mean independence—it means attached ijtihad bound by the imam's principles.

This passage tackles a crucial yet often misunderstood point in Hanafi legal theory: the status of the two great disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Their numerous disagreements with Abu Hanifa lead some to assume they were independent mujtahids. The passage, however, affirms they are 'mujtahid muntasib'—attached mujtahids—who operate within the usul of their teacher. This distinction shapes how we weigh their opinions in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual claim
The passage opens by stating that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagreed with Abu Hanifa in matters of principles (usul) extensively—'kathira ghayr qalila'. Recognize this as the premise.
The word 'usul' here refers to legal methodology, not specific rulings.
2
Note the authority's estimate
Imam al-Ghazali in al-Mankhul famously estimated that they opposed Abu Hanifa in two-thirds of his madhhab. This is a dramatic figure that underscores the extent of ikhtilaf.
Al-Ghazali is a Shafi'i scholar, so his testimony is external but accepted.
3
Understand the conclusion
Despite such frequent opposition, the correct view is that they are 'mujtahid muntasib' (attached mujtahids). They did not break away to form an independent methodology.
Attached ijtihad means they follow Abu Hanifa's usul even when deriving different furu'.
4
Recognize the authorities
The passage cites al-Sha'rani in al-Mizan and al-Dihlawi in his works as confirming this classification. These are later Hanafi and traditionalist authorities.
Al-Sha'rani and al-Dihlawi are renowned for their works on tasawwuf and hadith, respectively, adding weight.

Amali Abi Yusuf: The Genre of Scholarly Dictations in Hanafi Iftaa
Definition of the genre Amali and its transmission method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain what al-Amali are and identify their significance as a method of transmitting Hanafi legal knowledge, including the parallel term 'Ta'liqah' in the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Amali are scholarly dictations that became books — a key oral tradition in Hanafi knowledge transmission.

This lesson defines a specific genre of Hanafi legal literature called Amali, exemplified by the work of Imam Abu Yusuf. By understanding how these dictations were produced and compiled, you will appreciate the role of oral transmission in preserving early Hanafi rulings. The lesson also highlights the Shafi'i equivalent 'Ta'liqah', showing cross-madhhab terminology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its linguistic root
Recognize that 'al-Amali' is the plural of 'imla'', meaning dictation. This sets the foundation for understanding the genre as a product of oral teaching.
The term is derived from the act of a scholar speaking and students writing.
2
Describe the scholarly setting
Visualize the scene: a scholar seated with students holding inkwells and paper, speaking spontaneously from memory. Students write down what they hear, then compile the notes into a book.
This method was common among jurists, hadith scholars, and linguists.
3
Compare with Shafi'i nomenclature
Note that Shafi'i scholars use the term 'Ta'liqah' for similar works. Understanding this equivalency helps in cross-referencing sources across madhhabs.
The passage explicitly draws this comparison.
4
Assess the relevance to iftaa
Recognize that Amali works like those of Abu Yusuf are authoritative sources for Hanafi fatwa, as they preserve the direct teachings of early imams.
Oral transmission was a trusted method; the loss of this practice is lamented.

Fatrat al-Shari‘ah: No Mufti, No Obligation
Legal ruling on total absence of mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of intifa’ al-taklif to a scenario where a person cannot access any mufti, and distinguish it from cases where seeking a ruling is merely difficult.

Core Idea
When access to any mufti is impossible, taklif is lifted entirely—no obligation, no accountability.

What happens when a Muslim faces a legal question but cannot find any scholar or transmitter of rulings? The classical Hanafi answer, grounded in usul al-fiqh, is that the person enters a state analogous to the pre-revelation period: the obligation to follow a ruling is removed. This lesson explores the principle of 'fatrat al-shari‘ah al-usuliyyah' and its practical implications for the mufti and the mustafti.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Condition
The person (sahib al-waqi‘ah) cannot find a mufti nor anyone who can convey the ruling, neither in his own country nor elsewhere. This is the complete absence of access.
The phrase 'لا في بلده ولا في غيره' is critical—it rules out any possibility of obtaining a ruling through reasonable effort.
2
Recognize the Analogy
This situation is equated to the period before revelation (qabl wurud al-shar‘). Just as there was no obligation before the Shari‘ah was revealed, here the obligation is lifted.
This is an usuli analogy, not a literal claim that Shari‘ah is absent; it is functionally absent for this person.
3
State the Ruling
The correct opinion (al-sahih) is that taklif is lifted—no obligation (wujub), no prohibition (tahrim), and no other ruling is established for that person regarding this incident.
The text uses 'intifa’ al-taklif' and emphasizes that nothing is established 'لا إيجاب ولا تحريم ولا غير ذلك'.
4
Note the Consequence
The person is not held accountable (la yu’akhadh) for any action he takes in that situation.
Accountability (mu’akhadhah) here refers to liability in the Hereafter or legal punishment; the act is not valid or invalid per se, but there is no blame.
5
Understand the Limit
This principle applies only when all avenues of knowledge are completely cut off. If even a remote possibility of finding a mufti or ruling exists, this rule does not apply.
The condition is extremely rare; modern communication makes it nearly impossible, but the principle remains valid for theoretical and historical cases.

Commentary
This passage addresses the tenth rule (al-āshirah) concerning a situation where a person (sāhib al-wāqi‘ah) cannot find a mufti or anyone to convey the ruling of his incident in his own land or elsewhere. The Sheikh (likely Ibn al-Salāh or Nawawi) calls this the 'period of the Shariah vacuum' (fatra al-sharī‘ah al-usūliyyah). The ruling is the same as before the revelation of the Shariah: obligation (taklīf) is negated from the slave, meaning no ruling (ḥukm) is established — no obligation, prohibition, or otherwise. Therefore, the person is not held accountable for whatever he does. The extensive footnote (from I‘lām al-Muwaqqi‘īn by Ibn Qayyim) elaborates: the absence of a guide makes him like one who never received the call (da‘wah). There is disagreement: some say it parallels the mujtahid facing conflicting evidences (whether to choose the lighter, stricter, or be free to choose). Others say he must exert himself to find truth through signs, sound nature, dreams, even inspiration. Only if all signs are absent does obligation fall. The hadith of Hudhayfah confirms this: at the end of time, when only the kalimah remains, that will suffice for salvation. Thus, in complete absence of knowledge, taklīf is lifted.

Key Terms
فترة الشريعة (fatra al-sharī‘ah): A period when no qualified mufti or transmitter of the ruling exists, analogous to the pre-Islamic era.
انتفاء التكليف (intifā’ al-taklīf): Absolute absence of legal obligation; no ruling (ḥukm) applies.
صاحب الواقعة (sāhib al-wāqi‘ah): The person who faces a legal incident and needs a fatwa.
