Liability of a Mufti for Erroneous Fatwa
Dispute on mufti liability and refinement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi mufti adab framework to determine when a mufti is liable for an erroneous fatwa causing harm.

Core Idea
A qualified mufti is not liable; an unqualified mufti is liable.

The question of a mufti's liability when his fatwa leads to loss is a critical issue in mufti adab. This passage from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa presents a dispute: al-Ustadh Abu Ishaq holds a qualified mufti liable if he contradicts definitive evidence, while the author finds this problematic and suggests alternative approaches. Understanding the nuances protects both the mufti's authority and the mustafti's rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What is the legal question being addressed? It is whether a mufti is financially or legally liable if someone acts on his fatwa and suffers harm after the fatwa is found erroneous.
The harm could be loss of life or property.
2
Analyze al-Ustadh Abu Ishaq's position
He distinguishes between a qualified and unqualified mufti: a qualified mufti who contradicts definitive evidence (qati') is liable because his error stems from extreme negligence; an unqualified mufti is not liable because the mustafti was negligent in seeking him.
Definitive evidence = Quran, mutawatir hadith, ijma'.
3
Understand the author's criticism
The author (al-Nawawi) finds this view problematic and proposes two alternative approaches: (a) derive liability using the two opinions on ghurur (deception) from the chapters of usurpation and marriage, or (b) decisively rule no liability because a fatwa does not compel or coerce.
This shows a critical refinement of the earlier view.
4
Examine the ghurur analogy
If a mufti presents himself as qualified when he is not, he deceives the mustafti. In Hanafi fiqh, there are two opinions on liability for ghurur: one imposes liability, the other does not. Applying this to the mufti, his liability depends on which opinion is followed.
The hadith about a self-proclaimed physician supports liability for the unqualified.
5
Apply the principle that fatwa does not compel
A fatwa is not binding; the mustafti chooses to follow it. If he acts on it without judicial enforcement, the mufti is not liable because the mustafti is free to accept or reject. This is stronger for the mufti than for a judge, whose ruling is binding.
Ibn al-Qayyim expands this: if the mufti is qualified, no liability; if unqualified, liability.

Commentary
This passage from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa addresses the question of a mufti's liability when his erroneous fatwa leads to loss of life or property. The author first reports the view of al-Ustadh Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini: a qualified mufti who contradicts definitive evidence (qati') is liable, while an unqualified mufti is not liable because the mustafti was negligent in seeking fatwa from one who is not an expert. However, the author finds this problematic. He suggests that liability should be determined according to the two opinions on deception (ghurur) found in the chapters of usurpation and marriage, or that there should be no liability whatsoever because a fatwa does not compel or force the mustafti to act. The commentary then elaborates: Ibn al-Qayyim explains the disagreement, noting that if the mufti is qualified, he is not liable because the mustafti chooses to follow him voluntarily; but if he is unqualified, he is liable by analogy to the hadith about a self-proclaimed physician. The discussion highlights the tension between protecting the mustafti and respecting the mufti's scholarly authority.

Key Terms
القاطع (al-qati'): Definitive evidence from Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma that admits no interpretation.
الغرور (al-ghurur): Deception; here refers to a mufti misleading a mustafti by presenting himself as qualified.
الإلزام (al-ilzam): Compulsion or binding force; fatwa does not have ilzam, unlike a judge's ruling.

Prohibition of Carelessness in Fatwa and Duty of Verification
Forbids tashahul and rushing in fatwa; explains its forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the ruling on carelessness in fatwa, IDENTIFY its forms, and APPLY the exception for prior knowledge.

Core Idea
Carelessness in fatwa is forbidden; a mufti must verify before answering unless he already knows.

This lesson covers a foundational adab of the mufti: the absolute prohibition of tasahul (carelessness) and the requirement of tathabbut (verification). The early scholars modelled promptness only when they had prior knowledge, not as a license for haste. The commentary warns that haste often stems from pride.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
Locate the explicit statement that tasahul is haram and the consequence for a mufti known for it.
The text says: 'يحرم التساهل في الفتوى، ومن عرف به حرم استفتاؤه'.
2
Define the first form of tasahul
Identify the behavior described as 'lack of verification and hurrying' and note the exception.
Exception: if the mufti already knows the answer, promptness is allowed.
3
Understand the rationale
Examine the commentary: what drives a mufti to rush? How does the author weigh slowness vs. speed?
The commentary says haste is often mistaken for proficiency, but slowness that avoids error is better.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a scenario where a mufti answers quickly without research, determine if he violates the prohibition and what he should have done.
Consider whether he had prior knowledge.

Commentary
This passage from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa lays down three important rulings. First, carelessness (tashahul) in issuing fatwas is absolutely forbidden. A mufti known for being careless should not be consulted. Second, one form of carelessness is failing to verify the issue and rushing to give a fatwa without adequate reflection. However, if the mufti already possesses prior knowledge about the matter, immediate response is allowed, as was the practice of early scholars. The commentary warns that haste often stems from the mistaken belief that speed indicates skill, whereas it is better to be slow and correct than to hurry and cause error. Third, another form of carelessness is allowing corrupt motives—like personal bias—to lead one to search for forbidden or disliked legal stratagems or exploit ambiguities to give lenient rulings for friends or strict rulings for enemies. The commentary defines hila as a means to achieve an end through a hidden path, and notes that scholars categorize stratagems based on the motive.

Key Terms
التساهل (al-tasahul): Carelessness in fatwa; lack of thoroughness and verification.
التثبت (al-tathabbut): Verification; taking time to confirm the ruling properly.

Corrupt Motives and Legal Stratagems in Fatwa
Forbids tashahul and rushing in fatwa; explains its forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to IDENTIFY corrupt motives that lead to tasahul, DEFINE hila (legal stratagem), and CLASSIFY hiyal based on their motive.

Core Idea
Pursuing forbidden or disliked stratagems due to bias makes a fatwa invalid.

This lesson focuses on the second form of carelessness described in the passage: allowing personal bias to dictate the search for legal stratagems or ambiguities. The commentary defines hila and explains that its ruling depends on the motive. Muftis must avoid ruling leniently for friends or harshly for enemies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the second form of tasahul
Locate in the passage where corrupt motives are mentioned as causing the mufti to pursue forbidden/disliked hiyal and cling to ambiguities.
The text says: 'تحمله الأغراض الفاسدة على تتبع الحيل المحرمة أو المكروهة'.
2
Define hila legally
Read the commentary's definition: 'ما يتوصل به إلى مقصود بطريق خفي' (that by which one reaches an objective through a hidden path).
Linguistically: cleverness in managing affairs; legally: a hidden means to an end.
3
Classify hiyal based on motive
The commentary states that scholars divide hiyal 'بحسب الحامل عليها' (according to the motive). Determine when a hila is permissible, disliked, or forbidden.
If the motive is to circumvent a clear prohibition or harm another, it is forbidden; if to achieve a legitimate goal through permissible means, it is allowed.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given a scenario where a mufti uses a known hila to allow riba (interest) by structuring it as a sale, determine if this is tasahul and why.
This would be tasahul if the motive is to benefit a friend, even if the hila is technically permissible.

Prohibition of Laxity in Fatwa
Ruling on prohibition of tasahul and its two forms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two forms of tasahul in fatwa and apply the ruling that a mufti known for tasahul is not to be sought for fatwa.

Core Idea
Laxity in fatwa is prohibited; a mufti must be thorough or his fatwa is invalid.

This passage from the Hanafi adab al-fatwa literature establishes a fundamental ethical and legal principle: tasahul (laxity) in issuing fatwas is haram. The text distinguishes two distinct manifestations of tasahul—one of procedure and one of intention. Understanding these forms is crucial for any mufti or student of iftaa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching ruling
Read the first line: 'يحرم التساهل في الفتوى'. State that tasahul is haram, and whoever is known for it, it is prohibited to seek his fatwa.
This is the core rasm rule.
2

When you issue a fatwa, you are transmitting the madhhab of an imam. But not every book with the imam's name is reliable. Commentary
This passage covers the seventh rule from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa, addressing the mufti's relationship with the madhhab texts he relies upon. The rule states that a mufti who transmits fatwa based on an imam's madhhab must only use books that are trustworthy in two respects: first, the book itself must be authentic (i.e., its text is reliable), and second, it must correctly represent that imam's madhhab. If the mufti is confident about the original work but the particular copy in hand is not reliable, he should cross-check with other copies. The commentator then expands with two quotations. First, Mulla Ali al-Qari articulates a universal principle: transmitting religious knowledge is only permissible from widely circulated books because they are safe from heretical forgeries; rare books lack this safeguard. Second, Imam al-Qarafi al-Maliki discusses the broader principle: ideally fatwa should require narration (riwaya) from a trustworthy chain, but due to practical necessity, reliance on famous books is accepted. The commentator adds a nuance: if the marginal note's content is found in the motherbooks or clearly attributed to a known scholar's handwriting, it may be used; otherwise, al-Qarafi's prohibition stands. This lesson emphasizes the importance of textual authentication in fatwa and the danger of relying on unverified sources.

Ruling on Issuing Fatwa from an Unreliable Copy
Discussion on when a mufti may use an unreliable manuscript

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on relying on an unreliable manuscript and apply the principle of ghalabat al-zann to determine a mufti's permissible action.

Core Idea
A mufti may rely on an unreliable copy if predominant probability supports its authenticity.

In Hanafi fatwa practice, a mufti often encounters rulings recorded in manuscripts of uncertain reliability. This passage from Radd al-Muhtar presents a crucial debate: must the source be mutawatir, or is predominant probability sufficient? Understanding this rule protects the mufti from both reckless attribution and abandoning valuable heritage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the scholars involved
The discussion concerns a mufti who finds a legal ruling only in an unreliable copy (nuskha ghayr mawthuq biha). Abu Amr, Ibn Abidin, and the author (likely Ibn Abidin himself in a later reflection or a commentator) present differing views.
2
Examine Abu Amr's condition and the mufti's qualification
Abu Amr requires the mufti to check if the ruling aligns with madhhab usul and if the mufti is qualified to perform takhrij (derivation). If so, he may give fatwa, but when attributing, he must use cautious phrasing like 'I found from such-and-such'.
If not qualified, he may only mention it without issuing a fatwa.
3
Analyze Ibn Abidin's strict requirement
Ibn Abidin holds that reliable transmission requires either a personal chain (sanad) or a book that is widely circulated (mutawatir). Based on this, rare copies like al-Nawadir cannot be attributed directly to early imams unless quoted in well-known books like al-Hidayah.
This view aims to prevent forgery (tazwir).
4
Critique the strict view and apply ghalabat al-zann
The author argues that requiring tawatur is impractical; it would lead to abandoning most classical fiqh works. Instead, predominant probability (ghalabat al-zann) suffices, evidenced by scholars quoting the book, finding multiple copies, or seeing endorsements on the manuscript.
The author supports this with the analogy of judges consulting via letter and the fact that chains themselves do not require tawatur.
5
Determine the operative ruling for fatwa today
The author's view (ghalabat al-zann) is presented as the stronger one, especially for later times, to avoid the loss of valuable heritage. The mufta bihi is that predominant probability is sufficient.
This aligns with practical necessity and the principle of preventing hardship (raf' al-haraj).

Commentary
This passage discusses the rule for a mufti when he finds a legal ruling only in an unreliable manuscript. The passage begins by stating the view of Abu Amr: he must examine it. If the ruling is in accordance with the principles (usul) of the madhhab, and the mufti himself is qualified to derive such a ruling independently (i.e., he is at the level of takhrij within the madhhab), then he may give fatwa based on it. However, when attributing it to the imam, he must not claim it definitively (e.g., 'Al-Shafi'i said'), but only report what he found. If the mufti is not qualified for takhrij, he cannot issue a fatwa from an unreliable copy; he can only mention it without giving a fatwa, clarifying his source.

Ibn Abidin then adds a condition for reliable transmission: either a personal chain (sanan) or a well-known book that has been widely circulated ('tadawul'), such as the books of Muhammad ibn al-Shaybani. These are akin to mutawatir reports. Based on this, he argues that the rare transmissions (nawadir) found in isolated copies today cannot be attributed to the early imams. However, if such a rare ruling is quoted in a widely accepted book like al-Hidayah, that quotation becomes the reliable source.

The author (likely Ibn Abidin or the commentator) responds by criticizing this strict requirement. He argues that requiring actual tawatur (mass transmission) for books is impractical; rather, predominant probability (ghalabat al-zann) suffices. He supports this by noting that scholars have always transmitted from famous works like al-Mabsut and al-Bada'i, even if copies are limited. Moreover, a chain of transmission does not require tawatur. He emphasizes that applying the strict rule would lead to abandoning most classical works, which is harmful. Therefore, the mufti should rely on predominant probability by seeing that scholars quote the book and find the quoted content within it, or by seeing multiple copies.

Mufti Must Not Rely on Few Books
Warning against limited book reliance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a mufti cannot rely on only a few books and explain the reasoning based on internal school differences.

Core Idea
A mufti must consult many sources because a few may contain anomalous opinions that contradict the relied-upon school position.

This passage from al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa addresses a fundamental adab for muftis: you cannot limit yourself to one or two books, even respected ones, because the school's authoritative view is only established through comprehensive review of its literature. The text warns that a small number of works may present a shadhdh view as if it were the madhhab. This principle applies universally, including in Hanafi fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What does al-Nawawi say about a mufti relying on few books? The act is prohibited (la yajuz).
The ruling is impermissibility (tahrim) due to the risk of error.
2
Understand the illa (reason)
Why is it impermissible? Because of 'kathrat al-ikhtilaf' — abundant differences among scholars in what they present as the decisive or preferred view. A few books may not reflect the rajih.
The illa is the existence of ikhtilaf and the possibility of shadhdh opinions.
3
Trace the consequence
What can happen if a mufti relies on few books? He may adopt a shadhdh opinion that is contrary to the majority, the preferred view, or even the explicit text (nass) of the imam.
The consequence is issuing a fatwa that does not represent the madhhab.
4
Apply to Hanafi fatwa practice
How does this apply in Hanafi ifta? The mufti must consult the mu'tamad sources (e.g., al-Hidayah, al-Durar, al-Multaqa) and later compilations that filter the correct view, not just one or two texts.
Hanafi fatwa manuals emphasize relying on the mu'tamad and tarjih works.

Repeating a Fatwa for a Similar Incident
Rule on issuing fatwa for identical new case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule for repeating a fatwa for a similar incident, distinguishing when fresh reasoning is required.

Core Idea
A mufti may repeat a previous fatwa without fresh investigation if the new case is identical and the evidence is known.

This lesson addresses a practical question for muftis: when a new case arrives that is exactly like one already judged, must the mufti start from scratch? The Hanafi tradition grants an efficiency: if the mufti remembers the previous ruling and its supporting evidence, he can simply reissue it. However, if he suspects his reasoning might change upon re-examination, he should reinvestigate. This rule balances the need for consistent rulings with the integrity of ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Confirm case identity
First, verify that the new incident is exactly similar to the previous one for which a fatwa was given. Any material difference would require fresh reasoning.
Similarity must be in all legally relevant aspects.
2
Check memory of fatwa and evidence
If the mufti remembers both the fatwa and its evidence (for a mustaqill: evidence from primary sources; for a muntasib: evidence from the school), no fresh reasoning is needed.
The evidence must be retained in memory or easily retrievable.
3
Consider any new factors
Even if the fatwa and evidence are remembered, the mufti must consider whether any new factors (e.g., a change in circumstances or a new indication) would alter the ruling. If a suspicion of change arises, fresh reasoning becomes preferable.
The phrase 'إلا أن يظن بأمارة أنه لو أعاد النظر تغير اجتهاده' provides the exception.
4
Decide on fresh reasoning
If no such suspicion exists, issue the same fatwa without fresh reasoning. If suspicion exists, renew the reasoning to ensure correctness.
This step ensures the fatwa is based on knowledge and sincerity.

Mufti Must Give Decisive Answer, Not Just State Disagreement
Rule: mufti must give decisive answer, not list differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ruling that the mufti must provide a decisive preponderant answer and apply it by constructing a fatwa that resolves the questioner's doubt.

Core Idea
A fatwa that only lists differing opinions is no fatwa—the mufti must state the preponderant view.

This passage from the Rasm al-Mufti outlines a critical procedural rule: the mufti must not merely inform the questioner that scholars differ. Instead, he must give a binding answer based on the preponderant view (al-rajih). The passage warns against vague responses that leave the questioner in confusion, emphasizing the mufti's duty to provide decisive guidance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The passage states that the mufti should not restrict his fatwa to saying 'there is disagreement' or 'there are two opinions' etc.
The text lists several evasive phrases: خلاف, قولان, وجهان, روايتان.
2
Understand the rationale
The purpose of the mustafti (questioner) is to know what to act upon. Merely listing opinions does not fulfill this need.
The mufti's answer must be actionable.
3
Extract the positive duty
The mufti should give a decisive answer based on what is preponderant (الراجح).
This implies the mufti must have the capacity to determine tarjih.
4
Note the fallback
If the mufti does not know the preponderant view, he must withhold until it becomes clear, or refrain from giving fatwa altogether.
The passage cites senior Hanafis who refrained from issuing a fatwa on the issue of 'hanth al-nasi' due to its complexity.

Ruling on Repeating Questions for Frequently Occurring Issues
Ruling on when a layperson must re-ask a fatwa question

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the rule that a layperson must re-ask for a recurring issue and APPLY the exception for frequently occurring matters.

Core Idea
A layperson must re-ask for a recurring issue unless it is frequent and burdensome.

When a mufti issues a fatwa, his ruling is based on his current understanding, which may change over time. Therefore, if the same issue recurs, the layperson should ask again to confirm. However, if the issue occurs very frequently and repeating the question would cause hardship, the first answer suffices. This balances the need for accurate fatwa with the principle of removing difficulty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The text states that if a layperson asks about an issue and then it happens again, he must ask again (general rule).
2
Understand the reason
The underlying reason is that the mufti's opinion may have changed, so the previous fatwa may no longer be valid.
3
Identify the exception
The text says: unless the issue is very frequent and repeating the question is burdensome, then the first answer suffices.
4
Note the basis of the exception
The exception is based on the principle of removing hardship (raf' al-haraj) in Islamic law.
This corresponds to the maxim 'al-mashaqqah tajlib al-taysir' (hardship begets facility).

Correcting Errors in the Written Query – Mufti's Duty
Rule on correcting ambiguous words and errors in fatwa requests

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule for correcting ambiguous words and grammatical errors in a written fatwa request, and explain the importance of verifying the questioner's intent using the al-Karkhi example.

Core Idea
A mufti must clarify every ambiguity in the written query before issuing a fatwa, or risk grave error.

A written fatwa request (ruqʿa) may contain unclear words, grammatical mistakes, or omissions that alter the intended meaning. The mufti is not to guess or rely on assumptions; he must actively seek clarification from the questioner and properly annotate the text. The classical example of Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi demonstrates how a failure to dot and vowel led even learned jurists astray, and how a careful mufti rectifies the matter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defect
Scrutinize the query for three types of defects: ambiguous words (mushtabiha), gross grammatical errors (lahn fāhish), and mistakes that change the meaning (khaṭā yuḥīl al-maʿnā). Each requires a specific corrective action.
Not every spelling mistake needs correction – only those that affect legal meaning.
2
Determine the corrective action
For ambiguous words: ask the questioner directly, then add dots (nuqaṭ) and vowel marks (shakl) to fix the ambiguity. For gross errors: correct them in the text. For meaning-changing mistakes: correct them as well.
The mufti should record his corrections on the original paper or a copy.
3
Apply the rule to the al-Karkhi anecdote
The questioner wrote 'You are divorced if' and stopped. The original jurists misread it because the query lacked diacritical marks – they thought it referred to a man named 'Abdān's endowment. Al-Karkhi recognized the true context (a cut-off conditional divorce) and answered accordingly, showing how careful verification prevents error.
This illustrates the necessity of the rule: without clarification, a fatwa can be completely wrong.

Rule on filling blank spaces to prevent fraudulent additions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the precaution a mufti must take against blank spaces in written fatwas and apply this adab to prevent fraudulent additions.

Core Idea
A mufti must fill every blank to protect his fatwa from sabotage.

A written fatwa is not only a legal answer but a document that can be tampered with. This lesson teaches a critical adab: securing the physical space of the fatwa sheet. The passage explains the duty to cross out or fill blanks, illustrated by a historical case that shows the consequences of neglect.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling on blank spaces
The mufti must cross out or fill any blank (bayāḍ) in the middle or end of a line of the fatwa request.
This applies before handing back the fatwa sheet.
2
Explain the reasoning
The blank could be exploited by someone wishing to harm the mufti by adding text that corrupts the fatwa.
The Arabic phrase 'ربما قصد المفتى بالإيذاء' clarifies the malicious intent.
3
Reflect on the historical case
Judge Abu Hamid al-Marwarudhi gave a correct fatwa on inheritance; the questioner added 'and a father' in a blank space after the question, changing the ruling and embarrassing the judge.
This scandal underscores the necessity of the rule.
4
Apply the adab in practice
When a written request for fatwa contains blanks, the mufti should either strike them out or fill them with a line, the word 'filled', or other content.
Check for blanks even after writing the answer; leave no empty space.

Forbearance in Answering Fatwa Questions
Quotes on the virtue of delaying fatwa for thorough understanding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle of tathabbut (deliberation) and apply it to evaluate a mufti's conduct when issuing a fatwa.

Core Idea
A mufti must prioritize understanding over speed—even a correct answer without understanding is blameworthy.

Imam Abu Hanifa taught that it is better to err with understanding than to be correct without it. This lesson explores the virtue of tathabbut (deliberation) in fatwa, as exemplified by the early imams. We will examine two key quotes and derive the operative adab for muftis: that thorough comprehension (fahm) is the foundation of a sound fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two quotes and their sources
Locate the statements from Imam Abu Hanifa and al-Khalil ibn Ahmad as transmitted by Ibn al-Salah. Note the chain: the first is from Nawazil Abi al-Layth, the second from Fatawa Ibn al-Salah.
Both quotes are adab (ethical) directives, not substantive fiqh rulings.
2
Extract the core lesson from each
Abu Hanifa says erring with understanding is better than being correct without understanding. Al-Khalil says he blames the one who answers quickly and is correct, and praises the one who deliberates and errs. Both prioritize the method over the outcome.
The key term is 'fahm' (deep understanding).
3
Determine the operative rule for muftis
The operative principle (mufta bihi) is that a mufti must delay answering until he has achieved true fahm. If he does not know, he should say 'I do not know' or ask for time to research.
Delaying for up to a year, as Abu Hanifa did, is permissible.
4
Note the authority and reliability of the transmission
The quotes are recorded in later fatwa collections (Nawazil Abi al-Layth, Tanqih al-Fatawa al-Hamidiyyah) and are accepted as authoritative adab in the Hanafi school.
Although not from the six core books of Zahir al-Riwayah, the principle is unanimous.
5
Apply to a modern nawazil scenario
Consider a mufti answering an online query: if he responds immediately without verifying, his fatwa is blameworthy even if correct. He should take time to research or consult.
Modern online fatwa services must balance promptness with accuracy.

Consulting Peers Before Issuing Fatwa
Encouragement for mufti to consult and discuss with other scholars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a mufti should consult qualified peers before issuing a fatwa, and identify the exceptions when consultation is not recommended.

Core Idea
A mufti must consult even juniors before fatwa, except when secrecy or harm prohibits it.

A mufti does not issue fatwas in isolation. Following the Prophetic model and the practice of the Companions, the Hanafi tradition strongly recommends that the mufti consult with other scholars, even those who are his juniors, before giving a definitive answer. The passage states it is mustahabb for the mufti to read the question to qualified attendees and consult them with gentleness and fairness.

Consultation follows the way of the Salaf and serves to uncover aspects that may be hidden from the mufti alone.
It also trains and sharpens the minds of students.
3
Note the evidence
The primary evidence is the Qur'anic verse 'And consult them in the matter' (Surah Al Imran: 159) and the consistent practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab and other Companions.
Umar even consulted the youngest Companion, Ibn Abbas.
4
Recognize the exceptions
Consultation is avoided if revealing the question would be ugly, if the questioner prefers secrecy, or if publicizing would cause mischief (mafsadah).
This applies also to dream interpreters and physicians.

This is a practical ruling of mufti adab regarding the physical writing of the fatwa.

الحبر (al-hibr): Standard ink that is more permanent and suited for long-term preservation

Signing the Fatwa: Name, Lineage and Madhhab
Instructions for signing off a fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the adab of signing a fatwa by constructing a signature that includes name, genealogy, and madhhab, and identify when simplification is permissible.

Core Idea
A fatwa signature must clearly identify the mufti through name, lineage and madhhab, unless he is well-known.

When you conclude a fatwa with 'والله أعلم', your signature is not just a formality — it is a mark of accountability. The mufti's adab requires that you write your name, your genealogy (e.g., tribe or town), and your madhhab (Hanafi, Shafi'i, etc.). This ensures the questioner knows who gave the ruling and which school was followed. However, if the mufti is widely known, a shorter signature is acceptable. This lesson walks you through the specific elements and the condition that allows abbreviation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the closing phrase
The fatwa ends with a phrase like 'والله أعلم'. The signature follows immediately after this phrase.
The phrase 'والله أعلم' is the standard closing; other similar phrases are also acceptable.
2
Construct the full signature
Write 'كتبه فلان بن فلان الفلاني' — i.e., your name, your father's name, and your attributive (nisbah) that identifies you by tribe, town, or characteristic. Then add your madhhab, e.g., 'الحنفي'.
The format 'فلان بن فلان الفلاني' includes genealogy (nasab) and attribution (nisbah). Madhhab is placed last.
3
Determine if abbreviation is allowed
Ask: Am I so well-known by name alone that my signature is unmistakable? If yes, you may limit the signature to just your name (e.g., 'كتبه فلان').
The key criterion is renown (shuhrah). If your name alone suffices for identification, you need not write the full genealogy.
4
Apply the adab in a real scenario
Practice by writing a hypothetical signature: 'كتبه محمد بن أحمد القرشي الحنفي'. Then consider if 'محمد القرشي' would be sufficient if you are well-known in your locality.
Always err on the side of clarity. The mufti's goal is to be clearly identifiable.
