The Ruling on Legal Stratagems (Hila) and Their Conditions
Discusses the permissibility of legal stratagems with evidence and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify legal stratagems by their legal ruling based on intention and outcome, and apply the Hanafi conditions for permissible hila.

Core Idea
A hila is judged by its intention: escaping haram is good, invalidating a right is forbidden.

This lesson examines the permissibility of legal stratagems (hila) in Islamic law, drawing from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa and Hanafi sources. While Hanafis are known for their use of hila, they are strictly conditioned on righteous intent. We will explore the textual evidence, the governing rule from al-Muhit, and the warnings against misuse by those who take shortcuts in ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the taxonomy of hila by ruling
List the categories of hila given in the passage based on their outcome: haram if it invalidates a right or affirms falsehood; wajib/mustahab if it affirms a right or repels falsehood; mustahab/mubah if it avoids disliked matters; makruh if it leads to abandoning a recommended act.
The ruling depends on the intended result, not the means alone.
2
Extract evidence for permissible hila
Locate the Quranic verses and hadith cited: verse about the weak (4:98), story of Ayyub (38:44), verse on taqwa (65:4), hadith of Bilaal selling a camel for dirhams, and the principle of istithna (saying "in sha Allah").
These show that the Shariah permits lawful devices to avoid hardship.
3
Extract evidence for forbidden hila
Identify the evidence against evil hila: the story of Sabbath-breakers, the hadith about selling forbidden fat, the prohibition of najsh (fraudulent bidding), and the condemnation of muhallil (marriage to permit remarriage).
These show that hila that circumvent established prohibitions or harm others are unlawful.
4
Analyze the Hanafi position and its conditions
Note that Abu Yusuf authored a book on hila, but Hanafis condition hila on righteous intent. The rule from al-Muhit states: if for escaping haram and avoiding sin, it is good; if for invalidating a Muslim's right, it is sin and transgression.
The Hanafi school is not unique in using hila; it is the condition of intent that matters.
5
Differentiate between permissible hila and blameworthy ones
The passage contrasts the praiseworthy hila (e.g., for releasing an oath) with the blameworthy hila al-sarrijiyya, which arose after the third century and is used to nullify a right. Also distinguish the two states of al-mutasahhil: one who shortcuts in evidence (deficient ijtihad) and one who seeks licenses through false interpretation (transgressive).
The mufti must avoid both extremes: being too lax or too strict.

Commentary
This passage from Imam Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa discusses the ruling on legal stratagems (hila). It begins by categorizing hila based on intention and outcome: if it leads to invalidating a right or affirming falsehood, it is forbidden; if to affirming a right or repelling falsehood, it is obligatory or recommended; if to avoid disliked matters, it is recommended or permissible; if to abandon a recommended act, it is disliked. Then it presents evidence for permissible hila from the Quran (e.g., verse about the weak, story of Ayyub, verse on taqwa) and Sunnah (e.g., hadith of Bilaal about selling a camel for dirhams and buying another). Evidence for forbidden hila includes the story of the Sabbath-breakers and the hadith about selling fat after it was forbidden. The text notes that Hanafis are famous for hila because Abu Yusuf wrote a book on them, but they condition hila on righteous intent. Al-Muhit gives a rule: if for escaping haram, it is good; if for invalidating a Muslim's right, it is sin. Then it quotes al-Sam'ani on two states of the one who takes the easy way: (1) shortcutting in evidence – deficient in ijtihad, not allowed to give fatwa; (2) seeking licenses through false interpretations – transgressive. Finally, it mentions the Sarrijiyya stratagem as blameworthy and continues with supporting quotes from Ibn Qayyim. The commentary explains that the Hanafi position aligns with the rule that hila are permissible only when used for a legitimate purpose.

Key Terms
الحيلة (al-hila): A legal device or stratagem; a means to achieve an end. The text gives a rule from al-Muhit: if used to escape from haram or sin, it is good; if used to invalidate a Muslim's right, it is forbidden.
المتسهل (al-mutasahhil): The one who takes the easy way in seeking evidence and rulings. Two states: deficient in ijtihad (first state) or seeking licenses through false interpretation (second state).
الحيلة السريجية (al-hila al-sarrijiyya): A specific blameworthy stratagem that originated after the third century, used to nullify a legal right or obligation. The text says it is blameworthy because it involves ambiguity.

Permissible Legal Stratagems (Hila) with Good Intentions
Rule on permissible hila with scholarly commentary and quotes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a permissible hila and apply them to a fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
A legal stratagem is praiseworthy if it has no ambiguity, is sought with sound intention, and extricates from a difficulty.

This passage from Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa establishes the ruling on legal stratagems (hila) in Islamic law. The author distinguishes between praiseworthy and blameworthy stratagems. The praiseworthy hila is one that has no ambiguity (shubha) and is sought with a sound intention to extricate oneself from a difficulty such as a broken oath. The author supports this with the practice of prophets and the principle of rukhsah (concession). As a mufti, understanding the conditions for a permissible hila is essential to guide seekers to lawful alternatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hila and its purpose
First, understand what the person wants to achieve: to free themselves from a difficulty (e.g., an oath) or to reach a lawful end.
The text says 'li-takhallus min warṭat yamīn wa naḥwihā' (to free from a difficulty of an oath or the like).
2
Check for ambiguity (shubha)
Ensure the stratagem has no ambiguity or doubt in its permissibility. The text says 'lā shubha fīhā' (no ambiguity).
Ambiguity here refers to any element that could make the stratagem questionable according to Shari'ah.
3
Verify sound intention
Confirm that the person's intention is sound (man ṣaḥḥa qaṣduh). The hila should not be used to circumvent obligations or commit the unlawful.
The intention is key: it must be to comply with Shari'ah, not to cheat.
4
Determine if it leads to halal or avoids haram
The outcome of the hila should be that it leads to a lawful result or avoids an unlawful one. The text says 'aḥsanu al-makhārij mā khaṣṣala min al-maʾāthim' (the best ways out remove from sins).
If the hila leads to dropping an obligation or committing a prohibition, it becomes blameworthy.

The Suriyajiyya Hila and Its Invalidity
Detailed analysis of a blameworthy stratagem in divorce and the scholarly dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the mechanism of the Suriyajiyya hila, state the Hanafi critique on legal, linguistic, and rational grounds, and apply the ruling that both conditional and immediate divorces take effect.

Core Idea
A logical paradox cannot suspend the operation of a valid legal pronouncement.

The Suriyajiyya hila is a famous stratagem in divorce law that attempts to circumvent the occurrence of divorce by creating a circular condition. This lesson examines why the Hanafi school, along with the majority of jurists, rejects this hila as invalid, violating textual evidence, linguistic structure, and logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the hila's mechanism
Identify the exact wording of the Suriyajiyya hila: a man says, 'Whenever I divorce you, you are divorced three times before that.' Note the conditional and the temporal 'before'.
The key is the word 'before' (qablahu).
2
Identify the claimed logical paradox
Ibn Surayj argued that if the immediate divorce occurs, the conditional (three divorces) would occur first, preventing the immediate divorce. Thus, the immediate divorce cannot occur, leading to a paradox.
This is the core of the dispute.
3
Analyse the Hanafi rebuttal on three fronts
The Hanafis counter that the paradox is illusory. They critique the hila on three grounds: legal (the texts permit divorce), linguistic (the consequence follows the condition, not precedes), and rational (cause precedes effect).
Each ground independently invalidates the hila.
4
Determine the practical outcome
Under the Hanafi ruling, the conditional divorce takes effect when the condition is fulfilled (the immediate pronouncement), and the word 'before' is void. Thus, both divorces occur, resulting in a final divorce if three are pronounced.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under the opposing view, no divorce occurs.
5
Reflect on the adab of forbidding such hiyal
The author condemns the hila as blameworthy, citing the Quranic verse about concealing knowledge. This highlights the ethical dimension: using sophistry to circumvent clear rulings is a transgression.
Muftis must guard against such stratagems.

(3) His saying: 'The Suriyajiyya stratagem is a stratagem that arose after the third century. Its form is that he says: Whenever I divorce you or whenever my divorce falls upon you, you are divorced three times before it. They said: So divorce cannot occur after that because if it occurs, then what is conditional upon it, which is the three, would occur, and if the three occur, this immediate one cannot occur. So its occurrence leads to its non-occurrence, and whatever leads to its non-existence does not exist. This is the choice of Abu al-Abbas ibn Surayj, and a group of Shafi'i scholars agreed with him. But the majority of jurists from the Malikis, Hanafis, Hanbalis, and many Shafi'is rejected it. They said: This contradicts the law, language, and reason. The correct view is the occurrence of both the immediate and the conditional. As for legally: because making a divorce in the past is making it in the present, and because the texts speak of the legality of divorce, and accepting this stratagem leads to its nullification. As for linguistically: because the consequence occurs after the condition or with it, not before it. As for rationally: because the object of the conditional particle is the cause and the consequence is the effect, and the effect cannot precede the cause. So his saying 'before it' is absolutely void, and the divorce remains as the consequence of the condition unrestricted by the prior. See: I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in 3/251, Radd al-Muhtar: Matlab Talaq al-Dawr 3/229, Karachi print, and Fath al-Qadir 3/372, Qunta print.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Suriyajiyya hila, a controversial stratagem attributed to Abu al-Abbas ibn Surayj of the Shafi'i school. It is a circular divorce problem: a man says, 'Whenever I divorce you, you are divorced three times before that.' The claim is that this creates a logical impasse preventing any divorce from occurring. The author shows that this hila is blameworthy and delves into the dispute. The Hanafi position (and the majority) rejects this hila as contradictory to texts, language, and reason. The Hanafis argue that the conditional divorce occurs when the condition is fulfilled, and the word 'before' is meaningless. The practical effect is that the hila is invalid, and both the immediate and conditional divorces take effect. This lesson highlights the importance of not using sophistry to circumvent clear Shari'ah rulings.

Key Terms
حيلة سريجية (hila suriyajiyya): A specific stratagem in divorce involving circular conditional divorce, attributed to Ibn Surayj.
منجز (munjiz): An immediate, unconditional pronouncement (e.g., divorce).
معلق (muallaq): A conditional pronouncement (e.g., divorce tied to a future event).

Legal Stratagems (Hila) for Oaths: Hanafi Guidance from Abu Hanifa
Three hila cases from Abu Hanifa's fatwa practice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the Hanafi method of resolving oath dilemmas using permissible legal stratagems (hila).

Core Idea
Hila are lawful means to avoid sin without breaking the oath, grounded in the principle of easing hardship.

The Hanafi school is known for its pragmatic approach to legal dilemmas, using permissible stratagems (hila) to avoid harm. This passage presents three classic cases from Abu Hanifa, showing how oaths can be resolved without sin or injustice.

This passage presents three practical hila (legal stratagems) from the early Hanafi school, demonstrating how Abu Hanifa resolved oath-related dilemmas without necessitating sin or harm. The first case involves a husband who swore not to spend on his wife; the solution is to give her from her dower, a loan, or a sale, then discharge her, fulfilling the financial obligation without directly spending. These illustrate the Hanafi approach of using lawful means (hila) to avoid unlawful outcomes, based on the principle of alleviating hardship.
