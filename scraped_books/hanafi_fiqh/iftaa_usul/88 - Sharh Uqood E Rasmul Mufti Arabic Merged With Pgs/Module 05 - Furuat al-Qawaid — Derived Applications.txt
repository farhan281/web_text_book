Two Foundational Qawaid: Mafhum and Urf
Two rules: acting on mafhum of narrations and considering custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two qawaid (acting on mafhum and considering custom), identify their conditions and scope, and apply them in a fatwa context.

Core Idea
A mufti may use implied meanings and custom, but never against an explicit text.

This lesson presents two concise and memorable principles from the Hanafi fatwa tradition. The first allows a mufti to derive rulings from the implied meaning (mafhum) of narrations, provided no explicit text contradicts them. The second affirms the legal weight of custom ('urf) as a basis for rulings. Together, they empower the mufti to address new cases while remaining anchored to the primary sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify each qawida
Read the couplet and separate the two rules. The first is about acting on mafhum; the second is about considering custom.
The poem uses 'wa' (and) to connect them.
2
Understand the condition for mafhum
The first rule states that mafhum is used only if it does not contradict an established explicit text (sarih thabit). This is a key restriction.
Mafhum includes implication of a command, prohibition, or permission from context.
3
Understand the scope of custom
The second rule states that custom has consideration in Shari'ah; many rulings are based on it. This is a broad principle applicable across fiqh.
Custom must not contradict a clear text or ijma.
4
Note the relationship between the two rules
Both rules expand the mufti's toolbox without overriding clear primary sources. They operate in areas of silence or ambiguity.
When a text is explicit, neither mafhum nor custom can override it.

Preferring Istihsan over Qiyas: The Rasm Rule and Its Exceptions
Rasm rule on tarjih between istihsan and qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that istihsan is preferred over qiyas in Hanafi law, identify the exceptions where qiyas prevails, and apply the three types of istihsan to new cases.

Core Idea
Istihsan prevails over qiyas unless the basis of istihsan is weak; only hidden-qiyas istihsan can be extended by analogy.

This lesson introduces a fundamental rule in Hanafi legal theory: when a case involves both qiyas (analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference), istihsan is to be favored. However, this rule has important exceptions. The passage also clarifies the types of istihsan and explains which can be extended to new cases. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for any mufti applying Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
The rule states: when there is both qiyas and istihsan in a matter, istihsan is preferred over qiyas. This is the default position across Hanafi works.
The rule is not absolute; it has exceptions.
2
Recognize the exceptions
The rule has exceptions where qiyas prevails over istihsan. The exact number varies among scholars (e.g., 11 according to al-Natifī, 22 according to al-Nasafī). In these exceptional cases, the basis of istihsan is weak, so one does not deviate from qiyas.
The exceptions are not listed in this passage, but the principle is clear.
3
Differentiate the three types of istihsan
Istihsan can be based on: (a) nass (Quran or Hadith), (b) ijma' (consensus), or (c) darura (necessity). Each type has its own scope, e.g., contracts like salam are based on nass despite being contrary to qiyas.
Istihsan is not arbitrary; it must have a stronger evidentiary basis.
4
Understand extensibility
Only istihsan based on hidden qiyas can be extended to other cases by analogy. The other types (nass, ijma', darura) are limited to their specific contexts.
This distinction is crucial for applying istihsan to novel cases.

Key Terms
استحسان (Istihsan): Juristic preference; turning away from the ruling of analogous cases to another ruling due to a stronger reason.
قياس جلي (Qiyas Jali): Clear analogy; its ratio legis ('illa) is immediately understood upon hearing the ruling.
قياس خفي (Qiyas Khafi): Hidden analogy; its ratio legis is only grasped through reflection and careful thought.
استحسان بالنص (Istihsan bi al-nass): Preference based on a revealed text (Quran or Hadith) that overrides general analogy.
استحسان بالإجماع (Istihsan bi al-ijma'): Preference based on consensus of the scholars on a ruling contrary to qiyas.
استحسان بالضرورة (Istihsan bi al-darura): Preference based on necessity, setting aside qiyas to avoid hardship.

Mafhum al-Muwafaqah: Definition and Evidence
Defining mafhum al-muwafaqah with example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mafhum al-muwafaqah (dalalat al-nass), distinguish it from qiyas, and apply it to derive a ruling from the verse 'Do not say to them uff'.

Core Idea
Mafhum al-muwafaqah is a direct linguistic implication, not requiring ijtihad — it is binding proof.

In this passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, the author introduces a fundamental concept in Hanafi usul: mafhum (implicit meaning). The first type, mafhum al-muwafaqah, is unanimously accepted and is the basis for a fortiori reasoning from texts. We will explore its definition, the classical example from the Quran, and how Hanafis distinguish it from qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Define mafhum and its division
Explain what mafhum is and that it divides into muwafaqah and mukhalafah. Mafhum is the indication of the word to an unmentioned meaning.
2
Analyze the Quranic example
Examine the verse 'Do not say to them uff' (Quran 17:23) and how it implies the prohibition of hitting parents a fortiori.
The example shows mafhum al-muwafaqah in action.
3
Identify the effective cause
Recognize that the effective cause is harm (idha'), and hitting involves greater harm than saying 'uff'.
The understanding is immediate for any Arabic speaker.
4
Distinguish from qiyas
Note that this understanding is immediate and does not require technical deduction; it is distinct from qiyas which requires ijtihad.
This is a key Hanafi distinction.
5
Confirm Hanafi terminology
Understand that Hanafis call this 'dalalat al-nass' and consider it a binding proof by consensus.
Terminological nuance is important for accurate usage.

Explanation of Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. 160. The concept (mafhum) and its divisions: establishing the ruling of the spoken (mantuq) for the unspoken by mere understanding of the language, i.e., without stopping on opinion and ijtihad, like the indication of 'Do not say to them 'uff'' on the prohibition of hitting. (2) The summary: words are molds for meanings derived from them. Sometimes they are derived from the direction of utterance explicitly, and sometimes from its direction implicitly. The first is the mantuq, and the second is the mafhum. So the mantuq is the indication of the word to a mentioned thing, and it includes the expression (ibarah) of the text and its allusion (isharah). The mafhum is the indication of the word to an unmentioned thing. Mafhum al-muwafaqah is that the unmentioned is consistent with the mentioned in ruling, and mafhum al-mukhalafah is the opposite. The first is called by us 'dalalat al-nass' and it is valid by consensus. The other is 'takhsis al-shay' bi al-dhikr' (specification by mention), and it is disputed: according to us, it is among the corrupt inferences in the speech of the Lawgiver, and it is only considered in other speech. According to the Shafi'is, it is valid in the speech of the Lawgiver and other speech with certain conditions, and the complete discussion will follow. Then know that both mantuq and mafhum are divisions of meaning terminologically. As for language, mantuq is what is uttered, which is the word itself, not the meaning; and mafhum is what is derived from the word, which is the meaning. So it becomes clear that applying mantuq to meaning is only a terminological reality, not linguistic, because meaning is not uttered; rather, the word indicating it is uttered. (1/360, 361) (3) He said: 'Mafhum muwafaqah is also called tanbih al-khitab. (Mazhar) And it is what the Hanafis express as dalalat al-nass. It is called mafhum al-muwafaqah because the unmentioned is consistent with the mentioned in ruling. So mafhum al-muwafaqah: 'what the unmentioned is consistent with the uttered; if it is more deserving of the ruling than the uttered, it is called fahwa al-khitab, i.e., its implication, and if it is equal to it, it is called lahn al-khitab.' Lahn al-khitab linguistically: understanding something without explicit expression, including His statement: 'And you will surely recognize them in the tone of speech' i.e., in slips of speech without explicit hypocrisy. (1) He said: 'without stopping on opinion and ijtihad' is an indication of the difference between mafhum al-muwafaqah, i.e., dalalat al-nass, and qiyas. This is because mafhum al-muwafaqah is called dalalat al-nass by us, and it does not depend on reflection and deduction; rather, anyone who is a native speaker knows it without needing to arrange premises and reflection. If one needed that, it would be qiyas which only a mujtahid can grasp, not a mafhum. (2) He said: 'indication of 'Do not say to them 'uff'' on the prohibition of hitting' - for the usual meaning of it is the prohibition of the child addressing his parents with this word placed for annoyance and vexation, then from that it moves to the intended prohibition for which the prohibition is established, which is harm, and by its indication the prohibition of hitting them or insulting them is established a fortiori from the prohibition of saying 'uff' to them, considering the effective cause of its prohibition understood by everyone who knows the language, which is harm; for harm in hitting them is greater than harm in saying 'uff'. (Mazhar) (1) He said: 'Mafhum mukhalafah is also called dalil al-khitab. (Mazhar) That is, because its proof is of the same type as the speech, or because the speech indicates it. (2) He said: 'It is divisions' - the types of mafhum according to those who accept it reached ten categories varying in strength and weakness. The commentator mentioned five famous ones, and the remaining five are: mafhum al-lughah (linguistic concept): like someone saying 'I forbade wine because of its intoxication'; mafhum al-hasr (concept of restriction): like the Prophet's saying 'Riba is only in delayed payment' and 'Shuf'a is only in what has not been divided'; and mafhum al-hal (state), al-zaman (time), and al-makan (place). But in reality they are included in mafhum al-sifah because, according to the usuliyyun, what is meant is the qualitative attribute that reduces commonality and decreases participation, not the adjective known to grammarians. They only mentioned these three separately to complete the benefit. Refer: Irshad al-Fuhul: 2/61 and Ihkam al-Ahkam: 3/67. (3) He said: 'Mafhum al-sifah' is the indication of a word that indicates a ruling qualified by a description, establishing the opposite of that ruling for the unmentioned thing from which that description is absent. An example is His statement: 'And whoever among you cannot afford to marry free believing women, then from what your right hands possess of believing slave girls.' The noble verse indicates by mafhum al-muwafaqah the permissibility of marrying believing slave girls when unable to marry free women, and indicates by mafhum al-mukhalafah the prohibition of marrying non-believing slave girls. (4) He said: 'like 'on pasturing animals there is zakat' - it indicates the negation of zakat on stall-fed animals. The commentator said in 'al-Tahrir': 'Then this being an example of mafhum al-sifah is related from the majority of Shafi'is, and Taj al-Din al-Subki mentioned: 'The more apparent is that there is no concept for it due to the corruption of speech in it like a title (laqab), and the first is more appropriate.'

Commentary
This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti is a definitional and classificatory discussion of mafhum (implicit meaning) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by defining mafhum: it is the establishment of a ruling for an unmentioned thing based solely on linguistic understanding, without requiring opinion or ijtihad. The example given is the verse 'Do not say to them 'uff'' (Quran 17:23), which by its clear prohibition of the mildest expression of annoyance implies the stronger prohibition of hitting parents. This is mafhum al-muwafaqah, also known as dalalat al-nass in Hanafi terminology. The author then explains that words are molds for meanings: meanings are derived either explicitly (mantuq) or implicitly (mafhum). Mantuq includes 'ibarah (explicit expression) and isharah (allusion). Mafhum is divided into mafhum al-muwafaqah (where the unmentioned ruling agrees with the mentioned) and mafhum al-mukhalafah (where the unmentioned ruling is opposite). Hanafis accept mafhum al-muwafaqah by consensus, but consider mafhum al-mukhalafah (which they call 'takhsis al-shay' bi al-dhikr') as a corrupt inference in the speech of the Lawgiver (i.e., in Quran and Sunnah), though it is valid in other speech like human agreements. Shafi'is accept it with conditions. The author notes a terminological nuance: in language, mantuq is the uttered word itself, not the meaning; but in usul terminology, mantuq refers to the meaning derived from uttered words. The commentary elaborates that mafhum al-muwafaqah is called 'tanbih al-khitab', and if the unmentioned is more deserving, it is 'fahwa al-khitab' (a fortiori), and if equal, 'lahn al-khitab' (co-extensive). The distinction between mafhum al-muwafaqah and qiyas is crucial: mafhum is understood directly by any native speaker without reflection, while qiyas requires technical deduction and is only accessible to a mujtahid. The passage then turns to mafhum al-mukhalafah, defined as the indication of the word that the opposite ruling applies when a condition is absent. It has several types, of which five are famous: mafhum al-sifah (attribute), mafhum al-shart (condition), mafhum al-ghayah (limit), mafhum al-adad (number), and mafhum al-ism (name). The commentator lists five additional types: mafhum al-lughah (linguistic), mafhum al-hasr (restriction), mafhum al-hal (state), mafhum al-zaman (time), and mafhum al-makan (place), but notes that these fall under mafhum al-sifah. An example of mafhum al-sifah is 'In pasturing animals there is zakat' — this implies no zakat on stall-fed animals. The Shafi'is generally accept this as a valid concept, but some Hanafis argue against it because the mention of 'sa'imah' might be merely by chance. The example from Quran 4:25 shows both mafhum al-muwafaqah (permission to marry believing slave girls) and mafhum al-mukhalafah (prohibition of marrying non-believing slave girls). The passage is dense but systematically clarifies Hanafi methodology on deriving meanings from texts.

Key Terms
مفهوم الموافقة (mafhum al-muwafaqah): The implicit meaning where the unmentioned case agrees with the mentioned case in ruling, either a fortiori (fahwa al-khitab) or co-extensively (lahn al-khitab). Also called dalalat al-nass by Hanafis.
دلالة النص (dalalat al-nass): Hanafi term for mafhum al-muwafaqah; indicates that the text's implication is clear without need for qiyas, based on direct linguistic understanding.

Mafhum al-Mukhalafah: Hanafi Position and Practical Application
Defining mafhum al-mukhalafah and its types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mafhum al-mukhalafah, identify its types, explain why Hanafis reject it in the Lawgiver's speech, and compare with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Hanafis consider mafhum al-mukhalafah a corrupt inference in divine speech; it is valid only in human speech.

The second type of mafhum is mafhum al-mukhalafah, where the unmentioned case takes the opposite ruling. Hanafis sharply distinguish between divine and human speech regarding its validity. This lesson unpacks the concept, its subtypes like mafhum al-sifah, and the inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'is.

Guided Path
1
Define mafhum al-mukhalafah
It is the implication that when a ruling is attached to a restriction, the opposite ruling applies when the restriction is absent. Also called dalil al-khitab.
2
List the famous types
The five famous types are: mafhum al-sifah (attribute), al-shart (condition), al-ghayah (limit), al-adad (number), al-ism (name). Additional types exist but fall under these.
Mafhum al-sifah is the most discussed.
3
Examine the Hanafi objection
Hanafis reject it in the Lawgiver's speech because restrictions may be incidental or for other purposes (e.g., addressing a common occurrence). The default is that the ruling applies generally unless there is strong contextual indication.
Example: 'Do not kill your children for fear of poverty' does not permit killing for other reasons.
4
Compare Shafi'i perspective
Shafi'is accept mafhum al-mukhalafah with conditions: the restriction must be purposive, not for praise or blame, not for a common occurrence, etc. They view divine speech as eloquent and every restriction as meaningful.
5
Apply to an example
Analyze the hadith 'On pasturing animals (sa'imah) there is zakat'. Shafi'is infer no zakat on non-pasturing animals via mafhum al-sifah. Hanafis do not rely on this inference; they may establish zakat on non-pasturing animals through qiyas or other proofs.
Practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

The first is the mantuq, and the second is the mafhum. So the mantuq is the indication of the word to a mentioned thing, and it includes the expression (ibarah) of the text and its allusion (isharah). The mafhum is the indication of the word to an unmentioned thing. Mafhum al-muwafaqah is that the unmentioned is consistent with the mentioned in ruling, and mafhum al-mukhalafah is the opposite. The first is called by us 'dalalat al-nass' and it is valid by consensus. The other is 'takhsis al-shay' bi al-dhikr' (specification by mention), and it is disputed: according to us, it is among the corrupt inferences in the speech of the Lawgiver, and it is only considered in other speech. According to the Shafi'is, it is valid in the speech of the Lawgiver and other speech with certain conditions, and the complete discussion will follow. Then know that both mantuq and mafhum are divisions of meaning terminologically. As for language, mantuq is what is uttered, which is the word itself, not the meaning; and mafhum is what is derived from the word, which is the meaning. So it becomes clear that applying mantuq to meaning is only a terminological reality, not linguistic, because meaning is not uttered; rather, the word indicating it is uttered. Commentary
This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti is a definitional and classificatory discussion of mafhum (implicit meaning) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by defining mafhum: it is the establishment of a ruling for an unmentioned thing based solely on linguistic understanding, without requiring opinion or ijtihad. The example given is the verse 'Do not say to them 'uff'' (Quran 17:23), which by its clear prohibition of the mildest expression of annoyance implies the stronger prohibition of hitting parents. This is mafhum al-muwafaqah, also known as dalalat al-nass in Hanafi terminology. The author then explains that words are molds for meanings: meanings are derived either explicitly (mantuq) or implicitly (mafhum). Mantuq includes 'ibarah (explicit expression) and isharah (allusion). Mafhum is divided into mafhum al-muwafaqah (where the unmentioned ruling agrees with the mentioned) and mafhum al-mukhalafah (where the unmentioned ruling is opposite). Hanafis accept mafhum al-muwafaqah by consensus, but consider mafhum al-mukhalafah (which they call 'takhsis al-shay' bi al-dhikr') as a corrupt inference in the speech of the Lawgiver (i.e., in Quran and Sunnah), though it is valid in other speech like human agreements. Shafi'is accept it with conditions. The author notes a terminological nuance: in language, mantuq is the uttered word itself, not the meaning; but in usul terminology, mantuq refers to the meaning derived from uttered words. The commentary elaborates that mafhum al-muwafaqah is called 'tanbih al-khitab', and if the unmentioned is more deserving, it is 'fahwa al-khitab' (a fortiori), and if equal, 'lahn al-khitab' (co-extensive). The distinction between mafhum al-muwafaqah and qiyas is crucial: mafhum is understood directly by any native speaker without reflection, while qiyas requires technical deduction and is only accessible to a mujtahid. The passage then turns to mafhum al-mukhalafah, defined as the indication of the word that the opposite ruling applies when a condition is absent. It has several types, of which five are famous: mafhum al-sifah (attribute), mafhum al-shart (condition), mafhum al-ghayah (limit), mafhum al-adad (number), and mafhum al-ism (name). The commentator lists five additional types: mafhum al-lughah (linguistic), mafhum al-hasr (restriction), mafhum al-hal (state), mafhum al-zaman (time), and mafhum al-makan (place), but notes that these fall under mafhum al-sifah. An example of mafhum al-sifah is 'In pasturing animals there is zakat' — this implies no zakat on stall-fed animals. The Shafi'is generally accept this as a valid concept, but some Hanafis argue against it because the mention of 'sa'imah' might be merely by chance. The example from Quran 4:25 shows both mafhum al-muwafaqah (permission to marry believing slave girls) and mafhum al-mukhalafah (prohibition of marrying non-believing slave girls). The passage is dense but systematically clarifies Hanafi methodology on deriving meanings from texts.

Key Terms
مفهوم المخالفة (mafhum al-mukhalafah): The implicit meaning where the unmentioned case takes the opposite ruling of the mentioned case due to the absence of a specified restriction. Also called dalil al-khitab.
مفهوم الصفة (mafhum al-sifah): A type of mafhum al-mukhalafah where a ruling is restricted by an attribute (e.g., 'in pasturing animals zakat' implies no zakat on non-pasturing).
دليل الخطاب (dalil al-khitab): Another name for mafhum al-mukhalafah; indicates that the speech itself (by mentioning a restriction) implies the opposite ruling.

Mafhum Mukhalafa: The Five Types and Their Application
Defining Mafhum Mukhalafa and listing its five types with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the five types of Mafhum Mukhalafa and explain the Hanafi position regarding their validity as legal proofs.

Core Idea
Mafhum Mukhalafa: a restricted expression implies the opposite ruling when the restriction is absent.

This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti defines Mafhum Mukhalafa and enumerates its five main types: description, condition, end, number, and title. Each type is illustrated with Quranic or prophetic examples. The passage also notes that while Mafhum al-Muwafaqa (concept of agreement) is universally accepted, Mafhum Mukhalafa is subject to scholarly debate. Understanding these concepts is crucial for a mufti to derive correct rulings from textual evidence.

Guided Path
1
Define Mafhum Mukhalafa
Recognize the definition as the implication of the opposite ruling for what is unspoken (maskut 'anhu) when the spoken text (mantuq) contains a restricting qualifier.
The opposite ruling is the negation of the original ruling when the restriction is absent.
2
List the five types
Identify each type: sifah (description), shart (condition), ghayah (end/limit), 'adad (number), and laqab (title/noun).
Some scholars add five more types, but these are the famous ones.
3
Examine examples
For each type, study the given example (Quran/hadith) and understand how the opposite ruling is inferred.
Example for sifah: 'In grazing livestock is zakat' implies no zakat on stall-fed animals.
4
Note the internal dispute
Understand that the concept of title (laqab) is weaker and often rejected; the example of sifah is debated among Shafi'is like Taj al-Din al-Subki.
The dispute centers on whether the restricting description is intentional or incidental.
5
Apply the hierarchy
Recognize that the validity of each type varies; the Hanafi position generally accepts Mafhum Mukhalafa as proof with conditions.
The mufta bihi is to accept Mafhum Mukhalafa unless evidence shows the restriction is incidental.

This passage from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti defines and categorizes the legal concept of 'Mafhum Mukhalafa' (concept of opposition). The author begins by defining Mafhum Mukhalafa as the implication from a restricted statement that the opposite ruling applies when the restriction is absent. He then lists five well-known types: Mafhum al-Sifah (concept of description), Mafhum al-Shart (concept of condition), Mafhum al-Ghayah (concept of end), Mafhum al-‘Adad (concept of number), and Mafhum al-Laqab (concept of title). Each is illustrated with Quranic or Hadith examples. The passage notes that the total number of types reaches ten, but only five are famous. Importantly, the text states that the first division (Mafhum al-Muwafaqa, or concept of agreement) is unanimously accepted, while Mafhum al-Mukhalafa is subject to dispute among scholars. The commentary also mentions a Shafi'i view denying the concept for the example 'In grazing livestock zakat' due to linguistic issues. Overall, this passage provides the foundational classification of implied meanings (mafhum) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, essential for a mufti's understanding of how rulings are derived from texts.

Key Terms
مفهوم المخالفة (mafhum al-mukhalafa): The implication of a restricted expression that the opposite ruling applies when the restriction is absent.
مفهوم الصفة (mafhum al-sifah): The implication from a ruling qualified by a description that the ruling changes when the description is absent.
مفهوم الشرط (mafhum al-shart): The implication from a ruling conditioned on a condition that the ruling is opposite when the condition is absent.
مفهوم الغاية (mafhum al-ghayah): The implication from a ruling limited by an endpoint that after that endpoint the ruling is reversed.
مفهوم العدد (mafhum al-'adad): The implication from a ruling specified by a number that the ruling does not apply to other numbers.
مفهوم اللقب (mafhum al-laqab): The implication from a ruling attached to a proper noun or generic noun that it is confined to that noun.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the maxim 'Change of rulings with change of custom' (تغير الأحكام بتغير العرف) through multiple concrete examples of fatwa adjustments. The author, Ibn Abidin, lists fifteen instances where later Hanafi jurists departed from the rulings of Zahir al-Riwayah (the well-known transmitted corpus) due to changing social conditions or corruption of the times (فساد الزمان). Each example shows a shift from the original legal presumption (e.g., acceptance of a claim without proof, or the default rule that the denier's word is taken) to a new rule that better fits the reality of the era. Examples include: (1) prohibiting a husband from traveling with his wife even if dower is paid, because of moral decay; (2) not accepting a husband's claim of an exception in an oath of divorce without a witness, contrary to the original narration; (3) not believing a wife's claim of non-receipt of prompt dower after consummation, because custom shows she would not surrender herself without payment; (4) interpreting the ambiguous phrase 'every permissible upon forbidden' as divorce according to common usage, even though Imam Muhammad ruled it requires intention; (5) in disputes about a daughter's trousseau (جهاز), custom determines whether it is a gift or loan, overriding the default rule that the father as owner has the final say; (6) accepting the woman's word regarding the amount of deferred dower, despite the general rule that the denier's word prevails; (7) giving priority to the statements of both parties in sharecropping (مزارعة), mu'amala (a type of partnership), and waqf due to widespread need and necessity, overriding the original preference for the owner's word. The commentary shows that the Hanafi school did not rigidly adhere to texts, but allowed fatwas to evolve with time and custom under the guidance of qualified jurists.

Key Terms
عرف ('urf): Customary practice that is consistent, reasonable, and not contradicting a clear text.
فساد الزمان (fasad al-zaman): Corruption or moral decay of the times, justifying departure from original rulings to prevent harm.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The well-known transmitted rulings from Imam Muhammad's six books; most authoritative in the school.
القول للمنكر (al-qawl li al-munkir): Legal presumption that the denying party's statement is taken (with oath) in disputes.

Core Idea
Rulings can change when custom and time change, even overriding the most authoritative texts.

Understanding this principle is essential for a mufti to issue relevant and context-sensitive fatwas while remaining within the framework of the madhhab.

Commentary
This passage is a qawida (maxim) with its furuat (applications) under the principle 'Change of rulings by change of custom'. The author, Ibn Nujaym, lists several examples from Hanafi jurisprudence where the ruling has changed from the apparent transmitted ruling (zahir al-riwaya) due to a change in circumstances, either because of necessity (darura) or because of the prevailing custom (urf) and people's needs. The commentary from Radd al-Muhtar and other sources explains the reasoning. 

Example 16: Pre-emption (shuf'a) – According to Muhammad al-Shaybani, if the pre-emptor delays claiming his right for a month without excuse, his right is lost. This is to prevent harm to the buyer, and it departs from the general rule that pre-emption is not lost by delay. Some give fatwa by this due to change of time.

Example 17: A free, sane, adult woman marrying herself to a non-kuf (non-compatible) man. According to a narration from Hasan b. Ziyad from Abu Hanifa, it is not valid. But the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it is valid, and fatwa is given by their view due to need and by analogy to mudaraba. However, later scholars (mutakkhirin) chose the narration of Abu Hanifa due to corruption of the time (fasad al-zaman). This shows that the relied-upon fatwa can change again when circumstances change.

Example 18: Mud of the street – Fatwa was given that the mud of the street is pardoned (i.e., does not make clothing impure) even if mixed with impurities, due to necessity, because people cannot avoid it.

Example 19: Bay' al-wafa – A sale with the condition that when the seller returns the price, the buyer returns the item. It is treated as a pledge (rahn) in effect, though the form is sale. Fatwa is given for its validity due to necessity.

Example 21: Drinking from a water-skin – It is permitted to pay for drinking from a water-skin without specifying the amount, because of consensus and custom.

Example 22: Entering a bathhouse – Renting a bathhouse is valid without specifying the duration or amount of water used, because of the Prophet's action and custom.

Example 23: Borrowing bread or dough – According to Muhammad, it is permissible to borrow bread by weight and count, and fatwa is upon this due to people's need and custom.

The commentary from Radd al-Muhtar explicitly states: 'their fatwa is contrary to the apparent narration due to change of time, so the apparent narration is not given preference over it even though it is also authentic.' This highlights that the authority of the fatwa can override the textual authority of the narration when circumstances change.

Custom Contradicting Shariah is Invalid
Ruling on invalid custom with definitions and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sound and corrupt custom (al-'urf al-sahih and al-'urf al-fasid) and apply the rule that custom contradicting Shariah is invalid.

Core Idea
Custom that contradicts Shariah is invalid — only Sharia-compliant custom has legal weight.

Custom ('urf) is a recognized source of law in the Hanafi school, but it is not absolute. This lesson presents a foundational rule from rasm al-mufti: custom is invalid if it opposes explicit Shariah rulings. You will learn to classify customs as sound or corrupt and see real-world examples that a mufti must evaluate before issuing a fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the core legal maxim: لا يعمل بالعرف إذا خالف الشريعة (Custom is not acted upon if it contradicts the Shariah). This is an absolute principle governing the use of custom in legal reasoning.
2
Distinguish sound vs corrupt custom
Sound custom (al-'urf al-sahih) does not make halal what is haram or vice versa. Corrupt custom (al-'urf al-fasid) does. This classification is critical for a mufti.
3
Apply to examples
The passage gives examples: paying a deposit in istisna' is sound; usury and gambling are corrupt. Practice classifying other customs you encounter.
4
Recognize universal scope
This qawida applies across all fiqh chapters, especially in mu'amalat. A mufti must always check whether a custom violates nass before relying on it.

Legal Stratagems: Definition and Ruling
Definition and classification of hiyal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of al-hila, classify it by purpose into five rulings, and apply the Hanafi criterion to determine whether a given stratagem is permissible or prohibited.

Core Idea
A hila is not inherently good or bad – its ruling depends entirely on the intention and outcome.

Legal stratagems (hiyal) are often misunderstood as mere trickery. In fact, the Hanafi school provides a nuanced framework: a hila is a hidden means to an objective, and its ruling varies from wajib to haram based on what it seeks to achieve. In this lesson, you will unpack the definition, classification, and evidence for hiyal, and grasp the Hanafi criterion that separates legitimate cleverness from prohibited deception.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition
Understand the linguistic and technical meaning of al-hila: linguistic skill in management, technical: 'that by which one reaches an objective through a hidden means'.
The hiddenness is key – a hila is not direct but indirect.
2
Master the fivefold classification
The passage divides hiyal into five rulings based on the motive: establishing right/removing falsehood (wajib/mustahabb); nullifying right/validating falsehood (haram); avoiding disliked action (mustahabb/mubah); abandoning commendable (makruh).
Memorize this grid – it is the core of the lesson.
3
Examine the evidence
Identify the Quranic verses (4:98, 38:44, 65:4) and hadith (Bilal's camel, istithna') used to support permissible hiyal, and the proofs (Sabbath-breakers, najsh, tahlil) for prohibited ones.
Note how each proof is linked to the classification.
4
Apply the Hanafi criterion
Learn the criterion from al-Muhit: 'If it is for fleeing from haram and staying away from sin, it is good; if it is for nullifying a Muslim's right, it is sin.' The mufti must evaluate the intention.
This criterion is the fatwa touchstone.

الحيل جمع حيلة وهي لغة الحذق في تدبير الأمور، وهو تغليب الفكر حتى يهتدى إلى المقصود وشرعا: "ما يتوصل به إلى مقصود بطريق خفي" . وهي عند العلماء على أقسام بحسب الحامل عليها، فإن توصل بها بطريق مباح إلى إبطال حق أو إثبات باطل فهى حرام، أو إلى إثبات حق أو دفع باطل فهي واجبة أو مستحبة، وإن توصل بها بطريق مباح إلى سلامة من وقوع فى مكروه فهي مستحبة أو مباحة، أو إلى ترك مندوب فهى مكروهة . ومن أدلة مشروعية الحيل المباحة قوله سبحانه وتعالى : {إلا المستضعفين من الرجال والنساء والولدان لا يستطيعون حيلة ولا يهتدون سبيلا} (النساء : 98) وقوله تعالى : {وخذ بيدك ضغثًا فاضرب به ولا تحنث} (ص : 44) وقد عمل به النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم في حق الضعيف الذي زني، وهو من حديث أبي أمامة بن سهل في السنن، ومنه قوله تعالى: {ومن يتق الله يجعل له مخرجًا} (الطلاق : 4) وفي الحيل مخارج من المضايق، ومنه مشروعية الاستثناء، فإن فيه تخليصاً من الحنث، وكذلك الشروط كلها، فإن فيها سلامة من الوقوع في الحرج، ومنه حديث أبي هريرة وأبي سعيد في قصة بلال : "بع الجمل بالدراهم ثم ابتع بالدراهم جنيبا". و من أدلة تحريم حيل المحرمة قصة أصحاب السبت، وحديث حرمت عليهم الشحوم فجملوها فباعوها وأكلوا ثمنها، وحديث النهي عن النجش وحديث لعن الله المحلل والمحلل له . وقد اشتهر القول بالحيل عن الحنفية لكون أبي يوسف صنف فيها كتاباً، ولكن المعروف عنه وعن كثير من أئمتهم تقييد أعمالها بقصد الحق، قال صاحب المحيط : أصل الحيل قوله تعالى : {وخذ بيدك ضغثا} الآية، وضابطها: "إن كانت للفرار من الحرام والتباعد من الإثم فحسن، وإن كانت لإبطال حق مسلم فلا، بل هي إثم وعدوان". وقد يقال في بيان الضابط : "إن كان فيه خلاص مظلوم مثلا فهو مطلوب، وإن كان فيه قوات حق فهو مذموم" . كذا في الأشباه والنظائر : 294/3.
Al-hiyal is the plural of hila, which linguistically means skill in managing affairs, overwhelming thought until the objective is reached. Legally: "that by which one reaches an objective through a hidden means." According to scholars, it is divided into categories based on the motive: if one uses a permissible means to nullify a right or establish falsehood, it is haram; or to establish a right or remove falsehood, it is wajib or mustahabb; if one uses a permissible means to avoid falling into a disliked thing, it is mustahabb or mubah; or to leave a commendable act, it is makruh. Among the proofs for the legality of permissible hiyal are His statement, Glorified and Exalted: "Except the oppressed among men, women, and children who cannot devise a plan and are not guided to a way" (Quran 4:98), and His statement: "And take in your hand a bunch [of grass] and strike with it and do not break your oath" (Quran 38:44). The Prophet (peace be upon him) acted upon this in the case of the weak person who committed adultery, from the hadith of Abu Umamah ibn Sahl in the Sunan. And from it is His statement: "And whoever fears Allah, He will make for him a way out" (Quran 65:4). In hiyal there are ways out of difficulties. And from it is the legislation of istithna' (exceptional condition), for it contains deliverance from breaking an oath. Likewise all conditions, for they contain safety from falling into hardship. And from it is the hadith of Abu Hurairah and Abu Sa'id in the story of Bilal: "Sell the camel for dirhams, then buy with the dirhams a jenib camel." Among the proofs for the prohibition of prohibited hiyal are the story of the people of Sabbath, the hadith "Fats were forbidden to them, so they melted them, sold them, and consumed their price," the hadith forbidding najsh, and the hadith "Allah curses the muhallil and the one for whom it is made permissible." The statement on hiyal is well-known from the Hanafis because Abu Yusuf authored a book on them, but what is known from him and many of their imams is that their use is restricted by the intention of truth. The author of al-Muhit said: The origin of hiyal is the verse {And take in your hand a bunch}, and its criterion: "If it is for fleeing from haram and staying away from sin, it is good; and if it is for nullifying a Muslim's right, then no, rather it is sin and transgression." And it is said in explaining the criterion: "If it contains rescue of an oppressed person, for example, it is sought; and if it contains violation of a right, it is blameworthy." Thus in al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir 3/294.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive discussion of legal stratagems (hiyal) as a rasm rule in fatwa. It begins with a definition: hila is a hidden means to achieve an objective. The author then classifies hiyal into five rulings depending on the intention: if it establishes a right or removes falsehood, it is obligatory or recommended; if it nullifies a right or validates falsehood, it is haram; if it avoids a disliked action, it is recommended or permissible; if it leads to abandoning a recommended act, it is makruh. This classification is crucial because it shows that the means (hila) is judged by its end. The text then provides evidence from the Quran, Sunna, and sayings of the Companions for permissible hiyal: the verse about the oppressed, the story of Ayyub's oath, the hadith about the adulterer, and the commercial stratagem of Bilal. For prohibited hiyal, the examples include the Sabbath-breakers and the prohibition of najsh and tahlil. The passage then clarifies the famous Hanafi association with hiyal, noting that Abu Yusuf wrote a book on them, but the madhhab's position is conditional: hiyal are only permissible when intended to achieve right, not to wrong others. The criterion from al-Muhit is quoted: fleeing from haram is good; nullifying a Muslim's right is sin. This aligns with the maxim from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. This passage is a foundation for understanding that the Hanafi school does not permit trickery to evade obligations or harm others, but does permit cleverness to achieve legitimate objectives. The mufti must evaluate the intention behind any proposed hila before endorsing it.

Key Takeaway
A legal stratagem (hila) is judged by its motive: permissible when it achieves a legitimate objective without harming others, prohibited when it nullifies a right or validates falsehood.
