Correct Pronunciation of al-Siyar al-Kabir
Linguistic and terminological correction of a book title.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct pronunciation of al-Siyar al-Kabir and explain why it is pronounced as a plural.

Core Idea
Say 'al-Siyar al-Kabir' with a kasra on the sin, not a fatha — it is a plural, not a singular.

The title of Imam Muhammad's great work on siyar is often mispronounced. This lesson clarifies its linguistic basis and correct pronunciation, which is essential for accurate reference in fatwa and teaching. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion between the legal work and the general term for 'travel'.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Plural Form
Understand that al-siyar (السير) is the plural of sira (سيرة), meaning 'way' or 'path'. In Islamic law, it refers specifically to the Prophet's military expeditions.
This plural usage is emphasized in al-Hidayah.
2
Note the Adjective Agreement
The adjective 'al-kabir' is masculine singular because grammatically it modifies the omitted noun 'kitab' (book), not the plural 'siyar'.
This explains why the masculine adjective is used despite the noun being feminine plural.
3
Memorize the Correct Vowels
The correct pronunciation is with kasr on the sin (السِّير) and fatha on the ya, as a plural. The common error is to read it as al-sayr (السَّير) with fatha on sin and sukun on ya, which is incorrect.
This rule is taken from al-Mughrib and confirmed by later commentators.

This passage is a terminological clarification (rasm al-mufti) regarding the correct pronunciation and meaning of the book title al-Siyar al-Kabir. The author first gives the linguistic definition: السير (al-siyar) is the plural of سيرة (sira), meaning a path or way. Key Reference Works in Hanafi Usul and Iftaa
Listing of key books referenced in the commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key works cited in Sharh al-Uqud and explain their relevance to Hanafi usul al-fiqh and iftaa.

Core Idea
These works form the backbone of Hanafi usul and iftaa — knowing them is essential for a mufti.

This passage from Sharh al-Uqud lists several important reference works that the commentator relied upon. Understanding these texts helps you see the scholarly network behind Hanafi fatwa methodology. We will examine each work and its role.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Tahrir and its commentaries
Find al-Tahrir in the text. Note that it is a book on usul al-fiqh comparing Shafi'i and Hanafi schools. It has two commentaries: al-Taqrir wa al-Tahbir (3 vols) and Taysir al-Tahrir (4 vols). The commentator quotes from al-Taqrir wa al-Tahbir often.
Al-Tahrir is a key usul text for Hanafi-Shafi'i comparative study.
2
Identify al-Musayarah and its commentary
Find al-Musayarah fi al-Aqa'id. This is a text on kalam (theology) arranged according to al-Ghazali's al-Risalah al-Qudsiyyah. Its commentary is called al-Musamarah by a Shafi'i scholar.
Though the commentator is Shafi'i, the work is used for theological foundations.
3
Identify Zad al-Faqir
Find Zad al-Faqir, a treatise on fiqh issues collected during travels.
This is a practical fiqh work.
4
Reflect on the combination
Consider why a book on usul, a book on kalam, and a book on fiqh are all cited together. They cover different aspects needed for a mufti: methodological principles (usul), sound creed (kalam), and positive law (fiqh).
A mufti must be well-versed in all three.

When 'al-Kamal' Means Ibn al-Humam
Clarification of a common abbreviation in Hanafi texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the term 'al-Kamal' refers to Ibn al-Humam in Hanafi texts and cite the authority for this usage.

Core Idea
In Hanafi works, 'al-Kamal' always means Ibn al-Humam.

Hanafi literature is rich with abbreviations and honorifics. One of the most common is 'al-Kamal', which is a shorthand for the great jurist Kamal al-Din ibn al-Humam. This brief note, citing Ibn Abidin, firmly establishes that identification. Memorize it well, because misreading 'al-Kamal' can lead to confusion about which authority is being invoked.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the abbreviation
When you see the Arabic term 'الكمال' (al-Kamal) standing alone in a Hanafi text, do not assume it refers to any scholar named 'Kamal'. Recognize it as a technical abbreviation.
It is often used in phrases like 'قال الكمال' (al-Kamal said).
2
Recall the referent
The intended scholar is Kamal al-Din Muhammad ibn al-Humam (d. 861 AH), a leading Hanafi jurist and author of Fath al-Qadir.
Ibn al-Humam is also known as 'Ibn al-Humam al-Hanafi'.
3
Verify the authority
This usage is confirmed by Ibn Abidin in Radd al-Muhtar, the primary reference for later Hanafi fatwa. Reliance on this authority ensures correctness.
Ibn Abidin explicitly states: 'When al-Kamal is used absolutely, it refers to Ibn al-Humam.'
4
Apply consistently
In all subsequent readings, substitute 'Ibn al-Humam' whenever 'al-Kamal' appears. This will give you accurate access to his positions.
This rule applies across all Hanafi texts, not just fatwa collections.

Core Idea
The list of referenced works in a commentary reveals the scholarly lineage and weight of the rulings cited.

This lesson examines a bibliography from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. Understanding the hierarchy of Hanafi sources is crucial for a mufti: not all texts carry equal authority. This list includes foundational works like Al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Radd al-Muhtar, along with lesser-known commentaries and fatwa collections. By learning to navigate such lists, you gain the ability to trace the provenance of rulings and judge their reliability.

1
Identify the nature of the list

Commentary
This passage from Sharh Uqood Rasm al-Mufti serves as a bibliographic appendix, listing and describing the key reference works that a Hanafi mufti must be familiar with. It begins by noting that the biography of the author (likely the compiler of the matn) is found in al-Tamimi's work and in al-A'lam. Then it enumerates five major biographical dictionaries of Hanafi scholars: al-Jawahir al-Mudi'a by al-Qurashi (the first such work), Taj al-Tarajim by Ibn Qutlubugha, Wafayat al-A'yan by al-Tarsusi, Kata'ib A'lam al-Akhyar by al-Kafawi (still in manuscript), and al-Fawa'id al-Bahiyya by al-Laknawi (a printed summary of the previous). Next, it discusses al-Tiraz al-Mudhahhab, clarifying that despite multiple books with that name, the one intended in the text is by al-Halwaji al-Shafi'i (not Hanafi), which is notable for its expertise in fara'id. Then comes al-Zahiriyya (al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyya) by Abu Bakr Zahir al-Din al-Bukhari, a major Hanafi fatwa collection. The passage also mentions a dispute about its authorship, with al-Qari attributing it to 'Ali al-Marghinani, but this is refuted. Next is al-'Iqd al-Farid by al-Shurunbulali on the permissibility of taqlid. Finally, a detailed biography of al-Babarti, author of al-'Inaya (a famous commentary on al-Hidayah), is given, including his teachers, students, works, and a note on the disagreement about his father's and grandfather's names. The passage emphasizes the importance of these sources for Hanafi fiqh and fatwa.

Essential Reference Works for the Hanafi Mufti
Lists four Hanafi reference works and provides biography of al-Babarti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the content and significance of four key Hanafi reference works and identify the authorship dispute regarding al-Zahiriyya.

Core Idea
A mufti's library must include reliable fatwa collections, a commentary on al-Hidayah, and a treatise on taqlid.

The second part of this passage introduces four specific works that complement the biographical dictionaries: a Shafi'i work on fara'id (al-Tiraz al-Mudhahhab), a major Hanafi fatwa collection (al-Zahiriyya), a treatise on the permissibility of taqlid (al-'Iqd al-Farid), and a famous commentary on al-Hidayah (al-'Inaya). The detailed biography of al-Babarti highlights the scholarly credentials behind al-'Inaya. Together, these works form a crucial part of a mufti's reference library.

Identify the four works and their authors.
List each work with its author as given in the passage.

For al-Tiraz: fara'id; for al-Zahiriyya: fatwa collection; for al-'Iqd: taqlid; for al-'Inaya: commentary on al-Hidayah.
Each serves a different function in a mufti's library.

Analyze the authorship dispute of al-Zahiriyya.

His work al-'Inaya is praised for including major proofs.

For example, using al-'Inaya to find proofs, al-Zahiriyya for fatwa precedent.
