Renewal of Ijtihad for the Mufti and Judge
Ruling on renewing ijtihad for mufti/qadi with two views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two views on renewing ijtihad and apply the preferred Hanafi position (tajdid al-nazar) to a mufti's practice.

Core Idea
When the same legal issue recurs, a mufti must renew his ijtihad — his prior ruling is not automatically binding.

A mufti often encounters the same legal question repeatedly. This passage addresses whether he can rely on his previous ijtihad or must start afresh. The ruling hinges on the possibility of change in reasoning or new evidence. The preferred view requires renewal, reflecting the dynamic nature of ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The passage asks: when a mufti has already issued a fatwa based on ijtihad and the same case arises again, must he repeat his ijtihad or may he rely on his earlier ruling?
2
State the two opposing views
The first view (قيل) permits reliance on the previous ijtihad due to ease and continuity. The second (الأصح) obligates renewal of ijtihad because his opinion may change or new information may surface.
The latter is the more correct (الأصح) position.
3
Examine the proofs
The first view argues burden of re-examination and presumption of stability. The second counters that ijtihad is not static; new knowledge or opposing evidence can emerge, making renewal necessary for accuracy.
The passage quotes al-Qadi Abu Tayyib to elaborate on when renewal is needed.
4
Apply the rule to analogous cases
The same principle applies to a judge (qadi) who has ruled by ijtihad and the case returns. It also extends to finding water for tayammum and determining qiblah by ijtihad — both analogized to the mufti's situation.
For tayammum and qiblah there are two views, but the preferred view mandates renewal.
5
Identify the fatwa reliance (mufta bihi)
The text clearly states that the more correct view (الأصح) is the obligation to renew ijtihad. This is the operative position for fatwa, supported by later Hanafi authorities.
Thus, a mufti must re-examine the issue each time it arises.

Rule 112: Presuming Absence of Inheritance Impediments
A rasm al-mufti rule on implicit conditions in inheritance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a mufti assumes the absence of slavery, disbelief, murder, and other impediments when answering inheritance queries.

Core Idea
The mufti presumes no inheritance impediments unless the query indicates otherwise.

In Hanafi fatwa practice, when asked about inheritance, the mufti does not need to explicitly list all possible impediments. Instead, the default assumption is that they are absent. This rule streamlines fatwa writing and avoids unnecessary repetition, as established by al-Saymari and Abu 'Amr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the inheritance query
Read the question to determine which heirs are involved and whether any impediment is mentioned.
Impediments include slavery, difference of religion, and homicide.
2
Recognize the default assumption
Understand that the mufti should not add conditions like 'provided they are not slaves' unless the query raises doubt.
The absolute statement is carried upon the absence of impediments.
3
Issue the fatwa
State the shares based on the apparent absence of impediments, without listing them.
If evidence suggests an impediment exists, the mufti must address it.

Rule 113: Specifying Lineage for Relatives in Inheritance
A rasm al-mufti rule on specifying lineage in inheritance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a mufti must specify whether relatives are full, consanguine, or uterine when stating inheritance shares.

Core Idea
When mentioning brothers or uncles, specification of lineage is required because shares differ.

Unlike the previous rule on impediments, when the query uses terms like 'brothers' or 'uncles' without qualification, the mufti must specify their exact lineage connection. This ensures the correct allocation of shares, as shares vary dramatically based on whether siblings are full, consanguine, or uterine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the relative term used in the query
Look for words like 'brothers', 'sisters', 'paternal uncles', or 'their sons'.
These are ambiguous without lineage specification.
2
Recognize the need for specification
Understand that the absolute term is insufficient because inheritance shares differ by type: full, consanguine, or uterine.
Full siblings inherit as asabah; consanguine only if no full; uterine get fixed shares.
3
State the lineage in the fatwa
Include the phrase 'from father and mother' (full), 'from father' (consanguine), or 'from mother' (uterine) in the answer.
The mufti may need to ask for clarification if the query does not specify.

Fatwa Phrasing for 'Awl: The Minbariyya Case
Rasm rule on phrasing for the Minbariyya 'awl case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the correct fatwa phrasing for the Minbariyya inheritance case, applying the Hanafi rasm rule on 'awl wording.

Core Idea
When 'awl reduces shares, the mufti must not say the reduced fraction directly but express it as 'a'ila or quote 'Ali's formula.

In inheritance cases involving 'awl, the total prescribed shares exceed the estate, causing proportional reduction. The Minbariyya case is a classic example. This lesson teaches the precise phrasing a Hanafi mufti must use to describe the wife's share, avoiding incorrect and unprecedented expressions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Minbariyya case
Recognize the heir set: wife, both parents, and two daughters. This is the classic case where 'awl applies.
The base shares sum to more than 1, requiring proportional reduction.
2
Apply 'awl arithmetic
Calculate the total shares: wife 1/8, parents 1/3 each (but reduced to 1/6 each due to daughers), two daughters 2/3. Sum to 1/8 + 1/6 + 1/6 + 2/3 = 27/24, so 'awl increases denominator to 27.
Wife's share becomes 3/27, which is approximately 1/9.
3
State the wife's share correctly
Do not say 'one-ninth' directly. Instead say 'one-eighth as an 'a'ila' (ثمن عائلا) or state the exact shares (3 of 27), or quote 'Ali's saying that her eighth became a ninth.
This phrasing indicates reduction and follows the Salaf's usage.
4
Cite the authority
Base your phrasing on the practice of the Salaf and the explicit statement of 'Ali ibn Abi Talib, which is the relied-upon Hanafi position.
This ensures the fatwa is grounded in transmitted precedent.

Precise Phrasing in Fatwa Answers: Munasakhat and Bequests
Rules for fatwa phrasing on successive deaths and priority of debts/bequests.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a precise fatwa answer for munasakhat cases and correctly qualify the order of debts and bequests to prevent misunderstanding.

Core Idea
Precision in inheritance fatwa wording prevents legal error and misinterpretation by laymen.

The mufti's answer must be clear and legally accurate. This lesson teaches the exact wording to use in cases of successive deaths (munasakhat) and how to mention debts and bequests without causing misunderstanding.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the case is a munasakhah
If multiple deaths occur before the estate is distributed, treat each transmission separately. Identify the order of deaths.
Munasakhat are common when heirs die before receiving their shares.
2
Specify the source of each share
For each heir, state the inheritance from each deceased person, e.g., 'from father, then from mother, then from brother'.
This prevents confusion about which estate generated which portion.
3
Choose an acceptable phrasing
Use either the concise formula or the detailed one with specific shares, as both are acceptable per al-Saymari.
The detailed phrasing is more precise if the numeric shares are known.
4
Mention the priority of debts and bequests
Always state that the estate is divided after removing debts, then bequests are subject to the one-third limit.
This clarifies the order of distribution to the layman.
5
Qualify the bequest
Do not say 'after removing the bequest' unconditionally; explain that bequests are limited to one-third and cannot go to heirs without consent.
The footnote from al-Adhru'i emphasizes this caution to avoid misuse.

The Seventeenth Rule: When to Cite Evidence in a Fatwa
Ruling on when to cite evidence in a fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule for citing evidence in a fatwa by distinguishing between a lay seeker and a jurist, and identify the exceptions for qadi-related fatwas, correcting mistakes, and ambiguous cases.

Core Idea
A mufti cites evidence only to qualified jurists; laypeople receive the ruling without proof, unless special circumstances demand it.

The mufti's role is to convey the ruling, not to teach. Yet, there are times when citing evidence is not only permissible but commendable. This lesson from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa clarifies the conditions under which a mufti may mention textual evidence, the method of ijtihad, or emphatic statements like consensus. It balances the need for clarity with the risk of turning fatwa into a teaching session.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline rule
Recognize the primary distinction: for a layman ('ammi), the mufti does not cite evidence; for a jurist (faqih), he may cite clear, concise textual proof.
This prevents confusion for the layperson and respects the knowledge of the jurist.
2
Understand the rationale
The goal is to provide the ruling effectively without overburdening the asker. Ibn Qayyim supports citing evidence when possible, but only if the evidence is clear and the recipient can appreciate it.
3
Note the exceptions
When a fatwa relates to a qadi's judgment, when correcting a mistaken fatwa, or when the matter is ambiguous, the mufti may allude to the evidence or the path of ijtihad.
These exceptions allow the mufti to support authority and clarify complex cases.
4
Recognize the limits and emphatic language
Avoid excessive evidence as it turns fatwa into teaching. However, in certain situations (e.g., public interest), the mufti may use strong language like 'this is consensus' or 'whoever opposes has sinned'.
This balances between providing necessary emphasis and maintaining the fatwa's distinct nature.
5
Resolve the scholarly disagreement
Al-Hawi's absolute prohibition is outweighed by the detailed view allowing evidence for jurists and specific cases. The later Hanafi tradition adopts this detailed approach.
Adab al-fatwa requires contextual judgment, not a blanket rule.

This precaution protects the public from confusion and innovation.

The rule prevents laypeople from engaging in speculative theology that may lead to confusion or deviation. Hanafis follow the Salaf in consigning the meaning of ambiguous attributes to Allah (tafwid).

Commentary
This passage is a rasm rule defining two key concepts: the mustafti (seeker of fatwa) and taqlid (following a qualified scholar). The author begins by stating that anyone who has not reached the level of a mujtahid mufti is a mustafti and a muqallid of his mufti. The passage underscores the hierarchy of knowledge and the obligation of the laity to seek fatwas from qualified scholars.

Consulting Among Multiple Qualified Muftis
Ruling on whether a layperson must seek the most knowledgeable among multiple qualified muftis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi position that a layperson may consult any qualified mufti without obligation to seek the most learned.

Core Idea
A layperson may consult any qualified mufti; searching for the most learned is not obligatory.

When multiple qualified muftis are available, a layperson may wonder whether he must seek the most knowledgeable among them. This passage from Rasm al-Mufti addresses exactly that question, presenting two views and settling on the Hanafi relied-upon position. Understanding this rule is essential for both muftis and laypersons to avoid unnecessary burden in seeking fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the passage deals with the case of two or more qualified muftis (ahl al-fatwa) being present. The term 'qualified' here means they are competent to issue fatwas (either mujtahids or muqallids with ability to tarjih).
The footnote clarifies that if both muqallids in the same madhhab and there is no ikhtilaf, there is no issue.
2
State the two views
The passage presents two views: (1) It is not obligatory to seek the most learned; the layperson may consult any of them. (2) He must strive to find the most learned, most pious, and most reliable to follow exclusively.
The first view is attributed to the Iraqi Hanafis and most of our scholars; the second is implied as a possibility.
3
Analyze the Hanafi tarjih
The correct view for fatwa is the first: no obligation to seek the most learned. The reasoning: all are qualified, and the layperson is not required to perform ijtihad in selection. Ibn al-Humam and al-Amidi support this, and it is the relied-upon position.
Ibn al-Humam even says the layperson may follow the easier ruling; following concessions is not prohibited.
4
Apply the ruling
In practice, a layperson may ask any qualified mufti and act on his answer, even if another mufti is more learned. He does not need to compare their knowledge or piety.
This prevents hardship and respects the principle that the layperson's duty is simply to follow a mujtahid.

Must a Layman Seek the Most Learned Mufti?
Dispute on obligation of layman to seek best mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the preferred Hanafi opinion on whether a layperson must investigate to find the most knowledgeable mufti, and apply the rule when multiple muftis are present.

Core Idea
A layman may follow any qualified mufti without searching for the best, but if he knows of a better one, he must follow him.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: when multiple muftis are available, does a layperson have to seek the most learned? Imam al-Nawawi presents two views, and the Hanafi position is clear. We will examine the evidence and the relied-upon ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the question
The subject is the layperson (al-'aami) who does not have ijtihad. The question: when there are two or more qualified muftis, is he obligated to search for the most knowledgeable and pious among them?
2
State the two opinions
First opinion: no obligation, he may ask any. Second opinion: obligation, because he can perform limited investigation. Identify which is supported by the Iraqi Hanafis and majority.
3
Examine the evidence for the preferred view
The practice of the Sahaba (early generations) did not require such investigation; their silence constitutes an implicit consensus (ijma) that it is not obligatory. This is the key proof.
See the marginal quote from al-Amidi in the source.
4
Understand the exception
If the layman becomes aware of a more trustworthy mufti (mutali'a), then he must follow that one, analogous to preferring the stronger proof in ijtihad.
5
Apply the hierarchy between knowledge and piety
If one mufti is more knowledgeable and another more pious, the layman follows the more knowledgeable. This is the most correct view according to the passage.

Listing and analyzing five opinions for a layperson facing conflicting fatwas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five scholarly opinions on handling conflicting fatwas and apply the Hanafi preferred view (mufta bihi) to guide a layperson.

Core Idea
The truth is not always in the easier option; seek the most knowledgeable and pious mufti.

When a layperson receives conflicting fatwas, which one should they follow? Scholars have proposed five approaches, from choosing the easier ruling to free choice. This lesson presents these opinions and explains the Hanafi relied-upon view: prioritize the mufti who is both more knowledgeable in fiqh and more pious.

Commentary
This passage discusses the procedure for a layperson or a mufti when faced with conflicting fatwas. The text enumerates five opinions among scholars on how to handle such a situation. The first opinion is to strive and select the fatwa of the more knowledgeable and pious mufti. The second opinion is to pick the easier ruling. The third, attributed to al-Sam'ani al-Kabir and also held by Imam al-Shafi'i in the context of qibla, is to make an effort and choose the most knowledgeable and pious. The fourth is to ask another mufti and follow whoever agrees with your initial inclination. The fifth is to simply choose whichever you prefer, which is considered the most correct by al-Shirazi and al-Khatib al-Baghdadi. The Hanafi preferred view, as stated in al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Radd al-Muhtar, is that when two muftis differ, one should take the fatwa of the more fiqh-knowledgeable (afqah) provided they are also more pious (awra'). This applies when both are mujtahids. For muqallids, if the books have not clearly preferred one view, then one must follow what the majority of scholars have favored, or the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya), or the Imam's statement. The passage then presents Shaykh Abu Amr's view, which prioritizes searching for the most reliable mufti and acting on his fatwa; if still unclear, ask another and follow the one who agrees; if that fails and the issue is about halal/haram, choose the stricter (haram) for caution; if equal in all respects, the layperson may choose. However, the text criticizes this as not strong, and suggests that the most apparent view is the fifth (free choice) for the layperson, as he is not a mujtahid and simply needs to follow a qualified scholar. The distinction with the qibla case is mentioned but not fully detailed here.

The Layperson's Duty When Muftis Disagree: Seek the Most Knowledgeable and Pious
Exposition of rulings for the layperson facing conflicting fatwas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for a layperson facing conflicting fatwas and apply it by identifying the mufti who is most knowledgeable and pious.

Core Idea
When muftis differ, the layperson must investigate and follow the most knowledgeable and pious among them.

This passage from Imam al-Nawawi’s *Adab al-Fatwa* presents a crucial rule for the mustafti (fatwa-seeker) when confronted with contradictory fatwas. While the Shafi'i school has debated several views, the Hanafi position, endorsed by later authorities, is clear: the layperson is not free to choose arbitrarily but must investigate the qualifications of the muftis and follow the most reliable. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
What is the scenario? A layperson (mustafti) receives conflicting fatwas from multiple muftis. What should he do? This is the central problem addressed in the passage.
The layperson is not a mujtahid and must follow taqlid.
2
State the Hanafi position
The layperson must investigate the most knowledgeable (a'lam) and most pious (awr') mufti and follow his fatwa. This is derived from the passage's concluding endorsement: 'فيجب البحث عن الأعلم الأورع والأخذ بقوله'.
This applies when muftis are not equal in these qualities.
3
Understand the basis (dalil)
The rationale: the indicator (amarah) of correctness in fatwa is accessible through external inquiry—reputation, hearsay, and people's trust. This is similar to sensory indicators for qibla direction. The layperson can do this without ijtihad, so it becomes obligatory (wajib).
The Hanafi response to the Shafi'i objection that the indicator is purely rational.
4
Identify the exception
If the two muftis are completely equal in knowledge and piety, the layperson may choose (takhyir) between their fatwas. This is a rare case of necessity.
The text says: 'وإن تساويا من كل وجه خيرناه بينهما'.
5
Contrast with opposing views
Some Shafi'is hold that the layperson may choose any qualified mufti because the indicator is rational and hard to evaluate. The Hanafi response maintains that investigation is possible and obligatory, as outlined.
The thamarah al-khilaf: in fatwa practice, Hanafis require investigation; Shafi'is may allow free choice.

This passage from Imam al-Nawawi's 'Adab al-Fatwa' addresses the critical issue of how a layperson (mustafti) should act when faced with conflicting fatwas from multiple muftis. The text presents several opinions within the Shafi'i school and then concludes with the Hanafi position. Core Idea
A fatwa is binding only when no other mufti exists or when the mufti is A'lam Awthaq.

This lesson examines the binding nature of a mufti's fatwa on the mustafti. While a fatwa is generally non-binding, the text presents specific circumstances that create an obligation to follow it. Understanding these conditions is crucial for both muftis and mustaftis in the Hanafi tradition.

This is the relied-upon Hanafi position (mufta bihi).

When to Repeat a Fatwa Question: The Ruling on Tajdid al-Su'al
Determining when a fatwa must be re-sought for the same recurring issue

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle 'al-asl istimrar al-mufti alayhi' to determine whether a mustafti must renew his inquiry when the same issue recurs, and identify the exceptions.

Core Idea
The sounder view: a mustafti need not repeat the question unless the matter is rare and asking again causes hardship.

This passage from the Adab al-Fatwa tradition addresses a practical dilemma: if a person already received a fatwa on a matter, and later the exact same situation arises, must he ask again? The text presents two views, weighs them, and introduces the key maxim 'continuity of the mufti's ruling'. As future muftis, you need to understand when the first fatwa suffices and when renewal is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two views
The mustafti received a fatwa, same event occurs again: must he ask again? First view: yes, because mufti's opinion may have changed. Second view (sounder): no, because he already knows the ruling.
2
Examine the exception for frequently occurring matters
Qadi Abu Tayyib adds: if the matter is frequent and asking again is burdensome, the first fatwa suffices. This is an exception to the second view.
3
Apply the principle 'al-asl istimrar al-mufti alayhi'
The default is continuity; the mufti's view is presumed unchanged unless evidence to the contrary. This supports the second view.
4
Analyze the refinement by al-Shamil
Al-Shamil restricts the disagreement to a living mufti; for a fatwa reported from a dead scholar, it is definitive that no repetition is required because the dead scholar's opinion does not change.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The correct view (as per Adhra'i's margin) is that the restriction is not absolute: even a muqallid of a dead scholar might reinterpret the madhhab. However, this is questioned. The relied-upon ruling: for a report from a dead scholar, no repetition; for a living mufti, the sounder view with the exception for frequent matters.

Commentary

Seeking Fatwa in Person or by Proxy
Ruling on mustafti seeking fatwa personally or by proxy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the valid methods for a mustafti to obtain a fatwa and apply the conditions for proxy and written fatwas.

Core Idea
A fatwa may be sought in person or through a trustworthy proxy, and written fatwas are valid if handwriting is verified.

This lesson examines the foundational rule for how a mustafti (seeker of a fatwa) may obtain the mufti's response. The text presents two permissible modes: direct personal inquiry and indirect inquiry via a reliable representative. It also addresses the important practical scenario of written fatwas—when can the mustafti rely on the mufti's handwriting? These rules ensure the integrity of the fatwa while accommodating practical needs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two options
The text states the mustafti may seek the fatwa himself or send a trustworthy person (thiqa) whose report he relies on. These are two distinct but equally valid methods.
The proxy must be reliable; the mustafti must trust the representative's report.
2
Examine the condition for written fatwas
The mustafti may rely on the mufti's handwriting if (a) a trustworthy person informs him that the writing is indeed the mufti's, or (b) he personally recognizes the handwriting without any doubt.
Doubt negates reliability; certainty (or reliable confirmation) is required.
3
Synthesize the legal principle
The underlying principle is that the fatwa is a legal answer; its transmission does not require direct oral communication if authenticity is assured through trust or personal knowledge.
This facilitates fatwa practice in distant or written correspondence.

The seventh: 149 - That he [the mustafti] seeks a fatwa in person, and he may send a trustworthy person whose report he relies on to seek a fatwa for him, and he may rely on the mufti's handwriting if a person whose word he trusts informs him that it is his handwriting, or if he knows his handwriting and has no doubt that that answer is in his handwriting.
Source pages p. 424

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the method of seeking a fatwa. The mustafti has two options: he can ask in person, or he can send a trustworthy representative. Additionally, when receiving a written fatwa, he can rely on it if a trustworthy person confirms it is the mufti's handwriting, or if he recognizes the handwriting himself without doubt. This establishes the permissibility of indirect fatwa-seeking and reliance on written fatwas under conditions of trust.

Key Terms
ثقة (thiqa): A trustworthy person sent to seek a fatwa on behalf of the mustafti.
اعتماد على خط المفتى (i‘timād ‘alā khaṭṭ al-muftī): Relying on the mufti's handwriting as authentic for the fatwa.

Continuity of the Fatwa: The Mustafti's Reliance on a Received Ruling
Principle and critical refinement of a fatwa continuity rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of continuity (استمرار المفتى عليه) to determine whether a mustafti must re-ask for a fatwa, and to evaluate the critique against the distinction between a living and a deceased mufti.

Core Idea
A mustafti may continue acting on a fatwa indefinitely; the mufti's possible change does not obligate re-asking.

When a mustafti receives a fatwa and acts upon it, a crucial question arises: must he stop and re-ask if he learns the mufti's opinion may have changed? This lesson examines the Hanafi-backed principle of continuity, which spares the mustafti from perpetual inquiry, and refines a misguided distinction that would treat fatwas from deceased scholars differently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Locate the statement 'الأصل استمرار المفتى عليه' — the default ruling is continuity. Understand that this is the foundation: the mustafti may rely on the fatwa he received without needing to track potential changes in the mufti's view.
This principle is rooted in istishab (presumption of continuity).
2
Recognize the Opposing Distinction
Find the specification made by 'صاحب الشامل': he restricted the disagreement to cases where the mustafti imitated a living mufti. If the fatwa came from a deceased mufti, he ruled decisively that the mustafti is not bound to change.
The opposing view treats a living mufti's opinion as potentially mutable, a deceased mufti's as fixed.
3
Analyze the Refinement
Examine the author's response: the correct (sahih) view rejects the distinction. Even the mufti on the madhhab of a dead scholar may change his answer as his research deepens. Thus, the dead/living distinction fails.
This shows that scholarly authority is ongoing; no fatwa is immune to revision by a later qualified scholar.
4
Apply the Rule
Now apply the rule: a mustafti who received a fatwa from Mufti X (whether dead or alive) may continue acting on it. He need not re-ask unless he obtains clear evidence that X retracted the fatwa.
The burden of proof is on the mustafti's opponent to show the fatwa has changed.

And the author of al-Shamil specified the disagreement to when he imitated a living [scholar], and he decisively [ruled] that if it was a report from a dead [scholar], he is not bound. And the correct [view] is that it is not specific; for the mufti according to the madhhab of the dead may change his answer according to his madhhab.
Source pages p. 424

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa. The principle is that the mustafti (seeker of a fatwa) may continue acting on a fatwa he received, even if the mufti's opinion might have changed over time, because the default assumption is continuity. The author of al-Shamil (a commentary) limited this disagreement: if the mustafti imitated a living mufti and the mufti changes his view, the mustafti may need to re-ask; but if the fatwa was from a deceased mufti, the mustafti is not required to do anything (since the dead mufti's opinion is fixed). However, the correct (sahih) view according to the author is that this distinction is not valid; even the mufti on the madhhab of a dead scholar might change his answer according to that madhhab (e.g., if he discovers a stronger transmission). This passage guards against the misunderstanding that a fatwa from a deceased mufti is immune to revision by a living mufti following that madhhab.

Key Terms
مفتى عليه (muftā ʿalayhi): The legal ruling received from a mufti upon which the mustafti is acting
استمرار (istimrār): Continuity; the default ruling that a prior state persists
مستفتي (mustaftī): The person seeking a fatwa
