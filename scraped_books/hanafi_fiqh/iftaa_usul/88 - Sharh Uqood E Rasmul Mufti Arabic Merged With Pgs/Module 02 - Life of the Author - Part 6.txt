Biography of al-Muzani and His Role in Comparative Fiqh
Biographical entry of a leading Shafi'i scholar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key biographical facts about al-Muzani and explain why his works are important for Hanafi-Shafi'i comparative fiqh.

Core Idea
Al-Muzani's Mukhtasar is the standard Shafi'i reference, yet he wrote independently of al-Shafi'i.

The Hanafi mufti must be able to identify the major sources of other schools. This biography introduces Imam al-Muzani, whose Mukhtasar became the most widely used Shafi'i manual. Understanding his stature and his works equips you to navigate ikhtilaf discussions with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the figure
Read the first line: full name, nisba, and region. Note that al-Muzani was Egyptian and from the tribe Muzaynah.
The nisba 'al-Muzani' is a tribal attribution.
2
Note the description of character
Look at the adjectives: imam, pious, ascetic, supplication answered, detached from the world. Also al-Shafi'i's praise: 'If he debated Satan, he would overcome him.'
This shows he was both spiritually and intellectually distinguished.
3
List his major works
Identify the books mentioned: al-Mabsut, al-Mukhtasar, al-Manshur, al-Masa'il al-Mu'tabarah, al-Targhib fi al-'Ilm, Kitab al-Daqa'iq wa al-'Aqarib, and a separate book on his own madhhab.
Al-Mukhtasar is the most significant for comparative fiqh.
4
Understand his independence
Note that he compiled a book on his own madhhab, not strictly following al-Shafi'i. This shows ijtihad within the Shafi'i school.
This independence makes his Mukhtasar a reliable statement of the Shafi'i school as understood by a leading student.
5
Memorize key dates
He was born in 175 AH and died in 264 AH. He was buried near al-Shafi'i in al-Qarafah, Cairo.
These dates place him in the early third century, a formative period for madhhab development.

٤٦ - المزنى : أبو إبراهيم إسماعيل بن يحيى بن إسماعيل المزني، المصرى، كان إماماً ورعاً، زاهدا، مجاب الدعوات، منقلا من الدنيا، وكان معظماً بين أصحاب الشافعي، وقال الشافعى فى حقه : لو ناظر الشيطان لغلبه صنف رحمه الله كتبا ، منها : المبسوط و المختصر و المنشور و المسائل المعتبرة و الترغيب في العلم و كتاب الدقائق والعقارب سمى بذلك لصعوبته، وصنف كتاباً مفرداً على مذهبه لا على مذهب الشافعى. ولد سنة خمس وسبعين ومائة، وتوفى لست بقين من شهر رمضان سنة أربع وستين ومائتين، ودفن بالقرافة بالقرب من قبر الإمام الشافعي . والمزنى منسوب إلى مزينة وهى قبيلة معروفة. 46. Al-Muzani: Abu Ibrahim Isma‘il ibn Yahya ibn Isma‘il al-Muzani, al-Misri. He was an imam, pious, ascetic, whose supplications were answered, detached from the world, and he was venerated among the companions of al-Shafi‘i. Al-Shafi‘i said about him: 'If he were to debate Satan, he would overcome him.' He authored, may Allah have mercy on him, books, including: al-Mabsut, al-Mukhtasar, al-Manshur, al-Masa’il al-Mu‘tabarah, al-Targhib fi al-‘Ilm, and Kitab al-Daqa’iq wa al-‘Aqarib, which was so named due to its difficulty. And he authored a separate book according to his own madhhab, not according to the madhhab of al-Shafi‘i. He was born in the year 175 AH, and died when six nights remained of Ramadan in the year 264 AH. He was buried in al-Qarafah near the grave of Imam al-Shafi‘i. Al-Muzani is attributed to Muzaynah, which is a well-known tribe. (Tabaqat al-Shafi‘iyyah by al-Isnawi: 1/34-36)

Commentary
This biography covers Imam al-Muzani, one of the foremost students of Imam al-Shafi‘i. The text emphasizes his piety (war‘), asceticism (zuhd), and the efficacy of his supplications (mujab al-da‘awat). Al-Shafi‘i's high praise—that he could debate even Satan—shows his intellectual prowess. He authored several important works, including al-Mukhtasar, which is a foundational text in the Shafi‘i school. Interestingly, he also wrote a book according to his own independent reasoning (madhhabihi), not strictly following al-Shafi‘i, indicating his scholarly independence. His death is recorded in 264 AH. He was buried in al-Qarafah, Cairo, near al-Shafi‘i's grave. The nisba 'al-Muzani' traces to the tribe of Muzaynah. For Hanafi students, understanding al-Muzani's role is important because his works, especially al-Mukhtasar, are key references in Shafi‘i fiqh and often compared in ikhtilaf discussions.

Key Terms
المزنى (al-Muzani): The name of a prominent Shafi'i scholar, derived from the tribe Muzaynah.
المختصر (al-Mukhtasar): A concise manual of Shafi'i fiqh authored by al-Muzani.

Biography of Makhool: Evaluating a Narrator for Fatwa
Biographical entry with evaluation of narrator reliability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key factors in a narrator's biography that affect his reliability for fatwa, specifically tadlis, and apply a cautious approach when using such narrations.

Core Idea
A narrator's tadlis demands careful scrutiny but does not automatically invalidate his reports.

Muftis must know the reliability of the sources they rely on. This biography of Makhool, a great Syrian scholar, introduces the concept of tadlis (concealing chain weaknesses) and shows how even a respected faqih can have this trait. Understanding tadlis helps you evaluate hadith and fatwa transmissions critically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scholar's credentials
Read the biography and note Makhool’s titles: 'alim (scholar), faqih, hafiz. Recognize his geographical scope (Syria, Egypt, Iraq, Madinah) and his teachers and students.
Credentials give initial trust but do not guarantee immunity from criticism.
2
Note the criticism: tadlis
The text states he 'yudallisu' (practices tadlis) from certain senior companions. Understand what tadlis means: using ambiguous wording to hide a weakness in the chain.
Tadlis is a red flag for hadith authenticity but not a total rejection.
3
Assess the impact on fatwa
Consider how a mufti would treat a hadith narrated by Makhool with tadlis. The default is to investigate the specific narration and see if it is corroborated or if the tadlis is known to affect his reliability in that case.
Hanafi approach: a mudallis (one who does tadlis) is accepted only if he explicitly states hearing directly (samā‘) or if the narration is supported.
4
Apply the principle of verification
In practice, a mufti must verify the chain of a narration from Makhool before using it as evidence. He should check if the tadlis is from senior companions (who are trustworthy) and if other narrators confirm the report.
This aligns with the adab al-mufti: only rely on sound transmissions.

٤٧ - مكحول : عالم أهل الشام أبو عبد الله بن أبي مسلم الهذلي، الفقيه الحافظ، مولى امرأة من هذيل، وأصله من كابل . وقيل : هو من أولاد كسرى، و داره بدمشق بطرف سوق الأحد ، يرسل كثيراً ويدلّس عن أبي بن كعب، وعبادة ابن الصامت وعائشة، والكبار . وروى عن أبي أمامة الباهلي، وواثلة بن الأسقع وأنس بن مالك، ومحمود بن الربيع، وعبد الرحمن بن غنم، وأبي إدريس الخولاني وأبي سلام . قال ابن إسحاق سمعت مكحولا يقول: طفت الأرض في طلب العلم . وروى أبو وهب عن مكحول : قال : عتقت بمصر فلم أدع بها علمًا إلا حويته في ما أرى، ثم أتيت العراق ثم المدينة فلم أدع بهما علماً إلا حويت عليه فيما أرى، ثم أتيت الشام فغربلتها. وقال الزهري : العلماء ثلاثة فذكر منهم مكحولا. وقال أبو حاتم : ما أعلم بالشام أفقه من مكحول . توفى سنة ثلاث عشرة ومائة . وقال أبو نعيم ودحيم : سنة اثنتى عشرة . وقيل : غير ذلك . (تذکرة الحفاظ : ۱/ ۱۰۸،۱۰۷)
47. Makhool: The scholar of the people of Syria, Abu ‘Abdullah ibn Abi Muslim al-Hudhali, the jurist and memorizer (of hadith), freed slave of a woman from Hudhayl. His origin is from Kabul. It is said: he was from the descendants of Kisra (the Persian king). His house in Damascus was at the side of the Sunday market. He used to send (hadith) frequently and engage in tadlis (concealing imperfections in isnad) from Ubayy ibn Ka‘b, ‘Ubadah ibn al-Samit, ‘A’ishah, and the senior companions. He narrated from Abu Umamah al-Bahili, Wathilah ibn al-Asqa‘, Anas ibn Malik, Mahmud ibn al-Rabi‘, ‘Abd al-Rahman ibn Ghanm, Abu Idris al-Khawlani, and Abu Salam. Ibn Ishaq said: I heard Makhool saying: 'I toured the earth in search of knowledge.' Abu Wahb narrated from Makhool that he said: 'I settled in Egypt and did not leave any knowledge in it except that I encompassed it as far as I can see, then I came to Iraq, then to Madinah, and I did not leave any knowledge in them except that I encompassed it as far as I can see, then I came to Syria and sifted through it.' Al-Zuhri said: 'The scholars are three' – and he mentioned Makhool among them. Abu Hatim said: 'I do not know in Syria anyone more juristically learned than Makhool.' He died in the year 113 AH. Abu Nu‘aym and Duhaym said: the year 112 AH. And other opinions are mentioned. (Tadhkirat al-Huffaz: 1/107-108)

Commentary
This biography introduces Makhool, a major Syrian scholar of the tabi‘un generation. He is described as a faqih and hafiz (master of hadith memorization). The text notes that he often engaged in tadlis (a form of hadith narration that may obscure weaknesses) from certain senior companions, which is a critical observation for hadith criticism. His extensive travel to collect knowledge is highlighted through his own statements: he toured the earth, and specifically visited Egypt, Iraq, Madinah, and then Syria to 'sift' knowledge. Al-Zuhri, a towering scholar, counted him among the three great scholars. Abu Hatim declared him the most knowledgeable faqih in Syria. His death date is given as 113 or 112 AH. For Hanafi students, Makhool is important as a source of hadith and fatwa in the early period; his rulings in fiqh are referenced in earlier works. The note about tadlis is a reminder to check the authenticity of transmissions from him.

Key Terms
مكحول (Makhool): A prominent Syrian tabi‘i scholar, faqih, and hadith memorizer (hafiz).
يدلّس (yudallisu): He practices tadlis, i.e., concealing a weakness in the chain of narration, often by using ambiguous terms.
غربلتها (gharbalatuha): He sifted through it, meaning he thoroughly examined and extracted the knowledge from Syria.

The Biography of Al-Haytham ibn Jamil and Its Significance for Hadith Reliability
Biographical entry of a hadith scholar and memorizer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key biographical details of al-Haytham ibn Jamil and explain why his status as a hafiz and his praise from Ahmad ibn Hanbal matter for hadith authenticity in Hanafi legal methodology.

Core Idea
A narrator's praise from Imam Ahmad signals exceptional reliability; Hanafi scholars rely on such verification when accepting hadith.

This lesson introduces you to al-Haytham ibn Jamil, a prominent hadith scholar from the early Islamic period. While he is not a Hanafi imam himself, his narrations are used by all schools, including the Hanafi. Understanding his biography helps you appreciate how classical scholars assessed transmitters—a skill essential for a mufti who must weigh hadith evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the person
Note his full name: Abu Sahl al-Haytham ibn Jamil al-Antaki al-Baghdadi. The nisbas indicate his origin (Antakya) and residence (Baghdad).
The title 'al-Hafiz' is a high rank in hadith memorization.
2
List his teachers and students
He narrated from major figures like Hammad ibn Salamah, Imam Malik, and al-Layth ibn Sa'd. His students include Ahmad ibn Hanbal, al-Dhuhli, and others.
This network shows his centrality in hadith transmission.
3
Evaluate the praise he received
Ahmad al-'Ijli called him 'trustworthy, a man of the Sunnah.' Ahmad ibn Hanbal considered him the 'most memorizer' among three prominent scholars.
Such praise from rigorous critics like Ahmad ibn Hanbal is a strong indicator of reliability.
4
Understand the term 'hafiz'
Al-Hafiz refers to a scholar who has memorized hundreds of thousands of hadiths with their chains. This level of mastery ensures accuracy in transmission.
In Hanafi usul, a hafiz's narrations carry greater weight because of their precision.
5
Connect to Hanafi fiqh
A mufti must verify the chain of a hadith before using it as evidence. Knowing the biographies of narrators like al-Haytham helps in assessing the authenticity of a hadith that may be cited in Hanafi texts.
For example, Malik's narrations are common in Hanafi fiqh; al-Haytham's transmission from Malik adds another layer of verification.

48. Al-Haytham: He is Abu Sahl al-Haytham ibn Jamil al-Antaki, al-Baghdadi, the great memorizer, the hadith scholar of Antakya. He narrated from Hammad ibn Salamah, Malik, al-Layth, and others. Commentary
This biography presents al-Haytham ibn Jamil, a prominent hadith scholar from Antakya (in present-day Turkey) but associated with Baghdad. He is described as a 'great memorizer' (hafiz kabir) and 'muḥaddith of Antakya'. He transmitted from well-known authorities like Hammad ibn Salamah, Imam Malik, and al-Layth ibn Sa‘d. His narrators include Ahmad ibn Hanbal (the famous Hanbali imam) and al-Dhuhli. Praise from Ahmad al-‘Ijli and Ahmad ibn Hanbal underscores his reliability and exceptional memory. Ahmad ibn Hanbal considered him the most memorizer (ahfazuhum) among three prominent hadith scholars. He died in 213 AH. In the context of iftaa, al-Haytham's role as a hadith authority means his narrations carry weight in legal proofs, especially for Hanbalis, but also for Hanafis who rely on hadith authenticity.

Key Terms
الحافظ (al-hafiz): A master of hadith memorization, typically one who knows hundreds of thousands of hadiths and their chains.
الأنطاكي (al-Antaki): From Antakya (Antioch), a city in present-day Turkey.
