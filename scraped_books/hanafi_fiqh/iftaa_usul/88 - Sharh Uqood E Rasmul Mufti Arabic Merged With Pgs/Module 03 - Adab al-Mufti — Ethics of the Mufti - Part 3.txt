Supplications for the Ruler in Fatwas: Permitted and Prohibited Phrases
Ruling on supplication phrases for the ruler in fatwas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the recommended supplications for the ruler in a fatwa and state the legal ruling (makruh) for saying 'أطال الله بقاءك'.

Core Idea
Avoid praising the ruler with phrases foreign to the Salaf; use only supplications attested by the early generations.

When a mufti issues a fatwa involving the ruler, he should include a supplication for him. However, not all good-sounding phrases are appropriate. The Salaf used specific expressions, and certain phrases, like 'May Allah prolong your life', have been deemed disliked (makruh) by the majority of scholars. This lesson guides you on which phrases to use and which to avoid.

Guided Path
1
Recognize when a fatwa involves the ruler
Identify any case where the ruling has implications for the state or its authority, such as matters of public policy, land, or governance.
2
Recall the recommended supplications
From the passage, the mufti may say: أصلحه الله (may Allah rectify him), سدّده الله (may Allah guide him), قوى الله عزمه (may Allah strengthen his resolve), أصلح الله به (may Allah rectify through him), or شدّ الله أزره (may Allah strengthen his support).
3
Apply the prohibition on specific phrases
The phrase أطال الله بقاءك (may Allah prolong your life) is explicitly prohibited. It is not from the expressions of the Salaf and is considered makruh by the consensus of scholars.
4
Analyze the evidence
The transmitted agreement (ittifaq) from Abu Ja'far al-Nahhas and others, plus the hadith of Umm Habiba in Sahih Muslim, provide the basis for the ruling.
The hadith indicates it is better to leave such supplications.
5
Derive the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that أطال الله بقاءك is makruh; the mufti must use the recommended alternatives.

Conciseness and Clarity in Fatwa Answers
Mufti's conduct: conciseness and clarity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of conciseness and clarity when crafting fatwa responses, and identify when exceptions require more detailed explanation for matters of public interest.

Core Idea
A mufti's answer must be concise and clear enough for the common person to understand the ruling.

This lesson presents the eighth rule of mufti adab: the mufti should keep his answer short and at a level the general public can grasp. The rule balances the need for brevity with the occasional need for thoroughness in politically or socially significant matters. Understanding this rule helps ensure that fatwas serve their purpose—guiding the questioner—without confusion or unnecessary detail.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Read the passage and extract the core directive: the mufti should abbreviate his answer and make it understandable to the common people.
The rule is stated at the beginning: 'ليختصر جوابه، ويكون بحيث تفهمه العامة' (Let him abbreviate his answer, and it should be such that the common people understand it).
2
Understand the default approach
The author of al-Hawi recommends reducing answers to simple binary terms: permissible/not permissible, true/false. This is the default for most questions.
This avoids confusion and gives the questioner a clear directive without needing technical reasoning.
3
Recognize the exception
Al-Qarafi's footnote in al-Ihkam states that when the issue involves rulers and public interests, the mufti should provide more detailed explanation, including evidence and consequences.
The exception aims to ensure full compliance and protect public welfare, overriding the general rule of brevity.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider a question about a personal matter (e.g., a minor transaction) vs. a question about a new public policy. For the former, a concise ruling suffices; for the latter, more elaboration is needed.
The mufti must gauge the scope of the question and the potential impact on the community.

Ruling on Saying 'أطال الله بقاءك' in Fatwa
Mufti adab: ruling on a specific phrase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ruling on the phrase 'أطال الله بقاءك' and apply the Hanafi distinction based on the addressee.

Core Idea
The phrase 'أطال الله بقاءك' is makruh due to its origin with the Zanadiqa, but some scholars permit it for righteous scholars and just rulers.

This lesson addresses a subtle point of mufti etiquette: the use of certain phrases that may carry historical baggage. The phrase 'أطال الله بقاءك' (May God prolong your life) is generally considered disliked (makruh) because it was introduced by the Zanadiqa (heretics). However, a nuanced view exists, permitting it for people of virtue. As a mufti, understanding this distinction helps maintain proper decorum in correspondence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Phrase and Its Origin
Recognize that the phrase 'أطال الله بقاءك' is under discussion. Note its historical association: the Zanadiqa were the first to use it in correspondence.
Isma'il bin Ishaq reported that the Zanadiqa introduced this phrase.
2
State the General Ruling
The majority of scholars (including Abu Ja'far al-Nahhas) hold that it is makruh (disliked). Some even call it the 'greeting of the Zanadiqa'.
The text transmits 'consensus' (ittifaq) on karahah, but later notes disagreement.
3
Recognize the Conflicting Opinion and Differentiation
Al-Ardi disputed absolute karahah. He distinguished: for people of religion, knowledge, and just rulers, it is a good deed (qurbah); for others, it is disliked or even haram.
This differentiation is found in al-Adhkar al-Muntakhaba and its commentary.
4
Apply the Etiquette for the Mufti
As a mufti, you should generally avoid the phrase in correspondence, especially for unknown or unworthy recipients. You may use it for a respected scholar or just ruler if it aligns with local custom and does not cause fitnah.
The preferred position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is the general karahah, but the exception is noted.

Brevity in Delivering Fatwas
Instruction to keep fatwa answers brief and understandable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of brevity in fatwas and identify the exceptional cases where elaboration is necessary.

Core Idea
Brevity is recommended but not absolute: important matters require detailed evidence.

The mufti's adab includes conciseness, but overgeneralizing this rule leads to inadequate guidance. This passage clarifies the balance, showing that brevity is the default for simple questions, while important public matters demand thorough explanation with evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
The passage states the mufti should shorten his answer so that common people understand. This is the primary instruction: be concise and accessible.
The example from al-Hawi shows answers can be as simple as 'yes' or 'no'.
2
Recognize the scope of brevity
Brevity is recommended for clear matters (amr jali) where no elaboration is needed, and for answers to common people in routine fatwas.
This prevents unnecessary complication for the lay questioner.
3
Identify the exception
For important matters (masa'il muhimma), especially those concerning rulers or public interests (matalib al-umara', masalih al-'amma), full elaboration with evidence, reasoning, and addressing objections is recommended.
Al-Qarafi's statement: 'except in matters concerning rulers… then it is good to elaborate with encouragement, clarification, evidence, and explanation of the rule and consequences.'
4
Apply the rule
Given a fatwa scenario, determine whether brevity or detail is appropriate based on the context: Is it a simple, common issue? Or does it involve public welfare, important legal implications, or a need for compliance?
This step exercises the mufti's judgment (ijtihad) in balancing efficiency and thoroughness.

Caution in Fatwa on Apostasy and Capital Punishment
Etiquette for mufti in capital cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a proper fatwa response for cases of potential apostasy, qisas, and ta'zir by including conditions (proof, istitaba, intention) and avoiding absolute declarations.

Core Idea
Never declare capital punishment without conditions: proof, istitaba, and a qualified answer.

When a mufti is asked about statements that may imply apostasy, or about crimes warranting qisas or ta'zir, a common mistake is to issue a swift, unconditional ruling. This lesson, based on the guidance of al-Saymari and al-Khatib, teaches you to always qualify your answer by mentioning the necessary prerequisites. By the end, you will be able to craft a cautious, condition-based fatwa that prompts the ruler to verify the requirements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario type
First, determine whether the query involves a clear blasphemous statement, an ambiguous statement, a killing or injury, or an act needing ta'zir. Each scenario has a distinct requirement for the mufti's answer.

Guidelines for Answering Inheritance Questions
Rules for mufti's phrasing in inheritance questions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi adab rules for answering inheritance questions, including proper phrasing in 'awl cases and clear statement of exclusions.

Core Idea
A mufti must use precise and clear language in inheritance answers, avoiding simplified fractions and ambiguous terms.

The mufti's role in inheritance questions goes beyond mere calculation; he must communicate shares clearly to laypeople. This passage from 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti outlines five practical guidelines for phrasing fatwas on mirath, covering presumptions, specification of lineage, the famous munbariyya case, and clarity in exclusions and distributions.

Guided Path
1
Understand presuppositions of heirs (rule 112)
When the question lists heirs without explicitly stating the absence of impediments (slavery, disbelief, homicide), the mufti should assume they are absent; he does not need to stipulate them.
This is the default presumption in Hanafi inheritance.
2
Specify lineage for ambiguous relatives (rule 113)
If the question mentions generic terms like 'brothers' or 'uncles' without specifying whether they are full, consanguine, or uterine, the mufti must clarify the lineage in his answer.
Ambiguity leads to incorrect shares.
3
Handle 'awl cases with precise language (rule 114)
In the munbariyya case (wife, both parents, two daughters), the mufti should not say 'her eighth' or 'her ninth' without qualification; instead, he should say 'the 'ailing eighth' or quote 'Ali's phrase that 'her eighth became a ninth.'
This preserves the accurate fractional shares after 'awl (e.g., 3/27).
4
State exclusions clearly (rule 115)
If a person listed in the query does not inherit, the mufti should explicitly say 'so-and-so is excluded.' If the exclusion is context-dependent, he must qualify it to avoid misunderstanding.
Prevents the impression of permanent exclusion.
5
Avoid confusing terminology in distributions (rule 116)
When asked about shares for males and females (e.g., brothers and sisters), the mufti should avoid the technical phrase 'for the male is the share of two females' and instead give the actual numerical shares.
Laypersons may misinterpret the ratio.

Rules for endorsing a qualified mufti and for striking unqualified ones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm al-mufti rules for responding to a fatwa request that already contains a fatwa from another mufti, distinguishing between qualified and unqualified writers.

Core Idea
A mufti may endorse a qualified colleague's fatwa but must strike out an unqualified one to avoid tacit approval.

When a mufti receives a fatwa request that already has a written answer, his response depends on the writer's qualification. Procedure when a mufti sees a fatwa by an unqualified person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct procedure when a mufti encounters a fatwa from an unqualified person and explain why endorsing it is prohibited.

Core Idea
A mufti must never give a concurrent fatwa with an unqualified person, as it endorses an unlawful act.

This passage from 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti addresses a delicate situation: what should a mufti do when he sees a fatwa written by someone not qualified? The ruling is clear and firm, yet nuanced. It protects the integrity of the fatwa institution by prohibiting any tacit approval of incompetence, while also respecting the rights of the paper's owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Recognize the situation: a mufti sees a fatwa in the handwriting of an unqualified person. The primary ruling, according to al-Saymari, is that he should not issue a fatwa alongside it.
The unqualified person may be an ignoramus or layman.
2
Determine the mufti's obligations
The mufti must have the paper's owner strike out that fatwa, or he may do so himself with permission. He may not detain the paper without the owner's consent. Additionally, he may rebuke the questioner for seeking a fatwa from an unqualified source.
Striking out is a physical act of disapproval.
3
Explain the rationale
Even if the unqualified person's answer is correct, allowing it to stand constitutes an implicit endorsement (taqrir) of the unlawful act of giving fatwa without qualification. This is akin to testifying to his qualification.
The act of giving fatwa by an unqualified person is itself objectionable (munkar).
4
Note the exceptions and limits
If there is a fear of greater fitna (harm), the mufti may refrain from striking out, but only as a necessity. The mufti must still not issue a concurrent fatwa.
The toolbox mentions 'Fear of greater fitna' as a preventer (mani') to striking out.
5
Apply the adab of a mufti
The mufti should maintain professional conduct: rebuke the questioner gently but firmly, and educate him on the importance of seeking qualified scholars.
This falls under mufti adab (manners).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When correcting an unqualified fatwa causes fitna, the lesser harm may permit issuing alongside.

This lesson presents a nuanced rule from the Mufti's code: striking out an unqualified person's fatwa is the default duty, but when it risks social upheaval (fitna), a qualified mufti must weigh options. The source passage by Abu Amr details two scenarios: one of simple avoidance and one where the unqualified fatwa dominates, requiring the mufti to issue a fatwa alongside as the lesser harm, while subtly indicating the deficiency.

Guided Path

The principle 'ahwan al-ḍararayn' (choose the lesser of two harms) dictates that issuing a fatwa alongside is permissible, as it is less harmful than leaving the community misled.
This is a branch of the general maxim 'al-ḍarar yuzāl'.
4
Note the etiquette of disclosure
When issuing alongside, the qualified mufti should subtly and gently show the unqualified person's deficiency to those unaware, without causing fitna.
The goal is correction without strife.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Taqlid is accepting a mujtahid's ruling based on trust, not on your own verification of the proof—even if you know the proof yourself.

This lesson unpacks the definition of taqlid as presented by Imam al-Nawawi in his Adab al-Fatwa. Learning Objective

Core Idea
A layperson must adopt a specific madhhab to prevent picking and choosing based on desire.

This passage addresses a core question in usul al-ifta: Is a layperson (al-‘āmmī) required to follow a single madhhab, or may they freely choose among scholars? The text transmits two views from Ibn Burhān, with the second view—that obligation is necessary—decisively adopted by Abu al-Hasan al-Kiyā. The reasoning highlights the danger of selecting rulings according to personal whims. Understanding this discussion is crucial for a mufti when guiding mustaftis and ensuring consistency in fatwa practice.

Core Idea
A mufti must not give fatwa to please questioners; personal taqlid is required to prevent opportunistic picking of rulings.

This lesson examines a powerful critique from Ibn al-Qayyim, endorsed by later Hanafi authorities, against the corrupt practice of tailoring fatwas to suit the questioner's desires. Binding Nature of a Mufti's Fatwa on the Mustafti
Rules on binding nature of a mufti's fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on when a mustafti is bound by a mufti's fatwa, distinguishing the conditions when one or multiple muftis exist.

Core Idea
A fatwa is binding on the mustafti if the mufti is the most qualified available; otherwise, choice remains.

This passage from Adab al-Fatwa tackles a key question in mufti-mustafti relations: once a fatwa is given, is the seeker obligated to act? The discussion contrasts the views of al-Khatib al-Baghdadi, al-Sam'ani, and the preferred Hanafi position. Understanding these nuances is essential for a mufti to know the scope of his authority and for a mustafti to know his obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Determine whether there is only one mufti available or multiple. Also note if the mufti's qualifications are known or unclear.
The ruling changes based on these factors.
2
Apply the single-mufti rule
If only one qualified mufti exists, his fatwa is binding immediately, without need for commitment or inner conviction.
This is the Hanafi position, counter to al-Sam'ani's view.
3
Apply the multiple-mufti rule
If multiple muftis exist, the mustafti must investigate to find the most knowledgeable and pious. If he can identify that one, his fatwa is binding. If not, the mustafti may choose among them.
If the muftis are equal in qualification, the mustafti has a choice.
4
Consider the effect of judge's ruling
If a judge rules on the matter, the disagreement is resolved, and the ruling becomes binding on all.
This is because a judge's ruling lifts the khilaf.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf
Understanding the practical consequence of the dispute: under the Hanafi view, the mustafti must act quickly on a fatwa from the best mufti; under al-Sam'ani's view, he may delay or seek another until convinced.
This affects real-time practice.

Commentary
This passage from Adab al-Fatwa presents a detailed discussion on whether a mustafti (the person seeking a fatwa) is legally bound by the fatwa he receives. The discussion begins by citing two prominent scholars. Al-Khatib al-Baghdadi holds that if there is only one mufti in the location, his fatwa is binding. The author then presents his own ruling, which is based on Hanafi principles: the mustafti must investigate and follow the most knowledgeable and pious mufti. In practice, this means that when only one mufti is available, his fatwa is immediately binding without any additional condition. However, if the muftis agree or a judge issues a ruling, that becomes binding. The commentator also emphasizes that this Hanafi position is the most suitable for the present time, unless the muftis are equal in knowledge and piety, in which case the mustafti may choose. The passage also includes two important footnote clarifications: first, that a single mufti's fatwa is binding like a judge's ruling (except in certain cases), and second, that a judge's ruling resolves the disagreement among schools, making the matter effectively settled.

The Layman and Requesting Proof from the Mufti
Ruling on the layman requesting proof (rule 157)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the layman should not demand evidence during the fatwa session, and apply the exception: he may ask after accepting the fatwa abstractly or in a separate session.

Core Idea
The layman should not demand proof from the mufti; if he seeks reassurance, he may ask after accepting the fatwa abstractly.

One of the key debates in adab al-fatwa is whether the lay questioner (mustafti) can request evidence (dalil) from the mufti. The Hanafi school, as presented in this passage, discourages such demands during the fatwa session. This lesson explores the preferred opinion and the alternative view of al-Sam'ani, highlighting the practical implications for fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the default rule
The passage asks: may the layman ('aammi) demand evidence from the mufti? The default Hanafi rule is: 'he should not demand evidence, nor say: why did you say that?' This is the starting point for etiquettes of the session.
2
Recognize the exception (permissive option)
If the layman feels uncertain and wants to hear the proof to settle his heart, he may do so in a separate session or in the same session after having accepted the fatwa abstractly (qabūl al-fatwā mujarradah). This means he first accepts the ruling and then asks for the evidence casually, not as a condition of acceptance.
The key phrase is 'ba'd qabūl al-fatwā mujarradah' — after accepting the fatwa without conditions.
3
Present al-Sam'ānī's opposing view
Al-Sam'ānī held that the layman should not be prevented from seeking evidence, and the mufti is obliged to provide it if the evidence is definitive (maqṭū' bih), but not if it depends on ijtihad. This view is more permissive.
Al-Sam'ānī's position recognizes that some proofs are clear enough for a layman to understand.
4
Analyze the preferred opinion (al-Ṣawāb al-awwal)
The text states 'wa al-Ṣawāb al-awwal' (the first opinion is correct). This indicates that the prohibition during the fatwa session is the relied-upon position. The rationale is that the layman's understanding is insufficient for ijtihad, and demanding evidence distracts from the mufti's authority and the smooth flow of iftā'.
This is the mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.
5
Apply the practical consequence
In practice, if a layman asks 'why?' during a fatwa session, the mufti may politely decline to answer and explain that he may ask later. The mufti should not feel obligated to provide detailed proofs unless the questioner is a scholar or the issue demands it.
This preserves the mufti's role and prevents argumentation.

The writer of the question sheet should be someone who knows how to ask well, and place it appropriately, with clarity of handwriting and expression, and protect them from what leads to misreading. Al-Saymari said: He should be eager that its writer be from the people of knowledge. Commentary
This passage contains the ninth topic (al-tasi'ah) of Adab al-Fatwa, consisting of two rules. Rule 156 emphasizes the importance of the one who writes the question paper (kaatib al-ruq'a). He should be skilled in formulating questions clearly in both handwriting and wording, avoiding ambiguity that could lead to misinterpretation (tasheef). Al-Saymari advises that the writer should be from the people of knowledge ('ilm), and some high-ranking jurists only answered questions written by a specific knowledgeable person, ensuring precision. Rule 157 addresses a key etiquette: the layman (aammi) should not demand the dalil (proof) from the mufti or ask 'Why?' The accepted opinion (al-Sawab al-awwal) is that he should refrain. If he wants to hear the justification to reassure himself, he may ask in a separate session or after accepting the fatwa. Imam al-Sam'ani held a permissive view, but the first is preferred. This reflects the Hanafi position that the mufti's authority suffices, and the layman is not obligated to understand ijtihad.

The Layperson Should Not Demand Evidence
Rule prohibiting the layperson from demanding evidence from the mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ruling regarding a layperson demanding evidence from a mufti and apply the correct position (mufta bihi) in a fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
The layperson must not demand proof from the mufti; acceptance precedes inquiry.

In the etiquette of fatwa, a key rule governs the interaction between the mufti and the questioner. This lesson clarifies whether a layperson may ask the mufti for evidence, and under what conditions. The passage from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa presents the main position and a dissenting view.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on mufti-adab (etiquette of the mufti and the questioner). It begins with a note that a mufti should only give fatwa in a written document from a specified qualified scholar. Then it states the rule: the layperson (al-ammi) should not demand the evidence (dalil) from the mufti, nor ask 'why did you say?'. If he wants to hear the proof for his own reassurance, he may ask for it in a separate session, or in the same session after accepting the fatwa as is—i.e., after having already accepted the ruling without proof. Imam al-Sam'ani disagreed, saying the layperson is not prohibited from asking for proof, and the mufti is obligated to provide it if the proof is decisive (maqtū' bih), but not if it is non-decisive because that requires ijtihad beyond the layperson's understanding. The author states that the correct position is the first (i.e., the prohibition). Thus, the relied‑upon view is that the layperson should not demand evidence.

Key Terms
العامى (al-‘āmmī): The layperson who is not a scholar of fiqh; one who is not qualified to evaluate evidence.
المفتى (al-muftī): The qualified jurist who issues fatwas; he must have expertise in istinbat.
الدليل (al-dalīl): The scriptural or rational evidence for a ruling; demanding it from the mufti is discouraged in the first view.
