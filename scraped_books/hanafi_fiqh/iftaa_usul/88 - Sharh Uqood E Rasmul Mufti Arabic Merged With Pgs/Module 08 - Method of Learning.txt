The Rasm Rule: Verify Every Fatwa Against Its Original Source
Methodological rule warning against blind reliance on secondary sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rasm rule requiring verification of fatwas against original sources (al-ma'khadh al-asli) and identify when a secondary source is unreliable.

Core Idea
A fatwa repeated in twenty books may still be wrong — always trace back to the original source.

This passage from Ibn Abidin's Rasm al-Mufti establishes a foundational rule for any mufti: never trust a fatwa simply because it appears in many later books. Errors can originate from a single mistake and propagate through generations of scholars. The only safeguard is to refer back to the original source — the direct statements of the founding imams or the canonical texts of the madhhab (Zahir al-Riwayah). In this lesson, we will examine the rule and two case studies of propagated errors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rasm Rule
The text states: 'It may happen that a statement is transmitted in about twenty books of later scholars, and the statement is an error.' What is the rule being established? The mufti must not rely on the mere frequency of transmission; he must verify the original source.
This is the core methodological warning.
2
Understand the Mechanism of Error Propagation
How does a single error multiply? The author explains: 'First poser makes an error, then others transmit it without verification.' This can happen due to slips of the pen, confusion of rulings, or blind copying.
Errors often begin with one scholar and spread uncorrected.
3
Apply to the First Example: Suspension of Contracts
Ibn Nujaym in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq corrected a widespread mistake about which contracts can be suspended (ta'liq). The error had appeared in many later works but was traced back to a single erroneous statement.
This shows the rule in action in the chapter of sales.
4
Apply to the Second Example: Trusts Becoming Guaranteed
Qadikhan stated that trusts become guaranteed upon death due to ignorance except in three cases. Al-Bahr found seven more exceptions, revealing that the original statement by al-Natafi was erroneous and then circulated.
Again, frequency of repetition did not validate the error.
5
Extract the Mufti's Duty
The mufti must always trace a ruling back to its original source (al-ma'khadh al-asli) — the direct statements of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad, or the canonical texts of Zahir al-Riwayah. Secondary compilations are not sufficient.
This is the practical takeaway for issuing fatwas.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological rule (rasm al-mufti) emphasizing the critical importance of verifying fatwas against original sources (al-ma'khadh al-asli) rather than relying on secondary compilations. The author relates that a ruling may appear in numerous later works yet originate from a single error. He provides two concrete examples from Hanafi fiqh. First, the issue of what contracts can be suspended (ta'liq) — Ibn Nujaym in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq corrected a widespread mistake. Second, the famous error regarding hiring for Quran recitation: the correct Hanafi position (agreed upon by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad) permits hiring for teaching the Quran, not for mere recitation. Yet many later authors like the author of al-Siraj al-Wahhaj and al-Jawhara mistakenly reported the fatwa as permitting hiring for recitation, and others followed them, even branching rulings about Hajj upon this error. The text also mentions a mistake by Qadikhan about three exceptions to trusts becoming guaranteed, which was corrected by the author of al-Bahr who found seven more. The lesson for a mufti is clear: always trace a ruling back to its primary source (the founding imams or the canonical texts of the madhhab) and do not blindly trust later compilations, no matter how many books repeat the same statement.

Key Terms
المأخذ الأصلى (al-ma'khadh al-asli): The primary source of a ruling, such as the direct statements of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, or the canonical works of the madhhab (Zahir al-Riwayah).
العهدة (al-'uhdah): The responsibility or guarantee of the truthfulness of a transmitted report; the narrator bears the 'uhdah of the report.
الاستئجار على تلاوة القرآن المجردة (al-isti'jar 'ala tilawat al-Qur'an al-mujarradah): Hiring someone to recite the Quran alone, without teaching or leading prayer.

Case Study: The Error on Hiring for Quran Recitation
Case study of a propagated error corrected by original sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the correct Hanafi position on hiring for Quran recitation and explain how the error propagated through later works.

Core Idea
The Hanafi Imams agreed: hiring for mere recitation is invalid; the mistaken fatwa comes from a single error in a later work.

This passage uses the issue of hiring for Quran recitation as a vivid example of the rasm rule. The correct Hanafi position, agreed upon by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad, is that hiring for mere recitation is invalid. However, the author of al-Siraj al-Wahhaj mistakenly reported the fatwa as permitting it, and many later authors followed him. This lesson traces the error and its correction.

Guided Path
1
State the Correct Hanafi Position
The three imams agreed: hiring for mere recitation is invalid; only hiring for teaching the Quran is permissible by istihsan.
This is the ruling from the original sources.
2
Identify the Error
The author of al-Siraj al-Wahhaj and al-Jawhara wrote that 'the fatwa is on the validity of hiring,' confusing teaching with mere recitation.
He likely intended teaching but omitted the qualification.
3
Trace the Propagation
Many later authors (al-Qahistani, Mulla Miskin, al-Haskafi, etc.) copied this statement without verification, and some even branched rulings about Hajj upon it.
The error multiplied because later scholars trusted the earlier statement.
4
Apply the Rasm Rule to Correct It
Al-Ramli and others pointed out the error by referring to the original sources (al-Tatarkhaniyyah, etc.), showing that the mufta bihi is only for teaching.
This demonstrates the correct methodology.
5
Understand the Thamarah al-Khilaf
A mufti must not issue a fatwa permitting hiring for mere recitation based on widespread later texts; he must follow the consensus of the founding imams.
Practical consequence: a wrong fatwa would lead to invalid contracts.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological rule (rasm al-mufti) emphasizing the critical importance of verifying fatwas against original sources (al-ma'khadh al-asli) rather than relying on secondary compilations. The author relates that a ruling may appear in numerous later works yet originate from a single error. He provides two concrete examples from Hanafi fiqh. First, the issue of what contracts can be suspended (ta'liq) — Ibn Nujaym in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq corrected a widespread mistake. Second, the famous error regarding hiring for Quran recitation: the correct Hanafi position (agreed upon by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad) permits hiring for teaching the Quran, not for mere recitation. Yet many later authors like the author of al-Siraj al-Wahhaj and al-Jawhara mistakenly reported the fatwa as permitting hiring for recitation, and others followed them, even branching rulings about Hajj upon this error. The text also mentions a mistake by Qadikhan about three exceptions to trusts becoming guaranteed, which was corrected by the author of al-Bahr who found seven more. The lesson for a mufti is clear: always trace a ruling back to its primary source (the founding imams or the canonical texts of the madhhab) and do not blindly trust later compilations, no matter how many books repeat the same statement.

Types of Mafhum al-Mukhalafah and the Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Defines four types of mafhum al-mukhalafah and discusses the ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of mafhum al-mukhalafah and explain why the Hanafi school rejects it as a proof.

Core Idea
Mafhum al-mukhalafah is not a proof in Hanafi usul; it is considered qiyas, not direct linguistic indication.

In this lesson, we examine the concept of mafhum al-mukhalafah (opposite implication) and its four subtypes as defined in classical usul. The majority of usul scholars, including the Shafi'is, accept most types as valid proofs, but the Hanafi school rejects them, treating them as forms of analogy (qiyas). Understanding this dispute is crucial for correctly deriving rulings from qualifiers in legal texts.

Guided Path
1

The text defines mafhum al-shart (condition), mafhum al-ghayah (limit), mafhum al-‘adad (number), and mafhum al-laqab (title).
Each type is illustrated with a Quranic verse or hadith.
2
For each type, identify the qualifier and the claimed opposite implication
The qualifier (condition, limit, number, noun) is said to imply the opposite ruling when absent.
E.g., 'If they are pregnant, spend on them' implies no maintenance if not pregnant.
3
Recognize the scope of agreement and disagreement
Mafhum al-muwafaqah is agreed upon; mafhum al-mukhalafah is disputed. Shafi'is accept except al-laqab; Hanafis reject all.
The text explicitly states the disagreement.
4
Analyze the Hanafi reasoning
Hanafis argue that without a qualifier, the statement is silent; no negative implication is derived. The absence does not establish the opposite; it remains on original absence (adam asli).
The Hanafi response treats the inference as qiyas, not direct textual proof.
5
Apply the dispute to a concrete example
The text gives the example of zakat: from 'on grazing animals zakat is due', Shafi'is derive no zakat on fodder-fed animals; Hanafis do not derive this and require separate evidence.
This illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

Commentary
This passage from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti is a definitional and dispute-oriented text. It begins by defining the four types of mafhum al-mukhalafah (opposite implication): mafhum al-shart (condition), mafhum al-ghayah (limit), mafhum al-'adad (number), and mafhum al-laqab (title). The Shafi'is consider mafhum al-mukhalafah as a valid proof, except for mafhum al-laqab. The Hanafis, however, do not consider it valid, treating it as a form of qiyas (analogy) which is not a primary proof. The passage also notes that the majority (including Shafi'is) view the indication as linguistic, not analogical, because qiyas requires the appropriate meaning (munasabah) in the branch to be stronger than in the original, which is not satisfied here. The Hanafi position is that without a qualifier, the statement remains silent on what is not mentioned, and no negative implication is derived.

Key Terms
مفهوم المخالفة (mafhum al-mukhalafah): The implication that when a ruling is restricted by a qualifier, the opposite ruling applies when the qualifier is absent.
مفهوم الشرط (mafhum al-shart): The implication that a ruling conditioned on a condition is negated when the condition is absent.
مفهوم الغاية (mafhum al-ghayah): The implication that a ruling before a limit is reversed after the limit.
مفهوم العدد (mafhum al-'adad): The implication that a ruling specified for a particular number is negated for any other number.
مفهوم اللقب (mafhum al-laqab): The implication that a ruling attached to a noun (e.g., 'Zayd') is restricted to that noun.
مفهوم الموافقة (mafhum al-muwafaqah): The implication that a ruling for a mentioned subject applies to an unmentioned subject with a more appropriate reason.

Commentary
This section from the editorial introduction of 'Adab al-Fatwa' by Imam al-Nawawi details the methodological steps taken by the editors in preparing the critical edition. The passage is a clear enumeration of six tasks: (1) collation of manuscript copies and noting variants in footnotes, (2) referencing hadith and athar sources, (3) providing explanatory annotations where needed, drawing on the work of earlier scholars, (4) identifying books and biographical entries for figures mentioned, with a separate appendix for Shafi'i jurists to keep the commentary concise, (5) numbering all paragraphs for clarity, and (6) preparing an index. The editors also explain how marginal notes from manuscripts are attributed, and they acknowledge the contributions of Mufti Sa'id Ahmad al-Mazahiri. This passage exemplifies scholarly rigor in textual editing within the Islamic tradition. It also reflects a humble attitude, with the editors praying for acceptance of their work.
