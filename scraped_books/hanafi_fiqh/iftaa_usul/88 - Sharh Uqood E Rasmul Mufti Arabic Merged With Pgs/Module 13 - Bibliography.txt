Navigating the Hanafi Reference Library: Key Works in Fatwa and Fiqh
A bibliography of Hanafi sources from a fatwa commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize major Hanafi works cited in Rasm al-Mufti commentaries and identify their authority level (zahir al-riwayah, fatwa collection, etc.).

Core Idea
A mufti's authority rests on the texts he cites; knowing their hierarchy prevents fatwa errors.

This passage presents a list of books cited in Sharh Uqood Rasm al-Mufti. These range from foundational usul texts to fatwa collections. For a student of iftaa, knowing which work is considered zahir al-riwayah versus later fatwa compilations is crucial for evaluating the strength of a ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of works
Scan the list for categories: usul (e.g., Kashf al-Asrar), fiqh (e.g., al-Mabsut), fatwa (e.g., al-Fatawa al-Bazzaziyyah), faraid (e.g., Fara'id al-Shaykh Siraj al-Din).
Some titles include keywords like 'فتاوى' for fatwa collections.
2
Note the authors and their era
Recognize early authors like Imam Muhammad (d. 189 AH) versus later ones like al-Bazzazi (d. 827 AH). This helps place the work in the school's historical development.
Earliest works are often the most authoritative for usul.
3
Recognize zahir al-riwayah core
Identify works from the six books of Imam Muhammad: e.g., al-Mabsut li'l-Imam Muhammad. These form the backbone of the school.
Not all zahir al-riwayah works are listed, but al-Mabsut is present.
4
Understand the role of fatwa collections
Later collections like al-Fatawa al-Bazzaziyyah and Fatawa Qadikhan compile authoritative positions for fatwa; they often reflect the mufta bihi.
Fatwa collections are not primary sources but are essential for contemporary practice.
5
Associate works with citation styles
Learn common abbreviations: 'البزازية' for al-Fatawa al-Bazzaziyyah, 'المبسوط' for al-Mabsut. This aids in reading scholarly references.
Many works are known by short titles.

Mapping the Hanafi Fatwa Library: Sources Cited in Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti
Enumeration of Hanafi works cited in the Sharh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize the major Hanafi fiqh and usul works cited in this commentary by their authority level and identify which are Zahir al-Riwayah, which are later commentaries, and which are nawazil collections.

Core Idea
The works cited in a Hanafi fatwa commentary form a hierarchy: early mutun, later shuruh, and nawazil collections.

This lesson introduces the list of books referenced in the Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. These works span from the foundational texts of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani to later commentaries and case law compilations. Understanding the type and authority of each source is essential for a mufti to apply the correct ruling. We will explore the major categories and learn to distinguish between primary legal texts (Zahir al-Riwayah), secondary commentaries, and practical fatwa collections.

Guided Path
1
Identify Zahir al-Riwayah texts
Scan the list for works by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani and early authoritative works that form the core of the Hanafi school. Key examples include al-Mabsut li al-Imam Muhammad (110) and al-Kafi li al-Hakim al-Shahid (105).
These are the first sources a mufti should consult.
2
Recognize major later commentaries
Look for works that explain or expand upon earlier texts. Notable examples: Fath al-Qadir (96, commentary on al-Hidayah), al-Mabsut al-Sarakhsi (111, commentary on al-Kafi), and Kashf al-Asrar (107, commentary on Usul al-Bazdawi).
These carry weight but are secondary to Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Differentiate mutun and shuruh
Distinguish between concise primary texts (mutun) like Mukhtasar al-Quduri (124) and Matn al-Tanwir (114), and their commentaries (shuruh) like al-Mabsut al-Sarakhsi. The list contains both.
Mutun provide the core rules; shuruh provide reasoning and tarjih.
4
Note nawazil and fatawa collections
Identify collections of case rulings and later fatwas: al-Nawazil (106), Majmu' al-Nawazil (118), al-Qunyah (101), and al-Muhit al-Burhani (119). These address novel issues not explicitly covered in earlier texts.
Nawazil are crucial for modern fatwa practice.
5
Understand the role of usul works
Spot works on usul al-fiqh (principles of jurisprudence) such as Kashf al-Asrar (107) and al-Manar (131). These provide the methodology for deriving rulings from sources.
Usul texts ensure that fatwas are principled, not ad hoc.

List of Hanafi works cited in the commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major Hanafi works used in fatwa practice and categorize them by type (matn, sharh, nawazil).

Core Idea
A mufti's authority rests on a canon of texts — knowing their hierarchy ensures correct istidlal.

This lesson introduces the list of works cited in Sharh Uqood Rasm al-Mufti, a key text on mufti conduct. These works form the backbone of Hanafi fatwa practice. Understanding which texts are primary (zahir al-riwaya) and which are later compilations is crucial for evaluating the strength of a ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of each work
Scan the list and classify each work as a matn (primary text), sharh (commentary), nawazil (juridical incidents), usul (principles), or other. For example, al-Hidayah is a matn, al-Muhit is a nawazil collection, and al-Manar is a usul work.
Use the title or known authorship to infer type.
2
Note the author and time period
For each work, recall (or research) the author and approximate century. This helps place the work in the Hanafi tradition. Early works like Mukhtasar al-Quduri (4th century AH) are more foundational than later ones like al-Nahr al-Fa’iq (10th century).
Later works often compile earlier rulings but may introduce tarjih.
3
Understand the work's role in fatwa hierarchy
Rank each work according to the Hanafi fatwa hierarchy: zahir al-riwaya (primary), nawadir (rare transmissions), fatawa mutakhkhirin (later fatwas). For instance, al-Hidayah belongs to zahir al-riwaya, while Majmu‘ al-Nawazil is a secondary collection.
This hierarchy determines which ruling is mufta bihi (operative).

Core Idea
Knowing the authority level of a Hanafi work is essential for a mufti to weigh evidence correctly.

This passage is a bibliography of works referenced in a Hanafi commentary. Each entry provides clues about the work's authorship, transmission, and status within the madhhab. As a mufti, you must be able to distinguish between foundational texts (Zahir al-Riwayah) and later commentaries, and know which are relied upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its author
For each entry, locate the title and author name. Note the full name and death date (if given) to assess the author's era.
Authors like al-Samarqandi (d. 600s AH) are earlier; al-Tahtawi (d. 1231 AH) is later.
2
Check for transmission details
Look for phrases like 'masail narrated by' or 'collected by'. This indicates the chain of transmission and reliability.

3
Classify authority level
Determine if the work is from Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books), Nawadir (rare transmissions), or a later gloss (Hashiya).
Works like al-Jami' al-Kabir are Zahir al-Riwayah; Hashiyat al-Tahtawi is a later gloss.
4
Note publication status
The text often mentions if the work is printed or manuscript. This affects accessibility for a mufti.
Hashiyat al-Ramli 'ala al-Bahr is noted as not printed yet.
5
Cross-reference with known fatwa sources
For glosses, note if they are commonly used in fatwa. For example, Hashiyat al-Tahtawi is widely consulted.
This helps prioritize sources when issuing a fatwa.

Al-Jurjaniyat: A Case Study in Nawadir
Detailed entry on al-Jurjaniyat from the bibliography

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the status of Nawadir in the Hanafi school and evaluate the authority of al-Jurjaniyat as a source for fatwa.

Core Idea
Nawadir (rare transmissions) have lower authority than Zahir al-Riwayah but can be used when no other text exists.

This passage highlights a specific work, al-Jurjaniyat, described as a collection of masail compiled by Imam Muhammad in Jurjan. The entry details its transmission through Ali ibn Salih al-Jurjani, marking it as Nawadir. Understanding the authority of such works is crucial for a mufti to weigh evidence correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its compiler
Note that al-Jurjaniyat is attributed to Imam Muhammad (al-Shaybani), but it is not one of his six core books.
The core six are al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat.
2
Trace the transmission chain
The passage states it was narrated by Ali ibn Salih al-Jurjani from Imam Muhammad. This single chain indicates a rare transmission.
Compare with the widespread transmission of Zahir al-Riwayah texts.
3
Classify as Nawadir
Because it is not from the six core books and has a limited chain, it is classified as Nawadir.
Nawadir are still attributed to the early imams but with weaker transmission.
4
Evaluate its use in fatwa
A mufti should only rely on Nawadir when no ruling is found in Zahir al-Riwayah or later authoritative texts.
Al-Jurjaniyat may be cited, but with an explanation of its lower authority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the authority weight (zahir al-riwayah, nawadir, later fatwa works) of each major Hanafi source listed in Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti and apply the hierarchy in evaluating fatwa derivations.

Core Idea
Not all Hanafi sources are equal: know the origin and transmission of each work to weigh its authority.

This lesson introduces the key Hanafi fiqh source books referenced in the commentary Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. Understanding where each source stands in the Hanafi hierarchy is crucial for any mufti or advanced student. You will learn about the methodology of Ibn Abidin in Radd al-Muhtar, the unique Nawadir works of Muhammad al-Shaybani, and other supplementary treatises, and how they fit into the fatwa process.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source and its author
For each book listed, note the title and author. Determine if the author is a founder (Muhammad al-Shaybani) or a later commentator (Ibn Abidin).
Author's era gives clue to authority weight.
2
Classify the work by transmission category
Determine if the work belongs to Zahir al-Riwayah (core six books of Muhammad), Nawadir (rare transmissions like al-Ruqqiyat), or later fatwa compilations (Radd al-Muhtar).
See the definitions in key terms.
3
Assess the scope and purpose
Is it a comprehensive manual, a specific treatise on certain issues, or a commentary/gloss? This affects its use in fatwa.
E.g., Risalah al-Biri only covers 17 issues.
4
Understand the tarjih mechanism
Some works specifically compile preferred opinions (e.g., Risalah al-Biri for Zufar's views). Note how later scholars like Ibn Abidin verify and correct earlier citations.
This is essential for mufti to know which opinion is operative.
5
Apply the hierarchy to a hypothetical fatwa scenario
If a ruling is found in al-Ziyadat (Zahir al-Riwayah) and contradicted in Radd al-Muhtar (later commentary), which should be relied upon? The earlier source generally has higher authority, but later works may correct transmission errors.
The mufta bihi is often determined by later tarjih.

Two Commentaries on Nazm al-Kanz
Bibliographic entry on two commentaries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify two Hanafi commentaries on Nazm al-Kanz and state their authors and status.

Core Idea
Two unpublished Hanafi commentaries survive on Nazm al-Kanz; knowing them enriches research.

This passage from Rasm al-Mufti introduces two commentaries on Nazm al-Kanz, a versification of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. Both are unpublished but significant for Hanafi fiqh studies. Let's map out these works.

Guided Path

Identify the base text
Nazm al-Kanz is a poetic summary of Kanz al-Daqa'iq by 'Ali al-Maqdisi (d. 1004 AH). Understand its role as a condensed Hanafi fiqh text.
2

First, Sharh Nazm al-Kanz by Shaykh al-Islam 'Ali al-Maqdisi (the same person? Actually it's by a different 'Ali al-Maqdisi). Second, Awdah Ramz 'ala Nazm al-Kanz by Ibn al-Fasih (d. 755 AH).
Authors are distinct from the poet.

Both are described as 'ghayr matbu'in' – unpublished. This is crucial for researchers: manuscripts need to be consulted.
4
Understand the connection to Shurunbulali
The commentator 'Ali al-Maqdisi was the teacher of Hasan al-Shurunbulali, author of Nur al-Idah. This shows chains of transmission.

Evaluating Hanafi Commentaries: The Case of al-Qahistani's Jami' al-Rumuz
Biographical entry on al-Qahistani and his commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the reliability of a Hanafi commentary by identifying signs of weak scholarship and explain why verification (tashih) is critical in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
Not all classical commentaries are equal — verification (tashih) distinguishes reliable scholarship from uncritical compilation.

This passage introduces al-Qahistani's commentary on al-Nuqayah, Jami' al-Rumuz. While praised for its breadth, it also contains a sharp critique from Mawla 'Isam al-Din, who questions al-Qahistani's qualifications and accuses him of mixing sound and weak reports. Understanding such evaluations is essential for a mufti to know which sources to rely upon.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its author
The passage describes Jami' al-Rumuz by al-Qahistani as a commentary on al-Nuqayah. Note the author's background: a resident of Bukhara, reference for fatwa in Transoxiana, died around 963 AH.
Al-Qahistani is also known as Shams al-Din Muhammad al-Khurasani.
2
Note the praise and the critique
The text praises the commentary as 'the greatest in benefit, most precise in indication and symbolism.' But then it quotes a harsh critique by Mawla 'Isam al-Din, who claims al-Qahistani was a book dealer who did not know fiqh and mixed sound and weak reports.
The critique is presented as a counterpoint, suggesting not all scholars agreed on its value.
3
Understand the basis of the critique
The critique focuses on al-Qahistani's lack of proper training as a faqih and his failure to verify (tashih) and scrutinize (tadqiq) the reports he included. The metaphor 'khatb al-layl' (night gatherer) implies he collected indiscriminately.
This highlights the importance of isnad and matn criticism in Hanafi scholarship.
4
Recognize the implications for fatwa practice
A mufti must be aware that not all classical works are equally reliable. The presence of weak or abandoned reports in a commentary can lead to incorrect fatwas. Therefore, muftis should rely on works that have been verified and are part of the zahir al-riwayah or later authoritative summaries.
This passage serves as a reminder to check the credentials and methodology of a source before using it.

١٤٤ النهاية : النهاية في شرح الهداية أحد شروح "الهداية " بل هو أولها للشيخ الحسين بن على السغناقي الفقيه الحنفى والنحوى الجدلی، توفي سنة ۷۱۱. هي أبسط شروح الهداية وأشملها ، قد احتوى على مسائل كثيرة، وفروع لطيفة، أوله : "الحمد لله الذي أعلى معالم العلوم ... إلخ"، ومصنفه الإمام السغناقي وهو الحسن أو الحسين بن على بن حجاج بن علی حسام الدين السغناقي نسبة إلى "سغناق" (بكسر السين المهملة وسكون الغين المعجمة ثم نون بعدها ألف فقاف) بلدة في تركستان، تفقه على حافظ الدين الكبير محمد بن محمد بن نصر البخاري وفوض إليه الفتوى، وهو شاب وتفقه أيضاً على فخر الدین محمد بن محمد بن إلياس المايمرغى .

. ومن مصنفاته : شرح التمهيد في قواعد التوحيد ، و الكافي شرح أصول البزدوى"، و "شرح المفصل"، و "النجاح"، و "شرح الهداية المذكور، فرغ عنه في شهر ربيع الأول سنة سبعمائة (٧٠٠) وكان فقيها جدليا نحويا ، أخذ النحو عن العجذوالي، وغيره . ودخل بغداد ودرس بمشهد الإمام أبي حنيفة، ثم توجه إلى دمشق حاجا فدخلها سنة ٧١٠هـ ، وكان أول من شرح الهداية على مـا ذكـره السيوطي في طبقات النحاة ، وقيل : أول من شرحه حمید الدین علی بن محمد الضرير البخاري، المتوفى سنة سبع وستين وستمائة . وممن تفقه عليه صاحب المعراج وصاحب الكفاية . ذكر صاحب الكشف وفاته عند ذكر "الهداية" ، و "شرح التمهيد"، و "شرح المفصل"، و النجاح"، و "شرح أصول البزدوي ، سنة عشرة وسبعمائة وفيه شبهة، وذكر عند شرح منتخب الإخسيكشي سنة إحدى عشرة وسبعمائة، ونقل في الفوائد عن مدينة العلوم : أنه توفى فى رجب سنة إحدى عشرة وسبعمائة بحلب . ثم اعلم أن صاحب الكشف ذكر النهاية للعيني أيضاً : حيث قال : وشرح القاضي بدر الدین محمود ابن أحمد العيني، المتوفى سنة خمس وخمسين وثمان مائة، الهداية في مجلدات ، وسماه النهاية. انتهى. وهكذا ذكره مولانا عبد الأول الجونفورى فى مفيد المفتى، وهو وهم منهم كما لا يخفى، فإن اسم شرحه البناية (بالباء الموحدة بعدها نون لا النهاية . وقد صرح به العينى فى مقدمة ذلك الشرح .
The books mentioned in the commentary

140. al-Nutaf fi al-Fatawa: It is al-Nutaf al-Hissan (with damm of the nun and fatha of the fawqaniyya) by the shaykh, the imam, the allama al-Sughdi (with damm of the important sin), Shaykh al-Islam Abu al-Hasan Ali ibn al-Husain al-Bukhari al-Hanafi, died 461 AH. He was a Hanafi jurist from the notables of the fifth century. al-Nutaf is the plural of nutfa, meaning a small amount. It is said: he gave him a nutfa of food, i.e., he gave him a little food. And he benefited him a nutfa of knowledge, i.e., he gave and taught him a little knowledge. al-Hissan is the plural of hasana. al-Nutaf al-Hissan means: a collection of small amounts of knowledge; because it is a selection of fatwas. Its manuscript exists in the Topkapı Museum in Istanbul, Turkey. Al-Allama al-Sa'di has a commentary on al-Jami' al-Kabir. Qadi Khan and al-Bazzaziyya frequently quote from him. He was a student of Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi, may Allah have mercy on him, and he was a judge in Bukhara.

141. Nashr al-'Urf: It is a treatise by al-Allama al-Shami, containing discussions related to custom (urf). The allama explained therein the meaning of custom and the evidence for acting upon it, and made it a basis for many issues, its types and rulings, building rulings upon it and its analogues. Its name is: Nashr al-'Urf fi Bina' Ba'd al-Ahkam 'ala al-'Urf. The allama says in its introduction: "To proceed: the faqir Muhammad Abidin - may the Lord of the Worlds pardon him - says: When I explained my urjuza which I named 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, and in my commentary I reached my statement: 'al-Urf in the law has consideration, therefore rulings may rotate upon it,' I discussed what the Generous Opener facilitated, and the pen flowed in its course for the sake of clarification, and I did not realize until the light of dawn appeared, while there remained hidden corners that needed exposition. So I saw that fulfilling the purpose would take the commentary beyond the usual, so I confined myself in it to a small portion of explanation, and I wanted to devote a separate treatise to the discussion of the couplet in order to make the purpose manifest; because I have not seen anyone who gave this topic its due, nor who expended its deserved explanation. And I named this treatise: Nashr al-'Urf fi Bina' Ba'd al-Ahkam 'ala al-'Urf."

14- al-Niqaya: It is an abridgment of al-Wiqaya, by Sadr al-Shari'a al-Asghar Ubayd Allah ibn Mas'ud. He also wrote al-Tanqih and others. He died 747 AH.

144. al-Nihaya: al-Nihaya fi Sharh al-Hidaya is one of the commentaries on al-Hidaya, indeed it is the first of them, by al-Shaykh al-Husain ibn Ali al-Sighnaqi, the Hanafi jurist and the dialectical grammarian, died 711 AH. It is the simplest and most comprehensive of the commentaries on al-Hidaya, containing many issues and subtle branches. Its opening: "Praise be to Allah who elevated the landmarks of the sciences... etc." Its author is Imam al-Sighnaqi, who is al-Hasan or al-Husain ibn Ali ibn Hajjaj ibn Ali Husam al-Din al-Sighnaqi, attributed to Sighnaq - a town in Turkestan. He studied fiqh under Hafiz al-Din al-Kabir Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Nasr al-Bukhari, who entrusted him with giving fatwas while he was young, and also studied under Fakhr al-Din Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Ilyas al-Ma'ymarghi. Among his works are: Sharh al-Tamhid fi Qawa'id al-Tawhid, al-Kafi Sharh Usul al-Bazdawi, Sharh al-Mufassal, al-Najah, and the aforementioned Sharh al-Hidaya, which he completed in the month of Rabi' al-Awwal in the year 700. He was a jurist, dialectician, and grammarian. He studied grammar under al-'Ajadhwali and others. He entered Baghdad and taught at the shrine of Imam Abu Hanifa, then headed to Damascus for pilgrimage, entering it in 710 AH. According to al-Suyuti in Tabaqat al-Nuhat, he was the first to comment on al-Hidaya, although it is also said that the first was Hamid al-Din Ali ibn Muhammad al-Darir al-Bukhari, died 667 AH. Among those who studied fiqh under him are the author of al-Mi'raj and the author of al-Kifaya. The author of al-Kashf mentioned his death in connection with al-Hidaya, Sharh al-Tamhid, Sharh al-Mufassal, al-Najah, and Sharh Usul al-Bazdawi as 710 AH, but there is some doubt, and mentioned in connection with Sharh Muntakhab al-Ikhsikthi as 711 AH. It is quoted in al-Fawa'id from Madinat al-'Ulum that he died in Rajab 711 AH in Aleppo. Then know that the author of al-Kashf also mentioned al-Nihaya by al-'Ayni, as he said: "And the commentary of Qadi Badr al-Din Mahmud ibn Ahmad al-'Ayni, died 855 AH, on al-Hidaya in volumes, and he named it al-Nihaya." End quote. Mawlana 'Abd al-Awwal al-Jawnpuri mentioned it similarly in Mufid al-Mufti, but this is an error on their part, as is not hidden, because the name of his commentary is al-Binaya (with a ba' followed by a nun, not al-Nihaya). Al-'Ayni himself stated this in the introduction of that commentary.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliographic listing of key works referenced in the commentary on Rasm al-Mufti. The author provides details about each work, its author, and its significance for Hanafi fiqh and ifta. The first entry is al-Nutaf fi al-Fatawa by al-Sughdi, a collection of selected fatwas. The title al-Nutaf al-Hissan means 'the good pluckings' indicating small but valuable pieces of knowledge. The author notes its manuscript in Topkapı and that it was frequently quoted by later Hanafi jurists. The second entry is Nashr al-'Urf by Ibn Abidin (al-Allama al-Shami), which is a treatise on the legal principle of custom ('urf). The author explains its origin as a supplement to his commentary on Rasm al-Mufti, where he felt the need to expand on the couplet about 'urf. The third entry is al-Niqaya, an abridgment of al-Wiqaya by Sadr al-Shari'a al-Asghar. The fourth entry is al-Nihaya by al-Sighnaqi, an early commentary on al-Hidayah. The author emphasizes that al-Sighnaqi's commentary was the first, and clarifies a common mistake: some confuse it with al-'Ayni's commentary named al-Binaya, not al-Nihaya. The passage gives detailed biographical information to avoid such confusion and to establish the authority of these works. This listing serves as a guide for the reader to understand the sources the commentary draws upon.

Key Terms
النتف الحسان (al-Nutaf al-Hissan): A collection of selected fatwas; 'nutaf' means small amounts, 'hissan' means good, so 'good selections'.
نشر العرف (Nashr al-'Urf): A treatise on the role of custom (urf) in building legal rulings, specifically in Hanafi fiqh.
النقاية (al-Niqaya): An abridgment of al-Wiqaya, a core Hanafi text on furu' al-fiqh.
النهاية (al-Nihaya): The first commentary on al-Hidayah, by al-Sighnaqi. Not to be confused with al-'Ayni's al-Binaya.
