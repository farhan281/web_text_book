Necessity Permits Hiring for Worship: The Rasm al-Mufti Rule of Contextual Change
Change in fatwa on hiring for worship due to necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm al-mufti principle that a mufti may change a fatwa due to necessity (darura) when the underlying cause changes, and identify the limits of this exception.

Core Idea
A mufti may change a ruling when necessity threatens religion, but not beyond what is needed.

This passage from the Sharh al-Uqud al-Rasm al-Mufti demonstrates a foundational rasm al-mufti skill: adapting a ruling when circumstances change. The early Hanafi position forbids hiring for acts of worship like teaching Quran, but later jurists permitted it to prevent loss of religious knowledge due to widespread laziness and the cutting of state stipends. You must understand why the change happened, its justification, and its boundaries to apply this rule correctly in your own fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ruling
What is the classic Hanafi position on hiring for acts of worship? List the proofs (Quran, hadith) and the underlying rationale that worship is personal.
The original ruling is that hiring for ta'at is invalid and no wage is obligatory.
2
Identify the necessitating cause
What changed in society that made later jurists reconsider? Specific factors: laziness in religious matters, cutting off of treasury stipends for teachers, fear that Quran would be lost.
Necessity (darura) is fear of losing the religion (din).
3
Analyse the tarjih mechanism
How did later jurists justify departing from the original ruling? They used istihsan (juristic preference) based on necessity, and argued that Abu Hanifa and his companions would have agreed if they were in that time.
This is a classic example of tahkim al-maslahah within the madhhab.
4
Distinguish the scope of the exception
What exactly did they permit? Teaching Quran, adhan, and imamate. They explicitly did not permit hiring for mere recitation without the necessity of teaching, because the necessity is preservation of knowledge.
The exception is tightly tied to the 'illah of preserving the Quran/din.
5
Extract the rasm al-mufti rule
Formulate the rule: when the rationale ('illah) of a ruling is no longer present or when necessity (darura) overrides, a mufti may adopt a different ruling from the madhhab's nawadir or give a new fatwa based on the later scholars' view.
This rule is a pillar of mufti's methodology for dealing with changing times.

Commentary
This passage discusses a critical rasm rule for muftis: when necessity arises, a mufti may depart from an established ruling of the madhhab if the earlier imams would have changed their position in the new context. The original Hanafi position, from the Zahir al-Riwayah, is that hiring for acts of worship (such as teaching Quran, fiqh, adhan, Hajj) is invalid because acts of worship are personal and cannot be performed for a wage; the reward belongs to the one performing them. The text cites the Quranic verse 'And that man shall have nothing but what he strives for' and several hadiths prohibiting taking payment for the Quran. However, later scholars like al-Marghinani, the author of al-Kanz, and Abu al-Layth observed that due to increased laziness and the cutting off of state stipends, if hiring for teaching were not permitted, the Quran would be lost. Therefore, they made an exception based on necessity (darura), allowing payment for teaching Quran, adhan, and imamate. They argued that the earlier imams would have agreed given the circumstances. The passage then warns against extending this exception to 'mere recitation' without genuine necessity. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that a mufti must consider the underlying ‘illah (cause) of a ruling and adapt when the cause changes.

Key Terms
الاستئجار على الطاعات (al-isti'jar 'ala al-ta'at): Hiring a person to perform an act of worship for the hirer's benefit
الضرورة (al-darurah): Necessity that would lead to loss of religious knowledge (specifically Quran) if hiring not permitted
المتأخرون (al-muta'akhkhirun): Later Hanafi jurists after the first three generations (e.g., al-Marghinani, Abu al-Layth)

Conditions for Ifta by Takhrij and the Dispute
Presenting conditions and different opinions on ifta by takhrij

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five qualifications required for a mujtahid fi al-madhhab and apply the Hanafi ruling that its possessor may issue fatwas by takhrij, while distinguishing the four positions in the dispute and identifying the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Only a mujtahid within the madhhab with five specific skills may derive fatwas; mere transmission does not require ijtihad.

This passage from a Hanafi usul work lays out the conditions under which a person may issue fatwas by way of takhrij (derivation from the imam’s principles) rather than just transmitting exact rulings. The central issue is whether a muqallid without deep knowledge can perform takhrij. The text presents the mainstream position (mukhtar) requiring five qualifications, then reviews three opposing views and resolves the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is being permitted? Takhrij-based fatwa. The ruling is that it is permissible only for a qualified mujtahid fi al-madhhab.
2
List the five conditions
The text specifies five qualities a person must have to issue fatwas by takhrij.
These are: acquaintance with sources, eligibility for ijtihad, ability to derive particulars, skill in differentiation/comparison, ability to defend the school.
3
Map the four positions
The passage mentions four views on who may perform takhrij: chosen (mukhtar) requiring the five conditions, permissible only in absence of a mujtahid, absolutely permissible, and absolutely impermissible.
4
Analyze the key distinction
Understand the difference between naql (transmission of exact rulings) and takhrij (derivation). Takhrij requires ijtihad, while naql does not.
The text explicitly says: 'بل في التخريج' and 'فالنقل خارج عن البحث'.
5
Identify the evidence
The main proof for the relied-upon view is the continuous consensus (ijma') of the Muslim community accepting fatwas from such qualified scholars.
The passage says: 'والدليل على الجواز هو الإجماع من كل عصر على قبول مثل هذا النوع من الفتوى'.

Commentary
This passage is about the conditions under which a person may issue fatwas by way of takhrij (deriving rulings from the imam's principles) as opposed to simply transmitting the imam's exact words. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general and specific custom, identify when each can override madhhab texts, and apply the Hanafi hierarchy for custom in fatwa.

Core Idea
General custom can specify texts and analogy; specific custom binds only its locale but cannot oppose the school's primary sources.

Custom (urf) is a pivotal tool in Hanafi fatwa, allowing rulings to adapt to changing circumstances. However, not all custom carries the same weight: the school distinguishes sharply between general and specific custom. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its practical consequences for the mufti, especially when custom appears to conflict with the relied-upon texts of the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of custom
Read the author's classification: general custom (al-urf al-amm) practiced by the majority, and specific custom (al-urf al-khass) limited to a town, region, or group.

2
Distinguish the two scenarios of conflict
The author treats two cases: custom vs. primary legal evidence (Quran/Sunnah/consensus) and custom vs. Zahir al-Riwaya (the madhhab's transmitted opinions).

Ruling on Mufti's Retraction: Effects on Questioner
Ruling on mufti's retraction and its effect on the questioner's actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of mufti's retraction to determine whether a questioner must undo his action based on the timing and the strength of evidence.

Core Idea
A mufti's retraction only requires undoing past actions if they contradict a definitive proof; ijtihad-based actions stand.

This passage from a rasm al-mufti work addresses a critical issue: when a mufti changes his fatwa, what is the obligation of the one who sought and acted upon the original fatwa? The ruling distinguishes between whether the questioner acted before or after the retraction, and whether the fatwa was based on definitive evidence or ijtihad. Understanding this distinction is essential for a mufti's ethical practice and for maintaining legal stability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger event
The mufti gives a fatwa on something, then later retracts it. This retraction can occur before or after the questioner acts.
The mufti's retraction is a change in his ijtihad, not necessarily a correction of error.
2
Scenario A: Retraction known before action
If the questioner knows of the retraction and has not yet acted on the first fatwa, it is not permissible for him to act on it. He must follow the new fatwa.
This is straightforward: the later fatwa supersedes the earlier one for future actions.
3
Scenario B: Acted before retraction, contradictory to qat'i dalil
If the questioner acted on the first fatwa before the retraction, and the original fatwa contradicted a definitive proof (qat'i dalil), the questioner must undo his action. For example, if the fatwa permitted a marriage that was actually prohibited by a clear text, the marriage must be annulled.
The definitive proof overrides the fatwa's validity retroactively because it cannot be a valid ijtihad.
4
Scenario C: Acted before retraction, area of ijtihad
If the matter was one of ijtihad (no definitive proof), the questioner is not obliged to undo his action. The principle 'al-ijtihad la yunqad bil-ijtihad' applies: a ruling based on ijtihad is not overturned by a subsequent ijtihad.
This ensures stability and avoids constant disruption of past actions.
5
Analogous case: change of ijtihad on qibla
The text draws an analogy: if a muqallid changes his ijtihad regarding the direction of qibla during prayer, he must adjust. But this is about ongoing action, not completed action.
The analogy is used to illustrate the principle of following the latest ijtihad for future actions.

Renewing the Question for a Repeated Incident
Whether a mustafti must ask again if the same issue recurs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a mustafti need not renew his question for a recurring issue, and identify the exception based on hardship (mashaqqa).

Core Idea
A fatwa stands for a recurring issue; renewal is not required unless hardship demands an exception.

Imagine you receive a fatwa on a matter, then the same situation arises again. Must you consult the mufti once more? This passage from Adab al-Fatwa presents two scholarly opinions and settles on the sounder view, with a crucial exception from a marginal note. Understanding this rule is essential for both mufti and mustafti to avoid unnecessary burden while maintaining legal certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core question
The passage asks: when the same incident recurs, must the mustafti ask the mufti again? Recognize that this is a procedural question in fatwa practice.
2
State the two views
The first view obligates renewal due to possible change in the mufti's ijtihad. The second view (al-ashah) says renewal is not obligatory because the first ruling is known.
3
Analyze the sounder view's basis
The sounder view relies on certainty: the first fatwa is a known ruling, and mere possibility of change does not remove that certainty. This aligns with the qawida: 'al-yaqin la yazul bi al-shakk'.
4
Examine the marginal note exception
The margin from al-Adhra'i quotes al-Qadi Abu al-Tayyib: renewal is normally required, but if the issue occurs frequently and re-asking is burdensome, the first fatwa suffices. This exception is based on hardship (mashaqqa) invoking the qawida 'al-mashaqqa tajlib al-taysir'.
5
Synthesize the combined Hanafi position
The mufta bihi is that renewal is not required generally. The hardship exception confirms that even if one held the first view, frequent recurrence removes the obligation. Thus, the operative ruling is that the first fatwa stands unless there is clear reason to doubt its applicability.
