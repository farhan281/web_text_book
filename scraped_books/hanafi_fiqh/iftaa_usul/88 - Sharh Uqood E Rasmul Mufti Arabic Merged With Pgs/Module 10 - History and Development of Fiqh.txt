The Six Books of Zahir al-Riwayah and Their Role in Fatwa
Poem listing the six core Hanafi books of manifest narrations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah, explain their authority in Hanafi fatwa, and distinguish them from Nawadir and Nawazil.

Core Idea
Zahir al-Riwayah: the six foundational books of Imam Muhammad that anchor all Hanafi fatwa.

This lesson introduces the core source hierarchy for Hanafi fatwa. The poem on this page lists the six books that constitute Zahir al-Riwayah—the manifest narrations that form the primary authority for rulings. Understanding which books belong to this category and how they relate to later works (Nawadir, Nawazil) is essential for proper fatwa derivation.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the six book names
Use the poem to commit the six titles to memory: al-Jami‘ al-Saghir, al-Jami‘ al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Ziyadat, and al-Mabsut.
The poem numbers them; you can create a mnemonic.
2
Understand the significance of 'tahrir'
The poem says Imam Muhammad 'harara' (refined/edited) the madhhab in these books, meaning he carefully compiled and purified the rulings from weak or extraneous material.
This explains why these books are authoritative.
3
Recognize variations in definition
The commentary notes that some scholars exclude al-Siyar al-Saghir or even both Siyar works from Zahir al-Riwayah. Understand that the list is not universally agreed upon, but the majority accept all six.
These variations are about terminology, not about the reliability of the books themselves.
4
Distinguish Zahir al-Riwayah from Nawadir and Nawazil
After listing the six, the poem mentions Nawadir (rare narrations with weaker chains) and Nawazil (later issues derived by mashayikh). Grasp the hierarchy: Zahir al-Riwayah is the first resort, then Nawadir, then Nawazil.
This hierarchy is a fundamental rule for a mufti.

Commentary
This passage is a poetic summary of the six books that constitute Zahir al-Riwayah – the foundational sources for fatwa in the Hanafi school. The poem, attributed to the author of Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, lists: (1) al-Jami' al-Saghir, (2) al-Jami' al-Kabir, (3) al-Siyar al-Kabir, (4) al-Siyar al-Saghir, (5) al-Ziyadat, and (6) al-Mabsut. These were all authored by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, the disciple of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. The commentary notes differences of opinion: some scholars did not include al-Siyar al-Saghir, and some did not include any part of al-Siyar. Others defined Zahir al-Riwayah as only al-Jami'ayn, al-Mabsut, and al-Ziyadat. Yet others equated it with al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, and al-Siyar al-Kabir. The author also distinguishes Nawadir (rare issues with non-manifest chains) and Nawazil (later issues derived by mashayikh). The footnotes clarify that 'tahrir' means editing, and 'al-Jami' al-Saghir' has over forty commentaries. This lesson is essential for understanding the hierarchy of sources in Hanafi ifta.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six books authored by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani: al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Ziyadat, and al-Mabsut.
النوادر (al-Nawadir): Rare narrations that are not part of Zahir al-Riwayah; their chains are not manifest (ghair zahir).
النوازل (al-Nawazil): Contemporary issues derived by later mashayikh using proofs and qiyas, not directly from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Identifying Major Hanafi Compendia and Correcting a Misconception about al-Mabsut
Listing Hanafi compendia and correcting a misconception about al-Mabsut

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key Hanafi compendia (Mabsutat) and correct the common error that al-Mabsut of al-Sarakhsi is a commentary on al-Mukhtasar rather than al-Kafi.

Core Idea
The absolute al-Mabsut refers to al-Sarakhsi's commentary on al-Kafi, not al-Mukhtasar.

You have likely encountered references to 'al-Mabsut' in Hanafi fiqh works. But which al-Mabsut? This passage traces the major Hanafi compendia and clarifies that when used without qualification, al-Mabsut refers to al-Sarakhsi's extensive commentary on al-Kafi by al-Hakim al-Shahid. A mufti must know his sources precisely to avoid citation errors that can affect the authority of a fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Enumerate the Mabsutat
Read the list of scholars who authored Mabsut compendia: Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, al-Jurjani, al-Khawahirzada, Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani, Abu al-Yusr al-Bazdawi, Ali al-Bazdawi, Nasir al-Din al-Samarqandi, Abu al-Layth. Note that Muhammad's Mabsut is called al-Asl.
The list is not exhaustive but covers the most famous works.
2
Identify the default al-Mabsut
The passage states: 'wa haythu utliqa al-Mabsut, fa al-murad bihi Mabsut al-Sarakhsi' – when used absolutely, it refers to al-Sarakhsi's work, which is a commentary (sharh) on al-Kafi.
This rule is crucial for interpreting references in other texts.
3
Learn about al-Hakim al-Shahid and al-Kafi
Al-Hakim al-Shahid authored al-Kafi, a summary of the books of Muhammad al-Shaybani (Zahir al-Riwaya). He was a judge in Bukhara, a vizier, a hadith scholar, and martyred in prayer in 334 AH.
Al-Kafi is a primary source for the madhhab.
4
Correct the misconception
Some scholars thought al-Mabsut explains al-Mukhtasar. The passage refutes this: al-Sarakhsi frequently quotes from al-Kafi, and his own introduction does not require that he comment on al-Mukhtasar. He actually comments on al-Kafi.
The misconception arose because al-Hakim al-Shahid also wrote al-Mukhtasar.
5
Understand the significance for fatwa
Accurate textual identification is part of adab al-mufti. Citing the wrong base text can misrepresent the authority of a ruling.
Always verify which 'al-Mabsut' is intended.

Commentary
This passage introduces Imam al-Nawawi's monumental work 'al-Majmu', which is a commentary on al-Shirazi's al-Muhadhdhab but far exceeds a mere commentary. The text emphasizes its comprehensive nature: it covers not only Shafi'i fiqh but also the positions of other schools, hadith criticism, Arabic language, history, and even reconciliation of conflicting hadith. The author intended it to be a reference that makes other texts unnecessary for the Shafi'i school. The passage also records the work's incomplete state, with al-Nawawi dying after completing only about a quarter of al-Muhadhdhab. Various scholars lamented its incompletion, and al-Nawawi had allegedly provided his student Ibn al-Attar with materials to finish it, but this never materialized. From a Hanafi ifta' perspective, this example illustrates the ideal of comprehensive scholarship in fatwa work and the importance of citing authoritative sources.

Key Terms
المجموع (al-Majmu'): The title of al-Nawawi's unfinished commentary on al-Muhadhdhab, covering multiple disciplines.
المهذب (al-Muhadhdhab): A Shafi'i fiqh text by al-Shirazi that al-Nawawi based his commentary on.
