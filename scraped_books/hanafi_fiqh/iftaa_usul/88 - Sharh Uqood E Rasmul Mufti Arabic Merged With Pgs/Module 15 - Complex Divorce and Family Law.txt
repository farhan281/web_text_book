Talaq al-Dawr: Circular Divorce and the Hanafi Ruling
Legal dispute over circular divorce condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on talaq al-dawr to resolve conditional divorce statements involving circular conditions.

Core Idea
In circular divorce, the contradictory element is void; both the immediate and conditional divorces take effect.

When a husband says, 'Whenever I divorce you, you are thrice divorced before it,' a logical paradox arises. Some scholars argue that no divorce occurs, but the majority of Hanafi, Maliki, Hanbali, and many Shafi'i jurists rule that both divorces are valid. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position and its justifications from sharia, language, and reason.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wording and the paradox
The husband utters a condition: 'Whenever I divorce you (immediate), you are thrice divorced before it.' This creates a circle: if the immediate divorce occurs, the three divorces must have occurred before it, which negates the immediate divorce.
The term 'talaq al-dawr' refers to this circular condition.
2
State the two opposing positions
Ibn Surayj (Shafi'i) and his followers argue that the logical impossibility means no divorce occurs. The majority (Hanafis, Malikis, Hanbalis, many Shafi'is) hold that both divorces occur.
Identify the schools: Hanafi, Maliki, Hanbali on one side; a minority of Shafi'is on the other.
3
Analyze the majority's three-pronged argument
The ruling is supported by: (1) Sharia: texts permit divorce and this stratagem would nullify it; (2) Language: consequences follow conditions, not precede them; (3) Reason: cause and effect cannot be reversed.
The word 'before' (قبله) is deemed void, so the conditional divorce stands unrestricted.
4
Conclude the Hanafi position and practical effect
The Hanafi school, following the majority, rules that both the immediate and conditional divorces take effect. The wife is irrevocably divorced (three divorces).
This is the mufta bihi position, confirmed by later authorities like Ibn Abidin.

Whenever I divorce you, or whenever my divorce falls upon you, you are thrice divorced before it, they said: So divorce cannot be conceived after that; because if it occurred, it would necessitate what is suspended upon it, which is three. And when the three occur, this immediate divorce becomes impossible. So its occurrence leads to its non-occurrence, and what leads to its non-existence does not exist. This is the choice of Abu al-Abbas ibn Surayj, and a group of Shafi'i scholars agreed with him. The majority of jurists among Malikis, Hanafis, Hanbalis, and many Shafi'is rejected this. They said: This opposes the ruling of sharia, language, and reason. The correct view is that both the immediate and suspended divorces occur. As for sharia, because making something occur in the past is making it occur in the present, and because the texts declare the legitimacy of divorce and accepting this stratagem leads to its abolition. As for language, because consequences occur after the condition or with it, not before it. As for reason, because the particle of condition indicates a cause and the consequence is an effect. It is inconceivable for the effect to precede the cause. So his statement 'before it' is entirely void. Thus, the divorce remains as a consequence of the condition unrestricted by the word 'before.' See: I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in 3/251, Radd al-Muhtar (Matalib Talaq al-Dawr) 3/229 (Karachi print), Fath al-Qadir 3/372 (Kunta print).

Commentary
This passage deals with the famous case of 'talaq al-dawr' (circular divorce). The issue arises when a husband says to his wife: 'Whenever I divorce you, you are thrice divorced before it.' This creates a logical paradox: if the conditional divorce takes effect, it would require the fulfillment of the condition (the immediate divorce), but the immediate divorce cannot exist because the three divorces already occurred. Some scholars, led by Ibn Surayj (Shafi'i), argued that no divorce occurs at all. The majority (Hanafis, Malikis, Hanbalis, and most Shafi'is) ruled that both divorces take effect. Their reasoning is based on sharia (the texts allow divorce and this stratagem would nullify divorce), language (the consequence follows the condition, not precedes it), and reason (cause and effect cannot be reversed). Therefore, the word 'before' is considered ineffective, and the suspended divorce stands without that restriction. The Hanafi position, as indicated in the references, is that the divorce is valid. This case illustrates how apparent logical contradictions are resolved by overriding them with the intended effect.

Key Terms
المنجز (al-munajjaz): Immediate divorce (the one without condition)
المعلق (al-mu'allaq): Suspended/conditional divorce (the three divorces conditioned on the immediate divorce)
طلاق الدور (talaq al-dawr): Circular divorce: a situation where the validity of one condition depends on the existence of another condition, creating a logical circle
