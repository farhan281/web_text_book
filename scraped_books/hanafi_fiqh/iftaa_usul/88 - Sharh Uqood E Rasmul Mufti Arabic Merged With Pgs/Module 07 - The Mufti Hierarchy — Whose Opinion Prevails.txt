The First Tabaqa: Absolute Mujtahids (Mujtahid fi al-Shar')
Begins classification of jurists into seven hierarchical levels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining characteristics of the first level of jurists (mujtahid fi al-shar') and explain why they are not bound by taqlid.

Core Idea
Absolute mujtahids derive Sharia rulings directly from the four sources, unbound by any imam in usul or furu.

The Hanafi classification of jurists into seven levels is essential for muftis to weigh opinions correctly. At the apex stands the absolute mujtahid, who establishes legal principles and derives rulings without following any other scholar. This lesson examines the first level, its exemplars, and its place in the hierarchy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of tabaqat al-fuqaha
Understand that the classification helps muftis prioritize opinions: higher tabaqa opinions override lower ones.
This is a foundational tool for tarjih (preferencing) in Hanafi fatwa.
2
Define the first level
The mujtahid fi al-shar' is one who establishes usul and derives furu directly from the four evidences (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) without following any other scholar in either usul or furu.
Taqlid is forbidden for them; their rulings are primary sources.
3
Recognize the examples given
The four Imams (Abu Hanifa, Malik, Shafi'i, Ahmad) and others like al-Thawri and al-Awza'i are mentioned.
These are considered absolute mujtahids by the author.
4
Address the dispute about existence
Some later scholars (e.g., Ibn 'Abidin) denied the continued existence of absolute mujtahids, but the author affirms their existence and includes them in the classification.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the majority of Hanafis uphold the first tabaqa.

Commentary
This passage introduces a classification of jurists (fuqaha) into seven hierarchial levels, known as tabaqat al-fuqaha. The author begins with the highest level: the absolute mujtahid (mujtahid mutlaq) who does not follow any other scholar in either usul or furu, but derives rules directly from the four sources (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas). Examples given are the four Imams (Abu Hanifa, Malik, Shafi'i, Ahmad) and others like al-Thawri, al-Awza'i, and Ibn Abi Layla. This classification is essential for the mufti to know whose opinions carry more weight when performing tarjih. The text notes that the commonly mentioned seven levels include the absolute mujtahid, while some scholars like al-Laknawi restrict to five levels excluding the absolute ones. The author cautions against the mistake of Ibn 'Abidin (Sahib al-Durr al-Mukhtar) who denied the existence of absolute mujtahids, asserting that the first level certainly exists.

The Second Layer: Mujtahidun fi al-Madhhab and the Status of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Defining the second layer of Hanafi jurists and debating the true status of its exemplars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mujtahidun fi al-madhhab and independent mujtahids, apply the criteria to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, and identify the practical implications for fatwa hierarchy in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad are mujtahidun fi al-madhhab in usul adherence, but their independent ijtihad means their opinions carry unique weight in Hanafi fatwa.

The classification of jurists within the Hanafi school is essential for understanding the hierarchy of legal authority. The second layer, mujtahidun fi al-madhhab, includes scholars who follow Imam Abu Hanifa's usul but may differ in furu'. However, the status of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the two most prominent students, is debated: were they bound to Abu Hanifa's usul or independent mujtahids? This lesson explores the definitions, the dispute, and the practical significance for modern fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Define the Criteria
Identify the defining characteristics of a mujtahid fi al-madhhab from the base text: capable of deriving rulings from evidences according to the principles established by the teacher, and generally following the teacher in usul even if differing in furu'.
This definition sets the baseline for the dispute.
2
Examine the Base Text's Claim
The base text includes Abu Yusuf and Muhammad in this layer, stating they are 'qadirun ala istikhraj al-ahkam' and 'yuqallidunahu fi qawaid al-usul.'
This is the standard classification.
3
Analyze the Commentator's Objection
The commentator argues that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad frequently contradicted Abu Hanifa even in usul principles (e.g., on conflicting evidences, metaphor). He cites al-Dabusi's 'Tasis al-Nazar' and al-Kardari's 'Radd al-Mankhul' to show they were independent mujtahids by Abu Hanifa's own instruction.
The objection is based on historical records of their disagreements.
4
Understand the Reconciliation
The commentator reconciles by stating they follow Abu Hanifa in most usul and attribute themselves to his madhhab out of reverence. Their differences were because indications appeared to them contrary to his proof, and they acted on Abu Hanifa's command to follow their ijtihad.
This preserves the unity of the madhhab while acknowledging their independence.
5
Apply to Fatwa Hierarchy
Determine the practical impact: The mufta bihi position is that they are mujtahidun fi al-madhhab, but their opinions carry significant weight. In cases of intra-madhhab disagreement, a mufti must consider the strength of their evidence and whether their view aligns with the majority of the school.
This step links classification to actual fatwa practice.

Commentary
This passage from Rasm al-Mufti defines the second layer of Hanafi jurists: the mujtahidun fi al-madhhab (mujtahids within the madhhab), exemplified by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The author describes them as capable of deriving rulings from the primary sources (the 'mentioned evidences') based on the principles laid down by their teacher, Abu Hanifa. Even if they disagree with him in some subsidiary rulings (furu'), they follow him in the principles of usul. However, the commentator (the writer of the hashiya) objects to this classification, arguing that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad did often contradict Abu Hanifa even in usul, to the extent that it is said they differed in two-thirds of the madhhab. They had their own distinct principles on matters such as the treatment of conflicting evidences, metaphor, etc. The commentator cites al-Dabusi's 'Tasis al-Nazar' as a source for these differences. He then quotes Shams al-A'imma al-Kardari, who explains that Abu Hanifa himself acknowledged their ijtihad and commanded leaving his own opinion when his proof was not apparent. Thus, they are actually independent mujtahids, but out of respect they followed his method and attributed themselves to his madhhab. The commentator concludes by noting that their ijtihad agreed with his in most usul, reconciling the apparent contradiction. This discussion is crucial for understanding the hierarchy of Hanafi authority and the nature of ijtihad within the madhhab.

Key Terms
مُجْتَهِدٌ فِي الْمَذْهَب (mujtahid fi al-madhhab): A jurist who exercises ijtihad within the framework of Imam Abu Hanifa's principles, capable of deriving rulings from the sources according to those principles, and generally follows the imam in usul even if differing in furu'.
قواعد الأصول (qawa'id al-usul): The fundamental principles of jurisprudence that govern the derivation of rulings, such as the treatment of amm, khas, etc.

The Third Layer: Mujtahidun fi al-Masa'il and the Debate on Their Independence
Defining the third layer of Hanafi jurists: mujtahidun fi al-masa'il

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining characteristics of the mujtahidun fi al-masa'il layer and evaluate the tension between the classical definition and the historical practice.

Core Idea
The third layer is bound to the Imam's principles, yet history shows they often challenged his rulings—revealing the dynamic nature of ijtihad within the madhhab.

The Hanafi school classifies its jurists into layers, each with a different scope of ijtihad. The third layer, mujtahidun fi al-masa'il, is said to be limited to deriving rulings from Abu Hanifa's principles, unable to contradict him. However, this characterization is debated, as historical records show significant independence. In this lesson, we'll parse the definition and the counterarguments from the commentary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining traits of the third layer
Extract from the base text the three key characteristics: (1) they exercise ijtihad in issues with no transmitted ruling from Abu Hanifa, (2) they cannot contradict him in usul or furu', (3) they derive rulings based on his principles.
2
Examine the commentator's critique
Read the marginal note that challenges the claim of inability to contradict. Note the examples provided: al-Karkhi's views on 'amm ba'd al-takhsis and the crescent of Dhu al-Hijja, and al-Tahawi's statement about arguing with evidence.
3
Analyze the implications for ijtihad within the madhhab
Reflect on how this tension affects the understanding of the Hanafi hierarchy. Are these jurists truly bound, or does the definition idealize their role? Consider both perspectives as valid within the school.
4
Synthesize the operative hierarchy for fatwa
Determine the mufta bihi position: these jurists are authoritative within the madhhab, and their independent rulings (takhrij) are recorded and may be relied upon, even if they differ from Abu Hanifa, as long as they follow his principles.

Commentary
This passage defines the third layer of Hanafi jurists: the mujtahidun fi al-masa'il (mujtahids in specific issues). According to the base text, these jurists cannot contradict the Imam in either usul or furu', but they derive rulings for new cases not explicitly found in his transmitted rulings, based on his principles. However, the commentator challenges this characterization, citing actual examples like al-Khassaf, al-Tahawi, and al-Karkhi who did contradict Abu Hanifa in both usul and furu'. He argues that their disagreements are numerous and documented. For instance, al-Karkhi held unique views on the general after specification and on solitary reports. Despite this, the commentator acknowledges that these scholars are still considered part of the madhhab, as they refer to themselves as followers of Abu Hanifa. This shows a tension between the idealized hierarchy and the actual practice of ijtihad among later Hanafis. The commentator also provides parallels from other schools for those who reached ijtihad within their madhhab.

Key Terms
مُجْتَهِدٌ فِي الْمَسَائِل (mujtahid fi al-masa'il): A jurist who exercises ijtihad in specific legal issues for which there is no explicit ruling from the imam, but remains bound by his usul and cannot contradict him in principles or branches.
تخريج (takhrij): The process of deriving rulings for new cases by applying the principles of the imam to analogous situations.

The Fourth Layer: Ashab al-Takhrij min al-Muqallidin
Defining the fourth layer of Hanafi jurists: ashab al-takhrij

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fourth layer of Hanafi jurists (ashab al-takhrij) and distinguish them from higher layers in terms of authority and methodology.

Core Idea
Ashab al-takhrij are muqallids who derive rulings within the madhhab, but lack independent ijtihad.

The Hanafi school classifies jurists into layers based on their juristic capability. The fourth layer consists of ashab al-takhrij, muqallids who can extend the madhhab's rulings to new cases by applying its principles. This lesson defines their role, capacity, and how they fit into the fatwa hierarchy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the layer's label
The passage states: 'The fourth: the layer of the people of takhrij from among the muqallids.' Identify the Arabic term and its English equivalent.
The example given is al-Razi (al-Jassas).
2
Understand 'takhrij' in this context
'Takhrij' here means deriving subsidiary rulings by applying the madhhab's established principles and precedents, not independent ijtihad.
This is a form of analogical extension within the madhhab.
3
Distinguish from higher layers
Contrast with the third layer (mujtahidun fi al-masa'il) who can perform limited ijtihad. Ashab al-takhrij are muqallids who rely on the imam's principles.
They cannot contradict the imam's methodology.
4
Place within the fatwa hierarchy
Their rulings are subordinate to those of higher layers. A mufti should consult their works but ultimately follow the mufta bihi from above.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi fatwa hierarchy to determine the mufta bihi ruling and identify when the minority view (takhayyur) is considered.

Core Idea
When issuing a fatwa, follow the Imam's choice first; then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar and Hasan.

The Hanafi school has a precise method for preferring opinions when the early imams differ. This hierarchy ensures consistency and authority in fatwa. The passage presents the order as an anchor for memorization, with the primary rule: follow the Imam's choice (ikhtiyar); if absent, move down the list. Minority views exist but are not relied upon.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Imam's ikhtiyar
First check if Imam Abu Hanifa has an explicit choice (ikhtiyar) on the issue. If yes, that is the mufta bihi ruling.
The Imam's choice is given absolute precedence, even if his companions disagree.
2
If absent, move to Abu Yusuf
When no ikhtiyar of Abu Hanifa is known, adopt the choice of Abu Yusuf (Ya'qub), his senior companion.
Imam Muhammad refers to Abu Yusuf by his kunyah except when alongside Abu Hanifa.
3
Then Muhammad al-Shaybani
If Abu Yusuf's choice is also unknown, the opinion of Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani is given precedence.
He is the next in rank after Abu Yusuf.
4
Then Zufar and Hasan ibn Ziyad
If none of the above have a known choice, follow Zufar and Hasan ibn Ziyad. Their statements are considered equal in rank, though some sources (al-Nahr) give priority to Hasan after Zufar.
This completes the standard hierarchy.
5
Note minority views
The passage mentions two minority views: al-takhayyur (mufti has freedom when sahibayn oppose the Imam) and preference based on stronger evidence. These are not the relied-upon positions.
The relied-upon view is to follow the hierarchy strictly; takhayyur is rejected by later Hanafi scholars.

Two Meanings of the Imams' Statement on Fatwa
Explaining two interpretations of a key maxim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two interpretations of the imams' maxim 'no one may give fatwa until knowing our source' and apply each to the appropriate mufti tier.

Core Idea
The imams' maxim on fatwa has two meanings: one for knowing proof before any fatwa, another for knowing principles before takhrij.

The Hanafi masters often repeated a cautious maxim: 'It is not permissible for anyone to give fatwa with our sayings until he knows from where we said it.' But what does this actually require? In this lesson, we unpack two distinct interpretations that resolve apparent tensions in the maxim and clarify who is addressed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its apparent meaning
The maxim 'لا يحل لأحد أن يفتي بقولنا حتى يعلم من أين قلنا' appears, on its face, to require every mufti to know the evidence behind any Hanafi ruling before issuing a fatwa. This seems to impose a heavy burden on all muftis.
2
Understand the first interpretation
The first interpretation takes the maxim at face value but restricts its application to the 'mujtahid within the madhhab' (المجتهد في المذهب). For such a scholar, knowing the evidence and its strength/weakness is required before giving fatwa, even on a clearly stated ruling. A mere muqallid is not burdened with this.
This interpretation addresses the second tier (mujtahid fi al-madhhab) in the Hanafi hierarchy.
3
Understand the second interpretation
The second interpretation applies the maxim to cases of takhrij (derivation) of rulings for new incidents. Anyone who wishes to derive (takhrij) a ruling from the school's principles must know the basis and origin of the existing sayings. This addresses the higher tiers (third, fourth, fifth) who engage in takhrij.
Ordinary muftis who only transmit established rulings are not required to perform takhrij.
4
Note the objection and its answer
The author (Ibn Nujaym or similar) raised an objection to the first meaning: how can a muqallid give fatwa without knowing the evidence? The answer, summarized in the passage, is that the muqallid is not addressed by the maxim; he may transmit rulings if he is qualified to understand and apply texts.
This resolves the apparent contradiction between the maxim and common practice.
5
Apply the fatwa from Ibn al-Shilbi
Ibn al-Shilbi's fatwa reinforces that judges and muftis must not deviate from Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling unless later mashayikh have explicitly adopted another position. This shows that the maxim does not permit arbitrary departure from the school's established rulings.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the widely quoted maxim attributed to the Hanafi imams: 'It is not permissible for anyone to give fatwa with our sayings until he knows from where we said it.' The author presents two distinct interpretations. The first interpretation takes the statement at face value: a mufti must know the evidence for a ruling before issuing a fatwa, even if the ruling is clearly stated in the school. However, this applies only to a 'mujtahid within the madhhab' (المجتهد في المذهب), not to a mere muqallid. The muqallid is not required to know the evidence; he may transmit rulings as long as he is capable of understanding and applying texts. The second interpretation restricts the statement to cases of takhrij (deriving rulings for novel issues from the school's principles). In that context, a mufti must know the basis of the existing rulings to properly extend them. This interpretation addresses the higher tiers of muftis (third, fourth, fifth). The passage also includes a fatwa from Ibn al-Shilbi emphasizing that judges and muftis must not deviate from Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling unless the later mashayikh have explicitly adopted another position. The overall lesson teaches that the famous maxim is not a blanket prohibition but a nuanced guideline that depends on the mufti's level of qualification.

Core Idea
An affiliated mufti can be a mujtahid within the madhhab, following methodology, not blindly.

In the hierarchy of muftis, after the independent mujtahid, we have the non-independent mufti. For centuries, fatwa has been issued by those affiliated to the four imams. The author of Uqud Rasm al-Mufti describes four states of such a mufti. The first state is when the mufti is not a taqlid follower of his imam but rather follows his method of ijtihad. The passage notes that for a long time, no independent mufti existed, so fatwa shifted to affiliated muftis.

This means he does not blindly follow his imam's rulings; he has the qualifications of ijtihad.

The Continuation of Independent Ijtihad After al-Shafi'i
Abu ‘Amr and al-Laknawi argue that independent mujtahids continued after al-Shafi‘i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy in the claim that absolute ijtihad ended after al-Shafi'i and provide historical evidence for the continuation of independent mujtahids.

Core Idea
Ijtihad is a divine mercy that never ceases; independent mujtahids existed after al-Shafi'i.

This passage addresses a misconception held by some methodologists that no independent mujtahid (mujtahid mustaqill) appeared after Imam al-Shafi'i. Abu 'Amr and Imam al-Laknawi refute this claim, arguing from both principle and historical evidence. Understanding this refutation is crucial for a mufti to correctly assess the hierarchy of ijtihad and avoid overly restrictive views that would deny the possibility of fresh legal reasoning in later eras.

Guided Path

Read the passage carefully. The text reports that some scholars claimed 'no independent mujtahid existed after the era of al-Shafi'i.' Note how this claim is presented and who is said to have made it.
The claim is attributed to 'some of our scholars of Usul'.

The passage quotes al-Muzani reporting that al-Shafi'i prohibited taqlid of himself and others, encouraging the seeker to examine the evidence. This shows that al-Shafi'i himself did not consider his own ijtihad as final or exclusive.
This quote supports the possibility of independent ijtihad after al-Shafi'i.
3
Analyze al-Laknawi's Refutation
Al-Laknawi points out that the claim of cessation of ijtihad contradicts the nature of ijtihad as a divine mercy (rahmah) that is not restricted to any time or person. This refutation implies that the rank of absolute ijtihad (mujtahid mustaqill) is theoretically possible in any era. In usul al-iftaa, this affects how a mufti views his own capacity and the authority of later scholars.
The hierarchy includes mujtahid mustaqill, mujtahid muntasib, and muqallid.

Commentary
This passage combines two interrelated discussions on the possibility of independent ijtihad after the early imams. The first part, attributed to Abu ‘Amr (likely a Shafi‘i scholar), refutes the absolute claim that taqlid was absent among the early scholars; he also reports a claim that no independent mujtahid existed after al-Shafi‘i. The text then reproduces al-Muzani’s statement from the introduction to his Mukhtasar, where he cites al-Shafi‘i’s prohibition of taqlid of himself and others, encouraging the seeker to examine the evidence personally. The passage further discusses the significance of al-Muzani’s Mukhtasar and its commentaries. The second part is a lengthy footnote by Imam al-Laknawi, who clarifies that the claim of the cessation of independent ijtihad is false. He argues that ijtihad is a divine mercy not limited to any era, and he provides examples of independent mujtahids after the four imams, such as Abu Thawr, Dawud al-Zahiri, and Imam al-Bukhari. The footnote ends by referring to al-Nafi‘ al-Kabir. The overall message is that absolute ijtihad remains possible and that the claim of its complete cessation is erroneous, though the consensus of the ummah may acknowledge certain mujtahids as independent. This passage serves to guard against the misconception that ijtihad ended with the four founding imams, a critical point in usul al-iftaa for understanding the ranks of mujtahids.

The Restricted Mujtahid: Definition and Fatwa Authority
Definition of muqayyad mujtahid and rules of his fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions of a muqayyad mujtahid and explain the ruling on his fatwa within the Hanafi school, including who the mustafti follows.

Core Idea
A muqayyad mujtahid derives rulings within his imam's school; his fatwa is taqlid of the imam, not himself.

In the Hanafi hierarchy of muftis, the 'restricted mujtahid' occupies a key position: he is not fully independent but exercises ijtihad within the framework of his imam's principles. This lesson unpacks his definition, conditions, and the legal effect of his fatwa, including an important debate about whether his activity fulfills the collective duty. Understanding this level is essential for tracing the authority of fatwas issued by later Hanafi masters like al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category
Recognize that this passage defines the second type of mufti: the restricted mujtahid (al-mujtahid al-muqayyad). Contrast him with the absolute independent mujtahid.
The muqayyad is not fully independent; he is bound by his imam's usul.
2
List the conditions
Extract the required qualifications: deep knowledge of fiqh and usul, mastery of qiyas and istinbat, but with a deficiency in some tools (like hadith or Arabic) that prevents full independence.
This deficiency makes his ijtihad contain a 'taint of taqlid'.
3
Analyze the effect on fatwa
Understand that the muqayyad takes his imam's texts as authoritative sources, deriving rulings similarly to an independent mujtahid, but the mustafti (seeker) does taqlid of the imam, not the muqayyad.
The fatwa of the muqayyad is considered an expression of the imam's school.
4
Evaluate the debate on fard kifayah
Consider the two views: the apparent position that the muqayyad does not fulfill the collective obligation, and Abu Amr's view that he does, at least for the act of fatwa.
This ikhtilaf hinges on whether the deficiency of taqlid prevents full attainment of the communal duty.

Commentary
This passage from Imam al-Nawawi's Adab al-Fatwa discusses the second category of mufti: the restricted mujtahid (al-mujtahid al-muqayyad). The passage type is a classification with conditions and rulings. The author defines this level as one who works within the framework of his imam's school, independently establishing its principles but not going beyond them. He is knowledgeable in fiqh and its foundations, skilled in qiyas and istinbat, but may lack full mastery of hadith or Arabic, thus having a trace of taqlid. Such a mufti takes his imam's texts as principles for derivation, similar to how a mutlaq mujtahid uses the primary texts. He may even rely solely on his imam's proof without seeking opposing evidence. This describes the 'ashab al-wujuh' (companions of faces) in the Hanafi school, like al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi. The fatwa of this mufti is considered taqlid of the imam, not of himself. There is a dispute whether issuing fatwa at this level fulfills the collective obligation (fard al-kifayah): the apparent view says no, but Abu Amr holds it does for the act of fatwa itself. The passage then affirms that the muqayyad may become independent in specific issues, and he may give fatwa by deriving rulings where his imam has no text. The mustafti (seeker of fatwa) is considered a muqallid of the imam, not of the mufti. The footnote elaborates on the permissibility of taqlid of a deceased mujtahid, citing two positions from the Hanbali and Shafi'i schools, with the preponderant view being permissibility, as statements do not die with their speakers.

مجتهد مقيد (mujtahid muqayyad): A mujtahid who is restricted to the principles and rulings of his imam's school, deriving rulings within that framework without exceeding it.
أصحاب الوجوه (ashab al-wujuh): Scholars whose statements and derivations are considered authoritative 'faces' (wujuh) within the madhhab, also called 'ashab al-takhrij'.
تقليد الميت (taqlid al-mayyit): The practice of following the legal opinion of a deceased mujtahid without verifying it through evidence.

The Third Category of Muftis: Later Compilers
Describing qualifications of third-category muftis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the qualifications and limitations of the third category of muftis (the later compilers) and evaluate their role in preserving and expanding the Hanafi madhhab.

Core Idea
The later compilers were not independent mujtahids, but their work in organizing, editing, and preferring opinions made the madhhab accessible for later generations.

In the previous lesson, you learned about the mujtahids within the madhhab (ashab al-wujuh) who could derive new rulings. The passage now describes the next tier of muftis: those who did not reach that rank but were still expert jurists. These 'later compilers' authored the foundational texts we rely on today. Let's examine their exact qualifications and limitations as outlined by Imam al-Nawawi.

Guided Path
1
Locate the third category in the hierarchy
Recall the mufti hierarchy: mujtahid mutlaq → ashab al-wujuh (mujtahid fi al-madhhab) → this third category. Note that they are below the ashab al-wujuh but above the general muqallid.
The passage states: 'lam yablugh rutbat ashab al-wujuh'.
2
Identify their positive qualifications
They are described as 'faqih al-nafs', memorizer of the madhhab, knowledgeable of proofs, and capable of taswir, tahrir, taqrir, tamhid, tazyeef, and tarjeeh. Understand each of these six actions.
The commentary elaborates on each verb in footnotes.
3
Understand their limitations
They fall short of ashab al-wujuh in three areas: memorization of the madhhab, practice in istinbat (derivation), or knowledge of usul and other tools. They lack the ability for full takhreej.
The phrase 'wa lam yalhaqu alladhina qablahum fi al-takhreej' is key.
4
Recognize their contributions
Despite limitations, they organized the madhhab, authored works (e.g., al-Quduri for Hanafis), and issued fatwas with expansion, using qiyas beyond the obvious. They preserved the madhhab for later centuries.
The passage acknowledges their fatwas were expansive.
5
Examine examples and significance
Al-Quduri (Hanafi) and al-Muzani (Shafi'i) are cited as exemplars. Understand how these compilers enabled later scholars to rely on their works.
This connects to the importance of the rasm al-mufti tradition.

Third and Fourth Categories of Muftis: Abilities and Limits
Describing the third and fourth ranks of muftis and their jurisprudential capacities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the third and fourth categories of muftis, identify their qualifications and limitations, and apply the rules for giving fatwa based on their rank.

Core Idea
A mufti's rank determines his scope: the third can weigh and prefer, the fourth must rely on transmitted texts.

In this passage from Imam al-Nawawi's Ādāb al-Fatwā, we continue the classification of muftis beyond the higher ranks of mujtahids. The third and fourth categories represent the majority of practicing muftis in later centuries. Understanding their capacities and legal boundaries is essential for anyone who wishes to issue fatwas or evaluate the authority of a given fatwa. We will parse the characteristics of each group, the extent of their ijtihad, and the strict conditions that govern their practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third category mufti
Locate the description of the third category: one who does not reach the rank of aṣḥāb al-wujūh but is a faqīh al-nafs. Note the key abilities: he can depict, edit, establish, prepare, refute, and weigh/prefer. Also note his deficiencies: shortcoming in memorization, practice in istinbāṭ, or knowledge of uṣūl.
This category includes many later scholars like al-Quduri (Hanafi) and al-Muzani (Shafi'i).
2
Identify the fourth category mufti
Find the description of the fourth category: he memorizes and transmits the madhhab, understands clear and problematic issues, but is weak in presenting evidences and editing analogies. His fatwa is based on transmitted texts and derivations of mujtahids within the madhhab.
He is essentially a transmitter; he has no independent ijtihad.
3
Analyze the limits of analogy for the fourth category
The fourth category may only analogize a new case to a transmitted one if the meaning is clearly identical without great intellectual effort, or if it falls under an established principle (ḍābiṭ). Otherwise, he must refrain from giving fatwa.
Imam al-Haramayn notes such situations are rare due to the comprehensiveness of the madhhab.
4
Extract the common condition: faqīh al-nafs
Both categories share the requirement of being faqīh al-nafs (natural juristic acumen). This is the minimum threshold for issuing fatwa beyond mere transmission. Also, for both, it suffices to have most of the madhhab committed to memory with the ability to quickly access the rest.
Without this natural aptitude, one is not qualified even for these lower ranks.

Commentary
This passage from Imam al-Nawawi's Ādāb al-Fatwā continues the classification of muftis by defining the third and fourth categories. The third category comprises those who do not reach the rank of aṣḥāb al-wujūh (the second category, who are mujtahidūn fī al-madhhab) but are nevertheless faqīh al-nafs, meaning they possess a natural juristic aptitude. They have memorized the madhhab (i.e., the corpus of rulings from their imam and prominent disciples, such as Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad), know its proofs, and can articulate them. They can depict issues, edit texts, establish proofs, prepare principles, refute weak opinions, and weigh/ preponderate among different narrations. However, they fall short due to incomplete memorization, less practice in istinbāṭ (deduction), or insufficient knowledge of uṣūl al-fiqh and auxiliary disciplines (like tafsir, hadith, Arabic). The author notes this describes many later scholars (e.g., al-Quduri for Hanafis, al-Muzani for Shafi'is) who organized the madhhab and authored foundational works. Their fatwas are extensive and they engage in qiyās beyond the simple qiyās jaliyy, but their fatwas do not carry the same weight as those of aṣḥāb al-wujūh regarding authoritative attachment to the madhhab.

The fourth category is lower: such a person memorizes and transmits the madhhab, understands clear and difficult issues, but is weak in presenting evidences and formulating analogies. He must rely solely on transmitted texts from the imam and the derivations of the mujtahids of the madhhab. For new cases, if he finds a transmitted ruling whose meaning clearly covers the new case (without great intellectual effort), he may analogize and give fatwa. Similarly, if the case falls under an established principle (ḍābiṭ) in the madhhab, he may proceed. Otherwise, he must refrain. Imam al-Haramayn notes such cases are rare because the madhhab is comprehensive. The essential qualification for both categories is being faqīh al-nafs with a good portion of fiqh. Abu 'Amr adds that it suffices for them to have most of the madhhab in memory, with ability to quickly find the rest. Notably, the commentary at the end clarifies that such a person has no ijtihad; they are like transmitters, and their fatwa is accepted only if they meet the conditions of a reliable narrator (like in hadith).

تخريج (takhrīj): Deriving subsidiary rulings from the imam's established principles or extending the madhhab to new cases.

Nullifying a Mufti's Retraction When It Conflicts with the Imam's Nass
A rule on nullifying a mufti's retraction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a muqallid mufti must nullify his retraction if his original fatwa definitively contradicts his imam's explicit text, and explain the rationale using the analogy with an independent mujtahid.

Core Idea
For a muqallid mufti, the imam's nass is as binding as revelation for a mujtahid.

This lesson addresses a critical rule in fatwa procedure: when a mufti who follows a specific imam retracts a fatwa, but then discovers that his original fatwa actually contradicts the imam's explicit text, he cannot simply abandon his fatwa; instead, he must nullify his retraction. This protects the authority of the imam's nass for the muqallid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Actor and Context
Recognize that the rule applies to a mufti who is a muqallid (follower) of a particular imam, not an absolute mujtahid. He issues fatwas based on that imam's madhhab.
2
Recognize the Sequence of Events
The mufti first gives a fatwa, then later retracts it because he (mistakenly) believes it contradicts his imam's madhhab. However, he then realizes that the original fatwa does in fact definitively (قطغا) contradict the imam's explicit text (نص مذهب إمامه).
The retraction was based on a perceived contradiction; the realization is that the contradiction actually exists.
3
Apply the Obligatory Ruling
The text states 'وجب نقضه' – it is obligatory (واجب) to nullify the retraction. This means the original fatwa stands, and the retraction is invalid.
The ruling is not merely recommended; it is wajib.
4
Understand the Rationale (Ta'lil)
The reason is that for a muqallid, the imam's explicit text has the same authoritative weight as the text of the Lawgiver (Qur'an/Sunnah) for an independent mujtahid. Just as an independent mujtahid cannot go against a clear revelatory text, the muqallid cannot go against his imam's nass.
This underscores the binding nature of the imam's madhhab for followers.
5
Derive the Practical Consequence
The mufti must maintain the original fatwa, even if it appears to contradict his earlier retraction. The retraction is legally void, and he must adhere to the imam's text.
If the fatwa had not actually contradicted the imam, the retraction might have been valid.

Two opinions on taqlid of a deceased mufti with tarjih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on taqlid of a deceased mufti and evaluate the two opinions presented in the text.

Core Idea
A deceased mufti's opinion remains valid; schools outlive their founders, like witness testimony.

This lesson addresses a practical question in fatwa practice: may a layperson continue to follow the opinion of a scholar after their death? The text presents two views, with the correct Hanafi position affirming continuity. Understanding this ruling is essential for muftis who rely on classical works for fatwa.

1
Identify the legal issue
The text asks whether taqlid of a deceased mufti is permissible. Note that this concerns a layperson following the opinion of a scholar who has passed away.
2

For the first view: reason 1 – madhhabs survive their founders; reason 2 – analogy with witness testimony not invalidated by death. For the second view: reason – death removes qualification (ahliyyah), like sinfulness.

Commentary
This passage addresses the controversial question of whether a layperson may continue to follow the opinion of a deceased mufti (taqlid al-mayyit). The text presents two opinions. The first opinion, which is considered correct (al-sahih), argues that it is permissible. The reasoning is that legal schools (madhahib) survive their founders; they are counted in consensus (ijma) and disagreement (ikhtilaf) even after the founders' death. An analogy (qiyas) is drawn: a witness's death after giving testimony does not invalidate the testimony, unlike the case where the witness becomes sinful (which does invalidate). The second opinion, which the author deems weak, holds that taqlid of a deceased is impermissible because the mufti's qualification (ahliyyah) ceases with death, similar to a sinful person (whose testimony is rejected). The author explicitly says this opinion is weak (da'if), especially in later times (hadhihi al-a'sar). This is a practical ruling in rasm al-mufti, ensuring continuity in fatwa practice.
