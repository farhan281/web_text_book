Prohibited Degrees of Marriage (Qarāba Māniʿa)
Ruling on prohibited relatives for marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify relatives into prohibited and permissible categories for marriage according to Hanafi law.

Core Idea
Marriage is absolutely forbidden with ascendants, descendants, and siblings' descendants, but permitted with first cousins.

This lesson presents the foundational Hanafi rules on prohibited degrees of marriage (qarāba māniʿa). These rules are derived from the Quran and Sunnah and are essential for any mufti advising on marriage contracts. Understanding these categories prevents invalid marriages and ensures compliance with Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of relatives
The text divides relatives into three groups: ascendants (uṣūl), descendants (furūʿ), and descendants of parents (siblings and their children). Marriage with any of these is absolutely forbidden.
This includes grandparents, grandchildren, and nieces/nephews.
2
Note the exception for first cousins
First cousins (children of paternal uncles/aunts and maternal uncles/aunts) are permissible. The text explicitly states: 'the daughter of an uncle and the daughter of an aunt are permissible to marry.'
This exception is often misunderstood; many assume all cousins are prohibited.
3
Apply the rule to in-laws
After marriage, each spouse is prohibited from marrying the ascendants or descendants of the other. For example, a man cannot marry his wife's mother or daughter.
This prohibition is based on marital relationship, not blood.

Polygamy: A Balanced Permission
Note on the permissibility of polygamy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on polygamy as a permissibility conditioned by justice and social realities.

Core Idea
Polygamy is permitted but not absolute; it is allowed within limits and conditions considering social realities.

This lesson examines the Islamic legal stance on polygamy, which the author describes as a middle path between absolute prohibition and absolute permissibility. Hanafi law adopts this balanced approach, allowing polygamy only when conditions of justice and ability are met.

Guided Path
1
Understand the middle position
Islamic law neither forbids polygamy outright nor allows it unconditionally. It permits it within limits, recognizing social realities and potential necessities.
The default ruling (aṣl) is permissibility based on Quran 4:3, but it is conditioned.
2
Identify the conditions implied
The text mentions 'limits and conditions' and 'social facts and compelling necessities'. The key condition is justice among co-wives (Quran 4:129). If a man fears inability to be just, he should limit to one.
Detailed conditions are not in the passage; they are elaborated in fiqh works.
3
Apply in fatwa context
A mufti must assess the individual’s ability to be just and the necessity behind polygamy. The ruling can vary from recommended to obligatory depending on circumstances.
The mufti should also consider legal restrictions in non-Muslim countries.

Consequences of Marriage: Duties and Rights
Financial and non-financial duties arising from marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five key consequences of marriage under Hanafi law and classify them as financial or non-financial obligations.

Core Idea
Marriage imposes financial duty on the husband only; the wife's role is domestic, and lineage is protected even in invalid marriage.

This passage from Mustafa al-Zarqa's al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm serves as a foundational rasm (rule) for marriage consequences. It is a concise memorization anchor that every mufti must internalize before issuing fatwas on marital obligations. Understanding these duties is essential for giving proper legal guidance in family matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage first divides consequences into financial and non-financial obligations. Read the first sentence and note that these are the two overarching types produced by marriage according to Shari'ah.
This is the main organizing principle of the passage.
2
List the financial obligations
Under 'a)', we learn that the man is responsible for maintenance (nafaqa) of wife and children to a customary standard. Also, the wife has no financial duty—so she can focus on home management and child-rearing.
Memorize: husband provides nafaqa; wife has no financial obligation.
3
List the non-financial obligations
Under 'b)', we find: the wife must obey her husband within Shari'ah limits, she must follow him and live with him, and both spouses must treat each other with kindness and affection.
These are mutual and reciprocal duties.
4
Cover lineage (nasab)
The passage then states that children's lineage is established from both valid (sahih) and invalid (fasid) marriages to protect their rights. It also prohibits adoption (tabanni) as a means of creating false parentage; lineage is only through actual birth.
This protects the child and prevents confusion of inheritance and guardianship.
5
Memorize the five points
Commit to memory: (1) husband's nafaqa, (2) wife's no financial duty, (3) wife's obedience, (4) mutual kindness, (5) lineage and adoption ban.
This is the memorization anchor.

Distinguishing Wilayah and Wisayah
Parsing the definitions and priorities of wilayah and wisayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between wilayah (guardianship over a minor's person) and wisayah (executorship over a minor's property), apply the Hanafi priority rules for each, and identify the role of maslaha mursala in regulating executorship.

Core Idea
Wilayah is over the person; wisayah is over property. One is by kinship, the other by appointment.

Islamic law carefully separates two types of authority over a minor: guardianship of the person (wilayah) and management of property (wisayah). This lesson unpacks the Hanafi definitions, priority hierarchies, and how the principle of maslaha mursala was used in the Ottoman era to supervise executors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms and their core domains
Read the passage and note that al-wilayah concerns the minor's person (upbringing, education), while al-wisayah concerns the minor's property (preservation, investment).
The distinction is foundational—wilayah is about the child's development, wisayah about financial stewardship.
2
Examine the priority rules for each
For wilayah, the order is the closest male agnate (father, then grandfather, then brother, then uncle). For wisayah, the father's appointed executor comes first, then the grandfather's appointee, then the judge's selection.
The judge only appoints if no testator nominated an executor.
3
Recognize the conditions and limitations
Both require the person to be competent (ahliyya) and trustworthy. Bad character or incapacity disqualifies. The judge holds the executor accountable.
The mother may have custody (hadana) but not wilayah over the person in Hanafi law.
4
Understand the Ottoman reform and maslaha mursala
The late Ottoman period created a council under the qadi to supervise executors, justified by unrestricted public interest (maslaha mursala), which allows the ruler to regulate when primary sources are silent.
This shows the flexibility of Hanafi law in adapting to new needs.

Foundational Principles of Islamic Inheritance
Outlines three foundational rules of inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three foundational principles of Islamic inheritance law (compulsory inheritance, debt priority, bequest limit) and apply them to a fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
Inheritance in Islam is compulsory — the testator cannot disinherit a legal heir.

This passage introduces the three bedrock principles of Islamic inheritance (fara'id) from a Hanafi perspective. These principles distinguish Sharia inheritance from pre-Islamic customs and modern civil laws. Understanding them is essential for issuing correct fatwas on estate distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Principles
Read the passage and list the three foundational rules: compulsory inheritance, debt before inheritance, and bequest limit to one-third.
Each principle is explicitly stated in the text.
2
Analyze the Deathbed Divorce Example
Consider how the first principle applies: a husband divorcing his wife during terminal illness to exclude her from inheritance is ineffective. Explain why this is considered an abuse of rights.
This illustrates the compulsory nature of inheritance.
3
Distinguish Debt Liability
The passage contrasts Islamic inheritance with modern laws where heirs may inherit debts. Identify that the deceased's debts are attached to the estate, not the heir personally.
Heirs are not personally liable beyond the estate.
4
Understand Bequest Limitations
The testator may only bequeath one-third of the net estate (after debts). Any excess requires heirs' consent. Relate this to the principle of compulsory inheritance.
This limitation protects the fixed shares of heirs.
5
Visualize Estate Distribution Order
Draw a simple flow: Estate → Funeral expenses → Debts → Bequests (max 1/3 of remainder) → Inheritance (remaining 2/3). This is the Hanafi order.
Memorize this sequence for fatwa application.

The Will (Wasiyyah): Definition and Rulings
Defines the will contract and its conditions regarding the one-third limit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the will (wasiyyah), state its one-third limit, and distinguish between bilateral and unilateral wills in Hanafi law.

Core Idea
A will is a deferred donation limited to one-third; for a named person it's a contract requiring acceptance, for public benefit it is unilateral.

In this lesson, we examine the legal definition of the will (wasiyyah) as presented by Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa. The will is a unique contract that only takes effect after death, balancing charitable giving with the rights of heirs. Understanding its form and limits is essential for proper estate planning in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and object
Name the three elements: the testator (musi), the legatee (musa lahu), and the bequeathed property (musa bihi). Note that the will is a donation attached to after death.
2
Apply the one-third limit
The will can only cover up to one-third of the estate without heirs' consent. Any excess is suspended pending their permission; if denied, the excess goes to heirs.
This limit protects heirs' shares.
3
Determine the contract type
If the legatee is a specific person, the will is a bilateral contract (like a gift) requiring acceptance (or lack of rejection) after death. If for a public benefit (mosque, school, poor), it is a unilateral disposition effective upon death without acceptance.
The nature of the beneficiary changes the legal form.
4
Understand acceptance and rejection
Acceptance or rejection is only considered after death. Silence (no rejection) counts as acceptance. If the legatee dies after the testator without known acceptance or rejection, the bequest passes to the legatee's heirs.

Property-Based Obligations on a Child's Wealth: Taxes and Maintenance
Listing property-based obligations on a child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that taxes and family maintenance are property-based obligations, thus binding on a minor's wealth.

Core Idea
Taxes and maintenance are tied to property, not to legal capacity — the child pays from his wealth.

This passage from Mustafa al-Zarqa's al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm examines two types of financial obligations that fall on a child's property: public taxes (like crop tithe and land tax, and their modern equivalents) and private family maintenance (nafaqah). The key insight is that these are not acts of worship requiring personal intention; rather, they are rights of the public treasury or of relatives that attach to the very existence of wealth. Therefore, the minor who owns property is liable just as an adult would be.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of obligations
Read the passage and list the two categories: (1) taxes imposed on property (ḍarā'ib) and (2) social maintenance obligations (ma'ūnāt ijtimā'iyyah). Note the examples given for each.
Taxes: crop tithe, land tax, property tax, income tax, customs. Maintenance: support for needy relatives (father, brothers, etc.).
2

For taxes: the 'illah is that they are public rights (ḥuqūq al-'ibād) imposed on property, nourishing the public treasury — they are not personal obligations tied to the individual's capacity. For maintenance: the 'illah is that it is like self-expenditure (nafaqah al-insān 'alā nafsih) required by social solidarity.
Both 'illahs share the feature of being property-based, not capacity-based.

Since the 'illah is the existence of wealth (property), the ruling (wujūb) applies equally to minors and adults. The minor's lack of legal responsibility (taklīf) does not remove the obligation from his property.
This is a standard Hanafi position: obligations that arise from property (like zakāt on crops, maintenance) are due from the property itself.
4

From the passage, we can derive the sub-maxim (ḍābiṭ): 'Al-iltizāmāt al-māliyyah allatī ta'allaqu bi al-māl lā bi al-dhimmah tajibu 'alā ṣāḥib al-māl sawā'an kāna bālighan aw ṣaghīran' (Property obligations that attach to wealth itself, not to the personal capacity, are due from the owner whether adult or minor).
This distinguishes such obligations from purely devotional acts (like prayer), which require personal intention and capacity.
5
Apply the maxim to modern scenarios
Test the ruling: a minor inherits rental property — is income tax due? A minor owns agricultural land — is 'ushr due? A minor has wealthy parents but poor uncle — is nafaqah due from the minor's wealth? Answer each using the same 'illah.
The modern examples show the continuing relevance of the classical principle.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions for valid interdiction (hajr) of a permitted minor and determine the effect of ratification after majority based on the guardian's prior rejection or the nature of the contract.

Core Idea
Whoever can grant legal capacity can revoke it, but only with proper notification.

This passage from Mustafa al-Zarqa's work extends the principles of guardianship over a minor's financial capacity. It first details the conditions under which a guardian may validly revoke a minor's permission to trade, emphasizing the necessity of informing the minor and, if permission was public, the market at large. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Personal guardianship follows inheritance priority; financial guardianship prioritizes the father's chosen executor.

In Hanafi family law, guardianship over a minor splits into two distinct domains: guardianship of the person (tarbiyah, marriage, etc.) and guardianship of property (management of assets). The rules for who holds each type differ significantly. This passage from al-Zarqa's al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-Amm clarifies the hierarchy for personal guardianship based on agnatic inheritance order, and the separate, executor-based system for financial guardianship.

ب - وأما الولاية على مال القاصر من بعد موت أبيه فقد فوّضها الشرع مبدئياً إلى من يختاره قبل موته ليقوم على أموال أولاده القاصرين وصيانة حقوقهم سواء أكان ذلك الشخص الذي يختاره الأب هو من أسرته أو غيرها، وسواء أكان رجلاً أو أمرأة. وعندئذ تأخذ ولاية هذا الشخص المختار اسماً خاصاً هو الوصاية والشخص نفسه يسمى : وصياً (ولا يأخذ اللفظ في الأنثى علامة تأنيث، بل يقال: امرأة وصي). فإذا لم يعهد الأب بالوصاية على أولاده القاصرين قبل مماته إلى أحد، انتقلت الولاية على أموالهم إلى جدهم العصبي (أبي أبيهم)، إضافة إلى ولايته على النفس (يتضح من ذلك أن وصي الأب مقدّم على الجد نفسه في الولاية المالية على القاصر، لأن وصي الأب كامتداد لسلطة الأب وإرادته). وَوَصي الجد بعد موته يقوم مقام وصي الأب. والوصي الذي يختاره الأب أو الجد قبل موته يسمى: الوصي المختار، أما الذي ينصبه القاضي فيسمى وصي القاضي. ويجوز أن يقام على القاصر وصيَّان معاً، وعندئذ ليس لأحدهما أن ينفرد بالتصرف، بل يجب أن يجتمع رأياهما فيه، إلا فيما لا يحتاج إلى الرأي كطلب الدين ورد الوديعة وشراء الضروريات (ر: رد المحتار ٥/٤٤٩ - ٤٥٠).
B - As for guardianship over the property of a minor after the death of his father, the Law initially entrusts it to whomever the father chooses before his death to manage the property of his minor children and safeguard their rights, whether that person chosen by the father is from his family or others, and whether male or female. Then this chosen person's guardianship takes the specific name 'al-wisayah' (executorship), and the person himself is called 'wasi' (executor). (The term does not take a feminine marker; rather, one says 'imra'ah wasi'.) If the father did not entrust executorship over his minor children to anyone before his death, guardianship over their property transfers to their paternal grandfather, in addition to his guardianship over their persons. (It is clear from this that the father's executor is given precedence over the grandfather himself in financial guardianship over the minor, because the father's executor is an extension of the father's authority and will.) And the grandfather's executor after his death stands in the place of the father's executor. The executor chosen by the father or grandfather before his death is called 'al-wasi al-mukhtar' (the chosen executor), while the one appointed by the judge is called 'wasi al-qadi' (judge's executor). It is permissible that two executors be appointed jointly over the minor; then neither may act independently, but rather their opinions must concur, except in matters that do not require opinion, such as demanding a debt, returning a deposit, and purchasing necessities (refer to Radd al-Muhtar 5/449-450).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the types and hierarchy of guardians (wasi) over a minor's property in Hanafi law. The author, Mustafa al-Zarqa, explains that upon the father's death, the primary right to manage the minor's property goes to whomever the father appointed as executor (wasi) in his will. This appointee can be a relative or a stranger, male or female. The term 'wasi' does not change for females; one says 'imra'ah wasi' (a woman executor). If no executor was appointed, then the paternal grandfather takes over both the guardianship of the person and property of the minor. The father's executor takes precedence over the grandfather because he represents the father's direct choice. The grandfather's own executor (appointed by the grandfather in his will) has the same status as the father's executor. There are two types of executors: the 'chosen executor' (appointed by father or grandfather in their will) and the 'judge's executor' (appointed by the court when no will exists). If multiple executors are appointed, they must act jointly and cannot act independently except in urgent matters like collecting debts or buying necessities. This rule is based on the Hanafi authoritative text Radd al-Muhtar.

Key Terms
وصي (wasi): A person appointed by the father, grandfather, or judge to manage the property of a minor after the father's death.
الوصاية (al-wisayah): The office or function of being a wasi; executorship over a minor's property.
الوصي المختار (al-wasi al-mukhtar): An executor chosen by the father or grandfather in their will.

Key Takeaway
The father's appointed executor is the primary guardian over a minor's property; only in his absence does the paternal grandfather take charge, and the judge steps in when no family executor exists.
