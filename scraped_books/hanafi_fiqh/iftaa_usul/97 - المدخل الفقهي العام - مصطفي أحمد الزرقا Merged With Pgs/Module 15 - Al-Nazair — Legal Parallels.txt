Kafala bil Mal: Conditional Suretyship
Defining a specific form of suretyship (kafala bil mal) with an example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that triggers liability in a conditional kafala bil mal and apply the ruling to evaluate a binding suretyship agreement.

Core Idea
Promise to pay a debt upon failing to produce the debtor creates binding suretyship when the condition fails.

This lesson examines a specific type of suretyship known as kafala bil mal (suretyship for property) where the surety's liability is conditional on failing to produce the debtor. The passage from the Majalla provides a clear example: 'If I do not bring the debtor to you by such time, then I am responsible for what he owes.' Understanding this conditional structure is essential for muftis dealing with financial guarantees.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The surety (kafeel), the debtor (madeen), and the creditor. The surety makes a conditional promise to the creditor.
The promise is made to the creditor, not to the debtor.
2
Identify the condition
The surety says: 'If I do not bring the debtor to you by such and such time.' This is a condition precedent – the surety must fail to produce the debtor by the deadline.
The condition is a negative event: failure to produce.
3
Identify the consequence
The surety becomes liable for the debt of the debtor ('what he owes'). This is the triggered liability.
The liability is for the debt amount, not for any penalty.
4
Distinguish from unconditional suretyship
In unconditional kafala, the surety is liable immediately. Here, liability is suspended until the condition occurs (failure to produce).
The promise itself is binding from the start; only the liability is conditional.

Al-Hawala: Transfer of Debt Liability
Defining hawala and contrasting with kafala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hawala, identify its parties (muhil, muhal, muhal 'alayh, muhal bihi), and distinguish it from kafala by stating that hawala transfers liability completely while kafala adds liability without releasing the original debtor.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers debt liability entirely; kafala adds a surety without releasing the original debtor.

In Islamic commercial law, hawala is a mechanism for transferring debt obligations from one party to another. Understanding its precise definition and key terms is essential for muftis and legal practitioners, especially to avoid confusion with kafala (suretyship). This lesson unpacks the definition from the Majalla and clarifies the structural difference between the two contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the contract
The passage defines hawala as a contract whose subject is the transfer of liability for a debt from the original debtor to a third party. Note that it is a transfer of liability, not just payment.
This is the core legal effect.
2
Map the parties and terms
The muhil (original debtor) transfers the debt; the muhal (creditor) receives the right to claim from the new debtor; the muhal 'alayh (third party) assumes the debt; and the muhal bihi is the debt itself.
Memorize the four terms with their roles.
3
Analyze the creditor's right after hawala
After a valid hawala, the creditor can only claim from the muhal 'alayh. The original debtor is released. This is the key difference from kafala.
Contrast: in kafala, the creditor can claim from both.
4
Distinguish hawala from kafala
In kafala, the surety's liability is added to the principal's as security; the creditor retains the right against both. In hawala, liability is transferred, so the original debtor is freed.
This distinction is crucial for correct fatwa.

Commentary
This passage defines hawala (transfer of debt) as a contract that shifts the responsibility for a debt from the original debtor (muhil) to a third party (muhal 'alayh). After the transfer, the creditor (muhal) can only claim from the muhal 'alayh, who becomes solely liable. This distinguishes hawala from kafala (suretyship), where the surety's liability is added to that of the original debtor, and the creditor can pursue both. In hawala, the original debtor is released from the debt. The Hanafi school, as reflected in the Majalla, holds this as the standard ruling.

Rahn as a Security Contract: Definition and Comparison with Kafala
Definition and comparison of rahn with kafala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the contract of rahn according to the Hanafi school and distinguish it from kafala in terms of security strength, applying the rule to a case of debtor insolvency.

Core Idea
Rahn secures a debt by detaining property; kafala secures by personal liability, so rahn is stronger.

This passage from the Majalla defines the contract of rahn (pawn/pledge) and compares it with kafala (suretyship). Understanding this distinction is essential for a mufti when advising clients on security instruments. The text highlights that rahn provides stronger security because it involves holding an asset, giving the creditor priority over other creditors if the debtor becomes insolvent.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition
Identify the key elements: rahn is a contract whose subject is detaining property in return for a right that can be satisfied from that property. The parties are the rahin (pledgor/debtor) and murtahin (pledgee/creditor).
The Arabic word 'ihtibas' (detention) is essential; without it, the contract is not valid.
2
Extract the security mechanism
Rahn works by physically detaining the asset under the murtahin's control. This gives the murtahin a tangible claim on the asset. If the debtor defaults, the debt can be satisfied from that specific property.
Contrast with kafala: suretyship is a personal guarantee, not asset-based.
3
Apply to insolvency scenario
If the debtor becomes insolvent and only has the pawned property, the murtahin has priority over other creditors. The surplus, if any, is distributed proportionally among the other creditors. This illustrates the strength of rahn.
The text explicitly says 'ghurama' (other creditors) cannot compete.
4
Compare with kafala
Kafala secures a liability by another liability (personal guarantee). If both the principal debtor and the surety become insolvent, there is no material asset to recover. Hence, rahn is stronger because it is backed by a specific asset.
This is the key distinction: asset-based vs. liability-based security.
5
Identify the legal ruling (hukm)
The passage presents a settled rule from the Majalla. There is no ikhtilaf mentioned. The rule is that the murtahin has precedence. This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
Authority level: fatawa mutakhkhirin (Majalla is a later codification).

Commentary
This passage defines rahn (pawn/pledge) as a contract whereby property is detained as security for a debt that can be satisfied from that property. The creditor becomes the murtahin (pledgee) and the debtor the rahin (pledgor). Rahn provides stronger security than kafala because it involves physical detention of an asset. If the debtor becomes bankrupt, the murtahin has priority over other creditors regarding the pawned property. If the property's value exceeds the debt, the surplus is distributed among other creditors proportionally. In contrast, in kafala, if both the principal debtor and surety are insolvent, there is no tangible asset to recover. This ruling is from the Hanafi school as codified in the Majalla.

Bay' al-Wafa: A Hybrid Contract Between Sale and Pledge
Defining bay' al-wafa as a hybrid contract combining sale and pledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining features of bay' al-wafa and apply its hybrid rules to distinguish it from a true sale or pledge.

Core Idea
Bay' al-wafa is a sale in form but a pledge in substance—the buyer gets usufruct but not full ownership, and the property must be returned upon repayment.

Bay' al-wafa is a unique Hanafi legal device that facilitates secured lending while allowing the creditor to benefit from the property. This lesson parses its hybrid nature, highlighting how it borrows elements from both sale and pledge, and clarifies the practical implications for both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract's basic nature
Read the opening definition: bay' al-wafa is a security contract in the form of a sale, based on the mutual right to return the exchange items. Recognize that it is a hybrid.
The term 'wafa' refers to the buyer's fulfillment of returning the property upon repayment.
2
Distinguish the sale-derived elements
From section (A), identify that the buyer owns the usufruct (manafi') and can use or lease the property. This is unlike a pure pledge where the creditor cannot use the pawned item.
The buyer can even lease to the seller, which is a significant departure from rahn.
3
Distinguish the pledge-derived elements
From section (B), list the five rules: no consumption or transfer of ownership, obligation to return upon redemption, no pre-emption, seller bears maintenance, and buyer's liability limited to the price if property perishes.
These rules show that the property's corpus is not fully transferred; it remains with the seller in substance.
4
Analyze the practical consequences
Consider the implications: the buyer cannot sell or mortgage the property, the seller must maintain it, and the property is not subject to shuf'a (pre-emption). This reinforces the security function.
The hybrid means each party has a mix of rights and duties that differ from standard sale or pledge.
5
Conclude with the governing principle
The passage states that the rules of pledge are predominant. This means in case of conflict, the pledge-like restrictions and obligations take precedence over sale-like freedoms.
This principle guides the interpretation of any ambiguous situations in bay' al-wafa.

8/46 - (6) Bay' al-Wafa: (Majalla/118-119 and 396). It is a security contract in the form of a sale based on the two parties retaining the right of return of the two exchange items. So it is a hybrid contract of sale and pledge, but the rules of pledge are predominant in it: (A) From the meaning of sale, it has rulings, the most important of which is that the buyer by wafa owns, by virtue of the contract, the usufruct of the thing sold by wafa, so he may use it himself and may exploit it by leasing it to the seller himself or to others, unlike a pledge which requires detention of the pawned property from all disposition; so if it were leased with the consent of the murtahin, the pledge would be invalid due to the loss of the detention that secures the debt. (B) From the meaning of pledge, it has the following rulings: 1- The buyer does not have the right to consume the sold item, nor to transfer its ownership to anyone for consideration or without consideration, nor to pawn it, nor to create a real right over it for anyone; rather he must preserve and maintain it. 2- The buyer is obliged to return the item sold by wafa to the seller whenever the seller returns the price to him. This is the meaning of 'wafa' (fulfillment) in its name, and also the buyer may, whenever he wishes, demand the return of the price and re-surrender the sold item like any creditor demanding his debt. 3- The real estate sold by wafa is not subject to pre-emption (shuf'a) because it is subject to return to its seller. 4- If the item sold by wafa requires maintenance and repair, its expense is on its seller, because it remains in his ownership. 5- The sold item in the buyer's hand is guaranteed by him with the guarantee of a pledge; i.e., if it perishes in his hand, he is liable for its value up to the amount of the price paid, just as a murtahin is liable for the value of a perished pawn up to the amount of the secured debt only. And set-off is applied between them on this basis.

Commentary
This passage explains bay' al-wafa (sale with right of redemption), a security device that mimics a sale but is essentially a pledge in substance. The buyer gains ownership of usufruct and can use or lease the property (even to the seller), unlike a strict pawn where the property is detained without use. However, the rules of pawn dominate: the buyer cannot dispose of the property's corpus, must preserve it, and must return it upon repayment. The property is not subject to pre-emption (shuf'a) because it is intended to be returned. Maintenance costs fall on the seller (the real owner). If the property perishes in the buyer's hands, his liability is limited to the price paid, akin to a murtahin's liability. This contract is recognized in the Hanafi school and codified in the Majalla.

Key Terms
بيع الوفاء (bay' al-wafa): A sale with the condition that the seller can redeem the property by returning the price.
التراد (al-tarad): The mutual right to return the exchange items (the sold item and the price).
منافع (manafi'): Usufruct or benefits of the property.
استغلال (istighlal): Exploitation of the property, such as by leasing.
شفعة (shuf'a): Pre-emption right of a co-owner or neighbor.
