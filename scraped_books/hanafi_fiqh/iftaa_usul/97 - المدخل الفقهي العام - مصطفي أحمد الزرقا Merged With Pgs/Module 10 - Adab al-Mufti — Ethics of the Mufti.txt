The Prohibition of Blind Taqlid: Statements of the Early Imams
Statements on the necessity of evidence by Abu Yusuf, al-Shafi'i, and Ahmad ibn Hanbal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for issuing a fatwa according to Abu Yusuf and explain the distinction between taqlid and ittiba' according to Ahmad ibn Hanbal.

Core Idea
A mufti must know the evidence — blind taqlid without proof is forbidden.

This lesson presents a set of foundational statements from three major imams — Abu Yusuf, al-Shafi'i, and Ahmad ibn Hanbal — all emphasising the necessity of evidence in issuing fatwas. These principles form a core part of the adab al-mufti (etiquette of the mufti) and establish that a mufti must base his fatwa on knowledge of the proofs, not on blind imitation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Key Statements
Read each imam's statement and note the central command or warning. Abu Yusuf forbids speaking without knowing the source; al-Shafi'i warns of danger; Ahmad forbids taqlid and defines ittiba'.
Memorise these statements as anchor points for mufti ethics.
2
Understand the Distinction
Ahmad ibn Hanbal explicitly distinguishes taqlid (blind imitation) from ittiba' (following with knowledge). Understand that ittiba' is to follow the Prophet and Companions, while taqlid of later scholars without evidence is impermissible.
This distinction is crucial for the mufti's responsibility.
3
Apply the Principle to Yourself
Reflect on how these statements apply to a mufti today: before issuing a fatwa, one must know the dalil (evidence) from the Qur'an, Sunnah, or established Hanafi methodology.
The mufti is not a parrot but a scholar who understands the reasoning.

This passage presents a set of foundational statements from three major imams—Abu Yusuf (the leading student of Abu Hanifa), al-Shafi'i, and Ahmad ibn Hanbal—all emphasising the necessity of evidence in issuing fatwas. This establishes that taqlid of later scholars without knowing the evidence is impermissible. The passage type is a compilation of adab al-mufti principles: the mufti must base his fatwa on knowledge of dalil.

Modern Collective Ijtihad: Fiqh Academies and Multidisciplinary Fatwa
Describes the establishment and function of modern collective ijtihad institutions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the role of modern collective ijtihad institutions (fiqh academies) and apply the principle of integrating specialist knowledge in issuing fatwas on contemporary issues.

Core Idea
Collective ijtihad by fiqh academies, combining fuqaha and specialists, is the modern mechanism for issuing authoritative fatwas on complex issues.

This passage introduces the major international and national fiqh academies that have emerged in the contemporary period to address issues requiring collective expertise. You will learn how these academies function, the types of issues they address, and crucially, how they integrate non-fiqh specialists (doctors, astronomers, economists) into the fatwa process. Understanding this model is essential for the contemporary mufti who must navigate both traditional usul and modern multidisciplinary realities.

Guided Path
1

Note the different sponsoring organizations.
2

These are issues that require interdisciplinary knowledge.
3
Explain the multidisciplinary decision-making process
The text describes how academies invite specialists from other sciences (medicine, astronomy, biology) to provide factual input, then the fuqaha members issue rulings by consensus or majority based on that input.

It includes scholars from multiple schools of fiqh, reflecting a multi-madhhab approach.

Chapter Three: Stages of Development of Islamic Jurisprudence and Its Characteristics

On the topic of the academy, first the Islamic Research Academy of al-Azhar was established in 1961 CE. Then the Muslim World League in Mecca established the Islamic Fiqh Academy, holding its first session in Sha'ban 1398 AH (mid-1978 CE). Then the Organization of the Islamic Conference established the Islamic Fiqh Academy, which held its first session in Mecca in the month of 1405/2 AH (11/1984 CE), and this academy includes a full member from each member state of the Organization of the Islamic Conference.
Each has a permanent office and holds sessions, dealing with important issues to determine the position of Islamic jurisprudence on them, whether permissibility or prohibition.
Decisions:
Examples of topics that these academies dealt with and issued rulings on:
- Transplantation of human organs, artificial insemination, and test-tube babies.
- Determining prayer times in countries near the North Pole and beginnings of lunar months.
- Literary and intellectual property rights and expropriation for public purposes.
- Zakat on modern forms of wealth.
- A great many decisions related to economic matters, Islamic banks, and financial markets.
These academies publish scientific journals containing the research presented at the academy's sessions, discussions, and issued decisions.

Chapter (17): The Eighth Stage: 1355 AH - Present
Thus ijtihad was revived from its slumber in the form of collective ijtihad.
(1)
It is noted that the previous academies are international in the sense that they include members from multiple countries. There are also national academies, most notably the Islamic Ideology Council in Pakistan, which was mandated by the constitution in the early 1950s and became active since the early 1970s. It includes scholars from various schools of jurisprudence and provides its Sharia recommendations regarding legislation presented to the National Assembly. One of the greatest achievements of this council was the detailed plan it prepared over more than two years, then submitted to the government in June 1980 on how to eliminate usurious interest from the Pakistani economy and replace it with permissible Sharia transactions. The council enlisted the help of a group of economists and banking experts in preparing this plan. The council also helped draft the important law on the application of zakat in Pakistan, which was issued in June 1980. It is worth noting and emphasizing that the aforementioned fiqh academies, in all cases where the fiqh ruling depends on information from other sciences such as medicine, astronomy, and biology, invite specialists in those sciences to present their information, expertise, and opinions, record them, and then the academy members build their decision by consensus or majority on the Sharia ruling on the subject under research based on the scientific facts explained by the specialists.

Commentary
This passage is a historical and descriptive account of the modern stage of Islamic jurisprudence (the eighth stage from 1355 AH to present), focusing on the emergence of collective ijtihad through institutional bodies. The author, Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa, outlines three major international fiqh academies: the Islamic Research Academy of al-Azhar, the Islamic Fiqh Academy of the Muslim World League, and the Islamic Fiqh Academy of the Organization of Islamic Cooperation. These academies address contemporary issues by issuing collective rulings (qararat) on topics such as organ transplantation, artificial reproduction, prayer times in extreme latitudes, intellectual property, and modern forms of wealth for zakat. The passage emphasizes that these academics operate with a permanent office and hold regular sessions. They also publish scholarly journals. The author then highlights the existence of national academies, using the example of Pakistan's Islamic Ideology Council, which played a key role in eliminating interest from the economy and implementing zakat. A crucial methodological point is that these academies involve specialists from other sciences (medicine, astronomy, biology) to provide factual input before the fuqaha' issue a ruling by consensus or majority. Finally, the passage introduces the renewal in fiqh teaching and authorship, noting that the first writer on collective ijtihad was Rafiq Bey al-Azm. This passage is significant for usul al-iftaa as it demonstrates the practical implementation of collective ijtihad and the integration of multidisciplinary knowledge in fatwa issuance.

Key Terms

Redefining Muhsan: The Rationale Behind the Penalty of Stoning
Critical refinement of muhsan definition based on illah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the traditional definition of muhsan and evaluate the author's critique based on the illah of stoning.

Core Idea
A mufti must test definitions against the rationale — the harsher punishment fits the married adulterer, not the merely ever-married.

This passage examines the traditional Hanafi definition of muhsan (the person liable to stoning for zina) and raises a critical question: does the definition serve the underlying purpose of the severe punishment? The author, a contemporary jurist, argues that earlier scholars missed the justifying reason, and a mufti should consider whether the offender had a spouse at the time of the crime. This lesson walks you through the author's argument and helps you apply the principle of considering maqasid in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two punishments
Recognize that the Quran prescribes flogging for zina, but the Sunnah specifies stoning for the muhsan. The bikr (virgin) is flogged, while the muhsan is stoned.
The distinction is based on hadith, not the Quranic verse alone.
2
Understand the traditional definition of muhsan
Earlier scholars (including Hanafis) defined muhsan as anyone who has ever had valid marital intercourse, without requiring a current spouse at the time of zina.
This definition comes from the general understanding of the term and early practice.
3
Grasp the author's critique
The author argues that the severe penalty of stoning is justified by the greater societal harm caused by a currently married person committing zina. The traditional definition misses this illah.
The critique is methodological: definitions must align with the rationale (ta'lil).
4
Formulate the alternative definition
The author suggests that muhsan should require having a spouse at the time of zina. This better serves the purpose of the harsh punishment.
This is a contemporary ijtihad, not adopted in classical fiqh.
5
Evaluate implications for fatwa
A mufti using this approach would apply stoning only to those currently married, potentially reducing the penalty for divorced or widowed individuals who later commit zina.
This shows how engaging with maqasid can affect fatwa outcomes.

Commentary

It is part of the author’s mufti adab, guiding muftis to consider the rationale (illah) behind the punishment. He argues that the mufti should stipulate that the offender must have a spouse at the time of zina to justify the harsher punishment. This aligns with the Hanafi emphasis on the reasoning (ta'lil) behind rulings and the consideration of maqasid. The author does not present a final fatwa but raises a methodological critique, urging muftis to re-examine the definition in light of the rationale.
