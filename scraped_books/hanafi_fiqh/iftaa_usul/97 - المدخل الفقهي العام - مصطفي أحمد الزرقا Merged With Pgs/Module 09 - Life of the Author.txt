The Fourth Jurisprudential Period and the Founding of the Four Major Madhhabs
Historical overview of fiqh expansion and madhhab formation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the historical context of the fourth jurisprudential period, state the founders of the four major Sunni madhhabs, and explain why some schools survived while others did not.

Core Idea
The four surviving madhhabs were preserved by capable students; many other valid schools were lost.

This lesson traces the development of Islamic jurisprudence in the fourth period (early 2nd to mid-4th century AH). During this time, fiqh expanded and numerous schools emerged. Among them, only the Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali schools have endured to the present. Understanding why these schools survived – and others did not – is crucial for grasping the structure of Islamic legal authority and the role of later scholars in preserving and transmitting fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the time frame and significance of the fourth period
Explain that this period saw the flourishing of fiqh after the foundational era. It spans from early 2nd to mid-4th century AH.
This is the period of madhhab formation.
2
List the four surviving madhhabs and their founders
Recognize the names, birth/death dates, and regional affiliations as given in the text.
Hanafi from Kufa, Maliki from Medina, Shafi'i originally from Gaza later Egypt, Hanbali from Baghdad.
3
Note the key characteristics of each school
For Hanafi: Ahl al-Ra'y, istihsan; for Maliki: moderate between ra'y and hadith; for Shafi'i: closer to hadith, rejection of istihsan; for Hanbali: heavily based on hadith.
These characteristics influenced their methodologies.
4
Identify the role of students in preserving each madhhab
For Hanafi: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Sahibayn) codified and spread it. For others, similar preservation efforts are implied.
The survival of a madhhab depended on capable students.
5
Explain why other schools did not survive
Lack of capable students to preserve and spread the school, as stated in the final paragraph.
Many great mujtahids existed but their schools were lost.

Chapter Thirteen: The Fourth Jurisprudential Period: From the early second century to the mid-fourth century. 1/13 - In this vast expanse, jurisprudence expanded greatly and rose gloriously after its previous foundational era. And during this vast period, many schools and jurisprudential Ijtihads emerged: among them the four schools, and many others. (1) The four schools are: the Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali. A - The Hanafi school is attributed to its Imam Abu Hanifa al-Nu'man ibn Thabit, of Persian origin, called the Greatest Imam. Born 80 AH, died 150 AH. He was the Imam of the Iraqis. The leadership of the Ahl al-Ra'y transferred to him and settled upon him, and he consolidated the method of Istihsan. He was renowned for strength of argument, swift and decisive responses, and genius of understanding and deduction. His most famous students are two: First: Abu Yusuf Ya'qub ibn Ibrahim, who held judicial authority in the Abbasid state during the reign of Harun al-Rashid, and authored for him the book 'Al-Kharaj'. Second: Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani, who was among the brilliant genius jurists and also a leading scholar of language. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad are called 'the two companions' (al-Sahibayn). To them primarily belongs the credit for codifying Abu Hanifa's fiqh and spreading his madhhab. B - The Maliki school is attributed to its Imam Malik ibn Anas al-Asbahi, who was the scholar of Medina and the Imam of the Hijazis. Born 93 AH, died 179 AH. His school is considered moderate, balanced between the people of ra'y and hadith, due to its heavy reliance on hadith, since his narrations had spread especially in the city of the Prophet (peace be upon him). However, Malik is also considered among the people of ra'y, and his school and principles are more closely related to ra'y. The disagreement between the two schools of ra'y and hadith intensified at the beginning of this period, then ra'y became established as a valid jurisprudential method within its limits and Islamic principles, which its proponents clarified and with which they repelled doubts from this method, distancing it from speaking based on desire and whim devoid of Islamic evidence. Professor Sheikh Muhammad Abu Zahra says: But the difference did not last long between the people of ra'y and hadith, for the generation... He benefited from his position in Medina through constant contact with scholars from all regions during the pilgrimage season, and he met Abu Hanifa there, debated with him, and said about him: 'He is a faqih' (see: book 'Malik' by Professor Sheikh Muhammad Abu Zahra p. 1). C - The Shafi'i school is attributed to its Imam Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafi'i al-Qurashi. He was born in 150 AH in Gaza, raised an orphan, and traveled to Iraq and the Hijaz, learning from the companions of Abu Hanifa and from Malik. Then he settled in Egypt, left his old opinions, and founded his new madhhab. He died in 204 AH in Fustat. He was possessed of many talents, and his madhhab is considered closer to the people of hadith. He criticized his teacher Malik's method of Istihsan. D - The Hanbali school is attributed to its Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal al-Shaybani, a student of Imam al-Shafi'i. He was born in Baghdad in 164 AH, specialized in hadith, excelled in it, and became famous. Therefore his madhhab was more imbued with hadith. He died in 241 AH. Besides these four, there were many other great mujtahids, contemporaries and successors, who had magnificent fiqh and valuable opinions, some of which are still recorded in books that document the differences of jurists. But their schools did not endure, for reasons including that they were not blessed with students of capability and activity who could preserve and spread them. Among those mujtahids are the teachers of these four imams, i.e., their masters, such as Hammad ibn Abi Sulayman, the teacher of Abu Hanifa; Ibrahim al-Nakha'i; al-Sha'bi, both teachers of Hammad; Rabi'a al-Ra'y; Ibn Shihab al-Zuhri; and Yahya ibn Sa'id, from the teachers of Malik. There are also many others besides their teachers, contemporaries like Ja'far al-Sadiq, Zayd ibn Ali Zayn al-Abidin, al-Awza'i, 'Abd al-Rahman ibn Abi Layla, Ibn Shubruma (contemporaries of Abu Hanifa), and al-Layth ibn Sa'd in Egypt, one of the greatest mujtahids contemporary with Abu Hanifa and Malik, between whom and Malik there were jurisprudential debates and correspondences that were preserved and published — see the two exchanged letters between Malik and al-Layth ibn Sa'd in the book 'Malik' by Professor Sheikh Muhammad Zahra, section one, pp. 103-114.

Commentary
This passage is a historical overview of the fourth period of Islamic jurisprudence, covering roughly from the early 2nd century AH to the mid-4th century AH. The author, Mustafa al-Zarqa, describes this period as one of tremendous expansion and flourishing of fiqh after its founding era. He notes that many schools of thought (madhhabs) emerged, including the four that have survived to this day, as well as many others that did not endure. The text then provides brief biographical sketches of the founders of the four major Sunni madhhabs. For the Hanafi school, he mentions Imam Abu Hanifa's origin, his title 'al-Imam al-A'zam', his birth and death dates, his role as leader of the Ahl al-Ra'y, and his method of istihsan. He highlights his two main students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, who are called 'al-Sahibayn' and who were instrumental in codifying Abu Hanifa's fiqh. For the Maliki school, he notes Imam Malik's status as scholar of Medina, his moderate position between ra'y and hadith, and his reliance on hadith. He also mentions Malik's meeting with Abu Hanifa and his acknowledgment of Abu Hanifa's juristic ability. For the Shafi'i school, he describes Imam al-Shafi'i's education under both the Hanafi school and Malik, his later development of a new madhhab in Egypt, and his critique of istihsan. For the Hanbali school, he mentions Imam Ahmad's specialization in hadith and the resulting hadith-heavy character of his madhhab. Finally, the author explains that many other mujtahids existed but their schools disappeared due to the lack of capable students to preserve and spread them. He lists several examples, including teachers of the four imams and other contemporaries. This passage sets the stage for understanding the historical context of the Hanafi madhhab and its relationship with other schools.

Key Terms
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): A method of juristic preference where a ruling is derived by preferring one analogy over another based on textual or rational grounds, or by departing from an existing analogy for a stronger evidence.
أهل الرأي (ahl al-ra'y): The school of jurists who relied heavily on independent reasoning (ra'y) and analogy (qiyas) in deriving rulings, as opposed to those who strictly adhered to transmitted texts (ahl al-hadith).
أهل الحديث (ahl al-hadith): The school of jurists who prioritized the literal texts of hadith over personal opinion in deriving rulings.

The Fatawa Hindiyya: Imperial Patronage and Hanafi Authority
Biographical note on the compilation of al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the historical context and authority of al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya within the Hanafi school and apply it as a source for fatwa.

Core Idea
Al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya is a cornerstone of Hanafi fatwa, compiled by imperial decree from authoritative sources.

This lesson introduces a landmark in Hanafi legal literature: al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya, commissioned by the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb. Understanding its origins and methodology is essential for any mufti, as it remains a key reference for fatwa in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the patron and his motivation
Recognize that Aurangzeb, a pious ruler, sought to compile a comprehensive and reliable Hanafi fiqh reference. His personal piety and resources enabled the project.
2
Understand the compilation methodology
The king gathered leading Hanafi jurists under Shaykh Nizam al-Din. They selected the most correct rulings from the entire corpus of Hanafi works, attributing each to its source.
This selection process gave the Fatawa Hindiyya its authority, as it distilled the mufta bihi positions.
3
Assess its place in the Hanafi tradition
The work is considered a mutakhkhirin (later) authority, often cited in fatwa. It is a standard reference for Hanafi muftis today.
It is also known as al-Fatawa al-Alamgiriyya.

This paragraph provides a brief biography of the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb Alamgir (1069-1119 AH), who commissioned the famous Hanafi legal compilation known as al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya (or al-Fatawa al-Alamgiriyya). The compiler's note highlights the king's piety and the scholarly effort involved. The Fatawa Hindiyya is a key reference in the Hanafi school, containing selected rulings from authoritative works.

Key Terms
الفتاوى الهندية (al-Fatāwā al-Hindiyya): The Indian Fatwas, a comprehensive Hanafi legal compilation commissioned by Aurangzeb.
الشيخ نظام الدين (al-Shaykh Niẓām al-Dīn): The lead scholar who supervised the compilation of al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya.
الفتاوى العالمكيرية (al-Fatāwā al-ʿĀlamgīriyya): Alternate name for al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya, named after Aurangzeb's title 'Alamgir.'
