Deriving Rulings for New Transactions: Takhrij, Qiyas, and Istislah
Explaining the methodology of takhrij for new transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi method of deriving rulings for new transactions using qiyas and istislah, and identify the historical precedents of bay' al-wafa, istisna', and al-rahn al-musta'ar.

Core Idea
New transactions are ruled by deriving legal analogies from established principles (qiyas) or public interest (istislah), not by silence.

Islamic jurisprudence did not stop at the transactions explicitly mentioned in the primary sources. The Hanafi school developed a robust method for deriving rulings for new transactions that emerge over time. This lesson explores the passage from Dr. Mustafa al-Zarqa's work, which outlines the process of takhrij (derivation) through qiyas and istislah, supported by historical examples of contracts like bay' al-wafa and istisna'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the new transactions mentioned
The text introduces modern financial contracts like insurance (tamin) and joint-stock companies (al-mughfala). Note that these were not present in classical fiqh but require rulings.
2
Understand the method of derivation
The author states that rulings for such new transactions can be derived (takhrij) and generated (tawlid) from foundational principles (ummahat) and legal maxims (qawa'id) using qiyas (analogy) or istislah (consideration of public interest). This is the core methodological claim.
Takhrij here refers to extending existing rulings to new cases by identifying the effective cause (illa).
3
Examine the historical examples
The text lists three contracts that were new in their time: bay' al-wafa (sale with right of redemption), istisna' (commission to manufacture), and al-rahn al-musta'ar (borrowed pledge). The footnote provides details on each, especially bay' al-wafa's origin in 5th century AH Bukhara and its hybrid nature.
These examples demonstrate that the method of takhrij is not an innovation but a continuous practice of the fuqaha.
4
Analyze the nature of bay' al-wafa
The footnote describes bay' al-wafa as a contract that combines elements of valid sale, invalid sale, and pledge. The jurists assigned it rulings from each of these contracts as appropriate to its purpose.
This illustrates the flexibility of qiyas and the use of multiple analogies when a single analogy is insufficient.
5
Apply the method to modern scenarios
Consider how the same method would be applied to insurance: identify the underlying essential elements (e.g., risk transfer, premium, indemnity) and find analogous contracts in classical fiqh (e.g., kafala, ji'ala, or mugharasa). This is the task of the mufti in contemporary fatwa.
This step bridges the historical method to current practice.

Key Terms
تخريج (takhrij): The process of deriving rulings for new cases from established principles and sources through qiyas or other methods.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning: extending a ruling from an original case (asl) to a new case (far') due to a shared effective cause (illa).
الاستصلاح (al-istislah): Consideration of public interest or maslaha; a method of deriving rulings based on benefit not explicitly mentioned in the primary sources.
بيع الوفاء (bay' al-wafa): A sale with the right of redemption; a contract where the seller can repurchase the property upon repayment of the price.
الاستصناع (al-istisna'): A contract of commission to manufacture; an order to produce a specific item.

The Four Causes of Ownership in Islamic Law
Identifying the four legitimate causes and rejecting others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four recognized causes of ownership in Islamic law and distinguish them from rejected causes such as acquisitive prescription.

Core Idea
Islamic law only accepts four causes of ownership; all other claims, including acquisitive prescription, are invalid.

Ownership is a fundamental concept in fiqh. Islamic legislation has approved specific causes for acquiring ownership. This lesson surveys the four causes and contrasts them with pre-Islamic and foreign concepts that were rejected, particularly the concept of acquisitive prescription.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four causes
From the text, list the four causes: ihraaz al-mubahat, contracts, substitution (khalfiyya), and growth from owned property.
2
Contrast with rejected causes
Note the rejected causes: internal conquest, debtor enslavement, and acquisitive prescription. Understand why each is rejected.
3
Analyze the treatment of prescription
Islamic law accepts prescription only as a procedural bar (mani' sam' al-da'wa), not as a substantive cause of ownership. This protects ownership rights while facilitating legal process.
Distinguish between qada and diyanah here.
4
Apply to a fatwa scenario
Consider a case where someone claims ownership of land by long possession. According to this lesson, the claim should be heard if evidence exists, and possession alone does not transfer ownership.

Chapter (23) Definition of Ownership and Explanation of Its Causes
Second Topic: Causes of Ownership
23/4 - The causes of acquisition that Islamic legislation has approved are limited to:
1. Acquisition of permissible things (mubahat).
2. Contracts.
3. Substitution (al-khalfiyya).
4. Growth from owned property.
In the Arab Jahiliyyah and other nations, there were causes of ownership that Islamic legislation rejected; some it denounced and considered among the most severe crimes, and some it forbade and considered unlawful.
The first - like internal conquest among Arab tribes and others, and like enslaving a debtor who is unable to pay among the Romans and in the Arab Jahiliyyah. The second - like acquisitive prescription (1) which existed in the late Roman legislation, and European laws inherited it and it still remains in them.
Islamic jurisprudence accepted the rule of prescription not as an acquisitive cause of ownership, but as a bar to hearing the claim of a right over which time has passed.
(1) Prescription, also called passage of time, is the expiration of a specified period (such as fifteen years, or more or less) over a right in the liability of a person or over a specific thing belonging to another in his possession without the owners claiming them while being able to claim. This prescription is either considered a bar to hearing the claim of right before courts, while considering the ownership or right in reality remaining as before - and this is barring prescription because it bars the claim; or it is considered a cause of ownership for the possessor and a complete termination of the right of the negligent owner - and this is acquisitive prescription because it gives a person ownership of something that originally belonged to another.

23/5 - (First) - Acquisition of permissible things:
Mubah is property that has not entered into protected ownership and there is no legal impediment to owning it; like water in its sources, grass in its pastures, trees in unowned wilderness, and land and sea game, etc.
Every person may take possession of as much of these mubahat as he can. Whatever he takes possession of with the intention of ownership, he owns it and it becomes his exclusive property. This taking possession with the intention of ownership is ihraaz (acquisition).
Ownership of mubahat by acquisition depends on two conditions:
First - That no previous person has preceded in acquiring the mubah.
If a person collects rainwater in a vessel and leaves it, no one else may take it, because it has left the state of permissibility (ibahah) by the first acquisition and has become owned by him, because the rule is: whoever precedes to a mubah owns it.
Similarly, if a person collects firewood from the wilderness and leaves it, no one else may take it, and so on.
Second: Intention to own.
If a mubah comes into a person's possession without his intention to own it, he does not own it.

Commentary
This passage is a theoretical exposition from Dr. Mustafa al-Zarqa's 'Al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm' on the causes of ownership in Islamic jurisprudence. The author states that Islamic law recognizes four and only four causes of acquisition: (1) acquisition of mubah (permissible unowned things), (2) contracts, (3) substitution (e.g., inheritance or compensation), and (4) growth from owned property (like offspring of owned animals). He contrasts these with pre-Islamic and non-Islamic causes that Islam rejected, such as internal tribal conquest and enslavement of debtors, as well as acquisitive prescription found in Roman and European law. The author emphasizes that while Islamic law acknowledges the concept of prescription (taqadum), it does not treat it as a substantive cause of ownership. Instead, prescription only bars the hearing of a claim in court due to the passage of time and the resulting doubt about the right. This is tied to the distinction between judicial rulings (qada) and religious duty (diyaanah): if the opponent confesses, the doubt disappears and the claim is heard. Then the author explains the first cause - acquisition of mubahat. He defines mubah as property not already owned and not prohibited to own. The key is that taking possession (istila') with the intention of ownership is called ihraaz. There are two conditions: (1) that no one else has already acquired it (the rule 'man sabaqa ila mubah fa qad malakahu'), and (2) the intent to own. Simple possession without intent does not transfer ownership.

Key Terms
الملكية (al-milkiyya): Ownership as a legal relation between a person and a thing, giving exclusive rights.
المباح (al-mubah): Property that is not owned by anyone and not prohibited to own.
الإحراز (al-ihraaz): Physical taking possession of mubah with the intention of ownership.
التقادم (al-taqadum): Prescription, or the passage of time over a right without claim.

Acquisition of Mubah: The Rule 'Whoever Precedes Owns'
Explaining the first cause: ihraaz al-mubahat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of mubah, identify the two conditions for acquiring ownership by ihraaz, and apply the qawida 'man sabaqa ila mubah fa qad malakahu' to new cases.

Core Idea
Whoever physically takes possession of an unowned thing with the intent to own, owns it exclusively.

The first cause of ownership is acquisition of mubah (permissible unowned property). Islamic law gives priority to the first acquirer. This lesson explains the concept of ihraaz, its conditions, and the famous qawida that governs many disputes over natural resources.

Guided Path
1
Define mubah and ihraaz
Mubah is property that is not owned and not prohibited to own. Ihraaz is taking possession with intention to own.
2
Identify condition 1: No prior acquisition
If someone has already acquired the mubah, it becomes his property and is no longer mubah. The qawida: man sabaqa ila mubah fa qad malakahu.
This prevents disputes over resources.
3
Identify condition 2: Intention to own
Mere possession without intent does not create ownership. The thing remains mubah unless there is qasd al-tamalluk.
This links to the principle al-umur bi-maqasidiha.
4
Apply the qawida to examples
The text gives examples: collected rainwater and gathered firewood. Both become owned by the first collector, even if left unattended.
Discuss what happens if a second person takes it.

Commentary
This passage is a theoretical exposition from Dr. Mustafa al-Zarqa's 'Al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm' on the causes of ownership in Islamic jurisprudence. The author states that Islamic law recognizes four and only four causes of acquisition: (1) acquisition of mubah (permissible unowned things), (2) contracts, (3) substitution (e.g., inheritance or compensation), and (4) growth from owned property (like offspring of owned animals). He contrasts these with pre-Islamic and non-Islamic causes that Islam rejected, such as internal tribal conquest and enslavement of debtors, as well as acquisitive prescription found in Roman and European law. The author emphasizes that while Islamic law acknowledges the concept of prescription (taqadum), it does not treat it as a substantive cause of ownership. Instead, prescription only bars the hearing of a claim in court due to the passage of time and the resulting doubt about the right. This is tied to the distinction between judicial rulings (qada) and religious duty (diyaanah): if the opponent confesses, the doubt disappears and the claim is heard. Then the author explains the first cause - acquisition of mubahat. He defines mubah as property not already owned and not prohibited to own. The key is that taking possession (istila') with the intention of ownership is called ihraaz. There are two conditions: (1) that no one else has already acquired it (the rule 'man sabaqa ila mubah fa qad malakahu'), and (2) the intent to own. Simple possession without intent does not transfer ownership.

The Hanafi Ruling on Pure Gross Inadequacy (Ghaban Mujarrad)
Explaining the Hanafi ruling on ghaban mujarrad and its exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ghaban mujarrad, identify its three exceptions, and apply the mufta bihi position in a fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
Pure gross inadequacy without deception is not a defect of consent in Hanafi law, except in the rights of the orphan, waqf, and public treasury.

In this lesson, we explore a fundamental Hanafi principle: mere price inadequacy does not give the buyer a right to rescind the contract. Understanding when and why this rule applies—and the exceptional cases where it does not—is critical for issuing sound fatwas in commercial transactions. We will dissect the legal definition of ghaban mujarrad, examine its rationale, and see how the Hanafi school handles internal disagreements.

Guided Path
1
Define the term
Ghaban mujarrad is gross inadequacy in price without any deception or fraud (khilabah). Identify the key elements: excessiveness and absence of deceit.
Distinguish from ghaban with deception, which gives the option to rescind.
2
State the general Hanafi ruling
The rule: Ghaban mujarrad does not vitiate consent and does not allow rescission of the contract. The buyer bears the loss due to negligence.
This is the mufta bihi position, though some Hanafis differ.
3
Examine the rationale
The law does not penalize the seller for seeking profit; it only intervenes against fraud or harm to the community. The buyer must exercise due diligence.
The hadith 'Dau al-nas yarzuq Allah ba'dahum min ba'd' supports this freedom.
4
Identify the three exceptions
Hanafi law exempts three cases: contracts involving orphan property, waqf (endowment), and bayt al-mal (public treasury). In these, even pure inadequacy is grounds for rescission.
The exceptions exist because these entities need special protection due to frequent negligence of guardians.
5
Note the internal ikhtilaf and tarjih
Some Hanafi scholars allow rescission for pure inadequacy, but the preponderant view (al-arjah) is that it is not allowed unless accompanied by taghrir (misrepresentation). The mufta bihi is the latter.
See Fath al-Qadir and Radd al-Muhtar for references.

Section: The Ruling on Pure Ghaban (Mere Gross Inadequacy) and Its Exceptions.
7/35 - It is clear from the foregoing that gross inadequacy alone in contracts does not vitiate consent unless accompanied by some deception. Most ijtihadāt (scholarly opinions) have settled upon this. This is because pure gross inadequacy, free from any fraud, only indicates negligence on the part of the deceived party in investigating prices and knowing the fair equivalent; it does not indicate deceit by the other party. Every person may seek greater profit through lawful means without fraud or cheating, as long as it does not harm the community. In that case, his freedom is restricted and his profit is limited, as in the case of hoarding essential goods and sellers' control over prices, where the ruler sells hoarded property forcibly and resorts to price fixing if they collude and control prices.
As for ordinary transactions between people outside situations of hoarding and control, it is not the role of legislation to prevent mere gross inadequacy free from fraud and deception. Rather, its role is to place the contracting parties on equal footing in terms of capacity and freedom. Then each person must open his eyes and protect himself from loss. Every person has, due to his care for his own interest, a sufficient motive to seek what is best for him and avoid otherwise. The burden of negligence must be on his own account.
This is the view of the majority of Islamic ijtihadāt regarding pure gross inadequacy.
Note on the following exceptions:
8/35 - The exceptional cases in which pure gross inadequacy is considered a defect of the will:
Hanafi ijtihad and others except three areas in which gross inadequacy is prohibited even if it is pure, not accompanied by deception. These three excepted areas are: the rights of the orphan, the endowment (waqf), and the public treasury (bayt al-mal).
Thus, any loss occurring through a contract in the property of any of these three is to be returned to the loss-maker (ghābin) by law, because these three entities need increased protection through legislation due to frequent negligence of those responsible for safeguarding their rights (see: Majalla / 356).
However, Hanbali ijtihad also excepts two additional cases in which pure gross inadequacy is considered a defect of the will:
(1) Their proof from texts: the saying of the Prophet (peace be upon him): 'Let people be, Allah provides for some of them through others.' Narrated by Muslim in Sahih, Book of Sales (1522), al-Tirmidhi (1223), and al-Nasa'i (4496) from the hadith of Jabir (may Allah be pleased with him). When the Lender Cannot Freely Revoke: Exceptions in 'Ariyah
Stating the rule and two exceptions for 'ariyah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two exceptions to the non-binding nature of 'ariyah in Hanafi fiqh and state the legal consequences for the lender in each case.

Core Idea
A non-binding loan becomes binding when the borrower suffers harm or a third party's right is involved.

The general rule in Hanafi fiqh is that the contract of 'ariyah (loan of chattel for use) is non-binding for both parties—the borrower may return it at any time, and the lender may reclaim it at any time. However, this passage from a classical text on usul al-iftā' highlights two critical exceptions where the lender's right to revoke is restricted. Understanding these exceptions is essential for a mufti to issue correct guidance when loans involve fixed terms or pledges.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Identify the baseline ruling: 'ariyah is non-binding, meaning both parties can cancel at any time without prior cause.
This is the default position in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the first exception: fixed-term loan
Note that if the loan was for a specified period, the lender who revokes early must compensate the borrower for any harm suffered because the borrower relied on the loan (maghrur).
Harm here includes wasted expenses or lost opportunities caused by the premature revocation.
3
Identify the second exception: loan for pledge (rahn)
If the loaned property was given so the borrower could use it as collateral, the lender cannot reclaim it directly. Instead, he must first pay off the debt to the pledgee, then seek reimbursement from the borrower.
This protects the pledgee's security interest.

Given a scenario, determine whether the lender's revocation is unqualified or triggers a duty to compensate or pay the debt.

Hanbali Classification of Conditions: A Comparative Analysis for Hanafi Muftis
Hanbali classification of conditions into four types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four Hanbali types of contractual conditions, and apply the Hanbali distinction between void (laghw) and invalidating (mubtil) when consulted in cross-madhhab fatwa practice.

Core Idea
In Hanbali law, conditions are classified by effect: valid-binding, valid-nonbinding, void-harmless, void-invalidating the contract.

The Hanbali school offers a clear fourfold classification of conditions in contracts, which differs from the Hanafi approach. While Hanafi fiqh generally categorizes conditions as sahih (valid), fasid (defective), or batil (void) depending on their compatibility with the contract's essence, the Hanbali system introduces a nuanced category: conditions that are valid but not binding, preserving certain rights. Understanding this classification helps a Hanafi mufti in comparative fatwa, especially when encountering legal questions from Hanbali practitioners or in jurisdictions where multiple schools operate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two axes of the classification
The Hanbali classification operates on two dimensions: validity (sahih vs. invalid) and binding effect (lazim vs. ghayr lazim). The first two types are valid; the latter two are invalid. The binding effect is independent of validity.
The source uses 'yasihhu' (is valid) and 'yalsamu' (is binding).
2
Distinguish muqtada al-'aqd from ghayah al-'aqd
Type 1 and 3 revolve around muqtada al-'aqd (inherent requirements), while type 4 revolves around ghayah al-'aqd (the purpose/end of the contract). A condition contradicting muqtada is void but contract stands; contradicting ghayah voids the entire contract.
Muqtada: e.g., in marriage, the right to intercourse. Ghayah: the very purpose, e.g., making marriage lawful.
3
Analyze the second type: valid but non-binding
This type restricts a right that the Law intends to preserve (e.g., husband's right to divorce or polygyny). The condition is valid in the sense that violation gives the other party the right to annul the contract, but it is not binding because the right cannot be permanently waived.
The source calls it 'yasihhu wa la yalzim'. The 'thamar' (fruit) is the right to annul.
4
Contrast with Hanafi classification
In Hanafi fiqh, a condition that contradicts muqtada al-'aqd is usually fasid (defective). It does not invalidate the contract but the condition itself is void (laghw) and triggers no right of annulment automatically. The Hanbali second type has no exact parallel in Hanafi—it resembles a combination of sahih and makruh rulings.
For fatwa, a Hanafi mufti must note that a Hanbali condition valid-but-nonbinding may be treated as fasid in Hanafi.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contracts are either immediate and absolute or qualified; the qualification may suspend existence, modify effects, or defer operation.

In Hanafi contract law, verbal dispositions can be issued either unconditionally or tied to a condition. This lesson introduces the three fundamental ways a condition can qualify a contract: suspending its existence (ta‘liq), restricting its effects (taqyid), or deferring its operation (idafa). Understanding this classification is essential for analyzing the validity and enforceability of conditional contracts.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the default: munjaz mutlaq
A contract uttered without any condition is immediate and absolute; all legal effects follow instantly. This is the baseline from which conditional deviations are measured.
Example: 'I sell you this horse' without any qualification.
2
Identify ta‘liq (suspension of existence)
When the contract's very existence is made contingent on a future uncertain event. The contract is not concluded until the condition occurs. Example: 'I sell you my share if my partner agrees.'
The contract is under the possibility of existence or non-existence.
3

When the contract is concluded immediately but its ordinary effects are modified by additional obligations or exceptions. Example: 'I sell you the car on condition that I use it for a month.'
The sale is valid now; the condition only alters the consequences.

Identify idafa (deferral of effect)
When the operation of the contract is postponed to a future time, even though the contract itself is concluded now. Example: 'I lease you this house effective from next month.'
The contract is binding immediately, but its effects (e.g., possession, rent) begin later.
5
Apply the classification to new scenarios
Given a conditional contract, ask: does the condition affect existence, effects, or timing? Use the mental checklist: (1) Is the contract's existence uncertain? → ta‘liq. (2) Does it modify obligations without affecting formation? → taqyid. (3) Does it postpone commencement? → idafa.
Practice with the examples in the passage and then with novel cases.

Chapter Forty-Three: Types of Contractual Conditions: Suspension, Restriction, and Deferral.
1/43 - Verbal dispositions, including contracts, have two general states with respect to absoluteness and restriction:
(a) Either they are issued by the speaker as immediate and absolute, i.e., free from any restriction or condition. In that case, the disposition or contract exists in legal consideration, and its rulings and effects follow immediately upon its creation. For example, if one of the two contracting parties says to the other, 'I sell you this horse,' or 'I lease you this house for one year for such and such dinars,' and the other accepts, then the contract is concluded and takes effect immediately. The buyer owns the sold item, and the lessee owns the usufruct in exchange for the specified consideration due upon it. This is called an immediate and absolute contract.
(b) Or they are issued by the speaker tied to something intended to:
- suspend the existence of the contract, i.e., linking its existence to the existence of something else such that the contract does not exist unless that thing exists,
- or restrict its ruling and effects,
- or defer its effect to a specific time:
1 - For example, if the seller says, 'I sell you my share in this house for such and such if my partner agrees,' and the buyer accepts, the two contracting parties do not intend to conclude the sale immediately; rather, they have suspended its existence and linked it to the partner's consent, which may or may not be given. Thus, the sale is subject to the possibility of existence or non-existence.
2 - If the seller says, 'I sell you this car for such and such on condition that I use it for a month before delivery,' or 'on condition that I repair it whenever a defect occurs for two years,' and the buyer accepts, the two parties have intended to conclude the sale immediately but wanted to modify its effects with a restriction that preserves for the seller a right in the benefits of the car for a month after it leaves his ownership, or obligates him to repair any defect that occurs. Without the condition, the effect of the sale would be that ownership of the car transfers and delivery is due immediately, and then the seller has no right to anything in it or its benefits, nor is he responsible for any subsequent defect occurring in the buyer's possession.
- If the lessor says, 'I lease you this house for one year for such and such, effective from the first of next month,' and the lessee accepts, they have deferred the ruling of the lease and added it to a future time. Without this specified restriction, its ruling would have begun and the lessee's rights would have been established immediately after the contract.
2/43 - Through these three examples, it becomes clear that the conditions by which a contracting party imposes a restriction on his contract, deviating from the path of immediacy and absoluteness, are of three types:
First: The condition that suspends the formation of the contract and similar verbal dispositions, making its existence dependent on another matter. This is called suspension upon a condition, as in the first example.
Second: The condition that does not obstruct formation but rather restricts the ruling of the contract, modifying its original effects by imposing obligations between the parties that would not have been required had the contract been issued absolutely without the condition.

This passage presents a foundational classification in Hanafi contract law regarding how a contracting party may qualify a verbal disposition. الإضافة (al-iḍāfah): Deferring the legal effect of a contract to a future time, such as starting the lease from a later date; the contract is concluded now but its obligations and rights arise later.
المنجز المطلق (al-munjaz al-muṭlaq): A contract that is executed immediately and without any condition or restriction; all legal effects follow instantly.

Set-off and Union of Liability: Two Modes of Debt Extinction in Hanafi Fiqh
Explains two methods of debt extinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between set-off (muqasah) and union of liability (ittihad al-dhimma) and apply the conditions for each in a fatwa context.

Core Idea
Union of liability extinguishes a debt when one person becomes both debtor and creditor; set-off requires two reciprocal and immediately due debts.

Contracts are dissolved not only by performance but also by other mechanisms. In Hanafi law, two important modes of debt extinction are set-off (al-muqasah) and union of liability (ittihad al-dhimma). This lesson clarifies their definitions, conditions, and the critical difference between them, equipping you to identify which applies in a given case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the debt configuration
Determine whether you are dealing with one debt involving two roles (debtor and creditor) or two separate reciprocal debts. This distinction determines which mechanism applies.
Union requires a single debt; set-off requires two debts.
2
Check conditions for set-off
For set-off to be valid, the two debts must be of the same type (e.g., both monetary), same reality (e.g., both due), and both immediately due (not deferred). If one is deferred, set-off is not permitted.
The source states: 'One of them is immediately due and the other's debt is deferred, so there is no set-off between them.'
3
Apply union of liability
If one person becomes both debtor and creditor of the same debt (e.g., by inheritance), the debt is extinguished. No other condition is needed beyond the merger of roles.
Example: debtor inherits from creditor.
4
Articulate the difference
Summarize: union = one debt, one person, extinction by role merger; set-off = two debts, two persons, extinction by mutual cancellation.
This is the key takeaway for fatwa application.

Bai' al-Wafa: Legal Definition, Hybrid Nature, and Detailed Rulings
Defines and lists detailed rulings of bai' al-wafa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bai' al-wafa, state its six pledge-like rulings, and apply the rule of limited liability upon destruction to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Bai' al-wafa is a security contract that gives the buyer usufruct but imposes pledge-like liability and return obligation.

This lesson unpacks a contract that is widespread in practice but often misunderstood. Bai' al-wafa is not a true sale; it is a collateral arrangement with beneficial use. You will learn the precise rulings that govern it, and see how Hanafi fiqh balanced the parties' interests while avoiding riba.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the dual nature
Identify the sale element (ownership of usufruct) and the pledge element (limited liability, return obligation). Note that pledge rulings dominate.
The buyer can use or lease the property, unlike a pure pledge where the pledgee cannot.
2
Internalize the six pledge rulings
Study each ruling: (1) no consumption or transfer, (2) return upon price repayment, (3) no pre-emption, (4) seller bears maintenance, (5) liability upon destruction is limited to the price, (6) judicial sale if seller fails to redeem.
These rulings stem from the contract's real nature as a security device.
3
Apply the liability rule
For destruction scenarios with equal, less, or greater value, determine who owes what and how set-off applies.
If value equals price, both debts cancel; if less, buyer recovers difference; if more, seller cannot claim excess unless buyer negligent.
4

The contract was devised because a pure pledge does not allow the creditor to benefit; people withheld loans without benefit; bai' al-wafa provided a lawful alternative to usury.
This is based on the principle of custom ('urf) and necessity.

Chapter (46) Presentation of the Most Important Nominate Contracts. 8/46 - (6) Bay' al-Wafa (Sale of Fulfillment): (Majalla/ 118-119 /and/ 396). It is a security contract in the form of a sale based on the parties retaining the right to return the countervalues. Thus it is a mixed contract of sale and pledge, but the rulings of pledge are predominant: (a) From the meaning of sale, the most important rulings are that the buyer in a wafa' sale by virtue of the contract owns the usufruct of the thing sold as wafa', so he may benefit from it himself and may exploit it by leasing it to the seller himself or to others, unlike the pledge, which requires the retention of the pledged property from any disposition; if he leased it with the consent of the pledgee, the pledge would become void due to the loss of the retention that secures the debt. (b) From the meaning of pledge, the following rulings: 1 - The buyer does not have the right to consume the sold item, nor to transfer its ownership to anyone for compensation or without compensation, nor to pledge it nor to create a real right over it for anyone; rather he must preserve and maintain it. 2 - The buyer is obligated to return the sold item as wafa' to the seller whenever the latter returns the price to him. This is the meaning of 'fulfillment' in its name, just as the buyer may, whenever he wishes, demand the recovery of the price and return of the sold item, as any creditor demands his debt. 3 - Real estate sold as wafa' is not subject to pre-emption because it is subject to return to its seller. 4 - If the item sold as wafa' needs maintenance and repair, its expense is on its seller, because it remains in his ownership. 5 - The sold item in the buyer's hands is guaranteed with the guarantee of pledge. That is, if it perishes in his hands, he is liable for its value to the extent of the paid price, just as the pledgee is liable for the value of the perished pledge only to the extent of the amount of the debt secured by it. Set-off occurs between them on this basis. If the value of the item sold as wafa' equals the price paid for it, then by its perishing the obligation to return the price falls from the seller, and the buyer has no recourse against him for anything. If its value is less than the price, the value of the sold item is deducted from the price, and the buyer recovers the remainder from the seller. If its value is more than the paid price—and this is usually the case—then the excess is a trust not guaranteed upon the buyer: so the obligation to return the received price falls from the seller due to the perishing of what corresponds to it of the sold item as wafa' in the buyer's possession, and thereafter the seller may not pursue the buyer for the excess of the value of the sold item, unless its perishing occurred due to the buyer's transgression or negligence in its preservation. 6 - If the seller in a wafa' sale does not return the price and recover his sold item, the buyer has the right to request the judge to sell it on his behalf; then the judge sells it at the seller's expense, and from its new price the buyer's right in the wafa' sale (i.e., his old price) is paid; if anything remains, it is for the seller; if the new price is less than the old one, the seller in the wafa' sale is responsible for the remainder. From this it appears that the sold item and the price in a wafa' sale have the ruling of the pledged property and the debt secured thereby, not the ruling of sold item and price in reality. And this contract was needed because the pledge does not grant the pledgee the right to benefit from it or to lease it in exchange for the debt, and if the debtor-pledgor permits the pledgee to benefit, he may revoke this permission legally. So they devised the wafa' sale to introduce, through the rulings of sale upon the rulings of pledge, the right of benefit for the credit-holder by virtue of the contract, because people withheld their wealth from lending without benefit. Thus this contract became customary in the form of purchase and benefit, but in the meaning of pledge, so that people might find an alternative to resorting to usury. The author first defines it as a security contract in sale form where both parties retain the right to return the countervalues. Under sale aspects, the buyer owns the usufruct and can use or lease the property, unlike a pure pledge where the pledgee cannot benefit. Under pledge aspects, six rulings are listed: (1) the buyer cannot consume or transfer ownership; (2) the buyer must return the property upon repayment of the price; (3) real estate in bai' al-wafa is not subject to pre-emption because it is liable to be returned; (4) the seller bears maintenance costs because ownership remains with him; (5) the property is in the buyer's hands with the same liability as a pledge—if it perishes, the buyer's liability is limited to the price, and set-off applies with detailed rules for cases of equal, lesser, or greater value; and (6) if the seller fails to redeem, the buyer may ask the judge to sell the property to satisfy the debt. The author concludes that this contract was devised because a pure pledge did not allow the creditor to benefit from the property, which led to a practical need to avoid usury. The Hanafi school, as reflected in the Majalla, recognizes bai' al-wafa as a valid contract, though other schools may consider it void due to the stipulation of return. This contract is an example of how Islamic law provides flexible solutions within its framework.

Financial Sanctions for Jinayat: Diya, Arsh, Hukumat al-Adl, and Kaffarah
Defines financial sanctions for bodily harm: diya, arsh, hukumat al-adl, and kaffarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define diya, arsh, hukumat al-adl, and kaffarah; differentiate their applications; and state their legal rulings in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Financial compensation for unlawful aggression is either fixed (diya/arsh) or assessed (hukumat al-adl); expiation (kaffarah) is a devotional duty for accidental killing.

In Hanafi fiqh, when qisas (retaliation) is inapplicable, the perpetrator faces ta'zir (discretionary punishment) and must pay monetary compensation. The type of compensation depends on the nature of the harm: diya for life, arsh for limbs, and hukumat al-adl for injuries without fixed amounts. Additionally, accidental killing obligates kaffarah as a devotional expiation. This lesson dissects these legal definitions and their practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching principle
Recognize that every unlawful aggression without qisas entails ta'zir and financial compensation. This is the general rule.
The hierarchy: qisas first, then compensation.
2
Define diya and arsh
Diya is fixed compensation for loss of life; arsh is fixed compensation for loss of a limb. Both amounts are predetermined by Sharia.
Diya for life is 100 camels or equivalent; arsh varies per limb.
3
Define hukumat al-adl
This is compensation assessed by the ruler with expert witnesses for injuries not covered by fixed diya/arsh e.g., wounds without specified amounts.
It is a discretionary monetary assessment based on injury severity.
4
Understand kaffarah
For accidental killing, kaffarah is a devotional wajib: freeing a believing slave or fasting two consecutive months. It is a religious duty not enforced by courts.
It is wajib diyani (duty towards Allah), not qada'i (legal claim).
5
Identify the legal maxim underlying these definitions
The implicit qawida: 'Every unlawful aggression not subject to qisas entails financial compensation, either fixed by Sharia or assessed by experts.'
This maxim governs the fiqh of jinayat.

Hanafi Method of Executing Qisas and the Wali's Right

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Qisas means extinguishing life with ihsan—a swift beheading, not mimicking the killer's brutality.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on how qisas (retaliation) is to be carried out. Unlike some other schools, the Hanafis do not require the execution method to mirror the killer's actions. Instead, they mandate a humane beheading, citing the prophetic command to excel in killing. The passage also addresses the modern permissibility of using quicker means like the guillotine, and affirms the wali's right to personally execute the sentence.

Guided Path

2

The hadith 'إن الله كتب الإحسان على كل شيء... فإذا قتلتم فأحسنوا القتلة' is cited. Note its source (Muslim, etc.) and how it supports the ruling: the best killing is swift and minimizes suffering.
The hadith is also used in other contexts like animal slaughter.
3
Explore the Flexibility
The text states 'ولا يوجد مانع شرعي من استعمال ما يظهر من وسائل أخرى إذا كانت مساوية للسيف كالمفصلة أو أسرع وأسهل قتلاً'. This shows the ruling adapts to modern methods as long as they are humane.
The authority to choose the method rests with the ruling authority.
4

The passage says: 'ويحق لولي الدم الشرعي، إن شاء أن يكون هو الذي يكلف من قبل صاحب السلطان بتنفيذ حكم الإعدام'. The wali may personally execute under state supervision, provided he is capable.
This preserves the victim family's right while ensuring order.
5

The Hanafi position opposes those who argue for talion-like execution (e.g., burning if victim was burned). The rebuttal is based on the hadith and the principle that qisas is only about taking life, not inflicting equivalent pain.
The practical difference: a poisoner is still beheaded under Hanafi law.

Liability for Goods Received in a Void Sale
Exposition of two views on buyer's liability in a void sale with the preferred Hanafi position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on liability for goods in a void sale, distinguish between mithli and qimi, and apply the analogy (qiyas) with trial purchase to justify the rule.

Core Idea
Possession based on exchange imposes liability even when the contract is void.

This passage addresses a critical nuance in Hanafi contract law: does a void sale have any legal effect on possession? While the contract itself is invalid, the buyer's possession may be treated differently if it was obtained on the basis of exchange. The author presents two scholarly views and establishes the preponderant Hanafi position through clear reasoning and analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two views
The author presents two views on buyer's liability for goods received in a void sale. The first view treats the goods as a trust (amanah) with no liability. The second view holds the buyer liable for the like or value.
2
Examine the evidence for the trust view
The trust view argues that since the sale is void, only permission to possess remains, which like a deposit (wadiʿah) does not impose liability unless the possessor transgresses.
3
Examine the evidence for the liability view
The liability view argues that possession was handed over on the basis of exchange (muʿawadah). The voidness prevents ownership transfer but does not erase the exchange intention.
4
Analyze the analogy (qiyas) with trial purchase
The author strengthens the second view by analogizing to goods received for trial purchase (sawm al-shiraʾ), where stating a price indicates exchange and imposes liability.
This is a qiyas jaliy (explicit analogy).
5
Apply the distinction between mithli and qimi
The liability is fulfilled by returning the like (mithl) for fungible goods, or the value (qimah) for non-fungible goods. Understand these categories.
6
Note the tarjih and practical consequence
The second view is preponderant in the Hanafi school and agreed by the other three imams. In practice, this means accidental destruction of the goods still requires compensation.
See Radd al-Muhtar 4/105.

Transformation (Inqilab) of a Void Contract into a Valid One
Rasm rule on void contract transformation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a void contract can be transformed into a valid contract under Hanafi law and apply the condition that the new contract must not conflict with the parties' purpose.

Core Idea
A void sale without price may transform into a valid gift if elements allow.

This passage explores an exceptional effect of void contracts: inqilab al-'aqd, where a void contract is reinterpreted as a valid contract of a different type. The key example is a sale with explicitly negated price, which is void as a sale but may be considered a valid gift. This shows the Hanafi school's nuanced approach to contract validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the void contract and its missing element
Determine which essential element is negated. In the example, the price is explicitly negated, making the sale void (batil).
2
Check for elements of another valid contract
Examine the contract's terms to see if they satisfy the pillars of a different contract type, such as a gift (hiba) which does not require a countervalue.
3
Assess alignment with parties' purpose
The new contract must not contradict the apparent intention of the parties. If the seller intended to transfer ownership for free, the transformation to gift is plausible.
4
Apply the transformation
The void contract is legally treated as the valid contract, achieving the parties' objective. The ruling on voidness is bypassed by this exceptional effect.
5
Distinguish from corruption (fasd)
If the price is merely omitted (not negated), the contract is fasid (corrupt), not void. Inqilab may not apply because the price is considered intended but unknown.

Voidness of Iqrar and Ibra Based on a Void Contract
States the rule that iqrar and ibra are void if based on a void contract.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when an iqrar or ibra is considered void due to its dependence on a void contract and apply the rule to a given scenario.

Core Idea
An iqrar or ibra built on a void contract is itself void.

A mufti frequently encounters cases where a party makes a declaration or discharge that appears valid but was based on an underlying contract later found null. This lesson presents a key rule from the Hanafi fatwa tradition: any iqrar or ibra that derives from a void contract loses its validity. You will learn to identify this dependency and apply the rule to protect the integrity of subsequent legal acts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Rule
Read the passage carefully: if a person makes an iqrar (declaration of a right) or ibra (discharge) that is based on a contract (e.g., sale or sulh), and that contract is subsequently proven void (batil), then the iqrar or ibra also becomes void. The rule applies whether the ibra is specific or general.
The key is the causal link: the declaration's legal basis is the contract.
2
Define Key Concepts
Recall the definitions: iqrar is a unilateral acknowledgment of a right; ibra is a unilateral release. A batil contract is one that is void ab initio, having no legal existence. Ensure you can distinguish these from fasid (voidable) contracts.
In Hanafi law, batil contracts produce no effects; fasid contracts may produce some effects until annulled.
3
Identify the Dependency
The rule hinges on the phrase 'mabniyyan alayh' (built upon). The iqrar or ibra must be founded on the void contract. If the declaration is independent (e.g., iqrar of a pre-existing debt unrelated to the contract), it remains valid even if the underlying contract is void.
Example: A says 'I owe B $100' because of a void sale. The iqrar is void. But if A acknowledges a debt from a prior loan, it is valid.
4
Apply to Scenarios
Given a factual situation, identify whether the iqrar or ibra is tied to a void contract. If yes, state that it is void; if no, it stands. Consider the references from Radd al-Muhtar and the Mejelle as authoritative support.
This is a mufti's tool: always check the basis of any declaration.

Gharar as a Cause of Contract Invalidity
Explaining gharar with examples and linking to gambling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify gharar in contractual stipulations and apply the Hanafi rule that excessive uncertainty resembling gambling invalidates the contract ( fasid ).

Core Idea
Stipulating an exact uncertain amount creates gharar, rendering the contract fasid.

Gharar is a general cause of invalidity in Hanafi contract law. This lesson trains the mufti to recognize prohibited uncertainty through classic examples in sales, partnerships, and sharecropping, applying the principle that gharar resembles gambling (qimar).

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of uncertainty
Examine the contract stipulation: does it involve an exact unknown outcome (like a fixed amount of milk from a cow)?
This step flags potential gharar.
2
Assess whether it is excessive like gambling
Compare to qimar: is the uncertainty so great that the contract resembles a game of chance? If yes, it is gharar.
The text explicitly links gharar to qimar.
3
Apply the ruling of fasād
If gharar is present, the contract becomes fasid (invalid). In Hanafi law, fasid does not mean void ab initio; it may still transfer ownership if executed.
Fasid vs batil is nuanced; see next lesson.
4
Distinguish from valid descriptive stipulations
A qualitative description (e.g., 'milking cow') is not gharar. If untrue, the buyer has a legal option (khiyar) to rescind.
The difference is between an exact uncertain number and a general attribute.
5
Apply to partnerships and sharecropping
Fixed profit or crop shares create gharar. Valid contracts require proportional shares (half, quarter, percentage).
The same principle extends to all commutative contracts.

The Hanafi Distinction between Fasid and Batil
Clarifying terminological laxity between fasid and batil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between fasid and batil contracts in Hanafi law and explain the practical legal consequences of each.

Core Idea
In Hanafi contract law, fasid is defective but not void; batil is void with no effect.

The passage notes that many jurists use 'fasid' and 'batil' loosely, but the Hanafi school maintains a precise distinction. This lesson clarifies that gharar contracts are fasid, not batil, and explains the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Define fasid
Fasid is a contract that has a defect in a subsidiary element, not in its essence. It is invalid but may still transfer ownership if executed.
Example: sale with gharar
2
Define batil
Batil is a contract that lacks an essential pillar or condition for validity. It is void ab initio and produces no legal effects.
Example: sale of stolen goods
3
Identify the defect in gharar contracts
Gharar affects a stipulation (subsidiary), not the core of the contract. Therefore, it is fasid, not batil.
Contrast with selling what one does not own – batil
4
Determine the legal effect
A fasid sale can transfer ownership if the goods are delivered and consumed; the buyer must pay market price. A batil sale cannot transfer ownership.
Practical consequence for fatwa
5
Apply to examples from the text
Stipulating fixed profit in partnership is fasid – not batil – because the defect is in the profit distribution, not in the partnership itself.
The Majalla's use of 'batil' was lax

Duress and Contracts: Invalid or Suspended?
Presenting the Hanafi dispute on duress: invalidates vs. suspends

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two Hanafi views on duress, distinguish between fasid and mawquf, and apply Zufar's view to conclude that duress bars enforceability, not validity.

Core Idea
Duress suspends, it does not invalidate — the contract awaits ratification.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ikhtilaf on the effect of duress in contracts. Imam Abu Hanifa held that duress renders the contract invalid (fasid), while his student Zufar considered it valid but suspended (mawquf). Understanding this difference is crucial for a mufti, as it determines whether ownership can transfer before ratification and whether subsequent consent can cure the defect. The text shows that Zufar's view was ultimately preferred and codified in the Majalla.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
List the views of Abu Hanifa and Zufar regarding the effect of duress on contracts. One says invalid (fasid), the other says valid but suspended (mawquf).
Duress is ikrah; fasid means voidable; mawquf means suspended.
2
Note the legal consequence of each view
For Abu Hanifa, if the contract is executed (e.g., delivery), ownership transfers, but the contract remains defective and can be rescinded. For Zufar, no ownership transfers until the victim ratifies.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf — the practical difference.
3

Zufar argues that the contract can be ratified after duress ceases, which is only possible if the contract is valid but suspended. A truly invalid contract cannot be ratified.
Ratification = ijazah; fasid contracts require rescission, not ratification.
4
Apply the tarjih (preference)
The text states that Zufar's view is stronger (أوجه) and that Article 1006 of the Majalla chose it. Thus, in fatwa, duress is a bar to enforceability, not a cause of invalidity.
Tarjih is based on the strength of the dalil and later adoption.
5
Relate to fatwa hierarchy
This ruling belongs to the category of fatawa mutakhkhirin, as the preferred view is from a later mujtahid (Zufar) and adopted by the Majalla, which is a codification of Hanafi law.
The mufta bihi is the view that duress suspends enforceability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that hudud punishments are not waived by the victim or ruler, explain the distinction from qisas, and apply the principle to a novel scenario.

Core Idea
Hudud are public rights (huquq Allah) — they cannot be waived, unlike qisas which is predominantly a personal right.

In this lesson, you will learn the fundamental rule that hudud (fixed penalties) are considered rights of Allah, i.e., public rights, and therefore cannot be waived by any individual, including the victim or the ruler. In contrast, qisas (retaliation) is predominantly a personal right, so it can be waived by the victim or guardian. Understanding this distinction is essential for fatwa in criminal cases.

(1) I opine that the reasoning of unlawful result applies only to the case of the mortgaged item, not the leased one. A- In mortgage, the mortgaged item may be sold judicially to satisfy the debt; so if the earlier buyer's waiver of his option to rescind were valid, his right to receive the item becomes merely potential, and this is excessive uncertainty (gharar) that is unlawful. B- As for lease, it has a fixed term that ends, so there is no gharar. The correct reasoning in my view for the invalidity of the buyer's waiver of his right to rescind when the item is leased is that lease in our fiqh is a time-based contract, i.e., continuous (see: F 14/47), and they explicitly state that it is like contracts renewed moment by moment. So if the buyer waives his right to rescind the purchase, a new right to rescind is renewed with the supposed renewal in the lease contract.

Commentary
This passage discusses a condition for the validity of waiving a right: it must not lead to an unlawful result. The author provides a practical example: if someone sells an item that was previously leased or mortgaged, the buyer has an option to rescind the contract. If he waives this option, the waiver is invalid because it would leave his right to the item in suspense, causing him pure harm (darar mahd) without a legal basis. The author then distinguishes between the mortgage and lease cases. In mortgage, if the buyer waives his option, his right becomes contingent on the repayment of the debt, which involves gharar (excessive uncertainty). In lease, the author contends that the reasoning is different: lease is a continuous contract that renews moment by moment; thus, even if the buyer waives his option once, a new option arises with each renewal of the lease. This is a nuanced Hanafi analysis of the interaction between contracts.

Key Terms
