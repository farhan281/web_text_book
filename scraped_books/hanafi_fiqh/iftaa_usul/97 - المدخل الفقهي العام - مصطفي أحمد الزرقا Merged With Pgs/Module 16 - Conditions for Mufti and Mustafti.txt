The Rule of Contradiction in Testimony
Definition and ruling of contradiction in testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of contradiction in testimony to distinguish when a contradiction nullifies testimony before judgment and when it does not after judgment.

Core Idea
Contradiction before judgment nullifies testimony; after judgment, it does not nullify the verdict.

This lesson examines a key legal maxim from the Majalla: the effect of a witness's contradictory statements. The rule balances the need for reliable evidence with the finality of judicial decisions. You will learn the precise conditions under which contradiction renders testimony worthless and when it instead triggers witness liability.

Guided Path
1
Define tanāquḍ
Understand that tanāquḍ means two statements from the same person, one negating the other. This definition is the starting point for applying the rule.
The contradiction must be in the testimony that establishes the claim, not in unrelated matters.
2
Identify the timing of the contradiction
Determine whether the contradicting statements occur before the judge passes judgment or after. This timing is decisive for the ruling.
Timing is the key variable; the same act of contradiction has completely different consequences based on when it occurs.
3
Apply the before‑judgment ruling
If the contradiction occurs before judgment, the testimony collapses and judgment cannot be based on it.
Example: a witness first says the debt is a loan, then says it is the price of a sale – testimony invalidated.
4
Apply the after‑judgment ruling
If the contradiction appears after judgment (e.g., witnesses retract), the judgment remains valid. The witnesses become liable for damages.
Rationale: it is not known which statement is true; allowing invalidation would open the door to fraud.
5
Note the condition for retraction
Retraction must take place before the judge; retraction outside court is not considered, either before or after judgment.
This condition is from Majalla article 1731.

Contradiction is that two statements issue from a person, one of which contradicts the other. Contradiction has extensive research, detailed rulings, and wide-ranging applications and subdivisions in the topics of claim, testimony, and confession from the fiqh books. The contradiction intended in this rule is the contradiction of the witness in his testimony that establishes the claim. So if contradiction occurs in the testimony of a witness before judgment is passed on it, for example, if he testifies in a claim of debt that the debt is a loan, then he says it is the price of a sale, then the argument with his testimony collapses and judgment is prevented. As for if contradiction appears in the evidence after judgment, for example, if the witnesses retract their testimony, or admit to something that contradicts it after judgment, then the judgment that occurred is not invalidated, rather the witnesses guarantee for the person judged against what was judged against him. And it is required in the retraction of the witness that it be before the judge. So if he manifests retraction outside the court session, his retraction is not considered, neither before nor after judgment. (Majalla article 1731). And the judgment is not invalidated because it cannot be certain that the second statement of the witnesses contradicting their first testimony is correct rather than the first. So if invalidation were allowed, it would be possible to cancel many judgments by bribing witnesses. Hence it is a rule that judgment is protected from cancellation as much as possible. 103/81 - end of classification and explanation of the ninety-nine rules that began the Majalla.

Commentary
This passage presents a rule from the Majalla regarding the effect of contradiction (tanāquḍ) in testimony. The author first defines tanāquḍ as two mutually negating statements from the same person. He then specifies that the relevant context is testimony that establishes a claim. The rule distinguishes two scenarios: if the contradiction occurs before the judge passes judgment based on the testimony, the testimony becomes inadmissible and cannot form the basis of a judgment. However, if the contradiction appears after judgment—such as the witnesses retracting or confessing to contradictory facts—the judgment remains valid, and the witnesses become liable for damages to the losing party. An important condition is that retraction must take place before the judge; retraction outside the court is disregarded. The rationale given is that it is impossible to be certain which of the two contradictory statements is true; allowing invalidation would enable the nullification of many judgments through witness tampering. Hence the maxim that judgments are protected from annulment as much as possible.
