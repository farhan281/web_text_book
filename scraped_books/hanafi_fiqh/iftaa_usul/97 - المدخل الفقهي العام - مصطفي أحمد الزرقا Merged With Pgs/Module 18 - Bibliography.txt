Navigating the Hanafi Reference Hierarchy
Lists references by subject for systematic study

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major Hanafi references in usul al-fiqh and fiqh, and classify any given work by its subject and scholarly rank using the bibliography from al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-Amm.

Core Idea
A mufti must know the source hierarchy: tafsir, hadith, usul, then furu' – each with its own authoritative texts.

This lesson unpacks the bibliography from al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-Amm. You will learn not just the titles, but how to locate and evaluate Hanafi references. Understanding this list is like having a map of the madhhab's official library – it shows you which books to trust and which commentaries to consult. We will walk through the categories and highlight the key Hanafi works that form the backbone of fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject categories
The bibliography groups works into 13 subject categories. Look at the headings: Quranic exegesis, Hadith, Usul al-Fiqh, Hanafi Fiqh, etc. Each category represents a layer of knowledge required for a mufti.
The categories are hierarchical: tafsir > hadith > usul > furu'.
2
Locate the Hanafi-specific works
Within each category, find the authors who are Hanafi (e.g., al-Jassas in tafsir, al-'Ayni in hadith, al-Bazdawi in usul, al-Kasani in fiqh). These are the primary sources for Hanafi rulings.
Death dates (Hijri) indicate the scholarly generation.
3
Distinguish between matn and sharh
Note how the author lists commentaries after the original work (e.g., al-Bazdawi's Usul followed by Kashf al-Asrar). This shows the hierarchy – the matn is the core, the sharh clarifies.
This helps you know which book to consult first for a ruling.
4
Assess the authority weight
Works by early Hanafi masters (al-Kasani, d. 587) are from the mutaqaddimun; later works like Ibn Nujaym (d. 970) are from the muta'akhkhirun. For fatwa, later works often carry the mufta bihi.
Al-Zarqa includes both; the mufti must know which position is operative.
5
Apply in practice
When faced with a nawazil, use this bibliography as a checklist: first check tafsir for Qur'anic basis, then hadith, then usul principles, then furu' works. This ensures systematic istinbat.
The bibliography is a tool, not a fatwa source itself.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography (ثبت بالمراجع) from al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-Amm by Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa. It lists the major references used in the book, categorized by subject: Qur'anic exegesis, Hadith, Usul al-Fiqh, and Hanafi Fiqh. The author explains that he has organized the books within each category alphabetically by the first word of the title, except for commentaries, which are placed after the primary work. The death dates of the authors (Hijri) are given in parentheses. This bibliography is valuable for understanding the sources of Hanafi fiqh and usul. Notable Hanafi works include al-Jassas's Ahkam al-Qur'an, al-'Ayni's 'Umdat al-Qari, al-Bazdawi's Usul (with its commentary Kashf al-Asrar by al-Bukhari), Ibn al-Humam's al-Tahrir (with al-Taqrir wa al-Tahbir), and al-Kasani's Bada'i' al-Sana'i'. Also included are standard works from other schools and Shafi'i and Maliki usul works. The list is comprehensive for a Hanafi jurist's library.

Navigating the Fiqh Bibliography: A Mufti's Research Toolkit
List of references with classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify major fiqh references by madhhab and evaluate their utility for Hanafi iftaa research.

Core Idea
A mufti's bibliography is his arsenal—knowing where to find rulings is half the fatwa.

This lesson examines the bibliography of Mustafa al-Zarqa's renowned work _Al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm_. While it may seem like a simple list, this collection reveals the comparative and interdisciplinary approach essential for modern Hanafi iftaa. You will learn to identify key texts from each madhhab, distinguish their roles in fatwa derivation, and apply this knowledge to streamline your research.

Guided Path
1
Understand the classification system
The bibliography is divided into sections: classical fiqh works (grouped by madhhab), works on legal theory, history of legislation, contemporary studies, and foreign law. Note how the author groups them—this reflects the sources he deems authoritative.
Pay attention to the section headings: 'كتب الفقه على المذاهب' (fiqh books by school), 'كتب تاريخ التشريع' (history of legislation), etc.
2
Categorize each work by madhhab
For each listed book, identify the madhhab it belongs to (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali, Zaydi). For example, 'Bidāyat al-Mujtahid' is Maliki, 'Kashshāf al-Qināʿ' is Hanbali, and 'Nihāyat al-Muḥtāj' is Shafi'i. Note that Hanafi works are not explicitly listed in this bibliography—al-Zarqa assumes the reader already knows them or he covers them elsewhere.
The absence of direct Hanafi titles is notable; consider why a Hanafi scholar might include other schools' works in his bibliography.
3
Identify the author's methodological approach
Al-Zarqa includes works from all four Sunni schools and even Zaydi Shi'a. This indicates his comparative fiqh methodology. He also includes works on legal theory (e.g., 'Qawāʿid al-Aḥkām') and history, showing that iftaa requires both fiqh and an understanding of its development.
This multidisciplinary approach is recommended for muftis to handle contemporary issues comprehensively.
4
Connect references to Hanafi iftaa practice
Even though the bibliography lacks explicit Hanafi texts, these references are used in Hanafi fatwa through comparison and istidlal. For instance, when a Hanafi mufti encounters a novel case, he may consult Maliki or Shafi'i rulings to inform istihsan or qiyas.
The tool 'Al-Mudawwana' (Maliki) or 'Al-Mughnī' (Hanbali) can provide alternative perspectives that strengthen a Hanafi argument.
5
Apply the classification to a research scenario
Imagine you are asked for a fatwa on a modern financial contract. Which references from this list would you use? You would start with Hanafi sources (not solely listed here), but then check 'Qawāʿid al-Aḥkām' for maxims, 'Tārīkh al-Tashrīʿ' for historical context, and 'Al-Fiqh ʿalā al-Madhāhib al-Arbaʿa' for comparative positions.
This step simulates a real mufti's research process.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the categories of references in a Hanafi fiqh bibliography and explain why each category matters for legal scholarship.

Core Idea
A bibliography reveals the intellectual foundation and methodology of a scholarly work.

This passage is a bibliography from Mustafa al-Zarqa's 'Al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-Amm'. It is not a ruling but a map of sources that shaped his comparative fiqh approach. Learning to read such a list helps you evaluate a scholar's reliability, trace influences, and build your own research foundations.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Structure
Identify that the bibliography is divided into distinct sections (French civil law, language/grammar, literature/history, other related works, and post-second edition additions). Each section serves a different purpose in the author's research.
Note the numbering jump (121 after 119) – likely a pagination artifact, not a gap.
2
Categorize Each Entry
For each entry, determine whether it is a classical Islamic source (e.g., works by al-Zamakhshari, Ibn Manzur) or a modern legal work (e.g., French civil law texts). Observe the mix.
Some entries are in Arabic, some in French (mentioned in parentheses).
3
Assess the Author's Methodology
Why does a fiqh introduction include French civil law? This shows al-Zarqa's comparative approach – he uses Western legal thought to contrast and clarify Islamic rulings, a common method in modern Hanafi scholarship.
Compare with classical Hanafi works that rarely cite non-Islamic sources.
4
Trace Chronological Depth
Note the death dates (AH) of classical authors. This shows the time range of sources – from early (al-Jahiz, 225 AH) to later (al-Laknawi, 1304 AH). Understanding the era of a source helps assess its authority.

Apply to Your Own Research
As a student of Hanafi fiqh, you can use similar categories to build a reference list for your studies: core Hanafi texts, language tools (for better understanding of Arabic terms), historical context works, and modern discussions of contemporary issues.
This skill is especially valuable for a mufti who must verify sources before issuing fatwas.
