The Imperative as Offer: General Rule and Marriage Exception
Clarifying that the imperative mood does not constitute an offer except in marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi rule that the imperative is not an offer, explain the marriage exception with its two juristic interpretations, and evaluate how custom ('urf) could extend this exception to all contracts.

Core Idea
The imperative is a request, not an offer—except in marriage, where it is treated as an offer or agency; custom may now generalize the exception.

In Hanafi contract law, the words used to form an offer and acceptance are strictly regulated. The imperative form (sigha al-amr) is generally considered a request, not the creation of an offer. However, the marriage contract (nikah) is a well-known exception, where jurists debate whether the imperative serves as an offer or as an appointment of agency. Contemporary custom ('urf) might justify extending this exception to all contracts, reflecting the evolution of commercial practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
State the Hanafi position that the imperative is not an offer; it is a request. The offer is the response.
The rule is codified in Majallah article 172.
2
Recognize the exception for nikah
Note that marriage contracts are treated differently; the imperative can lead to a valid contract.
3
Analyze the two juristic explanations
Option A: the imperative is an offer by exception; Option B: it is an appointment of agency.
Both are valid within the Hanafi school.
4
Evaluate the role of custom
Consider the author's suggestion that if 'urf changes, the rule could be generalized.
Custom is a recognized source (Majallah article 36).
5
Apply in fatwa
Determine the mufta bihi and the impact of 'urf in contemporary cases.
Mufti must weigh established custom against default rule.

Commentary
This passage discusses a rasm rule in contract formation: the imperative form (e.g., 'Sell me') is not considered an offer or acceptance. The rule is that the imperative is merely a request (talab) and a charge (taklif), not the creation of a legal obligation. The offer is the speaker's response (e.g., 'I sold to you'). The exception is the marriage contract (nikah), where Jurists disagree: some treat the imperative as an offer by exception, others treat it as a form of agency (tawkeel), where the responding party acts as agent for both sides. The author suggests that modern custom could justify generalizing this exception to all contracts, since custom ('urf) is a recognized source in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
صيغة الأمر (sīghat al-amr): The imperative verbal form (e.g., 'Sell me' or 'Marry me') used in contractual contexts.
إيجاب (ījāb): The offer made by one party to initiate a contract.
توكيل (tawkīl): Agency, where one party deputizes another to act on their behalf.

Definition and Ruling of the Fuduli in Hanafi Law
Defines fuduli with examples and boundary condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of the fuduli, identify the three legitimate sources of delegation that prevent fuduli status, and explain the ruling (suspension) of a fuduli's contract according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A fuduli's contract is suspended until the principal ratifies; without delegation it is void if rejected.

This passage from Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa's introduction to fiqh defines the fuduli (unauthorized agent) and clarifies the ruling on his contracts. Understanding this term is crucial for distinguishing valid from suspended transactions in Hanafi muamalat. We will parse the definition, examine the sources of legitimate delegation, and apply the concept to practical examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition
Locate the precise definition of fuduli: one who verbally disposes of another's rights without lawful delegation. Note the restriction to verbal (qauli) acts.
The definition excludes physical acts, which become ghasp.
2
Distinguish from valid representation
Identify the three sources of legitimate delegation (niyaba): sharia (guardianship), judiciary (court-appointed executor), and contract (agency). A person acting under any of these is not a fuduli but a representative.
If delegation is absent from all three, the person is a fuduli.
3
Examine the examples
Review the examples: selling/leasing another's property, marrying a woman without her consent, partner disposing of entire shared property. Confirm the ruling: the contract is suspended (mawquf) regarding the principal's share.
Even a father marrying his adult daughter without her permission is a fuduli.
4
Note the boundary condition
Understand the crucial distinction: if a verbal fuduli act is followed by physical delivery, the actor becomes a ghasib (usurper), subject to the rules of ghasp (tort).
This shows the interplay between contract and tort in Hanafi law.

The Three Types of Transactions for a Discerning Minor and the Maxim of Apparent Benefit
Ruling on the third stage of capacity: types of transactions for a discerning minor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of transactions for a discerning minor and apply the maxim 'Apparent benefit in the present is not relied upon' to determine a transaction's validity.

Core Idea
A minor's transaction is either pure benefit, pure harm, or mixed; apparent benefit is not enough—the guardian assesses the real outcome.

This passage from Al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm explains the third stage of legal capacity, discernment (tamyiz), and how a minor's transactions are classified. The key rule is that the apparent immediate benefit of a transaction is not the criterion; rather, the guardian evaluates the ultimate interest of the minor. This lesson will help you understand the three categories and apply the maxim that governs them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of transactions
From the passage, list the three categories: pure benefit (e.g., accepting a gift), pure harm (e.g., donation), and mixed (e.g., sale).
Pure benefit is valid without guardian; pure harm is void; mixed is suspended.
2
Determine the ruling for each type
For each category, state the legal status: valid (nāfidh), void (bāṭil), or suspended (mawqūf).
Suspended transactions can be ratified by the guardian.
3
Apply the rule of gross fraud (ghabn fāḥish)
If gross fraud harms the minor in a mixed transaction, it becomes void like pure harm; if the minor is the defrauder, it remains suspended.
Gross fraud is overlooked for authorized minors (ma'dhūn) due to necessity.
4
Understand the maxim 'apparent benefit is not relied upon'
The passage states that apparent present benefit is not the criterion; the guardian assesses the transaction's ultimate outcome for the minor.
This is a qā'ida sughrā (minor maxim) in the chapter of legal capacity.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the third stage of legal capacity, the stage of discernment (tamyiz). The text delineates three types of transactions for a discerning minor (saghir mumayyiz): (1) pure harm (e.g., donation), which is void ab initio and cannot be ratified even after maturity; (2) pure benefit (e.g., accepting a gift), which is valid without guardian approval; (3) mixed benefit and harm (e.g., sale), which is suspended on the guardian's approval. The passage also introduces the concept of 'gross fraud' (ghabn fahish): if such fraud affects the minor in a mixed transaction, it becomes void like type one, protecting the minor. However, if the minor is the defrauder, the contract remains suspended. The overarching principle is that apparent present benefit is not the criterion; rather, the guardian assesses the ultimate benefit to the minor. This is a key rule (dabt fiqhi) in Hanafi law of capacity.

Key Terms
تمييز (tamyiz): The stage of discernment: the minor's ability to understand the nature of transactions and their benefits/harms, typically from age 7-15.
صغير (saghir): A minor, someone who has not reached puberty.
إجازة (ijaza): Ratification; retrospective approval of a contract by the guardian or the minor after maturity.
غبن فاحش (ghabn fahish): Gross fraud; a significant discrepancy between price and market value that is not normally tolerated in trade.
