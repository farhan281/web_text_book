List of six maxims on harm, authority, and exceptions with Majalla references.

Core Idea
Private harm is tolerated to prevent public harm; the lesser evil is chosen; special authority outweighs general; what is inexcusable at start is excused to continue; accessories are excused; translator accepted.

These six maxims guide a mufti in balancing harms and authorities. When public interest conflicts with private, the public takes precedence. In choosing between two evils, the lighter is taken. Additionally, exceptions for continuation and accessories recognize human fallibility.

These are used in emergencies and policy.

Identify authority types
Maxim 91: special authority (e.g., father over child) is stronger than general authority (e.g., judge). This affects guardianship cases.
Special authority is more specific.

Apply exception maxims
Maxim 95: if a situation was excused at the start (e.g., building a toilet near wall), it is excused to continue. Maxim 96: what is not allowed in others is allowed in accessories (e.g., saliva on sleeve in prayer).
These provide flexibility.

Maxim 97: the statement of a translator is accepted absolutely (without oath) unless challenged. Important in court procedure.

Ignorance Does Not Excuse from Liability: The Hanafi Legal Maxim
Statement of a qawida with an exception for kufr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim regarding ignorance and liability, distinguish between civil and criminal consequences, and identify the exception for unknowing utterances of kufr.

Core Idea
Ignorance of the law does not absolve civil liability, but may affect criminal liability; unknowing kufr is an exception.

This lesson examines a foundational Hanafi qawida: ignorance of the shari ruling does not automatically excuse one from liability. The author distinguishes civil (financial) consequences from criminal consequences and provides a crucial exception for cases of kufr. Understanding this maxim is essential for both muftis and students of fiqh, as it governs many everyday legal scenarios and aligns with the universal legal principle that ignorance of the law is no excuse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its scope
Read the first sentence: the passage states that ignorance does not excuse from civil (financial) consequences at all. Recognize that this is a general rule with no exceptions in civil matters.
Civil liability includes compensation for damages, debts, etc.
2
Distinguish civil from criminal consequences
Note that the author separates civil consequences (mandatory liability) from criminal consequences (penal). In criminal matters, ignorance may be considered within specific limits, as detailed in books of punishments.
This distinction is crucial for a mufti: the default is liability, but in criminal law, intent and knowledge matter.
3
Identify the exception for kufr
The passage explicitly exempts a person who utters words of kufr while ignorant that those words constitute kufr. Such a person is not judged a disbeliever. This is an exception to the criminal aspect of the rule.
The condition is that the person truly did not know (jahl) that the utterance was kufr. This is based on the Hadith 'Actions are judged by intentions.'
4
Locate the textual basis and sources
The passage cites classical Hanafi sources: al-Khadimi's Khatimat Majami' al-Haqa'iq and al-Hamzawi's Qawa'id. These works confirm the exception and its rationale.
In Hanafi usul al-ifta, rulings are traced to their authoritative sources. The mufti must know these references.
5
Apply the maxim to a modern scenario
Consider a person who damages another's property claiming he did not know it was prohibited. The maxim applies: ignorance does not excuse civil liability, so he must compensate. Conversely, if he utters a blasphemous phrase online without knowing it was kufr, the exception may apply.
This shows the practical difference between civil and criminal/creedal consequences.

Define key terms: shart and mashrut
A shart (condition) is an external prerequisite for the ruling, while mashrut (conditioned) is the act or ruling that depends on it. Conditions are distinct from rukn (integral) in that they are prerequisites outside the act's essence.
In Hanafi usul, this distinction is crucial; missing a rukn invalidates entirely, but missing a condition also invalidates per this qawida.
3
Identify the scope
This qawida applies to any legal act that is conditioned by multiple prerequisites, such as prayers (taharah, facing qibla), contracts (offer and acceptance, capacity), and legal rights.
It is a universal (kulliya) qawida governing all chapters of fiqh.
4
Apply to a simple case
Take wudu: conditions include intention, water being pure, and washing the limbs. If one condition is absent (e.g., not washing the face), the wudu is invalid, not partially valid.
The qawida does not allow partial fulfillment; all conditions must be present.
5
Note the istithna (exception)
This rule applies to conditions of validity (shurut al-sihhah), not conditions of obligation (shurut al-wujub). A wajib condition, if missed, may require compensation (e.g., sajdah al-sahw) but does not invalidate the act itself.
This is a common misunderstanding; the qawida is strict for validity conditions.

The Major Qawida: Whatever an Obligation Cannot Be Completed Without Is Obligatory
Definition of a major Hanafi legal maxim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the qawida 'ma la yatimmu al-wajibu illa bihi fahuwa wajib', explain its meaning, and apply it to a fiqh scenario to determine whether a means becomes obligatory.

Core Idea
If an act is indispensable for fulfilling an obligation, that act itself becomes obligatory.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim (qawida kulliya) in the Hanafi school: 'What an obligation cannot be completed without is itself obligatory.' This qawida is central to deriving obligations for means (wujub al-wasila). Understanding its precise scope—that only the minimal necessary means are obligatory—is crucial for correct fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic text
Begin by memorizing the qawida exactly as transmitted: 'ما لا يتم الواجب إلا به فهو واجب'.
This qawida is often cited with the abbreviation (ر : ف ٢/٦٤) referring to Fatawa.
2
Understand the literal meaning
Break down the Arabic: 'ma la yatimmu' (what is not completed), 'al-wajibu' (the obligation), 'illa bihi' (except by it), 'fahuwa wajib' (then it is obligatory). So: if an obligation cannot be fulfilled without a certain act, that act becomes obligatory.
The term 'wajib' here refers to an obligatory act in the Shariah.
3
Identify the scope and limit
Note that the qawida applies only to indispensable means. Supererogatory or recommended acts that are not strictly necessary do not become obligatory.
Common misunderstanding: every act that helps fulfill an obligation is not necessarily wajib; only the minimal means are.
4
Apply to a concrete fiqh example
Consider the obligation of the five daily prayers. Without wudu (ablution), the prayer is invalid. Therefore, wudu becomes obligatory by virtue of this qawida. Similarly, covering the 'awrah and facing the qibla are obligatory because they are necessary for the prayer's validity.
This application shows how the qawida operates in deriving secondary obligations.

Rights Are Subject to Stipulations (مقاطع الحقوق عند الشروط)
Qawida definition attributed to 'Umar ibn al-Khattab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the qawida 'مقاطع الحقوق عند الشروط' and explain its legal meaning as a foundational Hanafi principle governing the extent of rights in contracts.

Core Idea
Rights are defined and limited by valid conditions.

This short but powerful statement from Caliph 'Umar ibn al-Khattab is one of the great legal maxims (al-qawa'id al-kubra) in the Hanafi school. It establishes that the rights of parties in a transaction are determined by the conditions they agree upon, provided those conditions are valid in Islamic law. This principle is essential for muftis when evaluating contractual stipulations.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic phrase
Learn the exact wording: مقاطع الحقوق عند الشروط. This is a memorization anchor for later application.
2
Understand the lexical meaning
'Maqati'' means 'cut-off points' or 'boundaries'; 'shurut' means 'conditions'. So literally 'the cut-off points of rights are at the conditions'.
3
Grasp the legal import
Rights are not absolute but are bounded by the conditions stipulated. The condition sets the limit of the right.
4
Distinguish valid vs. invalid conditions
Only conditions that are valid in shari'ah (e.g., no riba, no contradiction with the contract's essence) are binding. Invalid conditions do not affect rights.
This is a critical nuance often missed.
5
Apply the qawida to a practical scenario
Example: In a sale, the buyer stipulates delivery by a certain date. If delivery fails, the buyer's right to cancel arises from that condition.
