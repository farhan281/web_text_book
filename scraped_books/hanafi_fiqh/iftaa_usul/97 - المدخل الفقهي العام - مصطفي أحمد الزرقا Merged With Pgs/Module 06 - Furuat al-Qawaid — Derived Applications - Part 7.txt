Limitation of an Heir's Admission and Its Exception
Analyzing the rule of limitation of admission and its exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of qusur al-iqrar (limitation of admission) to inheritance cases and identify the exception for a father's admission of dower receipt, explaining the role of custom.

Core Idea
An heir's admission binds only his own share; except a father's admission of his daughter's dower binds her.

In Hanafi inheritance law, an heir who admits a debt against the estate does not obligate the other heirs; only his own share is affected. This principle, called qusur al-iqrar, ensures fairness. However, an important exception exists: when a father (or grandfather) admits receipt of his adult virgin daughter's dower at marriage, his admission binds her, even if she later denies it. This is based on the customary right of the father to receive the dower. In this lesson, you will explore the rule, its application, and the rationale behind the exception.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its scope
Identify the Arabic maxim: 'المقر لا من حصة بقية الورثة' (the one who admits does so only from his share, not from the shares of the remaining heirs). Understand that this applies to admissions made by heirs regarding debts of the deceased, within inheritance law and judicial procedure.
The maxim is a wusta (intermediate) level qawida.
2
Apply the maxim to the primary furu
Consider the case where an heir admits a debt owed by the deceased. According to the maxim, the admission only affects his own share of the inheritance, even if the debt is larger. The creditor can only recover from that heir's portion.
This contrasts with evidence (bayyina) which, if established against one heir, binds the entire estate.
3
Identify the istithna (exception)
Recognize the exception: a father (or grandfather) admitting receipt of his adult virgin daughter's dower. This admission binds the daughter, overriding the general rule of qusur al-iqrar. The reason is that custom ('urf) grants the father the right to receive the dower, and whoever has the right to receive also has the right to admit receipt.
The governing qawida here is 'العادة محكمة' (custom is authoritative).
4
Analyze the interplay between the rule and the exception
Understand that the exception does not invalidate the general rule. It shows that qawida can be restricted by another maxim or by custom. In fatwa practice, one must first apply the general rule, then check if the conditions for the exception are met.
This is a common pattern in Hanafi legal reasoning: general principle + specific exception based on custom.

Commentary
This passage, from al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm by Mustafa al-Zarqa, provides an analytical view of the rules of evidence (qawa'id al-ithbat). It begins by reiterating the established principle that an heir's admission (iqrar) of a debt owed by the deceased only binds his own share of the inheritance; it does not bind the co-heirs because their companion's admission does not take effect on them. The author then contrasts this with the case where a creditor produces evidence (bayyina) against one heir: if judgment is based on that evidence, the debt is recovered from the entire estate, even if it exhausts all heirs' shares, because the evidence takes effect on all. The practical consequence is that a creditor who has an heir's admission should still request a delay in judgment to present evidence, so that the evidence binds all heirs, avoiding the need for a separate lawsuit. The author notes that admission is normally stronger than evidence for the one who admits, but the priority here is to bind the others. Then an exception (istithna') is given: if a father or grandfather admits receipt of the dower (mahr) of his adult virgin daughter at the time of her marriage, that admission is binding on her, even if she later denies receipt. This is because the father has the customary right to receive the dower, and whoever has the right to receive has the right to admit receipt. This is based on the theory of custom ('urf). The passage concludes that these principles are part of the system of judicial proof in Islamic law.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): An admission or acknowledgment of a right or debt; a form of proof that binds only the one who admits.
بينة (bayyina): Legal evidence, typically the testimony of two witnesses; when established, it binds all parties to the case.
قصور الإقرار (qusur al-iqrar): The principle that an admission is confined to the person who makes it and does not extend to others.

A maxim is only useful if you can find it—the index turns a scattered list into a searchable tool.

Subsequent Ratification as Prior Agency
Introduction and first supplementary maxim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-ijaza al-lahiqah ka al-wikala al-sabiqa' to determine the legal effect of retroactive ratification in contracts and agency.

Core Idea
A ratified contract is as valid as if it had prior authorization from the start.

This lesson introduces the first supplementary maxim from al-Zarqa's list. The principle of retroactive ratification is fundamental in Hanafi contract law, allowing subsequent approval to perfect an otherwise unauthorized act. Understanding this maxim is essential for muftis dealing with issues of agency, sale, and marriage ratification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and initial lack of authority
Determine if an act was performed without prior permission (e.g., a person sells another's property without agency).
The act must be capable of ratification; void acts are excluded.
2
Check for subsequent ratification
Look for an express or implied approval by the owner after the fact.
Ratification can be explicit or by conduct.
3
Apply the maxim
Once ratification occurs, treat the act as if it was authorized from the beginning, giving it full legal effect.
This retroactive validation is unique to Hanafi fiqh; some schools require prior agency.
4
Consider exceptions
If the act is void (batil), it cannot be ratified (see maxim 7). Also, ratification does not affect third-party rights acquired before ratification.
Refer to details in the Majalla (article 1453).

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the third section (Chapter 83) of the tenth chapter on general maxims. The author, Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa, indicates that he is appending additional general qawaid to the ninety-nine already explained. He arranges them alphabetically for ease of reference. The first rule states that subsequent ratification (ijaza) is like prior agency; once a contract is ratified later, it is as if the agent had authority from the start. The second rule is the well-known maxim 'The original ruling of things is permissibility' (al-asl fi al-ashya' al-ibaha), meaning that everything is permissible unless there is evidence of prohibition. The third rule concerns the binding force of contracts: consent is the basis, and the contract's effect is what the parties have agreed. Al-Zarqa notes parallels in French and Syrian civil law. The fourth rule says a trustee (amin) is believed on oath regarding his discharge of responsibility. The fifth rule equates spending by judicial order to spending by the owner's permission; for instance, spending on a lost item by court order allows recourse to the owner later. The sixth rule limits the trustee's word: he is believed only to clear himself, not to impose a liability on others. The seventh states that a void (batil) act cannot be ratified to become valid. The eighth rule says that if a condition is attached to something already existing, it becomes immediate. The ninth rule declares that ignorance of the law in a Muslim land is not a valid excuse. These rules are fundamental in Hanafi ifta and are frequently referenced.

Key Terms
الإجازة اللاحقة (al-ijaza al-lahiqah): Subsequent ratification of a contract, which retroactively makes it valid as if prior agency existed.
الوكالة السابقة (al-wikala al-sabiqa): Prior agency that is given before an act, as opposed to subsequent ratification.

Original Ruling of Things is Permissibility
Second supplementary maxim: permissibility as default

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-asl fi al-ashya' al-ibaha' to determine whether an action or object is permissible in the absence of prohibitory evidence.

Core Idea
Everything is legally permissible unless there is clear evidence of prohibition.

This lesson covers the second supplementary maxim, which is one of the foundational principles of Islamic law. It establishes that the default ruling for all things and actions is permissibility (ibaha). This principle is crucial for fatwa, especially in dealing with new matters not explicitly addressed in the primary sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object or action in question
Determine what is being evaluated (e.g., a food, a transaction, a behavior).
2
Search for prohibitory evidence
Check the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, or Qiyas for a clear prohibition (tahrim or karaha).
3
Apply the default ruling
If no valid prohibitory evidence is found, the ruling is permissibility (ibaha).
4
Consider exceptions
Note that some things may be prohibited by default in certain contexts (e.g., acts of worship require permission).

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the third section (Chapter 83) of the tenth chapter on general maxims. The author, Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa, indicates that he is appending additional general qawaid to the ninety-nine already explained. He arranges them alphabetically for ease of reference. The first rule states that subsequent ratification (ijaza) is like prior agency; once a contract is ratified later, it is as if the agent had authority from the start. The second rule is the well-known maxim 'The original ruling of things is permissibility' (al-asl fi al-ashya' al-ibaha), meaning that everything is permissible unless there is evidence of prohibition. The third rule concerns the binding force of contracts: consent is the basis, and the contract's effect is what the parties have agreed. Al-Zarqa notes parallels in French and Syrian civil law. The fourth rule says a trustee (amin) is believed on oath regarding his discharge of responsibility. The fifth rule equates spending by judicial order to spending by the owner's permission; for instance, spending on a lost item by court order allows recourse to the owner later. The sixth rule limits the trustee's word: he is believed only to clear himself, not to impose a liability on others. The seventh states that a void (batil) act cannot be ratified to become valid. The eighth rule says that if a condition is attached to something already existing, it becomes immediate. The ninth rule declares that ignorance of the law in a Muslim land is not a valid excuse. These rules are fundamental in Hanafi ifta and are frequently referenced.

Key Terms
الأصل في الأشياء الإباحة (al-asl fi al-ashya' al-ibaha): The default legal ruling for all things (except acts of worship) is permissibility until evidence of prohibition arises.

Consent as Basis of Contracts
Third supplementary maxim: consent in contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-asl fi al-'aqd rida al-muta'aqidayn' to determine the validity and interpretation of contracts in Hanafi law.

Core Idea
Contracts are built on mutual consent; their effect is what the parties agreed upon.

This lesson examines the third supplementary maxim, which emphasizes that consent (rida) is the foundation of contractual validity. The contract's outcome is determined by the parties' agreement. This principle is central to Hanafi contract law and is echoed in modern civil codes. Al-Zarqa notes parallels with the French Civil Code and Syrian law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and the consent of parties
Ensure that each party freely agrees to the contract without duress or fraud.
2
Determine the terms agreed upon
The contract's effects are limited to what the parties have explicitly or implicitly agreed to.
3
Apply the maxim to resolve disputes
If there is disagreement, look to the expressed terms and default rules that supplement consent.
4
Consider limitations on party autonomy
Consent does not override divine commands; contracts that contradict shariah are invalid despite consent.

Ignorance of the Law Is No Excuse in Civil Liability
Subsidiary legal maxims with explanations and exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that ignorance of Islamic rulings does not exempt civil liability and identify the exception for apostasy statements.

Core Idea
Ignorance of the law does not excuse civil financial liability but may affect criminal penalties.

This maxim is a cornerstone of legal responsibility in the Hanafi school. The passage explains that claiming ignorance after committing a civil or criminal act does not automatically absolve one from consequences, especially financial ones. However, it carves out an exception for apostasy uttered in genuine ignorance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim and Its Scope
Locate the maxim in the source: 'الجَهْل بِالأحْكَام فِي دَار الْإسْلَام لَيْسَ عُذْرًا'. Understand that it applies to both civil and criminal acts, but the effect differs.
The maxim is rule 9 in the text.
2
Analyze the Civil Consequences
The text states that ignorance does not absolve from civil (financial) consequences at all. This means financial obligations arising from a contract or tort remain binding.
The phrase 'مطلقاً' emphasizes absolute non-exemption.
3
Examine the Criminal Consequences
For penal consequences, ignorance may have an effect if established, but it is limited to specific cases known from the sections on punishments.
The text says 'إذا تحقق تأثير ضمن حدود تعرف في مواطنها'.
4
Identify the Exception
The exception: if a person speaks words of apostasy while ignorant that they are apostasy, he is not judged an apostate. This shows that intention matters for acts of kufr.
This exception is based on the principle that apostasy requires intent.
5
Apply the Maxim to a Modern Scenario
A mufti faces a query: a person entered a forbidden contract claiming ignorance. The maxim implies he must still fulfill the financial obligations (e.g., return the object or its value).
The maxim upholds legal stability and prevents abuse.

Spending by Judge’s Order Is Like Spending by Owner’s Order
Subsidiary legal maxim with two illustrative applications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that expenses incurred by judicial order give the spender the right to reclaim from the owner, just as if the owner had ordered the spending.

Core Idea
Spending under judicial authority creates a right of recourse against the owner, unlike voluntary spending.

This subsidiary maxim clarifies the legal status of a person who spends money on property under a judge's order. Unlike a volunteer, such a spender is not a mere benefactor; he can recover his expenses from the owner. The passage provides two classic applications: spent on lost property and spent on jointly owned property subject to division.

Guided Path
1
State the Maxim and Its Legal Effect
Write the maxim: الإنفاق بأمر القاضي كالإنفاق بأمر المالك. Understand that it equates judicial authorization with the owner's own authorization, giving the spender a right of recourse.
The key consequence is that the spender is not a volunteer (متبرع).
2
Analyze the Lost Property (Luqata) Application
If someone finds a lost animal and the judge orders him to spend on its upkeep, the spender can recover those expenses when the owner appears.
Without the judge's order, the finder would be a volunteer and could not claim reimbursement.
3
Analyze the Jointly Owned Property Application
If co-owners of a divisible property have one partner spend on its upkeep by judicial order, that partner can reclaim the proportionate share from the others.
This prevents unjust enrichment of the other owners.
4
Differentiate from Voluntary Spending
Contrast with spending without judicial order: if a person spends voluntarily on another's property, he is a donor and cannot reclaim, unless he had explicit permission from the owner.
The maxim highlights the importance of authorization.
5
Apply the Maxim to a Modern Scenario
A modern analogy: a court-appointed guardian spends on the maintenance of an orphan's property. The guardian can reclaim the expenses from the orphan's estate when it is available.
The principle remains the same: judicial order substitutes for owner's consent.

Rights Do Not Lapse by Prescription
Subsidiary maxim on the permanence of rights despite the passage of time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that rights are not extinguished by the passage of time (prescription) and distinguish between this and secular statutes of limitation.

Core Idea
Under Hanafi fiqh, a right is not lost merely by delay in claiming; it remains enforceable until explicitly waived or barred by specific rules.

This subsidiary maxim addresses the fate of rights over time. In many legal systems, a right may lapse if not exercised within a certain period (statute of limitations). The Hanafi position, articulated in this maxim, is that time alone does not extinguish a right. However, there are nuances: certain claims may become unenforceable due to lack of evidence or procedural bars, but the substantive right remains.

Guided Path
1
State the Maxim and Its Literal Meaning
Write: الحق لا يسقط بالتقادم. Understand 'حق' as a legal right (e.g., ownership, debt, claim). 'تقادم' means lapse of time or prescription. The maxim negates that time alone extinguishes the right.
The word 'لا يسقط' emphasizes that the right does not fall away.
2
Identify the Hanafi Basis
The Hanafi school holds that rights are divinely sanctioned and cannot be annulled by mere human inaction. Proof: the Quranic command to fulfill debts (e.g., 2:282) implies that debts remain due regardless of time.
Contrast with secular statutes that cut off rights after a period.
3
Distinguish from Evidentiary Bars
Although the right does not lapse, practical obstacles may arise: witnesses die, documents are lost. The judge may dismiss a case for lack of proof, but the right still exists in conscience.
This is different from the right being extinguished.
4
Apply to Debts and Property Claims
For example, a lender can demand repayment of a loan even after 20 years, as long as he can prove it. The debt is still owed; the borrower remains liable.
Exception: if the debtor claims payment and the creditor cannot disprove, the debtor's oath may suffice.
5
Apply to Modern Fatwa Scenarios
A person inherits property but only claims it after 30 years. According to this maxim, the inheritance right is not lost; however, if the property changed hands, the claimant must prove ownership and overcome adverse possession claims.
Adverse possession (istihkam) may create a separate right under certain conditions.

The Ruling Revolves with Its Cause: Existence and Non-existence
Subsidiary maxim about the coextensiveness of ruling and its cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi causal principle that a ruling is contingent upon its effective cause (illah): it is established when the illah exists and ceases when the illah is absent.

Core Idea
The legal ruling is tied to its cause; when the cause is present, the ruling applies; when absent, the ruling does not apply.

This is one of the most frequently cited causal maxims in Hanafi fiqh. It expresses the principle of causality: every ruling (hukm) has an underlying reason ('illah). The ruling is coextensive with its cause. Understanding this maxim is essential for istihsan and for identifying when a ruling can be extended or restricted. The passage references its wide usage by Hanafi jurists.

Guided Path
1
Paraphrase the Maxim
State: الحكم يدور مع علته وجوداً وعدماً. Literally: The ruling revolves with its cause in existence and non-existence. That is, if the cause exists, the ruling exists; if the cause is absent, the ruling is absent.
Note that 'يدور' indicates a direct, necessary relationship.
2
Identify the IIIah in the Text
The passage does not give an explicit example but says the maxim is used for reasoning. So we need to apply it: for example, the prohibition of wine revolves around intoxication as the illah.
The passage references Fatawa references for further study.
3
Differentiate From Other Maxims
This maxim is about the existence of the illah. It is related to but distinct from 'al-hukm yatba'u al-illah wujudan wa adaman' (the ruling follows the cause in existence and non-existence).
It is a foundational tool for analogy (qiyas).
4
Apply to a Known Ruling
Example: The permissibility of shortening prayer while travelling. The illah is travel. If travel exists, the ruling of qasr applies; if travel ends, the ruling of full prayer applies.
The ruling is contingent on the illah.
5
Use for Mufti Reasoning
When a mufti faces a new case, he must identify the illah of the existing ruling and see if it is present in the new case. If yes, the ruling extends; if no, it does not.
This is the core of istihsan and qiyas.

Core Idea

In Hanafi legal theory, not all evidence carries equal weight. Ẓāhir is only valid in the absence of stronger evidence. If a party brings a clear proof (bayyinah), the ẓāhir collapses. Thus, the maxim operates in the realm of presumptions and external indications, not in the face of decisive proof.

Memorize the exact wording: 'مقاطع الحقوق عند الشروط'.
This is a concise legal principle.
2
Understand the literal meaning
The phrase means that the boundaries (مقاطع) of rights (الحقوق) are determined by the conditions (الشروط).
Conditions set the limits of what each party can claim.
3
Identify the legal scope
This maxim applies to all contracts and transactions, such as sale, lease, marriage, and settlement agreements.
It is especially relevant when interpreting stipulations.
4
Recognize the limitation
Conditions that contradict Shariah are void and do not define rights.
For example, stipulating interest (riba) is invalid despite mutual agreement.

Maxim: Agent and Principal Are One
States a maxim about agency from Hanafi law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim al-wakil ma'a al-asil ka-al-shakhs al-wahid, apply it to agency cases within authority, and identify exceptions where the principal is not bound.

Core Idea
The agent's actions are legally attributed to the principal as if done by the principal himself.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim in the chapter of agency (wakalah): 'The agent and the principal are like one person.' This maxim, recorded in Qawa'id al-Hamzawi, encapsulates the core idea that when a person appoints an agent, the legal effects of the agent's actions within the scope of authority flow directly to the principal. Understanding this maxim is essential for correctly analyzing issues of representation in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Recite the Arabic maxim and its translation. Understand that it is a legal fiction treating the agent as the principal for legal purposes.
Memorize the Arabic: الوكيل مع الأصيل كالشخص الواحد
2
Identify the scope
The maxim applies when the agent acts within the limits of the authority granted. The agent's act is then considered the principal's act in law.
This is not a statement of identity but of legal attribution.
3
Apply and note exceptions
Apply to a simple case: agent buys goods; principal must pay. Then note exceptions: if agent exceeds authority, acts negligently, or the matter requires personal action by the principal (like swearing an oath), the maxim does not apply.
Exceptions are governed by other principles like 'al-wakil amin' or 'al-wakil iza tajarraza 'an hadd al-wakalah'.
