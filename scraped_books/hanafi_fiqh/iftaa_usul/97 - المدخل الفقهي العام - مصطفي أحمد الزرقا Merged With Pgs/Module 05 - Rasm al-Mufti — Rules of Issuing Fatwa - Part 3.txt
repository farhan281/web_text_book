The Absolute and Restricted in Legal Maxims
Explains the maxim with examples and a dispute on custom as a restrictor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-mutlaq yajri ala itlaqihi ma lam yaqum dalil al-taqyid' to agency contracts and identify when custom serves as a restricting indication for fatwa purposes.

Core Idea
An absolute command binds the agent to his action unless a clear textual or contextual restriction exists.

The maxim that absolute terms remain unrestricted is foundational in Hanafi mu'amalat. This lesson explores its application in agency (wakalah) and the ikhtilaf on whether custom can restrict an absolute command. You will analyze cases where the principal's intent is overridden by the literal wording, and where contextual indications limit the scope. Understanding this maxim is essential for constructing accurate fatwas in commercial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its meaning
State the maxim: 'al-mutlaq yajri ala itlaqihi ma lam yaqum dalil al-taqyid'. Understand that absolute terms are left unrestricted unless a restriction (textual or indicational) is proven.
The maxim is from Majalla article 64.
2
Apply to agency cases: when is the principal bound?
Analyze the examples: if a principal says 'buy a horse' without qualification, the agent's purchase of any horse (red, white, etc.) binds the principal. The principal's hidden intent (e.g., 'I wanted white') is disregarded.
This illustrates the default operation of the maxim.
3
Distinguish restriction by text vs. by indication
Restriction can be explicit (e.g., 'buy for twenty') or implicit via contextual indication (qarina al-hal) such as the status of the principal (porter vs. racer) or temporal context (after Eid, after winter).
Implicit restriction requires evidence of a clear indication.
4
Analyze the ikhtilaf on custom as a restrictor
Abu Hanifa holds that custom does not restrict the absolute; the literal wording prevails. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) hold that custom (urf) is a valid restricting indication. The text notes that the Sahibayn's view is more appropriate (أوجه).
This ikhtilaf affects whether an agent can deviate from customary market terms.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
For fatwa, the relied-upon position is that of the Sahibayn: custom restricts. Thus, an agent cannot sell at a price below market or on credit without explicit authorization, because custom indicates the principal's intent.
This is the operative ruling in contemporary Hanafi fatwa.

Cases of restriction: If there is something that restricts the absolute, whether by text or indication, then it becomes restricted. Restriction by text: e.g., if the principal says to his agent for sale 'for twenty', he cannot sell for less. If the lender specifies a period, the borrower cannot use after it. Restriction by indication: e.g., if a porter appoints someone to buy a horse and he buys an expensive thoroughbred racehorse, the purchase is not binding because his condition indicates the intended horse is for porterage, even though the word is absolute. Commentary
This passage presents a fundamental legal maxim: absolute terms are left unrestricted unless a valid restriction (textual or indicational) is established. The author first defines absolute (mutlaq) as an unqualified term and restricted (muqayyad) as a qualified one. Physical and Legal Impossibility in Acknowledgement
Defining two types of ta'addhur with examples from iqrar and kafalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of ta'addhur (impossibility) to invalidate acknowledgements and sureties that contradict physical reality or definitive shariah rulings, and identify when a lawful interpretation saves the act.

Core Idea
Physical or legal impossibility nullifies an acknowledgement, but if a lawful excuse exists, the statement is binding.

This lesson unpacks a foundational rule from the Majalla: that impossibility—whether physical or legal—renders a legal statement meaningless and non-binding. Using vivid examples from acknowledgement (iqrar) and surety (kafalah), Mustafa al-Zarqa shows how the Hanafi school distinguishes between clear contradiction and cases where a lawful interpretation salvages the act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of impossibility
Begin by distinguishing physical impossibility (al-ta'adhdhur al-hissi) from legal impossibility (al-ta'adhdhur al-shar'i). Physical impossibility occurs when the claim contradicts observable reality; legal impossibility occurs when the claim contradicts a definitive ruling of shariah.
The passage explicitly labels these two types and gives examples for each.
2
Analyze the physical impossibility examples
Examine the examples: claiming or acknowledging that a hand was cut off while it is present, or acknowledging someone as a son when the person is of equal age or has a known lineage. In each case, reality contradicts the statement, so the statement is invalid and non-binding.
The key is that the contradiction is clear and not subject to interpretation.
3
Analyze the legal impossibility example
Consider the example of acknowledging that a sister inherits double a brother's share. This contradicts the Quranic ruling that a sister's share is half that of a brother (if no other heirs). Since the ruling is definitive (qat'i), the acknowledgement is legally impossible and invalid.
Legal impossibility is based on shariah texts, not just reason.
4
Examine the exception to legal impossibility
The passage contrasts the invalid acknowledgement with a valid one: if a brother acknowledges that his sister deserves an amount equal to his share without specifying inheritance, it is valid because it could be due to a lawful cause like a loan. This shows that if a lawful interpretation is possible, the statement is not considered impossible.
This exception demonstrates the principle 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by intentions).
5
Apply the rule to surety (kafalah)
Finally, apply the same logic to surety: if someone stands surety without specifying whether it is for the person (kafala bi al-nafs) or for property (kafala bi al-mal), it is invalid because the intent is unknowable and enforcement is impossible.
The ruling is from Radd al-Muhtar and aligns with the general rule of ta'addhur.

Commentary
This passage from al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-Aam by Mustafa al-Zarqa explains the classification of impossibility (ta'addhur) as physical and legal, with applications in the Hanafi school. The author first states that impossibility may be either physical (hissi) or legal (shar'i). For physical impossibility, he gives examples from claims and acknowledgements: if one claims or admits to cutting off a hand while the hand is present and visible, the claim is impossible and rejected. Similarly, if one acknowledges someone as his son when the person is of equal age or has a known lineage from another, the acknowledgement is impossible. For legal impossibility, he gives the example of acknowledging that one's sister inherits double his share from their father's estate, which is legally impossible under Islamic inheritance law (where a sister's share is half of a brother's if there is no other heir, not double). He then contrasts this with a valid acknowledgement: if a brother acknowledges that his sister deserves an amount of money equal to or more than his share without specifying inheritance, it is valid because she could be entitled through other lawful means, such as having lent the deceased money. This demonstrates the principle that impossibility, whether physical or legal, prevents validity and enforceability in legal acts.

Limits of Ijtihad: When Clear Text Prohibits Personal Reasoning
Explaining when ijtihad is prohibited due to clear text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm al-mufti rule that prohibits ijtihad when a clear, qat'i text exists, and distinguish between prohibited reinterpretation and permissible scrutiny of evidence.

Core Idea
Ijtihad is forbidden where the text is qat'i — the mufti's task is application, not legislation.

This passage from Dr. Mustafa al-Zarqa's work outlines a fundamental boundary for the mufti: the point at which ijtihad ceases. When a Quranic verse or hadith is unequivocal in meaning and authentic in transmission, no personal reasoning can reverse its ruling. This lesson parses the key terms—nass, ijtihad, qat'i al-thubut wa al-dilala—and applies the rule to real cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions that prohibit ijtihad
A text must be 'thabit' (established in transmission) and 'wadih al-dilala' (clear in meaning). Only then is ijtihad against it prohibited.
In Hanafi usul, qat'i al-thubut is limited to Quran and mutawatir hadith; ahad hadith are zanni al-thubut.
2
Recognize the example: the hadith on burden of proof
The hadith 'al-bayyinah ala al-mudda'i' assigns evidence to the claimant and oath to the denier. Its wording is explicit, so any ijtihad reversing this allocation is invalid.
This hadith is considered clear in meaning even if its transmission is not mutawatir; the author treats it as a clear text.
3
Distinguish prohibited vs. permissible ijtihad
Prohibited ijtihad contradicts the clear text's allocation. Permissible ijtihad, like accepting counter-evidence for weighing, does not deny the claimant's right to prove.
The measure of counter-evidence scrutinizes evidence without overturning the rule.
4
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Consider a clear Quranic verse on inheritance shares. A mufti may not use ijtihad to change the shares, but he may interpret the verse's conditions.
The clear text binds the ruling; adaptation to context is allowed as long as the core hukm is preserved.
5
Note the exception: disputed authenticity
When jurists disagree on a hadith's authenticity, ijtihad may be used because the text is not thabit. This is a legitimate area of ikhtilaf.
This exception is mentioned in the passage: some jurists follow the hadith, others resort to analogy.

Evidence Transmissible, Admission Non-Transmissible

Core Idea
Evidence binds all; admission binds only the admitter — collusion risk limits its reach.

This lesson introduces a fundamental principle for muftis: legal proofs differ in their binding scope. Evidence (bayyinah), when properly established, binds everyone, while an admission (iqrar) binds only the person who makes it. Second result: Evidence is a transmissible proof, and admission is a non-transmissible proof. The transmissible proof is one whose probative force is not limited to the person against whom it is established, but extends beyond him to others. So evidence is a transmissible proof, meaning that when it is established in the valid legal manner, it is binding upon the person against whom it is established and also binding upon other people. Unlike admission, it only binds the person who makes the admission, because the person making it has authority only over himself. Thus he can bind himself as he wishes, but he has no power to bind others. The reason evidence is transmissible and admission is non-transmissible is that it is possible the person admitting may be lying in his admission and colluding with the beneficiary to waste the right of a third party. For admission is a manifestation and report, not a creation; it is nothing more than a mere claim by the person admitting, without evidence. So insofar as it is a claim from its maker, it binds its maker, because a person has authority over himself, and it is assumed that his admission against himself is stronger and more probative against him than the testimony of witnesses.
