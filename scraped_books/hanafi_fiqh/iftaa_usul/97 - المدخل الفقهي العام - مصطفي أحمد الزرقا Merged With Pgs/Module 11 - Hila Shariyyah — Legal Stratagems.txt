Bay' al-Mu'amalah: A Legal Stratagem for Loans
Describes the legal stratagem of bay' al-mu'amalah used by later Hanafis for loans with profit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the mechanism of bay' al-mu'amalah and identify its conditions, ruling, and place within later Hanafi jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Bay' al-mu'amalah allows a loan with profit through a sale, permissible only for genuine need.

Later Hanafi jurists developed a practical stratagem to address the need for loans while avoiding explicit riba. This hila, called bay' al-mu'amalah, involves a sale of a cheap item at an inflated price, effectively providing the borrower with funds and the lender with a profit. In this lesson, we will explore how this mechanism works, its conditions, and its status as a permissible legal device according to later Hanafis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem addressed
Understand that the stratagem aims to provide a loan with benefit to the lender without falling into riba (interest), which is prohibited.
The need (hajah) for loans is the driving factor.
2
Analyze the mechanism
Examine the structure: the borrower buys an inexpensive item from the lender at a price significantly above market value. The lender gets a profit from the inflated price, and the borrower receives the item plus deferred payment that functions as a loan.
The transaction is a genuine sale, not a loan in form, but in substance it serves as a loan.
3
Evaluate the conditions for permissibility
The passage states it is 'permissible for need' (ja'iz li'l-hajah). Determine what constitutes genuine need and ensure the sale is not merely a sham but has real elements.
The condition of need distinguishes it from a mere device for riba.
4
Assess the ruling and its basis
The later Hanafi position, as cited from al-Durr al-Mukhtar, is that bay' al-mu'amalah is permissible. Understand that this is a later development and may not be accepted by earlier Hanafis or other schools.
The authority level is from the muta'akhkhirin (later scholars).
5
Consider the controversy and application
Recognize that this stratagem is controversial. Other scholars may view it as a trick to circumvent the prohibition of riba. In modern contexts, it relates to banking interest.
The author presents it for discussion, not as a settled ruling.

Commentary
This passage presents a hila (legal stratagem) used by later Hanafi jurists to circumvent interest (riba) in loans. The author, Mustafa al-Zarqa, introduces it as an example connected to the principle of the ruler's authority, though he presents it without full endorsement, inviting discussion. The stratagem, called 'bay' al-mu'amalah' (sale of mutual dealing), involves the borrower buying an inexpensive item from the lender at an inflated price, effectively providing a loan with a hidden profit. The author notes that al-Haskafi in al-Durr al-Mukhtar mentions it in two places: at the end of the loan section as 'bay' al-mu'amalah', and earlier at the end of the murabahah section as 'al-murabahah'. In the latter form, the creditor sells an item to the debtor at a price above its market value, so that the seller (creditor) gains a profit equivalent to the interest he would have charged for deferring the debt. The key condition is that it is considered permissible only for need (hajah). The author indicates that this is a controversial issue and will be discussed further in relation to banking interest. The stratagem is a practical adaptation within the Hanafi school to address economic needs while avoiding explicit riba.

بيع المعاملة (bay‘ al-mu‘āmalah): A sale where the buyer purchases a cheap item at a high price to obtain a loan from the seller, considered permissible for need by later Hanafis.
المرابحة (al-murābaḥah): A sale with a disclosed profit margin; here used synonymously with bay' al-mu'amalah, where the creditor sells at a price higher than market value to earn the desired profit.

Bay' al-Mu'amalah: Conditions and Application of a Hanafi Lending Stratagem
Explains a legal stratagem and its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions of bay' al-mu'amalah to determine its validity in a given scenario, and state the mufta bihi position among later Hanafis.

Core Idea
Bay' al-mu'amalah is a permissible stratagem for lending with profit, provided the profit is proportional to the term and adjusted upon early payment.

In later Hanafi fiqh, a sale-based stratagem called bay' al-mu'amalah (or murabahah) was developed to address the need for loans with a return without falling into explicit riba. It involves selling a cheap item at an inflated price on credit, effectively creating a loan with hidden interest. This lesson parses the conditions and rulings associated with this hila, including the critical rule that the profit must be proportional to the time the loan remains outstanding. Understanding this mechanism is crucial for fatwa practice in modern financing contexts.

Guided Path
1
Define the hila and its ruling
Identify bay' al-mu'amalah as a sale where a buyer purchases a cheap item at an inflated price on credit from the seller, who is also the lender. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) when there is a need (hajah).
This ruling is specific to later Hanafi scholars; earlier Hanafis might have prohibited it.
2
Analyze the condition of proportional profit
Note that if the debtor pays early or dies before the term, the creditor is only entitled to profit proportional to the elapsed time. Any excess profit must be refunded. This condition distinguishes it from outright riba.
This shows that the profit is considered compensation for the time value of money, not a guaranteed return.
3
Identify the authorities and fatwa basis
The passage cites multiple later Hanafi authorities (Najm al-Din, al-Hanuti, Abu al-Su'ud, al-Imadi) who gave fatwas permitting this stratagem. This establishes the mufta bihi position among the muta'akhkhirin.
The authority weight is 'fatawa_mutakhkhirin' since it relies on later jurists, not the early imams.
4
Evaluate the effect of the ruler's order
The passage discusses that mere violation of the sultan's order does not automatically invalidate the contract, unless the order explicitly declares the contract void. This is a nuance in the interaction between state authority and private contracts.
This adds a layer: the hila may be invalidated by an explicit ruling, not just by implicit prohibition.

Evaluating Bay' al-Mu'amalah: When a Hila Ceases to Be Valid
Critical evaluation of a historical Hanafi hila as obsolete

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the continued validity of a historical Hanafi hila by applying the maxim 'al-hajah al-ammah tanzilu manzilat al-darurah' and determining whether the underlying need persists.

Core Idea
A hila is only valid as long as its underlying necessity remains; once alternatives exist, the hila becomes impermissible.

This passage from Mustafa al-Zarqa's al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm critically examines the Hanafi hila known as bay' al-mu'amalah, a transaction used historically to circumvent riba by adding a predetermined profit over a loan. Al-Zarqa argues that while later Hanafi jurists permitted it due to overwhelming public need for financing in the absence of Islamic financial institutions, that need has now been met by the establishment of Islamic banks. Consequently, the fatwa is no longer operative. This lesson will equip you with the analytical tools to assess when a legal stratagem or fatwa based on necessity loses its validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hila and its original ruling
State what bay' al-mu'amalah is and its historical fatwa status: a transaction where the ruler sets a fixed profit on a loan, structured as a sale but functioning as interest. Later Hanafis permitted it as a hila.
The original ruling was permissibility due to need.
2
Locate the 'illah behind the permissibility
Recognize that the permissibility was based on the legal cause ('illah) of overwhelming public need (hajah 'ammah) for financing and the absence of Islamic alternatives.
This 'illah is captured by the maxim 'al-hajah al-ammah tanzilu manzilat al-darurah'.
3
Evaluate the persistence of the 'illah today
Determine whether the need that justified the hila still exists. According to al-Zarqa, the establishment of Islamic financial institutions has removed the need.
If the 'illah no longer exists, the ruling based on it ceases.
4
Apply the qa'idah with its istithna'
Conclude that when the need ceases, the permissibility ceases. The istithna' (exception) to the maxim is that if alternatives exist, the hila becomes impermissible.
The governing qa'idah 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' ensures the ruling aligns with the underlying purpose.

Critique of Bay' al-Mu'amalah as a Hila for Riba
Critique of bay' al-mu'amalah as disguised riba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the hanafi position on bay' al-mu'amalah by identifying the conditioning need (hajah) and assessing its continued validity.

Core Idea
Changing the name of interest to 'benefit' does not remove the essence of riba.

This passage examines a controversial legal stratagem (hila) known as bay' al-mu'amalah, which later Hanafi jurists permitted to address a pressing societal need for financing. The author, Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa, argues that this solution is nothing more than disguised riba, and that the justifying cause has now vanished with the availability of Islamic financial alternatives. Understanding this critique is essential for contemporary fatwa practice when evaluating inherited rulings that relied on temporary needs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hila and its ruling
First, recognize that bay' al-mu'amalah is a contractual arrangement where a ruler prescribes a fixed financial benefit above a loan, and that later Hanafis ruled it permissible due to general need.
This is a conditional permissibility tied to a specific cause.
2
Examine the author's critique
Al-Zarqa argues that determining a fixed benefit is equivalent to setting an interest rate, regardless of terminology. The act remains riba in substance.
The critique targets the essence, not the name.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil) for the original permission
The later Hanafis permitted it because of severe general need (hajah 'ammah) for financing and the lack of alternative mechanisms. This reasoning is the basis of the hila.
Hajah is the illa (effective cause) for the ruling.
4
Assess the continued validity of the cause
Al-Zarqa asserts that the need has disappeared with the establishment of Islamic financial institutions free from riba. Therefore the hila loses its justification.
This is a key critical step: if the cause is no longer present, the ruling changes.
5
Apply the lesson to contemporary fatwa
As a mufti, you must evaluate whether the conditions that justified a previous ruling still exist today. If not, the earlier ruling cannot be followed.
This illustrates the dynamic nature of fatwa based on changing circumstances.

Debtor's Waqf: The Later Hanafi Fatwa on Creditor Protection
Later Hanafi fatwa restricting debtor's waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the later Hanafi fatwa regarding a debtor's waqf and explain its rationale and condition.

Core Idea
A debtor's waqf is ineffective to the extent of his debt unless creditors consent.

In earlier Hanafi law, a debtor could validly endow his property as waqf. However, this created a loophole for debtors to evade creditors by hiding assets in waqf. To close this loophole, later Hanafi jurists (mutā'akhkhirūn) issued a fatwa restricting the effectiveness of a debtor's waqf. This passage presents that fatwa and its rationale, forming a classic example of a protective legal device (hīla) in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The earlier Hanafi position allowed a debtor to make a waqf regardless of his debts; waqf is generally valid.
This is the default permissibility.
2
State the Later Hanafi Fatwa
The later Hanafis ruled that the waqf of a debtor does not execute (lā yanfudh) with respect to the amount equivalent to his debts unless the creditors consent.
This is a restriction on the general rule.
3
Explain the Rationale (Talīl)
The purpose is to prevent debtors from using waqf to evade creditor claims, which would harm creditors and contradict the wisdom of legislation.
The rationale is based on sadd al-dharāʾiʿ and maslahah.
4
Determine the Condition for Effectiveness
The waqf becomes fully effective only if the creditors give their consent. Without it, the waqf is mawqūf (suspended) regarding the debt‐equivalent portion.
Consent is the key condition for nafādh.

The real beneficiary, not the nominal party, owns the contract's fruits, but fraud nullifies this protection.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling on simulation regarding the person (al-ism al-musta'ār). Rulings on Destruction of Sold Item in Bai' al-Wafa
Details three scenarios of destruction and the right to judicial sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the three scenarios of item destruction in bai' al-wafa and determine the liability of buyer and seller.

Core Idea
In bai' al-wafa, excess value is a trust; seller cannot reclaim it unless buyer is negligent.

This lesson focuses on a critical rule for muftis: what happens when the item sold in a bai' al-wafa transaction perishes? The Hanafi school provides a clear framework based on the relationship between the item's value and the price paid. Understanding this rule is essential for issuing fatwas in modern cases involving collateral arrangements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three value scenarios
Compare the market value of the perished item with the price paid by the buyer. There are three possibilities: equal, less, or more.
The most common scenario is the excess case.
2
Apply the corresponding consequence
If equal: seller freed from returning price; no claim. If less: buyer recovers difference. If more: excess is a trust, seller cannot claim it unless buyer at fault.
Memorize the three outcomes.
3
Understand the trust principle
The excess value is an amanah ghayr madhmoonah. The buyer is not liable for accidental loss, only for transgression or negligence.
This mirrors the rule for mortgages.
4
Learn the judicial sale option
If seller refuses to redeem, buyer can force a court sale to recover his price. Any surplus goes to seller, any shortfall is seller's responsibility.
This is a practical remedy for the buyer.

If the value of the sold item in a bai' al-wafa equals the paid price, then with its destruction the liability of returning the price is lifted from the seller, and the buyer has no recourse against him for anything. If its value is less than the price, then the value of the sold item is deducted from the price, and the buyer recovers the remainder from the seller. If its value is more than the paid price, which is usually the case, then the excess is a trust not guaranteed by the buyer: so the return of the received price drops from the seller's liability due to the destruction of what corresponds to it of the sold item as wafa with the buyer, and thereafter the seller cannot pursue the buyer for the excess of the value of the sold item, unless its destruction was due to the buyer's transgression against it or negligence in preserving it. That – if the seller in a bai' al-wafa does not return the price and reclaim his sold item, the buyer has the right to request the judge to sell it under him, so the judge sells it at the seller's expense, and from its new price he fulfills the buyer's right of wafa, i.e., his old price; if anything remains, it is for the seller; and if the new price falls short of the old one, the seller in wafa is responsible for the remainder.

Commentary
This passage presents the legal rulings concerning the destruction of the sold item in a bai' al-wafa transaction. The author outlines three scenarios: (1) If the value of the sold item equals the price paid, the seller is freed from the obligation to return the price, and the buyer has no claim. (2) If the value is less, the buyer recovers the difference from the seller. (3) If the value is more (the common case), the excess is a trust in the buyer's hands, so the seller cannot reclaim it unless the buyer was negligent or transgressed. Additionally, if the seller refuses to return the price and reclaim the item, the buyer can force a judicial sale to recover his price. These rules treat the transaction as akin to a mortgage: the buyer's right is secured against the item, but he is not entitled to profit from any excess value unless he is at fault.

Key Terms
بيع الوفاء (bai' al-wafa): A contract resembling a sale but intended as a security, where the buyer takes possession and can benefit from the item, with the seller having the right to redeem by returning the price.
أمانة غير مضمونة (amanah ghayr madhmoonah): A trust that is not guaranteed; the holder is not liable for loss unless negligent.

The Legal Nature and Rationale of Bai' al-Wafa
Explains why bai' al-wafa is needed and how it differs from mortgage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why bai' al-wafa was developed as a hila shariyyah, state its legal nature as a security arrangement with the creditor's right to use the asset, and distinguish it from a true sale and a mortgage (rahn).

Core Idea
Bai' al-wafa: a lawful sale in form, a mortgage in meaning, giving the creditor the right to use the asset as compensation.

This passage explores the legal device known as bai' al-wafa, a contract that combines elements of sale and mortgage to solve a practical problem: how to allow a creditor to benefit from a pledged asset without falling into riba. The author explains why a simple mortgage (rahn) was insufficient and how scholars designed this hila to provide a lawful alternative. Understanding this contract is essential for modern fatwa practice, where similar structures are used in Islamic finance.

Guided Path
1

Recognize why a basic mortgage was inadequate: the mortgagee cannot benefit from the property without permission, and such permission can be revoked. This created a disincentive for lending without profit.

Commentary
This passage explains the legal nature and rationale behind bai' al-wafa. The author emphasizes that it is not a true sale; rather, the item and price function like mortgaged property and debt. The contract was developed because a regular mortgage (rahn) does not allow the mortgagee to benefit from the property without the owner's permission, which can be revoked. To encourage lending, scholars created bai' al-wafa, which gives the buyer (lender) the right to use the property by contract. This avoided riba (usury) by providing a lawful means of compensation. The author also notes that bai' al-wafa applies primarily to real estate, with disagreement on movables. Finally, he asserts that this contract is independent and should be placed after mortgage in legal compilations, as it borrows from both sale and mortgage.
