The Meaning of Fiqh: A Foundational Distinction
Defining fiqh and distinguishing its two senses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two meanings of fiqh as a science and as rulings, and identify which matters are included and excluded from its scope.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of practical shari'i rulings, not beliefs — a distinction that shapes the entire discipline.

This passage introduces the foundational definition of fiqh that underpins all later study. The author, Mustafa al-Zarqa, clarifies that fiqh has two distinct senses: as a science (knowledge from evidence) and as the rulings themselves. Understanding this distinction is essential for approaching any fiqh text or fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings
Read the first sentence: 'للإسلامي فقه للفقه معنيان' (Islamic fiqh has two meanings). List them: (1) knowledge of practical shari'i rulings with evidence, (2) the rulings themselves.
2
Analyze the components of the first meaning
Break down 'العلم بالأحكام الشرعية العملية مع أدلتها المجلة' into its parts: 'ahkam' (rulings) are commands/regulations from the Lawgiver, 'shari'i' means derived from Divine command, 'amaliyya' (practical) excludes beliefs, and 'adillatiha al-mujmala' (detailed evidence) indicates the source of knowledge.
The term 'ahkam' encompasses all practical rulings, not just worship.
3
Understand the examples
The author gives two examples: the usurper's liability and the trustee's liability. These illustrate practical rulings that organize social life and transactions.
Notice how these examples are not about worship but about civil liability.
4
Appreciate the exclusion of doctrinal matters
The restriction 'amaliyya' excludes 'masa'il i'tiqadiyya' (belief issues). Fiqh does not cover beliefs (like tawhid); those belong to another science (kalam).
This boundary is crucial for correctly defining the scope of fiqh.
5
Recognize the second meaning
After explaining the first meaning, the passage notes that 'fiqh' is also used for the rulings themselves. This is seen in phrases like 'fiqh says such-and-such'.
This sense is common in fatwa literature when citing established rulings.

Ijma': Definition, Types, and Historical Context
Explains Ijma' as a source, its types, and its historical limitations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between explicit and tacit consensus, identify the historical conditions for a valid Ijma', and apply the Hanafi view on Ijma' in fatwa practice.

Core Idea
Ijma' is a binding source of law, but its practical scope is limited to the early era of the Companions.

Ijma' (consensus) is a fundamental source of Islamic law, but its nature and applicability have evolved. This lesson unpacks the definition, types, and historical context of Ijma', focusing on its role as a proof and the conditions that govern its validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and proof status of Ijma'
Read the opening statements: Ijma' is a proof (hujja) in itself, but it does not require its supporting evidence to be transmitted with it. Understand that the proof is the consensus itself, not the underlying dalil.
2
Examine the examples of rulings based on Ijma'
Review the three examples: grandfather's inheritance, hajb of siblings by father, and validity of Istisna'. Recognize that these are settled rulings that rely on the consensus of the Companions.
These illustrate that Ijma' often fills gaps where qiyas might be opposed or need is pressing (e.g., Istisna').
3
Distinguish between explicit (qawli) and tacit (sukuti) consensus
Note the two types: qawli involves verbal agreement after discussion; sukuti occurs when a scholar issues a fatwa and others remain silent. The first is universally accepted, the second is disputed.
The Hanafi school generally accepts sukuti as a proof if it is possible for others to object and they do not.
4
Analyze the historical conditions for Ijma'
Understand why Ijma' was easy in the time of 'Umar (Companions centralized in Madinah) and became impossible after their dispersal in 'Uthman's era. The text states that agreement of scholars of one city is not Ijma'.
This historical limitation means that claims of Ijma' today generally refer to early consensus.
5
Evaluate implications for a mufti today
Consider how a mufti should handle claims of Ijma' in contemporary fatwa. They must verify if the alleged consensus meets the historical conditions (universal, from early period). If not, it is not binding Ijma'.
This guides the mufti in using Ijma' as an authority and avoiding false claims.

Commentary
This passage discusses Ijma' (consensus) as a source of Islamic fiqh. It first clarifies that consensus itself is a proof, but it does not require the proof it relies on to be explicitly transmitted. It then gives three examples of rulings established by consensus: (1) the grandfather's inheritance of one-sixth when there is a son, (2) the exclusion of full and consanguine siblings by the father, and (3) the validity of the Istisna' contract. Next, it divides consensus into two types: qawli (explicit), where scholars openly agree, and sukuti (tacit), where a scholar gives a ruling and others remain silent. The passage notes that explicit consensus is undisputed authority, while tacit consensus is highly disputed regarding its validity and conditions. Finally, it provides historical context: in the early Islamic period, consensus was easy because the Companions were centralized in Medina under 'Umar, but after their dispersal to various regions during 'Uthman's caliphate, the practical possibility of achieving universal consensus ended. Consequently, all rulings based solely on consensus date back to the early period.

الإجماع السكوتي (al-ijmā‘ al-sukūtī): Tacit consensus where a scholar issues a ruling and other scholars remain silent without expressing opposition or support.

Definition and Authority of Qiyas
Defines Qiyas and explains its necessity due to limited nusus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of Qiyas, explain its rank among the sources of fiqh, and justify its necessity using the finite-text/infinite-events principle.

Core Idea
Qiyas applies the ruling of one case to another sharing the same effective cause; texts are finite, events infinite, so Qiyas is indispensable.

This passage from al-Zarqa’s al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-‘Amm defines Qiyas (analogical deduction) and establishes its foundational role in Islamic legal methodology. While Qiyas ranks fourth after the Qur’an, Sunnah, and Ijma‘ in authority, the author emphasizes that it is far more extensively used because Ijma‘ became historically limited after the early generations. The passage underscores a key principle: sacred texts are finite but human events are infinite, making Qiyas essential for deriving rulings for new incidents.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the definition
Break down the Arabic definition: إلحاق أمر بآخر في الحكم الشرعي لاتحاد بينهما في العلة. Understand each element: attaching (ilhāq), one matter (amr), to another (bi-ākhar), in the legal ruling (fī al-ḥukm al-shar‘ī), due to commonality in the effective cause (li-ttiḥād baynahumā fī al-‘illa).
Focus on the concept of ‘illa — it is the cause that triggers the ruling.
2
Identify the rank of Qiyas
The passage states Qiyas is the fourth source after the Qur’an, Sunnah, and Ijma‘ in terms of authority (ḥujjiyya). This is the standard hierarchy in Hanafi uṣūl.
Although fourth, its practical application is greater than Ijma‘.
3
Analyze the contrast with Ijma‘
Ijma‘ is limited because it requires universal agreement of scholars, which became unattainable after the first generations. Qiyas does not require such agreement; each mujtahid may perform it independently.
The author mentions the 'diversion of scholars from the principle of general scientific consultation' as a reason for Ijma‘’s limitation.
4
Understand the rationale for Qiyas
The passage concludes with a key principle: nusūṣ (Qur’an and Sunnah) are finite, while nawāzil (events) are infinite. Qiyas is the tool to bridge this gap.
This rationale justifies the necessity of ijtihād by qiyās.

Qiyas in Practice: The Option of Payment (Khiyar al-Naqd)
Concrete examples of qiyas in sales contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of qiyas (asl, far', illa) in the example of khiyar al-naqd and explain the analogical reasoning used.

Core Idea
Qiyas extends a ruling from an original case to a new case due to a shared effective cause.

This lesson focuses on two concrete examples of qiyas from Islamic commercial law. You will see how jurists used analogical reasoning to permit the option of condition for the seller and to create the option of payment (khiyar al-naqd). Understanding these examples will help you grasp the structure of qiyas and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case (asl) and its ruling
In the first example, the original case is the buyer's right to stipulate an option (khiyar al-shart). The ruling is that such a stipulation is permissible. Determine the legal basis for this ruling.
This is established by the general principle of contractual freedom unless prohibited.
2
Identify the new case (far')
The new case is the seller also stipulating an option of condition. The jurists extended the permissibility to the seller. Then they analogized further to create khiyar al-naqd, where the seller conditions that if payment is not made on time, the contract is rescinded.
Both the seller's option and khiyar al-naqd are new cases derived via qiyas.
3
Find the shared effective cause (illa)
The illa is the need (haja) that justified the stipulation. In the original case, the buyer needs time to decide; in the new cases, the seller needs protection against delayed payment or non-payment. The shared illa is a legitimate need to rescind the contract after conclusion.
The text states: 'because his need for it is like the buyer's need'.
4
Confirm the extension of the ruling
Since the illa is present in the new cases, the same ruling (permissibility of the stipulation) is extended. The analogy is valid and yields the same legal effect.
This demonstrates the method of qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) because the illa is evident.

The author, Mustafa al-Zarqa, begins by providing concrete examples of qiyas from the area of sales contracts. These examples illustrate the vast application of qiyas in Islamic jurisprudence.

The author then emphasizes the crucial role of qiyas in expanding the scope of limited textual sources to cover endless new events. He points out that a general text (amm) only covers cases that fall under its literal meaning, whereas qiyas allows the inclusion of new cases that share the same effective cause (illa). This creates an ever-growing body of juristic rulings derived from the original texts, and these derived rulings themselves become new principles for further analogies. This explains the comprehensiveness of Islamic fiqh, which can address all past and future occurrences.

For Hanafi students, this passage underscores that qiyas is a valid and essential method of ijtihad. Qiyas as a Source: Expanding the Law and Responding to the Zahiris
Theoretical justification of qiyas and refutation of the Zahiri school

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the role of qiyas in expanding the scope of legal texts and evaluate the Hanafi response to the Zahiri rejection of qiyas.

Core Idea
Qiyas is necessary to cover new events not explicitly addressed by texts, and its rejection leaves law incomplete.

The author now shifts from examples to the theoretical importance of qiyas. He argues that qiyas is essential because texts are finite while events are infinite. General texts (umum) are insufficient; qiyas allows specific texts to cover new cases by the common illa. He also refutes the Zahiri school that rejects qiyas, citing al-Muzani's statement that jurists have always used analogy. This lesson will help you understand the epistemological foundation of qiyas in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem: finite texts vs. infinite events
The author states that texts are limited but new occurrences are endless. Recognize that without a method like qiyas, many new cases would have no ruling.
2
Distinguish the role of general texts (amm) from qiyas
A general text covers only what falls under its literal meaning. Qiyas, however, can bring new cases that do not fall under the text's wording but share the illa. Understand that qiyas expands the applicability of texts beyond their literal scope.
This is a key distinction in Hanafi usul.
3
Note the chain of derivation: rulings become new principles
The passage explains that derived rulings themselves become sources for further qiyas, creating an unlimited expansion. This is the 'secret of comprehensiveness' of Islamic fiqh.
This highlights the dynamic nature of fiqh.
4
Analyze the Zahiri position and the Hanafi response
The Zahiris rejected qiyas. The author dismisses their school as lacking life and contradicts legislative necessities. Then he quotes al-Muzani to show the consensus of jurists on using qiyas.
The Hanafi position is that qiyas is indispensable.

Defining 'Urf: Custom as a Subordinate Legal Source
Defining 'urf linguistically and technically with Quranic evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'urf linguistically and technically, identify its Quranic basis, and explain its role as a subordinate source in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Custom (‘urf) is the habitual practice of the majority—a valid subordinate source of law if it aligns with primary texts.

Custom, or 'urf, is a crucial subordinate source in Islamic law, particularly for matters not explicitly detailed in the primary sources. This lesson unpacks its dual definition: linguistic and technical. You will see how the Quran itself commands adherence to 'urf, and how Hanafi jurists like Ibn ‘Ābidīn have formalized its definition.

Guided Path
1
Parse the Linguistic Meaning
Note that 'urf linguistically derives from 'knowledge' and evolves to mean what is familiar and approved by sound intellects. This shift from epistemology to social acceptance is key.
2
Extract the Technical Definition
The technical definition is 'the habit of the majority of a people in speech or action.' Identify the elements: majority, habit, community, and domain (speech/action).
This definition is composed from multiple juristic formulations, notably from Ibn ‘Ābidīn.
3
Locate the Quranic Evidence
Verse 7:199 commands 'enjoin the ‘urf'. Recognize that this verse is the primary textual basis for considering 'urf a valid source. Also note the parallel with 'surplus' in 2:219.
The verse is cited as a dalīl naqli (textual evidence) for the authority of 'urf.
4
Understand the Role as Subordinate Source
As a subordinate source, 'urf operates within the bounds of the primary sources. It can inform rulings on transactions and customs but cannot override a clear Quran or hadith.
This limitation is critical for a mufti applying 'urf in fatwa.

The Sixth Chapter: The Subordinate Sources: Custom
1/6 - The meaning of custom (al-‘urf) linguistically and technically:
Al-‘urf, with a dammah on the ‘ayn, originally in language means 'knowledge'. Then it was used linguistically to mean the known, familiar, approved thing that sound intellects accept. Based on it is the saying of the Most High: 'Take the pardon and enjoin the al-‘urf and turn away from the ignorant' (Sūrat al-A‘rāf 7:199). In the technical fiqh terminology, al-‘urf is: the habit of the majority of a people in speech or action.

Distinguishing Shari'ah Conditions (Shara'it) from Contractual Stipulations (Shurut)
Proposing a terminological convention for two types of conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply al-Zarqa's terminological convention to classify a given condition as a Shari'ah condition (sharita) or a contractual stipulation (shart) and explain the basis for the classification.

Core Idea
Shari'ah conditions (shara'it) are imposed by the Lawgiver; contractual stipulations (shurut) are chosen by parties—distinguishing them prevents conceptual confusion.

In legal analysis, precision in terminology is essential for clear reasoning. Dr. Mustafa al-Zarqa, a prominent Hanafi jurist, observed that classical fiqh uses 'shart' and 'sharita' interchangeably. He proposed a convention to reserve 'shara'it' for conditions originating from the Shari'ah (e.g., legal capacity) and 'shurut' for conditions originating from the will of individuals (e.g., contractual stipulations). This lesson unpacks his reasoning and equips you to apply this distinction when analyzing contracts and issuing fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Recognize traditional synonymy
Note that in both classical Arabic and juristic terminology, 'shart' and 'sharita' are synonyms. This is the starting point for understanding the need for a refined convention.
Al-Zarqa acknowledges this explicitly to avoid confusion.
2
Identify the source of the condition
The core of the distinction is the origin: Shari'ah conditions come from the Lawgiver's will (iradat al-shari'), while contractual stipulations come from the individual's will (iradat al-shakhs).
This is the decisive factor for classification.
3
Understand the rationale for distinction
Al-Zarqa argues that because the two types have different sources and different effects (one is legislation, the other a personal act), they should be distinguished in naming to avoid confusion in legal analysis.
The classification does not change substantive rulings but aids clarity.
4
Apply the convention to examples
Given a condition, ask: is it imposed by the Shari'ah for the validity of an act (e.g., capacity, offer and acceptance)? If yes, it is a sharita. If it is a voluntary stipulation (e.g., condition of delivery, payment term), it is a shart.
Practice with scenarios to internalize the classification.

Contract Formation by Indication: Lisan al-Hal and Ta'ati
Methodological rule on contract formation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a contract is formed by lisan al-hal or ta'ati and apply the rule that marriage requires explicit verbal offer and acceptance.

A contract can be formed by contextual indication, except marriage which requires explicit verbal offer and acceptance.

This lesson equips you with the Hanafi rule that contracts can be formed by 'lisan al-hal' (the language of the situation) and 'ta'ati' (mutual giving). Prohibited Contracts: Classification and Nullity
Classifies prohibited contracts and states they are void

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a contract as prohibited (mamnū‘) based on categories of public morals and public order, and state the Hanafi legal consequence (bāṭil) with the underlying usul principle.

Core Idea
A prohibited contract is legally void (bāṭil) because it lacks a condition of formation, producing no effects.

This passage from a modern introduction to fiqh identifies specific contracts that are prohibited (mamnū‘a) and non-legitimate (ghayr mashrū‘a). It grounds the nullity in the absence of a condition for contract formation (sharṭ al-in‘iqād). As a mufti or student of fatwa, you must recognize these categories and understand that the ruling is not mere moral censure but legal voidness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited contracts listed
Read the passage and list each example given: donation from a minor's property, contracts violating public morals (indecencies), contracts violating public order (hiring for crime), and temporary marriage (nikāḥ al-mut‘a).
2
Extract the legal basis for prohibition
Note that the prohibition is not based on a single text but on general principles: public morals (al-ādāb al-‘āmma) and public order (al-niẓām al-‘āmm). These are derived from the maqāṣid al-sharī‘a (objectives of law).
In Hanafi uṣūl, contracts that conflict with these principles are bāṭil even without explicit textual prohibition.

Distinguish bāṭil from fāsid

Example: a contract to produce obscene material violates public morals and is bāṭil.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A fāsid contract exists legally but is defective; a bāṭil contract is legally non-existent.

Contracts are not simply 'valid' or 'invalid' in Hanafi law. The school distinguishes between ṣaḥīḥ (fully valid) and fāsid (defective but existent). This lesson unpacks that distinction, showing how subsidiary conditions determine validity and why the Hanafi tradition excludes bāṭil contracts from formal classification.

Guided Path

Identify the two classes

2
Define ṣaḥīḥ contract
A ṣaḥīḥ contract fulfills all general and specific legal conditions in its essence and subsidiary aspects. Note the examples: sale of valuable property for a known price (cash or credit with a known term), lease of a specific thing with known rent and duration.
Both general and particular conditions must be present.

Define fāsid contract
A fāsid contract lacks some conditions related to subsidiary aspects (shurūt far‘iyya). Examples: sale with unknown price, or known price but unknown term; lease with ignorance of rent or duration.
The missing condition is subsidiary, not essential to the contract's existence.

The author notes they did not classify contracts as mun‘aqid (concluded) and bāṭil (void) because bāṭil is legally non-existent—it has no legal existence and therefore does not constitute a category of contract.
In Hanafi theory, bāṭil contracts are treated as if they never occurred.

Core Idea
Delivery completes a real contract; mere agreement completes a consensual contract.

This passage from al-Zarqa's al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-‘Amm introduces a key methodological distinction in Hanafi contract law: real contracts require physical delivery of the object for legal completion, while consensual contracts are fully effective upon offer and acceptance. Apply the classification to the case

This distinction matters for liability and compensation rulings.

Takhsees of General Text by General Custom in Hanafi Usul
Explaining how a general practical custom can specify a general text in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a general contemporaneous practical custom specifies a general text, and apply it to examples like istisna' and the prohibition of deception.

Core Idea
A general custom existing when a text was revealed indicates the Lawgiver did not intend its generality.

In this passage, Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa explains how a general practical custom ('urf 'amali 'amm) can specify a general text in Hanafi usul. This rule is a key tool for the mufti harmonizing texts with established practice without nullifying the text.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish verbal vs practical custom
Recognize that verbal custom (urf lafdhi) affects the meaning of words, while practical custom (urf amali) concerns practices. This lesson focuses on practical custom as a specifier.
Verbal custom was discussed earlier; here we focus on practical.
2
Understand the condition of contemporaneity
The custom must have been present (muqarin) at the time the general text was revealed. If it arises later, it is abrogation (naskh), not specification (takhsees).
This is a critical distinction in Hanafi usul.
3
Distinguish specification from abrogation
Specification narrows the scope of a general text from the beginning, leaving it operative on other cases. Abrogation removes the ruling after it was in effect.
Custom can only specify, never abrogate, according to this rule.
4
Apply to the example of istisna'
The general prohibition of selling what one does not possess is specified by the widespread custom of manufacturing contracts (istisna'), which existed at the time of the Prophet (PBUH).
This shows how custom allows necessary transactions.
5
Note the exception: custom must be general
A local or specific custom cannot specify a general text; only a truly general custom (al-urf al-amm) suffices.
This limits the application and prevents fragmentation.

Commentary
This passage from al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-‘Aam by Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa deals with the theory of ‘urf (custom) in the Hanafi madhhab, specifically the rule that a general practical custom existing at the time of revelation can specify (takhsees) a general text (nas ‘aam). The author first distinguishes between verbal custom (‘urf lafdhi) and practical custom (‘urf ‘amali). Verbal custom gives a word a customary meaning that takes precedence over the lexical meaning when interpreting a text; examples include the fiqh terms 'sale', 'prayer', etc. But the focus is on practical custom as a mukhassis (specifier).

The key rule: If at the time a general text was revealed there existed a general practical custom that contradicts the apparent generality of the text, then in Hanafi usul that custom is considered a qareenah (contextual indication) that the Lawgiver did not intend the generality. This does not nullify the text; rather, the text continues to apply to everything except the customary practice. This is specification, not abrogation (naskh). The condition is that the custom must be muqarin (contemporaneous) with the text; if evidence comes later, it would be abrogation, not specification.

The author provides two examples: (1) The Quranic verse 'Allah has permitted trade and forbidden usury' – the word 'trade' is general, but the prohibition of usury specifies it by excluding interest-based transactions. (2) The contract of istisna’ (manufacturing contract) – despite the general prohibition of selling what one does not possess, custom allowed istisna', and the Hanafis regard that as a specification by custom. Similarly, the prohibition of deception (ghish) from other texts specifies the general permissibility of trade.

The Hanafi requirement for a specifying custom is that it must be general (shamil) and contemporaneous. This rule demonstrates the madhhab's method of harmonizing texts with established practice, avoiding hardship, and applying both text and custom together.

General Custom as a Specifier of General Texts
Explaining the Hanafi rule that custom can specify a general text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida that general custom specifies general texts, distinguish between general and specific custom, and identify the requirement of contemporaneity.

Core Idea
General custom can narrow a general text without nullifying it, but only if it existed when the text was revealed.

This lesson examines how Hanafi usul al-fiqh allows general custom ('urf 'amm) to specify the scope of a general textual proof (nass 'amm). The passage distinguishes this from abrogation and from the weaker effect of specific custom. Understanding this rule is crucial for a mufti when reconciling established practices with primary texts, especially in transactions like istisna' contracts.

Guided Path
1
State the Qawida
Identify the legal maxim: 'General custom, when it opposes a general text, specifies it.' Recognize that this is a wusta (intermediate) level maxim governing the relationship between custom and text.
This qawida is a subset of the major maxim 'al-'aadah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative).
2
Understand the Condition of Contemporaneity
The Hanafi school requires that the specifying custom exist at the time the general text was revealed. If the custom arises later, it cannot specify; instead, later conflicting evidence is considered abrogation (naskh), not specification (takhsis).
This is a point of ikhtilaf with the Shafi'i school.
3
Distinguish General vs. Specific Custom
General custom ('urf 'amm) is universal across regions and communities; specific custom ('urf khas) is limited to a locality or profession. Only general custom can specify general texts; specific custom lacks the authority to do so.
This distinction prevents localized practices from overriding divine texts.
4
Apply the Qawida to Istisna'
The Prophet's general prohibition of selling what is not possessed includes istisna' (manufacturing contract). However, universal custom permits it out of necessity. Hanafi jurists treat this custom as a specifier, effectively excluding istisna' from the prohibition—analogous to the explicit exception of salam.
Istisna' remains valid despite the general text, but other types of selling non-existent items are still prohibited.
5
Identify the Istithna'
The qawida has an exception: specific custom cannot specify a general text. This is captured in the sub-maxim 'al-'urf al-khas la yukhasis al-nass al-'amm'.
The reason is that specific custom does not reliably indicate the Lawgiver's intent and lacks universal strength.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Index numbers refer to paragraphs, not pages; a star (*) marks a general legal maxim.

Dr. Mustafa al-Zarqa's al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm is a reference work organized by numbered paragraphs (fawāṣil). Its index uses these paragraph numbers, not page numbers, for precise location. Guided Path

A ح (short for ḥāshiya) to the left of a paragraph number indicates that the discussion is in a footnote.

General legal maxims (qawāʿid kulliyya) are marked with a star (*) to the right of the entry, right after the term.

Decode multiple references
When a topic has multiple references, the original location is given in parentheses. Other numbers are additional locations.
Example: 'البطلان تعريفه : 2/51, 4/55ح' means definition of invalidity is in Ch.51 para.2 and in Ch.55 para.4 footnote.
5
Apply the alphabetical ordering
Entries are sorted alphabetically ignoring the definite article (al-). For example, 'الإجازة' is alphabetized under 'j' (ج) after dropping 'al-'.
This is standard in Arabic indices.

Commentary
This passage is the front matter of the subject index (فهرس هجائي) of 'al-Madkhal al-Fiqhi al-'Amm' by Dr. Mustafa Ahmad al-Zarqa. It explains the conventions used to locate topics in the book. The index uses paragraph numbers (فقرات) rather than page numbers, which is typical for reference works where the paragraph numbering is consistent across editions. The ordering ignores the definite article 'al-' (ال) for alphabetization. Footnotes are marked with the letter ح (short for حاشية) to the left of the paragraph number. When a topic appears in multiple places, the original location is given in parentheses. General maxims (القواعد الكلية) are indicated by a star (*). The passage provides examples of how to read index entries: e.g., 'الإجازة 5/38 و9-10' means 'Permission' is found in Chapter 38, paragraph 5 and paragraphs 9 to 10. The star next to 'بيت المال خطأ القاضي في بيت المال 13/83' signals that this is a fiqh maxim. This index methodology is crucial for navigating the book's content, especially for locating legal maxims and their applications.

Key Terms
القواعد الكلية (al-qawa'id al-kulliyya): General legal maxims that apply across multiple chapters of fiqh; in this index they are marked with a star (*).
الفقرة (al-faqra): A numbered paragraph in the book, used as the unit of reference in the index instead of page numbers.
