The Mufti's Primary Source: Obligation to Follow Zahir al-Riwayah
Opens the poem with purpose and first rule of fatwa hierarchy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the first rule of fatwa from Ibn Abidin's poem, identify the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah, and apply the hierarchy of Hanafi fatwa sources.

Core Idea
The mufti must follow a preponderated view or Zahir al-Riwayah; the six books of Imam Muhammad are the primary source.

This lesson introduces the foundational rule for every Hanafi mufti: when issuing a fatwa, you must rely on the most authoritative transmission within the school. Ibn Abidin opens his poem 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' by establishing the obligation to follow either a preponderated view (marjuh) or, if no preponderance has been made, the narration that is Zahir al-Riwayah—the widely transmitted corpus of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. This rule ensures consistency and prevents arbitrary selection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Purpose of the Poem
Read the opening lines. Ibn Abidin states he composed this work for the 'amil (practitioner) and the mufti. This sets the context: the poem is a practical guide for fatwa-giving.
2
Extract the First Rule
Locate the line: 'اعلم بأن الواجب اتباع ما ترجيحه عن أهله قد علما أو كان ظاهر الرواية'. Understand that the mufti must follow a preponderated view OR, if none is preferred, the narration that is Zahir al-Riwayah.
This is the first substantive ruling in the poem.
3
Memorize the Six Books
The poem lists the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah: al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Ziyadat, and al-Mabsut. Commit these to memory as the authoritative core.
These are also called 'al-Usul'.
4
Understand the Hierarchy of Authorities
The poem later states that when Abu Hanifa has made a choice, it is followed; otherwise, the choice of Abu Yusuf (Ya'qub) is taken by consensus. This establishes a priority order.
The sahaba (companions) include Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
5
Apply the Rule to a Scenario
Given a case with two narrations—one in al-Mabsut, one in Nawadir—determine which to follow if no tarjih exists. The Zahir al-Riwayah (al-Mabsut) is the default.
This avoids arbitrary selection.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of Ibn Abidin's poem 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti', which sets out the rules of fatwa-giving in the Hanafi school. The author begins with the basmala and praise, then states his purpose: to compose a work needed by both the practitioner (who acts on fatwa) and the mufti himself. The first substantive ruling is that a mufti must follow either a preponderated view (marjuh) within the school or, if no tarjih has been made, the narration that is Zahir al-Riwayah—the widely transmitted version attributed to Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. The poem then lists the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah: al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Ziyadat, and al-Mabsut (also called al-Asl). These are the core texts of the Hanafi madhhab, transmitted with authentic chains. After these come the Nawadir (rare narrations) and Nawazil (later cases). The author notes that al-Siyar al-Kabir was composed last so it is authoritative, and al-Mabsut is the basis (al-Asl). Al-Kafi by al-Hakim al-Shahid summarizes the six. The strongest commentary is al-Mabsut of al-Sarakhsi, which is practically definitive. Finally, the hierarchy of fatwa sources within the school is given: first, the explicit choice of Abu Hanifa; where none exists, the ijtihad of Abu Yusuf (Ya'qub) takes precedence, as agreed by the companions.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The widely transmitted rulings from Imam Muhammad in his six core books, considered the primary source of fatwa in the Hanafi school.
المبسوط (al-Mabsut): One of the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah authored by Muhammad al-Shaybani; also known as al-Asl (the foundation).
رفاق (rifaq): The companions of Abu Hanifa, i.e., his senior students like Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Hierarchy of Hanafi Fatwa Sources: The Mufti's Guide to Tarjih
Didactic poem on fatwa source hierarchy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchy of Hanafi fatwa sources and apply the tarjih rules to determine which opinion to follow in a given case.

Core Idea
The mufti follows a fixed hierarchy: Zahir al-Riwayah, then Abu Hanifa, then Sahibayn, then later majority.

This lesson unpacks the foundational hierarchy that every Hanafi mufti must internalize. The poem by Ibn Abidin lays out the sources of authority in a clear sequence, from the six core books of Zahir al-Riwayah down to the opinions of later scholars. Understanding this ladder prevents arbitrary fatwa-giving and ensures consistency with the school's methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary sources
Recognize the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah: al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Ziyadat, and al-Mabsut. These form the bedrock of Hanafi fatwa.
Al-Mabsut is also known as al-Asl.
2
Check for a ruling in Zahir al-Riwayah
If the case is explicitly covered in these six books, that ruling is authoritative unless a later tarjih has overridden it. The poem states that they are 'relied upon and not deviated from'.
3
If absent, trace narrations from Abu Hanifa
Where no Zahir al-Riwayah ruling exists, look for a direct narration from Abu Hanifa. If found, his opinion is followed, especially in ibadat.
If there are multiple narrations, one must check which was selected by the school.
4
Apply Sahibayn and later hierarchy
If no direct narration from Abu Hanifa, then follow Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then Zufar, then later scholars. In judicial matters, Abu Yusuf is preferred; in dhawi al-arham, Muhammad is preferred.
When the Sahibayn differ from Abu Hanifa in non-ibadat areas, the mufti must exercise ijtihad.
5
Apply area-specific rules
The poem gives three specific rules: in ibadat, Abu Hanifa's view is absolute; in qada, Abu Yusuf is selected; in dhawi al-arham, Muhammad's view is followed. Also, istihsan is preferred over qiyas except where qiyas is unambiguous.

Commentary
This is a didactic poem (rajaz) outlining the fundamental rules of tarjih (preference) in issuing fatwas according to the Hanafi school. The passage type is a rasm al-mufti rule, specifically a hierarchy of authority. The poem begins by stating that the mufti must follow what has been given preference by the scholars of the school, and if no tarjih exists, then the ruling of Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani) is followed. It then lists the six books: al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Ziyadat, and al-Mabsut. Al-Mabsut is also called al-Asl. The poem notes that Masail al-Nawadir and Masail al-Nawazil have lower authority. The commentary also states that if there is a direct transmission from Abu Hanifa, his opinion is preferred; if not, then Abu Yusuf's (Ya'qub) opinion is chosen. If neither, then Muhammad's opinion. If the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) agree on something against the Imam, the mufti may choose based on evidence. For a fully qualified mujtahid mufti, if the Sahibayn differ from Abu Hanifa, there is no automatic preference except by detailed ijtihad. The poem further mentions that for certain issues, Zufar's opinion is given preference in 27 cases. Later scholars like al-Tahawi are given weight based on majority. Finally, it gives specific rules: in matters of worship (ibadat), Abu Hanifa's opinion is preferred unless there is a clear narration from him that contradicts; in judiciary matters (qada), Abu Yusuf's opinion is selected; in inheritance of distant kindred (dhawi al-arham), Muhammad's opinion is adopted; and istihsan is preferred over qiyas except where the qiyas is unambiguous. This is a comprehensive guide for the Hanafi mufti on which source to give weight.

Rules of Tarjih in Hanafi Fatwa
A poem listing tarjih rules for muftis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi tarjih hierarchy to resolve conflicting opinions within the madhhab.

Core Idea
The mufti must prefer the apparent transmitted, earlier authoritative texts, and opinions with explicit proofs or corrections.

This lesson unpacks a classical poem on tarjih, which is the art of preferring one legal opinion over another. For a Hanafi mufti, tarjih is essential when faced with conflicting narrations from the early Imams. The poem provides a clear hierarchy and specific criteria that must be applied systematically, ensuring consistency and adherence to the madhhab's methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overriding principle
The poem begins by stating that the apparent transmitted narration (zahir al-marwi) should not be abandoned without strong justification. This is the baseline: the mufti must start with the transmitted reports and not substitute personal reasoning lightly.
Zahir al-marwi includes the teachings of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
2
Determine the source hierarchy
Next, the poem describes which texts take precedence: commentaries (shuruh) over fatwa collections, earlier texts over later ones (unless a later correction is explicit). Specific works like al-Khaniyah and Multaqa al-Abhar have special weight, while in later works the later opinion is often the verified one (al-muharrar).
For example, the rulings in al-Hidayah are considered authoritative until a later corrected ruling appears.
3
Apply the internal criteria
When two opinions conflict, the mufti looks for internal indicators: a proof (ta'lil) given for one, explicit correction (tas-hih) of one, being the statement of Imam Abu Hanifa, being the view of the majority, being based on istihsan, benefiting awqaf, suiting the time, or being clearer in proof.
If both are corrected, the mufti may choose unless one is declared 'asahh' (soundest) or fatwa is prescribed on one.
4
Handle conflicts and missing corrections
If correction (tas-hih) conflicts or none exists, the mufti must seek any murajjih (preferring factor) from the list. The poem provides a fallback: take the opinion that aligns with narrations unless they contradict an explicit text.
This step ensures that a ruling is never made arbitrarily.

Commentary
This passage is a versified set of rules (manzumah) for a Hanafi mufti when faced with conflicting narrations or opinions. It is a memorization anchor for the hierarchy of tarjih (preference). The poem begins by stating that the apparent meaning of a transmitted report (zahir al-marwi) should not be abandoned for a contrary view. It warns against abandoning diraya (knowledge/juristic reasoning). Statements that negate disbelief (i.e., avoid takfir) are to be favored. When a mujtahid retracts an opinion, his retraction is considered. The poem then outlines the hierarchy: commentaries (shuruh) are preferred over fatwa collections; among works with tarjih, the earlier text is given precedence unless a later correction has been made. For example, al-Khaniyah and Multaqa al-Abhar are older and thus carry special weight; in other works, the later opinions are relied upon because they represent the final verified position (al-muharrar). The poem also discusses cases where one opinion is supported by a proof (ta'lil) while the other is not, or when one of two opinions is explicitly declared 'sahih' (correct). In such cases, the corrected opinion is taken. If both are corrected, then the mufti may choose unless one is 'sahih' and the other 'asahh' (soundest), or it is explicitly stated that fatwa is given on one. Further criteria include: the opinion being in the matn (core text) or the statement of Imam Abu Hanifa, being the apparent of the transmitted, being held by the majority of great scholars, being based on istihsan (juristic preference), bringing clear benefit to awqaf (charitable endowments), being more suitable for the time, or being clearer in proof. When these criteria conflict or when no explicit correction exists, the mufti must apply the appropriate murajjih (preferring factor) from the list. Finally, he should act upon the understood import of narrations as long as they do not contradict an explicit text. This is the basis of issuing fatwa.

Key Terms
ظاهر المروي (zahir al-marwi): The apparent/literal meaning of a transmitted report (narration) from the founding Imams.
الدراية (al-diraya): Juristic knowledge and reasoning, as opposed to mere transmission (riwaya).
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juristic preference, a method of departing from a strict analogy in favor of a stronger evidence or public benefit.

Preference Criteria for the Mufti (Tarjih al-Riwayat)
Final verses of a poem on mufti methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key factors (murajjihat) that give preponderance to one riwayah over another and apply the rule of acting on mafhum when no explicit text is found.

Core Idea
The mufti must prefer the apparent, majority-supported, istihsan-based, more beneficial, timely, and clearer proof – and may act on implied meaning when no explicit text contradicts.

These are the closing verses of Ibn Abidin's didactic poem on the methodology of issuing fatwas. They encapsulate the core tarjih principles that every Hanafi mufti must internalize. The poem lists specific criteria to resolve conflicting transmissions and provides guidance when no explicit text exists, ensuring fatwa practice remains grounded in the school's authoritative sources.

Guided Path
1
Recite the list of criteria
Read the poem lines carefully and enumerate the factors mentioned: ظاهر المروي (apparent report supported by majority), الاستحسان (istihsan), زيادة نفع الأوقاف (benefit for endowments), أوفق للزمان (suitability for the time), أوضح في البرهان (clarity of proof).
These factors are not exhaustive; they are examples from the poem.
2
Understand the context of conflict
The poem states this applies 'when correction is conflicting' (تعارض التصحيح) or 'if there is no clear text originally' (لم يكن أصلاً به تصريح). This triggers the need for tarjih.
This context is crucial – not every disagreement requires these criteria; only when texts appear equally strong.
3
Apply the murajjah rule
When a conflict exists, take the view that has a preponderant factor (murajjaḥ). The mufti must weigh which criteria are met and prioritize accordingly.
Multiple criteria may coexist; the mufti exercises ijtihad in balancing them.
4
Act on mafhum when no explicit text
If there is no clear text (taṣrīḥ), rely on the implied meaning (mafhūm) of narrations, provided it does not contradict an explicit text.
Hanafis accept mafhūm al-muwāfaqa but restrict mafhūm al-mukhālafa. This line grants permission to use mafhūm in fatwa.
5
Memorize the sequence
Internalize the order: first look for explicit text; if none, apply tarjih criteria from the poem; if still unresolved, fall back to mafhum (with the condition of no contradiction).
This hierarchy is foundational for mufti training.

Commentary
This passage is the concluding portion of Ibn Abidin's poetic summary on the methodology of issuing fatwas. The verses enumerate criteria for preferring one transmission or ruling over another (tarjih) when texts appear to conflict or when no explicit textual support exists. The mufti is instructed to give weight to what is more apparent (zāhir) in transmission, what is adopted by the majority of great scholars (jull al-a‘āmm), what is based on istihsan, what provides greater benefit for endowments (awqāf), what is more appropriate for contemporary circumstances, and what is clearer in proof. When there is no explicit text (taṣrīḥ), the mufti should act upon the mafhūm (implied meaning) of reliable narrations, provided it does not contradict an explicit text. These rules are part of the foundational principles (uṣūl al-iftā’) that ensure consistency and authority in Hanafi fatwa practice.
