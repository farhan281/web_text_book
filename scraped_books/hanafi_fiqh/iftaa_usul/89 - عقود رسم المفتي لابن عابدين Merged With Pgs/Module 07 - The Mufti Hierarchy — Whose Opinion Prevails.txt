The Six Books of Zahir al-Riwaya and Their Hierarchy
Explains the naming and order of the six authoritative books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six books of Zahir al-Riwaya, explain why al-Mabsut is called al-Asl, and apply the rule that the later-authored book takes precedence when narrations conflict.

Core Idea
Later-authored books in Zahir al-Riwaya override earlier ones—order determines authority in fatwa.

The six books of Zahir al-Riwaya form the foundation of Hanafi fiqh. A mufti must know not only their names but also their order of composition, because that order determines which ruling is considered the final position of Imam Muhammad. This passage introduces the books, explains the title al-Asl for al-Mabsut, and gives the key rule for resolving internal conflicts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Six Books
List the six books written by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani that constitute Zahir al-Riwaya: al-Mabsut (al-Asl), al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat, al-Siyar al-Saghir, and al-Siyar al-Kabir. Note that the passage mentions only five, but the sixth (al-Siyar al-Saghir) is also part of the set.
Memorize the order of authorship: al-Mabsut (first), then al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat, al-Siyar al-Kabir. Al-Siyar al-Saghir is also a later work.
2
Understand the Meaning of 'al-Asl'
Al-Mabsut is called 'al-Asl' because it is the first comprehensive work, serving as the foundation for all later Hanafi fiqh. The passage provides a scholarly explanation: the book contains the core issues and principles laid down by Abu Hanifa and his students, and later works expand upon it.
The term 'asl' can also mean 'primary source' in a generic sense, but for Hanafis it became a proper name for this book.
3
Apply the Rule of Later Authorship
When narrations in the books of Zahir al-Riwaya conflict, the opinion in the later-authored book is taken, and the earlier one is considered abandoned. This rule identifies the final position of Imam Muhammad.
For example, if al-Mabsut and al-Siyar al-Kabir differ on a matter, al-Siyar al-Kabir prevails because it was written after al-Mabsut.

Rule on a Mujtahid's Retracted Opinion
Statement of the rule that a retracted opinion is treated as abrogated

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a mujtahid's retracted opinion is treated as abrogated (mansukh) in fatwa issuance.

Core Idea
A retracted opinion loses all operative force; the later opinion becomes the madhhab.

In the Hanafi school, the rulings of the founding imams evolved over time. A critical skill for the mufti is to know which of Abu Hanifa's opinions came last. This lesson introduces a foundational rule: any opinion from which the mujtahid retracted is treated like an abrogated text—it cannot be used for fatwa. Understanding this prevents reliance on outdated positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'raja'a 'anhu'
Recognize that this phrase indicates retraction and is a term of tarjih (preference).
It is not always an explicit revocation but signals that the earlier view is superseded.
2
Distinguish known vs. unknown chronology
If we know which opinion came later, that is the madhhab; if not, both are reported without tarjih.
Ibn Amir Hajj's quote highlights this distinction.
3
Apply the rule in fatwa
When issuing a fatwa, use only the later, unretracted opinion. The retracted one is marjuh and cannot be acted upon.
This ensures the fatwa reflects the mujtahid's final position.
4
Understand the metaphor of naskh
The retracted opinion is like mansukh in effect, but this is a juristic convention, not actual naskh.
This is important to avoid literal interpretation.

Commentary
This passage presents a rasm rule (methodological rule for issuing fatwas) regarding a mujtahid's retracted opinion. The rule states that when a mujtahid (here specifically Abu Hanifa or other founding imams) recants a previous opinion, that opinion becomes like a mansukh (abrogated) ruling—it loses its legal force and cannot be used for fatwa. The author explains this with a quote from Ibn Amir Hajj, who says: if we know which opinion came later, that is his madhhab, and the former is considered abrogated; if we do not know the chronological order, then both opinions are reported but no determination is made. In the Hanafi school, the term 'raja'a 'anhu' (he retracted from it) functions as a term of tarjih (preference)—it signals that the retracted view is marjuh (weak) and the new view is rajih (strong) for practice. This is important for muftis: they must be aware of the chronology of an imam's rulings and follow the later one. The passage also notes that explicit statements of retraction are made when the strength of the later opinion becomes established. This rule ensures that the fatwa reflects the final matured position of the mujtahid, not earlier tentative views.

Key Terms
رجع عنه (raja'a 'anhu): An expression indicating that the mujtahid (Abu Hanifa) withdrew from a previous opinion; the retracted opinion is no longer valid.
منسوخ (mansūkh): Treated like an abrogated ruling—no longer operative for fatwa despite having been a valid prior opinion.

Fatwa Terms and the Mufti's Choice
Methodological rules for mufti's choice between corrected opinions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the hierarchy of fatwa terms (alfaz al-tarjih) and the rule of considering the jurist and book to determine the mufta bihi ruling in a given fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
Tarjih terms are not absolute; authority of the jurist and book outweighs mere wording.

A mufti often encounters multiple corrected opinions within the Hanafi school. How does he choose which one to issue as fatwa? This lesson from Ibn Abidin's 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' provides the guiding principles: a hierarchy of tarjih terms like 'ashbah', 'awjah', 'sahih', 'asah', and the stronger phrases 'bihi yufta' and 'alayhi al-fatwa'. Yet, crucially, Ibn Abidin warns against relying solely on the wording; the stature of the jurist (qā'il) and the reliability of the book (kitāb) are more decisive. We will unpack this methodology step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the tarjih terms
Scan the passage and list the different Arabic phrases used to indicate preference (e.g., الأشبه، الأوجه، الصحيح، الأصح، به يفتى، عليه الفتوى). Note that the passage defines each.
The passage states that all these terms are equal except where specified.
2
Analyze the dispute on sahih vs asah
Examine the disagreement: some say 'asah' is stronger (comparative form), others say 'sahih' is stronger (opposing it is error). Ibn Abidin gives the preferred view: 'asah' is stronger only when the sayings are from different persons; otherwise, consider the speaker and book.
This is a key nuance: wording alone is not decisive.
3
Understand the special strength of 'bihi yufta' and 'alayhi al-fatwa'
The passage declares these two phrases as stronger than other tarjih terms because they combine preference and practice. However, this strength is not absolute; the mufti must still weigh the authority behind them.
Even these stronger terms can be overridden by a higher‑ranking jurist or a more authoritative book.
4
Apply the principle: prioritize the speaker and book
When multiple tarjih terms appear, the mufti should give weight to the jurist (e.g., Qadi Khan’s correction precedes others) and the book (e.g., a correction in a more relied‑upon text is stronger). The wording itself is secondary.
Ibn Abidin quotes Ibn Qutlubugha: 'What Qadi Khan corrects is given precedence... because he is a jurist in soul.'
5
Follow the rules when multiple corrected opinions exist
If no preponderator is clear, the mufti may choose any corrected opinion with a sincere heart. If preponderators exist: for the same jurist, use the later opinion; for different jurists, prefer explicit over implicit correction, and stronger wording over weaker.
This step synthesizes the entire passage into practical guidance for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from Ibn Abidin's 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' (with its commentary) explains how a mufti should navigate conflicting rulings when multiple opinions have been 'corrected' (i.e., given preference) in the Hanafi school. It is a rasm al-mufti passage—methodological rules for issuing fatwa.

First, the passage defines key terms of preference (alfaz al-tarjih) such as 'ashbah' (most similar to texts in narration) and 'awjah' (more directed by evidence). All such terms are considered equal in strength; no single term automatically outweighs another. However, there is a disagreement about 'sahih' vs 'asah': some say 'asah' is stronger as a comparative, others say 'sahih' is stronger because opposing it is outright error. Ibn Abidin leans that 'asah' is stronger only when the two sayings come from different individuals—otherwise, the better approach is to consider the authority of the book and the scholar.

Second, the passage states that the phrases 'by it fatwa is given' (بِهِ يُفْتَى) and 'upon it the fatwa' (عَلَيْهِ الْفَتْوَى) are stronger than other terms because they directly instruct practice. Nevertheless, the ideal method is not to rely on wording alone but to prefer based on the stature of the jurist (qā'il) and the reliability of the book (kitāb). For example, a correction by Qadi Khan is given precedence over others due to his juristic depth.

Third, if a mufti encounters multiple corrected opinions with no clear preponderator, he may choose any of them, following his heart's inclination while avoiding caprice. However, if preponderators exist, he must follow them: e.g., if both corrections are from the same scholar, the later one (if known) prevails; if from different scholars, explicit corrections take precedence over implicit ones, and stronger wordings (like 'به يفتى') outweigh weaker ones.

This passage thus equips the mufti with a structured methodology for tarjih, emphasizing that context (author, book, recency) often matters more than the specific wording used.

Key Terms
به يفتى (bihi yuftā): A phrase indicating that this ruling is given in fatwa, considered stronger than other tarjih terms because it explicitly directs practice.
صحيح (ṣaḥīḥ): A ruling that is correct; opposed to it is error (khaṭa').
أصح (aṣaḥḥ): Comparative form: more correct than ṣaḥīḥ; indicates a higher degree of correctness.
أشبه (ashbah): Most similar (to the texts); a term of tarjih indicating that the ruling is closer to the primary sources in narration and/or reasoning.
أوجه (awjah): More appropriate or more directed by evidence; a term of tarjih.
