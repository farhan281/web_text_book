Authenticity and Title of Uqud Rasm al-Mufti
Establishing the authenticity of Ibn 'Abidin's poem and describing manuscripts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of evidence used to establish the authenticity of a Hanafi iftaa text and explain the significance of the manuscript tradition for the poem 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'.

Core Idea
Ibn 'Abidin's own testimony, cross-references, and biographers jointly prove the authenticity of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'.

Before delving into the content of Ibn 'Abidin's poem on mufti methodology, it is essential to establish its authenticity and precise title. This lesson examines the editor's threefold proof of authorship, the distinction between the formal and common titles, and the nature of the manuscript sources used for verification. Understanding these textual foundations is crucial for any mufti or scholar relying on this work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of evidence
Locate the three distinct types of proof the editor provides: the author's own statement in the poem's introduction, cross-references in Ibn 'Abidin's other works (Radd al-Muhtar, Nashr al-'Urf), and external attribution by biographers like his son and al-Shatti.
These categories form a standard method for authenticating classical texts.
2
Distinguish the full title from the common name
Note that the full literal title is 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti', but it is more commonly referred to descriptively as 'Manzumat Rasm al-Mufti'. Both refer to the same poem.
The common name is used in most citations within Radd al-Muhtar.
3
Understand the manuscript situation
The editor explains that no independent manuscript of the poem exists; it survives only within the commentary manuscripts. The poem was extracted from three sources: Nashr al-'Urf, A'yan Dimashq, and Radd al-Muhtar.
This highlights the importance of commentary traditions for preserving primary texts.

Commentary
This passage is from the modern scholarly edition of Ibn 'Abidin's poem 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'. It comprises three sections: first, proof of authorship; second, the exact title; third, description of manuscripts used for verification. The editor asserts with certainty that the poem belongs to Ibn 'Abidin, citing the author's own statement in the preface, cross-references in his other works (e.g., Radd al-Muhtar, Nashr al-'Urf), and endorsements by biographers like his son and al-Shatti. The full title is 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti, though it is commonly referred to as Manzumat Rasm al-Mufti. The editor explains that no independent manuscript of the poem exists; rather, it is extracted from the commentary manuscripts. The three sources for collation are: Ibn 'Abidin's own commentary Nashr al-'Urf, the biographical work A'yan Dimashq by Ibn 'Abidin's son, and cross-references in Radd al-Muhtar. This establishes the textual basis for the edition.

Key Terms
عقود رسم المفتي ('Uqud Rasm al-Mufti): The full title of Ibn 'Abidin's poem on the rules of issuing fatwas, meaning 'The Necklaces of the Outline of the Mufti'.
منظومة رسم المفتي (Manzumat Rasm al-Mufti): The commonly used descriptive name for the same poem, meaning 'The Poem on the Outline of the Mufti'.

Manuscript Sources for Ibn Abidin's 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'
Manuscript description for critical edition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three manuscript copies used in the critical edition of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' and explain why no independent manuscript of the poem exists.

Core Idea
The poem survives only within its commentary – no separate manuscript exists.

This lesson introduces the manuscript basis for the critical edition of Ibn Abidin's seminal work on fatwa methodology. Understanding the textual sources is essential for assessing the authority of the rulings derived from this text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its structure
Recognize that the work consists of a poem (manzuma) and a prose commentary (sharh) by the same author. The editor extracted the poem from the commentary manuscripts.
The poem is the authoritative summary; the commentary explains it.
2
Note the source limitation
Understand that no independent manuscript of the poem was found – it always accompanies the commentary. This affects how we cite and verify the text.
Critical editions rely on multiple copies of the commentary.
3
Examine each manuscript copy
Review the three copies: (A) from al-Azhar, (B) from King Abdulaziz Library, (C) from Al-Alukah. Note their folio counts, line numbers, and physical features.
These details help assess reliability and completeness.
4
Assess the editorial methodology
Understand that the editor collated the poem from these three copies to produce a single reliable text. Variations in handwriting, annotations, and completeness inform the critical apparatus.
This is standard procedure in Islamic manuscript editing.

Commentary
This passage is from the introduction of the critical edition of Ibn Abidin's 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. The editor, Dr. Salah Abu al-Hajj, first clarifies the nomenclature of the work, noting that Ibn Abidin himself referred to it as 'Manzumat Rasm al-Mufti' in his Radd al-Muhtar. The editor then explains that no independent manuscript of the poem exists; it is always found together with Ibn Abidin's own commentary. Therefore, to produce the edition, he extracted the poem from three manuscript copies of the commentary. This information is typical of critical editions and helps establish the textual basis for the work.

The Critical Role of Manuscript Descriptions in Hanafi Fatwa Texts
Description of three manuscript copies for the edition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of a manuscript description and explain its importance in establishing the reliable text of a Hanafi fatwa manual.

Core Idea
A mufti's ruling is only as authentic as the manuscript chain behind his text.

This passage describes three manuscripts used to produce the critical edition of Ibn 'Abidin's 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. While it may seem like dry bibliography, understanding manuscript descriptions is essential for any serious student of Hanafi fatwa: it tells us which copy is most complete, which has corrections, and which edition we can rely on when issuing rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify manuscript features
Each copy is described by location, number of folios, lines per page, handwriting quality, and unique features like color coding or glosses. Note these details for each copy.
Pay special attention to completeness and corrections.
2
Assess reliability for fatwa
For fatwa, a complete copy with corrections (like Copy B) is more valuable than an incomplete one. Understand how these details affect textual authority.
More folios do not always mean a better copy—extra content may inflate count.
