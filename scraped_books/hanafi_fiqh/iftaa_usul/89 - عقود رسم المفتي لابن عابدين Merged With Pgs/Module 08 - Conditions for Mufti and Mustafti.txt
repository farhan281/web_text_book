The Hierarchy of Fatwa Authority in the Hanafi Madhhab
A rasm al-mufti passage establishing the hierarchy of fatwa authority and the binding nature of later mujtahids' preferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five stages of fatwa authority and apply the rule of following later mujtahids' tarjih when earlier rulings are absent.

Core Idea
The ruling passes from Quran/Sunnah through a chain of mujtahids to the mufti — later scholars' preferences are binding when no earlier clear ruling exists.

This lesson explains the chain of authority in Hanafi fatwa: from absolute mujtahid to common person. You will learn how later mujtahids (like al-Tahawi, Abu Hafs) can correct and prefer opinions, and that their rulings are binding for the mufti. Understanding this hierarchy ensures fatwas are issued correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five stages of authority
List the stages: Quran/Sunnah → absolute mujtahid (e.g., Abu Hanifa) → mujtahid in the madhhab (e.g., al-Tahawi) → mufti → common person. The ruling must pass through each stage, ensuring scrutiny.
This is the foundational schema for fatwa in the Hanafi school.
2
Understand the role of mujtahid fi al-madhhab
The mujtahid fi al-madhhab does not derive new rulings from primary sources but applies tarjih (preference) among existing opinions of the founding imams. His preferred opinion becomes binding for the mufti.
Example: Al-Tahawi is such a mujtahid.
3
Apply the rule of later scholars' consensus
If no clear ruling exists from Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, or Muhammad, the mufti must take the opinion held by the majority of the later scholars (masha'ikh). If they speak with one voice, it is taken as binding.
This is a form of ijma (consensus) within the madhhab.
4
Analyze the example of Zufar's cases
Later mujtahids preferred Zufar's opinion in seventeen (or twenty, per Ibn Abidin) cases. This shows that even if a founding imam's view existed, the later tarjih can override it.
The number variation is due to different classifications; the principle remains.
5
Recognize the source of authority
The passage quotes al-Maqdisi: if no answer from independent mujtahids, and later scholars speak with one opinion, it is taken. This is the practical rule for muftis.
This is from al-Hawi al-Qudsi.

Commentary
This passage is a rasm_rule that establishes the hierarchy of authority in Hanafi fatwa. The poetry states that as long as there is no objection to the corrected opinion, we take the one that has been clarified. The commentary expands: the opinions of the mujtahids in the madhhab (such as al-Tahawi, Abu Hafs, etc.) are binding for the mufti. This is part of a chain: Quran/Sunnah → absolute mujtahid → mujtahid in the madhhab → mufti → common person. The ruling passes through these stages to ensure proper scrutiny. If no clear opinion exists from the earlier imams, then the later mujtahids' consensus or preferred opinion is taken. An example is given: the later scholars preferred Zufar's opinion in seventeen (or twenty) cases. Thus, the mufti must follow the tarjih (preference) of the later mujtahids.

Key Terms
مجتهد مطلق (mujtahid mutlaq): An absolute mujtahid who can deduce rulings directly from Quran and Sunnah, such as Abu Hanifa.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwaya): The apparent or well-known transmission of a ruling from the founding imams, considered the primary source for fatwa.
ظاهر عبارات الكتب (zahir 'ibarat al-kutub): The explicit wording of the books of the madhhab that indicates the preponderant opinion.

The Great Principle: Avoiding Takfir with Weak Evidence
Expansion of a fatwa rule to avoid takfir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qawida that prohibits declaring a Muslim a kafir if any favorable interpretation or disagreement exists, even based on a weak narration, and identify the exception for denial of daruriyyat.

Core Idea
A Muslim is not declared a kafir if his words can be favorably interpreted or if any disagreement exists, even from weak evidence.

This passage from Ibn Abidin's Rasm al-Mufti establishes a foundational principle for fatwa: protecting the inviolability of a Muslim's faith. The mufti must exhaust every possible favorable interpretation and consider even the weakest scholarly disagreement before even entertaining takfir. This lesson unpacks the qawida, its proof from hadith, and the crucial exception for denying matters known by necessity (daruriyyat).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Qawida
Read the opening statement from Ibn Nujaym and Ibn Abidin. Formulate the rule in your own words: a Muslim is not given a fatwa of disbelief if there is any possible favorable interpretation or any disagreement, even from a weak narration.
This qawida is a specific application of the broader maxim 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' but with a heightened precaution for takfir.
2
Understand the Extension of 'Darura'
Ibn Abidin states that the usual prohibition on using weak hadiths for fatwa is set aside in cases of necessity (darura). Here, necessity includes the gravity of takfir, so weak narrations can be used to avoid it.
The concept of darura is expanded to include protection of a Muslim's faith and life.
3
Study the Supporting Evidence
Examine the creedal statement of Imam al-Tahawi and the two Sahih hadiths cited. The first hadith defines a Muslim by shared rituals and warns against violating Allah's covenant. The second commands fighting until tawhid and practices are established, then blood and property are protected.
These hadiths establish the general principle that outward Islam is accepted; inner states are left to Allah.
4
Define 'Daruriyyat'
Al-Kashmiri's definition: matters known from the religion by necessity (tawatur, widespread knowledge) such as tawhid, prophethood, resurrection, prayer, zakat, and prohibition of wine. Denial of these after proper instruction constitutes kufr.
Only these core certainties can trigger takfir; beyond that, even weak doubts prevent it.
5
Apply the Qawida to a Case
Take a hypothetical scenario: a Muslim says he does not believe in the obligation of zakat. The mufti must first ask: is there a possible interpretation (e.g., he meant it is heavy)? Is there any disagreement among scholars (some say zakat is not obligatory on certain assets)? If either exists, takfir is avoided.
The mufti must lean towards preserving Islam unless certainty is reached.

Commentary
This passage is a rasm rule (rule for issuing fatwas) from Ibn Abidin's work on the methodology of fatwa. It establishes the principle that a Muslim should not be declared a kafir (disbeliever) if there is any possibility of interpreting his words favorably or if there is any disagreement among scholars (even based on a weak narration) about his disbelief. The author quotes Ibn Nujaym to define the rule, then Ibn Abidin extends the regular exception for using weak narrations in cases of necessity to include avoiding takfir. He calls this a 'great principle' (qāʿida ʿaẓīmiyya). He then cites al-Tahawi's creedal statement affirming that Muslims are not declared kafir due to sins unless they deem the sin permissible. Evidence is provided from two Sahih hadiths: the first defines a Muslim by shared rituals (prayer, qibla, diet) and warns against violating the covenant; the second commands fighting until people testify to tawhid and adopt Islamic practices. The passage concludes by restricting takfir to denial of matters known by necessity (ḍarūriyyāt) from the religion, listing examples like tawhid, prophethood, resurrection, prayer, zakat, and prohibition of wine. As a Hanafi mufti, this means one must never issue a fatwa of disbelief unless the person has clearly denied an absolutely certain and universally known belief or practice. The principle is designed to protect the inviolability of Muslims' faith and lives.

Key Terms
الضعيف (al-daʿīf): A weak hadith that does not meet the conditions of authenticity; here used as evidence to avoid takfir.
الضرورة (al-ḍarūra): Matters known to be from the religion by necessity, transmitted via tawatur and known to the general public; denial of such matters causes kufr.

Conditions for Issuing Fatwa with Weak Opinions and Adopting Other Madhhabs
Rasm al-mufti rule with exceptions and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule against weak opinions in fatwa, identify its exceptions, and apply the four conditions for issuing fatwa with another madhhab.

Core Idea
Weak opinions are prohibited in fatwa, but necessity and qualified scholarship allow exceptions if strict conditions are met.

This passage by Ibn Abidin clarifies the Hanafi rasm al-mufti: although weak opinions cannot normally be used, the school recognises genuine necessity and the role of qualified mujtahids. It also sets out a framework for adopting another madhhab when widespread need demands it—a crucial tool for modern muftis navigating complex fiqh scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
Locate the explicit prohibition: weak reports and marjuh opinions are not to be acted upon or issued as fatwa. Ibn Qutlubugha says this is contrary to consensus.
The rule is categorical but not absolute.
2
Find the Exceptions
Note two exceptions: (1) a lay person in pressing necessity may act on a weak opinion for himself; (2) a mujtahid faqih within the madhhab may give preponderance to a weak opinion if he finds a benefit, custom, need, ease, or necessity.
The second exception requires the mufti to be qualified to weigh evidence.
3
Understand the Permissibility of Another Madhhab
Recognise that acting on the opinion of another madhhab is allowed when there is need and necessity, as stated by later Hanafi jurists like al-Quhustani and Ibn Abidin.
This is not a free license—it is tied to umum al-balwa (widespread affliction).
4
List the Four Conditions for Adopting Another Madhhab
Extract the four conditions from al-Uthmani: (1) genuine severe need, (2) collective fatwa (not alone), (3) thorough understanding of the other madhhab, (4) taking the entire opinion with its conditions to avoid talfiq.
These conditions ensure the fatwa remains within scholarly discipline.
5
Apply the Judge Rule
Note the additional rule: judges cannot base judgments on weak opinions; if they do, the judgment is not enforceable. This reinforces the importance of using only rajih opinions in the judiciary.
The judge rule is a practical consequence of the main principle.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive statement of the Hanafi rasm al-mufti rules regarding the use of weak opinions and the adoption of opinions from other madhhabs in fatwa. It begins by stating the general rule: it is not permissible to act on or issue fatwa with weak reports or marjuh (preponderated against) opinions. This is supported by a quote from Ibn Qutlubugha, who states that such practice contradicts ijmā‘. However, the passage immediately provides exceptions. The first exception is when a person is faced with a pressing necessity (darurah muliḥḥah) — in that case, he may act on a weak opinion for his own personal practice. The second exception applies to a qualified mujtahid faqīh within the Hanafi madhhab, who is capable of tarjīḥ (weighing evidence). Such a person may give preference to a weak opinion if he finds a benefit (maṣlaḥah), custom (ʿurf), need (ḥājah), facilitation (taysīr), or necessity (ḍarūrah). The passage then addresses the adoption of opinions from other madhhabs. It states that acting on the opinion of a different madhhab is permissible if there is a need and necessity, and Hanafi scholars have explicitly stated this in certain issues. The author then quotes al-Uthmani, who discusses the increasing complexity of modern transactions and the widespread needs of people. He recommends that muftis make things easy by adopting what is more convenient in matters of common affliction (umum al-balwa), even if it is from another one of the four madhhabs. This is supported by the authority of later Hanafi scholars like al-Kankuhi and al-Thanawi. However, this permissibility is conditional. The passage lists four stringent conditions that must be met before a mufti can issue a fatwa based on a different madhhab: (1) the need must be genuine and widespread, not merely imagined; (2) the mufti should not act alone but seek collective fatwa; (3) he must thoroughly understand the other madhhab's terminology and methodology, preferably by consulting its scholars; (4) the entire opinion must be taken with all its conditions, to avoid talfīq (mixing parts of different madhhabs in a single issue). Finally, the passage adds a separate but related rule for judges: a judge cannot base his judgment on a weak opinion; if he does, the judgment is not enforceable. This is especially relevant because later judges are explicitly restricted to the rajī (preponderant) view of the madhhab during their appointment.

Key Terms
رواية ضعيفة (riwayah da'ifah): A report or narration from the madhhab's sources that has been deemed weak by the scholars of tarjih.
قول مرجوح (qawl marjuh): An opinion within the madhhab that has been preponderated against by the majority or later authorities.
تقليد (taqlid): Adopting the opinion of a different madhhab under conditions of need or general affliction.
عموم البلوى (umum al-balwa): A widespread affliction or need affecting a large number of people, justifying leniency.

Four Conditions for Issuing Fatwa from Another Madhhab
Conditions for off-madhhab fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for issuing a fatwa according to another madhhab and evaluate their application in a contemporary case.

Core Idea
A Hanafi mufti may only depart from his madhhab when need is real, the other school is properly understood, and talfiq is avoided.

The Hanafi tradition, while generally requiring a mufti to adhere to his own madhhab, recognizes that severe need or widespread calamity may justify issuing a fatwa according to another recognized school. However, this is not a license for convenience. The classical authorities and later scholars like al-Uthmani set forth stringent conditions to ensure that such fatwas remain scholarly and do not lead to arbitrary picking and choosing (talfiq). This passage enumerates those conditions, which every mufti must know before departing from the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Hanafis normally require taqlid within the madhhab. The exception is severe need or umum al-balwa.
2
List the four conditions
The passage gives four: genuine need, consultation with others, thorough verification of the other madhhab, adherence to all its conditions to avoid talfiq.
3
Analyze each condition
For each, note its rationale and what it prevents.
Condition 1: prevents imagined needs. Condition 2: prevents solo fatwa errors. Condition 3: prevents superficial understanding. Condition 4: prevents talfiq.
4
Apply to a scenario
Test a hypothetical: a mufti sees a convenient ruling in a Shafi'i book and issues it without checking the full context. Which condition is violated?
All conditions may be violated, especially 3 and 4.

Commentary

This is a rasm rule for muftis operating in contexts where strict adherence to one madhhab becomes difficult.
