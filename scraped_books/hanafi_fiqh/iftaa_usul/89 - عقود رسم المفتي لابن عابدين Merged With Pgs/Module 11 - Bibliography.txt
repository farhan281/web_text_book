Knowing the authoritative Hanafi sources is essential for sound fatwa and legal derivation.

Identify which entries are primary texts (e.g., Al-Asl, Kanz al-Daqa'iq) and which are commentaries (e.g., Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, Bada'i' al-Sana'i'). Note that some are modern works on ifta' (e.g., Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuhu).

When preparing a fatwa, a mufti should first consult the primary texts, then authoritative commentaries, and finally modern works that compile and clarify the tradition.

Reading a Hanafi Bibliography: Sources for Usul al-Iftaa
List of references from a critical edition of 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify key Hanafi usul and fiqh references from a bibliography, classify them by genre and authority level, and explain their role in the fatwa process.

Core Idea
A bibliography maps the scholarly hierarchy; knowing where a source sits (matn, sharh, hashiya) is essential for correct fatwa derivation.

This passage is a bibliography from the critical edition of Ibn Abidin's 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'. It lists over a dozen Hanafi works, including usul commentaries, fiqh matns, and historical biographies. While not a legal text, this list reveals the layered authority structure that a mufti must navigate. Learning to parse such references trains you to identify which source is primary, which is explanatory, and which is the relied-upon fatwa reference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the entry components
Each entry typically includes a sequential number, the full title of the work, the author's name with his death date (in Hijri), and publication details (editor, publisher, year, edition). The author's school is often indicated by 'al-Hanafi'.
Death dates help determine the chronological order of authorities.
2
Classify the genre
Determine whether the work is a matn (original text), sharh (commentary), hashiyah (super-commentary), tarikh (history), or modern study. For example, 'Tanwir al-Absar' is a matn, 'al-Durr al-Mukhtar' is a sharh, and 'Radd al-Muhtar' is a hashiyah.
The genre determines the source's place in the hierarchy of fatwa.
3
Assess the authority level
Classify each work according to the Hanafi hierarchy: Zahir al-Riwayah (core texts like al-Mabsut), Nawadir (rare transmissions), or Fatawa Mutakhkhirin (later authoritative opinions). For instance, 'al-Durr al-Mukhtar' is considered a relied-upon matn for fatwa.
Later works like 'Radd al-Muhtar' are authoritative but depend on the earlier matn.
4
Note cross-references and relationships
Many works are linked: 'Tanwir al-Absar' is commented upon by 'al-Durr al-Mukhtar', which in turn is the subject of 'Radd al-Muhtar'. Understanding these chains is crucial for tracing the derivation of a ruling.
The relationship between a matn and its commentaries shows the evolution of Hanafi law.
5
Recognize the scholarly apparatus
Modern editions often include study and verification (tahqiq) by contemporary scholars like Salah Abu al-Hajj. This adds historical context but does not change the authoritative weight of the original text.
Distinguish between the classical text and the modern editorial work.

This bibliography from Ibn Abidin's 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti gives you the key references that later Hanafi scholars rely on. Identify the Authoritative Sharh
Find Radd al-Muhtar by Ibn Abidin. This is the commentary (sharh) that explains and weighs different opinions, often providing the mufta bihi position.

A mufti must be familiar with the major works of the madhhab. This passage lists essential Hanafi sources from fiqh commentaries to hadith collections. Understanding their authority level helps you determine which opinion is mufta bihi.
