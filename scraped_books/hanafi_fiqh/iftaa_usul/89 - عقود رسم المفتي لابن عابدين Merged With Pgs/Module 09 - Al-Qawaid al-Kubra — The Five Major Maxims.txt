The Major Rule: No Takfir with Weak Evidence
Presentation of a major maxim on refraining from takfir with evidence from hadith and scholarly consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'لا يُفتى بكفر مسلم...' to assess whether a given statement or action warrants a fatwa of apostasy, including identifying the exception for daruriyyat al-din.

Core Idea
A Muslim is never declared a disbeliever if any charitable interpretation exists or if any scholarly disagreement (even weak) remains.

This lesson examines one of the most important maxims in Hanafi iftaa practice: the presumption of remaining Muslim when there is any doubt. The maxim protects the inviolability of a Muslim's life and faith (hurmah al-dam wa al-‘ird) and emphasizes that takfir is a grave matter. We will study the text of the maxim, its evidence from the Quran, Sunnah, and the practice of the early scholars, and its limit: the denial of matters known by necessity from religion (daruriyyat al-din).

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Maxim
Understand the wording and scope of the qawida as stated by Ibn Nujaym and Ibn Abidin: لا يُفتى بكفر مسلم أمكن حمل كلامه على محمل حسن أو كان في كفره اختلاف ولو رواية ضعيفة.
The verse at the beginning poetically endorses the principle.
2
Identify the Rationale
Recognize the gravity of takfir (apostasy) and the principle of giving benefit of doubt. Ibn Abidin says they deviated from giving fatwa by the sahih because apostasy is a great thing.
3
Examine the Evidence
Review the hadiths cited: the hadith about praying our prayer, facing our qibla, and eating our slaughter, and the hadith about fighting until people say la ilaha illallah. Also the statement of Imam al-Tahawi and the spread opinion from the imams.
These establish the default: a Muslim is defined by outward actions; his inner state is left to Allah.
4
Apply the Maxim to Cases
Work through the furuat provided: charitable interpretation, existence of ikhtilaf, weak narration. In each case, the ruling is to avoid takfir.
The presence of any of these factors is sufficient to prevent takfir.
5
Note the Exception
Identify when the maxim does not apply: denial of matters known by necessity from the religion (daruriyyat al-din) such as tawhid, prophethood, resurrection, obligation of prayer and zakat, prohibition of wine. For these, takfir is issued because the denial is clear kufr.
Al-Kashmiri defines darura as matters transmitted by tawatur and known to the common people.

63. Contracts of Drawing the Mufti by Ibn Abidin: Study and Investigation. A verse came negating disbelief from a Muslim even if weak, more appropriate. Its meaning: as Ibn Nujaym said in al-Bahr 5:135: 'What is established is that one is not given a fatwa of apostasy of a Muslim whose words can be carried to a good interpretation, or if there is disagreement concerning his apostasy, even if a weak narration.' And Ibn Abidin said in Sharh Rasm al-Mufti 1:50: 'What has passed that it is not for the mufti to act upon the weak and give fatwa by it is carried on other than a place of necessity... And it should be attached to necessity that he does not give a fatwa of apostasy of a Muslim concerning whose apostasy there is disagreement, even if a weak narration, for they have deviated from giving fatwa by the sahih because apostasy is a great thing. So this is a magnificent principle among the jurists; Muslims should not be heedless of it. Al-Tahawi said in al-Aqida al-Tahawiyya pp. 20-21: 'We name the people of our qibla Muslims, believers, as long as they acknowledge what the Prophet brought and believe in everything he said and informed... And we do not declare anyone from the people of the qibla a disbeliever for a sin, unless he considers it lawful.' And witness to that is his statement: 'Whoever prays our prayer, faces our qibla, and eats our slaughter, that is the Muslim who has the covenant of Allah and His Messenger, so do not betray Allah regarding His covenant' in Sahih al-Bukhari 1:153 and Sahih Muslim 3:1552, and his statement: 'I was commanded to fight people until they say la ilaha illallah, and if they say it, pray our prayer, face our qibla, and slaughter our slaughter, then their blood and wealth are forbidden to us except by right, and their account is with Allah' in Sahih al-Bukhari 1:153. And Muhammad Sajjad al-Hanafi said: 'It has spread from the imams that we do not declare anyone from the people of the qibla a disbeliever.' See Ikfar al-Mulhidin pp. 163-164. So one is not judged as a disbeliever except for denial of something known of the religion by necessity. And the meaning of 'necessity' as al-Kashmiri explained in Ikfar al-Mulhidin pp. 2-3: 'What is known to be from the religion of Muhammad by necessity, i.e., by tawatur and istifada, and the common people know it, such as oneness, prophethood, the resurrection and requital, the obligation of prayer, zakat, the prohibition of wine, and the like. It is called necessary because everyone knows that this matter is from the religion of the Prophet necessarily, so its being from the religion is necessary, and it enters into faith.'

Commentary
This passage presents a major qawida (legal maxim) in Hanafi jurisprudence: a Muslim is not declared a disbeliever (takfir) if there is any possibility of interpreting his words favorably or if there exists any disagreement (ikhtilaf) regarding his apostasy, even if that disagreement relies on a weak narration. The passage opens with a poetic line endorsing this principle. Ibn Nujaym from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and Ibn Abidin from his Sharh Rasm al-Mufti are quoted to establish the rule. Ibn Abidin adds that the prohibition against using weak narrations in fatwa is lifted in cases of necessity, and the avoidance of takfir is treated as a necessity—even a weak narration suffices to protect a Muslim from the grave charge of kufr. The passage then cites Imam al-Tahawi's creed stating that Muslims are not declared disbelievers due to sins unless they consider them lawful. Two hadiths from Bukhari and Muslim are adduced as evidence: one defines a Muslim by prayer, facing the qibla, and eating their slaughtered meat; the other commands fighting until people testify, pray, and face the qibla, after which their life and property are inviolable. The author concludes with the condition: takfir is only for denial of matters known by necessity from the religion (daruriyyat al-din). The definition of darura is given from al-Kashmiri: matters transmitted with tawatur and known to the common masses, such as tawhid, prophethood, resurrection, obligation of prayer and zakat, prohibition of wine, etc. This is a foundational rule for every mufti: when in doubt, err on the side of avoiding takfir.

Key Terms
أَحْرَى (ahra): More appropriate or worthy; used to emphasize the rule that even weak evidence is sufficient to avoid takfir.
اختلاف (ikhtilaf): Disagreement among mujtahids on whether a statement or act constitutes kufr.
الضرورة (al-darura): Matters known by necessity from the religion, transmitted via tawatur and known to the common people.

Key Takeaway
The inviolability of a Muslim's faith (hurmah al-din) requires muftis to exhaust all possible charitable interpretations and disregard weak evidence before ever issuing a fatwa of apostasy.
