Tenth Cause: Unclear Manuscript Writing
Rule on reliability of unclear manuscripts for fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a manuscript with unclear writing is not reliable for fatwa unless certainty of correct reading is achieved.

Core Idea
A fatwa cannot be based on a text whose correct reading is uncertain.

This lesson addresses a practical challenge for muftis: how to handle manuscripts with unclear handwriting or poor digitization. The author of Naseem al-Mufti identifies this as the tenth cause that renders a book unreliable for fatwa. The core principle is that a mufti must be certain of the text before relying on it. This rule safeguards the integrity of the fatwa process.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The manuscript's writing is unclear, making it difficult to read the correct text.
This can include poor handwriting, faded ink, or low-quality digitization.
2
Determine the default ruling
Such books are considered unreliable (ghayr mu'tabar) for issuing fatwas.
The default is that they cannot be used.
3
Identify the exception
When the mufti is certain (yaqin) of the correct reading, the book becomes permissible to use.
Certainty overcomes the default unreliability.
4
Apply to modern contexts
Digitization (e.g., PDFs) can worsen clarity, increasing the need for caution.
The rule remains: only when certainty is achieved.
5
Conclude the condition
The key condition for reliability is certainty of the text, not mere availability.
This protects the fatwa from error due to misreading.

Tenth cause: Unclear writing. Some manuscripts have writing that is not clear and clean, and reading the correct text is extremely difficult, especially when these manuscripts are converted to PDF files and uploaded on the internet, then their readability becomes even more difficult and complicated. I myself have many manuscripts in my phone whose writing is so unclear that reading even two pages is difficult. Therefore, such books are unreliable for issuing fatwas, but when certainty about the correct reading is achieved, then there is no harm in issuing fatwas.

Commentary
This passage is a rasm rule: a condition for the reliability of a written source for fatwa. It identifies the tenth cause that renders a book unreliable: unclear handwriting or printing. The author notes that manuscripts with illegible writing are common, and digitization (PDF) can worsen the problem. The legal principle is that a text is not considered reliable for fatwa unless the reader is certain of the correct reading. Once certainty is attained, it becomes permissible to rely on it. This rule preserves the integrity of the fatwa process by ensuring that the source text is accurately understood.

Key Terms
غیر معتبر (ghayr mu'tabar): Not reliable or authoritative as a source for issuing fatwa.
کتابت (kitabat): The writing or script of a manuscript.

Fifteenth Principle: Giving Fatwa According to Another Madhhab
Explains a rasm al-mufti rule with one exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a muqallid mufti may give fatwa according to another madhhab and apply the concept of darurah (necessity) to determine permissibility.

Core Idea
A muqallid mufti may depart from his madhhab only when necessary — severe hardship, not life-threatening.

This lesson introduces a crucial principle in Hanafi ifta: the restriction of a muqallid mufti to his own madhhab and the exceptional cases where he may turn to another madhhab. The passage explains the rationale behind the restriction and details the first exception: darurah as a lower form of hardship that permits this departure. Understanding this principle is essential for muftis when facing situations where strict adherence would cause unbearable difficulty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
Read the first part of the passage: it states the default rule that a muqallid mufti must give fatwa only by the rajih (preponderant) opinion of his own madhhab. Note the justification given: the mustafti comes expecting the ruling of his own imam.
2
Recognize the Exception
The passage then notes three exceptional cases. Focus on the first: darurah. Identify that this exception allows leaving the madhhab's opinion due to severe hardship.
3
Define Darurah in This Context
The passage specifies that darurah here is NOT classical idhtirar (life-threatening), but a lower level of hardship causing unbearable trouble. Underline that distinction.
4
Connect to the Maxim of Hardship
Relate this to the qawida 'al-mashaqqatu tajlibu al-taysir' (hardship begets ease). This maxim is the underlying legal basis for the exception.
5
Apply to a Scenario
Consider a situation where acting on the rajih opinion of the Hanafi madhhab would cause extreme financial hardship to a mustafti without endangering life. Would the fatwa be permissible by another madhhab? Apply the definition of darurah.

(محاضرات اصول فقہ : ۳۶۵، جدید فقہی مباحث ، معروف به مقالات اسعدی ۲۳۲/۳)
Fifteenth Principle: Giving Fatwa According to Another Madhhab. It is necessary for a muqallid mufti to give fatwa only by the preponderant opinion of his own madhhab. It is not permissible for him to give fatwa according to another imam's madhhab, leaving his own imam's madhhab. The reason is that the mustafti comes to this mufti so that he may know the ruling of his own imam in the relevant issue and act accordingly. Therefore, it is necessary for the mufti to give fatwa according to his own and the mustafti's madhhab. However, there are three cases where it is permissible to leave one's own imam's madhhab and act on the opinion of another imam in giving fatwa and judgment. These three cases are as follows: First case: Giving fatwa on the basis of necessity. The specific circumstances in which it is permissible to leave one's own imam's madhhab and give fatwa according to another madhhab is the case of necessity, extreme difficulty, hardship and constriction, and general affliction. That is, when in a particular issue of one's own madhhab there is such hardship that acting upon it results in unbearable trouble, then to remove this constriction and fulfill the need of the mustafti, it is permissible to give fatwa by a weak opinion. It should be clear that by 'necessity' here is not meant the well-known condition that jurists term 'idhtirar'; rather, it is a state lower than idhtirar, due to which a person falls into severe hardship, although there is no danger to life or limb.
Source pages p. 132

Commentary
This passage explains a key principle in Hanafi ifta: a muqallid mufti is generally bound to give fatwa according to the preponderant opinion of his own madhhab. The rationale is that the person seeking the fatwa (mustafti) comes expecting to learn the ruling of his own imam, so the mufti must respect that. However, there are three exceptional cases where departure from the madhhab is allowed. The first case detailed here is necessity ('darurah'), defined not as extreme life-threatening necessity (idhtirar) but as a lower level of hardship that causes unbearable difficulty. This allows adopting a weak opinion or even another madhhab's opinion to alleviate hardship. The text notes that the necessity here is less than the classical idhtirar; it includes situations of severe practical difficulty. This principle is used in contemporary fiqh to ease hardship for Muslims. The other two cases are mentioned but not elaborated in this passage.

Key Terms
ضرورت (darurah): A situation of severe hardship, less than life-threatening necessity (idhtirar), that permits departing from the preferred opinion.
مقلد مفتی (muqallid mufti): A mufti who follows a specific madhhab and is not a mujtahid, bound to the rajih opinion of his madhhab.
مستفتی (mustafti): The person who asks a mufti for a fatwa regarding a religious ruling.

Key Takeaway
