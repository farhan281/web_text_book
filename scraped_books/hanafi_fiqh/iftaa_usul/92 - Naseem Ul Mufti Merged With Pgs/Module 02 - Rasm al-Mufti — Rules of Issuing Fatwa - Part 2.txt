Fifth Principle: When the Three Imams Differ
Determines which opinion to follow when the three imams each have a different view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the fifth principle of Rasm al-Mufti to determine the mufta bihi opinion when the three Hanafi imams differ and no external tarjih exists.

Core Idea
When the three imams differ and no tarjih exists, the mufti must follow Imam Abu Hanifah.

This lesson presents the fifth foundational rule from Rasm al-Mufti, which guides the mufti when the three primary Hanafi imams hold three distinct opinions on a matter and no later scholar has deemed one preponderant. In such a situation, the mufti is not free to choose; he must adhere to the opinion of Imam Abu Hanifah unless a severe necessity compels otherwise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Confirm that each of the three imams (Abu Hanifah, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) has a separate, distinct saying on the same issue.
This is a true three-way ikhtilaf.
2
Check for tarjih by ashab al-tarjih
Determine if any later authority (e.g., classical muftis or scholars of tarjih) has clearly resolved which view is preponderant. If yes, follow that tarjih.
If no tarjih exists, proceed to next step.
3
Apply the default: follow Imam Abu Hanifah
In the absence of tarjih, the mufti must give fatwa according to the saying of Imam Abu Hanifah. This is necessary (lazim).
Deviation is impermissible without severe necessity.
4
Check for convergence with a Sahib
If one of the two companions (Abu Yusuf or Muhammad) agrees with Imam Abu Hanifah, then that saying is even more strongly preponderant.
This strengthens the case for following Abu Hanifah.
5
Note the exception: severe necessity
Only a severe necessity (idṭirār shadīd) can justify departing from Imam Abu Hanifah's view. Such necessity must be genuine and not mere convenience.
Examples of severe necessity are rare; consult detailed fiqh sources.

Sixth Principle: Preference Based on Changing Custom
Explains the custom-based tarjih rule for muftis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the tarjih rule that when Imam Abu Hanifa and his companions differ due to changing custom, the mufti adopts the later opinion.

Core Idea
When custom changes, the later companion's ruling becomes the operative fatwa over Imam Abu Hanifa's original view.

This lesson explains a key principle in Hanafi fatwa methodology: how to resolve apparent conflicts between Imam Abu Hanifa and his students. The rule acknowledges that some differences stem from evolving social conditions, not from textual disputes. When that happens, the mufti follows the later, custom-sensitive view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Spot a case where Imam Abu Hanifa holds one position and his companions (Sahibayn or others) hold another. This is the starting point for applying the principle.
The conflict must be real, not fabricated.
2
Determine the basis of difference
Investigate whether the difference is grounded in a variation of custom ('urf) and time (zaman) or in a pure textual disagreement.
If the difference is purely textual (e.g., different hadith interpretation), this principle does not apply.
3
Recognize the preference rule
If the basis is changing custom, then the companions' view is preponderant (rajih) because rulings change with changing customs.
This is explicitly stated by the source: 'then the saying of the two companions will be preponderant.'
4
Apply in fatwa practice
When issuing a fatwa on a contemporary issue, adopt the ruling that aligns with current custom, even if it is not Imam Abu Hanifa's original view.
Always verify that the change in custom is genuine and widespread.

This is grounded in the legal maxim that rulings change with changing customs. Thus, the mufti follows the view that is more suitable for the current context.

Seventh Principle: Intra-Madhhab Ikhtilaf on Non-Custom Matters
Rasm al-Mufti: Intra-madhhab ikhtilaf resolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi opinions on which view to follow when Imam Abu Hanifah and his companions disagree on a matter not based on custom, and apply the second opinion (mufti's choice) to a novel case.

Core Idea
When difference is not custom-based, some say always follow Abu Hanifah; others give the mufti choice.

This passage introduces the seventh principle of Hanafi fatwa methodology: how a mufti should choose when Imam Abu Hanifah and his companions (Sahibayn) differ on a ruling that is not tied to changing customs. Since the disagreement is purely juristic, not contextual, the Hanafi school itself has two internal positions on which view to prefer. Understanding this prepares you for navigating ikhtilaf within the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifah and his companions (Sahibayn) is not based on changing customs or times (causal factor = pure juristic difference).
Custom-based differences are covered by a different principle; this principle applies only when custom is not the basis.
2
Recall the first opinion
Abdullah ibn Mubarak held that in all such cases, the mufti must follow Imam Abu Hanifah absolutely and not deviate.
This is a strict taqlid position.
3
Recall the second opinion
Other scholars held that the mufti has absolute choice (mukhtar) to follow either Imam Abu Hanifah or the Sahibayn.
The passage is cut off but likely continues to affirm this discretion.
4
Understand the implication for fatwa
Under the second opinion, the mufti may choose based on other factors like ease or need, but he is not forced to always follow one side.
Later Hanafi practice leans towards granting the mufti choice.
5
Apply to a new case
When faced with a non-custom-based difference, first identify whether you follow the first or second opinion. If following the second, you may select the view you consider stronger or more suitable.
This flexibility is a hallmark of later Hanafi fatwa methodology.

When all three Hanafi imams differ and no tarjih is given, the mufti must follow Imam Abu Hanifa unless severe necessity demands otherwise.

This lesson presents the fifth principle from the text 'Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti', which governs the mufti's obligation in cases of internal Hanafi ikhtilaf. It is a foundational rule for ensuring consistency in fatwa when multiple opinions exist without clear preponderance. Muftis must know this rule to avoid arbitrary selection among the three founding authorities.

Identify the situation
Confirm that all three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) have distinct opinions on the matter. This is a necessary condition for the rule to apply.

Determine whether later scholars (ashab al-tarjih) have already declared one opinion preponderant. If they have, that opinion is followed instead of this default rule.
The rule applies only 'when the scholars of tarjih have not declared any opinion as preponderant'.
3
Apply the default: follow Imam Abu Hanifa
If no tarjih exists, the mufti must give fatwa according to Imam Abu Hanifa's qawl. This is obligatory (lazim).
The text uses 'lāzim' (necessary).
4
Consider the exception: severe necessity
Deviation from Imam Abu Hanifa's qawl is only permitted if there is a severe necessity (shadīḍ ḍarūrat). This must be a genuine exigency, not mere preference.
Examples: widespread hardship, inability to practice the default view, or urgent need to prevent greater harm.
5

If one of the Sahibayn agrees with Imam Abu Hanifa, his qawl remains preponderant. This reinforces the default.

Ninth Principle: Following the Later Mashayikh
Explains how to refer to later scholars when earlier sources are silent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ninth principle of Rasm al-Mufti to determine the mufta bihi ruling when the foundational Hanafi sources are silent.

Core Idea
When Imam Abu Hanifa and his companions are silent, the consensus of later mashayikh becomes binding.

In the Hanafi madhhab, there is a clear hierarchy of authority. When the founding imams have not explicitly addressed a matter, the school does not fall silent—it turns to the later authoritative jurists (mutakhireen). This lesson explains how to locate and apply their rulings, including what to do when the later scholars themselves differ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger condition
Check if the case has no transmitted ruling from Imam Abu Hanifa or any of his immediate companions (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar, etc.). This is the essential prerequisite for applying this principle.
2
Survey later mashayikh for consensus
Research the writings of later Hanafi scholars (mutakhireen). If they are unanimous on the ruling, that consensus (ittifaq) becomes binding and must be followed.
Consensus here means all known later authorities agree; it is not a conditional ijma but an operational default.
3
Handle disagreement among later scholars
If no consensus exists, identify the most prominent and widely recognized jurists among them—such as Imam al-Tahawi, Imam Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi, and Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi. Their view takes precedence over less renowned scholars.
The prominence is determined by scholarly recognition and acceptance in the Hanafi tradition, not by personal preference.
4
Issue the fatwa accordingly
Base the fatwa on the outcome: either the consensus or the view of the prominent authorities. This ensures consistency and reliability within the school.
This principle upholds the evolving nature of ijtihad within the madhhab while maintaining a clear chain of authority.

Principles for Preferring Zahir al-Riwayah: First Two Principles
First two of six principles for preferring opinions based on book authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the first two principles for preferring opinions based on book authority and apply them to determine which opinion a non-mujtahid mufti should follow.

Core Idea
Zahir al-Riwayah is binding; when conflict arises within it, the later work prevails.

Not all Hanafi books carry the same authority. Some were compiled with careful authentication, while others are later summaries. To ensure consistent fatwa, classical scholars laid down principles (rasm al-mufti) for preferring one transmitted opinion over another. This lesson covers the first two of six such principles, focusing on the primary source: the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah.

Guided Path

They are also known as usul al-madhhab.

If an issue appears in any of these six books without internal conflict, the ruling is wajib to follow. Deviation is not allowed unless later preferers (ashab al-tarjih) explicitly choose another view.
This holds even if later works (mutun, shuruh) suggest otherwise.

Six principles for preference based on books
Sometimes, the preference among differing opinions in Hanafi fiqh is based on the books in which those opinions are transmitted, because some books are well-known in terms of authenticity and their authors committed to writing the preponderant and correct opinion, while other books are not like that. In some books, along with transmitting issues, the proofs and methods of reasoning for those opinions are also discussed, and also the determination of the preponderant opinion among conflicting opinions and the reason for preference are mentioned, while some books are devoid of these characteristics. Therefore, when giving a fatwa, adherence to the following principles is necessary:
First principle: giving fatwa based on Zahir al-Riwayah is obligatory.
When an issue is mentioned in Zahir al-Riwayah, i.e., in Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's Mabsut, Jami' al-Saghir, Jami' al-Kabir, Ziyadat, Siyar al-Saghir, and Siyar al-Kabir, without any disagreement, then acting upon it and giving fatwa accordingly is necessary, whether the jurists have explicitly stated that opinion to be mufta bih or not, because these six books are well-known from Imam Muhammad and are transmitted with strong chains. So acting upon these books is obligatory and deviating from them is not permissible, unless the preferers deem another opinion to be preponderant, in which case their preference is to be considered. (Fatawa Qadi Khan: 1/9, Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: 43)
In case of disagreement in any issue of Hanafi fiqh, the issues of Zahir al-Riwayah take precedence over non-Zahir al-Riwayah, the matun, the shuruh, and the issue of the fatawa, and it is not permissible for the mufti to deviate from Zahir al-Riwayah. (Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: 126, 132)
Second principle: In Zahir al-Riwayah, the later work will be considered.
When different and conflicting opinions are narrated concerning an issue in Zahir al-Riwayah, i.e., in the aforementioned books of Imam Muhammad, then the mujtahid mufti will determine which opinion is preponderant based on the evidence, and that will be the preponderant opinion. If no preference is transmitted from the mujtahid mufti, then for the non-mujtahid mufti, the ruling is that he rely on the later composition. For example, in case of conflict between Mabsut and Jami' Kabir, the opinion of Jami' Kabir will be adopted, because Jami' Kabir was written after Mabsut. (Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: 76, al-Madhhab al-Hanafi: 1/261)

Commentary
This passage introduces the first two of six principles (rasm rules) for preferring one Hanafi opinion over another based on the authority of the books in which they are recorded. The introductory paragraph explains that not all books are equal: some are more authentic and their authors consciously recorded the preponderant (rajih) opinion, while others may lack such rigour. Therefore the mufti must follow certain rules. The first principle states that if an issue is found in Zahir al-Riwayah (the six foundational works of Imam Muhammad) without internal disagreement, it is obligatory (wajib) to give fatwa according to that opinion. The second principle addresses when there is disagreement within Zahir al-Riwayah itself (i.e., contradictory reports from Imam Muhammad). In such cases, if a mujtahid mufti (one capable of weighing evidence) can determine the preponderant view, his tarjih is followed. If not, then a general mufti (non-mujtahid) must rely on the later authored work among the six. The example given: Mabsut vs. Jami' Kabir — Jami' Kabir takes precedence because it was written later. This principle is based on the likelihood that later works incorporate earlier revisions and final positions of Imam Muhammad.

Fourth Principle: Priority of Reliable Matn Texts
Rasm al-Mufti rule on textual hierarchy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the fourth principle of Rasm al-Mufti to resolve conflicts between matun, shuruh, and fatawa, and identify which matn are considered reliable.

Core Idea
When matun and shuruh conflict, the reliable matn prevails – its compilers deliberately stated the preponderant view.

As a mufti, you will often encounter varying rulings across different Hanafi texts. The fourth principle of Rasm al-Mufti provides a clear hierarchy: the concise, authoritative matn texts take precedence over commentaries (shuruh) and fatwa collections. This rule safeguards the integrity of the madhhab by prioritizing texts that were carefully crafted to reflect the strongest positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sources involved
When a conflict appears, list which matn, sharh, and fatwa collections are cited. Note the exact titles and authors.
e.g., matn: al-Quduri; sharh: al-Lubab; fatwa: al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya
2
Verify the reliability of the matn
Check whether the matn is among the mu'tabir (reliable) matn recognized by the later Hanafi masters. Only these qualify for priority.
Reliable matn include al-Mukhtar, Kanz al-Daqa'iq, al-Quduri, al-Wiqaya, etc.
3
Compare the rulings
Determine the exact ruling in each source for the same issue. Ensure the conflict is genuine and not due to different contexts.
If the matn is silent, the sharh or fatwa may fill the gap without conflict.
4
Apply the priority rule
If there is a direct conflict and the matn is reliable, the matn's ruling is the mufta bihi (operative position).
Exception: if later tarjih (preference) by major scholars explicitly overrides the matn, that alternative is followed.
5
Conclude and document
State the final ruling, citing the matn as primary evidence, and note the other sources for completeness.
This ensures transparency and adherence to the madhhab's hierarchy.

First Principle of Tarjih by Evidence: The Sahih Hadith Prevails
Rasm rule for tarjih - first principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the first principle of tarjih by evidence to determine which opinion is preponderant when one is based on a famous sahih hadith and the other on a weak narration or no hadith.

Core Idea
A fatwa must be based on a sahih hadith; if an opinion relies on a weak narration, it cannot be preferred.

The Hanafi school often has multiple opinions on a single issue. To choose the correct one for fatwa, scholars established principles of tarjih (preference) based on the strength of evidence. The first and most fundamental principle is that an opinion supported by a sahih and famous hadith takes precedence over an opinion based on a weak narration or no hadith at all. This lesson will explain this rule and how to apply it.

Declare the opinion with the sahih hadith as preponderant (rajih). It is not permissible to give fatwa according to the weak opinion. This is the ruling for the mufti.

Third principle: Istihsan takes precedence over qiyas

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides qiyas, but scholars differ: absolute override (Taftazani) or mere superiority (Bazdawi).

This lesson examines a core rule of Hanafi fatwa methodology: when istihsan and qiyas conflict, the position based on istihsan is generally preferred. However, later Hanafi authorities disagreed on whether this preference completely invalidates qiyas or simply makes it less preferred. Understanding this nuance is crucial for a mufti in weighing evidence.

Explicit Tarjih Overrides Implicit Tarjih

Core Idea
Explicit tarjih overrides implicit tarjih when they conflict.

A mufti often encounters multiple opinions in Hanafi texts. To determine which opinion is relied upon, he must know how to recognize indications of preference. Core Idea
An author's consistent style reveals their preferred opinion: notice what comes first or which proof comes last.

In Hanafi usul al-ifta, when authors do not explicitly state their preferred opinion, they often follow a consistent style throughout their work. By learning to recognize these styles, you can infer which opinion they consider rajih. This lesson introduces the first two styles: presenting the rajih opinion first, and presenting its proof after the weak opinion's proof.

Guided Path

Understand the concept of uslub

First uslub: opinion order. Second uslub: proof order. Mixing them leads to wrong conclusions.
Always verify which element (opinion or proof) is being ordered.

Five Authorial Styles for Identifying the Preferred Opinion
Lists five authorial methods for identifying the preferred opinion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five authorial styles used in Hanafi texts to indicate the preferred opinion (raajih/mufta bihi) and apply the correct interpretive approach when reading a Hanafi work.

Core Idea
In Hanafi texts, the order and presentation of conflicting opinions reveal which is relied upon, but each author has a unique style.

When you open a Hanafi fiqh book, you often find multiple conflicting opinions on the same issue. How do you know which one to take for fatwa? Over centuries, Hanafi scholars developed distinct authorial styles—or 'signatures'—to signal the preferred opinion. This lesson unpacks those five styles, giving you the interpretive key to any Hanafi reference.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the problem of conflicting opinions
Understand that Hanafi fiqh often contains multiple (muta'arid aqwal) on a single issue. A mufti must determine which opinion is the relied-upon (raajih/mufta bihi) for issuing fatwa.
2
Identify the five authorial styles
Memorize the five methods: (1) mention strong first, (2) delay evidence of strong, (3) only mention evidence of strong, (4) refute evidence of weak, (5) when three opinions, first or third is strong unless author places strong in middle.
Each style is associated with specific classical texts: Qadi Khan & Halabi use style 1; Marghinani & Sarakhsi use style 2; etc.
3
Apply style to a given text
When reading a Hanafi work, first ascertain the author's preferred style (often stated in the introduction or known from his other works). Then interpret the order and evidence accordingly.
Not all authors use the same style; assuming a uniform rule leads to error.
4
Understand the fifth style nuance
When exactly three opinions exist, the default is that the middle is weak, but the author's habitual practice can override this. Check the author's methodology.
This style is clarified by 'Allama Sayfi and 'Allama Shami.
5
Bridge to verbal clues
The passage then introduces that besides these five styles, there are ten principles based on verbal cues (alfaz al-tarjih) that also indicate preference. This lesson focuses on the five styles; verbal clues are covered separately.

Commentary
This passage presents five distinct methodological styles used by classical Hanafi jurists to indicate which of several conflicting opinions is the relied-upon (راجح) and fatwa-worthy (مفتی بہ) position. The Fourth and Fifth Styles of Identifying the Rajih Qawl
Explains two authorial methods for indicating the preferred opinion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the fourth and fifth authorial styles to identify the rajih qawl when encountering conflicting statements in a Hanafi fiqh text.

Core Idea
An author's silence on a proof indicates acceptance; the placement of opinions reveals preference.

In the Hanafi tradition, authors often indicate the preferred opinion (rajih) through subtle cues rather than explicit labels. This lesson introduces you to two such authorial styles: the fourth style, which uses proof citation and refutation to signal the non-preferred opinion, and the fifth style, which relies on the order of listing three conflicting opinions. Mastering these styles will enable you to navigate primary texts like al-Hidayah and al-Mustasfa with greater precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify conflicting opinions
Locate the muta‘āriḍ aqwāl in the text. Determine how many opinions are presented and which ones are being compared.
If only two opinions, the fourth style may apply; if three, consider the fifth style.
2
Apply the fourth style
Check if the author provides a proof for one opinion and then refutes it, while remaining silent on the proof of the other opinion. The opinion with unmentioned proof is the rajih.
The author's silence on the proof indicates acceptance of that opinion.
3
Apply the fifth style
If three opinions are listed, in principle the first or third opinion is rajih, unless the author consistently places the rajih in the middle according to his habit.
The middle opinion is presumed marjuh unless an exception is known.
4
Consider authorial habit
If you know the author's consistent practice (e.g., always placing the rajih in the middle), that overrides the general rule of the fifth style. Research the author's methodology when in doubt.
This highlights the importance of studying each author's approach.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the first principle of preference in Rasm al-Mufti by correctly identifying which opinion is rajih when one is marked with an ijma-indicating phrase and the other with a 'fatwa' word.

Core Idea
Words implying ijma override 'fatwa' in preference — consensus is stronger than individual opinion.

In the Hanafi tradition, muftis often have to choose between conflicting reports. The classical jurists developed a hierarchy of preference words. This lesson covers the first principle: when one opinion is described with terms like 'upon this the ummah acts' and another merely as 'fatwa', the former takes precedence because it signals consensus.

First Principle: When sahih is by consensus words.
When different words of tarjīḥ are used for the correction of two conflicting narrations, for example, one statement is preferred with the word 'fatwa' etc., and the other statement is preferred with 'upon which the Ummah has acted' or 'upon which the scholars and Muslims have transmitted' or similar words, then the second statement is preponderant, because these words contain the meaning of consensus. (Sharh 'Uqūd Rasm al-Muftī with amendment: 142)

Second Principle: When a statement is corrected with the word 'fatwa'.
When there is a disagreement among the people of tarjīḥ regarding the correction and preference of an issue, and on one side there is the word 'fatwa' and on the other side there are other words of tarjīḥ, then the statement that has been preferred with 'fatwa' and its derivatives (e.g., 'bihi yufatā', 'alayhi al-fatwā', 'al-fatwā 'alayh') is preponderant, because a statement being muftā bihi is a sign of its correctness and reliability in the madhhab. (Sharh 'Uqūd Rasm al-Muftī: 143)

Third Principle: When both statements are preferred with the word 'fatwa'.
When in a matter of disagreement, the word 'fatwa' is used for the correction of both statements, then if both sides use identical words, e.g., both sides have 'bihi yufatā', then the mufti may give fatwa on whichever he wishes. But if the words on both sides differ, then the side where 'fatwa' is mentioned with restriction is preponderant. For example, one side has 'bihi yufatā' or 'alayhi al-fatwā', and the other side has 'al-fatwā 'alayh', then the first statement is preponderant, because these words indicate that the fatwa is only confined to this statement, and it is not permissible to give fatwa on the other statement. (Sharh 'Uqūd Rasm al-Muftī: 142)

Fourth Principle: When the level of the correcting scholars is the same.
When there is a disagreement among the people of tarjīḥ regarding the correction and preference of an issue, and both statements have been corrected with identical words, e.g., both sides have 'huwa al-sahih' or 'huwa al-asahh', etc., and the scholarly status of the correcting jurists is also equal, then the mufti has choice in giving fatwa; he may give fatwa on whichever of the two statements he wishes. Commentary
This passage is from a work on the art of issuing fatwas (Adab al-Mufti). It presents six principles (usul) for a mufti to determine the rājiḥ (preponderant) opinion when there are conflicting statements corrected with different terms of tarjīḥ. The author begins by outlining the general scenario: if one word is corrected with 'fatwa' and another with 'sahih', the one with 'fatwa' is more likely to be rājiḥ because 'fatwa' inherently indicates its use in practice, but this is not always absolute. Therefore, detailed principles follow.

**First Principle:** When two statements are corrected using different tarjīḥ terms—one with 'fatwa' and the other with expressions like 'upon which the Ummah has acted' or 'upon which the scholars have transmitted' (which indicate consensus), the latter takes precedence because consensus is a stronger evidence.

**Second Principle:** When the dispute is between 'fatwa' and other tarjīḥ words (like 'sahih' or 'asahh'), the statement preferred by 'fatwa' or its derivatives is rājiḥ, as 'fatwa' signals its practical adoption in the madhhab.

**Fourth Principle:** If both statements are corrected with the same words (e.g., both 'sahih' or both 'asahh') and the scholars are of equal rank, the mufti has the choice.

**Fifth Principle:** If the scholars differ in rank, the correction of the higher-ranked scholar is preferred, e.g., Qādī Khān over the author of al-Bazzāziyyah, and Sarakhsī over Muslimī.

**Sixth Principle:** When one statement is 'sahih' and the other 'asahh', with different scholars and books, there is disagreement among later jurists; the text does not resolve this, implying it requires further investigation.

These principles guide the mufti in navigating conflicting tarjīḥ in Hanafi law, ensuring consistency and authority in fatwa issuance.

Fourth Principle: Equal Authenticators – Mufti’s Discretion
Explains the rule when two authenticating scholars are equal in rank

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fourth principle of tarjih when two authenticators are equal in rank and apply it to determine the mufti's discretion in a given fatwa scenario.

Core Idea
Equal authenticators with identical wording give the mufti full choice between opinions.

In the Hanafi tradition, not every difference of opinion is resolved by weighing evidence; sometimes the authorities themselves are evenly matched. This principle equips the mufti with a clear rule for such cases, preserving flexibility while maintaining procedural discipline. When two scholars authenticate different opinions using the same phrase (e.g., 'al-sahih') and are of equal scholarly rank, the mufti may freely choose either opinion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting opinions
Determine the two opinions that have been authenticated by different scholars. Note the subject matter and the exact ruling each supports.
Ensure that the disagreement is on the same legal question.
2
Verify the authenticators' rank
Confirm that both authenticating scholars are of equal scholarly rank. This may involve checking their era, reputation, and recognition within the Hanafi school.
If one is clearly more authoritative, a different rule applies.
3
Check the authentication wording
Examine whether both scholars used identical phrases for authentication, such as 'huwa al-sahih' or 'huwa al-asahh'. If the wording differs, the principle may not apply.
Identical wording ensures the same level of endorsement.
4
Apply the rule of discretion
Since the conditions are met (equal rank, identical wording), the mufti has discretion (ikhtiyar) to choose either opinion for issuing the fatwa.
No external preference is forced; the mufti uses ijtihad.
5
Issue the fatwa
The mufti selects one opinion and issues the fatwa accordingly, documenting the choice if necessary.
The fatwa should be based on sound reasoning and suitability for the questioner.

Fifth Principle: The Higher-Ranked Authenticator Prevails
A rule for resolving authentication conflicts based on scholar rank.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the fifth principle of rasm al-mufti to resolve conflicts when two scholars authenticate opposite rulings with identical wording but differ in rank.

Core Idea
When wording is equal, the higher-ranked jurist's authentication takes precedence.

This lesson introduces the fifth principle in the Hanafi mufti's methodology. It deals with a specific scenario where two jurists have authenticated opposite rulings using the same phrase. The principle then looks beyond the wording to the rank of the jurists, giving priority to the one with higher standing. This ensures that fatwa practice follows the most authoritative source.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conflict
Recognize that both opinions are authenticated with identical expressions (e.g., 'wa bihi na'khudh', 'asahh'). This triggers the need to compare scholar ranks.
The principle only applies when the wording is the same; if wording differs, other rules apply.
2
Compare the Ranks
Determine which scholar is higher in the Hanafi hierarchy. This is based on recognized ranking: earlier scholars (e.g., Qadi Khan, al-Sarkhasi) are generally more authoritative than later ones (e.g., al-Bazzaziyya, al-Muslimi).
Rank may also be based on scholarly depth, breadth of works, and mastery.
3
Apply the Rule
Give precedence to the authentication of the higher-ranked scholar. This is the mufta bihi position.
The lower-ranked scholar's authentication is not rejected but is overridden in this specific conflict.
4
Learn the Examples
Internalize the examples: Qadi Khan over the author of al-Bazzaziyya; Shams al-A'imma al-Sarkhasi over Imam Abu 'Ali al-Muslimi.
These examples are from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti.
5
Internalize the Principle
This is a fixed rule in fatwa methodology. As a mufti, you must be aware of the relative ranks of major Hanafi jurists and consistently apply this principle.
Ignorance of rank can lead to unreliable fatwa decisions.

Sixth Principle: Sahih vs Asahh by Different Scholars
Discusses which saying prevails when one is called sahih and another asahh by different scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that when two conflicting opinions are authenticated as 'sahih' and 'asahh' by different scholars in different books, the mufta bihi is the one designated as 'asahh'.

Core Idea
When sahih and asahh conflict from different sources, asahh takes precedence — its opposite may be invalid.

In the Hanafi tradition, scholars often use the terms 'sahih' (correct) and 'asahh' (more correct) to authenticate opinions. But what happens when two differing opinions are labeled with these terms by different authorities in separate books? This sixth principle resolves that conflict, guiding the mufti on which opinion to prefer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting opinions and their authentication terms
Recognize that one opinion is described as 'sahih' by one scholar in one book, and the other as 'asahh' by another scholar in a different book.
The scholars and books are distinct.
2
Recognize the two scholarly views
Note the internal disagreement: some hold 'sahih' to be preponderant because it implies the opposite is invalid (fasid); others hold 'asahh' preponderant because its opposite may be invalid.
Both views are recorded in the madhhab.
3
Understand the reasoning behind each view
For 'sahih' view: 'sahih' suggests the opposite is fasid, while 'asahh' suggests the opposite is still correct. For 'asahh' view: 'asahh' indicates a higher degree of correctness, and its opposite could be fasid.
The linguistic nuance of comparative 'af'ala' form is key.
4
Apply the mufta bihi rule
The well-known and relied-upon opinion is that 'asahh' is preponderant. Therefore, the mufti should give fatwa based on the opinion authenticated with 'asahh'.
This is the settled position in later Hanafi works.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that when a single scholar labels one opinion 'sahih' and another 'asahh', the 'asahh' opinion is preferred, and identify the exception when a third opinion exists.

Core Idea
Within a single scholar's work, 'asahh' trumps 'sahih' unanimously.

This lesson introduces a key principle in Hanafi tarjih (preferring legal opinions). When a jurist uses both the terms 'sahih' and 'asahh' for two conflicting opinions, there is unanimous agreement that the opinion labeled 'asahh' is to be preferred. This helps muftis resolve contradictions within a single scholar's writings.

Guided Path

Identify the two conflicting sayings

2
Check which saying is labeled 'asahh'
If one saying is labeled 'asahh' and the other 'sahih', the 'asahh' is preferred because the scholar himself indicated it is more correct.
This is bil-ittifaq (unanimous).
3
Apply the preference
Give precedence to the 'asahh' saying when issuing fatwas or choosing between the two.
If a third saying exists, then the same ikhtilaf from the sixth principle applies, but the well-known view still gives preference to 'asahh'.

Eighth Principle: Preference when a Later Musannif Declares One Corrected Saying as Asahh
Presents a rule for fatwa hierarchy when a later author declares one correction as more correct.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that when a later Hanafi author (musannif) declares one of two corrected sayings as 'asahh', that saying becomes the mufta bihi in fatwa, overriding earlier designations.

Core Idea
A later musannif's declaration of 'asahh' overrides earlier corrections and is followed by consensus.

In Hanafi fatwa methodology, multiple corrections may exist for conflicting sayings. This eighth principle clarifies the hierarchy: when a later authoritative author (musannif) explicitly marks one correction as 'asahh' (more correct), that designation takes precedence regardless of the earlier labels. This rule empowers later scholars to exercise tarjih and settle disputes for the community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting sayings
Begin by noting the pair of sayings (qawlayn) from the earlier imam that are in conflict. Each has been subject to a correction (tas'hih) by a previous verifier (muhaqqiq).
These corrections are labeled with either 'sahih' or 'asahh'.
2
Recognize the earlier corrections
Determine which saying was corrected as 'sahih' and which as 'asahh'. This establishes the prior stage of tarjih.
The earlier faqih may have preferred one over the other.
3
Locate the later musannif's intervention
Find the passage where a later author (e.g., al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah) mentions this issue and declares one of the two corrections as 'asahh' relative to the other.
The musannif's statement overrides the previous labels.
4
Apply the consensus rule
By agreement (bil-ittifaq), the saying whose correction the musannif designates as 'asahh' becomes the preponderant (rajih) view for fatwa.
It does not matter if that saying was originally 'sahih' or 'asahh' in the earlier correction.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Conclude that the mufti must adopt the saying identified by the later musannif as 'asahh'. This becomes the operative ruling for issuing fatwas.
This principle affirms the authority of later scholars in the madhhab.

The Ninth Principle: Preference via Ism Tafdil
Rule on resolving conflicting sayings using ism tafdil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ninth principle of Rasm al-Mufti to identify which of two conflicting sayings is preferred when one is validated with an ism tafdil term and the other is not.

Core Idea
Ism tafdil (e.g., ashbah) in tarjih signals stronger preference—act on that saying.

When a mufti faces two conflicting sayings, the method of validation can indicate which one is stronger. The ninth principle from Naseem Ul Mufti teaches that if one saying is explicitly described with a comparative/superlative term like 'ashbah' (most resembling) or 'arfaq' (most convenient), and the other is not, the saying with that term takes precedence. This principle helps resolve ta'arud (conflict) efficiently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting sayings
In the fatwa case, determine the two sayings (aqwal) that are in apparent conflict.
2
Check each saying for ism tafdil validation
Look for whether the correction (tas'hih) of one saying uses a term on the pattern of ism tafdil, such as ashbah, arfaq, aliyaq, aslah li-zamanihi, etc.
3
Apply the preference rule
If only one saying has such a term, that saying is preferred (arjah) and acting upon it is necessary (wajib). If both or neither have ism tafdil, this principle does not apply and other tarjih rules must be used.
4
Issue the fatwa accordingly
The mufti should base the fatwa on the preferred saying, stating its reasoning.

Introduction to 14 principles for harmonizing conflicting preferences, with two sub-rules given.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the first two sub-principles of the third principle of rasm al-mufti to determine which correction prevails when conflicting corrections use terms like 'sahih', 'asahh', and 'fatwa'.

Core Idea
When corrections conflict, 'asahh' outranks 'sahih'; 'fatwa' outranks all other terms.

In Hanafi fatwa methodology, different scholars often correct conflicting rulings. The mufti cannot simply follow any correction; he must prioritize them using established principles. The third principle of rasm al-mufti provides 14 sub-principles. This lesson focuses on the first two: the preference of 'asahh' over 'sahih', and the preference of 'fatwa' over other corrective terms (like 'sahih', 'asahh', or 'ahwat'). Mastering these rules is essential for a mufti to issue reliable fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting corrections
Read the two conflicting sayings and note the correction term attached to each. Look for phrases like 'huwa al-sahih', 'huwa al-asahh', 'huwa al-ahwat', or 'al-fatwa ala hadha'.
Correction terms may be explicit or implied.
2
Apply the first sub-principle
If one correction is marked 'asahh' and the other 'sahih', the saying marked 'asahh' takes precedence. 'Asahh' means 'more correct' and is given priority over 'sahih' (correct).
This is the well-known position according to the source.
3
Apply the second sub-principle
If one correction is marked with the word 'fatwa' (or its derivatives) and the other with any other term like 'sahih', 'asahh', or 'ahwat', the saying marked 'fatwa' prevails. 'Fatwa' indicates the operative opinion that should be issued.
These two sub-principles together form the basis for resolving many conflicts.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
Based on the above, decide which correction is to be followed in fatwa. This becomes the mufta bihi (relied-upon opinion) for that issue.
If both principles apply (e.g., one saying is 'asahh' and the other is 'fatwa'), the fatwa principle overrides the first. The second sub-principle explicitly covers 'asahh' under 'other terms'.

Third principle: Fourteen (14) principles for harmonizing the preferences of the mashayikh. Most of the disputed issues in Hanafi fiqh are such that there is disagreement among the very people of tarjih regarding their correction and preference, and for this reason you will see countless issues in which the fuqaha have different sayings and each saying has been corrected. Thus, one faqih has declared one saying as sahih, while another faqih has declared the opposite saying as asahh; one faqih has used the word sahih with one saying, while another faqih has used the word fatwa with another saying. Hence, in such situations, the mufti needs to employ extreme deliberation and meticulousness, and when giving fatwa, adherence to the following principles will be necessary. It is clear that except for a few principles, the explanation of all the following principles has already been given in detail under various titles earlier; therefore, they will be stated here briefly.

First principle: Asahh will prevail over sahih. When among two conflicting corrected sayings, the correction of one saying uses 'huwa al-sahih' and the correction of the other saying uses 'huwa al-asahh', then according to the well-known saying, the saying with asahh will have preference.

Second principle: The saying with fatwa will prevail over sahih. When among two conflicting corrected sayings, for the correction of one saying, 'fatwa' and its derived words are used, and for the correction of the other saying, other words of correction such as 'huwa al-sahih', 'huwa al-asahh', 'huwa al-ahwat' are used, then the saying with fatwa will have preference.

Six Principles for Preference Among Conflicting Hanafi Rulings
Enumeration of the first six rules for preferring one tarjih over another in Hanafi fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the six principles of preference (tarjih) when facing conflicting corrected sayings in Hanafi fatwa, identifying which designation takes precedence.

Core Idea
When Hanafi rulings conflict, follow this order: asahh > sahih, fatwa > sahih, Imam Abu Hanifa > Sahibayn, Zahir al-Riwayah > others, mutun > shuruh, major jurists > minor.

A mufti often encounters conflicting sayings that have been corrected by different later scholars. How does he decide which to follow? This lesson presents the first six of fourteen principles that form a clear hierarchy. Memorizing these rules is essential for issuing sound fatwas in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Classify the designations
Examine whether one saying is declared 'asahh' (more correct) or 'fatwa' (operative) while the other is only 'sahih'. According to the first two principles, 'asahh' and 'fatwa' override mere 'sahih'.
These two principles are the first line of tarjih.
2
Check the authority level
If designations are equal, determine whether the saying originates from Imam Abu Hanifa or his companions (Sahibayn). The third principle gives preference to Imam Abu Hanifa's view. Also check if the saying is from Zahir al-Riwayah or not; the fourth principle prefers Zahir al-Riwayah.
The chain of authority is considered.
3
Evaluate the textual source
If authority is equal, check whether the saying is found in the mutun (concise legal texts like Al-Quduri) or in commentaries/shuruh. The fifth principle states that the matn rulings are preferred because their authors committed to recording the soundest opinion.
This rule highlights the reliability of matn works.
4
Consider the consensus of major jurists
If all previous factors are equal, the sixth principle gives weight to the view that has the agreement of major renowned Hanafi jurists (e.g., al-Tahawi, al-Sarkhasi, Abu al-Layth) over the view of lesser-known scholars.
The collective authority of the 'akabir' (seniors) establishes precedence.

Commentary
This passage lists the first six of fourteen principles (usul) for reconciling conflicting preferences (tarjih) among Hanafi scholars. These rules guide the mufti when facing contradictory corrected sayings. First principle: When one saying is declared 'sahih' and another 'asahh', the 'asahh' (more correct) takes precedence. Second principle: When one saying is accompanied by 'fatwa' and the other by 'sahih' or similar, the 'fatwa' saying is followed. Third principle: If the sayings are of Imam Abu Hanifa and his disciples (including Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), and all other factors equal, Imam Abu Hanifa's view is preferred. Fourth principle: Sayings from Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Muhammad al-Shaybani) override those from non-Zahir al-Riwayah. Fifth principle: The ruling in the 'mutun' (concise legal texts like Al-Quduri) is preferred over that in commentaries (shuruh) and fatwa collections. Sixth principle: The consensus of major Hanafi jurists (e.g., al-Tahawi, al-Sarkhasi, Abu al-Layth) overrides the view of lesser scholars. These principles are referenced from 'Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' (p. 145) and summarized here.

Key Terms
اصح (asahh): A designation indicating a saying is 'more correct' than one merely labeled 'sahih'.
ظاہر الروایہ (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani: al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Ziyadat.
متون (mutun): Concise legal texts such as Mukhtasar al-Quduri, al-Hidayah, Kanz al-Daqaiq, etc.
