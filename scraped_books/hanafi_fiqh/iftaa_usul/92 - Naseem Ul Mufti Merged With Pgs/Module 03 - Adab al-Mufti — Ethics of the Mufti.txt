Ten Causes for Unreliability of a Book
Enumerating ten reasons why a book is considered unreliable for fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the ten causes that render a book unreliable for issuing fatwas in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
A book's authority depends on its author, content, and scholarly acceptance, not its age.

A mufti must rely on authoritative sources. This lesson presents ten concrete criteria, derived from Hanafi usul, to judge whether a book is reliable for fatwa. Memorizing these causes equips you to critically evaluate any text you encounter in your studies or practice.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the ten causes
Learn the ten causes in order: 1) Unknown author, 2) Weak sayings, 3) Overly concise wording, 4) Declared weak by a muhaqqiq, 5) Author declared weak by a muhaqqiq, 6) Doubtful attribution, 7) Non-fiqh discipline, 8) Rarity, 9) Printing errors, 10) Unclear handwriting. Use mnemonics if helpful.
The order is not hierarchical; all are equally important.
2
Understand the rationale
For each cause, ask: why does this undermine the book's authority? For example, an unknown author cannot be verified in his piety and knowledge; weak sayings contradict the mufti's duty to rely on strong evidence.
Connecting each cause to a broader usul principle will deepen retention.
3
Apply to hypothetical scenarios
Given a description of a book, identify which causes of unreliability are present. This bridges memorization to practical evaluation.
Focus on distinguishing between 'weak' content and 'unknown' author — both are separate causes.

The Mufti's Foundation: Scholarly Authorities and the Duty of Caution
Lists early Hanafi scholars and admonishes mufti's caution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the core early Hanafi authorities whose statements a mufti must rely on, and explain the ethical imperative to avoid boldness in issuing fatwas.

Core Idea
A mufti's authority rests on transmitted statements of early Hanafi masters, and he must act with fear of Allah.

This passage from a classical usul al-iftaa text reminds the aspiring mufti that his opinions are not his own—they are anchored in the school's towering figures. It also delivers a sharp warning: issuing fatwas with audacity is a sign of wretchedness, while fearing the divine reckoning is the hallmark of the pious. Understanding these scholars and this admonition is essential for any mufti's training.

Guided Path
1
Identify the listed scholars
Read the list of names: Hafs al-Kabir, Ja'far Bulayt, Tahawi, Khassaf, Karhi, Halwani, Sarakhsi. Recognize that these are major early Hanafi jurists whose statements carry authoritative weight in the school.
Hafs al-Kabir was a direct student of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani; Sarakhsi authored al-Mabsut, a foundational text.
2
Understand their role in fatwa
The passage implies that a mufti must search for the rulings of these scholars. Their statements form the basis of the mufta bihi (relied-upon) positions in the Hanafi school.
If a mufti does not find a statement from these authorities, he faces difficulty and must proceed with caution.
3
Analyze the poetic admonition
The couplet contrasts boldness in fatwa (work of a wretched person) with fear of questioning (conduct of the pious). Reflect on the ethical requirement for the mufti: taqwa (fear of Allah) and humility.
The phrase 'shaki' (wretched) indicates severe criticism of those who issue fatwas without proper care.
4
Apply to the fatwa process
Combine the two parts: the mufti must rely on the transmitted school positions and must approach every fatwa with fear of Allah. This leads to a methodical, cautious methodology.
The thamarah (practical result) is that a mufti should never give a ruling based on personal opinion alone.
5
Memorize for future reference
Commit the list of scholars and the couplet to memory. These are frequently cited in Hanafi fatwa literature as the primary authorities.
Knowing these names helps in navigating the school's hierarchy of sources.

The Limits of Tarjih Rules and the Mufti's Role
Note on the limits of general rules and the mufti's role

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when tarjih factors conflict, explain why no universal rule governs such conflicts, and describe how a mufti employs dhawq sahih and malakah fiqhiyyah to resolve them.

Core Idea
Tarjih rules are not absolute; when they conflict, the mufti's trained judgment, not a fixed hierarchy, determines the preferred opinion.

Permissibility of Weak Opinion in Necessity (First Scenario)
Explains the first scenario allowing weak opinion for the distressed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition of necessity (idhtirar) that permits a mufti to give fatwa on a weak (marjuh) opinion and apply it to a concrete Hanafi fiqh case.

Core Idea
A weak opinion may be used for fatwa when the questioner is in dire necessity and the strong opinion causes extreme hardship.

This lesson introduces a key rasm rule: the mufti is normally bound to give fatwa according to the preponderant (rajih) opinion. However, when a person is in a state of necessity (mudtar) and the strong opinion imposes extreme difficulty, the mufti may resort to a weak opinion. This permission is an exception grounded in the principle of ease (taysir) in the Hanafi school. The lesson unpacks the condition, the supporting evidence, and a classic example from ghusl rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the general rasm rule
Identify the default rule: the mufti must only give fatwa on the preponderant (rajih) opinion. The weak (marjuh) opinion is normally not permitted.
This is the baseline rule established by scholars like Qasim ibn Qutlubugha.
2
Identify the exception
Recognize the first scenario: when a person is compelled (mudtar) such that acting on the strong opinion is extremely difficult, and the weak opinion provides ease, then acting on and giving fatwa on the weak opinion is allowed.
The exception applies both to the layperson and the mufti.
3
Analyze the condition of necessity
What constitutes necessity? It is a subjective hardship, not mere inconvenience. The individual must find the strong opinion genuinely burdensome.
The standard is relative to the person's circumstances.
4
Apply to the ghusl example
Work through the intra-Hanafi dispute: the preponderant opinion (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) holds that ghusl becomes wajib when semen exits with desire, regardless of desire at the moment of exit. Abu Yusuf's weak opinion requires desire at exit. The guest with a wet dream fears accusation if he performs ghusl; necessity allows him to follow Abu Yusuf's opinion to avoid hardship.
The example illustrates the practical application of the rasm rule.
5
Note the supporting authorities
Later Hanafi authorities like Ibn Nujaym and 'Allamah Shami confirm the exception. Ibn Nujaym cites Fakhr al-A'imma that giving fatwa on weak opinions for ease in necessity is good. 'Allamah Shami clarifies that the general prohibition applies only outside necessity.
These statements strengthen the rule's validity.

Commentary
This passage states a rasm rule (methodological rule for issuing fatwas) regarding the permissibility of giving fatwa according to a weak opinion in cases of dire need. The author first reiterates the general principle: a mufti must follow the preponderant (rajih) opinion and cannot give fatwa on a weak (marjuh) opinion. Then he introduces the exception: the weak opinion may be used when a person is compelled (mudtar) and acting on the rajih opinion imposes extreme hardship. This exception applies both to the layperson acting and to the mufti issuing the fatwa. The text provides a concrete example from Hanafi fiqh: the preponderant opinion (of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) holds that ghusl becomes wajib when semen exits with desire, regardless of desire at the moment of exit. Abu Yusuf's weak opinion requires desire at exit. A guest who has a wet dream and fears accusation if he performs ghusl may follow Abu Yusuf's opinion to avoid suspicion. The validity of this exception is further supported by statements from Ibn Nujaym and 'Allamah Shami, confirming that necessity permits deviation from the strong opinion. The passage also clarifies that the general prohibition of using weak opinions applies only in non-necessary contexts.

مضطر (mudtar): A person in a state of dire necessity where acting on the strong opinion causes extreme hardship.

Caution in Rulings of Kufr (Second Exception)
Rule: Caution in takfir allows following weak opinion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the rule of caution in takfir and apply it to determine whether a weak opinion can be followed in issuing a fatwa of kufr.

Core Idea
In rulings of kufr, caution overrides the preponderant opinion, allowing adoption of a weak view to avoid takfir.

This passage from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti presents a crucial exception to the standard rule that a mufti must base his fatwa on the preponderant (rajih) opinion. When the issue involves potential takfir—declaring someone a disbeliever—the mufti must exercise extreme caution (ihtiyat). This caution may permit adopting a weak (marjuh) opinion that avoids kufr, even if the stronger opinion would deem the statement as kufr. Understanding this principle is essential for any mufti or student of ifta'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the passage addresses situations where a ruling of kufr (takfir) is being considered.
Takfir has severe consequences, hence the special treatment.
2
Recall the general rule for fatwa
Normally, a mufti must give fatwa based on the preponderant (rajih) opinion. State this rule.
General rule: follow the strongest evidence.
3
Recognize the exceptional principle
The passage introduces ihtiyat (caution) as an overriding principle in matters of kufr. Understand that this allows deviation from the general rule.
Exception justified by the gravity of takfir.
4
Understand the rationale
The rationale is the extreme delicacy of declaring someone a disbeliever—it has worldly and eternal consequences. Caution demands avoiding takfir unless absolutely certain.
Harm in false takfir outweighs benefit of correct takfir.
5
Apply the rule
When faced with a statement that could be kufr according to the rajih but not according to a marjuh, the mufti may adopt the marjuh to avoid takfir. This is the practice of ihtiyat.
The mufti should exhaust all possible interpretations that avoid kufr.

Mufti Acting on Weak Opinion: The Role of Expertise (Dhi Ra'y)
Rules for mufti acting on weak opinion if he is an expert

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a Hanafi mufti may act upon a weak opinion and distinguish between an expert mufti (dhi ra'y) and a common person in this regard.

Core Idea
A mufti with insight (dhi ra'y) may follow a weak opinion if evidence supports it; a common person may not.

In the Hanafi tradition, muftis are not mere transmitters of rulings; they often need to weigh evidence. This lesson examines when a mufti may deviate from the preferred opinion of his madhhab and act upon a weaker one. The key is his level of expertise: only those who possess 'dhi ra'y' – the ability to evaluate and prefer evidence – can do so. This ensures the integrity of the fatwa process.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mufti's level of expertise
Determine whether the mufti is a pure transmitter (riwaya) or possesses insight (diraya). The passage distinguishes between a common mufti and a dhi ra'y mufti.
Dhi ra'y is not a full mujtahid but can perform ijtihad within the madhhab (tajazzi al-ijtihad).
2
Understand the concept of weak opinion (qawl da'if)
Recognize that within the Hanafi madhhab, opinions are ranked by evidence strength. A weak opinion has less authoritative evidence but may be supported by stronger reasoning in a specific context.
The mufti must be able to assess this evidence.
3
Apply the condition of tajazzi al-ijtihad
The mufti with partial ijtihad may weigh evidence and prefer one opinion over another. This act of preference is his own ijtihad, which he is bound to follow.
This is based on the principle that a mujtahid must act according to his own ijtihad.
4
Contrast with the common person (ammi)
A common person lacks the expertise to evaluate evidence. Therefore, they may not arbitrarily follow a weak opinion. They must follow the mufti or the standard ruling.
The conditioning of permissibility with dhi ra'y implies prohibition for common people.
