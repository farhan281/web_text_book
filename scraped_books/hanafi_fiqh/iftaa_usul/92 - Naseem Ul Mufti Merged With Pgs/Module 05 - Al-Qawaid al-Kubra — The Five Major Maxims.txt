Dabit: Prefer Imam Abu Hanifa in Ibadat
Established rule: in ibadat, Abu Hanifa's qawl is absolute unless a contrary riwaya from him is acted upon by others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the dabit of preferring Imam Abu Hanifa's qawl in ibadat, identifying when his opinion is absolute and when the exception (authentic riwaya taken by others) applies.

Core Idea
In ibadat, Abu Hanifa's view is default unless a contrary riwaya from him was acted upon by others.

This lesson introduces a crucial sub-maxim (dabit) in Hanafi usul al-iftaa: the precedence of Imam Abu Hanifa's opinion in all acts of worship. However, this precedence is not unconditional; it is subject to an exception when an authentic narration from the Imam himself is followed by the Sahibayn or other authorities. Understanding this dabit is essential for any mufti navigating ikhtilaf in ibadat.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its scope
Identify the dabit: 'في كل أبواب العبادات ربح قول الإمام مطلقا' – In all chapters of worship, the saying of the Imam is given precedence absolutely.
The scope is exclusively 'ibadat (worship), not mu'amalat.
2
Identify the exception condition
Read the condition: 'ما لم تصح رواية بها الغير أخذ' – as long as there is no authentic narration from him which others have taken. If such a riwaya exists, it overrides the default precedence.
The 'others' typically refers to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) or later authorities.
3
Apply the maxim to the given furu'
Consider the example: tayammum for a person who throws dates into water (impuring it). Apply the dabit: follow Imam Abu Hanifa's qawl unless a contrary riwaya from him is authentically reported and acted upon by others.
Check the madhhab's recorded positions on this specific case.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
Decide the operative ruling: follow the Imam's well-known qawl unless the exception applies. The mufti must verify the existence and authenticity of the conflicting riwaya.
This dabit itself is a tarjih mechanism that resolves internal ikhtilaf.
5
Explain the rationale behind the dabit
Understand why ibadat receive this special rule: worship is based on divine command (tawqif), and the Imam's deep knowledge and caution in these matters warrant deference. The exception prevents blind following when the Imam himself provided an alternative.
Related maxim: 'العبادات مبنية على التوقيف' – worship is founded on divine prescription.

The Authority of 'Urf in Fatwa
Stating that custom is legally recognized unless it contradicts a clear text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on 'urf, identify its condition, and apply it to determine when custom can be used as a basis for fatwa.

Core Idea
Custom is a valid basis for fatwa unless it contradicts a clear, proven text.

In Hanafi fiqh, custom ('urf) is a recognized source for deriving rulings, especially in matters of mu'amalat. The mufti frequently encounters cases where local practice must be considered. This lesson presents the foundational maxim governing the authority of 'urf and its crucial limitation.

Guided Path
1
Extract the maxim
Read the couplet and identify the two directives: act upon transmitted practices, and the general principle that custom is recognized in Shariah.
2
Understand the condition
Note the phrase 'as long as it does not contradict a clear proven text'. This is the essential limitation that restricts the otherwise broad scope of custom.
The condition is expressed in the Arabic: 'مالم يخالف لصريح ثبتا'.
3
Recognize the scope
The maxim applies broadly across all chapters of fiqh, especially mu'amalat (contracts, endowments, transactions). In ibadat, custom is less determinative but still considered where texts are silent.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given a hypothetical custom, test it against the condition. If there is a clear, proven text opposing it, the custom is invalid. Otherwise, it is valid and can be used.
Example: A custom of delayed payment in a sale is valid unless it involves riba prohibited by clear text.

The Hierarchy of Textual Indications: 'Ibarat, Ishaarah, Dalaalah, Iqtidaa
Hierarchy of textual indications and madhhab comparison

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim of textual strength hierarchy to resolve conflicts between indications and contrast the Hanafi fourfold classification with the Shafi'i twofold division.

Core Idea
عبارۃ النص أقوی من اشارۃ النص، و اشارۃ النص أقوی من دلالۃ النص، و دلالۃ النص أقوی من اقتضاء النص

In Hanafi usul, a single text can convey meaning in multiple ways: explicitly, by allusion, by necessary implication, or by implication of ellipsis. These are not equal in strength. This lesson introduces the hierarchical maxim that governs preference when these indications conflict, and clarifies that all four are forms of mantooq—direct textual import—in contrast to the Shafi'i division into mantooq and mafhoom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types of textual indication
From the passage, name the four types: 'ibarat al-nass (explicit statement), ishaarat al-nass (allusion), dalaalat al-nass (implication by necessary consequence), and iqtidaa al-nass (implication by ellipsis).
All four are subcategories of mantooq in Hanafi terminology.
2
Learn the hierarchy of strength
The passage states: 'ibarat > ishaarah > dalaalah > iqtidaa. Memorise the order.
This is a qawida (maxim) used in tarjih (preference) when two indications conflict.
3
Understand the definitive nature (qat'i)
Rulings derived from any of these four are definitive (qat'i) unless contradicted by explicit evidence. This means they are binding in istinbaat.
The passage emphasises this point.
4
Contrast with Shafi'i classification
Shafi'is divide textual indication into mantooq (uttered meaning) and mafhoom (implied meaning). Hanafis place all four under mantooq, which is broader.
The practical effect is that Hanafis are more willing to derive rulings from iqtidaa and dalaalah as direct proof.
5
Apply the maxim to a conflict
Given a hypothetical conflict between an explicit text and an allusive text, apply the maxim: the explicit (ibarat) prevails.
This step prepares you for furu' applications.

Custom as the Interpretive Key for Verbal Acts
Statements on custom in interpretation and mufti's choice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that verbal acts (waqf, will, oath, vow, contract) are interpreted according to the speaker's custom, and identify when a mufti should adopt the easier opinion based on custom.

Core Idea
Custom governs the meaning of words in legal acts, not literal Arabic.

This lesson introduces the first and most practical application of the maxim 'al-'aadah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative). The great Hanafi jurist Qasim bin Qutlubugha teaches us that when a person makes a waqf, will, oath, vow, or contract, his words are to be interpreted according to his own customary usage, even if that meaning differs from the literal Arabic or the technical legal definition. This principle is essential for fatwa because words are the vehicle of legal obligations, and their meaning must be understood from the context of the speaker's community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five verbal acts mentioned
List waqf, will, oath, vow, and contract. Note that all involve speech that creates legal effects.
2
State the principle of interpretation
The words of the actor are interpreted according to his or her own custom (urf) in speech, not according to classical Arabic or technical fiqh definitions.
This is the core of the qawida.
3
Apply to a concrete example
Imagine a person says 'I give this house as sadaqah' but in his custom sadaqah means temporary use, not permanent transfer. The ruling follows his custom.
4
Understand the mufti's choice when scholars differ
When transmitted opinions conflict without preference, the mufti should consider the custom of the time and adopt the opinion that is lenient, customary, and has strong proof.
This shows custom's role in tarjih.
5
Recognize the limitation
Custom that contradicts Shariah (like riba) is not valid. The principle only applies to customs that are compliant with Islamic law.
Refer to the istithna in the qawida panel.

The Maxim al-'Adah Muhakkamah: Custom as an Overriding Principle
Establishes custom as a governing principle over literal meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim al-'Adah Muhakkamah, identify its scope and conditions, and apply it to cases where custom overrides literal meanings in fatwa.

Core Idea
Custom is an authoritative independent principle that overrides literal meanings in legal interpretation.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym establishes the foundational maxim that custom ('urf) holds authoritative weight in Hanafi fiqh. As a mufti, you must recognize that customary usage can supersede the literal meaning of words when issuing fatwas. This maxim is one of the five major qawaid al-kubra in the Hanafi school and is especially relevant in mu'amalat (transactions).

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its authority
The maxim 'al-'Adah Muhakkamah' means custom is authoritative. Ibn Nujaym declares it an independent usul principle, not merely a subsidiary consideration.
This is a qawida kubra, one of the five major maxims of Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the scope of application
The maxim applies broadly, especially in areas where words are used in contracts, oaths, and other legal instruments. Customary meanings override literal dictionary definitions.
The scope includes all fiqh chapters, but it is most frequently applied in mu'amalat.
3
Recognize the istithna' (exception)
Custom cannot override an explicit nass from the Quran or Sunnah. If there is a clear textual command, that takes precedence over custom.
The exception is captured by the companion maxim 'النصوص مقدمات على العادات' (texts take priority over customs).
4
Apply to a case in fatwa
When issuing a fatwa, the mufti must first identify the literal meaning, then investigate if a consistent and accepted custom differs. If so, the customary meaning governs, provided it does not contradict a clear text.
Custom must be continuous, prevalent, and not contrary to sound legal principles.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling establishing the authority of custom (urf) in Hanafi fatwa. Ibn Nujaym states that custom is not merely a subsidiary consideration but an independent usul principle. Consequently, when a word has a literal meaning but custom has assigned it a different meaning, the customary meaning takes precedence. For example, the word 'salah' literally means 'du'a' but in Islamic law it refers to the ritual prayer due to custom. The qawida 'al-'Adah Muhakkamah (custom is authoritative) is one of the five major maxims of fiqh. This passage lays down the rule that a mufti must consider customary usage when interpreting texts.

The Legal Status of Specific Custom (Urf Khass) in Hanafi Fatwa
Discussion on the validity of specific custom in specifying textual rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that specific custom ('urf khass) establishes only a specific ruling, not a general ruling, and distinguish it from general custom ('urf amm) in fatwa practice.

Core Idea
Specific custom binds only its own people; general custom binds all.

In Hanafi fatwa, custom ('urf) plays a vital role, especially when it contradicts a textual ruling. However, not all customs carry the same weight. This lesson focuses on the rule for specific custom ('urf khass) – a practice limited to a region or community. The passage clarifies that while such custom cannot override a general text to create a new general rule, it can specify that text for its own people. This nuance is critical for muftis dealing with local practices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its counterpart
State the two maxims: 'Specific custom does not establish a general ruling' (العرف الخاص لا يثبت به الحكم العام) and the implied sub-maxim 'Specific custom establishes a specific ruling' (العرف الخاص يثبت به الحكم الخاص).
The passage explicitly states both.
2
Understand the distinction between 'urf amm and 'urf khass
Contrast the two: 'urf amm is universal and can establish a general ruling and specify a text; 'urf khass is local and can only specify a text for its own people.
Ibn Abidin's quote is the key.
3
Trace the ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school
Note that the formal madhhab (Zahir al-Riwayah) does not consider 'urf khass, but many later mashayikh (including Ibn Abidin and al-Hamawi) permit its use for takhsis. This is not a contradiction but a development in fatwa practice.
The issue is about scope: general vs specific ruling.
4
Apply to the furuat (derived cases)
Consider the two examples in the passage: the Balkh cloth practice (rejected because not universal) and istisna' (accepted because universal). For a local custom, apply the sub-maxim.
See the qawida panel for details.
5
Formulate a fatwa using 'urf khass
Given a real scenario: a mufti in a locality must check if the custom is widespread there. If yes, he can issue a fatwa specific to that locality, citing the custom as a basis for specification.
Ensure not to extend the ruling to other regions.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion on the status of specific custom (urf khass) in Hanafi fatwa. The text begins by addressing a concern: if we accept the practice (ta'amul) of a specific region as authoritative, we might have to abandon a hadith. The author clarifies that abandonment of hadith is never permissible, only specification (takhsis) is allowed. The scholars of Balkh attempted to specify a hadith based on a local practice, but later Hanafi scholars rejected this specification because the practice was not universal. This establishes a key rule: specification by custom is only accepted when the custom is widespread (urf amm), not when it is limited to one region. The text then cites Imam Ibn Nujaym, who states that the formal madhhab does not consider urf khass, but many mashayikh gave fatwa considering it. This apparent contradiction is resolved by explaining that urf khass can be used for takhsis (specification) of a text, but not to establish a general ruling. In other words, urf khass can narrow the application of a general text to the people of that custom, but it cannot create a new general rule. The passage then quotes Ibn Abidin, who explicitly distinguishes between urf amm (establishes a general ruling for all regions) and urf khass (establishes a specific ruling only for that locality). This distinction is crucial for the mufti: when issuing a fatwa, the mufti must consider the custom of the people. If the custom is general, the ruling applies generally; if it is specific, the ruling applies only to that community. The passage also mentions that al-Hamawi inferred from the statement 'a general ruling is not established by specific custom' the converse: a specific ruling is established by specific custom. This passage thus provides a practical rule for muftis dealing with local customs.

Key Terms
عرف خاص (urf khass): A custom practiced in a specific region or by a specific community, not universally.
عرف عام (urf amm): A custom practiced universally across all regions and communities.
تخصیص (takhsis): The specification or narrowing of a general ruling to apply only to certain cases or people.
