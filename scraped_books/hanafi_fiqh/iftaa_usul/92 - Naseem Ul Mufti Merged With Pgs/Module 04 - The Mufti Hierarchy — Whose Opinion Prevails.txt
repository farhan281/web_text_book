Core Idea
A mufti must know his level; issuing fatwa beyond one's authority is invalid.

In Hanafi legal tradition, not every jurist is qualified to issue fatwas. The Ottoman scholar Ibn Kamal Pasha (d. 940 AH) classified Hanafi jurists into seven hierarchical levels. Understanding these levels helps you know who can derive rulings, who can weigh opinions, and who must simply follow. A mufti must at least belong to the fifth level (ashab al-tarjih) to give fatwa.

3
3. Identify the mufti's minimum level
Determine which class is the minimum for issuing fatwa. According to Hanafi usul, a mufti must be at least ashab al-tarjih (level 5).
This is the operative point for fatwa practice.

Three tiers of rulings exist; you must always prioritize the well-known transmitted rulings (Zahir al-Riwayah).

In the Hanafi school, not all legal rulings carry equal authority. The later scholars classified the vast corpus into three distinct tiers. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for any mufti: it tells you which ruling to rely upon when multiple narrations exist. This lesson anchors that classification.

Fiqh Books Take Precedence over Usul Books
Rasm rule: fiqh books prioritized over usul al-fiqh books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that fiqh books take precedence over usul al-fiqh books when resolving a conflict in a fatwa.

Core Idea
When a ruling in usul books conflicts with fiqh books, the fiqh book's ruling prevails.

A mufti must know which sources to prioritize. This lesson presents the sixth principle of the Hanafi fatwa hierarchy: fiqh books (kutub al-fiqh) are given precedence over usul al-fiqh books. The reasoning is that usul books mention fiqh rulings only as examples to illustrate principles, while fiqh books discuss rulings directly and in depth. Therefore, in case of conflict, the fiqh book's version is more reliable for issuing a fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sources involved
Determine which texts are being compared: a fiqh book (e.g., al-Hidayah) and an usul book (e.g., Usul al-Pazdawi).
2
Recognize the nature of the conflict
Ensure the conflict is about a specific fiqh ruling, not about a methodological principle.
Usul books may state a ruling as an example; fiqh books state it as the primary subject.
3
Apply the precedence rule
Decide that the fiqh book's ruling is to be followed, because it discusses the issue primarily and independently.
The hadith: 'Fiqh books are primary; usul books are incidental.'

Preference Based on Strong Hadith
Rasm rule: prefer saying based on famous hadith over weak or no hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rasm al-mufti principle that a saying based on a sound famous hadith takes precedence over one based on weak or no hadith when resolving conflicting positions within the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A mufti must prefer a qawl supported by a sound famous hadith over weak or unsupported sayings.

In the Hanafi school, conflicting sayings (aqwal) are resolved through principles of preference (tarjih). One of the most fundamental rules is that the strength of the underlying hadith determines which opinion carries weight. This lesson introduces the first principle: a saying rooted in a sound and widely known hadith is preponderant over one based on a weak tradition or no hadith at all. The mufti is bound to follow the stronger evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting sayings
List the two opposing Hanafi positions on the issue at hand. Recognize that there is an ikhtilaf within the school.
2
Examine the evidence for each saying
Determine the basis of each qawl: is it supported by a hadith? If so, what is its level of authenticity (sahih, hasan, weak)? Is the hadith famous (mashhur)?
The text emphasizes that the hadith must be both sound (sahih) and famous (mashhur).
3
Apply the first principle
If one saying is based on a sound famous hadith and the other is based on a weak tradition or no hadith, the former is preponderant (rajih) and must be given precedence.
Giving fatwa based on the weak tradition is not permissible when a stronger alternative exists.
4
Issue the fatwa accordingly
The mufti should adopt the preponderant saying as the mufta bihi and issue his fatwa based on it.
If there are more than two sayings, the principle still holds: the strongest supported opinion wins.

ساتواں طبقہ ان مقلدین فقہاء کا ہے جو فقہاء کے مذکورہ بالا وظائف میں سے کسی بھی وظیفہ پر قدرت نہیں رکھتے ہیں، ان میں نہ تو ادلہ اربعہ سے براہ راست مسئلہ مستنبط کرنے کی صلاحیت ہوتی ہے اور نہ ہی اپنے امام کے اصول کی روشنی میں جزئیات مستنبط کر سکتے ہیں، نہ تو نئے مسائل میں اجتہادی صلاحیت ہوتی ہے اور نہ ہی اصول کی روشنی میں کسی مسئلہ کی تخریج کر سکتے ہیں، مختلف اقوال میں ترجیح تو دور کی بات ہے ان اقوال میں سے صحیح قول کی تمیز پر بھی قدرت نہیں رکھتے ہیں، کھوٹے اور کھرے( کارآمد اور نکھے ) کے درمیان فرق نہیں کر سکتے ہیں، دائیں اور بائیں کے درمیان تمیز نہیں کر سکتے ہیں، بلکہ رات میں لکڑیاں چننے والے شخص کی طرح یہ لوگ جو کچھ پاتے ہیں اسے جمع کر لیتے ہیں۔
Fifth level: The People of Tarjih (Preference). The fifth level are those muqallids who do not have ijtihad ability but have the ability to prefer one statement over another on the basis of evidence, such as Imam Abu al-Husayn Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Quduri, Imam Ali ibn Abi Bakr al-Farghani al-Marghinani, and jurists of similar stature. Their style of authorship is that they prefer one narration over another using phrases like 'this is better', 'this is more correct in narration', 'this is clearer', 'this is more in line with qiyas', 'this is more beneficial for the people', etc.

Sixth level: The People of Tamyiz (Distinction). The sixth level are those muqallid jurists who have the ability to distinguish among the different and conflicting statements of the madhhab between the stronger, the strong, the weak, the Zahir al-Riwayah and non-Zahir al-Riwayah, such as the authors of reliable texts (i.e., the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq Imam Abu al-Barakat Abdullah ibn Ahmad al-Nasafi, the author of al-Mukhtar li al-Fatwa 'ala Madhhab Abi Hanifah al-Nu'man Imam Abdullah ibn Mahmud al-Mawsili, the author of Wiqayat al-Riwayah fi Masa'il al-Hidayah Taj al-Shari'ah Mahmud ibn Ahmad ibn Ubayd Allah al-Hamawi [al-Hamawi?], the author of Majma' al-Bahrayn Muzaffar al-Din Ahmad ibn Ali known as Ibn al-Sa'ati, and others like them). Their style of authorship is that they mention only the preferred (rajih) and fatwa-worthy statements in their books, and where it is necessary to narrate weak statements, they mention them along with clarifying their weakness and being outweighed.

Seventh level: The Pure Muqallid. The seventh level are those muqallid jurists who do not have ability for any of the aforementioned functions: they cannot directly derive a ruling from the four sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas), nor can they derive subsidiary rulings in light of their Imam's principles; they have no ijtihad ability in new issues, nor can they perform takhrij of any issue in light of principles; far from preferring among differing statements, they cannot even distinguish the correct statement; they cannot differentiate between bad and good (useful and useless), cannot distinguish right from left; rather, like a person gathering sticks at night, they collect whatever they find.
Source pages p. 82

Commentary
This passage presents the classification of muqallid jurists into three levels, as explained in Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. The author describes the hierarchy based on the jurist's ability to engage with the transmitted rulings of the Hanafi school. The fifth level, Ashab al-Tarjih (People of Preference), are those who cannot perform independent ijtihad but can weigh conflicting narrations using evidentiary criteria. They evaluate which narration is more authentic, more consistent with qiyas, or more practical for people. Examples include al-Quduri and al-Marghinani. The sixth level, Ashab al-Tamyiz (People of Distinction), can differentiate between narrations that are strong, weak, or from Zahir al-Riwayah vs. non-Zahir al-Riwayah. They typically compile books that present only the relied-upon (mufta bih) views, clarifying any weak narrations they include. The seventh level, Muqallid Mahd (Pure Muqallid), lacks all such capacities; they merely collect and transmit rulings without discernment, likened to someone gathering sticks in the dark.

Key Terms
اصحاب الترجیح (Ashab al-Tarjih): Muqallid jurists who have the ability to prefer one narration over another based on evidence, though they cannot perform ijtihad.
اصحاب التمیز (Ashab al-Tamyiz): Muqallid jurists who can distinguish between different categories of narrations (e.g., Zahir al-Riwayah vs. non-Zahir, strong vs. weak).
مقلد محض (Muqallid Mahd): A pure follower who has no ability to derive, prefer, or even distinguish among rulings; they simply follow what they receive.

The Third Stratum of Fatwa: Fatawa and Real Incidents
Describes the third stratum of fatwa rulings and lists its scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the third stratum of Hanafi fatwa sources and explain why rulings of later scholars remain within the madhhab despite occasional opposition to earlier authorities.

Core Idea
Derivations by later mujtahids are part of the madhhab even when they dissent from earlier texts.

In the Hanafi school, fatwa authority is stratified. The third stratum consists of rulings derived by later mujtahids who, when faced with new questions, performed ijtihad rather than relying solely on the books of Imam Abu Hanifa. This lesson introduces that stratum and its key scholars.

Guided Path
1
Define the Third Stratum
Understand that this stratum includes rulings (fatawa) and real incidents (waqiat) derived by later jurists when they did not find explicit answers in the foundational texts.
The stratum is referred to as 'طبقہ فتاوی اور واقعات' in the source.
2
Identify the Scholars' Lineage
Recognize that the jurists of this stratum are students (direct or indirect) of Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn).
3
List Notable Names
Memorize the listed scholars: 'Isam ibn Yusuf, Ibrahim ibn Rustam al-Marwazi, Muhammad ibn Sama'a Abu Sulayman al-Juzajani, Abu Hafs al-Bukhari (students of Sahibayn); Muhammad ibn Salama, Muhammad ibn Muqatil, Nasir ibn Yahya, Abu al-Nasr Qasim ibn Sallam (students of students).
These names appear in tabaqat works for further study.
4
Analyze the Authority of Their Rulings
Note that these scholars sometimes oppose earlier narrations based on stronger evidence, yet their statements are still considered part of the madhhab.
This illustrates the dynamic ijtihad within the Hanafi school.

مسائل کے تیسرے طبقہ پر فتاوی، واقعات اور تخریجات مشائخ کا اطلاق ہوتا ہے، اس طبقہ میں ان مسائل کو شامل کیا جاتا ہے جن کا استنباط مجہتدین متاخرین نے اس وقت کیا جب ان سے ان مسائل کی بابت سوال کیا گیا اور انہوں نے ان مسائل کا جواب اپنے امام کی کتابوں میں نہیں پایا تو بذات خود اجتہاد کر کے ان کا جواب دیا، ان مسائل کو مستنبط کرنے والے صاحبین کے بالواسطہ یا بلا واسطہ شاگرد ہوتے ہیں، ان کی تعداد بہت زیادہ ہے اور ان کے تفصیلی احوال جاننے کے لئے ہمارے اصحاب کی طبقات اور تاریخ کی کتابیں ہیں۔ صاحبین کے شاگردوں میں عصام بن یوسف، ابراہیم بن رستم المروزی، محمد بن سماعه ابوسليمان الجوز جانی اور ابو حفص البخاری کے اسماء قابل ذکر ہیں اور صاحبین کے شاگردوں کے شاگردوں میں محمد بن سلمہ محمد بن مقاتل بنصیر بن یحیی اور ابوالنصر قاسم بن سلام کے اسماء تذکرہ کے قابل ہیں، یہ حضرات بسا اوقات دلائل میں غور و فکر کی بنیاد پر اصحاب مذہب کی مخالفت بھی کرتے ہیں اور ان کے اقوال مذہب سے خارج نہیں کہلاتے ہیں۔
Stratum: Fatwas and Incidents. The third stratum relates to fatwas, incidents, and the derivations of the mashayikh. This stratum includes those rulings deduced by the later mujtahids when they were asked about them and did not find answers in the books of their Imam, so they themselves performed ijtihad and answered. Those who deduced these rulings are the students of the two Sahibs [Abu Yusuf and Muhammad] directly or indirectly. Their number is many, and for details of their states, there are books of tabaqat and history of our associates. Among the students of the two Sahibs, the names of 'Isam ibn Yusuf, Ibrahim ibn Rustam al-Marwazi, Muhammad ibn Sama'a Abu Sulayman al-Juzajani, and Abu Hafs al-Bukhari are notable. And among the students of the students of the two Sahibs, the names of Muhammad ibn Salama, Muhammad ibn Muqatil, Nasir ibn Yahya, and Abu al-Nasr Qasim ibn Sallam are worth mentioning. These people sometimes, based on reflection on evidences, oppose the people of the madhhab, and their statements are not considered outside the madhhab.

Commentary
This passage defines the third stratum of rulings in the Hanafi madhhab. It consists of fatwas and real incidents derived by later mujtahids (mutakhireen) who were asked about new issues. When they could not find explicit rulings in the books of Imam Abu Hanifa, they performed ijtihad and gave answers. These mujtahids are students (direct or indirect) of Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The passage then lists notable scholars from this stratum: 'Isam ibn Yusuf, Ibrahim ibn Rustam al-Marwazi, Muhammad ibn Sama'a Abu Sulayman al-Juzajani, and Abu Hafs al-Bukhari (students of Sahibayn); and Muhammad ibn Salama, Muhammad ibn Muqatil, Nasir ibn Yahya, and Abu al-Nasr Qasim ibn Sallam (students of students). Importantly, these scholars sometimes oppose the narrations of the earlier madhhab authorities based on stronger evidence, yet their opinions remain within the madhhab. This is because the Hanafi madhhab allows for ijtihad within the school, as long as one follows the usul of the school.

Key Terms
طبقہ (tabaqa): Stratum or level of scholarly authority in the Hanafi madhhab, referring to a generation of jurists.
تخریجات مشائخ (takhrijat al-mashayikh): Derivations or legal inferences made by later shuyukh, not found in the foundational texts.
