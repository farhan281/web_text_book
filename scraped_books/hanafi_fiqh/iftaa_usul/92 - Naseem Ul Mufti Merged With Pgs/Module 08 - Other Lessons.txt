Definition of Ibara al-Nass with Example
Defining the term 'Ibara al-Nass' with Quranic example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Ibara al-Nass and apply it by identifying the direct, intended meaning from a Quranic text without requiring inference.

Core Idea
Ibara al-Nass: the text's direct, intended meaning understood without reflection.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding how a text conveys its rulings is foundational. One of the key types of textual indication is 'Ibara al-Nass' – the explicit meaning directly intended by the text. This lesson breaks down its definition using the classic Quranic verse on trade and usury, showing how both primary and secondary intended meanings are included.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core components of the definition
Read the definition carefully. It has three conditions: (1) the meaning is understood from the text, (2) without need for reflection or inference, and (3) that meaning is actually intended by the text (not incidental).
2
Understand the Quranic example
Look at the verse 'Allah has permitted trade and forbidden usury'. The text directly gives two meanings: the distinction between trade and usury, and the ruling (halal/haram). Both are understood immediately.
3
Distinguish primary and secondary intended meaning
The author notes that the first meaning (trade and usury are distinct) is the primary intent (to rebut the polytheists), while the second (halal/haram) is also intended but secondary. Both count as ibara.
This shows that ibara covers all intended meanings, not just the most prominent.
4
Contrast with other types of dalaala
Consider how ibara differs from ishara (implied meaning) or iqtida (implied necessity). Here, the meaning is explicit and does not require additional reasoning.
This step is for deeper understanding but not explicitly in the passage.

Definition of Ibara al-Nass with example: The text's indication to a meaning that is understood from the text without reflection and that same meaning is intended by the text is called Ibara al-Nass, whether that meaning is intended primarily or secondarily. For example, Allah's saying: 'Allah has permitted trade and forbidden usury' (Surah al-Baqarah: 275). From this verse, two meanings are understood as ibara: first, that trade and usury are two separate things, not one, as the polytheists thought, and this is the primary intended meaning because the verse was revealed to refute those who considered them the same; second, it is understood that trade is halal and usury is haram, and this meaning is also intended by the verse.

Commentary
This passage defines the first type of textual indication in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: 'Ibara al-Nass'. It is the direct and explicit meaning that the text conveys without any need for reflection or inference. The author notes that this meaning can be either primary (al-ma'na al-istiqlali) or secondary (al-ma'na al-tabi'i). The example given is the Quranic verse on trade and usury, which directly indicates two rulings: the distinction between trade and usury, and the permissibility of trade and prohibition of usury. Both are intended, one primarily and the other secondarily. The commentary emphasizes that no external reasoning is required to extract these meanings; they are self-evident from the text.

Key Terms
عبارة النص (Ibārat al-Naṣṣ): The text's direct indication to its intended meaning without need for reflection.
دلالة النص (Dalālat al-Naṣṣ): Textual indication – the method by which a ruling is derived from the wording.
