Introduction to 'Naseem al-Mufti' and the Hanafi Fatwa Methodology
Title page and table of contents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure and key contents of 'Naseem al-Mufti' and explain its purpose as a Hanafi text on the principles of issuing fatwas (Usul al-Iftaa).

Core Idea
This book teaches muftis how to navigate conflicting Hanafi opinions and issue fatwas based on the most authoritative sources.

Every mufti must know the hierarchical method for choosing among different rulings within the Hanafi school. 'Naseem al-Mufti' is a modern commentary on the classical poem 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti', which codifies these rules. The front matter you have just read gives you the roadmap: it tells you what the book covers and how it will train you to become a competent Hanafi mufti.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bibliographic details
Note the book's full title, the author (Maulana Mufti Ridwan Naseem Qasimi), the publisher, and the edition dates. These establish the text's modern relevance.
The author is a contemporary Hanafi scholar teaching fiqh and ifta.
2
Examine the table of contents structure
The book has two main chapters. The first introduces the poem 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti' and its commentaries. The second covers the principles and methods of ifta, including how a mufti chooses the preponderant opinion.
This organisation mirrors the sequence of a mufti's training: first memorise the maxims, then learn to apply them.
3
Focus on the second chapter's first two principles
The first principle is that a mufti must give fatwa according to the preponderant (rajih) opinion. The second principle lists the rules for preference among conflicting opinions, with thirteen sub-principles based on the authority of the transmitting scholar.
Pay special attention to the second sub-principle: 'Giving fatwa according to Imam A'zam's opinion' — this is a key pillar of Hanafi tarjih.
4
Recognise the hierarchy of sources
The sub-principles create a ladder: first, if only one opinion is transmitted, that is followed; second, if multiple, prefer Imam A'zam's view; third, if still conflicting, apply detailed tarjih rules; fourth, if consensus exists, that overrides all.
This hierarchy ensures consistency and prevents arbitrary fatwa-giving.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tarjih in Hanafi ifta rests on person, book, and evidence — a threefold hierarchy.

The table of contents of Naseem al-Mufti reveals the systematic structure of Hanafi fatwa methodology. It lists three distinct categories for preferring one ruling over another: principles based on the person (such as Imam A'zam vs. Sahibayn), principles based on the authority of books (with Zahir al-Riwayah at the top), and principles based on evidence (like istihsan over qiyas). This lesson introduces this framework, which is essential for any mufti navigating conflicting reports in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1

The TOC shows two main chapters: Chapter 1 on the poem 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti and its commentaries, and Chapter 2 on the principles and methods of ifta and fatwa. Focus on Chapter 2 as the core of the book.
Chapter 2 contains the tarjih principles.

Types of Nass and Mafhum for Muftis
Listing types of nass and mafhum with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the four types of nass (ibarah, isharah, dalalah, iqtida') and the types of mafhum, and apply them to derive rulings from texts.

Core Idea
A text has multiple layers of meaning; the mufti must extract rulings from explicit text (ibarah), implication (isharah), inference (dalalah), and necessary implication (iqtida').

This passage introduces the classification of texts according to indication of meaning: ibarat al-nass, isharat al-nass, dalalat al-nass, and iqtida' al-nass. It also defines mantiq (explicit meaning) and mafhum (implicit meaning), including mafhum muwafiq and mafhum mukhalaf. These tools are essential for a mufti to correctly understand the scope of legal texts.

Guided Path
1
List the four types of nass
From the text: ibarat al-nass (explicit statement), isharat al-nass (allusion), dalalat al-nass (inferred meaning), iqtida' al-nass (required implication).
These are ordered by clarity and directness.
2
Define each with a Hanafi example
For example, the verse 'وَلَا تَقْرَبُوا الزِّنَا' (do not approach zina) has ibarah (prohibition of zina), isharah (prohibition of all means leading to it), dalalah (prohibition of any form of illicit sexual activity), iqtida' (implies the prohibition of everything necessary to avoid zina).
The examples will be given in the source text.
3
Understand mantiq and mafhum
Mantiq is the explicit meaning derived from the words. Mafhum is the implicit meaning, divided into mafhum muwafiq (harmonious) and mafhum mukhalaf (differing).
The text lists subtypes of mafhum mukhalaf: sifah, shart, ghayah, adad, laqab.
4
Apply in fatwa
A mufti must consider all these levels to give a complete ruling. For example, if a text mentions a condition, mafhum al-shart indicates that the ruling is absent when the condition is absent.
But mafhum mukhalaf may not be accepted if it contradicts a stronger indication.

Commentary
This passage is the preface (front matter) of 'Naseem al-Mufti', a simplified commentary on Ibn 'Abidin's 'Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'. The author explains the motivation behind the book: Ibn 'Abidin's original work, while comprehensive, is difficult for ordinary students because of its method of relying heavily on citations from other scholars. The author therefore has summarized and simplified the work into easy Urdu, and filled in gaps where the original was incomplete. He then offers thanks to Allah, his parents, and various scholars who encouraged him. He also notes that the first edition was well-received and sold out, leading to this second edition. In this edition, he has made revisions and transferred some topics (like the strata of jurists) to another book, 'Asan Usul al-Ifta'. The passage concludes with a request for prayers and contact information.

The Compiler's Choices: Revising and Transferring Topics
Author explains revisions and transfer of topics between his works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two main changes the author made in the second edition of Naseem al-Mufti and explain why certain discussions were moved to another book.

Core Idea
A compiler's revisions reflect the evolving pedagogical structure; transferring topics to a companion volume keeps each book focused.

This lesson examines the author's decisions in preparing the second edition. He corrected errors and reorganized content. Most notably, he removed discussions on the strata of jurists (tabaqat al-fuqaha'), objections and responses, and fiqh terminology, transferring them to 'Asan Usul al-Ifta'. This shows the author's deliberate effort to separate foundational principles from advanced topics, a common practice in Hanafi compilations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the revisions made in the second edition
The author says he reviewed the book and corrected errors in discussions and arrangement. This shows he took his readers' feedback seriously.
2
List the topics that were removed
From the preface: 'objections on the strata of jurists and their answers, fiqh codes and terminology, and a brief introduction of famous Hanafi jurists.'
3
Understand the reason for removal
The author says it was 'due to some inevitable reason' and that these topics were included in his other work 'Asan Usul al-Ifta'. The implication is that they were moved to make Naseem al-Mufti more concise and focused on the essentials of ifta'.
4
Consider the pedagogical impact
By separating these topics, the author allows a student to first master the core methodology in Naseem al-Mufti, then later study the more detailed discussions on jurist strata and terminology in the companion volume.

Commentary
This passage is the front matter of the book 'Naseem Ul Mufti', which is a summary or commentary on 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'. It contains two main parts: a bibliography of Urdu sources used in the book, and endorsements (taqariz) from several prominent Hanafi scholars. The bibliography lists important Urdu works on ifta, usul al-fiqh, and fiqh, establishing the scholarly foundation of the book. The endorsements from scholars like Maulana Khalid Saifullah Rahmani, Mufti Muhammad Rashid A'zami, Mufti Salman Mansurpuri, and Mufti Muhammad Junayd Alam praise the accessibility and utility of the work for students and teachers of ifta. This front matter serves to credit sources and build trust in the work's reliability within the Hanafi tradition.

Key Terms
تقاریظ (taqariz): Endorsements or commendations from scholars, often printed in the front matter of a book.
مصادر ومراجع (masadir wa maraji'): Sources and references used in compiling the work.
رسالہ (risalah): A treatise or booklet; here referring to the work being summarized.
