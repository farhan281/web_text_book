Custom (Ta'amul) as a Proof: The Weaver's Ijarah Case
Application of the qawida 'al-ta'amul hujja' to the weaver's ijarah case in the Dhakheera.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'al-ta'amul hujja' to validate a transaction based on customary practice, and identify its limitations (istithna') using the weaver's ijarah and istisna' examples from the Dhakheera.

Core Idea
Customary practice (ta'amul) can specify a hadith and override qiyas, but does not abrogate it.

In this passage from the Dhakheera, the author discusses how the jurists of Balkh permitted an ijarah contract that would otherwise be invalid due to qiyas and a hadith. The key is the concept of ta'amul (customary practice) which acts as a proof that can specify the scope of a hadith. This lesson will walk you through the legal reasoning and the underlying qawida, and show how it applies to similar cases like istisna'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
The passage relies on the qawida that ta'amul is a proof that can abandon qiyas and specify hadith. State the maxim in Arabic: التعامل حجة يترك به القياس ويخص به الحديث.
2
Locate the furu' application
The main case is the weaver's ijarah: giving spun cotton for a share of the cloth. Identify the problem: the hadith about the miller's measure and qiyas would normally prohibit it.
The hadith is not quoted but concerns hiring for a share of the product.
3
Understand the role of ta'amul
The Balkh jurists permitted it because ta'amul (customary practice) in their region of weaving cloth justified specifying the hadith to apply only to the miller, not the weaver.
Ta'amul is a proof (hujja) that can specify a text.
4
Distinguish specification from abandonment
The hadith is not abandoned; it still applies to the miller's case. This is specification (takhsis), not naskh. Parallel with istisna' shows that only certain exceptions are carved out.
5
Identify the istithna'
The exception: if a specific local practice like a miller's measure becomes prevalent, it is not permissible. The custom must be general and widespread.
This sets the condition for applying the qawida.

In the Dhakheera, in the eighth chapter of the Book of Ijarah, where this issue is mentioned: If someone gave spun cotton to a weaver to weave cloth in exchange for one-third of the finished cloth, the author of the Dhakheera writes that the jurists of Balkh, such as Nusayr b. Sakki, Muhammad b. Salama, and others, consider this ijarah permissible due to the customary practice (ta'amul) in their region regarding the weaving of cloth. And ta'amul is such a proof that analogy is abandoned and specification is made in the narration. The permissibility of this ijarah due to ta'amul in weaving means specification of the hadith that was reported concerning the non-miller; because that hadith was about the measure of the flour miller, not about the weaver. However, the weaver is analogous to him, so the hadith would also apply to him. Then when we did not act upon that hadith for the weaver and acted upon it for the flour miller, this is specification of the hadith, not complete abandonment. And specification of a hadith due to ta'amul is permissible. Indeed, we consider istisna' permissible due to ta'amul, even though it involves selling something not in the seller's possession, and a hadith prohibits selling what one does not possess. Declaring istisna' permissible due to ta'amul is a specification of that hadith, not its complete abandonment, because we act on that hadith in other cases besides istisna'. The scholars have also stated that this case is entirely different from a situation where a particular miller's measure becomes prevalent in a region; then it would not be permissible. And the practice of those people is considered.

Commentary
This passage is a furu' application of the earlier rule that general custom can specify a text. The Dhakheera uses ta'amul (customary practice) to justify an ijarah contract that would otherwise be invalid under qiyas and a hadith. The hadith likely prohibits hiring for a share of the product or selling what is not possessed. The jurists of Balkh relied on regional custom to specify the hadith, limiting its application to the miller's case but not to the weaver's. This is permissible because it does not completely abandon the hadith; it merely restricts its scope. Additionally, the case of istisna' (manufacturing contract) is cited as a parallel. The passage also notes a condition: if a specific practice (like a miller's measure) becomes prevalent in a region, it may not be permissible, indicating that custom must be general enough.

Key Terms
تعامل (ta'amul): Customary practice or prevalent usage in transactions among people in a particular region.
استصناع (istisna'): A contract of manufacturing where a buyer orders an artisan to produce a specific item; considered permissible despite the general prohibition of selling non-existent items.
