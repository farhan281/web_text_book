Istihsan vs Qiyas: The Meaning of Tarjih in Hanafi Iftaa
Explains the rule and the ikhtilaf on its meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two positions on the preference of istihsan over qiyas and apply the correct view in fatwa according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
When istihsan and qiyas conflict, istihsan is preferred, but scholars differ on whether qiyas is completely abandoned or merely less preferred.

In Hanafi legal methodology, istihsan often takes precedence over qiyas. However, later scholars debated what this 'preference' (tarjih) actually means. Does it render qiyas invalid, or does it merely indicate that istihsan is better while qiyas remains permissible? This lesson unpacks the ikhtilaf between two major Hanafi jurists and its practical implications for issuing fatwas.

Guided Path
1
State the foundational rule
Identify the principle that istihsan is preponderant (rajih) over qiyas. This is the baseline for the discussion.
This rule is well-established in Hanafi usul; the dispute is about its interpretation.
2
Analyze Taftazani's interpretation
Understand Taftazani's view: tarjih here means qiyas is completely abandoned and acting upon istihsan becomes obligatory. Qiyas is no longer permissible.
This is the stricter position, often associated with the school of the Mutakallimun.
3
Analyze Bazdawi's interpretation
Understand Bazdawi's view: tarjih means istihsan is 'awla' (better), but qiyas remains permissible. The mufti may still act on qiyas, though istihsan is preferred.
This is the more flexible position, often attributed to the Hanafi fuqaha.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Contrast the two views: under Taftazani's view, qiyas cannot be used at all when istihsan is present; under Bazdawi's, qiyas remains a valid option, though istihsan is recommended.
This affects fatwa issuance: a mufti following Taftazani would never issue a fatwa based on qiyas if istihsan applies; following Bazdawi, he may.
5
Apply to fatwa practice
In contemporary Hanafi iftaa, the preponderant view (mufta bihi) is usually the more flexible one (Bazdawi), allowing use of qiyas when needed, but the mufti should be aware of the ikhtilaf.
This is consistent with the Hanafi principle of facilitating practice.

Definition of Dalaalat al-Nass with Example
Defines dalaalat al-nass and provides Quranic example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalaalat al-nass, distinguish it from other types of textual indication, and apply it to derive a ruling from a Quranic verse using the principle of greater reason.

Core Idea
Dalaalat al-nass derives a necessary implication from the text without deliberation — it is not analogy but direct textual indication.

In Hanafi usul, texts indicate meanings in multiple layers. Beyond the explicit 'ibarah and the indicated isharah, there is dalaalah — where the text points to a meaning that is a necessary consequence, understood by any rational person without reflection. This lesson defines dalaalat al-nass and illustrates it with the famous verse on parents from Surah al-Isra'.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Read the definition in Arabic and English. Identify what dalaalat al-nass is NOT (not primary or secondary intent, not entailed) and what it IS (understood without reflection).
Contrast with 'ibarat al-nass (explicit meaning) and ishaarat al-nass (implied meaning requiring some ijtihad).
2
Examine the Quranic example
Look at the verse 'فَلا تَقُلْ لَّهُما أَفٌ' and note its explicit meaning (ibarah): saying 'uff' is prohibited.
3
Derive the dalaalah
Apply the rule of أولویت (first priority/greater reason): since beating causes more harm than 'uff', and 'uff' is prohibited, beating is prohibited by a fortiori reasoning. This is dalaalat al-nass.
This reasoning is immediate, not requiring ijtihad.
4
Distinguish from qiyas
Note that dalaalat al-nass is not qiyas. In qiyas, you need to identify a shared effective cause ('illah) between the original and the new case. Here, the implication is direct from the wording.
This distinction is important for understanding Hanafi usul.

Definition of Dalaalat al-Nass with example: The text indicating a meaning that is neither intended primarily nor secondarily, nor is it entailed by the meaning for which the speech was brought; rather, that meaning is understood from the text without reflection or deliberation, then it is called dalaalat al-nass. For example, Allah's statement: 'So do not say to them 'uff' and do not repel them' (Bani Isra'il: 23). The 'ibarat al-nass of this verse is that saying 'uff' to parents is prohibited. But as dalaalat al-nass, it is also understood that hitting parents is prohibited, because the harm of beating is greater than saying 'uff'. So when saying 'uff' is not permitted, then beating is a fortiori not permitted. No reflection is needed to understand this meaning; every rational person can understand it. This is the dalaalat al-nass of this verse.

Commentary

This is a classic Hanafi illustration of dalaalat al-nass, which is considered a stronger indication than iqtidaa al-nass but weaker than 'ibarat al-nass and ishaarat al-nass.

Key Terms
دلالۃ النص (dalaalat al-nass): The text's indication of a meaning that is not the primary or secondary intent, nor entailed, but understood without deliberation.
عبارۃ النص (‘ibarat al-nass): The explicit meaning of the text for which it was uttered.

Definition of Iqtidaa al-Nass with Quranic Example
Definition of iqtidaa al-nass with Quranic example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define iqtidaa al-nass, identify the omitted word in a given text, and apply the concept to derive a ruling.

Core Idea
Iqtidaa al-nass: the text requires an omitted word for legal validity.

In Hanafi usul, some Quranic verses and hadiths carry an implicit word that must be assumed for the ruling to be valid. This mechanism, called iqtidaa al-nass, bridges apparent contradictions and ensures rulings apply to actions, not essences. Let's explore its definition and a classic example.

Guided Path
1
Define iqtidaa al-nass
Start with the definition: the text's indication of an omitted word necessary for its validity in Shari'ah or reason.
The omitted word is not explicitly mentioned but required for correct meaning.
2
Identify the condition for applying iqtidaa
Check if the text is incomplete without assuming the omitted word. If the text is self-sufficient, iqtidaa does not apply.
Necessity can be legal (Shari'ah) or rational (logic).
3
Analyze the Quranic example
Examine the verse 'حُرِّمَتْ عَلَيْكُمُ الْمَيْتَةُ'. Why must we assume a word like 'eating' or 'using'? Because Shari'ah prohibits actions, not essences.
The omitted word is understood from context and legal principles.
4
State the derived ruling
The prohibition applies to the action of eating/using carrion, not to the carrion itself (which has no inherent ruling).
This avoids absurdity and aligns with legal theory.
5
Distinguish from other textual tools
Iqtidaa al-nass is different from mafhum (implied meaning) because it assumes a missing word, not a derived meaning from the existing text.
Hanafis treat it as part of mantooq (explicit meaning).

Definition of Iqtidaa al-Nass with example: The text indicating an omitted word upon which the validity of the text depends, whether by religious or rational necessity, is called iqtidaa al-nass. That is, without assuming that word is hidden, the meaning of the text is not correctly understood. For example, Allah's statement: 'Forbidden to you are carrion, blood, and the flesh of swine' (al-Ma'idah: 3). In this verse, the word 'consumption' (or 'use') is omitted before 'carrion', because without assuming that word, the meaning of the text is not correct—since in Shari'ah, prohibition is not attached to the essence of a thing but to an action related to it, such as benefit. So the meaning of the text is that benefiting from carrion, i.e., eating it, is not permissible. And this meaning is understood through iqtidaa al-nass.

This is a key Hanafi concept used in interpreting apparent verses.

Mafhum Muwafiq: Fahwa and Lahw al-Khitab
Definition and subtypes of Mafhum Muwafiq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Mafhum Muwafiq and its two subtypes, apply the distinction to Quranic examples, and evaluate the Hanafi acceptance of these inferential methods.

Core Idea
Mafhum Muwafiq extends the ruling from the mentioned case to the unmentioned case — either with greater priority or equal priority.

In Hanafi usul, understanding the implied meanings of texts is crucial. This lesson explores Mafhum Muwafiq, where the unmentioned case shares the ruling of the mentioned case. We will examine two subtypes: Fahwa al-Khitab (a fortiori) and Lahw al-Khitab (equal degree), with Quranic examples. Mastering these helps you derive rulings from scripture with precision.

Guided Path
1
Define Mafhum and its two main types
Distinguish Mafhum Muwafiq (same ruling for unmentioned case) from Mafhum Mukhalif (opposite ruling). Note alternative terms: tanbih al-khitab and dalil al-khitab.
Mafhum Muwafiq is the focus of this lesson.
2
Identify the two subtypes of Mafhum Muwafiq
Fahwa al-Khitab applies the ruling with greater priority; Lahw al-Khitab applies with equal priority. Understand the relationship between mantuq and maskut.
The 'illah (reason) is shared in both.
3
Analyze the Fahwa example: Quran 17:23
From the prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents, deduce the prohibition of striking them. Explain why striking is more severe.
This is a minori ad maius (a fortiori) argument.
4
Analyze the Lahw example: Quran 4:10
From the warning against consuming orphans' wealth, deduce the same warning for burning it. Show that both are forms of destruction with equal gravity.
The 'illah is destroying orphans' property.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi acceptance
Recognize that Hanafis accept both subtypes as valid sources of inference, unlike some other schools that may restrict only to Fahwa.
This is part of Hanafi usul.

(Quran 4:10); this verse mentions a warning for consuming wealth unjustly, but also implies a warning for burning it, because the purpose is to forbid destroying orphans' property, and destruction in both forms is of equal degree. So both rulings are established equally. (al-Taqrir wa al-Tahbir: 1/147, Usul al-Fiqh al-Zuhayli: 1/362, al-Wajiz fi Usul al-Fiqh: 2/154)

Mafhum al-Muwafiq (Coincident Meaning) in Usul al-Fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mafhum al-muwafiq, distinguish between its two types (awlawi and musawi), and apply it to derive rulings from Quranic texts.

Core Idea
The text's ruling extends to unmentioned cases when the ratio legis is identical.

This lesson introduces a foundational concept in Hanafi usul: mafhum al-muwafiq, also called lahn al-khitab or fahwa al-khitab. It is a type of textual implication where the ruling of an explicit text applies to a case not mentioned, either with greater priority or equal force. Understanding this concept is crucial for deriving rulings beyond the literal wording, staying within the text's own logic without resorting to qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of mafhum al-muwafiq
The passage mentions two forms: fahwa al-khitab (priority implication) and lahn al-khitab (equal degree implication). Distinguish them based on the relationship between the mentioned and unmentioned case.
Fahwa al-khitab: unmentioned case is more deserving of the ruling. Lahn al-khitab: both cases are equal.
2

Both eating and burning constitute itlaf (destruction).

Formulate the definition: lahn al-khitab is when the ruling of the uttered text applies to the unmentioned case in an equal degree, because the 'illah is the same.

Note that mafhum al-muwafiq is a direct implication from the text itself, not an analogical extension. The ruling is already understood from the text's purpose, not derived through qiyas.
This distinction affects the strength of evidence (hujjah vs. qiyas).

Statement on Verbal Custom and Practical Custom, quoted from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti. According to subject and locus, custom is of two types: one is verbal custom, also called lexical custom, and the second is practical custom, also called actional custom. Their details are as follows: Verbal custom is when a word or speech becomes prevalent among people in a specific meaning such that whenever that word is uttered, without any contextual clue or rational evidence, that same specific meaning is understood. (al-Urf wa al-'Adah fi Ra'y al-Fuqaha': 18; al-'Adah Muhakkamah, Ya'qub bin 'Abd al-Wahhab: 37) Practical custom means the specific act of people that all or most people repeatedly do; in other words, the method of people regarding an act that has gained common prevalence is called practical or actional custom. (al-'Adah Muhakkamah: 37; al-Urf wa al-'Adah fi Ra'y al-Fuqaha': 18) Evidence for the validity of both verbal and practical custom is found in Islamic textual sources and the statements of jurists, as the mention of these proofs occurred under the section 'Proofs for the validity of custom'. The jurist of the era, Maulana Khalid Saifullah Rahmani, in his article 'The Importance of Custom and 'Adah in Islamic Fiqh and Its Principles and Conditions for Validity' has discussed in detail the validity of verbal and practical custom with reference to all four schools of fiqh; studying it is extremely beneficial. For details, see: Muhadarat Usul al-Fiqh: 167-179.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional classification of custom (urf) in the Hanafi Usul al-Iftaa. It begins by dividing custom into two types based on its nature: verbal (qauli) and practical (amali). Verbal custom refers to the conventional usage of a word in a specific meaning that is immediately understood without need for context. For example, the common use of the word 'salah' for the Islamic prayer, or 'sawm' for fasting. Practical custom refers to repeated actions that have become standard practice in society, such as terms of hire for public baths. The author then states that both types are valid as legal evidence, citing that proof exists from primary texts and juristic consensus. He references a contemporary work for further study. Note: The authority of custom is subject to conditions; this passage only establishes the basic validity. A key point: in the Hanafi school, custom is considered a subsidiary source of law and is given weight as long as it does not contradict a clear text (nass).

Key Terms
عرف (urf): Custom or common practice that is accepted as a source of law.
عرف قولی (urf qauli): Verbal custom: the conventional meaning of a word without context.
عرف عملی (urf amali): Practical custom: common practice.

Custom vs. Text: Rules of 'Urf Aam and 'Urf Khaas
Explains the rules when custom opposes or specifies textual evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general and specific custom, and apply the Hanafi rule that only general custom can specify a general text or override qiyas.

Core Idea
General custom specifies texts; specific custom does not.

Custom ('urf) plays an important role in Hanafi fiqh, but not all custom carries the same weight. This lesson clarifies the distinction between general custom ('urf aam) and specific custom ('urf khaas) and their respective legal effects when interacting with textual evidence. Understanding this distinction is crucial for a mufti when determining whether a local practice can modify a general ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of custom
First, recognize that 'urf 'aam is widespread across regions, while 'urf khaas is limited to a specific area or group. The passage explicitly defines both.
The Hanafi school gives legal weight only to 'urf 'aam.
2
Distinguish between complete and partial conflict
If custom completely opposes a text (i.e., acting on custom requires abandoning the text), both types are invalid. If the conflict is partial (custom specifies the text), further analysis is needed.
Complete conflict is a rare scenario; partial conflict is more common.
3
Apply the rule for general custom
When the conflict is partial and the custom is general, it can specify the text and override qiyas. This is by consensus.
General custom is a recognized source of specification in Hanafi usul.
4
Apply the rule for specific custom
Specific custom cannot specify a general text or override qiyas. This is the relied-upon position of the madhhab, due to the doubt arising from regional variation.
Some scholars disagreed, but the Hanafi position is clear.
5
Evaluate the dispute and the tarjih
The passage mentions disagreement on whether specific custom can specify. The Hanafi response, confirmed by Ibn Abidin, is that it cannot.
This is an example of legitimate ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi is the negation.

From established texts: If custom conflicts with the text such that acting on custom requires completely abandoning the text, then both types of custom – general custom and specific custom – are unanimously invalid. If it does not conflict completely but rather specifies the text, then general custom is valid by consensus and it is permissible to specify the text with it. If the evidence is general and custom conflicts in some of its particulars, or the evidence is analogical, then custom is considered if it is general, for general custom can serve as a specifier and analogy is abandoned for it. If the custom is specific, it is not considered, and that is the madhhab. Then there is disagreement among scholars about whether specific custom can specify the text. Some say no, because if a specific custom in one region demands specification of the text, its absence in another region demands non-specification, so due to doubt, specifying the general ruling is not permissible. Ibn Abidin says: Know that there are two types of custom: general and specific. General custom establishes a general ruling and can serve as a specifier for analogy and hadith. Specific custom is different; it establishes a specific ruling, provided it does not oppose analogy or hadith, because specific custom cannot be a specifier (mukhasis).

Commentary
This passage is a rule regarding the interaction between custom and textual evidence in the Hanafi school. It clearly distinguishes between two types of custom: general custom ('urf aam) and specific custom ('urf khaas). If custom completely opposes the text, it is rejected. If it only opposes in some particulars, then general custom can specify the text and override analogy; specific custom is not considered a valid specifier. There is disagreement on whether specific custom can specify; the preferred view (which is the madhhab) is that it cannot. Ibn Abidin clarifies that general custom can specify both qiyas and hadith, while specific custom cannot be a specifier. This is a fundamental principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, especially in matters where local practices conflict with general rulings.

Key Terms
عرف عام ('urf 'aam): Custom widely practiced without regional restriction, which can specify a general text and override qiyas.
عرف خاص ('urf khaas): Custom specific to a particular region or group, which is not considered a valid specifier for a general text.
تخصیص (takhsis): Specification: restricting the general meaning of a text to some of its applications.
