Three Views on 'Urf and 'Adah in Hanafi Usul
Explains three scholarly views on 'urf vs 'adah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three scholarly views on the relationship between 'urf and 'adah and state the operative Hanafi position for fatwa.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fatwa, 'urf and 'adah are terminologically identical — the distinction is only linguistic.

The relationship between 'urf (custom) and 'adah (habit) is a foundational discussion in Hanafi legal theory, as both are sources for deriving rulings when no explicit text is found. However, scholars differ on whether they are synonymous or distinct. This lesson unpacks the three main views, their proponents, and the relied-upon position for a mufti.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Views
Read the passage carefully and list the three positions: (1) 'urf is more general than 'adah, (2) 'urf is more general than 'adah but 'adah is a subset (practical 'urf), (3) they are identical in technical usage.
The first view is from Mustafa al-Zarqa; the second from Ibn al-Humam; the third (majority) from Ibn 'Abidin, al-Nasafi, and others.
2
Analyse the Scope of Each View
For each view, determine what falls under 'urf (sayings and/or actions) and what falls under 'adah (only actions or both). This clarifies the logical relationship.
3
Note the Authorities and Their Weight
Identify which scholars support each view. The third view includes prominent Hanafi authorities (Ibn 'Abidin, al-Nasafi, Khalid Saifullah al-Rahmani) and is labelled as the correct (sahih) position.
The passage explicitly says the third view is affirmed as correct by al-Rahmani.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi Position
Based on the authorities cited, the majority of later Hanafi masters hold that 'urf and 'adah are synonymous in terminological sense. This is the operative view for issuing fatwas.
This does not deny linguistic differences; personal habits (like menstruation) may still be called 'adah linguistically.
5
Apply to Fatwa Context
Explain that a mufti should treat 'urf and 'adah as interchangeable when relying on custom to derive a ruling. The distinction, if any, does not affect the legal outcome.
This prevents unnecessary confusion when a custom is referred to as either 'urf or 'adah.

Commentary
This passage is a legal-philosophical discussion of the relationship between 'urf (custom) and 'adah (habit) as technical terms in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It presents three scholarly views. The first view, adopted by Mustafa al-Zarqa, holds that 'urf is more general than 'adah: every 'urf is an 'adah but not vice versa. The second view, attributed to Ibn al-Humam, considers 'adah as a subset of 'urf (practical 'urf), meaning 'urf is more general as it includes both sayings and actions, while 'adah is restricted to actions. The third view, held by many leading Hanafi scholars including Ibn 'Abidin and Khalid Saifullah al-Rahmani, states that 'urf and 'adah are identical in terminological usage; the distinction is only linguistic. The text follows the author's order, first listing the views and then citing authorities. It carefully distinguishes between linguistic and technical meanings, emphasizing that in fiqh, the two terms are often used interchangeably. This is crucial for a mufti to understand when deriving rulings from custom, as the term used may affect the evidentiary weight.

Key Terms
عرف ('urf): Custom; practices prevalent among the general public, both in sayings and actions.
عادت ('adah): Habit; according to most Hanafis, synonymous with 'urf in technical usage, but linguistically often restricted to habitual actions.

Six Distinctions Between 'Urf and Ijma: A Parsing Exercise for the Mufti
Lists six distinctions between 'urf and ijma' as sources of law.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'urf and ijma' across six criteria and correctly apply that distinction when assessing the authority of a source in fatwa.

Core Idea
Ijma' is binding and irreversible; 'urf is flexible and yields to text.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, both 'urf (custom) and ijma' (consensus) rely on agreement, but they differ fundamentally in authority, scope, and applicability. This lesson parses six key differences that every mufti must internalise to properly weigh sources when issuing fatwa. By the end, you will be able to state each distinction and explain its practical impact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sources
Be clear on the definitions of 'urf and ijma' as given in the text. Note that both involve agreement, but the agents differ.
2
List the six differences one by one
Read the passage carefully and enumerate each distinction in order. Pay attention to the specific wording: agents, consensus threshold, decisiveness, relation to text, repetition requirement, and effect on ijtihad.
The text presents five numbered points, but the last point contains two sub-points, making six total.
3
Understand the underlying rationale
For each difference, ask: why is the rule set that way? For example, why is ijma' irreversible? Because it is a decisive proof (qat'i) that closes ijtihad.
This connects the distinctions to Hanafi usul principles.
4
Apply the distinction to a hypothetical scenario
Given a case, decide whether the authority is 'urf or ijma' and what the implications are for the mufti. For instance, if a practice is widespread but not agreed upon by all mujtahids, it is 'urf, not ijma'.
5
Reflect on the role of the mufti
How does knowing these differences affect fatwa-giving? The mufti must weigh sources correctly, especially when 'urf conflicts with nass or when a ruling may need to change due to evolving custom.

Relationship and difference between 'urf and ijma'. There is another term close to 'urf, which is called ijma'. The relationship between 'urf and ijma' is that both involve agreement and generality, especially general 'urf and ijma' share much similarity. Despite this, there is a difference between 'urf and ijma' due to the following reasons: 1. 'Urf comes into existence from the sayings and actions of the common people, while in ijma' the opinion of the common people has no role; it comes into existence only from the agreement of the mujtahids. For ijma', it is necessary that all mujtahids agree on it, with no single one dissenting, while for 'urf, the agreement of the majority and most people is sufficient. 2. The ruling established by ijma' is decisive (qat'i), with no scope for change, whereas the ruling established by 'urf can change; indeed, sometimes it is obligatory to change the ruling due to change of 'urf. 3. Ijma' is authoritative even in the face of a text, because it is itself a decisive proof, but 'urf is not authoritative in the face of a text; meaning that if 'urf conflicts with a text such that only one can be acted upon, we will by consensus abandon the 'urf and act on the text. 4. Ijma' comes into effect by agreement once, while for 'urf to be authoritative, repetition and continuity are necessary. 5. Once ijma' has been concluded on an issue, there remains no scope for ijtihad in that issue, but the case of 'urf is different, because despite the existence of 'urf in a matter, the door of ijtihad remains open. Commentary
This passage distinguishes 'urf (custom) from ijma' (consensus) as sources of law in Hanafi usul. It first notes their similarity: both involve agreement and generality, especially general 'urf resembles ijma'. Then it lists six differences (though the text enumerates five main points with the last split into two, suggesting six). These differences are crucial for a mufti to understand the relative authority of each. The key practical implications are: ijma' is binding on all and cannot be changed, while 'urf can change over time; ijma' requires unanimity of mujtahids, while 'urf only requires majority practice; when conflicting with a clear text, 'urf yields but ijma' does not; ijma' is established once, while 'urf requires repetition; and ijma' closes ijtihad on that issue, but 'urf leaves it open. The text follows the author's order, listing each difference with supporting reasoning. This knowledge is essential for fatwa, as a mufti must know which source to prioritise and when custom can override a ruling.

Key Terms
اجماع (ijma'): Consensus of all mujtahids of the ummah on a legal ruling; a decisive (qat'i) proof.
عرف عام ('urf 'amm): General custom; practices prevalent among the majority of people, not necessarily all.

Rational Proof for the Authority of Custom (Urf)
Rational argument that ignoring custom causes hardship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the rational (aqli) proof for the validity of custom using the principle of la haraj.

Core Idea
Ignoring custom causes hardship — and Shariah's removal of hardship demands its recognition.

The authority of custom (urf) is often justified by texts, but here we explore a rational argument. This lesson unpacks the qiyas-based reasoning that links custom to the maqasid principle of avoiding hardship. Understanding this proof strengthens your ability to apply custom in fatwa and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Premise
Recognize that people naturally speak and contract according to their local customs. This is an observed reality: every person's language and transactions are shaped by their 'urf.
This premise is empirical and universally acknowledged.
2
Recognize the Consequence
If custom were not given weight, every person would face hardship (haraj) because they would be held to standards outside their accustomed practice. This hardship is concrete and widespread.
The consequence is a direct result of disregarding the premise.
3
Apply the Shariah Principle
Invoke the Quranic principle {وما جعل عليكم في الدين من حرج} (al-Hajj: 78) which establishes that Shariah does not impose hardship. This is a fundamental maqsad (objective) of the law.
This principle is qat'i (certain) in thubut and dalalah.
4
Derive the Ruling
Since ignoring custom causes hardship and hardship is prohibited, custom must be recognized as a valid source of law. Thus, urf is authoritative by rational necessity.
This is a qiyas jali: the illa (hardship) connects the far' (ignoring custom) to the asl (prohibition of hardship).

Commentary
This passage provides a rational (aqli) proof for the authority of custom, using qiyas. The argument is: if custom were not valid, then people would constantly face hardship because they naturally speak and contract according to their customs. Since Islamic law prohibits hardship (ref: Quran 22:78), ignoring custom would contradict the law's objectives. This is a classic usuli argument that links the consideration of custom to the maqasid (higher objectives) of Shariah, specifically the removal of hardship. The proof is not a direct naqli text but a logical deduction from the general principle of la haraj.

Key Terms
حرج (haraj): Hardship, difficulty, or constraint that the law aims to remove
عرف (urf): Custom or established practice of a community that is not contrary to Shariah
