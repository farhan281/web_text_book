Distinguishing Mantuq and Mafhum in Hanafi Usul
Defines Mantuq and Mafhum according to Hanafi methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Mantuq and Mafhum in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and state the three types of dalalat included under Mantuq.

Core Idea
Mantuq is the direct meaning; Mafhum is the inferred meaning; Hanafis uniquely include 'ibarah, isharah, and iqtida' under Mantuq.

This lesson introduces two foundational terms in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: Mantuq and Mafhum. Understanding their precise definitions and the Hanafi classification of textual indications is essential for a mufti when deriving rulings from primary sources. The passage you will read establishes the criteria for direct and inferred meanings, a distinction that shapes legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms
Locate the definitions of Mantuq and Mafhum in the passage. Note that Mantuq is the meaning directly indicated by the words, while Mafhum is understood indirectly.
The passage also states that Hanafis call Mafhum 'dalalat an-nass'.
2
Understand the Hanafi classification of Mantuq
The passage explicitly says that under Mantuq, Hanafis include three types: 'ibarah an-nass, isharah an-nass, and iqtida' an-nass. Memorize these three.
This is distinctive to the Hanafi school; other schools may subdivide differently.
3
Apply the distinction to an example
Consider the verse 'أَقِيمُوا الصَّلَاةَ' (establish prayer). Its direct command is Mantuq. The implied obligation is Mafhum. Identify which type of Mantuq applies ('ibarah).
Examples of isharah: the verse 'وَلِلَّهِ عَلَى النَّاسِ حِجُّ الْبَيْتِ' indicates the sanctity of the House. Iqtida': 'لا تَقُلْ لَهُمَا أُفٍّ' implies prohibition of beating.
4
Distinguish Mafhum from Mantuq
Mafhum is not directly expressed but is understood from the context. For instance, from 'لا تَقْرَبُوا الزِّنَا' (do not approach zina), we understand that zina itself is forbidden – this is Mafhum.
The passage clarifies that examples of 'ibarah etc. are examples of Mantuq, not Mafhum.

I'tibar of Mafhum Muwafiq and Mukhalaf
Discusses authoritative weight of mafhum types and ikhtilaf on mafhum mukhalaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which types of mafhum are accepted in the Hanafi school for fatwa and differentiate between mafhum muwafiq, mafhum al-laqab, and other types of mafhum mukhalaf.

Core Idea
Mafhum muwafiq universally accepted; mafhum al-laqab universally rejected; other mafhum mukhalaf types are subject to madhhab difference.

The mufti must know which inferential tools are valid. This lesson clarifies the authoritative weight of different mafhum types, distinguishing the universally accepted from the disputed. Understanding this prevents erroneous istidlal in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Define the two categories of mafhum
Identify mafhum muwafiq (coherent meaning via analogy) and mafhum mukhalaf (opposite meaning from restriction like shart, ghayah, adad, laqab).
Mafhum muwafiq is further divided into awlawi (higher priority) and musawi (equal).
2
Identify the universally accepted type
Both types of mafhum muwafiq are accepted in Shariah and common speech with no ikhtilaf.
Example: 'Do not say uff' implies prohibition of hitting (awlawi).
3
Identify the universally rejected type
Mafhum al-laqab (proper noun) is unanimously invalid as evidence.
No scholar accepts mafhum al-laqab.
4
Locate the ikhtilaf
For other types of mafhum mukhalaf (shart, ghayah, adad), Hanafis accept them; Shafi'is accept all including laqab.
The dispute is on the validity of these types.
5
Apply to fatwa
The mufta bihi in Hanafi school is to reject mafhum al-laqab but accept other mafhum mukhalaf types.
A mufti should not derive rulings from a mere noun.

Mafhum Mukhalif: Hanafi and Shafi'i Positions on Opposite Implication
Discusses the validity of mafhum muwafiq and mafhum mukhalif in religious and non-religious texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a text's implication (mafhum mukhalif) is accepted in the Hanafi school and apply the exception for punishments (uqubat) in deriving rulings.

Core Idea
Hanafis reject mafhum mukhalif in religious texts except for punishments; Shafi'is accept it universally.

This lesson examines a key difference between Hanafi and Shafi'i usul: the acceptance of mafhum mukhalif (opposite implication). Understanding this distinction is vital for a mufti when deriving rulings from divine texts versus everyday speech. We will explore the general rule, the exception for punishments, and the practical implications through examples.

Guided Path
1
Define mafhum muwafiq and mafhum mukhalif
Begin by clearly distinguishing between the two types of implied meaning. Mafhum muwafiq (consistent implication) is accepted by all. Mafhum mukhalif (opposite implication) is the point of dispute.
Mafhum al-laqab (title-based implication) is universally rejected.
2
State the Hanafi principle regarding mafhum mukhalif
The Hanafi position: mafhum mukhalif is not accepted in religious texts (nusus shar'iyyah) except in matters of punishments (uqubat). It is accepted in non-religious texts, such as everyday speech and juristic expressions.
This principle is attributed to al-Khabbazi and endorsed by later scholars.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Shafi'is accept mafhum mukhalif in both religious and non-religious texts without exception. Understand the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) in fiqh rulings.
For example, the hadith 'Zakat is on sheep' would, for Shafi'is, imply no zakat on other animals; for Hanafis, it does not negate zakat on other animals.
4
Examine the Hanafi proof from al-Khabbazi
The Hanafi proof: specification (takhsis) in divine speech does not indicate negation of the ruling from other than the specified. In human speech, it does indicate negation. This is illustrated by the dirham example.
Quoted from Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti: 'specifying something in the speech of the Lawgiver does not indicate negation... in people's conversation it does indicate negation.'
5
Apply the distinction to the dirham example
Analyze the two statements: 'You do not owe me more than one hundred dirhams' implies only 100 owed (mafhum mukhalif accepted). 'You owe me neither more nor less than one hundred dirhams' implies nothing owed (mafhum al-adad not used for negation).
This shows how mafhum mukhalif operates in non-religious contexts.

Core Idea
Hanafis accept mafhum mukhalif in everyday speech and juristic works, but not in divine texts except criminal penalties.

This lesson explains a key principle in Hanafi ifta: when can a mufti derive a ruling from the implied opposite of a statement? The Hanafi school makes a careful distinction between divine texts (Qur’an and Hadith) and other texts. Understanding this distinction is essential for proper fatwa derivation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of texts discussed
The passage mentions shar'i texts (divine), non-shar'i texts (common speech, urf, fiqh books), and uqubat (punishments).
2
State the Hanafi rule for each category
In shar'i texts, mafhum mukhalif is not valid except in uqubat. In non-shar'i texts, it is valid. In uqubat from divine texts, it is valid.
This is the core position.

Commentary
This passage is a rasm al-mufti discussion on the validity of mafhum mukhalif (the contrary meaning derived from a conditional or qualified statement) in the Hanafi school. The author begins by stating the general rule: in contexts outside the divine law (nusus shar'iyya) such as everyday speech and juristic expressions, mafhum mukhalif is considered valid and used as evidence. However, in the divine texts (Qur'an and Hadith), it is not considered valid by the Hanafis, except in matters of punishments (uqubat) where they do accept it. This is a nuanced position that distinguishes Hanafi usul from other schools (e.g., Shafi'is who accept mafhum mukhalif broadly). The passage then provides supporting statements from prominent Hanafi jurists. First, Ibn al-Humam's view that it is invalid only in the Lawgiver's speech, and al-Khabbazi's narration from al-Kardari confirming that specific mention in divine texts does not negate the ruling for others. Conversely, in human discourse, custom, transactions, and rational proofs, it does indicate such negation. Later scholars embraced this distinction, applying it to a case from Khizanat al-Akmal: saying 'you have no more than 100 dirhams against me' implies exactly 100, whereas saying 'neither more nor less than 100' implies nothing is owed. Second, from al-Nahr al-Fa'iq: mafhum mukhalif is valid by consensus in narrations (riwayat) - meaning the transmitted statements of jurists, including Companions, provided those statements are based on ijtihad. This excludes statements from Companions that are considered prophetic (marfu'), as those are treated like divine texts. Ibn 'Abidin clarifies that Companions' statements derived from ijtihad are not on par with revelation, so mafhum mukhalif is accepted in those. Third, from Ghayat al-Bayan: in the issue of a woman not needing to undo her braids in ghusl, the restriction to 'woman' excludes men by mafhum mukhalif, and this is valid in juristic narrations by agreement, unlike divine texts where such restriction does not negate. Thus the Hanafi position is consistent: mafhum mukhalif is a tool for interpreting non-revelatory texts, not for the Qur'an and Sunnah (except in criminal law for deterrence). The practical implication for a mufti is to know when to apply this principle in deriving rulings from fiqh books versus from primary sources.

Key Terms
مفہوم مخالف (mafhum mukhalif): The implied contrary meaning; the understanding that mention of something specifically negates the same ruling for all else.
نصوص شرعیہ (nusus shar'iyya): Divine texts: Qur'an and Sunnah (including hadith).
عقوبات (uqubat): Criminal punishments, especially ta'zir and hadd.

Mafhum al-Mukhalafah: Valid in Fiqh Narrations, Not in Nusus
Explaining when mafhum al-mukhalafah is valid (fiqh narrations) and invalid (nusus).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on mafhum al-mukhalafah to distinguish when it is valid evidence (fiqh narrations, everyday speech) and when it is not (Qur'an and hadith).

Core Idea
Mafhum al-mukhalafah is valid in fiqh narrations but not in primary texts — a key distinction for fatwa practice.

This lesson unpacks a critical rule of Hanafi fatwa methodology (rasm al-mufti): when can a mufti rely on the converse meaning of a restricted statement? The passage from Nasim al-Mufti clarifies that mafhum al-mukhalafah is valid by consensus in fiqh narrations (riwayat fiqhiyyah) and in everyday speech, but not in the Qur'an and hadith (nusus). Understanding this distinction prevents misapplication of texts and ensures sound legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the text type
Determine whether the source is a fiqh narration (e.g., a ruling from a mujtahid in Hidayah, Kanz al-Daqa'iq) or a primary nass (Qur'anic verse or prophetic hadith). This decides whether mafhum al-mukhalafah can be applied.
Companion statements based on ijtihad fall under fiqh narrations; those beyond ijtihad (like marfu' hadith) are treated as nusus.
2
Check the origin of the statement
For companion sayings: was it based on personal reasoning (ijtihad) or is it something that cannot be known through opinion? Only the former allows mafhum.
Ibn Nujaym added this condition to include only opinions of the Companions, not their reports of the Prophet.
3
Apply the mafhum principle
If the text is a valid fiqh narration (or everyday speech, rational proof), then specification (e.g., 'woman' in the ghusl rule) negates the ruling for others. In nusus, such specification does not negate.
Example: Hidayah's rule about women's braids in ghusl — by mafhum, men are obliged to undo their braids.
4
Note the internal Hanafi dispute
The zahir al-riwaya (authoritative transmitted position) rejects mafhum even in fiqh narrations, but the later relied-upon position (for fatwa) accepts it based on Imam Muhammad, al-Khasaf, and al-Sarakhsi.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi is the accepting view.
5
Apply to fatwa practice
When issuing a fatwa from a fiqh book, a mufti may use mafhum al-mukhalafah to deduce rulings. However, he cannot do so from the Qur'an or hadith without additional evidence.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in methodology.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the methodological rule (rasm al-mufti) regarding the validity of mafhum al-mukhalafah (the converse meaning of a restricted statement) in different types of texts. The author begins by citing al-Nahr al-Faiq which states that mafhum al-mukhalafah is valid by consensus in fiqh narrations (riwayat) and includes the sayings of the Companions. However, Sahib al-Nahr (Ibn Nujaym) qualifies this: only those Companion sayings that are based on opinion/ijtihad are included, not those that are beyond ijtihad (which are akin to nusus). Allama Ibn Abidin clarifies further: Companion sayings that cannot be known through ijtihad are like marfu' hadith, and in hadith, mafhum al-mukhalafah is not valid. So 'riwayat' in fiqh books refer to the narrations from mujtahids (including Companions when they opine), and in such books, mafhum is valid. The passage then illustrates this with two examples from Ghayat al-Bayan. First, the issue of ghusl: Hidayah says a woman need not undo her braids; the restriction 'woman' excludes men, and in fiqh narrations such specification negates others, unlike nusus. Second, the issue of a muhrim killing a predator: the narration of Umar implies that if the muhrim did not initiate, no penalty; this is an application of mafhum. Finally, it presents an internal Hanafi dispute: the zahir al-riwaya (authoritative transmitted position) of the Hanafi school, according to al-Zahiriyyah and Allama al-Birri, holds that mafhum al-mukhalafah is not permissible as evidence. However, Imam Muhammad in al-Siyar al-Kabir, Imam al-Khasaf, and others allowed it. The author resolves by noting that in fiqh narrations, everyday speech, and rational proofs, specification does negate others, but not in nusus (primary texts). This is the relied-upon position for ifta: mafhum al-mukhalafah is valid in fiqh narrations and related contexts, but not in Quran and hadith.

Key Terms
مفهوم مخالف (mafhum mukhalaf): The converse meaning of a restricted statement; the implication that the ruling applies only to the mentioned case and its opposite is negated.
روایات فقہیہ (riwayat fiqhiyyah): Narrations found in later Hanafi fiqh books (e.g., Hidayah, Kanz) that report rulings from mujtahids, including Companions, Successors, and later imams.
نصوص شرعیہ (nusus shar'iyyah): Primary revealed texts: the Qur'an and the Sunnah (hadith).

Mafhum al-Mukhalafah in Fatwa: Hanafi Rasm al-Mufti Rule
Discussion of mafhum al-mukhalafah in fatwa with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on mafhum al-mukhalafah, reconcile the apparent transmission with Imam Muhammad's view, and apply the condition that it must not contradict an explicit text.

Core Idea
Mafhum al-mukhalafah is valid in people's speech but not in the Lawgiver's; and never if it contradicts an explicit text.

This lesson examines a critical rule for muftis: can one derive a ruling from the opposite meaning? The Hanafi school has an internal divergence, resolved by distinguishing between divine and human speech. We'll trace the dispute and its fatwa application.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem from the 'Umar example
Examine the Hajj case: a muhrim kills a wild beast. 'Umar gave compensation only when he initiated the attack. The inference drawn is that without initiation, no compensation is due. This is an application of mafhum al-mukhalafah: the condition implies the opposite ruling when absent.
This example sets the stage for the dispute; note that the source is 'Umar's action, not direct Qur'an or Sunnah.
2
Identify the conflicting transmissions within the Hanafi school
The apparent transmission (zahir al-riwayah) holds that mafhum al-mukhalafah is not a valid proof. However, Imam Muhammad in al-Siyar al-Kabir and Imam al-Khassaf in Kitab al-Hiyal accept it and base rulings upon it. This creates a contradiction that must be reconciled.
Zahir al-riwayah is the primary authority for fatwa, but the later position of Imam Muhammad carries weight.
3
Recognize the reconciliation: distinction between Lawgiver's speech and others' speech
Allamah al-Barri and Allamah al-Shami resolve the conflict by distinguishing the speech of the Lawgiver (Qur'an and Sunnah) from the speech of ordinary people and jurists. In the Lawgiver's speech, mafhum is not relied upon because it is eloquent and may have purposes other than negation. In people's speech and juridical narrations, it is valid because their speech is straightforward.
The example from Qur'an 4:23 ('stepdaughters in your care') shows that the condition is descriptive, not limiting; it does not imply permissibility when the condition is absent.
4
Apply the condition of non-contradiction with an explicit text
Allamah al-Shami adds that even in permissible contexts, mafhum al-mukhalafah is only valid as long as it does not contradict a clear (sarih) text. If it does, the explicit text takes precedence.
This condition protects against overriding definitive rulings with mere inference.
5
Summarize the mufta bihi rule
The relied-upon position for fatwa: mafhum al-mukhalafah is not a proof in the Lawgiver's speech, but it is a valid inference in the speech of jurists and ordinary people, provided it does not contradict an explicit text.
A mufti may use it when interpreting earlier Hanafi jurists, but not when deriving rulings directly from the Qur'an and Hadith.

This passage is a discussion of the rasm rule regarding the validity of inference from mafhum al-mukhalafah (understanding the opposite meaning from a conditioned statement) in the context of issuing fatwas. It begins with an example from the rules of Hajj: if a wild beast attacks a muhrim (one in the state of ihram) and he kills it in self-defense, no compensation is due. This is based on the practice of 'Umar (RA), who gave compensation only when he initiated the attack. From this, the point is derived that the mention of a condition indicates the opposite ruling when the condition is absent. The passage then presents an internal Hanafi ikhtilaf: the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwayah) holds that mafhum al-mukhalafah is not a valid proof, while Imam Muhammad in al-Siyar al-Kabir and Imam al-Khassaf in al-Hiyal accept it. Al-Barri's commentary on al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir reconciles these views by explaining that in the speech of the Lawgiver (the Qur'an and Sunnah), mafhum al-mukhalafah is not used because the Lawgiver's speech is eloquent and may have purposes other than negation. For example, the verse about stepdaughters in one's care does not mean that stepdaughters not in one's care are permissible to marry; the condition is merely descriptive of the common situation. However, in people's ordinary speech and juridical narrations (riwayat fiqhiyyah), the understanding of the opposite meaning is valid because people's speech is straightforward and not laden with eloquence. Finally, al-Shami adds a critical refinement: mafhum al-mukhalafah is accepted only when it does not contradict an explicit (sarih) text. This is the relied-upon rule for fatwa.

مفهوم المخالف (mafhum al-mukhalafah): Inference from the opposite meaning: deducing the opposite ruling for the case where the mentioned condition is absent.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The apparent transmission: the well-known transmitted rulings from the early Hanafi masters, particularly from the six books of Imam Muhammad.
شارع (shari'): The Lawgiver: God through the Prophet (Qur'an and Sunnah).

Mafhum Mukhalif: Validity in Lawgiver's Speech vs. People's Speech
Explaining the rule of mafhum mukhalif and its condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on the validity of mafhum mukhalif, distinguish between Shar'i and non-Shar'i speech, and apply the condition that it must not contradict an explicit text.

Core Idea
Mafhum mukhalif is valid only in people's speech, not in the Lawgiver's, unless it contradicts an explicit text.

In fatwa, understanding when an inferred opposite meaning (mafhum mukhalif) can be used is crucial. This passage from 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti explains that the Hanafi school restricts its use: it is valid only in ordinary people's speech, not in the speech of the Lawgiver (Quran and Sunnah). Additionally, even in people's speech, mafhum mukhalif is overridden by any explicit text. Let's dissect this rule step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source and its authority
This passage is from Sharh 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti by Allama Shami (Ibn Abidin). It is a later Hanafi fatwa manual that synthesizes earlier views. The summary by Allama Shami is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Note the sources cited: Sarakhsi, Sirajiyya, Barri, Khasaf, and Tarsusi.
2
Understand the basic rule: mafhum mukhalif is valid in non-Shar'i speech
Allama Shami states that mafhum mukhalif is considered valid evidence only in the speech of people (kalam al-nas), not in the speech of the Lawgiver (Shari'). The reason is the eloquence of Shari' speech, which may have purposes beyond apparent restriction.
Example: Quran 4:23 'your stepdaughters in your custody' – the phrase 'in your custody' is descriptive, not restrictive.
3
Recognize the exception: condition against explicit text
Even in people's speech, mafhum mukhalif is only valid if it does not contradict an explicit (sareeh) text. If there is a clear direct statement, the explicit text takes precedence over the inferred opposite meaning.
The principle 'al-sareeh muqaddam 'ala al-mafhum' (explicit over implied) is used.
4
Apply the rule to a fatwa scenario
When issuing a fatwa, a mufti cannot rely on mafhum mukhalif from a Quranic verse or hadith. For example, from 'do not eat pork' one cannot infer that eating other meats is permissible by mafhum; the explicit general permissibility of halal meats covers it. Similarly, in people's contracts, if someone says 'I sell you this house if you pay within a month', the mafhum (if not paid, no sale) is valid, provided no explicit clause contradicts it.
Contrast with Shafi'i school: they may accept mafhum mukhalif in some Qur'anic contexts.

Distinction Between Custom (ʿUrf) and Consensus (Ijmāʿ)
Critical refinement of the distinction between custom and consensus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state five differences between ʿurf and ijmāʿ and apply each difference to evaluate the legal authority of a given practice.

Core Idea
Custom is mutable, majority-based, and open to ijtihād; consensus is definitive, mujtahid-based, and closes ijtihād.

In Hanafi usul, custom (ʿurf) and consensus (ijmāʿ) share some resemblance but differ fundamentally. This lesson dissects five key distinctions that determine how each source is used in legal derivation. Understanding these differences is essential for a mufti when weighing the authority of common practice against settled scholarly agreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of each source
Note that custom arises from common people's sayings and actions (aqwāl wa afʿāl), while consensus requires unanimous agreement of all mujtahids. The scope of inclusion is different: majority suffices for custom, but unanimity is required for consensus.
2
Compare definitiveness and changeability
Consensus produces a definitive (qaṭʿī) ruling that cannot change; custom yields a ruling that can change, and sometimes must change when custom evolves (taghyīr al-ʿurf).
3
Assess authority against textual proof (naṣṣ)
Consensus is authoritative even when facing a text, because it is itself a definitive proof. Custom, however, is overridden by any clear text (naṣṣ qaṭʿī). If custom contradicts text, text prevails.
4
Examine establishment conditions
Consensus is formed by a single occurrence of agreement (ittifāq wāḥid); custom requires repetition and continuity (takrār wa istimrār) to be authoritative.
5
Determine impact on ijtihād
Once consensus is formed, no further ijtihād is allowed on that matter. Custom, however, leaves the door of ijtihād open even after it is established.

Commentary
This passage is a lesson in Hanafi usul al-fiqh distinguishing between two important sources of law: custom (ʿurf) and consensus (ijmāʿ). It lists five precise differences. First, custom arises from common people's practice, while consensus requires unanimous agreement of all mujtahids; a majority suffices for custom but not for consensus. Second, consensus yields a definitive (qaṭʿī) ruling that cannot change, whereas a ruling based on custom is subject to change, and sometimes must change when custom evolves. Third, consensus is authoritative over a clear text (naṣṣ) because it is itself definitive, but custom is not: if custom contradicts a text, the text is followed. Fourth, consensus is established by a single occurrence, while custom requires repetition and continuity (takrār wa istimrār) to be authoritative. Fifth, once consensus is formed, no further ijtihād is allowed, but custom leaves room for ijtihād even after it is established. After these distinctions, the text provides the evidentiary basis for the authority of custom. The primary proof is Quran 2:233, where 'maʿrūf' (known good) refers to the customary maintenance in society. Second, the hadith of Hind bint ʿUtbah allows her to take her husband's wealth 'bi al-maʿrūf', i.e., according to what is customary among their peers. Third, the text cites the consensus of jurists that custom is a valid source; even scholars who rarely accept it affirm its overall authority. This passage reinforces the Hanafi position that custom, while not as strong as consensus or text, is a legitimate secondary source for deriving rulings, especially in transactions and family law.

اجماع (ijmāʿ): Consensus of all mujtahids of an age on a legal ruling, which is definitive (qaṭʿī) and closes the door of ijtihād.
معروف (maʿrūf): That which is recognized as good and customary in a given society; used in the Quran and hadith to indicate the standard of custom.

Custom is a binding source in Hanafi iftaa, supported by Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and juristic practice.

Custom (urf) is one of the recognized sources of legislation in the Hanafi school, especially in non-ritual matters such as contracts, family law, and oaths. This lesson surveys the evidence for its authority from four pillars: the Quran, the Sunnah, the consensus (ijma) of the ummah, and the explicit statements of later Hanafi jurists. By examining these proofs, you will understand not only why custom is authoritative but also how a mufti should apply it responsibly in his era.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
General custom can specify texts; specific custom has limited authority — their definitions determine their juristic effect.

Understanding the two types of custom (‘urf) is essential for a mufti because each carries a different legal weight. This lesson defines ‘urf ‘āmm and ‘urf khāṣṣ as presented by the majority and by a contemporary scholar, setting the foundation for knowing when a custom can override a general ruling or must be set aside.

Guided Path
1

Both definitions agree on the concept of widespread prevalence.

The key is limited scope; this restricts its legal authority.

Validating Custom Against Scriptural Texts: General vs. Specific Custom
Explaining when general and specific custom are legally valid despite conflicting with texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for validating general and specific custom when they conflict with scriptural texts, and identify the relied-upon position for specific custom.

Core Idea
Custom can specify a text, but only general custom is valid for specification; specific custom is rejected due to uncertainty.

This lesson explores the Hanafi methodology for evaluating custom ('urf) when it appears to conflict with clear texts. Understanding when custom is accepted over a text or analogy is crucial for issuing fatwas in complex cases. We will learn the three scenarios of conflict and the special treatment of general versus specific custom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of custom
Determine whether the custom is general ('urf 'amm) or specific ('urf khass). General custom is widespread and known to the majority; specific custom is confined to a particular region or group.
This distinction is the foundation for the different rulings.
2
Assess the degree of conflict with the text
Classify the conflict into one of three categories: no conflict, total conflict (acting on custom would require abandoning the text entirely), or partial conflict (custom only specifies some instances of a general text).
The ruling varies based on this classification.
3
Apply the rule for general custom
If the conflict is partial, general custom is valid by consensus and can serve as a specifier (mukhassis) for the text. It can also override analogy (qiyas).
This is the majority Hanafi position.
4
Apply the rule for specific custom
Under partial conflict, specific custom is not valid for specification per the relied-upon position (al-madhhab). The reason given is that its limited scope creates doubt, and specification based on doubt is impermissible.
Some scholars disagreed, but the tarjih is against specific custom.
5
Consider the fatwa implications
When issuing a fatwa, verify whether the custom is truly general or specific. If specific, the text prevails; if general and only partially conflicting, custom may be accepted.
This safeguards against arbitrary overruling of clear texts.

Distinction Between General and Specific Custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'general custom establishes a general ruling, specific custom establishes a specific ruling' to identify the scope of a custom's legal effect and determine its authority relative to Zahir al-Riwayah and qiyas.

Core Idea
Custom's scope determines its authority: general custom binds all, specific custom binds only its locality.

As a mufti, you will often encounter local customs that seem to contradict established Hanafi texts. This lesson from Rasm al-Mufti provides a crucial distinction: general custom ('urf 'amm) affects all regions, while specific custom ('urf khass) is limited to its locality. Understanding this principle will help you determine when to override Zahir al-Riwayah and when to restrict a custom's application.

Identify the distinction in effect
General custom creates a ruling that applies to all regions and people within the scope of that custom. Specific custom creates a ruling that applies only to its particular locality.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the distinction between general custom ('urf 'amm) and specific custom ('urf khass) in the context of a mufti's practice. The author begins by stating a rule: a general ruling (hukm 'amm) is not established by a specific custom. Then he reports that al-Hamawi inferred from this by contrapositive (mafhum mukhalif) that a specific ruling can be established by a specific custom. Ibn 'Abidin is then quoted in support, presenting a question-and-answer format: if a mufti must follow custom even contrary to Zahir al-Riwayah texts, is there a difference between general and specific custom? The answer is no difference in principle—both types of custom are to be followed—but the effect differs: general custom establishes a general ruling applicable everywhere, while specific custom establishes a specific ruling only for its locality. This is the core principle: custom's ruling applies to its people, general or specific. Then the commentary elaborates in Urdu, explaining that if either general or specific custom contradicts a ruling of Zahir al-Riwayah that is not based on an explicit primary text (nass), then Zahir al-Riwayah is abandoned and custom is followed. This is supported by Ibn 'Abidin and by the Islamic Fiqh Academy seminar. The passage ends with a discussion of custom vs. qiyas: by consensus, general custom takes precedence over qiyas and qiyas is abandoned. However, specific custom does not ordinarily justify abandoning qiyas—except, as Maulana Khalid Saifullah Rahmani notes, if the qiyas is not based on an explicitly textually-designated cause (illah mansusah), then specific custom may also override qiyas.

Key Terms
عرف عام ('urf 'amm): A custom prevalent in all regions or among all people, establishing a general ruling.
عرف خاص ('urf khass): A custom limited to a specific locality, establishing a ruling only for that locality.
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The established and well-known transmitted texts of the Hanafi school, primarily the six books of Imam Muhammad.

While the general rule requires a mufti to follow the preponderant (rajih) opinion, the Hanafi school recognizes exceptional circumstances. Duress here means that acting upon the rajih opinion becomes 'extremely difficult' (intihai dushwar), and the weak opinion provides ease. This is not a mere preference but a genuine hardship.

This is a case-by-case assessment, not a blanket permission.

Commentary
This passage clearly distinguishes between the roles of mufti and qadi regarding the use of weak opinions. While a mufti may, under certain conditions (as discussed in previous lessons), issue a fatwa based on a weak opinion, a qadi is absolutely bound to judge only by the strong (rajih) and well-known (mashhur) opinion of his madhhab. The principle that a weak opinion becomes strong by the qadi's judgment applies only to a mujtahid qadi, not to a muqallid.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A mufti with insight may use weak narrations; a qadi is bound by the rajih opinion.

In Hanafi fatwa practice, not all scholarly opinions carry equal weight. This passage from Khazanat al-Riwayat explains a crucial distinction: a knowledgeable mufti can sometimes act on a weak narration, even against his madhhab, while a qadi is strictly bound to the preponderant (rajih) opinion. Understanding why this difference exists is essential for anyone issuing fatwas or serving as a judge.

Three Exceptions to Giving Fatwa from Another Madhhab
Explains three permissible scenarios for leaving one's madhhab in fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three scenarios where a Hanafi mufti may depart from his madhhab and apply each exception correctly.

Core Idea
A muqallid mufti must follow his madhhab's rajih opinion, but necessity, clear hadith, or a mujtahid qadi's ruling permit departure.

The default rule in Hanafi ifta' methodology is that a muqallid mufti is bound to issue fatwa according to the preponderant (rajih) opinion of his own madhhab. However, there are three exceptional situations where departing from one's madhhab becomes permissible. This lesson unpacks each exception, explaining the conditions and rationale behind them, as outlined in the classical work 'Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuh' and its commentary.

Guided Path
1
Understand the default rule
State the general principle: a muqallid mufti must only give fatwa from the rajih opinion of his own madhhab because the mustafti seeks that madhhab's ruling.
This is the baseline; exceptions are limited to three cases.
2
Analyze the first exception: Darurah
Identify the first scenario: when following one's madhhab causes severe hardship (but not life-threatening). Distinguish darurah from idtirar. Note that it permits a weak (da'if) opinion from another madhhab.
The author emphasizes darurah here is a lower level than idtirar.
3
Analyze the second exception: Expert Mufti with Clear Hadith
Recognize the second scenario: the mufti is an expert in his madhhab and discovers a clear, authentic hadith that contradicts his imam's view without strong evidence for the imam's position. He may then adopt another mujtahid's view that matches the hadith.
This is conditional on the mufti's expertise; he may not publicize it without need.
4
Analyze the third exception: Mujtahid Qadi's Ruling
Understand the third scenario: a mujtahid qadi, not bound to any madhhab, issues a judgment contrary to his own madhhab on a mujtahid fih issue. That judgment is enforceable, and the mufti must confirm its validity even if it conflicts with his madhhab.
Based on the principle 'hukm al-hakim yarfa' al-khilaf'.

Those three scenarios are as follows: First scenario: Giving fatwa on the basis of necessity (darurah). Among the specific situations in which it is permissible to abandon one's own imam's madhhab and give fatwa according to another madhhab, the first is necessity, severe difficulty, hardship, constriction, and widespread affliction. That is, when in a particular issue of one's madhhab there is such constriction that acting upon it would cause unbearable hardship, then to remove that constriction and fulfill the need of the mustafti, it is permissible to give fatwa according to a weak (da'if) opinion. (Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuh, 244) It is clear that here 'darurah' does not mean the well-known type that the jurists term 'idtirar' (dire necessity); rather, it is a state lower than idtirar, due to which a person falls into severe hardship, even though there is no threat to life or limb. (Muhadarat Usul al-Fiqh: 365, Jadid Fiqhi Mubahith, known as Maqalat As'adi 3/232) Second scenario: The mufti being expert in his own madhhab. The second state where it is permissible to abandon one's own madhhab and act according to another madhhab is that the mufti is expert in his madhhab, has good knowledge of evidences, has deep insight into the Qur'an and Sunnah, even though he does not attain the level of ijtihad. However, if he acquires knowledge of a certain authentic hadith whose indication to its meaning is absolutely clear, and he does not find any strong evidence for his imam's statement in opposition to that hadith, then it is permissible for him to adopt the statement of that mujtahid whose practice conforms to that noble hadith. But it is not permissible for him to give fatwa according to that statement without necessity. (Derived from Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuh 259, Muhadarat Usul al-Fiqh: 349) Third scenario: A mujtahid qadi's judgment contrary to his madhhab. Shaykh al-Islam Mufti Taqi 'Uthmani says: The third scenario of permissibility of ifta' according to a non-madhhab is that the ruler appoints a person who is a mujtahid to the office of qada', and at the time of appointment, he does not bind that qadi to follow any specific madhhab. Then that qadi gives a judgment regarding an issue that is contrary to his own madhhab; that judgment will be enforceable, provided the issue is among those where ijtihad is permissible (mujtahid fih). Therefore, if a mufti is asked regarding such judgments of the qadi, the mufti will give fatwa of the enforceability of those judgments, even if that judgment is contrary to the mufti's own madhhab. The proof for this statement is that the eminent jurists are agreed that the ruler's order and the qadi's judgment terminate the prior disagreement (i.e., when the issue is subject to ijtihad and the qadi gives a judgment, it becomes obligatory for the parties to act upon it). (Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuh li al-'Uthmani: 263)

Commentary
This passage presents a fundamental rasm al-mufti principle: a muqallid mufti is bound to give fatwa according to the rajih (preponderant) opinion of his own madhhab. The reason is that the mustafti comes to him precisely to learn the ruling of that madhhab. However, the text then provides three exceptions where it becomes permissible to depart from one's madhhab. 

First exception: Darurah (necessity). Importantly, the author clarifies that 'darurah' here does not mean the classical 'idtirar' (which threatens life or limb). Rather, it means a situation of severe hardship (haraj) and constriction, where following one's madhhab would cause unbearable difficulty. In such a case, the mufti may give fatwa according to a weak (da'if) opinion from another madhhab to relieve the hardship. This is a broader concession than the standard rule of idtirar.

Second exception: When the mufti is a specialist (expert) in his own madhhab and finds a clear, unambiguous authentic hadith that contradicts his imam's position, and he cannot find a strong evidence for his imam's view. Then he may adopt the statement of another mujtahid that conforms to that hadith. However, this is conditional on the mufti's expertise and the clarity of the hadith. Even then, he may not give fatwa according to that opinion without necessity (i.e., he should not publicize it unnecessarily).

Third exception: When a mujtahid qadi, who was not bound to a particular madhhab, issues a judgment contrary to his own madhhab, and the issue is a matter of ijtihad (mujtahid fih). That judgment is enforceable, and a mufti asked about it must affirm its enforceability, even if it contradicts his own madhhab. This is based on the principle that a judge's ruling resolves prior disagreement (hukm al-hakim yarfa' al-khilaf).

The passage is from a later Hanafi work (Naseem Ul Mufti) dealing with the rules of ifta'. The source references include Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuh by Mufti Taqi 'Uthmani, which is a modern Hanafi work. Therefore, the authority weight is from later fatwa literature, not from the classical Zahir al-Riwayah. The ruling is widely accepted in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
مقلد (muqallid): A non-mujtahid who follows a specific madhhab (here, the Hanafi madhhab)
مفتی (mufti): A scholar qualified to issue fatwas, typically a muqallid if not a mujtahid
راجح (rajih): The preponderant or stronger opinion within the madhhab
ضرورت (darurah): A situation of severe hardship (not life-threatening) that permits departure from one's madhhab
مجتہد فیہ (mujtahid fih): An issue where ijtihad is permissible, i.e., not definitively settled by clear text or consensus

Conditions for a Mufti to Follow Another Madhhab Due to a Clear Hadith
Rule from Usul al-Iftaa on when a mufti may depart from his madhhab.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The rule is highly restrictive: it requires deep expertise, a sound hadith with unambiguous indication, and the absence of any strong counter-evidence from his own imam. This principle balances adherence to the madhhab with fidelity to clear textual proof.

Mujtahid Judge's Verdict Overriding Madhhab: The Mufti's Duty
Explains that a mujtahid judge not bound to a madhhab can issue verdicts that override earlier differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a mufti must affirm a judge's verdict that contradicts his own madhhab, and apply the maxim that a judge's ruling removes disagreement.

Core Idea
A mujtahid judge's verdict in a mujtahad fih matter is binding even if it contradicts the mufti's madhhab.

This lesson addresses a key scenario in fatwa practice: when a mufti is asked about a judge's verdict that goes against his own madhhab. The ruling is not about the mufti's personal preference but about the binding force of a qualified judge's decision. The central principle is the consensus (ittifaq) among fuqaha that a judge's ruling removes prior disagreement, provided the matter is open to ijtihad (mujtahad fih). Understanding this rule is crucial for muftis to maintain legal finality and consistency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three key roles
The scenario involves a ruler (who appoints the judge), a mujtahid judge (who is not bound to a specific madhhab), and a mufti (who is asked about the verdict). Recognize that the judge's ijtihad capacity is central.
2
State the condition for the verdict's enforceability
The verdict is enforceable only if the matter is 'mujtahad fih'—i.e., open to ijtihad, not settled by definitive text (nass qat'i). This is the key prerequisite.
3
Apply the maxim 'Hukm al-Hakim Yarfa' al-Khilaf'
The consensus-based maxim holds that a judge's ruling ends any prior difference of opinion between the parties. Here, the judge's verdict becomes binding, and the mufti cannot reject it based on his own madhhab.
4
Determine the mufti's duty
When asked, the mufti must affirm the enforceability of the verdict, even if it contradicts his own madhhab. The mufti does not have the authority to overturn the judge's decision in mujtahad fih matters.
