Error vs. Jest in Sarih Talaq: Qada and Diyanah
Distinguishing qada and diyanah in erroneous and jesting divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between qada and diyanah to cases of erroneous and jesting sarih talaq, and cite the hadith that creates the exception for jest.

Core Idea
Jesting divorce binds in both qada and diyanah; mistaken divorce binds only in qada.

This passage tackles a subtle but critical distinction in Hanafi fatwa: the difference between a divorce uttered by mistake (khiṭāʾ) and one uttered in jest (hazl). Both lack genuine intent, yet they are treated differently because of a clear textual exception. Understanding this will sharpen your ability to issue correct fatwas when intention is contested.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Locate the two situations discussed: error (khiṭāʾ) – e.g. intending to say 'Subḥānallāh' but saying 'Anti ṭāliq' – and jest (hazl) – saying divorce playfully without serious intent.
Both involve lack of true intention, but the ruling differs.
2
Apply the default rule for ṣarīḥ
Recall that ṣarīḥ words of divorce are effective in qaḍā (judicially) without need for intention, but in diyānah (religiously) intention is required. This is why erroneous divorce is valid in court but not before Allah.
The default rule is based on the principle that explicit words are taken at face value for outward rulings.
3
Note the exception for jest
The author cites a hadith: 'Three things are serious whether done in earnest or in jest: marriage, divorce, and revocation.' This overrides the analogy with error, making jesting divorce binding in both qaḍā and diyānah.
The hadith is found in Sunan Abī Dāwūd (2194) and is accepted by the Hanafis.
4
Apply in fatwa
When a mufti receives a case of contested intention, he must first classify the utterance as ṣarīḥ or kināyah. If ṣarīḥ, he then checks whether the scenario is error (qada only) or jest (both).
This distinction prevents wrong rulings that could lead to illegal continuation of marriage.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi rasm rule concerning the effect of intention on sarih (explicit) words of divorce. The author distinguishes between two scenarios: khiṭā (mistake) and hazl (jest). In the case of mistake—when a person intends to say something else but slips and says 'Anti taliq'—the divorce is effective in qaḍā (judicially) but not in diyānah (religiously, before Allah). This is a key rule for muftis: when issuing a fatwa on divorce, they must know the difference between sarih and kinayah, and when intention is required.

Intention in 'Anti Taliq' and 'Anti al-Talaq'
Ruling on validity of intention in sarih and masdar divorce phrases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm rule to determine whether a husband's intention of a specific number of divorces is valid in the phrases 'Anti taliq' and 'Anti al-talaq' and state the resulting divorce.

Core Idea
Intention of three or bain is invalid in 'Anti taliq'; intention of three is valid but intention of two is invalid in 'Anti al-talaq'.

This lesson examines a key rule from Hanafi rasm al-mufti: when a husband uses explicit divorce wording, his intention of a specific number is not always effective. The rule distinguishes between the adjective 'taliq' and the masdar 'al-talaq' based on linguistic principles. Understanding this distinction is essential for muftis to correctly adjudicate claims of intended multiplicity.

Guided Path
1
Classify the phrase
Determine whether the utterance is 'Anti ṭāliq' (an adjective) or 'Anti al-ṭalāq' (a masdar). Both are ṣarīḥ (explicit).
2
Apply the rule for 'Anti ṭāliq'
Because 'ṭāliq' is a singular feminine attribute, it necessarily implies a single divorce by iqtiḍā. Any intention of three or bain is an intention of a meaning the word does not bear, so it is invalid. Only one rajʿī divorce occurs.
This is the ruling from Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah.
3
Apply the rule for 'Anti al-ṭalāq'
The masdar 'al-ṭalāq' inherently indicates oneness (tawaḥḥud). However, this oneness can be essential (dhātī) or generic (nawʿī). Intention of three is valid because it interprets the masdar as a type (nawʿī). Intention of two is invalid because two is a specific number that contradicts oneness.
No intention needed for one rajʿī default.
4
Consider the slave exception
If the wife is a slave, then in 'Anti al-ṭalāq', intention of two becomes valid because for a slave the complete limit is two, so two is considered a type of oneness (nawʿī).
For a free woman, only three can be intended as nawʿī oneness.

This passage is a Hanafi rasm rule about the validity of intention in two types of divorce wording: (1) 'Anti ṭāliq' (a ṣarīḥ word) and (2) 'Anti al-ṭalāq' (a masdar word). Kinayah Talaq: The Role of Intention and Muzakarah in Diyanah and Qada
Ruling on kinayah divorce requiring intention and the role of muzakarah in qada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on kinayah divorce to distinguish between diyanah and qada, and identify when intention or muzakarah is required.

Core Idea
Kinayah words require intention for divorce in diyanah; in qada, muzakarah suffices.

This lesson examines a key rasm rule in Hanafi fiqh: how kinayah (allusive) divorce words are treated. In diyanah, intention is necessary; in qada, prior discussion (muzakarah) can substitute for intention. There is an exception for the word 'al-haram' when custom dictates. Understanding this is critical for muftis giving fatwas and qadis issuing judgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main legal rule
State the general rule for kinayah: divorce occurs only with intention in diyanah.
2
Distinguish diyanah and qada
Understand that intention is required from a religious perspective (diyanah), but for judicial ruling (qada), context can stand in.
3
Apply the muzakarah substitute
Recognize that 'muzakarah' (prior discussion) replaces intention only in qada, not in diyanah.
4
Handle the exception for 'al-haram'
Note that custom ('urf) can make 'al-haram' a direct indication of divorce, so intention or muzakarah is not needed for either diyanah or qada when the husband's community uses it that way.
5
Summarize practical implications
When a mufti encounters a kinayah word, he must inquire about intention (for diyanah) and about prior discussion or custom (for qada).

Commentary

Intention in Ruj'ah: Sarih vs Kinayah Words
Ruling on intention in ruj'ah for sarih and kinayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule distinguishing explicit and implicit words in ruj'ah and determine when intention is required for validity in qada.

Core Idea
Explicit words need no intention; implicit words require intention in qada.

In Hanafi law, the validity of revoking a revocable divorce (ruj'ah) depends on the type of words used. The school distinguishes between explicit (sarih) and implicit (kinayah) expressions. This lesson explains when intention is required for ruj'ah to be effective in qada (court). This distinction is important for muftis and judges who must rule on ambiguous statements.

Guided Path
1
Define ruj'ah
Ruj'ah is the act of a husband taking back his wife during her iddah after a revocable divorce. Understand its purpose: seeking continuation of marriage.
2
Distinguish sarih and kinayah
Sarih words clearly indicate ruj'ah without context (e.g., 'I take you back'). Kinayah words are ambiguous and require context or intention (e.g., 'You are my wife').
This distinction is common in Hanafi fiqh for divorce, oaths, and other chapters.
3
State the rule for sarih
For sarih words, intention is not required for validity in qada. The court considers the apparent meaning sufficient.
4
State the rule for kinayah
For kinayah words, intention is required. Without niyyah, the ruj'ah is invalid in qada.
5
Apply to examples
Test given scenarios: 'I take you back' (sarih, no intention needed) vs. 'You are my wife' (kinayah, intention needed).
Also understand that if a husband uses kinayah without intending ruj'ah, the marriage is not restored.

When a Name Becomes a Divorce Word: The Role of Intention
Furuʿ applying the maxim of intention in speech acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim that intention is required for legal effect in speech acts, distinguish between judicial acceptance and religious belief, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of this principle.

Core Idea
A word used as a name does not effect a legal consequence without intention; but court and conscience differ.

This passage presents several derived rulings (furuʿ) from the principle that intention shapes the legal effect of speech acts. The central case: if a husband calls his wife 'Taliq' because that is her name, and he did not intend divorce, no divorce occurs according to the famous Hanafi position. The same applies to calling a slave 'Hurr' as a name. Yet there is an intra-madhab dispute, and a further nuance arises when a man claims his immediate divorce was intended as a condition. Understanding these distinctions is critical for a mufti who must navigate between judicial reality and private conscience.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Base Maxim
Recognize that the passage builds on the major maxim 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by intentions). The ruling hinges on whether the speaker intended a legal effect (talaq or itq) or simply used a name.
This maxim is one of the five major maxims (al-qawaʿid al-kulliya al-khams).
2
Analyze the Primary Furuʿ
Examine the case of saying 'ya taliq' to a wife named Taliq. The mashhur position: no divorce occurs because the word is a name, not a declaration. Contrast with al-Mahbubi's distinction between talaq and itq.
The analogy with 'ya hurr' for a slave named Hurr is explicitly used.
3
Map the Intra-Madhab Dispute
Al-Mahbubi held that manumission (itq) occurs even without intention, while divorce requires intention. The text rejects this as khilaf al-mashhur. Determine the thamarah al-khilaf in practice.
The mashhur position is relied upon for fatwa.
4
Distinguish Qada and Diyana
Study the second furuʿ: if a man utters an immediate divorce (tanjiz) but claims he intended a conditional (taʿliq), the court does not accept his claim because the apparent wording is clear. However, in his private religious conscience (diyanatan), he is believed if truthful.
This distinction is vital for a mufti: judicial decisions are based on external evidence, but religious accountability considers internal states.

Commentary
This passage presents a furu' (derived ruling) that applies the principle that literal expressions require intention for legal effect. The text examines the case where a husband calls his wife 'O Taliq' because that is her name, without intending to divorce her. The Hanafi scholars ruled that no divorce occurs because the word was used as a name, not as a declaration. This is analogous to calling a slave 'O Hurr' when that is his name, where manumission does not occur if not intended. However, al-Mahbubi in al-Talqih made a distinction: for divorce, it does not occur, but for manumission, it does occur even without intention. The text notes that this distinction is contrary to the famous (mashhur) position, which treats both similarly based on intention.
