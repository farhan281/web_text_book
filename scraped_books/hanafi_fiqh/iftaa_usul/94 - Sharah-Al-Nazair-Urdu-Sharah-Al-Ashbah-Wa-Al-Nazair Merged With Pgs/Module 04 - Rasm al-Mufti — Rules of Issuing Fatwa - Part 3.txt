The Divorced Woman's Claim of Extended Purity and Ongoing Iddah
Rasm al-mufti ruling on evidentiary weight of woman's claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm al-mufti rule that a divorced woman's claim of ongoing iddah is accepted, and identify the exception for pregnancy claims including the two-year limit and no-recourse provision.

Core Idea
The woman's word is law for iddah; pregnancy claims get two years of maintenance with no repayment.

This lesson examines a key ruling from the Hanafi rasm al-mufti on how a mufti should handle a divorced woman's claim that her iddah has not ended. The ruling is grounded in the principle of istishab (presumption of continuity), includes an important exception for pregnancy, and sets a maximum duration beyond which the husband cannot reclaim maintenance. Understanding this rule is essential for fatwa issuance in family law cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and default ruling
The divorced woman claims her period of purity (tahr) has extended and her iddah has not ended. The default ruling is that her statement is accepted and she is entitled to maintenance (nafaqah).
The default does not require her to bring witnesses or proof.
2
Understand the legal basis: istishab
The ruling relies on the principle of istishab (presumption of continuity): the original state (asl) is that the iddah continues until proven otherwise. Since the woman is the one experiencing the bodily state, her claim is given weight.
This principle is a major maxim: 'al-asl baqa' ma kana 'ala ma kana'.
3
Note the exception: pregnancy claim
If the woman claims that she is pregnant (haml), the rule changes: she still gets maintenance, but only for a maximum of two years. After two years, if it becomes clear there was no pregnancy, the husband cannot reclaim the maintenance paid.
The two-year period is based on the Hanafi view of the maximum duration of pregnancy (see Fath al-Qadir).
4
Apply the time limit and no-recourse rule
If two years pass and it is revealed that she was not pregnant, the husband has no right to take back the maintenance already given. This prevents hardship for the woman.
This is a protective measure for the woman's financial stability.
5
Recognize the source authority
This ruling is cited from Fath al-Qadir, a standard Hanafi fatwa reference. It is part of the relied-upon (mufta bihi) fatwa in the Hanafi school.
Fath al-Qadir is a commentary by Ibn al-Humam, widely used for fatwa.

Rule of Original Innocence in Financial Disputes
Establishing and applying the principle of original innocence in financial claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the Hanafi principle of asl bara'at al-dhimma to resolve disputes over financial obligations, and construct a fatwa using the defendant's statement under oath.

Core Idea
The default is freedom from liability; the claimant bears the burden of proof.

This passage from al-Qadeer lays out a fundamental rasm rule in Hanafi fatwa: the presumption of innocence (bara'at al-dhimma). In financial disputes, the burden is on the claimant to prove their claim, and the defendant's statement under oath is accepted because it aligns with the original state. We'll explore several practical applications and address a subtle objection regarding the minimum number of dirhams in an acknowledgment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default principle
Understand the statement: 'the original state is that a person's liability is free.' This is the key presumption (al-asl) in Hanafi mu'amalat.
al-asl bara'at al-dhimma – the default is innocence.
2
Apply the burden of proof
For each scenario (sale, usurpation, acknowledgment), identify who is the mudda'i (claimant) – the one whose claim contradicts the default. The mudda'i bears the burden of proof (bayyinah).
The defendant's statement is accepted because it aligns with the default.
3
Determine whose statement is accepted
In each case, see whose word is taken: the buyer (liability for price not proven), the usurper/destroyer (liability for excess not proven), the acknowledging person (he defines the obligation).
The defendant's oath is required if no evidence.
4
Resolve the dirhams objection
Acknowledging 'dirhams' (plural) triggers a dispute: is it 2 or 3? The Hanafi position: the well-known meaning of the plural is at least 3, so 3 dirhams are owed.
Linguistic 'urf' overrides the general presumption of minimal liability.
5
Synthesize the rule for fatwa
Formulate the rule: The mufti must first identify the default state, then allocate burden of proof. The defendant's statement with oath is accepted unless the claimant provides bayyinah.
This is a core tool for issuing fatwas in financial disputes.

Commentary
This passage presents a rasm rule of Hanafi fatwa: the default principle (al-asl) is that the liability (dhimma) of a person is free from obligations. Therefore, when there is a dispute over financial obligations, the burden of proof falls on the claimant (mudda'i), while the defendant's statement under oath is accepted because it aligns with the original state. Several applications illustrate this: (1) In a sale where the parties dispute the price or description, the buyer's word is taken because the seller's liability for the price is not established by default. Relatedly, one witness is insufficient to prove an obligation; two are required. (2) In disputes over the value of destroyed or usurped property, the destroyer/usurper's statement (claiming a lower value) is accepted because excess over the admitted amount is not proven. (3) In cases of iqrar (acknowledgment) where the value is unspecified, the acknowledging person's statement is accepted. The passage resolves an objection: if one acknowledges 'dirhams' (plural), the minimum plural is three dirhams by well-known usage, so three are owed, not two. This rule is a subset of the general principle that certainty is not removed by doubt.

Key Terms
اصل برائت ذمہ (asl bara'at al-dhimma): The default presumption that a person's liability is free from any obligation until proven otherwise.
مدعی (mudda'i): The claimant who asserts a right against another, which goes against the original state.
بینه (bayyinah): Legal proof, typically two witnesses or equivalent, required to establish a claim against the default.

Doubt in Sajdah and Rak'ahs during Fajr
Presents Hanafi rules for resolving doubt in prostration and rak'ah count during Fajr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for doubt in the second prostration of Fajr and apply Imam Muhammad's rectification method for doubt occurring in the first prostration, justifying the tarjih.

Core Idea
Doubt in Fajr's rak'ah count is rectifiable only in the first prostration; second prostration invalidates.

This passage from Bazzaziyya provides essential rules for a mufti dealing with doubt (shakk) in Fajr prayer. The ruling depends critically on when the doubt occurs: during the first or second prostration. Understanding this distinction and the underlying reasoning of Imam Muhammad is key to applying the relied-upon fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two doubt scenarios
The passage distinguishes between doubt occurring in the second prostration of a rak'ah (generic) and doubt about the total number of rak'ahs in Fajr (two vs. three). Focus on the second scenario.
2
Distinguish rulings based on prostration stage
If doubt about rak'ah count occurs in the second prostration, the prayer is invalid (fasid). If it occurs in the first prostration, rectification is possible according to Imam Muhammad.
The first prostration is the one where the rak'ah is not yet fully established.
3
Understand Imam Muhammad's reasoning
Imam Muhammad holds that the rak'ah is completed by raising the head from the first prostration. Abandoning that prostration nullifies it like a hadath (event), so the prayer can be saved by standing and adding a rak'ah.
This is based on his view of mahiyyah (essence) of the rak'ah.
4
Note the intra-madhhab dispute
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf disagree; they apply the principle that certainty is not removed by doubt, leading to invalidation. The passage sides with Muhammad.
The dispute is recorded in Bazzaziyya, a later fatwa compilation.
5
Apply the tarjih (mufta bihi)
The relied-upon position (al-mukhtar) is Imam Muhammad's view: rectification is possible in the first prostration. This is the operative fatwa.
A mufti must know this to give correct guidance.

Commentary
This passage from Bazzaziyya provides rulings on doubt in Fajr prayer. The first rule: if one doubts in the second prostration of a rak'ah (whether it is the first or second rak'ah), he must complete that prostration because it is obligatory in any case. After raising his head from that prostration, he sits (for the first tashahhud) and then stands to perform an additional rak'ah with two prostrations, and concludes with sajdat al-sahw. The second rule deals with doubt about the number of rak'ahs: if a person in Fajr is uncertain whether he has prayed two or three rak'ahs, the ruling depends on which prostration the doubt occurs. If the doubt occurs during the second prostration of a rak'ah, the prayer becomes invalid because it might be a third rak'ah, and adding a non-existent rak'ah invalidates the prayer. If the doubt occurs during the first prostration, Imam Muhammad holds that the prayer can be rectified because, according to him, the rak'ah is completed by raising the head from the first prostration; if he abandons that prostration, it becomes void like a nullifying event. So he should stand up (abandoning the prostration), sit for tashahhud, then stand and perform a complete rak'ah with two prostrations, and then perform sajdat al-sahw. The passage also mentions that if one remembers after prayer that he omitted a verbal pillar (e.g., qira'ah), the prayer is invalid; if he omitted an action pillar (like ruku'), it is treated as leaving ruku', and he must prostrate, sit, then stand and perform a new rak'ah with two prostrations.

Accepting Single Testimony in Breastfeeding: The Diyanat Principle
Ruling on single testimony in breastfeeding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm al-mufti rule that in diyanat, a single trustworthy testimony is accepted in breastfeeding cases, using the hadith of 'Uqba as a precedent.

Core Idea
In diyanat, one just witness suffices to avoid lifelong suspicion.

In Hanafi fatwa methodology, certain matters fall under diyanat (religious conscientiousness) where the standard rules of evidence are relaxed to prevent harm. This lesson examines a key rasm al-mufti rule: in breastfeeding disputes, a single just person's testimony is enough to advise separation, even though judicial courts require two witnesses. The ruling is rooted in the prophetic precedent of 'Uqba ibn Harith, where the Prophet issued a fatwa based on a mere report.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
The passage states that in diyanat matters (here, breastfeeding), the testimony of a single just person is accepted. This is an exception to the general rule of two witnesses.
Diyanat covers matters where the individual's religious conscience is primary.
2
Understand the Rationale
The reason is that suspicion of prohibition (here, breastfeeding leading to marital prohibition) creates lifelong entanglement. Marriage is meant for tranquility, so even a shadow of doubt is avoided.
The maxim 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' applies: the purpose of marriage is peace, so hesitation from suspicion outweighs standard evidentiary requirements.
3
Examine the Dalil
The hadith of 'Uqba ibn Harith: a woman reported breastfeeding; the Prophet said 'كيف وقد تيل' (How can you remain when it has been said)? This was a fatwa, not a judicial verdict, ordering separation due to suspicion.
The Prophet's instruction was advisory (fatwa), not a binding court judgment (qada).
4
Distinguish Qada and Fatwa
In judicial matters (qada), strict evidence rules apply. In fatwa, the mufti can give precautionary rulings based on weaker evidence if it protects religious interests.
This distinction is central to rasm al-mufti: the mufti's role includes preventive guidance.
5
Conclude the Mufta Bihi
The author states that the ruling of al-Khaniyya (accepting single testimony) is the mufta bihi position, despite being contrary to some earlier matn authors.
The later Hanafi tradition (mutakhkhirin) preferred this precautionary view.

Exception to Precaution: Joint Ownership of a Slave Girl
Ruling on handling a slave girl jointly owned when one partner fears misconduct

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the exception to the general rule of ihtiyat in matters of private parts (furooj) and explain the rationale based on the maxim of respecting ownership (hashmatan lil-milk).

Core Idea
Ownership rights override mere suspicion — the jointly-owned slave girl stays with each partner.

This passage presents a critical nuance for muftis: although the Hanafi school exercises extreme caution in matters involving private parts, there is a notable exception when ownership rights are at stake. When two partners jointly own a slave girl, mere suspicion of misconduct does not justify removing her from one partner's custody. Instead, respect for ownership requires that she continue to alternate between them. This ruling illuminates the balance between precaution and property rights, a key element of mufti adab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The text begins by stating that our scholars (ashabuna) exercised precaution (ihtatu) in matters of private parts (furooj). This is the default principle: strict caution in cases involving sexual propriety.
This rule is not absolute, as the passage will show.
2
Identify the Exception
The exception arises when a slave girl is jointly owned by two partners, and each claims fear of the other committing fornication with her. The request to place her with a trustworthy third party is rejected.
The exception is explicitly introduced with 'illa' (except).
3
Identify the Ruling
Instead of a third party, the slave girl remains with each partner alternately (e.g., one day each) – a practical arrangement that respects both owners' rights.
The ruling prioritizes ownership over suspicion.
4
Identify the Legal Cause ('Illah)
The reason given is 'hashmatan lil-milk' (out of respect for ownership). Ownership rights are considered a stronger legal interest than unproven apprehension.
This 'illah is the key to applying the exception to similar cases.
5
Implication for Muftis
A mufti must recognize that the principle of ihtiyat does not automatically override consensual property rights. When issuing fatwas on co-ownership, the rights of ownership take precedence over hypothetical misconduct.
This is a rule of mufti adab and rasm al-mufti.

This passage presents an exception to the general rule of precaution in matters of private parts. The Hanafi scholars (ashabuna) normally apply strict precaution in matters concerning private parts (furooj). However, in the case of a jointly owned slave girl where each partner fears the other may have illicit intercourse with her, they do not allow placing her with a third party. Instead, out of respect for ownership (hashmatan lil-milk), the slave girl remains with each partner alternately, either by days or according to their ownership shares. This shows that ownership rights override mere suspicion, and the principle of precaution does not extend to depriving a co-owner of his right to access his property. The request to place her with a neutral person is rejected because it would cause harm to the other partner's right of possession.

حَشْمَةً لِلْمِلْكِ (hashmatan lil-milk): Out of respect for ownership; the reason the slave girl is not taken away from the partners.

The Wording 'Li' in Iqrar – Ownership vs. Action Reports
Rule on iqrar wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between statements that constitute iqrar of ownership and those that report an action, and apply the Hanafi rule based on the linguistic implication of 'li'.

Core Idea
Saying 'this house is for Zaid' is iqrar of ownership; saying 'Zaid cultivated' is not.

In Hanafi law of iqrar (admission), the exact wording matters. A statement like 'this house is for Zaid' (using the preposition 'li') is treated as an admission that Zaid owns the house. However, statements like 'Zaid cultivated this land' are not admissions of ownership—they merely report an action. This distinction arises from the linguistic rule that 'li' indicates ownership (tamlik), while verbs of action do not. In this lesson, we break down a classical ruling from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair and explore its application in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase in an iqrar context
Look at the wording: 'hādhihi al-dār li Zayd'. The key is the particle 'li'. Under Hanafi linguistics, 'li' is a preposition that can indicate ownership (al-lām li al-milk). If someone says this, it is an iqrar of Zaid's ownership.
Other particles like 'fi' or 'inda' do not have the same force.
2
Distinguish between statements of ownership vs. action reports
The text contrasts 'li Zayd' with 'zaraca Zayd' (Zaid cultivated). The verb 'zaraca' does not contain any ownership indication. It merely reports that Zaid performed the act of cultivation. Similarly for 'gharasa' (planted) and 'bana' (built).
The difference is rooted in the nature of the word: one is a possessive construction, the other is a factual report.
3
Apply the rule of al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair
The passage from al-Bazzaziyya reinforces that even saying 'fulān sākin hādhihi al-dār' (so-and-so is a dweller) is an iqrar of the dweller's ownership? Actually, careful: the text says that statement is an iqrar from him that the house belongs to him? Wait: 'qawluhu fulān sākin hādhihi al-dār iqrāran minhu bi kawnihā lahu' – that statement is an iqrar that it is for him. The translation needs attention: The Urdu says 'us kī taraf se us kā iqrār hai' – it is an iqrar from him (the speaker) that it (the house) belongs to him (the dweller). So even stating that someone dwells is considered an iqrar of ownership in some contexts? But the main rule is clear: using 'li' is ownership; verbs are not.
The dweller example is a nuance: it is considered iqrar because dwelling implies possession. However, the general principle is that verbs of action (zaraca, gharasa, bana) are not iqrar of ownership.
4
Note the exception and the rationale
If someone says 'Zaid cultivated/planted/built' and then claims the work was for wages (bi al-ajr), the land is not considered Zaid's. The iqrar-maker (muqirr) retains ownership because the statement is about an action, not ownership.
The rationale: the verb does not imply any right of ownership; it only reports an activity that could have been done under a contract.

Commentary
This passage deals with iqrar (legal acknowledgment). If a person says 'This house is for Zaid' (hādhihi al-dār li-Zayd), the word 'li' (lām) indicates ownership (tamlik). Therefore, it is an iqrar of ownership. Even if the speaker later claims he only meant that Zaid lives there (suknā), it is not accepted because the apparent meaning is ownership. In contrast, if someone says 'So-and-so cultivated/planted/built on this land' and later claims the work was done for wages, that statement is not an iqrar of ownership of the land; rather, it is a report of an action. The land remains with the owner (muqirr). The key difference is that the lām in 'li-Zaid' signals ownership, whereas verbs like 'zaraca' do not. This is a fine point in the law of iqrar: plain statements about possession or attribution can be interpreted differently based on the wording.

المقر (al-muqirr): The person making the iqrar (admission).
اللام للملك (al-lām li al-milk): The preposition 'li' used to indicate ownership.
