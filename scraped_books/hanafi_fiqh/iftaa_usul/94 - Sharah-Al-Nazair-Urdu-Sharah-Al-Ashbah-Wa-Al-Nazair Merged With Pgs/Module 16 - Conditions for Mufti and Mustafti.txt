Acts Valid Without Intention: A Hanafi Rasm al-Mufti Rule
Expansion of a rasm rule listing acts valid without intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six acts valid without intention and apply this rule to reject a post-hoc claim of missing niyyah in a fatwa consultation.

Core Idea
Confession, agency, deposit, loan, false accusation, and theft are valid even without intention — external act suffices.

In Hanafi fiqh, intention (niyyah) is a condition for many acts, especially acts of worship. However, for a set of acts in mu'amalat and jinayat, the external act itself creates legal consequences regardless of the actor's internal state. This lesson examines the six acts listed by Imam Ibn Nujaym in al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair, and explains how a mufti should apply this rule when a person later claims absence of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
Read the first sentence: 'As for confession and agency, they are valid without it (intention), and likewise deposit, loan, false accusation, and theft.' This is the core rasm rule.
2
Understand the Legal Basis
These acts affect the rights of others or involve public order; therefore, the external manifestation overrides the internal state. The legal effect is based on the act itself, not the actor's intent.
3
Apply the Rule
If someone performs one of these acts and later claims lack of intention, the claim is legally irrelevant. The act remains valid. Memorize the consequence: post-hoc denial of intention is not accepted.
4
Examine the Definitions
The passage provides lexical and technical definitions from Hanafi sources. Study the definitions to understand the precise scope of each act.
5
Recognize the Authority Level
This rule is from the mutakhkhirin tradition, specifically Ibn Nujaym's Ashbah wa al-Nazair, which is a standard source for fatwa.

As for confession and agency, they are valid without it (intention), and likewise deposit and loan for use, and likewise false accusation and theft. Translation: And as for confession and agency, both are valid without it, and similarly depositing and giving on loan, and similarly false accusation and theft. Commentary: Confession linguistically means affirmation; legally it is informing of a right which is partly creative. Agency linguistically means delegation; technically it is entrusting disposal and protection to the agent. Deposit linguistically from 'wada'a' meaning to leave; technically it is empowering another to safeguard one's property explicitly or implicitly. Loan for use: al-Jawhari says it is attributed to 'ar (shame) because asking for it is shameful; al-Azhari says it is derived from 'ara ya'iru (to come and go); technically it is gratuitous transfer of usufruct. False accusation linguistically means throwing; technically it is accusing of zina. Theft linguistically means taking something secretly; technically it is the taking by a legally responsible, speaking, sighted person of ten good dirhams or its value, with intention to take out secretly from a proper possession, from a place of safekeeping, in one go, without any ambiguity or misinterpretation. Ibn Nujaym al-Misri said: Confession and agency are not dependent on intention; therefore, if someone first confesses and later says 'I did not intend to confess', or appoints an agent and later says 'I did not intend agency', it is not accepted. Same for deposit and loan: if someone deposits or lends something and later claims no such intention, it is not accepted. Similarly, false accusation and theft: if someone falsely accuses and later says 'I did not intend to accuse', or a thief says 'I did not intend to steal', it is not accepted.

Commentary
This passage enumerates a set of rasm rules from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair. The common element is that these acts are legally effective without the presence of a specific intention (niyyah) at the time of the act. The acts are: iqrar (confession of a right), wakalah (agency), wadi'ah (deposit), i'arah (loan for use), qadhf (false accusation of zina), and sariqah (theft). For each, the author provides a lexical and technical definition from Hanafi sources such as Radd al-Mukhtar and al-Shami. The Urdu commentary then illustrates the practical consequence: if someone performs one of these acts and later claims a lack of intention, the claim is rejected. This is because these acts are governed by external manifestations rather than internal states. The rule is authoritative from the mutakhkhirin tradition, specifically from Ibn Nujaym.

Key Terms
اقرار (iqrar): Legally it is informing of a right (liability) which is partly creative (insha').
وکالت (wakalah): Entrusting disposal and protection to the agent.
وديعت (wadi'ah): Empowering another to safeguard one's property.
عاريت (i'arah): Gratuitous transfer of usufruct.
قذف (qadhf): Accusing someone of zina (adultery).
سرقة (sariqah): Taking another's property secretly, with conditions: maturity, sanity, sight, value of 10 dirhams, from a proper place of safekeeping, no ambiguity.

Interchanging Ada and Qada Intention: The Hanafi Position
Explains permissibility of interchanging ada and qada intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on interchanging ada and qada intention to cases of doubt and error in prayer, fasting, and Hajj.

Core Idea
The heart's intention of the obligation suffices; error in labeling ada or qada is forgiven.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi fatwa principle: the interchangeability of intentions for ada (timely performance) and qada (makeup) in acts of worship. You will learn when a mistaken intention still yields a valid act, based on the maxim that the essence of intention (asl al-niyyah) is what matters, not the verbal label. This is a practical tool for muftis dealing with common scenarios of doubt and forgetfulness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario: certainty vs. doubt about time
First, determine whether the person was certain the time remained, certain it had exited, or was in doubt. The ruling differs: if certain time remained but it actually exited, the act is valid; if certain it exited and intended the fard of that time, it is invalid; if in doubt, it is valid.
The key is the heart's intention of the obligation (fard al-waqt), not the verbal specification.
2
Apply the rule from al-Tatarakhaniyya and Kashf al-Asrar
The chosen (mukhtar) view allows interchange if the heart intended the correct obligation. Examples from the text: a prisoner confused about Ramadan who fasts with ada intention but it turns out to be after Ramadan; or one who intends qada of Zuhr assuming time exited but time still remains.
Error in assumption (zann) is excused; the act is valid because the essence of intention was present.
3
Note the exception for Jumu'ah
For Jumu'ah prayer, due to disagreement whether it replaces Zuhr, one must specifically intend Jumu'ah, not just the general fard of the time. This avoids confusion between madhhabs.
This is a precautionary measure (ihtiyat) to ensure validity across differing views.
4
Extend to fasting and Hajj
The same principle applies to fasting: a mistaken intention (e.g., intending qada when actually performing Ramadan) is valid. For Hajj, intention to distinguish between ada and qada is not required at all.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on the heart's intention over verbal precision.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position and its basis
The relied-upon view for fatwa is permissibility, as stated in al-Tatarakhaniyya and Kashf al-Asrar. The maxim 'certainty is not removed by doubt' supports the validity when in doubt.
Some later scholars held a stricter view, but the mukhtar position is the one muftis should follow.

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi rasm al-mufti ruling concerning the interchangeability of intentions for ada (timely performance) and qada (makeup) in acts of worship, particularly prayer and fasting. Core Idea
Islam is a condition for valid intention; wudu and ghusl are valid from a disbeliever because they don't need intention.

The First Condition of Intent: Islam and Purification Acts
Expanding the condition of Islam for intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that intention is not a condition for wudu/ghusl, and distinguish between means and ends in acts of purification when issuing fatwas.

Core Idea
Intention is not required for wudu/ghusl because they are means, not ends; a non-Muslim's purification is valid upon embracing Islam.

In this lesson, we examine the first condition of intention: Islam. The Hanafi school holds that a non-Muslim's wudu and ghusl are valid even without intention, because these are merely means (wasail) to purification, not devotional ends (maqasid). In contrast, tayammum requires intention, so it is invalid from a non-Muslim. This distinction has practical consequences for fatwa: a non-Muslim who performs wudu and then embraces Islam can pray with that wudu, unlike in the Shafi'i school which requires intention for all purification acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Governing Qawida
Recognize that this passage expands upon the maxim 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by their intentions) by defining a key condition: Islam is necessary for valid intention. Acts that are purely devotional (maqasid) require intention; acts that are means (wasail) do not.
The author of al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair is discussing the tenth condition under this maxim.
2
Apply the Distinction to Tayammum vs. Wudu/Ghusl
Note that the passage explicitly states: 'Tayammum of a non-Muslim is void, not his wudu.' Because tayammum is a devotional substitute for purification and requires specific intention; wudu and ghusl are merely physical cleansings and do not.
The Hanafi proof: the hadith 'Actions are by intentions' applies to worship, but wudu is a means, not an end.
3
Determine the Consequence for Fatwa
If a non-Muslim performs wudu or ghusl and then embraces Islam, he may pray with that purification. If he had performed tayammum, it would be invalid and he must redo it after becoming Muslim.
This is the mufta bihi position; the madhhab dispute with al-Shafi'i is noted.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i Position
Imam al-Shafi'i requires intention for all purification acts. Therefore, in his school, a non-Muslim's wudu would be invalid, and the person must repeat wudu after embracing Islam.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is clear: the Hanafi view is more lenient in this scenario.

Commentary
This passage is the tenth discussion on conditions of intention. The first condition is Islam. The author applies this: a non-Muslim's tayammum is invalid because it requires specific intention. However, his wudu and ghusl are valid because they do not require intention; they are means (wasail). Therefore, if a non-Muslim performs wudu or ghusl and then embraces Islam, he can pray with that purification. This is the Hanafi position, in contrast to al-Shafi'i, who requires intention for all purification acts. The reasoning is that intention is only a condition for acts that are purely devotional (like tayammum), not for cleansing acts.

Key Terms
نية (niyyah): Intentional determination to perform an act of worship; a condition for some acts but not all.
وسيلة (wasilah): Means; an act that is a preliminary to worship, such as wudu.
مقصد (maqsad): End; an act that is a goal in itself, such as salat or tayammum.
