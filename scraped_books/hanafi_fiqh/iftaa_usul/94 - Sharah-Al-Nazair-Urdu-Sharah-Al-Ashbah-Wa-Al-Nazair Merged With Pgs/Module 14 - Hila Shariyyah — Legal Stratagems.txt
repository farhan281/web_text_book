Forced Writing of Talaq: The Role of Necessity
Ruling on talaq by a coerced person through writing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that writing substitutes speech only when necessary to determine the validity of a written talaq under coercion.

Core Idea
Writing stands in for speech only when necessary; coercion alone does not create that necessity.

In Hanafi law, talaq is primarily effected by spoken words. Writing is a valid substitute, but only when there is a genuine need—such as inability to speak. What happens when a person is forced to write a talaq statement? Does the coercion itself make writing necessary? This passage from Fatawa Qadikhan clarifies that it does not, and explains the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is writing talaq under coercion. The ruling from Fatawa Qadikhan is that talaq does not occur (ghayr wāqiʿ).
2
Identify the ʿilla (effective cause)
The reason given: writing stands in place of utterance only when necessary (ḍarūrah). In this case, there is no necessity because the person can speak.
Necessity here means inability to speak, not mere coercion.
3
Apply the rule to determine validity
Since the condition of necessity is absent, the writing does not effect talaq. The coercion alone does not create the need for writing.
4
Distinguish from spoken talaq under coercion
Spoken talaq under coercion is generally valid (in Hanafi) because speech is the primary mode. Writing is a secondary mode with a stricter condition.
This distinction prevents misuse of written talaq under duress.

Commentary
This passage presents a specific case: forced writing of talaq. The ruling from Fatawa Qadikhan is that talaq does not occur. The reasoning is that written words stand in place of spoken words only out of necessity (e.g., when the person cannot speak). Since in this case the person could speak but was forced to write, the necessity is absent. Therefore, the writing does not effect talaq. This differs from the general rule that spoken talaq under coercion is effective. The ruling highlights that writing is not the primary mode of talaq; it is a substitute that requires a genuine need.

Key Terms
کتابت (kitābat): Writing as a mode of expressing talaq.
ضرورت (ḍarūrah): Necessity; the condition that justifies using a substitute mode.

Advancing Zakat on Pregnant Camels: A Hila Case
Ruling on ta'jil al-zakat for pregnant animals and their unborn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which ta'jil al-zakat is valid for pregnant livestock and construct the correct niyyah for a valid hila.

Core Idea
Advance zakat on unborn offspring is valid only if they are born before the hawl ends — existence at payment is key.

This passage from al-Khaniyah illustrates a specific hila (legal stratagem) involving advance zakat on pregnant camels. The ruling hinges on the timing of the offspring's birth: if born within the current zakat year, the advance payment covers both mother and young; if paid for future offspring yet to be conceived, it is invalid. This lesson will parse the conditions for a valid ta'jil.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage and identify the two scenarios: (1) paying advance zakat for existing pregnant camels and their current unborn, and (2) paying for future offspring that will be born in the next year.
The first scenario is valid; the second is invalid.
2
Locate the condition (shart)
The condition for validity is that the wealth for which zakat is paid must exist in ownership at the time of payment. Here, the unborn are considered existent if they are in the womb and will be born before the hawl.
Existence is the key factor.
3
Apply the hila to the valid case
If the owner pays one goat for the five camels and one goat for the unborn, and the offspring are born before the hawl completes, the zakat is valid.
The payment covers both.
4
Distinguish the invalid case
If the owner pays for offspring that will be born next year (not yet conceived), the payment is invalid because those offspring do not exist at the time of payment.
It counts only as nafl charity.
5
Extract the legal principle
The underlying principle: 'Zakat is due on existing owned wealth; future contingent wealth is not subject to zakat until it comes into existence.'
This is consistent with the Hanafi condition for zakat.

Translation: And also in al-Khaniyah it is that if someone has five pregnant she-camels and he paid in advance two goats on behalf of the she-camels and on behalf of what is in their wombs, then before the completion of the year five offspring are born, it is sufficient. And if he paid in advance on behalf of those that will be born in the second year, it is not permissible. Explanation: In al-Khaniyah it is written that if someone owns five she-camels and all five are pregnant, and the owner pays zakat in advance: one goat on behalf of the five she-camels and one goat on behalf of the offspring that are in the wombs of the she-camels, then this advanced zakat is valid. And if he pays advanced zakat with the intention that one goat is for the five she-camels and one goat is for the offspring of the she-camels that will be born in the coming year from pregnancy, then it is not permissible. Because in the first case, both the she-camels and the pregnancy exist even if birth has not occurred. But in the second case, the she-camels exist but the offspring have no existence yet; their existence is future. While for zakat, ownership of wealth is a condition. The second case is like giving zakat for future offspring when the she-camels are not pregnant yet; it will count as nafl charity.

Commentary
This passage presents a specific hila (legal stratagem) concerning the permissibility of advancing zakat (ta'jil al-zakat) on pregnant livestock. The ruling distinguishes between two scenarios: (1) If the pregnant animals are already in one's possession and they give birth before the hawl (year) ends, then paying zakat on both the mothers and the unborn (as part of the same niyyah) is valid because the unborn are considered part of the existing wealth (they are connected to the mothers). (2) If one intends to pay zakat for offspring that do not yet exist and will only be born in the following year, it is invalid because those offspring are not yet owned at the time of payment. The key condition is that the object of zakat must exist in one's ownership at the time of payment. This ruling is from al-Khaniyah, a Hanafi fatwa collection. The example illustrates that advancing zakat is permissible only when the wealth for which zakat is paid actually exists or is determinable at the time of payment.

Key Terms
زکوٰۃ معجل (zakāt mu‘ajjal): Zakat paid before the completion of the lunar year (hawl), i.e., advance zakat.
حول (hawl): The lunar year that must pass for zakat to become obligatory on livestock.
