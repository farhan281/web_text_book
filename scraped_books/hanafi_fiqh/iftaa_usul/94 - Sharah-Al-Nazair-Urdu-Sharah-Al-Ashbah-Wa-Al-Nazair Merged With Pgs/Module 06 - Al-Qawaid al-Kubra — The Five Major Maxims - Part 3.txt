Doubt in Breastfeeding and the Qawida of Certainty
Illustrates the qawida that doubt in the cause does not establish the ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'Certainty is not removed by doubt' to breastfeeding scenarios and identify when a trustworthy witness overrides doubt in fatwa.

Core Idea
Doubt in breastfeeding does not establish prohibition; certainty or trustworthy testimony is required.

In matters of family law, especially breastfeeding that creates permanent prohibition, doubt often arises. The Hanafi school relies on the major maxim 'Certainty is not removed by doubt' to resolve such cases. This lesson examines three scenarios from classical fatwa collections to see how a mufti uses this qawida and its exception.

Guided Path
1
State the governing qawida
Begin by recalling the maxim 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' (Certainty is not removed by doubt). This is a kubra (major) qawida that applies across all fiqh chapters.
This qawida is the foundation for all three furuat below.
2
Analyze the first furu: doubt about milk reaching the stomach
When a woman breastfeeds a baby but there is doubt whether milk actually entered the baby's stomach, no prohibition is established. The default is permissibility until certainty.
This is the direct application of 'certainty not removed by doubt'.
3
Analyze the second furu: woman's claim of no milk
If a woman breastfeeds a baby girl but later claims there was no milk in her breast at the time, her word is accepted (diyaanatan) because only she knows the state of her body. Her son may marry that girl.
The doubt here is resolved by the person with exclusive knowledge; the default of no prohibition remains.
4
Analyze the third furu: general suspicion of breastfeeding
When there is only suspicion (shubhah) of breastfeeding between a boy and girl, marriage is allowed unless a trustworthy person ('adl thiqah) reports the breastfeeding with certainty.
This introduces the exception: a trustworthy report can override doubt and establish prohibition.
5
Identify the istithna' and its governing qawida
The exception is when a trustworthy witness gives definite information. This is governed by the qawida 'The report of a trustworthy person is accepted in matters of permission and prohibition' (الخبر الثقة يقبل في الحظر والإباحة).
This sub-maxim limits the main qawida in cases where reliable external evidence exists.

Commentary
This passage presents and applies the major qawida: 'Certainty is not removed by doubt' (اليقين لا يزول بالشك). The author uses the example of breastfeeding: if there is doubt whether milk actually reached the baby's stomach, the prohibition of marriage due to milk-relationship does not arise. The ruling is taken from al-Walwalji. Then from al-Qunyah: a woman breastfeeds a baby girl but later claims she had no milk at that time; her claim is accepted because only she knows the state of her breast (diyaanat, not qadaa). Consequently, her son can marry that girl. Finally, from al-Khaniyah: if there is only a suspicion of breastfeeding between a boy and girl, marriage is allowed unless a trustworthy person reports the breastfeeding. If the report comes after marriage, caution dictates separation. This shows that in matters of prohibition originating from doubt, the default is permissibility until certainty.

رضاع (rada'): Breastfeeding that establishes milk-relationship (mahramiyyah)
عدل ثقة (adl thiqah): A fair and trustworthy witness whose report is accepted in diyaani matters

The Qawida of Caution in Private Parts: Al-Mudmarat and the Shafi'i Refutation
Explaining the principle of caution in sexual matters and refuting the Shafi'i view on slave-girls from Dar al-Harb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi qawida 'الأولى الاحتياط في الفروج', apply it to determine the ruling on slave-girls of unknown status, and evaluate the Shafi'i position as wara' not binding.

Core Idea
Caution in private parts is preferable but not obligatory; rulings depend on certainty, not mere possibility.

This passage examines a key Hanafi maxim regarding caution in sexual matters. It cites al-Mudmarat's recommendation to marry one's slave-girl before intercourse, then refutes a Shafi'i view that slave-girls from Dar al-Harb are automatically forbidden. Understanding the distinction between binding rulings (hukm) and piety (wara') is essential for a mufti.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its rank
Identify the Arabic text of the qawida and note that it is a minor (sughra) maxim that restricts the general principle that private parts are prohibited by default.
The qawida is 'الأولى الاحتياط في الفروج' meaning 'Caution is preferable in matters of private parts'.
2
Analyze the first far' (marrying one's slave-girl)
Examine the case from al-Mudmarat: a man marries his own slave-girl before intercourse to avoid the possibility of illegality. This is recommended (hasan), not obligatory.
The possibility that the girl might be free, someone else's freed slave, or subject to an emancipation oath triggers the recommendation.
3
Examine the second far' (slave-girls from Dar al-Harb) and the Shafi'i objection
Some Shafi'is rule that slave-girls from Dar al-Harb (Rome, India, Turkey) are absolutely forbidden unless properly distributed by the imam.
The Hanafi response distinguishes between a binding ruling (hukm lazim) and a recommendation of scrupulousness (wara').
4
Identify the Hanafi response: reliance on the possessor's word
For a slave-girl of unknown status, the Hanafi school refers to the possessor's statement if she is a minor, or to her own confession if she is an adult, rather than declaring her automatically forbidden.
The principle 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' (certainty is not removed by doubt) underpins this approach.
5
Recognize the exception when status is known
The qawida has an istithna': when the slave-girl's status is known (e.g., from proper distribution), caution is not needed and the ruling is clear.
This exception is governed by the broader maxim of certainty.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle of precaution (ihtiyat) in matters of private parts. The author first cites al-Mudmarat, which recommends that a man marry his own slave-girl before having relations with her to avoid the possibility that she might actually be free, someone else's freed slave, or subject to an emancipation oath. This is a prudential measure, though not legally obligatory. Then, the author refutes a ruling attributed to some Shafi'i scholars that slave-girls brought from Dar al-Harb (like Rome, India, Turkey) are absolutely forbidden unless they come through proper imam distribution. The Hanafi response is that this is an expression of wara' (piety), not an enforceable ruling (hukm lazim). In the Hanafi school, the status of a slave-girl of unknown condition is determined by the possessor's statement if she is a minor, or by her own confession if she is an adult. If her status is known, there is no problem. Thus, the passage refines and limits the Shafi'i view.

ورع (wara‘): Piety or scrupulousness; an optional act of caution beyond the legal obligation. The text uses it to describe the Shafi'i ruling as non-binding.
احتياط (iḥtiyāṭ): Caution; the principle of acting with extra care when there is doubt, especially in sexual matters.

Maxim: Default in Speech is Literal Meaning with Furu'
Maxim with furu' including marriage and zina.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-aslu fi al-kalam al-haqiqah' to interpret Quranic terms and derive fatwa in family law cases involving marriage and prohibition.

Core Idea
The literal meaning of 'nikah' is marriage, so the father's fornicated woman is forbidden like his wife.

This lesson introduces a core Hanafi usul maxim: the default in speech is the literal meaning (haqiqah). The passage then demonstrates its application through several furu' (subsidiary rulings). The first and most detailed furu' concerns the interpretation of the word 'nikah' in Quran 4:22, leading to the prohibition of a father's fornicated woman (mazniyah). Understanding this maxim is essential for muftis when interpreting texts where terms have both literal and metaphorical meanings.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its meaning
Begin by memorising the maxim: 'al-aslu fi al-kalam al-haqiqah' – the default in speech is the literal meaning. This means when a word has a clear literal sense, that sense is taken unless there is a valid reason (qarinah) to shift to a metaphorical meaning.
This maxim is a foundational principle in Hanafi usul for interpreting texts.
2
Identify the term being interpreted
In the verse 'wa la tankihu ma nakaha aba'ukum min al-nisa', the key term is 'nikah'. The text states that here nikah is taken to mean 'intercourse' (wat'i) because the literal meaning of marriage (the contract) implies consummation, but in the context of prohibition, nikah refers to the act of marrying (including the marital relationship).
The Hanafi school distinguishes between nikah as contract and nikah as intercourse based on context.
3
Apply the maxim to the verse
Since the default is literal meaning, 'nikah' in the verse is understood as the full marital relationship. Therefore, the prohibition covers not only women whom the father married through a valid contract but also those with whom he had a marital-like relationship, including fornication (zina) because the literal meaning of 'nikah' includes the act of intercourse.
This analogy extends the ruling to the father's mazniyah.
4
Derive the ruling and note the exception
Thus, a father's mazniyah is forbidden to the son like his actual wife. The text contrasts this with a woman whom the father merely touched (mamsusah) without marriage; a ruling permitting her would be different. The distinction is clarified in the commentary on Dhihar.
This shows that the maxim is applied with nuance, and exceptions exist based on further evidence.

Default in Speech is Literal Meaning: The Case of Nikah
Maxim with furuat on nikah's literal meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-asl fi al-kalam al-haqiqah' to identify the literal meaning of 'nikah,' derive the ruling on the prohibition of the father's mazniyah, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i position on this issue.

Core Idea
Default is literal: 'nikah' means intercourse, not contract, changing prohibitions and oaths.

This lesson examines one of the most fundamental Hanafi usul maxims: that speech is interpreted literally unless a context signals otherwise. By applying this to the word 'nikah' in the Quranic verse prohibiting marriage to women ‘married’ by one’s father, the Hanafi school derives that the prohibition extends to women with whom the father had intercourse, even outside a valid marriage. This leads to practical consequences in judicial rulings and oath-taking, as well as a clear cross-madhhab difference with the Shafi'i school. You will work through the maxim, its scope, and its applications.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Identify the qawida in Arabic and English: 'الأصل في الكلام الحقيقة' — 'The default in speech is the literal meaning.' Understand its rank (wusta) and type (kulliya).
This maxim governs interpretation across all fiqh chapters involving speech.
2
Identify its scope
Recognize that this maxim applies wherever a word has both a literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) meaning. In the absence of a contextual clue (qarinah), the literal meaning is taken.
Key exception: if a qarinah indicates majaz, the literal meaning is overruled — but no such qarinah exists for 'nikah' in the verse.
3
Work through the furuat
Apply the maxim to each derived case in the text: (1) 'nikah' in the verse means intercourse → father's mazniyah prohibited; (2) a Shafi'i judge's ruling of permissibility is not enforced; (3) in an oath 'if I marry you,' the reference is to intercourse.
Each case shows how the literal meaning produces a specific fiqh outcome.
4
Analyze the cross-madhhab dispute
Compare Hanafi and Shafi'i positions: Shafi'is interpret 'nikah' as the contract, so they limit the verse's prohibition to women married by contract. Hanafis apply the literal meaning (intercourse), thus prohibiting the mazniyah. Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).
This dispute shows how a usul principle directly impacts fatwa.
5
Apply the maxim in a new case
Consider a scenario not in the text, e.g., a man says 'I have married my daughter to Zayd.' Apply the maxim to interpret 'married' — does it mean the contract of marriage (giving in marriage) or intercourse? According to the maxim, literal meaning is the contract act (tazwij) in this context, but test how the maxim guides.
This step reinforces the method of applying the qawida consistently.

The author first presents five cases that seem to contradict this maxim, then resolves each to show the maxim remains intact. Maxim of Certainty Not Removed by Doubt
Introducing the maxim with three applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-yaqin la yazoolu bi al-shakk' to derive the ruling in cases where certainty of purity or impurity is challenged by doubt.

Core Idea
Certainty of purity or impurity is not overridden by mere doubt — the prior state prevails until proven otherwise.

This lesson introduces one of the five major maxims of Hanafi fiqh: 'Certainty is not removed by doubt.' The author presents this maxim along with three concrete cases from taharah where the maxim determines the ruling. Understanding this maxim is essential for any mufti dealing with doubts in purity, contracts, or worship.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its authority
Memorize the Arabic text 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' and its English rendering. Recognize that this is a kulli (universal) maxim from the qawaid kubra, accepted across all chapters of fiqh.
The maxim originates from the Hanafi tradition and is also accepted by other schools with minor nuances.
2
Identify the legal principle
The principle: When certainty exists (e.g., one is certain of having purity), a later doubt (e.g., suspecting impurity) does not remove that certainty. The status quo ante remains until solid evidence overcomes it.
The doubt must be genuine and not a baseless whim.
3
Examine the first furu' (mustahadah/mutahayyirah)
A woman with chronic bleeding (mustahadah) or who is confused about her cycle (mutahayyirah) is required to perform ghusl for each fard prayer, even though she doubts whether she is actually impure at the moment. The certainty of impurity (due to the ongoing bleeding) is not removed by the possibility that the bleeding may have stopped.
This is the sahih (correct) view in the Hanafi school.
4
Examine the second and third furuat
Second: If a person finds wetness and is unsure whether it is mani (requiring ghusl) or madhi (requiring only wudu), they must perform ghusl—the certainty of major impurity (if it is mani) is not removed by doubt. Third: If a dead mouse is found in a vessel used for wudu, and the time of fall is unknown, the wudu is invalid—the certainty of the vessel's purity before the mouse is lost, and the certainty of impurity after the mouse's fall prevails.
In the third case, it is uncertain when the mouse fell, so the earlier certainty of purity is not overridden—rather, the new certainty (impurity from the mouse) takes effect, and since it cannot be linked to a specific time, it invalidates the current wudu.
5
Apply the maxim to a new case
Practice: A person is certain they performed wudu, but later doubts if they passed wind. What is the ruling? (Answer: Wudu remains valid because certainty of purity is not removed by doubt.)
This step cements the maxim's operation in daily fiqh.

Certainty is not removed by doubt, except where precaution overrides.

This passage lists specific cases where the Hanafi school requires acting on doubt rather than certainty. These are not contradictions to the maxim but necessary precautions (ihtiyat) for situations where ignoring doubt would lead to certain error. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for applying the maxim correctly in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Maxim
State the base maxim: 'Certainty is not removed by doubt.' The default rule: do not act on doubt when certainty exists.
This maxim is one of the five major legal maxims (al-qawa'id al-kubra) in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the Exceptions
List the four exceptions given: (1) mustahadah mutahayyirah – ghusl for each prayer; (2) wetness unknown as maniy or madhiy – ghusl; (3) dead rat in water with unknown time – repetition of prayers; (4) first-time doubt about takbir, hadath, or masah – repeat the act.
The author marks these as 'fawa'id' (benefits) – additional important points under the maxim.
3
Analyze the Rationale (Ihtiyat)
For each exception, identify why precaution overrides the maxim. The rationale: the doubt is constant (mustahadah) or the consequence of ignoring it is certain janabah or invalid worship.
Precaution is applied because the doubt itself arises from a valid possibility that cannot be ignored.
4
Distinguish First-Time vs. Frequent Doubt
Note that the fourth exception applies only to first-time doubt. For waswasa (obsessive doubt), the original maxim applies: ignore the doubt.
This distinction prevents the exception from being misused for obsessive thoughts.
5
Apply to Fatwa Practice
When issuing a fatwa, check whether the case falls under these exceptions. If yes, the mufta bihi is to act on the doubt. Otherwise, adhere to the base maxim.

Commentary
This passage enumerates four exceptions to the universal legal maxim 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' (Certainty is not removed by doubt). Normally, one is not obliged to act on doubt when one has certainty. However, in specific cases, the law requires precaution (ihtiyat) due to the frequency or seriousness of doubt. The first exception: a mustahadah mutahayyirah (a woman with irregular bleeding who cannot distinguish menses from istihadah) must perform ghusl for every prayer, acting on the possibility of purity. The second: wetness found with uncertainty whether it is maniy or madhiy requires ghusl as a precaution. The third: finding a dead rat in water used for wudu, with unknown time of contact, requires repeating prayers within the likely period. The fourth: first-time doubt regarding the opening takbir, occurrence of hadath, or wiping the head must be acted upon by repeating the action. These exceptions are not contradictions to the maxim but rather applications of a subsidiary rule: when the doubt is originally grounded in a valid possibility, precaution takes precedence. The author clarifies that these are detailed in earlier sections.

Certainty and Doubt in Traveler's Prayer
Extracts ruling from al-Tatar Khaniyyah based on the maxim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk' to resolve traveler's doubt scenarios in Salat al-Safar.

Core Idea
Certainty is not removed by doubt — the traveler's default is full prayer until certainty of concession.

Travel often brings situations where a person is not sure whether his journey has ended or whether he intended a prolonged stay. This lesson examines two such cases from our text and shows how the Hanafi legal maxim 'Certainty is not removed by doubt' (اليقين لا يزول بالشك) governs the obligation to pray four rak'ahs. Understanding this maxim and its application in travel rulings is essential for any mufti or fiqh student.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original certainty
Before doubt arises, the traveler is certain of his status as a traveler. This status carries the rukhsah (concession) of shortening prayers. That certainty is the starting point.
2
Recognize the doubt
The doubt is whether the journey-ending event (reaching home or forming a 15-day intention) has occurred. This doubt does not alter the original certainty.
3
Apply the maxim
Apply 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk': the original traveler status remains intact, so the concession of qasr is not allowed.
The maxim overrides any inclination to choose the easier option based on probability.
4
Derive the ruling
The traveler must pray four rak'ahs out of precaution, treating himself as a resident for that prayer.
This is the mufta bihi position.
5
Analogize to in-prayer doubt
The text from al-Tatar Khaniyyah extends the same logic: if during prayer one doubts his status, he prays four and sits after the second rak'ah to cover both possibilities.

Definitions of Shakk, Zann, Wahm, and Ghalib al-Zann
Defining and distinguishing key epistemological terms in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shakk, zann, wahm, and ghalib al-zann and state which has legal effect according to Hanafi fuqaha'.

Core Idea
Only ghalib al-zann carries legal weight; ordinary conjecture is treated as doubt.

In Hanafi legal theory, not every conjecture is actionable. This passage, drawn from the Ashbah wa al-Nazair tradition, defines four epistemological states that govern a mufti's assessment of evidence. Understanding these distinctions is essential for evaluating claims and issuing sound fatwas.

Guided Path
1

Pay attention to the specific wording: equality, preponderance, and acceptance by the heart.
2
Classify the legal weight
Determine which of these terms are considered legally operative. The passage states that only ghalib al-zann is 'mu'tabar' (considered) by fuqaha'.
Ordinary zann is treated as a type of shakk and thus has no legal effect.
3
Analyze the Hanafi treatment of zann
Note that Hanafis regard zann as belonging to the category of shakk because both involve hesitation (taraddud) between existence and non-existence.
This is a key distinction from other madhahib that may treat zann differently.
4
Apply to a practical case
Review the example from Kitab al-Iqrar: a person says 'upon me is a thousand dirhams in my zann.' The ruling is that nothing becomes obligatory because it is shakk.
This illustrates the practical consequence of the definition.
5
Connect to the maxim

The exception is ghalib al-zann, which approaches certainty and may be acted upon.

Commentary
This passage, under 'Second Benefit,' provides technical definitions of key epistemological terms used in Hanafi fiqh and usul. Shakk (doubt) is exactly equal probability; zann (conjecture) is a preponderance towards correctness; wahm (misgiving) is preponderance towards error; and ghalib al-zann (overwhelming conjecture) is when the heart is satisfied and it becomes the basis for action. The lesson for a mufti is that only ghalib al-zann has legal weight; ordinary conjecture is disregarded.

Istiṣḥāb: Definition and Authority
Defines istiṣḥāb and presents the Hanafi preferred view on its authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istiṣḥāb according to the Hanafi school, identify the three positions on its authority, and apply the muftā bihī view to a scenario involving doubt.

Core Idea
Istiṣḥāb is a proof only for maintaining the status quo (daf'), not for claiming new rights (istihqāq).

Istiṣḥāb, or presumption of continuity, is a foundational legal maxim that helps muftis resolve cases of doubt by preserving a previously established state. However, its authority is debated: is it an absolute proof, no proof at all, or only a defensive tool? This lesson unpacks the definition and the Hanafi preferred position, which limits istiṣḥāb to repelling claims of change rather than establishing new entitlements.

Guided Path
1
Define istiṣḥāb
Extract the definition from the text: 'the ruling on the continuation of a confirmed matter whose non-existence is not conjectured.' Identify its key components: a previously established state, a presumption of continuity, and the absence of evidence of change.
The definition comes from al-Taḥrīr, a key Hanafi uṣūl text.
2
Identify the ikhtilāf on authority
Note the three positions: (1) absolute proof, (2) not a proof at all, (3) proof for daf' (repelling) only. Attribute the third to the three great scholars (Abū Zayd, Shams al-A'immah, Fakhr al-Islām) as the well-known view.
The text calls this the 'mashhūr' view among jurists.
3
Understand the reasoning for daf' only
Analyze the argument: istiṣḥāb is essentially the continuation of original non-existence. Since the cause of existence does not imply continuation, ruling on continuation without evidence is invalid. Thus, it can only defend the prior state, not create new rights.
This reasoning is given in the text: 'موجب الوجود ليس موجب بقائه'.
4
Apply to a case
Take a typical scenario: a person who had wuḍū' and doubts if it was broken. Apply istiṣḥāb to rule that wuḍū' remains (daf'). Contrast with a scenario where one claims ownership based on past possession – istiṣḥāb cannot establish the right (istihqāq).
This illustrates the practical difference in fatwā.

Commentary
This passage defines istiṣḥāb (presumption of continuity) as per al-Taḥrīr: it is the ruling that a previously established state of affairs continues to exist as long as there is no evidence to the contrary. The text then discusses the ikhtilāf over its authority as a legal proof. There are three positions: (1) it is an absolute proof, (2) it is not a proof at all, (3) it is a proof only for repelling (daf‘) i.e., maintaining the status quo, not for establishing new rights (istihqāq). The third position is attributed to three eminent Hanafi scholars (Abū Zayd, Shams al-A'immah al-Ḥalwānī, and Fakhr al-Islām al-Pazdawī) and is described as the well-known view among the jurists. The text also presents the reasoning that istiṣḥāb is essentially the continuation of original non-existence, and since the cause of existence does not necessarily cause continuation, ruling on continuation without evidence is unjustified. Therefore, the reliable position is that istiṣḥāb is valid only for defense—to deny change—not to claim new entitlements.

الاستصحاب (al-istiṣḥāb): The ruling on the continuation of a confirmed matter whose non-existence is not conjectured
دفع (daf‘): Repelling or maintaining the status quo; using istiṣḥāb to deny a claimed change
استحقاق (istihqāq): Entitlement or acquisition of a right; istiṣḥāb cannot be used for this
