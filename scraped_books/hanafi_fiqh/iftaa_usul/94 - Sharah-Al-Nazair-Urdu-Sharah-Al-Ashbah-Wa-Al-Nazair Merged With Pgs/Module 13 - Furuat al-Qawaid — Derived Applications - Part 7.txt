The Maxim of Certainty in Prohibition and Its Applications
Qawida with furuat: restrictive maxim with slave and rada' examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'prohibition requires certainty' to cases of rada' and milk al-yamin, and identify the historical exception for reliable witness testimony.

Core Idea
Prohibition is established only by certainty; doubt in the cause leaves the original permissibility intact.

The Hanafi school places a premium on certainty when it comes to declaring something prohibited. This lesson explores a specific formulation of that principle: a prohibition requires a verified cause (sabab muhaqqaq). If there is doubt whether the cause exists, the prohibition is not triggered. Through a series of examples involving slave women and fosterage (rada'), you'll see how this maxim operates in practice and where an exception arises when a trustworthy witness provides testimony.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Identify the core ruling: prohibition requires a verified cause; doubt in the cause is not considered.
The maxim is a restriction of the broader qawida 'certainty is not removed by doubt'.
2
Work through the slave woman cases
Apply the maxim to scenarios where one slave is freed but unknown. Distinguish between multiple owners and a single owner, and note the ruling on intercourse.
Each owner may continue with his own slave because the cause (freedom) is not certain for that specific slave.
3
Apply to rada' cases
Examine scenarios involving doubt about milk entry, the woman's own claim of no milk, and mere suspicion of rada' between children. Confirm that no prohibition arises without certainty.
The woman's statement about her own milk is credited because only she knows.
4
Identify the istithna'
Note that if a reliable and trustworthy witness (adl thiqah) reports the rada', their testimony is accepted and creates a practical certainty, overruling the doubt.
This is governed by the subsidiary maxim 'khabar al-adl ka al-mushahadah'.
5
Determine the practical takeaway for fatwa
Summarize how a mufti should apply this maxim: do not prohibit based on doubt, but act upon trustworthy evidence when available.
The mufta bihi position is that the underlying permissibility remains until certainty of prohibition.

Commentary
This passage presents a legal maxim from the Hanafi school: A prohibition is only established with certainty; doubt does not create a prohibition. The author first applies this to a case of slave women where one is freed but unknown. Since there is no certainty which specific slave woman is freed, each owner may continue to have intercourse with his own slave, as the prohibition is not confirmed. However, if one has a strong presumption (ghalabat al-zann) that he freed his own slave, it is recommended (mustahabb) to refrain, but not obligatory. The same principle is then applied to rada' (fosterage) cases. If a woman breastfeeds an infant but there is doubt whether milk entered the stomach, no prohibition (mahramiyya) is established because the cause is uncertain. Similarly, if a woman is known to have breastfed a girl but later claims she had no milk at the time, her statement is accepted (since only she knows) and her son may marry that girl. In the case of a boy and girl with mere suspicion of rada', marriage is allowed until a reliable witness confirms the rada'. If such testimony comes after marriage, the couple should separate as a precaution. The maxim 'certainty is not removed by doubt' is the foundation here.

Key Terms
سبب محقق (sabab muhaqqaq): A confirmed cause that legally establishes a ruling (e.g., rada', milk al-yamin).
شك (shakk): Doubt that does not amount to certainty or strong presumptive knowledge.
حرمة (hurma): Legal prohibition, e.g., of marriage due to rada' or milk al-yamin.

Acceptance of Single Witness for Permissibility of Intercourse
States the rule that despite default prohibition, a single witness suffices for permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'البضع يقبل في حله غير الواحد' to identify when a single person's testimony suffices for permissibility of intercourse, and distinguish the condition of trustworthiness.

Core Idea
For private matters like intercourse, a single witness can override the default prohibition.

This lesson presents a subtle qawida that makes an exception to the general rule requiring multiple witnesses. Because sexual relations are inherently private, the Hanafi school accepts the testimony of a single trustworthy person to establish permissibility. The author illustrates with two examples from sales and gifts of slave-girls.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Read the Arabic maxim and translate it literally. Note the key terms: البضع (intercourse), الأصل الحظر (default prohibition), غير الواحد (a single person).
Memorize the maxim: 'البضع وإن كان الأصل فيه الحظر يقبل في حله غير الواحد'
2
Identify the default rule and its exception
The default is prohibition. The exception is that a single person's testimony is accepted for permissibility. Understand that this is a dabt (sub-maxim) that restricts the general principle of requiring multiple witnesses.
This exception applies because of the private nature of the act.
3
Apply to the first fur'
Consider the case: buyer of a slave-girl is told by a third party (Bakr) that the seller authorized the sale. Based on this single testimony, intercourse becomes permissible. Identify the underlying illa: privacy and necessity.
4
Apply to the second fur'
A slave-girl claims her master gifted her to a man. If the man has a strong presumption of her truthfulness, intercourse is permissible. Note the condition of trustworthiness (ظن صدقها).
This example is debated; some scholars reject it.
5
Note the istithna' (exception)
The qawida does not apply if the witness is unreliable (fasiq). The governing maxim is 'لا يقبل خبر الفاسق' based on Quran 49:6. The mufti must verify trustworthiness.
This exception ensures the integrity of testimony.

Commentary
This passage introduces another qawida related to testimony in matters of sexual relations. The default rule (الأصل) is that sexual intercourse is prohibited (حظر) due to the sanctity of the womb. However, for establishing its permissibility (حل), the statement of even a single person is accepted, because such matters are private and often only known by individuals. The author provides two furuat: (1) If a man buys a slave-girl from Zayd, and a third party (Bakr) claims that Zayd authorized him to sell, that single testimony suffices to make the slave-girl halal for the buyer. (2) If a slave-girl herself comes claiming her master gifted her to a man, and the man has a strong presumption of her truthfulness, he may have intercourse with her. This is based on the principle that in diyanah (conscience), a single trustworthy witness is accepted, especially in matters where the truth is hard to verify otherwise. The commentator (al-Hamawi) is noted to have refuted the specific slave-girl example, but the principle stands.

Key Terms
بضع (bud'): Sexual intercourse; also used for the pudendum
غير الواحد (ghayr al-wahid): A single person (i.e., testimony of one individual)

Legal maxim on acceptance of single witness for permissibility of private parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida that a single person's testimony is accepted for permissibility of private parts, identify its furuat, and evaluate the objection raised by Hamawi regarding the gift case.

Core Idea
In matters of private parts, the word of one person suffices to shift the default from prohibition to permissibility.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi legal maxim: that in matters concerning the private parts (budu'), although the default is prohibition, the testimony of a single person is accepted to establish permissibility. The maxim is applied to two scenarios—agency in sale and gift—and also addresses an objection by Allama Hamawi, which is resolved through the concept of mu'ata (exchange by mutual action). As a mufti, you must know when this maxim applies and how to respond to apparent conflicts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qawida and its default principle
Read the qawida: 'البضْعَ وَإِن كَانَ الأصْلُ فِيهِ الحَظرُ يُقْبَلُ فِي حِلهِ غَيْرُ الْوَاحِدِ'. Note that the default for private parts is absolute prohibition (hurma), and the qawida states that a single person's report is sufficient to prove permissibility.
This is a sughra (minor) qawida, derived from the broader principle 'al-asl fi al-furuj al-hazr'.
2
Work through the furuat cases
Examine the two cases presented: (1) If someone buys a slave woman and another claims to be the seller's agent, intercourse is permissible based on that single claim. (2) If a slave woman says she is a gift from her master and the man strongly believes her, intercourse is permissible.
Both cases rely on the qawida: the single claimant's word is accepted for permissibility.
3
Understand Hamawi's objection
Allama Hamawi objected to the second furua: a gift requires offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul), which are absent here. He argued that permissibility of intercourse cannot be based on mere statement.
His objection highlights a potential conflict between the qawida and contract law.
4
Recognize the Hanafi response via mu'ata
The author responds that the contract can be valid by mu'ata (exchange without formal verbal contract), which is accepted in Hanafi fiqh for such transactions. Custom ('urf) supports this, as gifts of slave women were often given and received without explicit words.
Thus, the qawida's application is preserved, and the objection is resolved.

The Qawida 'Al-Asl fi al-Furuj al-Hazr' and Its Application to Agent Death
Ruling on agent death and caution in sexual matters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'al-asl fi al-furuj al-hazr' to a scenario where an agent dies before delivery and identify the mufta bihi ruling regarding the heir's word.

Core Idea
If an agent dies before handing over a slave girl, she is forbidden to the principal—caution is paramount in private matters.

This lesson examines a specific furu' (subcase) of the Hanafi qawida 'al-asl fi al-furuj al-hazr' (the default in private parts is prohibition). The author presents a scenario from wakalah where an agent appointed to buy a slave girl on described attributes dies before delivery. The default prohibition applies because the agent could have bought her for himself. The heir's statement is taken as the most reliable indicator of the agent's intention. We will dissect the qawida, its application, and the practical ruling for a mufti.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qawida
State the maxim 'al-asl fi al-furuj al-hazr' and understand its rank as an intermediate (wusta) universal principle governing all matters of private parts.
This maxim is foundational in Hanafi fiqh for marriage, concubinage, and related contracts.
2
Recognize the scenario
An agent (wakil) is appointed to buy a non-specific (ghayr mu'ayyan) slave girl with described attributes. He buys one matching the description but dies before delivery.
The agent could have bought for himself because he was not restricted to buy for the principal only.
3
Apply the qawida
The default prohibition renders the slave girl haram on the principal, despite the purchase matching the description. The possibility that the agent intended for himself triggers the prohibition.
The apparent (dahir) permissibility is overridden by the default (asl).
4
Determine the procedural rule
The heir (warith) of the agent is the successor (qa'im maqam) and his statement is taken to ascertain the agent's intention. The principal must rely on the heir's word.
This is due to the heir's presumed knowledge and succession.
5
Note the istithna'
The passage from al-Mudmarat adds an exception: if an official appointed by the Imam verifies the ownership chain, then suspicion is removed and permissibility may be established.
But in this scenario, no such verification exists, so the default prohibition stands.

Applying the Maxim 'Default is Prohibition in Private Parts' to Agency Cases
Ruling on an agent buying a slave-girl with specified attributes and dying before delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'الأصل التحريم في الفروج' to resolve doubts in ownership of slave-girls and construct a fatwa based on the heir's statement.

Core Idea
Default prohibition in private parts overrides apparent conformity until certainty is established.

This lesson examines a novel case derived from the Hanafi qawida that the default ruling in matters of private parts is prohibition. The author, having not found a direct textual ruling, applies the maxim to a scenario where an agent dies before delivering a slave-girl. You will learn how the maxim operates, its scope, and how it interacts with the principle of certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
A principal appoints an agent to buy a slave-girl with described attributes. The agent buys a slave-girl matching the description and dies before delivery. What is the ruling for the principal?
2
Recognize the legal problem
There is a doubt: did the agent intend the purchase for the principal or for himself? The agent could legally buy for himself since the item was not physically specific (non-ayn).
Al-wakil bi-shira' ghayr al-mu'ayyin lahu an yashtariyah li nafsih.
3
Apply the qawida
The default in matters of private parts (furuj) is prohibition. Therefore, despite the apparent conformity of the purchase with the description, the slave-girl is forbidden to the principal due to the possibility of self-purchase.
Al-asl al-tahrim fi al-furuj.
4
Determine the practical resolution
The heir (warith) stands in the agent's place and is asked about the intention. His statement is taken because he has knowledge of the agent's affairs.
Yanbaghi al-ruju' ila qawl al-warith li-annahu khalifatuh.
5
Note the istithna'
If the agent explicitly stated at the time of purchase that he was buying for the principal, then certainty overrides the probability, and the slave-girl becomes permissible.
Al-yaqin la yazul bi al-shakk.

Commentary
This passage applies the qawida that the default ruling (asl) in matters of private parts (furuj) is prohibition (tahrim). The author raises a case not explicitly discussed in earlier texts: when a principal appoints an agent to buy a slave-girl with specified attributes, and the agent buys such a slave-girl but dies before delivering her. The implied rule is that the slave-girl is forbidden to the principal because there is a possibility that the agent intended the purchase for himself, as an agent for an unspecified item can buy it for himself. The fact that the agent bought exactly the described attributes suggests it was for the principal, but the default (asl) is prohibition. Therefore, to resolve the doubt, the heir (who stands in the agent's place) should be asked. The author notes there are similar applications (nazair) in fiqh, indicating this is a recurring pattern governed by the same maxim.

The Maxim ‘Speech is Interpreted According to Its Literal Meaning’ and Its Applications
Explaining the qawida with furuat on nikah and walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi qawida that speech is interpreted literally, apply it to the words 'nikah' and 'walad' in relevant fiqh chapters, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-position regarding the meaning of 'nikah'.

Core Idea
Words are literal unless context forces metaphor — the meaning of nikah and walad shapes fatwa and qada.

This passage from Sharah al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair introduces a foundational qawida (legal maxim): 'Speech is interpreted according to its literal meaning' (الكلام يحمل على الحقيقة). While this may seem simple, its application in fiqh leads to significant differences in rulings, particularly in the interpretation of key terms like 'nikah' and 'walad'. The author explains the principle with examples from oaths, Quranic prohibitions, waqf, and wasiyyah, and contrasts the Hanafi position with that of the Shafi'i school. Understanding this qawida is essential for any mufti or faqih because it determines how legal texts and utterances are understood when no contextual indication (qarinah) shifts the meaning to the metaphorical.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qawida and its scope
State the maxim in Arabic and English. Recognize that it is a sughra (minor) maxim that applies across all fiqh chapters. The default interpretation of any word is its literal (haqiqi) meaning.
This principle complements the rule that custom can override literal meaning when established.
2
Analyze the furu‘ on 'nikah'
Work through the first application: the word 'nikah' in oaths and in the Quranic verse 'Wa la tankihu ma nakaha abaukum'. According to Hanafis, the literal meaning is intercourse (wati'). Understand the ruling on oath-breaking and the prohibition of the father's muzniyah.
The Shafi'i school differs, holding that 'nikah' literally means the marriage contract.
3
Examine the furu‘ on 'walad'
Analyze the second application: the word 'walad' (singular) in waqf and wasiyyah. It literally means biological child (walad li-sulbihi). Thus, grandchildren are not included if a biological child exists. The plural 'awlad' may include all descendants if custom (urf) so dictates.
The exception for custom shows the interaction between this qawida and the maxim 'Custom is authoritative'.
4
Understand the inter-madhhab implications for qada
Consider the scenario where a Shafi'i judge rules that a father's muzniyah is permissible for the son. The Hanafi position is that such a ruling is not enforced because it contradicts the clear text (nass) of the Quran as understood by Hanafis. Contrast this with cases based on ijtihad (e.g., prohibition through touching with lust) where a Shafi'i judge's ruling may be enforced.
This illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of the different interpretations.
5
Synthesize the qawida with custom and other maxims
Note that the literal meaning can be overridden by custom (urf) when it is the dominant understanding. This connects to the istithna' (exception) mentioned in the passage. Also relate to complementary maxims like 'لا عبرة بالاحتمال النادر' and 'العبرة في العقود للمقاصد والمعاني'.
The mufti must weigh the literal meaning against any established custom or contextual indication.

Oaths: Direct Action vs. Agency – Applying the Qawida of Literal Meaning
Determining when an oath is broken by direct action and by agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-yamīnu 'alā al-ḥaqīqati illā idhā ta‘adhdharat' to determine whether an oath is violated by direct action or agency, and identify exceptions based on custom and the swearer's status.

Core Idea
An oath is broken by direct action; agency only breaks it when custom or status makes agency the reality.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair demonstrates a core qawida in action: oaths are interpreted literally unless custom dictates otherwise. You will learn the default rule that only direct action (mubāshara) constitutes hanath, and exceptions for persons like judges who never act directly, and for actions routinely done by proxy. By mastering this, you will be able to issue sound fatwas on whether an oath is broken when an agent is involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling for ordinary persons
For a typical person who swears not to do an act (e.g., sell, buy), only his own direct action violates the oath. Appointing an agent does not count as hanath because the literal meaning of the verb refers to the swearer himself.
The Arabic phrase 'lā yaḥnathu illā bi al-mubāsharah' establishes this default.
2
Recognise the exception for those who never perform the act directly
If the swearer is a judge, amir, or similar figure who never personally performs the act (e.g., a judge never sells directly), then both direct action and agency violate the oath, because for him, agency is the reality.
The text says 'illā an yakūna mithluhu lā yubāshiru dhālika al-fi‘l'.
3
Apply the tiebreaker when the swearer does both
If a person sometimes acts directly and sometimes through an agent, consider which mode is more frequent (al-aghlab). If he mainly delegates, then agency counts; if mainly direct, then agency does not.
The dabt 'wa in kāna yubāshiruhu marratan wa yuwakkilu fīhi ukhrā fa-innahu yu‘tabaru fīhi al-aghlab'.
4
Identify actions where agency always violates
The passage lists specific actions for which both direct action and agency automatically violate the oath, regardless of frequency: marriage, divorce, khul', emancipation, etc. These are actions customarily done by proxy.
The list is given after 'mā yaḥnathu bihimā'.
5
Synthesise the fatwa approach
When a mufti receives a case, he must first identify the swearer's normal practice and status, then apply the default or exception, and finally check if the action is in the special list.
This yields a clear ruling: hanath or no hanath.

The Qā‘ida of Majority Practice in Oaths: Personal Action vs. Agency
Ruling on considering majority practice when a person acts directly or via agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qā‘ida 'If direct action and agency are combined, the majority is considered' to determine oath-breaking in given scenarios.

Core Idea
An oath is broken according to your custom—if you usually do it yourself, an agent's act still breaks the oath.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqā’iq presents a crucial dabt (sub-maxim) for oaths: when someone sometimes acts personally and sometimes through an agent, we look at what is predominant (aghlab). This rule bridges the two qawā‘id of 'al-umūr bi-maqāṣidihā' and 'al-‘ādah muḥakkamah'. The mufti must ascertain the oath-taker's established habit before issuing a fatwa. Without this rule, many oaths would be rendered meaningless by the mere appointment of an agent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the oath
What specific act did the person swear to do? For example, 'I will not sell this item' or 'I will not give charity.'
The act must be something the person can do directly or through an agent.
2
Classify the modes
Does the person do the act directly (mubāsharah), through an agent (tawkīl), or both? If both, proceed to the next step.
If only one mode is used, the ruling is straightforward.
3
Determine the majority (aghlab)
Ascertain which mode is the predominant practice of the oath-taker. This requires knowing his habit—does he usually do it himself or delegate?
The qā‘ida says الاعتبار للأغلب.
4
Apply the qā‘ida
If the predominant mode is direct action, then performing the act through an agent also breaks the oath. If the predominant mode is agency, then direct action may not break it. If both are equally prevalent, consult further details.
The list in Kanz gives many actions where both modes cause oath-breaking when the person is accustomed to both.
5
Consider exceptions
Is there a custom that overrides? The rule is supported by custom (al-‘ādah muḥakkamah). If the oath-taker exclusively uses agency, direct action may not break the oath.
The istithnā’ is that the oath is considered according to his custom.

When Do Oaths Cover Invalid Acts? — The Hanafi Rule
General ruling on oaths and agency covering invalid acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on whether an oath on an act or contract includes invalid (fasid) instances and apply it to cases of agency, marriage, sale, prayer, hajj, and fasting with correct tense distinction.

Core Idea
An oath's scope depends on the act and tense: past tense covers both sahih and fasid; future tense covers only sahih.

This passage from the Ashbah wa al-Nazā'ir tradition clarifies a critical principle for muftis: when a person swears an oath or gives an agency, does it include invalid (fasid) transactions or only valid (sahih) ones? The fuqaha have laid down specific rules that distinguish between different types of contracts and tenses. By internalizing these rules, you will be able to issue precise fatwas regarding oaths and agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act or contract
Determine what the oath or agency concerns: sale, marriage, prayer, hajj, fasting, etc. Each type has its own rule.
The text specifies sale, marriage, prayer, hajj, fasting.
2
Check the tense of the oath
If the oath is in the past tense (e.g., 'I have not married'), it includes both valid and invalid instances. If in the future tense (e.g., 'I will not marry'), it only covers valid instances.
This applies to marriage, prayer, hajj, fasting.
3
Distinguish between sale and marriage agency
For agency (tawkīl) in sale, both valid and invalid sales are included because an invalid sale still has some legal effect. For agency in marriage, only valid marriage is included because an invalid marriage is not considered a marriage at all.
The key difference: sale agency is broader; marriage agency is narrower.
4
Apply the analogy to other acts
The rule for oaths on prayer, hajj, fasting, and sale follows the same pattern as marriage: past tense includes both, future tense only valid.
These are explicitly mentioned in the text.

Literal Meaning in Oaths and Wills
Applications of the maxim that literal meaning governs in oaths and wills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that oaths and wills are interpreted literally and identify when custom overrides this literal interpretation.

Core Idea
Oaths and wills default to literal meaning, but custom can override—the mufti must weigh both.

This lesson explores the Hanafi legal maxim that in oaths (ayman) and wills (wasaya), words are taken in their literal sense (haqiqah). However, the school also recognizes that custom (urf) can shift the meaning. We will examine several furuat (applications) from classical Hanafi texts that demonstrate this principle and its exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key word in the oath or will
Find the term that is the subject of the oath or bequest. For example, 'sheep', 'date palm', 'wheat', or 'sons'.
2
Determine its literal meaning (haqiqah)
What is the default linguistic meaning of the word? Consult lexicons and common usage.
3
Check if literal meaning is possible
If the literal meaning is physically possible (e.g., eating the grain itself is possible), then it is taken literally. If impossible, metaphorical meaning applies.
4
Consider customary usage (urf)
If there is a strong custom that gives the word a different meaning, that custom may override the literal meaning. For example, 'placing foot' is customarily understood as entering.
5
Apply the ruling and note exceptions
Based on the above, determine whether the oath is broken or the will includes certain beneficiaries. Remember that some exceptions exist, like including grandsons in a will for 'sons' when seeking protection.

Commentary
This passage expands the maxim that literal meaning (haqiqah) governs in oaths and wills by presenting several applications (furuat). The author first discusses oath cases: swearing not to eat from a sheep only breaks by its meat, not milk or offspring; swearing not to eat from a date palm includes fruit and spathe but not manufactured products like molasses, unless the tree has no fruit then breaking occurs by anything bought with its price; swearing not to eat from wheat breaks only by the grain itself, not bread, because the literal meaning is the grain; swearing not to drink from the Tigris breaks only by lapping water directly from the river, not by using a cup or hand, unless the oath specifies 'from the water of the Tigris' where any mode breaks the oath. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk' to determine when an oath regarding prayer is broken, distinguishing between general and specific oaths.

Core Idea
The precise legal definition of 'salah' determines when an oath is broken.

Oaths related to prayer often hinge on what exactly constitutes 'salah' in legal terms. The Hanafi school, guided by the maxim 'Certainty is not removed by doubt', requires a clear definition to avoid premature oath-breaking. This passage walks through several scenarios—general oaths, oaths using 'la yusalli', oaths for specific prayers like Dhuhr, and oaths concerning congregation—showing how the definition of prayer interacts with the intention of the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim at work
The maxim 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk' means that a certain state (e.g., not having broken an oath) continues until it is removed by certainty (e.g., performing the act that breaks the oath). Understand that doubt does not change the ruling.
This maxim is foundational in oaths and vows.

The Hanafi maxim 'certainty is not removed by doubt' has seven exceptions where precaution overrides the maxim to protect purity and worship.

The maxim 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' is a cornerstone of Hanafi jurisprudence across all chapters. This lesson examines the seven exceptions listed in the text, each illustrating how doubt triggers an obligation rather than being ignored.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the Hanafi legal maxim 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' (Certainty is not removed by doubt). The author, while affirming the maxim, presents seven exceptional rulings (مستثنیات) where despite the presence of doubt, the law requires action based on precaution. These are not contradictions to the maxim but rather applications where the Sharī‘ah considers the probability of certainty (e.g., due to a prior state) or where the doubt is so pervasive that it is treated as a continuous state. The passage first discusses three exceptions under the first benefit: (1) a mustahādah mutaḥayyirah (a woman with irregular bleeding who cannot distinguish menstruation from istihāḍah) must perform ghusl for every prayer; (2) someone who finds wetness on his clothes without knowing whether it is manī or madhī must perform ghusl; (3) someone who finds a dead animal in a well after using its water for wuḍū' must repeat the prayers of one day and night if the time of the animal's fall is unknown. The fourth exception deals with a person who doubts for the first time whether he said the takbīr or made wuḍū' or wiped his head; he must repeat. The fifth exception: if najāsah touches one's garment but the exact spot is unknown, the entire garment must be washed. The sixth exception: if a hunter shoots game, it disappears, and is later found dead without known cause, it is ḥarām, though there is a condition in al-Kanz that the hunter must have ceased seeking it. The seventh exception involves a cat that eats a rat and then drinks water; according to Imam Abū Ḥanīfah, if it drinks immediately, the water becomes najis, but if it delays, it remains pure due to the possibility of self-cleaning with saliva. Imam Muḥammad disagrees, holding that impurity is only removed by pure water. All these exceptions are derived from the general principle of precaution (iḥtiyāṭ) and were discussed in earlier references like al-Ẓahīriyyah.

Key Terms
مستحاضة متحيرة (mustahādah mutaḥayyirah): A woman with chronic uterine bleeding (istihadah) who has no fixed menstrual pattern and cannot distinguish between menstrual and istihadah blood.
تكبير تحریمہ (takbīr taḥrīmah): The opening takbir (Allahu Akbar) that initiates the prayer and makes it obligatory.
نجاست (najāsah): Impurity that prevents the validity of prayer unless removed from the body, garment, or place.

Hanafi Definitions of Shakk, Zann, and Wahm and Their Application in Prayer
Defines key terms and applies them to prayer cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shakk, zann, and wahm as defined by Hanafi jurists, and apply these distinctions to determine the validity of prayer actions in cases of doubt.

Core Idea
Equal doubt does not invalidate; preponderant opinion (zann) suffices; preponderance of error (wahm) is not relied upon.

In Hanafi fiqh, certainty is a high bar, but doubt comes in degrees. This passage from 'Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir' defines three key terms—shakk, zann, and wahm—and illustrates their practical impact through concrete prayer scenarios. Understanding these definitions is essential for a mufti who must assess when a worshipper's doubt affects the validity of an act, and when it does not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three states of doubt
From the passage, extract the definitions: shakk (تساوي الطرفين), zann (ترجيح جهة الصواب), and wahm (رجحان جهة الخطأ). Note that Hanafi jurists classify zann as a type of shakk because both involve hesitation.
2
Distinguish akbar ar-ra'y/ghalib al-zann
Recognize that akbar ar-ra'y and ghalib al-zann are synonymous and refer to a strong preponderance accepted by the heart, which is the standard for action in Hanafi fiqh.
This is stronger than mere zann but still less than certainty.
3
Apply to the takbir case
Work through the three scenarios from al-Tatarkhaniyya: if the follower's preponderant opinion is that he said takbir after the imam, prayer is valid; if before, invalid; if equal doubt, valid because his action is assumed correct.
Equal doubt = shakk; preponderance = zann/akbar ar-ra'y.
4
Apply to the excuse case
For a person with a chronic excuse who doubts whether the excuse has ceased, his wudu is not considered valid for prayer because the doubt prevents the certainty required for purity.
This shows that doubt does not remove the original certainty (istishab).
5
Synthesize the ruling framework
Derive the principle: in cases of doubt, the original certainty prevails (al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk). For preponderant opinion (zann), the stronger side is followed. For equal doubt, the original state remains.
This is the mufta bihi approach in Hanafi fatwa.

Preponderant Belief is Attached to Certainty
Principle that preponderant belief equals certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-ghalib mulhaq bi al-yaqin' to distinguish between preponderant belief and mere doubt in determining rulings on wudu and talaq.

Core Idea
Preponderant belief is treated as certainty in Hanafi fiqh — doubt does not break certainty.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-ghalib mulhaq bi al-yaqin' (preponderant belief is attached to certainty). The maxim governs cases where a person has a strong inclination but not absolute certainty. Through examples in wudu and divorce, you will learn how Hanafi jurists operationalize this principle, and how it interacts with the companion maxim 'al-yaqin la yazul bi al-shakk' (certainty is not removed by doubt).

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its rank
Identify the Arabic text of the maxim and its place among Hanafi qawaid (kubra/wusta/sughra). Understand that it is a wusta (intermediate) maxim governing many specific rulings.
The maxim is often paired with 'al-yaqin la yazul bi al-shakk'.
2
Define key terms: ghalabah, zann, shakk
Distinguish between preponderant belief (ghalabah), mere supposition (zann), and doubt (shakk). In the passage, zann is used synonymously with shakk, while ghalabah is higher in certainty.
Ghalabah is like 'preponderant probability', not just a slight inclination.
3
Apply the maxim to the wudu furu
Consider the case: a person doubts if his wudu is broken. If he has mere doubt, original state remains. If he has preponderant belief it is broken, wudu is nullified. The maxim activates only at the level of ghalabah.
4
Apply the maxim to the talaq furu
Consider the case: a person doubts if he uttered divorce. Mere doubt does not effect divorce; preponderant belief does. The maxim establishes that preponderant belief yields a ruling of occurrence.
5
Identify the complementarity with the companion maxim
The two maxims work together: 'al-yaqin la yazul bi al-shakk' preserves the original state under doubt; 'al-ghalib mulhaq bi al-yaqin' overrides it when preponderance is reached.

Istishab: Presumption of Continuity in Hanafi Usul
Defining istishab, its evidenciary value, and three furu' from Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of istishab, distinguish between the three positions on its hujjiyyah, and apply it to the furu' of shuf'ah, mafqud, and impurity claims.

Core Idea
Istishab is a proof for defense, not for establishing new rights.

In this lesson, we examine the legal principle of istishab (presumption of continuity) as defined by the Hanafi school. The passage presents three scholarly positions on its validity as a legal proof and then applies the chosen view—that istishab is only a proof for repelling claims (daf'), not for establishing rights (istihqaq)—to three practical branches from Hanafi fiqh. Understanding this distinction is crucial for a mufti when allocating the burden of proof.

Guided Path
1
Define istishab and its conditions
Identify the definition from al-Tahrir: 'the ruling of the continuation of a confirmed matter whose non-existence is not supposed'. Note the conditions: there must be a previously established state (amr muhaqqaq) and no presumption of its non-existence.
The condition 'lam yuẓann 'adamuhu' distinguishes istishab from mere conjecture.
2
Map the three positions on hujjiyyah
List the three views: (1) absolute proof, (2) absolute rejection, (3) the chosen Hanafi view: proof for daf' only. Identify the proponents of the third view: Abu Zayd, Shams al-Aimmah, and Fakhr al-Islam. Recognize the talil (reason): the cause of existence is not the cause of continuation.
The author of al-Tahrir seems to lean towards the second view (not a proof at all), but the mashhūr among fuqaha is the third.
3
Apply istishab to the first branch: shuf'ah
In the case where a buyer denies the preemptor's ownership, the buyer's statement is accepted because his claim of non-ownership is supported by istishab (original state of no ownership). The preemptor must bring evidence (bayyinah) to establish entitlement.
This illustrates daf': the buyer repels the claim using istishab.
4
Apply istishab to the second branch: mafqud
A missing person (mafqud) does not inherit nor is inherited from because his life or death is uncertain; istishab of his original state (alive or dead?) is not sufficient to establish inheritance rights. Instead, specific evidence is needed.
This shows that istishab is insufficient for istihqaq (acquiring inheritance).
5
Apply istishab to the third branch: oil spill case
When someone spills oil and the owner claims damages, but the spiller claims a mouse fell in (making it najis and thus non-liable), the spiller's denial of liability is accepted. The witnesses only attest to the spilling, not to the absence of impurity. Istishab supports the spiller's claim that the oil was pure at the time of spill, but the claim of impurity is a defense (daf') against liability.
The spiller uses istishab to repel the claim of liability, not to establish a new right.

Core Idea
Certainty is presumed in property, but in blood, stricter rules apply to prevent chaos.

This lesson explores how the major Hanafi maxim 'Certainty is not removed by doubt' operates differently in property and blood claims. Through concrete cases of oil, meat, and murder, you will see that the same maxim yields different rulings due to maqasid considerations. The passage also presents an objection and response that refines the maxim's scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qawida and its statement
Locate the major maxim 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' in the passage. Understand that it is a universal rule governing the presumption of continuity (istishab).
This qawida is from the five major maxims of Hanafi fiqh.
2
Examine property cases (oil, meat) and apply the maxim
Read the cases where oil is poured or meat is destroyed. The defendant claims the property was already impure or carrion. Apply the maxim: since the original state (purity or slaughter) is doubtful, the defendant's claim is accepted with oath, and no compensation is due.
The key is that the denier (munkir al-daman) is believed with oath when there is no evidence against him.
3
Understand the role of istishab and the denier's oath
Istishab (presumption of continuity) supports the defendant's claim because the previous state of the property is not proven to have changed. The denier's oath is sufficient in property matters.
This is the standard Hanafi position in itlaf cases.
4
Identify the blood exception: killer's claim of apostasy is rejected
Contrast with the blood case: if a killer claims the victim was an apostate or a murderer, his claim is not accepted. This is the exception to the maxim.
The exception is derived from Kitab al-Istihsan.
5
Analyze the rationale: preventing aggression and gravity of blood
The passage explains that accepting such claims would open the door to aggression (فتح باب العدوان). Blood is too serious (أمر الدم عظيم), so a stricter rule applies: the killer must be imprisoned until he confesses or swears, and fifty oaths are required (qasama) instead of one.
This exception is based on maqasid al-shari'ah and the subsidiary maxim 'الضرر يزال'.

Commentary
This passage applies the major qawida 'اليقين لا يزول بالشك' (Certainty is not removed by doubt) to two distinct areas: property damage and blood crimes. The author first presents a case from the Bazzaziyya: a person pours away another's oil. The owner claims compensation. The pourer defends that the oil was already impure (due to mice). Since the owner cannot prove the oil was pure (the original state of purity is doubted), and the witnesses only saw the pouring not the impurity, the pourer's word is accepted with an oath. This is because he is the denier of the guarantee. The same applies to meat: if someone destroys butcher's meat and claims it was carrion, his statement is accepted if there is no proof otherwise, based on istishab of the original state (the meat is presumed slaughtered). The author then raises an objection from Kitab al-Istihsan: why is the same principle not applied in blood? If a killer claims the victim was an apostate or a murderer, why is his claim rejected? The response is that blood is more serious (أمر الدم عظيم). Accepting such claims would open the door to widespread violence, as every killer would fabricate excuses. Therefore, the law distinguishes: in property, the defendant's oath suffices; in blood, fifty oaths are required (qasama) or the accused is imprisoned until he admits or swears. This is a critical refinement of the maxim: while certainty is generally presumed, in blood cases a stricter rule applies due to the higher stakes and to prevent abuse. The lesson demonstrates that قواعد كلية (universal maxims) are not absolute; they have استثناءات (exceptions) based on maqasid.

Key Terms
استصحاب (istishab): Presumption of continuity of a previous state (e.g., purity or life) until evidence of change.
منكر ضمان (munkir al-daman): One who denies liability for compensation; his statement is accepted with oath if no evidence against him.
قصاص (qisas): Retributive justice in murder or wounding; retribution in kind.
سد الذرائع (sadd al-dhara'i): Blocking the means to evil; a principle that prohibits actions that may lead to harm.

Accepting Claims Without Evidence: Property vs Homicide
Expansion of a Hanafi dabt with furuat and objection-response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'قبول القول في المال دون النفس' to distinguish rulings on accepting claims in property and homicide, and identify when evidence overrides the presumption.

Core Idea
A defendant's claim is accepted in property to prevent trivial disputes, but not in homicide to prevent abuse and protect life.

This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi dabt that governs liability when evidence is absent. Through two furuat and a critical objection-response, we explore the legal reasoning (ta'lil) behind the distinction between property and life. Understanding this maxim is essential for a mufti when adjudicating claims lacking proof.

Guided Path
1
State the Maxim and Its Application Scope
First, state the maxim in Arabic and English. Identify the areas of fiqh it governs: qiṣāṣ, jināyāt, ḍamān.
The maxim is conditional on the absence of evidence (bayyinah).
2
Identify the Underlying Ratio Legis (Ta'lil)
Why does the Sharia treat property and homicide differently? The text states: property damage is 'ahwan' (trivial) compared to life, which is 'ahamm' (more important). Accepting a claim in homicide would open the door to widespready killing.
This ta'lil is crucial for analogical reasoning.
3
Apply the Maxim to the Furuat
Work through the two cases: (1) the destroyer of meat claims it was carrion – his claim is accepted, no liability. (2) the killer claims apostasy or revenge – his claim is rejected, liability remains.
Both assume the claimant has no evidence.
4
Analyze the Objection (Iktiraash) and Hanafi Response
The objector asks: if property damage and homicide both lack evidence, why treat them differently? The Hanafi response emphasizes the danger of accepting claims in homicide – it would lead to chaos. This strengthens the maxim by showing its rationale.
This step demonstrates how a mufti defends the school's position.
5
Recognize the Exception and the Shift When Evidence Exists
When the plaintiff has clear evidence (bayyinah), the maxim is overridden by the rule 'al-bayyinah 'ala man idda'a'. Both in property and homicide, judgment is based on proof, not the defendant's claim.
The dabt 'إذا لم تكن بينة' restricts the maxim.

They say that he will not be liable, and there was an objection against them from a case in Kitab al-Istihsan: if a man killed another, and when demanded retaliation, he said 'He had apostatized' or 'He had killed my father, so I killed him in retaliation or for apostasy', then his statement is not accepted because it would open a door for anyone to kill and claim that he did it for that reason, and killing would go unpunished. Replying, the Qadi said: if his statement were accepted, it would result in extreme oppression and transgression, which is not to be allowed, contrary to property, which is of a lower degree compared to blood. For property, judgment is given based on the claim, while for blood, he is imprisoned until he confesses or takes an oath. In property, one oath suffices, while in blood, fifty oaths are required. The words of Bazzazi are complete. Explanation: Similarly, if someone destroyed a butcher's meat, and the butcher claimed compensation, but the destroyer said it was carrion, so no indemnity is due, then his statement is accepted and no indemnity is imposed. Both cases are when the owner has no evidence against the destroyer. If he presents the item or establishes testimony, judgment is given according to the claim. Objection: In both cases you did not impose liability on the destroyer, while in homicide you impose liability on the killer even when he claims the victim was apostate or had killed his father, whereas just as you did not impose indemnity on the one causing property damage, similarly you should not impose indemnity on the killer. The answer is that property damage is trivial compared to life damage. Therefore, if the killer's statement were relied upon, a series of murders would occur and lives would be insecure. Every killer would claim his victim was apostate and escape retaliation or blood money. Hence the difference between the two rulings. 'Wa iktafa bi-yamin wahid': Property damage is trivial compared to life damage, and life damage is more important in Sharia. Therefore, if a murder occurs and the killer is unknown, then qasama is adopted: fifty men of the locality take an oath saying 'We did not kill and we do not know the killer' – then only the claim of retaliation or blood money from the locals is dropped, while in a property case, the defendant takes one oath and it is accepted.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of a legal maxim from the Hanafi school regarding the difference between accepting the claim of a defendant in property versus homicide cases. The text begins by presenting a criticism (i'tirad) from a case in Kitab al-Istihsan: if a killer claims the victim was apostate or had killed his father, why is his claim not accepted? The reply by Imam Bazzazi (the author of the commentary, likely based on earlier Hanafi authorities) explains that accepting such claims in homicide would open a door to chaos and oppression, since any killer could fabricate an excuse. In contrast, in property matters, the risk is lower, so the defendant's claim is sufficient to avoid liability, unless the plaintiff has clear evidence (bayyinah). The text then gives a furu': a butcher's meat is destroyed, the destroyer claims it was carrion – his claim is accepted, and he is not liable. However, if the butcher has evidence, judgment goes against the destroyer. An objection (ikhtiraash) follows: why is property treated differently from homicide? The answer is that property is trivial compared to life; to protect lives, stricter rules apply. Finally, the text touches on qasama (compurgation) where fifty oaths are required in homicide versus one oath in property, reinforcing the hierarchy of importance. This passage demonstrates the Hanafi principle of 'al-ahwan' (the lesser) and 'al-ahamm' (the more important), and how taqawwul (acceptance of a claim without evidence) is limited accordingly.

Key Terms
تقول (taqawwul): Acceptance of a claim or statement without requiring evidence, based on the presumed truthfulness of the defendant.
قسامت (qasāma): A procedure where fifty men from the locality take oath to exonerate themselves in a homicide case where the killer is unknown.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi maxim 'قبول القول في المال دون النفس' protects life by rejecting unsubstantiated claims in homicide while accepting them in property to avoid trivial disputes.
