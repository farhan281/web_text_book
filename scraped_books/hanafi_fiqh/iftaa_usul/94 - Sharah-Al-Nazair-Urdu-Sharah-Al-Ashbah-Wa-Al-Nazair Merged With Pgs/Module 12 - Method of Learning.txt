Key Terminological Definitions in Usul al-Iftaa
Defining key terminological distinctions for Hanafi usul al-iftaa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ishtimal, shumul, dhabitah, qa'idah, qanun, istithna'at, and ilham, and apply these definitions to identify their roles in Hanafi legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Ishtimal covers parts, shumul covers particulars; dhabitah is broader than qa'idah, and ilham is divine inspiration for the righteous.

This passage establishes the foundational vocabulary needed to navigate Hanafi usul al-iftaa. Without precise definitions of terms like ishtimal, shumul, dhabitah, and qa'idah, the subtle differences between legal maxims become blurred. We will unpack each definition and its practical significance for deriving rulings and constructing fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Define ishtimal and shumul
Understand each term separately: ishtimal = whole in relation to its parts; shumul = universal concept in relation to its particulars. Then contrast them to see how they describe different inclusion relationships.
Shumul is used for universals (like 'humanity') while ishtimal is for concrete wholes (like 'the body').
2
Distinguish dhabitah, qa'idah, and qanun
Place these three on a spectrum of generality: dhabitah applies to its particulars and is more general than qa'idah; qa'idah is a universal form; qanun is even more general, protecting the mind from error.
The text says dhabitah is 'more general than qa'idah' – some later scholars reverse this, but follow the source here.
3
Note the pluralization of istithna'at
Recognize that istithna'at is pluralized to indicate types, even though it is a masdar. This shows how technical terms can deviate from normal language rules.
Pluralization is permissible when diversification is intended.
4
Handle the objection to ilham
Identify the Quranic verse (91:8) that challenges the definition of ilham as a source. Explain the Hanafi response: ilham in the verse means 'teaching' or 'clarification', not the technical ilham.
Ilham is accepted for righteous individuals but is not a primary source of law.

Commentary
This passage provides lexical and terminological foundations for the study of usul al-iftaa. The author clarifies key terms: ishtimal (inclusion by the whole of its parts) vs shumul (inclusion by the universal of its particulars); dhabitah (a universal ruling that applies to its particulars) vs qa'idah (a universal form) vs qanun (a legal principle that can be partial or universal, protecting the mind from error); istithna'at (exceptions); and ilham (divine inspiration). The distinction between dhabitah and qa'idah is important: dhabitah is more general than qa'idah (some say opposite). The author also notes that istithna'at is pluralized to indicate types. For ilham, the author defends against a possible objection from the Quranic verse by interpreting ilham as 'teaching' or 'clarification'. These definitions set the stage for understanding the qawa'id (maxims) that follow.

Key Terms
الاشتمال (al-ishtimal): Description of the whole in relation to its parts (inclusion by whole)
الشمول (al-shumul): Description of the universal concept in relation to its particulars
الضابطه (al-dhabitah): A universal rule that applies to its particulars, more general than qa'idah
القاعده (al-qa'idah): A universal form by which all its particulars can be known
القانون (al-qanun): A legal instrument (either partial like a ruler or universal like a rule) that protects the mind from error
الاستثناءات (al-istithna'at): Plural of istithna; exceptions intended to indicate types
الالهام (al-ilham): Inspiration from Allah casting tranquility into the heart of righteous servants

The Seven Sections of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir
Outlines the seven divisions of the work for the reader

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and describe each of the seven sections of Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir and explain their role in a mufti's training.

Core Idea
The seven sections form a complete curriculum for muftis, from universal maxims to rare transmissions.

This passage is the author’s own table of contents for his work. He lists seven distinct sections, each designed to build a different skill in the mufti. Understanding this structure is key to using the book as a reference and to appreciating the depth of Hanafi legal methodology.

Guided Path
1
List the seven sections in order
Scan the Arabic passage and note each section number (thani, thalith, rabi', khamis, sadis, sabi') and its name. Write them down as a sequence.
2
Identify the purpose of each section
For each section, think: what specific skill does it train? Dawabit help the mufti handle exceptions; jam' wa farq teaches comparison; alghaz sharpens detection of hidden 'illa; hiyal shows lawful maneuvering; ashbah wa naza'ir groups similar cases; the seventh collects rare reports.
3
Analyze the author's emphasis on dawabit
The author says the second section (dawabit) is the most beneficial for teacher, mufti, and judge. Why? Because it deals with what is included and excluded from a rule, which is central to accurate fatwa. He also notes he added extra exceptions to prior works.
4
Connect the sections to mufti competence
Each section corresponds to a real-world mufti need: mastering exceptions (dawabit), distinguishing similar cases (jam' wa farq), solving puzzles (alghaz), lawful stratagems (hiyal), analogical reasoning (ashbah wa naza'ir), and knowing authoritative transmissions (seventh section).
5
Reflect on the interconnectedness
The sections are not isolated; for example, dawabit often rely on ashbah wa naza'ir to identify exceptions. A mufti uses all sections together when issuing a fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is the author's description of the book's structure. He enumerates seven sections: (1) Qawa'id al-Kulliyya (major maxims, presumably covered first), (2) Dawabit (sub-maxims) with inclusions and exclusions, (3) Jam' wa Farq (similarities and distinctions between rulings), (4) Alghaz (enigmatic rulings where the underlying cause is concealed), (5) Hiyal (legal stratagems to avoid haram within Sharia), (6) Ashbah wa Naza'ir (similar and analogous cases), (7) Transmissions from Imam Abu Hanifa and his companions and the early and later scholars, including various types of correspondences and rare narrations. The author explains that the section on dawabit is especially beneficial for teachers, muftis, and judges, and he notes that he will enhance previous authors' work by adding more exceptions. The commentary in Urdu further clarifies each term, e.g., jam' wa farq is illustrated by comparing the rulings that apply to both a Muslim and a dhimmi, and those that differentiate them.

Key Terms
ضوابط (dawabit): Sub-maxims or controlling rules that govern specific chapters of fiqh, often with exceptions.
جمع و فرق (jam' wa farq): The methodology of identifying both the commonalities and distinctions between legal cases.
ألغاز (alghaz): Riddles or puzzling legal issues where the underlying reason (illa) is deliberately hidden in the wording.
حيل (hiyal): Legal stratagems that use the letter of the law to achieve a permissible outcome while avoiding prohibitions.
أشباه و نظائر (ashbah wa naza'ir): Similar and analogous cases; a discipline that groups rulings by their shared underlying principles.
