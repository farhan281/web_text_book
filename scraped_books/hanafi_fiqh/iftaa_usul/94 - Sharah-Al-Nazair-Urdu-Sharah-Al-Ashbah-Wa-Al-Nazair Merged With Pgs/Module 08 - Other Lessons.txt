Three Types of Followers in Congregational Prayer
Classifies followers in prayer as mudrik, masbuq, and lahiq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mudrik, masbuq, and lahiq in Hanafi fiqh and apply the ruling for each type in congregational prayer.

Core Idea
A follower's relationship to the imam determines whether he recites independently or follows silently.

Congregational prayer in the Hanafi school has detailed rules about the role of the follower (muqtadi). This lesson introduces the three categories of followers based on when they join or miss rak'ahs. Understanding these categories is essential for a mufti to advise on correct prayer performance, especially regarding recitation and compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
From the passage, list mudrik, masbuq, and lahiq. Note that each has a distinct definition based on the timing of joining or missing rak'ahs.
2
Define mudrik
Mudrik follows the imam from the beginning to the end. He does not recite any part alone; he remains silent behind the imam.
3
Define masbuq
Masbuq joins after the imam has completed one or more rak'ahs. He is considered independent (munfarid) for the missed rak'ahs and must recite Thana, Ta'awwudh, and Surah al-Fatiha.
The passage quotes: 'المسبوق من سبقه الإمام بها أو ببعضها وهو منفرد حتى يشي ويتعوذ ويقرأ'
4
Define lahiq
Lahiq starts with the imam but misses one or more rak'ahs due to an excuse (e.g., wudu breaking, falling asleep). He too is independent for the missed rak'ahs.
The passage mentions 'بعذر ورحمة' (due to an excuse and mercy).
5
Distinguish masbuq and lahiq
The key difference: masbuq misses the beginning of the prayer, while lahiq misses the middle or end after having followed. Both are independent for missed rak'ahs.
Common misunderstanding: they are identical. Actually the timing of the disconnect differs.
6
Apply to a scenario
Given a description of a follower's prayer, determine his category and state his recitation obligations. This practical step cements the classification.

Intention Concurrency and Livestock Zakat
Ruling on zakat for grazing livestock when intention for trade changes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that intention for trade must be concurrent with the act of trade to determine zakat liability on livestock, and evaluate when livestock qualifies as sa'imah versus ma'lufah for zakat purposes.

Core Idea
Intention alone does not trigger zakat on trade goods—it must be concurrent with the act of trade; livestock zakat depends on intended use.

This lesson parses a key Hanafi rule regarding zakat on livestock and the role of intention. You will learn when an intention to trade is valid for zakat purposes and how the classification of livestock (grazing vs. fodder-fed, intended use) affects liability. The passage applies the maxim that intention must accompany action, a principle a mufti must apply carefully.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? (Owning livestock with a new intention to trade). Ruling: No zakat of trade.
2
Locate the rationale
The reasoning: intention must accompany act (muqaranah). No explicit dalil from Quran/Sunnah, but a principle from usul.
3
Distinguish the two scenarios
(a) existing grazing animal with new intention vs. (b) purchased animal with certain intention. For (b), the intention at time of purchase matters.
4
Apply the condition of milk/breeding for sa'imah
Why is this necessary? To qualify as sa'imah for zakat, the animal must be raised for milk and breeding, not for meat or labor.
5
Identify the fatwa hierarchy
Which authority level does this ruling come from? (Zahir al-Riwayah, from imams like Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani). This ruling is mufta bihi.

Trustee Liability for Using a Deposit: The Rule of Ta'addi
Ruling on when a trustee becomes liable for wearing a deposit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of transgression (ta'addi) to determine when a trustee is liable for a deposit in cases of wearing and returning an item.

Core Idea
A trustee's liability hinges on whether transgression continues at the time of loss.

This lesson unpacks a classic Hanafi rule from the Ashbah wa al-Nazair tradition, focusing on the liability of a trustee (mudi') who uses a deposited item without permission. The key is understanding that transgression (ta'addi) is not a binary state; its continuity determines liability even after the item is physically returned. We'll parse the rule through concrete examples to see how intention plays a decisive role.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal question
Determine what triggers liability (daman) for the trustee when he uses the deposit. The text presents different scenarios to illustrate.
2
Classify the trustee's actions
Distinguish between wearing the cloth (use) and returning it safely. Note the presence or absence of intention to use again.
Use without permission is ta'addi.
3
Apply the condition of continuity
Check if the transgression is ongoing at the moment of loss. If the trustee has returned the item and has no intent to use it again, ta'addi ends. If intent remains, ta'addi is considered continuous.
The ruling treats continued intent as constructive possession.
4
Derive the fatwa rule
From the scenarios, extract the general rule: The trustee is liable if loss occurs while ta'addi is present (either during use or after use with intent to reuse). He is not liable if ta'addi has ceased and loss occurs later without fault.
5
Link to broader Hanafi maxims
Connect this rule to the maxim 'Al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by intentions) and the principle that liability follows from transgression.

Core Idea
Boycotting a Muslim beyond three days is prohibited only if personal, not religious.

This lesson examines a classical Hanafi rule regarding the prohibition of cutting ties with a fellow Muslim for more than three days. The ruling is not absolute; it hinges on the intention behind the boycotting. Understanding this maxim is essential for muftis who must assess the motive in disputes.

Prohibition is established by a clear prophetic text.

Intention (qasd) is the key variable.
3
Identify the exception
When the boycott is motivated by religious concerns – e.g., avoiding a person of innovation – it becomes not only permissible but obligatory until repentance.
Suyuti's comment distinguishes between personal and religious motives.
4
Understand the source
The hadith from Abu Huraira provides the basis for the three-day limit, but its application is qualified by intention as per the maxim.
The hadith does not specify intention; the jurists deduce it from other principles.
5

The text from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir states that a woman who refrains from adornment beyond the three-day mourning period for a non-husband is not committing a prohibited act unless she specifically intends that abstention for the sake of the deceased. This distinction is essential for issuing correct fatwa in similar cases where outward actions may have multiple possible intentions.

Advanced Zakat on Unowned Property: Void and Non-Transferable
Ruling on void advanced zakat with nuance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on advanced zakat when the property proves unowned and determine whether the payment transfers to another property.

Core Idea
Zakat advanced on unowned property is void; it cannot be transferred to another distinct property.

This passage addresses a practical issue in zakat law: what happens when you pay zakat early on property that later turns out not to be yours? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling based on the principle that the advancement is contingent on ownership. Understanding this helps muftis issue correct fatwas in cases of disputed ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its condition
What is the act? Advancing zakat before the year ends. The condition for validity is that the property must be owned by the payer until the end of the year.
2

The event is 'istiḥqāq' – a claim that proves the property was owned by someone else. This can happen before or after the year's end.
3

The text states that the advancement becomes void (batal al-ta'jīl). The reason is that the payer advanced for what was not his property.

If the two properties are distinct and separate, the void advancement does not transfer to the second property. The payer must still pay zakat on the second property separately.

If it is a single nisab (e.g., 40 mithqals of gold kept together) and part is claimed, the advanced zakat may count for the remaining portion. But if the nisab is split (e.g., separate locations), each portion is treated as distinct.

Tayammum for Quran Reading: When Does It Suffice for Prayer?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on tayammum for reading Quran to determine its sufficiency for prayer, distinguishing between hadath and janabah and applying the principle of intended worship.

Core Idea
For a junub, reading Quran is intended worship; tayammum for it suffices for prayer; for one in hadath, it does not.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a critical question: if a person performs tayammum with the specific intention of reading the Quran, can they later use that same tayammum to offer prayer? The answer hinges on whether the person is in a state of minor impurity (hadath) or major impurity (janabah), and whether the act of reading Quran is considered an intended act of worship (عبادة مقصودة) for that person. The Hanafi school adopts a detailed view (tafsil), which resolves the apparent contradiction between different narrations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the specific act for which tayammum was performed (e.g., reading Quran) and note that the ruling on its sufficiency for prayer depends on the impurity state.
2

Recognize that for a person in hadath, reading Quran is permissible without tayammum; for a junub, it is prohibited. This difference makes reading Quran an 'intended worship' for the junub but not for the one in hadath.
3
Apply the principle of intent
The Hanafi principle: the intention at tayammum is considered like the intention for the act. If the intended act is 'worship intended for its own sake', the tayammum is valid for all similar acts.
4
Evaluate the ikhtilaf
Compare the general scholars' absolute prohibition with the Hanafi detailed view. Identify which view is relied upon for fatwa (tarjih) and why.
The tarjih is based on al-Bada'i and al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Commentary
This passage discusses a subtle fiqh ruling: when a person performs tayammum with a specific intention (e.g., to read Quran), does that tayammum also permit them to pray? The author distinguishes between two states. If the person is in a state of minor impurity (hadath) and performs tayammum to read Quran, the general opinion is that this tayammum is not sufficient for prayer because reading Quran for a person in hadath is not an act of worship that is intended for its own sake (غير مقصودة). It is a preparatory act. However, if the person is in a state of major impurity (janabah) and performs tayammum to read Quran, then that tayammum does suffice for prayer. This is because the act of reading Quran for a junub person is a worship in itself (عبادة مقصودة), similar to prayer. The passage also extends the same rule to tayammum for funeral prayer, prostration of recitation, etc. The key principle is that the intention (niyyah) at the time of tayammum is considered like the intention of the act itself. This is a Hanafi position that takes a middle path between absolute permissibility and absolute prohibition.

The Six Types of Worshippers Regarding Niyyah in Salah
Classification of six types of worshippers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the six types of worshippers from al-Qunyah and apply the Hanafi ruling on the validity of salah based on their knowledge and intention.

Core Idea
Knowledge of fard/sunnah can substitute for explicit intention in salah, but ignorance of the meaning invalidates it

This passage from al-Qunyah presents a detailed classification of six types of worshippers based on their knowledge and intention during salah. Understanding this classification is crucial for a mufti to evaluate the validity of a person's prayer when there is ambiguity about their intention. The Hanafi school emphasizes that niyyah is a condition (shart) for obligatory prayers, but the level of knowledge affects whether explicit mention of 'fard' is required. Let us walk through the six categories systematically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six categories
The text lists six types of worshippers. Read each description carefully. Note the key factor that distinguishes each: their knowledge of the meaning of fard/sunnah, their ability to distinguish between them, and their actual intention.
The categories range from complete knowledge (type 1) to complete ignorance (type 6).
2
Determine the ruling for each type
For each type, the text states whether the prayer is valid (tujzi'uhu) or invalid (la tujzi'uhu). Mark these rulings. Pay attention to exceptions, such as the fourth type where praying in congregation may change the ruling.
Types 1, 2, and 5 are valid; types 3, 4 (unless with congregation), and 6 are invalid.
3
Analyze the role of knowledge
Notice how knowledge of the meaning of fard and the ability to distinguish fard from sunnah affect validity. The first type has full knowledge and only needs to intend the specific prayer. The third type intends fard but lacks understanding of its meaning, so his prayer is invalid. This shows that intention must be informed by basic comprehension.
Knowledge of the consequences (reward/punishment) is key.
4
Identify the ikhtilaf (dispute)
The text includes a variant opinion (qila) for the fourth type: if he prays in congregation and intends the imam's prayer, it suffices. This reflects a difference within the Hanafi school. Also, there is an implicit disagreement with the Shafi'i school regarding the necessity of explicitly intending 'fard'.
The Hanafi position is that knowledge can substitute for explicit mention of fard.
5
Apply the classification to a new scenario
Given a scenario about a person's knowledge and intention, determine which type they correspond to and whether their prayer is valid. This will solidify your understanding of the criteria.

And in al-Qunyah: The worshippers are six: First: one who knows the obligatory (fard) and sunnah acts, and knows the meaning of fard that it is that for which reward is deserved by doing it and punishment by leaving it, and sunnah is that for which reward is deserved for doing it and no punishment for leaving it. Second: one who knows that, and intends fard as fard, but does not know what parts are obligatory and sunnah within it, it suffices him. Commentary
This passage from al-Qunyah presents a classification of six types of worshippers regarding the validity of their prayer based on their knowledge and intention. The classification is important for understanding how niyyah (intention) operates in the Hanafi school. The first type knows the definition of fard (obligatory) and sunnah (supererogatory) and understands the difference; even if he only intends the specific prayer (e.g., Dhuhr) without explicitly saying 'fard', his prayer is valid because his knowledge means his intention automatically covers the obligatory nature. The second type knows the definitions but does not know which parts of the prayer are fard and which are sunnah; still, his explicit intention of 'fard' suffices. The third type intends 'fard' but does not understand the meaning of fard; his prayer is invalid because he lacks the basic comprehension. The fourth type knows that some prayers are fard and some are nafl, but does not distinguish them in his own prayer; his prayer is invalid unless he follows an imam and intends the imam's prayer (according to one opinion). The fifth type mistakenly believes all prayers are fard; his prayer is valid because he intends obligation. The sixth type has no knowledge that prayer is obligatory at all, merely prays out of habit; his prayer is invalid because he lacks the requisite intention of obedience. The Hanafi position emphasizes that the validity of prayer depends on a proper intention that includes the legal classification (fard/sunnah), but the level of knowledge affects whether explicit mention is needed.

Key Terms
النية (an-niyyah): The intention in the heart to perform a specific act of worship; it may include designation (ta'yin) and legal classification (fard/sunnah).
الفَرْض (al-fard): An act commanded by Allah, the performance of which earns reward and the omission of which earns punishment.
السُّنَّة (as-sunnah): An act that is recommended; performing it earns reward, but omitting it does not incur punishment.

Intention in Cases of Doubt and Mistake: The Ruling of Fard al-Waqt
Rasm al-mufti: intention rulings for doubt and mistake

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that intention of ada/qada is interchangeable and distinguish between cases of certain exit, doubtful exit, and Jumu'ah to issue a correct fatwa.

Core Idea
Doubt preserves the validity of intending the current time's prayer, even if time actually passed.

In Hanafi fiqh, intention plays a crucial role in the validity of prayer, especially when the worshiper is mistaken or uncertain about the time. The madhhab provides flexibility: a person who prays assuming the time is still in, but later discovers it had passed, is still valid. However, the ruling becomes stricter if the time has certainly exited. This lesson unpacks the nuances of fard al-waqt intention under doubt, certainty, and Jumu'ah, and includes a cautionary note about the term 'mukhtar' in the text.

Identify the core rule from Fath al-Qadir
Ibn al-Humam states that if one intends ada assuming time remains, but later finds time had exited, it suffices. Likewise the opposite (intending qada when time remains) is also valid. This establishes interchangeability of ada and qada intentions.
This is the foundational principle.
2
Contrast with al-Nihaya's ruling when time certainly exited
If the time has certainly passed and one intends 'fard al-waqt' (current time's obligation), the prayer is invalid because it contradicts reality. This is not considered a valid qada intention either.
Certitude overrides interchangeability.
3
Apply the principle of doubt
If one doubts whether time has exited, and intends fard al-waqt, it is valid. Doubt does not remove the presumption that time remains, so the intention aligns with the apparent reality.
Based on the maxim: certainty is not removed by doubt.
4
Understand the Jumu'ah exception
Because of the ikhtilaf on Friday's obligation (Jumu'ah vs Zuhr), one should specifically intend Jumu'ah and not the generic 'fard al-waqt' to avoid potential invalidity.
A precaution due to conflicting scholarship.
5
Recognize the hierarchy note about 'mukhtar'
The Urdu commentary warns that the author (Ibn Najim) used the term 'mukhtar' in reverse: the opinion he labeled preferred is actually non-mukhtar in the madhhab, and vice versa. The actual reliable opinion for fatwa is that permissibility is the mufta bihi.

A ruling on joining a congregation with imperfect knowledge of its nature

When uncertain of a congregation's prayer, intend your owed fard; the prayer is valid if it matches, else it becomes nafl.

Imagine you arrive late and see a congregation praying. You are not sure if it is the Isha fard or Tarawih. Can you join? And among them: the owner of an excuse when he doubts its discontinuation and prays with his purification, it is appropriate that it is not valid. And among them: one came from in front of the imam and doubted whether he is ahead of him or not. And among them: doubt about whether he preceded the imam in takbir or not. Then I saw in al-Tatar Khaniyyah: When the follower does not know whether he preceded his imam in the takbir or not, if his predominant opinion is that he said takbir after him, it suffices him; if his predominant opinion is that he said takbir before him, it does not suffice him; and if the two opinions are equal, it suffices him because his matter is presumed correct until his error becomes apparent. End. And it is appropriate that the ruling of the previous issue, which is doubt about being ahead or behind, should be likewise. Translation: … Explanation: A person is excused, e.g., he has continuous urine leakage, and he makes separate wudu for every prayer. But his illness was about to cure, so he performed the next prayer with the previous wudu. Since he doubts about his excuse having ended, the prayer with the previous wudu is not valid. And among them: 'one came from in front of the imam' – a follower doubts whether he is ahead or behind the imam. Until he is absolutely certain, his prayer behind that imam is not valid regarding whether he is ahead or behind. In the first scenario, the prayer can never be valid, but in the second scenario it becomes valid. And among them: doubt about whether he preceded the imam in takbir – if a person doubts whether he said the takbir before the imam, if his predominant opinion tells him that he did not say it before, then his prayer with that takbir is valid; if his predominant opinion tells him that he did say it before, then it is not valid.

Commentary
This passage covers three distinct cases of doubt in prayer: (1) A person with a chronic excuse (like continuous urine leakage) who doubts whether his excuse has ended. If he prays a second prayer with the wudu made for the first prayer, his prayer is not valid because he is uncertain whether the excuse has ceased, and certainty is required. (2) A follower who doubts whether he is positioned ahead of the imam. The ruling is that until he is certain of his correct position, his prayer is not valid because being ahead of the imam invalidates the prayer. (3) A follower who doubts whether he said the takbir before the imam. The ruling from al-Tatar Khaniyyah is that if his dominant opinion (ghālib al-zann) is that he said it after the imam, his prayer is valid; if his dominant opinion is that he said it before, it is invalid; if both opinions are equal, it is valid because his action is presumed correct until error is evident. The author extends this principle to the case of doubt about being ahead of the imam, stating that the same rule applies.

Key Terms
صاحب العذر (ṣāḥib al-‘udhr): A person who has a continuous excuse that invalidates wudu, such as urine leakage or continuous nosebleed.
غالب گمان (ghālib al-ẓann): Predominant opinion; a level of certainty that is more than 50% but less than 100%.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, doubt in prayer does not automatically invalidate it; predominant opinion or equal doubt can preserve validity when the matter is presumed correct.
