The First Three Ranks of Hanafi Jurists
Classification of the higher ranks of Hanafi fuqaha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define the first three ranks of Hanafi jurists and determine which rank is qualified to issue fatwas without taqlid.

Core Idea
Only absolute mujtahids and those within the madhhab may originate rulings; lower ranks are muqallids.

The Hanafi madhhab, like other schools, developed a hierarchy of jurists based on their interpretive authority. This lesson introduces the top three ranks: the absolute mujtahid (mujtahid mutlaq), the mujtahid within the madhhab (mujtahid fi al-madhhab), and the mujtahid in issues (mujtahid fi al-masa'il). Understanding these ranks is crucial for a mufti to know the limits of his own authority and to correctly attribute rulings to the appropriate level of scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first rank
Locate the description of the absolute mujtahid (mujtahid mutlaq). Note that they derive rulings directly from the four sources without following any madhhab.
These are the founders of the madhhabs, e.g., the four imams.
2
Identify the second rank
Find the description of the mujtahid within the madhhab (mujtahid fi al-madhhab). They follow the uṣūl of their founder but may differ in furūʿ.
Examples: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
Identify the third rank
Find the description of the mujtahid in issues (mujtahid fi al-masa'il). They can derive rulings only when no clear transmission exists from the earlier authorities.
Examples: al-Tahawi, al-Karkhi, al-Sarakhsi.
4
Compare the three ranks
Note the key differences: the first two have authority to differ fundamentally; the third is bound by the principles and branches of the founder.
The third rank cannot oppose the founder in uṣūl or furūʿ.
5
Fatwa implications
Reflect on how this hierarchy affects fatwa: a mufti must know his own rank and not claim authority he does not possess.
Only the first two ranks can issue fatwas that are not bound by prior school rulings.

The classes of fuqaha are six: The first class is that of absolute mujtahids (mujtahidun mutlaq) who performed ijtihad in the Shari'ah, such as the four imams and the authoritative imams who followed their method, who established the principles of usul al-fiqh and who derived subsidiary rulings from the four proofs (Qur'an, Hadith, Ijma', and Qiyas) without taqlid of anyone in principles and branches. The second class is that of mujtahids within the madhhab (mujtahidun fi al-madhhab), such as Imam Abu Yusuf, Imam Muhammad, and other students of Imam A'zam, who are fully capable of deriving rulings from the four proofs in light of the principles and regulations set by their teacher. Although they opposed their teacher in some details, in principles they follow their teacher. The third class: mujtahids in issues (mujtahidun fi al-masa'il), for issues where there is no transmission from Imam A'zam and his students, these people state rulings based on their own ijtihad, e.g., al-Tahawi, al-Karkhi, al-Halwani, al-Sarakhsi, al-Bazdawi, and Qadi Khan, etc. They cannot oppose their Imam in principles or branches. The fourth class: people of takhrij (ashab al-takhrij), who are muqallids, e.g., al-Jassas al-Razi and his peers. They do not have absolute capacity for ijtihad, but because they have thoroughly preserved the principles and are aware of their sources, they can detail and determine from a transmitted summary statement from a mujtahid student of the founder that has two faces, or a ruling that has two possibilities, using their God-given ability, keeping the principles of their Imam in view, and measuring against similar cases. The fifth class: people of tarjih (ashab al-tarjih), also muqallids, without absolute ijtihad capacity, e.g., al-Quduri, the author of al-Hidayah, and similar. Their work is to prefer one of the different transmissions; for this they commonly use phrases like 'this is more appropriate', 'more correct', 'more evident', 'more suitable', 'this is more beneficial for people'. The sixth class: people of tamyiz (ashab al-tamyiz), also muqallids, but they can distinguish between stronger and weaker, and also between Zahir al-Riwayah, Zahir al-Madhhab, and Nawadir, e.g., the authors of 'Matan' [such as al-Mughni], al-Kanz, al-Mukhtar, al-Wiqayah, and al-Majma'. Their work is to not transmit rejected statements and weak transmissions in their books.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological classification of Hanafi jurists into six hierarchical levels. It is essential for a mufti to know his own level so he does not exceed his authority. The first class are the absolute mujtahids who derive rulings from primary sources independently, without taqlid. The second class are the mujtahids within the madhhab (like Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) who follow the uṣūl of their founder but may differ in furūʿ. The third class are those who can perform ijtihad on issues where no clear transmission exists from the earlier masters. The fourth class can extract (takhrīj) rulings from ambiguous statements of earlier mujtahids by applying the principles of the school. The fifth class can weigh and prefer (tarjīḥ) between different transmitted opinions using specific phrases. The sixth class can distinguish (tamyīz) between strong and weak transmissions and exclude weak ones. This classification ensures that the fatwa issued is consistent with the school's authoritative position. Note that the highest two classes (mutlaq and fi al-madhhab) are not bound by the later hierarchy; they are the founders. The lower four are muqallids with increasing levels of discernment. The passage implicitly warns against muftis claiming a rank they do not possess.

Key Terms
مجتہدین مطلق (Mujtahidun Mutlaq): Independent jurists who derive rulings directly from the four sources (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas) without following any particular madhhab.
مجتہدین فی المذہب (Mujtahidun fi al-Madhhab): Jurists who can derive rulings within the framework of the founder's principles, sometimes differing in details.
مجتہدین فی المسائل (Mujtahidun fi al-Masa'il): Jurists who can exercise ijtihad on issues where no clear transmission exists from the founder or his direct students, but they cannot oppose the founder in principles or branches.

The Lower Three Ranks: Takhrij, Tarjih, and Tamyiz
Classification of the lower three ranks of Hanafi fuqaha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Ashab al-Takhrij, Ashab al-Tarjih, and Ashab al-Tamyiz, and apply their competencies in evaluating transmitted rulings.

Core Idea
The lower three ranks of muqallids progressively refine the school's transmitted rulings through extraction, preference, and discrimination.

Below the ranks of mujtahids, the Hanafi madhhab places three additional classes of muqallid jurists who perform vital functions in preserving and transmitting the school's heritage. The fourth class (ashab al-takhrij) can extract rulings from ambiguous statements; the fifth (ashab al-tarjih) can prefer one transmission over another; the sixth (ashab al-tamyiz) can distinguish strong from weak transmissions. These ranks ensure that the fatwa issued is based on the most authoritative position of the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fourth rank
Find the description of Ashab al-Takhrij. Note that they are muqallids but can detail ambiguous rulings from earlier mujtahid students of the founder.
Example: al-Jassas al-Razi.
2
Identify the fifth rank
Find the description of Ashab al-Tarjih. They prefer one transmission over another using specific phrases like 'هذا أولى'.
Examples: al-Quduri, author of al-Hidayah.
3
Identify the sixth rank
Find the description of Ashab al-Tamyiz. They distinguish between strong and weak transmissions and exclude weak ones from their works.
Examples: authors of al-Mughni, al-Kanz, al-Mukhtar.
4
Compare the three lower ranks
Note the progressive specialization: takhrij extracts, tarjih prefers, tamyiz filters.
All are muqallids; they do not have independent ijtihad capacity.
5
Fatwa implications
These ranks determine whose opinion carries weight in fatwa: a mufti from a lower rank must defer to the preferences and distinctions made by these specialists.
A mufti of rank 6 (tamyiz) can exclude weak opinions; a mufti of rank 5 (tarjih) can choose among conflicting transmissions.

Zakah: Intention and Compulsory Collection by the Ruler
Ruling with dispute on intention and compulsory collection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that zakah requires intention, distinguish between apparent and hidden wealth, and state the mufta bihi position on compulsory collection.

Core Idea
Compulsory collection suffices for apparent wealth but not for hidden; intention is essential for zakah.

The Hanafi school treats zakah as a devotional act (ibadah maqsuda) that requires intention (niyyah). However, a question arises when the government forcibly collects zakah from a reluctant owner. Does this discharge the obligation? The answer depends on the type of wealth, and the madhhab has a clear hierarchy of opinions. This lesson unpacks the rule and the dispute.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule on intention
The passage begins by affirming that zakah is not valid without intention. Identify the phrase that establishes this foundational principle.
The rule is stated as 'وَأَمَّا الزَّكوة فَلَا يَصِحُ أَدَاءُ هَا إِلَّا بِالنِّيَّةِ'.
2
Distinguish apparent and hidden wealth
The passage divides zakah wealth into two categories: amwal zahirah (apparent) and amwal batinah (hidden). Identify which type each example falls into.
Apparent: cattle, crops. Hidden: cash, gold, silver.
3

Core Idea

The Principle of Concurrent Intention and the Ruling on Delay
Parsing the rule on intention timing and rebutting weak opinions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that intention must be concurrent with the start of the act, identify exceptions, and evaluate the status of delayed intention in fatwa.

Core Idea
Intention must accompany the start of the act; delay invalidates except in genuine necessity.

This passage from al-Khulasah and al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair establishes a fundamental rule: the best and required timing for intention is at the very beginning of the act. The text then introduces a dispute about whether intention can be delayed after takbirat al-tahrimah. You will learn the relied-upon position and why the contrary views are considered weak.

Guided Path
1
Identify the consensus and the preferred timing
Read the opening from al-Khulasah. Note that the Hanafi masters agree (ijma') that it is afdal to have the intention concurrent with starting the act. Understand the reasoning: without intention at the start, the initial part is not worship, and the whole cannot be split.
Consensus (ijma') is a strong proof here.
2
Extract the general qa'ida from al-Anfa'
The text quotes a qa'ida: 'The principle regarding intention is that it should be concurrent except in necessity.' Identify this as a wusta (intermediate) maxim. Note the exception: fasting where eating sahur serves as intention.
This qa'ida is a dabt (sub-maxim) restricting the general principle of concurrence.
3
Analyze the Zahir al-Riwayah ruling on delayed intention
The passage cites Zahir al-Riwayah: if one begins prayer without intention and intends after takbir, the prayer is not valid. Understand that this is the primary authoritative transmission (mufta bihi).
Zahir al-Riwayah is the core of fatwa.
4
Examine the dissenting view attributed to al-Karkhi
Ibn Wahban narrates that some shaykhs outside the madhhab, following al-Karkhi, permit delaying intention up to certain points (thana', Mu'awwidhatayn, ruku', raf'). Identify the range of opinions.
These views are considered weak (da'if).
5
Conclude with the mu'tamad (relied-upon) position
The text states that 'there is no escape from concurrence in reality or ruling' and that al-Karkhi's opinion is given no consideration. Understand that for fatwa, the requirement of concurrent intention is absolute.
Concurrence can be haqiqah (real) or hukman (in ruling).
