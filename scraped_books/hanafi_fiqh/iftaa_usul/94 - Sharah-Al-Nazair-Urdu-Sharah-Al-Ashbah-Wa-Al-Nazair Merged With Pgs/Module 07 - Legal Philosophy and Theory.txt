Definitions of Mustahabb and Wajib in Hanafi Fiqh
Defining mustahabb and wajib with distinguishing features

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definitions of mustahabb and wajib according to the Hanafi school and distinguish between them based on evidence and consequences.

Core Idea
Wajib is established by zanni evidence; mustahabb is occasional practice with reward but no sin for omission.

This passage clarifies two key categories in Hanafi fiqh: mustahabb (recommended) and wajib (obligatory based on speculative evidence). Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correct application of rulings, especially in ibadat. The Hanafi school uniquely differentiates wajib from fard, and mustahabb from sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of mustahabb
State that it is an action performed occasionally by the Prophet; doing it brings reward, not doing it has no sin. Note its synonyms: masnun, mandub, tatowwu', sunan zawai'd.
Mustahabb is also called sunnah ghayr mu'akkadah? No, the passage says sunnah ghayr mu'akkadah has more reward than mustahabb. So they are distinct.
2
Recall the definition of wajib
State that wajib is an action whose binding is established by zanni (speculative) evidence. It is more necessary than sunnah mu'akkadah and close to fard. Deliberate omission incurs sin but does not invalidate the act; forgetting requires sajdat al-sahw.
The denier of wajib is not called a disbeliever, unlike fard.
3
Identify the key distinguishing features
Compare the two in terms of reward, sin, evidence strength, consequence of omission, and relation to sunnah and fard.
4
Recognize the Hanafi uniqueness
Note that the Hanafi school uniquely separates wajib from fard based on evidence strength. Most other schools treat wajib as synonymous with fard.
5
Apply to a practical example
Classify witr prayer as wajib (based on ahad hadith) and two rak'ahs after wudu as mustahabb (occasional practice).

Defining Wajib: The Hanafi Obligation from Speculative Proof
Definition of wajib with distinctions from fard and sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define wajib according to the Hanafi school and explain the practical differences from fard, including consequences for omission and the status of its denier.

Core Idea
Wajib is a binding obligation from speculative proof, equal to fard in practice but its denier is not a kafir.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi definition of wajib, a critical term in fiqh and ifta. Understanding its precise boundary from fard on one side and sunnah on the other is essential for a mufti. The passage from the text clarifies the evidential basis and practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Category
Begin by recognizing that wajib is a distinct category of obligatory act, positioned between fard (definitive proof) and sunnah muakkadah (strongly recommended). The passage explicitly states it is 'more necessary than Sunnah Muakkadah and closer to Fard.'
2
Locate the Evidential Basis
Understand that wajib is established by a zanni (speculative) dalil, whereas fard is established by a qat'i (definitive) dalil. This is the foundational distinction quoted from Ibn 'Abd al-Muti.
‘ثبت لزومه بدليل ظني’
3
Trace the Practical Consequences
Analyze the two scenarios of omission: if omitted forgetfully, sajdah sahw becomes obligatory; if omitted deliberately, the prayer remains valid but must be repeated (i'adah wajib). This shows that wajib is enforceable like fard in action.
Deliberate omission does not invalidate the act, but repetition is required.
4
Note the Takfir Ruling
A crucial difference from fard: denying the obligation of a wajib does not render one a kafir. This is repeatedly emphasized in the passage, both from Ibn 'Abd al-Muti and from the author of al-Idah.
‘لا يكفر جاحده’
5
Apply the Definition to Fatwa
As a mufti, recognize that when a ruling is based on strong but not absolutely certain evidence, you classify the act as wajib. This affects the fatwa: the act must be performed, but denial does not lead to takfir.
This classification is essential for correct ifta.

The Scope of Ja'iz in Hanafi Usul
Defining Ja'iz as a supercategory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of Ja'iz in Hanafi usul and identify which categories of acts it includes and excludes.

Core Idea
Ja'iz is a supercategory encompassing all acts not prohibited, including wajib, mandub, mubah, and makruh tanzihi — but not makruh tahrimi.

This lesson clarifies the Hanafi technical meaning of Ja'iz, which is broader than common usage. Understanding this terminology is essential for correctly interpreting fatwa literature and legal classifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of Ja'iz
Read the source statement: 'Ja'iz applies to that which is not prohibited by Shariah.' This is the core definition.
2
List the included categories
From the text: Ja'iz includes mubah, makruh tanzihi, mandub, and wajib. Note that these all share the quality of not being strictly prohibited.
Wajib is included because the act itself is permissible; the prohibition is on leaving it.
3
Identify the excluded category
Makruh tahrimi is excluded because it is 'prohibited by Shariah' (mamnu'). The text states that it is near-haram or haram.
The two Shaykhs say it is close to haram; Imam Muhammad says it is haram.
4
Understand the intra-Hanafi difference
The passage notes that for makruh tahrimi, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider it close to haram, while Imam Muhammad holds it as haram. This affects whether it can be called Ja'iz.
The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is that makruh tahrimi is haram according to Imam Muhammad, but the majority view is that it is merely close to haram.
5
Apply the definition in practice
When a fatwa states an act is 'Ja'iz', it could be any of the included categories. The mufti must specify further if needed.
Common error: assuming Ja'iz always means mubah.

The Definition and Usage of Mubah and 'La Ba's'
Defining Mubah and explaining 'la ba's'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mubah (ibahah) and explain the Hanafi usage of the phrase 'la ba's' for makruh tanzihi.

Core Idea
Mubah is an act equally permissible to do or leave, while 'la ba's' often signals makruh tanzihi where avoidance is preferred.

After defining Ja'iz broadly, the passage gives a precise definition of Mubah (Ibahah) and clarifies the common phrase 'la ba's'. This helps muftis avoid ambiguity when describing permissibility.

Guided Path

State the definition of Ibahah
From the text: 'Ibahah is that in which both doing and leaving are permissible, neither one is preferred over the other.' This is the precise definition of mubah.

Contrast with Ja'iz
Mubah is a subset of Ja'iz. Ja'iz includes acts with preference (like wajib and mandub), while mubah has no preference.
3
Understand the phrase 'la ba's'
The passage notes that 'la ba's' (no harm) is often used for makruh tanzihi. This indicates that the act is permissible but omitting it is better.
It is not the same as mubah; it carries a slight disapproval.
4
Distinguish from mubah
Mubah: exact equal permission. 'La ba's': permissible but disliked (makruh tanzihi). The phrase does not mean fully mubah.
5
Apply in fatwa context
When a mufti says 'la ba's', he implies the act is not sinful but preferable to leave. Using 'mubah' implies no preference at all.
Common error: treating 'la ba's' as mubah.

Definition of Wajib in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines wājib and distinguishes it from farḍ

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of wājib and distinguish it from farḍ based on the type of evidence and the consequences of denial and omission.

Core Idea
Wājib is obligatory in practice like farḍ, but its denier is not a kāfir and omission requires sajda sahw or repetition, not nullification.

This passage from the Hanafi legal tradition draws a critical line between two levels of obligation. Understanding this distinction is essential for any student of fiqh, as it governs how rulings are derived from speculative evidence and how fatwas are issued. Let's unpack the key elements of the definition and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the first sentence: wājib is that which is proven by ẓannī evidence. Note that its practical rank equals farḍ.
Ẓannī evidence includes isolated hadith (āḥād) and qiyās.
2
Distinguish from farḍ
The key difference is that denying farḍ makes one a kāfir, whereas denying wājib does not. This is a core epistemological distinction.
Denying wājib is a sin but not apostasy.
3
Examine consequences of omission
If a wājib is omitted unintentionally, sajda sahw becomes necessary. If intentionally omitted, the prayer is valid but its repetition becomes wājib.
This shows wājib is not like sunna; it carries a remedial obligation.
4
Review the Īḍāḥ definition
The author of Īḍāḥ confirms: wājib deserves reward for performance, sin for omission, but its denier is not a kāfir.

Core Idea
Makruh tahrimi: prohibited by zanni evidence; makruh tanzihi: no prohibition, but avoidance is better.

In the Hanafi school, the term 'makruh' is divided into two distinct categories: tahrimi and tanzihi. This distinction is not merely terminological; it carries real legal consequences for sin, fatwa, and ritual validity. This lesson unpacks the definitions, evidence, and a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf over the boundary between makruh tahrimi and haram.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
State the two types of makruh: tahrimi and tanzihi. This is the foundational division.
Memorize the Arabic terms: مکروہ تحریمی and مکروہ تنزیہی.
2
Define makruh tahrimi
Describe it as an action whose prohibition is proven by ẓannī (speculative) evidence, such as a non-mutawatir hadith or a famous report. It is closer to haram.

3

Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani considers such actions haram, while the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and later scholars) classify them as makruh tahrimi to avoid confusion with qaṭʿī prohibitions.

4

An action for which there is no evidence of prohibition; leaving it is better than doing it. It is not sinful but avoidance is rewarded.

5
Apply to fatwa practice
A mufti must determine the strength of evidence: if ẓannī, the ruling is makruh tahrimi (or haram according to Imam Muhammad); if no evidence, it is makruh tanzihi.
The mufta bihi (operative position) is the majority view that it is makruh tahrimi.

Distinguishing Ja'iz from Mubah: A Key Hanafi Precision
Defining key fiqh terms and explaining their relationships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ja'iz and mubah in Hanafi terminology and explain why a makruh act is ja'iz but not mubah.

Core Idea
Ja'iz is broader than mubah; a makruh act remains ja'iz but is not mubah.

This lesson unpacks four foundational fiqh terms—tamyiz, ja'iz, fasid, and hukm—as defined in the commentary on Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. The most critical distinction for a Hanafi mufti is between ja'iz and mubah: a ruling can be ja'iz (not prohibited) yet still be makruh (disliked). Mastering these nuances is essential for accurate fatwa and scholarly precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key definitions
Read the definitions of tamyiz, ja'iz, fasid, and hukm as given in the passage. Note how each term is rooted in Quranic usage and Hanafi usul.
Tamyiz is lexical; ja'iz and fasid are key fiqh categories; hukm is the foundational concept of divine address.
2
Analyze the ja'iz-mubah distinction
The passage states: 'Every mubah is ja'iz, but not every ja'iz is mubah.' Work through the logic: jawaz is opposite of hurmah (prohibition), ibahah is opposite of karahah (dislike). Thus a makruh act is ja'iz (not haram) but not mubah (because it is disliked).
This distinction is uniquely important in Hanafi fatwa where 'ja'iz' often means 'permissible but possibly disliked'.
3
Connect definitions to hukm
The passage defines hukm as Allah's address concerning the mukallaf's actions. This overarching category encompasses all fiqh rulings, including the terms just defined.
Understanding hukm as divine address underlines the authority of fiqh rulings.
4
Apply the distinctions to a practical scenario
Consider a mufti asked about eating a slightly disliked food. The act is not haram (hence ja'iz), but it is makruh (therefore not mubah). The mufti's answer should reflect this precision.
This example shows the real consequence of the ja'iz-mubah gap.

Al-Tamyiz: separating something from another, making it apparent; this is in al-Qamus and in al-Misbah: mayyaztu al-shay'a i.e. I separated it and distinguished it from others; the taf'il is for exaggeration, i.e. it is used for exaggeration in prohibitions, as in the Holy Quran: 'That Allah may distinguish the wicked from the good'. Al-Ja'iz: passing, being valid; here what is meant is mubah; the noble scholars have explained the difference between mubah and ja'iz: every mubah is ja'iz, but every ja'iz is not mubah, because jawaz is the opposite of hurmah, and ibahah is the opposite of karahah; so when jawaz is negated, hurmah is established and ibahah is also negated; and when ibahah is negated, its opposite, i.e., karahah, is established, and jawaz is not negated by that, because jawaz combines with karahah; it is similar in al-Inayah. Al-Fasid: the opposite of salah; here by fasād is meant everything that is legally prohibited. And by wujuh al-ahkam is meant the ways of rulings. Al-Ahkam is the plural of hukm; and by hukm is meant Allah's address that relates to the actions of the obligated ones. In the author's phrase 'bi-hadha' refers to the aforementioned attributes of fiqh; here 'hādha' is used to connect what follows to what precedes, as if what precedes is the cause and what follows is the effect; as in the Noble Quran: 'This for the transgressors is an evil abode'. The continuity of the common and elite on the path of uprightness, social cohesion, and the permanence and continuation of man in the way of civilization, depends on his knowing the halal and haram, and distinguishing between ja'iz and fasid, and acting accordingly; only then can a servant tread the straight path, attain coolness of eyes, joy of heart, and expansiveness of chest; and these things depend on knowing the science of fiqh and acting upon it. The author of the book, Allamah Ibn Nujaym al-Misri, in this phrase expressed the importance of fiqh, its authenticity, and its benefit being like medicine, along with the fact that despite such a long passage of time, no weakness has occurred in fiqh, nor has any loss occurred in its honor and dignity despite such much expenditure and use; in every era many new sciences appear and after time passes either they end or some reduction occurs in their honor and dignity, but the science of fiqh occupies a separate station such that its importance is increasing day by day. Commentary
This passage is from the commentary on Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, serving as an introductory explanation of key terminological distinctions and a praise of the science of fiqh. The commentator begins by defining 'tamayyuz' (distinguishing) as separating something from others and making it apparent, citing Quranic usage. Then he defines 'ja'iz' and distinguishes it from 'mubah'. In Hanafi fiqh, 'ja'iz' is broader than 'mubah': every mubah act is ja'iz, but not every ja'iz act is mubah. The reason is that 'jawaz' is the opposite of prohibition (hurmah), while 'ibahah' is the opposite of dislike (karahah). Thus, a makruh act can still be ja'iz (permissible in the sense of not being haram), but it is not mubah (recommended or indifferent). This is a key precision: in Hanafi terminology, 'ja'iz' often means 'not prohibited', while 'mubah' means 'there is no legal objection'. Next, he defines 'fasid' as the opposite of 'salah,' meaning anything legally prohibited. He then explains 'ahkam' as the plural of 'hukm', which is Allah's address concerning the actions of the obligated ones. The commentator links these definitions to the necessity of fiqh: human society and the straight path depend on knowing halal/haram and distinguishing ja'iz from fasid. The second part of the passage is a beautiful description of fiqh using metaphors: its vastness like a surging ocean, its topics like flourishing gardens, its principles like shining stars, its roots firm (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas), its branches ever-growing (furū' like fard, wajib, etc.), its treasure never depleted, its honor not fading. This emphasizes the enduring value and comprehensiveness of Hanafi fiqh. The commentary concludes by noting that the author Ibn Nujaym highlights that other sciences may decline, but fiqh only increases in importance.

Key Terms
التمييز (al-tamyīz): Separating something from another and making it apparent; used for distinguishing halal from haram.
الجائز (al-jā'iz): What is not prohibited; broader than mubah because it includes acts that are makruh.
الفاسد (al-fāsid): Anything that is legally prohibited; opposite of salah (validity).
الحكم (al-ḥukm): Allah's address that relates to the actions of the mukallaf.

The Enduring Treasure of Fiqh: Metaphors of Ocean, Gardens, and Stars
Praising fiqh through metaphors of ocean, gardens, and stars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the metaphors used in the Ashbah commentary to describe fiqh's comprehensiveness, firm roots, and eternal relevance.

Core Idea
Fiqh is like a surging ocean, lush gardens, and shining stars—its principles are firm, its branches ever-growing, and its value never diminishes.

This lesson explores the beautiful metaphorical description of the science of fiqh as found in the commentary on Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir. The commentator paints a vivid picture: fiqh is a surging ocean, its topics like flourishing gardens, its stars shining, its roots firm (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas), and its branches ever-sprouting with rulings. This passage is not merely ornamental; it conveys deep truths about the nature of Hanafi fiqh—its comprehensiveness, authenticity, and timelessness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three primary metaphors
The passage describes fiqh as 'a surging ocean', 'lush gardens', and 'shining stars'. Each metaphor highlights a different quality: vastness, beauty/fertility, and guidance/brilliance.
Ocean suggests depth and abundance; gardens suggest order and liveliness; stars suggest clarity and guidance.
2
Analyze the description of principles and branches
The passage then speaks of 'roots firm' (usul) and 'branches sprouting' (furu'). The roots are the four sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas. The branches are the derived rulings: fard, wajib, sunnah, etc.
This imagery underscores that fiqh is built on solid foundations and continuously yields new rulings.
3
Understand the claim of inexhaustibility and timelessness
The text asserts that fiqh's treasure does not deplete with use, nor does its honor fade over time. This counters the notion that old knowledge becomes obsolete.
The commentator contrasts fiqh with other sciences that decline, emphasizing fiqh's perpetual relevance.
4
Appreciate the purpose and impact of the praise
This praise serves to motivate students and justify the study of fiqh. It is not mere rhetoric but a theological assertion of the divine origin and enduring value of the discipline.
Such front-matter passages are common in classical texts to elevate the subject's status.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary on Ibn Nujaym's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, serving as an introductory explanation of key terminological distinctions and a praise of the science of fiqh. The commentator begins by defining 'tamayyuz' (distinguishing) as separating something from others and making it apparent, citing Quranic usage. Then he defines 'ja'iz' and distinguishes it from 'mubah'. In Hanafi fiqh, 'ja'iz' is broader than 'mubah': every mubah act is ja'iz, but not every ja'iz act is mubah. The reason is that 'jawaz' is the opposite of prohibition (hurmah), while 'ibahah' is the opposite of dislike (karahah). Thus, a makruh act can still be ja'iz (permissible in the sense of not being haram), but it is not mubah (recommended or indifferent). This is a key precision: in Hanafi terminology, 'ja'iz' often means 'not prohibited', while 'mubah' means 'there is no legal objection'. Next, he defines 'fasid' as the opposite of 'salah,' meaning anything legally prohibited. He then explains 'ahkam' as the plural of 'hukm', which is Allah's address concerning the actions of the obligated ones. The commentator links these definitions to the necessity of fiqh: human society and the straight path depend on knowing halal/haram and distinguishing ja'iz from fasid. The second part of the passage is a beautiful description of fiqh using metaphors: its vastness like a surging ocean, its topics like flourishing gardens, its principles like shining stars, its roots firm (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas), its branches ever-growing (furū' like fard, wajib, etc.), its treasure never depleted, its honor not fading. This emphasizes the enduring value and comprehensiveness of Hanafi fiqh. The commentary concludes by noting that the author Ibn Nujaym highlights that other sciences may decline, but fiqh only increases in importance.

The Hanafi Distinction Between 'Ilm and Ma'rifah
Legal-philosophical analysis of 'ilm and ma'rifah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between 'ilm and ma'rifah according to the Hanafi perspective and explain why this distinction matters for legal interpretation.

Core Idea
Ma'rifah requires prior knowledge, not ignorance — a key distinction for accurate textual interpretation.

In Hanafi legal theory, precise understanding of terms is crucial. This passage examines the subtle difference between 'ilm (knowledge) and ma'rifah (recognition), arguing that ma'rifah actually presupposes prior knowledge. Grammatical evidence from Sibawayh and Akhfash, along with a famous hadith about recognizing Allah in the Hereafter, support this view. Understanding this distinction helps the mufti avoid misinterpretation of sacred texts and apply correct meanings in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Determine what the text is arguing about the relationship between 'ilm and ma'rifah. The author concludes that ma'rifah requires prior knowledge, contrary to those who claim it requires prior ignorance.
The claim is stated explicitly at the end: 'So they say that ma'rifah requires previous ignorance, contrary to knowledge; rather the matter is reversed.'
2
Analyze the grammatical distinction
Examine the grammatical examples cited from Sibawayh and Akhfash. Sibawayh stated that 'alimtu' and 'araftu' differ in transitivity: 'araftu' takes one object referring to the essence, while 'alimtu' takes two objects referring to states. Akhfash noted that in the Quran, 'la ta'lamunahum' is used in the sense of 'la ta'rifunahum' (you do not recognize them).
This grammatical analysis shows that ma'rifah is specific to essence, while 'ilm is broader.
3
Evaluate the hadith evidence
Consider the hadith 'تعرف الله في الرخاء يعرفك في الشدة' (Recognize Allah in prosperity, He will recognize you in adversity) and the longer hadith about recognizing Allah on the Day of Judgment. The text uses these to argue that recognition (ma'rifah) of Allah in the Hereafter requires prior knowledge from this world.
This hadith demonstrates that ma'rifah is not a new, ignorant encounter but a re-cognition based on prior 'ilm.
4
Synthesize the practical implication for iftaa
Understand how this distinction affects legal interpretation. If a text uses 'ma'rifah', it implies a pre-existing knowledge that qualifies the subject. This can affect rulings on faith, testimony, or contractual consent where recognition is a condition.
For example, a witness must recognize the defendant; this recognition must be based on prior acquaintance, not spontaneous 'ilm.

The Hadith 'Innama al-A'mal' Refers to Otherworldly Rulings
Defending the interpretation of the hadith as referring to otherworldly rewards/punishments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between hukm dunyawi and hukm ukhrwi to interpret the hadith 'Innama al-a'mal bil-niyat' and identify why obligations cannot be derived from speculative (zanni) evidence.

Core Idea
The hadith governs otherworldly reward, not worldly validity; it cannot establish obligation due to its speculative status.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi defense of the correct interpretation of the famous hadith 'Innama al-a'mal bil-niyat'. You will see how scholars distinguish between worldly legal effects (hukm dunyawi) and otherworldly rulings (hukm ukhrwi), and why the hadith is understood to refer to the latter. The passage also addresses objections and clarifies the epistemological weight of khabar wahid in deriving obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed term
Note that the word 'hukm' is homonymous (mushtarak lafzi). The objection claims it could be interpreted either as worldly or otherworldly. Recognise that this ambiguity is the starting point of the debate.
The term 'hukm' appears in the hadith as 'al-a'mal bil-niyat' without explicit 'hukm', but it is understood as part of the intended meaning.
2
Distinguish the two types of hukm
Understand that hukm dunyawi is the worldly effect (e.g., validity/invalidity), while hukm ukhrwi is the afterlife ruling (reward/punishment). Note that a valid action may lack reward and an invalid action may be forgiven.
This distinction is crucial in Hanafi thought and often overlooked by beginners.
3
Apply the principle of mushtarak lafzi
In Hanafi usul, a homonym cannot carry multiple simultaneous meanings. Once one meaning is chosen, the other is excluded. The interpreter must provide evidence (qarinah) for preferring one meaning.
The author argues that there is no qarinah to prefer hukm dunyawi; the context of the hadith (reward/punishment) indicates otherworldly.
4
Evaluate the evidence for preferring hukm ukhrwi
Consider the consequences if hukm dunyawi were taken: it would imply naskh of the Quran's absolute command to wash limbs in wudu, which is impermissible with khabar wahid. Also, the Prophet's teaching of wudu to the Bedouin omitted intention, which would be negligent if intention were a condition.
These arguments show that the otherworldly interpretation avoids conflict with stronger texts.
5
Assess the objection from Ibn Qutlubugha
Examine Ibn Qutlubugha's suggestion that the hadith applies only to acts of worship ('ibadat maqsuda). The Hanafi response: this would derive obligation (wujub) from a zanni source, which is not allowed. At most, it can indicate recommendation (sunniyyah).
This step reinforces the epistemological point: zanni hadith cannot establish obligations.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the Hanafi interpretation of the hadith 'Innama al-a'mal bil-niyat'. The author first refutes an objection that the hadith could be interpreted to refer to worldly legal rulings (hukm dunyawi) equally as otherworldly rulings (hukm ukhrwi). He explains that worldly rulings are effects of actions in this world, whereas otherworldly rulings are about reward and punishment in the afterlife, which are not always directly tied to the validity of actions. Then the author addresses a second objection: that since the word 'hukm' is a homonym, one could arbitrarily choose either meaning. The author responds by showing that the original approach of taking otherworldly meaning is correct because the alternative leads to problems: if one takes worldly meaning, then the hadith would abrogate the absolute command of wudu in the Quran (which is not permissible via a solitary hadith) and would imply that the Prophet omitted mentioning intention when teaching wudu, which would be negligent if intention were required. Next, the author considers an objection from Ibn Qutlubugha, who suggested that the hadith refers specifically to acts of worship ('ibadat maqsuda), where intention is obligatory by consensus. The Hanafi response is that this would entail deriving obligation (wujub) from a hadith that is only speculative in both authenticity and meaning, whereas obligations cannot be established from such evidence at most it can indicate recommendation. The author concludes by noting that the author of the book (likely al-Ashbah) alludes to the principle that reward is certain for good deeds but punishment (though deserved) may be forgiven, hence the word 'istihqaq' (deserving) is used with punishment.

Key Terms

حکم اخروی (hukm ukhrwi): The otherworldly ruling regarding reward (thawab) or punishment (iqab) in the afterlife.
مشترک لفظی (mushtarak lafzi): A word that has multiple meanings; here 'hukm' can mean both worldly and otherworldly rulings.
خبر واحد (khabar wahid): A solitary hadith (not mutawatir), which provides speculative knowledge (zann).

Definitions of Ibadah, Ta'ah, and Qurbah

In Hanafi fiqh, intention (niyyah) is required for the validity of 'ibadah but not for ta'ah or qurbah.

This passage clarifies three key terms that appear frequently in Hanafi legal discourse. The Tool as Substitute for Intent in Qisas
Explains the qawida that the tool is a substitute for intent in qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim that the tool used in homicide substitutes for internal intent to classify killings as 'amd, shibh 'amd, or khat' and determine the resulting ruling, including the mufta bihi on diyah.

Core Idea
The tool used for killing substitutes for hidden intent: if it customarily severs, it is 'amd and qisas applies.

This lesson unpacks a key legal maxim from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair: the tool is a substitute for intention in homicide cases. Since intention is internal and hard to prove, Hanafi jurists rely on the customary lethality of the weapon to infer intent. Understanding this maxim is essential for correctly applying qisas and distinguishing between categories of homicide. We will also examine the ensuing dispute on diyah payment forms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its categories
Recognize the three categories of homicide: 'amd (intentional with a severing tool), shibh 'amd (quasi-intentional with a non-severing but usually lethal tool), and khat' (accidental).
2
Understand the problem of proving intent
Since intent is internal (amr bātini), it cannot be directly observed. Hillajurists needed a reliable external indicator.
3
Recognize the tool as substitute (qawida)
The maxim 'al-āla maqām al-qasd' establishes that the tool used becomes a legal substitute for intent. The tool's customary effect determines the legal category.
This is a sughra (minor) qawida within the broader maxim 'al-umur bi-maqāṣidihā'.
4
Apply the tool criterion to differentiate categories
Apply the criterion: if the tool customarily severs body parts (e.g., sword, gun), it is 'amd and qisas is wajib. If it does not sever but usually kills (e.g., heavy stick), it is shibh 'amd and no qisas per Imam Abu Hanifa. If it is a permissible act causing accidental death, it is khat'.
5
Note the ruling and diyah dispute
For 'amd: qisas is wajib. For shibh 'amd: diyah and kaffarah are wajib. For khat': diyah on the 'āqila. Note the Hanafi dispute: diyah is 100 camels (or equivalents), with Sahibayn allowing other forms like cattle and cloth, while Shafi'i and Maliki set 12,000 dirhams.
The mufta bihi today follows Sahibayn for flexibility.

Riya that enters after sincere intention does not invalidate prayer but reduces its reward.

Commentary
This passage from Tatarkhaniyya and other sources deals with the effect of riya (showing off) on worship, specifically prayer and fasting. The key ruling: if one begins a prayer sincerely for Allah and then riya enters the heart during the prayer, the original intention prevails and the prayer is valid. However, if the riya affects the performance (e.g., one prays better in public than alone), then the reward is reduced—only the basic prayer reward remains, not the reward for excellence. Notably, riya does not invalidate fasting because fasting is hidden and cannot be shown off. The passage then records different opinions on praying intentionally with riya: some say no reward and sin, some say takfir, some say no reward and no sin (it is void). This shows a range within the Hanafi school. Finally, advice from Walwaljiyya: do not abandon worship out of fear of riya, as that fear is often hypothetical. The passage underscores the importance of sincere intention while acknowledging human weakness; accidental riya does not destroy worship, but intentional riya is harmful. The authority_weight is from later fatwa works (Tatarkhaniyya, Yanabi, Walwaljiyya), not Zahir al-Riwayah, indicating this is a later development of the school.

الصوم (al-sawm): Fasting, the act of abstaining from food, drink, and marital relations from dawn to sunset with intention.

Riya in Worship: Effects and the Three Stages of Action
Riya in worship and three stages of action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the effect of riya on prayer and fasting according to Hanafi scholars, and apply the principle of not abandoning worship due to fear of riya when issuing a fatwa.

Core Idea
Riya can nullify the reward of excellence (ihsan) but not necessarily the basic obligation; fear of riya should not lead to abandoning worship.

This passage from al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair addresses a common concern: how does showing off (riya) affect the validity and reward of worship? It distinguishes between acts where riya can infiltrate (prayer) and acts where it cannot (fasting), presents three scholarly opinions on riya in prayer, and offers a practical principle for the mufti: worshippers should not abandon their practice due to mere fear of riya, because every action passes through stages of effort, show, and sincerity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the type of riya
First, determine whether the act is one where riya can affect the intention (prayer, fasting, recitation). For prayer, riya can reduce the reward; for fasting, riya only occurs in informing, not in the act itself.
Riya requires possibility of disclosure; fasting is inherently hidden.
2
Determine the effect on reward
If riya is present in prayer, the basic obligation may be fulfilled, but the reward of ihsan (excellence) is lost. The hadith on ihsan indicates that worshipping with full awareness of Allah's presence brings extra reward.
Ihsan is a quality, not a pillar.
3
Consult the three opinions on riya in prayer
The text records three views: (1) no reward and a sin (Ibrahim ibn Yusuf), (2) becomes kafir (some jurists), (3) no reward and no sin, as if not prayed (others). The mufti must know these to advise accordingly.
The third view implies the act is invalid but not sinful.
4
Apply the principle of three stages
When a person fears riya, do not tell them to stop the worship. Instead, explain that actions go through stages: mashq (effort), riya (show), ibadah (worship). Persevere and renew intention.
This is a psychological and spiritual principle, not a legal one.
5
Issue the fatwa
The mufti should recommend continuing the worship while renewing sincerity, and caution against abandoning a sunnah or fard due to waswasa (whispers). The mufta bihi is likely the view that maintains the validity of the act while encouraging sincerity.
Tarjih is not explicit; but the third opinion (no reward, no sin) is often considered lenient.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the effect of riya (showing off) in worship. It begins by explaining that performing prayer in front of people with extra care while being careless in private results in loss of the reward of ihsan (excellence), though the basic obligation is fulfilled. The hadith on ihsan is cited. Then it discusses riya in fasting, stating that riya cannot truly occur in fasting because riya requires the possibility of people knowing, but fasting can be hidden. However, one can still show off by informing others of one's fast. The text presents three scholarly opinions on praying with riya: (1) Ibrahim ibn Yusuf: sin, no reward; (2) some jurists: becomes kafir; (3) some jurists: no reward and no sin, as if not prayed. Finally, it advises not to abandon worship due to fear of riya, because actions go through three stages: effort, show, and worship. One should overcome satanic thoughts by renewing sincerity.
