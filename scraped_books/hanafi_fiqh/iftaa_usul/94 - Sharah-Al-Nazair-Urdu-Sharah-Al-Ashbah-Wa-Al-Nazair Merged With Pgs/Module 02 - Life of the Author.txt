Imam Abu Hanifa's Formative Years
Biographical sketch of Imam Abu Hanifa's life and education

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key events in Imam Abu Hanifa's early life and explain their influence on his development as a mujtahid.

Core Idea
Imam Abu Hanifa's Persian heritage, early mastery of Quran, and 18-year apprenticeship under Imam Hammad forged his independent legal reasoning.

This passage details the early life of Imam Abu Hanifa, from his lineage to his formation as a scholar. Understanding these biographical facts is crucial for appreciating the foundations of the Hanafi school and the development of its usul. Pay attention to how his background shaped his approach to fiqh and his role as a teacher.

Guided Path
1
Examine his lineage
Identify the tribes and regions associated with his family. Note the wala relationship to Banu Taym Allah and the Persian origin of his father.

2
Trace his education timeline
Note the ages and durations: Quran memorization by age 8, grammar/literature by age 10, theology (kalam) for 5 years, then joining Imam Hammad's circle at 18.
This sequence shows a structured pursuit of knowledge: Quran first, then language, then theology, then fiqh.
3
Identify his key teacher and study circle
Imam Hammad's circle was the largest in Kufa; note the 18-year duration and the geographic diversity of students from Makkah to Central Asia.
This explains the wide dissemination of Hanafi fiqh and the method of systematic teaching.
4
Reflect on the prophetic hadith

This underscores the belief that his scholarly status was divinely blessed.

The four great imams of Islamic jurisprudence: Nu'man ibn Thabit, Imam Abu Hanifa, whose lineage is: Nu'man ibn Thabit ibn Zuta ibn Mah. According to some, his grandfather Zuta was from Kabul or Babylon, was a slave of Banu Taym Allah, later freed, so by clientage he is called Taymi. His father Thabit ibn Zuta was Persian, so he is called of Persian descent. Historians wrote that his father took him to Ali and requested a prayer; its effect was that in a short period of two years he memorized the entire Quran at age eight. Then in two years he completed grammar and literature, then spent five years in theology. In 103 AH, he joined the study circle of the famous hadith scholar and jurist Imam Hammad in Kufa, and remained in that circle for about eighteen years. In 91 AH, he made his first Hajj and continued Hajj thereafter, benefiting from scholars of the Two Holy Sanctuaries. After completing studies, he began teaching in 120 AH, because in 130 AH his teacher Imam Hammad passed away, and the people of Kufa unanimously chose him as successor. According to historians, his study circle included students and scholars from faraway lands like Mecca, Medina, Damascus, Basra, Wasit, Mosul, Ramla, Egypt, Yemen, Yamama, Nishapur, Bukhara, Samarkand, and Mada'in. The spiritual and outer purification attained by Imam Azam was greatly influenced by the glad tidings and prophecy of the Prophet. Abu Huraira reported: We were in the presence of the Prophet when Surah Jumu'ah was revealed. When he recited the verse 'and others of them who have not yet joined them,' someone asked who these others are. The Prophet remained silent, but when the question was repeated, he placed his hand on Salman al-Farisi's shoulder and said: 'If faith were in the Pleiades, people from among these would attain it.' This hadith refers to Imam Azam. Ibn Hajar al-Makki narrated from Jalal al-Din al-Suyuti that the hadith refers to Imam Azam. His honor as a Tabi'i: Historians say Imam Azam met with Companions, and their number is disputed. Hafiz Ibn Hajar says he met a group, including Anas ibn Malik. Allama Ibn Hajar wrote he met eight Companions. He always avoided positions of power. Because he refused the post of qadi, Caliph Abu Ja'far Mansur imprisoned him and ordered the jailer to torture him. They severely restricted food and drink and exceeded all limits in imprisonment, until some say he was poisoned. His age was near seventy when he was certain of death, so he prayed at home, went into prostration, and in that state surrendered his soul in 150 AH in Sha'ban or according to others Rajab or Shawwal. Imam Malik ibn Anas: His lineage: Malik ibn Anas ibn Malik ibn Abi 'Amir ibn 'Amr ibn al-Harith ibn Ghyaman ibn 'Aliya 'Amr ibn al-Harith. His mother's name is 'Aliya bint Shurayk al-Azdiyya.

Imam Abu Yusuf combined relentless scholarship with judicial independence, even under powerful rulers.

Core Idea
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's six books are the canonical foundation of all Hanafi fiqh.

Every fatwa in the Hanafi school ultimately traces back to a set of six books known as Zahir al-Riwaya. These were compiled by a single man: Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. But who was he, and why do his works carry such authority? This lesson introduces you to the life, teachers, and legacy of the scholar whose corpus is the backbone of Hanafi legal practice.

Commentary
This passage is a eulogy for the fuqaha (jurists) in general and specifically for Imam Abu Hanifa and his followers. It opens by declaring that the fuqaha are the pillars (qawam) and maintainers (qawwamun) of the religion—through them the religion is set straight and well-ordered. They are the refuge in both worlds and the ultimate reference for teaching (tadris) and issuing fatwas (fatwa). The author emphasizes the special distinction of the Hanafi school: 'our companions' (ashabuna) have precedence (sabq) in this field, and people are their followers (atba'). More strikingly, he states that all people in fiqh are like dependents ('iyal) upon Abu Hanifa—meaning they rely on his foundational work. Imam al-Shafi'i's famous testimonial is quoted: whoever wants to delve deeply into fiqh should study Abu Hanifa's books. The author draws an analogy between Abu Hanifa's status and that of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq—he gets his own reward and also the reward of everyone who compiled fiqh and derived rulings from his principles until Judgment Day. The remaining portions of the text consist of lexical explanations of key terms: 'qawam' (pillar), 'qawwam' (manager), 'al-marji' (reference), 'tadris' (teaching), 'fatwa' (legal opinion), 'sabq' (precedence), 'khususiyyah' (specificity), 'ashabuna' (Hanafi scholars), 'atba' (followers), 'iyal' (dependents), 'insaf' (justice), 'tabahhur' (deep knowledge), 'Harmalah' (a narrator), and 'dawwana' (to compile). This linguistic analysis serves to clarify the precise meanings of the terms used in the eulogy, and highlights the rigorous methodology of the Hanafi tradition in defining technical terms.

Key Terms
