Three Categories of Hanafi Rulings
Classification of Hanafi rulings into three tiers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify Hanafi rulings into Zahir al-Riwayah, Nawadir, and Nawazil, and identify their relative authority in fatwa.

Core Idea
Hanafi rulings are tiered: Zahir al-Riwayah is primary, Nawadir secondary, Nawazil for new cases.

In this lesson, we examine the threefold classification of masail in the Hanafi school. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for a mufti to know which ruling to prioritize when issuing fatwas. The passage from Ashbah wa al-Nazair categorizes rulings into Usul (core), Nawadir (rare), and Nawazil (new incidents).

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the passage and list the three categories of rulings: Masail al-Usul, Masail al-Nawadir, and Masail al-Nawazil.
2
Distinguish the sources for each category
For each category, note which books or scholars it comes from. For Usul, it's Imam Muhammad's six books. For Nawadir, it's other books and companions. For Nawazil, it's later jurists' ijtihad.
3
Understand the authority hierarchy
Recognize that fatwas are generally based on Zahir al-Riwayah first. Nawadir are secondary, and Nawazil apply when no earlier ruling exists.
This is a key principle for muftis.

The rulings of our three imams consist of three categories. Commentary
This passage outlines the three-tier classification of Hanafi legal rulings. First, Masail al-Usul (core rulings) are from Imam Muhammad's six canonical works (Zahir al-Riwayah), which form the authoritative foundation of the madhhab. Second, Masail al-Nawadir are rare transmissions from Imam Muhammad or other early Hanafi authorities, less authoritative but still part of the school. Third, Masail al-Nawazil are new cases after the age of ijtihad, resolved by later jurists using qiyas and other methods. This hierarchy is crucial for muftis to know the weight of each ruling when issuing fatwas.

Key Terms
ظاہر الروایہ (Zahir al-Riwayah): The well-attested narrations from Imam Muhammad in his six books; the most authoritative layer of Hanafi rulings.
مسائل النواد (Masail al-Nawadir): Rare rulings transmitted from Imam Muhammad or other early Hanafi scholars outside the six books.
مسائل النوازل (Masail al-Nawazil): New incidents and cases that arose after the era of the early mujtahids, resolved by later scholars using evidence.

Classifying Sunnah: Muakkadah, Ghayr Muakkadah, 'Ayn, Kifayah
Defining sunnah muakkadah, ghayr muakkadah, 'ayn, and kifayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of Sunnah and apply the Hanafi mufta bihi definition when classifying a recommended act.

Core Idea
Sunnah is not monolithic; its subtypes determine reward, blame, and obligation level for each act.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi classification of Sunnah. You will learn the key distinction between emphasized (muakkadah) and non-emphasized (ghayr muakkadah) sunnahs, based on the Prophet's consistency. Additionally, the categories of 'ayn (individual) and kifayah (communal) sunnah are introduced. Understanding these gradations is essential for a mufti to assess the legal weight of recommended acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary division
Recognize that Sunnah is first divided into muakkadah (emphasized) and ghayr muakkadah (non-emphasized). This is the foundational split.
2

Muakkadah: the Prophet always performed it without excuse. Ghayr muakkadah: he sometimes performed and sometimes left it. This criterion determines the level of recommendation.
3
Note the definitional variation among Hanafi authorities
Some scholars (Umdat al-Ri'ayah, Hashiyat Sharh al-Wiqayah) define only muakkadah as 'doer rewarded, abandoner blamed', while others (Durr al-Mukhtar, Mulla 'Ali Qari) apply that definition to absolute Sunnah. This affects the blame for abandoning ghayr muakkadah.
The mufta bihi is the stricter definition: even ghayr muakkadah carries blame? Actually, the text says ghayr muakkadah abandoner is not sinful, so the broader definition must be contextualized. We'll resolve in fatwa hierarchy.
4
Recognize the secondary division
Sunnah 'ayn (individual) must be performed by every person; sunnah kifayah (communal) is fulfilled if some perform it. Examples: thrice washing in wudu ('ayn) and i'tikaf (kifayah).
5

Core Idea
Mustahab: rewarded when done, no sin when left, lower than sunnah ghayr muakkadah.

In Hanafi legal theory, supererogatory acts are categorized by their emphasis. Mustahab is a category for acts the Prophet occasionally performed, with clear implications for reward and sin. This lesson clarifies its definition and where it stands in the spectrum of recommended deeds.

A marriage contracted jokingly is valid if the speaker knew it would effect marriage; witnesses must understand the words.

Valid Preceding Intention: Instant Response Test and Walking as Non-Interruptive
Explaining the condition for valid preceding intention in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi test for a valid preceding intention (niyyah mutaqaddimah) in prayer by distinguishing acts that indicate turning away (i'rad) from those that do not, and state the ruling for a mufti when a person walks to the mosque after making intention.

Core Idea
A preceding intention is valid if no act of turning away (i'rad) intervenes; walking to prayer does not break it.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's Sharh al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir addresses a practical issue for muftis: how to determine if a preceding intention is still valid when the person walks to the prayer place. It introduces the instantaneous response test and clarifies that walking is not an act of i'rad, resolving an apparent contradiction in earlier texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Understand the discussion: validity of preceding intention (niyyah mutaqaddimah) in prayer. The passage begins with an analogy from fasting, where eating sahur itself constitutes intention.
Preceding intention is allowed in Hanafi school.
2
Apply the instant-response test
From Muhammad ibn Salamah: if at the start of prayer the person can instantly answer which prayer they are performing without thought, the intention is complete. If they need to think, it is not valid.
This test ensures the intention is present and decisive at the moment of commencement.
3
Understand the condition of no incompatible act
The jurists conditioned that no act outside the genus of prayer should occur between intention and start. However, walking to the mosque is such an act, yet they still deem the intention valid.
This creates an apparent contradiction that needs resolution.
4
Resolve the contradiction: i'rad
The passage from Fath al-Qadir resolves this by specifying that 'not from the genus' refers to acts that indicate turning away (i'rad) from prayer, like speaking or eating. Walking does not indicate i'rad; it is considered preparatory and thus part of prayer's acts.
The key is the intent behind the act, not its outward form.
5
Conclude the Hanafi ruling for fatwa
The mufta bihi is that a preceding intention is valid as long as the person does not engage in any act that shows abandonment of prayer intention. Walking, internal thoughts, or waiting do not break it.
This ruling is attributed to Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani and is relied upon in fatwa.

Rulings on Non-Muslims: The Principle of Non-Address with Subsidiary Rulings (Furu')
Compilation of independent Hanafi rulings on non-Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that non-Muslims are not addressed with subsidiary rulings to cases of kitabiyya menses, teaching non-Muslims, and validity of oaths/expiation.

Core Idea
Non-Muslims are not obligated for furu' rulings like ghusl and expiation, making intercourse halal upon cessation of menses without ghusl.

This lesson examines several rulings from classical Hanafi texts that illustrate the general principle that non-Muslims are not addressed with the subsidiary rulings of Shariah (furu'). We will analyze how this principle governs the kitabiyya wife's menses, the permissibility of teaching non-Muslims fiqh, the invalidity of their expiation and oaths, and the exceptional case where intention is considered.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle
Recognize that the common thread in all these rulings is that non-Muslims are not addressed with furu' (subsidiary) obligations. This explains the kitabiyya case, the invalidity of oaths, etc.
This principle is a well-known Hanafi qawida: 'al-kafir la yukhattab bi furu''.
2
Analyze the Kitabiyya Case
Apply the principle: a kitabiyya is not required to perform ghusl after menses because it is a furu' obligation. Thus, intercourse becomes halal immediately upon cessation of blood, without waiting for ghusl.
Contrast with a Muslim woman, for whom ghusl is obligatory before intercourse. The difference stems from address of obligation.
3
Explore the Teaching Ruling
Examine Abu Hanifa's ruling: teaching non-Muslims fiqh and Qur'an is permissible for da'wah, but they should not touch the mushaf unless they bathe. If they bathe, it is permissible, showing that their purification is valid even though they are not obligated.
This ruling balances da'wah with respect for the mushaf, and affirms the validity of purification from a non-Muslim.
4
Examine Expiation and Oaths
A non-Muslim's expiation and oath are invalid because they are not obligated for furu' and their covenants are interpreted as formal agreements only. The Quranic verse is interpreted accordingly.
The verse 'inkān nakathū aymānahum' is explained as referring to covenants, not oaths in the legal sense.
5

The passage ends with a note that a non-Muslim's intention is considered only in one specific case (not detailed here). This shows the principle is not absolute; there are exceptions to the general rule.

Niyyah is a Condition (Shart) Not a Pillar (Rukn) in Worship
Definition and classification of niyyah as shart, with internal ikhtilaf on takbirat al-ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that niyyah is a condition (shart) not a pillar (rukn) in worship, identify the internal ikhtilaf on takbirat al-ihram, and apply the distinction to practical cases.

Core Idea
Niyyah is a prerequisite for validity, not an integral part of the act — and that distinction matters.

This passage establishes a fundamental principle of Hanafi fiqh: intention (niyyah) is a condition (shart) for the validity of all acts of worship, not a pillar (rukn). While all Hanafi scholars agree on this, they differ on the status of the opening takbir (takbirat al-ihram) — the relied-upon view treats it as a condition too. Understanding the difference between shart and rukn is crucial for correct application in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on niyyah in the Hanafi school
State that niyyah is a condition (shart) by consensus (ittifaq al-as'hab) for all acts of worship.
This is a qaw'ida (legal maxim) derived from the texts.
2
Distinguish shart from rukn
A shart is an external prerequisite that must be present before or during the act but is not part of the act's essence. A rukn is an integral part whose omission invalidates the act entirely.
The distinction has practical consequences for compensation and validity.
3
Note the internal ikhtilaf on takbirat al-ihram
While niyyah is unanimously a shart, there is a known ikhtilaf regarding the opening takbir. The relied-upon (mu'tamad) view is that it is also a shart. A minority view considers it a rukn.
This ikhtilaf affects whether the takbir can serve for a different prayer if intention changes.
4
Determine the mufta bihi for fatwa
The relied-upon position is that takbirat al-ihram is a shart like niyyah. Thus, if one intends fard with the takbir, it can also suffice for nafl if intention changes during the prayer.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa (mufta bihi).

Conditions are external prerequisites that must be present before the act, while pillars are integral parts of the act itself. There is an internal Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding takbirat al-ihram (the opening takbir): the relied-upon (mu'tamad) view is that it is also a condition, like niyyah; but a minority view considers it a pillar. The practical consequence is that if one says takbir intending fard, the same takbir can serve for nafl if the intention changes, because it is a condition not a rukn.

Key Terms
شرط (shart): A prerequisite that must be present before or during the act but is not part of the act's essence.
ركن (rukn): An integral part of the act; without it the act is incomplete.
تكبيرة الإحرام (takbirat al-ihram): The opening 'Allahu Akbar' that begins the prayer.

Forgetting Qira'ah in One Rak'ah: Repeating Prayers
Provides rule for when qira'ah omitted in unknown prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for when a person forgets qira'ah in one rak'ah but cannot identify the prayer, and apply the principle of caution (ihtiyat) to determine which prayers must be repeated.

Core Idea
When qira'ah is forgotten in an unknown prayer, repeat the shortest possible prayers to cover the loss.

This lesson addresses a scenario where a person has completed all daily prayers (the five obligatory prayers plus Witr) and later recalls that he omitted the Qur'anic recitation (qira'ah) in one rak'ah, but cannot remember which prayer it was. In the Hanafi school, qira'ah is a wajib (obligatory) act in every rak'ah; its forgetful omission without performing sajdat al-sahw renders the prayer invalid. The ruling prescribes repeating the prayers with the fewest rak'ahs as a measure of caution (ihtiyat). You will learn how this principle is applied when the omission is one, two, or four rak'ahs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the missing act
The person has completed all prayers of the day and night but later remembers that he omitted qira'ah in one rak'ah. He does not know which prayer (Fajr, Zuhr, Asr, Maghrib, Isha, or Witr) contained the omission. Qira'ah is a wajib in every rak'ah.
Witr is included because it is wajib in the Hanafi school.
2
Apply the rule for one or two rak'ahs omitted
The text states: if he omitted qira'ah in one rak'ah (or two rak'ahs), he must repeat Fajr (2 rak'ahs) and Witr (3 rak'ahs). This is because these are the shortest prayers, and the omission likely lies in one of them due to the small number of rak'ahs.
Fajr and Witr together cover a total of 5 rak'ahs, which is the maximum possible loss from a single rak'ah omission.
3
Apply the rule for four-rak'ah prayers
If he recalls that the omission occurred in a four-rak'ah prayer (Zuhr, Asr, or Isha) but does not know which one, then all three four-rak'ah prayers must be repeated. Each prayer has the same number of rak'ahs, so caution requires repeating all of them.
The text uses the word 'shirk' (likely a scribal error for 'shakk' meaning doubt) to indicate doubt about the four-rak'ah prayers.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The ruling is based on the Hanafi principle of caution (ihtiyat) when the exact location of an omission is unknown. The mufti chooses the option that ensures the obligation is fulfilled with minimal burden, but without allowing any prayer to remain in doubt.
This is an example of applying the maxim 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk' (certainty is not removed by doubt) in reverse: doubt is resolved by repeating the minimally required prayers.
