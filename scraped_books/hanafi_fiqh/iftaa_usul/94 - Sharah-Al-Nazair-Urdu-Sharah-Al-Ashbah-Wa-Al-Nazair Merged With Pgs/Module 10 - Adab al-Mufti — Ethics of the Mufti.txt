Ruling on Mentioning Salah and Salam: Hanafi Position and Burden of Proof
Hanafi ruling on salah and salam: no karaahah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi position on the ruling of mentioning salah and salam, distinguishing between the Prophet Muhammad and other prophets, and identifying the burden of proof for claims of karaahah.

Core Idea
In Hanafi, mentioning only salah or only salam is not makruh for the Prophet; the claim of karaahah lacks evidence.

A common concern among muftis is whether it is proper to write only 'sallallahu alayhi wa sallam' or only 'alayhi al-salam' when saluting the Prophet. The author of al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair mentioned both to avoid a disagreement, but the Hanafi school clarifies that restricting to one is not makruh. This lesson examines the ruling, its scope, and the evidentiary burden for those who claim otherwise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed claim
The author of the matn mentioned both salah and salam to avoid the disagreement of those who claim that mentioning only one is makruh. Identify this claim from the passage.
The claim is external to the Hanafi school.
2
Extract the Hanafi position
The commentator states that according to the Hanafi school, it is not makruh to mention only salah or only salam for the Prophet. This is explicitly stated in 'Madaniyat al-Mufti'.
Notice the authority: 'Madaniyat al-Mufti' is a later Hanafi fatwa collection.
3
Distinguish the scope of the alleged karaahah
The claim of karaahah is specific to the Prophet Muhammad; for other prophets, there is no karaahah at all. The commentator restricts the disagreement.
This shows precise legal reasoning (takhsis).
4
Evaluate the burden of proof
Whoever claims karaahah for the Prophet bears the burden of evidence. The commentator implies no such evidence exists.
This is a key principle: claims without evidence are not legally weighty.
5
Apply in fatwa practice
When writing a fatwa or scholarly work, a Hanafi mufti may use either salah, salam, or both, without any legal blame. The choice may be based on custom or preference.
This is part of rasm al-mufti – the proper form in official writing.

Writing Tashahhud in Khutbahs: The Hanafi Rule
Addressing whether tashahhud must be written in the khutbah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between oral and written obligations to resolve apparent conflicts between hadith and practice.

Core Idea
The hadith on tashahhud in khutbahs requires spoken recitation, not written inscription — a rule for muftis in writing treatises.

When a mufti writes a khutbah or treatise, he may be challenged by the hadith that every khutbah must have tashahhud. This lesson examines the Hanafi response: the obligation is oral, not written. We will analyse the arguments and understand the methodology of handling such objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The author of the text did not write tashahhud in his khutbah, prompting a question from those who cite the hadith.
The objection assumes the hadith applies to written khutbahs.
2
Cite the conflicting hadith
The hadith states: 'Every khutbah that does not contain tashahhud is like a cut hand.' This seems to require tashahhud in every khutbah.
Imam Tirmidhi graded it hasan; Nawawi and Bayhaqi authenticated it.
3
State the Hanafi response
Tashahhud in writing is not necessary; verbal recitation suffices. The hadith does not specify written form.
Allamah Hamawi supports this: the hadith does not mandate writing.
4
Evaluate alternative explanations
Some restrict the hadith to marriage khutbahs; others interpret 'tashahhud' metaphorically as praise. Both are rejected: the first because the hadith is general, the second because moving from literal to metaphorical requires evidence.
Generality remains unless specified; literal meaning is preferred.
5
Derive the rule for muftis
When writing a khutbah or fatwa, it is sufficient to verbally recite or intend the tashahhud; writing is not mandatory.
This ruling is a fatwa hierarchy: later Hanafi works rely on this.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the three responses to the objection that a written khutbah must include tashahhud and apply the mufta bihi Hanafi position to a mufti's adab scenario.

Core Idea
A written khutbah need not include tashahhud text; oral recitation during delivery fulfills the hadith requirement.

You may encounter an objection: a mufti's khutbah (introduction) does not contain the written tashahhud, yet the Prophet ﷺ said every khutbah without tashahhud is like a severed hand. How does a Hanafi mufti defend himself? This lesson parses three scholarly answers, identifies the strongest, and clarifies the operative ruling for fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection and primary proof
Read the question: the author's khutbah lacks written tashahhud, while the hadith 'kullu khutbah…' demands it. Note the hadith's authenticity (hasan/sahih).
The hadith is from Tirmidhi, authenticated by Nawawi and Bayhaqi.
2
Summarize the three answers
List each answer: (1) Hamawi: writing not required, oral suffices. (2) Unnamed scholars: hadith restricted to marriage khutbahs. (3) Turpisti: 'tashahhud' means praise (hamd), not testimony.
3
Evaluate each answer's strength
The commentator rejects (2) because the hadith is general (amm) and cannot be specified without evidence. He rejects (3) because shifting from literal to metaphorical meaning lacks proof. Answer (1) is accepted as it preserves the hadith's generality while accommodating practical need.
Note the usul principle: al-amm yabqa ala umumihi hatta yarida dalil al-takhsis.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and its justification
The strongest answer is the first: tashahhud may be oral. The hadith does not specify 'written'. This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
This answer aligns with Hanafi practice and avoids arbitrary restriction or metaphorical interpretation.
5
Apply to mufti adab
In practice, a mufti writing a fatwa or book introduction need not write tashahhud, but he must recite it orally when delivering the khutbah or lecture.
Adab al-mufti: ensure the oral tashahhud is present; the written text is a convenience.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on terms and concepts related to the faqih and mufti, and it extols the Hanafi school's precedence in teaching and iftaa. The author begins by defining key lexical terms: al-marji' (source), al-tadris (teaching), al-fatwa (legal response), al-mufti, and al-faqih. He explains that 'fatwa' is derived from either al-fatuh (generosity) or al-fati (strength), emphasizing the mufti's need for scholarly power and generosity. The term 'ashabuna' refers specifically to Imam Abu Hanifa and his students, and 'fi hadha al-shan' means in the field of teaching and fatwa. The grammatical analysis of 'wa al-nas lahum attiba'' is provided, showing two possible readings. Then, the passage discusses the derivation of 'al-nas' and 'al-attiba' (followers). Ibn Nujaym is quoted to establish the fuqaha as the pillars of fiqh. The special precedence of Hanafis is justified because Imam Abu Hanifa was the first to codify fiqh and usul al-fiqh. The objection that Imam Abu Hanifa wrote only one book is refuted by citing multiple works attributed to him and by noting that the claim of a single book is a Mu'tazili fabrication. Imam Shafi'i's praise of Abu Hanifa is then narrated, including an anecdote from Imam Malik affirming Abu Hanifa's persuasive power. The passage compares Abu Hanifa to Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (the truthful), implying his decisive authority in scholarly matters.

المفتي (al-mufti): One who gives fatwa; he prepares and resolves issues using his strength and knowledge.
اصحابنا (ashabuna): The scholars of the Hanafi madhhab, especially Imam Abu Hanifa and his students.

The Precedence of the Hanafi School in Teaching and Iftaa
Historical praise and argumentation for Hanafi precedence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school claims precedence in teaching and iftaa, citing the codification of fiqh and testimonies from Imam Shafi'i and Imam Malik.

Core Idea
Imam Abu Hanifa, as the first codifier of fiqh, is the fountainhead for all later schools.

This passage establishes the unique status of the Hanafi school in the field of iftaa. It quotes Ibn Nujaym to affirm the fuqaha's role, then argues that the Hanafis hold precedence because Imam Abu Hanifa was the first to systematically codify fiqh and usul al-fiqh. This is supported by Imam Shafi'i's advice to study Abu Hanifa's books and an anecdote from Imam Malik praising Abu Hanifa's argumentation. The passage also refutes the objection that Abu Hanifa authored only one book.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The passage claims that the Hanafis have precedence (khususiyyat al-sabaq) in teaching and fatwa.
This is the central thesis.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is that Imam Abu Hanifa was the first to codify fiqh and usul al-fiqh, making all other schools dependent on his work.
Codification means systematic compilation.
3
Analyze the objection and refutation
The objection that Abu Hanifa wrote only one book (Fiqh al-Akbar) is refuted by listing several books and attributing the claim to the Mu'tazila.
This defends the authority of Hanafi sources.
4
Consider the testimonies
Imam Shafi'i's statement that one should study Abu Hanifa's books and Imam Malik's anecdote are presented as external endorsements.
These testimonies strengthen the Hanafi position.

Jama' wa Furq: The Art of Distinguishing Shared and Distinct Rulings
Defines the third discipline: recognizing shared and differing rulings across cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of jama' wa furq to identify shared and distinct rulings between two cases in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Similar rulings in one area do not guarantee similarity in another; discern both to avoid fatwa errors.

A mufti must not only know individual rulings but also see patterns across cases. The third of the seven arts, jama' wa furq (combination and differentiation), trains him to compare and contrast cases that share some rulings but differ in others. This lesson unpacks the definition and applies it to a classic example.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Identify that jama' wa furq means recognizing when two cases share a ruling in one aspect (jama') but differ in another (furq).
2
Examine the given example
Consider the mumayyiz child and the adult: both invalidate prayer by speaking (shared ruling). But their divorce rulings differ: adult's divorce valid, child's not.
The difference arises from legal capacity (mukallaf vs. not fully capable).
3
Identify the shared element (jama')
Determine which ruling is common – here, speaking in prayer breaks it for both because both can deliberately speak.
This shows a similarity in causation.
4
Identify the distinctive element (furq)
Find the differing ruling – divorce. The adult's divorce takes effect; the child's does not because he lacks full legal capacity (mukallaf).
The furq is due to a difference in the subject's legal personality.
5
Apply to a new pair
Practice: compare a Muslim and a dhimmi (mentioned). They share basic obligations but differ in certain rulings (e.g., dhimmi not obliged to pay zakat on certain items). Identify jama' and furq.

The Discipline of Alghaz (Legal Riddles)
Defines the fourth art: alghaz (legal riddles) and gives prophetic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the purpose of alghaz in mufti training and apply the method of deducing hidden legal causes from prophetic examples.

Core Idea
Alghaz train the mufti to uncover hidden causes behind rulings.

This passage introduces the fourth discipline of mufti training: alghaz or legal riddles. These are not mere puzzles but serious pedagogical tools where the legal cause (ʿilla) is deliberately concealed to challenge the student's analytical ability. The Prophet ﷺ himself employed this method with his Companions, as seen in the examples of 'maghribun' and the identity of Jibril. Mastering alghaz sharpens a mufti's skill in deducing the underlying rationale of rulings.

Identify the definition

The term 'alghaz' is the plural of 'lughz' (riddle).
2
Examine the prophetic examples
Analyze the two examples given: the Prophet's question about 'maghribun' and his inquiry about the questioner (Jibril).
Both examples show concealment of a key element to prompt deduction.
3
Identify the concealed element
In the first example, the concealed cause is that 'maghribun' refers to people with whom jinn associate. In the second, the concealed identity is Jibril.
The hidden cause is the 'illa for the ruling or the context.
4
State the pedagogical purpose

Not Seeing Is Not Proof of Absence
Clarifying that the author's lack of finding a ruling does not mean it does not exist

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle 'adam al-ru'yah la yadullu 'ala adam al-tasrih' to evaluate the completeness of legal research in fatwa preparation.

Core Idea
Not finding a ruling in your sources does not prove it was never stated.

In the Hanafi tradition, a mufti must exercise caution when stating that no one has addressed a point. The commentator on al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair warns us that the author's phrase 'لم أَرَ مَنْ نَبَّهَ' does not mean the point is absent from the school—it may simply reflect the limits of his research. This lesson teaches you to distinguish between absence of evidence and evidence of absence, a vital skill for responsible ifta'.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Phrase
Identify the phrase 'لم أَرَ مَنْ نَبَّهَ' (I did not see anyone who pointed it out) and what it literally means. Understand that it is a statement about the author's personal research, not an objective claim about the school.
This phrase appears frequently in later Hanafi works as a tool for expressing uncertainty.
2
Differentiate Absence from Non-Existence
Understand the logical principle: not seeing X does not prove X does not exist. Apply this to fiqh research: a ruling may exist in sources the author did not consult or that are not widely known.
This is a methodological caution against premature conclusions.
3
Apply to a Fatwa Scenario
Imagine you are asked about a ruling on nafl fasts. You check several books and do not find a clear statement. Using this principle, you would not say 'no one says this'; instead, you would continue searching or note the uncertainty.
A mufti must be humble about the scope of his research.
4
Consider the Impact on Fatwa
Recognize that a mufti's credibility depends on thorough research. Relying on 'I did not see it' can lead to incorrect fatwas if the ruling actually exists. Always indicate the limitation of your search.
This is part of adab al-mufti—the ethics of giving fatwa.

Then he said: When a man does not know whether he has something from the missed prayers or not, the best is that in the four sunan of zuhr, asr, and isha' he recite al-Fatiha and a sura. Explanation: If someone has some prayers and he doubts whether he has performed them or not, and the missed prayers remain on his responsibility, then he should act on preponderant conjecture. If there is no preponderant conjecture, then at least in prayers more than six, the order (tartib) is dropped. He should perform qada for six because in the chapter of qada, more than six are many; up to six, a person remains obligated to order. And when a person remembers that he missed a prayer but does not remember whether he made it up, then in that case he should pray the four-rakah sunna of zuhr, asr, and isha' with the intention of sunna, reciting al-Fatiha and a sura in each rakah. It is possible that if the obligatory prayer was not missed or was made up, then by praying the sunna, the sunna will be performed. And in the Tatarkhaniyya: If someone doubts about missed prayers, whether they remain on him or not, the author of Tatarkhaniyya says that merely due to doubt, intending qada prayer is disliked, otherwise it would necessitate giving room for repeated doubt.

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi ruling on how a person should act when uncertain about missed (fā'itah) prayers. The text first states a general principle: if a person's prayer appears outwardly correct, we do not invalidate it unless error becomes manifest. Then it addresses the specific case of a person who has missed prayers (fawā'it) and doubts whether he has performed their qada. The ruling from al-Tātārkhāniyyah is that it is makrūh (disliked) to intend qada when merely in doubt. Instead, the preferred course is to recite al-Fātiḥah and a sūrah in the four rakʿahs of sunnah prayers for ẓuhr, ʿaṣr, and ʿishā'. The commentary clarifies that if the number of missed prayers is six or less, order (tartīb) is mandatory; beyond six, order is dropped. The key lesson for a mufti is that mere doubt does not justify intending qada; one should engage in supererogatory acts until the matter is clarified.
