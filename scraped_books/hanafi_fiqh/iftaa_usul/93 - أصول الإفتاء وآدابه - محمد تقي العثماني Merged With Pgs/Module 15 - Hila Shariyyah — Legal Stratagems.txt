Hila Shariyyah — Legal Stratagems
intermediate
6 min
Guiding to a Lawful Stratagem (Hila) in Fatwa
Permissibility of guiding questioner to a lawful way out

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a mufti may guide a questioner to a lawful stratagem and apply the Hanafi position on permissible hila to concrete examples.

Core Idea
A mufti may guide to a permissible way out, as long as it harms no one and follows prophetic precedent.

This passage from Al-Saymari and Al-Sarakhsi addresses the delicate issue of legal stratagems (hiyal). While some stratagems are blameworthy, there are lawful ways out (makharij mashru'ah) that the mufti can show to relieve hardship. The key is that the hila avoids sin without causing unjust harm. In this lesson, we will examine the conditions from the text and see how the Prophet himself taught a stratagem to avoid riba.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mufti's duty in hardship
The mufti should recognize when a questioner is in a difficult situation and is seeking a way out that avoids sin. The mufti's role is to show a permissible path.
This duty is based on the principle of relieving hardship.
2
Analyze the examples given
Examine the two examples: the man who swore not to spend on his wife (guided to use mahr or loan then waive) and the man who swore to have intercourse in Ramadan daytime (guided to travel). Note how each stratagem changes the situation without breaking the oath.
Travel changes the ruling because it is a valid excuse in Ramadan.
3
Examine the proof from Ibn Umar and the Prophet
Ibn Umar taught a stratagem to avoid riba in currency exchange by using gold as an intermediary. The Prophet taught the agent of Khaybar to sell dates for goods first. These show that such guidance is commendable.
The proof is dalil naqli (narrative evidence).
4
Distinguish permissible hila from blameworthy trickery
A permissible hila avoids a prohibited act or fulfills an obligation without harming others. Blameworthy trickery involves deception or subverting the Shari'ah. The passage clarifies that the mufti's guidance is not blameworthy.
Intentions matter: the goal is to obey Allah, not to cheat.
5
Formulate the condition: no harm to others, avoidance of haram
The overriding condition is that the stratagem does not harm others unjustly and leads to avoiding the haram. This ensures the hila is legitimate and permissible.
This is explicitly stated in the text: 'as long as it does not harm others unjustly'.

16. Al-Saymari said: 'It is appropriate for the mufti, when he sees a way for the questioner, to guide him to it, or to alert him to it—meaning as long as it does not harm others unjustly—such as someone who swore not to spend on his wife for a month; he says: cover her expenses from her dowry or loan or sale, then she waives it. And as it is related that a man said to Abu Hanifa: 'I swore that I would have intercourse with my wife in Ramadan (i.e., during its day) and I [want to] neither expiate nor sin.' He said: 'Travel with her.' The conclusion is that if the questioner is in hardship, the mufti shows him a permissible way out to relieve the hardship. Al-Sarakhsi adduced a proof from what Abu Jabala narrated: I asked Abdullah ibn Umar (may Allah be pleased with them): We go to Syria, and we have silver coins of current value, and they have the light coins of [؟] value; should we buy their ten with our nine and a half? He said: 'Do not do that. Rather, sell your coins for gold, and buy their coins with gold, and do not separate until you take possession, even if he jumps up with you.' Al-Sarakhsi said: 'In this is evidence of Ibn Umar's retraction from his view about the permissibility of excess, as is the madhhab of Ibn Abbas, and that there is no value for quality in currencies, and that if a mufti understands the answer to what he was asked, there is no harm in him showing the questioner a way to achieve his goal while avoiding the forbidden. This is not among the blameworthy teaching of stratagems; rather, it is following the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) when he said to the agent of Khaybar: Why didn't you sell your dates for goods, then buy these dates with your goods?' (1) Taken from Al-Fatwa fi al-Islam by Jamal al-Din al-Qasimi, p. 94 (2) Al-Mabsut by al-Sarakhsi, beginning of Kitab al-Sarf, 14:4

Commentary
This passage is a hila case (legal stratagem) and a rule of mufti conduct. It explains that a mufti may show a permissible way out when the questioner is in hardship, as long as it does not harm others. Examples include a man who swore not to spend on his wife—the mufti suggests covering expenses from mahr or a loan, then waiving it. Another example is a man who swore to have sex in Ramadan daytime—Abu Hanifa advised traveling, which changes the ruling. The story of Ibn Umar shows the permissibility of avoiding riba through a currency exchange with gold as an intermediary. Al-Sarakhsi concludes that this is not blameworthy hila but following prophetic practice, as the Prophet guided the date seller to sell for goods first. The key condition is that the hila should be a legitimate way to avoid sin.

Key Terms
مخرج مشروع (makhraj mashru'): A lawful way out from a hardship or a forbidden situation.
حيلة (hila): A legal stratagem to achieve a permissible end while avoiding a prohibited means.
التفاضل (tafadul): Excess in exchange of the same type of item (riba al-fadl).
