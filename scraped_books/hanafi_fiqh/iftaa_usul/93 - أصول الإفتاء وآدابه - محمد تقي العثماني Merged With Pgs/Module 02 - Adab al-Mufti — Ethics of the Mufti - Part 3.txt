Citing Primary Sources for Fundamentals in Fatwa
Instruction to cite primary sources for fundamentals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that when a fatwa concerns usul al-din or qat'iyyat al-shari'ah, the mufti must cite evidence from the Quran and Sunnah directly, not merely from fiqh works.

Core Idea
For fundamental beliefs and definitive rulings, the mufti must go back to the primary sources, not just secondary fiqh books.

This lesson clarifies an important distinction in fatwa-giving. While many everyday issues can be answered from fiqh manuals, questions about the fundamentals of religion (usul al-din) or definitive Shariah matters (qat'iyyat) require direct citation of the Quran and Sunnah. This ensures the questioner understands the scriptural basis and reinforces the authority of the answer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger
The text says: 'If the istifta concerns the fundamentals of religion or the definitive matters of Shariah' — this is the condition that changes the requirement.
Usul al-din includes creedal beliefs; qat'iyyat includes rulings established by certain textual evidence.
2
Identify the obligation
The mufti must present evidence from the Book and Sunnah, not just from fiqh books.
This means he should directly quote the Quranic verse or hadith that establishes the ruling.
3
Understand why
Because these matters are foundational; relying on secondary sources may obscure the strength of the evidence and may not persuade the questioner.
It also reinforces the questioner's connection to the primary sources.
4
Recognize the limit
This rule applies only to usul al-din and qat'iyyat; for other issues, citing fiqh works may be acceptable.
The text does not say that fiqh works are forbidden for other issues.

The Warning Against Shadhdh Opinions in Fatwa
Warning against shadhdh opinions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a shadhdh opinion and apply the rule that the mufti must avoid it regardless of the source.

Core Idea
A shadhdh opinion must be rejected even if from a great jurist – evidence is king.

In this lesson, we examine a critical principle of mufti conduct: the prohibition against relying on shadhdh (abnormal) opinions in issuing fatwas. The passage cites a famous statement from the eminent scholar Abd al-Rahman ibn Mahdi and expands on its implications, warning against the dangers of adopting rulings that contradict the stronger evidence, especially when they come from unqualified sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the authority and his statement
Read the quote from Abd al-Rahman ibn Mahdi. He gives three criteria for being an imam: not taking shadhdh, not narrating from everyone, and not narrating everything heard.
This is a foundational adab for the mufti – he must be selective.
2
Understand the scope of shadhdh
The author explains that even if a shadhdh opinion comes from a reliable major jurist, it is not acceptable. Then he contrasts that with opinions from ignorant or extremist individuals.
The principle is absolute: evidence trumps personality.
3
Recognize the correct approach
The mufti must adopt what is stronger in evidence and argument, considering the sources and noble objectives of the Shariah.
This is the operative rule for fatwa.
4
Apply to a practical scenario
Consider a case: a respected scholar gives an opinion that contradicts clear nass. The mufti should not follow it. Then consider an opinion from a layperson with no credentials - even more so reject it.
The standard is the same for all.

Core Idea
No pressure — political, popular, or personal — can justify altering a fatwa; only Allah is to be feared.

This lesson explains a fundamental rasm al-mufti rule: the mufti must resist any pressure when delivering a fatwa. The reasoning is rooted in the Quranic description of those who convey Allah's messages. We will examine the rule and the two verses cited by the author.

No exception is made for any source.

The Fatwa Duty: Evidence for Fundamentals vs. Branch Rulings
A rasm al-mufti rule distinguishing evidence sources for fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between usul al-din (fundamentals) and furu' fiqhiyyah (branch rulings) and identify the proper evidence source for each in a fatwa.

Core Idea
For fundamental beliefs, the mufti must cite Quran and Sunnah directly; for furu', fiqh books suffice.

When a question about tawhid or the prohibition of zina arrives, can the mufti simply quote a fiqh manual? This passage from Usul al-Ifta wa Adabuha explains a key rule: the evidence requirement depends on the subject matter. Understanding this distinction protects the integrity of fatwa in creedal matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage and list the two types of issues: usul al-din/qat'iyyat and furu' fiqhiyyah. Note the examples given for each.
Usul al-din includes tawhid, prophethood, afterlife; qat'iyyat includes khamr, lying, zina.
2
State the evidence rule for each
For the first category, evidence must come from Quran and Sunnah directly. For the second, it is sufficient to mention books of fiqh.
The passage says 'لا من كتب الفقه فقط' for fundamentals.
3
Understand the rationale
The reason is that fundamentals require ijtihad (independent reasoning) and do not permit taqlid (blind following). Branch rulings allow taqlid.
This is a principle from usul: taqlid is allowed in furu' but not in usul.
4
Apply to given examples
Test yourself: For a question about the punishment for zina, what evidence should you cite? For a question about the validity of a sale contract, what evidence is enough?
5
Recognize the mufti's duty
The mufti must not be lazy; he must go back to primary sources for fundamental questions, even if it requires more effort.
This is part of adab al-mufti (etiquette of the mufti).

Procedure for Certifying Another Mufti's Fatwa
Procedural rules for tasdiq (fatwa certification)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi procedure for certifying (tasdiq) another mufti's fatwa, and identify when it is permissible to write 'the answer is correct'.

Core Idea
Certification requires checking the mufti's qualification, the answer's correctness, and the evidence's correctness.

When a mufti is asked to certify another person's fatwa, he must not simply endorse it without due diligence. This passage, from Muhammad Taqi Uthmani's work on mufti etiquette, lays out a step-by-step procedure. Understanding this procedure is crucial for maintaining the integrity of fatwa issuance and preventing the spread of unsound legal opinions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the certification scenario
A mufti receives a fatwa written by another person and is asked to certify it by signing or writing 'the answer is correct'. The mufti must follow a specific procedure before giving his endorsement.
This is a common practice in fatwa councils and publications.
2
Assess the first mufti's eligibility
The mufti must first determine whether the original mufti is qualified to issue fatwas. Qualifications include knowledge of the Quran, Sunnah, consensus, analogy, Arabic, and other prerequisites.
Eligibility is a condition for even considering the fatwa.
3
Examine the correctness of the answer
If the original mufti is eligible, the certifying mufti then examines whether the answer (hukm) itself is correct according to his own scholarship and the Hanafi school.
If incorrect, the certifying mufti writes his own answer.
4
Scrutinize the evidence (dalil)
Even if the answer is correct, the certifying mufti must check the evidence cited. If the evidence is weak, incomplete, or requires correction, he must write his own corrected fatwa.
A correct answer based on flawed evidence cannot be certified.
5
Decide on certification
Only when the original mufti is qualified, the answer is correct, and the evidence is sound, may the certifying mufti write 'the answer is correct' and sign.
This ensures the certifier's authority is not misused.

The Adab of Endorsing and Countersigning Fatwas
Adab al-mufti when signing tasdiq and tawqi' on fatwas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of al-Qarafi to determine whether a mufti should countersign a fatwa based on the qualifications of the original signatory.

Core Idea
Never endorse an unqualified fatwa, even if correct; endorse a superior's fatwa with humility.

In the previous lesson, we covered the general conduct of the mufti when issuing fatwas. Here, al-Saymari and al-Qarafi address a specific scenario: what should a mufti do when presented with a fatwa that already bears the signature of another scholar? The answer depends entirely on the competence and piety of that original mufti. Al-Qarafi draws a sharp line: if the original signatory is unqualified, the mufti must not countersign, regardless of the correctness of the content. If the signatory is more knowledgeable, the mufti should still avoid appearing arrogant. This lesson unpacks these two situations and their underlying principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Extract the two distinct cases from al-Qarafi's statement: when the fatwa comes from someone unqualified (lā yuṣliḥu lil-fatwā), and when it comes from someone more learned (a‘lam wa a‘ẓam martabah).
The unqualified are those lacking either knowledge or piety, or both.
2
Derive the ruling for each scenario
For the unqualified: the mufti must not write with him (i.e., not countersign). For the more learned: the mufti should not say 'the answer is correct' but rather 'such is my answer' (kadhālik jawābī).
The ruling is not about the correctness of the answer but about protecting the integrity of the iftā' institution.
3
Understand the rationale
Countersigning an unqualified person gives credibility to an incompetent fatwa practice, which is a 'great calamity' – even if the answer happens to be right. For the learned, saying 'the answer is correct' implies arrogance; the mufti is merely agreeing with a superior, so humility demands he phrase it as an echo.
The rationale is rooted in preventing harm (darar) and preserving humility (tawāḍu‘).
4
Apply the principle to modern scenarios
Consider a fatwa from an online source: if the author is known to be unqualified, a mufti should not endorse it even if the ruling seems correct. If the author is a senior scholar, the mufti may say 'I concur' rather than 'Correct' to avoid self-praise.
This adab protects the chain of authority and avoids misleading the questioner.

Referring the Questioner to Another Mufti
Rule on referring questioner to another qualified mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition that triggers the referral rule and apply it to scenarios where a mufti lacks clarity.

Core Idea
A mufti uncertain of an answer must refer the questioner to a qualified colleague, not issue a guess.

This lesson addresses the adab (etiquette) of a mufti when faced with a question he cannot answer. The rule is simple yet profound: the mufti's primary duty is to provide correct guidance, not to give an answer at all costs. When in doubt, referral to another qualified mufti is the proper course.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger condition
The rule applies when the mufti does not have clarity (lam yatabayyan) on the answer, or when the questioner explicitly requests a referral. Recognize that 'not clear' includes both lack of knowledge and uncertainty.
The mufti must be honest about his ability.
2
State the prescribed action
The mufti should direct the questioner to another mufti whom he believes is qualified (ahliyyat al-ifta). This is described as 'al-munasib' (appropriate), indicating a strong recommendation.
The referral is not a mere suggestion but a duty to guide.
3
Identify the underlying principle
The basis is avoiding fatwa without knowledge (al-ifta bi ghayr ilm), which is prohibited. The mufti's responsibility is to convey God's ruling, not personal opinion.
This principle overrides the desire to appear knowledgeable.
4
Apply to a sample scenario
Consider a mufti asked about a rare transaction. He is uncertain. He should say, 'I do not know, but I recommend you consult Mufti X who is knowledgeable in this area.'
The referral must be to a specific person, not just vague advice.
5
Distinguish from related rules
Contrast with the rule that a mufti may give a fatwa based on predominant probability (ghalabat al-zann) when he is confident. This rule applies only when certainty is lacking.
The threshold is lack of clarity, not just difficulty.

Showing a Legitimate Exit (Makhraj) for the Questioner
Guiding mufti to show lawful exit from hardship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of makhraj to construct a legitimate exit for a questioner facing hardship while avoiding blameworthy hiyal.

Core Idea
A mufti must show a lawful way out, not a trick that harms others or violates Shariah.

When a questioner faces a religious difficulty due to an oath or situation, the mufti has a duty to guide them to a permissible solution (makhraj). This lesson examines the examples from the sources—the husband who swore not to spend, the man who swore to have intercourse in Ramadan, and the currency exchange case—to understand how legitimate exits are constructed. We also distinguish this from blameworthy legal tricks (hiyal).

Guided Path
1

2
Determine the underlying obligation
What is the Shariah obligation that the questioner is trying to avoid or fulfill?
3
Check for harm to others
Does the proposed exit harm someone else without right? If yes, it is not a legitimate makhraj.
The text says 'as long as it does not harm others unjustly.'
4
Find a permissible alternative path
Using the examples in the text, see how the mufti can redirect the questioner to achieve his objective without violating the oath or law.
E.g., covering wife from dower; traveling to make fasting optional.
5
Verify the proof
Check the dalil (Quran, Sunnah) or precedent that supports this path.
The Prophet's instruction to the Khaybar factor is a key precedent.

List of practical guidelines for writing fatwas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six etiquettes of writing a fatwa and explain the rationale behind each.

Core Idea
The written fatwa is a legal document: legibility, anti-forgery, and authenticity are paramount.

The mufti's written fatwa is a formal legal opinion that can be cited and preserved. This lesson presents six practical guidelines from classical Hanafi adab al-mufti literature to ensure the fatwa is clear, trustworthy, and defensible against forgery.

Guided Path

These are practical steps, not theoretical; each has a rationale.

Apply to a real scenario

A mufti’s personal conduct—dress, actions, intentions, and consistency—directly impacts the credibility of his fatwa.

A mufti is not merely a conveyor of rulings; he is a living embodiment of the Shariah. This passage from Ibn Abidin’s 'Rasm al-Mufti' outlines four foundational etiquettes that the mufti must cultivate within himself. These cover his external appearance, his public conduct, his inner sincerity, and the harmony between his teachings and his practice. Practice what you preach—otherwise the fatwa loses credibility.

الثالث: وينبغى للمفتى أيضاً أن يُصلح سَرِيرته ويستحضر النيات الصالحة من الخلافة عن رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم في البيان، والوفاء بعهد الله تعالى، وإصلاح أحوال الأمةِ المُختارة حسب أحكام الله سبحانه وتعالى، وليكنْ همه طلب رضوان الله سبحانه وتعالى في كل شيء، دون اكتساب مدح الناس وجَلْبِ الشهرة والسمعة، كما ينبغي له أن يُدافع النيات الخبيثة من العُلو في الأرض، والتمتُّع بتعظيم النّاس و تحصيل ثنائهم ومدحهم أو الحصول علي المنافع المالية والمكاسب المحرمة. و ينبغي له أيضاً أن يُعالج قلبه ممّا قد يعرض لمن يتولى مثل هذا المنصب من الغُرور والكبرياء والتعالى على عباد الله ومشابهة الفضلاء ذوى الأقدار، ومن الإعجاب بما يقوله وما يُجيب به، و خاصة إن أجاب فأحسن الجواب حيث قصر غيره عن معرفة الصواب. ونقل ابنُ حَمْدَان عن الإمام سُحْنُون رحمه الله تعالى أنه قال: "فتنة الجواب بالصواب أشد من فتنة المال"

الرابع: وينبغى أن يكون المفتى عاملاً بما يُفتى به من الخير حتى أنه ذكر بعض الأصوليين أن الفتيا لا تصح من مُخالف مقتضى العلم، قال الشاطبي رحمه الله تعالى: "فأمّا فُتياه بالقول؛ فإذا جَرَتْ أقواله علي غير المشروع، وهذا من جملة أقواله، فيُمكنُ جَرَيانُها علي غير المشروع، فلا يُوثق بها... فإنّ المفتي إذا أمر مثلاً بالصمت عمّا لا يَعْنى؛ فإن كان صامتاً عما لا يعنى ففتواه صادقة. وإن كان من الخائضين فيما لا يَعْنى، فهي غير صادقة. واذا دلك على الزهد في الدنيا، وهو زاهد فيها، صدقت فتياه، وإن كان راغباً في الدنيا، فهي كاذبة. وإن ذلك على المحافظة على الصلاة، وكان محافظاً عليها، صدقت فتياه، وإلا فلا. وعلى هذا الترتيب سائر أحكام الشريعة في الأوامر، ومثلها النّواهى؛ فإذا نهى عن النظر إلى الأجنبيات من النِّساء، وكان في نفسه منتهياً عنها، صدقت فتياه، أو نهى عن الكذب، وهو صادق اللسان، أو عن الزنا وهو لا يزني، أو عن التفحش، وهو لا يتفحش، أو عن مخالطة الأشرار،
Etiquettes of the Mufti in Himself
First: The appointed mufti should improve his appearance and dress, adhering in that to the religious guidelines: observing purity and cleanliness, covering the awrah, avoiding silk, gold, and garments that contain any symbols of disbelievers or resembling them. Al-Qarafi (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'The mufti should be handsome in dress according to the Shariah, for people are naturally inclined to respect external forms, and if he is not respected in people's eyes, they will not accept guidance from him nor follow his word.'

Second: He should also improve his conduct, make his actions conform to the [sunnah?], and weigh his words by the scale of the Shariah. For by his position as an expounder of Allah, he becomes a role model for people in what he says and does. His action achieves a great degree of exposition. It is not enough that his actions be those of the moderate; rather, he should be among the foremost, because eyes are turned toward him and souls depend on following his guidance.

Third: The mufti should also rectify his inner self, summon righteous intentions of deputizing for the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) in exposition, fulfilling the covenant of Allah, and reforming the affairs of the chosen community according to the rulings of Allah (Exalted is He). His concern should be seeking the pleasure of Allah in everything, not acquiring people's praise or gaining fame and reputation. Likewise, he should repel evil intentions such as arrogance in the land, enjoyment of people's veneration, seeking their praise and commendation, or obtaining financial benefits and unlawful gains. He should also treat his heart from what may afflict those who undertake such a position: pride, arrogance, self-importance over Allah's servants, comparing himself to virtuous people of high rank, and self-admiration for what he says and answers, especially if he answers correctly while others fall short. Ibn Hamdan transmitted from Imam Sahnun (may Allah have mercy on him): 'The trial of answering correctly is more severe than the trial of wealth.'

Fourth: The mufti should act upon what he gives fatwa about of goodness, to the extent that some usul scholars mentioned that fatwa is not valid from one who contradicts the demands of knowledge. Al-Shatibi (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'As for his fatwa by speech, if his words run contrary to the Shariah, and this is part of his statements, it is possible that they are not according to the Shariah, so they are not reliable... For example, if the mufti commands silence about what does not concern him, if he himself is silent about what does not concern him, his fatwa is truthful. But if he indulges in what does not concern him, it is untruthful. If he advises zuhd in the world while he is zahid, his fatwa is truthful; if he is desirous of the world, it is false. Likewise regarding maintaining prayer—if he is punctual, his fatwa is truthful; otherwise not. And upon this arrangement are all other rulings of the Shariah concerning commands, and similarly prohibitions. So if he forbids looking at non-mahram women and he himself refrains, his fatwa is truthful; or if he forbids lying while he is truthful, or forbids adultery while he does not commit it, or forbids obscenity while he is not obscene, or forbids associating with evil people...'

Commentary
This passage from Ibn Abidin's 'Rasm al-Mufti' (or a similar work) outlines four essential etiquettes (adab) for the mufti concerning his personal conduct. It is a classic treatment of the mufti's integrity. The first etiquette covers external appearance: the mufti must dress in a manner that commands respect and complies with Shariah—avoiding silk, gold, and imitation of non-Muslims. Al-Qarafi emphasizes that people judge by appearances, so a dignified appearance aids acceptance of his fatwa. The second deals with public conduct: the mufti must align his actions and speech with the Shariah, being a role model. He should be exemplary, not just average. The third focuses on inner sincerity: the mufti must purify his intentions, avoiding pride, arrogance, self-admiration, and seeking fame or wealth. The anecdote from Sahnun warns that even answering correctly can be a trial if it leads to pride. The fourth stresses consistency: the mufti must practice what he preaches. Al-Shatibi argues that a mufti's own actions affect the credibility of his fatwa. If he commands good but does not practice it, his fatwa is hollow. The passage uses examples like silence, zuhd, prayer, and prohibitions to illustrate that his personal conduct must match his teachings. This is a foundational lesson for any mufti: the office demands both outward and inward conformity to the Shariah.

Key Terms
الزي الشرعي (al-zayy al-shar'i): The dress prescribed by the Shariah, emphasizing cleanliness, covering the awrah, and avoiding silk, gold, and imitation of non-Muslims.
السريرة (al-sariira): The inner state or secret (heart), particularly intentions and hidden motives.
الغرور (al-ghurur): Self-deception and pride, especially from one's knowledge and position.

The Fourth Condition: Acting on One's Fatwas
Defining the fourth condition and its rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fourth condition for a mufti (acting on his fatwas) and explain how al-Shatibi argues that a fatwa's truthfulness depends on the mufti's practice.

Core Idea
A mufti's fatwa is truthful only when his actions match his words.

This lesson explores the fourth requirement for a mufti: he must practice what he preaches. Drawing on al-Shatibi's insights, we examine why a mufti's credibility—and the benefit of his fatwa—hinges on the consistency between his speech and action.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
State the fourth condition from the passage: the mufti should act upon the good he enjoins.
2
Understand al-Shatibi's argument
Al-Shatibi argues that the truthfulness of speech (sidq al-qawl) requires conformity of action to speech. When a mufti's actions contradict his words, his fatwa is not reliable because it is not truthful.
He uses Quranic verses to support this.
3
Review the examples
Go through the examples given: commanding silence while talkative, promoting asceticism while worldly, etc. Each shows that the fatwa is truthful only when the mufti acts accordingly.
4
Identify the Quranic proof
The passage cites verses (Al-Ahzab 23 and At-Tawbah 75-77) to prove that truthfulness requires matching action to speech.
The first verse praises those who fulfill their covenant; the second condemns those who break it and lie.
5
Formulate the principle
Conclude that the sign of a truthful fatwa is that the mufti's actions align with his words. This is the ideal, even if not always achieved.

We say: It is obligatory for the qualified scholar to take up the position and give fatwa absolutely, whether his word matches his action or not, but his fatwa will not be beneficial, nor will it be sustained even if benefit occurs.

The Objection and Hanafi Resolution
Responding to the objection about perfect practice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the objection that perfect practice is impossible and state the Hanafi response distinguishing between the legal obligation to issue fatwa and its practical benefit.

Core Idea
A mufti's fatwa remains legally valid even if he does not practice it, but its benefit depends on his conformity.

This lesson addresses a common objection to requiring a mufti to practice his fatwas: no one is perfect. The Hanafi response, drawn from the passage, clarifies that while the mufti is obligated to practice, his fatwa is still legally valid. The benefit, however, is lost when his actions contradict.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objection: requiring perfect conformity would make fatwa impossible, as no one is sinless. Scholars have noted that it is not a condition for one who enjoins good to be perfect himself.
2
Identify the Hanafi response
The response distinguishes between the legal validity of the fatwa (its legal status) and its benefit (intifa') in practice. The fatwa remains valid even if the mufti does not practice, but people will not benefit from it.
The mufti is still obligated to appoint himself and give fatwa.
3
Apply the distinction
Understand that the fatwa itself (as a legal ruling) is sound as long as the mufti is qualified. However, the mufti's personal conduct affects the authority and acceptance of his fatwa.
4
Review the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis accept fatwas from knowledgeable but sinful muftis, but discourage taking fatwa from notorious sinners. Other schools may reject it outright.
5
Conclude with the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position: The mufti must strive to practice, but his fatwa is not invalidated by personal weakness.

Core Idea
A mufti's fatwa remains legally valid even if his actions contradict his words, but people only benefit if he is truthful.

Learning Objective

The goal is to balance personal piety with facilitation for the community.

Commentary
This passage from 'Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuhu' by Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani discusses the personal conduct and spiritual discipline required of a mufti. It opens with a quote from Imam al-Shatibi permitting a mujtahid to voluntarily undertake more than the obligatory level of worship, while also advising him to conceal such extra efforts to avoid being followed by those unable to bear them. The anecdote about Shaykh Ashraf Ali al-Thanwi illustrates this: he gave lenient fatwas to the public but himself avoided fruits due to a widespread practice of selling before appearance. The sixth point stresses the mufti's continuous pursuit of knowledge, quoting Abu Hanifah on focusing one's concerns and cutting worldly ties, and al-Shafi'i on sacrificing comfort for learning. The seventh point emphasizes the mufti's precedence in voluntary worship, with Abu Qilabah linking knowledge to worship and Ibn Khaldun distinguishing between merely transmitting the Shariah and embodying it. The biographical note about Imam al-Qushayri serves as an example of such embodied knowledge and piety.

The Mufti's Need for Worship and Piety
Explaining that a mufti must combine knowledge with worship and piety.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a mufti must combine knowledge with worship and piety, and interpret the hadith 'One faqih is more severe against Satan than a thousand worshippers' according to the Hanafi view.

Core Idea
A mufti without piety is merely a transmitter; true inheritance requires embodying the Shari'ah through worship.

This passage from 'Usul al-Ifta' emphasizes that a mufti's role is not merely technical—he must be a person of worship and piety. The author clarifies that the famous hadith praising the faqih over the 'abid refers to a faqih who also has a strong share of worship, not a mere theoretician. As a Hanafi mufti, you are called to be a true inheritor of the prophets by combining deep knowledge with sincere devotion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim
Read the passage and locate the central argument about the mufti's character. What qualities must a mufti possess beyond knowledge?
Look for the distinction between mere transmission and actual embodiment.
2
Analyze the supporting quotes
Examine the quotes from Imam al-Shafi'i, Abu Qilabah, and Ibn Khaldun. How does each support the need for worship and piety alongside knowledge?
Focus on the phrases 'being characterized' and 'actualizing' from Ibn Khaldun.
3
Examine the hadith clarification
The author explains the hadith 'One faqih is more severe against Satan than a thousand worshippers.' According to the passage, what does 'faqih' mean here? Who is the intended faqih?
The author explicitly says the faqih is one who has a great share of worship and turning to Allah.
4
Apply the distinction
Why does the author say that a worshipper ('abid) is more deserving of inheritance than a jurist who is not a worshipper? What does this reveal about the hierarchy?
The 'abid inherited by quality (sifah) while the non-pious jurist inherited nothing.
5
Conclude with practical implication
How should this lesson shape a mufti's daily life and approach to fatwa? Consider the modern context.
A mufti must prioritize personal worship and piety alongside academic study.

Commentary
This passage, from the chapter 'Ahkam al-Ifta wa Manhajuhu' of Usul al-Ifta, discusses the essential quality of a mufti: he must be a person of worship and piety, not merely a repository of legal rulings. The author begins by citing Imam al-Shafi'i's emphasis on sacrificing personal comfort for knowledge, and the example of al-Shafi'i's own diligence. Then he quotes Abu Qilabah and Ibn Khaldun to stress that true inheritance of the prophetic legacy comes from embodying the Shari'ah, not just transmitting it. The passage then clarifies the famous hadith 'One faqih is more severe against Satan than a thousand worshippers,' explaining that the faqih praised here is one who combines deep knowledge with sincere worship, while the 'worshipper' in the comparison is one devoted primarily to supererogatory acts but lacking in knowledge transmission. The author notes that many contemporary jurists fall short in this regard, except a few righteous ones. He concludes by observing that the great jurists of the past were also devoted to worship, citing al-Qadi [likely al-Qadi Abu Yusuf or similar]. The footnote on Imam al-Qushayri provides a biographical illustration of a scholar who combined both aspects. For the Hanafi mufti, this is a crucial reminder that technical expertise must be accompanied by taqwa and 'ibadah to truly benefit the ummah and merit the title 'warith' (heir of the prophets).

Core Idea
True inheritance of prophethood demands both knowledge and worship; the faqīh who only talks theory inherits nothing.

This passage from Usūl al-Iftā' wa Ādābuhu by Muḥammad Taqī al-‘Uthmānī clarifies a crucial point for anyone entering the path of iftā': a scholar-mufti must be both learned and devout. Guided Path
1
Identify the two attributes
The passage sets forth two fundamental attributes: knowledge (al-‘ilm / al-fiqh) and worship (al-‘ibādah). Recognize that both are necessary for the true inheritance of prophethood.
‘Ābid and faqīh are terms of art here.
2
Understand the claim about inheritance
The author states that only the one who combines both attributes is the true scholar and inheritor. If only one attribute is present, the worshipper has a stronger claim to inheritance than the jurist who lacks worship.

The ḥadīth 'A single faqīh is more severe against Shaiṭān than a thousand worshippers' is presented. Note its wording and the fact that its authenticity is not fully established („alā taqdīr ṣiḥḥatihī).

The common misunderstanding is that any faqīh, even one who merely transmits statements about practice (ṣāḥib aqwāl), is superior to a devoted worshipper. The author rejects this view.

The faqīh praised in the ḥadīth is one who has a great share of worship and turning to Allāh but whose main occupation is fiqh. The worshipper who is inferior is one whose main exertion is worship and who is not a transmitter of statements.
Thus the ḥadīth actually supports the author's combined ideal.

Adab of the Mustafti: Respectful Questioning and Conduct
Etiquette of the mustafti in speech and demeanor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the specific prohibitions in a mustafti's speech and demeanor toward a mufti.

Core Idea
A mustafti must ask with humility, not test or challenge the mufti.

A fatwa is a sacred exchange. The questioner (mustafti) carries responsibilities of adab to preserve the dignity of the process. Ibn al-Salah outlines four specific prohibitions that protect the mufti from disrespect and the mustafti from arrogance. Let's learn them.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the four prohibitions
List the four things Ibn al-Salah forbids the mustafti from doing. This is a memorization anchor. They are: 1) gesturing with hand in the mufti's face, 2) asking 'What do you know?' or 'What is your imam's opinion?', 3) saying 'That is my opinion' or 'This happened to me' after the answer, 4) mentioning another mufti's fatwa to the current mufti.
Focus on the principle: the mustafti should not imply superiority or test the mufti.
2
Understand the rationale behind each prohibition
For each forbidden action, ask: why would it undermine respect? Gesturing with hand is rude. Asking 'What do you know?' challenges the mufti's competence. Claiming the answer matches one's own opinion or mentioning another fatwa suggests the mustafti is evaluating or comparring.
The goal is humility and acceptance of the fatwa as authoritative.
3
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
Imagine a mustafti who says to the mufti: 'But Shaykh X told me something different.' Which prohibition does this violate? It violates the fourth prohibition: mentioning another fatwa. This shows disrespect by implying the current mufti should conform or be tested.
Even if the mustafti is confused, the correct adab is to ask simply without invoking others.
4
Relate to Hanafi fatwa practice
In the Hanafi school, the mufti is a mujtahid or a faqih. The mustafti's adab protects the authority of the fatwa. Though Ibn al-Salah is Shafi'i, Hanafi manuals (e.g., Uṣūl al-Iftāʾ of Muḥammad Taqī al-‘Uthmānī) cite similar rules.
Adab is universal across madhhabs; the Hanafi tradition emphasizes it in works like Rasm al-Muftī.

Etiquette of Asking the Mufti: Timing and Evidence
Further rules on when to ask a mufti and whether to demand proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a mustafti should refrain from asking a mufti and when a layperson may request evidence, applying al-Sam'ani's condition of definitive evidence.

Core Idea
A mustafti must respect the mufti's state and should not demand proof unless the evidence is definitive.

This passage from Usul al-Iftaa presents two key etiquettes: one regarding the timing of asking a mufti, and another concerning the layperson's demand for proof. You will learn the general rule and its exception from al-Sam'ani, which balances respect for the mufti with the mustafti's need for reassurance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first etiquette
Read the first part: the mustafti should not ask the mufti when the mufti is standing, in a hurry, distressed, or preoccupied. Note the rationale: these states occupy the heart and impair the fatwa.
This shows consideration for the mufti's ability to give a sound fatwa.
2
Identify the second etiquette
The second part states that a layperson should not demand proof (hujjah) from the mufti, nor ask 'Why?' or 'How?' The default is to accept the fatwa without evidence.
This preserves the mufti's authority and avoids unnecessary debate.
3
Understand al-Sam'ani's nuance
Al-Sam'ani adds that if the layperson asks for evidence out of caution (ihtiyat), it is not prohibited. The mufti must then provide the evidence if it is definitive (maqtu' bihi).
Definitive evidence does not require ijtihad; the layperson can understand it directly.
4
Differentiate between definitive and non-definitive evidence
If the evidence is not definitive, the mufti is not obliged to provide it because it involves ijtihad beyond the layperson's capacity.
This distinction is rooted in Hanafi usul: qat'i vs. zanni.

Core Idea
Renewal of a fatwa is not required because the ruling is presumed to continue.

When a mustafti receives a fatwa on a particular matter and then the same situation recurs, should he ask again? This lesson examines the two views presented by Ibn al-Salah and the preferred Hanafi approach based on the principle of istishab (presumption of continuity).

Commentary
This passage from 'Usul al-Ifta' provides rules for the mustafti (the one seeking a fatwa) regarding his conduct with the mufti. The author begins by stating a general principle: if a person receives a fatwa but does not know if it is based on a clear text or consensus, he is obliged to repeat the question to ensure its validity. Adab al-Mustafti: Proper Conduct When Seeking a Fatwa
Etiquette rules for the mustafti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the etiquettes a mustafti must observe when interacting with a mufti, as prescribed by Ibn al-Salah.

Core Idea
Honour the mufti both in words and actions; the mustafti must never be rude, arrogant, or untimely.

Seeking a fatwa is a sacred act that requires proper adab. This lesson covers the respectful behaviors the mustafti must adopt: honouring the mufti, avoiding inappropriate gestures and statements, and choosing the right time to ask.

Guided Path
1

The mustafti should be polite, not interrupt, and accept the fatwa graciously.
3
Understand the timing condition.
The mustafti should not ask when the mufti is standing, in a hurry, distressed, or preoccupied, as this disturbs his concentration.
This ensures the mufti can give a well-considered answer.
4
Apply these rules to a hypothetical scenario.
Consider a mustafti who, while the mufti is leaving for prayer, shouts a question from across the room. This violates multiple etiquettes.
The proper approach is to wait for an appropriate time.

Demanding Proof: The Layman and the Mufti's Evidence
Rules on demanding proof from the mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the mustafti's right to demand proof for definitive (qat'i) vs. speculative (zanni) evidence and apply the rule to a scenario.

Core Idea
A layman may not demand proof; if he does, the mufti only provides definitive proofs.

Should a lay mustafti demand evidence from the mufti? This lesson presents the general etiquette and the exception when the evidence is definitive. It also discusses al-Sam'ani's view allowing demand for caution.

Guided Path
1
Understand the general rule: the layman should not demand proof.
The text states it is not befitting for a layman to demand proof or ask 'why' and 'how' for the fatwa. This preserves the mufti's authority.
The layman is not qualified to evaluate ijtihad.
2
Recognize the permitted method if he wants to hear proof.
If he wishes to satisfy his soul, he may ask in a separate session or after accepting the fatwa without demanding proof during the initial consultation.
This shows respect.
3
Analyze al-Sam'ani's exception: demanding proof for definitive evidence.
Al-Sam'ani says it is not prohibited to demand evidence for caution. If the proof is definitive (qat'i), the mufti must provide it; if speculative (zanni), he is not obliged because it requires ijtihad beyond the layman's grasp.
This is a balanced position.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario.
A mustafti asks the mufti: 'Prove that this transaction is valid from the Quran.' If the ruling is based on a clear verse, the mufti must present it; if based on analogy, he may decline.
The mustafti should accept the authority.
