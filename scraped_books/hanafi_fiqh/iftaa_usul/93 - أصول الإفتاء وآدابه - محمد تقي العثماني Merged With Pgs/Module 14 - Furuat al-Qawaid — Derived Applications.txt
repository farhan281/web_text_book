Custom Overriding Literal Text: Abu Yusuf's Ruling on Measurement
Analyzes an intra-Hanafi dispute on custom vs. literal text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'Rulings change with changing times' to evaluate when custom overrides a literal textual ruling in Hanafi iftaa.

Core Idea
Custom can change the application of a hadith if the ruling's cause was based on custom.

This passage presents a classic Hanafi ikhtilaf on whether the textual mention of 'kayl' (volume) in the riba hadith binds all times or is merely a reflection of the then-prevailing custom. Abu Yusuf's view, preferred by later authorities, opens the door for adapting fiqh to changing customs without violating the text. This lesson will walk through the dispute, the underlying legal reasoning, and its practical application for muftis today.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its scope
The passage opens with the maxim 'Rulings change with changing times'. Recognise this as a major legal maxim governing application of texts when customs shift.
This maxim is not used to invalidate clear texts but to adapt rulings based on 'urff.
2
Analyse the conflicting positions
Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and al-Shafi'i hold that the hadith's literal wording ('kayl') fixes the standard. Abu Yusuf holds that the 'illah is equality in customary measure, so the standard shifts with custom.
The dispute is about the method of extracting the 'illah.
3
Evaluate the Hanafi response to the literalist view
Ibn 'Abidin explains that following the new custom is not opposing the text but actually following it, because the text's cause was the customary norm. This is critical refinement of the textual operation.
This is the key tarjih – the text remains respected; its application changes.
4
Apply to a modern scenario
The passage applies Abu Yusuf's view to selling dirhams by count (rather than weight) in our time. Extend this to other cases where customary measures change.
The mufta bihi view allows flexibility without falling into riba.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle of changing rulings due to changing times, specifically regarding the standard of measurement in transactions of ribawi goods. The author presents the difference between Abu Hanifa and his students. Abu Hanifa, along with Muhammad al-Shaybani and Imam al-Shafi‘i, adhered to the literal wording of the hadith 'measure for measure,' meaning that the standard of measurement (kayl) must be used, regardless of custom. They did not consider the change in customary practice. Abu Yusuf, however, looked at the underlying reason ('illah) of the ruling, which is equality in the customary measure. He noted that the hadith used the term 'kayl' because at that time, the common standard for measuring these goods was by volume. Since custom changed and these goods are now sold by weight, the standard changes accordingly. He considered that following the new custom is not a violation of the text but rather adherence to its spirit. Ibn ‘Abidin supported this view and stated that if people now sell dirhams by count (i.e., by number rather than weight), it would not be riba because the customary standard has changed. The author praises Abu Yusuf for solving a practical problem for later generations. This is a key example of how custom ('urf) can affect the application of a textual ruling when the underlying cause is based on custom.

Custom Negates the Cause of Prohibition: The Bathhouse and Conditions in Sales
Explains how custom negates the cause of prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'rulings change with changing times' to identify when custom negates a legal cause and derive the fatwa for modern transactions.

Core Idea
When the cause of a prohibition is dispute, custom that removes dispute changes the ruling.

This passage from Usul al-Ifta' explores a critical dimension of fatwa: when a textual ruling is based on a specific cause (illah), and that cause ceases to exist due to customary practice, the ruling changes for those particular cases. The author illustrates this with the classic examples of bathhouse hire and conditions in sales, and then extends the principle to modern warranties. Understanding this mechanism is essential for a mufti to issue sound fatwas in changing circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling and its cause
First, identify the textually prohibited act (e.g., gharar in transactions, stipulating conditions in sales) and the legal cause (illah) that triggers the prohibition—here, ignorance leading to dispute.
The cause is explicitly stated: 'al-jahalah al-mufdiyah ila al-niza’' (ignorance leading to dispute).
2
Check if custom eliminates the cause
Examine whether customary practice (urf or ta'amul) in a particular context removes the cause. If people commonly engage in the transaction without dispute, the cause is negated.
3
Apply the qawida: ruling changes for those particulars
Since the ruling was based on the cause, and the cause is absent, the prohibition no longer applies in those specific instances. The ruling changes without overriding the original text.
The text remains authoritative, but its application is restricted by the absence of the cause.
4
Distinguish from overriding a definitive text
Note the important caveat: this reasoning does not apply if the text is definitive in meaning (qat'i al-dalalah). Custom cannot override a clear, unambiguous text; it only overrides analogical reasoning (qiyas) based on the text's cause.
Ibn 'Abidin's clarification: custom does not override the hadith, but rather the qiyas.
5
Extend to modern furuat
Apply the same logic to contemporary cases: e.g., warranty conditions in sales of appliances and cars. Since these are customary and do not lead to dispute, they are permissible despite the general prohibition of stipulations.
This demonstrates the living nature of Hanafi fatwa.

Commentary
This passage presents the second point about how rulings change due to changing times when the underlying cause ('illah) of a textual ruling is negated by custom. The author gives two examples: (1) Entering a bathhouse for a fee – although there is uncertainty (gharar) in the amount of water used and time spent, which is generally prohibited, scholars allowed it because people commonly engage in this transaction without dispute, so the reason for prohibition (leading to dispute) is absent. (2) Conditions in sales – the Prophet (upon him be peace) prohibited stipulating conditions that are not part of the contract. However, Hanafis made an exception for conditions that are customary among traders, such as a seller stitching soles or sewing a bag, because custom establishes these conditions as valid. Al-Sarakhsi explains that what is established by custom has the force of a legal proof, and abandoning it would cause hardship. The author then clarifies that this reasoning does not apply if the text is definitively clear (qat‘i al-dalalah), but here the prohibition was based on an underlying cause (the potential for dispute), and custom removes that cause. Ibn ‘Abidin's quote clarifies that custom does not override the hadith itself, but rather overrides the analogical reasoning (qiyas) that would normally prohibit such transactions, because the hadith's purpose is to prevent dispute, and custom achieves that. Finally, the author applies this principle to modern transactions, such as warranty conditions on appliances and cars, which are customary and therefore permissible.

Custom as a Determinant in Legal Rulings
Explains the interaction between custom and definitive texts, with examples from sales and hire.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'what is established by custom is established by a legal proof' to assess the validity of customary conditions in contracts, and to distinguish when custom cannot override a definitive textual ruling.

Core Idea
Custom removes the cause of prohibition but cannot override a definitive text.

Custom plays a vital role in Hanafi legal reasoning, especially in mu'amalat. This passage explains how customary practices can validate conditions that would otherwise be prohibited, as long as they eliminate the underlying cause of prohibition. It also clarifies the limit: custom cannot override a clear textual ruling but can restrict its analogical extension. Understanding this distinction is essential for issuing sound fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause of prohibition
Determine the reason (illa) behind the general prohibition of conditions in sales: uncertainty (jahalah) that leads to dispute (munaza'ah).
The prohibition is not absolute; it is tied to this cause.
2
See how custom removes the cause
When a condition becomes customary among merchants, it eliminates uncertainty and dispute, thus the cause of prohibition is removed.
This is the basis for permitting conditions like seller making the sandal.
3
Apply the qawida
Use the maxim 'what is established by custom is established by a legal proof' to validate customary conditions, but only if they do not contradict a definitive text.
Al-Sarakhsi's example from al-Mabsut illustrates this.
4
Distinguish the exception
In cases where a definitive text (nas qati' al-dalala) exists, such as the hadith prohibiting qafiz al-tahhan, custom cannot override the text itself, but can limit its analogical extension.
Ibn Abidin clarifies that custom overrides analogy, not the text.
5
Recognize the fatwa implication
When issuing a fatwa, a mufti must first determine if a condition is customary, then check if it conflicts with a definitive text, and then apply the appropriate ruling.
Modern examples include maintenance and installation conditions.

The example of the miller's measure (qafiz al-tahhan) shows how a specific prohibition can be extended by analogy to similar cases (like hiring a grinder for a portion of the flour). Thus, the original prohibition remains for the specific case mentioned in the hadith, but custom may influence analogous cases only if it aligns with the text's rationale.

The Fifth Principle: Rulings Based on Apparent State Change with Time
Explains fifth principle: rulings based on zahir al-hal change when custom changes

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When custom changes, the apparent state shifts, and so does the ruling built upon it.

Imagine a judge ruling on a dower dispute. Normally, the husband's word is taken because custom presumes the wife would not submit without payment. But in societies where women do submit without payment, that presumption collapses. This lesson explores the fifth principle from the Usul al-Ifta' tradition: rulings that rely on apparent states (ẓāhir al-ḥāl) are not fixed—they evolve with custom and time. We'll see this through two examples: a marital dower case and the requirement to investigate witnesses' integrity.

Changing Rulings with Changing Times (Taghayyur al-Ahkam bi Taghayyur al-Zaman)
Explaining a key qa'idah with multiple furuat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the principle of taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-zaman and apply it to identify when a Hanafi fatwa may change due to changing customs or conditions.

Core Idea
Rulings built on custom change when custom changes; rulings built on definitive texts do not.

This lesson introduces a key qa'idah in Hanafi iftaa: rulings may change with changing times. This is not arbitrary change but a recognition that many fiqh rulings are based on custom ('urf) or prevalent conditions. When those conditions change, the ruling may shift to preserve the maqsad al-shari'ah. We will study the principle and its application through several classical examples from the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Qa'idah and its Scope
Start by recognizing the qa'idah 'taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-zaman' and understand that it applies to rulings built on custom, not to those based on definitive texts from Quran and Sunnah.
The qa'idah is wusta (intermediate) in rank and covers many chapters of fiqh.
2
Understand the Condition for Change
The change is triggered when the underlying custom or prevalent condition shifts. The ruling returns to its original principle (asl) but applied to the new reality.
Example: the ruling on wife's statement in prompt mahr changes when the custom of women surrendering without payment becomes prevalent.
3
Work Through the Furuat (Examples)
For each example (mahr, tazkiyah, ikrah, sa'i, zafar), trace how the original ruling was based on a certain custom and how the later fatwa changed when that custom changed.
Note the mechanism: often the later Hanafi masters (mutakhkhirun) adopt the view of the Sahibayn or a new ijtihad.
4
Identify the Istithna' (Exception)
Recall that rulings from definitive texts (Quran, mutawatir hadith) do not change. This ensures the qa'idah is not abused to alter established obligations.
The governing qa'idah for those texts is 'al-ibrah bi umum al-lafz la bi khusus al-sabab'.
5
Apply the Principle to Modern Scenarios
As a mufti, use this qa'idah to evaluate whether a current custom justifies revisiting an earlier fatwa. Ensure the change is within the school's methodology and does not contradict a clear nass.
This requires deep understanding of both the school's positions and the contemporary context.

Commentary
This passage discusses a key qa'idah in Hanafi fiqh: rulings can change with changing times (تغير الأحكام بتغير الزمان). It is not a principle that arbitrary changes can be made, but that when the underlying custom ('urf) or the prevalent condition of people changes, the ruling that was built on that custom may be modified. The passage provides several examples to illustrate this principle. First, in the issue of a wife's claim for prompt dowry: originally, the default rule is that the wife's statement is taken (since she denies receipt) because custom was that she would not surrender herself without receiving the dowry. But if custom changes such that women commonly surrender without payment, then the ruling changes: the husband's statement is preferred because apparent evidence (the prevailing practice) now supports him. Second, in witness verification: Imam Abu Hanifa held that apparent probity suffices, and tazkiyah is only needed if the opponent challenges. His two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) required secret and public inquiry. Al-Hidayah explains this as a difference of time: in early times, Muslims were generally upright, so apparent Islam was sufficient; later, when sin became widespread, verification became necessary. Third, duress (ikrah): Abu Hanifa held that only the ruler could exert duress, because power was monopolized by the state. When times changed and non-state actors also wielded power, Imam Muhammad acknowledged duress from others, and this is the relied-upon position today. Fourth, liability of the informer (sa'i): originally, a false informer is not liable because he is only a cause, and the direct harm is from the ruler. But due to increased corruption, Imam Muhammad ruled for liability to deter. Fifth, the issue of taking one's right from a debtor's property (zafar): originally, one could only take a property of the same type as the debt. Later, due to people's disobedience, fatwa allowed taking from any property. This passage is from a later work (likely by Muhammad Taqi 'Uthmani) and discusses the application of this qa'idah across several chapters of fiqh.

Key Terms
تغير الأحكام بتغير الزمان (taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-zaman): A legal principle that rulings based on custom or prevalent conditions may change when those customs or conditions change.
الساعي (al-sa'i): One who informs or complains against another, causing harm through the authority.
الظفر (al-zafar): A creditor taking his right from a debtor's property without the debtor's permission when the debtor is procrastinating.

Changing Rulings with Changing Customs
Defining the qawida and its application with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the qawida 'Rulings change with the change of time and custom' to identify when a mufti may follow a new custom, and distinguish valid custom from invalid custom.

Core Idea
A mufti must consider contemporary custom; ignoring it causes harm, but only custom that does not violate Sharia may be followed.

This lesson introduces a foundational qawida in Hanafi fatwa methodology: rulings change with changing times and customs. The passage, drawn from Ibn Abidin's works, emphasizes that a mufti cannot rigidly adhere to transmitted texts without considering the custom of his own time. However, this principle has conditions: the custom must not contradict a clear text (nass) or consensus (ijma), and the mufti must have sound knowledge and training. We will unpack the qawida, its application, and its limitations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qawida and its source
Locate the main principle: 'تغير الأحكام بتغير الزمان والعرف'. Note that it is presented through Ibn Abidin's treatise 'Nashr al-Urf' and his commentary on 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti'.
This qawida is ranked as wusta (intermediate) and is kulliya (universal) in scope, governing all fiqh chapters, especially mu'amalat.
2
Understand the hypothetical question and answer
Read the question: 'If custom changes again and again, can the mufti oppose the transmitted text and follow the new custom?' Ibn Abidin answers 'Yes' but only if the custom arose after the Imam's era and the mufti is qualified.
The answer relies on the precedent of later scholars (mutakkhirin) who changed rulings due to new customs.
3
Extract the conditions for applying the qawida
List the conditions: (1) The custom must not contradict nass or ijma. (2) The mufti must have sound opinion, knowledge of Sharia principles, and ability to distinguish valid from invalid custom. (3) He must know the custom of his time and train under a skilled teacher.
The passage stresses that even memorizing all Hanafi books is insufficient without apprenticeship.
4
Analyze the scope and exceptions
Review the dabt (restriction): 'لا عبرة للعرف إذا خالف نصاً أو إجماعاً'. The main exception is when custom contradicts a clear textual ruling or ijma. In such cases, the text prevails.
This exception is governed by the qawida 'النصوص مقدم على العرف'.
5
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
Imagine a new financial practice becomes widespread. The earlier Hanafi ruling presumed a different custom. Using the qawida, the mufti may adopt a new ruling if the custom is sound and he fulfills the conditions. Work through the steps: case – qawida – application – ruling.
This demonstrates the practical operation of the qawida in fatwa issuance.

The Two Situations Where Need Affects Rulings
Explains the effect of need on rulings and its two situations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two situations in which need (al-haja) can influence legal rulings and apply them to new cases.

Core Idea
Need changes rulings only when texts allow or when the ruling is probabilistic.

In this passage, the author explains that need—whether general or specific—can alter the ruling to bring ease, but only under specific conditions. He identifies two scenarios: when the primary texts explicitly consider the need, and when the ruling is based on probabilistic evidence open to ijtihad. You will learn to distinguish these and see their application in classic Hanafi examples.

Guided Path
1
Define general and specific need
From the text, note the definitions: general need affects all or most people; specific need affects a group or individuals.
Both types can influence rulings within limits.
2
Identify the first situation
Recognize when Quran and Sunnah texts explicitly consider a need, e.g., salam (advance sale) and silk for men in war.
These rulings are exceptions that the Shariah itself established.
3
Identify the second situation
Recognize when the original ruling is probabilistic or subject to ijtihad, and need tips toward permissibility, e.g., woman's face covering in testimony or severe crowding.
Need does not change a definitive ruling, only probabilistic ones.
4
Distinguish from definitive prohibitions
Note that need does not affect definitive (qat'i) rulings unless reaching darura (necessity).
This prevents misuse of the principle.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle of sadd al-dhara'i' as a tool of ijtihad, noting that when the Lawgiver has not explicitly commanded a particular means to be blocked, jurists differ. The classic example is the sale of 'aynah, a transaction that circumvents riba. Imam Malik prohibited it absolutely, Imam Shafi'i allowed it as a valid sale, and Hanafis divided: Imam Muhammad condemned it as resembling riba, while Abu Yusuf considered it permissible and even rewarded as a way to avoid the unlawful. Ibn al-Humam reconciled by distinguishing cases where the original asset returns to the seller (makruh) versus where it does not (permissible). The passage then introduces Imam Shatibi's classification of means into three types, beginning with those blocked by consensus (e.g., making idols that lead to insulting Allah). This shows the systematic treatment of the principle in Hanafi usul.

The Principle of Blocking Means (Sadd al-Dhara'i) in Ifta
Explaining the rule of blocking means to evil with its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of Sadd al-Dhara'i to determine when a permissible action becomes impermissible, and distinguish between the mufti's response based on certainty/overwhelming probability vs mere possibility.

Core Idea
A permissible act is only prohibited if it leads to evil with certainty or overwhelming probability, not mere possibility.

This lesson explores the Hanafi legal principle of Sadd al-Dhara'i—blocking the means to evil. You will learn the precise conditions under which a permissible act becomes prohibited, and how a mufti should appropriately counsel a questioner when the risk is less than certain. The famous hadith of Ali and the daughter of Abu Jahl illustrates the nuanced application of this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two levels of risk
The passage distinguishes between certainty/overwhelming probability (yaqin/ghalabat al-zann) and mere possibility (ihtimal). Identify which level triggers prohibition.
Overwhelming probability is practically equivalent to certainty in legal causation.
2
Determine the ruling for each level
For certainty/overwhelming probability: the act becomes impermissible (ghayr ja'iz) because 'whatever leads to prohibited is prohibited'. For mere possibility: the act remains permissible, but the mufti should discourage it.
The mufti's expression should match the level of risk.
3
Analyze the prophetic example
Examine the hadith of Ali and the Prophet's response. Note that the Prophet did not declare the marriage haram but said he would not permit it due to fear of harm to Fatima.
The Prophet explicitly said 'I do not make lawful unlawful', showing the marriage was not inherently haram.
4
Recognize the role of juristic skill
The application of Sadd al-Dhara'i depends on the mufti's malakah fiqhiyyah, acquired through supervised practice. Judgment varies by circumstance.
This is not a mechanical rule but requires scholarly expertise.

In summary, the jurists considered blocking the means (sadd al-dhara'i) as a principle upon which they built many rulings. What becomes apparent after examining the issues built upon this principle is that if it is established that a permissible matter leads to a prohibited outcome with certainty or with overwhelming probability, then that permissible matter is ruled as impermissible, because whatever leads to a prohibited thing is itself prohibited. As for when falling into the prohibited is merely possible, not on the level of certainty or overwhelming probability, then it is not explicitly ruled impermissible, but the mufti chooses an appropriate expression to deter the questioner from that permissible act, such as saying: 'It is not befitting for you' or 'You should avoid it' or 'I do not permit you' or 'I do not advise you' and the like. This is what the Messenger of Allah, peace and blessings be upon him, did when he prevented Ali, may Allah be pleased with him, from marrying the daughter of Abu Jahl. He said: 'Then I do not permit, then I do not permit, then I do not permit, unless the son of Abu Talib wants to divorce my daughter and marry his daughter. For she is a part of me; what troubles her troubles me, and what harms her harms me.' In another narration: 'Fatima is from me, and I fear that she will be tried in her religion.' But in the same narration he said: 'I do not make lawful forbidden, nor do I make forbidden lawful, but by Allah, the daughter of the Messenger of Allah and the daughter of the enemy of Allah will never be brought together.' So the Messenger of Allah, peace and blessings be upon him, made clear that this marriage is not forbidden in itself, but it is feared that it would lead to a major prohibition, namely the distress of the noble Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, due to the distress of his noble daughter if the daughter of Abu Jahl were her co-wife. From this hadith it becomes evident that the ruling on matters of means—whether they are blocked or not—can vary from person to person and from situation to situation. The reference in that is to juristic skill and sound taste, which can hardly be attained except through long practice under the supervision of those who possess this skill. And Allah Almighty knows best.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle of 'Sadd al-Dhara'i' (blocking the means to evil) as applied by Hanafi jurists. It opens by stating that the jurists consider this principle as a foundational basis for many rulings. The text then clarifies the rule: if a permissible action leads to a prohibited outcome with certainty or overwhelming probability (ghalabat al-dhann), then that permissible action is ruled impermissible because 'whatever leads to prohibited is prohibited.' The author distinguishes between two levels of probability: certain/overwhelming probability vs. mere possibility. In the first case, the action is explicitly forbidden. In the second case, the mufti should not forbid it outright but should use discouraging phrases like 'it is not befitting for you' to steer the questioner away. The author then cites a well-known hadith where the Prophet (saw) prevented Ali (ra) from marrying the daughter of Abu Jahl. The Prophet's words show that the marriage itself was not haram (he said 'I do not make lawful unlawful'), but he forbade it due to the likely harm and distress it would cause Fatima (ra), his daughter and Ali's wife. This hadith illustrates that the application of blocking means depends on circumstances and the degree of probability. The passage concludes that the judgment on whether to block a means depends on juristic intuition (malakah fiqhiyyah) acquired through long supervised practice. The author emphasizes that this is a matter of scholarly expertise, not a mechanical rule.

Key Terms
سد الذرائع (sadd al-dharā'i‘): The principle of blocking the means that could lead to a prohibited outcome.
غَلَبَة الظَّنِّ (ghalabat al-ẓann): Overwhelming probability that is tantamount to certainty in practical terms.
مَلَكة فقهيّة (malakatu'l-fiqhiyyah): Juristic skill or intuitive expertise acquired through extensive study and practice under scholars.
