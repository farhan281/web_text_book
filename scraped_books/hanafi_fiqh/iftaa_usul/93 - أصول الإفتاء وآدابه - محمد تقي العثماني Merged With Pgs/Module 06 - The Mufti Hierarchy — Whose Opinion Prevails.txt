The Prolific Muftis Among the Companions
Historical listing of prolific Companion muftis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recall the seven most prolific Companion muftis listed by Ibn al-Qayyim and explain why their fatwas are foundational in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Fatwa from over 130 Companions is preserved; only seven were the most prolific – knowing them anchors Hanafi transmission.

Ibn al-Qayyim's I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in provides a key historical statistic: over 130 Companions issued fatwas, but the overwhelming majority of transmitted rulings come from just seven individuals. This list is not mere trivia; these seven are the backbone of early Islamic legal authority. In Hanafi fiqh, their sayings are frequently cited as primary sources alongside the Quran and Sunnah. Understanding who they are and why they were so prolific helps you appreciate the chain of transmission that grounds our school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the total number
Start with Ibn al-Qayyim's figure: over 130 Companion muftis. This immediately establishes that fatwa activity was widespread among the Sahaba.
130+ means neither rare nor exclusive.
2
Memorize the seven names
The passage names seven: Umar, Ali, Ibn Mas'ud, Aisha, Zayd ibn Thabit, Ibn Abbas, and Ibn Umar. These are the 'mukthirun'—the most prolific. Their names must be committed to memory because they recur constantly in Hanafi arguments.
Notice that Abu Bakr and Uthman are not among the seven, even though they were caliphs.
3
Connect to Hanafi usul
Hanafi fiqh relies heavily on the rulings and legal reasoning of these Companions. For example, Abu Hanifa often adopts the positions of Ibn Mas'ud and Ali. Recognizing this lineage reinforces the school's methodology.
This list is not exclusive—other Companions contributed, but these seven are the heavy lifters.
4
Understand the footnote on Ibn Hazm
The footnote introduces Ibn Hazm, a Zahiri scholar who rejected qiyas. By contrast, the Hanafi school embraces qiyas and istihsan. This contrast shows that not all early scholars agreed on methodology, even while respecting the same Companion sources.
The footnote is a reminder that the list of muftis is widely accepted across madhahib, but the methods of using their fatwas differ.

Degrees of Muftis: Third and Fourth
Defines the third and fourth degrees of muftis and the necessity of occasional taqlid for absolute mujtahids.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the third and fourth degrees of muftis and explain why an absolute mujtahid still needs taqlid.

Core Idea
Even absolute mujtahids practice taqlid of earlier authorities when no explicit text exists.

In this lesson, we explore the higher echelons of muftis: those who, while following an imam in fundamentals, can exercise ijtihad within the madhhab (third degree), and those who are independent in deriving rulings but still occasionally rely on the statements of the salaf (fourth degree). Understanding this hierarchy prevents the misconception that absolute ijtihad means never following anyone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two degrees described
Read the passage carefully and note which two categories of muftis are being defined: the 'muqallid mujtahid fi al-madhhab' and the 'mujtahid mutlaq'.
These are the third and fourth degrees according to Ibn al-Qayyim's classification.
2
Understand the third degree
The third-degree mufti follows his imam in usul (principles) but has developed enough expertise to derive rulings for new cases (takhrij) and to prefer between conflicting reports (tarjih). He is not independent but operates within the madhhab's framework.
Examples include al-Tahawi, al-Karkhi, and al-Sarakhsi (Hanafi).
3
Understand the fourth degree
The absolute mujtahid can independently derive rulings from Quran and Sunnah. Yet, when no explicit text exists, he looks to the statements of Companions and Followers and gives them precedence over his own opinion. This is a form of taqlid that does not diminish his ijtihad.
The passage gives examples: Abu Hanifa followed Ibrahim al-Nakha‘i; al-Shafi‘i followed Ibn Jurayj; Malik followed one of the seven jurists of Medina.
4
Recognize the reason for occasional taqlid
The necessity arises because not every case has an explicit textual ruling. In such situations, the opinions of earlier authorities provide guidance, and the absolute mujtahid prioritizes them over his independent reasoning.
Ibn al-Qayyim is quoted to reinforce this point.
5
Note the practical implications for ijtihad
This classification teaches humility and respect for scholarly tradition. Even the greatest imams engaged in taqlid when needed. It also helps a mufti understand his own level and the authority he can claim.
The footnote references I‘lam al-Muwaqqi‘in, which divides muftis into four categories.

Commentary
This passage identifies two higher degrees of muftis beyond the common muqallid. It first describes the third degree: a mujtahid within the madhhab (مجتهد في المذهب). Such a scholar follows his imam in usul (fundamentals) but has developed sufficient expertise to derive rulings for new cases or perform takhrij (extrapolation) and tarjih (preference between conflicting reports). They are not independent mujtahids but operate within the framework of their school. The fourth degree is the absolute mujtahid (مجتهد مطلق), who can independently derive rulings from primary sources. However, even such a scholar must sometimes practice taqlid of earlier authorities, because not every issue has an explicit textual ruling. The author gives examples: Imam Abu Hanifa often followed Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, Imam al-Shafi'i followed Ibn Jurayj, and Imam Malik followed one of the seven jurists of Medina. Ibn al-Qayyim is quoted to show that occasional taqlid does not negate ijtihad; even al-Shafi'i once stated he said something out of taqlid of 'Ata. This teaches humility and the importance of respecting the scholarly tradition. The passage concludes with the source footnote indicating that these categories are from Ibn al-Qayyim's I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in, which divides muftis into four types.

Key Terms
مقلد مجتهد في المذهب (muqallid mujtahid fi al-madhhab): A scholar who follows his imam in fundamental principles but has ijtihad in secondary rulings or new incidents, enabling takhrij and tarjih.
مجتهد مطلق (mujtahid mutlaq): An absolute mujtahid who can independently derive rulings from Quran and Sunnah, but still occasionally practices taqlid of earlier authorities when no explicit text is found.

Tabaqat al-Fuqaha: The Hierarchy of Hanafi Jurists and Its Impact on Fatwa
Classification of Hanafi jurist ranks and their interpretive capacities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the third and fourth classes of Hanafi jurists, distinguish their capacities, and apply the hierarchy to determine the authority of a fatwa.

Core Idea
Fourth-class jurists (as'hab al-takhrij) cannot do independent ijtihad but can detail ambiguous rulings.

This passage from Muhammad Taqi Usmani's work on usul al-iftaa lays out the classes of Hanafi jurists, specifically the third and fourth. Understanding these categories is essential for a mufti to know whose opinion carries more weight and how far each class can go in deriving new rulings. The third class (mujtahidun fi al-madhhab) can derive rulings from the imam's principles, while the fourth class (as'hab al-takhrij) can only elaborate on ambiguous transmitted statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two classes described
Read the passage and locate the descriptions of the third class and the fourth class. Note the key capabilities and limitations of each.
The third class is introduced after the biographies of al-Karkhi, al-Halwani, al-Sarakhsi, and al-Bazdawi. The fourth class is explicitly called 'tabaqat al-takhrij'.
2
Analyze the definitions
For the third class: they cannot oppose the imam in usul or furu', but can derive rulings for new cases using his principles. For the fourth class: they are muqallids who cannot perform ijtihad, but due to mastery of usul, they can detail ambiguous transmitted rulings.
The phrase 'يستنبطون الأحكام عن المسائل التي لا نص فيها' for the third class shows derivation ability.
3
Connect biographical examples
Match the scholars mentioned to their classes: al-Karkhi, al-Halwani, al-Sarakhsi, al-Bazdawi are third class; al-Razi (Abu Bakr al-Jassas) is fourth class. Qadikhan is also third class.
Biographies illustrate the chain of knowledge and scholarly output.
4
Determine practical consequence for fatwa
The hierarchy dictates that fatwa should prefer rulings from the highest available class. The third class views carry more authority than the fourth class.
A mufti must know the class of the jurist he cites to correctly weigh opinions.
5
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Given a hypothetical new case, decide whether a fourth-class jurist can issue a ruling and how it should be considered relative to a third-class opinion.
The fourth-class jurist can only elaborate on existing ambiguous rulings; he cannot derive from scratch.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the classes (tabaqat) of Hanafi jurists, a key concept in usul al-iftaa. It begins by listing prominent scholars from the third class: Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi, Shams al-A'immah al-Halwani, Shams al-A'immah al-Sarakhsi, and Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi. These are followed by biographical details that illustrate their stature and chain of knowledge. The text then explicitly defines the two relevant classes. The third class consists of jurists who are unable to oppose Imam Abu Hanifa in either usul (principles) or furu' (branches), but they can derive rulings for new cases based on his established principles and rules. Examples given are Fakhr al-Din Qadikhan and others. The fourth class is 'as'hab al-takhrij' (those capable of ijtihad through takhrij) among the muqallids, like al-Razi (Abu Bakr al-Jassas al-Razi). They cannot perform ijtihad independently, but due to their mastery of usul and sources, they can detail ambiguous transmitted statements or rulings that have two possible interpretations. The author then provides biographies of Qadikhan and al-Jassas as representatives of these classes. The commentary emphasizes that this classification determines the authority weight of a jurist's opinions: those of the third class carry more weight than those of the fourth, and fatwa should be given according to the highest available authority.

Key Terms
الطبقة الرابعة (al-tabaqah al-rabi'ah): The fourth class of Hanafi jurists: those who perform takhrij from transmitted rulings
أصحاب التخريج (as'hab al-takhrij): Muqallid jurists who can derive rulings by expanding upon ambiguous transmitted statements
مقلدون (muqallidun): Jurists who follow an imam without independent ijtihad

The Fifth and Sixth Layers of Hanafi Jurists: Preference and Distinction
Defines the fifth and sixth layers of Hanafi jurists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the qualifications of the fifth and sixth layers of Hanafi muqallid jurists and explain the difference between tarjih (preference) and tamyiz (distinction).

Core Idea
Only muqallids of the fifth and sixth layers may issue fatwas—by preferring or distinguishing between transmitted rulings.

This passage from a Hanafi manual on fatwa principles defines the hierarchy of jurists. The fifth layer are 'as-hab al-tarjih' who can prefer one narration over another; the sixth layer can distinguish between strong and weak narrations but cannot perform tarjih. Understanding this classification is essential for any mufti to know the limits of his authority.

The Authority of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad in Hanafi vs. Al-Muzani in Shafi'i Madhhab
Compares the hierarchical rank of Hanafi Sahibayn and Shafi'i al-Muzani

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the hierarchical status of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad with that of al-Muzani and explain the fatwa hierarchy rule that determines when a student's opinion is considered usul-level opposition vs. mere takhrij.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's disagreements with Abu Hanifa are foundational; al-Muzani's choices are mere extensions (takhrij) in the Shafi'i school.

This passage from 'Tabaqat al-Fuqaha' sets a critical distinction for muftis: not all ikhtiyarat within a madhhab carry equal weight. The Hanafi school gives special status to the opinions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad—even when they contradict Abu Hanifa—because they were direct students who debated at the level of usul. In contrast, Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni considered the choices of al-Muzani (a student of al-Shafi'i) as mere takhrij (derivations) appended to the madhhab, not as independent positions that could override the founding principles. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for a mufti when determining which opinion to prefer in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key figures and their roles
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) are direct students of Imam Abu Hanifa. Al-Muzani is a direct student of Imam al-Shafi'i. Recognize that each madhhab has its own internal hierarchy.
These figures are not just any students; they are primary transmitters and elaborators of their respective madhhab.
2
Distinguish between 'mukhalafa fi al-usul' and 'takhrij'
Al-Juwayni states that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad 'oppose the principles' (yukhalifan usul) of Abu Hanifa, whereas al-Muzani's choices are 'takhrij mulhaq bi al-madhhab' (an extension annexed to the madhhab). Takhrij does not alter the foundational usul.
Mukhalafa fi al-usul means the student's ijtihad directly engages and sometimes overrides the imam's principles. Takhrij derives new rulings within the imam's framework.
3
Apply to fatwa hierarchy
In Hanafi, the Sahibayn's opposing views are part of the madhhab's ikhtilaf and must be considered. In Shafi'i, al-Muzani's views are subordinate; they cannot override al-Shafi'i's explicit usul.
This affects tarjih: a Hanafi mufti may adopt Abu Yusuf's view over Abu Hanifa's in certain cases, while a Shafi'i mufti would generally not do so with al-Muzani.
4
Recognize the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
When issuing fatwa, a Hanafi mufti may give weight to a Sahibayn opinion, even if it contradicts the imam. A Shafi'i mufti treats al-Muzani's opinion as a secondary reference, not a full-fledged school position.
This distinction is crucial for understanding why some Hanafi fatwas appear to depart from Abu Hanifa's stated view.

The Rank of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: Independent Mujtahids Affiliated to the Madhhab
Discussing the ijtihad rank of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the concept of al-mujtahid al-muntasib and apply it to evaluate the ijtihad rank of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani in the Hanafi hierarchy.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad were independent absolute mujtahids, but out of respect they ascribed themselves to Abu Hanifa.

This passage from Usul al-Ifta addresses a critical point in the Hanafi hierarchy: the status of the two greatest students of Abu Hanifa. It examines a dispute with Shafi'i scholars who questioned their rank and presents al-Laknawi's definitive position that they are mujtahid muntasib — independent but affiliated. Understanding this classification is essential for a mufti when weighing their opinions in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Read al-Juwayni's statement: he considers al-Muzani's work an extension of the Shafi'i madhhab, but holds that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad are lower because they contradict their master's principles. What is the issue here?
The issue is whether their disagreement with Abu Hanifa indicates a lower rank or independent ijtihad.
2
Analyze al-Laknawi's response
Al-Laknawi asserts that they are independent absolute mujtahids. He argues that their contradictions stem from their own ijtihad, not from inability. Their ascription to Abu Hanifa is due to veneration, not dependence.
Note the key phrase: 'except that out of good respect... they adopted his principles.'
3
Define the category 'mujtahid muntasib'
The passage presents two definitions: (1) al-Laknawi's: an absolute mujtahid who does not imitate anyone but ascribes himself to a teacher out of respect. (2) Ibn al-Salah's: an absolute mujtahid who follows the method of an independent imam.
Both agree on independence; the difference is the reason for affiliation.
4
Apply to fatwa hierarchy
Consider how this classification affects fatwa: if they are independent, their opinions carry the weight of ijtihad and are not mere extensions. A mufti can adopt their view even if it contradicts Abu Hanifa, as long as it aligns with the madhhab's later tarjih.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

The Hanafi tradition carefully distinguishes the levels of ijtihad to clarify who can derive rulings independently and whose opinions carry binding authority. Commentary
This passage is a classification of mujtahids within the Hanafi tradition, distinguishing three ranks based on their level of independence and authority. Commentary

Critique of Ibn Kamal's Tabaqat: The Mistake of Downgrading Iraqi Jurists
Critique of Ibn Kamal's tabaqat classification by Lakhnawi and Marjani

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the methodological error in Ibn Kamal's classification of early Hanafi jurists and state why their ranks should not be lowered based on titles.

Core Idea
Titles do not reflect juristic ability; early Iraqi masters outrank later grand-titled scholars.

This lesson examines the classical critique of Ibn Kamal Pasha's tabaqat (hierarchical layers) of Hanafi jurists. The objection centers on his downgrading of early Iraqi scholars like Abu Bakr al-Razi al-Jassas and al-Quduri, who, despite modest titles, were intellectually superior and temporally prior to later Khurasani jurists like Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani. Understanding this critique is essential for correctly weighing authorities in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the classification being critiqued
Ibn Kamal's tabaqat places certain jurists in lower categories. The passage specifically objects to the placement of al-Jassas, al-Quduri, and the author of al-Hidayah.
Focus on the examples: al-Jassas classed as 'incapable of ijtihad', while al-Halwani, al-Sarakhsi, al-Bazdawi, and Qadi Khan are classed higher.
2
State the basis of the classification
The text suggests Ibn Kamal was influenced by the titles and practices of Transoxiana scholars, who elevated their own with grand titles while simplifying Iraqi titles.
The basis is not purely juristic ability but external appearance.
3
Analyze the objection against al-Jassas
The author calls the downgrade 'very far-fetched' because al-Jassas is earlier in time, higher in rank, more extensive in knowledge, and more subtle in insight.
These criteria (temporal precedence, knowledge, subtlety) are presented as the real measures of rank.
4
Apply the same logic to al-Quduri
Al-Quduri's standing is said to be greater than Qadi Khan's, and the author of al-Hidayah is at least his equal. Placing Qadi Khan in a third rank and lowering the others is improper.
Note the implied hierarchy: al-Quduri ≥ author of al-Hidayah ≥ Qadi Khan.
5
Conclude the methodological lesson
Titles are cultural and do not determine juristic ability. Classification must be based on actual juristic contributions, knowledge, and precedence.
This principle is foundational for usul al-iftaa when weighing conflicting authorities.

The Fourth and Fifth Layers of Muftis
Classification of muftis into layers, focusing on fourth and fifth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the qualifications and limitations of the fourth and fifth layers of muftis and determine the scope of analogical reasoning permissible for each.

Core Idea
The fourth layer uses qiyas beyond manifest analogy; the fifth layer only uses qiyas with no difference or under a rule.

Not every mufti is a full mujtahid. Ibn al-Salah's classification helps us understand the different levels of expertise. The fourth and fifth layers are particularly relevant for later scholars who compiled the school's works and issued fatwas within defined boundaries. This lesson parses their capabilities and restrictions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fourth layer's qualifications
List the attributes: faqih al-nafs, preserves the school, knows evidences, can expound and weigh, but is below ashab al-wujuh. Note its three possible deficiencies: lower preservation, less training in derivation, or weaker usul al-fiqh.
2
Identify the fifth layer's qualifications
This layer preserves and understands but is weak in analyzing evidences and refining analogies. They rely on transmitted rulings and only analogize when no difference exists or under a rule.
3

The fourth layer can use non-manifest qiyas (e.g., from slave to slave-girl, man to woman). The fifth layer only uses qiyas dariq la farq or when under a rule.

Both layers must be 'faqih al-nafs' to properly depict issues. This is a prerequisite for all muftis.
5
Recognize the consequence of falling below
Ibn al-Salah warns that anyone who gives fatwa without meeting any of the five types is sinful. This establishes the ethical obligation.

The Three Layers of Hanafi Fiqh Sources: Authority Hierarchy for the Mufti
Classifying Hanafi legal sources into three hierarchical layers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given Hanafi fiqh ruling source into one of three layers (Zahir al-Riwayah, Nawadir, Fatawa) and apply the rule that Zahir al-Riwayah takes precedence in fatwa.

Core Idea
The Hanafi mufti must prioritize: Zahir al-Riwayah first, then Nawadir, then later Fatawa and Waqi'at.

The strength of a fatwa depends on the authority of its source. The Hanafi school classifies its fiqh literature into three distinct layers, each with a different weight. A mufti must know which layer a ruling belongs to in order to issue correct opinions. This lesson unpacks the hierarchy and a critical terminological nuance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three layers
List the three layers: Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Imam Muhammad), Nawadir (rare transmissions outside those books), and Fatawa/Waqi'at (later jurists' deductions).
Memorize the order: highest to lowest.
2
Distinguish Zahir al-Riwayah from Nawadir
Zahir al-Riwayah narrations are from Imam Muhammad via reliable chains (tawatur/istifadah) in his six books. Nawadir are found in other books by Muhammad (e.g., al-Kaysaniyat) or by other imams (e.g., al-Mujarrad by Hasan ibn Ziyad), or through individual transmissions.
The key is whether the narration is in the six books.
3
Understand the role of Fatawa and Waqi'at
When no direct narration exists from early imams, later mujtahids derive rulings for novel issues. These form the lowest layer.
These are case-based and may not have the same binding force.
4
Analyze the terminological dispute
The majority equates 'al-Usul' with 'Zahir al-Riwayah'. Ibn Kamal Pasha distinguishes: 'Usul' only for the six books, 'Zahir al-Riwayah' includes any sound transmission from the imams. Ibn Abidin refutes this by noting that even transmissions from others might be in the six books.
The practical effect: whether some Nawadir could be elevated to Zahir al-Riwayah.
5
Apply the hierarchy
Given a specific source (e.g., a ruling from al-Kaysaniyat), determine its layer and decide whether it can override a ruling from al-Mabsut.
Always check the highest layer first.

Commentary

Verdict of a Muqallid Judge Contrary to His Imam's Madhhab
Rule on enforceability of muqallid judge's verdict against his imam's madhhab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a muqallid judge's verdict contrary to his imam's madhhab is enforceable and apply the rule to cases of judicial appointment.

Core Idea
A muqallid judge's verdict against his imam's madhhab is unenforceable if the ruler appointed him conditionally; otherwise it stands.

This lesson examines the enforceability of a verdict issued by a muqallid judge who rules contrary to the madhhab of his imam. The key distinction lies in the terms of his appointment. Understanding this rule is crucial for muftis and judges in determining the validity of judicial decisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the judge's status
Determine whether the judge is a mujtahid or a muqallid. A mujtahid's own ijtihad is binding; a muqallid is bound by his imam's madhhab unless otherwise authorized.
The two agreed-upon enforceable cases are: 1) mujtahid ruling by his ijtihad, 2) muqallid ruling by his imam's madhhab.
2
Check the terms of appointment
Examine whether the appointing ruler explicitly restricted the judge to a specific madhhab. This is the critical variable.
The ta'lil from Fath al-Qadir states: the judge was appointed to judge by the madhhab of Abu Hanifa, so he cannot oppose it.
3
Analyze the consequence of deviation
If the judge was restricted and then ruled contrary to that madhhab, he is considered dismissed for that ruling; the verdict does not take effect.
The dismissal is limited to that specific ruling, not the entire judgeship.
4
Apply the exception
If the ruler did not restrict the judge to a specific madhhab, the verdict in matters of ijtihad is enforceable even if it contradicts his imam's madhhab.
This is the logical consequence of the ta'lil: the condition defines the authority.

The verdict of the muqallid judge contrary to his imam's madhhab.

All that has passed of detail regarding the enforceability of verdicts in matters of ijtihad applies to two cases by agreement: the first case is that the judge is a mujtahid and his verdict agrees with his own ijtihad. The second case is that he is a muqallid and he has given a verdict according to his imam's madhhab; then his verdict is enforceable upon all, whether the litigants are mujtahids and their opinion differs from the judge's imam's opinion, or they are muqallids of another imam. However, if the judge is a muqallid of a specific imam, and then he gives a verdict contrary to his madhhab, they have mentioned that it is not enforceable. And the scholars' statements regarding its ta'lil have differed. He (the author of Fath al-Qadir) gave the ta'lil with his words: 'As for the muqallid, he was appointed to judge according to the madhhab of Abu Hanifa, for example, so he does not possess the authority to oppose, thus he is dismissed with respect to that ruling.' Based on this, the non-enforceability of his verdict is founded on the fact that the imam (ruler) only made him a judge on condition that he judges according to the madhhab of Abu Hanifa. If he deviates from his madhhab, he becomes dismissed in that ruling, so it is not enforceable. And the consequence of this ta'lil is that if the imam did not restrict him to a specific madhhab, then his verdict in matters of ijtihad is enforceable even if it contradicts his imam's madhhab.

The passage first outlines two cases where a verdict is clearly enforceable: when the judge is a mujtahid and rules according to his own ijtihad, and when a muqallid rules according to his imam's madhhab. The contested issue is the muqallid judge ruling against his imam's madhhab. The scholars state it is not enforceable. This highlights that the restriction is based on the terms of appointment, not on the judge's personal adherence.
