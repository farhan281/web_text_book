The Rule on Hypothetical Questions in Fatwa
Discussion of a rasm rule with evidence and harmonization

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between permissible and impermissible hypothetical questions in fatwa and apply the Hanafi rasm rule based on the distinction between sincere inquiry and obstinate questioning.

Core Idea
Asking about hypothetical events is permissible for sincere learning, but forbidden when intended to confuse or entrap.

This passage by al-Khatib al-Baghdadi navigates an apparent contradiction: the Prophet ﷺ answered hypothetical questions, while 'Umar punished those who asked them. The key is intention. The passage establishes a crucial rasm (rule) for muftis: it is permissible to answer questions about events that have not yet occurred, provided the questioner seeks knowledge, not obstinacy. This lesson unpacks the evidence and the harmonization.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Ruling
Determine the general permissibility of asking about hypothetical events from the evidence. The author states that after the Prophet's death, the concern of revelation restricting a permissible matter no longer applies, so such questions are not inherently prohibited.
The default is permissibility (ibaha).
2
Examine the Primary Evidence
Analyze the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij and the man asking about fighting oppressive leaders. In both cases, the Prophet answered without rebuke, demonstrating tacit approval.
These are examples of sincere hypothetical questions.
3
Address the Apparent Objection
Consider 'Umar's harsh treatment of Sabigh and the hadith forbidding 'al-ughluthat'. These seem to prohibit hypothetical questions entirely.
This creates a conflict that must be resolved.
4
Apply the Harmonization
The author resolves the conflict by distinguishing between two intentions: sincere inquiry (tafaqquh) and obstinate, misleading questioning (ta'annut wa mughalata). 'Umar's actions targeted the latter.
Intention (niyya) is the decisive factor.
5
Confirm the Consensus
Note that many Companions and later jurists discussed hypothetical rulings, forming an implicit consensus (ijma') on the permissibility of sincere hypothetical questions.
This solidifies the Hanafi position.

Methodologies of fatwa among the Salaf. And fearing that Allah might forbid, upon the question of a questioner, something that was permissible before his asking about it, so the question becomes the cause of prohibiting what was beneficial for the ummah in its permissibility, thus hardship and harm enter upon them. And this meaning has ceased with the death of the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, and the rulings of Sharia have been established, so there is no prohibitor nor permissive after him. Then al-Khatib, may Allah have mercy on him, adduced evidence for the permissibility of asking about what has not yet occurred with the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij, may Allah be pleased with him, who said: 'I said: O Messenger of Allah, we fear that we will meet the enemy tomorrow and we do not have knives, so can we slaughter with reeds?' The Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, said: 'Whatever causes blood to flow and you mention the name of Allah over it, eat, except for teeth and nails.' The Messenger of Allah did not disapprove of Rafi's question about something that had not yet happened, because he said 'tomorrow' and he did not say to him: 'Why did you ask about something that has not yet occurred?' Likewise, al-Khatib adduced the hadith of Yazid ibn Salamah, from his father, that a man stood up to the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, and said: 'O Messenger of Allah, what do you think if there are leaders over us who ask us for what is right and withhold our right, should we fight them?' Then al-Ash'ath ibn Qays stood up and said: 'Do you ask the Messenger of Allah about something that has not yet happened?' He said: 'I will ask him until he stops me.' Then he said: 'O Messenger of Allah, what do you think if there are leaders over us who ask us for what is right and withhold our right, should we fight them?' He said: 'No! Upon you is what you are charged, and upon them is what they are charged.' Al-Khatib said: 'The Messenger of Allah did not prevent this man from his question, nor did he disapprove of it, but rather answered him without dislike.' And there are many similar traditions for what we have mentioned. As for Umar's harshness in asking about what has not yet occurred and his curse upon whoever did that, it is possible that he intended by it questioning in the manner of obstinacy and fallacy, not in the manner of seeking understanding and benefit. And for this reason he beat Sabigh ibn 'Asl and exiled him, and deprived him of his sustenance and stipend, when he asked about ambiguous letters of the Quran. Umar feared that by his questioning he intended to trouble the weak among the Muslims in knowledge, to cast doubt and misguidance into their hearts by distorting the Quran from the path of revelation, and turning it from correct speech about it to corrupt interpretation. And the like of this has been reported from the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, forbidding it and dispraising its doer. Then al-Khatib narrated the hadith of Mu'awiyah, may Allah be pleased with him, that the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, forbade 'al-ughluthat'. 'Isa said: 'Al-ughluthat: that which is not needed from how and how.' And the hadith of Thawban, may Allah be pleased with him, from the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, who said: 'There will be people from my ummah who will cause their jurists to err by difficult questions; those are the worst of my ummah.' And he narrated from al-Hasan al-Basri, may Allah have mercy on him, who said: 'The best of Allah's servants pick the worst of questions to blind Allah's servants.' Then al-Khatib said: 'It has been narrated from Umar ibn al-Khattab, Ali ibn Abi Talib, and other companions that they spoke about rulings of events before their occurrence, and debated in the knowledge of shares and inheritance, and the successors and the jurists of the regions followed them in this path. So that was a consensus from them that it is permissible, not disliked, and allowed, not forbidden. As for the hadith of Zayd ibn Thabit and Ubayy ibn Ka'b...'

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the rasm rule regarding the permissibility of asking questions about hypothetical events (al-su'al 'amma lam yakun) in fiqh. The author begins by explaining the wisdom behind the prohibition during the Prophet's era: asking could result in a revelation that restricts a previously permissible matter, causing hardship. With the death of the Prophet and the completion of revelation, this concern is gone. Then al-Khatib al-Baghdadi brings evidence from two hadiths: Rafi' ibn Khadij asking about slaughtering with reeds before the need arises, and a man asking about fighting oppressive leaders. The Prophet answered both without rebuke, showing such questions are permissible. The author then addresses the apparent contradiction of 'Umar's harshness toward hypothetical questions (e.g., with Sabigh ibn 'Asl). He harmonizes by distinguishing between sincere inquiry (tafaqquh) and obstinate, argumentative questioning (ta'annut wa mughalata). This is supported by the hadith forbidding 'al-ughluthat'—difficult, unnecessary questions—and the narration that those who trouble scholars with such are the worst of the ummah. Finally, the author notes that many Companions and later jurists discussed hypothetical rulings, forming an implicit consensus (ijma') on its permissibility when done properly. The key ruling: it is allowed to ask about hypotheticals for genuine learning, but prohibited if done for obstinacy or to confuse.

Key Terms
الأغلوطات (al-ughluthat): Difficult, unnecessary questions that are asked to confuse or entrap the jurist.
التعنّت (al-ta'annut): Obstinate and argumentative questioning without sincere intent to learn.
المغالطة (al-mughalata): Fallacious questioning aimed at misleading or causing error.

The Second Principle: When Only One Ruling Exists
States a procedural rule for muftis when the madhhab has a single ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Second Principle to determine which ruling a mufti must follow when the Hanafi madhhab has only one ruling on a matter, and identify the exception when the underlying cause is lost.

Core Idea
When the Hanafi madhhab has only one ruling on a matter, it must be followed regardless of its source — unless the cause is gone.

A mufti often encounters matters where the Hanafi madhhab has transmitted only one ruling. In such cases, there is no need to compare different sources or weigh authorities. This lesson explains the Second Principle of Usul al-Iftaa, which prescribes that the single ruling must be adopted, whether it appears in Zahir al-Riwayah, al-Nawadir, or later fatwa collections, with one critical exception.

Guided Path
1
Determine if multiple rulings exist
The mufti must first exhaustively search the madhhab to confirm whether the matter has only one transmitted ruling. If multiple rulings exist, other principles apply.
The division into source categories only matters when there are differing rulings.
2
Identify the source category of the single ruling
Whether the ruling comes from Zahir al-Riwayah, al-Nawadir, or later fatwa collections, it carries the same weight under this principle.
Do not reject a ruling simply because it is not from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Assess whether the ruling’s cause (illa) is lost
If it is known with certainty that the ruling is based on a specific cause that no longer exists, the ruling may be set aside. Otherwise, it must be followed.
This exception requires decisive knowledge, not mere doubt.
4
Adopt the ruling or apply the exception
If the cause is still present or its loss is uncertain, the mufti must adopt the sole ruling. If the cause is certainly lost, he may search for a different approach.
The default is adherence; the exception is narrow.

Commentary
This passage states a fundamental rule for the mufti: when the Hanafi madhhab has only one ruling on a given issue, regardless of the source (whether it is from the authoritative Zahir al-Riwayah, the rare narrations in al-Nawadir, or the later fatwa collections), that ruling must be taken and followed. The only exception is if it is absolutely clear that the ruling was based on a specific underlying cause that no longer exists. This principle prevents unnecessary searching for alternative views when there is unity in the madhhab.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani which are the most authoritative source of Hanafi rulings.
النوادر (al-Nawadir): Rare transmissions from the early Hanafi imams, not part of the six core books.
الواقعات والفتاوى (al-Waqi‘at wa al-Fatawa): Later cases and fatwas by later Hanafi scholars.

Rule on Fatwa from Overly Concise Books
Rule on issuing fatwa from concise books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the category of books that are too concise for safe fatwa and apply the rule of when and how to use them.

Core Idea
Concise books are authoritative but dangerous in isolation — verification from commentaries is essential.

This passage from Usul al-Iftaa addresses a critical principle: the mufti must not rely solely on abbreviated texts, no matter how famous. Even esteemed works like al-Durr al-Mukhtar require cross-referencing with detailed commentaries to avoid error.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category
Recognise that 'al-ikhtisar al-mukhkhil' refers to conciseness that impairs understanding. The passage lists al-Durr al-Mukhtar and al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir as examples.
These books are authoritative but their brevity is a risk factor.
2
Understand the ruling
Scholars state it is not permissible (la yajuuz) to issue fatwa from these books alone. The risk of error due to incomplete understanding is too high.
The ruling is one of prohibition in isolation.
3
Recognise the condition for permissible use
The prohibition is lifted if the mufti engages in deep consideration (nazar gha'ir), thorough deliberation, and consults the commentaries and glossaries of these books.
Certainty (tayaqqud) after verification permits fatwa.
4
Note the additional critique by Ibn Abidin
Ibn Abidin points out that these books contain omissions in transmission and preferences of weaker or outside-madhhab views, placing them also in the second category (books with internal errors).
This strengthens the need for caution.
5
Apply the rule in practice
When a mufti receives a question, he must avoid citing only these concise books. He must cross-check with Radd al-Muhtar (for al-Durr al-Mukhtar) and similar works.
This ensures the fatwa aligns with the mufta bihi of the madhhab.

The third aspect: Conciseness that impairs understanding. There are books whose lofty status and the trustworthiness of their authors is undoubted, but they contain conciseness that impairs understanding. Therefore the scholars said: It is not permissible to issue fatwa from them, such as al-Durr al-Mukhtar, al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, and other concise books. But this does not mean that these books are not authoritative in themselves; rather, because of their conciseness, the mufti is not safe from falling into error if he restricts himself to them. The ruling of this category is that one should not issue fatwa from them except after deep consideration, thorough deliberation, and consulting their commentaries and glossaries. If the mufti becomes certain after that, then there is no harm in issuing fatwa from them. Allamah Ibn Abidin (may Allah have mercy on him) mentioned in Sharh Uqud Rasm al-Mufti that al-Durr al-Mukhtar and al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir contain omissions in transmission in many places, and preferences that contradict the preferred (rajih), even preferences of the view of others that none of the madhhab’s scholars held. Thus, these books are also included in the second category.

Commentary
This passage states a rule from the usul al-iftaa: a mufti must not rely solely on concise books like al-Durr al-Mukhtar and al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, despite their recognized authority. The reason is the risk of misunderstanding due to brevity. However, the books themselves remain authoritative; the caution is against using them in isolation. The mufti must refer to their commentaries and glossaries, such as Radd al-Muhtar for al-Durr al-Mukhtar. Ibn Abidin notes additional problems: textual omissions and incorrect tarjih (preference of less strong opinions), which also place them under the second category (books with errors). This emphasizes the need for thorough verification.

Key Terms
الاختصار المُخل (al-ikhtisar al-mukhkhil): Conciseness that impairs proper understanding of the ruling, leading to potential error in fatwa.

Relying on Rare and Depleted Books in Fatwa
Explains criteria for relying on rare or recently reprinted books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the criteria for relying on rare or depleted books when issuing a fatwa.

Core Idea
A rare book is not authoritative until its textual integrity is verified through strong evidence.

When issuing a fatwa, the mufti must ensure that the texts he relies on are authentic and free from distortion. This lesson addresses a common challenge: rare or depleted books that have been recently reprinted. You will learn the conditions under which such books can be trusted, based on the Hanafi tradition of textual criticism.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the permissibility of talfiq (piecing together rulings from different madhhabs) in issuing fatwas. It begins by critiquing the argument that the prohibition of talfiq is only from later scholars, showing that absence of explicit prohibition does not imply permissibility. The author then quotes from al-Tahrir wa al-Tahbir a detailed example: a person who follows al-Shafi'i on the non-obligation of rubbing (dalk) in wudu and Malik on the non-invalidation of wudu by touching without desire—if he performs wudu without rubbing and touches without desire, his prayer is invalid according to both. This illustrates the condition that talfiq must not lead to a combination that is invalid by consensus. Al-Ruyani is quoted setting three conditions for moving between madhhabs: avoid combining two opinions that contradict consensus, as in the example of marriage without dowry, guardian, or witnesses—a case no scholar has upheld. The passage then cites Ibn Nujaym's view that talfiq is valid and that Ibn al-Humam's prohibition is only attributed to later scholars and is not the Hanafi madhhab. Finally, the famous story of Abu Yusuf is presented as evidence: he used the opinion of the Medinese (that water of two qullahs does not become impure) after finding a mouse in his ghusl water, thereby performing talfiq between his own madhhab (Hanafi) and the Maliki view. The passage notes the story is well-known but its chain is unknown. The overall message is that talfiq is permissible under conditions, especially to avoid hardship, but must not violate scholarly consensus.

The Permissibility of Talfiq in Issuing Fatwas
Critical discussion of talfiq permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on talfiq and evaluate the strength of the evidence from the story of Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Talfiq is permissible in the Hanafi school, but the story of Abu Yusuf only proves following another mujtahid, not combining opinions.

This lesson examines the contested issue of talfiq—combining rulings from different madhhabs in a single fatwa. You will learn the arguments of major Hanafi scholars and critically assess the famous story of Imam Abu Yusuf, often cited as proof. Understanding this debate is essential for a mufti navigating cases where no single madhhab provides a complete ruling.

Guided Path
1
Define talfiq and the core question
Talfiq means combining opinions of two or more mujtahids to form a ruling not found in any one madhhab. The question is whether this is permissible for a mufti.
Contrast talfiq with simple taqlid of another madhhab.
2
Identify the Hanafi positions
Many later Hanafis (Ibn Nujaym, Ibn Mulla Farrukh, al-Tahtawi) permit talfiq; others (Shaykh Hasan, as attributed to Ibn al-Humam) prohibit or condition it.
Ibn Nujaym explicitly states the prohibition is not the madhhab.
3
Analyse the story of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf led Friday prayer, then learned his ghusl was from water containing a mouse. He ruled using the Shafi'i opinion on two qullas. Examine whether this constitutes talfiq.
The author argues it only shows following another mujtahid, not combining two opinions.
4
Evaluate the evidence for permissibility
Ibn Nujaym's statement and the story of Abu Yusuf are key proofs. Assess their strength: the story's chain is unknown, and it does not demonstrate talfiq.
The passage questions the reliability of the narration.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
Based on later authorities like Ibn Nujaym, the relied-upon view is permissibility of talfiq, provided it does not contradict a definitive text or consensus.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Adopting Another Madhhab in a Specific Case Does Not Require Full Adherence
Explains that adopting another madhhab in one case is permissible without embracing it fully.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a Hanafi mufti may adopt a ruling from another madhhab in one case without being required to follow that madhhab in all cases.

Core Idea
Choosing another madhhab in one case does not obligate full conversion — each case is judged independently.

This lesson addresses a common doubt among students of Hanafi fiqh: if a judge or mufti follows the opinion of another school on a specific issue—such as judgment against an absent person—must he then adhere to that school in all matters? The classical scholars, as cited by Ibn Abidin, make clear that the necessity is limited to that particular case. The principle is that adopting another madhhab in one matter does not bind one to that madhhab in other matters, because each issue is independently subject to ijtihad and maslaha.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Read the opening sentence: 'As for when a person chooses a saying contrary to his madhhab in a matter, he is not obliged to adhere to that madhhab in other matters as well.' This is the core rule.
2
Examine the Example: Judgment Against the Absent
Study the example of qada' ala al-gha'ib. Many later Hanafis adopted the majority view of the three other imams to avoid hardship and preserve rights. This is a case of necessity (darura).
Note that the Hanafi school itself has two narrations on this issue, but the later scholars preferred the permissive view.
3
Understand the Reasoning
Ibn Abidin quotes Jami' al-Fusulain: the judge may rule on an absent person if evidence is strong and no forgery, 'to remove hardship and necessities and preserve rights from loss.' This is the rationale.
The ruling is executed because it is a matter of ijtihad.
4
Apply the Principle: No Binding to Other Madhhab
The text then explicitly states: if the judge adopts the majority view on this issue, he is not required to follow them in all cases. Example: pre-emption for neighbor (shuf'a) is a separate issue; he may rule per Hanafi school on that.
The two issues are from different chapters of fiqh, so no talfiq occurs.
5
Confirm with Al-Hindiya
The passage from al-Hindiya reinforces that even if the judge's ruling on the absent person relies on a minority view within the Hanafi school (or even another school), his judgment is valid, and he can apply different ijtihad in other cases.

Commentary
This passage, from the discipline of Usul al-Iftaa, discusses the rules regarding issuing fatwa according to a madhhab other than one's own (known as 'al-iftā’ bi-madhhab ākhar'). The author, Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani, explains that choosing a different juristic opinion in one specific case does not obligate one to follow that madhhab in all other matters. He provides several examples to illustrate this principle. The first example, cited from Ibn Farukh, concerns the ruling of qaḍā’ ‘alā al-ghā’ib (judgment against an absent person). Many later Hanafis adopted the view of the three other major imams (Abu Hanifa being the fourth) permitting such a judgment when the judge sees a public benefit, even though the dominant Hanafi position is that it is generally not allowed. Ibn Abidin supports this by quoting from Jāmi‘ al-Fuṣūlayn and al-Fatḥ, noting that this is permissible to remove hardship and preserve rights, and it does not contradict the earlier restriction that a judge in certain times was bound by the Hanafi madhhab, because here the judge is acting out of necessity. The author then reinforces that if a Hanafi judge adopts the majority view in this issue, he is not required to follow the majority in all other cases, such as a case of pre-emption (shuf‘a) for a neighbor against an absent defendant—these are separate issues from different chapters of fiqh, so no prohibited talfiq (mixing of madhhabs) occurs. The passage then transitions to the concept of talfiq itself, citing a key text from al-Hindiya and the analysis of Shaykh al-Tahanawi. The correct ruling, according to al-Tahanawi, is that talfiq is impermissible only when it occurs within a single act of worship or transaction, resulting in a combination that no recognized madhhab accepts, thus contradicting ijmā‘ (consensus). For example, if a person performs wudu without observing the order of washing (invalid in the Shafi‘i school) and also wipes less than a quarter of the head (invalid in the Hanafi school), then his wudu is invalid according to all madhhabs—this is impermissible talfiq. However, talfiq across two separate acts, each valid within its own madhhab, is permissible. The passage ends mid-sentence with an example of combining an invalid wudu (from one madhhab) with a prayer behind an imam without reciting al-Fatiha (another issue), but the overall ruling is clear.

The Rule on Talfiq: Single Act vs. Separate Acts
Discussion on permissibility and prohibition of talfiq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on talfiq by distinguishing between impermissible talfiq (single act breaking ijma) and permissible talfiq (separate acts) and identify the five prohibited cases.

Core Idea
Talfiq in a single act that breaks consensus is prohibited; talfiq in separate acts is permissible.

Talfiq refers to combining rulings from different madhhabs. Many assume it is always forbidden, but the Hanafi school distinguishes based on whether the combination occurs in one act or multiple acts. This lesson unpacks that rule and the specific conditions that render talfiq prohibited, using examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Key Ruling
Locate the central statement by Shaykh al-Tahanawi: talfiq in a single act that breaks ijma is prohibited; in two different acts it is permitted.
This is the mufta bihi position in the passage.
2
Distinguish Single Act vs. Separate Acts
Understand the difference: a single act like wudu (all its parts are one act) vs. separate acts like wudu and prayer. Applying rulings from different madhhabs within one act may break ijma; across separate acts it is allowed.
The text gives the wudu example as prohibited and wudu+prayer as permissible.
3
List the Five Prohibited Cases
From the International Islamic Fiqh Academy resolution, there are five cases where talfiq is prohibited: (a) chasing licenses out of desire, (b) overturning a judicial decision, (c) reversing a previous taqlid in one incident, (d) contradicting ijma, (e) creating a composite state no mujtahid approves.
These conditions prevent misuse of talfiq.
4
Apply to Given Scenarios
Test each scenario from the furuat: e.g., combining Shafi'i wudu and Hanafi prayer is permissible; combining blood and touch rulings in one wudu is prohibited.
Use the qawida to decide.

Commentary
This passage addresses a key issue in usul al-ifta: talfiq, i.e., combining rulings from different madhhabs in a way that produces a ruling not recognized by any single madhhab. The author, Mufti Muhammad Taqi al-Uthmani, begins by stating that talfiq is not automatically forbidden. He gives an example: a judge ruling in absentia (Shafi'i view) and granting pre-emption to a neighbor (Hanafi view) are separate issues from different chapters, so combining them is permissible. He supports this with a quote from al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya about a judge accepting testimony of sinners and women in different cases—each is an independent ijtihad, so the judgment stands. 

Then he contrasts with a problematic talfiq: taking Shafi'i view that ablution is not invalidated by flowing blood and Hanafi view that it is not invalidated by touching a woman. Both relate to the same act (wudu), so the person would not have a valid wudu according to either madhhab, which is impermissible. This leads to quoting Shaykh al-Tahanawi, who states the most balanced view: talfiq is not allowed in a single act that breaks ijma, but allowed in different acts, even if apparent ijma is broken in the combination. He illustrates with two examples: (1) combining invalid wudu according to both madhhabs (breaking ijma) vs. (2) performing wudu according to Shafi'i and prayer without Fatiha according to Hanafi (two separate acts, so talfiq is allowed). 

The author then gives his own example: al-Tahanawi gave a fatwa combining Hanafi view on affinity and Maliki view on annulment for defect—different issues, so permissible. Finally, he cites the resolution of the International Islamic Fiqh Academy, which defines talfiq as combining rulings in a single interconnected issue such that no mujtahid holds that combination. It lists five prohibited cases: (a) following licenses out of desire, (b) overturning a judicial decision, (c) reversing a previous taqlid in one incident, (d) contradicting ijma, and (e) creating a composite state no mujtahid approves. These conditions ensure that talfiq is not used to circumvent the Shari'ah or produce invalid combinations.

When Does Fatwa Become Individually Obligatory?
Lists three conditions making fatwa individually obligatory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions that make issuing a fatwa individually obligatory (fard 'ayn) and apply them to determine when a qualified mufti must answer.

Core Idea
Fatwa is normally communal, but becomes individually obligatory when you are the only qualified mufti, when delay causes sin, or when appointed by the ruler.

This lesson examines the legal classification of fatwa in the Hanafi school. While the default is a communal obligation (fard kifaya), there are specific cases where a qualified mufti must personally respond. Understanding these conditions is crucial for a mufti to fulfill his duty without falling into sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Begin by noting the basic principle: fatwa is a communal obligation (fard kifaya) upon qualified muftis. This means if some fulfill it, the obligation is lifted from the rest.
2
Recognize the three exceptions
The passage lists three conditions that make fatwa individually obligatory (fard 'ayn): (1) the mufti is the only qualified person available; (2) the questioner is in urgent need and delay may cause sin; (3) the mufti has been officially appointed by the ruler.
3
Examine the evidence for each
For conditions 1 and 2, the passage cites Quran 2:159 (concealing knowledge). For condition 3, it cites Quran 4:59 (obedience to those in authority). Understand how each verse supports the obligation.
4
Consider the cross-madhhab nuance
The passage includes a quote from Imam Nawawi (Shafi'i) discussing whether a mufti sins if he refuses when asked directly. This shows that even outside the three conditions, there may be different views. For Hanafi practice, the three conditions are the decisive triggers.

Commentary
This passage is a rasm rule (methodological rule for muftis) presenting the legal classification of fatwa and the conditions under which it becomes individually obligatory. The author begins by stating that the default ruling (asl) is that fatwa is a communal obligation (fard kifaya) on qualified muftis: if enough qualified persons exist, it is sufficient that some fulfill the duty; it is not required of every individual. However, it becomes an individual obligation (fard 'ayn) in three specific cases. The first case is when the mufti is the only qualified person available and he knows the ruling; he must answer because concealing knowledge is prohibited by the Quranic verse from Surah al-Baqarah. The second case is when the questioner is in urgent need such that delaying the fatwa would likely cause him to fall into sin; again the same verse is cited. The third case is when the mufti has been officially appointed by the ruler (wali al-amr); obedience to those in authority is required by the Quran. The author then cites Imam Nawawi, who affirms the communal obligation and discusses the situation where a qualified mufti is asked directly but refuses. Nawawi presents two views: one that he sins, and one that he does not, with the more correct view being that he does not sin. This reflects the Shafi'i school; the Hanafi position may differ slightly, but the author includes it as a comparative point. The lesson emphasizes that fatwa is not a personal matter but a communal responsibility, and its obligation becomes personal only under these limited circumstances.

Key Terms
فرض كفاية (fard kifaya): A communal obligation; if some members of the community perform it, the obligation is lifted from the rest.
فرض عين (fard 'ayn): An individual obligation that every qualified person must personally fulfill.
مفت مؤهل (mufti mu'ahhal): A mufti who has met all the conditions of qualification (shurut al-mufti) and is competent to issue fatwas.
مستفتى (mustafti): The person who seeks a fatwa by asking a question to the mufti.

When is Fatwa Prohibited?
States a prohibition on qualified muftis when ignorant of specific ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition that prohibits a qualified mufti from issuing a fatwa and apply the rule of tawaqquf (withholding) when the ruling is unknown.

Core Idea
A qualified mufti must not give a ruling on a specific issue he does not know.

We previously studied the conditions a person must meet to be qualified as a mufti. But qualification alone does not grant license to answer every question. This lesson presents the first of several situations where even a qualified mufti must withhold from giving a fatwa: when he lacks specific knowledge of the issue at hand and cannot deduce the ruling. The Prophet's warning to judges who judge out of ignorance applies equally to muftis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibition scenario
The passage states one specific situation: when the mufti does not know the ruling of the particular question asked and cannot deduce it (istinbat), or when the evidences are confusing and he cannot prefer one over the other (tarjih).
This condition is about specific ignorance, not general qualification.
2
Trace the dalil (proof)
The author cites the hadith: 'The judges are three: one in Paradise, two in Hell... A man who judged for people out of ignorance is in Hell.' The author extends this ruling from qada to ifta because both involve issuing a religious ruling.
The analogy (qiyas) between qada and ifta is explicit in Hanafi usul.
3
Parse the ruling consequence
When this situation arises, the mufti must do tawaqquf (withhold) until the ruling becomes clear, or direct the questioner to another mufti who can answer.
Tawaqquf is not blameworthy; it is an act of responsibility.
4
Distinguish from general qualification
General qualification (ahliyyah) is necessary but not sufficient. Even a fully qualified mufti must not answer a question he does not know. The hadith warns against judging out of ignorance.
This prevents muftis from making guesses or giving rulings based on incomplete understanding.

Commentary
This passage is a rasm rule stating a prohibition on a qualified mufti. The author first reminds us of the earlier discussion on the conditions of qualification. He then asserts that even a qualified mufti is not allowed to answer in one specific situation: when he does not know the ruling of the particular question asked and cannot deduce it from the sources, or when the evidence (adilla) is ambiguous and he cannot make a tarjih (preference) between them. The proof is the hadith of the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam) about judges (qadat), dividing them into three categories. The hadith is narrated by Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi. The author applies the same ruling to fatwa because both qada' and ifta involve issuing a ruling on a legal issue. Therefore, the mufti must withhold (توقف) his answer until he clarifies the ruling or refer the questioner to another mufti. This is a critical principle: general qualification does not make it permissible to answer every question; one must have specific knowledge of the issue at hand. The lesson emphasizes the responsibility and caution required in issuing fatwas, guarding against ignorance and guesswork.

Key Terms
استنباط (istinbat): Deduction of the legal ruling from the primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, ijma, qiyas).
ترجيح (tarjih): Preferring one evidence over another when they appear conflicting, using established criteria of preference.
التوقف (al-tawaqquf): The act of refraining from issuing a fatwa until the ruling becomes clear.

When a Mufti Must Abstain from Fatwa
Rasm al-mufti rule on abstaining from fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the situations in which a Hanafi mufti should refrain from giving fatwa and apply the principle of preventing harm over bringing benefit.

Core Idea
A mufti is not obliged to answer every question; he must abstain if it causes fitna or is useless.

Fatwa is a grave responsibility. This passage teaches that a mufti must consider not only the correctness of his answer but also its consequences. He is not a mere dispenser of rulings; he must weigh benefit and harm. The classical Hanafi tradition, drawing from the Qur'an, hadith, and the practice of the Salaf, grants the mufti discretion to decline answering when harm outweighs benefit or when the question is frivolous. In this lesson, we will explore the specific conditions that require or permit a mufti to abstain, and understand the evidence behind this important rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mufti's internal states that impair judgment
The passage first lists emotional and physical conditions (anger, fear, extreme joy, hunger, illness, etc.) that prevent sound reasoning. In such states, the mufti must refrain until he returns to normal.
This is wajib (obligatory) because issuing fatwa with impaired judgment leads to error.
2
Recognize the general principle: no obligation to answer every question
The author states clearly that the mufti is not obliged to answer every question. He answers when there is benefit and safety from fitna.
This sets the stage for the two specific scenarios of abstention.
3
Analyze the first scenario: fear of fitna or corruption
If the mufti fears that the answer will cause strife, be misused, or lead to manifest harm, he must abstain. The evidence is the principle that preventing harm takes precedence over bringing benefit.
Quote from al-Ajuri on how to politely decline.
4
Analyze the second scenario: useless or hypothetical questions
If the question is about something that does not concern the questioner and has no practical benefit, the mufti should not encourage it. The evidence includes the hadith 'Destroyed are the excessive questioners' and the practice of the Companions and Imam Ahmad.
The mufti should redirect to beneficial knowledge.
5
Apply the distinction between obligation and discretion
Understand that the Hanafi position gives the mufti discretion, but this is not arbitrary. The mufti must have valid reasons (fear of fitna, irrelevance) and must act in the best interest of the questioner and community.
Compare with the opposing view that a mufti must answer if he knows the ruling.

Ruling on Retracting a Fatwa When Ijtihad Changes or a Muqallid's Error
Presents the rule for retraction of a fatwa when ijtihad changes or when a muqallid discovers his imam's actual view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule for retracting a fatwa in cases of changed ijtihad (mujtahid) or discovered error in imam's text (muqallid), distinguishing between the views of Ibn al-Qayyim and Ibn al-Salah.

Core Idea
A muqallid mufti must retract a fatwa if he becomes certain it contradicts his imam's text, but actions taken in good faith remain valid.

When a mufti issues a fatwa and later changes his mind, when should the previous fatwa be overturned? This passage from Usul al-Ifta lays down the rule for both mujtahid and muqallid muftis. For a mujtahid, change of ijtihad allows a new ruling, as demonstrated by Caliph 'Umar's practice. For a muqallid, the matter is more delicate: two great scholars, Ibn al-Qayyim and Ibn al-Salah, offer different answers depending on whether the fatwa contradicted the imam's clear text. Understanding this distinction protects the integrity of the madhhab and the mustafti's actions.

Guided Path
1
1. Differentiate the two mufti types
Categorize the mufti as mujtahid (independent) or muqallid (follower of a specific imam). The rule for retraction differs.
Mujtahid changes his ijtihad; muqallid discovers his imam's actual text.
2
2. State the rule for mujtahid with proof
The rule: a mujtahid can change his fatwa when his ijtihad changes. The proof is the narration from Caliph 'Umar who changed his ruling and said 'That was on what we judged, this is on what we judge.'
This is universally agreed upon.
3
3. For muqallid: identify the two views
Ibn al-Qayyim treats the muqallid's error like a mujtahid's change: no retraction of past actions. Ibn al-Salah requires retraction if the fatwa contradicts a clear text of the imam, regardless of ijtihad.
The difference hinges on the authority of the imam's text.
4
4. Apply the key condition: clear text of imam
Under Ibn al-Salah, the muqallid must be certain that his fatwa opposed a clear nass of his imam. If so, the fatwa is invalid and must be retracted. If not certain, the previous fatwa stands.
Certainty is crucial.
5
5. Account for the mustafti's knowledge
Even under Ibn al-Salah, if the mustafti did not know of the retraction, his actions remain valid. This protects those who relied on the fatwa in good faith.
The mufti must inform the mustafti promptly upon retraction.

Commentary
This passage addresses the rule of retracting a fatwa when the mufti's ijtihad changes or when a muqallid mufti discovers he was mistaken about his imam's actual position. The author begins by noting that scholars like al-Saymari, al-Khatib, and Abu 'Amr agree on a certain rule (likely the permissibility of changing a fatwa upon change of ijtihad), and there is no explicit disagreement from al-Ghazali or al-Razi. He then provides a proof from the narration of al-Hakam ibn Mas'ud, where Caliph 'Umar ibn al-Khattab changed his ruling on inheritance shares for siblings in a case where the mother's siblings and father's siblings were involved. When questioned about the inconsistency, 'Umar replied, 'That was on what we judged, and this is on what we judge now,' indicating that a mujtahid can change his ruling when his ijtihad changes. The passage then distinguishes between two scenarios: First, when the mufti is a mujtahid and his ijtihad changes, his previous fatwa can be superseded by the new one, as in 'Umar's case. Second, when the mufti is a muqallid (one who follows a specific imam) and he gives a fatwa thinking it represents his imam's view, but then discovers that his imam's actual madhhab is different, and his previous fatwa was not directly contradicting a clear text but rather was consistent with another mujtahid's opinion. Here, there is a disagreement between two later scholars: Ibn al-Qayyim (al-Jawziyya) holds that this situation is akin to the mujtahid's change of ijtihad; thus the previous fatwa is not overturned if the mustafti (the one seeking the fatwa) already acted upon it. For example, if someone married based on the fatwa and consummated the marriage, the marriage remains valid and the wife is not automatically prohibited; rather, a fresh legal proof would be needed to declare her forbidden. Ibn al-Qayyim emphasizes that merely the mufti's retraction does not invalidate past actions, especially if the retraction is due to realizing a mistake about his imam's madhhab, and the fatwa was nonetheless correct according to another madhhab. In contrast, Ibn al-Salah (al-Shafi'i) argues that if the muqallid mufti becomes absolutely certain that his fatwa opposed the clear text of his imam, then the fatwa must be overturned, even if the issue was a matter of ijtihad. The reason is that for a muqallid, the imam's textual pronouncements hold the same authority as the Lawgiver's texts for an independent mujtahid. However, he adds an important exception: if the mustafti was unaware of the mufti's retraction, his situation remains as it was before the retraction. This means that if he acted in good faith before knowing about the retraction, his actions (like marriage) are not invalidated. The passage thus provides a nuanced ruling that depends on the mufti's status (mujtahid vs. muqallid), the clarity of the imam's text, and the mustafti's knowledge of the retraction.

مستفتى (mustaftī): The person who seeks a fatwa from a mufti.
نص الإمام (nass al-imām): The explicit textual statement of the imam (e.g., Abu Hanifa) that constitutes the binding authority for a muqallid.

Commentary
This passage addresses the legal consequences of a mufti changing his fatwa or being mistaken. It begins with an anecdote from Umar's practice, showing that a mujtahid's ruling can change with ijtihad. The key distinction is between a mujtahid and a muqallid mufti. Al-Nawawi finds this problematic and suggests analogizing to the rules of deception (ghurur) or holding no liability because fatwa does not compel action. The Hanafi position would be to examine the specific circumstances, but the preferred view is that a qualified mufti is not liable for ijtihad error, though if he was reckless, he may be.

Liability and Duty to Inform upon Fatwa Retraction
Rules on informing mustafti and liability for mistaken fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the mufti's duty to inform the mustafti of retraction and apply the Hanafi rules on liability for mistaken fatwa.

Core Idea
The mufti must inform the mustafti of retraction; liability for error depends on qualifications and nature of the harm.

When a mufti realizes his fatwa was mistaken, he has a duty to inform the mustafti, even if he does not know who he is. The passage illustrates this with the example of Hasan al-Lu'lu'i. Moreover, if the fatwa causes harm, the question of liability arises: is the mufti financially responsible? The passage presents a dispute among scholars, with al-Nawawi critiquing the reported view and suggesting analogies from the law of deception (ghurur). This lesson unpacks these rules, essential for a mufti's professional conduct.

Guided Path
1
Understand the duty to inform
The mufti must inform the mustafti of his retraction, whether the mustafti has acted or not. If the mustafti is unknown, the mufti should make a public announcement.
The example of Hasan al-Lu'lu'i shows this duty in practice.
2
Analyze the liability rules
Ibn al-Salah reports two views: liability if the mufti is qualified, no liability if unqualified (due to mustafti's negligence). But al-Nawawi finds this problematic.
Al-Nawawi suggests deriving liability from the rules of ghurur (deception).
3
Consider the ghurur analogy
In ghurur, one who misleads another into a transaction may be liable. Al-Nawawi suggests this principle might apply to fatwas causing harm.
Alternatively, he posits no liability because fatwa does not compel action.
4
Apply the Hanafi position
In the Hanafi school, a qualified mufti is generally not liable for ijtihad error (al-mujtahid la yadman), but may be liable if he was grossly negligent or not qualified.
The mustafti's due diligence also matters.
5
Synthesize: duty and liability in practice
A mufti must diligently correct errors and inform affected parties. His liability depends on his qualifications and the nature of the error.
The lesson concludes with practical implications for professional muftis.

4. Ibn Nujaym (may Allah have mercy on him) said: If his soul (i.e., the soul of the mustafti) is not at peace with the answer of the mufti, he may ask another, but it is not obligatory. And Ibn al-Salah (may Allah have mercy on him) said: What the principles require is that we detail and say: When the mufti gives him a fatwa, he looks: if there is no other mufti, he must take his fatwa, and this does not depend on his commitment, neither in acting upon it nor otherwise, nor does it depend on his soul being at peace with its correctness in reality, for his duty is to follow (taqlid) as is known. And if there is another mufti, then if it becomes clear that the one who gave the fatwa is the more learned and reliable, he must follow his fatwa, based on the sounder view of being determined as before; and if it does not become clear, then he is not bound by his fatwa merely by his giving it, because it is permissible for him to seek a fatwa from another and follow him, and he does not know that they agree on the fatwa. Then if agreement is found or a judge rules against him, he must follow then. (3)

Commentary
This passage addresses the scenario where a mustafti's heart does not feel at ease with a mufti's answer. Ibn Nujaym states that he may ask another mufti, but it is not obligatory. Ibn al-Salah then elaborates: If no other mufti exists, the mustafti must accept the first fatwa even if his heart is not at peace, because taqlid does not require such peace. But if another mufti exists, then if it is clear the first is more learned and reliable, he must follow him. If not clear, he may seek another mufti. However, if the two muftis agree or a judge rules accordingly, then he is bound.

طمأنينة النفس (tuma'ninat al-nafs): Peace of mind or satisfaction with the correctness of the fatwa.
مُسْتَفْتِي (mustafti): The person seeking a fatwa from a mufti.

When to Renew a Fatwa for a Recurring Event
Deciding whether a mustafti must re-ask for a fatwa on a reappearing situation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule on renewing a fatwa for recurring events and identify when a new request is required based on the strength of the evidence.

Core Idea
A fatwa based on nass or ijma does not need renewal; otherwise, the mustafti must re-ask due to possible change of mufti's opinion.

This passage presents a practical dilemma: when the same situation occurs again, must the inquirer obtain a fresh fatwa? Two leading scholars, Ibn Nujaym and Ibn al-Salah, offer nuanced answers. Understanding their criteria helps a mufti advise correctly and a mustafti act with confidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Recurring Event
First, determine whether the incident that originally prompted the fatwa has occurred again. The passage specifically addresses events that are repeatable (not unique historical occurrences).
2
Assess the Basis of the Original Fatwa
Ibn Nujaym's rule: if the mufti's answer was based on a clear Quranic text (nass) or scholarly consensus (ijma), renewal is not necessary. Otherwise, the mustafti must renew the question. Identify whether the original fatwa relied on such authoritative sources.
The key phrase is 'إن لم يعلم استناد الجواب إلى نص أو إجماع'.
3
Consider the Two Views of Ibn al-Salah
Ibn al-Salah presents two positions: one mandates renewal due to possible change of the mufti's opinion; the other (stronger) does not require renewal because the ruling is already known and the mufti is presumed consistent. Recognize which view is preferred.
The stronger view is 'لا يلزمه، وهو الأصح'.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi (Operative Ruling)
Although both views are recorded, the preferable practice is that renewal is not required when the mustafti knows the ruling and the original answer was sound. However, if the answer was based on weak evidence or personal reasoning, renewal is prudent. This aligns with Ibn Nujaym's condition.
Ibn al-Salah's 'asahh' view combined with Ibn Nujaym's condition yields a balanced approach.
5
Apply to a Modern Scenario
Take a concrete situation, e.g., annual zakat on a fixed salary. If the mufti ruled based on clear Quranic evidence (obligation of zakat) and ijma, the mustafti need not ask again each year. If the fatwa relied on a specific scholarly opinion on nisab valuation, a new request may be wise due to possible changes.

Ibn Nujaym (may Allah have mercy on him) said: "And if he was given an answer about an event that does not recur, then it occurs (again), he must renew the question if he does not know that the answer is based on a text (nass) or consensus (ijma)." And Ibn al-Salah (may Allah have mercy on him) said: "If he seeks a fatwa and is given a fatwa, then that same incident happens to him again, must he renew the question? There are two views: one: he must, due to the possibility of the mufti changing his opinion. The second: he does not have to, and it is the sounder, because he already knows the ruling, and the default is that the mufti continues upon it."

Commentary
This passage addresses whether a mustafti must renew his request for a fatwa if the same event occurs again. Ibn Nujaym says that if the original answer was based on a clear text (Quran or hadith) or scholarly consensus, renewal is not required; otherwise it is required because the mufti's opinion might change. Ibn al-Salah presents two views: one deems renewal necessary due to possible change of opinion, but the stronger view is that renewal is not required because the ruling is already known and the mufti is presumed to be consistent.

Key Terms
نص (nass): Quran or hadith text that explicitly settles the ruling.
إجماع (ijma): Consensus of the scholars on a ruling.
الأصح (al-asahh): The sounder or more correct opinion.
