Verbal Custom and Its Effect on Legal Rulings
Explains verbal custom with examples and a dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that verbal custom can transform kinayah to sarih and evaluate the validity of distorted expressions in contracts based on custom.

Core Idea
Local custom can redefine legal words, making implicit expressions explicit.

The words we use in legal contracts and declarations derive their meaning from context and common usage. In Hanafi law, the concept of 'urf lafzi (verbal custom) allows a mufti to interpret utterances based on what they mean in the local community, not just their literal origin. This lesson explores how general and specific customs affect rulings, with vivid examples from divorce and marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of verbal custom
Read the passage and note the distinction between general custom (amm) that applies everywhere and specific custom (khass) that is local. Understand how each type influences the scope of the ruling.
General custom establishes a universal ruling; specific custom is limited to that locale.
2
Analyze the story of Banu Ribah
Study the narration about the man from Banu Ribah who obligated a badanah. Determine how local custom defined 'badanah' as only camels, and how that changed the ruling on what animal suffices.
This illustrates that the meaning of a word can be restricted by local usage.
3
Trace the transformation of a kinayah to sarih
Examine the example of a husband saying 'sarrihtuk' (I have released you). Understand that originally it is a kinayah requiring intention, but if custom uses it exclusively for divorce, it becomes sarih and divorce occurs without intention.
The same expression can have different legal effects depending on regional custom.
4
Evaluate the dispute over distorted words
Consider the marriage contract using 'tajweez' instead of 'tazweej'. Compare the early view (Taftazani) that it is pure mistake vs the later Hanafi view (Haskafi, Ibn Abidin) that if the community adopts it intentionally, it becomes a new convention and the contract is valid.
Note that the mufta bihi position is the later view, relying on custom.
5
Formulate the qawida
Extract the general principle: verbal custom is considered in legal speech. Write it as a maxim and note its rank, scope, and whether it has exceptions.
This qawida governs many rulings in contracts, divorce, and oaths.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Ifta discusses the role of verbal custom ('urf lafzi) in changing legal rulings. The author begins by stating that verbal custom is considered in people's speech. If the custom is general (amm), it establishes a ruling that applies to all lands; if it is specific (khass), the ruling is limited to the region where that custom is prevalent. This is a rule for muftis: they must take into account the local linguistic usage when interpreting words in contracts and utterances.

The author then cites al-Sarakhsi's commentary on al-Siyar al-Kabir to illustrate the principle with a story: a man from Banu Ribah obligated a badanah (sacrificial animal), and Ibn Umar (or Ibn Abbas, per the footnote) ruled that a cow would not suffice for him because in his tribe's custom, 'badanah' only referred to camels. This shows that local custom affects the meaning of words used in legal obligations. Based on this, many rulings in marriage, divorce, and oaths are derived.

Another example is the husband's statement 'sarrihtuk' (I have released you) to his wife. Originally, this is a kinayah (metaphorical expression) that does not effect divorce without intention. However, if in a particular region the custom is that this phrase is used exclusively for divorce, jurists treat it as sarih (explicit) and divorce occurs without the need for intention. If the custom does not exist in a place, the original ruling (kinayah) remains.

The passage then addresses a more technical issue: a marriage contract using a distorted word like 'tajweez' instead of 'tazweej'. Some jurists, following al-Taftazani, held that such a word is a pure mistake (ghalat) and cannot be considered a valid declaration because it is neither a literal nor metaphorical usage due to the lack of a relationship. However, al-Haskafi in al-Durr al-Mukhtar and many later jurists (including Ibn Abidin) argued that if a community adopts this pronunciation deliberately, it becomes a new linguistic convention (wad' jadid) and the contract is valid. This reflects a nuanced understanding of how custom can even legitimize linguistic innovations as long as they are used intentionally.

The footnotes provide the original source for the story (with a note that the narration goes through Ibn Abbas rather than Ibn Umar) and a reference to Radd al-Muhtar for the divorce example. This passage is a classic exposition of the principle that 'urf (custom) can change the legal status of utterances, and it is among the foundational rules for a mufti when issuing fatwas.

Key Terms
العرف اللفظي (al-'urf al-lafzi): Customary usage of words in speech; the meaning that a word has acquired in a particular community through repeated usage.
كناية (kinayah): A word or phrase that implies a meaning but is not explicit; used in divorce law where intention is required for effect.
صريح (sarih): An explicit expression that produces the legal effect without need for intention.
تصحيف (tashif): Distortion or misspelling of a word, often due to ignorance or dialectal variation.

Practical Custom and Its Conditions: The Qa'ida of 'Urf 'Amali
Defines practical custom and its conditions for being legally binding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that general custom can specify a general text (takhsis) and override qiyas, while identifying when a custom is rejected for contradicting a clear text.

Core Idea
General custom is legally binding unless it contradicts a clear text; then it is rejected.

In Hanafi fiqh, custom ('urf) plays a significant role in shaping legal rulings, especially in transactions. But not every custom is acceptable. This lesson, drawn from Ibn Abidin's Nashr al-'Urf, clarifies the conditions under which practical custom is considered valid and can override even analogical reasoning (qiyas).

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two categories of custom
Ibn Abidin divides custom based on its relationship to evidence (dalil): (1) custom that completely contradicts a clear text (nass) – rejected; (2) custom that does not completely contradict – may be accepted.
The dividing line is whether the custom forces abandonment of a nass.
2
Identify when custom is absolutely rejected
If a custom directly violates an explicit Quranic or hadith prohibition (like riba, wine, silk for men), it is rejected without doubt, regardless of how widespread it is.
Examples given in the text: riba, wine, silk/gold.
3
Identify when custom is accepted
If the evidence is general (amm) and custom restricts some of its instances, or if the evidence is based on qiyas, then a general custom is accepted. It acts as a mukhassis (specifier) or overrides qiyas.
The custom must be general (widespread and consistent).
4
Apply the rule: custom as mukhassis or qiyas-override
General custom can specify a general text (takhsis) and can lead to abandoning a qiyas-based ruling. This is a binding principle for fatwa.
The qawida: 'General custom can serve as a specifier and qiyas is abandoned due to it.'
5
Work through the furuat examples
Apply the rule to the three classic cases: istisna', entering a bathhouse, drinking from a water skin. In each, qiyas would require specific conditions, but custom allows validity.
These examples show the practical impact of the qawida.

Commentary
This passage addresses practical custom ('urf 'amali), also called 'dealings' (ta'amul) or 'habit' ('adah). The author states that such custom can indeed cause rulings to change, but not all customs are legally acceptable. Ibn Abidin is quoted to clarify the conditions. He divides customs into two categories in relation to evidence (dalil):

First, custom that directly contradicts a clear textual injunction (nass) without any room for reconciliation. This is absolutely rejected (mardud). Examples include people's widespread practice of riba (interest), drinking alcohol, wearing silk and gold (for men), etc. Since their prohibition is explicit in the Quran and Sunnah, no custom can override them.

Second, custom that does not contradict the evidence completely. This happens in two ways: (a) when the evidence is general ('amm) and the custom conflicts with some of its applications (afrad), and (b) when the evidence is based on qiyas (analogy). In both cases, if the custom is general (shamil) and widespread, it is accepted. For general custom can act as a mukhassis (specifier) for a general text, as noted in al-Tahrir, and it can override a ruling derived from qiyas. The classic examples are the validity of istisna' (a contract of manufacture or commission), entering a public bathhouse for a fixed fee without precise measurement, and drinking from a water skin (selling water without exact quantification). In these cases, qiyas would require strict measurement or specification, but custom allows the transaction to be valid based on mutual acceptance and prevailing practice.

Ibn Abidin concludes that after examining numerous cases built on practical custom, he reached a position (though the passage cuts off). The footnote references his work 'Nashr al-'Urf' in his collected treatises.

This passage is a key rasm al-mufti rule: when issuing fatwas, a mufti must distinguish between customs that are impermissible (because they violate nass) and those that are acceptable (because they specify a general text or abrogate a qiyasi ruling). The latter are binding only if they are general and consistent.

Key Terms
العرف العملي (al-'urf al-'amali): Practical custom or habitual practice in transactions and dealings, also called 'ta'amul' or 'adah'.
مخصص (mukhassis): Specifier; something that limits the application of a general text (amm) to exclude some of its potential instances.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning; extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on shared effective cause (illah).
استصناع (istisna'): A contract of manufacture where a buyer orders a seller to produce a specific item for a price.

New Linguistic Convention (Wad' Jadid) in Changing Rulings
Resolving whether a new intentional usage becomes a new linguistic convention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of wad' jadid to determine whether a new linguistic usage affects legal interpretation and identify the conditions required for validity.

Core Idea
A new linguistic convention is valid if a community intentionally adopts it.

This lesson examines a key rasm al-mufti rule: can a new linguistic usage alter the meaning of legal texts? Al-Taftazani rejected any unintentional distortion, but al-Haskafi and later Hanafi jurists accepted that intentional community agreement establishes a new convention (wad' jadid). Understanding this rule is crucial for muftis when common language shifts.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish error from convention
Identify the two categories: unintentional distortion (tahrif/tas'hif) vs. intentional community agreement. The former is disregarded, the latter can become a new wad'.
Error lacks intention; convention requires intention.
2
Identify the conditions for wad' jadid
From Haskafi's formulation: (1) a community agrees on pronouncing the word in a new way, (2) the utterance is intentional (bi qasd). Both must be present.
Agreement and intention are necessary.
3
Contrast the two scholarly positions
Taftazani holds that any departure from original usage without proper linguistic relation is error and invalid. Haskafi holds that if intentional and agreed, it constitutes a new convention (wad' jadid).
The dispute is over the effect of intentional community usage.
4
Determine the operative rule for fatwa
Later Hanafi jurists, as reported by Ibn Abidin, adopted Haskafi's view. Thus, the mufta bihi is that intentional community agreement creates a valid new wad'.
This is a rasm al-mufti rule.
5
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider a community that uses the word 'bay'' to mean 'rent'. If they intentionally agree on this usage, it becomes a new wad' and the legal ruling on contracts may change accordingly.
The change is only valid if the conditions are met.

Conditions for Practical Custom to Change Rulings
Explaining when practical custom can alter legal rulings and when it cannot

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Ibn 'Abidin's rule to distinguish when custom can affect rulings and when it cannot, and identify the types of rulings that change with custom.

Core Idea
Custom can change rulings only when it does not contradict definitive text and when the ruling itself was based on custom.

Practical custom ('urf 'amali) is a significant source in Hanafi fiqh, but not every widespread practice is automatically accepted. This lesson unpacks the precise conditions under which custom can alter legal rulings, drawing on Ibn 'Abidin's definitive statement. You will learn the critical distinction between rulings that are built on custom and those that are independent of it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the evidence type
Determine whether the rule in question is based on a definitive text (nass qat'i), a general text, or analogy (qiyas). This classification is pivotal for the next steps.
Definitive texts include clear Quranic prohibitions like riba and wine.
2
Check the extent of opposition
Ask: Does the custom oppose the evidence completely (necessitating abandoning the text) or only in some particulars? If complete opposition, the custom is rejected.
Complete opposition means the custom directly contradicts the core ruling.
3
Apply Ibn 'Abidin's rule
If the evidence is a general text or analogy, and the custom is general and widespread, then the custom is considered. It can specify the general text or override the analogy.
The custom must be 'general' ('amm) in the sense of being prevalent and not restricted.
4
Determine if the ruling is custom-dependent
Even if custom is not opposing evidence outright, check whether the original ruling was based on custom. Custom-dependent rulings change when custom changes; ruling-independent ones do not.
Example: the hadith about milking livestock was based on a custom of generosity.
5
Formulate the fatwa
Combine the above steps to decide whether to apply the custom or adhere to the original evidence. Document the reasoning.
The mufta bihi position is that general custom is a valid source as per Hanafi methodology.

Practical custom ('urf 'amali), which may be expressed as 'dealings' or 'habit,' can affect the change of rulings. But not every practice is considered in the Shari'ah. Ibn 'Abidin (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'If custom opposes the evidence, then if it opposes it completely such that it necessitates abandoning the text, there is no doubt in its rejection, like people's widespread practice of many prohibitions such as usury, drinking wine, wearing silk and gold, and other things whose prohibition has come in the text. Commentary
This passage presents a rasm rule for muftis: when and how practical custom ('urf 'amali) can affect legal rulings. The author begins by defining 'urf 'amali as customary practice or prevailing dealing. He then cites Ibn 'Abidin's distinction: (1) If custom completely contradicts a clear textual ruling (nass qat'i), it is rejected outright, as in the case of widespread riba, wine, etc. (2) If custom only partially opposes a general text or opposes an analogy, it is acceptable and can specify the general text or override the analogy, provided the custom is general. The author adds his own conclusion after surveying fiqh issues: rulings that depend on custom for their basis (i.e., the text was revealed considering prevailing custom) change when custom changes; rulings that are independent of custom (like permanent prohibitions) remain unchanged. He then gives the first type of changing rulings: when a text is based on custom in certain particulars, the ruling changes with custom because the 'illah (effective cause) ceases. Examples: the hadith of Samurah about milking livestock of others (permitted based on the custom of generosity and lack of commercial dealing) and the hadith about eating from a garden (permitted based on custom). These rulings were conditional upon the prevailing custom; when custom changes, the rulings may change.

Key Terms
العرف العملي (al-'urf al-'amali): Practical custom or common practice of people in their transactions and dealings.
التعامل (al-ta'amul): Mutual dealings or common practice (synonym for 'urf 'amali in this context).
العادة (al-'adah): Habit or custom; used interchangeably with 'urf.

Changing Rulings with Changing Customs: Kayl vs Wazn
Explains how legal rulings adapt to changing customs with Hanafi imam disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a change in custom (urf) can override a textual ruling and apply the Hanafi methodology to a new scenario.

Core Idea
If a text was built on prevailing custom, a change in custom can change the ruling.

This lesson explores the delicate balance between textual rulings and evolving customs. The passage presents a classic case where the Prophet's explicit requirement of equality in volume for certain exchanges is challenged by a later shift to weighing. Two great Hanafi imams differ on whether the text stands or custom prevails. Understanding this debate is essential for a mufti navigating the interaction of nass and urf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hadith Conflict
The passage begins with apparent contradictions: permissive hadiths (eating fruit, drinking milk without permission) versus prohibitive hadiths (no milking without permission). Recognize that the permissive hadiths were based on a customary tolerance that existed at that time.
This sets up the principle that rulings can change if the underlying custom changes.
2
Extract the Principle
The author states that the best reconciliation is that the permissive rulings were based on the custom of that era. Therefore, if custom changes, the ruling changes. This is the core principle of 'changing rulings with changing times'.
3
Analyze the Concrete Example
The example: wheat, barley, dates, and salt were measured by volume (kayl) in the Prophet's time. The text requires equality in kayl. Later custom changed to weight (wazn). Does the requirement change? This is the focus of the dispute.
Note that the subject matter is ribawi goods (same kind exchange).
4
Compare the Two Positions
Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad uphold the literal text: equality in volume is required. Imam Abu Yusuf follows the new custom: equality in weight suffices. Understand each side's reasoning: text vs custom, and Ibn al-Humam's defense of Abu Yusuf.
The dispute is about the manat (ratio legis): is it the text's wording or the custom that conditioned the text?
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, but Abu Yusuf's view is recognized as a valid alternative, especially when custom has universally changed. As a mufti, know both positions and when to apply each.
The fatwa hierarchy: stronger textual evidence, but custom can shift the application in practice.

... So I was brought to the Prophet (peace be upon him) and he said: O boy, why do you throw at the palm tree? He said: I eat. He said: Do not throw at the palm tree, and eat from what falls at its base. Then he wiped his head and said: O Allah, fill his stomach. The hadith scholars, especially Hafiz Ibn al-Qayyim (may Allah have mercy on him) in 'Tahdhib al-Sunan', have discussed at length the reconciliation between these texts, but the best opinion in explaining the permissive hadiths is that they were based on the custom of that time, since owners of livestock and orchards were tolerant of such actions by passersby and travelers, so there was a customary permission from them for what the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) allowed. Based on this, if the custom changes and there is no customary permission, the ruling changes. The views of jurists differ on whether the text was based on custom or was an independent ruling unrelated to custom and practice. Whoever holds that the determining factor is custom, the ruling according to him changes according to the prevailing practice, and whoever holds that it is an independent ruling, he gives fatwa that the text is followed by its wording, and the ruling does not change with change of custom. An example: wheat, barley, dates, and salt were among things measured by volume in the time of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him); they were bought and sold by volume. Then practice changed, and they became sold by weight, bought and sold by weight. The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) prescribed that when they are sold for their own kind (like for like), they must be equal in volume. When practice changed and they became sold by weight, is equality in volume considered as per the text, or equality in weight according to the new custom? This is also the school of al-Shafi'i and Ahmad (may Allah have mercy on them). Commentary
This passage discusses the principle that legal rulings may change with changing customs (تغير الأحكام بتغير الزمان). It begins by presenting hadiths where the Prophet (peace be upon him) permitted eating fruits and drinking milk without explicit permission of the owner. These appear to conflict with other hadiths that prohibit taking others' property without consent. The author notes that the best reconciliation is that the permissive rulings were based on the custom of the time where owners were lenient. Thus, if that custom changes, the ruling changes accordingly. The author then gives a concrete example: wheat, barley, dates, and salt were sold by volume (kayl) in the Prophet's time, but later became sold by weight (wazn). The textual ruling requires equality in volume for riba-free exchange. When the custom changed to weight, a dispute arose: Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad held that the text is stronger, so equality in volume is still required, following the literal wording. This is also the view of al-Shafi'i and Ahmad. Imam Abu Yusuf, however, held that the new custom should be considered, requiring equality in weight. Ibn al-Humam defended Abu Yusuf by arguing that the text itself was based on the custom of the time, and when custom changes, the ruling changes. The passage illustrates the methodological tension between adhering to the literal text and considering custom as a source of law.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that custom (ta'amul) can override qiyas and specify a text only when the text's 'illah is not definitively established, using the case of qafiz al-tahhan.

Core Idea
Custom can override qiyas only when the text's cause is uncertain; the prohibition of the miller's measure itself remains.

This lesson explores a key principle for muftis: the interaction between custom ('urf) and textual evidence. Using the disputed case of the miller's measure (qafiz al-tahhan), you will see how Hanafi scholars balanced a hadith prohibition against widespread custom, leading to a refined methodological rule. The author, Muhammad Taqi Usmani, clarifies the limits of this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual basis and its extension
Recognise that a hadith prohibited a specific transaction (miller’s measure). Early Hanafis and Shafi’is extended this prohibition to all analogous transactions via qiyas based on an inferred 'illah (the worker's wage being part of his product).
The hadith is reported by al-Daraqutni.
2
Locate the intra-madhhab divergence
Understand that later Hanafis (Balkh and Nasaf) permitted some analogous transactions (carrying, weaving) due to custom, while still upholding the original prohibition for grinding.
They argued: al-qiyas yutraku bi al-ta'aruf.
3
Extract the methodological claim
Note the claim that custom can abandon qiyas and specify the text (al-nass yukhsasu bi al-ta'aruf). This is a powerful tool for muftis.
But the author warns it is not absolute.
4
Evaluate the limitation
Apply the author's refinement: this principle applies only when the text's 'illah is not definitively established (lam tathbut bi al-qat'). In the miller's measure case, the 'illah was disputed (partnership vs. ignorance), so custom could tip the balance.
If the 'illah is certain, custom cannot override.

The answer depends on whether the 'illah (wage being part of product) is considered certain or debatable.

Commentary
This passage discusses a methodological principle for muftis: the role of custom ('urf) in legal reasoning. It opens the third scenario of how rulings change with time: when a textual ruling exists for a specific case, jurists extend it to similar cases via textual indication or analogy. If custom later develops in those similar cases contrary to the analogy, jurists may consider custom in those cases while leaving the original text's ruling intact. The key example is the hadith prohibiting 'qafiz al-tahhan' (miller's measure)—a transaction where a miller is paid with a portion of the flour he grinds. Early Hanafis and Shafi'is derived a general principle: the hired worker cannot benefit from his work because that makes him a partner, invalidating the hire. They thus prohibited all analogous transactions (e.g., weaving cloth for a portion). However, later Hanafi scholars from Balkh and Nasaf, citing the custom of their region, allowed some forms (carrying food for a portion, weaving cloth for a portion) while strictly following the text on grinding. They argued that custom can override analogy (al-qiyas yutraku bi al-ta'aruf) and can even specify a general text (al-nass yukhsasu bi al-ta'aruf). The author, Muhammad Taqi Usmani, refines this: the principle is not absolute. He explains that custom can override only when the 'illah (legal cause) of the text is not definitively established. In the miller's measure case, the 'illah was disputed—Hanafis/Shafi'is said the wage is created by the worker's action leading to partnership; Malikis/Hanbalis said it is ignorance of the wage. This uncertainty allowed custom to tip the balance. Thus, the mufti must assess the definitiveness of the 'illah before applying custom to override a text.

تعامل (ta'amul): Customary practice of the people; considered by later Hanafis to override qiyas and specify texts.
علة ('illah): The legal cause or rationale behind a ruling; here, debated for the miller's measure.

Change of Rulings with Change of Time
Explains the principle that fatwas change with changing customs and times.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'Rulings change with time' to identify when a mufti may issue a fatwa contrary to the transmitted text, and evaluate the conditions that justify such a change.

Core Idea
Custom-based rulings change with time; fixed texts and worship do not.

This passage from Ibn 'Abidin and earlier Hanafi authorities establishes a critical principle for muftis: the rulings of the Hanafi school are not frozen in time. Custom (urf) is a dynamic source, and what was once ruled based on a good custom may need to change when custom becomes corrupted. The passage illustrates this through three concrete examples: coercion (ikrah), liability of false informers (tadmeen al-sa'i), and the creditor's right to seize property of a different type (al-zufr). It then emphasizes that the mufti must have deep knowledge and training to properly apply this principle, distinguishing between customs that justify change and those that do not.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on a fundamental methodological principle: that fatwas can change with changing customs and times, and that the mufti must consider the realities of his era. The author begins by establishing that in the time of Imam Abu Hanifa, only the sultan had the power to coerce, so coercion (ikrah) from a non-sultan was not legally considered. When times changed and people became corrupt, coercion from non-sultans became common, so Imam Muhammad ruled that such coercion is legally effective. This became the relied-upon fatwa. Similarly, the issue of the informer (sāʿī): originally, a false informer is not liable because he is only a cause (sabab) and the sultan is the direct agent (mubashir). But to deter corruption, Imam Muhammad held him liable, and later scholars adopted that. Another example is the issue of "al-zufr" (seizing property): a creditor may take his due from any property of a procrastinating debtor, even if not of the same type, due to changed circumstances. Ibn 'Abidin notes that the earlier permissibility was based on people's compliance; today, due to widespread injustice, the fatwa permits taking from any property.

The author then highlights Ibn 'Abidin's treatise "Nashr al-'Urf", which collects many rulings built on custom. From this, the author deduces that a mufti cannot blindly follow transmitted texts without considering the time and people. Then he addresses the question: can a mufti follow a new custom that contradicts earlier textual rulings? He answers yes, following the practice of later Hanafi scholars who did so. However, the mufti must have sound judgment, knowledge of Shari'ah principles, and training under a master. The passage ends by emphasizing that mere memorization is insufficient; a mufti must apprentice to gain practical guidance in fatwa.

The principle of blocking the means to evil (sadd al-dhara'i) is a key tool in the mufti's reasoning. However, not all blocked means are equal. This lesson will dissect the two types of dhara'i as defined by Hanafi jurists and show how the second type provides the basis for changing fatwas when circumstances shift. Commentary
This passage is a rasm rule discussion on the principle of sadd al-dhara'i (blocking the means to evil) and its role in changing fatwas with changing times. The author first defines al-dhari'ah according to Ibn Rushd al-Jadd and al-Qurtubi, establishing that the means itself is not inherently prohibited but leads to something prohibited. The Quranic verse prohibiting insulting idols (6:108) is the foundational proof: insulting idols is not inherently wrong, but it leads to reciprocation against Allah. Then the passage divides dhara'i into two types. The first type: those blocked by explicit texts of the Shariah, such as the prohibition of riba al-fadl (barter-based riba) because it is a means to riba al-qard (loan-based riba). Once the Shariah prohibits such means, it becomes an independent rule; even if in a particular case it does not lead to the prohibited end, the ruling stands. The second type: means that are not explicitly blocked by texts, but the end (a prohibited act) is established as prohibited. This type allows for variation of rulings according to time and circumstance. The example given is the marriage to Kitabiyyat (Jewish/Christian women). Though the Quran expressly permits it (5:5), Umar (ra) prohibited it temporarily because he saw it led to social harms. He ordered Hudhayfah to divorce his Jewish wife. The Hanafi position, as quoted from Imam Muhammad, is that it is not haram (eternally forbidden), but it is discouraged (khilaf al-awla) and Muslim women should be preferred. Ibn al-Humam adds that it is permissible but better not to, and if the woman is from a belligerent (harbi) group, it is disliked by consensus. This illustrates that for the second type of dhara'i, a mufti may issue a fatwa contrary to the apparent text when the public interest so demands, but the original ruling remains valid in principle.

Changing Rulings and Blocking Means to Evil in Hanafi Fatwa
Examples of fatwa changes due to temporal circumstances in Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principles of taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-zaman and sadd al-dhara'i' to identify when a fatwa ruling may change due to changing circumstances.

Core Idea
Rulings change when circumstances change, to block means to evil — a living fiqh, not a static code.

This passage from Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani's Usul al-Ifta explains two fundamental tools for the mufti: the principle that rulings can change with time (taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-zaman), and the principle of blocking means to evil (sadd al-dhara'i'). Through historical examples — women attending mosques, marriage without guardian, apostasy, and bay' al-'ina — it shows how Hanafi jurists adapted rulings to prevent harm while remaining faithful to the Shari'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ruling and its condition
For each example, note the original ruling (e.g., women permitted to attend mosques) and the condition attached (e.g., when no fitna). This is the baseline from which change occurs.
The Prophet ﷺ conditioned permission on women going out unperfumed.
2
Recognize the change in circumstances
What new factor arose that made the original ruling problematic? Umar saw fitna; women manipulated marriage and apostasy laws; bay' al-'ina emerged as a riba trick.
Circumstances change over time and place.
3
Apply sadd al-dhara'i'
The mufti blocks the means (dhari'a) that lead to harm, even if the means itself is not intrinsically prohibited. This is a form of ijtihad.
Sadd al-dhara'i' is a recognized principle in Hanafi usul, especially in mu'amalat.
4
Consult the mufta bihi hierarchy
Later Hanafi scholars often adopt weaker narrations (nawadir) over the zahir al-riwayah when necessary to block evil, due to 'fasad al-zaman' (corruption of times).
E.g., Hasan ibn Ziyad's narration in marriage without guardian.
5
Construct the fatwa
Combine the above steps to issue a fatwa that addresses the new context while upholding Shari'ah objectives. The mufti must consider the outcome (ma'ala).
Modern parallels: online interactions leading to fitna.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Ifta wa Adabuhu by Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani expounds the principle of 'changing rulings with changing times' (taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-zaman) and its application through the legal maxim of 'blocking the means to evil' (sadd al-dhara'i'). The author begins with the case of women attending mosques. The Prophet ﷺ initially permitted it, but Umar رضي الله عنه later restricted it when he saw that it led to fitna. This is not a contradiction of the Sunnah but an application of its own principle: the Prophet himself conditioned the permission with the absence of fitna, so when fitna arose, the ruling changed. The author then gives two Hanafi examples. First, marriage without a guardian’s consent to a non-compatible husband: the original ruling (Zahir al-Riwayah) holds the marriage valid but subject to annulment by the guardian. However, later scholars (mutakkhirin) adopted the weaker narration of Hasan ibn Ziyad that it is invalid from the outset, due to 'corruption of the times' (fasad al-zaman). This is a fatwa ruling (mufta bihi). Second, the case of apostasy: originally it dissolves the marriage, but later Hanafis in Samarkand and Balkh ruled that the apostate woman remains married to block women using apostasy to escape marriage. These examples show that sadd al-dhara'i' is an ijtihadi tool; scholars differ on what constitutes a valid 'means to evil'. The passage ends with the example of bay' al-'ina (a sale with pre-arranged buyback to circumvent riba). Imam Malik absolutely prohibits it; Imam Shafi'i permits it as a valid sale; and Hanafis differ, with Imam Muhammad condemning it strongly. The lesson is: muftis must consider the temporal context and potential harms when applying the Shari'ah.

تغير الأحكام بتغير الزمان (taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-zaman): The principle that rulings can change due to changes in circumstances, customs, or needs, as long as the underlying text remains open to such change.
بيع العينة (bay' al-'ina): A sale in which a person sells an item on credit and then buys it back immediately for less cash, often used as a legal trick for riba.

Core Idea
A retracted fatwa requires undoing past actions only if it contradicted qat'i evidence.

This lesson examines a crucial rasm al-mufti rule: when a mufti retracts a fatwa, does the mustafti have to undo actions already taken? The answer depends on the nature of the evidence behind the fatwa. If the fatwa contradicted definitive (qat'i) proof, the mustafti must reverse his action. If it was a matter of ijtihad, the action remains valid because ijtihad does not invalidate ijtihad. Understanding this distinction protects both the mufti's integrity and the mustafti's stability.

Endorsing Another Mufti's Fatwa: Conditions and Etiquette
Rule on endorsing another mufti's fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rasm al-mufti rule for endorsing another mufti's fatwa by evaluating the original mufti's qualifications, the correctness of the answer, and the soundness of the evidence.

Core Idea
A mufti may only endorse another mufti's fatwa if the original mufti is qualified, the answer is correct, and the evidence is sound.

When a fatwa from another mufti is presented for endorsement, the responsibility is serious. You must not simply approve the answer; you must first examine the original mufti's qualifications, the correctness of the ruling, and the strength of the evidence. This lesson walks through the detailed decision tree that safeguards the integrity of the fatwa process.

Guided Path
1
Assess the original mufti's qualifications
Determine whether the first mufti is fit for ifta (yaliq bil-ifta). If he lacks knowledge or piety, he is not qualified.
This is the first gate: if unqualified, you must not endorse.
2
If unqualified, write your own answer
Even if the answer itself is correct, you must not endorse it. Write your own answer separately to avoid giving credibility to an unqualified mufti.
Al-Qarafi warns that endorsing an unqualified mufti promotes his action, which is a great calamity.
3
If qualified, check the answer's correctness
If the original mufti is qualified, then evaluate whether the answer is correct in your opinion. If not correct, write your own answer separately.
4
If answer correct, check the evidence
If the answer is correct, then examine the evidence (dalil) cited by the first mufti. If the evidence is unsound or needs correction, write your own answer with the corrected evidence.
Do not endorse if the evidence is flawed, even if the conclusion is correct.
5
If evidence also correct, endorse
If both the answer and the evidence are sound in your view, you may write 'sahih' (correct) and sign. For a more senior mufti, write 'kadhalika jawabi' (my answer is like that) as a sign of humility.
This is the only scenario where endorsement is permitted.

Rules for the Mustafti When Muftis Differ
Rulings on seeking fatwa from multiple muftis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for selecting a mufti when fatwas conflict, and construct a fatwa hierarchy that prioritizes knowledge (a‘lam) over piety (awra‘) when they differ.

Core Idea
When muftis differ, the mustafti must prefer the more knowledgeable (a‘lam); if equal, the more scrupulous (awra‘) may be considered.

You have a legal question, but two qualified muftis give you opposite answers. Whom do you follow? This passage from a classical Hanafi work on ifta etiquette lays out the precise steps. It is not about judging which mufti is 'better' in general, but about a rational hierarchy: knowledge comes first, then piety, and if all else fails, precaution or free choice. Let's unpack the established rule and the secondary views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mustafti's duty before asking
The mustafti must verify the mufti's qualification and integrity. This can be through personal knowledge, trustworthy report, or widespread acceptance. He is not required to seek out the most learned scholar in the region.
Apparent integrity (zahir al-‘adala) suffices if the mufti's inner integrity is hidden.
2
Determine the primary criterion when two muftis disagree
The default is to prefer the mufti who is both more knowledgeable (a‘lam) and more scrupulous (awra‘) in the mustafti's view. If one excels in knowledge and the other in piety, the correct view (al-sahih) per Ibn Nujaym is to prefer the more knowledgeable.
Knowledge is the basis for correct fatwa; piety alone does not ensure accuracy.
3
Apply the secondary criteria when the primary is inconclusive
If both muftis are equal in knowledge and piety, the mustafti should seek a third mufti and follow the one who agrees with another. If that is not possible and the difference is between prohibition (hazr) and permissibility (ibaha), the mustafti should choose prohibition (ihtiyat). In rare cases where all efforts fail, free choice may be allowed.
This ladder is the author's chosen view (al-mukhtar).
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i faces and understand the Hanafi position
Imam Ibn al-Salah listed five faces: strictest, easiest, most trustworthy (awthaq – aligns with Hanafi), seek third mufti, free choice. Al-Nawawi criticized the ladder and favored free choice. The Hanafi view, however, trusts the mustafti to investigate and provides a clear hierarchy.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi requires active investigation; Shafi'i gives more discretion.

Commentary
This passage, from the chapter on the rulings of ifta and its method, addresses the rules for the mustafti (the one seeking a fatwa). It begins with fundamental guidelines: the mustafti must ensure the mufti is qualified, either through personal knowledge, trustworthy report, or widespread acceptance. Procedural Rule for the Mustafti with Conflicting Fatwas
Procedural rule for mustafti with conflicting fatwas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi procedure for a mustafti when muftis give conflicting fatwas, including seeking the most reliable mufti, consulting a third, and choosing caution when necessary.

Core Idea
When muftis differ, seek the most reliable; if equal, ask a third; only choose or take caution when all else fails.

This passage addresses a practical dilemma: a layperson receives two conflicting fatwas. What should he do? The author presents five opinions before settling on the preferred Hanafi view, which emphasizes hierarchy, caution, and limited choice. Understanding this procedure is essential for proper istifta' and ensures the mustafti fulfills his duty.
