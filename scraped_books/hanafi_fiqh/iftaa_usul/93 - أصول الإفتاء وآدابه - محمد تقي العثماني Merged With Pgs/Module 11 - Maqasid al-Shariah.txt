The Proper Role of Maqasid al-Shari'ah
Explains the relationship between Maqasid and textual rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on Maqasid al-Shari'ah and distinguish between a benefit recognized by Shari'ah and one based on desire.

Core Idea
Maqasid explain rulings, but Shari'ah alone determines true benefit.

Maqasid al-Shari'ah (objectives of Islamic law) are often misunderstood as independent sources that can override clear texts. This passage, drawn from a classical usul work, clarifies that Maqasid are meant to explain the wisdom behind rulings, not to replace them. The authority to define benefit rests solely with the Lawgiver, not human reason or desire.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's purpose
Read the first part: scholars wrote books on Maqasid to explain the benefits behind rulings, not to treat those benefits as independent rulings. The purpose is to show that every ruling has a benefit, and to consider Maqasid in permissible matters and where no text exists.
Distinguish between explanatory role and legislative role.
2
Define Maqasid and Maslaha
Maqasid are objectives like preservation of life, wealth, and honor. Maslaha is benefit, but the passage emphasizes that benefit is relative, not absolute, as per al-Shatibi.
Relativity means benefit can vary by situation, person, or time.
3
Apply the principle of Shari'ah as arbiter
The passage states that the judge of benefit is the Shari'ah and its texts, not bare intellect or desire. Therefore, any claimed benefit that contradicts a clear text is rejected as a mere whim.
This is a core Hanafi principle: no overriding of nass by maslaha.
4
Analyze the quote from al-Shatibi
Al-Shatibi says benefits and harms are relative (idafiyyah), not absolute. This reinforces that a thing may be beneficial in one context but harmful in another. Only divine legislation can determine true benefit.
This quote is used to support the Hanafi cautious approach.

مقاصد الشريعة
وقد ألف جماعة من العلماء كتباً في بيان مصالح الأحكام الشرعية، وبيان مناط مقاصدها، وليس غرضُهم أن تكون هذه المقاصد والمصالح هي الأحكام الشرعيّة دائماً بقطع النظر عن النصوص الشرعية، بل مقصودهم بيان المصالح لما جاء في النصوص من الأحكام، حتّى يتبيّن أن الشريعة لم تشرع حكماً إلا ووراءه مصلحة للعباد في الدنيا أو الآحرة، وأن تؤخذ هذه المقاصد في عين الاعتبار في المباحات، وفي الأمور التي ليس فيها نص شرعي. ولكن الحاكم في كونه مصلحةً هو الشرع ونصوصه، دون العقل المجرّد أو أهواء النّفوس. وذلك لأنّ هذه المقاصد، مثل الحفاظ على النفس والمال والعرض ليست مطلوبة مطلقاً وفي جميع الأحوال، بل الحق، كما قال الشاطبي رحمه الله تعالى: "أن المنافع والمضار عامتها أن تكون إضافية، لاحقيقية. ومعنى كونها إضافية أنها منافع أو مضار فى حالٍ دون حال، وبالنسبة إلى شخص دون شخص أو وقت دون وقت." فالذى يحكُم فى أمرٍ أنّه منفعة أو مضرةٌ هو شرع الله عز وجل، فالمصلحة الظاهرة التى تُعارِض نصاً من نصوص الشرع ليست مصلحة ولا منفعة في الحقيقة، وإنما هو وليد هوى النفوس الذى جاءت الشريعة لإبطال اتباعه.
Objectives of the Shari'ah
And a group of scholars have authored books explaining the benefits of legal rulings and explaining the basis of their objectives. Their purpose is not that these objectives and benefits become the legal rulings themselves, always disregarding the legal texts. Rather, their purpose is to explain the benefits that come in the texts of the rulings, so that it becomes clear that the Shari'ah did not legislate a ruling except that behind it is a benefit for the servants in this world or the Hereafter, and that these objectives are taken into consideration in permissible matters and in matters where there is no explicit text. But the judge of something being a benefit is the Shari'ah and its texts, not the bare intellect or the whims of souls. This is because these objectives, such as preserving life, wealth, and honor, are not required absolutely and in all circumstances. Rather, the truth is, as al-Shatibi (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'Benefits and harms, most of them, are relative, not absolute. The meaning of being relative is that they are benefits or harms in some situations but not others, and relative to some persons but not others, or at some times but not others.' So the one who judges a matter to be a benefit or harm is the Shari'ah of Allah Almighty. Thus, an apparent benefit that contradicts a text of the Shari'ah is not a benefit or advantage in reality; rather, it is a product of the whims of the soul which the Shari'ah came to abolish following it.

Commentary
This passage introduces the topic of Maqasid al-Shari'ah (objectives of Islamic law). The author clarifies that works on Maqasid aim to explain the benefits behind rulings, not to treat those benefits as independent rulings overriding texts. Maqasid are considered in permissible matters and where no text exists, but the arbiter of benefit is the Shari'ah itself, not human reason or desire. The author quotes Imam al-Shatibi, stating that benefits and harms are relative, not absolute. Therefore, any claimed benefit that contradicts a clear text is not a true benefit but a product of desire.

Key Terms
مقاصد الشريعة (maqasid al-shari'ah): The objectives and purposes of Islamic law, such as preservation of life, wealth, and honor.
مصلحة (maslaha): Benefit or interest; in this passage, it is defined as relative and determined by Shari'ah, not by human reason or desire.
