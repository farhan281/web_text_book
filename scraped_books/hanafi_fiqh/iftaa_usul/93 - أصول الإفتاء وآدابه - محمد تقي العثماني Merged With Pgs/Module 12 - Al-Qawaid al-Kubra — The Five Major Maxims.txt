Custom is Authoritative: The Maxim and Its Application in Fatwa
Introducing the maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' and the concept of 'urf in fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' to identify when a change in custom justifies a change in a legal ruling, and to state the conditions that must be met for custom to be authoritative.

Core Idea
When the legal cause is custom, the ruling changes with it.

The Hanafi school recognizes custom ('urf) as a valid source for legal rulings, especially in transactional matters. This lesson introduces the foundational maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' and explains how a change in custom can lead to a change in the ruling. Understanding this principle is essential for a mufti dealing with contemporary issues.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its role
State the Arabic maxim 'al-'adah muhakkamah' and recognize it as one of the five major maxims of fiqh.
The maxim summarizes that custom governs in the absence of clear textual evidence.
2
Define 'urf
Define 'urf linguistically and technically, noting its derivation from 'knowledge' and its meaning of commonly accepted custom.
Imam al-Ghazali's definition is quoted but cut off; understand the linguistic root.
3
Identify the condition for ruling change
Recognize that for custom to change a ruling, the 'illa (legal cause) of the ruling must be built upon custom, not on a clear text.
The source states: 'قد تكون علّة الحكم مبنيّة على العُرف' – the cause may be based on custom.
4
Apply the maxim to a scenario
Apply the maxim to a simple scenario, e.g., change in currency value affecting zakat rulings, or a community's practice in marriage gifts.
The custom must be general, consistent, and not contradictory to Shari'ah.

Change of Ruling Due to Change of Custom (al-'Urf)
Defining 'urf, its types, and the principle that custom prevails over linguistic meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the types of 'urf and apply the principle that custom overrides linguistic meaning when issuing a fatwa.

Core Idea
A mufti's ruling on changed custom is not a contradiction of the text but an application to a new context.

This passage introduces one of the foundational maxims of Hanafi fatwa: custom is authoritative. As a mufti, you must understand that the ruling of a revealed text is confined to the customary meaning at the time of revelation. When customs change, the same text does not automatically apply to the new meaning. This lesson will guide you through the definition, types, and practical implications of 'urf.

Guided Path
1
Define 'urf and distinguish it from 'adah
Start by reading the definitions of 'urf from al-Ghazali and Ibn al-Humam. Note that 'urf is what is accepted by sound nature and recurs without rational connection.
The linguistic root is 'ma'rifa' (knowledge).
2
Classify 'urf into types
Identify the two classifications: general vs. special (based on scope) and verbal vs. practical (based on subject). Understand that verbal custom concerns word usage.
Practical custom is also called 'ta'amul'.
3
Understand the conflict between language and custom
When a word's linguistic meaning differs from its customary meaning, custom takes precedence. This is the basis for the maxim 'al-‘ādatu muḥakkamah'.
This applies only when there is no explicit text (nass) that contradicts the custom.
4
Analyze the ruqba example
Examine the hadiths about ruqba. The Prophet's ruling was based on the customary meaning of ruqba at that time. If later custom changes, the hadith does not cover the new practice.
This illustrates that the mufti does not abandon the text but rules on something the text did not address.
5
Apply the principle to a mufti's fatwa
A mufti today must be aware of current customs. If a term's custom has changed, he may issue a fatwa according to the new custom without violating the original nass.
This prevents the mufti from being seen as contradicting the text.

Later changes in the meaning or practice of ruqba would be outside the scope of those texts.

How Changing Custom Alters Legal Rulings: The Example of Ruqba
Explaining how changing 'urf alters legal rulings with ruqba example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'Rulings change with changing times' to analyze how a shift in customary meaning can lead to a valid change in fatwa without contradicting the original textual evidence.

Core Idea
Custom can override linguistic meaning; a change in custom can change the ruling while the text remains the same.

This lesson explores the Hanafi legal maxim 'Rulings change with changing times' through the concrete example of ruqba (a form of gift). The passage explains how a shift in customary meaning can lead to a fatwa that appears to contradict a hadith but in fact applies to a different scenario. Understanding this principle is crucial for muftis dealing with changing customs in modern contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim and Its Scope
Begin by recognizing the maxim 'Rulings change with changing times' (تغير الأحكام بتغير الزمان). Note that it governs cases where custom is a factor, especially verbal custom.
The passage also defines custom ('urf) and distinguishes between verbal and practical custom.
2
Analyze the Ruqba Case in Two Eras
Examine the meaning of ruqba in the Prophet's time: a completed gift with a condition of return if the donee dies first. Then examine its meaning in Abu Hanifa's time: a gift contingent on the donor's death (suspended gift).
The legal effect of the condition differs drastically between the two eras.
3
Understand Why Abu Hanifa's Ruling Does Not Contradict the Hadith
The Prophet's hadith addressed the earlier meaning (completed gift with a void condition). By Abu Hanifa's time, the custom had changed, so the hadith no longer applied. His ruling was on a different transaction.
The passage emphasizes that the text (hadith) was not abandoned; it simply never covered the new custom.
4
Generalize the Principle
Whenever a verbal custom changes, the ruling based on that custom may change. A mufti must assess the current customary meaning before applying a text.
This is a core skill in iftaa: distinguishing between what the text explicitly covers and what falls under new customary usage.

Changing of rulings with changing of time
What is settled in souls from the aspect of speech, and sound natures receive it with acceptance. And Ibn al-Humam said: 'Custom is the recurring matter without an intellectual connection.' (1)
And if custom is limited to a group of people or to the people of a particular land, then it is called a specific custom. And if it encompasses all people and lands, then it is called a general custom.
Then custom is of two types: verbal custom and practical custom, which is called 'transactional custom'. We will mention the rulings of each of the two types, and Allah the Exalted is the grantor of success.
Verbal Custom
As for verbal custom, it means the use of a word or speech with a particular meaning that may differ from its linguistic meaning. And whenever there is a conflict between language and custom, custom is given precedence. If a text comes with a word according to its customary meaning known at the time of the text's revelation, the ruling is restricted to that meaning. If its customary meaning changes after that, the text does not apply to it. And the jurist may issue a fatwa according to its customary meaning that has changed in his era, so the observer might think outwardly that he gave a fatwa contrary to the text, or that he abandoned the text for custom, but in reality he did not abandon the text, nor did he give a fatwa contrary to it; rather, he gave a ruling on something that the text did not cover. An example of this is what was narrated by Jabir (may Allah be pleased with him) from the noble Prophet (peace be upon him) regarding al-ruqba, that he said: "Al-ruqba is for the one to whom it is given." And Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with them both) narrated that he (peace be upon him) said: "Al-ruqba and al-umra are not permissible."
(1) Mentioned by Ibn Abidin (may Allah have mercy on him) in his treatise Nashr al-Urf. Rasa'il Ibn Abidin (2:122)

"So whoever is given something as a life grant (umra), it is his, and whoever is given something as a reversionary gift (ruqba), it is his." The summary is that if someone says to another: "My house is for you as ruqba," then that is a completed gift, and the house becomes gifted to him forever according to the known conditions of gift. Therefore, the majority held that ruqba is like umra, so it is valid as a gift. And it is narrated from Imam Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him) that ruqba is invalid, meaning that this speech has no legal effect, so the house remains owned by the giver. The apparent view of this fatwa is that it contradicts the text, but in reality, the ruqba that Imam Abu Hanifa declared invalid is different from the ruqba that the noble Prophet (peace be upon him) executed as a gift. This is because ruqba in the time of the noble Prophet (peace be upon him) meant that it was a completed gift with the condition that if the donee dies before the donor, then the donated property returns to the donor. This condition is invalid, so the gift is valid and the condition is void because a gift is not invalidated by void conditions; rather, the condition becomes void. Therefore, he (peace be upon him) said: "Whoever gives something as ruqba, it is his." Commentary
This passage is a legal-philosophical discussion on the maxim 'Rulings change with changing times' (تغير الأحكام بتغير الزمان). It begins by defining 'urf (custom) as what the mind accepts and sound nature receives, and quotes Ibn al-Humam: 'Custom is a recurring matter without intellectual connection.' If the customary meaning changes later, the text no longer applies to the new meaning. A jurist may then give a fatwa based on the new custom, and an observer might think the fatwa contradicts the text, but in reality the text never addressed that new meaning.

The key example is the term 'ruqba' (الرقبى). In the Prophet's time, ruqba was a completed gift with a condition that the property returns to the donor if the donee dies first. The Prophet confirmed that such a gift is valid; the condition is void but the gift stands. The majority of scholars follow this. Imam Abu Hanifa, however, ruled ruqba as invalid. At first glance, this seems to contradict the hadith. But the author explains that the meaning of ruqba had changed by Abu Hanifa's era: it came to mean a gift contingent on the donor's death (a suspended gift). Since a gift cannot be suspended, Abu Hanifa ruled it invalid. Thus, his ruling did not contradict the text; the text applied to a different meaning. This illustrates how a change in customary meaning can lead to a different ruling without violating the original text.

The Sixth Principle: Rulings Change with Changing Conditions
Examines sixth principle: rulings based on general conditions change accordingly

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A ruling based on general conditions changes when those conditions change — the definition of coercion expands as society evolves.

This lesson examines the sixth principle in Hanafi legal philosophy: rulings that are built upon the general conditions of people are subject to change when those conditions transform. The classical example is the concept of legal coercion (ikrāh). Imam Abu Hanifa restricted valid coercion to the sultan, while his student Imam Muhammad extended it to any effective coercer. Al-Marghinani explains that this is not a doctrinal conflict but a reflection of changing times. This principle is essential for muftis to adapt rulings to evolving social realities.

Guided Path

Understand the example of ikrah

Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical difference: under Abu Hanifa's view, contracts made under non-state threats might be valid; under Imam Muhammad's, they are invalid. Recognize that later fatwa adopts Muhammad's view.

Principles of Issuing Fatwas
First: that there be fear for life or limb. Second: that the necessity be present, not awaited, meaning that fear of destruction or loss occurs by overwhelming probability based on experience, not mere imagination. Third: that there be no other means to repel harm from permissible things, and it is predominantly thought by the afflicted that repelling harm is expected by committing some forbidden things. Fourth: that the prohibited thing not cause similar harm to others, so it is not permitted to kill anyone, even in a state of compelling coercion. And when such necessity is realized, the afflicted is permitted to commit specified prohibitions to the extent of repelling the necessity, like the hungry person in dire need who fears destruction for himself; it is permissible for him to eat carrion or pork to the extent that repels destruction. This is the intended meaning of the famous maxim: "Necessities permit prohibitions." And what Allamah Khalid al-Atasi (may Allah have mercy on him) mentioned under this maxim summarizes the different states of necessities and their rulings. We quote it here verbatim. He (may Allah Most High have mercy on him) said: "Then this license is of three types: A type that is permissible, like eating carrion, blood, and pork, and drinking wine during famine, choking, thirst, or under complete coercion by killing or cutting a limb. These things are permitted when in dire need due to His statement: ﴿إِلَّا مَا اضْطُرِرْتُمْ إِلَيْهِ ﴾ [al-An'am: 119], i.e., severe famine compelled you to eat them. The exception from prohibition is permission (Bada'i'). And just as dire necessity is realized by famine, it is realized by coercion, so consumption is permitted, and refraining is not permitted, so if he refrained until he died or was killed, he is held accountable, because by refraining he became one casting himself into destruction, and that has been prohibited. And if the coercion is incomplete, like imprisonment or beating from which destruction is not feared, it is not permissible for him to do it.
And a type whose prohibition is not removed under any circumstance, but concession is given in it: like destroying a Muslim's wealth and slandering his honor, and uttering a word of disbelief on his tongue while his heart is at peace with faith, if the coercion is complete. It is in itself forbidden with the establishment of the concession, so the effect of the concession is in changing the legal consequence of the act, which is punishment, not in changing its description, which is prohibition. And refraining from it is better, so if he refrained and was killed, he is rewarded.
And a type that is neither permitted nor given concession at all, neither under complete coercion nor otherwise, like killing a Muslim, or cutting a limb of his without right, and adultery, and beating parents.
If you know this, then this maxim (i.e., "Necessities permit prohibitions") does not apply to the last type, because it is not permitted under any circumstance. Rather, it applies to the first type with its permission established, and to the second with its prohibition remaining, and the concession is only in lifting sin, like a doctor's looking at what is not legally permissible to be uncovered of a patient or wounded person; concession is in lifting sin, not prohibition. And like the dire need to eat the wealth of another during hunger, it does not remove the prohibition of another's wealth as will come in Article 33, rather it removes sin, and he must guarantee it or seek permission from its owner.

As for need, it is the motivator whose non-fulfillment results in distress, difficulty, hardship, and trouble, even if that hardship does not lead to destruction of life or wealth. Then need is of two types: general need and specific need.

Commentary
This passage discusses the famous qawida (legal maxim) "الضرورات تبيح المحظورات" (Necessities permit prohibitions). The author, Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani, first outlines the conditions for a genuine necessity: fear for life or limb, the necessity must be present not anticipated, no other permissible means, and the prohibited act must not cause similar harm to others. Then he cites Allamah Khalid al-Atasi's classification of licenses (rukhsah) under necessity into three types. The first type is where the prohibition is lifted entirely, making the act permissible (mubah), such as eating carrion when starving. The second type is where the prohibition remains but the sin is removed (rukhsah), like forced apostasy with a tranquil heart; the act is still haram but the person is not punished if he does it under compulsion. The third type is where no license applies at all, such as murder, zina, or beating parents. The maxim only applies to the first two types. The passage also distinguishes between darurah (necessity) and hajah (need), defining hajah as a situation causing hardship but not destruction. Hajah is divided into general and specific. This is a core principle of Hanafi fiqh and is widely applied in fatwa, especially in issues of medical necessity, coercion, and times of famine.

Key Terms
ضرورة (darurah): A situation where there is fear of destruction of life or limb, or severe harm, requiring immediate action.
حاجة (hajah): A need that, if unmet, leads to hardship and difficulty but not destruction of life or wealth.
رخصة (rukhsah): A concession granted by law, allowing an otherwise prohibited act under necessity or need.
إكراه ملجئ (ikrah mulji'): Compelling coercion that threatens life or limb.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A ruling based on a clear text can change when its application leads to certain harm—blocking the means is not overruling the text.

This passage explores the principle of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking the means to evil) as a tool for a mufti when times change. Using two classic examples—marriage to Kitabiyya and women attending mosques—the author shows how the Companions and later scholars modified rulings to prevent fitna. As a mufti, you must understand that a ruling's application is context-sensitive and that preventing harm may override a general permission. This lesson will train you to identify when to apply this principle and how to justify it within Hanafi usul.

Guided Path

Locate the original ruling

Check if the change is supported by the practice of the salaf (e.g., Umar's prohibition of women's attendance) and later jurists (e.g., dislike of marriage to Kitabiyya by the four madhhabs).
A mufti must cite precedent.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the principle of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking the means to evil) and how it justifies changes in rulings when times change. The author first addresses the ruling on marrying Kitabiyya (Jewish/Christian women). Although the Quran permits it, later scholars—including the four madhhabs—considered it makruh (disliked) based on Umar's directive, due to the risk of fitna. The author emphasizes that in contemporary times, when Muslims are politically and culturally weak, the fitna is even greater. The second example is women attending mosques. The Prophet allowed it, but Umar prohibited it when he saw that it led to fitna. Aisha supported this by saying the Prophet would have done the same. The author notes that the Prophet's permission was conditional on no fitna and women going unperfumed. The third example, from the Hanafi school, is a woman marrying without a wali's consent in a non-compatible match (ghair kuf'). The default ruling is that the marriage is valid, but the wali has the right to object. This too is an application of sadd al-dhara'i'. The passage teaches the mufti that rulings are not static; they must consider the maqasid and potential harms in changing contexts.

Key Terms
سد الذرائع (sadd al-dhara'i'): Blocking the means that lead to evil or harm, used as a principle to derive rulings or change them when circumstances necessitate.
الكتابية (al-Kitabiyya): A woman from the People of the Book (Jews or Christians) whom a Muslim man may marry according to the Quran.
الكفؤ (al-kuf'): Compatibility in marriage factors such as lineage, wealth, piety, and profession, relevant for the validity of marriage without wali's consent.
الولي (al-wali): The marriage guardian, typically the woman's father or closest male relative, whose consent is required in certain marriage situations.
