Second Rule: Rulings Change When the Cause Is Negated by Custom in Specific Cases
Presents a sub-rule about rulings changing when the underlying cause is negated by custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the sub-rule that custom can negate the illa (cause) of a ruling in specific cases, identify the practical examples where this occurs, and construct a fatwa based on this principle.

Core Idea
Custom can negate the cause of a prohibition in specific cases, changing the ruling for those cases only.

This lesson presents a key methodological rule for muftis: when a textual ruling is based on a specific cause (illa), and that cause is effectively negated by widespread customary practice in some particular cases, the ruling changes only for those cases. The general ruling remains unchanged. Two classical examples illustrate the principle: entering a public bath for a fee and buying a drink of water from a water-carrier. Both involve uncertainty (gharar), yet Hanafi jurists permitted them due to custom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling and its cause
Start by recognizing the general prohibition (e.g., gharar in contracts) and the underlying cause (uncertainty leading to dispute). This is the baseline ruling.
The cause must be clearly identifiable from the text or reasoning.
2
Survey the customary practice
Determine whether the custom (urf) is widespread and unopposed in a particular subset of cases. The custom must be general (am) in those cases, not just occasional.
Custom can be a legitimate source of law in Hanafi fiqh when it is consistent and not conflicting with a clear text.
3
Verify that the cause is negated
Assess whether the custom effectively eliminates the harm (darar) that the cause was meant to prevent. If the uncertainty becomes negligible due to common practice, the cause is negated.
Negation is specific to those cases only; the general cause remains valid elsewhere.
4
Apply the exception to the particular cases
Issue the fatwa that the ruling is changed (from prohibited to permissible) only for those cases where the custom negates the cause. The general rule remains intact.
This is not a broad license; it is a restricted exception.

Commentary
This passage presents a second methodological rule: when a textual ruling is based on a cause (illa), and that cause is negated by customary practice in some specific instances (not all), the ruling changes for those instances. The author provides two examples: entering a public bath for a fee and buying a drink of water from a water-carrier. In both cases, the conventional ruling is prohibition due to gharar (uncertainty) because the quantity is unknown. However, jurists permitted them because people commonly engage in these transactions, and the underlying cause of prohibition (gharar) is considered negligible in these customary practices. The text cuts off before completing the reasoning, but the principle is clear: custom can negate the cause in particular cases, leading to a change in the ruling for those cases only.

Key Terms
علة (illa): The effective cause or rationale behind a ruling; the attribute on which the ruling is based.
غرر (gharar): Uncertainty or risk in a transaction, typically prohibited.
جزئيات (juz'iyyat): Particular cases or instances as opposed to the general rule.
