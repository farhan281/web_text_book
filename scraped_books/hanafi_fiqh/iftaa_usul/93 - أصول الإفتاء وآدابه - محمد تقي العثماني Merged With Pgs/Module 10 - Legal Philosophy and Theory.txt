Why the Prophet Did Not Codify Fiqh
Explains absence of formal fiqh terminology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Shah Waliullah's argument for the absence of formal fiqh terminology in the Prophetic era and identify the evidence he uses to support his view.

Core Idea
The Prophet taught through practice, not terminology; later categories are systematic descriptions, not innovations.

Shah Waliullah al-Dihlawi, a seminal Hanafi legal philosopher, argues that fiqh was not a codified discipline during the Prophet's lifetime. The Companions learned directly from observing his actions, intuitively grasping what was obligatory, recommended, or merely permissible. In this lesson, we dissect his reasoning and its implications for Hanafi usul al-fiqh and the practice of iftaa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's main claim
Read the first sentence: the Prophet did not codify fiqh. Understand that this means no systematic categorization of rulings into pillars, conditions, etiquettes existed.
2
Contrast the Prophetic method with later jurists
The Prophet demonstrated through action; the Companions emulated without explicit labels. Later jurists 'explain with utmost effort' by defining each element distinctly.
The contrast highlights the shift from organic learning to systematic study.
3
Interpret the footnote's clarification
The footnote defends later jurists: they did not invent categories arbitrarily. The concepts (e.g., essential vs. recommended) were known from the Prophet's practice via contextual clues (qara'in).
Shah Waliullah is not criticizing; he is explaining historical development.
4
Apply to Hanafi methodology
Reflect on how this insight validates the Hanafi reliance on sunnah fi'liyyah and taqriri. The school's categories are grounded in evidence, not mere speculation.
This aligns with the Hanafi emphasis on deriving rulings from the Prophet's actions and tacit approvals.

Commentary
This passage is a legal philosophy excerpt from Shah Waliullah al-Dihlawi's Hujjat Allah al-Baligha, addressing why the Prophet did not formalize fiqh terminology. The author's point is that the early Muslims learned from the Prophet's actions directly; they understood from context what was obligatory, what was recommended, etc., without needing explicit labels like 'pillar' or 'condition.' This lesson is crucial for understanding the organic evolution of Hanafi fiqh: the school's methodology relies on deriving rulings from the actions and tacit approvals of the Prophet, not merely from codified texts.

Key Terms

آداب (ādāb): Recommended manners or etiquettes that enhance the act but are not required.

The True Meaning of 'Ra'y' in Early Fatwa Methodology
Historical and methodological clarification of the term 'ra'y'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'ra'y' as used by the Salaf, cite its proof from the hadith of Mu'adh and the letter of 'Umar, and correct the misconception that it refers to personal opinion or is exclusive to Hanafis.

Core Idea
Ra'y = disciplined analogical reasoning (qiyas) from texts, not subjective opinion.

The term 'ra'y' (opinion) is often misunderstood as arbitrary personal reasoning. In this passage, Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani clarifies that in early Islamic jurisprudence, 'ra'y' specifically meant analogical deduction (qiyas) grounded in the Qur'an and Sunnah. He supports this with the Prophet's approval of Mu'adh's ijtihad and Caliph 'Umar's instructions to Abu Musa, and he refutes the notion that 'as'hab al-ra'y' were only Hanafis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common misconception about 'ra'y'
Read the opening sentence: many people think 'ra'y' means personal opinion based on pure reason. Recognize that this is what the author is correcting.
2
Analyze the primary proof: the hadith of Mu'adh
The author traces the term to Mu'adh's statement 'I will exert my ra'y' (ajtahidu ra'y). Understand that the Prophet's approval establishes that 'ra'y' here means ijtihad through qiyas, not whim.
This hadith is the basis for the authority of ijtihad.
3
Examine the secondary proof: the letter of 'Umar to Abu Musa
The letter instructs the judge to 'recognize the parallels and similarities, then analogize (qis) the matters.' This explicitly defines 'ra'y' as analogical reasoning.
4
Note the critique of faulty qiyas from Abu Hanifa
Imam Abu Hanifa said urinating in the mosque is better than some people's qiyas. Realize that this condemns invalid analogy, not the method itself—showing that valid qiyas must be sound.
This proves that 'as'hab al-ra'y' were not careless with texts.
5
Recognize the broad application of the term 'as'hab al-ra'y'
The author lists many early jurists (e.g., Malik, Sufyan al-Thawri, Awza'i) as people of ra'y. Conclude that the label was not exclusive to Hanafis but included all who engaged in qiyas.
This corrects a common historical misconception.

Many people are deceived by the word 'ra'y' and claim that it means personal opinions based on pure reason. This is not the case. The word 'ra'y' in this nomenclature is taken from the hadith of Mu'adh (may Allah be pleased with him), which is the basis for the authority of ijtihad, in which he said: 'I will exert my ra'y.' What is meant by it is analogical reasoning (qiyas) of an unstated case to a stated one, as appears from the letter of 'Umar to Abu Musa (may Allah be pleased with them both) recorded by al-Bayhaqi from Idris al-Awdi who said: Sa'id ibn Abi Burda brought out a book to us and said: This is the letter of 'Umar to Abu Musa... Then he mentioned the hadith, and in it: 'Understand what arises in your heart regarding matters not reaching you in the Quran and Sunnah. Then recognize the parallels and similarities, and then analogize the matters accordingly. Aim for what is most beloved to Allah and most similar to what you see.' Far be it from the people of ra'y to advance their personal opinion over the texts of the Quran and Sunnah. Waki' narrated from Imam Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him), who was renowned as one of the foremost people of ra'y, that he said: 'Urinating in the mosque is better than some of their analogies.' And al-Hafiz Ibn 'Abd al-Barr (may Allah have mercy on him) narrated from al-Hasan ibn Salih who said: 'Al-Nu'man ibn Thabit was intelligent, knowledgeable, and firm in his knowledge. When a report from the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) was authenticated to him, he did not turn to anything else.' Similarly, the claim of some people that the people of ra'y are only the Hanafis is incorrect. This label was used for all jurists who devoted themselves to deriving legal rulings or delved deeply into the subdivision of particulars. It was also used for Maliki jurists. For this reason, al-Hafiz Ibn 'Abd al-Barr al-Maliki (may Allah have mercy on him) named his commentary on al-Muwatta' al-Istidhkar lima Tadammanahu al-Muwatta' min Ma'ani al-Ra'y wa al-Athar. And Ibn Qutayba (may Allah have mercy on him) devoted a chapter in his book al-Ma'arif to mention the people of ra'y, counting among them Ibn Abi Layla, Abu Hanifa, Rabi'a al-Ra'y, Zufar, al-Awza'i, Sufyan al-Thawri, Malik ibn Anas, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad ibn al-Hasan (may Allah have mercy on them all).

Commentary
This passage is a historical and methodological clarification defending the term 'ra'y' (opinion) in early Islamic jurisprudence. The author, Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani, begins by noting a common misconception: that 'ra'y' refers to subjective personal opinion devoid of textual basis. He refutes this by tracing the term to the hadith of Mu'adh ibn Jabal, where the Prophet (peace be upon him) approved Mu'adh's statement that he would exert his 'ra'y'—understood here as qiyas (analogical reasoning) grounded in the Qur'an and Sunnah. The author then cites the famous letter of Caliph 'Umar to Abu Musa al-Ash'ari, which instructs the judge to use analogy by identifying parallels and preferring what is most beloved to Allah. This proves that 'ra'y' was not arbitrary but a disciplined method. Far from prioritizing personal opinion over texts, the early jurists (the 'people of ra'y') held the texts supreme. To illustrate, the author quotes Imam Abu Hanifa's stark statement that urinating in a mosque is better than some people's analogical reasoning—a hyperbolic critique of faulty qiyas, not a rejection of the method itself. He also quotes al-Hasan ibn Salih praising Abu Hanifa's reliance on authenticated hadith. The passage then addresses another misconception: that 'people of ra'y' refers exclusively to Hanafis. The author shows that the label was applied broadly to many early jurists, including Malikis and others. He provides evidence: Ibn 'Abd al-Barr's commentary title includes 'ra'y', and Ibn Qutayba lists many scholars as 'people of ra'y', such as Ibn Abi Layla, Rabi'a al-Ra'y, Sufyan al-Thawri, and Malik ibn Anas, alongside Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Thus, the term originally denoted any jurist who engaged in ijtihad and qiyas, not a specific school. This reframes the Hanafi legacy as part of a broader tradition of textual reasoning.

Key Terms
الرأي (al-ra'y): In this passage, it refers to disciplined analogical reasoning (qiyas) from revealed texts, not personal opinion.
أصحاب الرأي (as'hab al-ra'y): Jurists who extensively used qiyas and deduction, including many early scholars not limited to Hanafis.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical deduction from a textual ruling to a new case sharing the same effective cause (illa).

Why Laypeople Cannot Pick Rulings After Madhhab Codification
Arguing why laity must follow one madhhab after codification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi reasoning for requiring laypeople to follow one madhhab after codification and identify the harm of talfiq (eclecticism) for non-mujtahids.

Core Idea
After codification, allowing laypeople to pick rulings leads to following desires, not evidence.

This passage from Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani's Usul al-Ifta addresses a foundational principle: why laypeople must follow a single madhhab after the schools were systematically recorded. It argues that before codification, people could seek fatwa from different scholars without harm, but after widespread knowledge of madhhab positions, picking and choosing leads to ittiba' al-hawa (following desires). Understanding this reasoning is essential for muftis who deal with laypeople in a multi-madhhab environment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the historical shift
The author distinguishes the period before and after 'tadwin al-madhahib' (codification of the madhhabs). Before, fatwa-seeking was flexible; after, the madhhabs became systematized and well-known.
This shift is the basis for the change in ruling.
2
Understand the rationale for mujtahids
Mujtahids choose opinions based on the strength of evidence, not personal desire. Therefore, their selection is legitimate and evidence-based.
The author emphasizes that 'each of these fuqaha only chose a statement based on the strength of its evidence according to him'.
3
Recognize the layperson's limitation
A layperson (al-'ammi) cannot compare evidences or evaluate the strength of proofs. They lack the ability to perform ijtihad or even comparative analysis.
The definition of 'ammi is central: one who cannot derive rulings from evidence.
4
See the danger of talfiq for laypeople
If laypeople are allowed to pick any ruling from any madhhab, they will choose what suits their desires, not what is supported by evidence. This undermines the Sharia.
The author warns: 'it is feared that he would take from these statements what agrees with his desire, not because of any legal evidence'.
5
Conclude the necessity of taqlid
Therefore, to preserve the integrity of Islamic law, laypeople must adhere to a recognized madhhab consistently. Taqlid of one madhhab becomes obligatory for them.
This is the operative ruling in Hanafi usul al-ifta.

Commentary
This passage presents the justification for adherence to a single madhhab (iltizam) after the codification of the four schools. The author explains that before codification, people could seek fatwa from different scholars without fear of talfiq (eclecticism) because they did not know the positions in advance. However, once the madhhabs were systematically recorded and became widely known, allowing laypeople to pick and choose rulings would lead to following personal desires rather than divine law. The key point is that the mujtahid's choice is based on evidence, but the layperson lacks the ability to evaluate evidence. Therefore, to prevent abuse, the layperson must follow a recognized madhhab consistently. This reasoning is foundational to the Hanafi principle of taqlid for non-mujtahids.

The Obligation of Madhhab Taqlid in Later Times
Historical and legal argument for obligatory madhhab adherence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the historical reasoning why adherence to one of the four madhhabs became obligatory, and apply the principle that prerequisites of the obligatory become obligatory when only one path remains.

Core Idea
Taqlid to a specific madhhab became obligatory when only the four schools remained reliably transmitted.

This passage presents a historical and legal argument for why Muslims today must adhere to one of the four Sunni madhhabs. The author cites four major scholars—Ibn Taymiyyah, al-Nawawi, Ibn Khaldun, and Shah Wali Allah al-Dihlawi—to show that while taqlid was not required in early Islam, later circumstances made it not only permissible but necessary. Understanding this evolution is crucial for a mufti to appreciate the scope and limits of ijtihad in the contemporary context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary Shar'i obligation
The original obligation is that the ummah must have access to the rulings of Shari'ah from their detailed evidences. This is an obligation on the community as a whole (fard kifayah).
2
Recognize the prerequisite (muqaddimah al-wajib)
A prerequisite of fulfilling that obligation is following a reliable path to those rulings. Since a necessary condition is itself obligatory (al-muqaddimah al-wajibah), one must take a trustworthy method.
Legal maxim: ما لا يتم الواجب إلا به فهو واجب
3
Understand the historical contingency
In the first two centuries, many mujtahids existed and their schools were actively taught. Multiple reliable paths were available, so no specific path was individually obligatory.
4
Apply the principle of restricted paths
When reliable ijtihad became scarce and only the four madhhabs were preserved and transmitted thoroughly, the only viable path became adherence to one of them. What was not obligatory before now becomes obligatory.
Shah Wali Allah's analogy: if multiple roads exist, any one suffices; but if only one road remains, that specific road must be taken.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence for the mufti
A mufti today must operate within the framework of the four madhhabs, and for Hanafis, within the dominant opinion in the school. Claiming absolute ijtihad is rejected as impractical and dangerous.

Commentary
This passage is a historical and legal analysis of the evolution of taqlid (imitation) in Islamic law, specifically addressing why adherence to one of the four Sunni madhhabs became necessary. The author, Muhammad Taqi al-Uthmani, presents arguments from four major scholars to demonstrate that while taqlid was not obligatory in the early centuries of Islam, it became obligatory later due to practical and methodological exigencies.

The passage begins with a citation from Ibn Taymiyyah, who warns against allowing unrestricted following of any school, as it would lead to picking and choosing rulings according to whim, undermining religious seriousness. Imam al-Nawawi then provides elaborate reasoning: if one could follow any school arbitrarily, he would select the easiest option in each case, effectively dissolving the obligation of Islamic law. He argues that in earlier times, when ijtihad was widespread, such flexibility was acceptable, but once the schools were refined and fixed, one must commit to a specific madhhab.

Ibn Khaldun's social-historical analysis explains that taqlid became confined to the four imams because of the increasing technicality of sciences and lack of qualified mujtahids. The scholars of the later periods, fearing chaos, declared ijtihad inaccessible and restricted valid fiqh to the transmitted schools. Shah Wali Allah al-Dihlawi adds a nuanced legal argument: the obligation to know the Shari'ah is primary, and its prerequisite—following a reliable path—becomes obligatory. When multiple paths (i.e., mujtahids) were available, no specific one was required; but when only the four madhhabs remained reliably transmitted, commitment to one became necessary.

The last part emphasizes the unique preservation of the four madhhabs: their thorough codification, continuous transmission, and widespread acceptance. This divinely ordained circumstance made them the only viable options for later Muslims, restricting ijtihad practically while preserving the essence of Shari'ah.

All examples and reasoning are based on the Hanafi framework implicitly, as the work is Hanafi, but the principles apply across the Sunni legal tradition.

Key Terms
تقليد (taqlid): Following a qualified mujtahid's opinion in legal matters without demanding direct evidence.
التمذهب (al-tamadhhub): Adhering consistently to a specific madhhab's rulings.
اجتهاد (ijtihad): Independent legal reasoning from primary sources to derive rulings.

The Permissibility of Adopting Rulings from Other Madhhabs within the Hanafi School
Explains the legitimacy of taqlid and the conditions for inter-madhhab adoption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi view on taqlid and identify the conditions under which a Hanafi scholar may adopt a ruling from another madhhab.

Core Idea
Taqlid is a practical necessity, but a qualified scholar may adopt another madhhab's ruling based on strong evidence, not caprice.

This passage by Muhammad Taqi Usmani clarifies a crucial principle for muftis and scholars: while non-mujtahids must follow a madhhab due to inability to derive rulings directly, a deeply learned Hanafi jurist may, in specific cases, issue a fatwa according to another madhhab if strong evidence supports it. The author supports this with historical examples and the rationale that adherence to an imam is not a legislative ruling but a practical measure to maintain order and prevent whims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of taqlid
Recognize that the ultimate objective is to follow the Quran and Sunnah. Taqlid is necessary for non-mujtahids who cannot derive rulings due to textual complexity or apparent contradictions.
The passage lists three reasons: inability to understand, multiple meanings, and apparent contradiction of evidences.
2
Distinguish taqlid for laymen vs. scholars
Laymen must rely on a trusted mujtahid or a recognized madhhab. However, a highly knowledgeable scholar (mutabahhir) who can weigh evidences may depart from his school in specific cases.
The scholar must have 'nazar fi adillah al-ahkam' (insight into the evidences).
3
Identify the condition for adopting another madhhab
The adoption must be based on strong evidence (dalil qawi) that has become apparent to the scholar, not on personal desire (tashahhi).
The passage explicitly prohibits tashahhi and requires evidence-based selection.
4
Review the examples and the underlying principle
Hanafi jurists gave fatwas contrary to Imam Abu Hanifa in cases like muzara'a (sharecropping), ijarah for teaching Quran, and khiyar al-maghbun (option of fraud). The reason is that taqlid of a specific imam is not a legal ruling itself but a fatwa of expediency to organize religious affairs and avoid chaos.
The author cites his father, Mufti Muhammad Shafi, as authority for this principle.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological discussion on the nature of taqlid (following a madhhab) and the permissibility of adopting rulings from other madhhabs within the Hanafi school. The author, Muhammad Taqi Usmani, begins by reaffirming that the ultimate goal of Islamic law is to follow the rulings of the Quran and Sunnah. For non-mujtahids, direct derivation is impractical due to inability to understand, multiple interpretations, or apparent contradictions among texts. Hence, they must rely on a trusted mujtahid or a known madhhab; this is called tamadhdhub or personal taqlid. The author then qualifies that a scholar who is mutabahhir (deeply learned and capable of weighing evidence) may, in a particular issue, adopt a ruling from another madhhab, provided he does so based on strong evidence—not whim or caprice. He supports this by citing numerous Hanafi jurists who gave fatwas contrary to Imam Abu Hanifa in specific cases, such as muzara'a (sharecropping leases), ijarah for teaching the Quran, and khiyar al-maghbun (the right to rescind a sale due to gross overpayment). The reason, as stated by Hanafi scholars, is that commitment to a specific imam is not inherently a binding legal obligation; it is a practical measure to maintain order and prevent religious manipulation and personal whims. The author concludes by noting that he heard this very principle from his father, the prominent Hanafi mufti Muhammad Shafi.

التمذهب (al-tamadhdhub): Following a specific Islamic legal school (madhhab) consistently in one's practice and fatwa
التقليد الشخصي (al-taqlid al-shakhsi): The practice of a non-mujtahid relying on the ijtihad of a specific imam, as opposed to non-personal taqlid of any qualified scholar
التشهي (al-tashahhi): Choosing a ruling based on personal desire or whim, without evidence

Personal Taqlid: Practical Necessity, Not Religious Obligation
Legal-philosophical discussion on taqlid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why personal taqlid (taqlid shakhsi) is not a legal obligation but a practical organizational necessity, and identify the conditions under which a scholar may engage in non-personal taqlid (taqlid ghayr shakhsi).

Core Idea
Personal taqlid is a necessity for order, not a divine command; qualified scholars may follow stronger evidence if secure from desire and public confusion.

This lesson examines the Hanafi view on taqlid. Many people mistakenly believe that adhering to a specific madhhab is an obligatory duty (fard) in itself. The great Hanafi scholars Ashraf Ali al-Thanawi and Rashid Ahmad al-Gangohi clarify the true nature of personal taqlid: it is a practical measure to maintain order in religious practice. They also outline the exceptional case of scholars who are qualified to weigh evidences and can follow stronger opinions from other madhhabs without causing public unrest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim about personal taqlid
Read the quote from al-Thanawi: he explicitly states that personal taqlid is not fard or wajib in itself, but rather it organizes religious affairs and prevents anarchy.
This is the foundational point; many confuse it with an obligation.
2
Understand the exception for scholars
Note the qualification: when a scholar is secure from following his own desires and does not cause disorder, he may adopt a stronger evidence from another madhhab. This is from al-Gangohi.
This condition must be met; it is not a blanket permission.
3
Distinguish between layperson and scholar
For the layperson, taqlid shakhsi is necessary to avoid confusion. For a qualified scholar, taqlid ghayr shakhsi can be permitted under strict conditions.
This distinction is crucial for fatwa-giving.

"Indeed, adhering to a specific madhhab is not a legal ruling in itself, but it is a fatwa that was issued so that the affairs of religion may be organized through it."

Imam Ashraf Ali al-Thanawi (1), may Allah have mercy on him, said in some of his sermons: "So we do not say that personal taqlid is obligatory or necessary in itself, but we say that through personal taqlid, the affairs of religion are organized, and in abandoning taqlid there is disorder."

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on taqlid (adherence to a madhhab) in the Hanafi school. It is a legal-philosophical discussion from the work 'Usul al-Ifta'. The author first quotes Shaykh Ashraf Ali al-Thanawi, who clarifies that adhering to a specific madhhab (taqlid shakhsi) is not an obligatory religious ruling in itself, but a practical fatwa issued to bring order to the affairs of religion. In other words, it is a necessary organizational measure to prevent chaos. The quote in Urdu reinforces this: personal taqlid organizes religion, while abandoning it leads to disorder.

Then follows a brief biography of Shaykh al-Thanawi, a towering figure in the Indian subcontinent known as 'Hakim al-Ummah'. The biography establishes his scholarly authority.

Next, the author states that a necessary implication of al-Thanawi's statement is that when a scholar is secure from following his own desires, there is no harm in his adopting the stronger evidence (i.e., preferring one madhhab's opinion over another based on dalil). This leads to the statement of Shaykh Rashid Ahmad al-Gangohi, who addresses taqlid ghayr shakhsi (non-personal adherence, or tatabbu' al-rukhas). He explains that the jurists generally prohibited this for the common people because of the evils it entails, such as following whims. However, he makes a crucial exception: a scholar who is not afflicted by those evils and who does not cause public unrest or confusion may still practice taqlid ghayr shakhsi today. This shows the Hanafi school's nuanced approach—taqlid ghayr shakhsi is not absolutely forbidden; it is permitted for those who have the qualification to weigh evidences and are free from negative consequences.

The second biography provides background on Shaykh al-Gangohi, highlighting his deep knowledge of fiqh and hadith, further validating his opinion.

In summary, the passage teaches that while personal taqlid is practically necessary for the community, qualified scholars may on occasion follow a stronger opinion from another madhhab, provided they are not motivated by desire and do not cause confusion among the masses.

Key Terms
تقلید شخصی (taqlīd shakhṣī): Adherence to a specific madhhab consistently in all matters.
تقلید غیر شخصی (taqlīd ghayr shakhṣī): Following different madhhabs in different issues (also known as tatabbu' al-rukhas).
فوضویة (fawḍawiyya): Anarchy or disorder resulting from lack of consistent adherence.

Defending hypothetical cases in fiqh

It recounts the origin of these supplements and addresses the criticism that they include far-fetched scenarios. But Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi (may Allah have mercy on him) justified it by saying: 'If it is said: Why did he mention these issues when every sensible person is certain that they do not occur and are not needed? We say: A person cannot know what he needs except by learning what he does not need, so everything becomes part of what he needs through this path. And one prepares for calamity before it descends.' And since this book is a supplement, it did not cover all chapters of fiqh, and most of its issues pertain to transactions. Many scholars commented on the book, among them Muhammad ibn Sama'a (d. 233 AH), Abu Nasr al-'Utabi (d. 580 AH), Burhan al-Din ibn Maza (d. 616 AH), and Taj al-Din al-Kardari (d. 562 AH).

Commentary
This passage is a legal-philosophical defense of the Hanafi method of including remote hypothetical cases in fiqh works. It first explains the origin of al-Ziyadat and Ziyadat al-Ziyadat: Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani wrote them as supplements to his magnum opus, al-Jami' al-Kabir, after remembering branches he had omitted. The narration about Abu Yusuf's remark and Muhammad's response shows that the compilation of al-Ziyadat was partly to demonstrate that even finer subdivisions were not beyond his ability. The objection—that such hypothetical cases are unrealistic—is addressed by Sarakhsi through a rational argument: knowing what is needed requires learning what is not needed, just as one prepares for disaster before it strikes. This reflects the Hanafi commitment to comprehensive jurisprudential preparation. The passage also notes that these supplements focus on transactions and lists key commentators, indicating their scholarly significance.

Key Terms
الزيادات (al-Ziyadat): A book by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani supplementing al-Jami' al-Kabir with additional derivative rulings (furu') he had initially omitted.
زيادات الزيادات (Ziyadat al-Ziyadat): A second supplement by al-Shaybani, intended to cover further branches, left incomplete.
التفاريع (al-tafari'): The derivation of subsidiary rulings from primary principles, often involving hypothetical scenarios.

Talfiq: Definition and Prohibition Conditions
Defining talfiq and stating its prohibitive conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the definition of talfiq to distinguish between prohibited and permissible combinations of madhhabs and identify which of the five prohibition conditions is violated in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Talfiq is only prohibited when it creates a composite ruling no single mujtahid endorses.

You may have heard that mixing rulings from different madhhabs is generally prohibited. But is it always wrong? This lesson clarifies the precise definition of talfiq and the specific conditions that make it forbidden. By parsing the wording of the rule, you will learn to spot prohibited talfiq and avoid it in your own practice or fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition of talfiq
Read the Arabic definition: 'that a follower combines in one issue with two or more interconnected aspects a manner that no mujtahid holds.' Pinpoint the key elements: one issue, interconnected aspects, and a combination unrecognized by any mujtahid.
2
Distinguish separate vs. integrated issues
The passage gives examples: wudu from Shafi'i and prayer from Hanafi are separate actions, so not talfiq. Understand that talfiq only applies when the aspects belong to the same legal unit.
Remember: actions are independent if one does not affect the validity of the other.
3
List the five prohibition conditions
Mentally note each condition: (a) taking licenses for desire, (b) annulling a judicial verdict, (c) contradicting a prior taqlid on the same incident, (d) violating consensus, (e) creating a composite no scholar approves.
Conditions (a)-(d) are specific abuses; (e) is the core harm.
4
Apply the definition to a borderline scenario
Test a scenario: combining two madhhabs within a single prayer (e.g., using Shafi'i rules for wudu and Hanafi for prayer in one act). Is that prohibited? Check condition (e): does it produce a ruling no mujtahid accepts?
This requires understanding that wudu and prayer are interconnected in the same worship unit.

Commentary
This passage defines talfiq (patching together different madhhabs) and outlines its prohibitive conditions. The author begins by clarifying that combining acts from different madhhabs across separate, unrelated issues—like performing wudu according to the Shafi'i school and prayer according to the Hanafi school—does not constitute prohibited talfiq, because each act is an independent legal unit. He supports this with an example from Imam al-Tahanawi, who ruled on two distinct questions using different madhhabs. Warning against using maqasid to override explicit texts

Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the types of legal cause ('illa) and their relative authority in the Hanafi usul al-iftaa framework. The author begins by quoting Wali Allah al-Dihlawi to affirm that legal rulings are to be followed regardless of whether one understands the underlying wisdom—obedience is itself the purpose. Then he categorizes 'illa into three levels: (1) 'Illa explicitly stated in the Quran, which gives definitive certainty (qat'i); (2) 'Illa explicitly stated in a hadith—since many hadiths are khabar al-wahid (solitary reports), the 'illa is conjectural (zanni), though still binding; (3) 'Illa not explicitly stated but derived by jurists. This third type is further divided into two: (a) 'illa that jurists have articulated in explicit terms, such as the Hanafi 'illa for riba al-fadl (measure and genus) versus the Shafi'i 'illa (foodstuff and currency value). Followers of each school must adhere to their own jurists' articulated 'illa. (b) 'Illa that is implicit but can be inferred. The author then provides a concrete Hanafi example: the rule that handwriting alone is not accepted as evidence because of the risk of forgery (the 'illa is that handwriting resembles handwriting, making forgery possible). Where that 'illa is absent—i.e., when there is security from forgery—the rule changes and handwriting can be accepted. This is why exceptions exist for brokers, sellers, money-changers, and official government registers where the likelihood of forgery is minimal. This illustrates the dynamic nature of fiqh rulings based on the presence or absence of their 'illa.

Changing Rulings by Changing Times: The Ruqba Case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'Rulings change with changing times' to resolve apparent contradictions between a hadith and a later ruling, using the ruqba case as a model.

Core Idea
A ruling built on custom changes when the custom changes; the ruqba case shows how.

This lesson unpacks a pivotal case from Hanafi fatwa methodology: the apparent conflict between the Prophet's hadith on ruqba and Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling that ruqba is invalid. You will see that the contradiction dissolves once we recognize that the customary meaning of 'ruqba' shifted between eras. This introduces the broader maxim that rulings based on custom evolve with time—a key tool for the mufti.

This is the key insight: the ruling is tied to customary practice.

Commentary
This passage from Shaykh Muhammad Taqi Usmani's work on the principles of fatwa deals with the critical legal maxim: 'Rulings change with changing times.' The author opens with a case study — the concept of ruqba (a form of gift). The hadith states that ruqba is valid and makes the property the recipient's. However, Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly ruled it invalid. This appears contradictory. The author resolves the tension by showing that the customary meaning of ruqba changed between the Prophetic era and Imam Abu Hanifa's time. In the Prophet's time, ruqba was an immediate gift with a return condition (which is invalid, but the gift stands). By Imam Abu Hanifa's time, ruqba had come to mean a gift suspended on the donor's death, which is invalid because gifts cannot be suspended. Thus, Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling was not a rejection of the hadith, but a recognition of changed custom. The author then generalizes: whenever a ruling is built on custom, it changes when custom changes. He cites Shaykh al-Anwar to reinforce this. Next, the author discusses linguistic custom (al-urf al-lafzi) — the customary usage of words. If a word has a general custom across all lands, its legal effect is universal. If the custom is local, the effect is limited to that locality. He gives the example of the term 'badanah' — a sacrificial animal. Among Banu Ribah, 'badanah' meant only camels, so a cow would not fulfill a vow of 'badanah' for them. Another example is the word 'sarahtuk' (I divorced you). Finally, the author mentions marriage contracts with distorted wording: using 'tajwiz' instead of 'tazwij' may invalidate the marriage according to some jurists, because the custom is to use the proper term. This passage thus grounds fatwa methodology in the dynamic interaction between text and custom, emphasizing that a mufti must understand the prevailing customary meanings of terms before issuing rulings.

Therefore the majority (jumhur) opined that ruqba is like umra, so it is a valid gift. Apparently this fatwa contradicts the text, but the truth is that the ruqba which Imam Abu Hanifa invalidated is different from the ruqba which the noble Prophet (peace be upon him) executed as a gift. As for the ruqba which Imam Abu Hanifa invalidated, it is a gift suspended on the death of the donor, and a gift does not accept suspension, so Imam Abu Hanifa invalidated it. Commentary
This passage from Shaykh Muhammad Taqi Usmani's work on the principles of fatwa deals with the critical legal maxim: 'Rulings change with changing times.' The author opens with a case study — the concept of ruqba (a form of gift). The hadith states that ruqba is valid and makes the property the recipient's. However, Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly ruled it invalid. This appears contradictory. The author resolves the tension by showing that the customary meaning of ruqba changed between the Prophetic era and Imam Abu Hanifa's time. In the Prophet's time, ruqba was an immediate gift with a return condition (which is invalid, but the gift stands). By Imam Abu Hanifa's time, ruqba had come to mean a gift suspended on the donor's death, which is invalid because gifts cannot be suspended. Thus, Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling was not a rejection of the hadith, but a recognition of changed custom. The author then generalizes: whenever a ruling is built on custom, it changes when custom changes. He cites Shaykh al-Anwar to reinforce this. Next, the author discusses linguistic custom (al-urf al-lafzi) — the customary usage of words. If a word has a general custom across all lands, its legal effect is universal. If the custom is local, the effect is limited to that locality. He gives the example of the term 'badanah' — a sacrificial animal. Among Banu Ribah, 'badanah' meant only camels, so a cow would not fulfill a vow of 'badanah' for them. Another example is the word 'sarahtuk' (I divorced you). Originally a kinayah requiring intention, but in many lands it became exclusively used for divorce, so jurists treated it as sarih (explicit). If a particular region does not share that custom, the original ruling applies. Finally, the author mentions marriage contracts with distorted wording: using 'tajwiz' instead of 'tazwij' may invalidate the marriage according to some jurists, because the custom is to use the proper term. This passage thus grounds fatwa methodology in the dynamic interaction between text and custom, emphasizing that a mufti must understand the prevailing customary meanings of terms before issuing rulings.

Key Terms
عرف لفظي (urf lafzi): Linguistic custom: the customary usage of a word in a particular community that determines its legal meaning.
كناية (kinayah): An indirect expression that requires intention (niyyah) to produce a legal effect, such as divorce.
صرّيح (sarih): An explicit expression that produces a legal effect without need for intention.
بدنة (badanah): A sacrificial animal, often a camel, but the specific meaning depends on custom.

Custom's Authority and Its Limits in Hanafi Fiqh
Analytical discussion of custom's legal authority and its limits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which customary practice can override analogy or specify a general text, and apply this to the cases of istisna' and the miller's qafiz.

Core Idea
Custom overrides qiyas only when the text's cause is not definitive; otherwise, the text prevails.

This passage from Mufti Taqi Usmani's treatise on iftaa principles delves into the classic Hanafi maxim 'al-ʿādah muḥakkamah' (custom is authoritative). It clarifies the nuanced scope of this maxim: while customary practice can set aside analogy and even specify general texts, it cannot overturn a definitive textual ruling. The author illustrates this with the historical debate among Hanafi jurists of Balkh regarding certain service contracts and the well-known case of the miller's qafīz. Understanding these boundaries is essential for a mufti who must navigate between established texts and evolving community practices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Maxim and Its Scope
The maxim 'al-ʿādah muḥakkamah' (custom is authoritative) is a cardinal principle in Hanafi fiqh. In this passage, the author examines its application to contracts and transactions, noting that custom can override qiyas (analogy) and even specify (takhṣīṣ) general texts under certain conditions.
Custom here refers to general, widespread practice (تعامل عام), not a local or specific custom.
2
Distinguish Cases Where Custom Overrides Qiyas
The scholars of Balkh permitted transactions like carrying food for a portion of the carried goods and weaving cloth for a portion of the woven cloth based on the custom of their locality, even though analogy would prohibit them because the wage is created by the worker's own action. This shows that custom can set aside qiyas.
These cases involve a known share (wage), so the prohibition due to gharar is removed.
3
Recognize the Exception for Definitive Texts
Despite permitting those, the Balkh scholars did not extend the leniency to grinding flour for a share of the flour (the miller's qafīz) because it is explicitly prohibited in the ḥadīth. This demonstrates that custom cannot override a definitive text (naṣṣ qaṭʿī).
The prohibition of the miller's qafīz is established by a clear ḥadīth (the Prophet's prohibition of المزابنة and المحاقلة, and the specific case).
4
Analyze the Role of the 'Illah (Cause)
The author offers his key insight: custom can override a general ruling only when the cause (ʿillah) of the textual ruling is not definitively established. If the ʿillah is certain, custom cannot override. In the miller's case, the Hanafis and Shāfiʿīs adopted a comprehensive ʿillah (wage arises from worker's action), while the Mālikīs and Ḥanābilah adopted a narrower one (ignorance of wage). Since the ʿillah is disputed, custom can set aside the precautionary ruling in specific instances where it is widespread.
This is a methodological refinement: when a text admits multiple possible ʿillahs, and scholars differ, custom can tip the balance towards permissibility in customary cases.
5
Apply to Istisnāʿ (Manufacturing Contract)
The author applies the same principle to the manufacturing contract (istisnāʿ), which has no explicit text. It resembles both ijarah (permissible) and sale of a non-existent object (prohibited). Due to widespread custom, jurists permit it, giving weight to permissibility over the prohibited resemblance.
This is not a practical precaution but an ijtihādi one; the custom is general and the text's cause not definitive.

ويتبين من هذا الحديث أن الحكم على الذرائع أنّها تُسَدُّ أو لاتُسَد يمكن أن يختلف من شخص إلى شخص ومن حال إلى حال. فالمرجع في ذلك إلى المَلَكةِ الفقهيّة والذوقِ السليم الذى لا يكاد يحصل إلا بممارسة طويلة تحت إشراف أصحاب هذه المَلكة. والله سبحانه أعلم.
Change of rulings with the change of time. And it becomes clear from this hadith that the ruling on means—whether they are blocked or not—can differ from person to person and from situation to situation. So the reference in that is to juristic skill and sound taste, which is hardly acquired except through long practice under the supervision of the possessors of this skill. And Allah, the Exalted, knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a legal-philosophical reflection on how fatwas adapt over time. The author, Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani, explains that the principle of sadd al-dhara'i (blocking means to evil) is not applied uniformly; rather, its application depends on the jurist's developed faculty of fiqh and sound juridical intuition. Such skill is not easily attained—it requires long-term apprenticeship under masters. This highlights the Hanafi emphasis on the personal qualification of the mufti and the contextual nature of fatwa-giving. The passage references a hadith (presumably discussed earlier) to ground the discussion in revelation, but stresses that understanding its application is a matter of trained judgment.

Key Terms
الذرائع (al-dhara’i‘): Means or pathways that lead to a legal result; in this context, the means that may be blocked (sadd) or left open according to juristic assessment.
الملكة الفقهية (al-malaka al-fiqhiyya): The deeply ingrained juristic skill and capability to derive rulings, often acquired through long practice under experts.
الذوق السليم (al-dhawq al-salim): Sound juridical intuition or taste that enables the jurist to correctly assess cases and apply principles.
