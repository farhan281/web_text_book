The Hierarchical Methodology of Fatwa: From Quran to Ijtihad
Establishes the sequential methodology for fatwa from Quran, Sunnah, to Ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct order of sources for fatwa according to the Salaf and explain why ijtihad is only permissible after exhausting textual sources.

Core Idea
Fatwa follows a strict hierarchy: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, then Ijtihad.

This lesson establishes the foundational methodology for issuing fatwa as practiced by the Companions and early generations. Through the famous hadith of Mu'adh ibn Jabal and the letter of 'Umar to Shurayh, we learn the sequential order of legal sources that a mufti must follow. Understanding this hierarchy prevents arbitrary rulings and ensures conformity with revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sequential methodology
Read the hadith of Mu'adh and extract the three-step order Mu'adh proposes: (1) Quran, (2) Sunnah, (3) Ijtihad.
The Prophet's approval makes this a normative methodology.
2
Analyze the Prophet's response
The Prophet strikes Mu'adh's chest and praises Allah, indicating acceptance. This validates ijtihad as a legitimate source only after the first two are exhausted.
3
Examine the letter of 'Umar
Review the letter to Shurayh which adds a fourth step: after Quran and Sunnah, check for community consensus (Ijma) before resorting to ijtihad. Also note the allowance to delay if uncertain.
This expands the methodology and demonstrates cautious practice.
4
Evaluate the hadith's authenticity
Consider the criticism of the chain and the defense by Ibn Qayyim. Note how the Ummah's acceptance overrides technical weakness in isnad for practical purposes.
Understanding this balance between hadith criticism and communal practice is key for a mufti.

Commentary
This passage is a legal philosophy and methodological discussion on the foundations of fatwa in early Islam. The author begins by citing the famous hadith of Mu'adh ibn Jabal, which establishes the sequential methodology for a judge or mufti: first the Quran, then the Sunnah, then ijtihad (independent reasoning). The Prophet (ﷺ) approved this approach, making it a core principle of Islamic jurisprudence.

Since some hadith scholars questioned the chain of this hadith, the author (Muhammad Taqi 'Uthmani) provides a detailed defense of its authenticity, quoting Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah and noting that the Companions of Mu'adh were all reliable, and the hadith is supported by the practice of the early community. He also cites a supporting hadith from Bukhari and Muslim about the judge who strives and is rewarded.

The author then presents a letter from 'Umar ibn al-Khattab to Shurayh al-Qadi, which further elaborates the methodology: (1) Quran, (2) Sunnah, (3) consensus of the people (ijma'), and if none, then ijtihad with an option to delay if uncertain. This shows the structured approach of the Salaf in issuing legal rulings.

The passage also includes a biographical note on Shurayh al-Qadi, a famous judge of Kufa, known for his long life and judicial service.

Key takeaway: The methodology of fatwa is not arbitrary; it follows a strict hierarchy of sources: Quran, Sunnah, consensus, and then ijtihad within the boundaries of these sources.

Key Terms
الاجتهاد (al-ijtihad): Independent legal reasoning by a qualified jurist to derive a ruling when no clear text from Quran or Sunnah is found.
السلف (al-salaf): The early generations of Muslims, including the Companions and the Followers, whose methodology in fatwa is authoritative.

The Hierarchy of Evidence in Fatwa: The Salaf's Methodology
Historical reports on fatwa methodology of early Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hierarchy of sources used by the Companions in issuing fatwas and apply it to a modern scenario.

Core Idea
The mufti must start with Qur'an, then Sunnah, then Ijma, then ijtihad — no shortcut.

This passage presents the consensus of the early Muslims (Salaf) on the methodology of issuing fatwas. The reports from the Companions — Umar, Ibn Mas'ud, Ibn Abbas, and Zaid ibn Thabit — consistently outline a clear hierarchy: first the Qur'an, then the Sunnah, then the consensus of the Muslims (Ijma), and finally personal reasoning (ijtihad) when no explicit text is found. This method later became the foundation of Hanafi usul al-fiqh and rasm al-mufti.

Guided Path
1
Extract the hierarchy from each narration
Read the reports from Umar, Ibn Mas'ud, Ibn Abbas, and Zaid ibn Thabit. Identify the order of sources they prescribe. Write down the sequence for each.
All narrations agree on the order: Qur'an → Sunnah → Ijma → Ijtihad, but some explicitly mention 'consensus' while others say 'people of opinion'.
2
Compare the narrations for consistency
Note any differences: e.g., Umar mentions 'look at what the people have agreed upon', Ibn Mas'ud says 'what the Muslims have agreed upon', Ibn Abbas adds 'from Abu Bakr and Umar' before his own opinion. All point to a hierarchy that exhausts scripture before resorting to ijtihad.
The variation highlights that ijtihad is only permitted after primary sources are exhausted.
3
Formulate the general rule
Synthesize the reports into one operational rule for a mufti: When asked a question, first search the Qur'an; if not found, search the Sunnah; if not found, search for ijma (consensus); if none, perform ijtihad (using qiyas or istihsan).
This rule is known in Hanafi works as 'al-mufti yaltafi ila al-qur'an thumma al-sunnah thumma al-ijma' thumma al-ijtihad'.
4
Connect to Hanafi rasm al-mufti
Recognize that this Salafi method is the same hierarchy adopted by the Hanafi school. The Hanafi mufti must follow this order, and any fatwa that jumps to ijtihad without checking texts is invalid.
Later Hanafi works like Rasm al-Mufti by Ibn Abidin codify this hierarchy explicitly.
5
Apply to a contemporary scenario
Given a modern financial issue (e.g., cryptocurrency), a mufti must first check if the Qur'an or Sunnah provides a direct ruling. If not, he checks for ijma among classical scholars. If none, he applies qiyas or istihsan.
This demonstrates the timelessness of the Salaf's method.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the historical development of fatwa methodologies among the early generations (salaf). The author begins by listing early scholars who compiled and organized hadith and fiqh works, such as Rabi' ibn Subayh, Sa'id ibn Abi 'Aruba, Ma'mar ibn Rashid, Ibn Jurayj, and others including Imam Abu Hanifa with his 'Kitab al-Athar'. The key point is the eventual division of scholars into two methodological camps: 'Ashab al-Hadith' (People of Hadith), who focused primarily on narration and collection, and 'Ashab al-Ra'y' (People of Opinion), who centered on deriving legal rulings through reasoning, including qiyas (analogy). The author strongly rebuts two common misconceptions: first, that Ashab al-Hadith reject qiyas entirely; second, that Ashab al-Ra'y prioritize their personal views over scriptural texts. He clarifies that 'ra'y' in this context is not arbitrary opinion but disciplined ijtihad based on qiyas, as evidenced by the hadith of Mu'adh ibn Jabal and the well-known letter of Caliph 'Umar to Abu Musa al-Ash'ari. The term 'ajtahidu ra'yi' means exerting effort to derive rulings through analogy from the revealed sources. The author stresses that all scholars ultimately prioritize the Qur'an and Sunnah, differing only in interpretive methodology. This passage serves to legitimize the Hanafi approach as rooted in textual evidence rather than mere rationalism, defending it against critics who misunderstand the term 'ra'y'.

Key Terms
أصحاب الحديث (Aṣḥāb al-Ḥadīth): The group of scholars whose primary focus was on narrating and transmitting hadith and athar, with less emphasis on deriving new rulings.
أصحاب الرأي (Aṣḥāb al-Ra’y): Scholars who dedicated themselves to deriving fiqh rulings using reasoning (particularly qiyas), entering into hadith narration only when needed for evidence.
الرأي (في التسمية) (al-Ra’y): Not personal opinion but rather ijtihad based on qiyas (analogical deduction) from the Qur'an and Sunnah, as used in the hadith of Mu'adh.

The Necessity of Taqlid and the Methodology of Fatwa
Explaining the need for taqlid of the four madhhabs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why taqlid of a codified madhhab is obligatory for non-mujtahids and identify the conditions under which a qualified scholar may adopt a ruling from another madhhab.

Core Idea
Taqlid of a codified madhhab is obligatory for laymen; qualified scholars can follow stronger evidence from other madhhabs.

This passage from Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuhu addresses a foundational issue in Islamic legal practice: who must follow a madhhab, and how strictly? The author clarifies that while a mujtahid's error is excused, a non-mujtahid must adhere to one of the four codified schools. Yet this adherence is not a straightjacket even for scholars: when stronger evidence from another madhhab becomes apparent, qualified scholars may adopt it. This lesson unpacks these principles and their practical implications for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from *Usul al-Ifta' wa Adabuhu* discusses the methodology of fatwa among the early generations and the necessities of taqlid in later times. The author begins by clarifying the correct view held by the majority (including the Hanafis) that in subsidiary juristic matters, there is only one correct ruling from Allah, but a mujtahid who errs is still rewarded. However, this does not permit negligence; a mujtahid who fails to exert his best effort sins by consensus. For non-mujtahids, the only proper path is taqlid of a specific madhhab. This taqlid, however, is not permissible for the Sahaba or Tabi'un whose views were not systematically codified, as their rulings were not compiled with all conditions and qualifications. The four established madhhabs have been thoroughly documented, with their general and absolute statements clarified. The passage then cites Imam al-Razi's consensus that laymen cannot directly follow the Companions. Moving to practical considerations, Shah Wali Allah al-Dihlawi explains the immense benefit of cleaving to the four madhhabs and warns against abandoning them. He gives a specific ruling for a layman in regions like India where only Hanafi scholars and books are available: it becomes obligatory to follow the Hanafi madhhab, and leaving it would be like discarding the harness of Shari'ah, leading to chaos. In contrast, in the Haramayn where all madhhabs are available, one must still act on reliable knowledge, not on mere guesswork. The passage concludes by affirming that the true goal is to follow the Shari'ah, and for non-mujtahids this is achieved through taqlid of a reliable mujtahid. Yet this does not rigidly lock a qualified scholar into a single madhhab; a deeply learned scholar who weighs evidences may adopt positions from other madhhabs when stronger evidence supports them. Hanafi scholars have done this in issues such as muzara'ah, hiring for Qur'anic instruction, and khyar al-maghbun (option of the defrauded). This flexibility is allowed because taqlid of a specific imam is not a primary injunction of Shari'ah but a means to maintain order and prevent capricious selection of rulings.

Change of Rulings with Change of Time
It is known in the expressions of the jurists that rulings change with the change of time. This principle is not universal such that all Shar'i rulings change by it, as some permissive people in our time have claimed. Rather, what is meant by this principle is that some rulings change with the change of time. This change occurs through one of the following four ways:
First: That the ruling is caused by a legal cause. If the cause disappears with the change of time, the ruling changes due to its disappearance.
Second: That the ruling is built upon custom and practice. If the custom changes, the ruling changes. This actually goes back to the first way, because the change of custom only changes the ruling if the previous ruling was caused by custom.
Third: That the ruling changes due to severe necessity or widespread affliction. The change occurs to the extent of the necessity.
Fourth: That the ruling changes to block the means (to evil).
We intend here to explain these four ways with some detail. Allah is the granter of success.
1- Change of ruling due to change of cause
It is accepted among the jurists that the ruling revolves around the cause, in terms of existence and non-existence.
(1) The jurists mentioned it in several places, e.g., Ibn 'Abidin (may Allah have mercy on him) mentioned it in the chapter on Witr and Nawafil, saying: 'Rulings change due to the difference of time in many matters according to interests' (Radd al-Muhtar: 370, paragraph 5916).

Change of Rulings with Change of Time
If the cause exists, the ruling is established; if it ceases, the ruling ceases. For the causes of these rulings are perpetual, never ceasing. And sometimes the cause of a ruling is subject to change and cessation; then the ruling changes with its change.
The distinction between cause and wisdom
But here it is necessary to know an important principle: that the Shar'i ruling revolves around its Shar'i cause, not around its wisdom. Perhaps some people are confused about this, thinking wisdom is the cause, and claiming that the absence of wisdom affects the change of the ruling, whereas there is a great difference between cause and wisdom that must be remembered. That is: the cause is a description that is a sign for the existence of the ruling. As for wisdom, it is the benefit expected to be obtained from acting upon the ruling. An example is the prohibition of drinking wine: the prohibition is the ruling, the substance being wine is the cause, and protecting man from that which removes his intellect is the wisdom. So the ruling of prohibition revolves around its cause, meaning the substance being wine; wherever wine is found, the ruling of prohibition is established, and it does not revolve with wisdom. If a man's intellect is not removed by drinking wine, the ruling of prohibition is not lifted for him, because the cause – the substance being wine – remains. Similarly, the ruling of shortening prayer: its cause is travel, and its wisdom is avoiding hardship. So the ruling revolves around its cause, which is travel, not its wisdom, which is hardship. If a traveller does not experience any hardship, as often occurs nowadays in travel by airplanes and fast cars, the ruling of shortening is not lifted, because the cause remains.

This passage from 'Usul al-Ifta' by Muhammad Taqi Usmani explains a crucial principle for muftis: rulings can change with time, but not arbitrarily. The author first clarifies that this principle is not absolute; it applies only to some rulings, contrary to the claims of some permissive modernists. He then lists four ways a ruling can change: (1) change in its legal cause (ʿilla), (2) change in custom (ʿurf) on which the ruling is based, (3) overwhelming necessity (ḍarūra) or widespread hardship (ʿumūm al-balwā), and (4) blocking the means to evil (sadd al-dharāʾiʿ). The author then elaborates on the first way, emphasizing that rulings are tied to their legal causes. If the cause is perpetual (e.g., the cause of prohibiting wine is its intoxicating nature, which is inherent), the ruling never changes. If the cause is subject to change, the ruling changes accordingly. A critical distinction is then drawn between the legal cause (ʿilla) and the wisdom (ḥikma) behind the ruling. Many confuse the two, but while the ruling follows the cause, it does not follow the wisdom. For example, the prohibition of wine is due to its being wine (cause), not because it removes intellect (wisdom); even if a person's intellect is unaffected, it remains prohibited. Similarly, shortening prayer is due to travel (cause), not hardship (wisdom); even if travel is easy, the concession remains. This distinction prevents muftis from incorrectly changing rulings based on perceived absence of benefit.

Change of Ruling with Change of Cause: Distinguishing ʿIlla from Ḥikma
Explains the first mechanism: change of cause, and distinguishes cause from wisdom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the legal cause (ʿilla) and wisdom (ḥikma), and explain why rulings do not change when wisdom alone is absent.

Core Idea
Rulings revolve around their cause, not their wisdom; absence of wisdom does not nullify the ruling.

This lesson delves into the first mechanism of change: alteration of the legal cause. We will examine the critical distinction between the cause (ʿilla) that dictates a ruling and the wisdom (ḥikma) that explains its benefit. Misunderstanding this distinction leads to errors in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Understand the relationship between ruling and cause
Accept that rulings are tied to their cause: if the cause exists, the ruling applies; if the cause ceases, the ruling ceases.
This is a fundamental usuli principle: 'al-hukm yaduru ma'a al-illati wujudan wa 'adaman'.
2
Distinguish perpetual vs changeable causes
Some causes are perpetual (e.g., intoxicating quality of wine) and never vanish; others are contingent (e.g., custom) and can change.
When the cause is perpetual, the ruling never changes.
3
Differentiate cause from wisdom
The cause (ʿilla) is the description that triggers the ruling; the wisdom (ḥikma) is the benefit expected. The ruling follows the cause, not the wisdom.
Example: wine prohibition's cause is being wine; wisdom is preserving intellect.
4
Apply to examples
Use the wine and shortening prayer examples: even if wisdom (intellect preservation, hardship) is absent, the ruling remains because the cause (wine, travel) exists.
This prevents muftis from changing rulings based on perceived lack of benefit.
5
Avoid the common error
Do not change a ruling because you think the wisdom is absent. Always verify whether the cause has actually changed.
Error: claiming that comfortable travel nullifies concession of shortening prayer.

The author then elaborates on the first way, emphasizing that rulings are tied to their legal causes. If the cause is perpetual (e.g., the cause of prohibiting wine is its intoxicating nature, which is inherent), the ruling never changes. If the cause is subject to change, the ruling changes accordingly. A critical distinction is then drawn between the legal cause (ʿilla) and the wisdom (ḥikma) behind the ruling. Many confuse the two, but while the ruling follows the cause, it does not follow the wisdom. For example, the prohibition of wine is due to its being wine (cause), not because it removes intellect (wisdom); even if a person's intellect is unaffected, it remains prohibited. Similarly, shortening prayer is due to travel (cause), not hardship (wisdom); even if travel is easy, the concession remains. This distinction prevents muftis from incorrectly changing rulings based on perceived absence of benefit.

Key Terms
العلة (al-ʿilla): The legal cause; a description that is a sign for the existence of a ruling, such as wine being the cause for prohibition.
الحكمة (al-ḥikma): The wisdom or benefit expected from the ruling, such as preserving intellect from the prohibition of wine.
يدور الحكم مع العلة وجوداً وعدماً (Yaduru al-hukm ma'a al-illati wujudan wa 'adaman): The ruling revolves with the cause in terms of existence and non-existence.

Methodology of Issuing Fatwa: Understanding the Issue
Mufti's duty to conceptualize the issue and distinguish relevant facts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify effective and extraneous facts in a fatwa scenario and apply the Hanafi principle that only relevant facts are considered.

Core Idea
A mufti must distinguish effective facts from extraneous ones — only what relates to the ruling matters.

This passage explains the crucial first step in issuing a fatwa: correctly understanding the question. The mufti must not rush; he must clarify ambiguities and focus on the legally relevant details, ignoring extraneous information. The Hanafi principle, articulated by al-Dabusi, underpins this methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two stages of fatwa
A fatwa requires two steps: first, conceptualize the specific incident (tasawwur al-surah), then apply the general ruling (takhyir shar'i). Recognize that the first stage is foundational—a mistake here leads to a flawed answer.
The passage explicitly states: 'الوصول إلى جواب صحيح يحتاج إلى مرحلتين: الأولى: الإدراك الصحيح للصورة المسئول عنها، والثانية: إدخال تلك الصورة في حكم كلّي'.
2
Recognize the duty of tasawwur
The mufti must precisely understand the question. If the question is ambiguous, he must not rush; he must remove the ambiguity by consulting the questioner. This is because 'الحكم على الشيئ فرع عن تصوره' (ruling on something is a branch of conceptualizing it).
3
Distinguish effective vs extraneous facts
The questioner may include details that do not affect the ruling (waqi'at tardiyya) and omit crucial ones (waqi'at mu'aththira). The mufti must filter out the irrelevant and focus only on what relates to the ruling, as per the Hanafi principle.
Al-Dabusi's principle: '... فلا عبرة لما لا يتعلّق به الحكم والعبرة لما يتعلّق به الحكم.'
4
Apply the Hanafi principle
When a statement contains both relevant and irrelevant parts, only the relevant part is considered. The mufti should mentally (or by amendment) treat the irrelevant part as if it were not mentioned. Use this to evaluate sample queries.
Example: In a talaq case, the dispute details are irrelevant; the actual words of divorce are decisive.

Methodology of Issuing Fatwa
Issuing a fatwa is the application of a general legal ruling to a particular incident. Accordingly, reaching a correct answer requires two stages: first, correct perception of the picture asked about, and second, inserting that picture into a general ruling, which in contemporary terminology is called "legal classification".
Conceptualizing the Picture Asked About
The duty of the mufti before everything is to understand the particular incident he was asked about with precise understanding, and to conceptualize it correctly, because ruling on something is a branch of conceptualizing it. If he conceptualizes the issue incorrectly, he will inevitably err in the answer. So it is not permissible for the mufti to rush to answer if there is ambiguity in the question; rather he must remove that ambiguity by consulting the questioner or by other means until the picture of the issue becomes clear to him. Since the lay questioner may not be aware of what is the basis of the legal ruling, he might mention in his question details that have no effect on the legal ruling. Therefore the jurists mentioned that the facts the questioner mentions in his question are of two types: first, effective facts that influence the ruling, and second, extraneous facts that have no entry or effect on the ruling. So the mufti must distinguish between them and focus his thought only on the effective facts. Al-Dabusi said (may Allah have mercy on him): "The principle according to Abu Hanifa is that whoever combines in his speech that which relates to the ruling and that which does not relate to the ruling, no consideration is given to that which does not relate to the ruling, and consideration is given to that which relates to the ruling, and the ruling is attached to it, as if he only mentioned in his speech that which relates to the ruling."
The questioner may also omit from his question what the correct answer depends on, and mention other details that have no relation to the legal ruling. In other words, he mentions extraneous facts and does not mention effective facts, like many laymen do when asking about the occurrence of divorce: they mention facts related to the dispute between the spouses, but do not mention the words used when effecting the divorce. Then the mufti must refine the question, ask the questioner to answer [the refinements] on the same question he presented to the mufti, then the mufti builds his answer on what has been refined of the picture of the issue. Sometimes the questioner may explain some important details orally before the mufti, so the mufti should not be content with just his statement, but rather refer to the question to complete it or add that detail to it, and there is no harm in adding it with his pen if the questioner asks him to. I say: If there is no room to add that detail in the question, it is also possible that the mufti begins his answer by stating that the questioner added this detail orally, and if this detail is true, then the ruling is such. Many times I saw my father and teacher (may Allah have mercy on them) do that.
The questioner may not be able, due to lack of knowledge, to clarify the matters on which knowledge of the legal ruling depends. Then the mufti should ascertain those matters through other means. Such cases occur frequently in questions about current transactions among people, for the questioner asks according to his understanding of them, and is not careful.
