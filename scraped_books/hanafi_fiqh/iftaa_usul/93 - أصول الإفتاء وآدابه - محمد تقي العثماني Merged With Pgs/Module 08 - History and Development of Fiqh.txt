Fatwa in the Era of the Tabi'in: The Bridge Generation
Historical introduction to the role of Tabi'in in fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the role of the Tabi'in in fatwa transmission and explain their significance as the intermediary generation between the Companions and the later madhhab imams.

Core Idea
The Tabi'in were the vital link between the Companions and the legal schools, spreading fatwa across Muslim lands.

After the Companions passed away, the responsibility of issuing fatwa fell to the senior Followers (Tabi'in). These scholars, scattered across the Islamic world, preserved and transmitted the rulings they had learned from the Companions. This lesson explores their historical role, geographical distribution, and the division of their jurists into two methodological camps—a foundation for understanding the later development of the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Generational Succession
Recognize that after the Companions (Sahaba), the authority for fatwa shifted to the Tabi'in. They were the next generation who had direct contact with the Companions and learned from them.
The Tabi'in are the second generation in the chain of transmission (isnad) for fiqh.
2
Trace Geographical Distribution
The Tabi'in were not confined to one region; they spread to lands conquered by Muslims, such as Kufa, Basra, Medina, Mecca, Syria, and Egypt. Each center had prominent jurists.
Kufa became the hub of juristic reasoning (ra'y), which influenced Imam Abu Hanifa.
3
Reference the Scholarly Source
Ibn al-Qayyim's I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in is cited as a primary source listing many Tabi'in fatwa authorities. This shows how later scholars compiled their biographies and rulings.
Many hadith scholars (huffaz) also wrote books on the tabaqat (classes) of the Tabi'in.
4
Anticipate the Two Divisions
The passage states the jurists of the Tabi'in were divided into two groups. Although the text cuts off, in Hanafi tradition these are the Ahl al-Hadith (traditionists) and Ahl al-Ra'y (rationalists). The Kufan school, rooted in Ahl al-Ra'y, laid the groundwork for the Hanafi madhhab.
Imam Abu Hanifa inherited the methodology of Kufan Tabi'in like Ibrahim al-Nakha'i and Hammad ibn Abi Sulayman.

Historicizing the two approaches to fatwa among the tabi'un

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the two methodological approaches to fatwa among the Tabi'un and explain the reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
The Tabi'un jurists split into those who confined fatwa to explicit texts and those who actively derived rulings for new cases.

This passage from Usul al-Ifta' presents a historical classification of the Tabi'un jurists into two groups based on their approach to fatwa. Understanding this divergence illuminates the early development of Islamic legal methodology and the debates that shaped later madhhabs.

The second group dedi cated themselves to fiqh, deriving rulings for hypothetical cases, and some compiled their works into books (e.g., al-Sha'bi, Makhul).

Fatwa in the Era of the Successors
After the Companions, the reference for fatwas was the senior Successors (Tabi'un), and they were spread throughout the lands that Muslims had inhabited in their conquests. Ibn al-Qayyim, in the beginning of I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in, counted many of them, and many hadith scholars authored books and volumes about their classes. The jurists among the Successors divided into two groups:
The first group: Those whose main occupation was narrating hadith, and they did not speak about fiqh except what was explicit in the Book and Sunnah, and they did not focus their attention on deriving detailed issues that had not yet occurred. This was because most of them disliked delving into opinion (ra'y) and analogy (qiyas), and they were hesitant about giving fatwa and derivation (istinbat) except when necessary and they found no alternative. They based this on what was narrated from the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, that he criticized asking many questions.
The second group: Those who dedicated themselves to fiqh and fatwa, and did not limit themselves to narrating hadith and reports, but rather exerted themselves in collecting issues and branching out detailed matters, so that they had a fatwa in every chapter of fiqh. Some of them compiled their fiqh in a book, such as al-Sha'bi and Makhul.
The Jurists Who Prohibited Giving Fatwa on What Has Not Occurred
As for the first group, they held that the mufti or jurist should restrict himself to actual real issues that were presented to him from someone who was afflicted with those issues, and that the jurist should not occupy himself with branching out details and explaining the ruling of matters that have not actually occurred, for he is not obligated to do so. Commentary
This passage from 'Usul al-Ifta' introduces the historical development of fatwa methodology among the Tabi'un (Successors). It first lists several modern encyclopedic works on the fiqh of prominent Companions, indicating the continued scholarly interest in their rulings. Then it describes the transition after the Companions: the Tabi'un became the primary reference for fatwas. The author, Muhammad Taqi Usmani, classifies the Tabi'un jurists into two distinct groups. The first group focused primarily on hadith narration and avoided engaging in opinion (ra'y) and analogy (qiyas) to derive rulings for hypothetical scenarios. They only issued fatwas on matters explicitly covered by the Quran and Sunnah, fearing excessive questioning as criticized by the Prophet (saw). The second group actively engaged in fiqh, deriving rulings for new issues and compiling their legal opinions in books. Examples given are al-Sha'bi and Makhul. The passage then elaborates on the view of the first group: they believed a mufti should confine himself to actual queries from those facing real situations, and not pre-emptively derive rulings for hypothetical or future events. This position is supported by a hadith criticizing excessive questioning. The passage ends with a biographical note on Makhul, a prominent jurist from the second group. This historical classification helps students understand the early divergence in legal methodology, which later influenced the development of systematic fiqh and the emergence of the madhhabs.

The Tabi'un and Their Ifta Methodology: Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib as a Model
Introduces the Tabi'un and their ifta diversity, then focusses on Sa'id's biography and scholarly weight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key characteristics of the Tabi'un ifta methodology and explain why Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib's mursal hadith are universally accepted.

Core Idea
The Tabi'un differed in their ifta methods but shared reliance on hadith and athar; Sa'id's reliability made his mursal accepted by all.

The generation of the Tabi'un inherited the vast treasury of Prophetic hadith and Companion rulings. Each major city had its leading mufti, and they approached ifta with varying degrees of reliance on hadith and ra'y. In Madinah, Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib stood out as a paragon of meticulousness and piety, to the extent that even his mursal narrations—normally contested—are accepted by all, including Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Tabi'un Generation
The Tabi'un are the second generation of Muslims who learned from the Companions. They were diverse in their approaches: some emphasized direct textual transmission, others exercised ra'y. But all were committed to preserving the sunnah and issuing fatwas accordingly.
2
Identify the Role of Local Imams
In each Islamic city, a leading scholar emerged whom the people followed in fiqh and ifta. In Madinah, it was Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib; in Kufa, it was others. This reflected the decentralized nature of early ifta.
3
Examine Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib's Credentials
He was one of the seven jurists of Madinah, his father and grandfather were Companions, and he married the daughter of Abu Hurairah. His dedication to congregation and knowledge was unparalleled.
4
Analyze the Acceptance of His Mursal
Mursal hadith (a Tabi'i narrating directly from the Prophet) is generally not accepted by many scholars. However, Sa'id's mursal are accepted by all, even by al-Shafi'i who normally rejects mursal. This shows the high trust in his transmission and his rigorous method.

The Seven Fuqaha of Madinah: Pillars of Early Fatwa
Biographical introduction with list and poems

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the seven jurists of Madinah, explain the variation in their lists, and articulate their role in developing the fatwa tradition of the Salaf.

Core Idea
The seven fuqaha of Madinah were the first generation of jurists after the Companions who established the methodology of fatwa in the Prophet's city.

In this lesson, we meet the legendary seven jurists of Madinah (al-fuqahā' al-sab'ah), the foremost authorities on fatwa among the Tābi'ūn. Understanding who they were and how they studied under the Companions — especially 'Ā'ishah and Ibn 'Abbās — gives us insight into the living chain of fiqh transmission that later shaped the classical madhhabs. This passage from Muḥammad Taqī al-'Uthmānī's work provides both biographical gems and a clear list with a mnemonic poem to help you remember them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the group and its core members
Locate the names listed at the beginning of the passage: Abu Salamah, 'Urwah, 'Ubaydullāh, Qāsim, Sulaymān. Then note that Sa'īd ibn al-Musayyab and Khārijah ibn Zayd are also part of the seven, though Sa'īd is implied in the poem. Write down all seven names.
The poem in the passage helps with memorization.
2
Understand the variation in the list
The passage notes that some scholars replace Abu Salamah with Abu Bakr ibn 'Abd al-Rahmān. Compare the two lists and consider why different scholars might include different names. This variation is a legitimate ikhtilāf about who exactly constituted the seven.
This shows that historical lists can have flexibility.
3
Analyze the biography of 'Urwah ibn al-Zubayr
Read the detailed account: his lineage (son of al-Zubayr), his teachers (especially his aunt 'Ā'ishah), his trials (amputation of leg, loss of son), and his profound patience. Note his death dates.
His connection to 'Ā'ishah is key to his fiqh.
4
Analyze the biographies of 'Ubaydullāh and Sulaymān
For 'Ubaydullāh, note his status as teacher of 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Azīz, his memory, and his blindness. For Sulaymān, note his beauty, his piety in the face of temptation, and his vision of Yūsuf (AS).
These personal details illustrate their moral authority.
5
Memorize the seven names using the poem

Commentary
This passage introduces the seven famous jurists of Madinah (الفقهاء السبعة) who were the primary authorities for fatwa during the tabi‘in period. They are: (1) Urwah ibn al-Zubayr, (2) Ubaydullah ibn Abdullah ibn Utbah, (3) Qasim ibn Muhammad, (4) Abu Salamah ibn Abd al-Rahman, (5) Sulayman ibn Yasar, (6) Kharijah ibn Zayd, and (7) Sa‘id ibn al-Musayyab (though Sa‘id is not mentioned here by name, but appears in the poem as 'Sa‘id'). However, there is a difference of opinion: some include Abu Bakr ibn Abd al-Rahman instead of Abu Salamah. The author provides detailed biographies of Urwah, Ubaydullah, Sulayman, and then mentions Kharijah and Abu Bakr in footnotes. These men were the students of the Companions, particularly Aisha and Ibn Abbas, and they established the fiqh tradition of Madinah. Understanding their stature is important for students of ifta because their methodology influenced later Hanafi and Maliki schools. The poem by Ibn al-Qayyim emphasizes their reliability and vast knowledge.

Key Terms
الفقهاء السبعة (al-fuqahā' al-sab‘ah): The seven most prominent jurists of Madinah among the Tabi‘un who were the primary authorities for fatwa after the Companions.
التابعون (al-tābi'ūn): The generation that followed the Companions of the Prophet, who learned from them and transmitted Islamic knowledge.
سير أعلام النبلاء (Siyar A'lām al-Nubalā'): A famous biographical dictionary of prominent Islamic figures by al-Dhahabi, frequently cited for early scholars' lives.

Fatwa Methodology in the Early Generations: Contextual Clues and Ijtihad
Historical description of early fatwa methodology and causes of dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Companions and Followers issued fatwas based on contextual clues (qara'in) and personal reasoning (ijtihad), and identify the causes of their differences in furu' matters.

Core Idea
Early fatwa relied on deep contextual understanding and ijtihad, not formal deduction—disagreement arose from different memorization and reasoning.

This lesson takes you into the early school of fatwa, where Islamic jurisprudence was not yet a formal discipline. The Companions learned directly from the Prophet's words and actions, understanding rulings through contextual clues rather than rigid categories. After the Prophet's era, as new issues arose, they exercised ijtihad based on the underlying causes (ilal) they had extracted from his teachings. This organic process naturally led to differences in subsidiary matters. Understanding this historical methodology is essential for appreciating the development of Hanafi usul al-ifta.

The footnote clarifies that later juristic terminology did not innovate new concepts but articulated what was already understood.

Commentary
This passage is a historical exposition on the use of qiyas (analogical reasoning) among the Salaf, demonstrating its legitimacy through two key examples. The first example involves the inheritance of the grandfather alongside siblings. The author narrates a story where Imam Abu Hanifa defends qiyas by citing the Companions Ali and Zaid ibn Thabit, who used analogical reasoning to show that the grandfather, like the grandson, should share with siblings rather than be excluded. The analogies use a tree branch and a canal to illustrate equal proximity. The second example concerns the hadd punishment for wine drinking. The author clarifies that the increase to eighty lashes by Umar was not establishing a new hadd via qiyas, but rather a preference between two transmitted possibilities (forty with a double whip or with sandals) based on a rational argument (qiyas) to prevent slander. This shows that qiyas was used to resolve ambiguous texts, not to create new rulings. The passage then discusses how the dispersal of Companions led to the formation of different fiqh schools, each relying on the teachings of their local Companions.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning: applying a ruling from an original case to a new case due to shared effective cause.
الجَدّ (al-jadd): Paternal grandfather in inheritance law.
الترجيح (al-tarjih): Preference: choosing one legal interpretation or transmitted account over another based on evidence or reasoning.

The Beginnings of Fiqh Codification
Historical overview of early fiqh compilation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the historical context and early stages of tadwin al-fiqh during the Successor period, and identify the role of early compilations like al-Abwab and Sunan.

Core Idea
Fiqh codification began not with the great imams, but with the Successors arranging hadith and athar by topic.

Before the great madhhab works, even before Abu Hanifa wrote al-Fiqh al-Akbar, early scholars felt the need to compile fiqh knowledge in an organized way. This lesson traces the shift from oral transmission to written chapters, a development that shaped all later Hanafi fiqh literature.

3

After Islam spread, both common and elite needed accessible fiqh references. This drove the Successors to compile written works.
4
Examine the method used by Successors
They recorded hadith and athar arranged by fiqh chapters (abwab). This is the earliest form of tadwin al-fiqh.
5
Note examples of early works
The author mentions al-Abwab by al-Sha’bi and Sunan by Makhul al-Shami as early examples.
These are not the full fiqh compilations we have today, but the first steps.

Understanding its compilation and status is essential for any student of Hanafi fiqh, as later scholars constantly refer to 'Muhammad said in Kitab X'—always referencing this work. We will see how al-Mabsut became a source of pride for Kufa and even led a Jewish scholar to embrace Islam.

Guided Path
1
Understand al-Mabsut's Origin
Imam Muhammad first wrote separate books on individual topics (e.g., Kitab al-Salat, Kitab al-Buyu‘), which were later collected into one comprehensive work called al-Mabsut (or al-Asl). This is the work referenced when jurists say 'Muhammad said in Kitab al-Mudaraba.'
Al-Mabsut is the same as al-Asl.
2
Recognize the Reference Pattern
When later Hanafi texts cite 'Muhammad said in Kitab...' they are quoting from one of the component books of al-Mabsut. This is a standard citation style for Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Appreciate the Scale and Method
The people of Kufa boasted of 27,000 legal issues derived by Imam Muhammad, all based on qiyas (analogical reasoning). Al-Muzani described him as 'the most prolific in deriving branches (tafri‘).'
This demonstrates the depth of Hanafi legal reasoning.
4
Note the Historical Impact
The passage reports that Imam al-Shafi‘i memorized al-Mabsut and that a Jewish scholar embraced Islam after reading it, impressed by its profundity. This underscores the work's influence beyond the Hanafi school.
The claim about al-Shafi‘i imitating al-Asl in his al-Umm is doubted by the author due to stylistic differences.

Commentary
This passage discusses the layers of Hanafi jurists, specifically focusing on Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (d. 189 AH). It explains how his book al-Mabsut (also known as al-Asl) was compiled, first as individual books on prayer, sales, oaths, and coercion, then collected into one volume. When later Hanafi scholars say 'Muhammad said in such-and-such book,' they refer to these individual parts of al-Mabsut. The passage then quotes al-Khatib al-Baghdadi narrating from Abu Ali al-Hasan ibn Dawud that the people of Basra boasted of four books, while the people of Kufa boasted of 27,000 legal issues derived by Imam Muhammad, all based on analogical reasoning (qiyas) that no one can afford to ignore. Al-Muzani, when asked about Imam Muhammad, described him as 'the most prolific in deriving branches (tafri‘).' Then Muhammad Zahid al-Kawthari adds that al-Mabsut is the largest work of Imam Muhammad that has reached us, and it is said that Imam al-Shafi‘i memorized it and wrote his al-Umm in imitation of it. A Jewish scholar embraced Islam after reading al-Mabsut, saying, 'This is the book of your Muhammad the smaller (Imam Muhammad), so how must be the book of your Muhammad the greater (the Prophet)?' The passage notes that al-Mabsut comprises six volumes, each about 500 pages, transmitted by Abu Sulayman al-Juzjani, Muhammad ibn Sama‘a al-Tamimi, and Abu Hafs al-Kabir al-Bukhari. The author then comments that al-Kawthari did not cite the source for his claim about al-Shafi‘i, suggesting it may come from al-Khatib's narration that al-Shafi‘i said he carried books from Muhammad ibn al-Hasan. However, the author doubts that al-Shafi‘i wrote al-Umm in imitation of al-Asl, noting the stylistic differences between the two works.

Al-Asl: The Foundation of Hanafi Fiqh and Its Methodology
Describing the book al-Asl and its method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the significance of Imam Muhammad's al-Asl, describe its structure and methodology, and evaluate the claim that al-Umm was modeled after al-Asl.

Core Idea
Al-Asl is the primary source of Hanafi fiqh, containing tens of thousands of rulings and establishing a method of presenting multiple Imam opinions.

This passage from a work on usul al-iftaa introduces the foundational book of the Hanafi school: Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's al-Asl (also known as al-Mabsut). Understanding this book is crucial for every mufti because it is the first source of the madhhab's rulings. The passage describes its size, transmission, author's method, and even a controversial claim about Imam al-Shafi'i. By studying this, you will grasp the authority structure of Hanafi fiqh and the importance of knowing how rulings were compiled and transmitted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Book
Locate the name 'al-Asl' and its alternate 'al-Mabsut'. Note its size (6 volumes, ~500 pages each) and that it contains tens of thousands of legal issues (furu').
Also recognize that it is one of the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Analyze the Transmission
List the key transmitters: Abu Sulayman al-Juzjani, Muhammad ibn Sama'a al-Tamimi, and Abu Hafs al-Kabir al-Bukhari. Understand that the famous recension is from al-Juzjani, and many issues originated from his questions.
The passage also provides a brief biography of al-Juzjani and Abu Hafs al-Kabir, including a controversial story about the latter.
3
Understand the Method
According to Imam Muhammad's introduction, the book presents the opinions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and his own view. When there is no disagreement, it is the collective position. Evidence (hadiths) is cited only where the evidence is not commonly known among jurists of his level.
This method shows the hierarchy within the madhhab: the three mujtahid imams, with their opinions given priority in that order.
4
Evaluate the Claim about al-Shafi'i
The passage mentions a report that Imam al-Shafi'i memorized al-Asl and authored al-Umm in imitation of it. The commentator (al-Kawthari) casts doubt on this due to stylistic differences. Consider the reliability of the report and the basis for doubting it.
This is a point of historical criticism that affects how we view inter-madhhab influence.
5
Note the Incomplete Publication
Al-Asl originally contained 53 books as listed by Ibn al-Nadim, but the printed edition covers only 16 books. Recognize that later scholars wrote commentaries on al-Asl.
This is important for research: the full text is not yet available in print.

The Third Tier of Hanafi Authority: Fatawa and Waqiat
Defines the third category of Hanafi legal texts: Fatawa and Waqiat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Fatawa and Waqiat as the third authority tier in the Hanafi school and explain their place in the hierarchy of sources for fatwa.

Core Idea
Fatawa and Waqiat are rulings derived by later Hanafi jurists, forming the third tier after Zahir al-Riwayah and Nawadir.

In the Hanafi madhhab, not all legal rulings come directly from the founding imams. Later scholars (muta'akhkhirun) faced new cases and derived solutions within the school's framework. This lesson introduces the third major category of Hanafi texts: al-Fatawa wa al-Waqiat, as defined by the great Hanafi jurist Ibn 'Abidin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of Hanafi texts
Recall that the Hanafi school organizes its legal literature into three tiers: Zahir al-Riwayah (direct transmissions from the founding imams), Nawadir (rare or less authenticated transmissions), and Fatawa/Waqiat (later juristic derivations).
This hierarchy is essential for determining the authority of a ruling in fatwa practice.
2
Define Fatawa and Waqiat
Using Ibn 'Abidin's definition, state that Fatawa and Waqiat are 'issues derived by the later mujtahids.' Understand that these rulings are not direct transmissions but ijtihad-based extensions within the school's principles.
This category includes famous works like al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya and al-Fatawa al-Bazzaziyya.
3
Place Fatawa and Waqiat in the authority hierarchy
Acknowledge that Fatawa and Waqiat come after Zahir al-Riwayah and Nawadir in authority. However, they are widely relied upon for fatwa on novel issues not explicitly covered in earlier texts.
The silence of earlier sources does not mean absence of a ruling; later jurists filled gaps through reasoning.

The silence of the earlier sources does not mean the absence of a ruling; rather, later scholars filled the gaps through reasoning within the school's framework.

Definition and Conditions of Darurah
Defines darurah and its four conditions, then categorises licit acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four conditions of darurah and apply them to determine when a prohibited act becomes permissible under Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Darurah permits the prohibited only to the extent necessary, with no alternative, and without causing equal harm to another.

Understanding darurah (necessity) is essential for a mufti because it is a key principle that can override prohibitions. However, not every difficult situation qualifies as darurah. This lesson defines darurah, outlines its four strict conditions, and distinguishes between complete and incomplete duress. The passage also explains that the concession is graded: some acts become permissible, while others remain prohibited. By mastering this, you will be able to issue fatwas that correctly balance necessity and Shariah limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of darurah
Locate the definition by Imam al-Jassas: 'Fear of harm from leaving eating, either to oneself or to one's limb.' Note that it applies to all prohibited acts under compulsion.
The definition is specific to eating but is generalized by the author.
2
List the four conditions for darurah
Extract each condition from the text: (1) fear for life or limb, (2) necessity present and probable, not imagined, (3) no permissible alternative, (4) no harm to another similar in severity.
These conditions are sequential and all must be present.
3
Distinguish complete vs. incomplete duress
The text contrasts ikrah mulji' (threat of death or limb loss) with ikrah ghayr mulji' (imprisonment or beating without life threat). Only complete duress permits prohibited acts.
This distinction is crucial for fatwa.
4
Understand the three types of concessions
From al-Atasi's quote: the first type (mubah) includes eating carrion, blood, pork, and drinking wine under complete duress. Note that refusing under complete duress is sinful.
The concession is limited to the extent needed.
5
Recognize the limits of the principle
The fourth condition prohibits causing equal harm to another, e.g., killing an innocent is never permitted even under compulsion. This is a boundary of 'al-darurat tubih al-mahzurat'.
This condition prevents abuse of the maxim.

Darurah: As for darurah, Imam Abu Bakr al-Jassas (may Allah have mercy on him) defined it when discussing acute hunger: 'Darurah is the fear of harm from leaving eating, either to oneself or to one of one's limbs.' This definition, though specific to the necessity of eating the prohibited, encompasses engaging in every prohibited act provided there is fear for life or limb, such as committing a lie or other prohibitions in a state of compelling duress. For darurah to be realized, [the following] matters are necessary: First: there must be fear for life or limb. Second: the necessity must be present, not anticipated, meaning that fear of destruction or perishing occurs by overwhelming probability based on experience, not mere illusion. Third: there must be no other permissible means to repel the harm, and the afflicted person [must] have a strong expectation that repelling the harm is achievable by committing some prohibited acts. Fourth: the prohibited act must not cause similar harm to another, so it is not permissible to kill anyone, even in a state of compelling duress. And when such darurah is realized, the afflicted person is granted a concession to commit specified prohibited acts to the extent of repelling the necessity, like a starving person who fears his own destruction; it is permissible for him to eat carrion or pork to the extent that repels his destruction. This is the intent of the famous principle: 'Necessities permit prohibitions.' And what Allamah Khalid al-Atasi (may Allah have mercy on him) mentioned under this principle summarizes the different states of darurah and their rulings. We will quote it here verbatim. He (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'Then this concession is of three types: a type that is permissible, like eating carrion, blood, pork, and drinking wine in severe hunger, choking, thirst, or under complete coercion of killing or cutting of a limb. These things are permitted under duress due to His statement: "except what you are compelled to" (al-An'am 6:119), meaning the severity of hunger drove you to eat it. The exception from prohibition is permission (from Bada'i). And just as duress is realized by starvation, it is realized by coercion. So consumption is permitted, and abstinence is not permitted; even if one abstains until he dies or is killed, he will be held accountable, because by abstaining he becomes one who throws himself into destruction, and that is prohibited. And if the coercion is incomplete, like imprisonment or beating where destruction is not feared, it is not lawful for him to do [the prohibited act].'

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling (rasm) on the definition and conditions of darurah (necessity). The author begins with the definition of Imam al-Jassas: fear of harm to life or limb from not consuming. Although initially about eating, it applies to all prohibitions under compulsion. Then four conditions are listed: (1) actual fear to life or limb; (2) necessity must be present and probable, not imagined; (3) no halal alternative; (4) the prohibited act must not cause equal harm to another (so no killing). When these conditions are met, concession is given to commit prohibited acts to the exact extent needed. The author then quotes al-Atasi who categorizes the concession into three types: first, acts that become permissible (mubah) like eating carrion, blood, pork, drinking wine in severe hunger or under full coercion (threat of death or limb loss). These are permitted by the Quranic exception. Notably, under such coercion, refusing to comply is not permissible; if one refuses and dies, he is sinful because he threw himself into destruction. If coercion is incomplete (mere beating or imprisonment without fear of death), the prohibited act remains unlawful. This detailed exposition sharpens the mufti's understanding of when necessity truly permits and when it does not.

Key Terms
إكراه ملجئ (ikrah mulji'): Compulsion that threatens death or loss of limb, leaving no choice.
ضرورة (darurah): Fear of harm to life or limb from abstaining, with no permissible alternative.
