Modern Compilations of Sahabi Fiqh: A Bibliographic Guide
List of modern compilations of Sahaba fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key modern encyclopedias on the fiqh of individual Companions and explain their relevance for fatwa derivation.

Core Idea
Modern encyclopedias on the fiqh of Sahaba are essential tools for Hanafi fatwa methodology.

In his discussion of the methodologies of fatwa among the Salaf, Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani provides a list of contemporary scholarly works that compile the legal opinions of the Companions. These works are not medieval texts but modern research tools, often produced at universities like Umm al-Qura. They serve as a bridge between the early precedents and the contemporary mufti.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the genre
Understand that these are modern scholarly compilations, not primary sources. They collect fatwas of Companions from various classical sources.
2
Identify the Sahaba covered
Note which Companions have dedicated encyclopedias: Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, Aisha, Ibn Mas'ud, Anas, Ibn Umar, and Ibn Abbas. Also note the comprehensive 'Dictionary of the Fiqh of the Salaf'.
3
Appreciate the editorial effort
These works involve gathering scattered fatwas, verifying attributions, and organising them thematically. They often include comparative analysis with other schools.
4
Connect to fatwa methodology
A mufti can use these encyclopedias to access the direct opinions of Sahaba, which are foundational for Hanafi usul. They help in understanding early ikhtilaf and tracing the origin of rulings.

Commentary
This passage appears as a front-matter listing of modern reference works that compile the legal opinions (fiqh) of individual Companions (Sahaba). The author, Muhammad Taqi Usmani, presents these as resources for understanding the early precedents of fatwa. The list includes encyclopedias on the fiqh of Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, Aisha, Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, Anas ibn Malik, Abdullah ibn Umar, and Ibn Abbas, as well as a comprehensive dictionary of the fiqh of the Salaf. A lengthy footnote provides a biography of Shah Wali Allah al-Dahlawi, an important Hanafi revivalist scholar of India who contributed to hadith and fiqh. This listing serves to orient the student to the available source material for studying early Islamic legal opinions, which forms the basis of the Hanafi school's methodology.

Key Terms
موسوعة فقه (mawsu'at fiqh): A comprehensive reference work collecting the legal rulings of a particular scholar, here a Companion.

Navigating the Scholarly Index: Key Authorities in Hanafi Ifta
Index of scholars with page references

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify key Hanafi authorities from the index and explain their significance in the Hanafi madhhab hierarchy for iftaa.

Core Idea
The scholar index is a roadmap to the authorities who shape Hanafi fatwa – knowing their hierarchy is essential for sound taqlid.

This passage presents an index of scholars mentioned in Muhammad Taqi Usmani's Usul al-Ifta. While it appears as a simple list, it is a powerful tool for tracing which authorities are relied upon in the book. The index includes early Hanafi masters (e.g., al-Hasan ibn Ziyad, Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl), later authoritative commentators (e.g., al-Haskafi, al-Timurtashi), and even scholars from other madhhabs. Understanding who these figures are and their relative rank in the Hanafi tradition will help you navigate the book and appreciate the chain of authority behind each fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Locate the scholar's entry
Find the scholar's name in the list. Note the full name, honorific titles (e.g., al-Imam, al-Allamah, Shams al-A'immah), and the page number(s) given.
Titles often indicate the scholar's renown – e.g., 'Shams al-A'immah' for al-Sarakhsi signals his high rank.
2
Identify the scholar's role in the Hanafi madhhab
Recall or research the scholar's contribution: was they a founder (Abu Hanifa's direct student), a compiler of zahir al-riwayah, a later authoritative commentator, or a scholar from another school? See which category they fall into.
The index mixes Hanafi and non-Hanafi scholars – note the madhhab affiliation for proper contextualization.
3
Connect the scholar to the hierarchy of iftaa
Determine where this scholar stands in the Hanafi hierarchy for fatwa: zahir al-riwayah, nawadir, or later fuqaha. For example, al-Haskafi (author of al-Durr al-Mukhtar) represents the late authoritative text (mufta bihi), while al-Hasan ibn Ziyad represents nawadir opinions.
This index helps you quickly find which scholars are treated and thus understand the evidential chain Usmani uses.

Commentary
This passage is an index (فهرس) of scholars mentioned in the book 'Usul al-Ifta' by Muhammad Taqi Usmani. It lists names with their full lineage, honorific titles, and the page numbers where they are discussed. The list includes both early Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Hasan ibn Ziyad, Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl) and later scholars (e.g., al-Haskafi, al-Timurtashi), as well as scholars from other schools (e.g., al-Bayhaqi, al-Shafi'i, Dawud al-Zahiri). The page numbers refer to earlier sections of the book where these scholars' views or biographies are presented. This index serves as a reference tool for readers to locate discussions on specific figures. It does not contain legal rulings but is a paratextual element aiding navigation.
