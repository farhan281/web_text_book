Unanimity Requirement for Collective Judgments in Hanafi Law
Rule on validity conditions for collective judicial decisions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a collective judgment must be unanimous, apply it to committee fatwas, and identify the evidence from al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
A shari'a committee's judgment is void unless unanimous — the opinion of one alone is not the opinion of two.

This lesson explores a key procedural rule in Hanafi qada and ifta: that when authority is delegated to a group (such as a shari'a committee or arbitration panel), a valid judgment requires the unanimous agreement of all members. The passage draws an explicit analogy to the classical rule for two arbiters, where both must agree. Understanding this principle prevents the common error of assuming majority voting suffices in Islamic legal collective decisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule from the passage
The text states that if a Shari'a committee unanimously agrees on a decision (e.g., divorce), it is valid; but if members differ, no judgment can be made, and any partial judgment is void.
The rule is explicitly stated: 'صرف وہ فیصلہ معتبر ہو گا جو کہ اتفاق ہو' (only a unanimous decision is valid).
2
Trace the evidence from al-Hidayah
The author cites al-Hidayah: when two men are appointed as arbiters, their meeting and agreement are necessary because it is a matter requiring consultation. The opinion of one alone is insufficient.
The principle is extended from two arbiters to any group.
3
Apply the analogy to larger groups
The passage argues: just as the delegation to two arbiters requires their agreement, so delegation to a group requires consensus of their opinions. This is the core qiyas (analogy).
The Maliki authorities quoted reinforce this, showing cross-madhab agreement.
4
Understand the consequence of dissent
If any member dissents, the judgment is void. The woman has a right to a second review, but no new decision can be made without new circumstances.
This protects against arbitrary or partial rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the conditions for the validity of judgments issued by a collective body such as a Shari'a committee or arbitration panel (panchayat). The author begins by stating that an ignorant person who neglects to consult scholars cannot have his judgment enforced, even if it happens to be correct. This establishes the principle that proper consultation is a prerequisite for a valid judgment. The text then explains that if a Shari'a committee unanimously decides on a matter (e.g., divorce), its decision is equivalent to a judge's verdict. However, if there is disagreement among the members, no judgment can be validly issued; a partial judgment by some is void. The author supports this by citing from al-Hidayah and other Hanafi sources: when two arbiters are appointed, they must meet and agree; the opinion of one alone is not sufficient. The same logic applies to a larger group: delegation to a group requires consensus. The passage also quotes Maliki authorities (Imam Malik, al-Baji, Sahnun, Ibn Kinana) to reinforce the point. The conclusion is that only unanimous decisions of a committee are valid, not majority decisions. The woman is given the right to request a second review, but no new decision can be made without new circumstances. This is a clear rule in the Hanafi method of issuing legal judgments and fatwas.

The Wife's Khiyar Due to Husband's Insanity: Hanafi Rasm al-Mufti
A fatwa ruling (rasm) on khiyar for defects, focusing on insanity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khiyar for defects in marriage and apply the rasm al-mufti rule for continuous insanity.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fatwa, continuous insanity (junun mutbiq) is treated like impotence, granting the wife option after one year.

This lesson unpacks a key fatwa ruling from the Hanafi school: when can a wife seek separation due to her husband's insanity? While the default Hanafi position denies the option (khiyar) for most defects, a significant exception exists for continuous insanity, analogized to castration. Understanding this exception and its conditions is essential for correct fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default Hanafi rule on defects
Begin by noting the statement in al-Durr al-Mukhtar: neither spouse has the option due to a defect of the other, even repulsive ones like insanity.
This is the baseline; exceptions are rare.
2
Note the inter-madhhab disagreement
Recognize that the three other Sunni schools (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) allow the option for all five defects absolutely. Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan gives the option for the first three defects (insanity, leprosy, white leprosy) if in the husband.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate and affects fatwa in non-Hanafi contexts.
3
Distinguish continuous vs. intermittent insanity
According to Muhammad, if insanity is newly occurring (hadith), the husband is given one year like the impotent; if it is continuous (mutbiq), it is like castration (jabb).
The type of insanity changes the ruling.
4
Apply the analogy to jabb to derive the fatwa
The mufta bihi position adopts Muhammad's view: continuous insanity = jabb, gives the wife the option to seek annulment after a one-year wait. Intermittent insanity gives no immediate option.
This is the operative rasm for Hanafi fatwa today.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on whether a wife can seek dissolution of marriage due to the husband's insanity. This is a nuanced fatwa based on the principle of hardship.

Current Jurisdiction: The Condition for a Qadi's Judgment
Explaining that a qadi's judgment is only valid if he has current jurisdiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that only current jurisdiction validates a qadi's judgment and distinguish it from mere past appointment.

Core Idea
A qadi's past authority is irrelevant; only current wilāyah validates his judgment.

A qadi's judgment carries weight only when he actually holds authority at the time of the ruling. This lesson examines a crucial condition from Hanafi fatwa methodology: that jurisdiction must be current, not merely historical. We will see why a judge's decree cannot rely on his former position and how this principle resolves a common objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
State the operative rule: a qadi's judgment is enforceable only if he has current jurisdiction (wilayat hal).
2
Understand the Objection
Recognize the hypothetical objection: if a qadi had jurisdiction before, why should his judgment not be valid? The passage considers this and rejects it.
3
Apply the Condition
Apply the rule to a scenario: a judge who was once appointed but later removed, or a qadi who was appointed for one region but now judges in another.
4
Distinguish from Similar Rules
Differentiate this condition (wilayat hal) from other conditions like proper appointment or freedom from disqualifications.

Procedure for Remarriage When No Qadi Exists
Fatwa hierarchy for emergency separation cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the correct sequence of authorities to approach when a woman wishes to remarry but no shar'i judge is available.

Core Idea
When no qadi exists, seek a Muslim ruler; failing that, follow the Maliki school by consulting a Muslim assembly.

In many modern contexts, a woman may be separated from her husband without a formal divorce and wish to remarry. This passage provides a hierarchy of authorities to approach for a shar'i separation (tafriq). It begins with the qadi, then a Muslim ruler appointed by the government, and finally, in the absence of both, a Muslim assembly acting as a collective body following the Maliki school. The text also hints at a precautionary principle requiring a verbal divorce statement.

1
Identify primary authority
The first resort is a shar'i judge (qadi). The woman should file a case and obtain a ruling of separation.
If a qadi exists, no need to proceed further.

Choosing an Islamic Court and Navigating Non-Islamic Judgments
Rules for resolving court choice disputes and handling non-Islamic judgments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct procedure for choosing an Islamic court when parties disagree and evaluate the validity of judgments from non-Islamic judges, applying the Hanafi rasm al-mufti hierarchy.

Core Idea
The claimant chooses the court; if both are claimants, first summons prevails or lots are cast. Invalid judgments need Islamic nullification.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario for muftis advising Muslims in non-Islamic legal environments. The passage from Al-Ḥīlah an-Nājizah provides a procedural framework for two issues: which Islamic council hears a disputed case, and how to handle a government judge whose rulings are not valid in Sharī‘ah. Understanding these rules is essential for issuing fatwas that protect both Sharī‘ah compliance and legal necessity.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish mudda‘ī from defendant
Determine which party is the claimant (mudda‘ī) according to the Hanafi definition: the one who would succeed if he abandons his claim. The mudda‘ī has the right to choose the court; the defendant cannot object.
If only one is mudda‘ī, his choice prevails.
2
Handle simultaneous summonses
If both parties are claimants, then the court that first issues a summons takes precedence. If summonses arrive at the same time, cast lots (qur‘ah) to decide which court hears the case.
Qur‘ah is a last resort to avoid conflict.
3
Assess the validity of a prior Islamic verdict
Once a verdict is given, it may only be overturned if it is absolutely contrary to Sharī‘ah. If it agrees with any valid opinion, it stands, even if another group considers it incorrect.
This respects the principle of legitimate ikhtilaf.
4
Evaluate a non-Islamic judge's judgment
Identify if the government-appointed judge meets Sharī‘ah qualifications (Muslim, just, knowledgeable, follows Hanafi rulings). If not, his judgment is invalid in Sharī‘ah, but may be used for legal protection.
The Islamic council must issue a nullification (faskh).
5
Determine the hierarchy of rulings
The Islamic council's verdict is the sole basis for action. The government judge's verdict, if used, must be supplemented by nullification from the Islamic council. Never rely solely on the government judge's verdict.
This reflects the mufti's role in protecting Sharī‘ah compliance.

Commentary
This passage is a procedural ruling (rasm al-mufti) from the addendum of Al-Ḥīlah an-Nājizah. It addresses two main issues: (1) how to resolve disputes between parties over which Islamic court (jamā‘at) should hear their case, and (2) the status of judgments from non-Islamic or non-compliant judges. The Rule of Fatwa from Other Madhhabs in Cases of Necessity
Taqriz endorsing fatwa from Maliki madhhab under necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule permitting fatwa from other madhhabs under necessity, identify the conditions, and construct a fatwa hierarchy for a case of marriage dissolution.

Core Idea
A Hanafi mufti may give fatwa from another madhhab when necessity demands it, provided the conditions are met.

This passage is a taqriz (endorsement) from the scholars of Madrasa Imdadiyya for a treatise on dissolving marriages due to impotence, insanity, or imprisonment. It articulates a key rule in Hanafi usul al-iftaa: that giving fatwa according to other madhhabs in times of necessity is widely accepted. This lesson unpacks that rule, its basis in Hanafi authority, and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its supporting authorities
Identify the key statement: 'al-fatwa bimadhahib al-aimma 'inda al-darurat mustaw'ab inda al-mashayikh'. Note the authorities cited: al-Qahistani (for missing husband) and al-Ramli (for obstinate husband).
The maxim is a specific application of the general maxim 'al-mashaqqa tajlib al-taysir'.
2
Identify the categories of cases where necessity is invoked
List the cases mentioned: impotent husband ('anin), insane husband (majnun), imprisoned husband (maqsūr), and afflicted husband (mubtala). Understand that each case involves a defect or situation preventing marital relations, causing hardship for the wife.
These are standard cases where Hanafi and Maliki rulings differ.
3
Trace the Hanafi internal basis for the insane husband
Note the passage says the ruling also has a basis in the Hanafi madhhab, even according to Imam Muhammad. Understand that Hanafi muftis can rely on internal ikhtilaf before resorting to other madhhabs.
This demonstrates the principle that ruling by the view of the Imam's companions (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) is not a departure from the madhhab.
4
Distinguish between adopting another madhhab vs. using Hanafi ikhtilaf
The passage clarifies that adopting the Maliki view is permissible under necessity, but there is also a Hanafi basis via Imam Muhammad. Identify the difference: when necessity is stronger, one may go outside; when a Hanafi variant exists, it is preferred to stay within.
This reflects the hierarchy: first search within Hanafi (nawadir), then other madhhabs.
5
Apply the maxim to a modern scenario
Take a case: a Hanafi mufti in a Western country is asked to dissolve a marriage where the husband is permanently impotent. Show how to apply the rule: identify necessity, check if Hanafi internal solution is adequate, then adopt Maliki ruling if needed, citing the endorsement in this taqriz.
Ensure the necessity is genuine and proportionate.

Commentary
This passage is a taqriz (endorsement) from the scholars of Madrasa Imdadiyya, Muradabad, for the treatise 'Al-Hilatun Najiza' by Mawlana Ashraf Ali Thanwi. The passage type is a rasm al-mufti rule, establishing when and how a Hanafi mufti may give fatwa according to another madhhab. The passage opens by praising Allah and the Prophet, then identifies the subject of the treatise: the dissolution of marriage (faskh al-nikah) for wives of impotent men, insane men, imprisoned men, and those afflicted with hardship (such as missing husbands), when there is dire need. The authors state they have adopted the Maliki madhhab in this matter. They then articulate the general rule: 'It is not hidden that giving fatwa according to the madhhabs of the imams in times of necessity is widely accepted among the mashayikh and people of religion.' They cite al-Qahistani on the missing husband and al-Ramli on the unreasonable husband. For the impotent man, a postponement period is a recognized means leading to separation (tafriq) in the Hanafi madhhab as well. For the insane man, they clarify that there is also a basis in the Hanafi madhhab, particularly on the authority of Imam Muhammad, since a ruling based on the view of the Imam's companions (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) is not considered a departure from the Imam's own view if it follows established usul. They reference al-Shami quoting from al-Durr al-Mukhtar to support this. The passage emphasizes that the transmission in the treatise is trustworthy, and the conditions of necessity are clearly met. This lesson is essential for understanding how a Hanafi mufti may legitimately adopt positions from other madhhabs under necessity, and the internal hierarchy of Hanafi rulings.

Validity of Khalwa for Incapable Spouses: Tarjih in Hanafi Fatwa
Discussion of rulings on khalwa for those who cannot have intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the mu'tamad opinion on khalwa for a minor or mad spouse and apply the tarjih mechanism from Qadikhan.

Core Idea
Khalwa requires capacity for intercourse; for the incapable, the relied-upon view is invalidity.

This fatwa guides the mufti through conflicting Hanafi opinions on khalwa (valid seclusion) when one spouse is minor, mad, or sleeping. The key is understanding how Qadikhan's choice settles the matter and recognising that different rulings apply to sleep (valid per Abu Hanifa) versus madness (invalid).

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the ruling
Determine who is being discussed: a minor incapable of intercourse (saghir la yaqdiru ala al-jima'), a mad person (majnun), or a sleeping person (na'im).
The text also mentions an imbecile (ma'tuh) as similar to a minor.
2
Locate the two qawls (opinions)
Find where the text states that there are two sayings regarding the khalwa of a minor who cannot have intercourse. The passage indicates this from al-Shamiyyah.
One opinion invalidates, the other validates.
3
Identify the tarjih (preference) and its source
See that Qadikhan chose the opinion of invalidity, making it mu'tamad (relied-upon). Note also that al-Dhakhirah restricts khalwa validity to those who have reached puberty.
Tarjih is based on the strength of the evidence or the authority of the preferring scholar.
4
Extend the ruling to analogous cases
Understand that a mad person is likened to a minor (without understanding), so his khalwa is also invalid. However, a sleeping person is treated differently; according to Abu Hanifa he has the ruling of an awake person, so khalwa may be valid if the wife enters knowingly.
This shows how analogy (qiyas) and distinction (tafriq) are used in fatwa.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah)
If an incapable husband dies after seclusion, the wife does not inherit because khalwa is invalid. If it were valid, she would inherit. This is the operative difference.
The mufti must apply the mu'tamad ruling in real cases.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling from a fatwa collection regarding the validity of khalwa (seclusion) in marriage for individuals who cannot physically consummate. The author establishes that for a minor who cannot have intercourse, khalwa is invalid according to the relied-upon opinion, as chosen by Qadikhan. The same applies to a mad person or imbecile, who are likened to a minor. The passage also discusses the case of a sleeping person: according to Abu Hanifah, a sleeping person has the status of an awake person, so khalwa can be valid if the wife enters while he sleeps and he would have known had he been awake. However, this is not universally agreed upon. The text emphasizes that insanity is not likened to sleep by the jurists, and thus the rulings differ. The author concludes by deferring to higher authority.
