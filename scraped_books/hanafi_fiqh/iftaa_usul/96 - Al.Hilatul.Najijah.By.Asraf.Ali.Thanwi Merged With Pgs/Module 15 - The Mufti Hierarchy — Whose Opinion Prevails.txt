Khiyar Faskh for Non-Kufu: The Virgin vs Thayyib Rule
Ruling on khiyar faskh for non-kufu: bikr vs thayyib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for khiyar faskh due to non-kufu to determine the effect of silence for a virgin versus a non-virgin woman.

Core Idea
A virgin's silence annuls her right to annul; a non-virgin's silence preserves it.

In Hanafi family law, the right to annul a marriage (khiyar faskh) for lack of equality (kufu) depends critically on the woman's marital history. This lesson clarifies the operative fatwa on how silence interacts with this right, based on the text of Ashraf Ali Thanwi. The distinction between a virgin (bikr) and a previously married woman (thayyib) is a fine but essential point for muftis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger for khiyar faskh
Khiyar faskh arises when a marriage is contracted with an explicit or implicit condition of kufu, and the husband is later found to be non-kufu.
The condition can be explicit (e.g., stated in the contract) or implicit (e.g., the wali relied on apparent status).
2
Recognize the woman's status
Determine if she is a virgin (bikr) or non-virgin (thayyib). This classification is key to the ruling.
A slave woman is treated like a free thayyib in this rule.
3
Apply the rule on silence
For a bikr: silence after learning the facts indicates consent and invalidates the option unless she objects immediately. For a thayyib: silence does not indicate consent; the option remains until explicit or tacit consent is found.
Tacit consent can be established through actions like the wali accepting mahr.
4
Note the same for slave woman
The text states that the ruling for a slave woman is identical to that of a free thayyib.
This is an important detail for completeness.
5
Connect to fatwa hierarchy
This ruling is the mufta bihi, based on Imam Abu Hanifa's view, as affirmed in Durr al-Mukhtar and Radd al-Muhtar.
The text explicitly says: 'والفتوى على قول الامام وعليه المتون قاطبة'.

This passage presents a ruling (rasm rule) on khiyar faskh in Nikah when a condition of kufu (equality) is made and breached. The text first establishes the Hanafi position: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the father (and grandfather) has authority to marry off minors even to non-kufu, unlike the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Wife's Apostasy: Dissolution or Forced Renewal?
Exposition of ikhtilaf on wife's apostasy and the relied-upon fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the effect of a wife's apostasy and explain why the later fatwa (Balkh) is preferred as mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A wife cannot use apostasy to escape marriage; the later Hanafi fatwa blocks this trick by forcing her to return to Islam and renew the marriage.

This passage tackles a critical refinement in Hanafi family law: does a wife's apostasy automatically dissolve her marriage? The Zahir al-Riwayah says yes, immediate separation. But later scholars—especially the mashayikh of Balkh—witnessed women using apostasy as a cheap trick to escape their husbands. Their fatwa changed the ruling: the judge forces her back to Islam and renews the marriage with her original husband. This lesson walks through the ikhtilaf, the rationale, and the mufta bihi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Zahir al-Riwayah position
The apparent madhhab (Zahir al-Riwayah) holds that apostasy by either spouse causes immediate separation (furqah) without divorce. This is based on the literal wording in classical texts like Al-Hidayah.
This position is the starting point but not the mufta bihi.
2
Recognize the problem: apostasy as a trick
Some women began apostatizing intentionally to dissolve the marriage and later remarry. This misuse of the ruling caused great harm—apostasy itself is a major sin, yet it became an 'easy' divorce.
The social and religious harm triggered a re-examination.
3
Learn the Balkh fatwa and its rationale
The mashayikh of Balkh and Samarqand ruled that if the wife apostatizes with the intent to escape marriage, separation does not occur. Instead, she is forced to return to Islam and renew the marriage with her first husband. This blocks the trick (hila) and preserves the marriage.
The maxim 'hardship brings ease' (al-mashaqqah tajlab al-taysir) supports this.
4
Examine the scope: forced renewal and judicial authority
Under this fatwa, the judge can renew the marriage with minimal effort (even a coin) and can impose a discretionary punishment (ta'zir) up to 75 lashes. The wife may not marry another; she must go back to the original husband.
The judge's role is central to implementing this fatwa.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its authority
Later authorities like Ibn al-Humam, al-Nahr, and Ibn Abidin (in Radd al-Muhtar) affirm that the Balkh fatwa is the relied-upon position. The Zahir al-Riwayah is not operative for fatwa in this scenario.
Tarjih is based on sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking pretexts) and public interest.

Commentary
This passage presents a crucial legal dispute within the Hanafi madhhab regarding the effect of a wife's apostasy (riddah) on her marriage. The author begins by warning against a simplistic reading of the ruling from Alamgiriyyah that states apostasy of either spouse causes immediate separation. Some people mistakenly applied this to wives, thinking any apostasy dissolves the marriage, and some women even used it as a trick to escape their husbands by apostatizing, only to later renew Islam and remarry someone else. The author clarifies that this is incorrect. The passage then details the ikhtilaf: The apparent madhhab (Zahir al-Riwayah) according to Imam Abu Hanifa is that apostasy causes immediate separation (furqah) without divorce. However, many later scholars, especially the mashayikh of Balkh and Samarqand, gave fatwa that if the wife apostatizes with the intent to escape marriage, separation does not occur. Instead, she is forced to return to Islam and renew the marriage with her original husband. This is to block the trick (hila) of using the major sin of apostasy to dissolve the marriage. The mashayikh of Bukhara favored immediate separation but also with forced renewal. The relied-upon fatwa (mufta bihi) according to later authorities like Ibn al-Humam, the author of al-Nahr, and others is the position of the mashayikh of Balkh: no separation, forced renewal. The passage also mentions that the judge can renew the marriage with the least amount (even a dinar) and can impose a discretionary punishment. The principle of 'hardship brings ease' (al-mashaqqah tajlab al-taysir) is invoked to support this fatwa, as implementing forced renewal is less burdensome than dealing with the consequences of immediate dissolution and the social harm. The later Hanafi jurist Ibn Abidin in Radd al-Muhtar debates this, noting that hardship in renewal doesn't automatically make the Balkh position preferable, but ultimately the fatwa stands due to supporting considerations.

Key Terms
ردة (riddah): Apostasy from Islam by a married woman.
فرقة (furqah): Legal separation between spouses, here specifically dissolution of marriage without divorce.
تجدید نکاح (tajdid al-nikah): Renewal of the marriage contract after the wife returns to Islam, required by the fatwa of the mashayikh of Balkh.
سد الذرائع (sadd al-dhara'i'): Blocking the means that lead to harm or sin. Here, blocking the trick of using apostasy to dissolve marriage.

Necessity of a Judge's Ruling for the Missing Person's Waiting Period
Discusses whether a judge's or Muslim community's ruling is required for the waiting period

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi procedural hierarchy for setting the waiting period of a missing person's wife and evaluate the role of a judge's ruling versus other authorities.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school requires a judge's ruling for the wife of a missing person to enter the waiting period; without it, the community's ruling is invalid if a judge exists.

This lesson examines a critical procedural question in Hanafi family law: is a judge's ruling necessary for the wife of a missing person to begin her waiting period (idda)? The passage from Thanwi's al-Hilah al-Najiyah discusses the hierarchy of authorities—judge, governor, community—and contrasts the Hanafi position with the Hanbali and Shafi'i schools. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for muftis handling missing person cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the procedural question and the Hanafi requirement
The passage first asks whether a judge's ruling or a community ruling is needed for the four-year waiting period. The Hanafi position, as expounded in the margin of Sharh al-Dardir, is that if a just judge exists, the wife must raise her matter to him; his ruling is valid. If she goes to the community instead, the ruling is invalid.
This establishes the primacy of the judge's authority in Hanafi procedure.
2
Distinguish the valid authorities in order of hierarchy
If no judge exists, the wife may raise her matter to the governor (wali) or the zakat collector (sa'i). If she goes to the community while these officials exist, the ruling is apparently valid only if the community is not oppressive and investigates her husband's condition. This shows a clear hierarchy: judge > governor/collector > community.
The passage also mentions an opinion on the fee of the emissary sent to search for the husband.
3
Compare the Hanafi duration with other schools
According to Hanafis, the wife is not divorced nor property inherited until after 70 years (or 120, 90, 80, 60) or according to the opinion of a Muslim judge. Hanbalis differentiate: 90 years if safe, 4 years if feared dead. Shafi'is have two views: old view (divorce after 4 years, inheritance after a long period), new view (no divorce or inheritance without proof).
The thamarah al-khilaf is the practical difference in waiting periods.
4
Recognize the importance of the judge's ijtihad
The Hanafi position allows the judge to set the duration based on ijtihad, not fixed at 70 years. The passage states 'او برأى حاكم المسلمين' (or according to the opinion of a Muslim judge). This flexibility is crucial for applying the rule in different contexts.
Students often miss this point and assume the 70-year period is absolute.

The Wife of a Missing Husband: Authorities and the Minimum Group Size

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the authorities to whom the wife of a missing husband may raise her case and apply the Hanafi qawida 'aqall al-jama'a thalatha' to determine the minimum number of just Muslims required.

Core Idea
The wife of a missing husband has three avenues of recourse, but the number of Muslim witnesses needed is contested between one, two, and three.

In this lesson, we examine a procedural rule from fatwa literature regarding the wife of a missing husband (mafqud). The passage clarifies that she may approach either the judge, the ruler, or a group of just Muslims to address her situation. However, scholars differ on the minimum number of Muslims required to constitute a valid group, invoking the well-known qawida 'aqall al-jama'a thalatha' (the minimum of a group is three). We will parse the text, understand the dispute, and see how the Hanafi fatwa reconciles linguistic principle with practical facilitation.

Identify the authorities
