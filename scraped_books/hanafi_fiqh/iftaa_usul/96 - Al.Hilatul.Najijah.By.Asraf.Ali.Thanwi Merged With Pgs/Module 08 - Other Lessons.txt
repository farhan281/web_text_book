Ruling on Muslim Woman's Marriage to a Non-Muslim and Apostasy's Effect
Ruling on interfaith marriage and apostasy effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on interfaith marriage and explain the effect of apostasy on an existing marriage.

Core Idea
A Muslim woman's marriage to any non-Muslim is absolutely forbidden; apostasy does not automatically annul the marriage.

This passage from Asraf Ali Thanwi's treatise presents two key rulings every mufti must memorize: the absolute prohibition of a Muslim woman marrying a non-Muslim, and the subtle effect of apostasy on an existing marriage. While apostasy makes the marriage forbidden, it does not automatically dissolve it in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding these rulings is essential for issuing correct fatwas on marriage and apostasy cases.

Guided Path
1
Extract the two prohibitions
Identify the two rulings: (1) a Muslim woman marrying any non-Muslim is absolutely forbidden; (2) a Muslim man marrying a non-Muslim woman other than a Kitabiyya is invalid and forbidden.
The passage explicitly states both.
2
Understand the exception for men
Note that a Muslim man may marry a Kitabiyya (Jewish/Christian) woman. The passage mentions 'other than Kitabiyya' implying this exception.
Kitabiyya is the only permissible non-Muslim wife for a Muslim man.
3
Analyze the effect of apostasy
The passage states that a Muslim woman's apostasy does not annul the marriage; it becomes forbidden until she returns to Islam during 'iddah. If she does not return, the marriage becomes permanently invalid.
The phrase 'نکاح فسخ نہیں ہوتا' indicates no annulment.
4
Memorize the key terms
Ensure you remember that 'kafir' includes all non-Muslims for the woman's prohibition, and 'kitabiyya' is the only permissible category for men.
These terms are central to the ruling.

Essential Conditions for a Shari'ah Judge (Qadi)
List of necessary qualifications for a qadi according to Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five essential conditions for a valid qadi in Hanafi fiqh and apply the exception for an ignorant qadi who consults scholars.

Core Idea
A qadi must be Muslim, adult, sane, with sound senses, appointed without bribery, and knowledgeable—but an ignorant appointee's judgments can be valid if based on scholar advice.

In Islamic law, the position of qadi carries immense responsibility. Hanafi jurists outline specific conditions to ensure justice. This passage from Ashraf Ali Thanwi's work summarizes the key shurut for a qadi, along with a practical exception that allows a less qualified judge to function if he properly consults experts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition (Islam)
The text states the qadi must be Muslim; a non-Muslim cannot judge Muslims. This is a fundamental requirement.
2
Identify the Mukallaf condition
The qadi must be adult and sane (mukallaf). A child or insane person is not qualified.
3
Identify physical soundness
The qadi must have working eyes, ears, and tongue. Being blind, deaf, or mute disqualifies him.
4
Identify appointment integrity
The qadi cannot have bribed for the position. If he did, his appointment is void and his verdicts are not enforceable.
5
Identify the knowledge condition and exception
The qadi must possess complete expertise in religious knowledge. However, an ignorant person who becomes qadi and issues a correct ruling after consulting scholars can have that ruling enforced.
This exception prevents disruption of judicial processes while maintaining the ideal of competence.

Distinction Between Impotence and Insanity in Dropping Khiyar
Ruling on khiyar al-ayb with distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule distinguishing when a woman's submission to intercourse (tamkeen) drops her option of annulment based on whether the husband's defect is apparent (insanity) or hidden (impotence).

Core Idea
Consent is inferred from submission only when the defect is apparent; hidden defects require explicit consent.

This passage from Ashraf Ali Thanwi's Al-Hilatul Najijah clarifies a key principle in khiyar al-ayb: when a woman knowingly submits to intercourse with a defective husband, does she lose her right to annul? The answer depends on whether the defect is apparent or hidden. The text distinguishes between the impotent man (al-mu'tarid), whose defect is hidden, and the insane man (al-majnun), whose defect is apparent. The ruling has practical implications for issuing fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is a woman's tamkeen (enabling/submission) to intercourse after she knows of her husband's defect. The ruling question: Does this submission drop her khiyar al-ayb (option to annul)?
2
Distinguish the Two Defects
Impotence (inn) is a hidden defect – its presence is not externally observable. Insanity (junun) is an apparent defect – it is manifest to the senses, especially in its severe form.
The text emphasizes that insanity is ظاهر (apparent), while impotence is not.
3
Determine the Legal Consequence
For impotence: tamkeen does NOT drop the option because it may be exploratory (testing ability), not consent. For insanity: tamkeen after knowledge DOES drop the option because it indicates consent to remain with the defective husband.
The reasoning is that one cannot immediately intend to enable an impotent man to test him, so the act is ambiguous.
4
Note the Conditions
The woman must have knowledge of the defect at the time of tamkeen. If she does not know, her option remains. Also, tamkeen must be voluntary – forced intercourse does not drop the option.
The text specifically conditions on 'knowing' (عالما بہ) and 'enabling' (مكنتہ), not force.

This passage is a ruling (rasm) about the option of defect (khiyar al-ayb) in the Hanafi madhhab, specifically focusing on the distinction between the impotent man (al-mu'tarrif/al-'inn) and the insane man (al-majnun). The commentator (Allamah Dardir) clarifies that enabling does not indicate acceptance in this case because the motive is not pure willingness but to ascertain the condition. In contrast, for the insane man, insanity is apparent, so if the woman knows of his insanity and willingly enables him, her action indicates consent to remain with him, thereby dropping her option to annul the marriage. The text also addresses a scenario where the insane man forces intercourse: the use of the word 'enabled' implies that forced intercourse does not drop the option. Establishing Ḥurmāt Muṣāharah through Illicit Acts
Ruling on ḥurmāt muṣāharah via zina, touch, kiss, or look with desire

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four acts that establish ḥurmāt muṣāharah, list the conditions for each (desire, barrier, lack of inzal), and apply the ruling to a case presented by a mustafti.

Core Idea
Illicit touch, kiss, look, or intercourse with desire creates permanent marriage prohibition with the other's ascendants and descendants.

The Hanafi school details precise acts that give rise to ḥurmāt muṣāharah, a permanent barrier to marriage. This lesson presents the core rule along with its conditions and exceptions as stated in classical texts. Understanding these details is essential for a mufti when advising on marriage prohibitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four acts
List the four acts that can establish ḥurmāt muṣāharah: zina (illicit intercourse), lams (touching with desire), taqbīl (kissing with desire), and naẓar (looking with desire at private parts).
Acts are not limited to intercourse.
2
Determine the effect
State who becomes forbidden: all ascendants and descendants (by lineage and fosterage) of the woman become forbidden to the man, and vice versa.
Effect is permanent, like blood relations.
3
Apply the conditions
For looking, only the looker must have desire. For touching/kissing, desire of either party is sufficient. Also, no thick cloth barrier may prevent heat sensation, and no inzal (ejaculation) must have occurred from the act.
Intention is not required, but desire is necessary.
4
Recognize exceptions
If inzal occurs from kissing/looking, prohibition is not established. Also, a thick barrier prevents prohibition for touching/kissing.
These are preventers (mawāniʿ).

Prohibition by marriage-affinity: If a person commits zina with a woman, or simply touches her with desire, or kisses her with desire, or looks with desire at the inner part of the private parts, then in all these cases hurmāt muṣāharah becomes established. That is, for that man, the woman's daughter, mother, etc., all ascendants and descendants by lineage and fosterage become forbidden. And for that woman, the man's son and father, all ascendants and descendants by lineage and fosterage become forbidden. Similarly, if a woman touches a man with desire, or kisses him with desire, or looks with desire at his private part, then also the relationship of muṣāharah is established, and the man's all ascendants and descendants by lineage and fosterage become permanently forbidden, and likewise the woman's all ascendants and descendants become permanently forbidden. For the establishment of hurmāt muṣāharah, it is not a condition that these acts be done intentionally. Rather, even if it happens unknowingly, the ruling is the same. Regarding the desire of the woman: in the case of touching and kissing, if the man had no desire but the woman had desire, the same ruling applies. Similarly, if the woman touched or kissed, it is sufficient that one of them had desire. However, for looking to cause prohibition, the condition is that the one who looks must have desire; desire only on the other side does not cause prohibition. Also, in touching and kissing, there is a condition that there be no cloth barrier that prevents feeling the heat of the body. So if someone, despite such a cloth being a barrier, touched or kissed over the cloth, that does not cause hurmāt muṣāharah. Another condition is that inzal (penetration) does not occur from these acts. Rather, if inzal occurs from kissing and looking only, then hurmāt muṣāharah is not established.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed ruling on hurmāt muṣāharah (prohibition through marriage-affinity) in the Hanafi school. It lists acts that establish this permanent prohibition: zina, touching with desire, kissing with desire, and looking with desire at private parts. The effect is that all ascendants and descendants (by lineage or fosterage) of the woman become forbidden to the man, and vice versa. Importantly, intention is not required; even if the act occurs unknowingly, the prohibition applies if desire is present. For touching and kissing, it is sufficient that either party has desire; however, for looking, only the desire of the looker counts. Further, there must be no thick cloth barrier preventing the sensation of heat. Additionally, if inzal (ejaculation) occurs from these acts, the prohibition does not take effect. The author does not cite proofs here, but this ruling is based on Hanafi fiqh principles.

Maliki Conditions for the Wife of a Missing Husband
Exposition of Maliki conditions for remarriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the Maliki conditions for the wife of a missing husband to remarry, and explain why these conditions constitute a legal stratagem (hila).

Core Idea
Maliki school: court case, proof, four-year wait, 'iddah = no easy remarriage.

In this passage, a Hanafi scholar corrects a common misconception about the Maliki ruling on the wife of a missing person. Many think the Maliki school makes remarriage easy, but in reality it imposes strict conditions: a court case, proof of marriage and disappearance, a judicial investigation, a four-year waiting period after the judge gives up hope, and an 'iddah of four months and ten days. This lesson unpacks these conditions as a legal stratagem (hila) for the woman to exit the marriage validly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The wife of a missing husband (mafqud al-khabar) is trapped in a marriage with no way to remarry without clarity on her husband's status.
The problem is both legal and personal.
2
List the Maliki conditions from the text
Go through the first answer in the passage: court case, testimony (including hearsay), proof of disappearance, judge's investigation, four-year wait, and 'iddah.
These are the shurut (conditions) for the hila.
3
Understand the judge's role
The judge must investigate personally before ordering the four-year waiting period. This is a key safeguard.
Judicial involvement is essential.
4
Note unresolved questions
The text raises questions about dar al-harb vs dar al-islam and absence of a judge, but does not fully answer them. Reflect on the implications.
This shows the hila is context-dependent.
5
Recognize the hila nature
The passage is presenting the Maliki ruling as a permissible way (hila) for the woman to exit the marriage, not as the only or preferred ruling.
The author is a Hanafi using Maliki fiqh for practical benefit.

Khiyar al-Faskh for Minors Married to Non-Kufu: Silence and Consent
Ruling with conditions on khiyar al-faskh for minors married to non-kufu

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on khiyar al-faskh for minors when a guardian marries them to a non-kufu, and apply the conditions under which the option expires or persists.

Core Idea
Silence never implies consent; the option of annulment remains until express or implied consent is given.

This passage from Ashraf Ali Thanwi's Al-Hilatul Najijah clarifies a critical point in Hanafi marriage law: the option to annul a marriage (khiyar al-faskh) for a minor married to a non-kufu is not lost by mere silence. Both the guardian and the minor have specific rights that require active consent to become binding. We will parse the sixth case to understand the exact conditions and the borderline scenario left to scholarly discretion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle on Silence
The author begins by stating that silence does not invalidate the option of annulment; only express (sarih) or implied (dalalah) consent does. Recognize that this is a foundational rule for khiyar in Hanafi fiqh.
Implied consent is exemplified by taking possession of the dower (mahr).
2
Parse the Sixth Case: Minor Married to Non-Kufu by Guardian
Focus on the scenario: a father or grandfather marries a minor to someone thought to be kufu based on the person's statement or a stipulated condition, but later discovered non-kufu. Note the two sub-scenarios: (a) with condition/statement, (b) without (guardian's assumption).
This case is specifically about khiyar al-faskh for kufu deficiency.
3
Determine Who Has the Option Before and After Maturity
Before maturity: only the guardian (father/grandfather) has the option. After maturity: both the now-adult minor and the guardian acquire the option. The marriage becomes binding only if both consent (actively, not by silence).
If the guardian approves after learning the truth, the marriage becomes binding immediately.
4
Analyze the Borderline Case (Guardian’s Assumption)
When the guardian married the minor based on his own assumption that the spouse was kufu (no statement or condition), the author indicates that according to the rules, the option should not be given. However, he leaves the final determination to scholars reviewing the details.
This reflects the caution of later Hanafi scholars in applying khiyar al-faskh.
5
Apply to Fatwa Practice (Mufti’s Role)
As a mufti, you must determine whether the conditions for khiyar al-faskh are met. The key distinction is whether the guardian had a reasonable basis (statement or condition) or merely assumed. In the latter case, the default ruling is no option, but exceptions may apply based on circumstances.

Legal Significance of an Acknowledgment (Iqrar Namah) for Arbitration
Sample form for unilateral acknowledgment of arbitration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the essential elements of a valid arbitration acknowledgment in Hanafi law and apply them to construct a fatwa regarding the binding nature of a qadi's decision.

Core Idea
A valid acknowledgment of arbitration requires free consent, capacity, and specific scope; it makes the qadi's decision binding.

This lesson examines a sample acknowledgment form used to appoint a Shariah arbitrator in a divorce case. The document embodies key Hanafi principles of iqrar (acknowledgment) and tahkim (arbitration). Understanding its structure equips a mufti to assess the validity of such agreements and advise on enforceability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the dispute
The form states the case is a divorce/annulment suit filed by one party. Identify the declarant (Tahir Husain) and the defendant.
The dispute must be clearly stated for the arbitration to be specific.
2
Locate the acknowledgment of authority
Find the phrase where the declarant accepts the qadi's decision as binding. This is the core iqrar.
The acceptance must be explicit and unconditional.
3
Check conditions for validity
The form mentions full mental capacity, consciousness, and lack of coercion. These are required for a valid iqrar in Hanafi law.
Iqrar of a sane adult made voluntarily is binding.
4
Examine the scope of arbitration
The qadi is given full authority to decide according to Shariah. The scope must cover the entire dispute.
If the scope is limited, the arbitrator cannot go beyond it.
5
Note the procedural context
The form is filed within a Shariah committee/court, indicating the arbitration is conducted under institutional auspices.
Institution-based arbitration may have additional rules.

Understanding the Hanafi Affidavit for Arbitrator Appointment (Hila Case)
Presents a sample legal document for appointing an arbitrator in a Shari'ah committee case.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the required conditions (shurut) for a valid arbitration appointment (tahkim) in Hanafi fiqh as reflected in this affidavit, and apply those conditions to evaluate a similar modern case.

Core Idea
A voluntary, informed, and written appointment of an arbitrator is valid and binding under Hanafi law, preventing subsequent court challenges.

This lesson presents a sample legal document (إقرار نامہ تقرر ثالث) from Maulana Ashraf Ali Thanwi's Al-Hilat al-Najiyah, a collection of legal stratagems (hiyal) in the Hanafi tradition. The document is a template for a party to a Shari'ah committee case to formally appoint the qadi as arbitrator and waive their right to appeal in civil courts. By parsing this document, you will learn the key conditions for a valid arbitration (tahkim) and how Hanafi jurists construct legal instruments to secure adherence to Shari'ah rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their consent
Examine the declarant (طاہر حسین) and the arbitrator (qadi). Note the text emphasizes free will (بلا جبر) and sound mind (ثبات عقل). These are essential for a valid tahkim.
In Hanafi law, coercion invalidates any contract, including arbitration.
2
Locate the arbitration clause
The declarant agrees to accept whatever decision the qadi gives (جو بھی فیصلہ ... قبول و منظور ہو گا). This indicates binding consent to the arbitrator's ruling.
3
Examine the waiver of appeal
The document states any challenge in civil courts would be invalid and false. This is a crucial effect: the parties relinquish their right to secular judicial review.
4
Note the formal requirements
The note advises writing on stamp paper and registering with a registrar for enforceability. This strengthens the document's legal force.
Registration is recommended but not a condition for validity in Hanafi tahkim.
5
Assess the Hanafi conditions for tahkim
Relate each element to Hanafi shurut: the arbitrator must be qualified (like a qadi), the dispute must be arbitrable (here, a marriage dissolution case), and both parties must consent freely.
The document must be witnessed (دستخط گواه) as additional evidence of consent.

This template serves as a legal stratagem (hila) to ensure that the parties submit to Shari'ah jurisdiction and avoid the state legal system. The document reflects Hanafi principles of arbitration (tahkim) where parties voluntarily agree to a qadi's jurisdiction.
