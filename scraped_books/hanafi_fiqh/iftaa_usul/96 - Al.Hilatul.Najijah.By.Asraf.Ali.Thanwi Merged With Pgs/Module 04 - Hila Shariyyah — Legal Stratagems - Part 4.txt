Classifying the Life-Imprisoned Husband: Gha'ib Ghair Mafqud
Fatwa query and response on wives of life prisoners

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a life-imprisoned husband under Hanafi fiqh as gha'ib ghair mafqud and identify the practical legal consequences for his wife.

Core Idea
A prisoner husband whose location is known is not missing — he is an absent party with known whereabouts, and existing rulings apply.

This passage presents a fatwa response to a recurring modern problem: wives of men sentenced to life imprisonment. The questioner asks for a shar'i solution, fearing the wife may fall into sin. The respondent clarifies that since the prisoner's location is known, he falls under the category of gha'ib ghair mafqud, not mafqud. Therefore, the existing legal mechanisms for dissolution of marriage due to the husband's absence are sufficient, and no new ruling is needed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Query
Read the question to understand the situation: a husband imprisoned for life, the wife may or may not receive maintenance, and she is at risk of adultery.
2
Locate the Key Distinction
Find where the respondent classifies the imprisoned husband. The phrase 'when his whereabouts are known, then he is an absent person who is not missing' is the key.
3
Extract the Legal Principle
Understand the criterion: mafqud (missing) is defined by unknown whereabouts. Since the prison location is known, the husband is gha'ib ghair mafqud.
4
Apply the Principle
Note that the respondent refers to existing rulings in the treatise, indicating that the established rules for absent husbands apply. No new category is created.
5
Note the Practical Outcome
The wife can seek a religious way out (makhlas shar'i) through existing mechanisms like khula' or judicial divorce, provided she proves harm.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling (hukm) issued in response to a query about wives of men sentenced to life imprisonment. The questioner, Khair Muhammad of Jalandhar, describes a common problem: husbands imprisoned for life, and their wives may or may not receive maintenance. In either case, the wife suffers from enforced celibacy and is at risk of committing adultery. He asks for a shar'i solution. The respondent, Husayn Ali ibn Muhammad ibn Abdullah (likely a scholar of the Hanafi school), answers: since the husband's whereabouts are known (the prison), he is not a missing person (mafqud) but rather an absent person whose location is known (gha'ib ghair mafqud). Therefore, the existing rulings in the treatise 'Al-Hilat al-Najizah' under the section 'Hukm al-Gha'ib Ghair al-Mafqud' apply to these wives. No new ruling is needed; the wife can seek dissolution of marriage through the court or khula' under the conditions already specified for an absent husband. The Hanafi position is that a wife of an absent husband can seek judicial separation if she suffers harm, but the exact conditions are detailed in the treatise. The authority weight is that of a fatwa from a later scholar, building upon the earlier work of Thanwi. The ruling clarifies that imprisonment does not create a new category; it falls under the existing category of gha'ib ghair mafqud.

Key Terms
غائب غیر مفقود (gha'ib ghair mafqud): An absent person whose location is known (e.g., imprisoned, traveling with known address). The opposite of mafqud (missing).
مخلصی شرعی (makhlas shar'i): A legally permissible way out of a difficult situation, such as divorce or khula'.

Why No Hila for the Wife of a Known Prisoner?
Correspondence on wife of lifelong prisoner

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key distinction between gha'ib ghair mafqud and mafqud, and apply the Hanafi principle that a hila is ineffective when the other party has power to resist.

Core Idea
A hila only works when the other party cannot resist; a known prisoner's wife has no hila because the husband retains legal capacity.

A recurring dilemma in Muslim family law is the plight of a wife whose husband is imprisoned for a very long term. This passage from the appendices of Al-Hilat al-Najiza presents a correspondence that clarifies the Hanafi position: such a wife cannot resort to a legal stratagem (hila) to dissolve her marriage because her husband's whereabouts are known and he retains legal capacity to oppose any such stratagem. The key is the classification of the husband as gha'ib ghair mafqud (absent but not missing) versus mafqud (missing), which determines the availability of relief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
Read the query: a wife whose husband is imprisoned for life or a very long term, with or without maintenance. She faces hardship and risk of sin.
The problem is emotionally and legally heavy.
2
Classify the Husband
Determine whether the husband is gha'ib ghair mafqud (whereabouts known) or mafqud (whereabouts unknown). The passage treats known prisoners as gha'ib ghair mafqud.
This classification is decisive for Hanafi rulings.
3
Apply the Rule on Hila
The response states that no hila is effective because 'the other party has more power' (husband can resist). Understand that a hila requires the other party's inability to prevent it.
The husband's legal capacity and knowledge make him able to resist.
4
Conclude the Fatwa
The wife remains married; there is no quick stratagem. She may explore other options (e.g., judicial divorce for lack of maintenance), but those are separate from hila.
The mufti must avoid creating false hope.

The passage is a practical illustration of the limits of hila in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Learning Objective

A prisoner whose location is known is not mafqud; only when location is unknown does the ruling of mafqud apply.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Look for the terms gha'ib (absent) and mafqud (missing) in the passage. The passage states that a prisoner whose location is known is gha'ib, while one whose location is unknown is mafqud.
The distinction is based solely on knowledge or ignorance of the husband's whereabouts.
2
Understand the legal effect
For a gha'ib, the wife's situation is different from a mafqud. The passage references separate detailed treatises for mafqud rulings, indicating that the rules for missing persons are more complex and involve waiting periods and potential remarriage after a set time.
The query asks about a prisoner of 14-15 years; if his location is known, he is gha'ib, not mafqud.
3
Recognize the practical implication

This illustrates the adab of fatwa: thorough investigation before issuing a ruling.

Validity of Seclusion with an Insane Person (Majnoon)
Critical analysis of a margin note on seclusion with an insane person.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi ruling on the validity of seclusion with an insane person and apply the qiyas (analogy) used in this passage to defend the ruling against objections.

Core Idea
An insane person's seclusion is valid because he retains sexual desire, just like a sleeping person.

A marginal note in Al-Hilatul Najijah questioned whether seclusion (khalwah) with an insane husband triggers legal effects like dowry and 'iddah. This lesson walks through the Hanafi analysis, which defends validity by analogizing the insane to a sleeping person and distinguishing from a minor. We will parse the key attributes and understand why duration of unconsciousness does not affect the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The passage addresses whether seclusion with an insane person (majnun) is valid (sahihah) to establish legal effects such as dowry and 'iddah.
This issue arises from a margin note on page 25 of the original text.
2
Parse the key attributes
Identify three attributes: sexual desire (shahwah), consciousness (shu'ur), and duration. The minor (saghir) lacks desire, the sleeper (na'im) lacks consciousness temporarily, and the insane lacks consciousness permanently but retains desire.
The author cites Imam Muhammad's classification.
3
Compare with sleeper and minor
The seclusion of a minor is invalid due to lack of desire. The sseclusion of a sleeper is valid because desire remains despite temporary unconsciousness. The insane person, like the sleeper, has desire but lacks consciousness.
The passage argues that the key factor is desire, not consciousness.
4
Analyze the qiyas (analogy)
The asl (original case) is the sleeper; the far' (new case) is the insane. The illa (effective cause) is the presence of sexual desire and the absence of a permanent physical incapacity. The objection based on duration is dismissed as legally irrelevant.
The analogy is strengthened by noting that the seclusion of a murahiq (near-adult) is valid even though he is not mukallaf, showing that legal capacity is not a condition.
5
Evaluate the opposing view and Hanafi response
Some scholars (qila yamna'an) hold that insanity prevents valid seclusion, comparing the insane to a vicious dog. The Hanafi response: the sleeper analogy is stronger, and the vicious dog comparison is overruled by the presence of desire in the insane person.
The passage concludes that the mufta bihi is the validity of seclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of a margin comment in Al-Hilatul Najijah regarding whether seclusion (khalwah) with an insane person (majnun) is legally valid (sahihah) to establish effects like the obligatory dowry or 'iddah. The author, a Hanafi scholar, defends the position that the seclusion of an insane person is valid by strong analogy (qiyas) with a sleeping person (na'im), despite objections based on the difference in duration. The discussion engages with Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's classification of the insane person as resembling a minor (saghir) in some aspects and a sleeper in others. The commentator argues that the key factor is the presence of sexual desire (shahwah) and the capacity for intercourse, or at least the absence of a permanent physical impediment. Since the insane person has desire and is physically capable (unlike a minor who lacks desire, or a sleeping person who temporarily lacks consciousness), seclusion is valid. The analogy with sleep is defended by pointing out that the quick reversibility of sleep does not affect the legal ruling, as evidenced by the Hanafi stance that sleep does not prevent the validity of seclusion. Furthermore, the commentator distinguishes between sleep and insanity: while some scholars consider sleep a preventer due to loss of consciousness, insanity is not a preventer because its duration does not negate the potential for sexual activity. The passage concludes that the relied-upon position is the validity of seclusion with an insane person, aligning with the Hanafi school's methodology of giving precedence to analogical reasoning over superficial differences.

Key Terms
خلوت صحیحہ (khalwah sahihah): A valid seclusion between a legally married couple where there is no physical or legal impediment to intercourse, even if actual intercourse does not occur. It has the same legal effects as actual intercourse in terms of dowry, 'iddah, and lineage.
مجنون (majnun): An insane person who lacks intellectual capacity ('aql) but may retain physical desires and capabilities.
نائم (na'im): A sleeping person who temporarily lacks consciousness but retains physical desires and capabilities.

Explaining and defending Hanafi hiyal for qada in dar al-kufr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi forms for establishing qada under non-Muslim rule and explain how the tadamun al-kafir objection is resolved using the distinction between investiture and condition.

Core Idea
Non-Muslim rule does not nullify qada; Muslims appoint via mutual consent or a wali, using the ruler as a messenger for executive power.

This passage comes from a scholarly exchange discussing how to establish Islamic judiciary (qada) in lands where a non-Muslim government holds power. The critic objects that appointment by a disbeliever contradicts Quran 4:141. The Hanafi response clarifies that the appointment is merely a condition for executive power, not true authority. Understanding this reveals the Hanafi method of using legal stratagems (hiyal) to preserve essential Islamic institutions under constraint.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two Hanafi forms
Locate the two forms mentioned: (1) a qualified Muslim becomes qadi by mutual consent of Muslims (bassir al-qadi qadiyan bi-taradi al-muslimin); (2) Muslims collectively fund a wali who then appoints a qadi (an yunfiqu 'ala wahid yaj'alunahu waliyan fa-yuwalli qadiyan). These are the proposed hiyal.
The forms are described by the author as existing in the recipient's treatise but omitted.
2
Understand the objection (tadamun al-kafir)
The objector argues that the non-Muslim ruler's appointment gives authority to disbelievers over believers, citing Quran 4:141: 'Allah will never give the disbelievers a way over the believers.' This is the core of the objection.
The term 'tadamun al-kafir' refers to the non-Muslim ruler's sponsorship or guarantee of the qadi's position.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The author distinguishes between investiture (tawliyat al-qada) and a condition for executive power. The ruler's appointment is not a true delegation of authority; rather, it removes the obstacle of executive power. The real delegation comes from the Muslim community, and their silence constitutes consent.
The response uses the concept of 'quwwat tanfidh' (executive power) as a separate element.
4

The author mentions that Shams al-A'imma justified a similar case where a qadi appointed by a rebel (baghi) is valid. This analogy strengthens the Hanafi position by showing precedent from within the school.
This qiyas is found in the margin of Tamh al-Rifaq.
5
Derive the fatwa consequence
The practical outcome (thamarah al-khilaf) is that these hiyal are valid and mufta bihi. Muslims facing non-Muslim rule can legally establish qada using these stratagems, and the qadi's rulings are enforceable.
The passage indicates that the Hanafi position is supported by major works (Shami, Bahr, Nahr, Fath al-Qadir).

The Shar‘i Arbitration Agreement: A Hila for Enforcing Shari‘ah Decisions
Sample legal document for shari'ah arbitration agreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid hila to enforce shari‘ah arbitration and identify its conditions for enforceability under both shari‘ah and secular law.

Core Idea
A written, registered arbitration agreement can bind parties to shari‘ah rulings and prevent them from resorting to civil courts.

In Muslim-minority contexts like India, shari‘ah decisions risk being disregarded when parties appeal to secular courts. The Hanafi jurists recognized the need for legal stratagems (hiyal) that are shari‘ah-compliant yet practically effective. This passage presents a concrete hila: a binding arbitration agreement (iqrar nama) that, when registered, acquires legal force under state law. The lesson unpacks the conditions and mechanisms of this hila.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
The core problem: a party may lose a case before a shari'ah committee and then file an appeal in a civil court, undermining the shari'ah ruling.
This is a common scenario in plural legal systems.
2
Recognize the Hila
The hila is a pre-emptive written agreement (iqrar nama) wherein the parties declare that any challenge to the shari'ah decision in civil court is invalid and false.
The agreement is a voluntary contract (ta'aqud) with binding terms.
3
Determine Enforceability Conditions
The hila recommends: (1) write it on stamp paper by a petition writer, (2) preferably register it with the registrar to gain legal teeth under state law.
Registration makes it a legally binding contract under the Arbitration and Conciliation Act.
4
Evaluate Shari'ah Validity
The hila is valid because it is a mutual agreement (rida) and does not involve any haram. It avoids the greater evil of ignoring shari'ah rulings.
Hanafi usul: al-umur bi-maqasidiha — the intention is to uphold shari'ah.

نوٹ : واضح رہے کہ یہ مندرجہ بالا اقرار نامہ عرائض نویس سے اقرار نامہ کے اسٹامپ پر تحریر کرایا جائے اور زیادہ بہتر یہ ہے کہ اقرار نامہ کو رجسٹرار کے یہاں رجسٹرڈ کرالیا جائے ۔ تو اس اقرار نامہ کی رُو سے کوئی فریق شرعی کمیٹی کے فیصلہ کا قانون حکومت کی سے بھی خلاف کرنے کا مجاز نہ ہوگا۔ (خورشید حسن قاسمی)
The person making the agreement shall not avoid the decision of the qadi or the decision of the shari'ah committee and shall not file any case or appeal in any court of India against the decision of the qadi; if he does, it will be invalid and false. Therefore, this document is written as an arbitration agreement with full consent and willingness, so that it may be a proof and useful when needed.

Source pages p. 377

Commentary
This passage provides a practical legal stratagem (hila) to enforce shari'ah arbitration and prevent parties from resorting to secular civil courts in India. The document is an iqrar nama (declaration) in which the person binding himself agrees not to challenge the decision of a qadi or a shari'ah committee in any Indian court. Any such challenge is declared invalid and false. The text then gives instructions: the agreement should be written on stamp paper by a petition writer, and preferably registered with the registrar. Registration gives it legal force under Indian law, so that no party can violate the shari'ah committee's decision by appealing to government law. This is a complete hila: the problem is the potential violation of shari'ah rulings by going to civil courts; the solution is a binding, registered agreement that precludes such action. The note by Khurshid Hasan Qasimi emphasizes that registration ensures enforcement.

Key Terms
اقرار نامہ (iqrar nama): A written declaration or agreement, in this context an arbitration agreement binding the parties to abide by shari'ah decisions.
فیصلہ قاضی (faisala qazi): The decision of a qadi (Islamic judge), which the parties agree to accept as final.
شرعی کمیٹی (shar'i committee): A committee of Islamic scholars appointed to resolve disputes according to shari'ah.
