Responding to the Objection of Islam's Inadequacy in Judicial Matters
Response to an objection against Islam's sufficiency in judiciary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct locus of responsibility when Islamic legal institutions are absent and apply the principle that Islam's obligation is only to provide guidance, not to enforce it.

Core Idea
Islam provides the guidance; Muslims bear the burden of implementation.

Objectors claim that Islam fails to offer solutions for disputes in the absence of a Sharia judge. But this confuses the role of Islam with the actions of Muslims. The author clarifies that Islam's duty is to prescribe solutions; Muslims must strive to appoint qualified judges. If they fail, the fault lies with them, not with the religion.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
Identify the claim that Islam is inadequate because it cannot resolve disputes without a Sharia judge.
The objection blames Islam for a lack of practical implementation.
2
Clarify the role of Islam
Explain that Islam functions to show the way (tadbīr) and does not enforce it. Muslims must act upon the guidance.
The author emphasizes that Islam's work is only to provide the solution.
3
Identify the responsibility of Muslims
Muslims are obligated to strive to appoint qualified rulers/judges or ensure that unqualified ones seek fatwas from scholars.
The author suggests practical steps: demanding from government, or binding the ruler to consult scholars.
4
Apply the Quranic principle
The verse 'No soul bears the burden of another' (Quran 6:164, etc.) supports that blame for failure falls on Muslims, not on Islam.
This principle is used to argue that objecting to Islam for Muslims' shortcomings is unjust.
5
Conclude the refutation
Thus the objection is misdirected; it is against the Quranic principle to blame Islam for human shortcomings.
The author implies that the objection itself violates Islamic teachings.

Faskh due to Husband's Poverty: Hanafi Position vs. Other Schools
Comparing Hanafi and other schools on faskh due to husband's poverty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi position on faskh due to husband's poverty by comparing its dalil with the opposing schools and apply the relevant fatwa hierarchy.

Core Idea
Poverty does not break the marriage bond in the Hanafi school; patience is prescribed.

When a husband cannot provide maintenance due to poverty, is the wife entitled to annul the marriage? This question has been debated among the schools. The Hanafi school, based on a clear Qur'anic verse, holds that no annulment is possible, while the majority allow it under certain conditions. In this lesson, we will analyze the proofs, understand the thamarah al-khilaf, and learn how a mufti should approach this issue.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling of the Hanafi school: no faskh for the wife if the husband is unable to provide nafaqah due to poverty.
The wife must be patient and cannot seek dissolution.
2
Identify the opposing positions and their proofs
List the schools that allow faskh (Ali, Umar, Abu Hurairah, Malik, Shafi'i, Ahmad, Zahiris) and the proofs they rely on, such as the hadith 'La darar wa la dirar'.
The text mentions that the first opinion has stronger proof, but does not detail the hadith.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
Explain how the Hanafis rebut the opposing view: the husband's inability is not blameworthy; Allah does not oblige beyond capacity; the verse commands patience.
The Hanafi proof is the Qur'anic text 'Allah does not burden a soul beyond what He has given it'.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
State the practical consequence: Hanafis do not allow faskh, so the marriage continues; others allow it with conditions (e.g., after a deadline).
5

Commentary
This passage presents the legal debate on whether a wife can seek annulment (faskh) of her marriage when her husband fails to provide maintenance (nafaqah) due to poverty. The Hanafi position, which is the author's madhhab, denies the right to faskh in this situation. The Hanafi proof is the Qur'anic verse (Surah al-Talaq, 65:7) 'Allah does not burden a soul except with what He has given it.' Since the husband is incapable, he is not obliged to spend, and his non-compliance does not constitute a sin or a valid ground for separation. The wife is instead encouraged to be patient and await ease. In contrast, the majority of the Sahaba (Ali, Umar, Abu Hurairah) and later imams (Malik, Shafi'i, Ahmad, Zahiris) allowed faskh, albeit with differences on the time limit: Malik allowed a month or two, Shafi'i three days. The passage also discusses details of the nature of the divorce (whether it is revocable) and the conditions for the validity of revocation if the husband later becomes able. This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on avoiding harm to the husband while protecting the wife's rights through patience rather than dissolution. The authority weight of the Hanafi ruling is strong as it is based on explicit Qur'anic text and consistent with the principle of legal capacity.

Key Terms
النفقة (al-nafaqah): Maintenance that the husband must provide for his wife, including food, clothing, and shelter within his capacity.
الفسخ (al-faskh): Annulment of the marriage contract by a judge due to a defect or inability to fulfill obligations, such as non-payment of maintenance.
الإعسار (al-i'sar): The husband's financial inability to provide maintenance.
