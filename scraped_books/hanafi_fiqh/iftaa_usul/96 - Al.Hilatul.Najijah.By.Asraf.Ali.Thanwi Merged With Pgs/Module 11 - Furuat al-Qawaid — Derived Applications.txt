Necessity and Custom as Grounds for Departing from the Madhhab
Applies the maxim of necessity to hiring for Quran recitation and discusses the role of custom in fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that necessity (darurah) permits departure from the original madhhab only for textualized exceptions, and identify when custom ('urf hadith) may override a textual ruling.

Core Idea
Necessity permits leaving the madhhab only where earlier scholars explicitly carved exceptions; new analogies are barred after 400 AH.

This lesson explores a key principle for the mufti: when can you set aside the established ruling of the Hanafi school? Through the case of hiring for Quran recitation, we see that the three imams absolutely prohibited it, but later scholars permitted certain acts (teaching, imamah) due to necessity. The passage also introduces the powerful role of custom in revising fatwas over time. By unpacking this text, you will learn how to balance textual authority with the needs of the community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ruling and its exceptions
Pinpoint the base position: the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani) absolutely prohibit hiring for Quran recitation. Then list the exceptions textualized by later scholars: teaching Quran, fiqh, imamah, adhan, iqamah.
These exceptions are not analogies; they are directly transmitted from the mutun.
2
Understand the logic of exception as a tool of 'umum
In usul, exception (istithna) is a tool of specification (takhsis). The exceptions are part of the general prohibition – but they narrow its scope. Only those specific cases are allowed; you cannot extend by qiyas.
Remember: the general rule is prohibition; the exceptions are the only permissible instances.
3
Analyze the barrier to further analogy
The author states that qiyas after 400 AH is terminated (munqati'). A muqallid cannot analogize a new case to the textualized exceptions, even if the cause (necessity) appears present. Only the transmitted rulings (manqul) bind him.
This protects the madhhab from unbounded expansion by later jurists.
4
Examine the role of necessity as a general concept
The Urdu commentary emphasizes that 'necessity' is general, not restricted to a particular time. However, the necessity must be genuine and assessed by qualified scholars. The passage from 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti reinforces that custom (urf) can change rulings, and the mufti may follow a new custom even if it contradicts earlier texts.
Necessity opens the door to following another imam's madhhab, but only when the need is real.
5
Apply the dual principles: necessity and custom
Combine the two: (1) necessity permits departure from the original madhhab only within the textualized exceptions; (2) custom can override a ruling that was based on previous custom. The mufti must assess both the necessity and the prevailing custom to issue a fatwa.
These principles are complementary; custom may itself create a new necessity.

Commentary
This passage is a qawida with furuat discussion. Ibn Abidin's treatise argues about hiring for Quran recitation. The established Hanafi madhhab (of the three imams) absolutely prohibits taking wages for recitation. However, some later shaykhs allowed certain acts like teaching Quran, fiqh, imamah, adhan, etc., due to necessity (darurah). The author notes that the exception is a tool of 'umum, so only matters textualized as exceptions are allowed. Claiming necessity for new issues requires being a mujtahid, and after 400 AH qiyas is no longer valid for later scholars. So a muqallid cannot extend exceptions by analogy. The Urdu commentary clarifies that necessity cannot be restricted to a specific time; it is general. Then a quote from 'Uqud Rasm al-Mufti states that custom can change rulings; if a new custom arises, the mufti may follow it even if it contradicts earlier textual rulings, especially for rulings based on custom. This establishes two principles: (1) necessity permits departure from the original madhhab, but only for specified cases; (2) changing custom can alter fatwa, and the mufti must be aware of contemporary custom.

Key Terms
استیجار علی التلاوة (isti'jar 'ala al-tilawah): Hiring someone for pay to recite the Quran, which the text discusses regarding permissibility.
ضرورة (darurah): A necessity that permits violating the original ruling of the madhhab.
عرف حادث (urf hadith): A newly emerging custom that may cause a change in fatwa if it contradicts earlier texts.

Defining Necessity (Darurah) for Acting on Another Madhhab
Clarifying the condition of necessity for acting on another madhhab regarding wages for teaching

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi definition of darurah as unbearable hardship and apply the maxim 'al-darurah tuqaddar bi qadriha' to the case of wages for teaching fiqh, including evaluating different scholarly positions.

Core Idea
Necessity is measured by its extent; only unbearable hardship permits acting on another madhhab.

The permissibility of taking wages for teaching fiqh became a point of contention when necessity required it. This lesson clarifies that darurah is strictly defined as unbearable hardship, and this condition applies when adopting a non-Hanafi ruling. We will examine the historical development within the Hanafi school and the legal maxim that governs the scope of necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue and its historical background
Recognize that the original Hanafi position prohibited wages for teaching fiqh. Later, necessity led some jurists to permit it. The passage discusses the definition of necessity that justifies such a departure.
Focus on the shift from earlier ijma' to later permissive fatwa.
2
State the Hanafi position on wages for teaching fiqh
The original position was impermissibility. Later, under unbearable hardship, it became permissible. The passage cites Ibn Abidin and others.
Note the difference between the era of ijtihad and later times.
3
Explain the concept of necessity (darurah) as defined in the text
Darurah is defined as unbearable hardship (takleef la qabil e bardasht). Not every difficulty qualifies.
This is the key refinement in the passage.
4
Apply the qawida 'al-darurah tuqaddar bi qadriha'
The permissibility is limited to the extent needed to remove the hardship. One cannot go beyond that.
This is the governing principle from the qawida panel.
5
Note the ikhtilaf and the tarjih
Earlier scholars (Sarakhsi) held absolute prohibition, but later scholars (Ibn Abidin) gave fatwa of permissibility under necessity. The relied-upon position is the later one.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (tahlil naqdi) of the condition of necessity (darurah) when acting on a non-Hanafi madhhab, particularly in the context of taking wages for teaching fiqh. The author, based on Allamah Ibn Abidin's works, explains that the 'cause' in the phrase 'wa in wajadta fihi al-hila' refers to the legal cause of the original ruling in the madhhab, not the cause of necessity. He then provides two responses to an objection about how to determine the intended meaning. The first response uses the principle that probability harms the one making a claim (istidlal); since the author is not making a claim but merely refuting, the remaining probability does not harm. The second response argues that his interpretation is necessary to reconcile Ibn Abidin's own statements, because Ibn Abidin permitted wages for teaching fiqh under later necessity while acknowledging that it was impermissible in the classical period. The passage then traces a historical development: early Hanafi authorities (before 3rd century) allowed only wages for teaching Quran; subsequent scholars like Abul Layth Samarqandi (d. 375) and Imam Fadli (d. 387) extended to other acts including teaching fiqh; later after the 5th century, some jurists allowed under necessity. The author concludes that necessity (darurah) is defined as 'unbearable hardship' (takleef la qabil e bardasht), which is the measure for acting on another madhhab.

ضرورة (darurah): Unbearable hardship (تكليف لا يقبل برداشت)

Gross deception regarding the girl is that her dower is reduced from her proper dower to an extent that is generally not tolerated. Second, that he is not [known for bad choice?], meaning no prior event like that. If a child, male or female, becomes insane, then his foremost guardian is the son, and the marriage conducted by the son is equivalent in all rulings to that conducted by the father. This ruling applies when the father knew at the time of marriage about compatibility or its lack. And if he appoints an agent to perform the marriage by specifying the amount of dower and a specific person, then also the same ruling. But if he appoints an agent without specifying dower or the husband, saying 'marry my daughter anywhere', then that agent does not have authority to marry her to a non-compatible person or with gross deception; if he does, it is void.

Islamic law has given guardianship in a specific order to many people. The details can be learned from books of fiqh.

Commentary
This passage discusses the validity of marriages contracted by a father or grandfather (as guardians) when there is a disparity in compatibility (kufu) or a gross deception (ghaban fahish) in dower. It first defines gross deception in dower for both bride and groom. Then it states two conditions for such a marriage to be valid: the guardian must be sane at the time, and he must not be known for bad judgment (suing al-ikhtiyar). It also mentions that a son, if he becomes the guardian due to the father's insanity, has the same authority as the father. The passage clarifies that if the guardian was unaware of the disparity and later discovers it, the option of compatibility applies. It also distinguishes the authority of a general agent versus a specific agent.

Key Terms
غبن فاحش (ghaban fahish): Gross deception in dower; a significant difference from the proper dower that is generally not tolerated.
کفو (kufu): Compatibility in marriage; includes lineage, religion, profession, etc.
معروف بسوء الاختیار (maruf bi su' al-ikhtiyar): Known for bad judgment; a condition that nullifies the guardian's authority if present.

Types of Insanity in Hanafi Fiqh: Definitions and Rulings
Definition and legal effects of insanity types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the types of insanity (junun mustaw'ib, 'arid, asli, tari', hadith) and identify their legal effects on fasting and marriage according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Insanity's legal effect depends on its type: comprehensive absolution, incidental obligation, original disagreement.

This lesson examines a passage from Badai' al-Sanai' that classifies insanity into distinct categories, each triggering different rulings in fasting and marriage. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for a mufti when assessing obligations and capacity, especially in cases of partial or temporary insanity.

2

This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a quotation from Badai' al-Sanai' defining various types of insanity and their legal effects, followed by a Urdu commentary. It distinguishes between original insanity (junun asli), comprehensive insanity (mustaw'ib), temporary/incidental insanity (tari/'arid), and short-term insanity. For fasting: one mustaw'ib before Ramadan recovers after it: no qada' (make-up) due to lack of obligation period. For 'arid: qada' is obligatory because part of Ramadan was in sanity. For original insanity: if he recovers during Ramadan, there is disagreement (Imam Muhammad vs. Abu Yusuf). For marriage: the three Hanafi imams allow marriage of major insane persons, whether original or later. Also, chronic long insanity is like permanent, short-term insanity is like fainting (no qada'). The Urdu commentator notes that the definition of 'hadith' (newly occurring) is not clear from these expressions, and thus the caution in the treatise about giving a year's grace for absolute 'hadith' is necessary.

Key Terms
جنون مستوعب (junun mustaw'ib): Insanity that covers the entire period of obligation, e.g., entire Ramadan.
جنون عارض (junun 'arid): Incidental insanity that occurs during a period when the person is generally obligated.
جنون أصلي (junun asli): Original insanity present from birth or from puberty onward continuously.
جنون طاري (junun tari'): Temporary insanity that comes and goes.
جنون حادث (junun hadith): Newly occurring insanity that was not present originally.
