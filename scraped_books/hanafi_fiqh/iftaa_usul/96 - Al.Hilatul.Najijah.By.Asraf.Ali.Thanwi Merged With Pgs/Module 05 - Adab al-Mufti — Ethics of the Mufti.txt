Permissibility of Adopting Another Madhhab for Fatwa under Necessity
Ruling on giving fatwa from another madhhab under necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on adopting another madhhab for fatwa under severe necessity and state the conditions required for such adoption.

Core Idea
Under severe necessity, a Hanafi mufti can adopt another madhhab's ruling, but only with strict conditions.

This passage addresses a critical objection: that Islam provides no mechanism for legal separation without talaq or death. The author clarifies that in severe necessity, adopting the Maliki school's rulings is permissible, but only after fulfilling specific conditions. This lesson unpacks the boundaries of intra-madhhab fatwa and the mufti's adab. The key is recognising that the Hanafi school itself validates this practice when need arises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
The objection claims there is no legal separation possible without talaq or death. Identify that this is the core question the passage responds to.
Legal separation (tafriq shar'i) is a family law issue where Hanafi rules are restrictive.
2
Recognize the Hanafi Limitation
The author notes that Hanafi fiqh has limited solutions for certain hardships of women. Acknowledge that this limitation prompts seeking alternatives.
Not all cases have a remedy within the Hanafi school alone.
3
Understand the Solution
The author obtained fatawa from Maliki muftis in Madinah after correspondence. This act demonstrates the practical process of adopting another school's ruling.
The fatawa are included in the book's section on Maliki rulings.
4
Apply the Principle
In severe necessity, adopting the madhhab of another Imam is permissible with conditions and restrictions. State that the mufti must ensure the situation is one of dire need and that all conditions are met.
Conditions include verifying that no Hanafi remedy exists and that the other school's rules are properly followed.
5
Refute the Objection
The objection that 'Islam has no such measure requiring a judge' is shown to be baseless once the concession is established. Conclude that the mufti's adab involves knowing when to look beyond the school and how to do so correctly.
The passage reinforces the importance of scholarly integrity and resourcefulness.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the permissibility of a Hanafi mufti giving fatwa from another madhhab, specifically the Maliki school, under severe necessity. The author first uses a metaphor to criticize those who object without justification. He acknowledges that the previous answer (presumably about tafwid al-talaq) suffices, but raises the objection that even without talaq or death, is there any legal separation? The author states that although the previous answer is enough, he presents a further solution for the benefit of oppressed women. He notes that Hanafi fiqh has limited rulings for certain hardships of unmarried women (or women in difficult marriages), so he corresponded with Maliki muftis in Madinah and included their fatawa in the book. After this, he clarifies that in severe necessity, adopting the madhhab of another Imam with conditions and restrictions is permissible. Hence, the objection that Islam does not provide any solution requiring a judge is baseless. The key point is that the Hanafi school recognizes the validity of resorting to other madhhabs when need arises, but only with strict adherence to their conditions.

The Duties of the Mufti and the Layperson in Implementing Legal Stratagems
Concluding instructions for scholars and laymen on applying the hila

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two duties of a person applying a hila and explain the procedure for resolving doubts by consulting Maliki sources.

Core Idea
Acting on a hila requires prior consultation with a recognized mufti; at doubt, Maliki fiqh is the first resort.

This concluding passage from 'Al-Hilat al-Najijah' outlines the essential protocol for both laypersons and scholars. It emphasizes that legal stratagems (hiyal) cannot be applied casually; they require guidance from a qualified mufti. The author also provides a practical procedure for when doubts arise, directing the scholar to Maliki fiqh texts before escalating to correspondence with Maliki scholars in the Haramayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two duties
The passage states that those acting upon the rulings have two types of tasks. Read carefully and list them: (1) consult a qualified mufti before acting, (2) consult lawyers regarding government law.
2
Analyze the role of the mufti
The mufti must be renowned and recognized in fatwa-writing. The layperson must involve him fully, informing him of every detail. The mufti must act with insight and attention.
This is a condition for the fatwa's validity and safety.
3
Trace the doubt-resolution procedure
In case of doubt, the mufti should first consult Maliki fiqh books (Mukhtasar al-Khalil, al-Muntaqa, Sharh al-Muwatta, al-Mudawwanah, etc.). If unresolved, postpone action and contact Maliki scholars in Mecca or Medina.
This shows cross-madhhab consultation as a legitimate tool.
4
Understand the caution about scholars
The author warns that many claim knowledge, but reading a few pamphlets is insufficient. Extraordinary caution is needed to select truly qualified scholars.
This reflects the adab of iftaa: don't rely on shallow credentials.
5
Apply the instructions to a modern scenario
Imagine a mufti today receiving a case of a woman seeking dissolution. He feels uncertain about the Hanafi ruling. According to the passage, he should first consult Maliki references, and if still uncertain, contact a recognized Maliki scholar via email or phone.
The principle remains: don't shortcut the consultation hierarchy.

Caution in Selecting Scholars in Times of Fitna
Advice on careful selection of scholars during turbulent times

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the criteria for selecting a mufti in times of fitna and evaluate the sufficiency of superficial knowledge for fatwa issuance.

Core Idea
In times of fitna, only deeply trained scholars, not superficial readers, are qualified for fatwa.

This passage from Maulana Ashraf Ali Thanwi's Al-Hilatul Najijah provides critical guidance for muftis and those who seek fatwa. It warns that in times of social and moral corruption, many claim scholarship with minimal learning. The teacher stresses that extraordinary caution is needed in selecting scholars to guide the community. This lesson examines the ethical standards (adab) required for the mufti and the community in distinguishing authentic scholarship from pretension.

Guided Path
1
Identify the context
What does the author mean by 'fitna wa fasad'? It refers to social and moral turbulence affecting religious practice and scholarship.
This context sets the stage for the heightened caution.
2
Recognize the claim
The author speaks of a person who 'claims knowledge and ability'. This is a common occurrence in corrupt times.
Such claims are often baseless.
3
Understand the required level of caution
The author explicitly says 'extraordinary caution and extraordinary thought' are needed in selecting scholars.
Ordinary caution is insufficient.
4
Apply the criterion
The test: those who become famous as molvis by reading a few pamphlets are 'not sufficient for this work'.
This is a clear standard for evaluating a candidate.
5
Reflect on the implication
The community must seek deeply trained scholars, not just popular ones.
Responsibility lies on both the mufti and the seeker.

The Mufti’s Conditions in Times of Fitna
Explains the rarity of combined integrity and expertise, and the necessary fatwa procedure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for fatwa in times of fitna and apply the requirement of collective verification by scholars.

Core Idea
In times of fitna, a fatwa must be verified by at least two to four scholars due to the rarity of combined integrity and expertise.

When personal integrity and deep expertise are scarce, individual fatwa becomes unreliable. The Hanafi tradition has a procedure: a committee of verified scholars confirms necessity and then issues the fatwa. This lesson unpacks that procedure and why abandoning the madhhab is the consequence of ignoring it.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the problem
Understand that in times of fitna, it is rare to find a single person who combines perfect piety (diyanah) and perfect expertise (maharah). This makes individual fatwa risky.
Fitna here refers to widespread corruption or religious confusion.
2
Identify the necessary conditions
A mufti must be well-acquainted with the customs ('urf) and conditions of his time, and must have learned fatwa-giving from a proficient master teacher.
These conditions are often omitted in practice, but they are essential.
3
Apply the collective solution
The only reliable method is for at least two or four verified scholars (muhaqqiqun) to acknowledge the necessity of a ruling and then issue the fatwa according to the Imam's madhhab.
The number 2-4 reflects a small committee ensuring accuracy.
4
Note the warning
Permitting weak opinions or following another madhhab without these conditions leads to abandoning the Hanafi madhhab, which is impermissible.
This underscores the importance of staying within the madhhab's reliable positions.

Handling Ambiguity Between Faskh and Talaq: Precaution in Fatwa
Instruction to mufti on handling ambiguous separation cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of ihtiyat (precaution) when a mufti faces ambiguity between faskh and talaq, and identify the practical consequences of treating a separation as talaq.

Core Idea
When in doubt, treat a separation as talaq to err on the side of caution.

This passage instructs the mufti on handling cases where it is unclear whether a marital separation constitutes an annulment (faskh) or a divorce (talaq). The author notes that earlier scholars used different terminology, creating ambiguity. As a precaution, the mufti should treat the separation as talaq until he can research further. This ruling illustrates the Hanafi commitment to ihtiyat in fatwa, especially when the consequences affect the couple's future marital rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguity
Recognize that the separation could be either faskh or talaq based on conflicting scholarly expressions (e.g., 'fawra' vs. 'talaq').
2
Note the scholarly differences
Observe that Allamah Khalil and the commentator of Dardir used 'fawra' suggesting faskh, while Muntaka uses 'talaq'.
3
Apply ihtiyat (precaution)
Until research clarifies, treat the separation as talaq because precaution dictates preserving the maximum possible talaq count.
Precaution aligns with the principle of avoiding harm (darar).
4
Determine the consequence
If considered talaq, it reduces the husband's remaining talaq count; if only one talaq remained, it is exhausted; if two more are given, it becomes talaq maghlazah.
5
Apply the ruling in practice
The mufti must initially issue a fatwa considering it talaq, then consult local scholars for a definitive ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi precaution (ihtiyat) when issuing a fatwa on the wife of a missing person, and evaluate the practical steps needed to harmonize Hanafi and Maliki positions.

Core Idea
When issuing a fatwa on the mafqud, the Hanafi precaution of a second judicial ruling ensures certainty and respects both schools.

This passage from Ashraf Ali Thanwi's work discusses the Maliki school's procedure for a wife of a missing husband and the Hanafi recommendation of caution. Understanding how to reconcile the two schools and when to apply ihtiyat is essential for proper fatwa practice.

Understand that the Maliki school requires the wife to prove marriage in court (accepting testimony by common renown), the judge sets a four-year waiting period, and then she may remarry without a second ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a comparative fiqh discussion from a Hanafi perspective, addressing the Maliki school's ruling on a wife's separation from her missing husband (mafqud). The author, Ashraf Ali Thanwi, first presents the Maliki procedure: the wife must prove her marriage in court—testimony based on general renown suffices if direct witnesses are absent. The judge then investigates, and if hope is lost, orders a four-year waiting period. At the end of that period, the husband is considered dead, and the wife must observe four months and ten days of 'idda before remarrying. According to the Malikis, a second judicial ruling is not required for the death; the initial ruling granting the four-year wait suffices. Thanwi quotes from Maliki sources—al-Baji's al-Muntaqa and Sharh al-Dardir—to support this.

However, the author then advises precaution (ihtiyat) for Hanafi practitioners: even after the Maliki four-year period, the wife should re-apply to the judge to obtain a formal ruling of the husband's death. This aligns with the Hanafi requirement that such a ruling is conditional upon the death of all peers of the missing person, and it is based on the practice of Caliph Umar, as recorded in Hanafi texts like Durr al-Mukhtar and Fatawa Qadi Khan. The author notes that in the analogous case of 'ayin (another category of missing person), Maliki practice also requires a renewed application after the specified period, so consistency demands the same for mafqud. The passage thus instructs muftis to harmonize Maliki rulings with Hanafi principles through cautious procedure, while acknowledging that extreme hardship may permit dispensing with a second application. This reflects the Hanafi concern for practical ease (taysir) within the bounds of legal principle.

Fatwa Procedure for the Missing Husband: Maliki Model for Hanafi Iftaa
Maliki fatwa responding to multiple istifta' on the missing husband

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul al-iftaa principle of authority hierarchy to identify the required steps for a fatwa on the missing husband, contrasting with the Maliki approach.

Core Idea
A woman with a missing husband cannot remarry without a judge's ruling; the Muslim community may substitute only in the judge's absence.

This passage presents a Maliki fatwa request and response on the missing husband. As Hanafi students, we analyze it to understand the universal importance of authority in fatwa procedure and the conditions under which the community can replace a judge. The Maliki classification of the missing into six categories offers a detailed framework, while the Hanafi school has its own nuanced approach. Studying this comparative material sharpens your ability to parse fatwa questions and assess the need for judicial intervention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue and the questioner's assumption
The istifta' assumes a waiting period of four years; note the question about who must authorize the remarriage.
The core question is whether the woman can act independently or must obtain a ruling from an authority.
2
Locate the core Maliki ruling
The response states that the wife must raise the matter to a judge, governor, or the Muslim community. This establishes a hierarchy of authority.
The Maliki school requires a formal ruling before remarriage.
3
Examine the classification of the missing husband
The missing person is divided into six types based on residence and travel; this classification affects the waiting period and procedures.
Understanding the categories helps determine the exact ruling for a given scenario.
4
Analyze the authority hierarchy
Judge (hakim) > governor (wali) > Muslim community (jama'at al-muslimin). The community acts as a substitute only when no judge or governor is available.
This hierarchy is crucial for fatwa in lands without an Islamic judge.
5
Compare with Hanafi procedure
In Hanafi fiqh, the wife may remarry after four years of search without a judge in cases of necessity, though a judge's ruling is recommended. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The key difference is the necessity of official authority; Hanafis allow more flexibility under hardship.

Maliki Law on Revocable Annulment for Missing, Insolvent, or Oppressive Husbands
Maliki mufti's ruling on revocability of annulment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Maliki ruling on the revocability of annulment when the cause is removed during the waiting period ('iddah) and compare it with the Hanafi position.

Core Idea
In Maliki law, annulment for these causes is revocable; the wife returns to her husband if the cause is removed during 'iddah.

This lesson extracts a substantive legal rule from the Maliki fatwa. When a marriage is annulled due to the husband's absence, insolvency, or oppression, and later the cause is removed within the wife's waiting period, the marriage revives automatically. This is because the annulment is treated as a revocable divorce (raj'i), not a final separation. Understanding this helps Hanafi students correctly apply comparative fiqh, as Hanafi law often treats such annulments as final.

Guided Path
1
Identify the causes of annulment mentioned
List the situations: missing husband (mafqud), insolvent husband (mu‘sir), oppressive husband (mu‘tanit). Also note the impotent husband ('anin) appears in the student's questions but not in the mufti's answer here.
The mufti focuses on the first three in his ruling, but the fatwa implies the same rule for all.
2
Note the ruling on revocability
The mufti states: if the cause is removed during the 'iddah, the wife returns to her husband absolutely. He analogizes this to a revocable divorce.
Key phrase: 'الطلاق رجعياً لم تفصل فيه العصمة' (the divorce is revocable, the bond not being severed).
3
Find the evidence cited
The mufti quotes Khalil (a Maliki authority) and uses the principle that any divorce executed by the judge is revocable.
This shows the source of the ruling in Maliki school.
4
Contrast with Hanafi
In Hanafi fiqh, annulment for these reasons is often considered a final separation (faskh) that ends the marriage permanently, not revocable. The 'iddah is still observed, but return to husband requires a new contract.
This difference has practical implications for advising couples.
5
Practical implication
If a Hanafi scholar is advising a Maliki couple, he must know that the wife's waiting period is crucial: if the husband becomes able and returns during 'iddah, she must return to him automatically.
Ignoring this could lead to improper advice.

Definition and Legal Status of the Missing Husband (Mafqud)
Definition and primary rulings on mafqud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mafqud and state the Hanafi legal consequences for his wife, including the waiting period and the role of the sultan.

Core Idea
A missing husband is legally considered alive until proven dead – certainty is not removed by doubt.

The term 'mafqud' (missing person) appears frequently in family law, especially when a husband disappears without news. Islamic jurisprudence balances the rights of the wife and the presumption of life. This lesson clarifies the legal definition, the default presumption, and the procedural steps a wife must follow, drawing on the Hanafi school's reliance on the maxim 'certainty is not removed by doubt.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its conditions
Locate the definition of mafqud in the text: a person whose whereabouts and life/death status are unknown. Note the conditions: absence without news, no known death, no known survival.
The text uses 'al-mafqud' repeatedly; the conditions are implied through the scenarios.
2
State the default presumption
Under the Hanafi school, the missing person is presumed alive until proven otherwise. This is based on the qawida 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk' (certainty is not removed by doubt). The wife cannot remarry without confirmation.
This principle governs the entire discussion.
3
Explain the waiting period process
The wife must seek the sultan's intervention. The sultan imposes a four-year waiting period from the time the wife complains, after which she observes the 'iddat of death (four months ten days). Then she may remarry.
The text cites Umar's practice as the basis for the four-year period.
4
Note the role of the sultan
The sultan (or his deputy) is the authority who investigates, sets the period, and permits remarriage. Without his order, the wife cannot proceed.
Maliki school differs; they require the sultan's investigation even after twenty years.
5
Understand the consequence if husband returns
If the husband returns before the new marriage is consummated, the wife returns to the first husband. If after consummation, the second marriage is valid and the first has no claim. Inheritance also adjusts based on actual death time.
This illustrates the interplay of certainty and doubt.

The Hanafi-Maliki Dispute on the Mafqud Wife's Waiting Period
Dispute between Hanafi and Maliki on mafqud wife's waiting period

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Hanafi and Maliki positions on the mafqud wife's waiting period, identify the evidence for each, and state the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
Hanafi permits remarriage after 4 years + 'iddat with sultan's order; Maliki requires indefinite wait unless sultan personally investigates.

One of the key differences between the Hanafi and Maliki schools is the procedure for the wife of a missing husband. The Hanafi school, following the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab, sets a fixed four-year period plus the 'iddat of death, while the Maliki school insists on a lengthier investigation by the sultan without a fixed timeline. This dispute has significant practical consequences for women in such situations.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Recap: The wife must have the sultan impose a four-year waiting period from the date of her complaint, then observe 'iddat of death (4 months 10 days), after which she may remarry.
This is based on Umar's practice.
2
State the Maliki position
According to Malik, the wife cannot remarry even after twenty years without the sultan's intervention. She must raise the matter to the sultan, who investigates by writing to the place where the husband went, and only then may set a period.
The text explicitly says 'la' (no) to waiting four years without sultan's order.
3
Identify the evidence for each
Hanafi: Umar's practice as narrated by Ibn Shihab (via Ibn Wahb and Abd al-Jabbar). Maliki: The principle that only the sultan has authority to ascertain death or life; the wife cannot act alone.
The text quotes both sources.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Hanafi, the wife can expect to remarry after about 4 years and 4 months from the date of complaint. Under Maliki, she may have to wait indefinitely, potentially causing prolonged hardship.
This is a real-world difference affecting women's lives.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
In the Hanafi school, the position of Umar is the relied-upon view and is adopted by the majority of later Hanafi scholars. It is considered zahir al-riwayah.
The mufti should follow this position in giving fatwa.

The Wife's Right to Refer to Authority: Judge, Governor, or Community
Procedural options for the wife of mafqud to seek authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to advise the wife of a missing husband on whom she may approach for a fatwa, according to the Hanafi school, and explain the order of priority and validity of each option.

Core Idea
The wife may refer to the judge, governor, or community of Muslims, but the judge is the primary authority; in his absence, the community acts as his deputy.

A practical question for the mufti: if the wife of a missing husband needs to initiate the procedure, whom should she approach? The Hanafi school provides a hierarchy: the judge (qadi) is the first resort; if absent, the governor or even the local community of just Muslims can act. This delegation ensures that the wife is not left without recourse. The text from Khalil's Mukhtasar clarifies these options and their validity.

Guided Path
1
List the authorities the wife can approach
Identify from the text: the judge (qadi), the governor (wali), the governor of water (wali al-ma'), and the community of Muslims (jama'at al-muslimin).
The community is defined as just neighbors and trustworthy persons.
2
Explain the order of priority
If all three (judge, governor, community) are available, referring to the judge is obligatory (wajib); referring to another is prohibited but valid. If no judge, the wife may choose between governor and community.
This hierarchy ensures central authority where possible.
3
Define 'jama'at al-muslimin'
The community of Muslims are the just ('udul) neighbors and others, because they act as the imam in his absence. The text emphasizes that one person is not sufficient; it must be a group.
This is a collective body, not a single person.
4
Note the ruling if she approaches the wrong authority
If she refers to the community of Muslims while the judge is available, it is not valid (la yasihh). If she refers to the governor or community when the judge is absent, it is valid.
The validity depends on the presence of higher authority.
5
Apply in the absence of judge
When no judge exists, the wife may refer to the governor or the community. The text states that referring to the community with even one of the others (i.e., governor) is valid on the apparent view.
This flexibility ensures the wife's case is heard.

The Scope of Marja' fi al-Muhimmat
Definition of marja' in important matters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the broad scope of the marja' fi al-muhimmat and apply the Hanafi principle that every Muslim in a position of authority is a source of reference in important matters.

Core Idea
The marja' in important matters is not limited to the imam or judges; every guardian is a source of authority.

This passage answers a fundamental question for the mufti and the community: Who should be consulted when important decisions arise? The answer expands beyond judges and rulers to anyone entrusted with authority, from a father to a community leader, all of whom are 'shepherds' responsible for their flock.

The Community as Substitute for the Qadi: Conditions and Procedure
Fatwa on community's role as substitute qadi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which the Muslim community can act as a substitute for a qadi in family law cases, and apply the procedure for a wife of a missing husband.

Core Idea
When no qadi exists, the jama'ah of Muslims can investigate and judge family disputes as a collective judge.

This fatwa addresses a practical dilemma: women with missing husbands often have no access to a qadi. The mufti explains that the Muslim community (jama'ah) can step in to hear the case, investigate, set a waiting period, and even pronounce divorce. Understanding the conditions and limitations of this community authority is crucial for modern fatwa practice, especially where state courts are absent or inefficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering condition
Recognize that the community's authority activates only when a qadi is absent 'in reality and meaning'. This means no qualified judge is available or accessible.
Absence includes both physical and functional absence (e.g., a corrupt or non-Islamic court).
2
List the eligible women
The fatwa mentions: wife of a missing husband (mafqud), wife of an absent husband (gha'ib), wife of an impotent husband ('innin), and a woman with a rebellious husband (nashiz). All can raise their matter to the community.
The list is not exhaustive; similar cases of hardship qualify.
3
Understand the community's procedure
The community gathers, hears the case, investigates the woman's situation, and sets a term of four years for the missing husband. After that, she observes the waiting period of death and becomes free to remarry. If the husband appears later, the divorce remains valid.
The four-year term is from Maliki sources; Hanafi school has its own rules but this fatwa adopts the Maliki practice.
4
Note the community's limits
The community acts as a single judge; if any part of the community is absent (e.g., not all righteous people can gather), the authority is incomplete. The decision must be collective, not individual.
This upholds the principle of collective decision-making.
5
Recognize the source of authority
The fatwa cites Maliki scholars (al-Qabisi, al-Sawi, al-Sharnubi) to support the community's role. This illustrates cross-madhhab borrowing when Hanafi sources are insufficient.
In Hanafi usul, necessity permits adopting other school rulings.

Cross-Madhhab Fatwa: Citing Maliki Authorities in Hanafi Practice
Cross-madhhab fatwa methodology: citing Maliki sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a Hanafi mufti may legitimately cite non-Hanafi authorities and construct a cross-madhhab legal argument.

Core Idea
A Hanafi mufti can adopt a Maliki ruling when the Hanafi school is silent or necessity demands it.

This fatwa is a vivid example of cross-madhhab borrowing. The mufti explicitly cites Maliki scholars (al-Qabisi, al-Sawi, al-Sharnubi) to fill a gap in Hanafi fiqh regarding the community's role. Understanding when and why a mufti steps outside his own madhhab is crucial for modern iftaa, where complex issues often require blending different school rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi gap
The Hanafi school has general principles about the qadi's role but lacks detailed procedure for the community substituting when no qadi exists. The fatwa addresses this gap.
Hanafi texts often assume a qadi is available; colonial/post-colonial contexts changed that.
2
Recognize the need for expansion
The mufti is faced with a real case (nazila) that requires a practical solution. He cannot simply say 'no qadi, no solution'. Necessity (darura) demands an answer.
The principle of 'where there is necessity, rulings change' (al-darura tubih al-mahzurat) may apply.
3
Cite cross-madhhab sources
The mufti quotes Maliki scholars: al-Qabisi, Ahmad al-Sawi, and al-Sharnubi. He presents their rulings as authoritative evidence for the community's role.
This is not a rejection of Hanafi fiqh but an adoption of a detailed rule from another school.
4
Justify the borrowing
The mufti implies that the Maliki view is compatible with Hanafi principles (e.g., 'the texts of the jurists bear witness'). He uses phrases like 'the people of the school mentioned' to connect it to Hanafi tradition.
In later Hanafi practice, it is acceptable to follow a weaker opinion within the school or even another school if the stronger Hanafi view causes hardship.
5
Issue a clear fatwa
The mufti provides a concrete procedure: community investigates, sets term, and pronounces divorce. The fatwa is actionable, not theoretical.
A good fatwa balances authority, practicality, and transparency about sources.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary and endorsement from a scholar. It begins by praising the risala for providing ease to oppressed women. The scholar urges all Hanafi scholars to agree on this risala and encourages them to look for even easier rulings in fiqh books. Then the scholar discusses a specific fiqh issue: the waiting period (tarbus) for a missing husband (mafqud). The text presents various narrations: according to Imam Muhammad, the period is one year; according to another narration, nine months; according to Abu Hanifa (the Imam) in one narration, one month, and in another, no fixed period. The scholar opines that the narration of one year is more facilitative and should be adopted, as also preferred by the author of the risala. He adds that the year should start from the date the wife files a complaint, similar to the Maliki school. This passage demonstrates the principle of takhayyur (selecting the easiest ruling) in Hanafi fatwa practice, especially in cases of hardship. The scholar's signature indicates his endorsement.

تخير (takhayyur): The principle of selecting the easiest valid ruling from among multiple opinions within a school
مرافعة (marafi'ah): The act of filing a complaint or bringing a case before a judge

The Fatwa Consultation Process: From Scholarly Debate to Institutional Procedure
Historical account of fatwa consultation and a sample court notice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a fatwa issuance protocol that includes consultation, authoritative sourcing, and procedural documentation, based on the Hanafi model.

Core Idea
Fatwa issuance is a collective, multi-step process that relies on authoritative sources and formal procedures.

This lesson explores a historical example of fatwa consultation among Hanafi scholars, highlighting the collaborative and methodical nature of issuing religious rulings. It also examines a sample shariah court notice, showing how fatwa principles are translated into institutional practice.
