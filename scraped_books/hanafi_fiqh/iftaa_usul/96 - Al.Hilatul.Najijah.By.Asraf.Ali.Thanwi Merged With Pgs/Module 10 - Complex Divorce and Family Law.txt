When a husband delegates talaq to his wife, the precise phrasing determines the scope of her authority. This lesson parses the key phrases from authoritative fiqh works, showing how a single word changes the entire legal effect.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the word 'kabhi' renders conditions binding even after marriage restoration and identify that they cannot be suspended.

Core Idea
Once 'kabhi' is used, conditions bind forever—reconciliation does not reset them.

This short note from al-Hilatul Najijah clarifies a crucial point for muftis and drafters of kabinnamas. Many assume that after a divorce and remarriage, the original conditions of a conditional talaq delegation can be renegotiated. The author decisively rejects that view, affirming the perpetual effect of the word 'kabhi' (equivalent to Arabic 'kullama'). This lesson anchors that rule and its practical implications.

Even after ruju' (revocation) or a new nikah following a divorce, the originally stipulated conditions remain in full force.

The couple cannot mutually suspend or modify any condition, even if they find it impractical. The rule is strict and irreversible.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Once granted, the divorce delegation becomes the wife's property – the husband cannot take it back.

Delegating divorce to one's wife is a serious step. The Hanafi school holds that once the right is delegated, it becomes the wife's possession, and the husband loses all power to revoke it. This lesson clarifies that irrevocability and the need for careful deliberation before such a decision.

Guided Path

Identify the act of tafwid al-talaq

Tafwid is a unilateral declaration; it does not require the wife's acceptance to become effective.
2
State the ruling on revocation
According to the text, after the delegation, the husband cannot revoke (ruju') that right. The delegation is binding.
This is a settled Hanafi rule; any attempt at revocation is void.
3
Understand the consequence for the wife
The wife becomes the 'owner' (malik) of the divorce right. She can exercise it as per the terms of delegation.
Ownership here means she has full authority to use or decline the right within the agreed framework.
4
Note the practical admonition
The author stresses the husband must deliberate and consult scholars before delegating, to avoid later regret.

Hanafi Ruling on Impotence and Spousal Defects: Conditions of Khiyar
Compilation of rulings on 'a'innin and spousal defects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the impotent husband and apply the conditions for the wife's option (khiyar) due to spousal defects.

Core Idea
The wife of an impotent husband gets option after a one-year deadline, but only if the defect existed at the time of contract.

This passage from Al-Hilatul Najijah compiles rulings on spousal defects, focusing on the impotent husband. While it quotes Maliki sources, the Hanafi position is consistent: a wife is not obliged to remain in a marriage where consummation is impossible. The key is understanding the deadline, the distinction between total and intermittent impotence, and when the option arises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defect and its category
Recognize that impotence (al-'a'innin) is a defect of the private part specific to men. The passage lists four defects: insanity, leprosy, vitiligo, and disease of the private part.
For men, the private part defects include castration, impotence, hermaphroditism, and intermittent impotence (mu'tarid).
2
Determine the timing of the defect
The defect must have existed at the time of the marriage contract. Later-occurring defects generally do not give the option, except for contagious diseases like leprosy or vitiligo that cause inevitable harm.
The passage emphasizes that if the defect arose after the contract, the wife loses her option unless the harm is unavoidable.
3
Apply the deadline for treatment
For the totally impotent ('a'innin) and the intermittently impotent (mu'tarid), a one-year deadline from the date of the wife's complaint is given by the qadi for the husband to seek treatment.
The deadline is established by the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab and is accepted in the Hanafi school.
4
Wife's option after the deadline
If the husband fails to have intercourse within the year, the wife has the option (khiyar) to either remain in the marriage or seek annulment. The husband's claim of intercourse is accepted.
If the husband does have intercourse within the period, the wife's option falls.
5
Consequences of annulment
The annulment is a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i), similar to divorce for non-maintenance. Dower rules depend on whether consummation occurred and whether the defect predated it.
If the wife consummated the marriage knowingly, she may waive her dower rights.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of rulings from various sources (Maliki and others) regarding the 'a'innin (impotent man) and defects in spouses. It opens with the condition that a deadline for the impotent is only given by a judge or ruler, quoting Malik. It then addresses the wife's option: according to Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib, if a husband has insanity or harm (which includes impotence), the wife may choose to stay or separate. Al-Mudawwana adds that if insanity occurs after marriage, the wife is separated and a deadline for treatment is given; if no recovery, separation is final. For the 'a'innin specifically, a one-year deadline is set (from Malik). Then Ibn Juzayy in al-Qawanin lists five causes of option: fraud, non-maintenance, etc., and four defects for men: insanity, leprosy, vitiligo, and disease of the private part, with subcategories. The passage notes that the option exists only if the defect was present at the time of contract; if it occurs later, no option except for specific contagious diseases where harm is inevitable. For the woman's defect, if it existed before consummation, she gets no dower; after consummation, the dower is due unless she stayed long and waived it. For men, the distinction is made between those who can potentially have intercourse (like insane, leprous) and those who cannot (castrated, completely impotent). The intermittent impotent (al-mu'tarid) is given a year; if he fails to have intercourse, the wife has the option; if he does, her option lapses. The husband's claim of intercourse is accepted. Finally, the divorce of the impotent is revocable, similar to divorce for non-maintenance.

Key Terms
العنين (al-'a'innin): A man who is unable to have sexual intercourse due to impotence.
المعترض (al-mu'tarid): A man who has intermittent impotence, i.e., sometimes able and sometimes not.
الخيار (al-khiyar): The option given to a spouse to either continue the marriage or separate when a defect or condition is found.

The Missing Husband and Wife's Right to Self-Divorce
Ruling on missing husband and wife's self-divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a wife to divorce herself when her husband is missing and no maintenance is provided, and apply the Maliki rule as quoted in a Hanafi text.

Core Idea
A wife can divorce herself after a month and an oath if her missing husband left no maintenance and no one else provides for her.

This passage presents a legal ruling from the Maliki school as quoted by al-Tusuli in his commentary. It is included in a Hanafi text on legal stratagems (hiyal) to show a permissible solution for a wife in distress. Understanding the precise conditions—witnesses, one-month waiting period, oath, and the preventer of maintenance from others—is crucial for a mufti who may need to apply talfiq (combining rulings) in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Define the missing person
Identify the legal definition of al-mafqud: one whose news has ceased and can possibly be investigated.
2
Identify the condition for wife's right
The wife's right to divorce arises when the husband is absent and has left no wealth from which she can maintain herself.
This includes captives and missing persons across four categories mentioned elsewhere.
3
Outline the procedural steps
The wife must bring witnesses to testify to the husband's absence and lack of maintenance, then wait one month, and then take an oath to self-divorce.
The month is counted after the witnesses' testimony.
4
Note the preventer (mani)
If any relative or stranger provides her maintenance, the cause for separation (lack of maintenance) ceases, and she cannot divorce herself.
This is the relied-upon opinion (al-mu'tamad).
5
Contextualize within Hanafi fatwa practice
Recognize that this is a Maliki rule quoted in a Hanafi work on hiyal; a mufti may use it via talfiq under certain conditions.
Hanafi madhhab itself has different detailed rulings for mafqud.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from a Maliki source (al-Tusuli's commentary) but quoted in a Hanafi context. It presents the procedure for a wife whose husband is missing (mafqud) and has not left maintenance. She must first bring witnesses to establish the husband's absence and lack of support. The ruling emphasizes that if any relative or stranger provides her maintenance, the cause for separation disappears and she cannot divorce herself. This illustrates a legal stratagem (hila) for resolving the wife's hardship. Note: The Hanafi position differs in many details, but the author is presenting Maliki rules for the purpose of talfiq (combining rulings).

The Finality of Judicial Verdicts in Hanafi Law
Explains when a prior judgment cannot be overturned

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on overturning a judicial verdict, apply it to a scenario, and distinguish the Maliki exception.

Core Idea
A correct judgment cannot be overturned; stability in adjudication is paramount.

This passage from Thanawi's Al-Hilat al-Najijah addresses a critical question in judicial procedure: Can a verdict be overturned by another judge? The Hanafi position is clear: a valid judgment is final. But the passage also presents the Maliki view allowing limited exceptions. Understanding this rule is essential for muftis and judges to prevent forum-shopping and maintain legal certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
The passage states that if a judgment is issued correctly according to the judge's madhhab, it cannot be overturned by another judge.
Note the emphasis on 'correctly' – the rule applies only to valid judgments.
2
Examine the Maliki position
Malik permitted overturning a judgment if new evidence emerges that was unknown, as seen in the examples from al-Mudawwana.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
3
Apply the rule to procedural disputes
The passage also covers when parties disagree on which judge to approach and how to resolve it, emphasizing the claimant's right to choose.
This is secondary but reinforces the principle of finality.
4
Evaluate a modern scenario
Consider a losing party seeking a contrary fatwa from another mufti. Apply the rule: the fatwa cannot contradict a valid judgment.
This is the practical implication for muftis.

Determine the 'Iddah Type
Note that the 'iddah is three menstrual cycles (iddah of divorce), not four months and ten days (iddah of death). This is because the marriage ended by divorce, not death.
The text explicitly says 'عدت طلاق تین حیض'.
3
Analyze Revocation Scenario
If the husband returns within the 'iddah and revokes (by word or action), the marriage continues. This is the essence of talaq raj'i.
Revocation can be verbal or by physical action (e.g., returning to cohabitation).
4
Analyze Non-Revocation or Late Return
If the husband returns after the 'iddah, or does not revoke within the 'iddah, the divorce becomes bain (irrevocable). The wife is then free to remarry.
The text distinguishes between return within 'iddah with revocation vs. without revocation or after 'iddah.
5
Contrast with Faskh
Understand that this is a talaq (divorce) initiated by the judge, not a faskh (annulment). The difference is that talaq raj'i allows revocation; faskh does not.
This affects the woman's ability to return to the husband without a new contract.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Hilat al-Najizah addresses the complex case of a wife whose husband has gone missing (mafqud). The author first clarifies the basic ruling: the wife spends her 'iddah at the first husband's home, and the dower is due if valid seclusion occurred. However, the focus shifts to the hardship of waiting indefinitely. The Hanafi school typically requires waiting until the husband's death is proven or until he would be 90 years old, but here the author presents a legal stratagem: when the wife fears falling into sin due to prolonged celibacy and financial inability, she may follow the Maliki school which allows dissolution after only one year of waiting, provided that she has already waited a long time. The author then discusses whether the year is counted from disappearance or filing, noting that caution dictates counting from filing, but scholars of Saharanpur insist on four additional years in all cases. If he returns after the 'iddah or fails to revoke, the divorce becomes irrevocable, and she is free to remarry. This demonstrates the Hanafi methodology of combining taqlid of another school in cases of dire necessity while preserving the structural rulings of revocability.

Ruling on Separation (Talaq Raj'i) for Wife of Missing Husband
Explains judicial separation is revocable divorce with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on judicial separation for the missing husband's wife and apply the conditions for revocability.

Core Idea
The missing husband's wife's separation is a revocable divorce, preserving reconciliation if he returns during iddah.

This lesson examines the ruling when a wife of a missing husband obtains a judicial separation. The author clarifies that such a separation is a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i), not an irreversible one, preserving the possibility of reconciliation. Understanding the conditions of revocation is crucial for issuing correct fatwas in such cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify key terms
Parse the passage for technical terms: mafqood al-khabar (missing husband), talaq raj'i (revocable divorce), iddah (waiting period), and ruju' (revocation).

2
Understand the ruling type
Recognize that the judicial separation is classified as talaq raj'i, not talaq bain. This means the husband has the right to revoke during the iddah.

4

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the legal consequences of a judicial separation (tafriq) granted to the wife of a missing husband (mafqood al-khabar). The author clarifies that such a separation is not a final irreversible divorce but a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i).
