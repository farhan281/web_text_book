The Fatwa Process: Reaching Consensus on Appointing Judges under Non-Muslim Rule
Narrative of the scholarly process leading to consensus on the permissibility of accepting judgeship from a non-Muslim ruler.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of the fatwa process described in the passage and apply them to a contemporary fatwa issuance scenario.

Core Idea
Scholarly debate and written documentation are essential for issuing sound fatwas, especially when addressing novel issues.

This passage not only provides the legal ruling but also illustrates the process through which Hanafi scholars debated, wrote down objections, sent queries to other scholars, and finally published a supplement. Understanding this process is crucial for anyone involved in ifta, as it shows the importance of consultation, writing, and thoroughness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial doubt and the search for an answer
Maulana Sajjad raised an objection; Hakeem al-Ummah asked for a written response.
2
Trace the consultation process
The younger scholar searched in Ta'liq Imdad al-Ahkam and presented the answer; then objections were sent to Medina.
3
Note the role of consensus (ijtima)
After hearing the answer, Maulana Husayn Ahmad declared no objection left; Maulana Kifayatullah eventually conceded.
4
Understand the documentation and publication
The final answers were published as a supplement to Fatawa Manakiyya, indicating careful fatwa recording.

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative recounting a scholarly debate among Hanafi jurists regarding two interconnected issues: (1) the validity of appointing a judge from among the common people (nash al-qadi min al-'ammah), and (2) the permissibility of accepting a judicial appointment from a non-Muslim ruler (taqlid al-qada min al-kafir). The text records the positions of Maulana Sajjad, Maulana Kifayatullah, Maulana Husayn Ahmad, and others, and references earlier authorities such as Shams al-A'imma. The central legal reasoning revolves around the Qur'anic verse 'Allah will never make for the disbelievers a way over the believers' (Surah al-Nisa, 4:141). The Hanafi scholars resolve the apparent conflict by arguing that when a non-Muslim ruler appoints a qadi, he does not exercise true authority; rather, he merely facilitates the implementation of Islamic judicial functions. The actual appointment is considered to be from the Muslim community, as they are the ones who have the duty to establish a judiciary but lack the coercive power. The non-Muslim ruler's appointment removes the obstacle (mani') of enforcement, allowing Muslims to fulfill their obligation. Therefore, the appointee becomes a legitimate qadi from the perspective of Islamic law. The passage also mentions the use of panchayats (informal councils) as a practical alternative but notes their many conditions. The discussion concludes with the scholars reaching consensus (or at least Maulana Kifayatullah conceding) on the validity under the given circumstances. The later part of the text describes the process of documenting and publishing the final answers, indicating a careful methodology for issuing fatwas on contemporary issues.

Key Terms
نصب القاضي من العامة (nash al-qadi min al-'ammah): Appointment of a judge from among the common (non-specialist) people; the text discusses its validity.
تقلد القضا من الكافر (taqlid al-qada min al-kafir): Accepting the office of judgeship from a non-Muslim ruler; the text argues it is permissible as a means to fulfill the Islamic duty of establishing justice.
