Evolution of Permissibility of Wages for Worship
Historical analysis of wage permissibility expansion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the historical expansion of the permissibility of ijārah for acts of worship in the Hanafi school and apply the rasm al-mufti principle of necessity (ḍarūrah) to evaluate contemporary fatwa positions.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school initially restricted wages for worship to teaching Quran, but later expanded it under unbearable hardship.

This lesson examines a crucial development in Hanafi fiqh: the gradual widening of the exception that allows taking wages for acts of worship. While the classical position held that ijārah for purely devotional acts is invalid, necessity (ḍarūrah) and changing circumstances led later jurists to permit wages for teaching fiqh, adhān, imāmah, and more. This process exemplifies the rasm al-mufti principle that a mufti must consider both the authoritative texts and the realities of his time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original Hanafi position
Find the classical ruling on ijārah for acts of worship: generally invalid except for teaching Quran.
Early scholars like Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi and Imam Fadli permitted only teaching Quran.
2
Note the first expansion
Observe which scholars extended the exception to other acts (e.g., teaching fiqh, adhān, imāmah) and under what condition.
Post-fifth century, some jurists gave fatwas of permissibility due to necessity.
3
Contrast the restrictors
Identify scholars who maintained the original restriction, like Qadi Khan and the author of Kanz.
They limited salary to teaching Quran only.
4
Trace the later consensus
See how later texts (e.g., Mukhtasar al-Wiqaya, Tanwir al-Absar) gradually added more acts, but often still deemed them impermissible.
The expansion was not uniform; some texts added imāmah, others adhān.
5
Extract the rasm al-mufti principle
Understand that this evolution shows how a mufti may consider necessity and gradually expand exceptions while respecting the foundational texts.
The definition of necessity is 'unbearable hardship' (ḍarūrah).

Commentary
This passage traces the historical evolution of the Hanafi ruling on taking wages (ujrah) for acts of worship (ta'at). The author begins by noting that in the classical era of independent ijtihad, there was no precedent for receiving salaries for such acts. Early scholars (after the third century AH) prohibited taking wages for teaching the Quran. However, later scholars like Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi and Imam Fadli permitted it, and some extended the permission to adhan, imamah, and other acts. The passage then discusses the tightening of the ruling: Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi explicitly states that there is consensus (ijma') on the invalidity of ijarah for teaching fiqh. Yet, after the fifth century, due to necessity (dharurah), some jurists began giving fatwas of permissibility for other acts. The author contrasts those who maintained the original restriction (e.g., Qadi Khan, who limited salary to teaching Quran) with later scholars who expanded the exception. He mentions several texts and their authors: Majma' al-Bahrain (6th c.) included teaching fiqh with Quran; Mukhtasar al-Wiqaya (d. 790 AH) still considered both impermissible; al-Nuqaya added imamah; al-Islah wa al-Idah permitted teaching fiqh; Tanwir al-Absar (d. 1000? AH) added adhan but still deemed it impermissible. The author also notes that some later jurists included iqamah and preaching. Throughout, the driving factor is the concept of necessity (dharurah), defined as 'unbearable hardship.' This passage illustrates the rasm al-mufti principle that the mufti must consider changing circumstances and may expand exceptions when necessity arises, while still adhering to the foundational texts.

Key Terms
اجارہ (ijarah): Contract of hiring or wages; here specifically wages for performing acts of worship.
ضرورت (dharurah): Necessity that makes something forbidden permissible; defined as unbearable hardship.
طاعات (ta'at): Acts of worship and obedience to Allah, such as teaching Quran, fiqh, adhan, imamah, etc.

Void Marriage by Non-paternal Guardian or Corrupt Father
Ruling on minor's marriage by non-paternal guardian to non-competent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a marriage contract by a non-paternal guardian or a corrupt or intoxicated father is void and apply the Hanafi ruling.

Core Idea
A minor's marriage to a non-competent is void if contracted by a non-paternal guardian or by a corrupt or intoxicated father.

This passage specifies when a marriage conducted on behalf of a minor is entirely void. It covers two scenarios: (1) a guardian other than the father or grandfather marries the minor to a non-competent, and (2) the father or grandfather themselves marry the minor to a non-competent while they are corrupt, notorious for bad choices, or intoxicated. The underlying principle is that the guardian must act in the minor's best interest; failure to do so destroys the contract's validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the guardian type
Determine whether the marriage was conducted by the father/grandfather or another guardian (e.g., brother, uncle).
Only father and grandfather have inherent authority; others have limited authority.
2
Check the guardian's capacity
If the guardian is father/grandfather, assess whether they are 'fasiq mutahannik' (open sinner), 'sa' al-ikhtiyar' (notorious for bad choices), or intoxicated.
A righteous father/grandfather's marriage to a non-competent is valid (different ruling).
3
Check the spouse's competence (kafa'a)
Verify that the spouse is not a 'ghayr kuf'' (non-competent) in terms of lineage, religiosity, wealth, etc.
Kafa'a is a condition for the guardian's authority; marriage to a non-competent voids it under certain conditions.
4
Apply the ruling
If conditions are met (non-paternal guardian OR corrupt/intoxicated father + non-competent spouse), the marriage is batil (void) from the start.
This is not fasid but batil; no need for annulment, as if the contract never existed.

Ruling on Righteous Father's Marriage of Minor to Non-Competent
Ruling on binding marriage by righteous father

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a righteous father marries his minor child to a non-competent and identify the conditions that make the marriage binding.

Core Idea
A righteous father's marriage of a minor to a non-competent is binding – no annulment even if non-competence is known.

In Hanafi fiqh, the father's authority to marry his minor child (wilayat al-ijbar) is powerful. But what happens when he marries the child to someone who is not a suitable match (non-competent)? This lesson examines the third scenario from Asraf Ali Thanwi's Al-Hilat al-Najiyah: when the father is righteous and not known for bad judgment, the marriage is binding, and the child has no option to annul it upon puberty. Understanding this ruling is crucial for muftis dealing with family law cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Who is the father? He must be the actual father (or grandfather) of the minor, not a guardian appointed by will or other relative. The minor is the child being married. The spouse is non-competent (ghayr kuf').
2
Assess the father's character
The father must be 'righteous' – neither a shameless sinner (fasiq mutakhannik) nor known for bad choices (maruf bi su' al-ikhtiyar). This character condition is essential for the marriage to be binding.
If the father is corrupt or known for bad judgment, the marriage may be subject to khiyar al-bulugh.
3
Determine the legal effect
When the father meets the character condition, the marriage is valid and binding (lazim). The minor, upon reaching puberty, cannot annul it even if the spouse is non-competent. This applies whether the father knew of the non-competence or not.
Exception: If the marriage was conditioned on competence (kafa'ah) and the condition is breached, the ruling differs (covered in a separate scenario).
4
Apply the fatwa rule
As a mufti, you must first verify the father's reputation. If he is known to be righteous and not negligent, then the marriage is binding. If the father is corrupt, the minor may have the option of khiyar al-bulugh.
This ruling is based on the maxim that a righteous father's authority produces a binding marriage, even if he errs in judgment of suitability.

Marriage of Adult Woman to Non-Competent with Guardian Permission
Ruling on adult woman's marriage to non-competent with guardian permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on an adult woman's marriage to a non-competent: identify when silence suffices and when explicit consent is required, and distinguish the father/grandfather from other guardians.

The Hanafi Ruling on the Missing Husband (Mafqud)
States the Hanafi rule on divorce and inheritance of missing husband with inter-madhhab comparison

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on divorce and inheritance for the missing husband and compare it with other madhahib, applying the principle of certainty (yaqin).

Core Idea
The missing husband's wife remains married and his estate is not divided until the judge sets an estimated lifespan, because certainty is not removed by doubt.

This passage presents the Hanafi rule regarding the missing husband (mafqud), a scenario where a husband disappears without confirmation of death. The Hanafis maintain that the wife cannot remarry and the estate cannot be distributed until a judge determines an estimated lifespan (usually 120 years from birth). This position differs from other schools, which allow earlier dissolution in cases of hardship. The lesson will unpack the evidence and the principle of certainty (yaqin) that underpins the Hanafi view.

Commentary
