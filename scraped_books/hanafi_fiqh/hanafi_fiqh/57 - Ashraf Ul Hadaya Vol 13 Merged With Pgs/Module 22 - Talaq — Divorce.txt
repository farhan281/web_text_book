Woman's Reliance on News of Husband's Death or Divorce
Ruling on a woman acting on news of absent husband's death or divorce from a trustworthy or non-trustworthy source

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a woman may rely on news of her husband's death or divorce and apply the distinction between qati' tari and qati' muqarin.

Core Idea
A woman may act on credible news of her husband's death or divorce after due investigation, even from an untrustworthy source.

What happens when a husband is absent and a woman receives news of his death or divorce? Can she rely on that news and move on with her life? This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, citing Imam Muhammad's al-Jami' al-Saghir, provides the Hanafi ruling. It distinguishes between trustworthy and non-trustworthy informants and introduces the critical legal concept of qati' tari (supervening certainty) versus qati' muqarin (original certainty). Understanding this ruling protects women from being trapped in uncertain marriages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two cases: (1) a trustworthy informant gives news of death or triple divorce, (2) a non-trustworthy informant brings a written letter from the husband. Note that in both cases the informant is either credible or provides documentary evidence.
In the second scenario, the woman does not know the husband's handwriting.
2
Understand the requirement of taharri
For the non-trustworthy scenario, the woman must conduct investigation (taharri) to form an overwhelming presumption (ghalib al-zann) that the news is true. Only then can she act.
Taharri is a diligent search for truth, not a mere guess.
3
Recognize the legal basis: qati' tari
The ruling is justified by the concept of qati' tari (supervening certainty). The original certainty of the marriage is overcome by new evidence that carries no opposing claim. This distinguishes it from qati' muqarin (original certainty).
The text says 'there is no opposing party' meaning no contrary evidence exists.
4
Apply the ruling
If the conditions are met (trustworthy informant, or non-trustworthy with letter and taharri leading to ghalib al-zann), the woman may observe iddah and then remarry. No further proof is required.
The ruling is considered mubah (permissible) after due diligence.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah discusses the situation of a woman whose husband is absent and she receives news of his death or triple divorce. The ruling is based on a narration from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir. The key principle is that if the informant is trustworthy (thiqah), her reliance is valid without further investigation. If the informant is not trustworthy but brings a written document (divorce letter) and after investigation (taharri) she forms an overwhelming presumption (ghalib al-zann) that it is authentic, she may also act upon it. The reason provided is that the original certainty of the marriage (qati' muqarin) has been challenged by this supervening circumstance (qati' tari), and there is no opposing claim to contradict it. Therefore, it is permissible for her to observe the iddah period and then remarry. The passage ends with an explanation of the two types of qati': 1) qati' tari (supervening certainty) which arises from evidence that overcomes the original state, and 2) qati' muqarin (original certainty) which is the original state of affairs. This distinction is crucial in Hanafi legal theory for understanding how presumptions shift when new evidence arises.

Key Terms
قاطع طار (qati' tari): Supervening certainty: new evidence that overcomes the original presumption (e.g., news of death overriding the presumption of life).
قاطع مقارن (qati' muqarin): Original certainty: the inherent state of affairs (e.g., the presumption that the marriage continues)
تحری (taharri): Investigation or diligent search for truth, especially when initial evidence is unclear.

Acceptance of Thrice-Divorced Woman's Halala Claim
Ruling: thrice-divorced woman's claim of halala is accepted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a thrice-divorced woman's claim of halala is accepted in Hanafi law and apply them to a given scenario.

Core Idea
A thrice-divorced woman's word suffices for halala if no evidence contradicts her.

This lesson presents a straightforward ruling from Al-Hidayah concerning a woman who has been divorced three times (ba'in kubra). After such a divorce, remarriage to the first husband is only possible through a valid halala — marriage to another man, consummation, divorce, and completion of iddah. The critical question here is whose word is trusted. The Hanafi school accepts the woman's statement because there is typically no opposing party to challenge her claim, and the matter is based on her own knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Woman's Status
Determine that the woman is a mutallaqah thalath — thrice-divorced and completely prohibited from her first husband until halala.
This is the starting condition for the ruling.
2
Examine Her Claim
She claims: (1) her iddah from the first marriage ended, (2) she married another man, (3) he consummated the marriage, (4) he divorced her, and (5) her iddah from that second marriage ended.
All five elements must be present for halala to be valid.
3
Check for Opposing Evidence
The ruling applies only if there is no one to contest her claim. Since the second husband is not present to deny or confirm, her statement is accepted based on the principle of qati' tari (unopposed testimony).
If the second husband or other evidence contradicts her, the ruling changes.
4
Conclude Permissibility
If the conditions are met and no opponent exists, the first husband may remarry her. The act is mubah (permissible).
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is relied upon (mufta bihi).

Commentary
This ruling extends the previous principle to a thrice-divorced woman. After being divorced three times (ba'in kubra), she must marry another man (muhallil) before she can remarry her first husband. Here, she claims she did so and her iddah after the second marriage has passed. Her statement is accepted because there is no opposing evidence, based on the same principle of qati' tari.

Key Terms
مطلقہ ثلث (mutallaqah thalath): A woman who has been divorced three times by the same husband, making her haram for him until she marries another man (muhallil).
دخول (dukhul): Consummation of marriage; actual intercourse.
