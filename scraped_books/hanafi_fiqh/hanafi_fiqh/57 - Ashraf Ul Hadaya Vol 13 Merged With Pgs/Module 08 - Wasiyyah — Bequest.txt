Definition and Limitations of an Executor (Wasi) in Hanafi Law
Definition of a wasi and distinction from qayyim, with examples of invalid dispositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the term wasi (executor) in Hanafi law, distinguish it from qayyim (custodian), and identify when a wasi's sale is valid or invalid.

Core Idea
A wasi's authority to sell is limited: it must be in the estate's interest and not against heirs' rights.

This passage defines a key concept in Islamic inheritance and trust law: the wasi (executor). You will learn how Hanafi law distinguishes a wasi from a mere custodian (qayyim) and under what conditions the wasi's sale of estate property is valid or invalid. The examples given illustrate the boundaries of a wasi's authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of wasi
The passage defines wasi as one appointed for preservation and disposition (al-hifz wa al-tasarruf) of a deceased person's wealth and children.
Both functions are essential to a wasi.
2
Contrast with qayyim
The qayyim is entrusted only with preservation, not disposition. This is the key distinction.
A qayyim cannot sell or dispose of property.
3
Identify invalid dispositions (gross negligence)
If the wasi sells an item worth 25,000 for 1,000, the sale is invalid due to gross negligence.
This shows the wasi must act in the best interest of the estate.
4
Identify invalid disposition when heirs are adult and no debt
If all heirs are adults and the deceased has no debt, the wasi's sale is not valid.
The heirs' consent is needed, or the wasi lacks authority to override them.
5
Understand the principle of limited authority
The wasi's power is fiduciary; he is only a seller where his disposition is valid (in what he is permitted to sell).
The author's phrase 'فيما يجوز بيعه' limits the wasi's authority.

The wasi (executor) in Islamic law is the one who is appointed for the preservation and disposition of the wealth of a man and his children after death. The difference between a wasi and a qayyim (custodian) is that the wasi is entrusted with both preservation and disposition, whereas the qayyim is entrusted with preservation only without disposition. Thus in Qawa'id al-Fiqh, p. 543. In some situations, the wasi's disposition is not valid. For example, if he intends to sell an item worth twenty-five thousand for one thousand. Similarly, if all the heirs are adults, the sale by the wasi will not be valid. If the deceased has no debt, the author said 'in what he is permitted to sell' to indicate that the wasi is in the position of the seller only where his disposition is valid.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal term 'wasi' (executor) and distinguishes it from 'qayyim' (custodian). A wasi has the authority both to preserve and to dispose of the deceased's property, while a qayyim only has the authority to preserve. The passage then gives examples of invalid dispositions: if the wasi sells a valuable item for a grossly low price (indicating negligence), or if all heirs are adults (as their own consent is needed). The phrase 'in what he is permitted to sell' limits the wasi's authority to situations where his act is in the best interest of the estate.

Key Terms
وصى (wasi): Executor: one appointed to preserve and dispose of a deceased person's wealth and care for minor children.
قيم (qayyim): Custodian: one appointed only for preservation, not for disposition.
