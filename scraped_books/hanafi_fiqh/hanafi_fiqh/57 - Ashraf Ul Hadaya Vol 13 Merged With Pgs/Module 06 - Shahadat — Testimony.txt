When Does a Pre-emptor Need to Perform Ishhad?
Dispute on conditions for ishhad obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on when ishhad becomes obligatory for a pre-emptor and distinguish the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Ishhad becomes obligatory only when the informant meets shahadah standards, unless the informant is a litigant.

When a pre-emptor learns of a sale, must he immediately call witnesses to assert his right? The Hanafi school split on this: Abu Hanifa required a high standard of proof from the informant, while the Sahibayn accepted any credible report. This lesson unpacks the dispute and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position of Abu Hanifa
Identify the conditions Abu Hanifa sets: the informant must be two men, or one man and two women, or a single just person. Only then is ishhad obligatory.
This is a stricter standard, analogous to shahadah requirements.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's opposing view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that any single informant, regardless of status (free, slave, child, woman), triggers the obligation if the news is believed true.
They emphasize practical ease in transactions.
3
Trace the root of the dispute
The difference originates in a parallel dispute about dismissal of an agent: Abu Hanifa required shahadah standards for notification of dismissal; the Sahibayn accepted any single reporter.
The author references a previous discussion on page 136 (edition).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response and distinctions
Abu Hanifa distinguishes the case of a mukhayyarah (woman given divorce option) where he accepts any informant because there is no binding legal consequence. Similarly, the buyer's report suffices because he is a litigant.
Litigants are not held to the same justice standard.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, a pre-emptor may ignore a report from a single child or slave without penalty; under Sahibayn, he must act or lose his right.
This thamarah al-khilaf affects daily practice.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the conditions under which a pre-emptor (shafi') becomes obligated to perform ishhad (formal testimony of demand) upon learning of a sale. According to Abu Hanifa, ishhad is not obligatory unless the informant meets shahadah standards: two men, or one man and two women, or one just person. The Sahibayn hold that any informant, regardless of status or number, triggers the obligation as long as the news is believed to be true. The root of this difference lies in a prior dispute about the dismissal of an agent: Abu Hanifa required testimony standards for the notice of dismissal, while the Sahibayn accepted any single reporter. The author distinguishes this from the case of a mukhayyarah (woman given the option of divorce), where Abu Hanifa does not require testimony standards because there is no binding legal consequence. Similarly, if the buyer himself reports the sale, he is a litigant and his report suffices even according to Abu Hanifa, since justice is not a condition for litigants. A Q&A clarifies these distinctions, emphasizing that in shuf'ah there is a binding obligation (ilzam hukm) if the pre-emptor remains silent—he loses the right—whereas in the mukhayyarah case, her take on divorce is not immediately binding, so the stricter requirement is waived.

Key Terms
شفيع (shafi'): The pre-emptor, the person holding the right of shuf'ah.
إشهاد (ishhad): Formal announcement of demand in the presence of witnesses to preserve the right.
خصم (khasm): A litigant or party opposing the pre-emptor.

Testimony of Hired Partitioners: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Presents Imam Tahawi's opinion and its rebuttal by Sahib al-Hidayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the difference between Imam Tahawi and Sahib al-Hidayah on the testimony of hired partitioners, and explain why the mufta bihi is acceptance.

Core Idea
A hired partitioner's testimony is accepted when partners agree on completion; no self-interest.

In this passage, we encounter a disagreement within the Hanafi school. Imam Tahawi argues that when partitioners are hired, their testimony is not accepted because it is effectively a claim that they completed their work. However, Sahib al-Hidayah rebuts this, showing that since the partners agree the work is done, the partitioners have no personal stake in the testimony. This dispute teaches us how the concept of tuhma (suspicion of self-interest) is applied in the rules of testimony.

Guided Path
1
State the relied-upon Hanafi position
Identify what Sahib al-Hidayah concludes: the testimony of hired partitioners is accepted.
This is the mufta bihi.
2
Identify the opposing view
Note Imam Tahawi's position that such testimony is not accepted, supported by some mashayikh.
3
Trace the opponent's proof
Imam Tahawi argues that the partitioners are claiming fulfillment of their own work, making it a claim in meaning.
The principle: a claimant's testimony is not accepted.
4
Analyse the Hanafi rebuttal
Sahib al-Hidayah responds that the completion is not in dispute; only receipt is. So no tuhma.
The partitioners do not draw benefit from this testimony.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under Tahawi, hired partitioners cannot testify; under the relied-upon view, they can.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary
This passage presents a further dispute within the Hanafi school. Imam Tahawi argues that when partitioners are hired, their testimony is not accepted because they have a personal interest: by testifying that each partner received his share, they are effectively claiming that they completed their hired work. This, he says, is a claim in meaning, even if testimony in form. The principle is that a claimant's testimony is not accepted. Sahib al-Hidayah rebuts this: the partners already agree that the division work is complete; the only dispute is whether each partner actually received his share. Therefore, the partitioners have no self-interest (tuhma) in testifying about receipt. Their testimony is purely about the act of the partners, not about their own work. Hence it is accepted. The relied-upon position is the acceptance of testimony, even when the partitioners are hired.

Key Terms
اجیر (ajir): A hired person, here the partitioners hired for the task.
دعوی (da'wa): A claim; here, the partitioners' testimony is seen as a claim for completion.
تہمت (tuhma): Suspicion of self-interest that would disqualify testimony.

Ruling on Single Partitioner's Testimony
Ruling on single partitioner's testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the testimony of a single partitioner is not accepted and explain the condition requiring two witnesses.

Core Idea
A single partitioner's testimony is rejected because individual testimony is not accepted against others.

This lesson examines a clear evidentiary rule from the Hanafi school: the testimony of a single partitioner (qasim wahid) is not accepted. The reason given is that the testimony of an individual is not accepted against others. This reflects the general principle that property claims require at least two witnesses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that the testimony of a single partitioner is not accepted (la tuqbal).
This is a direct statement; no further qualification.
2
Understand the reason
The reason provided: 'because the testimony of an individual is not accepted against others.' This indicates a general evidentiary rule.
3
Recall the general principle
In Hanafi law, for claims involving property (and other rights), at least two male witnesses (or one male and two females) are required.
This is a well-established rule in shahadat.
4
Recognize the scope
The rule applies to partitioners specifically, but it is a manifestation of the general requirement for multiple witnesses in property disputes.
The same principle applies to other cases involving rights of others.

Ruling on Amin's Statement
Ruling on amin's statement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two legal effects of an amin's statement: it clears him from liability but does not impose liability on the recipient.

Core Idea
An amin's statement that he delivered is accepted to clear himself, but cannot bind the recipient.

This lesson addresses the legal value of a trustee's (amin) statement. When a qadi orders an amin to deliver money, and the amin claims he did so, his word is trusted to discharge him from liability. However, the same statement cannot be used to prove that the recipient received the money if the recipient denies. This balances trust in the amin with the right of the recipient to contest the claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the context
The qadi orders his amin (trustee) to deliver money to a specific person. The amin later states that he delivered the money.
The amin is appointed by the qadi, so he is a trusted official.
2
Note the first effect
The amin's statement is accepted to clear himself from liability (daman). He is discharged from the obligation to deliver.
This is because the qadi trusted him, and his word is sufficient regarding his own actions.
3
Note the second effect
The same statement is not accepted to impose liability on the recipient if the recipient denies receiving the money.
The recipient's denial shifts the burden of proof to the claimant (the qadi or the party seeking to prove receipt).
4
Understand the reasoning
The amin's word is sufficient to protect himself because he is a trustee. However, imposing liability on another requires stronger evidence, such as two witnesses or an admission.
This prevents the amin's statement from being used as a unilateral proof against a denying party.

Two procedural rulings from Al-Hidayah

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A single qasim's testimony is rejected; an amin's statement frees him from liability but cannot impose liability on a denier.

In this passage, Al-Hidayah addresses two procedural matters in Islamic judiciary: the value of a single qasim's testimony and the legal weight of an amin's statement when delivering property. These rulings protect the rights of all parties—the qazi's appointed divider, the trustee, and the recipient—by balancing evidentiary standards with practical fairness. Understanding the distinction between defensive and offensive proofs is key to applying these rules correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two separate issues
The passage presents two distinct rulings: first, whether a single qasim's testimony is accepted; second, whether the ameen's statement of delivery is accepted when the recipient denies.

The judge must avoid both extremes.

The Hanafi solution: accept the amin's statement only to free him from liability, but not to impose liability on the denier. This preserves trust in the ameen while protecting the recipient.

The amin's statement is a hujjat dāfi'ah (defensive proof) that repels liability from himself, not a hujjat mulzimah (offensive proof) that imposes liability on another.

Commentary
This passage discusses the distinction between mu'amalat (transactions) and diyanat (religious matters) regarding the admissibility of a person's statement. The ruling, from al-Quduri, states that a fasiq's statement is accepted in transactions but not in religious matters, where only a just Muslim's word is accepted. The reason (wajh al-farq) is that transactions are frequent and involve diverse people; requiring uprightness would cause hardship. The commentary then categorizes transactions into three types: (1) those with no liability (ilzam) like mudarabah and permission to trade—here only discernment (tamyiz) is needed; (2) those with pure liability like litigation rights—requiring full testimony conditions; (3) mixed cases like revocation of agency or interdiction (hajr)—where Imam Abu Hanifa considers number or uprightness necessary. The book clarifies that this ruling applies only to the first type. Further, the reason for the distinction is also that disbelievers cannot reside in Muslim lands without transactions, so necessity (darurah) dictates acceptance. For religious matters, since they are less frequent, stricter conditions can be imposed. The authority weight is from Zahir al-Riwayah via al-Quduri.

Accepting Statements: Diyanat vs. Mu'amalat
Explains the difference in conditions for accepting statements in religious vs transactional matters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on accepting statements in diyanat and mu'amalat, classify the three types of mu'amalat, and explain the rational basis (haraj/necessity) for the leniency in transactions.

Core Idea
In religious matters, only a just Muslim's word counts; in transactions, even a disbeliever's word is accepted out of necessity.

The Hanafi school carefully balances legal strictness with practical needs. This lesson examines a key distinction: why religious reports require a just Muslim narrator, while everyday transactions accept statements from anyone with basic discernment. The reason lies in the principle of hardship removal (raf' al-haraj), which the passage illustrates through a detailed classification of transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two domains
Distinguish diyanat (religious matters like hadith narration) from mu'amalat (transactions like sales). Note the default condition for each: only a just Muslim in diyanat; any person with tamyiz in mu'amalat.
The passage specifies that mu'amalat here refers only to the first of three subtypes.
2
Analyze the three subtypes of mu'amalat
List the three categories: (1) without accusation (mudarabah, trade authorization), (2) pure accusation (litigation), (3) mixed (dismissal of agent, hajr). Understand which subtype is covered by the blanket leniency.
Imam Abu Hanifa's view on the third subtype: either number of witnesses or justice required.
3
Trace the rational proof
Follow the 'wajh al-farq' (reason for difference): diyanat occur infrequently, so extra conditions cause no hardship; mu'amalat occur constantly, and rejecting non-Muslim statements would prevent them from living in Islamic lands, causing hardship (haraj).
This is an istihsan argument based on necessity (darurah).
4
Apply to scenarios
Practice with examples: a fasiq reports a hadith (rejected), a fasiq sells goods (accepted), a disbeliever claims partnership capital (accepted). Verify using the three-type classification.
Also note the tanbih: diyanat here means purely religious matters, not those involving property transfer.

But transactions (mu'amalat) occur among various types of people. If the condition of justice is imposed on them, great hardship would arise. For this reason, it was ruled that even the statement of a fasiq (immoral person) is accepted in transactions. Therefore, since this is the case, the just and the unjust, the disbeliever and the Muslim, the slave and the free, the man and the woman are all equal, and the statement of all is accepted. Here, 'transactions' does not mean absolute transactions, but rather those transactions in which there is no accusation (ilzam). Otherwise, other transactions do not have this ruling. The detail is that transactions are of three types: (1) Those without accusation, like mudarabah and authorization for trade. (2) Those with pure accusation, like rights in which litigation occurs. (3) Those that are accusatory in one respect and not in another, like the dismissal of an agent and restriction on a custodian (hajr). In this, the agent is accused because after dismissal the purchase is his responsibility, and after restriction the corruption of the contract is an accusation. The aspect of non-accusation is that the principal and master consider the transaction as pure disposal in their own right, so in this respect it is like authorization. Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi ruling on the acceptance of statements (qawl) in two categories: religious matters (diyanat) and transactions (mu'amalat). The commentator explains that in religious matters, only the statement of a just Muslim is accepted, because such matters are less frequent, so imposing the condition of 'adalah does not cause hardship. In transactions, which occur frequently among all types of people—including fasiqs, disbelievers, slaves, and women—accepting only the statement of a just Muslim would cause great hardship (haraj kathir). Therefore, due to necessity, the statement of any person is accepted in transactions, provided they have basic discernment (tamyiz). The passage further categorizes transactions into three types: (1) those without accusation (like mudarabah and commercial authorization), where only tamyiz is required; (2) those with pure accusation (like litigation), which require the full conditions of testimony; and (3) those with mixed aspects (like dismissal of an agent or restriction). In the third type, Imam Abu Hanifa requires either the number of witnesses or justice. The commentator clarifies that the word 'mu'amalat' in this context refers only to the first type. The 'reason for difference' (wajh al-farq) between diyanat and mu'amalat is provided: diyanat are infrequent, so stricter conditions can be applied; mu'amalat are frequent and involve non-Muslims who cannot live in Islamic lands without transactions, so their statements must be accepted out of necessity. Finally, a warning (tanbih) specifies that by 'diyanat' here is meant purely religious matters (like reporting a hadith or giving religious testimony), not subsidiary matters or those involving transfer of ownership (like sales or divorces).

Key Terms
دیانات (diyanat): Religious matters, such as reports about halal/haram or giving religious testimony where the outcome is about the hereafter or personal religious obligation.
معاملات (mu'amalat): Transactions and dealings between people, such as sales, partnerships, and agency, where the primary concern is worldly legal consequences.
عادل (adil): A person who is just, i.e., upright in religious observance and trustworthy, avoiding major sins and not persisting in minor sins.
الزام (ilzam): Accusation or imposition of liability; a situation where the statement can lead to legal consequences for another person.
حرج (haraj): Hardship or difficulty that would make practice impossible. Used as a basis for leniency (rukhsah).

Key Takeaway
In diyaanat, the sole criterion for accepting testimony is justice, not freedom or gender.
