The Scope of Ihtikar: Three Hanafi Views
Presents three Hanafi positions on the scope of ihtikar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three Hanafi positions on the scope of ihtikar and explain the underlying principle of harm each imam considered.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school has three definitions of ihtikar: food only (Abu Hanifa), anything harmful (Abu Yusuf), everything except clothes (Muhammad).

Ihtikar (hoarding) is generally prohibited, but what exactly constitutes ihtikar? The three leading Hanafi imams gave different answers based on their understanding of harm. This passage presents their views and the reasoning behind each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the position of Imam Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa restricts ihtikar to foodstuffs (aqwāt) such as wheat, barley, dried figs, and fodder. Note his criterion: customary and well-known harm (ḍarar ma‘hūd muta‘ārif).
2
Analyze the position of Imam Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf expands ihtikar to any item whose withholding harms the general public, including gold, silver, and cloth. His basis is actual harm (ḥaqīqat al-ḍarar).
3
Examine the position of Imam Muhammad
Muhammad holds that ihtikar does not apply to clothes; he restricts it to foodstuffs because people's sustenance depends on food, not clothing.
4
Compare the underlying principles
Abu Hanifa uses customary harm (limited to food in practice), Abu Yusuf uses actual harm (any harmful hoarding), and Muhammad uses a narrower view excluding non-essentials like clothes.
This shows how the same ruling (karāha taḥrīmiyya) can have different scope based on the principle applied.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, only hoarding food is problematic; under Abu Yusuf, hoarding any scarce necessity (e.g., medicines) is ihtikar; under Muhammad, clothes are excluded from ihtikar.
The muftā bihī is not specified here, but later practice often follows Abu Hanifa's restriction or Abu Yusuf's harm principle.

The Duration for Ihtikar: 40 Days or One Month?
Discusses the minimum period for hoarding to become makruh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi opinions on the duration of ihtikar and identify the proof for the 40-day view.

Core Idea
Hoarding becomes makruh only after a substantial period; two opinions: 40 days (hadith) or one month (reasoning).

Hoarding (ihtikar) is prohibited when it causes harm to the community. But how long must one hold goods for it to be considered harmful? The Hanafi school records two views: one based on a hadith specifying forty nights, another relying on a customary month. This lesson unpacks both proofs and their evidentiary basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is ihtikar (hoarding food). When the duration is short, it is not ihtikar; when long, it becomes makruh tahrimi.
The key is the duration: short (a few days) vs long.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The hadith narrated by Imam Ahmad: 'Whoever hoards food for forty nights has disassociated from Allah and Allah from him.' This is the direct proof for the 40-day threshold.
This is a hadith ahad, so it gives zanni certainty.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith explicitly links forty nights to severe spiritual consequence, indicating that hoarding beyond that period is severely disliked (makruh tahrimi).
4
Note the secondary opinion
Some Hanafi scholars set the threshold as one month, reasoning that less than a month is trivial and immediate, while a month and above is substantial and delayed.
This view relies on custom (urf) and analogy.
5
Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Both opinions are recorded in the school. The mufta bihi (operative view) is often 40 days due to the explicit hadith, but both are considered valid.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the madhhab.

Intention in Hoarding: Sin Varies by Purpose
Explains that sin varies based on intention even if duration is short.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ihtikar and explain how intention affects the sinfulness of the act.

Core Idea
Sin in hoarding depends on intention, not just duration—waiting for famine is worse than waiting for high prices.

The sin of hoarding (ihtikar) is not solely determined by the length of time goods are withheld; the underlying intention plays a crucial role. This passage clarifies that even a short period of hoarding can be sinful if the intention is malicious, such as waiting for a famine. The Hanafi school thus emphasizes moral accountability in trade, making intention a central factor in evaluating the gravity of the offense.

This does not prohibit all food trade but warns against practices that could harm consumers.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah discusses the ruling of ihtikar (hoarding foodstuffs). It opens by defining that hoarding is disliked (makruh) when the grain is brought to the town from elsewhere and the public's right is attached to it. The passage then presents the intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa considers hoarding disliked only when the grain is brought to the town and purchased there, but if someone brings grain from another city and stores it, it is not hoarding because the public had no previous right to it. Imam Muhammad extends the ruling to include grain from towns that habitually supply the city, as those towns are like suburbs (fina' al-madinah). However, grain from a distant city not customarily supplying the city is not subject to ihtikar. Imam Abu Yusuf holds hoarding absolutely disliked based on the absolute hadith 'The hoarder is cursed.' The commentator then notes the relied-upon (mukhtar) position is Imam Muhammad's, as stated in Sukb al-Anhur and Durr al-Mukhtar. The reasoning (ta'lil) centers on the attachment of public right: when the public expects the grain to be available, hoarding harms them.

Key Terms
احتکار (ihtikar): Hoarding foodstuffs with the intention of selling when prices rise, causing harm to the public.
فناء شہر (fina' al-madinah): The outskirts and surrounding areas that are considered part of the town for legal purposes, such as habitual supply zones.

The Default Prohibition and Necessity Exception for Price Fixing
States the default prohibition of price fixing, gives hadith proof, and introduces the exception for general harm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default Hanafi ruling on tas'ir (price fixing), identify the hadith proof, and explain the exception for general harm (darar 'amm).

Core Idea
The ruler must not fix prices by default; only when necessary to prevent widespread harm does it become obligatory.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses the sensitive issue of price controls in Islamic markets. Relying on a clear Prophetic tradition, the Hanafi school establishes a strong default prohibition. Yet the school also recognizes that absolute non-intervention can sometimes lead to greater injustice, creating a nuanced exception rooted in the principle of repelling harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Read the opening sentence: 'ولا ينبغى للسلطان ان يسعر على الناس' – It is not befitting for the ruler to fix prices. This establishes the default prohibition. Determine the hukm level (haram).
The term 'لا ينبغى' in classical fiqh often indicates prohibition (tahrim) when related to rights of Allah or others.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The text cites the hadith: 'لا تسعر فان الله هو المسعر القابض الباسط الرازق' (Do not fix prices; for Allah is the Fixer, the Constrictor, the Expander, the Provider). This is a direct textual evidence from the Sunnah.
The hadith is ahad (single narration), thus zanni al-thubut, but sufficient for a ruling of prohibition in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'لا تسعر' is a clear prohibition. The reason (ta'lil) given is that Allah alone is the true fixer of prices. This implies that human price-fixing usurps a divine prerogative. Hence, the prohibition is based on both the command and the underlying rationale.
In Hanafi usul, the imperative (amr) indicates obligation, and the negative imperative (nahy) indicates prohibition. The ta'lil reinforces the ruling.
4
Identify the exception and its condition
The text adds: 'الا اذا يتعلق به دفع ضرر العامة' – unless it is connected to repelling general harm. This creates an exception: when the market fails and widespread harm (darar 'amm) cannot be prevented otherwise, the ruler may—and even must—fix prices.
The phrase 'على ما تبين' refers to a detailed explanation later in the original text, indicating that the conditions and scope of the exception are elaborated in subsequent sections.
5
Understand the practical consequence (thamarah)
The default is no interference. However, if hoarding, monopolies, or other manipulations cause public hardship, the ruler's duty shifts to intervene. The ruling changes from haram (prohibited) to wajib (obligatory) to protect the community's economic welfare.
This exemplifies the Hanafi principle that necessity (darurah) can override prohibitions, but only to the extent needed to remove the harm.

Ruling on Selling Above the Qadi's Fixed Price
Explains the first issue regarding tas'ir: validity of selling above fixed price.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a sale that exceeds the qadi's fixed price and explain the reasoning behind its validity.

Core Idea
A sale above the fixed price is valid because the seller is not coerced into the sale itself.

The first issue addressed in the passage concerns a seller who deliberately sells at a higher price than the qadi has set. Despite the apparent defiance, the Hanafi school unanimously holds that the sale is valid and the qadi cannot annul it. However, Abu Hanifa and his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) arrive at this conclusion through different principles. Understanding this ruling highlights the school's emphasis on free will in contracts and the limits of judicial price control.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is selling goods above the price fixed by the qadi. The ruling is that the sale is valid (sahih) by consensus and the qadi will not annul it.
Validity means the contract fulfills its legal effects.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that interdiction (hajr) cannot be imposed on a free, sane adult without a valid cause. Therefore, the seller remains an independent agent, and his sale is effective.
His lack of compulsion means the contract is valid.
3
Examine Sahibayn's reasoning
Sahibayn allow interdiction, but only when applied to a specific, known group, not to the general public. Since the fixed price applies to all, it is not a valid interdiction, so the seller is free.
Thus, they also consider the sale valid.
4
Note the practical result
Despite different principles, both positions lead to the same conclusion: the sale stands. The qadi has no authority to rescind it.
This is an example of ikhtilaf in dalil but not in hukm.

Ruling on Selling at the Qadi's Fixed Price
Explains the second issue regarding tas'ir: validity of selling at the fixed price.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a sale at the qadi's fixed price is valid and why it is not considered coerced.

Core Idea
Forcing a seller to sell at a fixed price does not invalidate the sale because the coercion is only on the price term, not on the sale itself.

The second issue addresses the seller who complies with the qadi's fixed price. Some may think that since the seller is compelled to accept a specific price, the sale is invalid due to coercion (ikrah). However, the passage clarifies that the coercion is only on the price, and the seller freely chooses to sell. Therefore, the sale is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is selling goods at the price fixed by the qadi. The ruling is that the sale is valid (sahih).
2
Understand the nature of coercion
The seller is not forced to sell; he is only forced that if he sells, he must sell at that price. This is compulsion on the price, not on the act of selling itself.
Coercion (ikrah) that invalidates a contract must be on the act itself.
3
Conclude validity
Since the seller voluntarily engages in the sale, the contract is valid and binding.
This is consistent with the general Hanafi principle that ikrah on a condition does not invalidate the contract.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Forced Sale of Hoarder's Grain

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The qadi may forcibly sell a hoarder's grain to remove public harm, though Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn differ on the permissibility.

The third and most detailed issue deals with the hoarder (muhtakir) who refuses to sell his grain despite a price fixing. Can the qadi sell his grain without consent? The passage reports a difference between Abu Hanifa and his two companions, and also a second opinion that it is permissible by consensus. This dispute touches on the principles of interdiction (hajr) and public welfare.

This opinion reconciles the dispute by appealing to a broader principle.

The mufta bihi is that the qadi can sell by consensus to avert general harm.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Selling weapons to known rebels is makruh tahrimi; uncertainty about the buyer removes the prohibition.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from al-Quduri's matn, expanded by later Hanafi commentators. At first glance, selling weapons during civil strife (fitna) seems categorically disliked. However, the classical Hanafi position conditions this ruling on the buyer's known affiliation. The underlying principle is that prohibition attaches to aiding sin, not to mere possibility. We will unpack the proof from the Quran and understand how doubt affects legal rulings.

Guided Path
1

The act is selling weapons during fitna. The ruling is makruh (which in this context means makruh tahrimi, i.e., prohibited).

The prohibition is conditional: it applies only when the seller knows the buyer is from the people of fitna (ahl al-fitna). If the buyer's affiliation is unknown, the sale is not makruh.

The verse is qat'i in thubut and dalala, providing a strong basis.

The prohibition of cooperation is absolute. The act of selling weapons to a known rebel directly facilitates their sin, thus it falls under 'cooperating in sin.'

5

If the buyer's identity is unknown, the sale is permissible because the possibility of misuse is mere doubt, and rulings cannot be based on doubt.
The commentator contrasts this with a case where the buyer is known to be from fitna; the ignorance case removes the karahah.

Selling Grape Juice to a Winemaker: Permissibility and Proof
States permissibility of selling grape juice despite knowledge of intended use

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of taghyir (transformation) to distinguish permissible sales from impermissible ones, and evaluate the qiyas between selling grape juice and selling weapons.

Core Idea
Sin attaches only after transformation; permissible objects stay permissible.

This passage addresses a classic Hanafi ruling on the permissibility of selling grape juice to a known winemaker. The key insight is that the sin does not attach to the juice itself but only after it ferments into wine. The ruling is derived through a subtle qiyas (analogy) that distinguishes between objects directly used for sin and those that require transformation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is selling grape juice (asir) to a person who is known will make wine from it. The ruling is 'la ba'sa' (no harm), meaning permissible (ja'iz).
The ruling is permissibility, not merely tolerated.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is that the sin (ma'siyah) does not attach to the juice itself (bi 'aynihi) but only after transformation (taghyir). Thus the sale itself does not directly facilitate sin.
The dalil is a rational reasoning (ta'lil) based on the nature of the object.
3
Analyze the qiyas
The text contrasts with selling weapons during fitna, where the weapon is directly used for sin without transformation. Identify the illa: in the weapon case, sin attaches immediately; in the juice case, sin requires an additional human act (fermentation).
The qiyas is khafi (subtle) because the illa is not apparent from the weapon sale ruling alone.
4
Evaluate the objection
Some scholars (e.g., Shafi'i) argue that both are cases of aiding sin, so they should be alike. The Hanafi response: in the juice case, the connection to sin is less direct because the object must be transformed. Thus the ruling differs.
This illustrates the principle of sadd al-dhara'i (blocking means) is applied only when the means are inherently tied to sin.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon Hanafi position is permissibility. Some early Hanafis reportedly disliked it, but the zahir al-riwayah and the text affirm no harm.
The mufta bihi is ja'iz (permissible).

Placing Money with a Grocer as a Loan
Ruling on leaving money with a grocer for future purchases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on placing money with a grocer for future purchases, explain the prohibition of qard jarra nafa', and apply the alternative of wadi'ah.

Core Idea
A loan that benefits the lender is prohibited; to avoid it, use a deposit instead.

This passage addresses a common transactional scenario: leaving money with a grocer to take goods as needed. The Hanafi school identifies this as a prohibited beneficial loan and recommends a simple structural fix—turning it into a deposit (wadi'ah). By understanding the underlying principle, you learn to distinguish between a loan (qard) and a trust (wadi'ah), a crucial skill in Hanafi muamalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage describes a person placing a dirham with a grocer to take goods little by little. The ruling is that this is disliked (makruh). Determine the exact hukm level: makruh tanzihi.
2
Analyze the underlying contract
The reason for the dislike is that the money becomes a loan (qard) from the depositor to the grocer. The depositor then benefits from this loan by being able to take goods on credit, which is a prohibited benefit (qard jarra nafa').
The benefit here is the convenience of not paying each time.
3
Examine the proof (dalil)
The author cites the hadith: 'The Messenger of Allah forbade a loan that brings benefit.' From this, we understand that any loan that confers an extra benefit to the lender is invalid or prohibited. Here, the benefit is the credit facility.
The hadith is ahad but accepted in Hanafi school.
4
Recognize the recommended alternative
The author states: 'It is appropriate (yanbaghi) that he makes it a deposit (wadi'ah), then takes from it whatever he wishes as a price.' Instead of a loan, the money is left as a trust (amanah) with the grocer, who then acts as an agent to sell goods on the spot.
In wadi'ah, the depositor retains ownership, so no loan occurs.
5
Apply the principle to similar cases
Any arrangement where money is given to another with the expectation of receiving goods or services later, and the recipient uses the money, potentially falls under this ruling. The safe Hanafi practice is to use a wadi'ah structure or an agency (wakalah) with a deposit.

Commentary
This passage states a ruling from the Hanafi school regarding a common transaction: a person gives a sum of money (a dirham) to a grocer with the understanding that he will take goods from the grocer over time until the money is used up. The author declares this practice makruh (disliked) because it constitutes a loan (qard) that returns a benefit to the lender. Specifically, the lender (the person depositing the money) gets the benefit of taking goods without immediately paying each time. This is a form of qard jarra nafa' (a loan that brings benefit), which is prohibited by a hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The reason is that the money given is effectively a loan to the grocer; the grocer can use it, and the depositor later takes goods equivalent in value. The benefit the depositor receives is the convenience of delayed payment or the ability to take goods on credit, which is considered an extra benefit on the loan. To avoid this, the author recommends that the transaction be structured as a deposit (wadi'ah), where the money is left as an amanah (trust), and then the grocer is authorized to sell goods on the spot using that deposited money. In a deposit, the money remains owned by the depositor, and the grocer acts as an agent, so no loan is involved. This avoids the prohibition of a beneficial loan.

Ruling on Sale and Lease of Land in Mecca
Dispute on sale/lease of Meccan land with proofs and tarjih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on sale of Meccan land, state the proofs of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Mecca’s land: sacred as the Kaaba’s courtyard, but later fatwa permits its sale while leasing remains makruh.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi internal dispute on the permissibility of selling and leasing land in Mecca. Imam Abu Hanifa argued against sale due to sanctity, while the Sahibayn permitted it based on general practice. Later scholars reconciled the views, allowing sale but still considering leasing disliked. Understanding this dispute highlights how sanctity, practice, and tarjih interact in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts in dispute
Recognize that the passage deals with two distinct transactions: sale (bay') and lease (ijarah) of land in Mecca. The dispute primarily revolves around sale, while lease is agreed upon as makruh.
Lease is also discussed but all fuqaha agree on its karahah.
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa's position and proofs
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that sale of Meccan land is not permissible. His proofs: (1) a hadith equating leasing to usury (which also implies sanctity), and (2) a rational analogy: the Kaaba is waqf and unsellable, and Mecca is its courtyard, so it shares that sanctity.
The hadith is ahad, so its thubut is zanni.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proofs
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that sale is permissible. Their proofs: (1) the general practice (ta'amul) of people selling land and houses in Mecca without denial, and (2) a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa himself permitting sale.
Ta'amul is considered a strong proof in Hanafi usul when consistent.

The Prohibition of Loan with Benefit: Dirham with Shopkeeper
Ruling on placing dirham with shopkeeper

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on depositing money with a shopkeeper for future purchases, explain why it is makruh tahrimi due to qard yajurr naf'an, and identify the permissible alternative of wadi'ah.

Core Idea
A loan that brings any extra benefit to the lender is prohibited — the dirham-with-shopkeeper arrangement is makruh tahrimi.

Imagine you give a shopkeeper a dirham and tell him you will take goods from him from time to time until the money runs out. Is this a valid transaction? The Hanafi school rules this arrangement as makruh tahrimi (close to haram) because the dirham effectively becomes a loan to the shopkeeper, and you benefit by having goods available on demand. This extra benefit makes it a prohibited loan (qard yajurr naf'an). The text provides the ruling, the evidence from hadith, and a better alternative: make it a deposit (wadi'ah) instead.

Guided Path
1
Identify the arrangement
A person places a dirham with a shopkeeper with the understanding that he will take goods from time to time as needed. The shopkeeper holds the money and the customer takes goods in return.
This is a common practice of prepayment for future purchases.
2
Classify the transaction: loan or deposit?
The text explains that because the customer can take goods 'as he wishes' at any time, the money is considered a loan (qard) to the shopkeeper. The shopkeeper becomes owner of the dirham, and the customer has a debt claim for goods. This classification triggers the rule of qard yajurr naf'an.
If the money were deposited without any condition, it would be wadi'ah (trust). The condition of taking goods turns it into a loan.
3
Identify the prohibited benefit
The benefit is that the customer can obtain goods on demand without paying each time; the shopkeeper gains by having the money and retaining the customer's patronage. Any additional benefit to the lender beyond the principal makes the loan prohibited.
The hadith: 'The Messenger of Allah prohibited a loan that brings benefit.'
4
State the ruling
The arrangement is makruh tahrimi (disliked close to haram) because it is a loan that brings benefit, which is akin to riba.
The ruling is consistent across the Hanafi school (zahir al-riwayah).
5
Learn the preferred alternative
Instead of making it a loan, the customer should deposit the dirham as a wadi'ah (trust) with the shopkeeper, then take goods in exchange for the money gradually. In this case, the shopkeeper is not the owner, and if the dirham is lost without negligence, no liability arises.
Wadi'ah avoids the prohibition because no benefit accrues to the lender beyond the principal.

Ruling and explanation on placing a dirham as loan with shopkeeper

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on placing money with a shopkeeper under a condition to take goods, explain why it is makruh, and identify the permissible alternative.

Core Idea
Placing money to take goods is a loan with benefit, makruh; making it a deposit avoids the prohibition.

Many people routinely leave money with a local shopkeeper to pick up items as needed. Is this a harmless arrangement or does it violate Islamic commercial ethics? In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on this common practice, drawing on a key hadith and the distinction between loans and deposits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction
Recognize the scenario: a person places a dirham with a shopkeeper with the understanding that he can take goods little by little until the money is used up.
2
Determine the legal nature
The condition 'take whatever he wishes' turns the arrangement into a loan (qard) because the shopkeeper becomes owner of the dirham and owes its value back in goods. Without that condition, it would be a deposit (wadi'ah).

The Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade any loan that brings benefit to the lender. Here, the lender benefits by being able to take goods at will. Hence the ruling: makruh (disliked, close to haram).

If the money is placed as a deposit (wadi'ah) without any condition, the shopkeeper merely holds it as an amanah. The depositor can still take goods with the shopkeeper's permission, but it is no longer a loan with benefit. This is not makruh.
As a deposit, the shopkeeper cannot use the money, and the risk of loss remains on the depositor.

Ruling on Accepting Gifts and Invitations from a Merchant Slave
Hanafi ruling on gifts, invitation, and borrowing from a trading slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting gifts and invitations from a trading slave, distinguish between necessity and non-necessity items, and explain how istihsan overrides qiyas in this case.

Core Idea
A trading slave's small gifts are permissible by istihsan; valuable items remain void by qiyas.

When a slave permitted to trade offers you a small gift or an invitation, can you accept? By strict analogy, any donation from a slave is void. Yet the Hanafi school, following the Prophet's example, permits acceptance of necessities. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its foundation in istihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Determine the act being ruled: accepting gifts, invitations, borrowing from a trading slave. State the overall ruling from Imam Muhammad: no harm in small gifts/invitations/borrowing; makruh for valuable items (clothes, money).
The slave must be ma'dhun lahu fi al-tijarah (permitted to trade).
2
Note the distinction: necessity vs. non-necessity
Separate the cases: small food items, use of mount, or simple invitations are considered necessities of trade; valuable items like clothes and coins are not necessary.
Necessity is the key factor that triggers istihsan.
3
Understand the qiyas position
By qiyas, all donations (tabarru') from a slave are void because the slave does not fully own property. So accepting any gift would be invalid.
Qiyas is the default ruling before considering istihsan.
4
Apply istihsan and its proof
Istihsan overrides qiyas due to necessity and the Prophet's practice. The Prophet accepted gifts from Salman and Barirah (when they were slaves) and accepted a slave's invitation.
The hadith from Anas (Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah) confirms the Prophet accepted a slave's invitation.
5
Derive the practical rule
Conclude: for small necessities, acceptance is permissible (mubah); for valuable items, it remains makruh (disliked) because the need is absent.
The distinction protects the owner's property while allowing smooth trade interactions.

Commentary
This passage contains a ruling from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir regarding transactions with a merchant slave (slave permitted to trade). The ruling distinguishes between items of necessity (small gifts, food, borrowing a mount) which are permissible to accept, and valuable items like clothes and money, which are makruh. The basis is istihsan (juristic preference) overriding qiyas. By pure analogy (qiyas), any donation from a slave is void because a slave cannot own property (and thus cannot donate). However, due to necessity (the trader needs to engage in small exchanges for his business), istihsan allows acceptance of minor gifts and invitations. The proof is from the sunnah: the Prophet accepted gifts from slaves (Salman, Barirah) and accepted a slave's invitation. The distinction: when there is genuine need (like food or use of a mount), it is allowed; when there is no necessity (like valuable gifts), the original qiyas of voidness remains. Later commentators clarify that the slave must be ma'dhun (permitted to trade) and the gift must be small.

Key Terms
عبد تاجر (ʿabd tājir): A slave who is permitted by his owner to engage in trade (ma'dhun lahu fi al-tijarah).
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference; abandoning qiyas for a stronger evidence or necessity.
تبرع (tabarruʿ): Voluntary donation without compensation.

Return of Rizq When Recipient Dies Mid-Year: The Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Explains ruling and ikhtilaf on rizq return from kharaj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on returning rizq taken in advance from kharaj and explain the intra-Hanafi difference of opinion.

Core Idea
If the recipient dies mid-year, the asahh view requires returning the rizq, analogized to a wife's nafaqa.

This passage addresses a practical issue in state finance: when an official receives his annual stipend (rizq) from the land tax (kharaj) at the beginning of the year but dies before the year ends, must the family return the unearned portion? The matn records a well-known intra-Hanafi dispute, analogous to the case of a wife who dies after receiving annual maintenance in advance. The relied-upon view is that the rizq must be returned.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue and the Hanafi Position
State the specific case: a qadi or official receives the full year's rizq from kharaj in advance (because earlier practice collected kharaj at the start), then dies before the year ends. The Hanafi school's asahh (most correct) position is that the rizq must be returned to the treasury.
The hukm level is wajib (obligatory) according to the asahh view.
2
Recognize the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
The text says 'there is a well-known difference of opinion' (ikhtilaf ma'ruf). This means some Hanafi scholars hold that no return is required. However, the passage does not elaborate their evidence. The ikhtilaf is parallel to the case of a wife's nafaqa: if she dies mid-year after taking annual nafaqa in advance, some say no return, others say wajib.
The opposing view likely relies on the idea that the advance is a gift or compensation for past service, not a loan.
3
Analyze the Basis of the Asahh View
The asahh view analogizes the rizq to a wife's nafaqa. In Hanafi fiqh, if a husband gives his wife annual nafaqa in advance and she dies mid-year, the asahh view requires her heirs to return the unused portion because the nafaqa is for each day's maintenance. Similarly, the rizq is compensation for service over the entire year; if service is incomplete, the unearned portion must be returned.
This is a qiyas (analogy) from the nafaqa case to the rizq case.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
The practical difference is whether the qadi's estate owes money to the treasury. Under the asahh view, the estate must return the rizq for the remaining months. Under the opposing view, the family keeps the full amount. In fatwa, the asahh view is relied upon.
The commentator also notes that in his time, kharaj is collected at year-end, so rizq is given at year-end, which avoids the problem. But the ruling applies if advance payment occurs.

ترجمہ اور رسم جاری ہے رزق کو دینے کی سال کے شروع میں اسلئے کہ خراج لیا جاتا تھا سال کے شروع میں اور رزق دیا جاتا ہے اس سے ( خراج سے ) اور ہمارے زمانے میں خراج لیا جاتا ہے سال کے آخر میں اور جو خراج کیا جاتا ہے وہ سال گذشتہ کا خراج ہوتا ہے۔ یہی صحیح ہے اور اگر قاضی نے سال بھر کا رزق وصول کر لیا اور سال کے مکمل ہونے سے پہلے مر گیا۔ کہا گیا ہے کہ یہ اس اختلاف پر ہے جو پہچان لیا گیا ہے بیوی کے نفقہ کے بارے میں جبکہ وہ مر جائے سال کے درمیان میں سال کا نفقہ متجمل لینے کے بعد اور اصبح یہ ہے کہ واپس واجب ہے۔
تشریح مصنف فرماتے ہیں رزق وغیرہ خراج میں سے دیا جاتا ہے۔ اور متقدمین کے زمانہ میں خراج لینے کی عادت سال کے شروع میں تھی تو رزق بھی سال کے شروع میں دیا جاتا تھا لیکن ہمارے زمانہ میں خراج سال کے آخر میں لیا جاتا ہے تو رزق بھی سال کے آخر میں دیا جائے گا۔ اور اگر قاضی نے سال کے شروع میں سال بھر کا نفقہ وصول کر لیا اور سال کے مکمل ہونے سے پہلے ہی مر گیا تو فرماتے ہیں کہ اصح قول کے مطابق اس کی واپسی کرنی ہوگی ۔
The chapter of disliked matters. Before its completion, it is said that there is a well-known difference of opinion concerning the maintenance of a woman when she dies within the year after taking annual maintenance in advance. The most correct view is that it must be returned. Translation: And the custom continues to give rizq at the beginning of the year because kharaj used to be taken at the beginning of the year, and rizq is given from it. And in our time, kharaj is taken at the end of the year, and the kharaj taken is for the previous year. This is correct. And if the qadi received the rizq for the full year and died before the year's completion, it has been said that this is upon the known difference of opinion concerning the maintenance of a wife when she dies in the middle of the year after taking the year's maintenance in advance. And the most correct is that return is obligatory. Explanation: The author says rizq etc. is given from kharaj. And the practice of the predecessors was to take kharaj at the beginning of the year, so rizq was also given at the beginning of the year. But in our time, kharaj is taken at the end of the year, so rizq will also be given at the end of the year. And if the qadi received the full year's nafaqa at the beginning of the year and died before the year's completion, he says that according to the most correct opinion, it must be returned.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the Kitab al-Karahiyah regarding the return of rizq (stipend) taken in advance from kharaj (land tax). The matn states that there is a well-known difference of opinion (ikhtilaf ma'ruf) similar to the case of a wife's nafaqa when she dies within the year after taking annual nafaqa in advance. The asahh (most correct) view is that it must be returned. The commentary explains the custom of giving rizq at the beginning of the year because kharaj was collected at the beginning in earlier times, but in the commentator's era, kharaj is collected at the end, so rizq is given at the end. The ruling is that if the qadi receives the full year's rizq and dies before the year ends, the asahh opinion requires return, analogous to the wife's nafaqa case. The ikhtilaf is not detailed here, but the reliance is on the asahh view.

Key Terms
رزق (rizq): Stipend or salary given from kharaj (land tax) to officials or soldiers.
خراج (kharaj): Land tax collected from conquered lands; used to fund stipends.
نفقة (nafaqa): Maintenance, particularly a wife's financial support from her husband.
اصح (asahh): The most correct opinion among multiple views.
