The Validity of Muzara'ah: Sahibayn vs Abu Hanifa
Complete intra-Hanafi dispute on sharecropping validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on muzara'ah, explain their proofs, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of their disagreement.

Core Idea
Muzara'ah is invalid per Abu Hanifa due to wage uncertainty; Sahibayn validate it by analogy to mudarabah and necessity.

This passage presents one of the most well-known intra-Hanafi disputes: the validity of muzara'ah (sharecropping). Imam Abu Hanifa holds it invalid because of a prophetic prohibition and because the wage is unknown or non-existent, similar to the prohibited contract of qafiz al-tahhan. His two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argue that muzara'ah is valid by analogy to mudarabah and because people need such contracts. Understanding this dispute is crucial for appreciating how the Hanafi school handles contractual validity and how later practice often preferred the Sahibayn's view in mu'amalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa (invalid/fasid) and the Sahibayn (valid/sahih) regarding the contract of muzara'ah.
Both positions are within the Hanafi school.
2
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn analogize muzara'ah to mudarabah (both involve capital-labor partnership) and cite necessity (hajah) because people need such contracts.
They also respond to an objection about goats/chickens by noting the worker's labor must affect the increase.
3
Examine Abu Hanifa's proofs
Abu Hanifa cites the hadith forbidding mukhabarah (muzara'ah), analogy to qafiz al-tahhan (impermissible wage from subject matter), and the argument that the wage is unknown or non-existent.
The commentator notes that the hadith has been answered and that the ummah's practice supports validity.
4
Address the Khaybar incident
Abu Hanifa distinguishes the Prophet's transaction at Khaybar as kharaj muqasamah (land tax division), not a contract of muzara'ah. The Sahibayn had used it as proof for validity.
The absence of a fixed duration in Khaybar supports Abu Hanifa's interpretation.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the contract is invalid per Abu Hanifa, the worker gets ujrat al-mithl (market wage); if valid per Sahibayn, the agreed share is binding.
Later Hanafi fatwa generally adopts the Sahibayn's view.

Explanation: This is an answer to an objection. Mukhabarah is called muzara'ah. But here the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij and two other men was not considered; in response to them, the Prophet forbade muzara'ah. Several answers to this hadith are given in Nasb al-Rayah, vol. 4, p. 80. (2) This is like qafiz al-tahhan. Its detail: a miller grinds flour, and the owner of the grain gives the wage from that flour, e.g., a tenth, etc. That is not permissible. But qafiz al-tahhan's impermissibility is textually established, while the analogized case is not textually established, and the ummah's practice on it exists, and the ummah's practice on a non-textual case is a proof for permissibility. Commentary
This passage presents the complete intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of muzara'ah (sharecropping). The author first presents the position of Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani): they validate muzara'ah by analogy (qiyas) on mudarabah, since both involve partnership of capital and labor. Additionally, they cite necessity (hajah) because people need such contracts. The passage then addresses an objection: if muzara'ah is valid by this reasoning, why are contracts involving goats, chickens, or silkworms (where increase comes from the animals' actions, not the worker's labor) not valid? The answer distinguishes that these cases lack the worker's effect on the increase, so they do not constitute a valid partnership contract. Next, Imam Abu Hanifa's proofs are given: (1) a hadith forbidding al-mukhabarah (muzara'ah); (2) analogy to qafiz al-tahhan (the miller's measure), where wage is from the thing worked on, which is invalid; (3) ignorance (jahl) or non-existence of the wage invalidates the contract. The Khaybar incident is interpreted as kharaj muqasamah (land tax division), not muzara'ah. The commentator adds critical refinements: the hadith can be answered by noting its specific context; qafiz al-tahhan is textual but the analogized case is not, and the ummah's practice supports muzara'ah; the jahl in mudarabah does not invalidate because it is a form of partnership. This shows the depth of Hanafi legal reasoning.

Key Terms
المزارعة (al-muzara'ah): A contract of sharecropping where one party provides land and seed, the other works, and the produce is shared according to agreed proportion.
المضاربة (al-mudarabah): A partnership where one provides capital, the other labor, and profit is shared.
قفيز الطحان (qafiz al-tahhan): A contract where a miller is hired to grind grain, and his wage is a measure (qafiz) of the flour produced.

Imam Shafi'i's Argument for the Validity of Musaqat over Muzara'ah
Presents Imam Shafi'i's view that only Musaqat is valid independently, Muzara'ah only as subsidiary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Imam Shafi'i's qiyas-based argument for the independent validity of Musaqat and the subsidiary-only validity of Muzara'ah, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools.

Core Idea
Shafi'i uses qiyas to mudaraba: Musaqat resembles it, Muzara'ah does not — so only Musaqat is valid independently.

In this passage, we encounter a key inter-madhhab dispute regarding agricultural partnerships. Imam Shafi'i argues that Musaqat (tree sharecropping) is valid independently, while Muzara'ah (land sharecropping) is only valid when subsidiary to a Musaqat contract. His proof is based on qiyas (analogy) to the unanimously accepted contract of mudaraba (profit-sharing). Understanding this argument helps clarify the Hanafi position, which relies on istihsan and established practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contracts
First, understand the two contracts discussed: Musaqat (caring for trees in exchange for a share of fruit) and Muzara'ah (cultivating land in exchange for a share of the crop). These are both forms of agricultural partnership.
Musaqat involves trees; Muzara'ah involves land.
2
Recognize the asl: mudaraba
Shafi'i takes mudaraba (profit-sharing partnership where the worker provides labor and the investor capital) as the original case (asl) that is valid by consensus. He then uses qiyas to determine the validity of Musaqat and Muzara'ah.
Mudaraba is unanimously valid.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Shafi'i argues that Musaqat resembles mudaraba because in both, the worker shares in the increase (profit/fruit) but not in the original capital (trees/capital). In contrast, Muzara'ah involves the seed, which is capital; if the seed-owner stipulates to take seed first from produce, the contract becomes invalid. Thus, Muzara'ah does not fit the analogy.
Key point: sharing in capital vs. sharing in increase.
4
Note the conclusion
Based on this qiyas, Shafi'i rules that Musaqat is valid as an independent contract, while Muzara'ah is only valid if it is subsidiary (taba'an) to a Musaqat contract.
Muzara'ah is not outright invalid; it is valid when attached to Musaqat.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Shafi'i school, an independent Muzara'ah contract (e.g., just for bare land) is not valid; it must be part of a Musaqat arrangement. Under Hanafi school, both contracts are valid independently, based on practice of the Prophet and istihsan.
This difference affects how agricultural partnerships are structured in each school.

ترجمہ اور شافعی نے فرمایا کہ مساقات جائز ہے اور مزارعت جائز نہیں ہے مگر مساقات کے تابع ہو کر اسلئے کہ اصل اس میں مضاربت ہے اور مساقات کے زیادہ مشابہ ہے اس لئے کہ مساقات میں زیادتی کے اندر شرکت ہے نہ کہ اصل میں اور مزارعت میں اگر نفع کے اندر شرکت کی شرط کی جائے نہ کہ پیج میں اس طریقہ پر کہ پیداوار کی اس سے بیج کو اٹھانے کی شرط کی جائے تو عقد مزارعت فاسد ہو جاتا ہے تو ہم نے مساقات کو اصل قرار دیا اور مزارعت کو مساقات کے تابع بنا کر جائز قرار دیا جیسے زمین کی بیع میں شرب اور زمین کے وقف میں منقول ۔ تشریح یہ پوری عبارت امام شافعی کا مسلک اور دلیل بیان کرنے کیلئے ہے وہ فرماتے ہیں کہ مزارعت تو جائز نہیں ہاں مساقات جائز ہے۔ البتہ اگر مزارعت مساقات کے تابع ہوتو بر بناء تبعیت مزارعت بھی جائز ہے۔
مثلاً ایک باغ ہے جس کے دو حصوں میں درخت ہیں اور ایک حصہ میں خالی زمین پڑی ہوئی ہے تو عامل کو باغ کے ساتھ وہ زمین بھی دے دی کہ اسمیں کھیتی کرے تو جائز ہے ورنہ استقلالاً مزارعت جائز نہیں ہے۔
اب امام شافعی دلیل بیان فرماتے ہیں کہ مضاربت بالا تفاق جائز ہے اب ان دونوں میں سے مضاربت کے ساتھ جس کی مشابہت زیادہ ہو وہ جائز ہوگا۔
اور جب ہم نے غور کیا تو معلوم ہوا کہ مضاربت کے ساتھ مساقات کو زیادہ مشابہت ہے کیوں؟ اس لئے کہ مضاربت میں رأس المال میں مضارب کی کوئی شرکت نہیں ہوتی بلکہ نفع میں ہوتی ہے اور یہی حال مساقات میں ہے کہ عامل کی کوئی شرکت راس المال یعنی باغ میں نہیں ہوتی بلکہ پھلوں میں شرکت ہوتی ہے اور مزارعت اس کے برعکس ہے کہ وہاں بیج میں شرکت ہوتی ہے۔ یہاں تک کہ اگر بیج والا یہ شرط لگائے کہ میں پہلے اپنے پیج کی مقدار پیداوار سے لوں گے تو مزارعت فاسد ہو جاتی ہے الہذا ثابت ہوا کہ مضاربت کے ساتھ مساقات کا زیادہ جوڑ ہے تو ہم نے مساقات کو جائز کہا اور مزارعت کو نا جائز۔
البتہ تبعیت کے طریقہ پر مزارعت جائز ہے اور استقلال و تبعیت کے اعتبار سے بسا اوقات احکام میں تفاوت ہوتا ہے۔ جیسے شرب کی بیع مستقلاً جائز نہیں مگر زمین کے تابع کر کے جائز ہے جیسے حمام وقف کیا تو اس کے ساتھ اس کے لوٹے وغیرہ کا وقف بھی جائز ہے۔
Imam Shafi'i's perspective and proof on Musaqat. And al-Shafi'i said: Mu'amalah (Musaqat) is valid, and Muzara'ah is not valid except as subsidiary to Mu'amalah, because the underlying principle (asl) in this is mudaraba, and Mu'amalah (Musaqat) is more similar to it, because in it there is partnership in the increase (fruit) without the original capital (trees). And in Muzara'ah, if one stipulates partnership in profit without the seed, by stipulating that the seed be taken from the produce, it becomes invalid. So we made Mu'amalah (Musaqat) the original and allowed Muzara'ah as subsidiary to it, like the right of irrigation (sharb) in the sale of land, and movable property in the endowment of real estate. Translation: And Shafi'i said that Musaqat is valid and Muzara'ah is not valid except as subsidiary to Musaqat, because the original here is mudaraba and Musaqat is more similar to it, because in Musaqat there is partnership in the increase, not in the original capital. And in Muzara'ah, if one stipulates partnership in profit but not in the seed, such that the seed is taken from the produce, the contract becomes invalid. So we made Musaqat the original and allowed Muzara'ah as subsidiary to it, like the sale of irrigation rights with land, and movable property in endowment of real estate. Explanation: This entire statement is to explain Imam Shafi'i's school and proof. He says that Muzara'ah is not valid, but Musaqat is valid. However, if Muzara'ah is subsidiary to Musaqat, then on the basis of subsidiary status, Muzara'ah is also valid. For example, there is a garden with trees in two parts and one part is empty land; the worker is given that land along with the garden to cultivate, then it is valid. Otherwise, Muzara'ah independently is not valid. Now Imam Shafi'i presents his proof: mudaraba is unanimously valid. Now between these two (Musaqat and Muzara'ah), whichever is more similar to mudaraba will be valid. And when we considered, we found that Musaqat has more similarity to mudaraba. Why? Because in mudaraba, the worker does not share in the capital, only in the profit. Similarly, in Musaqat, the worker does not share in the capital (the garden), only in the fruit. And Muzara'ah is the opposite: there is sharing in the seed. So much so that if the seed-owner stipulates that he will first take his seed from the produce, the Muzara'ah contract becomes invalid. Therefore, it is proven that Musaqat has a stronger connection to mudaraba, so we declared Musaqat valid and Muzara'ah invalid. However, Muzara'ah is valid by way of subsidiary status. And sometimes there is a difference in rulings based on independence and subsidiary status. Just as the sale of irrigation rights independently is not valid, but when subsidiary to land it is valid; or when a bathhouse is endowed, its utensils etc. can also be endowed along with it.

Commentary
This passage presents the inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the validity of Musaqat and Muzara'ah. The text presents Imam Shafi'i's view and his proof. Shafi'i argues that the underlying principle (asl) for these contracts is mudaraba (profit-sharing partnership). Since mudaraba is unanimously valid, any contract that resembles it closely is valid. Shafi'i claims that Musaqat (sharecropping of trees) resembles mudaraba more than Muzara'ah does. In mudaraba, the worker (mudarib) does not share in the capital (ra's al-mal) but only in the profit. Similarly, in Musaqat, the worker does not share in the trees (the capital) but only in the fruit. In Muzara'ah, however, seed (which is capital) is involved, and if the seed owner stipulates that the seed be taken first from the produce, the contract becomes invalid according to Shafi'i. Therefore, Shafi'i concludes that Musaqat is valid as an independent contract, while Muzara'ah is only valid if it is subsidiary (taba'an) to a Musaqat contract—for example, when a garden has both trees and empty land, the worker can cultivate the land as part of the Musaqat arrangement.

The passage then elaborates on Shafi'i's reasoning with analogies: like the sale of irrigation rights which is only valid when sold with land, or the endowment of movable property that is only valid when accompanying an endowment of real estate. The Hanafi school, by contrast, holds both Musaqat and Muzara'ah as valid independently, based on the practice of the Prophet and Companions and on istihsan (juristic preference) overriding the qiyas (analogy) that Shafi'i uses.

Key Terms
مضاربة (muḍārabah): Profit-sharing partnership where one party provides capital and the other provides labor; used here as the asl (original case) in qiyas.
تبعية (tabaʿiyyah): Subsidiary status; a contract that is valid only when attached to another primary contract.
استقلال (istiqlāl): Independence; a contract undertaken on its own without dependence on another.

Key Takeaway
The Shafi'i school validates Musaqat independently via qiyas to mudaraba, and only permits Muzara'ah as subsidiary; the Hanafi school validates both independently based on practice and istihsan.
