Introduction to Qismah (Division) in Hanafi Fiqh
Q&A defining the key legal elements of qismah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition, cause, condition, and ruling of qismah according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Qismah divides joint property — but only upon demand and only if the property remains beneficial.

This passage opens the Book of Division by laying out its essential legal framework. It explains why division follows pre-emption and defines when partition is valid. Through a structured Q&A, you will learn the key parameters of qismah: what it is, what triggers it, what conditions must be met, and what legal effect it produces.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
Read the definition of qismah from the passage: it is the act of separating shares (iqtisam) among co-owners.
Note the synonyms: qismah, qism, iqtisam.
2
Understand the cause
Identify the sabab: the demand of all or some partners to obtain exclusive benefit from their shares.
Without demand, division is not valid.
3
Note the condition
The shart: the property must be divisible without loss of benefit (e.g., wells and watermills cannot be divided because their utility would be lost).
This is the key limitation.
4
Recognize the ruling
The hukm: determining each partner's share separately — the legal outcome of a valid division.
This ruling makes each share distinct and exclusively owned.
5
Relate to pre-emption
See why shuf'ah precedes qismah: complete ownership (tamlik kulli) is stronger than partial (tamlik juz'i), and shuf'ah is permissible while qismah sometimes obligatory.
This order teaches progressive complexity.

Commentary: The author mentioned the Book of Pre-emption (Shuf'ah) before the Book of Division. The reason for appropriateness between them is that just as the pre-emptor becomes owner of the buyer's property by force, similarly a partner (in some forms of division due to exchange) becomes owner of the other partner's share. And since in pre-emption the ownership is complete and in division it is partial, and because complete ownership is stronger than partial, the Book of Pre-emption was placed before the Book of Division. Also, pre-emption has permissibility while division sometimes has obligation; so the lower is placed before the higher for progression. Question: What is division? Answer: In al-Mughrib it is said to be the name for partition (iqtisam), and in al-Wasit it is also called partition. In al-Qamus, it is said qism is the name for division. But since the active participle qasim is used, it is appropriate to consider it the verbal noun of qasama al-shay', as mentioned in Qahastani. (Majma' al-Anhur vol.2 p.469, Shami vol.5 p.160) Question: What is the cause of division? Answer: All or some partners demand that they obtain such a right to benefit from their property that no other's partnership exists. Therefore, if they do not request division, it will not be valid. Question: What is the action of division? Answer: An act by which separation and distinction occurs between shares, such as weighing, measuring, or counting. Question: What is the condition of division? Answer: That benefit is not lost from the indivisible property; for this reason, a well, watermill, and similar things cannot be divided. Question: What is the ruling of division? Answer: Determining each partner's share separately. Question: Can its legitimacy be established from the Quran?

Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Division (Kitab al-Qismah) from the commentary Ashraf al-Hidayah. It begins by explaining the rationale for placing the chapter on pre-emption (shuf'ah) before division: both involve transfer of ownership, but pre-emption is a complete transfer while division is partial. The author then presents a series of questions and answers that define the key elements of division according to the Hanafi school. First, the definition: division (qismah) is a term for partition, referring to the act of separating shares among co-owners. The cause (sabab) of division is the demand of partners to obtain exclusive benefit from their property; without their request, division is not valid. The action (fi'l) of division involves physically separating shares through weighing, measuring, or counting. The condition (shart) for division is that the property must be divisible without loss of benefit; hence indivisible items like wells and watermills cannot be divided. The ruling (hukm) is simply the determination of each partner's share. The passage ends with a question about Quranic proof, but the answer is not provided in this excerpt. This structured Q&A format is typical for introducing the basic legal parameters of a topic in Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
قسمت (qismah): Division or partition of jointly owned property among co-owners.
تملیک (tamlik): Transfer of ownership; here used to contrast complete ownership (in pre-emption) with partial ownership (in division).
اقتسام (iqtisam): Partition; synonym for qismah.

Definition of Makruh and Scope of Kitab al-Karahiyyah
Introductory section on chapter placement and definition of makruh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of makruh (tahrimi) and distinguish the positions of Imam Muhammad, Abu Hanifa, and Abu Yusuf on its legal weight.

Core Idea
Makruh in the Hanafi school is not mere dislike; it is a prohibition without a decisive text.

This lesson opens the chapter on disliked actions (Kitab al-Karahiyyah) from Al-Hidayah. The author first explains why this chapter follows the chapter on sacrifice, reviews the various titles given to it by different Hanafi authorities, and then presents the central definition of 'makruh' as clarified by the founding imams. Understanding this definition is essential because it establishes that makruh tahrimi carries a legal weight near to haram, unlike the ordinary notion of mere disapproval.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chapter's context and titles
Read how the author of Al-Hidayah justifies placing Kitab al-Karahiyyah after Kitab al-Adhiyah. Then note the different titles given by other Hanafi scholars (e.g., Kitab al-Khatr wa al-Ibahah, Kitab al-Istihsan, Kitab al-Zuhd wa al-Wara').
This variety shows that the chapter covers both prohibited and permissible actions, and that the concept of makruh is linked to legal discretion (istihsan) and piety (zuhd).
2
Analyze the definition of makruh from the three imams
The passage records a key intra-Hanafi discussion: Imam Muhammad (nassan) says every makruh is haram, but since the textual evidence is not decisive (qati'), the term 'haram' is avoided. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say it is 'closer to haram' (ila al-haram aqrab). Understand the nuance: all agree the act must be avoided, but they differ on whether to call it haram directly.
This classification is the basis for the category 'makruh tahrimi' in later Hanafi fiqh.
3
Apply the definition to a practical example
Consider a reported prohibition that is based on a single, non-mutawatir hadith. Hanafi jurists would label it makruh tahrimi, meaning it is sinful to do, but the ruling is not as absolutely certain as haram from a decisive text. Distinguish this from makruh tanzihi (merely disliked).
The practical consequence: must avoid; if done, sin is incurred, and in some cases expiation may be required.

In Tuhfah and Khaniyyah, the title is 'Kitab al-Hathr wa al-Ibahah'. In Jami' al-Saghir and Al-Hidayah it is 'Karahiyyah', and in al-Mabsut and al-Dhakhirah it is 'Istihsan'. Commentary
This passage is an introductory section from the commentary on Al-Hidayah, explaining the placement and title of the chapter on disliked actions (Kitab al-Karahiyyah). The author begins by noting that Al-Hidayah's author placed this chapter after the chapter on sacrifice because many issues there involve dislikedness. However, 'Allamah 'Ayni objects that this is not unique to that chapter. He offers a better reason: both the issues of sacrifice and dislikedness are established by transmitted sources (athar and riwayah). The passage then reviews various titles given to this chapter by different Hanafi authorities: al-Quduri called it 'Kitab al-Khatr wa al-Ibahah' (Prohibition and Permission), others called it 'Kitab al-Istihsan' (Juristic Preference), and yet others 'Kitab al-Zuhd wa al-Wara'' (Asceticism and Piety). The purpose is to clarify that the chapter deals with actions that are either prohibited, permitted, or recommended to avoid out of piety. Next, the passage delves into the definition of 'makruh' (disliked). It reports three positions: from Imam Muhammad: every makruh is actually haram, but because there is no definitive text (nass qati') to prove it, the term haram is not applied. From Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: makruh is closer to haram. This establishes that in the Hanafi school, makruh (tahrimi) is not merely disliked but carries a weight similar to haram, differing only in the certainty of the evidence. The passage concludes by stating the book's contents, starting with a chapter on eating and drinking.

Key Terms
مكروه (makruh): In this passage, makruh is defined as an act that is prohibited (haram) in essence, but the term 'haram' is not applied because the evidence (dalil) for its prohibition is not definitive (qati'). It is either exactly haram (Muhammad's view) or close to haram (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf).

The Hanafi Definition of Makruh
Definitional probe on the term makruh and intra-Hanafi disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of makruh, distinguish between makruh tahrimi and tanzihi, and explain the disagreement between Imam Muhammad and the two shaykhs.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, unqualified 'makruh' means prohibitively disliked (tahrimi) unless explicitly labeled as 'tanzihi'.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah clarifies a crucial terminological point: what does 'makruh' mean in the Hanafi school? The author explains the position of the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) versus Imam Muhammad, and establishes the default understanding of the term when used without qualification. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying Hanafi rulings in daily practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term
The passage defines 'makruh'. What is the basic meaning according to the two shaykhs?
Note that the two shaykhs consider it a separate category between haram and mubah.
2
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
How does Imam Muhammad's usage differ from the two shaykhs? Why does he equate makruh with haram in some cases?
Imam Muhammad uses 'makruh' for what he considers haram when no definitive text exists.
3
Understand the default rule
When Hanafi jurists say 'makruh' without qualification, which meaning prevails? What is the significance of the term 'tanzihi'?
The default is makruh tahrimi; tanzihi must be explicitly stated.
4
Apply to examples
Consider a statement: 'It is makruh to swear falsely.' According to the two shaykhs, is this tahrimi or tanzihi? Why?
Swearing falsely is a major sin, but if evidence is zanni, it is makruh tahrimi, not haram.

Classification of Legal Evidence and Its Impact on Rulings
Methodological classification of legal evidence and corresponding rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify evidence according to its thubut (certainty of transmission) and dalala (certainty of meaning), and derive the corresponding hukm for each combination.

Core Idea
The strength of a ruling depends on both transmission and clarity of proof: four combinations of qat'i and zanni yield fard, wajib, sunnah, or makruh.

This passage provides a powerful analytical tool: a grid of evidence types based on the certainty of transmission (thubut) and certainty of meaning (dalala). Hanafi jurists use this classification to determine whether a ruling is fard, wajib, sunnah, mustahabb, or makruh tahrimi. Understanding this grid is essential for appreciating why some actions are obligatory while others are merely recommended.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two dimensions
Thubut (certainty of transmission) and Dalala (certainty of meaning). Each can be qat'i (certain) or zanni (probable).
Thubut refers to the authenticity of the source; Dalala to the clarity of its legal implication.
2
List the four combinations
Combine each possibility: (1) Zanni thubut & Qat'i dalala; (2) Zanni thubut & Zanni dalala; (3) Qat'i thubut & Zanni dalala; (4) Qat'i thubut & Qat'i dalala.
These are the only possibilities.
3
Assign the rulings
For each combination, determine the hukm: (1) wujub/karahah tahrimiyya; (2) sunnah/mustahabb; (3) wujub/karahah tahrimiyya (same as 1); (4) fard/haram.
Note that (1) and (3) yield the same result despite different thubut.
4
Interpret the result
Why does (4) give fard/haram while (1) and (3) give wajib? Because fard requires certainty in both dimensions; wajib requires certainty in at least one.
This explains why some obligations are called fard and others wajib in Hanafi terminology.
5
Apply to a familiar example
Consider the obligation of prayer: the Quranic verses are qat'i thubut and qat'i dalala (clear command) → fard. The sunnah prayers: proven by ahad hadith (zanni thubut) and the command is general (zanni dalala) → sunnah.
