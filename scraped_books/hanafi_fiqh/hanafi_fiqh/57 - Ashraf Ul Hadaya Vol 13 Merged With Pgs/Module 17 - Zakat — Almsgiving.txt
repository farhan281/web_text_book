Condition of 'Ushr First is Valid in Muzara'ah
Shows validity of 'ushr condition via proportional share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why a condition for a proportional share (musha') in muzara'ah is valid and distinguish it from a fixed quantity condition.

Core Idea
A condition for a proportional share preserves partnership and is valid; a fixed amount severs it and invalidates.

In muzara'ah, the share of each party must be a proportion (musha') of the total produce. A condition for a fixed amount introduces uncertainty and severs the partnership. This passage explains that even a condition to give one party 'ushr (one-tenth) first is valid because 'ushr is a known fraction, so it remains musha'. The key is the proportional nature, not the label.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and rating
The act is inserting a condition in a muzara'ah contract. The ruling is that it is valid (sahih) if the condition specifies a proportional share.
The condition itself is the focus, not the entire contract.
2
Locate the dalil
The author provides a rational analogy (qiyas) to the case of 'ushri land, where the tithe is taken out first and the remainder divided. This shows that a proportional deduction is acceptable.
The proof is aqli (rational), not naqli (textual).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The underlying principle is that the share must remain musha' (undivided proportion). In both cases, 'ushr (1/10) is a fraction of the total, so shuyu' (common ownership) is maintained.
Compare to a fixed number of units, which would break shuyu'.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The source indicates across-madhab agreement (muttafaq alayh). No internal Hanafi disagreement is mentioned.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The mufta bihi position is that such a condition is valid. The authority is Zahir al-Riwayah.
This is the relied-upon ruling in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof, distinguishing between a fixed amount and a proportional share. If the condition specifies a fixed amount, it is invalid; but if it specifies a proportional share like 'ushr (one-tenth), it is valid because it maintains shuyu' (proportionality). The comparison to 'ushri land (subject to tithe) clarifies that deducting a known fraction (like 1/10) before dividing the remainder preserves partnership, as the share is still proportional to the total produce. This covers both when 'ushr is for one party or for both (e.g., for the state).

مشاع (musha'): Undivided share, proportional part

Hanafi Proof for Permissibility of Fish and Prohibition of Other Sea Creatures
Presenting Hanafi proofs and rebutting opposing school's arguments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on sea creatures, identify the three proofs (Quran, hadith of frog, hadith of crab), and explain the Hanafi interpretation of the opposing school's evidence.

Core Idea
Only fish is halal from the sea; all other sea creatures are haram due to being impure.

The Hanafi school uniquely restricts halal sea creatures to fish alone. This lesson examines the textual proofs: the Quranic prohibition of impure things, the Prophet's forbidding of frog in medicine, and the prohibition of selling crab. We also see how the Hanafis reinterpret the opposing school's evidence from general permissibility.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that only fish is halal from sea creatures; all other sea creatures are haram.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Maliki and Shafi'i schools argue that all sea creatures are halal based on general proofs.
Their main proof: the hadith 'its water is pure and its carrion is halal'.
3
Trace the Hanafi proofs
The Hanafis cite three proofs: Quran 7:157 (prohibition of impure things), hadith forbidding frog in medicine, and hadith forbidding sale of crab.
Each proof is explained in the passage.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response to opponent's proofs
The opponent's verse about hunting is interpreted as referring to the act, not the meat; their hadith about carrion is specified to fish by the hadith of two dead animals.
The Hanafis use the concept of takhsis (specification) to limit the general texts.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Hanafis do not eat crab, shrimp, octopus, etc.; other schools permit them.
This is the practical consequence of the disagreement.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute. The Hanafi school presents its proof that all sea creatures except fish are haram, and fish is halal even when found dead. The proof consists of three elements: (1) The Quranic verse 'and He prohibits for them impure things' (Quran 7:157). The Hanafis argue that 'impure things' include all sea creatures except fish, which is explicitly excepted by the Sunnah. (2) The hadith forbidding medicine made with frog. If frog were halal, it would not be forbidden. (3) The hadith forbidding the sale of crab, which implies its meat is also haram. The passage then refutes the opposing school's arguments: The opponent's verse about hunting refers to the act of hunting, not the meat of all sea creatures. The opponent's hadith about 'carrion of the sea' is interpreted as referring specifically to fish, which is an exception. The Hanafis cite the well-known hadith: 'Two dead animals and two bloods have been made lawful for us: the two dead animals are fish and locust; the two bloods are liver and spleen.' This shows that fish is halal despite being dead, but other dead sea creatures are not included. The author emphasizes that all sea animals besides fish are inherently impure (khabith) and thus haram, in accordance with the verse. The authority weight is from the Zahir al-Riwayah, as this is a standard Hanafi position.

الحل ميتته (al-hallu maytatuhu): The halal of its carrion; a phrase from hadith indicating fish is halal even when dead.

Ruling on Floating Dead Fish (Tafi) and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Stating Hanafi ruling on floating dead fish and refuting opposing views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating floating dead fish (tafi), explain the opposing view of Malik and Shafi'i, and evaluate the Hanafi response using the hadith of Jabir.

Core Idea
A fish that floats dead without external cause is makruh tahrimi, not halal, due to the specific prohibition in the hadith of Jabir.

This lesson examines a specific inter-madhhab disagreement regarding the permissibility of eating fish that die naturally and float on water. The Hanafi school, based on a detailed hadith, considers it makruh, while the Maliki and Shafi'i schools consider it halal based on the general principle that the carrion of the sea is permissible. Understanding this dispute requires careful scrutiny of the proofs and the principles of specification.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling given by al-Quduri: eating tafi is makruh. Note that the commentary clarifies it is makruh tahrimi, close to haram.
The matn uses 'makruh' but later Hanafis specify makruh tahrimi.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that Malik and Shafi'i consider tafi halal. Their basis is the general hadith about sea carrion being halal.
They rely on the absolute wording of the hadith.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The opposing school uses the hadith 'the carrion of the sea is halal' (not quoted verbatim but referred to). They argue that tafi falls under this general permission.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis cite the more specific hadith of Jabir which explicitly prohibits tafi while allowing fish exposed by water or cast out. This hadith specifies (takhsis) the general ruling. Additionally, they define 'carrion of the sea' as only fish cast out by the sea, not those dying naturally.
The principle of takhsis al-amm applies here.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis avoid eating tafi, while Malikis and Shafi'is eat it without hesitation. This affects daily practice, especially for those following different madhhabs.
The dispute is well-known in fiqh of food.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning a specific type of dead fish: 'tafi', which is a fish that floats on the water, belly up, having died without any external cause. The Hanafi ruling, as stated by al-Quduri in the matn, is that eating tafi is makruh (according to the commentary, makruh tahrimi). Imam Malik and Imam al-Shafi'i consider it halal, relying on the general hadith that the carrion of the sea is halal. The Hanafi response is based on a more specific hadith narrated by Jabir ibn Abdullah, where the Prophet differentiated: fish that are exposed by water drying up or cast out by the sea are halal, but what floats (tafi) is not to be eaten. This hadith specifies the general ruling. The commentary further clarifies that the Hanafis define 'carrion of the sea' (maytah al-bahr) as fish that die due to an external cause (like being thrown out), not fish that die naturally and float. Thus, the general hadith is not applicable to tafi. The passage also includes a digression on Imam Malik's view about locust, which requires slaughter (beheading and roasting) for it to be halal, but the Hanafis disagree, citing the hadith of two dead animals including locust. The authority weight is from the Zahir al-Riwayah, as this ruling is standard in Hanafi fiqh books.

A father may not sacrifice from his minor child's wealth per Zahir al-Riwayah; the Shaykhan's view allowing it is not the fatwa basis.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: when a minor child has wealth, may the father or executor use that wealth to perform udhiyah (sacrifice) on the child's behalf? The great Hanafi imams differed, and the resolution involves understanding the nature of udhiyah as a pure act of worship, distinct from sadaqat al-fitr. We will walk through the positions, evidence, and the relied-upon ruling.

1
State the general Hanafi rule
Begin by identifying the baseline: the father is not obligated to sacrifice for his minor child according to Zahir al-Riwayah. This is based on the principle that pure acts of worship are not obligatory due to another person's maintenance.
There is a nawadir transmission that it is wajib, but the relied-upon view is that it is not.
2
Identify the dispute when the minor has wealth
Now consider the scenario where the minor possesses wealth. Note the two Hanafi positions: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow sacrifice from the minor's wealth; Muhammad and Zufar prohibit it, requiring the father to use his own funds.
Al-Shafi'i agrees with Muhammad.
3
Analyze the proof for each position
Examine the reasoning: The Shaykhan analogize to sadaqat al-fitr, where using the minor's wealth is permitted. The opponents argue that udhiyah is a pure qurbah; the bloodshed (iraqat al-dam) is the core worship, and the charity afterward is voluntary and cannot be imposed on the minor's property.
The text mentions an additional argument: the minor cannot eat all the meat, so the sale of leftovers for durable goods is a solution.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under the Shaykhan's view, the father can use the child's money; under Muhammad's view, he must use his own. This affects whether the child's savings are depleted or preserved.
5
Establish the tarjih (mufta bihi)
The text concludes: the fatwa is on Zahir al-Riwayah, which is the view of Muhammad (and Zufar). Thus it is not permissible to use the minor's wealth. However, al-Hidayah's 'asahh' (more correct) opinion prefers the Shaykhan's view, and some later Hanafis consider that mu'tamad.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya deals with the ruling of udhiyah (sacrifice) on behalf of a minor child. It is an intra-Hanafi dispute with cross-madhhab elements. The text first establishes the general rule: sacrifice is obligatory for oneself and for one's minor children, like sadaqat al-fitr. However, there are two transmissions from Abu Hanifa: one from Hasan ibn Ziyad (a nawadir transmission) that it is obligatory from the father's wealth for minor children, and the other from Zahir al-Riwayah (the relied-upon transmission) that it is not obligatory. The Zahir al-Riwayah position distinguishes between sadaqat al-fitr and udhiyah: sadaqat al-fitr becomes obligatory due to maintenance (nafaqah) and guardianship (wilayah), both of which exist for a minor, while udhiyah is a pure act of worship (qurbah mahdah) and acts of worship are not generally obligatory on someone due to another person. This mirrors their disagreement on sadaqat al-fitr. A third opinion (reported in al-Mabsut and al-Kafi) prohibits it entirely because sacrifice involves two purposes: bloodshed (which is loss of property) and charity of the meat; a father cannot cause loss to the minor's property nor give charity from it, and the minor cannot eat all the meat. Place of Sacrifice: Determining the Time of Udhiyah and the Hila for City Dwellers
Ruling and legal stratagem for early udhiyah based on animal location.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the time of sacrifice based on the animal's location and explain the legal stratagem (hila) for a city dweller to sacrifice early, using qiyas to zakat.

Core Idea
The place of the animal, not the owner, decides when udhiyah may be offered.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies that the permissibility of sacrificing before the Eid prayer depends on where the animal is located, not on the residence of the sacrificer. It also provides a practical legal stratagem (hila) for city dwellers who wish to sacrifice early, supported by a qiyas to zakat. Understanding this distinction helps avoid invalid sacrifices and provides flexibility within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage states that if the animal is in the countryside (sawad), sacrifice is valid at dawn; if in the city, only after the Eid prayer. Recognize that the decisive factor is the place of the animal (mahall), not the person (fa'il).
2
Understand the hila for city dwellers
A city dweller who wants to sacrifice early can send the animal to a rural area and sacrifice it at dawn. This stratagem changes the location of the animal, making early sacrifice permissible.
3
Analyze the qiyas to zakat
The author likens udhiyah to zakat: both obligations are waived if the wealth is destroyed before the time expires. In zakat, the nisab is considered; in udhiyah, the animal's location is considered. This analogy reinforces that the place of wealth determines the ruling.
4
Distinguish from sadaqat al-fitr
Unlike udhiyah and zakat, sadaqat al-fitr is not waived by loss of wealth after dawn. Thus, its ruling is tied to the person, not the place of wealth, explaining why the analogy does not extend to it.
5
Apply the ruling
Given a specific scenario, determine whether early sacrifice is valid by checking the animal's location. If the animal is in a rural area, sacrifice at dawn is valid; if in a city, sacrifice only after the prayer.

Qurbani's Location Consideration: Zakat Analogy
Explains why qurbani considers location like zakat, not person like fitr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers location (mahall) for qurbani by analogy to zakat, and distinguish qurbani from sadaqat al-fitr regarding the basis of obligation.

Core Idea
Qurbani's obligation considers where the wealth is, not where the owner is, just like zakat.

This passage clarifies a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: the basis of obligation in qurbani follows the model of zakat (location of wealth) rather than sadaqat al-fitr (location of the person). Understanding this analogy is essential for correctly fulfilling qurbani when the owner and wealth are in different places.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two analogies

Zakat considers mahall (place of wealth); fitr considers fa'il (person).
2
Understand the effect of wealth destruction
Examine how the timing of wealth destruction affects the obligation: before the time expires vs. after.
If wealth is destroyed before the days of nahr end, obligation falls; if after, it remains – like zakat.
3
Derive the principle of location
From the parallel in zakat, infer that the obligation is tied to the location of the wealth (mahall), not the owner's location (fa'il).
This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) preserving the analogy.
4
Apply to qurbani
Conclude that qurbani must be performed where the sacrificial animal (wealth) is located, not where the owner resides.
The same principle applies as in zakat: give to the poor where the wealth is.
5
Note the practical consequence
Understand that if the owner is in one city and the animal in another, the sacrifice should be made where the animal is.
This differs from sadaqat al-fitr, which follows the payer's location.

Preference of Sacrifice over Giving Value in Charity
Explains why actual sacrifice is better than charity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two reasons why the Hanafi school considers actual sacrifice superior to giving its price in charity.

Core Idea
Sacrifice is either wajib or sunnah and time-bound; charity is pure nafl and time-flexible — that is why sacrifice is better.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a common question: is it better to perform the sacrifice (udhiyah) during Eid al-Adha or to give the monetary value in charity? The text provides a clear ruling with two compelling proofs, including an elegant analogy to tawaf in Mecca for a distant traveler. Understanding this ruling helps students appreciate how the Hanafi school prioritizes acts of worship based on their legal status and temporal constraints.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules that sacrificing the animal during the days of nahr is superior (afdal) to giving its price in charity. First, note the act being compared and the conclusion.
2
Extract the first reason: hukm level
The text states: 'because it (the sacrifice) is either wajib or sunnah, and charity is pure nafl.' In Hanafi terminology, wajib and sunnah muakkadah are of higher legal rank than nafl. Even if we follow the Sahibayn's view that sacrifice is sunnah, it still outranks pure nafl.
Note the intra-Hanafi difference: Abu Hanifa says wajib, Sahibayn say sunnah muakkadah, but both agree on superiority over charity.
3
Extract the second reason: time-specificity
Sacrifice can only be performed in the specific days of nahr (10th-12th Dhul Hijjah), while charity can be given at any time. The opportunity for sacrifice is lost if the time passes, making it more valuable than a general good deed.
This mirrors the principle that acts with a limited window (mudhayyaq) are prioritized over those with ample time (muwassa).
4
Understand the analogy: tawaf vs prayer for the afaqi
The text analogizes to a person from outside Mecca (afaqi) who can only perform tawaf in Mecca but can pray nafl anywhere. Tawaf is superior in that context because it is location-specific, just as sacrifice is superior because it is time-specific.
This analogy shows how the 'qiyas' (analogical reasoning) is used: the illah (effective cause) is the restriction of the act to a specific time/location.

Making up for a Missed Sacrifice: Charity as Substitute
Ruling on missed sacrifice with qiyas rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a missed sacrifice and apply the qiyas to explain why charity is required.

Core Idea
When sacrifice days pass, obligation transfers to charity—rich give value, poor give live animal.

What happens if the days of sacrifice expire without performing the sacrifice? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling: the obligation does not drop; instead, it converts into a charitable substitute. This lesson unpacks the distinction between the rich and poor cases and the qiyas (analogy) that underpins the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation category
Determine if the person is rich (possesses nisab at the time of sacrifice) or poor (does not possess nisab). The rich are obligated by their wealth; the poor become obligated only by purchasing an animal with intention or by vowing.
2
Check purchase or vow for the poor
For a poor person, the sacrifice becomes wajib only if they purchased an animal with the intention of sacrifice or made a vow. If neither, no obligation existed and no substitute is required.
3
Determine the substitute
Rich: give the price (value) of a sheep in charity, whether they had purchased an animal or not. Poor (who had purchased): give the live animal as charity. The act of sacrifice is replaced by charity to clear the liability.
4
Understand the qiyas (analogy)
The ruling is based on qiyas to two cases: missed Friday prayer (made up as Zuhr) and missed fasting due to permanent incapacity (substituted by fidyah). In each, a time-specific obligation is replaced by a suitable substitute when the original act cannot be performed in time.
The illa (effective cause) is that the original act is time-bound; after its time, a substitute fulfills the obligation.
5
Note the special case for the poor
If a poor person gives the live animal in charity during the days of sacrifice itself, the obligation of shedding blood (actual slaughter) remains, so they must still sacrifice another animal. This is a later Hanafi detail from Shami.
Reference: Shami, vol. 5, p. 203

Commentary
This passage provides the ruling for a person who fails to perform the sacrifice within the appointed days. The text distinguishes between two cases: a rich person and a poor person. For a rich person (who is obligated to sacrifice due to his wealth), he must give the value (price) of a sheep in charity, whether he had already purchased an animal or not. For a poor person, who becomes obligated only by purchasing an animal with the intention of sacrifice or by vowing, he must give the animal itself alive in charity. The note adds that if a poor person gives the live animal in charity during the days of sacrifice itself (not after), then the obligation of sacrifice still stands—he must still slaughter another animal—because the specific act of shedding blood (the actual slaughter) remains obligatory. This is a meticulous detail from the later Hanafi text of Shami.

Mutual Halal After Eating the Sacrificial Meat
Ruling with proof by qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on mutual forgiveness (taḥlīl) to cases where both parties consumed the sacrificial meat before discovering the error, and explain the qiyas proof that supports it.

Core Idea
Mutual forgiveness (taḥlīl) completes a sacrifice when both parties ate the meat before knowing the mistake.

In the previous lesson, we learned that if the sacrificial meat is untouched, the owner takes it back. But what if both parties have already eaten the meat? Does the sacrifice become invalid, or is there a remedy? The Hidayah addresses this using a qiyas: just as feeding a rich person from the start is allowed, making it halal at the end is also valid. This lesson explores that ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The passage describes a situation where two people have exchanged sacrificial meat by mistake, eaten it, and only later become aware of the error. The meat is already consumed, so physical return is impossible.
This differs from scenarios where the meat is still intact.
2
State the Hanafi Ruling
The ruling is that each should mutually forgive the other (taḥlīl), and this makes the sacrifice valid (jā'iz). No compensation or repetition is required.
The imperative 'فليحالل' indicates it is necessary for validity.
3
Analyze the Dalil (Qiyas)
The proof is an analogy: if a rich person had been fed the sacrificial meat from the beginning (before distribution to the poor), it would still be permissible. Similarly, making it halal at the end—by mutual forgiveness—yields the same permissibility.
The qiyas is based on the underlying permission to allow consumption, not on the wealth status.
4
Understand the Wajh al-Dalalah
The wajh al-dalalah (indication) is that the permissibility of feeding a rich person at the start shows that the purpose of consuming the meat can be fulfilled regardless of the recipient's need. Thus, mutual forgiveness at the end achieves the same purpose.
The ʿilla (effective cause) is the permissibility of consumption by a non-eligible person.
5
Note Cross-Madhhab Differences
Some schools (e.g., Shafiʿi) may require actual replacement of the meat or compensation, as they do not accept mutual forgiveness as a remedy. This difference stems from different usul principles regarding ownership and qiyas.
The Hanafi position relies on istihsan and this qiyas; the mufta bihi is mutual halal.

Dispute and Compensation for Meat Value
Ruling when parties dispute over udhiyah meat value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two parties dispute over the value of sacrificial meat and explain why the compensation must be given in charity.

Core Idea
When parties dispute over udhiyah meat, each claims the value of his own meat but must give it in charity—it is like selling the meat.

This passage addresses a scenario where two people have sacrificed animals (udhiyah) and later consume each other's meat by mistake or mutual agreement, then dispute about the value. The Hanafi school clarifies that each owner is entitled to compensation for his meat, but because receiving money for the meat of a sacrifice is akin to selling it (which is prohibited), the compensation must be given in charity. This ruling is derived through qiyas (analogy) to the established ruling on selling sacrificial meat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Two parties (e.g., Zayd and Khalid) each sacrifice an udhiyah. They consume each other's meat, then dispute about the value or quality of their respective meat.
The passage assumes the meat was consumed or destroyed, so the original meat cannot be returned.
2
Recognize the right to compensation
Each party has the right to hold the other liable for the market value of his own meat. This is because the meat belongs to the owner of the sacrificed animal.
The compensation is wajib (obligatory) upon the one who consumed the other's meat.
3
Understand the implication of receiving value
Receiving money in exchange for the meat is analogous to selling the meat of the sacrifice. Selling sacrificial meat is prohibited, but if it occurs, the price must be given in charity.
The ruling on selling udhiyah meat is established in earlier fiqh sections.
4
Apply the established ruling through qiyas
The illa (effective cause) is receiving monetary value for the meat of udhiyah. Therefore, the value received in the dispute must be given in charity, just as in the case of a sale.
This is a clear (jali) qiyas, as both cases involve exchanging meat for money.
5
Note the underlying reason
The meat belongs to the owner of the sacrifice. Consuming another's meat necessitates compensation, but the charitable disposition prevents profiting from the sacrifice, preserving its spiritual purpose.
The goal is to uphold the inviolability of udhiyah meat and avoid any commercial benefit.

Sacrificing a Usurped Goat: Ownership via Ghasp
Ruling on sacrificing a usurped animal with proof and comparison

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sacrificing a usurped animal, explain why it is valid, and distinguish it from the case of a deposited animal.

Core Idea
Usurpation (ghasp) grants ownership for valid sacrifice, but the usurper must pay the value.

Sacrificing an animal you do not own usually invalidates the act. But what if you took the animal unlawfully? The Hanafi school distinguishes between a usurper and a trustee. This passage explains why the usurper's sacrifice is valid while the trustee's is not, based on the principle that ghasp itself is a cause of ownership in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage presents two scenarios: (1) a person who usurps a goat and sacrifices it, and (2) a person who holds a goat as a deposit (wadi'ah) and sacrifices it. Note the different outcomes.
2
State the ruling for each
For the usurper: the sacrifice is valid (ja'iz), but he is liable for the value (daman). For the trustee: the sacrifice is not valid, and he becomes liable only after slaughter.
3
Identify the legal reason (illa)
The text states 'because he owns it by the prior usurpation' (lānahu mallakahā bi-sābiq al-ghaṣb). In Hanafi law, ghasp is a cause of ownership (sabab al-milk) even though it is sinful. Thus, at the moment of slaughter, the usurper is the owner, satisfying the condition that the sacrificer must own the animal.
4
Analyze the contrast with wadi'ah
For a deposited animal, the trustee (mudi') does not become owner by accepting the deposit. He only becomes liable upon slaughter (since he destroys it), and ownership transfers only after slaughter. Since ownership was absent before slaughter, the sacrifice is invalid.
The key distinction is the timing of ownership: before slaughter (ghasp) vs. after (wadi'ah).
5
Consider the cross-madhhab perspective
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may not consider ghasp a cause of ownership, so for them the sacrifice would be invalid. Hanafi fiqh holds that possession acquired through usurpation is sufficient for ownership liability, thus the sacrifice is valid.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in differing usul regarding ownership.

Commentary: Wadi'ah (trust) is not a cause of ownership for the trustee, while ghasp (usurpation) is a cause of ownership for the usurper, even though it leads to impossibility of combining the substitute (value) and the object. Nevertheless, ghasp is a cause of ownership, and for the validity of sacrifice, it is necessary that the sacrificer be the owner before slaughter. So whoever usurped someone's goat and sacrificed it, the sacrifice is valid, and the usurper is liable for the value of that goat.
Source pages p. 303

Commentary
This passage distinguishes between two cases: (1) a person who usurps (ghasp) a goat and then sacrifices it for himself, and (2) a person who is entrusted with a goat as a deposit (wadi'ah) and then sacrifices it. In the first case, the usurper becomes the owner of the goat by virtue of the usurpation (ghasp is a cause of ownership, even though it is unlawful), so at the time of slaughter he is the owner, making the sacrifice valid. However, he is liable for the value of the goat to the original owner because of the usurpation. In the second case, the trustee (mudi') does not become owner by receiving the deposit; he only becomes liable for the value when he slaughters it (since he destroys it without ownership), so ownership transfers only after slaughter. Since ownership was not established before slaughter, the sacrifice is not valid for him. The key principle is that for a sacrifice to be valid, the sacrificer must be the owner of the animal at the time of slaughter.

Key Terms
الغصب (al-ghaṣb): Unlawful appropriation of another's property without permission.
الوديعة (al-wadīʿa): A deposit of property for safekeeping; the trustee (mudiʿ) does not acquire ownership.

Rational Evidence for Uncovering Face and Palms
Explaining the rational proof for necessity in uncovering face and palms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rational proof for permitting a woman to uncover her face and palms in Hanafi fiqh, and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and the relied-upon view.

Core Idea
Necessity for transactions permits uncovering the face and palms, but only to that extent.

This lesson explores the rational proof (dalil aqli) used by Hanafi scholars to justify the permissibility of a woman uncovering her face and hands in the presence of non-mahram men. The necessity (darurah) arises from her need to engage in economic and social transactions. However, this permissibility is limited, and there is an intra-Hanafi debate about whether it extends to the feet and forearms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its basis
The act is a woman uncovering her face and palms in front of non-mahram men. The basis is necessity (darurah) for transactions.
2
Understand the rational proof
The proof is aqli: because women need to deal with men in buying, selling, etc., they must show their face (for identification) and hands (for handling items). This necessity allows the otherwise prohibited action.
3
Note the extent of permissibility
The text specifies that this ruling implies looking at her foot is not permitted. This is the base view (Imam al-Quduri).
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa allowed looking at feet because of occasional need; Abu Yusuf allowed looking at forearms because they may become exposed habitually.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that only face and palms are permissible; feet and forearms are not. In later times, even looking at the face of young women is restricted if desire is feared.

Commentary
This passage presents a rational proof (dalil aqli) for the permissibility of a woman uncovering her face and hands in the presence of non-mahram men due to necessity (darurah). The necessity arises because women need to engage in transactions (e.g., buying, selling) with men, which requires showing the face (for identification) and hands (for handling money/goods). The text then clarifies that this ruling by Imam al-Quduri implies that looking at her feet is not permissible. However, there is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa considers looking at the feet permissible due to occasional need (e.g., walking barefoot), and Abu Yusuf extends permissibility to the forearms (also because they may become exposed during chores). The author notes that this ruling applied in the time of the Salaf, but in later times (including the commentator's era), looking at the face of young women is prohibited (makruh or haram).

Key Terms
ضرورة (darurah): Necessity that allows dispensation from the normal prohibition; here, the need for social and economic transactions.
معاملة (muamalah): Transactional dealings, including buying, selling, and social interactions.
