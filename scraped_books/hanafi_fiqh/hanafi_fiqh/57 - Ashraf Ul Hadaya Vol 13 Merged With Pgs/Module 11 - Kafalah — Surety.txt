Kafala bil Nafs and Shuf'a: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on kafala bil nafs vs shuf'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two narrations concerning kafala bil nafs when the surety offers payment, and explain the disagreement over whether the rule also applies to shuf'a.

Core Idea
When a surety pays to be released from personal suretyship, two narrations disagree whether the suretyship voids—but both agree no payment is due.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a specific question in kafala: if a surety offers money to the creditor to be released from kafala bil nafs, is the arrangement valid? The Hanafi school contains two narrations on this point, and scholars further debate whether the same rule applies to shuf'a. Understanding this dispute helps clarify the scope of analogical reasoning within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Case
A surety (kafil) offers the creditor a sum of money to release him from kafala bil nafs (surety of the person). The question is whether this payment voids the suretyship and whether the payment is due.
The suretyship here is of the person, not of a debt.
2
State the Two Narrations
First narration: the kafala becomes void and the payment is not obligatory (like shuf'a). Second narration: the kafala remains valid and the payment is not obligatory.
Both narrations agree that the payment is not wajib.
3
Analyze the Disagreement
The conflict is about the effect on the suretyship: is it voided or does it continue? The first narration treats it like shuf'a, where accepting payment to drop the right voids both the right and the payment. The second narration holds that the suretyship remains intact, but the payment is still not due.
The practical difference: whether the surety remains obligated to present the principal.
4
Examine the Extension to Shuf'a
Some Hanafi scholars claim there is a similar narration in shuf'a (that shuf'a does not become void with payment), while others restrict that narration only to kafala, retaining the original rule for shuf'a (both void).
The passage mentions two opinions on the scope of the second narration.
5
Note the Reference to al-Mabsut
The author states that the details of this issue are known in its place, i.e., in al-Mabsut. This indicates that the Hidayah passage is a summary and the full discussion is in the foundational work.
Students seeking complete treatment should consult al-Mabsut.

Is kafala bil nafs like shuf'a or not? And the kafala bil nafs in this matter is like shuf'a in one narration, and in another the kafala does not become void and the money is not obligatory. And it is said: this narration is in shuf'a, and it is said: it is specific to kafala. And it is known in its place. Translation: And kafala bil nafs in this context (regarding the voidance of kafala and compensation) in one narration is like shuf'a, and in another narration kafala does not become void and the money is not obligatory. And it was said that this narration is only in kafala and is known in its place. Explanation: A person stood surety for someone (kafala bil nafs), meaning the surety would present the principal to the creditor. Then the surety said to the creditor: 'Take a thousand rupees and release me from the suretyship.' He did. What is the ruling? It is said there are different narrations. According to one narration it is like shuf'a: the kafala becomes void and the compensation is not wajib. According to another narration, the money is not wajib and the kafala does not become void. Some Hanafi scholars said that there is a similar narration in shuf'a: shuf'a does not become void and compensation is not wajib. Others said no, this narration is specific to kafala; in shuf'a the original ruling is that compensation is void and shuf'a is void. Then the author said that the details of this issue are in its place, i.e., in al-Mabsut.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding kafala bil nafs (surety of the person). The case: a surety offers the creditor money to release him from the suretyship. The core issue is whether this is analogous to shuf'a (where accepting money to drop the right voids both the money and the right) or whether the kafala remains intact with no money due. There are two narrations: one treats it like shuf'a (both void), the other says kafala remains valid and no money is owed. A further nuance: some scholars extend the second narration to shuf'a, claiming there is a narration that shuf'a also does not become void. Others restrict that narration to kafala only, maintaining the original rule for shuf'a (both void). The author refers to al-Mabsut for details.

Key Terms
کفالت بالنفس (kafala bil nafs): Suretyship of the person, where the surety guarantees to present the principal (the one for whom he is surety) to the creditor.
شفعہ (shuf'a): Pre-emption, the right of a co-owner to purchase the share sold to a third party.
