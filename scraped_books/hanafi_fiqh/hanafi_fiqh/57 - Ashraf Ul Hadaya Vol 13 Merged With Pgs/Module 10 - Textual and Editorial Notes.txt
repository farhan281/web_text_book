Non-valuable Assets for Shuf'ah: Manafi', Dam, and 'Itq
Ruling that usufruct, blood, and emancipation are not valuable for shuf'ah with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of valuable property (mutaqawwim) for shuf'ah and apply it to distinguish between transactions that trigger shuf'ah and those that do not.

Core Idea
For shuf'ah, value means the ability to substitute in a specific intended meaning — usufruct, blood, and emancipation lack this.

In this passage, the Hanafi school clarifies a key restriction on pre-emption (shuf'ah): not everything considered valuable in other contexts (like hire or marriage) automatically triggers shuf'ah. The text defines what 'valuable' means for shuf'ah and applies that definition to usufruct, blood, emancipation, and two dower scenarios. By the end, you will understand why a house given as dower does not give rise to shuf'ah, but a house sold for dower does.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Locate the passage's main claim: usufruct (manafi'), blood (dam), and emancipation ('itq) are not valuable (ghayr mutaqawwim) for the purpose of shuf'ah. Note that this is a negative ruling — no right of pre-emption arises from their sale.
The ruling applies to both co-ownership and neighbor's shuf'ah.
2
Extract the Hanafi definition of 'value'
Find the definition: 'Value is that which can stand in place of another in the specific intended meaning' (al-qīmatu mā yaqūmu maqāma ghayrihi fī al-ma'nā al-khāss al-maṭlūb). Understand that this definition restricts value to property that can serve as a direct substitute in a specific legal purpose.
This definition excludes items whose benefit is personal or non-transferable.
3
Apply the definition to the three items
For each item (usufruct, blood, emancipation), test whether it meets the definition. Usufruct is time-bound and personal; blood is not a commodity; emancipation is a one-time act with no fixed value. None can substitute in the specific meaning required for shuf'ah.
The text acknowledges that these items have value in other contexts (e.g., hire, dower) but only by necessity.
4
Analyze the two dower cases
Read the two scenarios: (1) a house specified as dower after marriage, and (2) a house sold explicitly for dower al-mithl or named dower. In the first, the house is considered a countervalue for intercourse (not valuable for shuf'ah). In the second, it is a pure sale of property (triggers shuf'ah).
The key distinction is whether the transaction is a property exchange or a substitute for usufruct.
5
Compare the Shafi'i position and Hanafi response
Imam Shafi'i considers these items as valuable in all contexts. The Hanafi response invokes the maxim 'necessity is measured by its extent' (al-ḍarūra tuqaddaru bi qadri al-ḍarūra): valuation in nikah/ijara is an exception, not transferable to shuf'ah.
This illustrates a key Hanafi approach: limiting exceptional rulings to their necessity.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof explaining what is considered valuable (mutaqawwim) for the purpose of shuf'ah. The Hanafi position is that usufruct (manafi‘), blood (dam), and emancipation (‘itq) are not valuable assets, hence their sale does not give rise to a right of pre-emption. The proof is the definition of value: it must be able to substitute for another in a specific intended meaning, which these things cannot. The text addresses an implicit disagreement with Imam Shafi‘i, who considers such items valuable. The Hanafi response is that any valuation of these in other contexts (e.g., usufruct in hire, or dower in marriage) is solely due to necessity, and necessity is limited to its scope; it does not extend to shuf‘ah. The passage then applies this to two cases: (1) a house given as dower after marriage—no shuf‘ah, as it is like a dower fixed in the contract itself, being a countervalue for intercourse; (2) a house sold explicitly for dower al-mithl or named dower—shuf‘ah applies because it is a pure exchange of property for property (sale). The commentary clarifies that in the first case the house is a substitute for the usufruct of intercourse (which is not valuable for shuf‘ah), while in the second it is a sale of property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a narrated mas'alah is inconsistent with the matn and explain the role of later Hanafi commentators in verifying textual attributions.

Core Idea
Not every narration from a Hanafi authority fits the matn; later scholars scrutinize and sometimes reject them.

This passage is a critical note (tanbih) from the commentator of Ashraf al-Hidaya. He draws attention to a particular case (juz'iyyah) that earlier commentators had quoted from Qazi Khan and explained, but he asserts that this case does not align with the actual wording of the matn (Al-Hidayah). The commentator then points to Ibn al-Humam's Fath al-Qadir, which had already rejected this narration and clarified the inconsistency. This lesson demonstrates the careful textual criticism that is a hallmark of the Hanafi scholarly tradition—later masters do not accept every transmitted ruling blindly; they verify its fit with the authoritative text.

The passage mentions that the case was attributed to Qazi Khan. Qazi Khan was a respected Hanafi jurist, but his narrations are sometimes critiqued by later scholars.

Attribution of the Bayyinah Ruling in Division
Intra-Hanafi dispute over attribution of bayyinah ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two views on the attribution of the ruling on bayyinah in division of land and explain why the view of all three imams is preferred.

Core Idea
The necessity of bayyinah in division is the agreed ruling of all three Hanafi imams, not just Imam Abu Hanifa.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses an internal Hanafi debate: is the ruling that bayyinah (proof) is necessary for dividing land specific to Imam Abu Hanifa, or does it represent the consensus of the three master imams? The author resolves this by examining the nature of the division itself, concluding that it is a division of ownership that inherently requires proof, making it a ruling shared by all.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ikhtilaf
Precisely state what is disputed: the attribution of the ruling on the necessity of bayyinah in division. The ruling itself is not in question.
The dispute is not over the hukm but over whose opinion it is.
2
State the two views
View 1: The ruling is only Imam Abu Hanifa's statement. View 2: The ruling is the statement of all three imams (al-kull).
The text explicitly mentions these two views.
3
Examine the Hanafi dalil for View 2
The author argues that division of land is necessarily qismat al-mulk because qismat al-hifz is not needed for land (land is already preserved). Ownership division requires proof of ownership, thus bayyinah is required. This reasoning is general and applies to all imams.
The dalil is based on the distinction between the two types of division.
4
Understand the tarjih mechanism
The author declares the view of al-kull as al-asahh (most correct). This tarjih is based on the consistency of the reasoning and the texts.
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah makes this tarjih.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If the ruling is only Abu Hanifa's, it could be considered less authoritative or subject to exception. If it is from all three, it carries greater weight and is more binding in fatwa.
The practical consequence is in the hierarchy of legal authority.

بينة (bayyinah): Evidence or proof, typically two male witnesses or one male and two females, as required for legal claims.

Sharing a Cow Among Seven: Conditions and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on seven partners buying a cow for sacrifice with death and permission of heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of unified qurbah for valid shared sacrifice and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between qiyas and istihsan regarding the deceased partner's share.

Core Idea
Sacrifice for a deceased partner is valid by istihsan; without unified qurbah, the entire sacrifice is void.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya presents a classic Hanafi case of seven partners purchasing a cow for sacrifice. The ruling hinges on the intention of qurbah—closeness to Allah—and reveals an important intra-madhhab disagreement between Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf. Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of istihsan and qiyas in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and condition
Seven people buy a cow for sacrifice; one dies before slaughter; heirs allow inclusion of the deceased's share. The key condition: all partners must have intention of qurbah (piety), regardless of the specific type (qurbani, dam qiran, dam tamattu').
2
Recognize the definition of qurbah
Qurbah unites all acts of devotion. Shedding blood (dhakaat) for sacrifice is indivisible with respect to qurbah: if any partner lacks qurbah (e.g., Christian or meat-intender), the entire sacrifice is invalid.
3
Understand the istihsan-based ruling
Imam Muhammad (author of this passage) holds that with heirs' permission, the sacrifice is valid. This is istihsan (juristic preference), based on the Prophet's sacrifice for his Ummah and the principle that qurbah can be done on behalf of the deceased (like charity or hajj).
4
Contrast with qiyas (Abu Yusuf's position)
Qiyas would render it invalid because the heir's permission amounts to tabarru' bi al-itlaf (gratuitous destruction) of the deceased's share, which is not permissible on behalf of another, analogous to freeing a slave for the deceased (which imposes wala'). This is a narration from Imam Abu Yusuf.
5
Determine thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Yusuf's qiyas, the sacrifice would not count for any partner despite heir permission; under Imam Muhammad's istihsan, it counts for all. The mufta bihi is the istihsan position.

Quduri said: When seven people buy a cow to sacrifice it, then before slaughter one of them dies, and the heirs say: slaughter it for yourselves and for the deceased, it suffices for them. But if one of the six partners is a Christian or a person who intends meat, it is not permissible from any of them. The reason is that a cow suffices for seven, but the condition is that all have intention of qurbah (piety), even if the categories of qurbah differ, like qurbani, dam qiran, and dam tamattu', because in our view the purpose is united, and that is qurbah. This condition is present in the first case because the sacrifice is on behalf of another while it is qurbah. Do you not see that the Prophet (peace be upon him) sacrificed on behalf of his Ummah, as reported earlier. This condition is absent in the second case because the Christian is not eligible for qurbah, similarly intending meat contradicts qurbah. When some part is not qurbah, while shedding blood is indivisible with respect to qurbah, then qurbah does not occur at all, so permissibility of sacrifice is prevented. This is istihsan mentioned by Imam Muhammad. Commentary: For sacrifice, it is necessary that the entire act be qurbah; otherwise no one's sacrifice is valid. So if someone's purpose is meat-eating, no one's sacrifice is valid. Similarly, if a partner is Christian, since he is not eligible for qurbah, no one's sacrifice is valid. And shedding blood is indivisible for qurbah; so if some part is not qurbah, then no part is qurbah. In any case, intention of qurbah is necessary. If the categories of qurbah vary, there is no harm. So if one intends qurbani, another dam qiran, and a third dam tamattu', it is permissible because they are united in qurbah. Sacrifice on behalf of another is also qurbah, as the action of the Prophet (peace be upon him) indicates. So it is understood that when the heirs permit the partners to sacrifice on behalf of the deceased, it is permissible. But this ruling is istihsan; qiyas requires that it is not permissible. They say: Qiyas requires that the sacrifice is not valid even after permission from the heirs. And qiyas is that it is not permissible, and this is a narration from Abu Yusuf, because it is tabarru' bi al-itlaf (gratuitous destruction), which is not permissible on behalf of another, like freeing a slave on behalf of the deceased. But we say: qurbah can occur on behalf of the deceased, like giving charity, unlike freeing a slave because that imposes wala' (patronage) on the deceased. Commentary: Qiyas requires that even with the heirs' permission the sacrifice is not valid, and one narration from Abu Yusuf is also this, because after death the heir becomes owner of the share. Then the heir's permission for sacrifice is tabarru' bi al-itlaf, i.e., an act of wasting wealth, and tabarru' bi al-itlaf on behalf of another is not permissible, like freeing a slave on behalf of the deceased. But we have acted on istihsan, and the proof of istihsan is that qurbah can occur on behalf of the deceased, like charity and hajj. Because the heirs have the right to do rewardable deeds on behalf of the deceased. So by their permission, the deceased's share becomes qurbah like the shares of other partners, so the sacrifice is valid. As for the issue of freeing a slave on behalf of the deceased, the reason for its impermissibility is that wala' is for the manumitter, and the deceased no longer has the capacity to be a master, so it imposes wala' liability on the deceased, which is not allowed. In sacrifice, nothing is imposed on the deceased, so there is no objection to its permissibility.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of a shared sacrifice when a partner dies and the heirs permit the remaining partners to include the deceased's share. The base ruling from Quduri states that if seven people buy a cow for sacrifice, one dies, and the heirs allow the sacrifice to include the deceased, it is valid. However, if a partner is a Christian or intends mere meat (not worship), the sacrifice is invalid for all. The reason is that the cow suffices for seven only if all intend qurbah (piety). Different types of qurbah (e.g., qurbani, dam qiran, dam tamattu') are acceptable because they are united under the category of qurbah. The author then explains that the ruling for the deceased's case is based on istihsan (juristic preference). Qiyas (analogy) would render it invalid because authorizing destruction of the deceased's share is tabarru' bi al-itlaf (gratuitous waste), which is not permitted on behalf of another, similar to freeing a slave on behalf of a deceased person (which imposes wala'). This analogy is the position of one narration from Imam Abu Yusuf. However, the relied-upon position (Imam Muhammad's istihsan) is that sacrifice on behalf of the deceased is valid like charity or hajj, because the heirs may perform meritorious acts for the deceased. The difference from 'itaq (manumission) is that 'itaq would impose wala' on the deceased, which is not possible, whereas sacrifice imposes no obligation. Thus, the Hanafi school prefers istihsan for general practice.

Key Terms
قربت (qurbah): In this text, qurbah refers to an act of piety or devotion intended to draw near to Allah, specifically the intention behind sacrifice (qurbani).
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that overrides qiyas (analogy) in favor of a more suitable ruling, here allowing sacrifice on behalf of the deceased despite the qiyas of tabarru' bi al-itlaf.
تبرع بالاتلاف (tabarru' bi al-itlaf): A gratuitous act that results in the destruction of property without compensation; here applied to the heir's permission to destroy the deceased's share via sacrifice.

Sacrifice on Behalf of a Minor or Umm Walad: Permission of Guardian
Ruling with two scenarios on permission for minor/umm walad and heirs after death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for validly including a minor or umm walad in a sacrifice and apply the ruling when permission is absent.

Core Idea
Guardian permission makes a minor's or umm walad's sacrifice valid—they are eligible for qurbah.

A sacrifice involving multiple partners raises questions when one partner is a minor or an umm walad. This lesson clarifies that such individuals are capable of reward (qurbah) if Muslim, and their guardian's permission suffices for the sacrifice to be valid. We also examine the case where a partner dies and the remaining slaughter without heirs' consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Determine if the partner in the sacrifice is a minor or an umm walad. If so, identify the guardian: father for the minor, master for the umm walad.
The text assumes both are Muslims and eligible for qurbah.
2
Check permission
Ask: Did the guardian give explicit permission or perform the sacrifice on their behalf? If yes, the sacrifice is valid. If no, it is invalid.
Permission is akin to authorization for the share to be considered qurbah.
3
Handle death of a partner
If a partner dies before slaughter, the remaining partners must obtain permission from the heirs of the deceased to include his share. Without permission, the sacrifice is invalid for all.
The deceased's share must be offered as qurbah, which requires consent from those who inherit it.
4
Apply the ruling
State the outcome: if conditions met, sacrifice is sahih; if not, it is batil (invalid).
The validity hinges on permission, not on the individual's capacity to perform the sacrifice themselves.

Commentary
This passage addresses two scenarios: (1) When a minor child (saghir) or an umm walad (slave mother of her master's child) is among the partners in a sacrificial animal, and their respective guardian (father for the minor, master for the umm walad) permits the sacrifice on their behalf, the sacrifice is valid. (2) If a partner dies and the remaining partners slaughter the animal without the permission of the deceased's heirs, the sacrifice is invalid for all. The reason is that the deceased's share must be included as qurbah, which requires permission. The text reiterates that minors and umm walad, as long as they are Muslims, are capable of qurbah and reward, so their guardian's permission is sufficient. Without permission, the act lacks qurbah and hence is invalid. This is consistent with the earlier discussion on istihsan.

Key Terms
صغیر (saghir): A minor child who has not reached puberty; in this context, a partner in sacrifice whose share is managed by his father (guardian).
ام ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child to her master; she is the property of her master but cannot be sold. Here, her master (aqa) can permit sacrifice on her behalf.
