Tahayu' on Istighlal of a Single House: Ruling and Distinction
Ruling on alternating leasing of a house, with distinction from slave/animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tahayu' (alternation) in leasing a single house vs. a single slave/animal, and explain the rational basis ('illa) for the distinction.

Core Idea
For a house, stability ensures equality; for animals, change breaks equality.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on tahayu' in the exploitation (istighlal) of shared property. The Zahir al-Riwayah permits alternating leasing of a single house but prohibits it for a single slave or animal. The key lies in the stability of real estate versus the variability of living beings.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Tahayu' on istighlal of a single house is permissible in Zahir al-Riwayah; for a single slave or animal, it is not permissible.
This is the authoritative position of the school.
2
Identify the proof
The proof is a rational distinction (dalil aqli) based on the nature of the property. No direct textual evidence is cited; it is a qiyas-like reasoning.
The 'illa is derived from the stability of real estate.
3
Trace the 'illa
The effective cause is that real estate remains stable over time, ensuring continued equality between partners. Animals and slaves are subject to change (illness, death), so equality cannot be guaranteed.
The text states: 'causes of change occur successively in animals.'
4
Determine the thamarah
The practical consequence: partners can alternate renting a house but not a living being. This affects partnership agreements and the validity of such arrangements.
The ruling applies specifically to istighlal, not to direct usufruct.

Sharing Increased Rent in Tahayu': Distinction between Istighlal and Manafi'
Ruling on sharing increased rent when alternating leasing of a house

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that increased rent in tahayu' on istighlal is shared, while increased rent in tahayu' on manafi' is not shared, and distinguish the two cases.

Core Idea
In istighlal, partners share subsequent increase; in manafi', increase belongs to the current user.

This lesson examines a subsidiary ruling: when partners alternate leasing (istighlal) a shared house, any increase in rent during one partner's turn is shared to maintain equality. However, if they had agreed to alternate direct use (manafi') and then decided to lease, any increase is not shared. The distinction rests on what the original agreement covered.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between tahayu' on istighlal (leasing) and tahayu' on manafi' (direct usufruct). Each has a different ruling when there is an increase in rent.
Istighlal is about earning profit; manafi' is about personal use.
2
State the ruling for istighlal
If rent increases in one partner's turn compared to the other's, they share the increase to achieve equality.
The partners are considered agents for each other in leasing.
3
State the ruling for manafi'
If the agreement was on direct usufruct (e.g., residence), and then one partner leases during his turn, any increase is not shared. Equality was already achieved in the usufruct itself.
The increase is considered a separate benefit.
4
Understand the rationale
In istighlal, equality must be maintained in the income because the partners intended profit. In manafi', each is entitled to the benefits of his turn, so extra profit from leasing is his alone.
The text says: 'the increase in exploitation afterward does not harm him.'

Tahayu' on Istighlal of Two Houses: Ruling and Distinction from One House
Ruling with rational proof distinguishing two houses from one house.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tahayu' on istighlal of two houses, explain why surplus rent is not shared, and distinguish it from the case of a single house.

Core Idea
In two houses, each partner's time of taking is united, so no surplus sharing; in one house, alternation makes each an agent.

This passage extends the ruling of tahayu' on istighlal to two houses, clarifying that surplus rent is not shared. The key is whether the partners' use is simultaneous or alternating. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the correct legal treatment in ijara partnerships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Tahayu' on istighlal of two houses. The ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah is that it is permissible (ja'iz).
Note the authority weight: zahir al-riwayah.
2
Note the key distinction from a single house
If the yield of one house exceeds the other, the partners do NOT share the surplus. In a single house, they must share. This is the main legal consequence.
3
Trace the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The author provides a rational distinction: in two houses, the meaning of distinction (tamyiz) and segregation (ifraz) is predominant because the time of taking is united (ittihad zaman al-istifa). Each partner can exploit his house simultaneously.
This is a qiyas-based reasoning.
4
Understand the counter-case: single house
In a single house, receipt alternates (yata'āqab al-wusul). The one collecting rent in his turn is considered an agent (wakil) for the other, and the arrangement is treated as a loan (qard). Hence surplus must be shared.
This shows how the legal treatment flips based on the possibility of real segregation.
5
Apply the principle to any two separate properties
Given any two physically separate properties owned by partners, if they agree to alternate leasing each property individually, the same ruling applies: no sharing of surplus.
The key condition is physical separateness and simultaneous independent use.

The Muzara'ah Dispute: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on muzara'ah validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the arguments of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on muzara'ah and identify the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Sharecropping is valid per Sahibayn and the fatwa; Abu Hanifa's objection is matruk.

This lesson unpacks a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding muzara'ah (sharecropping), a contract where land is given to a cultivator for a fixed share of the produce. While Imam Abu Hanifa deemed it fasid due to unknown wages in ijarah, his students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani argued for its validity based on the Prophet's practice at Khaybar and analogy to mudarabah. The commentary reveals that despite Abu Hanifa's strong reasoning, the fatwa follows the Sahibayn because of the ummah's continuous practice.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Identify that Abu Hanifa rules muzara'ah with a fixed share (e.g., 1/3 or 1/4) as fasid (void). Understand his reasoning: it is essentially an ijarah (hiring) with an unknown wage, which is prohibited.
The source mentions his two proofs: a hadith forbidding a related practice (qayz al-tahhan) and rational argument about unknown wage.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
Note that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani consider muzara'ah valid (ja'iz). Their primary evidence is the Prophet's contract with Khaybar on half the produce, and secondary analogy to mudarabah partnership.
The Sahibayn emphasize that need justifies this contract, as capital owners may lack labor and laborers may lack capital.
3
Examine the evidence (dalil)
Analyze the Khaybar hadith: the Prophet made a contract on half the produce. The Sahibayn see this as a clear precedent. Abu Hanifa interprets it as jizyah (tribute), not muzara'ah.
The dalil_block details the hadith text and its wajh al-dalalah.
4
Identify the tarjih and mufta bihi
The commentary states that Abu Hanifa's view is matruk (abandoned) and the fatwa is on the Sahibayn's opinion because the ummah has consistently practiced sharecropping.
This is a key example of how later Hanafi jurists may override an early imam's ruling based on communal practice.

Commentary
This passage from the Kitab al-Muzara'ah (Sharecropping) of Al-Hidayah expands on the four scenarios for the validity of muzara'ah according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). It is an expansion_probe, as the author (al-Quduri in the matn) states the positions, and the commentator (al-Marghinani) provides reasoning and analogies. The first three scenarios are declared valid: (1) land and seed from one, ox and labor from another; (2) land from one, all else from another; (3) all except labor from one, labor from another. The fourth scenario (land and ox from one, seed and labor from another) is invalid according to Zahir al-Riwayah (the relied-upon transmission), but Abu Yusuf (in Nawadir) permits it. The dispute hinges on whether the ox's benefit (manfa'ah) is of the same genus as the land's benefit. Zahir al-Riwayah considers them different: the land's benefit is inherent nutritive power, the ox's benefit is an instrumental capacity for work; hence they cannot be combined under a single rental. Abu Yusuf analogizes to the scenario where all except labor are from the landowner, which is valid; here only the ox is added from the landowner, so it should also be valid. The Hanafi school follows the Zahir al-Riwayah position.

In a partnership, both parties contribute capital and share profit/loss. Here the worker contributes only labor, so it is a pure hire.
The Hanafi school emphasizes that the factor is an ajir khas (exclusive employee).

The Fourth Case: Land and Oxen from One Party, Seeds and Work from Another
Intra-Hanafi ruling dispute with detailed reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the dominant Hanafi ruling (Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah) invalidates the fourth sharecropping scenario and evaluate the counter‑argument of Imam Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Land and oxen benefits differ in genus; subordination fails, voiding the contract.

This lesson examines a specific sharecropping (muzāra‘ah) scenario where the provider of land and oxen is not the same as the provider of seeds and labor. The dominant Hanafi position (Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah) declares the contract void, while Imam Abu Yusuf considers it permissible. The dispute turns on whether the benefits of land and oxen are of the same genus, affecting the possibility of legal subordination (taba‘iyyah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the two positions
Read the passage to understand the fourth case: one party supplies land and oxen; the other supplies seeds and work. Note that the dominant ruling (Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah) says it is void, while Abu Yusuf says it is valid.
The 'third case' mentioned is when the worker also provides seeds or oxen, which is valid.
2
Understand the principle of subordination (taba‘iyyah) and genus (jins) of benefits
Focus on the reasoning: the benefit (manfa‘ah) of land is a natural power that produces growth; the benefit of oxen is a capacity to perform labour. Because these benefits belong to different genera, one cannot be subordinated to the other.
Subordination requires both items to be of the same genus.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's qiyas (analogy) to the third case
Abu Yusuf argues that if stipulating both seeds and oxen on the worker is valid, then stipulating only oxen should also be valid – by analogy to the worker’s side where the benefits (worker’s labour and oxen’s labour) are of the same genus.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response: why the qiyas fails
The dominant view rejects the analogy because in the third case the subordination is to the worker’s benefit (same genus), but here subordination would be to the land’s benefit (different genus). Hence the oxen cannot be legally dependent on the land.
Also, renting oxen for a share of produce is impermissible because oxen’s benefit is a service, not a natural increase.
5
Determine the practical consequence and the mufta bihi position
The conflict leads to different legal outcomes: in the dominant view the contract is void and no share is due; in Abu Yusuf’s view it is valid. The relied‑upon position for fatwa is Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah (invalid).
This difference is termed thamarah al-khilaf.

Worker's Payment When Landowner Refuses After Plowing
Rule on worker's entitlement with qada/diyanah distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a worker's wages when a landowner backs out after plowing, and explain the distinction between qada and diyanah.

Core Idea
Court gives nothing; religious duty demands compensation for the worker's wasted labor.

This passage addresses a scenario in muzara'ah where the worker has already plowed the land, but the landowner (who provides seed) decides not to proceed. The Hanafi school makes a critical distinction: in a court of law the worker cannot force payment, but before Allah the landowner must compensate the worker because he deceived him into working for nothing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and contract
Recognize that this is a muzara'ah contract where the landowner provides the seed and the worker provides labor. The contract is for sharecropping, not a simple hire.

2
State the court ruling (qada)
According to the passage, the worker gets nothing in court for his plowing work. The word 'تحكم' (taḥakkum) indicates a court's enforceable ruling.
The court does not compel the landowner to pay wages.
3
State the religious ruling (diyanah)
Between the landowner and Allah, it is obligatory (wajib) for the landowner to please the worker, i.e., compensate him for his labor.
This is a moral-religious duty, not a legal obligation.

Identify the reasoning (talil)
The reason given is ghurur (deception): the landowner misled the worker into doing work that ultimately was wasted.
The concept of ghurur is a recognized principle in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Distinguish between qada and diyanah
Qada rulings are enforceable by courts; diyanah rulings are obligations before Allah that are not enforced by human courts. This dual framework is a hallmark of Hanafi fiqh.
Always check which level of ruling is being discussed.

Death of a Party Voids Muzara'ah (Qiyas to Ijarah)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that muzara'ah is voided by the death of a party and explain the qiyas to ijarah, including the asl, far', and illa.

Core Idea
Death of either party voids muzara'ah, just as it voids ijarah.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from the Hanafi school: the muzara'ah (sharecropping) contract ends immediately upon the death of either the landowner or the worker. The ruling is not merely a fatwa but is derived by clear analogy (qiyas jaliy) to the contract of ijarah (hire). Understanding this analogy reveals the legal reasoning behind many Hanafi rulings on personal service contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states clearly: when one of the contracting parties dies, the muzara'ah becomes void (batalat). This is the operative hukm.
‘Void’ means the contract ceases to exist from the moment of death; it does not continue with the heirs.
2
Recognize the Dalil (Proof)
The proof is qiyas (analogy). The text says 'i'tibāran bil-ijārah' — by analogy to ijarah. This is not a direct text from Quran or Sunnah but a legal analogy.
Qiyas is a valid source of law in Hanafi usul.
3
Construct the Qiyas
The asl (original case) is ijarah, which is voided by death. The hukm of the asl is buṭlān (voidness). The illa (effective cause) is that the contract involves personal skill or labor that cannot be inherited. The far' (new case) is muzara'ah, which also involves personal labor and land use.
The illa is the dependence on the specific person of the contracting party.
4
Verify the Illa
Confirm that the same cause (dependence on the person) exists in muzara'ah as in ijarah. In both contracts, the parties' personal attributes matter; the contract cannot simply pass to heirs.
This is a jaliy (clear) qiyas; no hidden or contested element.
5
Apply the Ruling
Thus, upon death of either party, the muzara'ah is void. No rights or obligations pass to the heirs under this contract. Any prior work may be compensated separately if needed.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Compelling Performance in Muzara'ah: Seed-Provider vs. Worker
Ruling on compulsion in muzara'ah contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether each party in a muzara'ah contract can be compelled to perform and identify the valid excuses that dissolve the contract.

Core Idea
The seed-provider is not compelled, but the worker is; both may be excused by valid reasons that dissolve ijarah.

After a muzara'ah (sharecropping) contract is concluded, one party may refuse to start. Islamic law must balance contractual obligation against potential loss. This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah clarifies that the party providing seed cannot be forced to mix it with land due to immediate loss, while the worker can be compelled. However, certain excuses like illness or overwhelming debt dissolve the contract, just as they do in an ijarah (hire) contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties
Recognize the two contracting parties: the seed-provider (usually landowner) and the worker (who does not provide seed).
2
State the Default Ruling
The contract is binding (lazim) once concluded, so performance is normally obligatory.
3
Apply the Distinction for Refusal
If the seed-provider refuses, the ruler does NOT compel him because forcing him to use his property (seed) would cause immediate loss without guaranteed profit. If the worker refuses, the ruler compels him because he incurs no loss by performing labor.
Analogy to ijarah: hirer cannot force worker if hirer backs out; but worker can be forced if he backs out.
4
Identify Exceptions (Valid Excuses)
Certain excuses that dissolve an ijarah contract also dissolve muzara'ah: e.g., illness preventing work, or debt forcing land sale. In such cases, the contract is annulled and no compulsion applies.
5
Conclude the Practical Operation
A judge must first determine which party refuses and whether a valid excuse exists, then either compel the worker or accept the dissolution if an excuse is present.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Renting Property for Non-Muslim Worship or Wine Trade
Intra-Hanafi dispute on renting for non-Muslim worship and wine trade

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding renting property for non-Muslim worship or wine trade, and explain the basis of each position.

Core Idea
Permissibility depends on whether the lease is on benefit or assistance to sin.

This lesson explores a classic intra-Hanafi disagreement about renting a house to non-Muslims for religious worship or selling wine. Imam Abu Hanifa permits it based on contract theory, while the Sahibayn prohibit it as assisting sin. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply usul principles to concrete fiqh issues.

Guided Path
1
State the issue and Abu Hanifa's position
The issue is renting a house in rural areas (sawad) for use as a fire temple, church, synagogue, or wine shop. Abu Hanifa says there is no harm (la ba'sa).
The ruling is specific to rural areas; cities may have different rules.
2
Identify the opposing view of the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani say it is not appropriate (la yanbaghi) because it constitutes assistance upon sin (i'anah ala al-ma'siyah).
The Sahibayn base their view on the Qur'anic prohibition in al-Ma'idah 5:2.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
The lease is on the benefit (manfa'ah) of the house, not the sinful use. Rent becomes due simply upon delivery, and sin is attributed to the lessee as a free agent (fa'il mukhtar), cutting off attribution to the lessor.
This is a strict application of contract law: the subject matter is permissible.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
Renting with knowledge of sinful use is indirect assistance, which is prohibited. The lessor facilitates the sin, even if he does not perform it.
The Sahibayn prioritize the ethical principle over contract formalism.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa's view makes the contract valid; the Sahibayn's view renders it makruh tahrimi. The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's position due to ihtiyat (precaution).
However, in cases of necessity, takhayyur (selection) of Abu Hanifa's view may be allowed.

Wage for Carrying Wine: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on wage for carrying wine for a dhimmi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn on the wage for carrying wine for a dhimmi and explain the tarjih with its practical implication.

Core Idea
Carrying wine for a dhimmi is disliked (makruh) because it assists sin, though Abu Hanifa allows it.

This lesson unpacks an intra-Hanafi dispute from the Hidayah commentary. Imam Muhammad reports two opinions: Abu Hanifa permits the wage, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) deem it disliked. We will examine their proofs and see why the Sahibayn's view is considered preponderant (rajih).

Guided Path
1
State the Dispute
Identify the two rulings: Abu Hanifa says the wage is halal; Sahibayn say it is makruh. The act is carrying wine for a dhimmi for hire.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference, not a cross-madhhab one.
2
Examine the Proof of the Sahibayn
Their proof is twofold: (a) the principle that assisting sin (i'ana 'ala al-ma'siyah) is prohibited, and (b) the hadith cursing the carrier of wine.
The hadith is a dalil naqli; they take it at face value.
3
Examine Abu Hanifa's Response
Abu Hanifa argues that sin only lies in drinking, an act of a free agent (fa'il mukhtar), not in carrying. He restricts the hadith to carriers who intend the sin.
His argument is rational (dalil 'aqli) and limits the scope of assistance.
4
Evaluate the Tarjih
The commentary notes that Abu Hanifa's proof is weak (''illa') because it would wrongly permit making wine itself. Hence the Sahibayn's view is preponderant (rajih).
The mufta bihi is that the wage is disliked (makruh).

He (Imam Muhammad) said: Whoever carries wine for a dhimmi, then the wage is lawful for him according to Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: That is disliked for him, because it is assistance in sin. And it has been authentically reported that the Prophet, peace be upon him, cursed ten people regarding wine: the carrier and the one to whom it is carried. Abu Hanifa's proof is that the sin is in drinking it, and drinking is an act of a free agent, and drinking is not among the necessary consequences of carrying, nor is it intended by it. And the hadith is interpreted as referring to carrying accompanied by the intention of sin. Translation: Muhammad said in al-Jami' al-Saghir: Whoever carries wine for a dhimmi, then according to Abu Hanifa the wage is lawful for him. The two companions said: That is disliked for him, because it is assistance in sin. And it has been established as authentic that the Prophet cursed ten people regarding wine: the carrier and the one to whom it is carried. Abu Hanifa's proof is that the sin is in drinking it, and drinking is an act of a free agent, and drinking is not among the necessary consequences of carrying, nor is it intended by the carrier. And the hadith is interpreted as referring to carrying accompanied by the intention of sin. Commentary: If a Muslim carries wine for a dhimmi to earn wages, Imam Abu Hanifa declares the wage halal, while the Sahibayn declare it makruh because it is assistance in sin. In the hadith, among the ten cursed are the carrier and the one to whom it is carried, so the hadith establishes dislike. Imam Abu Hanifa says that the sin is only in drinking, and drinking is the act of a free agent, so by principle the act of drinking cannot be attributed to another. Also, drinking is not a necessary consequence of carrying, nor does the carrier intend to drink. The hadith about cursing refers to a carrier who carries with the intention of sin, i.e., his aim is to drink or serve it to someone. Note: Imam Abu Hanifa's proof is flawed because it is difficult to confine the sin to drinking only; otherwise, making wine would also not be prohibited, because the sin is drinking and a free agent's act intervenes. In any case, the statement of the Sahibayn is preponderant (rajih) and the apparent view is the statement of the Imam (Abu Hanifa).

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the ruling for a Muslim who carries wine for a dhimmi for hire. The matn from Imam Muhammad states the positions: Abu Hanifa says the wage is halal (yatibu); Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it is makruh. Their proof is the hadith cursing ten people involved with wine, including the carrier. Imam Abu Hanifa's evidence is rational (dalil 'aqli): the sin is in drinking, which is an independent act of the drinker, not necessitated by carrying; and the hadith is interpreted restrictively for cases where the carrier intends the sin. The commentary clarifies that the Sahibayn's view is more cautious. The author adds a note that Abu Hanifa's argument is weak because it would imply wine-making is not a sin, which is problematic. Thus the relied-upon view is the dislike (karahah). The passage follows the order: statement of ruling, then each proof, then the tarjih.

Key Terms
اعانة على المعصية (i'ana 'ala al-ma'siyah): Assistance in committing a sin; a basis for prohibition in Hanafi fiqh.
فاعل مختار (fa'il mukhtar): A free agent who acts by choice; used to argue that the sin of the drinker is not attributable to the carrier.
