Death of Shafi': Shuf'a Void, Not Inherited
Ruling on shuf'a inheritance with dispute and timing clarification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the inheritance of shuf'a when the shafi' dies before judgment, and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position and from the case of death after judgment.

Core Idea
Shuf'a right dies with the shafi' if judgment has not yet been passed—it is not inherited.

This passage addresses a classic inter-madhhab dispute: does the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) pass to heirs if the pre-emptor dies before the qadi issues judgment? The Hanafi school holds that the right is conditional on continuous ownership and thus becomes void upon death. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees. The text clarifies the critical timing and draws an analogy to the option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and dispute
State the Hanafi ruling (shuf'a is void) and the Shafi'i opposing view (it is inherited).
This is the core ikhtilaf addressed in the passage.
2
Locate the timing condition
Distinguish death before qadi's judgment (shuf'a void) from death after judgment but before payment/possession (shuf'a binding for heirs).
The author specifies this distinction in the commentary.
3
Trace the proof
Understand the condition: the shafi' must own the property from the time of sale until the time of judgment. Death breaks ownership, so the condition fails.
This is a rational proof (qiyas) based on the nature of shuf'a.
4
Note the analogy to khiyar al-shart
The text compares this dispute to the similar ikhtilaf regarding the option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart) in sales, where Hanafis also deny inheritance.
This shows consistent Hanafi methodology.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
Consider a co-owner who sells his share; the neighbor (shafi') dies before the qadi hears the case. Hanafi: the right vanishes; Shafi'i: the heirs can claim it.
This clarifies the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

He (the author of Hidayah) said: Its meaning is that if he dies after the sale but before the judgment for shuf'a, [the right is void]; but if he dies after the qadi's judgment before payment of the price and taking possession, the sale is binding for his heirs. Principle regarding the voidance of shuf'a upon death of shafi': And because by death his ownership of his property ceases, and ownership is established for the heir after the sale. The existence of ownership at the time of sale and its continuity for the shafi' until the time of judgment is a condition. So he does not deserve shuf'a without it.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with proof and an inter-madhhab dispute. The Hanafi position: if the shafi' (pre-emptor) dies before the qadi issues a judgment for shuf'a, the right of shuf'a is void and does not pass to heirs. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, holding that the right is inherited. The author clarifies the timing: death after the sale but before qada' (judgment) nullifies shuf'a; death after judgment but before payment or possession does not nullify—the sale becomes binding for the heirs (i.e., they take the property subject to the sale? Actually they have the right to complete the purchase?). The text analogizes this to the dispute over khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation): in sales, Hanafis do not allow inheritance of that option, Shafi'is do. The underlying principle (usul) is provided: shuf'a requires that the shafi' own the property at the time of sale and continue to own it up to the time of judgment. Death removes ownership, so the condition fails and shuf'a is void. This is a rational proof based on the nature of the right.

Key Terms
شفیع (shafi'): The person entitled to pre-emption (shuf'a), i.e., the co-owner or neighbor who has the right to purchase the sold share.
قضاء (qada'): The judgment or decision of the qadi (judge) regarding the right of shuf'a.

The Hanafi Dispute on Evidence for Dividing Inherited Land
Intra-Hanafi dispute on evidence requirement for dividing inherited land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the requirement of bayyinah for dividing land claimed as inheritance, and explain the proof for each position using the concepts of yad, iqrar, and qada' 'ala al-mayyit.

Core Idea
For division of inheritance land, Abu Hanifa requires evidence; Sahibayn rely on possession and admission.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a qadi must require evidence (bayyinah) when heirs claim land as inheritance and demand division. The disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples reveals deeper principles about proof, possession, and the nature of the estate before division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage outlines three situations: (1) land claimed as inheritance, (2) movable property claimed as inheritance, (3) land claimed as purchased. Note which scenario is disputed.
Only scenario 1 (inheritance land) is disputed; the other two are agreed.
2
State the positions
Imam Abu Hanifa: evidence is required. Sahibayn: evidence is not required; division can be based on admission.
3
Explain the Sahibayn's proof
Possession (yad) is evidence of ownership, admission (iqrar) is a sign of truth, and there is no opponent. They analogize to movables and purchased land.
Qadi records division as based on admission to limit effect.
4
Explain Abu Hanifa's proof
Division is a decree against the deceased (qada' 'ala al-mayyit) because the estate remains in the deceased's ownership until division. Admission is not a proof against the deceased, so evidence is necessary.
Evidence: bequests and debts from the estate before division show continued ownership.
5
Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
If qadi divides without evidence: Abu Hanifa says division invalid; Sahibayn says valid but binding only on parties who admitted.
Affects rights of absent heirs.

Types of Division: By Evidence vs. By Admission
Ruling on two types of division and their binding effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between division by evidence (qismah bil-bayyinah) and division by admission (qismah bil-iqrar), and explain the legal effect of each type on third parties.

Core Idea
Division by evidence binds all, including absent heirs; division by admission binds only the admitting parties.

The passage further explains that there are two types of division: by evidence and by admission. This distinction has critical practical consequences. A division based on evidence is binding on everyone, even absent heirs, whereas a division based on admission is limited to those who admitted. This is illustrated with the case of the umm walad and mudabbir.

Guided Path
1
State the two types
The passage distinguishes between division by evidence (qismah bil-bayyinah) and division by admission (qismah bil-iqrar).
2
Explain the binding effect of each
Division by evidence is binding on all, including absent heirs and third parties. Division by admission is binding only on the parties who made the admission.
The qadi records the division as based on admission to prevent it from affecting others.
3
Apply to the umm walad and mudabbir example
If division was by admission and then the deceased's umm walad and mudabbir claim freedom, the qadi does not decide on their freedom unless they prove the master's death. But if division was by evidence, the qadi decides on their freedom without that proof, because the division is binding on the deceased's estate.
This shows the practical difference.
4
Understand the qadi's recording obligation
The qadi must record in the division register that the division was by admission to avoid extending its effect to non-parties.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah discusses two types of division (qismah) in Islamic inheritance law: division by evidence (qismah bil-bayyinah) and division by confession (qismah bil-iqrar). The key difference is that division by evidence is effective and binding on all parties, including third parties, whereas division by confession is limited only to the parties involved and does not affect others. The author then applies this distinction to a specific case: if an umm walad (slave mother of a child) and a mudabbar (slave promised emancipation after death) claim 'ishq (a right to freedom or inheritance), and the division was by confession, the qadi will not rule on their claim unless they bring witnesses that the master died, because this division does not bind the deceased. However, if the division was by evidence, the qadi rules on their claim without requiring such evidence, because division by evidence is effective against the deceased's estate. The passage then presents Imam Abu Hanifah's proof: division before the estate is fully distributed is considered a ruling against the deceased (qada' ala al-mayyit), because the estate remains in the deceased's ownership until division. Evidence for this includes the fact that increases in the estate (like offspring of a bequeathed slave-girl) and debts are still subject to the deceased's bequests and debts before division. Since division is a ruling against the deceased, a mere confession by the heirs is not a valid proof against the deceased; therefore, evidence (bayyinah) is required. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argued that requiring evidence is unnecessary and futile, because the heirs' confession should suffice. The Hanafi response counters that establishing evidence is still beneficial: one of the heirs can be appointed as the adversary (khasm) on behalf of the deceased, and the other as the claimant. This is analogous to the case of an heir or executor who confesses a debt but the creditor still brings witnesses—the evidence is accepted despite the confession. Thus, the establishment of evidence is not futile because it allows the qadi to pass a ruling after hearing evidence from both sides. Finally, the Sahibayn attempted to analogize this case to movable property and the sale of a commodity, arguing that division by confession should be binding like those. The Hanafi response refutes this: movable property requires preservation and is subject to liability (damān), while real estate is self-protective. Additionally, in a sale, the sold item does not remain in the seller's ownership even if not divided, so there is no ruling against a third party. Hence, the analogy does not hold. The commentary emphasizes that Abu Hanifah's position is relied upon in the school, requiring evidence even when the heirs have confessed, to protect the rights of all parties, including the deceased's estate.

إقرار (iqrar): Confession or admission of a right or liability.

Division with Absent or Minor Heirs: Procedure and Dispute
Ruling on division with absent or minor heir and condition of proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for dividing inheritance when an heir is absent or minor, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the requirement of proof.

Core Idea
The judge must protect absent and minor heirs by appointing a deputy, but Abu Hanifa requires proof first.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a practical scenario in inheritance division: when some heirs are present and others are absent or minors. The ruling balances the efficiency of distribution with the protection of vulnerable heirs. We will examine the procedure and the key dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples over the necessity of establishing proof before division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and property
The scenario involves two present heirs (or more), an absent heir or a minor, and property (e.g., a house) in the possession of the present heirs.
The property is under the present heirs' control, but the absent/minor has a legal share.
2
State the judge's action
Upon the present heirs' request, the judge divides the property and appoints a wakil (deputy) for the absent heir to receive the share, or a wasi (guardian) for a minor heir.
The wakil acts on behalf of the absent adult; the wasi manages the minor's affairs.
3
Identify the rationale
The reason given is 'nazar li al-gha'ib wa al-saghir' (consideration for the absent and minor), ensuring their rights are protected.
This is a manifestation of the maqasid (objectives) of Shari'ah: preservation of property.
4
Note the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa stipulates that the present heirs must establish proof (bayyinah) of the death and the number of heirs before division. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) do not require this proof.
This ikhtilaf was mentioned earlier in the text; its practical impact is whether the judge can divide immediately or must wait for evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on property division when an absent heir exists and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and al-Sahibayn regarding the requirement of proof (bayyinah).

Core Idea
Division with an absent heir requires proof of death and number of heirs, even if property is in present heirs' possession.

In inheritance division, when some heirs are absent or minors, the judge must ensure their rights are protected. This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common scenario: two present heirs seek division of property that is in their possession, but a third heir is absent. How should the judge proceed? The Hanafi school provides a detailed ruling, including the appointment of a proxy and the necessity of establishing proof. However, there is an intra-madhhab dispute on the latter point.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
The passage describes two present heirs who seek division of a house in their possession; a third heir is absent. The judge may divide the property at their request and must appoint a proxy for the absent heir.
The judge's action is based on safeguarding the absent heir's interest.
2
Note the appointment of a proxy
For an absent adult heir, the judge appoints a wakil (proxy) to take possession of the share. For a minor heir, a wasi (guardian) is appointed. The rationale is 'nazar al-gha'ib' (looking after the absent person's interest).
This ensures the absent heir's rights are preserved until they or their representative can claim.
3
Understand the requirement of proof (bayyinah)
Imam Abu Hanifa rules that the present heirs must establish proof (bayyinah) of the deceased's death and the number of heirs before the division can proceed. This is to confirm the cause of inheritance.
Proof is needed even though the property is in the hands of the present heirs.
4
Contrast with al-Sahibayn's position
Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad hold that proof is not required because possession of the property implies ownership, which is sufficient evidence of inheritance.
Their view is that division can proceed immediately without witnesses.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The commentator sides with Abu Hanifa, making the requirement of proof the relied-upon position for fatwa. The practical consequence is that division is delayed until proof is provided.
This protects the absent heir from potential injustice if the present heirs are not truthful.

Claim of Error in Division After Affirmation of Receipt
Ruling on claim of error after division with prior affirmation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a claim of error in division after prior affirmation of receipt and explain why evidence is required.

Core Idea
A prior acknowledgment of receipt bars a later claim of error in division unless proven with evidence.

This lesson addresses a common dispute after property division: one partner later claims an error. Imam al-Quduri's ruling clarifies that if the claimant had previously affirmed receipt, his claim is not accepted without clear evidence. The reason is the principle of being bound by one's own acknowledgment (iqrar). We will examine the ruling, its proof, and the procedural steps if evidence is lacking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a claim of error (da'wa al-ghalat) after the division (qismah) has been executed. The ruling: the claimant is not believed (la yusaddaq) without evidence (bayyinah) because he had previously affirmed receipt (ishhad 'ala nafsihi bil-istifa').
The affirmation creates a presumption of satisfaction.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational (aqli): the claimant seeks to annul (faskh) the division after its occurrence, which contradicts his own prior acknowledgment. The law does not accept such a contradiction without strong evidence.
The text explicitly states: 'because he is claiming annulment after its occurrence.'
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal link)
The inconsistency between the affirmation and the claim undermines the claimant's credibility. Therefore, his word alone is insufficient; he must produce external proof (bayyinah) to override his own acknowledgment.
This is analogous to other cases where a person's previous statement binds him.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly difference)
Other schools (e.g., some Shafi'i views) may allow the claim without evidence if the error is plausible, to avoid potential injustice. The Hanafi school prioritizes the finality of completed transactions and the force of acknowledgment.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is whether the division can be reopened easily.
5
Understand the full procedure when evidence is lacking
If the claimant has no evidence, the qazi administers oaths to the other partners. If one refuses to swear, that refusal is treated as proof, and the shares of the claimant and refuser are combined and re-divided proportionally; the oath-taking partner is excluded.
This is mentioned in the Bada'i' example.

Ruling of Imam Abu Yusuf on Corrupt Partition
Explanation of Imam Abu Yusuf's ruling on corrupt partition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of Imam Abu Yusuf on a corrupt partition and calculate the compensation based on actual value (qimat).

Core Idea
In a corrupt partition, possession transfers ownership, but the taker owes the partner's share's value, not the agreed price.

This lesson examines Imam Abu Yusuf's ruling on a partition that is corrupt due to a prior defective sale. The passage clarifies that even though the partition is invalid (fasid), certain legal consequences arise, particularly regarding ownership and compensation. Understanding this ruling is important because it illustrates the Hanafi distinction between a void and corrupt transaction, and how liability is based on actual value rather than agreed price.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the partition is considered fasid (corrupt) according to Imam Abu Yusuf. The ruling is that it is not completely void (batil) but has some effects.
Fasid means corrupt or voidable; it is less severe than batil.
2
Understand the analogy to corrupt sale
Just as in a corrupt sale the buyer becomes owner by taking possession, in a corrupt partition the taker becomes owner of the allocated share.
The passage states: 'just as in a corrupt sale... likewise in a corrupt partition.'
3
Determine the liability
The taker cannot return the share; instead, he must compensate his partner by paying the value (qimat) of half of that share.
Compensation is based on actual value (qimat), not the price (thaman) agreed in the corrupt sale.
4
Distinguish key terms
Note the difference between thaman (agreed price) and qimat (actual market value). Liability in corrupt transactions follows qimat.
This distinction is crucial for calculating the correct compensation.
5
Recognize textual clarification
The passage clarifies the correct wording from authoritative sources: the liable party pays half of the partner's share (فيمضمن النصف نصيب صاحبه).
This avoids confusion from variant readings.

Effect of Debt Appearing After Partition
Ruling on partition invalidation due to debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partition when a debt appears after division and identify the exception when the partition remains valid.

Core Idea
Partition is revoked by any debt unless sufficient unpartitioned property remains to cover it.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common scenario in inheritance: the heirs have already divided the estate, but then a debt of the deceased comes to light. The ruling explains when the partition must be cancelled and when it can stand. Understanding this rule is crucial to protecting creditors' rights and ensuring that heirs do not acquire property that is not rightfully theirs until debts are cleared.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the triggering condition
The act is the partition (qismah) of the estate among heirs. The triggering condition is the appearance of a debt after the partition.
The debt may be known from the start or discovered later.
2
State the general ruling
If a debt appears, the partition is repudiated (naqd qismah) – it is rendered void.
This applies regardless of whether the debt covers the entire estate or not.
3
Distinguish the exception
Exception: if there remains in the estate some property that was not divided and that property is sufficient to pay the debt, then the partition is not revoked.
The key is whether the debt can be satisfied without disturbing the division.
4
Note additional curative factors
Even if no undivided property remains, the partition may become valid if the creditors release the debt (ibra) or if the heirs pay the debt from their own wealth.
This removes the obstacle (right of creditors) retroactively.

Ruling on partition validity after debt is resolved

Learning Objective

Partition invalid due to debt becomes valid when creditors forgive or heirs pay from their own wealth.

In the previous lesson, we learned that a partition of the estate is not valid when there is an outstanding debt because the creditors' right is attached to the estate. This lesson presents two ways by which that obstacle is removed, allowing the partition to stand. Understanding this clarifies how the rights of creditors and heirs interact in estate distribution.

Identify the obstacle
The obstacle (mani') is the attachment of creditors' right to the estate. This prevents the validity of the partition as long as the debt remains unpaid from the estate.

The text provides two scenarios: (a) the creditors grant a release (ibra) to the deceased from the debt after the partition, or (b) the heirs themselves pay the debt from their own personal wealth, not from the estate.

The ruling applies whether the debt was encompassing (i.e., greater than or equal to the estate value) or non-encompassing. The validity does not depend on the size of the debt.
This distinguishes it from the earlier requirement of first paying debt from the estate before partition.

In both scenarios, the partition becomes valid (ja'iz/sahih) because the obstacle has been removed. The original partition is not void; it is now ratified.
The ruling is a simple conditional: if obstacle removed, then partition valid.

The attachment of a right (haqq) to the estate is a temporary obstacle. Once the right is satisfied (by payment or release), the obstacle vanishes and the partition is upheld.
This aligns with the Hanafi principle that a right attached to an asset can be extinguished by external satisfaction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Participating in an estate partition admits all items are shared; claiming exclusive ownership contradicts that.

Claiming exclusive ownership of any particular item directly contradicts that admission.

Claim of a Specific Item by Co-Partitioner After Division
Rules that a claim to a specific item after division is inadmissible due to contradiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on claiming a specific item after division and explain why it is inadmissible due to contradiction.

Core Idea
Claiming a specific item after division contradicts the admission of shared ownership.

In the previous lesson, we saw that a claim of debt after division is admissible because it attaches to value, not form. Here we encounter the opposite: a claim to a specific item (al-‘ayn) after division is not heard. The reason is fundamental: by participating in division, the claimant acknowledges that the property was shared in its form. Later claiming exclusive ownership of a specific item directly contradicts that acknowledgment. This lesson unpacks that reasoning with examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Recognize that the act is a claim to a specific item (not a debt) after the estate has been divided. The ruling: the claim is not heard (lā yusma‘).
The claim is inadmissible, not voidable; it is rejected outright.
2
Understand the reasoning (contradiction)
The reason is clear contradiction. By initiating or agreeing to division, the claimant implicitly admitted that the divided property was jointly owned in its specific form. Claiming a specific item as exclusively his own later contradicts that admission.
The term used is 'tanāquḍ' (contradiction).
3
Distinguish from debt claims
Debt claims are admissible after division because debt attaches to the value or the estate as a whole, not to a specific item. A claim to a specific item attaches to the form, which contradicts the shared ownership established by division.
This distinction is crucial for proper application.
4
Apply to examples
Work through the examples given: claiming a horse in the estate based on deposit, lease, or gift—in all cases the claim is not heard. The common factor is that the claim targets a specific physical item.
The cause of the claim (wadī'ah, ijārah, etc.) does not matter; only the nature of the claim (specific item vs debt) does.

Commentary
This passage contrasts with the previous lesson. If a co-partitioner claims a specific item (al-‘ayn) after division, the claim is inadmissible (lā yusma‘). The reason is clear contradiction: by participating in division, the claimant acknowledged the property was shared (mushtarak). Claiming exclusive ownership later contradicts that acknowledgment. The commentary gives examples: claiming a horse in the estate as his own, whether based on deposit, lease, or any other cause, is not heard. This is because the division process itself is an affirmation of common ownership in the specific items. Thus, the key distinction: debt claims are admissible (attach to value), but claims to specific items are not (attach to form).

Fetus and Mother's Slaughter: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute with proofs and counter-arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's proof that a fetus requires separate slaughter and evaluate the counter-argument of the Sahibayn based on qiyas.

Core Idea
Fetus is independent; mother's dhabh cannot drain its blood, so separate slaughter is required.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether slaughtering a pregnant animal also slaughters its fetus. Imam Abu Hanifa argues for independence, while the Sahibayn analogize to hunting. We will follow the proofs and responses to understand the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the Issue
Identify the disputed legal question: Does the slaughter of a pregnant animal suffice for the slaughter of its unborn fetus?
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's Proof
List the four sub-arguments: independence in life and dhabh, separate obligation of ghurrah, validity of emancipation/bequest directed at fetus, and the impossibility of blood drainage from the mother's wound.
Each argument supports the fetus's legal independence (istiqlal).
3
Analyze Sahibayn's Qiyas
Examine the analogy to hunting (sayd) where a deficient wound suffices due to necessity, and the inclusion of the fetus in sale and emancipation as evidence of part-hood.
4
Follow the Hanafi Response
Understand Abu Hanifa's rebuttal: the hunting analogy fails because no blood exits the fetus; inclusion in sale/emancipation is for practical legal reasons, not part-hood.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
Conclude that the relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's: the fetus requires separate slaughter and is not halal by the mother's dhabh.
The text sides with Abu Hanifa, emphasizing caution (ihtiyat).

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf Ul Hadaya presents the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether the slaughter of a pregnant animal also suffices for the slaughter of its fetus. The text begins by expounding Imam Abu Hanifa's position that the fetus is an independent entity in terms of life and slaughter. His proof is multi-faceted: (1) The fetus can survive after the mother's death, so its dhabh must be separate. (2) Independent liability for ghurrah (diya for killing a fetus) shows it is a separate legal person. (3) A conditional emancipation or bequest involving the fetus is valid, treating it as an independent being. (4) The purpose of dhabh—draining blood—cannot be achieved through the mother's wound because the mother's blood does not cause the fetus's blood to exit. Therefore, the fetus cannot be considered a mere part (tabi') of the mother in this context.

Next, the text presents the counter-argument of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and Imam al-Shafi'i, who analogize the fetus to hunted game (sayd). In hunting, if a wounding shot causes the animal's death, it is considered valid dhabh even if blood does not flow fully, due to necessity. Similarly, they argue, the mother's slaughter should suffice for the fetus because the fetus is a part of the mother. They also point out that the fetus is included in the sale of the mother, and it becomes free upon the mother's emancipation, indicating it is part of her.

Imam Abu Hanifa's response refutes this qiyas on several grounds. The analogy to hunting is flawed because in hunting, the wound does cause some blood to exit, albeit deficiently; with the fetus, no blood exits at all from the mother's wound. Thus, the deficiency is not comparable. As for inclusion in sale and emancipation, these are not due to the fetus's being a part but for practical legal reasons: excluding the fetus from a sale would invalidate the contract due to gharar, and including it in emancipation avoids having a slave child born to a free mother. These are concessions, not indicators of part-hood.

The text concludes that due to these proofs and the principle of caution (ihtiyat), the fetus is not rendered halal by the mother's slaughter. A note adds that a hadith apparently supporting the opposite view is interpreted as a comparison (tashbih): if the fetus is born alive, it must be slaughtered separately like any animal; the hadith does not mean the mother's slaughter covers it. The correct position is that of Imam Abu Hanifa.

Key Terms
الذبائح (al-dhaba'ih): Plural of dhabihah; the book section on slaughtered animals, covering rulings on halal meat.
جنين (janeen): The fetus in the womb of an animal or human; in this context, the unborn offspring of a slaughtered animal.
غرة (ghurrah): A fine of a male or female slave (or its value) for causing a miscarriage or death of a fetus, fixed at five hundred dirhams.
استقلال (istiqlal): Independence; the state of being a separate legal entity, used here to argue that the fetus is not a mere part of the mother.

Key Takeaway
The mother's slaughter does not cover the fetus; separate dhabh is required due to the fetus's independence and the impossibility of blood drainage through the mother's wound.
