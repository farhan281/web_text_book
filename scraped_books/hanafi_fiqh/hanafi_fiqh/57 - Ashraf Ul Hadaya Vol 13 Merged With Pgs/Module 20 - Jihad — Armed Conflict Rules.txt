Wearing Silk in War: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on silk in war

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on wearing silk in war and explain the basis of the intra-madhhab disagreement.

Core Idea
Necessity in war permits silk according to the Sahibayn, but Abu Hanifa insists on mixed silk to avoid the prohibition.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on the permissibility of wearing pure silk in battle. The disagreement hinges on interpreting a prophetic permission and the extent of necessity. Let's dissect each side's reasoning and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the Sahibayn's position and their evidence
Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that wearing pure silk in war is permissible. They cite the hadith of al-Sha'bi that the Prophet (ﷺ) permitted silk and dibaj in war, and argue that pure silk provides better protection against weapons and instills awe in the enemy, fulfilling a valid necessity.
This view prioritizes the specific permission and practical need over general prohibition.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling and his reasoning
Abu Hanifa considers wearing pure silk in war makruh (disliked). He argues that the general prohibition of silk stands without explicit exception for war, and the necessity is adequately met by mixed silk (weft silk, warp other). Since mixed silk suffices, pure silk remains prohibited.
Abu Hanifa emphasizes adherence to general principles unless an explicit exception is proven, and he interprets the hadith as referring to mixed silk.
3
Analyze the conflicting dalil
Both sides rely on the same hadith but interpret it differently. The Sahibayn take it at face value as a permission for pure silk. Abu Hanifa interprets the permission as applying to mixed silk, thereby preserving the general prohibition for pure silk.
The key linguistic point: the hadith uses 'حرير' (silk) which could be general or qualified.
4
Understand the illa of necessity
Both acknowledge necessity (darurah) as a basis for exception. The disagreement is whether pure silk is a unique necessity or if mixed silk achieves the same purpose. Abu Hanifa says mixed silk suffices; Sahibayn say only pure silk provides full benefit.
The illa is the effective cause: repelling weapons and intimidating the enemy.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The dispute results in different legal rulings: Sahibayn permit; Abu Hanifa deems it makruh. In fatwa, later Hanafi scholars often prefer the Sahibayn's view due to prevailing needs in warfare, making it permissible (mubah).
The practical fatwa may vary; a mufti should assess the circumstances and available options.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on wearing pure silk clothing in battle. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) permit it based on a hadith where the Prophet allowed silk in war and due to necessity: pure silk provides better protection against weapons and instills awe in the enemy. Imam Abu Hanifa, however, considers it disliked (makruh) because there is no explicit differentiation in the general prohibition, and the necessity can be fulfilled by mixed silk (weft silk, warp other). The prohibited substance (pure silk) is only allowed under true necessity, and the hadith is interpreted as referring to mixed silk. Thus, the dispute centers on the strictness of necessity and the type of silk that suffices.

Key Terms
مخلوط (makhluṭ): Mixed silk cloth where the weft (luhma) is silk and the warp (sada) is another material.
حرير (harīr): Pure silk cloth.
ضرورة (ḍarūrah): Necessity that permits otherwise prohibited acts.
