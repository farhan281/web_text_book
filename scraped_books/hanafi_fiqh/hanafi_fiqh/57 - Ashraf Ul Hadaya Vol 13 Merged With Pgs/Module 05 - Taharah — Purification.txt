Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, 'makruh' in Kitab al-Karahiyah means makruh tahrimi, which is closer to haram than mere dislike.

This lesson takes you into the opening of Kitab al-Karahiyah, where the author clarifies a crucial terminological point. The term 'makruh' in this chapter does not simply mean 'disliked' (makruh tanzihi); it carries the weight of 'makruh tahrimi', an act prohibited by evidence that is not absolutely decisive. Makruh tahrimi is unique to the Hanafi school.

The prohibition of gold and silver vessels is the same for both genders. This is explicitly stated in the passage.

Sharing Excess Rent in Tahayu on Single House Renting
Ruling on sharing excess rent when tahayu is on renting out a single house

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on sharing excess rent in tahayu by distinguishing between tahayu on istighlal and tahayu on manafi.

Core Idea
In tahayu on renting, excess rent is shared; in tahayu on use, it is not.

This lesson explores the Hanafi position on how co-owners share rental income when they take turns renting out jointly owned property. The key principle is the distinction between tahayu on the right to rent (istighlal) versus tahayu on actual use (manafi). Understanding this distinction is crucial for equitable property management among partners.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of tahayu
Determine whether the agreement was on istighlal (renting) or manafi (personal use). This is the critical classification that determines the ruling.
Tahayu on istighlal means each co-owner has the right to rent out the property during their turn. Tahayu on manafi means each co-owner personally uses the property during their turn.
2
State the ruling for istighlal
If the tahayu was on renting (istighlal), then if one turn yields higher rent than the other, the excess rent must be shared equally between the co-owners to achieve equity.
The underlying principle is al-ta'dil (equity): the tahayu divides the right to rent, but the actual rental income may vary, so the excess is shared to prevent inequality.
3
State the ruling for manafi
If the tahayu was on personal use (manafi), and then one co-owner rents out the property during their turn and earns more than the other, the excess does not need to be shared.
The equality was already achieved in the usufruct itself (each gets equal time of use). The subsequent rental income is a separate matter that does not affect the original division.
4
Rationalize the distinction
The difference in rulings rests on the objective of tahayu: to ensure equitable distribution of the benefit. When the benefit is the right to rent, the rental amount is part of that benefit; inequality in rent undermines equity. When the benefit is personal use, the use itself is equal; any later rental profit is incidental.
The proof is rational (aql): the ruling follows from the very purpose of the tahayu agreement.

Valid Slaughter Tools and the Prohibition of Attached Nail and Tooth
Ruling on permissible slaughter tools and prohibition of attached nail and tooth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on permissible slaughter tools and explain why attached nail and tooth are invalid.

Core Idea
Attached nail and tooth cause death by pressure, making the animal carrion (mukhtaniqah), so slaughter with them is haram.

This lesson addresses the crucial distinction between valid and invalid tools for Islamic slaughter (dhabihah). The general principle is that any sharp object that can make blood flow is permissible, but there is a specific prohibition on using an attached nail or tooth. Understanding the reasoning behind this exception—death by pressure rather than proper cutting—is key to applying the rule correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its General Ruling
The act is dhabihah (Islamic slaughter). The default ruling is that any sharp tool causing blood flow is permissible. Confirm the tool's ability to cut and cause bleeding.
Tools like knives, stones, or bamboo sticks with an edge are valid if sharp.
2
Locate the Exception: Attached Nail and Tooth
Note that the text explicitly excludes nail and tooth that are still attached to the body. Even if sharp, they are not valid.
Detached nail or tooth may be permissible if sharp, as they are no longer 'attached'.
3
Understand the Legal Reasoning (Illah)
The illah (effective cause) is that the animal dies from pressure (bujh) rather than blood loss. This makes it equivalent to mukhtaniqah (strangled), which is carrion.
Quran 5:3 prohibits mukhtaniqah, so the meat becomes haram.
4
Note the Methodological Point
Imam Muhammad's caution when no explicit text exists is highlighted; but here he found a text in Jami' al-Saghir confirming the ruling.
This shows the importance of textual evidence in Hanafi fiqh.

Dhabh Purifies Non-Edible Animals: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Discusses purification of hide and meat through slaughter for non-edible animals, except humans and pigs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on dhabh's effect on non-edible animals and explain the thamarah al-khilaf between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools.

Core Idea
Dhabh purifies hide and meat of non-edible animals — except humans and pigs — by removing impure fluids, an independent benefit.

What happens when you slaughter a donkey or mule? Does the hide become pure for use? The Hanafi school says yes, while the Shafi'i school disagrees because they see dhabh's sole purpose as making meat permissible. This lesson dissects the dispute, focusing on whether purification is a primary or subsidiary effect of dhabh.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling and exceptions
Identify the starting claim: dhabh purifies both hide and meat of animals not eaten, except humans (due to honor) and pigs (due to intrinsic impurity).
The exceptions are crucial; they show that dhabh's effect is not universal.
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection and its proof
Recognize the opposing view: dhabh has no effect because its primary function is to make meat halal; purification is subsidiary and cannot stand alone.
The Shafi'i analogy is to Magian slaughter, which is not valid dhabh.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal: dhabh directly removes impure blood and fluids, which is a separate benefit comparable to tanning. This makes purification an intended ruling for the hide, not merely a byproduct.
The analogy with tanning (which purifies dead skin) is key.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Apply the difference: For Hanafis, the hide of a slaughtered donkey can be used as a prayer mat without tanning; Shafi'is require tanning even after slaughter.
This difference has practical implications for daily use of animal products.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the effect of dhabh (Islamic slaughter) on the animals whose meat is not eaten (e.g., mules, donkeys, etc.). The Hanafi position is that dhabh purifies both the hide and the meat of such animals, making them tahir (pure), regardless of whether the meat is permissible to eat. There are two exceptions: humans and pigs. The human is exempt due to dignity and sanctity; the pig is exempt due to its intrinsic impurity. The hide of such animals is purified by dhabh just as it is by tanning. The Shafi'i position is that dhabh has no effect on these animals because the primary purpose of dhabh is to make meat permissible to eat, and since the meat is not edible, no subsidiary effect (like purification of hide) can follow. They analogize it to the slaughter of a Magian (which is considered a killing, not proper dhabh). The Hanafi response is that dhabh is effective in removing the impure fluids (blood), and once these are removed, the hide and meat become pure. This is similar to tanning which purifies the hide. The fat of such animals also becomes pure; if it falls into water, the water remains pure. There is a difference of opinion on whether one may use the parts of such animals (like hide) for purposes other than eating. Some say no, considering that the meat is not eaten (so the hide is also not to be used), while others say yes, analogizing to oil mixed with carcass fat where if the oil is dominant, it is permissible.

Key Terms
ذكاة (dhakah): Islamic slaughter performed by cutting the throat of a halal animal. In this passage, it discusses its effect on animals not permissible to eat.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the effect of slaughter (dhabh) on non-edible animals. The Hanafi position (from al-Quduri) states that slaughter purifies both the meat and hide of any non-edible animal, except for humans (due to honor) and pigs (due essential impurity). The Prohibition of Gold and Silver Vessels

Core Idea
Using gold or silver vessels is haram — the hadith warning of Hellfire makes the prohibition absolute.

This lesson examines the ruling on vessels made of gold and silver. The Hanafi school holds it to be prohibited (haram) for both men and women, based on clear prophetic traditions. The prohibition covers not only drinking but also eating, applying oil, and using any item made of these metals.

Vessels of Lead, Glass, Crystal, and Agate: Dispute with Shafi'i
Ruling with inter-madhhab dispute over analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on vessels of lead, glass, crystal, and agate, explain the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate why the Hanafi school rejects the analogy to gold and silver.

Core Idea
Custom restricts the 'illa: boasting with vessels was specific to gold and silver.

This passage addresses the permissibility of vessels made from materials other than gold and silver. While the Hanafi school declares them permissible (mubah), Imam al-Shafi'i considers them makruh by analogy to the prohibited gold and silver vessels. The crux of the dispute lies in the effective cause ('illa): is it the mere fact of being a vessel for boasting, or is it restricted to the customary practice of the polytheists? The Hanafi school invokes the custom of the Arabs to limit the analogy.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling given in the text: 'la ba'sa' meaning no harm, hence permissible (mubah). Note that this includes vessels of lead, glass, crystal, and agate.
The Hanafi position is the default unless a specific prohibition is established.
2
Identify the opposing view
Al-Shafi'i declares these vessels makruh. Recognize that his ruling is based on analogy (qiyas) to gold and silver vessels, which are prohibited due to boasting and pride.
Makruh here likely means makruh tanzihi (disliked, not sinful), but the text does not specify the degree.
3
Follow the opposing proof
Al-Shafi'i argues: gold and silver vessels are haram because they are used for boasting (tafakhur). Lead, glass, etc., can also be used for boasting; therefore, they should also be prohibited (or at least disliked). This is a straightforward qiyas.
This reasoning assumes the 'illa is the act of boasting itself, not the material.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: 'We say it is not so, because their custom was not to boast with other than gold and silver.' The 'illa is not absolute boasting, but the customary boasting associated specifically with gold and silver vessels in the time of the polytheists. Since other materials were not used for that purpose, the 'illa is absent, so the original prohibition does not extend.
This is a ta'lil (identification of the 'illa) that restricts the effective cause to a specific customary context.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis allow using such vessels for dining, decoration, etc. Shafi'is consider it makruh and would recommend avoiding them. In fatwa, the Hanafi position is relied upon.
The dispute shows how differences in determining the 'illa lead to different rulings across madhhabs.

Ruling on Using Silver-Plated Vessels and Items
States ruling on silver-plated items per Abu Hanifa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using silver-plated items and identify the condition that makes them permissible.

Core Idea
Silver-plated items are permissible if you avoid direct silver contact.

This passage presents the ruling of Abu Hanifa on silver-plated utensils, saddles, and furniture. The key condition is avoiding direct contact with the silver. Understanding this nuance distinguishes Hanafi fiqh from other schools and from the absolute prohibition of solid silver.

Guided Path
1
Identify the items and the ruling
The passage mentions a vessel, saddle, chair, and bed. All are ruled permissible (mubah) according to Abu Hanifa.
The ruling applies to plated items, not solid silver.
2
Locate the condition
The condition is 'an yattaqu mawdi' al-fidda' – he must avoid the place of silver. This is the key to permissibility.
Avoidance means no direct contact.
3
Apply the condition to each item
For the vessel, the mouth must not touch silver. For the saddle and bed, the body must not touch it. For handling, the hand must avoid the silver.
The condition is practical: it depends on the part of the body in contact.
4
Note the scope of the ruling
The passage only attributes this view to Abu Hanifa. Other Hanafi imams may differ, but the mufta bihi position is the permissibility with condition.
This is a zahir al-riwayah position.

Ruling on Using Items Adorned with Gold or Silver
Ruling on using vessels, furniture, and tools with silver/gold plating, with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using items adorned with gold or silver, explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, and apply the principle of tabi' (subordinate) to distinguish permissible from makruh cases.

Core Idea
Gold or silver as a subordinate part is permissible if avoided; if inseparable, it is unanimously permissible.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute concerning everyday items like vessels, furniture, and tools that have gold or silver plating. Imam Abu Hanifa permits their use provided one avoids direct contact with the precious metal, while Imams Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (in one narration) consider it makruh. The key to the disagreement lies in whether the gold/silver is considered a subordinate element (tabi') that does not affect the ruling, or whether using any part of the item counts as using all parts. The passage also clarifies that if the gold/silver is merely a wash that cannot be separated, use is permissible by consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its base ruling
The act is using a vessel, chair, sword, or any item that has gold or silver plating or inlay. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, such use is mubah (permissible) as long as one avoids direct contact with the precious metal.
The prohibition of using pure gold/silver vessels is established; here we deal with mixed items.
2
Distinguish between separable and inseparable adornment
The dispute concerns only cases where the gold/silver can be separated (e.g., a rim or inlay). If it is merely a wash (tamwiyyah) that cannot be removed, there is consensus on permissibility.
Inseparable: like gold leaf or paint; separable: a solid band or fitting.
3
State the Sahibayn's argument (qiyas)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that using any part of the vessel is like using the whole vessel; therefore, using the silver part is makruh (prohibited). They draw an analogy (qiyas) to using the pure metal itself.
Their proof: 'One who uses a part of the vessel uses all parts.'
4
Apply Abu Hanifa's argument (istihsan via tabi')
Abu Hanifa counters that the precious metal is subordinate (tabi') to the main material (wood, metal, etc.). Subordinate elements are not given legal weight, similar to silk trim on a cloak or a gold nail in a gemstone. Hence, using the item is not makruh.
The usul principle: 'La i'tibar lil tawabi' (subordinates are not considered).
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
According to Abu Hanifa, one may use the item while avoiding the gold/silver area. According to Abu Yusuf, it is makruh even with avoidance. The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is Abu Hanifa's permissibility.
This difference affects rulings on drinking vessels, seating, clothing, etc.

Imam Abu Hanifa says: When it is not a pure silver vessel, but a vessel or tool made of something else with silver plating on its edges, its use is permissible provided one avoids the silver area—i.e., if it is a drinking vessel, do not place the mouth on the silver but on another part. Similarly, if silver is plated on the ground, do not sit on the silver part; sit elsewhere, that is permissible. Likewise, if a chair or bed has silver plating, in both cases, do not sit on the silver part; sit elsewhere, that is permissible. Note: The same ruling applies if gold is plated. (Shami vol.5 p.218) Mufaddad means ornamented and decorated. Imam Shafi'i wrote that one's mouth should fall on the wood even if hands touch the silver. Then he narrated another view that hands should also not touch it, with various references and detailed discussion. I say (the jurists): Abu Yusuf said it is makruh. Muhammad's statement is narrated with Abu Hanifa and also with Abu Yusuf. Commentary: Imam Abu Hanifa deems all these cases permissible, while Imam Abu Yusuf declares them makruh. As for Imam Muhammad, there are two narrations: some narrate that he is with Imam Abu Hanifa (permissible), others that he is with Abu Yusuf (makruh). The ruling on the use of gold and silver on vessels, chairs, swords, etc. And upon this ikhtilaf rests the vessel plated with gold or silver, the chair similarly plated, when it is done on a sword, sharpening stone, mirror frame, or on a Mushaf (making it gold or silver), and similarly the ikhtilaf applies to a bridle, stirrup, and crupper if silver-plated, and to a garment with writing in gold or silver. Commentary: That is, whether gold-plated or silver-plated, whether a vessel, chair, sword, sharpening stone, mirror frame, bridle, stirrup, crupper, or garment with writing in gold or silver, in all these cases the same ikhtilaf applies as mentioned. The ruling is the same; earlier the term mufaddad was used, now mudhabbab is used. Mudhabbab means bound—when a tooth is bound with gold or silver, it is called 'dabba asnanahu bi al-quinna'... and mufaddad is for beauty and strength. Note: 'Masjid' (sharpening stone), 'samam' (tool for sharpening knives), 'mira' (mirror), 'lijam' (bridle), 'nifar' (the strap at the back of the saddle, i.e., diichi). The point of ikhtilaf between Imam and the two Sahibayn: This ikhtilaf is about what is separable (yakhlas). As for gilding (tamwiyyah) that does not separate, it is unanimously permissible. Their proof: One who uses a part of the vessel uses all parts, just as when one uses the place of gold or silver (it is makruh). Abu Hanifa's proof: This (the precious metal) is subordinate, and subordinates are not considered, so it is not makruh, like a cloak trimmed with silk, a garment with embroidery, and a gemstone's golden nail. Commentary: The ikhtilaf between the Sahibayn and Imam is only when the gold or silver can be separated. If it is a wash (tamwiyyah) that cannot be separated, its use is unanimously permissible. The Sahibayn's proof: Using a part of the vessel is like using all parts; as using the gold/silver area itself is makruh, so this is also makruh. Imam says: Subordinates are not considered, so the gold/silver here is subordinate, hence not makruh, like a cloak with silk trim, a garment with silk embroidery, or a gemstone with a golden nail. Note: If it is not a piece of gold/silver but just a wash (paint), since it is only color that remains, color is not considered; then whether vessel, bridle, stirrup, all are unanimously permissible.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the use of items that have gold or silver plating, inlay, or wash. The base ruling from Imam Abu Hanifa is that such use is permissible provided one avoids direct contact with the precious metal (e.g., not putting one's mouth on the silver part of a vessel). Imams Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (in one narration) consider it makruh. The dispute is limited to cases where the gold/silver is a separate piece that can be removed; if it is merely a surface wash (tamwiyyah) that cannot be separated, the use is halal by consensus. The Sahibayn argue by qiyas: using any part of the vessel is like using the whole, so touching the silver part is makruh. Imam Abu Hanifa uses istihsan: the precious metal is subordinate (tabi'), and subordinate elements do not affect the ruling, analogous to silk trim on a cloak or a gold pin on a gemstone. The passage then extends this ikhtilaf to many items: vessels, chairs, swords, sharpening stones, mirrors, saddles, bridles, stirrups, garments with gold/silver writing, and even binding teeth. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Abu Hanifa's view due to the principle that subordinate elements are overlooked.

Key Terms
مفضض (mufaddad): An item (vessel, tool, etc.) adorned or plated with silver.
مضبب (mudhabbab): An item bound or plated with gold; used for gold as opposed to silver.
تمويه (tamwiyyah): A thin wash or coating of gold/silver that cannot be separated; not considered as actual metal.
تابع (tabi'): Subordinate element (the gold/silver) that is not the main substance; its ruling is disregarded.

Accepting the Word of a Magian Regarding Meat Purchase
Ruling on accepting a Magian's word in meat purchase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting a Magian servant's statement about the source of meat, distinguish between transactions (mu'amalat) and religious matters (diyanat), and apply the a fortiori argument (awlā) to both scenarios.

Core Idea
A Magian's word is accepted in transactions due to rationality and necessity, but not in religious matters.

Imagine you hire a Magian servant to buy meat. He returns and tells you its source. Can you trust his word? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling: his statement is accepted in transactions because he is rational and his religion forbids lying, and because rejecting it would cause daily hardship. We will trace the proof from the text, including a powerful a fortiori argument, and clearly separate transactional matters from purely religious testimonies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: the Magian servant says he bought meat from a Muslim, Jew, or Christian (first scenario), or from a Magian (second scenario).
The ruling differs based on the claimed source.
2
Understand the basis for acceptance
In the first scenario, the Magian's word is accepted because he is rational, his religion (even if false) considers lying forbidden, and there is widespread need due to the frequency of transactions.
This applies even though the Magian is not a Muslim or Kitabi.
3
Apply the a fortiori argument
The text argues that if the servant's word is accepted for permissible meat (when he claims from a non-Magian), then it is even more appropriate to accept it for prohibited meat (when he claims from a Magian), since prohibition requires more caution.
This logical priority (awlā) reinforces consistency.
4
Distinguish mu'amalat from diyanat
The passage clarifies that the servant is not testifying to permissibility or prohibition directly, but simply to a transaction (purchase). Thus the ruling belongs to mu'amalat, not diyanat, where a Magian's word would not be accepted.
This distinction is key to understanding the Hanafi position.

News of Water Impurity: Acceptance and Procedure (Ruling with Proof)
Ruling on acting upon news of water impurity from just vs. fasiq/mastur reporters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on news of water impurity, distinguishing between a just reporter and a fasiq/mastur, and determine the appropriate action (tayammum or wudu) based on taharri.

Core Idea
A just Muslim's report of water impurity is definitive; from a fasiq or mastur, it requires taharri — only dominant suspicion determines the ruling.

In Hanafi fiqh, news of water impurity falls under diyaanat (religious matters) and the reliability of the informant determines whether you must abandon wudu and perform tayammum. This lesson clarifies the ruling and the procedure of taharri, including the recommended precaution of pouring out the water.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of the informant
Determine whether the person reporting water impurity is a just Muslim ('adil), a fasiq (sinner), or mastur al-haal (unknown character). This classification is the basis for the ruling.
Justice ('adala) in Hanafi fiqh includes being Muslim, adult, sane, and free from major sins.
2
Apply the ruling for a just informant
If the informant is just, his report is accepted definitively. You must not perform wudu with that water and must do tayammum. There is no need to pour out the water as a precaution.
Justice eliminates the possibility of lying, so the ruling is absolute.
3
Perform taharri for a fasiq or mastur informant
When the informant is not just, you must engage in taharri (speculation or seeking predominant opinion). Consider the informant's character, the context, and any other factors to form a dominant suspicion.
Taharri is a rational process, not based on external evidence.
4
Determine action based on predominant opinion
If your predominant opinion (akbar al-ra'i) is that the informant is truthful, you must do tayammum and not use the water. If you suspect he is lying, you may perform wudu with the water and not do tayammum.
The ruling follows the stronger suspicion, not mere possibility.
5
Consider the recommendation to pour out the water
For a fasiq or mastur informant, it is recommended (ahwat) to pour out the water and then do tayammum, removing all doubt. This is not required for a just informant.
Ahwat is a precautionary step, not a binding ruling.

Ruling on Wearing Rings of Stone, Iron, and Brass
Prohibition of non-silver rings with hadith proofs and ikhtilaf on yashb stone

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on rings of different materials, explain the evidence from hadith and al-Jami' al-Saghir, and distinguish the ikhtilaf on yashb stone.

Core Idea
Only silver rings are permitted for men; rings of stone, iron, and brass are haram based on explicit hadith.

This lesson examines the ruling on wearing rings in the Hanafi school. While silver rings are explicitly permitted by Sunnah, rings made from other materials like stone, iron, and brass are forbidden based on the Prophet's ﷺ statements. We also look at the debate about the light stone called yashb, where some scholars allow it due to its lightness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wearing a ring (al-takhattum). The general ruling from al-Jami' al-Saghir is that only silver is permissible; all other materials are prohibited.
The ruling applies specifically to men; women have different rulings.
2
Locate the dalil
Two hadith are cited: the Prophet criticized a brass ring as having the smell of idols, and an iron ring as the ornament of Hellfire. These serve as primary proofs.
The hadith are ahad, so thubut is zanni, but dalala is considered qati' because of the explicit disapproval.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's words 'smell of idols' and 'ornament of Hellfire' clearly indicate prohibition. Brass and iron are used as examples, and by analogy, other non-silver metals fall under the same ruling.
The absolute phrase 'لا يتختم إلا بالفضة' from Imam Muhammad reinforces the prohibition.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is a difference of opinion regarding the stone called yashb (possibly jade). Some scholars say it is permissible because it is light and not a true stone; others, following Imam Muhammad's absolute wording, consider it prohibited.
The preferred view (al-ashah) is permissibility, as cited by al-Shami.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The mufta bihi position: rings of stone, iron, brass are haram. For yashb, the easier position is permission, as it is not considered a real stone. Unreliable opinions (e.g., Qahistani) permitting gold rings are rejected.
See al-Shami 5:259 and Shaikh al-Nahr 2:460.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A man may lawfully see only the face and hands of a non-mahram woman, and only when safe from desire.

This lesson examines a foundational Hanafi ruling on gender interaction. The text from Al-Hidayah and Al-Quduri establishes the permissibility of looking at a non-mahram woman's face and hands, grounded in Quran 24:31 and the necessity of social transactions. However, the school also contains nuanced differences regarding the feet and forearms. By working through the proof and the intra-madhhab debates, you will understand how the Hanafi school balances textual interpretation with practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a man looking at a non-mahram (ajnabiyyah) woman. The basic ruling from al-Quduri: permissible only to face and hands; looking at other parts is not permissible.
The ruling is for casual looking without necessity; different rulings apply for medical or marriage purposes.
2

The primary proof is the Quranic verse 24:31: '...nor let them display their adornment except that which is apparent thereof.'
The verse is in Arabic: وَلا يُبْدِينَ زِينَتَهُنَّ إِلَّا مَا ظَهَرَ مِنْهَا
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (indication of the proof)
The Hanafi school interprets 'zina' (adornment) as the places of adornment via majaz mursal (metonymy). The companions Ali and Ibn Abbas explained 'that which is apparent' as kohl and ring, meaning the eyes (face) and hands (fingers). Thus, the face and hands are excluded from the prohibition.
Majaz mursal uses the relationship of state (hāl) and place (mahall): the adornment (state) is mentioned but the body part (place) is intended.
4

Necessity (darurah) requires women to interact with men in transactions and daily life. This rational basis supports the permissibility of revealing the face and hands.
The text also mentions that this necessity does not extend to feet or forearms in the majority view.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and tarjih
While the majority (including al-Quduri) restricts to face and hands, Abu Hanifa permitted looking at the feet due to need, and Abu Yusuf permitted forearms due to custom. The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is the majority: only face and hands, with the additional condition that there must be no fear of desire (shahwah).
The tarjih is based on the principle that the original ruling is prohibition, and permissibility is granted only to the extent of necessity.

It begins with the base ruling, provides direct hadith evidence, and then defends the position against four opposing views: the Shafi'i inclusion of the navel, the Shafi'i exclusion of the knee, the Zahiri exemption of the thigh, and al-Kamari's reliance on custom. Understanding this passage will equip you to apply the ruling in practice and appreciate how the Hanafi school prioritizes textual evidence over customary practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
State the ruling: a man's 'awrah for another man is from below the navel to the knee inclusive. The navel is not 'awrah; the thigh and knee are 'awrah.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The hadith: 'عورة الرجل ما بین سرته الى ركبته' (The 'awrah of a man is what is between his navel and his knee) and the narration 'مادون سرته حتى تجاوز ركبته' (below his navel until it passes his knee).
The second narration is key for excluding the navel.
3
Analyze the Hanafi deduction
From 'between navel and knee', the navel is not included. The knee is included because of the separate hadith 'الركبة من العورة' and the rational proof that the knee is where the 'awrah (thigh) and non-'awrah (shin) meet, so prohibition prevails.
4
Enumerate the opposing views
Shafi'i: navel is 'awrah, knee is not. Zahiri: thigh is not 'awrah. Al-Kamari: below navel to pubic hair is not 'awrah, relying on custom.
Each opposes a specific part of the Hanafi boundary.
5
Examine the Hanafi response
Custom cannot override explicit text (the hadith is clear). The thigh is proven 'awrah by the Prophet's command to Juhud. The knee is included by the meeting-point argument and the separate hadith.
The severity of exposure is graded: knee (gentle admonishment), thigh (stern rebuke), private parts (discipline).

For a man, how much of another man's body may he see? Al-Quduri said: A man may look at any part of another man's body except what is between his navel and his knee because the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'The 'awrah of a man is what is between his navel and his knee.' And it is narrated: 'Below his navel until it passes his knee.' By this it is established that the navel is not 'awrah, contrary to what Abu 'Isma and al-Shafi'i say. And the knee is 'awrah, contrary to what al-Shafi'i said. And the thigh is 'awrah, contrary to the literalists. And what is below the navel to the point where hair begins is 'awrah, contrary to what Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn al-Fadl al-Kamari says, relying on custom, because custom is not considered when there is a text to the contrary. Commentary: The 'awrah of a man is from below the navel to the knee, meaning the knee is included in the 'awrah and the navel is not. Abu 'Isma and al-Shafi'i consider the navel 'awrah. Al-Shafi'i does not consider the knee 'awrah. The literalists do not consider the thigh 'awrah. Al-Kamari does not consider the area below the navel to be 'awrah, basing it on custom. The author responded that custom can only be a specifier if there is no text to the contrary, and here there is a text. And Abu Hurairah narrated from the Prophet that he said: 'The knee is part of the 'awrah.' And al-Hasan ibn 'Ali uncovered his navel, and Abu Hurairah kissed it. And the Prophet said to Juhud: 'Cover your thigh. Do you not know that the thigh is 'awrah?' And because the knee is the meeting point of the thigh bone and the shin bone, so what is prohibited (thigh) and what is permitted (shin) meet, and in such a case the prohibited prevails. And the ruling of 'awrah regarding the knee is lighter than regarding the thigh, and regarding the thigh is lighter than regarding the private parts, such that one who uncovers his knee is gently admonished, one who uncovers his thigh is sternly rebuked, and one who uncovers his private parts is disciplined if he persists.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling with proof on the definition of a man's 'awrah with respect to another man. The base ruling from al-Quduri's matn states that a man may look at all of another man's body except from the navel to the knee. The commentator then provides hadith evidence: the prophetic statement 'the 'awrah of a man is from the navel to the knee' and another narration 'below the navel until it passes the knee'. From this, it is deduced that the navel is not 'awrah (contra Abu 'Isma and al-Shafi'i), the knee is 'awrah (contra al-Shafi'i), the thigh is 'awrah (contra the Zahiri school), and the area from the navel down to where pubic hair grows is 'awrah (contra al-Kamari who relied on custom). A rational proof is also provided: the knee is where the thigh bone (which is 'awrah) and the shin bone (which is not) meet, so the prohibition prevails. Permissibility of Touching Where Looking is Permissible
Ruling linking permissibility of touching to permissibility of looking for same body parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule linking permissibility of touching to permissibility of looking, and apply it to determine what body parts may be touched.

Core Idea
If it is permissible to look, it is permissible to touch — looking and touching share the same 'awrah boundary.

This passage from al-Quduri establishes a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: the ruling for touching another person's body follows the ruling for looking. If a body part is not 'awrah, both looking and touching are permissible. This principle simplifies many questions of physical contact in daily interactions, such as in medical treatment or casual contact. The proof is based on the equality (musawat) of looking and touching when the body part is not 'awrah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is touching another man's body. The ruling is permissibility (mubah) for parts that are permissible to look at.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the statement: 'because they are equal regarding that which is not 'awrah.' This is an analogy (qiyas jaliy) linking touching to looking.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The effective cause ('illa) is that the body part is not 'awrah. Since looking is permissible for non-'awrah parts, touching is analogous.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The text indicates this ruling is agreed upon (muttafaq alayh) across schools, so no major dispute exists.
5
Apply the rule
For any body part that is not 'awrah (e.g., arms, legs, head), touching is permissible. For 'awrah parts (between navel and knees), touching is prohibited.
This rule applies only when there is no sexual desire (shahwah). If lust is present, touching becomes haram even for non-'awrah parts.

What a Woman May See of Another Woman's Body
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on women viewing each other's bodies and distinguish the two positions within the school, applying the preferred position to practical scenarios.

Core Idea
A woman may see another woman's body to the same extent a man may see a man's body — necessity makes it permissible.

The issue of women looking at each other's bodies arises in everyday contexts like bathing, medical examinations, or changing clothes. The Hanafi school generally permits it based on gender similarity, lack of inherent desire, and practical necessity. However, a narration from Abu Hanifa suggests a stricter rule equating it to mahram relations. This lesson unpacks the proof and explains why the more permissive view is preferred.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
Read the opening statement: 'A woman looks at another woman to the extent that it is permissible for a man to look at a man.' This is the default ruling.
The default is that the same parts are awrah: navel to knee is prohibited, the rest is permissible.
2
Understand the three reasons (adillah)
Three proofs are given: (1) gender similarity (mujanasah), (2) general absence of sexual desire, (3) practical necessity (darurah) among women for exposure.
These are aqli (rational) proofs combined with observation of common practice.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi narration from Abu Hanifa
A different view attributed to Abu Hanifa equates a woman's sight of another woman to a man's sight of his mahram, which is more restrictive (e.g., belly and back not allowed).
This is not the mufta bihi (operative) view.
4
Apply the tarjih (preference)
The commentator declares the first view as asahh (more sound) because necessity among women for exposure is greater, and the original analogy (man-man) is stronger.
The mufta bihi position is the first view.
5
Determine practical consequence (thamarah)
Under the preferred view, women may see each other's bellies, backs, and thighs (excluding the private parts). Under the other view, these areas are prohibited.
This affects rulings in baths, medical contexts, and everyday modesty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on istibra and apply the principle that the obligation follows the complete cause (sabab) of ownership and possession.

Core Idea
Istibra is wajib because the ruling follows the cause, not the wisdom; the cause is complete ownership and possession.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains the obligation of istibra after acquiring a slave girl. The key principle is that the ruling is attached to the sabab, the legal cause. The text provides five subsidiary cases to illustrate that the cause must be complete. You will learn how the Hanafi school consistently applies this principle, even when the wisdom behind the rule might suggest otherwise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and its cause
The passage repeatedly states that istibra is required when both ownership (milk) and possession (yad) are acquired. Note that the cause is 'istihdath al-milk wa al-yad'.
This is the fundamental principle of the lesson.
2
Understand the legal principle: rulings follow causes, not wisdoms
The text argues that istibra is wajib because the cause exists, even if emptiness of the womb is known. This distinguishes Hanafi fiqh from basing rulings solely on hikmah.
A key usul al-fiqh point: ahkam taduru ma'a asbabiha.
3
Examine the five subsidiary cases where a single menses is insufficient
Each case shows a scenario where either possession is lacking or the menses occurred before the cause was complete. The text explains why the menses does not count.
These cases concretize the principle.
4
Analyze Abu Yusuf's objection and the majority response
Abu Yusuf uses qiyas with divorce before consummation; the majority responds by distinguishing: the cause of istibra is specific to acquisition of ownership and possession, and the ruling cannot precede the cause.
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).
5
Apply the principle to a novel case
Using the same principle, determine the ruling for any acquisition where both ownership and possession are complete. The principle is general and can be extended.
Test your ability to apply the sabab concept.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Istibra' for a pregnant slave ends with delivery; for a non-menstruating slave, it is one month, by analogy to 'iddah.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies how istibra' (the waiting period for a female slave) is completed depending on the slave's condition. For a pregnant slave, it ends with delivery; for a non-menstruating slave (minor or menopausal), it is one month. The ruling is based on a hadith and an analogy to the 'iddah of a free woman who does not menstruate.

For non-menstruating slaves, the ruling is derived by analogy to the 'iddah of a free woman who does not menstruate—she waits three months (Quran 65:4). Since the slave's istibra' is shorter, it is set at one month, by analogy to the one menstrual cycle for menstruating slaves.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four Hanafi opinions on the duration of istibra' for a woman with extended purity and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The Prophet lodged disbelievers in his mosque – impurity is spiritual, not physical.

This lesson examines the classical disagreement over whether dhimmis may enter the masjid. The Hanafi school permits entry based on a clear Prophetic practice, while Shafi'i and Maliki schools rely on a Quranic verse about impurity. The discussion reveals how different interpretations of a single source lead to divergent rulings.

Additionally, the Hanafis argue that the impurity of polytheists is spiritual (in belief), not physical.
The verse 'innama al-mushrikuna najasun' is interpreted as referring to spiritual impurity and is specific to the Sacred Mosque after the year 9 AH.
4
Understand the opposing view
The opposing school (likely Shafi'i) holds that disbelievers are prohibited from entering any mosque based on the general import of verse 9:28 and the concept of impurity.
The opponent takes the verse at its apparent meaning and applies it universally.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the verse is not general but is specific to the Sacred Mosque and to the context of the year 9 AH when polytheists were defeated. The impurity is in their belief, not their bodies, so it does not defile the mosque.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that in Hanafi fiqh, disbelievers may enter mosques for trade or other purposes, while in the opposing school they are barred entirely.

Greeting an Open Sinner: Makruh Unless for Teaching
Tanbih from Shami on greeting those engaged in sin.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on greeting a fasiq mu'lin and apply the exception for teaching.

Core Idea
Greeting an open sinner is makruh tanzih unless done for teaching.

This lesson examines a tanbih from Shami commentary regarding those to whom giving salâm is makruh. The focus is on the fasiq mu'lin, a person who openly commits sin. The fuqaha ruled that greeting such a person is makruh, but later scholars like Ashraf Ali Thanwi allowed it for the purpose of teaching. Understanding this distinction helps avoid either unnecessary severity or laxity in social interactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
Determine who is considered a fasiq mu'lin: a person who openly commits sins without shame, such as a chess player while engaged, or one who shaves his beard.

2
State the default ruling
The default ruling is that giving salâm to a fasiq mu'lin is makruh tanzih (disliked, not sinful but best avoided).
This is because greeting can be seen as honoring the sinner and thus tacitly approving the sin.

Ashraf Ali Thanwi permits giving salâm if the intention is teaching (ta'lim) — i.e., to admonish or correct the person, not to honor them.
The intention changes the ruling from makruh to permissible (mubah).

In practice, assess your intention: if you greet to open a conversation about the sin, it is allowed; if merely out of habit or respect, it remains makruh.

Ruling on Iron Collar and Fetters for Slaves
Ruling on placing an iron collar and fetters on a slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi ruling on placing an iron collar versus fetters on a slave, and explain the proof (qiyas and sunnah) for each.

Core Idea
An iron collar on a slave is makruh for resembling Hellfire; fetters are permissible as a proven security measure.

In this passage, Imam Muhammad rules on two methods of restraining a slave: an iron collar around the neck and fetters on the feet. Though both serve to restrain, the Hanafi school distinguishes sharply because one resembles the punishment of the people of Hellfire, while the other follows the established practice of Muslims for security. This lesson will walk through the evidence and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acts and their rulings
First, recognize that Imam Muhammad gives two distinct rulings: placing an iron collar (طوق) is makruh (disliked), while placing fetters (قيد) is not makruh (permissible).
The text uses 'يكره' for the collar and 'لا يكره' for fetters.
2
Examine the proof for the collar: qiyas to Hellfire
The collar is analogized to the punishment of Hellfire, which involves iron collars and chains. This resemblance makes it makruh, similar to burning with fire (also prohibited).
The illa (effective cause) is 'resemblance to Hellfire punishment'.
3
Examine the proof for fetters: sunnah of Muslims
Fetters are proven by the 'sunnah of Muslims' – the practice of applying shackles to the foolish and wicked to prevent escape. This is a legitimate security measure, not a punishment.
The proof is the established practice of the Muslim community, not direct textual evidence.
4
Analyze the later tarjih (Durr Mukhtar and Shami)
Later scholars note that if running away is rampant, placing a collar as a mark is not considered harmful, but the original ruling of dislike persists when fetters are sufficient.
This is an example of tarjih based on changing conditions (urf).
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical consequence: in a situation where a slave is not prone to escape, only fetters should be used; if escape is widespread, the collar may be tolerated but remains disliked in principle.
The distinction helps avoid unnecessary harm while preserving security.

He said: It is disliked that a man places on the neck of his slave ar-rayah (and it is narrated ad-dāyah), which is an iron collar that prevents him from moving his head, and it is customary among oppressors because it is the punishment of the people of Hellfire, so it is disliked like burning with fire. Since this is pure oppression, it is prohibited. However, providing means for safeguarding one's property is obligatory. It is permissible to place fetters on his feet out of fear that he may run away. This method has been practiced among Muslims for slaves whose running away is feared. Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proofs. It addresses the permissibility of restraining a slave. Imam Muhammad states that placing an iron collar (taq) around a slave's neck is makruh (disliked) because it resembles the punishment of the people of Hellfire and is a practice of oppressors. The analogy is made to burning with fire, which is also prohibited. However, placing fetters (qayd) on the feet is not makruh because it is a sunnah of Muslims for the foolish and wicked, and it serves a valid purpose: preventing the slave from running away and protecting the owner's property. The Urdu commentary explains that while the iron collar was a form of torture, fetters are a means of safeguarding property. A note from Durr Mukhtar mentions that in later times (especially in Sudan where running away was prevalent), the collar was used as a mark for runaways, and the later scholars considered it permissible, though the original ruling of dislike remains for cases where fetters suffice. Shami clarifies that the dislike is clear when fetters are sufficient; if not, the collar may be used without dislike.

Key Terms
طوق (taq): An iron collar placed around the neck, preventing head movement, used as punishment.
قيد (qayd): Fetters or shackles placed on the feet to restrain movement.
راية/داية (rayah/dāyah): Another term for the collar, possibly meaning 'flag' or 'sign'.

Permissibility of Enema for Medical Treatment
Ruling with proof on enema for medical treatment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on enema (huqna) and identify the conditions that make it permissible or impermissible.

Core Idea
Enema is permissible for genuine medical treatment; using it for frivolous purposes or with haram substances is not.

This passage from Imam Muhammad's statement addresses the use of enemas for medical treatment. While medical treatment is generally permissible, the text sets clear boundaries: the enema must be intended for a real medical need, and the substances used must not be haram unless extraordinary necessity applies. Let's examine the proofs and conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is using an enema (huqna). The ruling is that it is 'la ba'sa' (no harm), meaning permissible (mubah), provided the intention is medical treatment (tadawi).
Imam Muhammad explicitly states 'la ba'sa' (no harm).
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The proof cited is ijma (consensus) and hadith. The text says 'because medical treatment is permissible by consensus, and the hadith has come permitting it.' This establishes the general principle.
The precise hadiths are not cited here, but the commentary lists six Companions' narrations.
3
Note distinctions and conditions
The text states there is no difference between men and women. However, it adds a condition: one must not use prohibited (haram) substances like wine. Using haram for treatment is itself haram.
The commentary clarifies that enema only for medical need; if for weight gain, it is not permissible.
4
Examine the commentary expansion
The Urdu commentary expands on conditions: enema is permissible only when intended to cure illness (e.g., colic). It also cites exceptions: under dire necessity and with a trustworthy Muslim physician, even haram substances like wine, blood, or carrion may become permissible if no alternative exists.
This exception follows the broader Hanafi principle of necessity (darurah).

He said: There is no harm in the enema (huqna); by it he intends medical treatment because medical treatment is permissible by consensus, and the hadith has come permitting it. There is no difference between men and women, except that one should not use prohibited things like wine and the like because seeking cure with a prohibited thing is prohibited. Commentary: Enema is permissible provided that medicine is intended, e.g., for colic etc., to remove illness. If the purpose is merely to become fat, it is not permissible. Hence the restriction to medical treatment. The permissibility of medical treatment is established by hadiths from six Companions: Usama, Abu al-Darda', Anas, Ibn Mas'ud, Abu Hurairah, Ibn Abbas. There is also the saying of two [?]. There is no difference between men and women in the permissibility of taking enema; it is permissible for all. However, things that are haram, like wine, their use is prohibited because seeking cure with a haram thing is haram. For a sick person, it is permissible to drink blood, urine, and eat dead meat as medical treatment when a trustworthy Muslim physician says that his cure lies in that and there is no permissible substitute. This is found in the books of fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proofs regarding the permissibility of using enemas (huqna) for medical treatment. Imam Muhammad states it is permissible based on ijma (consensus) and hadith. There is no difference between men and women in this ruling. However, using haram substances like wine for treatment is not permissible because seeking cure with haram is itself haram. The Urdu commentary clarifies that enema is only allowed when the intention is genuine medical treatment (e.g., for colic), not for frivolous purposes like gaining weight. It then lists several hadiths from six Companions supporting the permissibility of medical treatment in general. An additional note extends this principle: drinking blood, urine, and eating carrion are permissible for treatment if a Muslim physician confirms there is no alternative. This follows the broader Hanafi principle that necessity permits the otherwise prohibited.

Key Terms
حقنة (huqna): An enema, injection of fluid into the rectum for medical purposes.
تداوي (tadāwī): Medical treatment, seeking cure from illness.
استشفاء بالمحرم (istishfā' bil-muharram): Seeking cure using a prohibited substance.
