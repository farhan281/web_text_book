Dhakaat: The Legal Definition and Its Effect on Halal and Purity
Defining dhakaat as a condition for halal and taharah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dhakaat, state its legal effect on both halal and taharah, and distinguish between ikhtiyari and idtirari slaughter.

Core Idea
Dhakaat is wajib for meat to be halal and tahir.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi definition of dhakaat (Islamic slaughter) as a legal act that renders an animal's meat permissible (halal) and pure (tahir). The passage from al-Hidayah establishes that slaughter is a condition for both halal and purity, supported by the Quranic exception 'illa ma dhakkaytum'. It also introduces two modes of slaughter: the preferred throat incision (ikhtiyari) and emergency wounding (idtirari) when the throat is inaccessible.

Guided Path
1
Define dhakaat and its legal role
Identify the term dhakaat in the passage and note that it is defined as a condition (shart) for the slaughtered animal to be halal and tahir. Understand that without dhakaat, the meat is neither permissible nor pure.
Dhakaat literally means purity but here refers to the act of slaughter.
2
Identify the primary proof from the Quran
Find the verse quoted in the text: 'illa ma dhakkaytum' (except what you slaughter). Recognize that this exception implies that slaughter is a prerequisite for halal meat.
The proof is dalil naqli (textual evidence).
3
Explain the dual effect: halal and taharah
Note that slaughter not only makes the meat halal but also purifies it. This applies even to non-edible animals (e.g., lion) – their meat becomes pure though not halal.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position.
4
Distinguish the two types of dhakaat
The passage divides dhakaat into ikhtiyari (optional/planned: incision between the throat and jawbones) and idtirari (emergency: wound anywhere on the body when the throat cannot be reached). Understand that the second is a substitute valid only in necessity.
Obligation is according to capacity.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete scenario
Consider a case where an animal falls into a well and its throat is unreachable. Apply the idtirari ruling: a wound anywhere on the body suffices for halal and purity.
This shows the flexibility of Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage introduces Kitab al-Dhaba'ih. It defines key terms: dhabh (slaughter) and dhakaat (which literally means purity but here means slaughter). The author states that dhakaat (Islamic slaughter) is a condition for the meat to be halal (permissible) and tahir (pure). The primary proof is the Quranic verse 'except what you slaughter' (al-Ma'idah 5:3). The reasoning: slaughter distinguishes impure blood from pure meat. Furthermore, dhakaat also establishes taharah (purity) even for non-edible animals (e.g., lion) — their meat becomes pure though not halal. The hadith 'dhakaat al-ard yubsiha' is cited, but the commentator clarifies that this is not a prophetic hadith but a saying of Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyyah, as al-Ayni noted. Then the two types of dhakaat are explained: ikhtiyari (optional/planned) is the normal incision in the throat area; idtirari (compulsory/emergency) is wounding the animal anywhere on the body when one cannot reach the throat. The second is a substitute for the first, valid only when unable to do the first. This reflects the Hanafi principle that obligations are according to one's capacity.

Key Terms
ذكاة (dhakaat): Religious slaughter; cutting the throat and windpipe to allow blood to flow, making the animal halal and pure.
ذبائح (dhaba'ih): Plural of dhabihah; animals that are slaughtered.
اضطراري (idtirari): Emergency slaughter; wounding the animal anywhere when unable to access the throat.

Number of Persons Sharing a Sacrificial Animal
Ruling on sharing a cow or camel for sacrifice with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sharing a cow or camel for sacrifice and explain why the hadith overrides qiyas.

Core Idea
A cow or camel suffices for seven; a sheep for one—hadith overrides qiyas on indivisibility.

This lesson examines the classical Hanafi position on how many persons can share a single sacrificial animal. The default legal principle (qiyas) would require one animal per person, but a specific hadith from Jabir ibn Abdullah permits sharing a cow or camel among seven. Understanding this interaction between textual evidence (nass) and analogical reasoning (qiyas) is essential for grasping Hanafi usul in practice.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling from al-Quduri
Identify the explicit ruling: a sheep for one person, a cow or camel for seven persons.
This is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.
2
Understand the default qiyas
Recognise that analogical reasoning (qiyas) would limit a large animal to one person because the act of worship (bloodshed) is indivisible.
Qiyas here is the baseline rule.
3
Identify the athar (hadith) that overrides qiyas
Locate the hadith of Jabir: the Prophet (peace be upon him) shared a cow and a camel among seven.
This is the specific textual evidence (nass).
4
Note the scope of the exception
The hadith only applies to cows and camels; sheep remain under the original qiyas (one per person).
No text exists for sheep to override qiyas.
5
Appreciate the methodological principle
The Hanafi school gives precedence to a confirmed sunnah (athar) over qiyas, even when qiyas appears stronger.
This illustrates the principle 'al-ithar muqaddam 'ala al-qiyas'.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the number of persons who can share a single sacrificial animal. The matn from al-Quduri states that a sheep can only be sacrificed for one person, while a cow or camel (badanah) can be sacrificed for up to seven persons. The default rule (qiyas) would require one animal per person because the act of worship is shedding one animal's blood, and worship is not divisible. However, the Hanafis abandon this qiyas in favor of a specific hadith (athar) from Jabir ibn Abdullah, who reports that the Prophet allowed a cow to be shared by seven and a camel by seven. Since there is no such text for a sheep, it remains under the original qiyas and can only be for one person. The commentary emphasizes that the exception is only for large animals, and sheep are excluded. The reliance on athar over qiyas is a classic Hanafi principle in fiqh, where confirmed sunnah overrides analogical reasoning.

اراقت (iraqat al-dam): The shedding of the animal's blood, which is the essential act of sacrifice.
اثر (athar): A report or tradition, here referring to the hadith of Jabir.

Time for Udhiyah: City Dwellers After Eid Prayer, Villagers After Fajr
Ruling on udhiyah timing with hadith proof and refutation of Malik/Shafi'i.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school requires city dwellers to sacrifice after Eid prayer while villagers may sacrifice after Fajr, and refute the Maliki/Shafi'i condition of the Imam's sacrifice.

Core Idea
The timing of udhiyah hinges on whether Eid prayer is obligatory on the person – city dwellers must delay; villagers may start at Fajr.

This passage from Ashraf ul-Hadaya clarifies that the ruling on when to sacrifice (udhiyah) is not uniform for all Muslims. The key distinction is whether the person is a city dweller (misri) upon whom Eid prayer is obligatory, or a villager (qarawi) for whom Eid prayer is not prescribed. The hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) provides the basis, and the discussion extends to refuting the views of Imam Malik and Imam Shafi'i who add an extra condition of the Imam's sacrifice.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on timing
Identify the two categories: city dweller (misri) must sacrifice after Eid prayer; villager (qarawi) may sacrifice after Fajr on the Day of Nahr.
The condition of delay applies only to those upon whom Eid prayer is obligatory.
2
Identify the opposing view
Imam Malik and Imam Shafi'i require that the Imam sacrifices first before any individual can sacrifice after prayer.
Imam Malik’s condition is the Imam’s actual sacrifice; Imam Shafi’i conditions the completion of the khutba.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof
Two hadith: 'Whoever slaughters before the prayer, let him repeat his sacrifice; whoever slaughters after has completed his rite and followed the Sunnah.' And 'The first of our rites today is prayer, then sacrifice.'
The imperative 'falyu'id' indicates invalidity of pre-prayer sacrifice.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response to opponents
The hadith only mentions prayer, not the Imam's sacrifice. Thus, the condition of the Imam is an extra restriction not found in the text.
Al-Marghinani states the hadith is proof against Malik and Shafi'i.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Hanafis can sacrifice immediately after Eid prayer; Malikis and Shafi'is must wait for the Imam to sacrifice first (or complete khutba for Shafi'i).
This difference has practical implications for the timing of udhiyah in communities.

Sacrifice After Masjid Eid Prayer: Istihsan-Based Permissibility
Ruling with proof using istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of sacrifice after a masjid Eid prayer and explain the istihsan-based reasoning.

Core Idea
A valid Eid prayer, whether in masjid or eidgah, satisfies the condition for sacrifice.

The Eid prayer is a condition for the validity of sacrifice (udhiya). But what if the prayer is offered in a masjid rather than the open eidgah? This passage from the Hanafi tradition clarifies that any valid Eid congregation suffices, using the principle of istihsan (juristic preference). Understanding this ruling helps you appreciate how Hanafi fiqh uses ijtihad to facilitate worship while maintaining legal rigour.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is sacrificing (udhiya) after the Eid prayer. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) even if only a masjid congregation has prayed, not the eidgah.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is istihsan (juristic preference). The masjid prayer is a valid prayer (salat mu'tabara), so it serves as the condition for sacrifice.
Istihsan is used here to avoid hardship and to recognize the validity of the masjid congregation.
3
Understand the reasoning of istihsan
Strict qiyas (analogy) might require the official eidgah prayer as the standard, but istihsan overrides this because the purpose of the condition is to have performed a valid Eid prayer, which is achieved by any legitimate congregation.
The text explicitly states: 'because it is a valid prayer'.
4
Note the reverse case
If the eidgah prayed but the masjid did not, the ruling is the same: sacrifice is valid. This follows by analogy and is directly stated.
Symmetry of the ruling shows the underlying principle is the validity of the prayer itself, not the location.
5
Identify the alternative view
Some scholars (قيل) say it is permissible by both qiyas and istihsan, indicating that the ruling is well-established on multiple grounds.
This strengthens the mufta bihi position.

Days of Sacrifice: Hanafi vs Shafi‘i Dispute
Dispute on number of sacrifice days and virtue of first

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the days of sacrifice, explain the hadith proof, and apply the principle of certainty to resolve conflicting evidence.

Core Idea
Sacrifice is valid only on 10th, 11th, 12th—the certain days, not the 13th.

This passage addresses a well-known ikhtilaf between the Hanafi and Shafi‘i schools: how many days can one sacrifice the udhiyah? The Hanafi school limits it to three days based on a hadith from senior Companions, while the Shafi‘i school includes a fourth. The author presents the Hanafi proof and explains why the conflicting report is weak, establishing the Hanafi position as the relied-upon view. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to handle conflicting textual evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
What exactly is the ruling in dispute? It is the number of days on which the udhiyah sacrifice is valid. The Hanafi school says three days; the Shafi‘i says four.
The days in question are the 10th, 11th, 12th, and 13th of Dhul Hijjah.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafis hold that sacrifice is valid only on the 10th, 11th, and 12th. Their proof is the hadith of ‘Umar and Ibn ‘Abbas (RA): 'The days of sacrifice are three; the best of them is the first.' The author notes that this matter is not based on qiyas but on direct hearing from the Prophet ﷺ.
This hadith is considered strong (matin) and certain.
3
State the opposing (Shafi‘i) position and its proof
Imam al-Shafi‘i considers the days of tashriq (11th, 12th, 13th) as valid days of sacrifice. His evidence is another hadith that includes the 13th.
The author asserts that this hadith is weak (da‘if) and therefore not acceptable as evidence.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response to the opposing proof
The Hanafi response has two parts: (1) The opponent's hadith is weak and thus cannot outweigh a stronger hadith. (2) When evidence conflicts, the principle is to adopt the lesser number because it is certain (mutayaqqan). The three days are certain; including the 13th is doubtful.
This is a usul principle: al-akhdhu bi al-aqall 'ind al-ta‘arud.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference is clear: A Hanafi must sacrifice by the 12th; if he sacrifices on the 13th, it is not valid as udhiyah. A Shafi‘i can sacrifice on the 13th and it counts.
This highlights how madhhab differences affect actual practice.

Ruling on Sacrificing at Night
Clarifies permissibility and dislike of night slaughter and the classification of sacrifice days.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sacrificing the udhiyah at night and identify the temporal conditions for a valid sacrifice.

Core Idea
Night sacrifice is valid but disliked due to risk of error; the valid sacrifice days are 10th-12th of Dhul Hijjah.

In the Hanafi school, the udhiyah sacrifice is a highly recommended act, but its performance has specific temporal rules. This passage addresses a common concern: can one slaughter after sunset during the days of sacrifice? The text clarifies that while the act is permissible, it is disliked due to the risk of error in darkness. It also defines the exact days on which sacrifice is valid, distinguishing between the days of nahr and tashriq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is sacrificing the udhiyah at night. The ruling: ja’iz (permissible) but makruh (disliked). Note that the dislike is tanzihi (not sinful but better avoided).
Dislike is due to potential error, not inherent prohibition.
2
Understand the reason for dislike
The reason (illah) is the possibility of error in the darkness of the night – for example, cutting in the wrong place, which could invalidate the sacrifice.
This is a reason rooted in practical concern, not textual prohibition.
3
Distinguish the days of nahr and tashriq
The 10th is only a day of sacrifice (yawm nahr). The 13th is only a day of tashriq. The 11th and 12th are both: sacrifice is valid, and takbirat tashriq are recited.
Sacrifice is valid only on the first three days; the fourth day (13th) is for tashriq only.
4
Determine valid times for sacrifice
Sacrifice is valid during the day and night of the 10th, 11th, and 12th. Night slaughter is included but disliked.
The best practice is to slaughter during the daytime.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement on sacrificing at night. It states that slaughtering the udhiyah during the nights of the days of sacrifice (i.e., the nights of the 10th, 11th, and 12th) is permissible (ja’iz) but disliked (makruh tanzihan) because in the darkness there is risk of error in the slaughter (e.g., cutting in the wrong place). The passage then explains the classification of days: the 10th is purely a day of sacrifice (yawm nahr), the 13th is purely a day of tashriq (yawm tashriq), and the 11th and 12th are both: they are days of sacrifice (because slaughter is valid) and also days of tashriq (because the takbirat are recited). So four days in total are connected: 10 (only nahr), 11 (nahr and tashriq), 12 (nahr and tashriq), 13 (only tashriq). Sacrifice is valid only on the first three, but slaughtering at night of the 10th, 11th, and 12th is permissible yet disliked.

Gold and Silver Plating on Vessels: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute regarding gold/silver accessories on vessels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding gold/silver accessories on vessels and apply the principle of tabi' (accessory) to determine the ruling when accessories are detachable versus non-detachable.

Core Idea
When gold/silver is detachable from a vessel, Sahibayn rule it makruh; Abu Hanifa rules it permissible because accessories (tawabi') are not given independent consideration.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) concerning the use of vessels that have gold or silver accessories. The dispute hinges on whether the accessory can be detached and whether it should be treated as part of the vessel or as a subordinate element. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi principle that accessories follow the ruling of the main object and are not independently considered.

Guided Path
1
Identify common ground
Recognize that all scholars agree non-detachable gold/silver plating (tamwiyah la yukhlis) on vessels is permissible without any dislike. This consensus establishes a baseline.
Consensus (ijma') is a strong dalil in Hanafi usul.
2
Locate the point of ikhtilaf
Note that the dispute (ikhtilaf) only arises when the gold or silver accessory can be separated (yukhlis) from the vessel. This is the exclusive context of the disagreement.
The Arabic word 'yukhlis' indicates detachability.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn argue that using a part of a vessel is equivalent to using all its parts, based on the principle that the ruling of the whole applies to its parts. Therefore, using a vessel with a detachable gold/silver part is like using the vessel's own gold/silver, which is disliked.
Their dalil is a qiyas (analogy) based on the idea of part-whole uniformity.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Imam Abu Hanifa counters that the gold/silver accessory is a subordinate part (tabi') that follows the main vessel. The legal principle 'accessories are not given independent consideration' (la ibrata lil-tawabi) means the accessory does not change the ruling of the main object. He supports this with analogies: a cloak hemmed with silk, embroidery on a garment, and a gold nail in a ring bezel.
This is also a qiyas, but based on the subordinate nature of the accessory.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: according to the Sahibayn, using such a vessel is makruh (disliked); according to Abu Hanifa, it is not makruh. The mufta bihi position is not specified in this passage, but later Hanafi fatwa often favors Abu Hanifa's view.
Thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of disagreement) shows how the difference impacts daily practice.

Commentary

Attending a Walima Where There is Singing or Frivolous Entertainment
Ruling with proof and leader distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on attending a walima with prohibited entertainment and apply the distinction between leaders and non-leaders.

Core Idea
Accepting a walima invitation is a sunnah that overrides the presence of sin, unless you are a leader who must leave to protect the religion.

This lesson addresses a common dilemma: you are invited to a wedding feast (walima) but find that singing, music, or frivolous play is taking place. Should you stay or leave? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling that balances the sunnah of accepting invitations (ijabat al-da'wah) against the prohibition of participating in sin. It also draws an important distinction between ordinary attendees and religious leaders (muqtada), whose presence carries greater communal responsibility. We will examine the textual proofs and the reasoning behind these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is attending a walima invitation where prohibited amusement (lahw wa la'b) is present. Determine the default ruling for a non-leader.
The text states 'no harm in sitting and eating' (la ba'sa) for a non-leader.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary dalil is the hadith: 'Whoever does not accept the invitation has disobeyed Abul Qasim.' This makes accepting a sunnah, and the presence of sin from others does not nullify it.
The analogy to funeral prayer (salat al-janazah) performed despite wailing is also used.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'asā (disobeyed) in the hadith indicates strong emphasis. The Hanafis interpret it as sunnah mu'akkadah, not wajib, due to other evidences. The proof shows that the duty to accept overrides the sin of others.
The author uses the phrase 'sunnah' explicitly.
4
Note the ikhtilaf: leader vs. non-leader
A leader (muqtada) must leave if he cannot stop the sin, because his presence would disgrace the religion and encourage others. Abu Hanifa's own incident occurred before he became a leader.
The commentary explains the difference as 'shayn al-din wa fathi bab al-ma'siyah'.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi position is that non-leaders may stay; leaders must leave if they cannot prevent the sin. This balances the sunnah of invitation with the need to avoid public scandal.
The authority is from al-Jami' al-Saghir (Zahir al-Riwayah) and the Ashraf al-Hidayah commentary.

Commentary
This passage provides the Hanafi ruling on attending a walima (wedding feast) or any meal where prohibited entertainment such as singing, playing, and frivolous activities (lahw wa la'b) are present. The core ruling from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir is that it is permissible to sit and eat despite the sin, because the obligation to accept a valid invitation (which is sunnah mu'akkadah based on the hadith 'Whoever does not accept the invitation has disobeyed me') takes precedence. The presence of sin by others does not nullify one's own duty to accept. Abu Hanifa himself once encountered this and chose to sit and eat. The analogy is made to funeral prayer (salat al-janazah): it must be performed even if there is wailing (niyahah) by some attendees. However, a crucial distinction is made for a person who is a muktada (a religious leader or role model). If such a person attends and cannot stop the sin, he must leave and not sit, because his presence would bring disgrace to the religion and open the door to sin for others (shayn al-din wa fathi bab al-ma'siyah). Abu Hanifa's incident occurred before he became a prominent leader. Additionally, if the forbidden activities occur on the dining cloth itself (e.g., on the table), even a non-leader should not sit, based on the Quranic verse 'Do not sit with the unjust people after the reminder' (Al-An'am: 68). If one knows beforehand that the gathering will involve sin, he should not attend at all, since the right of the invitation does not become binding when sin is known in advance. However, if he arrives unexpectedly and the sin occurs, the right of invitation becomes binding. The passage concludes by affirming that all forms of amusement (malahi) are haram (forbidden) in the Hanafi school, including singing accompanied by stick-beating (al-taghanna bi-darb al-qadib).

In this lesson, we examine the ruling on wearing silk garments, a key topic in the chapter of clothing (kitab al-libas). Al-Quduri states clearly the difference between men and women, supported by explicit hadith. The prohibition for men is stern, linking silk to those who have no share in the Hereafter, while women are explicitly permitted. Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Silk Cushions and Their Use
Intra-Hanafi dispute on silk cushions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on using silk cushions, explain the key evidence for each, and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Silk cushions: Abu Hanifa permits, Sahibayn prohibit — the dispute turns on whether the general silk prohibition covers all uses or only wearing.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the use of silk cushions. While Abu Hanifa considered them permissible based on prophetic practice and analogy to small amounts of silk in clothing, the Sahibayn declared them disliked due to general prohibitions and resemblance to non-Muslim luxury. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the nuanced approach of the Hanafi school to silk.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two positions
The act in question is using a silk cushion (mirfaqah) for resting or sleeping on it. Abu Hanifa states 'la ba'sa' (no harm, permissible), while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it is makruh (disliked).
The dispute also covers hanging silk curtains or door covers.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's evidence
Abu Hanifa relies on a hadith that the Prophet (peace be upon him) sat on a silk cushion, and that Ibn 'Abbas had a silk cushion. He also uses qiyas: just as small amounts of silk in clothing (e.g., embroidery of three fingers width) are permissible, similarly using a small silk cushion is permissible because both are minimal and serve as a sample.
The common factor (jami') is that both are small amounts and not full garments.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's evidence
The Sahibayn cite general prohibitions (umum al-adillah) on silk for men, and argue that using silk cushions is part of the lifestyle of Persian kings (akasira) and tyrants (jababira), and imitating them is haram. They also quote 'Umar's warning: 'Avoid the appearance of the non-Arabs.'
They consider the general prohibition to apply to all forms of silk use, not just wearing.
4
Analyze the response and thamarah al-khilaf
Abu Hanifa restricts the general prohibitions to wearing silk only, distinguishing between wearing and using. The practical difference: according to Abu Hanifa, a man may use silk cushions without any dislike; according to the Sahibayn, it is makruh and one should avoid it.
The thamarah al-khilaf is clear: permissibility versus disliked status.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi scholars (e.g., al-Haskafi, Ibn 'Abidin) have favored the view of the Sahibayn, making the use of silk cushions makruh for men. However, Abu Hanifa's view is also well-known and may be followed in cases of need.
The mufta bihi is that such use is makruh tanzihi.

Only silver rings are permissible for men; all other materials (stone, iron, brass) are haram.

This passage from al-Jami' al-Saghir establishes a clear ruling: men may only wear rings made of silver. The Prophet's explicit criticism of brass and iron rings provides the evidence. Gold Rings Forbidden for Men: Ruling and Proof
Ruling on gold rings for men with evidence and technical clarification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gold rings for men, identify the proof from hadith, and apply the distinction between the ring's band (halqah) and setting (fass) to determine permissibility.

Core Idea
A gold ring is haram for men; the band determines the ruling, not the setting.

This lesson addresses a common question about the permissibility of gold rings for men. We will examine the explicit prohibition from the Prophet ﷺ, the underlying legal principle that gold is originally prohibited, and how necessity can justify permissibility only when fulfilled by a lesser metal like silver. Understanding the distinction between the band and the setting is crucial for applying this ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wearing a gold signet ring (al-takhattum bil-dhahab) for men. The ruling is haram (prohibited).
This is the clear position of the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is the hadith from Ali (radiallahu anhu) that the Prophet ﷺ prohibited wearing gold rings. The Arabic phrase 'nahā 'an al-takhattum bil-dhahab' is cited.
The prohibition (nahy) is taken as evidence for tahrim.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The original rule for gold is prohibition. Permissibility is granted only for a genuine need (e.g., sealing or a sample). However, that need is fulfilled by silver, a permissible metal. Thus, gold remains prohibited.
This is based on the principle al-asl fi al-dhahab al-tahrim.
4
Apply the technical detail: band vs setting
The band (halqah) is the legally significant part because the ring's integrity depends on it. The setting (fass) is not considered legally. Therefore, a silver band with a gold setting is permissible; a gold band is haram regardless of the setting.
The setting is legally irrelevant; its material does not affect the ruling.
5
Note the exception for women
Women are exempt from this prohibition because gold rings are considered adornment (zinah) for them, and thus permissible.
This aligns with the general permissibility of gold for women.

Wearing silk and gold for boys

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dressing male children in silk and gold and explain the qiyas (analogy) proof from the prohibition of wine.

Core Idea
Dressing a boy in silk/gold is prohibited because enabling a haram act is itself haram.

This lesson examines a brief but powerful ruling from Al-Quduri: it is makruh tahrimi to dress young boys in silk or gold. The commentator expands the ruling using a striking analogy to wine, showing that the prohibition extends from the act itself to the act of enabling it. By the end, you will understand how qiyas operates in Hanafi fiqh to derive rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is dressing male children in gold or silk. The ruling stated is 'makruh' (disliked), which is interpreted as prohibitively disliked (makruh tahrimi) in this context.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to the prohibition of wine. The text explicitly draws the parallel: just as drinking wine is haram, giving it to others to drink is also haram.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The analogy works by extending the ruling from the original act (wearing gold/silk for men = haram) to the act of dressing others in the same items. The effective cause (illa) is the prohibition of the object itself for males.
4
Note the illa
The illa is the prohibition of gold and silk for males. Since the object is haram for the wearer, enabling the wearing is also prohibited.
5
Evaluate the ruling
The ruling is makruh tahrimi, not absolute haram, because the child is not legally accountable. The sin falls on the guardian who enables the prohibited act.

The Gradation of 'Awrah: Knee, Thigh, Private Part
Explains the ruling on 'awrah gradation with hadith and rational proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the gradation of 'awrah for men according to the Hanafi school and explain the evidence from hadith and rational proof.

Core Idea
The knee, thigh, and private part are all 'awrah, but each carries a different level of sin and punishment.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on the gradation of 'awrah for men. Based on hadith and rational reasoning, the school distinguishes between different parts, with the knee being the lightest and the private part the most severe. Understanding this gradation is key to applying the correct level of admonishment or punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The ruling that each of these body parts is 'awrah (must be covered). Identify the three parts: knee, thigh, and private part.
The hukm is haram to uncover, but the severity varies.
2
Locate the textual evidence (hadith)
What proof does the author cite? The hadith of Abu Hurairah stating the knee is 'awrah, and the hadith of Jarhad stating the thigh is 'awrah. Also, the action of Abu Hurairah regarding the navel shows it is not 'awrah.
These are ahad hadith, zanni in transmission and meaning.
3
Analyze the rational proof
Why is the knee 'awrah despite containing the shin bone which is not 'awrah? Because the prohibited (thigh bone) and permissible (shin bone) combine, and the prohibited takes precedence. This is a form of istihsan or ghalabah reasoning.
This rational proof strengthens the necessity of covering the knee.
4
Determine the practical consequence (punishment)
What is the punishment for uncovering each part? The knee: gentle admonishment (inkar bi rifq). The thigh: strong rebuke (ta'nif). The private part: disciplinary beating (ta'dib) if persistence.
The gradation in punishment reflects the gradation in sin.
5
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Do other schools agree? The Shafi'i and Maliki schools consider the thigh as 'awrah but differ on details; some may not consider the knee as 'awrah. The Hanafi position is based on the hadith and rational proof.
The practical thamarah is in how strictly covering is enforced.

Core Idea
A woman may look at a man's non-awrah parts if safe from desire; desire prohibits.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar provides a clear ruling on a woman's gaze at a non-mahram man. It establishes that the permission is not absolute but conditioned on the absence of desire. Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a woman looking at a non-mahram man. The ruling is 'ja'iz' (permissible) as stated by al-Quduri.

Safety from desire includes both the absence of current desire and the confidence that desire will not arise.

This passage from Ashraf ul Hadaya presents a nuanced ruling regarding looking at a non-mahram member of the opposite gender. The proof is based on the nature of desire: women are overcome by desire (shahwa) such that it is legally considered always present in them. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Same gender and general absence of desire permit a woman to see another woman as a man sees a man.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common practical question: how much of another woman's body may a woman look at? The Hanafi school has a dispute between the relied-upon position (the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and a stricter view attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa himself. We will examine the proofs for each and see which one is preferred for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
State the ruling from al-Quduri: a woman may look at another woman to the extent a man may look at another man. Note the reasoning: same gender, absence of desire, and necessity (e.g., baths).

Note the exception in Abu Hanifa's view: women may see a man's belly/back, which is inconsistent with a strict mahram analogy.
4
Identify the tarjih
The text states 'al-awwal asah' – the first opinion is more correct. This is the mufta bihi position for fatwa.
The tarjih is based on the strength of the analogy and practical necessity.
5
State the thamarah al-khilaf
Under the preferred view, women may see each other's back and belly. Under Abu Hanifa's view, such looking is not permissible.

The Hanafi Ruling on Touching a Mahram
Ruling on touching mahram with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on touching a mahram, explain the condition of absence of desire, and identify the hadith proof.

Core Idea
Touching a mahram is permissible unless desire is feared; the hadith warns of the 'zina of the hands'.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on physical contact with mahram relatives – those whom one cannot marry. The key is intention and context: normally permissible due to need and lower temptation, but if desire arises, it becomes forbidden. We will also see the hadith cited as proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is touching a mahram on a body part that is permissible to look at. According to al-Quduri, the default ruling is 'la ba'sa' – there is no harm, i.e., it is mubah (permissible).
This applies only when there is no fear of desire (shahwa).
2
Note the condition that changes the ruling
If there is fear of desire – either on the part of the man or the woman – then looking and touching become haram. The text states: 'except when he fears desire… then he should not look and not touch'.
The presence of desire nullifies the permissibility.
3
Contrast with a non-mahram (ajnabiyya)
For a non-mahram woman, looking at the face and hands is permissible (in the Hanafi school), but touching is not, because desire is assumed to be complete. This shows that the permissibility for mahrams is exceptional, not general.
This contrast highlights the reasoning: mahram relationship reduces likelihood of desire.
4
Apply the hadith proof
The hadith 'The two eyes commit zina… the two hands commit zina…' is cited to show that looking and touching can be means to unlawful acts. Since zina with mahrams is especially grave, one must avoid even the means when desire is present.
The hadith is authentic (Bukhari and Muslim). It is used here to justify prohibition in case of desire, not to legislate a general prohibition.

Male Slave and Female Owner: Hijab Rules
Presents Hanafi ruling with rebuttal of Malik and Shafi'i opinions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a male slave looking at his female owner, explain the school’s dalil, and rebut the opposing school’s arguments.

Core Idea
A male slave is not a mahram — he may see only the face and hands of his owner, like any stranger.

This lesson addresses a specific inter-madhhab dispute: may a male slave see his female owner beyond what a stranger may see? The Hanafi school takes a strict position, limiting the slave's gaze to the face and hands. We will examine the proofs from both sides and understand why the Hanafi interpretation prevails.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling from the text: a male slave may only see of his female owner what a stranger (ajnabi) may see, i.e., the face and hands. The slave is not a mahram because marriage is potentially possible if she frees him.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Note that Imam Malik and one opinion of Imam al-Shafi'i consider the slave like a mahram, allowing her to appear before him as before a mahram relative.
The opposing school cites necessity and the Qur'anic phrase 'or what their right hands possess'.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the two arguments: (1) the general wording of Surah Nur:31 includes male slaves, (2) there is a confirmed need (hajah) for the slave to enter upon his owner without permission for service.
The opponent sees the necessity as strong enough to override the default prohibition of looking at non-mahram men.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: (a) the need is limited (hajat qasirah) because the slave works outside the house, so entry is not essential; (b) the verse is interpreted by early scholars (Said ibn Jubayr, Hasan al-Basri) as referring only to female slaves (takhsis al-amm).
The Hanafi school gives priority to the general prohibition of looking at non-mahram men over this ambiguous exception.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: In the Hanafi school, a female owner must observe hijab from her male slave just as from any other non-mahram man. According to Malik/Shafi'i, she may be uncovered before him as before a mahram.
This affects daily interactions in households with slaves.

The Hanafi Dispute on Men Kissing and Embracing
Intra-Hanafi dispute on male kissing/embracing with reconciliation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on men kissing/embracing, identify the evidence for each, and apply the mashayikh's reconciliation to determine the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Kissing/embracing men is makruh in an izar alone, but permissible by consensus with a shirt.

The Hanafi school has an internal difference on whether men may kiss or embrace each other. Imam Muhammad declares it makruh, while Abu Yusuf permits it based on the Prophet's action. The later mashayikh reconcile these views by restricting the disagreement to a specific context: when both men wear only a lower garment. This lesson unpacks the evidence, the reconciliation, and the operative ruling for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the iKhtilaf
Clearly state the two opposing views within the Hanafi school: one side (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) says makruh; the other (Abu Yusuf) says permissible.
Note that this is an intra-Hanafi dispute, not between madhhabs.
2
Examine each side's dalil
Abu Yusuf relies on the hadith of the Prophet embracing Ja'far and kissing him between the eyes. The other side relies on a hadith prohibiting al-mukama'ah (embrace) and al-muka'amah (kissing).
Both are ahad hadith, so the proof is zanni.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to opponent's proof
The response of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is that Abu Yusuf's hadith is abrogated (mansukh) because it occurred before the prohibition.
This response uses the principle of al-mansukh to resolve apparent conflict.
4
Understand the mashayikh's reconciliation
The later mashayikh (including the author of al-Hidayah) restrict the difference to when both men are wearing only a single lower garment (izar). If a shirt or cloak is worn, there is consensus on permissibility.
This limits the practical scope of the dispute significantly.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that kissing/embracing is permissible when a shirt or cloak is worn. In the case of only an izar, the more cautious view (makruh) is preferred.
This is affirmed by al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah.

The reason is that the rules of looking and touching (nazar and mass) for non-mahrams regarding slave women are the same as for mahrams, as established in an earlier discussion. Even though a umm walad cannot be sold, she remains a slave with ownership, so the same ruling applies. The commentary confirms this by referring back to the previous detail. The colophon marks the end of the first volume of commentary on the fourth volume of Al-Hidayah.

Key Terms
أمة (ama): A female slave owned by another person.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who gives birth to her master's child; she cannot be sold and becomes free upon her master's death.
محرم (mahram): A person permanently prohibited from marrying due to blood, marriage, or fosterage.
أجانب (ajnab): Non-mahram persons (those who are not prohibited to marry).
