The Ruling and Conditions of Slaughter in the Hanafi School
Commentary defining slaughter as a condition with proof and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of slaughter (ikhtiyari and idtirari), identify the conditions of a valid slaughterer, and explain the proof that dhakat is a condition for halal meat.

Core Idea
Slaughter (dhakat) is a condition for halal and pure meat; it has optional and emergency types.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that slaughter (dhabh) is a condition for meat to be permissible and pure. We explore the Quranic proof, the distinction between optional and emergency slaughter, and the specific conditions the slaughterer must meet according to the Hidayah commentary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act of slaughter (dhabh) is not a fard or wajib per se but a condition (shart) for the permissibility and purity of meat. The commentary explicitly states: 'al-dhakatu shartun hill al-dhabihah'.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic proof is the verse 'illā mā dhakkaytum' (except what you slaughter). Additionally, the principle that slaughter separates impure blood from pure meat is cited, and a hadith-like saying of Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyyah: 'the purification of the earth is its dryness'.
The hadith is noted as not being a prophetic saying, but rather a statement of a tabi'i.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Quranic exception indicates that only animals properly slaughtered are excluded from the general prohibition of carrion. The separation of blood and flesh by slaughter demonstrates both permissibility and purification.
The proof establishes dhakat as a necessary condition, not just a sunnah.
4
Distinguish the two types of slaughter
Optional (ikhtiyari) is cutting the throat between the chest and jaws. Emergency (idtirari) is any wound in the body when optional is impossible. The second is a substitute for the first, based on obligation according to capacity.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school; other schools may not differentiate.
5
Identify the conditions of the slaughterer
The slaughterer must be from the people of tawhid (Muslim by belief or Kitabi by claim), must be in a state of halal (not ihram), must be outside the Haram for game, and must understand and intend the tasmiyah while controlling the slaughter. These conditions are based on the text and analogy.
Children, insane persons, and women who can control the slaughter are included.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary on the Book of Slaughter in Ashraf al-Hidaya. It begins by stating that slaughter (dhabh) is a necessary condition for meat to be halal (permissible) and tahir (pure). The proof is the Quranic verse 'illā mā dhakkaytum' (except what you slaughter) (Quran 5:3). The commentator explains that slaughter removes the impure blood from the pure meat, thus establishing both permissibility and purity. Even the meat of a lion, though not halal, becomes pure through slaughter. The hadith 'The purification of the earth is its dryness' is cited but noted by al-Ayni to be the saying of Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyyah, not the Prophet. The passage then divides slaughter into two types: ikhtiyari (optional) and idtirari (emergency). Optional slaughter is the standard method of cutting the throat in the area between the chest (labba) and the jaws (lahyayn). Emergency slaughter applies when the animal is uncontrollable (e.g., wounded by an arrow) and can be done by making a wound in any part of the body. The emergency type is a substitute for the optional type, as it is less effective in blood removal but permissible when the optional is impossible due to capacity-based obligation. Next, conditions of the slaughterer are listed: he must be a follower of the religion of tawhid (monotheism) — either a Muslim (by belief) or a Kitabi (by claim, i.e., Jew or Christian). He must also be in a state of halal (not in ihram) and outside the Haram (sanctuary). The slaughter of a muhrim (person in ihram) is invalid only if he slaughters game; otherwise it is valid. Finally, the slaughter of a Muslim and Kitabi is halal, provided the slaughterer understands the tasmiyah (saying 'Bismillah') and can control the slaughter. This includes if the slaughterer is a child, insane person, or woman, as long as they understand. If they cannot control or understand, the slaughter is invalid. The uncircumcised and circumcised are equal. The text begins to discuss that the term 'Kitabi' includes dhimmi, harbi, Arab Christian, and Taghlibi, but the passage cuts off.

Key Terms
ذكاة (dhakāt): Linguistically means purity; legally means slaughter (dhabh) that makes meat permissible and pure.
ذبيحة (dhabīḥah): The animal that is slaughtered; the subject of the act of dhabh.
اختياري (ikhtiyārī): Optional slaughter; the standard method by cutting the throat between the chest and jaws.
اضطراري (iḍṭirārī): Emergency slaughter; any wound in the body when optional slaughter is impossible.
تسمية (tasmiyah): Saying 'Bismillah' (in the name of Allah) at the time of slaughter.

Conditions for Valid Slaughter: The Qualified Slaughterer
Conditions for valid slaughter – qualified slaughterer and tasmiyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid slaughterer in the Hanafi school and apply Quran 5:5 to determine the permissibility of meat.

Core Idea
Only a Muslim or Kitabi who understands tasmiyah and controls the slaughter may produce halal meat.

This passage from the Hidayah covers the indispensable conditions for a valid slaughterer. The school affirms that the religion of the slaughterer must be tawhid, whether by belief (Muslim) or claim (Kitabi). Additionally, the slaughterer must have the capacity to pronounce the tasmiyah and control the act. Notably, the school includes children, women, the insane, and the uncircumcised, provided they meet these criteria. Understanding these rules is essential for ensuring that meat consumed is halal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is slaughter (dhabh) of an animal. The ruling concerns whether the meat is halal or haram based on the slaughterer and method.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The key evidence is Quran 5:5, which declares the food of the People of the Book lawful for Muslims.
The verse is general, and the Hanafis apply it to all types of meat.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'ta'am' (food) includes meat from slaughtered animals, and 'hill' indicates permissibility. This is a clear, general command.
There is no restriction to specific methods or persons beyond being People of the Book.
4
List the conditions of the slaughterer
The slaughterer must be Muslim or Kitabi, understand tasmiyah, and be in control (ya'qil al-dhabh). Additional conditions apply for game: not in ihram and outside the Haram.
These conditions are derived from the text and from the requirement that tasmiyah be performed with intention.
5
Note the scope of 'Kitabi'
The term includes any person who claims to follow a revealed scripture: dhimmi, harbi, Arab, Taghlibi, and all branches of Jews and Christians.
The condition is merely the existence of the millah (religious community) of tawhid, not the authenticity of their scripture.

Commentary
This passage is a sharh (commentary) on the conditions for valid slaughter in Hanafi fiqh. It opens with a discussion of the two types of dhakah: ikhtiyari (voluntary, i.e., normal throat-slaughter) and idtirari (emergency, when the animal is uncontrollable). The key ruling is that the slaughterer must be a person of tawhid, either Muslim (by belief) or Kitabi (by claim). Additional conditions: the slaughterer must be halal (non-muhrim) and outside the Haram; if muhrim or inside Haram, the slaughter of game is invalid. The text then clarifies that the Muslim/Kitabi requirement applies to any slaughterer who understands tasmiyah and is in control of the slaughter, regardless of gender, age, or mental state. Even a child, a woman, or an insane person can slaughter if they fulfill these conditions. Uncircumcised (aqlaf) and circumcised are equal. The term 'Kitabi' is inclusive: dhimmi, harbi, Arab, Taghlibi—anyone who belongs to the religion of the Book. The proof is Quran 5:5 and the general principle that the condition is the existence of the millah (religious community) of tawhid.

Key Terms
ذبيحة (dhabihah): An animal that has been slaughtered in the prescribed manner.
تسمية (tasmiyah): Pronouncing the name of Allah (Bismillah) at the time of slaughter.
كتابي (kitabi): A person who follows a revealed scripture, specifically Jews and Christians, including all branches (dhimmi, harbi, Arab, Taghlibi).
اقلف (aqlaf): Uncircumcised male.
مجنون (majnun): A person who is insane, lacking legal capacity.
محرم (muhrim): A person in the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah.

Ruling on Slaughter of Majusi (Zoroastrian)
Prohibition of eating Majusi slaughtered meat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on consuming meat slaughtered by a Majusi and explain the basis in the hadith and the absence of tawhid.

Core Idea
The slaughter of a Majusi is haram because they do not claim tawhid, as per explicit hadith.

This lesson addresses the permissibility of meat from an animal slaughtered by a Majusi (Zoroastrian). You will learn the clear Hanafi ruling of prohibition and the two proofs: the Prophetic hadith that distinguishes Majusis from People of the Book in matters of marriage and slaughter, and the underlying rationale that Majusis lack belief in tawhid. This ruling has practical implications for Muslims interacting with Zoroastrian communities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act is being discussed? Slaughter (dhabiha) performed by a Majusi. What is the hukm? It is haram to eat.
The text begins with 'No eating of the Majusi's slaughter'.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is a hadith: 'Treat them like the People of the Book, except that you do not marry their women nor eat their slaughtered animals.'
This hadith is a Sunnah ahad (zanni al-thubut) but is accepted by the Hanafi school.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'do not eat their slaughtered animals' is explicit in prohibiting consumption. The exception shows that Majusis are not entitled to the same treatment as Kitabis regarding food.
The prohibition is unequivocal (zanni al-dalala but sufficient for the ruling).
4
Note the underlying rationale
The text adds: 'because he does not claim tawhid.' This explains why the exception applies: Majusis lack belief in the oneness of God, which is the basis for making slaughter permissible.
Tawhid is considered the essential hallmark of a revealed religion; its absence negates the validity of slaughter.

The Required Number of Veins for Halal Slaughter
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the required number of veins for halal slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the required number of veins for halal slaughter and evaluate the proofs of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
Any three veins suffice, but the majority of each is required – a difference in applying 'majority' to the set vs. each individual.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the number of veins that must be cut during Islamic slaughter. The three principal imams – Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani – each have a distinct ruling on whether the majority must be of the total set of four veins or of each individual vein. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens one's ability to apply Hanafi usul principles, especially the maxim that 'the majority stands in place of the whole.'

Guided Path
1
State the three Hanafi positions
Identify what Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf (initial and later), and Muhammad al-Shaybani require regarding the number of veins cut. Note that Abu Hanifa says any three veins; Muhammad requires majority of each; Abu Yusuf initially agreed with Abu Hanifa, then retracted to require throat, windpipe, and one jugular.
2
Identify the opposing proofs
For each position, understand the reasoning: Abu Hanifa uses the principle 'al-akthar qa'im maqam al-kull' (majority stands for the whole) to allow any three. Abu Yusuf distinguishes between throat/windpipe (different functions, both must be cut) and jugulars (both carry blood, one suffices). Muhammad argues each vein is an independent entity, so majority of each is needed.
Muhammad's view is also reported from Abu Hanifa.
3
Analyze the Hanafi responses
See how the text presents the refutation: The requirement of cutting the majority of each vein would increase hardship and torture, whereas the purpose (blood flow and death) is achieved by cutting any three.
The response is based on avoiding unnecessary suffering (ta'dhib).
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if a person cuts two complete veins and a third partially, under Abu Hanifa's view it is halal (since three are cut), but under Muhammad's view it may not be halal unless the majority of each of the three is cut. The mufta bihi position is Abu Hanifa's (any three), as adopted in fatwa.
Some later scholars may prefer caution.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya addresses a key issue in the law of slaughter (dhaba'ih): how many of the four veins (throat, windpipe, and two jugular veins) must be cut for the animal to be halal. It begins by citing Imam Muhammad's statement in al-Jami' al-Saghir that cutting half of the throat and half of the jugulars does not make the animal halal, but cutting the majority of them before death does. The commentary then provides the positions of the three Hanafi imams. Abu Hanifa holds that cutting any three of the four veins suffices (three any veins). Abu Yusuf initially agreed but later retracted, requiring the throat, windpipe, and at least one jugular. Muhammad al-Shaybani requires cutting the majority of each individual vein, meaning each vein must have more than half cut. This is also a narration from Abu Hanifa. The text then presents the proofs: Abu Yusuf argues that the two jugulars can deputize for each other since blood flows through both, but the throat and windpipe are different channels (food vs. breath) so both must be cut. Abu Hanifa argues that the majority stands for the whole in many Islamic rulings, and cutting any three veins achieves the purposes of blood flow and hastening death while avoiding excessive torture. The passage concludes with a reference to Majma' al-Nahr and a note about al-Quduri's phrasing. The overriding principle is that the majority of the required veins must be cut, with the difference lying in how 'majority' is applied: to the set of four veins (any three) or to each individually.

Slaughter with Attached Teeth and Nails: The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Hanafi rebuttal of Shafi'i using hadith and rational analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on slaughter with attached teeth/nails, explain the dalil (hadith and analogy), and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Attached teeth/nails kill by crushing, making carrion; detached ones cut and are permissible but disliked.

This passage presents the Hanafi position on slaughter tools, specifically addressing the status of teeth and nails. The Hanafis distinguish between attached and detached forms, while Imam al-Shafi'i holds a more permissive view. Understanding this dispute requires analyzing the primary texts and the rational analogy that underpins the Hanafi ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling
State the two-part ruling: attached teeth/nails render the animal carrion (haram); detached teeth/nails are permissible but disliked.
The key distinction is whether the tool is attached to the body or not.
2
Examine the Hanafi dalil
Two proofs are given: (1) the hadith 'Cause the blood to flow with whatever you wish' which the Hanafis interpret as referring to detached tools; (2) the analogy that attached tools kill by weight, like the strangled animal (al-munkhaniqah), which is explicitly prohibited in the Quran.
The Hanafis specify the general hadith using the context of the Abyssinians.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i position and its dalil
Imam al-Shafi'i relies on the same hadith but takes its generality at face value, permitting slaughter with any tool that causes blood flow, including attached teeth/nails.
The difference lies in the method of specification (takhsis).
4
Understand the Hanafi response
The Hanafis argue that the hadith is specified to detached tools because the Abyssinians used attached ones, and the Prophet's permission was for detached. Additionally, attached tools kill by crushing, not cutting, making them analogous to prohibited methods.
The ‘illa (effective cause) is the mode of killing: cutting vs. crushing.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Hanafi law, an animal slaughtered with an attached tooth or nail is carrion (haram); under Shafi‘i law, it is halal.
This difference affects actual slaughter practice in communities.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafis and Shafi'is regarding slaughter with teeth and nails. The Hanafi position is that detached teeth/nails (like a tooth removed from the jaw or a nail cut off) are permissible tools for slaughter, but attached teeth/nails render the animal carrion (maitah). The Hanafis present the hadith 'Cause the blood to flow with whatever you wish' as evidence, but they argue that the hadith refers to detached teeth/nails. The Abyssinians used attached teeth/nails, and the Prophet's permission was for detached ones. The Hanafi rational proof: detached teeth/nails act like a sharp tool, cutting the throat and causing blood to flow, similar to stone or iron. Attached teeth/nails kill by the weight of the animal or the force of the blow, crushing the neck rather than cutting, which is analogous to strangled animals (al-munhaniqah) which are forbidden in the Quran. Furthermore, using a part of the human body (like an attached tooth or nail) is disliked because it involves disrespect to humans and causes additional pain to the animal, contrary to the command of ihsan (excellence) in slaughter.

Slaughter Tools and Imam Muhammad's Methodology
Clarifies permissible slaughter tools and Imam Muhammad's cautious methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on permissible slaughter tools and explain Imam Muhammad's methodological principle of ihtiyat (caution) when no explicit text exists.

Core Idea
Any sharp tool that causes blood flow makes slaughter halal, except attached teeth and nails — Imam Muhammad relies on explicit text and exercises caution otherwise.

The Hanafi school permits slaughter with any sharp object that causes blood flow, whether it be a bamboo splinter (al-laytah) or a flint stone (al-marwah). However, the school strictly prohibits using teeth or nails that are still attached to the body, as they kill by weight rather than cutting. This lesson examines the textual basis for these rulings and highlights Imam Muhammad's cautious methodology where no explicit text (nass) exists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and rulings
Determine the two categories: (1) slaughter with al-laytah, al-marwah, or any sharp tool is permissible; (2) slaughter with attached teeth/nails renders carrion (maitah).
The ruling on attached teeth/nails is stated as carrion (mietah), a strong prohibition.
2
Classify the tools
Understand that al-laytah (palm bark) and al-marwah (flint) are examples of any sharp tool that causes blood flow. The key condition is that the tool must cause blood flow, not necessarily be metal.
3
Understand the exception
Attached teeth and nails are excepted because they kill by the animal's own weight, analogous to strangled animals (mukhtaniqah). Imam Muhammad explicitly ruled this based on a text (nass) he found.
The exception is firmly established by nass, not mere caution.
4
Recognize Imam Muhammad's methodology
When Imam Muhammad finds an explicit text, he rules accordingly. When no text exists, he exercises caution (ihtiyat): saying 'la ba'sa bihi' for permissibility and 'makruh' or 'la yu'kal' for prohibition.
This methodology shows careful adherence to textual evidence.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school permits slaughter with attached teeth/nails, while the Hanafi school prohibits. The practical consequence is that the meat is halal for Shafi'is but carrion for Hanafis.
This difference originates from varying interpretations of the hadith on slaughter tools.

Mustahabb of Slaughter: Sharpening the Knife Before Laying the Animal Down
Lists the recommended act of sharpening the knife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sharpening the knife before slaughter and explain the evidence from the hadith of Ihsan.

Core Idea
Sharpening the knife before laying the animal down is mustahabb to minimize suffering.

In Hanafi fiqh, slaughter is a sacred act requiring excellence (ihsan). The Prophet ﷺ commanded that the knife be sharpened before the animal is laid down, as sharpening after causes fear and pain. This lesson covers the recommended act and its evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Recommended Act
The matn states: 'It is recommended (yustahabb) that the slaughterer sharpen his knife.' This is the first mustahabb mentioned.
2
Understand the Evidence
The proof is the hadith: 'Indeed Allah has prescribed excellence in everything... Let one of you sharpen his knife and give comfort to his animal.' Also, the Prophet reproached a man who sharpened after laying down the animal, saying 'You intend to kill it with multiple deaths?'
The command 'li yahid' (let him sharpen) is understood as a recommendation (nadb), not obligation (wujub), because it is about voluntary excellence.
3
Contrast the Disliked Timing
Sharpening after laying the animal down is makruh, as it increases fear. The hadith explicitly criticizes this action.
4
Conclude the Ruling
The ruling: Sharpening before laying is mustahabb; sharpening after is makruh tanzihi. The animal remains halal in both cases.
There is no sin in omitting the recommendation, but one loses reward.

Slaughtering a Goat from the Back of the Neck
Ruling on slaughtering an animal from behind the neck

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A goat slaughtered from the back is halal if it remains alive until the veins are cut, but makruh due to unnecessary pain.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on an alternative method of slaughter: cutting the animal's throat from behind rather than the front. The key question is whether the meat is halal despite the unusual method. We will see that the ruling depends entirely on whether the animal is alive at the time the veins are cut, and that the act itself is considered makruh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the main ruling
The act is slaughtering a goat from the back of the neck. The ruling: if the animal remains alive until the veins (awdaj) are cut, the meat is halal. Identify that this is the core ruling.
The ruling distinguishes between two outcomes based on timing of death.
2
Understand the conditional nature
The permissibility depends on whether the animal survives the initial wound and remains alive until the veins are cut. If it dies before, it is not halal (it becomes carrion).
This condition is rooted in the requirement that death must result from the cutting of the throat, not from the initial wound.
3
Analyze the karahah ruling
Even when halal, the act is makruh (disliked) because it causes unnecessary pain to the animal. This is a ruling of makruh tanzihi (slightly disliked), not sinful.
The karahah is independent of the halal status of the meat.

Slaughter Method for Tame and Wild Animals
General rule with dispute with Imam Malik on emergency slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between required slaughter methods (dhabh vs 'aqr) based on the animal's current nature and apply the Hanafi principle of inability to justify emergency slaughter in a dispute with Imam Malik.

Core Idea
Slaughter method depends on current nature: tame → dhabh, wild → 'aqr when unable to dhabh.

This lesson clarifies the Hanafi rule for determining the correct slaughter method when an animal's nature changes. A domesticated animal that becomes wild or falls into a well requires emergency slaughter ('aqr) because normal slaughter is impossible. In contrast, a wild animal that becomes tame must be slaughtered normally. This principle leads to a scholarly dispute with Imam Malik, who considers these cases too rare to permit an exception.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two cases: 1) a domesticated animal becomes wild → 'aqr valid; 2) a wild animal becomes tame → dhabh required. Note the underlying condition: inability to perform normal slaughter triggers emergency slaughter.
The condition is 'ajz (inability), not the animal's origin.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam Malik holds that emergency slaughter is invalid in both cases because they are rare (nadir). He argues that rulings should not be based on rare occurrences.
Malik's view preserves the general rule of dhabh.
3
Analyse the opponent's proof
Malik's evidence: rarity of the situation means the general rule (dhabh) should not be abandoned. He considers these cases exceptional and not sufficient to change the established norm.
Rarity is an empirical observation, not a legal maxim in Hanafi usul.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafis rebut: the criterion is inability, not rarity. Since inability is realized in these cases, the alternative ('aqr) is valid. Furthermore, they argue these cases are not rare but common, so the objection based on rarity fails.
Hanafi response focuses on the effective cause (illa) of inability.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi the meat is halal; under Maliki it is not. This difference affects daily practice for Muslims who follow either school.
The dispute is legitimate (mukhtalaf fih) and each school has strong evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Imam Muhammad's distinction to determine when 'aqr (emergency slaughter) is valid for different animals in different locations.

Core Idea
For cows and camels, 'aqr is always valid; for goats, only in the wilderness.

The general Hanafi ruling in al-Quduri allows 'aqr for any domesticated animal that becomes wild. However, Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani refines this based on the animal's strength and the location. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and the resulting practical differences.

Imam Muhammad distinguishes between goats and cows/camels. For goats, location matters; for cows/camels, it does not.

The validity of 'aqr depends on genuine inability to perform normal slaughter. In the city, a goat can be caught due to weakness; a cow/camel cannot because they defend themselves.

The relied-upon position is Imam Muhammad's detail, as it is more precise and precautionary.
Ashraf al-Hadaya follows this view.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute. The general ruling in al-Quduri's book is absolute: when a domesticated animal becomes wild, emergency slaughter ('aqr) is permissible. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani provides a distinction: for cows and camels, 'aqr is valid whether in wilderness or city because they are strong and dangerous, making it difficult to catch them. For a goat, however, if it bolts in the city, it can be easily caught, so there is no inability ('ajz), and thus 'aqr is not valid; it must be caught and slaughtered normally. But if the goat bolts in the wilderness, it may hide and cannot be caught, so 'aqr is valid. Also, if a person is attacked by an animal and kills it while intending slaughter, it is halal. This shows that the criterion for emergency slaughter is genuine inability to perform normal slaughter, and this varies by animal and location.

Ruling on the Fetus Found Alive – An Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Slaughter
Explains Hanafi ruling on eating fetus found alive in slaughtered mother, refuting analogy to hunting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating a fetus found alive in a slaughtered mother and explain the evidence and response to the opposing view.

Core Idea
A fetus found alive requires its own slaughter; the mother's slaughter does not suffice.

This passage examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of eating a fetus that emerges alive from its mother after her slaughter. Some scholars analogize the situation to hunting and claim that the mother's slaughter suffices. The author refutes this analogy and defends Imam Abu Hanifa's position that a separate slaughter is required, based on the lack of blood from the fetus and a metaphorical interpretation of the relevant hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue and Hanafi Ruling
Determine the specific question: Is a fetus found alive in a slaughtered mother halal without separate slaughter? State the Hanafi position: it is haram unless separately slaughtered.
2
Recognize the Opposing Analogy
Note that some scholars analogize the fetus to hunting (shikar), where a wound causing some blood exit renders the animal halal in cases of necessity. They claim the mother's slaughter similarly covers the fetus.
This analogy is the crux of the dispute.
3
Analyze the Refutation of the Analogy
Understand the refutation: in hunting, at least some blood exits, but in the fetus no blood exits at all because the mother's blood does not cause its blood to exit. Thus the analogy is invalid.
The author emphasizes that neither complete nor defective blood exit occurs.
4
Interpret the Hadith
Examine the hadith 'The slaughter of the mother is the slaughter of the fetus.' The Hanafi view interprets this metaphorically: the method of slaughter is the same, not that it renders the fetus automatically halal. Also, one slaughter cannot cover two beings.
The conflicting evidence of permissibility and prohibition leads to preferring prohibition.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi and Practical Consequence
Conclude that the relied-upon position (Imam Abu Hanifa) requires separate slaughter. If the fetus is not slaughtered, it is not permissible to eat.

Eating any predator that habitually attacks and kills prey is haram, regardless of having fangs or talons.

The Hanafi school establishes the prohibition of eating predatory animals based on a clear prophetic hadith. Analogical Reasoning (Qiyas) in the Magpie Ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the qiyas arguments used by Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf for the magpie ruling.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa analogizes magpie to chicken; Abu Yusuf analogizes it to carrion-eating birds.

The difference in ruling on magpie stems from different analogies (qiyas). This lesson breaks down the qiyas structures used by each imam, highlighting the effective cause (illa) of predominant diet. Understanding this qiyas will sharpen your ability to reason about new cases involving animals that eat both clean and impure foods.

2
Identify the Farʿ (New Case)
In both qiyas, the farʿ is the magpie (العقعق). The goal is to extend the ruling from the aṣl to the farʿ.

Derive the Hukm for the Farʿ
Apply the ruling of the aṣl to the farʿ via the shared ʿillah. Abu Hanifa concludes it is permissible (mubah). Abu Yusuf concludes it is disliked (makruh).
The strength of the qiyas depends on the accuracy of the ʿillah determination.
5
Evaluate the Objections
A potential objection: Abu Yusuf might argue that the mixed diet is not sufficient to remove the attribute of filth-eating; the majority should be considered. Abu Hanifa might counter that the resemblance to chicken is stronger because the magpie eats both.
This lesson does not provide the full rebuttal, but you can infer the debate.

The reasoning involves Quranic inference, the horse's role in jihad, and the resolution of conflicting hadiths. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi usul principles like tarjih (preference) and the classification of karahah operate in practice.

1

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute regarding the ruling on horse meat. The text presents the positions: Imam Abu Hanifa holds it to be makruh (disliked), while his disciples Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) along with Imam al-Shafi'i consider it permissible. The Sahibayn rely on the hadith of Jabir in which the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade donkey meat but permitted horse meat on the day of Khaybar. Imam Abu Hanifa's proof is from the Quranic verse (16:8) where Allah mentions horses, mules, and donkeys for riding and adornment. The commentator explains that since the context is of bestowing favor, if eating were a higher benefit, Allah would have mentioned it. Instead, He only mentions riding and adornment, implying that eating is not intended. Additionally, the horse is an instrument of jihad and terrorizing the enemy, so eating it disrespects its noble status. For this reason, a share is allocated to horses in the spoils. Furthermore, if horse meat were permitted, it would reduce the number of horses available for jihad. The hadith of Khalid ibn al-Walid reportedly prohibits horse meat, and when there is a conflict between a permissive and a prohibitive hadith, the prohibitive takes precedence. Thus, Abu Hanifa rules karahah (dislike). There is a difference of opinion on whether this karahah is tahrimiyyah (prohibitive) or tanzihiyyah (indicative). The text initially says the first (tahrimiyyah) is more correct, but later references Sukk al-Insar indicating that the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is tanzihiyyah. As for mare's milk, it is permissible because drinking it does not diminish the instrument of jihad. The passage ends with a note that more detail is in Sukk al-Insar.

Key Terms
كراهة تحريم (karahah tahrimiyyah): Dislike close to prohibition; a ruling that requires avoidance but is not as severe as haram. According to the text, this is the stronger view for horse meat.
آلة الجهاد (alah al-jihad): The instrument or tool of jihad (holy struggle). The horse is considered alah al-jihad, and its preservation is important for military strength.
مقابله الحديثين (muqabilah al-hadithayn): The conflict between two hadiths, one permitting and one prohibiting. The principle is that when there is conflict between a halal and haram indication, the haram is given preference.

Ruling on Eating Rabbit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating rabbit and identify the two proofs from the Sunnah and legal analogy that support it.

Core Idea
Rabbit is halal because the Prophet ate it and it is neither a predator nor a scavenger.

This lesson examines the permissibility of eating rabbit according to the Hanafi school. The ruling is based on clear prophetic action and a legal analogy (qiyas) that classifies the rabbit alongside deer as a permissible animal. By understanding the proofs, you will see how the Hanafi school combines textual evidence and legal reasoning.

Identify the act and its ruling

This ruling appears in the core Hanafi texts of Zahir al-Riwayah, specifically Al-Quduri and Al-Hidayah, and there is no reported disagreement within the school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on al-tafi, explain the evidence from the hadith of Jabir (RA), and evaluate the opposing Maliki/Shafi'i position.

A fish that floats dead without external cause is makruh tahrimi — specific prohibition restricts the general permission of sea carcass.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses a classic inter-madhhab dispute about al-tafi, a fish that dies naturally and floats belly-up. The Hanafi school rules it makruh tahrimi, while the Maliki and Shafi'i schools consider it halal. Understanding the evidence and the Hanafi response reveals how general and specific texts interact in fiqh.

1

2
Identify the Hanafi dalil
Locate the specific hadith of Jabir ibn Abdullah (RA) which explicitly prohibits al-tafi. Note the three categories of fish.

3
State the opposing position and its dalil

They rely on the absolute nature of the hadith about sea carcass.

5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
State the practical consequence: Hanafis avoid eating al-tafi, while Malikis and Shafi'is consider it permissible.
The difference affects dietary practice in real life.

And the deduction from 'and the carcass of the sea is permissible' is not valid, because only fish whose death is attributed to the sea is called a carcass of the sea; i.e., what the sea casts out, that is declared permissible in the hadith, and we also consider it permissible. All types of fish are permissible, this is Imam Malik's viewpoint.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the ruling on eating al-tafi, a fish that dies without an external cause and floats belly-up on the water. The Hanafi school, as stated by al-Quduri, rules it makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked). Imam Malik and Imam al-Shafi'i consider it absolutely permissible (halal). Their proof is the general hadith that the carcass of the sea (maytah al-bahr) is halal, and since al-tafi is a sea carcass, it is included. The Hanafi response relies on a specific hadith from Jabir ibn Abdullah (RA) in which the Prophet (SAW) distinguishes three categories: fish left by receding water, fish cast out by the sea, and floating fish (al-tafi). Only the first two are permissible; the third is prohibited. The Hanafis interpret 'maytah al-bahr' narrowly to mean only those fish that die due to an external cause attributable to the sea (e.g., being thrown out by waves), not fish that die naturally without any apparent cause. This is supported by a group of Companions. Thus al-tafi is excluded from the general permission. The Urdu tashreeh clarifies that all other fish, whether alive or dead from an external cause, are halal; only al-tafi is makruh.

A fish that dies by an external affliction is halal; one that dies naturally is not.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi qā'idah kulliyyah (general principle) that governs the permissibility of eating fish. Commentary
This passage states the Hanafi general principle (qā'idah kulliyyah) for fish: a fish that dies due to an external affliction (āfah), such as being caught, cut, or injured, is halal. A fish that dies naturally without an external cause (hatf anfih) is not halal; this includes the tāfī (fish that floats dead on the surface). The principle has many derivative rulings (furū'āt). The commentator gives one example: if a part of a live fish is cut off and the fish dies as a result, both the cut-off piece and the remaining fish are halal. This is because the death is caused by an affliction (the cutting). Although cutting a limb from a living animal normally renders the limb carrion and impermissible, carrion from sea creatures is halal in the Hanafi school. Regarding fish that die from extreme heat or extreme cold, there are two narrations (riwāyatān) within the school: one declaring it halal, the other not. The relied-upon position (mukhtār) is permissibility, and a fatwa to that effect is recorded in Majma' al-Anhur. This passage is from a sharh (commentary) on al-Hidayah, explaining the matn and its branches.

The Istihsan Ruling for Mutual Slaughter of Sacrificial Animals
Istihsan argument for validity of mutual sacrifice without liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of istihsan to override qiyas in cases of mutual sacrifice, and state the conditions that remove liability.

Core Idea
If two people slaughter each other's sacrificial animals, the sacrifice is valid and no compensation is owed because the animal's earmarking implies the owner's permission.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi ruling where the principle of istihsan (juristic preference) overrides the analogical reasoning (qiyas) that would typically impose liability for unauthorized use of another's property. The passage from Al-Hidayah presents both the qiyas and istihsan arguments, then clarifies the scope of the ruling for different types of owners.

The animal is earmarked (ta'ayyun) for sacrifice, so the owner is deemed to have given implicit permission (idhn dalalah) to anyone capable of slaughtering, because the sacrifice must be done within the appointed days.

A hypothetical objection: the owner misses the mustahabb of slaughtering himself or being present. The answer: two other mustahabb acts are gained — sacrificing the specific earmarked animal and expediting the wajib.
This counter-argument shows that the owner's pleasure is inferred.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with proof from the Hanafi school regarding a scenario where two people have each purchased a goat for sacrifice, and before slaughtering, someone else (in this case, the other person) slaughters it. The ruling is that the sacrifice is valid for both and no liability (daman) is incurred. The passage first gives the qiyas (analogical reasoning) argument that would impose liability, comparing it to a butcher's case. Then it presents the istihsan (juristic preference) argument that overrides qiyas. The istihsan reasoning is that because the animal has been specifically earmarked (ta'ayyun) for sacrifice, the owner is deemed to have given implicit permission (idhn dalalah) to anyone capable of slaughtering, since the sacrifice must be performed within the appointed days and the owner might be unable to do it himself. This is analogous to a butcher tying a goat's legs in preparation for slaughter, where someone else slaughtering it would not be liable. The passage then clarifies three scenarios: (1) a poor person who buys an animal with the intention of sacrifice must slaughter that specific animal; (2) a person who vows to sacrifice and buys an animal for that vow must slaughter it; (3) a wealthy person who buys an animal for sacrifice is not obliged to slaughter that specific animal, but it is makruh (disliked) to replace it. The Opposite Scenario and the Principle of Priority
Expansion of the ruling with the opposite case and a legal principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that if a report is accepted for permissibility, it is more appropriately accepted for prohibition, and identify the opposite scenario where a Magian's report leads to prohibition.

Core Idea
If a report is accepted to declare something halal, it is even more acceptable to declare it haram due to caution.

This lesson builds on the previous one by examining the opposite case: if the Magian servant says he bought the meat from a Magian, the meat is not permissible. The commentary applies a logical principle that acceptance for permissibility implies acceptance for prohibition, with greater emphasis on caution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposite scenario
The servant reports purchasing from a Magian (or non-Ahl al-Kitab). The meat is then considered from a Magian slaughter, which is not halal.
2
Extract the ruling
The text states: 'He may not eat from it' (لم يسعه أن يأكل منه). It is prohibited (haram).
3
Understand the principle
The principle is: إذا قبل قوله في الحل أولى أن يقبل في الحرمة – if accepted in permissibility, it is more appropriate to accept in prohibition. The reasoning is that caution (ihtiyat) demands more care in prohibition.
4
Note the clarification
The commentary adds that hillah and hurmah are actually religious categories (diyanat), but here they are indirectly implied through the report of the transaction. The servant is not giving a religious ruling; he is reporting a fact.

He (Muhammad) said: And whoever sends a Magian hired worker or a servant, and he buys meat and says, 'I bought it from a Jew or a Christian or a Muslim,' it is within his capacity to eat it, because the statement of a disbeliever is accepted in transactions, for it is a valid report, arising from reason and a religion in which the prohibition of lying is believed, and there is a pressing need for its acceptance due to the frequency of transactions. And if it is otherwise, he may not eat from it; meaning if it is a slaughter of a non-book (people), because when his statement is accepted in permissibility, it is even more appropriate that it be accepted in prohibition.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary discusses the ruling on meat purchased by a Magian servant. The base text is from Imam Muhammad's al-Jami' al-Saghir. The ruling: if a Magian servant buys meat and states that it was purchased from a Muslim, Jew, or Christian (Ahl al-Kitab), the owner may eat it because the word of a disbeliever is accepted in transactional matters. The reasoning is threefold: (1) the servant is rational, so his report is likely truthful; (2) he adheres to a religion (even if false) that condemns lying; (3) there is a practical need to accept such statements due to frequency. The passage distinguishes between 'transactions' (mu'amalat) and 'religious matters' (diyanat): in religious rulings (like permissibility of food), the disbeliever's word is not relied upon directly; rather, his statement is about the source (transaction), not a fatwa on permissibility. The commentary then gives the opposite case: if the servant says he bought it from a Magian, then the meat is not permissible because it would be an animal not properly slaughtered. The principle applied is that if his word is accepted to declare something permissible, it is even more accepted to declare something prohibited (due to caution). A concluding note warns that permissibility and prohibition are religious categories, but here they are indirect; the servant's report is merely about the purchase transaction.

Key Terms
أولى (awla): More appropriate, more fitting; used to indicate a priority principle.
احتياط (ihtiyat): Caution, precaution; a principle in Hanafi fiqh to avoid sin.
ذبيحة (dhabihah): Slaughtered animal; the meat resulting from slaughter.
