Forced Division of Shared Utility Properties is Prohibited
Ruling statement from al-Quduri on division of shared utility properties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on forced division of shared utility properties and identify the condition under which division is permitted.

Core Idea
Shared utility properties cannot be divided by force—only mutual consent makes division valid.

This lesson addresses a classic Hanafi ruling from al-Quduri's matn: certain properties like bathhouses, wells, mills, and common walls are indivisible without unanimous consent. The reason is that forced division would render each share useless, causing harm. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi principle of preventing harm (darar) in property law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ruling
Read the first part of the passage: 'A bathhouse, a well, and a mill are not divided…' Identify which properties are mentioned and what the default ruling is regarding their division.
The ruling is a prohibition on division (by a judge) without consent.
2
Locate the condition for permissibility
Find the exception: 'unless the partners mutually consent.' Note that the prohibition is only for forced division; consent allows division.
This exception is crucial—it shows the ruling is not absolute.
3
Understand the legal rationale (illah)
The text explains why: 'because it involves harm to both sides, since each share will not remain capable of being used in the intended manner.' Identify the illah: darar (harm) resulting from loss of function.
The Hanafi school uses the maxim 'لا ضرر ولا ضرار' (no harm) to support this.
4
Apply the ruling to analogous cases
The passage includes a similar example: the common wall (ha'it) between two houses. Think of other properties that would be rendered useless if divided—such as a staircase, a narrow pathway, or a single water tap.
The key is indivisibility in terms of function.
5
Summarize the fiqh position
Conclude: The judge cannot force division of such properties. Only mutual consent makes division permissible. This is the mufta bihi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is reported here.

Commentary
This passage presents a clear ruling from Imam al-Quduri's matn, which is a foundational text of the Hanafi school. The ruling is that certain types of property—specifically a bathhouse (hammam), a well (bir), a mill (raha), and a common wall between two houses—cannot be divided by a judge without the unanimous consent of the partners. The reason is that such properties are indivisible by nature: after division, each portion would become useless for its intended purpose. A bathhouse requires a certain size and facilities to function; a well needs a certain depth and water source; a mill requires the mechanism; a wall loses its function if split. Therefore, forced division would cause harm (darar) to all partners. The judge is thus prohibited from forcing division. However, if all partners agree to divide anyway (perhaps they plan to convert the property to other uses), they may do so voluntarily. This ruling illustrates the Hanafi principle of preventing harm (la darar wa la dirar) in property division.

Key Terms
حمام (hammam): A public or private bathhouse, which requires a specific structure to function.
بیر (bir): A well, which is an indivisible source of water.
رحی (raha): A mill (usually water or wind powered), which is a functional unit.
ضرر (darar): Harm or detriment that would result from forced division, serving as the legal basis for prohibition.

Division of Shared Houses in a City: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on dividing multiple shared houses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between qismat fard and qismat jam', and state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn regarding the division of multiple shared houses in a city, including the extension to scattered lands.

Core Idea
For shared houses in one city, Abu Hanifa insists on separate division; Sahibayn allow mixing if beneficial, delegating to the judge's discretion.

When partners jointly own multiple houses in the same city, how should the judge divide them? Should each house be handled independently, or can they all be combined into a single pool and redistributed? In this passage, the Hanafi school witnesses an internal difference: Imam Abu Hanifa requires separate division, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, grant the judge flexibility to mix houses if it serves the partners' interests. Understanding this ikhtilaf reveals the Hanafi approach to balancing individual property rights with practical convenience.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
State the legal question: In a city with multiple jointly owned houses, must each house be divided separately (qismat fard) or can the judge combine all houses and then divide (qismat jam')?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa: Each house must be divided separately, so each partner receives a fractional share in every house. This respects the distinctness of each property.
The text says: 'كل دار على حدتها' – each house separately.
3
State Sahibayn's position
Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): The judge may mix the houses if it is more beneficial. Their reasoning: all houses are one genus as residences (jins wahid isman wa suratan), though their specific purposes vary; therefore, the judge has discretion.
The text says 'ان كان الأصلح لهم قسمة بعضها في بعض' – if mixing is more beneficial.
4
Apply to scattered lands
The same difference of opinion extends to scattered shared agricultural lands. This shows the principle is general, not limited to houses.
The text says 'وعلى هذا الخلاف الاقرحة المتفرقة المشتركة' – same ikhtilaf for lands.
5
Analyze the practical difference
Under Abu Hanifa, co-ownership of each house persists; partners cannot get a whole house without later exchange. Under Sahibayn, the judge can allocate whole houses to individuals, ending co-ownership more efficiently.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of the difference).

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the method of dividing multiple jointly owned houses located in the same city. The question arises: when partners share several houses, should each house be divided separately (qismat fard), or should all the houses be pooled together and then divided as a single block (qismat jam')? Imam Abu Hanifa opines that each house must be divided separately (each house is a separate unit). Therefore, the judge gives each partner a share in each house. Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that the judge has discretion: if combining all the houses and then dividing them into parcels (mixing shares from different houses) yields a more beneficial outcome for the partners (e.g., one partner might prefer a larger house in one location, another a smaller house elsewhere), the judge may adopt that method. Sahibayn's reasoning is that all houses, considered as residences, are of one genus in name and form (they are all dwellings). However, in terms of specific purposes and aspects of residence (location, size, etc.), they are multiple genera. Hence, the judge, being aware of the partners' interests, can decide which method is best. This ruling also applies to scattered shared agricultural lands. The practical outcome: under Abu Hanifa, each partner gets a fraction of each house; under Sahibayn, the judge may allocate whole houses to different partners if it is more equitable.

قسمت فرد (qismat fard): Individual division: each property (e.g., house) is divided separately, so each partner gets a share in each house.
قسمت جمع (qismat jam'): Combined division: all properties are pooled together into a single mass, then divided among partners, possibly giving whole properties to individuals.
مصر (misr): A large town or city; a single urban settlement.

Division of House and Shop: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Genus
Intra-Hanafi dispute on genus classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the basis of the two narrations on the genus of house and shop, and apply the Hanafi methodology of shubhat al-mujanasah to reconcile them.

Core Idea
A house and a shop are different genera for division, but the presumption of homogeneity in riba prohibits their exchange.

When dividing a house and a shop, should they be treated as separate properties or as one? The Hanafi school has two narrations. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind each, and shows how the commentator reconciles them using the principle of shubhat al-mujanasah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The passage addresses the division of a house and a shop. The key question is whether they belong to the same genus (jins) or different genera. This classification affects the method of division and riba implications.
Genus is crucial for riba: exchanging different genera allows surplus; same genus requires equality.
2
State the first narration (different genera)
Al-Quduri and al-Khassaf consider house and shop as different genera. Therefore, when dividing, each property is treated separately. This is the position stated in the opening of the passage.
This is attributed to the phrase 'He made the house and shop two genera'.
3
State the second narration (same genus from Mabsut)
In al-Mabsut, Imam Muhammad states that leasing the benefits of a house in exchange for a shop is invalid. This implies that house and shop are the same genus, because such an exchange would otherwise be permissible as a sale of different genera (allowing surplus).
The invalidity of the ijara is the evidence for same genus classification.
4
Reconcile the two narrations
The commentator (Ashraf al-Hadaya) offers two ways to reconcile: either accept both as valid narrations (riwayatan) for different contexts, or explain that the prohibition in riba is due to shubhat al-mujanasah (strong presumption of homogeneity), even though they are actually different genera. The presumption is treated as reality in riba to prevent usury.
This reconciliation preserves both texts: division uses actual genus; riba uses shubhah.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
For division, the relied-upon position is that they are different genera, following Quduri and Khassaf. The Mabsut narration is explained away via shubhah, not treated as a separate substantive ruling.
Thus, in practice, house and shop are divided separately.

Procedure for the Divider: Mapping, Measuring, Valuing, and Drawing Lots
Steps the divider should follow to ensure a proper partition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five steps the divider must perform when partitioning joint property and explain the purpose of each step.

Core Idea
Divide fairly: map, measure, value, separate rights, and draw lots.

This passage from al-Hidayah outlines the recommended procedure for a divider (qasim) when partitioning joint property. It is a practical checklist to ensure a complete and fair division, preventing future disputes. The text emphasizes that the divider must not only split the land but also secure each co-owner's access and water rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actor and action
The subject is the divider (qasim), and the passage describes actions he should take. The verb 'yambaghi' indicates these are recommended, not obligatory.
2
List the five preparatory steps
Extract the five key verbs: taswir (draw), ta'dil (equalize), tadhrī' (measure), taqwim (evaluate), and ifraz (separate with rights).
Each step has a specific purpose.
3
Understand the purpose of each step
Mapping aids memory; equalizing ensures fairness; measuring gives precise quantity; evaluating buildings allows later compensation; separating rights prevents entanglement.
The separation of path and water right is crucial to dissolve the partnership completely.
4
Note the final step: naming and lot-drawing
After preparation, the divider names shares and draws lots (qur'ah) to assign them randomly. The order of drawing determines who gets the first, second, third share, etc.
Lot-drawing is used when shares are equal and no agreement exists.
5
Recognize the legal effect
Once lots are drawn, each co-owner's share is uniquely determined, and joint ownership ends. The partition becomes final and binding.
This procedure minimizes future disputes.

Commentary
This passage describes the recommended procedure for a divider (قاسم) when partitioning joint property. The text is a ruling statement listing practical steps. First, the divider should draw a map of the property on paper (تصویر) to aid memory. Second, he should equalize the shares (یعدله/یسویہ) by making them as equal as possible in value. Third, he should measure the land (یذرعہ) to know its exact dimensions. Fourth, he should assess the value of any buildings (یقوم البناء) because later evaluation may be needed for compensation. Finally, he should name the shares by order (first, second, third) and then draw lots (قرعة). The one whose name is drawn first gets the first share, and so on. These steps ensure the division is complete and fair, preventing future litigation. The instructions are for the divider (who may be a judge or appointed expert) to follow meticulously.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the validity of testimony from qasims (dividers) when partners disagree after a partition. The core issue is whether the qasims can testify that a partner received his share. The text presents two positions: (1) The view of Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf's final position) that the testimony is accepted. (2) The view of Imam Muhammad, also supported by Imam Shafi'i and Abu Yusuf's earlier statement, that it is not accepted. The commentary explains that the dispute hinges on whether the qasims are testifying about their own act or the partner's act. Imam Muhammad argues that the qasims testify about their own act of dividing, which is invalid because one cannot testify in one's own favor. The analogue is a person who conditions his slave's freedom on another person's act; if that other person testifies that he performed the act, it is not accepted because it is self-serving. In contrast, Shaykhayn argue that the qasims' act is merely distinguishing shares (tamyiz), which is not the subject of testimony. The real issue is the partner's act of taking possession (qabd and istifa'). The qasims testify that the partner took possession, which is an act of another. Moreover, the division (tamyiz) is not binding until possession is taken; before that, partners can revoke. Therefore, the testimony is actually about the partner's act, not their own. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Shaykhayn, as the text presents it as the main ruling of al-Quduri and the commentary supports it. Note that this ruling applies whether the qasims are appointed by the judge or hired by the partners.

المتقاسمون (al-mutaqasimun): The partners who are dividing the property; also called shuraka' (co-owners) in this context.
التمييز (al-tamyiz): The act of distinguishing or separating shares, i.e., the qasims' task of marking boundaries.
الاستيفاء والقبض (al-istifa' wa al-qabd): Taking possession and receiving the share; the act performed by each partner after partition.

Superiority of Qismah over Muhayat
Explains why partition is stronger than time-sharing

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Partition gives complete usufruct at once; time-sharing is sequential; therefore partition prevails when demanded.

In joint ownership, when co-owners cannot agree on use, Islamic law provides two mechanisms: qismah (partition) and muhayat (time-sharing). This lesson explores why the Hanafi school considers partition the stronger option, supported by both logical reasoning and a Quranic precedent.

1

2

The passage states that partition provides complete benefit at a single time, whereas muhayat collects profits sequentially. Therefore, partition is stronger.
The strength lies in the completeness and immediacy of the benefit.
3

The proof is not a command but a tacit approval through narration.

Spatial Muhayat: Permissibility and Distinction from Exchange
Ruling on spatial muhayat with analogy to partition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish spatial muhayat from time-based muhayat, identify its basis in ifraz (segregation), and explain why time specification is not required.

Core Idea
Spatial muhayat is a permissible segregation of shares, not an exchange, so it requires no time limit.

This passage clarifies the second type of muhayat: spatial allotment. It establishes that when co-owners use different parts of the same property simultaneously, it is akin to partition and is permissible. The key is that it is a segregation (ifraz) of shares, not an exchange (mubadalah), thus avoiding riba concerns and the need for time specification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Spatial muhayat: co-owners use different parts of the same property at the same time. The ruling is permissible (jā'iz).
This is the second type of muhayat.
2
Recognize the analogy to partition
The author draws a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) to partition (qismah). Since partition in this manner is permissible, muhayat is also permissible.
Analogy is based on the permissibility of spatial division.
3
Distinguish ifraz from mubadalah
Spatial muhayat is ifraz (segregation) of shares, not mubadalah (exchange). In ifraz, each takes his own specific portion without transferring usufruct.
If it were exchange in a single genus, it would involve riba.

Derive the consequence
Since it is ifraz, no time specification is required. If it were exchange (like time-based muhayat), time would be necessary because it would be like a lease (ijarah).
Time specification is only needed when usufruct is transferred.

Core Idea
Muhaya'ah for service is permissible; the dispute is only on judicial compulsion — the mufta bihi view says yes, because service differences are negligible.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah examines a fine point in muhaya'ah: when two people jointly own two slaves and agree to allocate each slave's service to one owner, can a judge impose this arrangement if one party refuses? The Hanafi school's two leading disciples (Sahibayn) allow compulsion, while Abu Hanifa is reported to disallow it. Guided Path

He therefore limits compulsion to the physical slaves themselves, not their service.

The commentator declares Sahibayn's view as 'asahh' (more correct) and thus mufta bihi. The justification is that service differences are rarely vast; in most cases they are comparable. Therefore, the judge can compel muhaya'ah to prevent deadlock. Only if there is a clear huge disparity might the judge refrain, but the general rule is compulsion.
The tarjih authority is the later Hanafi tradition relying on the principle that the usufruct of service is generally uniform.

This passage addresses a common scenario in joint ownership: two people co-own two slaves and agree that each will bear the expenses of the slave who serves him. Judicial Compulsion in Muhaya'ah of Two Houses

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Judicial compulsion in muhaya'ah is valid because disparity in benefits is minimal, treating it as separation (ifraz) not exchange.

Muhaya'ah (temporal partition) allows co-owners to use property in turns. When two houses are involved, a dispute arises: can a judge compel such an arrangement without mutual consent? This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement and clarifies the operative ruling based on the principle of ifraz (separation) versus mubadalah (exchange).

Guided Path

State what muhaya'ah of two houses means: each co-owner lives in one house. Then determine the overall hukm: it is permissible (ja'iz) and the judge may compel it (yujbir al-qadi).

2

The key difference lies in how they analogize muhaya'ah to either ifraz or mubadalah.

Ruling on Muhayat in Animals and Permanent Assets

Core Idea
Muhayat is only valid for ephemeral benefits, not for permanent assets that can be divided.

Sharing property often requires flexibility, but Islamic law carefully distinguishes between assets that can be physically divided and those whose benefits are ephemeral. In this passage, we encounter two rulings on muhayat (temporal division of usufruct): a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding animals, and a unanimous ruling on trees and goats. Understanding the reasoning behind these rulings will sharpen your ability to apply the Hanafi framework to modern partnership scenarios.

Guided Path
