Paternity Claims on a Shared Mukātibah: The First Claimant's Priority
Hanafi intra-school dispute on paternity and liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paternity claims by co-owners of a mukātibah and explain why the first claimant becomes the owner of her as umm walad with liability for half her value.

Core Idea
The first claimant's paternity is decisive: she becomes his umm walad and he owes half her value to his partner.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: two co-owners jointly make their slave girl a mukātibah (earning freedom by installments). Each then has intercourse with her, each claims paternity of the resulting child, and then she becomes unable to pay ('ājiz). The Hanafi school, led by Imam Abu Hanifa, gives priority to the first claimant. Let us unpack the reasoning and the practical implications of this intra-Hanafi dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their actions
You have two men who co-own a slave girl. They grant her a mukātib contract. Later, each man has intercourse with her, and each claims the child born as his own. The slave girl then becomes 'ājiz (unable to pay) – this triggers the analysis of paternity and ownership.
2
Establish the timeline of claims
The first man's intercourse and claim occur before the second's. In Hanafi fiqh, the child is attributed to the first claimant because the child follows the bed (al-walad li al-firāsh). Since she is a mukātibah, her earnings belong to her, but paternity affects her status as umm walad.
The principle 'al-walad li al-firāsh' is usually applied to a married woman, but here it is extended to a slave woman with a single master.
3
Determine the legal consequence for the first claimant
Because his paternity is established, she becomes his umm walad (mother of his child). This means she will become free upon his death and cannot be sold. However, since she was co-owned, his action (intercourse) effectively takes the entire slave girl from the partnership. Therefore, he must compensate his partner for half her current market value.
The liability is for half the value at the time of the claim, not the original price.
4
Compare with the Sahibayn's view
The passage mentions that the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) have a different viewpoint. Their exact position is not detailed here, but it likely hinges on whether the paternity claim can be prioritized or whether the child is considered free regardless. The practical difference (thamarah al-khilāf) affects who inherits from her and who has wala' over the child.
The mufta bihi is Imam Abu Hanifa's view, which is relied upon in fatwa.

Core Idea
A mukātib's death does not end his contract; his estate pays the debt and his children become free.

This passage covers two scenarios: when the mukātib becomes unable to pay an installment, and when he dies. The Hanafi school carefully balances the rights of the master, the mukātib, and his heirs. We will parse the conditions and consequences laid out by the text.

Ikrah on Talaq and Itq: Hanafi Ruling and Compensation
States the Hanafi ruling on talaq and itq under ikrah with compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that talaq and itq are valid under duress and explain the compensation mechanism for the coercer's liability.

Core Idea
Talaq and itq under duress are valid; the coercer bears the financial loss.

In this passage, the Hanafi school addresses a critical question: if a man is forced to divorce his wife or free his slave, does the act take effect? The answer reveals a key principle: the verbal act is attributed to the coerced, but the property loss is shifted to the coercer as if the coerced acted as an instrument (āla) in destroying wealth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is talaq (divorce) or itq (emancipation) performed under duress (ikrah). The Hanafi ruling is that the act is valid and takes effect (sahih).
This applies to both ibadāt and mu‘āmalāt where verbal declarations are involved.
2
Locate the dalīl (proof)
The proof is a qiyās jaliy (clear analogy): the coerced acts as an instrument (āla) causing destruction (itlāf) of property (slave’s value or mahr). Just as a tool’s user is liable for destruction caused by the tool, the coercer is liable for the financial loss.
The verbal act itself (talaq/itq) is attributed to the coerced, but the property loss is attributed to the coercer.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalālah
The key phrase is 'صلح آلة' (becomes an instrument). The coerced is not the free cause of the loss; he is compelled, so the destruction is ‘added’ (yudāfu) to the coercer. This grounds liability on the coercer.
This is a qiyās with cases of itlāf where the direct cause is coerced.
4
Note the ikhtilāf
Imām al-Shāfi‘ī holds that duress invalidates the consent required for talaq and itq, so they do not take effect. The Hanafi response builds on the instrument theory and the principle that ikrah does not nullify the legal effect of such acts.
The difference is referenced to the Book of Divorce (Kitāb al-Ṭalāq) for full details.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjīḥ
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon (muftā bihī) view, as it harmonizes the validity of the act with compensation, preserving the wife’s/slave’s rights while punishing the coercer.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilāf is mentioned for this ruling.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on the validity of talaq (divorce) and itq (emancipation) under duress (ikrah). The reasoning is that the mahr obligation was on the verge of falling due due to separation from the wife's side, but divorce confirmed it, so the loss is attributed to the coercer as destruction. After consummation, the mahr is fully confirmed by consummation, not divorce, so there is no recovery from the coercer. This is an application of the principle that under ikrah, acts that are purely verbal (like talaq and itq) take effect, but the financial losses caused by the act are shifted to the coercer.

Ruling on Compelled Divorce and Emancipation
Elaborates Hanafi ruling on validity of talaq/itq under duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compelled divorce and emancipation and explain the financial liability of the coercer.

Core Idea
A coerced divorce or emancipation is valid, but the coercer bears the financial loss.

When a person is forced to divorce his wife or free his slave under duress (ikrah), does the act take effect? And if it does, who bears the financial consequences? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer: the act is valid, but the coercer (mukrih) is liable for the resulting financial losses. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this ruling and contrasts it with the Shafi'i position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Start by identifying the two acts discussed: divorce (talaq) and emancipation (itq). The ruling states that under duress, both take effect according to the Hanafi school. This is the core position.
2
Understand the attribution of destruction
The commentator explains that the coerced person is merely an instrument (aalah) for the coercer in causing destruction (itlaaf). The financial loss is therefore attributed to the coercer, not the coerced.
This is a key qiyas: the coerced is like a tool; the coercer is the real agent of destruction.
3
Analyze the liability for the slave
When a slave is freed under duress, the coerced can recover the slave's value from the coercer. The slave does not owe any sa'ayah (earning) because the free is complete and no other rights are attached.
Sa'ayah is required only for partial freedom or when third parties have rights.

Explains validity of agency under coercion by istihsan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of agency created under coercion and explain the istihsan argument that overrides qiyas.

Core Idea
Coercion invalidates consent but agency remains valid by istihsan due to its nature as an isqat (waiver).

The Hidayah presents a classic Hanafi example of istihsan overriding qiyas. By qiyas, agency under coercion should be invalid because coercion vitiates consent like jest (hazl). However, by istihsan, the agency is valid because coercion is analogous to a corrupt condition (shart fasid), and agency is not invalidated by corrupt conditions. This principle has practical consequences: if an agent appointed under coercion divorces the principal's wife or frees his slave, the action takes effect, and the principal can recover the loss from the coercer.

By istihsan, the agency is valid. The reason: coercion is like a corrupt condition (shart fasid) in a contract. Agency is an isqat (waiver of right) and is not invalidated by corrupt conditions. Therefore, the agent's actions take effect.
Istihsan is a recognized Hanafi source of law that prioritizes equity over strict analogy.

The coerced principal can recover the loss (half dower if divorce, value of slave if manumission) from the coercer by istihsan. The coercer's objective (removal of ownership) was achieved, so he is liable.
By qiyas, recovery would not be possible because the loss is caused by the agent's act, not directly by the coercer.
4

Note that the three imams (Abu Hanifa's opponents on this, i.e., Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali?) hold that agency under coercion is invalid by qiyas and do not apply istihsan. This highlights the Hanafi reliance on istihsan.

If one is coerced to appoint an agent for divorce or manumission, and the agent does it, it is valid by istihsan, because coercion influences the corruption of a contract, but agency is not invalidated by corrupt conditions. The coerced person recovers from the coercer by istihsan, because the coercer's objective is the removal of his ownership when the agent acts. A vow is not affected by coercion because it does not admit rescission. There is no recourse against the coercer for what becomes binding on the coerced, because there is no claimant in this world. Similarly, oaths and zihar are not affected by coercion because they do not admit rescission. Likewise, raj'a, ila', and verbal retraction in ila' are valid with jest, so coercion does not affect them. Khul' from the husband is divorce or an oath; coercion does not affect it. If he is coerced for khul' but not the wife, the compensation becomes binding on her due to her consent to the obligation. He said: If one is coerced to commit zina, the hadd penalty is obligatory upon him according to Abu Hanifa, unless the ruler coerces him. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: The hadd is not obligatory. We have mentioned this in the Book of Hudud.

Commentary
This passage from the Hidayah, with Urdu commentary from Ashraf al-Hidayah, covers several rulings on coercion (ikrah). The passage type is a series of rulings with proofs (istihsan and qiyas). The author follows the order of the matn, explaining each ruling.

First, coercion to appoint an agent for divorce or manumission: By qiyas (analogy) such agency would be invalid because coercion vitiates consent like jest (hazl), and agency is invalid by jest. But by istihsan it is valid because coercion is like a corrupt condition, and agency is not invalidated by corrupt conditions. The coerced principal can recover the loss (half dower or value of slave) from the coercer by istihsan, because the coercer's goal of removing ownership was achieved through the agent's act.

Second, vows (nazr): Coercion has no effect because a vow is like an oath (the Prophet ﷺ said 'The vow is an oath'), and oaths are not subject to rescission. Therefore, if coerced to make a vow, it becomes binding and the coerced cannot recover from the coercer.

Third, oaths (yamin) and zihar: Same ruling – coercion does not affect them because they do not admit rescission.

Fourth, raj'a (revocation of divorce), ila' (vow of abstinence), and verbal retraction in ila': These are valid even in jest, so coercion does not affect them.

Fifth, khul': From the husband's side it is either a divorce or an oath, so coercion does not affect it. If only the husband is coerced but not the wife, the compensation becomes binding on her because she consented.

Sixth, coercion to commit zina: According to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Zufar, the hadd penalty is obligatory because a man's erection does not occur without desire, indicating pleasure. However, if the coercer is the ruler (sultan), then the hadd is not obligatory because the purpose of hadd is deterrence, which is unnecessary under coercion, and the coerced acts to save his life, creating a doubt (shubha) that prevents hadd. The exception of the sultan is in the later relied-upon view of Abu Hanifa. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn), the hadd is never obligatory under coercion.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrah): Coercion that nullifies consent (rida) and corrupts choice (ikhtiyar), but does not necessarily invalidate all contracts.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): A method of juristic preference that overrides qiyas in favor of a more equitable ruling, used here to validate agency under coercion.
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or joking; acts that are valid despite being done in jest, indicating they are not subject to rescission and thus not affected by coercion.
الإسقاط (al-isqat): A contract of waiver where a right is relinquished; such contracts are less susceptible to invalidation by defects.

Acts Not Affected by Coercion: Vows, Oaths, and Similar
Explains rules for acts immune to coercion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which legal acts are not affected by coercion according to the Hanafi school and explain why they are immune.

Core Idea
Coercion does not affect acts that are not subject to rescission, such as vows, oaths, and zihar.

The Hidayah moves from contracts to unilateral acts. It states that vows (nazr), oaths (yamin), zihar, raj'a (revocation of divorce), and ila' (vow of abstinence) are not nullified by coercion. The unifying reason is that these acts are not revocable (لا يحتمل الفسخ). They are valid even when made in jest (hazl), so coercion, which is less severe than hazl in some ways? Actually, the text says coercion does not affect them because they do not admit rescission. This is consistent with the Hanafi principle that acts which are valid despite jest are immune to coercion. Additionally, the coerced person has no recourse against the coercer for any obligations arising from these acts, because there is no worldly claimant for such obligations—they are between the person and Allah.

These acts do not admit rescission (لا يحتمل الفسخ). They are, by nature, irrevocable once performed. Therefore, coercion cannot make them invalid.

Commentary
This passage from the Hidayah, with Urdu commentary from Ashraf al-Hidayah, covers several rulings on coercion (ikrah). The passage type is a series of rulings with proofs (istihsan and qiyas). The author follows the order of the matn, explaining each ruling.

First, coercion to appoint an agent for divorce or manumission: By qiyas (analogy) such agency would be invalid because coercion vitiates consent like jest (hazl), and agency is invalid by jest. But by istihsan it is valid because coercion is like a corrupt condition, and agency is not invalidated by corrupt conditions. The coerced principal can recover the loss (half dower or value of slave) from the coercer by istihsan, because the coercer's goal of removing ownership was achieved through the agent's act.

Second, vows (nazr): Coercion has no effect because a vow is like an oath (the Prophet ﷺ said 'The vow is an oath'), and oaths are not subject to rescission. Therefore, if coerced to make a vow, it becomes binding and the coerced cannot recover from the coercer.

Third, oaths (yamin) and zihar: Same ruling – coercion does not affect them because they do not admit rescission.

Fourth, raj'a (revocation of divorce), ila' (vow of abstinence), and verbal retraction in ila': These are valid even in jest, so coercion does not affect them.

Fifth, khul': From the husband's side it is either a divorce or an oath, so coercion does not affect it. If only the husband is coerced but not the wife, the compensation becomes binding on her because she consented.

Sixth, coercion to commit zina: According to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Zufar, the hadd penalty is obligatory because a man's erection does not occur without desire, indicating pleasure. However, if the coercer is the ruler (sultan), then the hadd is not obligatory because the purpose of hadd is deterrence, which is unnecessary under coercion, and the coerced acts to save his life, creating a doubt (shubha) that prevents hadd. The exception of the sultan is in the later relied-upon view of Abu Hanifa. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn), the hadd is never obligatory under coercion.

Key Terms
النذر (al-nazr): A vow to perform an act of worship or charity.
اليمين (al-yamin): An oath, usually by Allah, to do or not do something.
الظهار (al-zihar): A statement likening one's wife to a prohibited relative (e.g., 'You are like my mother's back'), which triggers a prohibition until expiation.
الرجعة (al-raj'a): Revocation of a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i) during the waiting period.
الإيلاء (al-ila'): A vow by a husband to abstain from sexual relations with his wife for a period of four months or more.

Khul' Under Coercion: Only the Husband's Side Matters
Explains ruling for khul' under coercion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ruling on khul' when only one party is coerced and identify which side's consent is considered.

Core Idea
Coercion does not affect the husband's khul' (talaq with compensation) because it is a form of talaq or oath, but the wife's consent is necessary for the compensation.

The Hidayah includes a specific ruling on khul' (divorce initiated by wife with compensation) under coercion. It states that khul' from the husband's side is either a divorce or an oath, so coercion does not affect its validity. However, if only the husband is coerced and the wife is not, the compensation (badal) becomes binding on the wife because she consented to it. This subtle ruling distinguishes between the effect of coercion on the dissolution of marriage (which is valid) and on the financial obligation (which depends on the wife's free consent).

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the khul' transaction
Khul' consists of two elements: the husband's declaration (which is a divorce or an oath) and the wife's acceptance to pay compensation. These are separable in terms of coercion.
2
Apply the ruling to the husband's declaration
The husband's khul' is either talaq (if he uses divorce language) or yamin (if he uses oath language). Both are not affected by coercion as established earlier. Therefore, the khul' is valid from his side even if coerced.
The text says: 'الخلع من جانبه طلاق او يمين لا يعمل فيه الاكراه'
3
Apply the ruling to the wife's compensation
The wife's side involves her consent to pay compensation. If she is not coerced, her consent is valid, and the compensation is binding on her. If she were coerced, the compensation would not be binding.
The text says: 'فلو كان هو مكرها على الخلع دونها لزمها البدل لرضاها بالالتزام'
4
State the conclusion
When only the husband is coerced: the divorce/khul' is effective, and the wife must pay the compensation. When both are coerced or just the wife: the compensation may not be binding (though not explicitly stated, it follows from the principle).

Commentary
This passage from the Hidayah, with Urdu commentary from Ashraf al-Hidayah, covers several rulings on coercion (ikrah). The passage type is a series of rulings with proofs (istihsan and qiyas). The author follows the order of the matn, explaining each ruling.

First, coercion to appoint an agent for divorce or manumission: By qiyas (analogy) such agency would be invalid because coercion vitiates consent like jest (hazl), and agency is invalid by jest. But by istihsan it is valid because coercion is like a corrupt condition, and agency is not invalidated by corrupt conditions. The coerced principal can recover the loss (half dower or value of slave) from the coercer by istihsan, because the coercer's goal of removing ownership was achieved through the agent's act.

Second, vows (nazr): Coercion has no effect because a vow is like an oath (the Prophet ﷺ said 'The vow is an oath'), and oaths are not subject to rescission. Therefore, if coerced to make a vow, it becomes binding and the coerced cannot recover from the coercer.

Third, oaths (yamin) and zihar: Same ruling – coercion does not affect them because they do not admit rescission.

Fourth, raj'a (revocation of divorce), ila' (vow of abstinence), and verbal retraction in ila': These are valid even in jest, so coercion does not affect them.

Fifth, khul': From the husband's side it is either a divorce or an oath, so coercion does not affect it. If only the husband is coerced but not the wife, the compensation becomes binding on her because she consented.

Sixth, coercion to commit zina: According to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Zufar, the hadd penalty is obligatory because a man's erection does not occur without desire, indicating pleasure. However, if the coercer is the ruler (sultan), then the hadd is not obligatory because the purpose of hadd is deterrence, which is unnecessary under coercion, and the coerced acts to save his life, creating a doubt (shubha) that prevents hadd. The exception of the sultan is in the later relied-upon view of Abu Hanifa. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn), the hadd is never obligatory under coercion.

Coerced Zina and Hadd: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Presents intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on hadd for coerced zina.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the hadd penalty for a person coerced to commit zina, and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa holds that hadd is obligatory unless the coercer is the sultan; Sahibayn hold that hadd is never obligatory due to doubt (shubha).

The final part of the passage addresses a significant intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: zina committed under coercion. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the hadd (capital punishment for a married person, 100 lashes for unmarried) is obligatory, except when the coercer is the sultan (ruler). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, stating that hadd is never obligatory because coercion creates a doubt (shubha) that prevents penal enforcement. The passage notes that this issue was already discussed in the Book of Hudud. This disagreement has practical consequences for the application of criminal law in Islamic states.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Imam Abu Hanifa: Hadd is obligatory on the coerced person unless the coercer is the sultan. The reasoning: male arousal (intishar al-ala) implies pleasure and consent; coercion by a non-sultan does not remove the element of desire. However, coercion by the sultan creates a doubt (shubha) because the coercion is to save one's life, which overrides the pleasure.
Imam Zufar concurs with Abu Hanifa.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: Hadd is never obligatory when zina is committed under coercion, regardless of who coerces. They argue that coercion itself creates a doubt (shubha) that prevents the hadd, and the act is not considered a crime of zina due to the lack of true consent.
Their proof: hadd is for deterrence, but under coercion the person is not a true offender.
3
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The difference is whether the hadd is applied. Under Abu Hanifa's view, if a private individual coerces, the hadd is applied; under Sahibayn's view, it is never applied.
4
Determine the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position
The text indicates that the exception of the sultan is from Abu Hanifa's later opinion (marjoo'alayhi). This suggests that he modified his view, making it closer to the Sahibayn's position. Later Hanafi fatwa relies on the Sahibayn's view that no hadd is applied under any coercion. This is more in line with the principle of avoiding hadd penalties through shubha.
The commentator notes this was mentioned in Kitab al-Hudud.

Commentary
This passage from the Hidayah, with Urdu commentary from Ashraf al-Hidayah, covers several rulings on coercion (ikrah). The passage type is a series of rulings with proofs (istihsan and qiyas). The author follows the order of the matn, explaining each ruling.

First, coercion to appoint an agent for divorce or manumission: By qiyas (analogy) such agency would be invalid because coercion vitiates consent like jest (hazl), and agency is invalid by jest. But by istihsan it is valid because coercion is like a corrupt condition, and agency is not invalidated by corrupt conditions. The coerced principal can recover the loss (half dower or value of slave) from the coercer by istihsan, because the coercer's goal of removing ownership was achieved through the agent's act.

Second, vows (nazr): Coercion has no effect because a vow is like an oath (the Prophet ﷺ said 'The vow is an oath'), and oaths are not subject to rescission. Therefore, if coerced to make a vow, it becomes binding and the coerced cannot recover from the coercer.

Third, oaths (yamin) and zihar: Same ruling – coercion does not affect them because they do not admit rescission.

Fourth, raj'a (revocation of divorce), ila' (vow of abstinence), and verbal retraction in ila': These are valid even in jest, so coercion does not affect them.

Fifth, khul': From the husband's side it is either a divorce or an oath, so coercion does not affect it. If only the husband is coerced but not the wife, the compensation becomes binding on her because she consented.

Sixth, coercion to commit zina: According to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Zufar, the hadd penalty is obligatory because a man's erection does not occur without desire, indicating pleasure. However, if the coercer is the ruler (sultan), then the hadd is not obligatory because the purpose of hadd is deterrence, which is unnecessary under coercion, and the coerced acts to save his life, creating a doubt (shubha) that prevents hadd. The exception of the sultan is in the later relied-upon view of Abu Hanifa. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn), the hadd is never obligatory under coercion.

الحد (al-hadd): A fixed punishment prescribed by Allah for certain crimes (e.g., zina, theft).
الشبهة (al-shubha): A doubt or ambiguity that prevents the application of a hadd punishment.
السلطان (al-sultan): The ruler or legitimate authority who can enforce punishments.

Coercion in Divorce and Emancipation: Recourse Against the Coercer
Ruling with proof on recourse after coerced divorce/emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on financial recourse after coerced divorce or emancipation and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas.

Core Idea
A coerced divorce or emancipation is valid, but the coercer must compensate because his purpose was achieved.

When a person is forced to divorce his wife or free his slave under coercion (ikrah), the act itself is not invalid. However, who bears the financial loss? By strict analogy (qiyas), the coerced individual would bear the loss, but the Hanafi school, through istihsan (juristic preference), shifts liability to the coercer. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this departure from qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The coerced person divorces or emancipates via tawkeel (agency). The ruling: divorce and emancipation are valid, and the coerced recovers half the dower (in divorce) or the price of the slave (in emancipation) from the coercer.
The act itself is sahih, but financial liability shifts.
2
Understand the qiyas argument
By qiyas, the coerced cannot recover because agency does not transfer ownership; the agent may not act, so the destruction is not attributable to the coercer.
Qiyas is based on normal agency rules.
3
Understand the istihsan argument
The coercer's purpose was to remove ownership through the agent's act; since that purpose was achieved, the coercer is liable. The agent's act was a means to removal, so guarantee follows.
Istihsan focuses on the coercer's intent and actual outcome.
4
Distinguish qiyas from istihsan
Recognize that istihsan is a valid basis for overriding qiyas in Hanafi usul when strict analogy leads to inequity or fails to achieve the purpose of the law.
Istihsan is a source of law in the Hanafi school.

Acts Where Coercion Has No Effect
List of acts where coercion is ineffective, with legal reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five acts that remain binding under coercion and explain the Hanafi reasoning based on the oath analogy.

Core Idea
Coercion does not invalidate oath-like acts: vows, oaths, zihar, raj'ah, and ila' remain binding.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes an important exception to the general rule that coercion can invalidate declarations. The Hanafi school identifies a category of acts akin to oaths (yamin) that are inherently binding and not subject to annulment. These include nadhr (vow), yamin (oath), zihar, raj'ah (return to wife after divorce), and ila' (vow of abstinence) including its verbal revocation. The reasoning is that these acts are valid even when done in jest (hazl), so coercion—a stronger external factor—cannot override them.

Guided Path

Identify the five acts
List the acts mentioned: nadhr, yamin, zihar, raj'ah, and ila'. Memorize them as a set.
These are all oath-like declarations.
2
Recognize the core proof
The hadith 'النذر يمين' (The vow is an oath) equates vows to oaths, establishing that they have the same legal effect.
This hadith is the primary dalil.
3
Understand the legal principle
Anything that is valid in jest (hazl) is not subject to annulment by coercion. Since these acts are valid in jest, coercion does not affect them.
This principle is derived from the nature of oaths.
4
Apply to each act
For each of the five acts, the ruling is the same: if performed under coercion, the act is still valid and binding.
There is no distinction among them.
5
Note the practical consequence
The coerced person must fulfill the obligation (e.g., fasting, expiation) and cannot recover anything from the coercer in this world.
No financial claim arises from these acts.

Core Idea
A coerced khul' from the husband is valid because it is like a divorce; the wife must pay the compensation.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a specific scenario: a husband is forced to grant khul' to his wife. The ruling is that the khul' is valid and the wife owes the agreed compensation. The text provides two analogies to support this: comparing khul' to talaq and to a yamin (oath). Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing between different types of coercion and their effects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the coerced party
The act is khul' given by the husband under coercion. The coercion is applied to the husband, not the wife. This is crucial because the ruling would differ if the wife were coerced.

The author argues that since coercion does not prevent an unconditional divorce (talaq), it also does not prevent a divorce with compensation (khul'). The illa is the nature of the husband's act of dissolving marriage.

The author also compares khul' to an oath, noting that coercion has no effect on oaths. This reinforces that the husband's utterance is effective regardless of duress.

Since the khul' is valid, the wife must pay the agreed compensation ('iwad). The coercer is not liable because the marriage is not considered property, and the coercer did not destroy a tangible asset.
This distinguishes khul' from cases of coerced divorce or emancipation where the coercer may be liable.

Ruling on coerced apostasy: marriage retained by istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced apostasy's effect on marriage, explain the role of istihsan, and evaluate the qiyas argument it overrides.

Core Idea
Coerced apostasy does not dissolve marriage; the husband's word is accepted by istihsan because belief is certain and coercion casts doubt.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a sensitive scenario: a person is forced to utter words of apostasy under threat. Does this terminate their marriage? The Hanafi school distinguishes between outer utterance and inner belief, applying istihsan to protect the marriage. The text also explores the dispute between spouses and contrasts coercion into apostasy with coercion into Islam, where a different rule applies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Initial Ruling
The passage begins by stating that if a person is coerced (ikrah) into apostasy (riddah), his wife does not become bainah (separated) from him. This is the core ruling.
The ruling applies even if the utterance of apostasy is clear.
2
Understand the Basis: Apostasy Relates to Belief
The justification is that apostasy is a matter of inner belief (i'tiqad), not mere utterance. The text uses the principle: if the heart is content with faith, the person does not become an unbeliever. Since coercion creates doubt about actual disbelief, separation cannot be established by doubt.
Certainty of faith is not overridden by doubtful apostasy.
3
Recognize the Qiyas Argument
By qiyas (analogy to divorce or willing apostasy), the wife's word might be accepted because the utterance of kufr is a cause for separation. The text implies that without istihsan, the wife would prevail.
Qiyas would produce hardship by destroying the marriage on mere utterance under coercion.
4
Apply Istihsan: The Husband's Word is Accepted
The text explicitly says 'f-al-qawlu qawlu-hu istihsanan' – his word is accepted by juristic preference. The reason: the word of apostasy is not placed (mawdu') for separation; separation occurs only by actual change of belief. Coercion prevents the inference of such a change.
Istihsan overrides qiyas to preserve marriage and fairness.
5
Contrast with Coercion into Islam
The passage notes that if a person is coerced into Islam, they are legally ruled Muslim (by zahir) because Islam is high and should be given preference. However, this is only a worldly ruling; in the sight of Allah, if they do not believe, they are not Muslim. Similarly, if they later recant, they are not killed due to the shubha (doubt) created by coercion.
The principle 'al-Islam ya'lu wa la yu'la' applies.

The Invalidity of Divorce and Manumission by a Minor or Insane Person
Explaining why divorce and manumission are not valid from a child or insane person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that divorce and manumission by a child or insane person are void and explain the Hanafi rationale using the distinction between pure harm and mixed benefit/harm.

Core Idea
A minor or insane person cannot divorce or free a slave because they lack awareness of the future benefit, making these acts void.

This lesson addresses a critical limitation in legal capacity: a child or insane person cannot validly perform divorce or manumission, even with guardian permission. The Hanafi school distinguishes these acts from ordinary contracts like sale, where a guardian can act. By understanding the underlying reasoning—lack of present awareness and inability to foresee future benefit—you will grasp why the Shariah protects such individuals from irreversible consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rulings
First, note that the passage declares both divorce (talaq) and manumission (itaq) by a minor or insane person as invalid. Manumission is pure harm, so it's obviously void. Divorce is a mix of harm and benefit, but the child cannot recognize its benefit now or later.
The ruling is absolute: no effect even with guardian's permission.
2
Analyze the Reasoning for Divorce
The author explains that divorce's benefit depends on future spousal incompatibility, which a child cannot foresee. The child lacks sexual desire now and cannot judge temperament later. Even the guardian cannot know future compatibility, so the act cannot be delegated.
3
Compare with Other Contracts
Contrast divorce/manumission with contracts like sale, purchase, gift, and charity. In those, the child can potentially recognize benefit (e.g., receiving a gift), so the guardian can act. But divorce/manumission are personal and uncertain, hence no delegation possible.
This shows the Hanafi use of 'illa (effective cause): the inability to know benefit.
4
Apply the Qiyas Structure
The passage implicitly uses qiyas: the original case (asl) is other contracts where guardian acts; the new case (far') is divorce/manumission. The 'illa is that the benefit cannot be recognized by the child or his guardian with certainty. Since this 'illa is absent in sale etc., the ruling differs.
This is a qiyas khafi (subtle analogy) because the 'illa is not obvious.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A slave's acknowledgment binds after freedom for property, but immediately for hudud, qisas, and divorce.

In Hanafi law, a slave’s legal acknowledgment (iqrar) is valid in his own right due to legal capacity, but not against his master to protect the master's property. However, the timing of its binding effect depends on the nature of the right acknowledged. Financial debts are deferred until freedom, whereas hudud (fixed punishments), qisas (retaliation), and divorce take effect immediately. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, the distinction, and the proof from the Sunnah.

2

If the slave acknowledges a hadd or qisas, it binds him immediately. The reason is that these penalties relate to the human aspect of the slave, which remains free from ownership.

The slave's divorce (talaq) is valid and effective immediately. The proof is the hadith: 'Indeed, divorce is for the one who takes hold of the shin' (Ibn Majah), indicating that the slave owns the right to divorce as he is the actual husband.

Due to the previous hadith: 'Every divorce is permissible except that of the insane and one overcome by reason.' Commentary
This passage is a ruling_with_proof explaining the validity of a slave's divorce. The text responds to an implicit invalidation scenario: if a master tries to nullify the slave's divorce, it is not considered because the nullification is implicit. The ruling: a slave's divorce is valid and effective. The proof comes from two hadiths: a general one stating all divorces are permissible except for the insane, and a specific hadith from Ibn Majah where the Prophet declares that divorce belongs only to the one who holds the shin (the husband). The passage also addresses a possible objection regarding the authenticity of the hadith about the slave not owning anything except divorce, noting it is weak, but then provides a stronger hadith through Ibn Abbas. The chain of this hadith includes Ibn Lahi'ah, whom some criticize, but the author notes that Imam Ahmad and Imam Tahawi considered him reliable, thus the hadith is sufficient evidence. The legal reasoning for the ruling is threefold: the precedent hadith that every divorce is permissible except the insane, the explicit hadith that a slave owns only divorce, and rational arguments: the slave recognizes his own interest in divorce, the divorce does not nullify the master's ownership, and it does not waste the master's benefits. Therefore, the divorce is valid.

Key Terms
طلاق (talaq): A slave's pronouncement of divorce is legally effective and valid.
غلام (ghulam): A male slave who is owned by a master (qinn).
بطلان ضمنی (butlan dhimni): An implicit invalidation that does not affect the primary ruling because it is implied and not explicit.

Master Manumitting the Indebted Ma'dhun: Liability and Effect on Debts
Ruling on master manumitting indebted ma'dhun; master's liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a master manumitting an indebted ma'dhun, explain the master's liability to creditors, and distinguish the case of a mudabbar or umm walad.

Core Idea
Master can free indebted ma'dhun, but creditors take slave's value as compensation.

This passage addresses a scenario where a master manumits a ma'dhun slave who has outstanding debts from trade. The key question is whether the manumission is valid and how creditors' rights are protected. The Hanafi ruling is clear: manumission is valid because the master's ownership remains, but the master must compensate the creditors with the slave's value, since the creditors' right to collect from the slave's person was destroyed by the manumission. Any remaining debt stays with the freed slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the master manumitting an indebted ma'dhun. The ruling is that the manumission is valid (ja'iz).
The text explicitly states 'fa-'itquhu ja'iz'.
2
Locate the proof for validity
The proof is that the master's ownership over the slave remains (li-anna mulkahu fihi baqi). Ownership gives the right to manumit.
This is a rational proof based on property rights.
3
Determine the master's liability
The master must pay the slave's value to the creditors because he destroyed their right (atlaqa ma ta'allaqa bihi haqqahum). The creditors had the right to sell the slave to recover debts, which the master eliminated by freeing him.
The liability is a guarantee (daman) for the value.
4
Calculate the extent of liability
If the debts exceed the slave's value, the master pays only the value and the slave remains liable for the rest. If debts are less, the master pays only the debts amount. The master's liability is capped at the creditors' actual right.
The text: 'in kana aqalla min qimatihi damina al-dayna la ghayr'.
5
Contrast with mudabbar and umm walad
If the master manumits a mudabbar or umm walad who are ma'dhuns and in debt, the master has no liability. Their persons cannot be sold to satisfy debts (because they are not transferable by sale), so the manumission does not destroy any creditor right.
The text mentions 'bi-khilaf ma idha a'taqa al-mudabbar wa umm al-walad al-ma'dhun lahum'.

If the master manumits the ma'dhun while he has debts, his manumission is valid because his ownership over him remains. The master is liable for his value to the creditors, because he destroyed what their right was attached to by sale and collection from its price. What remains of the debts is demanded after manumission because the debt is in his liability. The master is only liable to the extent of what he destroyed as a guarantee, so the remainder stays upon him as before. If it is less than his value, he guarantees only the debt, no more, because their right is to that extent. Contrary to if he manumits the mudabbar or umm walad who are ma'dhuns while debts have mounted, because the creditors' right is not attached to their persons for collection by sale, so the master has not destroyed their right and is not liable for anything.

Commentary
This passage contains a ruling with some rational proof. The master may manumit his indebted ma'dhun; the manumission is valid because the master still owns the slave. However, since the creditors' rights were attached to the slave's person (they could sell him to recover debts), the master has effectively destroyed that right by freeing him. Therefore, the master must compensate the creditors by paying the slave's value. If the slave's debts exceed his value, the master pays only the value, and the remaining debt stays on the slave after freedom. If the debts are less, the master pays only the debts amount. This does not apply to a mudabbar or umm walad (even if ma'dhuns) because their persons cannot be sold to satisfy debts, so the master's manumission does not destroy any creditor right.

Key Terms
ضامن (ḍāmin): Liable to pay compensation; here the master must pay the slave's value to creditors.
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave who has been promised freedom upon the master's death.
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave permitted by the master to engage in trade; debts incurred with permission bind the slave's liability.
