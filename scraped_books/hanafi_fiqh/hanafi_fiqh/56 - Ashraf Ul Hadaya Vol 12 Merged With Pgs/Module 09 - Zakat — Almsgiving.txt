Legal Capacity for Kitabah and Tazwij
Ruling on legal capacity for kitabah and tazwij.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which categories of individuals can perform kitabah and tazwij, and identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement on tazwij by ma'dhun, sharik 'inan, and mudarib.

Core Idea
Father, wasi, mufawid, and mukatab can write kitabah; ma'dhun, sharik 'inan, and mudarib cannot — and Abu Yusuf differs on tazwij.

This passage from Kitab al-Mukatab delineates the legal capacities of various guardians and partners regarding manumission contracts (kitabah) and marriage (tazwij) of slaves. Understanding who has authority is crucial for valid transactions. The text also highlights an intra-Hanafi dispute on tazwij of female slaves by certain agents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sets of individuals
First set: father, wasi, mufawid, mukatab. Second set: ma'dhun slave, sharik 'inan, mudarib.
2
State the ruling on kitabah
First set can write kitabah by istihsan. Second set cannot write kitabah by consensus.
Kitabah is a contract of gradual manumission.
3
State the ruling on tazwij al-amma (marrying off a female slave)
First set can do so by consensus. For second set, there is dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say invalid, Abu Yusuf says valid.
Tazwij al-amma is marrying off a female slave to a husband.
4
Note the consensus on tazwij al-abd (male slave)
None of the second set can marry off a male slave by consensus. First set also cannot? The passage says father, wasi, and mufawid cannot marry off a male slave (Sharh al-Tahawi).
First set also limited: they can only marry off female slaves, not male slaves.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement from the beginning of Kitab al-Mukatab. It presents two sets of individuals. The first set (father, wasi, mufawid, mukatab) can write kitabah (contract of manumission) for a slave by istihsan, but cannot free the slave for immediate payment. They can also marry off a female slave they own, by consensus. The second set (ma'dhun slave, sharik 'inan, mudarib) cannot write kitabah by consensus. Regarding marrying off a female slave, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say no, Abu Yusuf says yes. This passage establishes that the mufawid (partner in equal partnership) is treated like the first set with no disagreement on his ability to marry off a female slave.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has made a contract of kitabah with his master to earn his freedom by paying installments.
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave or minor who is permitted by his master or guardian to engage in trade.
شركة عنان (sharikat 'inan): A partnership in which each partner contributes capital and work, but they are not agents for each other in all matters (unlike mufawada).
مفاوض (mufawid): A partner in an equal partnership (mufawada) where each partner is fully authorized to act for the other.

Sadaqa Paid by Mukatab Becomes Lawful for Master After Inability
Ruling on sadaqa paid by mukatab to master after inability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sadaqa paid by a mukatab to his master when the mukatab becomes unable, and explain the legal reasoning using the principle of tabaddul al-milk and the hadith of Barirah.

Core Idea
Change of ownership (tabaddul al-milk) makes sadaqa lawful for the master.

A mukatab (slave under contract of emancipation) often receives sadaqa to help pay his kitabah. If he pays some of that sadaqa to his master and later becomes unable to continue payments, what is the legal status of the amount already paid? The Hanafi madhhab rules that it becomes lawful (halal) for the master. This lesson unpacks the legal principle behind this ruling, the prophetic proof from the hadith of Barirah, and the intra-Hanafi reasoning of Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Read the passage carefully. The mukatab takes sadaqa (zakat or voluntary charity) and pays it to his master as part of the kitabah. Then the mukatab becomes unable (عجز) to pay the remaining amount. The ruling: that paid wealth is lawful (طيب) for the master.
2
Understand the principle: tabaddul al-milk (change of ownership)
The passage gives the reason: 'تبدل الملک' (change of ownership). The mukatab owned the sadaqa as a beneficiary, and when he paid it to the master, the master took it as a price (عوض) for emancipation. This transfer of ownership transforms the legal nature of the wealth. Contrast with a mere permission (الإباحة) without transfer of ownership, which does not make sadaqa lawful for a rich person or Hashimi.
Change of ownership is key; mere permission to consume does not change ownership.
3
Analyze the dalil: hadith of Barirah
The passage cites the well-known hadith where the Prophet (ﷺ) said about meat given as sadaqa to Barirah: 'It is for her sadaqa, and for us a gift.' This demonstrates that the same object can change its legal classification (from sadaqa to gift) when ownership changes. The hadith is the prophetic indication (الاشارة النبوية) for this ruling.
The hadith is ahad, but accepted as proof for this analogy.
4
Explore the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Imam Muhammad vs. Imam Abu Yusuf
Imam Muhammad holds that the mukatab's inability (عجز) itself changes ownership directly, so the master's ownership is established by the inability. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that inability does not change ownership; it merely confirms (يتقرر) the master's pre-existing ownership. Yet both agree the sadaqa is lawful because there is no inherent impurity (خبث) in the sadaqa itself; the impurity is in the act of taking by a rich person as a humiliating act, and the master did not take it in that manner.
Both views lead to the same practical ruling, so the difference is methodological.
5
Apply to analogous cases
The passage analogizes to a traveler who receives sadaqa and then returns home, or a poor person who becomes rich after receiving sadaqa: any remaining sadaqa in their hands becomes lawful for them. Similarly, if the mukatab is freed and becomes rich, any remaining sadaqa in his hand becomes lawful for him. This reinforces that change in the recipient's status can alter the ruling.

And this is contrary to the case where someone makes it permissible for a rich person or a Hashimi, because the one for whom it is made permissible consumes it from the ownership of the one who made it permissible, so ownership does not change, hence it is not lawful. If he makes him owner, it becomes lawful. And if he became unable before paying to the master, the answer is the same. This is apparent according to Muhammad, because in his view inability changes ownership. Similarly with Abu Yusuf, even though in his view inability establishes the master's ownership, because there is no impurity in the sadaqa itself; impurity is only in the act of taking, since it is humiliating. So it is not permissible for the rich without necessity, nor for the Hashimi due to his increased honor. And since the taking did not occur from the master, it becomes like the traveler when he reaches his homeland, and the poor man when he becomes rich, while what they had taken of sadaqa remained in their hands: it becomes lawful for them. And upon this, if the mukatab is freed and becomes rich, what remains of the sadaqa in his hand is lawful for him.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Hidayah with commentary. It addresses a scenario where a mukatab (a slave contracting freedom) pays his master from funds he received as sadaqa (charity). If the mukatab subsequently becomes unable to pay the remaining kitabah, the master may lawfully keep that payment. The reason is change of ownership (tabaddul al-milk): the slave owned it as sadaqa, the master received it as the price of freedom. The hadith of Barirah illustrates this: the Prophet (ﷺ) said the meat was sadaqa for Barirah but a gift for them. This contrasts with a poor person making sadaqa mubah (permissible) for a rich or Hashimi to consume without transfer of ownership; there, it does not become lawful because ownership does not change. The commentary also explains the views of Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf: Muhammad holds that inability itself changes ownership, so the answer is clear. Abu Yusuf holds that inability only confirms the master's ownership, but since there is no impurity in the sadaqa itself—only in the act of taking as a humiliation—and the master did not take it with that intention, it becomes lawful. The analogy is drawn to a traveler who after reaching home still has sadaqa in hand, or a poor person who becomes rich: what remains of the sadaqa is lawful for them. Similarly, if the mukatab is later freed and becomes rich, any remaining sadaqa in his hand becomes lawful for him.

Key Terms
تبدل الملک (tabaddul al-milk): The change of ownership from one person to another, which legally transforms the status of an object (e.g., sadaqa becomes a gift).
خبث (khubth): Impurity or unlawfulness inherent in a transaction or item; here it refers to the moral/legal impurity of a rich person accepting sadaqa voluntarily.
صدقات (ṣadaqāt): Charitable payments, including zakat and voluntary sadaqa, given to those entitled.

The Exclusive Bond of Wala' for the Manumittor
Stating the ruling with hadith proof and linguistic analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wala' al-itaqah and explain how the hadith of Barirah proves its exclusivity.

Core Idea
Wala' belongs exclusively to the manumittor and cannot be transferred or inherited like property.

This lesson examines the foundational ruling that when a master emancipates a slave, the bond of patronage (wala') is established exclusively for the manumittor. We will analyse the primary proof: the Prophet's statement in the hadith of Barirah, and understand how the Arabic particles (lam al-jins and lam al-ikhtisas) indicate restriction. This ruling is a cornerstone of Hanafi inheritance and liability law concerning freed slaves.

Guided Path
1
State the core ruling
From the text: 'When a master emancipates his slave, his wala' belongs to him.' Identify that this is a clear legal ruling (hukm) establishing the manumittor's exclusive right.
The ruling is unconditional; it applies whether emancipation is with or without compensation.
2
Identify the hadith proof
Locate the hadith of Barirah: 'Emancipate her, for wala' belongs to the one who emancipates.' (فإنما الولاء لمن أعتق). Note that this is a sunnah ahad, but its indication (dalala) is strong due to the restrictive particle.
The hadith is narrated by the six major collections; its chain is sound.
3
Analyse the linguistic restriction
The phrase 'فإنما الولاء لمن أعتق' uses 'إنما' (innama) for restriction (hasr). Also, 'الولاء' has lam al-jins (generic lam) and 'لمن أعتق' has lam al-ikhtisas (specifying lam). Both indicate that the genus of wala' is exclusively for the one who emancipates.
This linguistic analysis is used to counter any claim that wala' could be transferred.
4
Note the effect of the ruling
The consequence is that the manumittor inherits from the freed slave when the slave dies without heirs, and the manumittor bears the blood wit if the slave commits wrongful killing.
These effects are expanded in the next lesson.

The Principle of Gain in Return for Liability (al-Ghunm bi al-Ghurm)
Explaining the qiyas and legal maxims supporting the ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the principle of al-ghunm bi al-ghurm and apply the qiyas of giving life (ihya) to justify the manumittor's inheritance.

Core Idea
The manumittor inherits because he bore the loss of emancipation, and he gave the slave a new legal life.

The previous lesson established the hadith proof for wala'. This lesson delves into the secondary proofs: the rational principle that 'gain is in proportion to liability' (al-ghunm bi al-ghurm) and the qiyas (analogy) to giving life. These proofs reinforce that the master, having sacrificed the slave's value and provided a new legal existence, is entitled to inherit from the freed slave. This lesson also clarifies the two effects of wala': inheritance (mirath) and blood wit (aql).

Guided Path
1
State the maxim al-ghunm bi al-ghurm
Identify the phrase in the text: 'ولان الغنم بالغرم' (and because gain is in return for loss). Understand it as a legal maxim meaning that the one who bears the loss is entitled to any corresponding gain.
This maxim is used in many fiqh chapters, such as sales and guarantees.
2
Apply the maxim to emancipation
The master loses the slave's value, labour, and potential profit by freeing him. This is a loss (ghurm). The corresponding gain (ghunm) is the right to inherit from the freed slave and to receive his blood wit.
The text explicitly says: 'فيرثه' (so he inherits from him) after stating the loss.
3
Understand the qiyas to giving life
The text analogises the master to a parent: a parent gives physical life to a child, and as a result inherits from the child. The master gives legal life by removing the legal death of slavery (slavery is akin to death, see Quran 6:122). Therefore, the master inherits.
The word 'أحياه معنى' (gave him life in a sense) is key.
4
Identify the two effects of wala'
The passage states that wala' has two effects: inheritance (mirath) and bearing blood wit (aql). The first is proven by the al-ghunm bi al-ghurm principle, the second by mutual support (tanasur).
The text says: 'ولان التناصر به فيعقله' (and because mutual support is through it, so he bears his blood wit).
5
Note the equality of compensated and uncompensated emancipation
The text concludes that wala' is the same whether emancipation is for payment or not, due to the absolute hadith. This means that even if the slave paid for his freedom, the manumittor still inherits.
This counters a potential argument that compensation changes the bond.

He said: And when a master emancipates his slave, his wala' belongs to him, due to his statement peace be upon him: 'Wala' belongs to the one who emancipates.' And because mutual support is established through it, so he bears blood wit on his behalf. And he (the master) has given him life in a sense by removing slavery from him, so he inherits from him, and wala' becomes like lineage. And because reward is proportional to liability. Similarly, a woman who emancipates, due to what we have narrated. And the freed slave of the daughter of Hamzah died leaving his female patron and a daughter, so the Prophet peace be upon him divided the property equally between them. And emancipation with compensation and without compensation are equal in this due to the absolute nature of what we have mentioned.
Translation: When a master emancipates his slave, his wala' belongs to him due to the prophetic command that wala' belongs to the one who emancipates. And because through emancipation mutual support occurs, he bears his liability. And the master has given him life meaningfully by removing his slave status, so he will inherit from him. And wala' becomes like lineage. And because gain is in return for loss. Similarly, a woman who emancipates, by the evidence of the hadith we narrated. And the freed slave of the daughter of Hamzah died leaving his mawlat (female patron) and a daughter, so the Prophet divided the property between them equally. And emancipation with money and without money are equal in this due to the absolute nature of the hadith we mentioned.
Commentary: His statement 'And when he emancipates...' etc. When a master emancipates his slave, and he dies leaving no heir, then his estate goes to the manumittor, because the six imams narrated from ‘Aishah that when she bought Barirah, her family stipulated that her wala' would be for them. ‘Aishah asked the Prophet and he said: 'Emancipate her, for wala' belongs to the one who emancipates.' The reasoning is that the lam in 'al-wala'' is lam al-jins (of genus) and in 'li man a’taq' is lam al-ikhtiṣāṣ (of specification). Both indicate that the genus of wala' is for the manumittor and not for others, similar to the verse 'al-ḥamdu lillāh' where both lams indicate that all praise belongs exclusively to Allah. In any case, wala' belongs only to the manumittor and does not transfer. This is narrated from ‘Umar, ‘Ali, Zayd ibn Thabit, Ibn Mas‘ūd, Ubayy ibn Ka‘b, Ibn ‘Umar, Abu Mas‘ūd al-Badri, and Usāmah ibn Zayd. And ‘Aṭā’, Ṭāwūs, Sālim ibn ‘Abdillāh, al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī, Ibn Sīrīn, al-Sha‘bī, al-Nakha‘ī, al-Zuhrī, Qatādah, Abu al-Zinād, Ibn Nushayṭ, Sufyān al-Thawrī, Isḥāq, Abu Thawr, Imām Mālik, Imām Shāfi‘ī, and the Ḥanafīs all hold this view. And from Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal, a group also narrates this. Shuraih went against and said: 'Wala' is like wealth, inherited from the manumittor, so whoever owns something in his lifetime, it goes to his heirs after his death.'
His statement 'And because mutual support...' – The effects of establishing wala' are two: inheritance and ‘aql (blood wit). The proof for the second is 'and mutual support is through it in his blood wit', and for the first is 'and because gain is in return for loss'. The explanation of the proof is that through emancipation mutual support is obtained, so the master, having sought help from him, also bears the penalty for his error. Thus if the freedman kills someone by mistake, the master along with his clan bears the burden of his blood wit. Moreover, the master has given him life in a sense by removing his slave status, because slave status is an effect of disbelief, and disbelief is akin to death. Do you not see the verse: 'Or he who was dead and We gave him life' – this refers to the disbeliever whom We guided. And a slave is considered legally dead, so many rulings related to life are not established for him, such as being a qadi, bearing witness, attending Friday prayer, going to Eid prayers, ownership of wealth. Through emancipation, all these rulings are established for him. So this is, in a sense, his giving life. And whoever gives life to someone in a meaningful sense inherits from him. Thus wala' is from wali (guardian).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof (ruling_with_proof) from the commentary on the Hidayah, explaining the rulings of wala' al-itaqah (patronage from emancipation). The core ruling is that when a master frees his slave, the wala' belongs exclusively to the manumittor. The commentator provides three proofs: First, the hadith 'al-walā’ li man a‘taq' (wala' belongs to the one who emancipates), which is linguistically analysed using lam al-jins and lam al-ikhtiṣāṣ to indicate exclusivity. Second, the principle of mutual support (tanāṣur): emancipation creates a bond of mutual support, so the master bears the freedman's blood wit (is guilty). Third, the principle of 'al-ghunm bi al-ghurm' (reward is in proportion to liability): the master incurs loss by freeing the slave (forgoing his value and potential service), thus he is entitled to the gain (inheritance). The commentary elaborates on the first proof with the story of Barirah (reported by the six major hadith books), and lists numerous Companions and early scholars who held this view, contrasting with Shuraih who considered wala' as inheritable wealth. The explanation also includes a qiyas (analogy) to ihya' (giving life): removing slavery is like giving new legal life, and the one who gives life is like a parent who inherits. The passage also notes that emancipation with or without compensation is equal in establishing wala', based on the absolute wording of the hadith. The example of the daughter of Hamzah shows that wala' gives inheritance rights to the manumittor alongside other heirs (here the freed slave's daughter).

Key Terms
الغنم بالغرم (al-ghunm bi al-ghurm): The principle that reward is in proportion to liability; the master bears the loss of emancipation so he is entitled to the gain of inheritance.
عقل (‘aql): Blood wit (diyah); here it means the liability to pay blood money for a homicide committed by the freed slave.

Scholarly Consensus and Shuraih's Dissent on Wala'
Listing scholarly consensus and noting a dissenting view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the scholars who supported the exclusive wala' ruling and evaluate Shuraih's opposing view in light of the majority consensus.

Core Idea
The vast majority of Companions, Successors, and imams affirm that wala' is exclusive to the manumittor, against Shuraih's lone opinion.

After presenting the proofs, the text lists a host of eminent Companions and Successors who held that wala' is for the manumittor alone. This consensus (ijma' in effect) is contrasted with the view of Shuraih, a judge in Kufa, who argued that wala' can be inherited like property. Understanding this ikhtilaf helps students appreciate the strength of the Hanafi position and the rare dissenting view.

Guided Path
1
List the supporting scholars
The text names Companions: 'Umar, 'Ali, Zayd ibn Thabit, Ibn Mas'ud, Ubayy ibn Ka'b, Ibn 'Umar, Abu Mas'ud al-Badri, Usama ibn Zayd. Also Successors and imams: 'Ata, Tawus, Salim ibn 'Abdillah, Hasan al-Basri, Ibn Sirin, al-Sha'bi, al-Nakha'i, al-Zuhri, Qatada, Abu al-Zinad, Ibn Nushayt, Sufyan al-Thawri, Ishaq, Abu Thawr, Malik, Shafi'i, and the Hanafis.
This list shows the broad acceptance across regions and schools.
2
Identify Shuraih's dissent
Shuraih said: 'Wala' is like wealth, inherited from the manumittor; so whoever owns something in his lifetime, it goes to his heirs after his death.' This means he thought wala' passes to the manumittor's heirs.
Shuraih was a prominent judge (qadi) in Kufa, but his view did not gain traction.
3
Evaluate the strength of the consensus
The number and authority of those holding the exclusivity view is overwhelming. This constitutes a practical consensus (ijma' 'amali) if not explicit. The hadith also supports it.
Hanafi usul holds that consensus is a strong proof; here it reinforces the text.
4
Understand Hanafi reliance on the majority
The Hanafi school takes the position of the majority, and this is the mufta bihi. Shuraih's view is considered a minority (shadh) opinion.
In the hierarchy of proofs, the hadith and consensus outweigh Shuraih's analogy to property.

Effects of Wala': Inheritance and Blood Wit Liability
Explaining the two effects of wala'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the two effects of wala'—inheritance (mirath) and blood wit (aql)—in concrete scenarios.

Core Idea
The two concrete legal effects of wala' are that the manumittor inherits from the freed slave and is jointly liable for the slave's blood wit.

Having established that wala' is an exclusive bond, this lesson focuses on its practical consequences: inheritance and blood wit. The text explicitly mentions these two effects: the master inherits (يرثه) and bears blood wit (يعقله). We will examine how these operate in the presence of blood heirs, and the principle of mutual support (tanasur) that underlies the liability for blood wit. Understanding these effects is crucial for any practical application of the wala' rules.

Guided Path
1

2
Explain the inheritance effect
The manumittor inherits from the freed slave as an asabah (residuary heir) when there are no blood heirs who take by asabah. The example of Hamzah's daughter shows that if there is a daughter (a Quranic heir), the manumittor takes the residue (half) after the daughter's share.
Inheritance by wala' is subordinate to blood asabah.
3
Explain the blood wit effect
If the freed slave commits accidental homicide, the manumittor, along with the freed slave's clan (if any), must pay the blood wit (diyah). This is because wala' establishes a bond of mutual support similar to lineage.
The text says 'التناصر' (mutual support).
4
Note the impact of other heirs
If the freed slave leaves blood asabah (e.g., sons), they exclude the manumittor from inheritance. Similarly, for blood wit, the clan (asaba) share the liability with the manumittor.
The text does not elaborate, but from fiqh, if the freed slave has a son, the son is a stronger asabah.
5
Practice with a scenario

Use the principle that wala' gives residual inheritance and shared liability.

Financial Obligations of a Safih: Zakat and Nafaqah vs. Oaths and Vows
Rulings on mandatory financial duties versus voluntary expiations for a safih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that obligations imposed by God (zakat, nafaqah) are paid from the wealth of a safih, while obligations arising from his own voluntary acts (oaths, vows, zihar) are expiated by fasting to prevent waste.

Core Idea
Zakat and nafaqah are taken from the safih's wealth; his oaths, vows, and zihar are expiated by fasting.

A safih is a person whose wealth is interdicted due to foolishness. This lesson examines which financial obligations are enforced from his wealth and which are not. The key distinction is whether the obligation is imposed by God (like zakat and maintenance) or arises from his own choices (like broken oaths and zihar). The Hanafi school protects the safih from wasting wealth while ensuring essential duties are fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Categories
Separate obligations that must be paid from the safih's wealth (zakat, nafaqah) from those that must be expiated by fasting (oath, vow, zihar).
2
Understand the Reasoning for Wealth-Based Obligations
Zakat and nafaqah are wajib without his action; they are necessities for dependents. The qadi ensures payment, but for zakat, the safih's intention (niyyah) is required, so he handles it with a trustee. For nafaqah, no intention needed, so money goes directly to a trustee.
3
Analyze the Contrast for Voluntary Obligations
For oaths, vows, and zihar, allowing wealth payment would let the safih squander money intentionally, so they are expiated by fasting only.
This prevents abuse of his interdicted wealth.
4
Grasp the Core Principle
Obligations imposed without his will are enforced from wealth; those arising from his own will are not, to protect his wealth.
The principle is based on preventing waste (tafrit) and ensuring preservation (hifz).

And zakat is taken out from the wealth of the safih, because it is obligatory upon him. And he spends on his children, his wife, and those whose nafaqah is obligatory upon him from his relatives, because preserving his children and wife is among his necessities, and spending on a relative is a right of kinship, which foolishness does not nullify. But the qadi takes the zakat and gives it to him so that he spends it in the channels of zakat, because his intention is considered due to zakat being an act of worship. But he sends with him a trustee so that he does not waste it in other than its proper way. As for nafaqah, he gives it to his trustee so that he does not spend it in other than its proper way, because it is not an act of worship, so intention is not considered. And this is contrary to when he swears an oath, or makes a vow, or pronounces zihar on his wife: that wealth is not obligatory upon him, rather he expiates his oath and zihar by fasting, because they are obligatory by his own action. For if we open this door for him, he would waste his wealth in these without necessity. His statement: 'And this is contrary to what...' etc. - That is, what is mentioned in the text that zakat is paid from the wealth of the safih, and that he spends on his wife and children and those whose nafaqah is his responsibility, this ruling is contrary to when this safih swears an oath and breaks it, or makes a vow, or pronounces zihar on his wife: in these cases, wealth is not obligatory upon him. Rather, he expiates the oath and zihar by fasting, because these are matters that become obligatory by his own action. Now if we open the door of payment from wealth, he would waste his wealth in this manner, because this action is within his control, unlike that which becomes obligatory initially without his action, which is not within his control.

Commentary
This passage covers two distinct rulings with a contrast. First: Zakat and nafaqah must be paid from the safih's wealth. Zakat is an obligatory act of worship (ibadah) that does not depend on his choice, so the qadi ensures it is paid. He takes the zakat, gives it to the safih, but sends a trustee to prevent misuse, because the safih's intention (niyyah) is required for zakat's validity. Nafaqah (maintenance) is not worship, so no niyyah needed; the qadi gives the money directly to a trustee who spends on the rightful recipients. Second: In contrast, obligations arising from the safih's own actions—breaking an oath (yamin), making a vow (nadhr), or zihar—are not paid from his wealth. Instead, he must expiate by fasting, because these are within his control; allowing monetary payment would enable him to waste wealth deliberately. The key principle: obligations imposed without his act (like zakat and nafaqah) are paid from wealth; obligations arising from his own volitional acts are not, to prevent abuse.

Key Terms
نفقہ (nafaqah): Maintenance/support for wife, children, and needy relatives, which is obligatory on the safih despite interdiction.
ظہار (zihar): A pre-Islamic form of divorce where a husband likens his wife to his mother's back; it is a sin requiring expiation (kafarah).
