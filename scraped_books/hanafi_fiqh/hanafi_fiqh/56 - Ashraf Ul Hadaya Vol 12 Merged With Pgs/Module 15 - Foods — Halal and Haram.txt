Coercion to Eat Carrion or Drink Wine: Ibahah vs Rukhsah
Fasl on when coercive threats permit consuming prohibited items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ibahah and rukhsah in Hanafi law and state the ruling on refusing carrion under coercion that threatens life or limb.

Core Idea
When necessity lifts the prohibition entirely, refusing is sinful; when it only grants a concession, refusing may be praiseworthy.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute: does coercive threat that endangers life or limb make carrion and wine mubah (permitted) or merely a rukhsa (concession)? The answer determines whether refusing is sinful or commendable. We'll explore the proofs and the practical consequences of the disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the coercion threshold
The act is consuming carrion, wine, blood, or pork under coercion. The threshold for permissibility is fear of death or loss of a limb. Lesser threats do not permit it.
2
State the general Hanafi position
When coercion reaches the level of necessity (idtirar), consuming becomes permissible. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) consider this ibahah (permission with the prohibition lifted).
3
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verse (e.g., 2:173) states that there is no sin on one who is forced by necessity, acting without transgression. This establishes a clear exception.
The verse is qati' al-thubut and qati' al-dalalah, so it overrides the general prohibition.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Yusuf holds that it is a rukhsah (concession) with the prohibition remaining, so refusing is not sinful. The majority responds that the exception removes the prohibition entirely, making it ibahah, so refusing is sinful because it aids self-destruction.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: it is ibahah, and a person who refuses when aware of the permission is sinful. This is the view adopted in the Zahir al-Riwayah and supported by the commentator.

Section: If he is coerced to eat carrion or drink wine, being coerced by imprisonment, beating, or shackling, it is not permitted for him unless he is coerced by something he fears for his life or a limb. When he fears that, it is permissible for him to proceed with what he is coerced to do. The same applies to blood and pork, because consuming these prohibited items is only permitted in necessity, as in the state of extreme hunger, because the prohibition remains in effect beyond it. Necessity only exists when he fears for life or a limb. Even if he is threatened with severe beating and it is likely in his opinion, it becomes permissible for him, and it is not permissible for him to endure what is threatened. If he endures until they carry it out and he does not eat, he is sinful because when it was made permissible, his refraining makes him an aider to others in destroying his own self, so he is sinful, as in the state of extreme hunger. From Abu Yusuf: He is not sinful because it is a concession (rukhsah), as the prohibition remains, so he acted upon the resolve (azimah). We (the mainstream Hanafis) say: The state of necessity is an exception by the text (nass), and it is speech about the remainder after the exception, so there is no prohibition. Thus it is a permission (ibahah), not a concession. However, he is only sinful if he knows the permissibility in this situation, because the removal of prohibition is obscure, so he is excused by ignorance, like ignorance of the law in early Islam or in the abode of war.

Commentary
This is a new section (Fasl) of the Kitab al-Ikrah, moving from coercion in rights of individuals (huquq al-ibad) to coercion in rights of Allah (huquq Allah). The passage specifically addresses the case where someone is forced to eat carrion (maytah) or drink wine. The ruling is that such coercion does not make these items permissible unless the threat is to life or a limb (like severe beating or imprisonment with fear of death or loss of limb). If the threat is less severe (e.g., mere beating without such fear), it remains prohibited. When the fear reaches the level of darurah (necessity), then consuming becomes permissible (ibahah), not merely a concession (rukhsah). The text draws an analogy to the state of extreme hunger (makhmasah). The author explains that if the person endures the punishment and does not eat, he becomes sinful because by refraining he is aiding his own destruction. However, Imam Abu Yusuf holds that he is not sinful because the prohibition remains and he is acting with resolve (azimah) by not taking the concession. The mainstream Hanafi position (the text says 'qulna' meaning 'we say') disagrees: the state of necessity is an exception established by the Quranic text (the verse permitting carrion in dire necessity), and after the exception, the prohibition is removed, so it is a clear permission (ibahah), not a mere concession. Therefore, refusing it is aiding in self-destruction, which is sinful. But this sin applies only if the person is aware of the permission; if he is ignorant (e.g., a new Muslim or in dar al-harb), he is excused. This is a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Yusuf and the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the majority view that it is sinful to refuse when one knows the permissibility.

Key Terms
اضطرار (idtirar): A state of dire necessity that makes prohibited items permissible.
رخصة (rukhsah): A concession allowing an otherwise prohibited act while the original prohibition remains.
عزيمة (azimah): The original, stricter ruling that is generally applicable.

Key Takeaway

Eating Haram Under Coercion: Ruling and Sin of Refusal
Expansion on ruling with proof and dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating haram under coercion and explain why refusal when under death threat is sinful.

Core Idea
When under threat of death, eating haram becomes wajib; refusing is sinful as it is self-destruction.

The passage expands on a well-known fiqh ruling: what happens when someone is forced to eat pork or drink wine under threat? The key is the level of threat – mere beatings do not permit, but fear of death or loss of a limb does. The author also addresses a dispute with Imam Abu Yusuf and an objection about the term 'mubah' used in the original text.

Guided Path
1
Classify the coercion
Identify the type of threat: if it is fear of death or loss of a limb, darurah applies; mere beatings or imprisonment do not reach the threshold.
This aligns with the Hanafi strict definition of darurah.
2
State the ruling for genuine threat
When the threat is to life or limb, eating the haram substance becomes permissible (mubah), and according to the relied-upon position, it even becomes wajib (obligatory) to preserve life.
The text says 'yubah' but later explains it is actually obligatory.
3
Determine the consequence of refusal
If the coerced person refuses to eat and dies, they are sinful (gunahgar) because they chose to die when a permissible (now halal) option could have saved them.
This is the mufta bihi view; Abu Yusuf disagrees.
4
Analyze the intra-madhhab dispute
Abu Yusuf argued that not eating is 'azimah (the original ruling of avoidance) and therefore choosing a higher path should not be sinful. The majority response is that necessity transforms the ruling, and refusal amounts to suicide.
The Quranic verse al-An'am 6:119 is the key proof.
5
Resolve the definitional objection
The author of al-Nata'ij notes that calling the act 'mubah' contradicts usul because when life is at stake, the act is actually wajib. The commentator implies the term is used loosely, but the substantive ruling is clear.
This is a refinement in terminology, not in the actual hukm.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the ruling on eating haram foods under coercion. The commentator explains that mere threats of beatings or imprisonment do not make eating pork, carrion, wine, or blood permissible because necessity only arises when there is fear of death or loss of a limb. If the threat is one of killing or cutting a limb, then consuming these is permissible. Moreover, if the coerced person refuses to eat and dies, he is sinful according to Imam Malik, Imam Shafi'i, and one narration of Imam Ahmad. Imam Abu Yusuf narrates that he is not sinful, but the majority position is that he is sinful because necessity makes the forbidden permissible; refusing a permissible act that saves one's life and then dying is akin to suicide. The commentator also addresses an objection by the author of al-Nata'ij regarding the use of the term 'mubah': when life is at stake, the act of eating becomes obligatory, not merely permissible. The Hanafi position, supported by the Quranic verse (al-An'am 6:119), is that necessity overrides prohibition, and refusal under genuine threat is sinful.

Key Terms
ضرورت (darurah): Necessity that permits otherwise forbidden acts, specifically fear of death or loss of limb.
رخصت (rukhsah): A concession that allows an otherwise prohibited act due to necessity.
عزیمت (azimah): The original ruling without concession; choosing not to eat under coercion.
