Coercion to Kill: The Hanafi Position on Qisas Liability
Intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute on qisas liability in coercion to kill.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas liability in coercion to kill and distinguish it from the positions of Zufar, Abu Yusuf, and al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Under Hanafi law, the coercer bears qisas; the coerced is merely an instrument.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a critical question: when a person is forced to kill another under threat of death, who is liable for retaliation (qisas)? The answer is not immediately obvious, and the Hanafi school itself has internal differences. We will examine the relied-upon Hanafi position and contrast it with other views within and outside the school. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the principles of causation (tasbib) and coercion (ikrah) in criminal liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the initial ruling
The passage first states that one must not kill under coercion—the act remains sinful. This establishes the moral gravity. Then the question of qisas arises. Focus on exactly what is being disputed: who is liable for the intentional killing?
2
List the four possible positions
The commentary divides the logical possibilities: qisas on the coercer only, on the coerced only, on both, or on neither. Each is represented by an imam. Trace which imam adopts which view.
The positions: Abu Hanifa & Muhammad → coercer only; Zufar → coerced only; Abu Yusuf → neither; al-Shafi'i → both.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad)
The Hanafi position relies on the concept of tasbib (causation). The coercer is the true cause; the coerced acts as an instrument under compulsion. Thus, the act is attributed to the coercer.
This reasoning is based on the principle that a mukrah (coerced) lacks free choice; his action is not his own.
4
Examine the opposing proofs
Zufar focuses on the direct act of the coerced. Abu Yusuf invokes doubt (shubha) due to conflicting causal factors. Al-Shafi'i gives weight to both direct action and causation. Understand each dalil in brief.
These are all legitimate ijtihad; the Hanafi school has given tarjih to the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The passage indicates that the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is the relied-upon view. In practice, only the coercer faces qisas. Know how to state this rule and its basis.
This is the view adopted by the majority of Hanafi fuqaha and is applied in fatwa.

Commentary
This passage focuses on the case of coercion to kill. It first reiterates the ruling: killing under duress is not permissible; one must endure patience. The commentary then discusses the dispute about qisas: who is liable for retaliation? The text lists four positions: 1) both (coercer and coerced) according to al-Shafi'i (and possibly others), 2) neither according to Abu Yusuf, 3) only on the coercer (the relied-upon Hanafi position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), and 4) only on the coerced according to Zufar. The passage then explains the reasoning behind each. The Hanafi position (of the majority, 'tarafayn') is that the coercer is the real cause and the coerced is an instrument, so qisas is on the coercer. Abu Yusuf argues that because both causation and direct action are present, doubt prevents qisas. Shafi'i holds both are effective. Zufar sees the direct action by the coerced as the basis. The commentary refutes the opposing views with arguments from analogy (e.g., the coercer is like a tool).

Key Terms
مكرَه (mukrah): The coerced killer; his act is considered that of an instrument.
مكرِه (mukrih): The coercer; the one who causes the killing through threat.
تسبيب (tasbib): Causation; the act of setting in motion a chain leading to the result.

Ikrah in Qisas: Hanafi vs Shafi'i and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains ikhtilaf on qisas under duress and within Hanafi school

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas under duress (ikrah), explain the role of shubah (doubt) in averting qisas, and compare the Hanafi position with that of Imam Shafi'i and Imam Zufar.

Core Idea
Qisas is averted by shubah when duress splits attribution between two parties.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a critical question: if a person is forced under threat of death to kill someone, should he face qisas? The Hanafi school answers that qisas is not imposed on either the coercer or the coerced due to shubah (doubt) arising from dual attribution. Imam Shafi'i and, within the Hanafi school, Imam Zufar disagree, holding that the coerced is directly liable. We will examine the proofs, focusing on the analogy with ikrah on emancipation and slaughter, and understand the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): qisas is not wajib on either the coercer or the coerced; instead, diyah is paid from their property.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam Shafi'i holds qisas wajib on the coerced because he directly causes death. Imam Zufar (within Hanafi) similarly holds qisas on the coerced because the act exists from him in reality and physically.
Imam Shafi'i equates causing (tasbib) with direct commission.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The coerced is the direct killer; the Shari'ah confirms the ruling of killing upon him, so he is sinful. For Shafi'i, that entails qisas. Zufar argues that since the act is existent from the coerced, qisas is due.
Both opponents rely on the principle that the direct doer bears full responsibility.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the act is attributed to both parties: to the coerced as the direct killer and to the coercer as the instigator. This dual attribution creates shubah (doubt), which averts qisas. The analogy with ikrah on emancipation (itq) and slaughter (dhabh) shows that the property aspect (itlaf) is transferred to the coercer, while the moral sin stays with the coerced.
The coerced is not a mere instrument; he acts by choice to save his life, so he remains sinful but not subject to qisas.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Under Hanafi law, if someone is forced to kill, he does not face qisas but may pay diyah with the coercer. Under Shafi'i, the coerced would face qisas. Under Zufar's view, only the coerced faces qisas, and the coercer is not liable for qisas.
The Hanafi position balances the sin with the duress, avoiding the extreme of execution.

Shafi'i also adheres to this (that the sin is upon the coerced) and makes qisas obligatory upon the coerced (mukrah) due to the presence of the cause of killing from his side. And according to Imam Shafi‘i, in such a situation, the ruling of causing (tasbib) is like committing the act itself, as in the case of witnesses to qisas. The proof of Imam Abu Yusuf is that the ruling of killing is confined to the coerced (mukrah) from one aspect due to his being sinful, and from another aspect it is attributed to the coercer (mukrih) because he is the instigator and cause. Thus doubt (shubah) arises on both sides. The proof of the two (Sahibayn) is that he (the coerced) is compelled to kill by his natural inclination, preferring his own life, so he becomes an instrument (ala) of the coercer in that which can be an instrument, i.e., the killing, in the sense that he throws himself upon him, but he cannot be an instrument in committing sin against his religion. So the act of killing remains confined to the coerced in terms of sin. Just as you say in the case of ikrah on emancipation, and just as you say in the case of a Muslim coercing a Magian to slaughter another's goat, that the act of destruction (itlaf) is transferred to the coercer, not the slaughter, to the extent that its meat is forbidden. So likewise here. Explanation: His statement 'for Zufar that the act...' etc. The proof of Imam Zufar is that the act of killing exists from him both in reality and physically. Also, the Shari‘ah has confirmed the ruling of killing upon him. Thus he is sinful. Imam Shafi‘i's adherence to the obligation of qisas upon the coerced is the same. As for the obligation of qisas upon the coercer, it is because he became the cause of the killing, as he is the instigator and cause. And in such a situation according to Imam Shafi‘i, the ruling of causing (tasbib) is like direct commission. The proof of Imam Abu Yusuf is that qisas is attributed to the coercer in one sense because he is the direct killer, and in another sense to the coerced because he is the instigator and cause, so doubt is established on both sides. Therefore, qisas is not obligatory on either of them, but rather diyah is paid from their property. His statement 'and for them (Sahibayn) that he is carried...' etc. The proof of the two (Sahibayn) is that the coerced is compelled to kill another by natural inclination to preserve his own life, and whoever is carried by nature to an act is an instrument, because an instrument is that which acts by nature, like a sword whose nature is to cut when used in a place, similarly fire's nature to burn and water's nature to drown. Since this is so, acting by natural inclination resembles an instrument. And it is clear that if a killer uses his instrument, i.e., a sword, unjustly on a person's life and kills him, qisas is obligatory on the killer. So likewise here, qisas would be obligatory on the coerced because he became the coercer's instrument in that which can be an instrument, i.e., killing, in the sense that he throws himself upon him. In any case, qisas will be on the coerced, not diyah nor kaffarah, because the act is attributed to the doer, not to the instrument. His statement 'and he is not suitable as an instrument for him...' etc. This is the answer to an implied question. The question is: if the coerced were an instrument, then the sin of killing would also be attributed to the coercer, but that is not the case. The answer is that the coerced cannot be an instrument for the coercer in committing sin against his religion, because killing involves two things: first, cutting the throat of the victim; in this the coercer has made the coerced his instrument, and he can be an instrument. Second, the sin necessarily accompanying the killing; in this he cannot be an instrument for the coercer, because the coercer only compelled him so that he would commit sin against his religion. (And if that transferred to the coercer, it would contradict the coercer's intent.) So the act, from the angle of the deed, is attributed to the coerced, and from the angle of sin remains confined to the coerced, i.e., the coerced himself is sinful. Like the issue of ikrah on emancipation. Page 244. Ashraf al-Hidayah Urdu commentary of Hidayah volume 12. The issue is: for example, Zayd compelled Khalid to free his slave, and Khalid freed him. You say that his freeing, in the sense that it destroys the slave's value, is transferred to the coercer, to the extent that the coercer (Zayd) is guarantor for the slave's price, but as a pronouncement of emancipation it remains confined to the coerced (Khalid), to the extent that the slave's wala goes to him. Similarly, in the issue of a Muslim compelling a Magian to slaughter another's goat, you say that the act of destruction (itlaf) is attributed to the coercer (the Muslim), to the extent that he is guarantor for the goat's price, but the slaughter is not attributed to him; rather it remains confined to the coerced (the Magian), to the extent that its meat is unlawful. So likewise here is the ruling of killing.
Source pages p. 244

Commentary
This is an inter-madhhab and intra-Hanafi dispute regarding qisas (retaliation) under duress (ikrah). The passage presents multiple positions: Imam Shafi'i holds that qisas is wajib upon the coerced (mukrah) because he directly causes the death, and the coercer (mukrih) is also liable as a cause. Among the Hanafis, Imam Zufar agrees with Shafi'i that qisas is on the mukrah. However, Imam Abu Yusuf and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argue that qisas is not wajib on either due to shubah (doubt) arising from the dual attribution: the act is attributed to the mukrah as the direct doer but also to the mukrih as the instigator. Their proof is analogical: just as in ikrah on emancipation (itq) or slaughter (dhabh), the property loss (itlaf) is attributed to the coercer while the verbal act or the slaughter itself remains with the coerced, similarly here the killing is attributed to both. Since the Shari'ah requires certainty for qisas, the shubah prevents it. The relied-upon Hanafi position is that of the Sahibayn: no qisas, but diyah is paid from the property of both parties. The mukrah remains sinful because he chose to preserve his life at the expense of another's life. The passage also includes a refutation of Zufar's argument that the mukrah is merely an instrument (ala) like a sword; the Hanafi response is that the mukrah cannot be an instrument for sin because sin requires intentional choice.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrah): Compelling someone to do an act against his will by threat of harm; duress.
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliatory punishment equal to the crime, specifically taking life for life in cases of intentional murder.
الشبهة (al-shubah): Doubt or ambiguity that prevents the application of a fixed penalty (hadd or qisas).

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, qisas is averted under duress due to shubah (doubt) from dual attribution; diyah is paid and the coerced remains sinful.
