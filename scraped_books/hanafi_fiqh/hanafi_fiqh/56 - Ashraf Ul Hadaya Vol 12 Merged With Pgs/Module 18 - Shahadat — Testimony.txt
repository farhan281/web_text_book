Hajr on the Safih: Dispute Between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute on interdiction of a prodigal adult

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state both Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on interdiction of a safih, explain their respective proofs (including qiyas and Quran), and identify the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A free sane adult safih is either fully competent or interdicted based on whether the harm of dehumanization outweighs the harm of waste.

This passage dives into one of the most famous intra-Hanafi disputes: whether a free, sane adult who squanders his wealth (safih) should be interdicted from managing his property. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that his humanity and legal capacity cannot be stripped away, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that the actual waste justifies interdiction to protect his own interest. The discussion also engages the Shafi'i school and uses both Quranic evidence and qiyas. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to weigh competing harms and apply usul al-fiqh principles.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi intra-school split
Identify the two positions: Abu Hanifa (no hajr on free sane adult safih) vs. Sahibayn (hajr is imposed). Note that the Sahibayn also agree with Imam al-Shafi'i.
This is a case where the madhhab's founding imam and his two main disciples differ.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
They cite Quran 4:5 ('Do not give your property to the foolish') and argue by qiyas on a child: since a child is interdicted for potential waste, the safih (actual waste) deserves it more strongly.
Pay attention to the concept of 'actual vs. potential' waste and the argument that mere hand-to-hand prevention is ineffective because the safih can waste by tongue (verbal gifts, acknowledgments).
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the safih is a mukhatab (addressee of divine commands) and sane; stripping his authority negates his humanity, which is a greater harm than waste. He analogizes to the rashid (mature person) who is not interdicted.
Notice the use of the 'greater harm' principle and the exception: if the safih causes general public harm (like a quack doctor or insolent mufti), even Abu Hanifa allows interdiction.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Abu Hanifa, the safih's sales, purchases, and gifts are valid. Under the Sahibayn, such transactions are void unless in his clear interest or non-financial (e.g., divorce, manumission).
This shows how the dispute directly affects the validity of everyday contracts.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
Although the matn opens with Abu Hanifa's view, the relied-upon position for fatwa in the Hanafi school is that of the Sahibayn. The chapter's structure and later commentary indicate this tarjih.
Remember: later Hanafi authorities (e.g., Ibn Abidin) adopt the Sahibayn's view as the fatwa basis.

Interdiction on the safih (foolish) is not permitted. Abu Hanifa said: Interdiction is not placed upon a free, sane, adult safih, and his disposition of his property is valid, even if he is wasteful and corrupt, destroying his property in matters where he has no purpose or benefit. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said, and it is the saying of al-Shafi'i: Interdiction is placed upon the safih, and he is prevented from disposing of his property. [Translation of Urdu commentary follows:] Imam Abu Hanifa says that a free, sane, adult person will not be interdicted due to his safah, rather his disposal of his property will be valid even if he is so wasteful that he spends his money on things with no benefit, e.g., throwing it in the river or burning it. The opinion of 'Ubaydullah ibn al-Hasan and Ibrahim al-Nakha'i is similar to Abu Hanifa. The Sahibayn and the three Imams hold that the safih should be interdicted and prevented from dispositions that are destructive, like sale, purchase, and lease, but not from matters that do not involve loss, such as divorce, manumission, acknowledgment of hudud and qisas, and marriage. The Sahibayn's evidence: because he wastes his property by spending it in a manner not required by reason, so he is interdicted for his own benefit, by analogy to a child, rather more so because in the case of a child there is only the possibility of waste, while in his case it is actual; for this reason the property was withheld from him. Then mere prevention without interdiction is useless because he would destroy with his tongue what is prevented from his hand. For Abu Hanifa: he is a responsible sane person, so he should not be interdicted by analogy to the mature person. This is because stripping him of his authority negates his humanity and equates him with beasts, which is a greater harm than waste; therefore the greater harm is not borne except to repel the lesser. Even if interdiction repels a general harm, like interdiction on the ignorant physician, the insolent mufti, and the bankrupt carter, it is permitted in what is narrated from him, because it is repelling the greater harm with the lesser. And qiyas on withholding property is not valid because interdiction is more severe in punishment, nor on the child because he is incapable of looking after himself, while this one is capable; shari'a has once looked after him by giving him the means of capacity, and his going contrary to it is due to his bad choice. Withholding property is beneficial because most safah occurs in gifts, donations, and charities, which depend on the hand. [Further commentary explains the proofs in detail.]

Commentary
This passage presents the classic Hanafi dispute on whether a safih (a spendthrift adult who wastes his wealth) can be interdicted (hajr) from managing his own property. The text begins by stating the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa that no interdiction is placed on a free, sane, adult safih, and his financial transactions are valid, regardless of how recklessly he spends. Then it presents the position of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) along with Imam al-Shafi'i, who hold that such a person should be interdicted. The passage then expands into a detailed discussion of the evidence for both sides. For the Sahibayn, the reasoning is qiyas (analogy) upon a child: just as a child is interdicted because of his inability to manage wealth, a safih is even more deserving because his wastefulness is actual, not merely potential. They also argue that simply preventing him from handing over money (without interdiction) is ineffective because he can still waste it verbally through gifts and acknowledgments. For Imam Abu Hanifa, the key argument is that an adult sane person is addressed by divine commands (mukhatab) and has the capacity to reason; to strip him of his authority (wilayah) over his property is to negate his humanity and liken him to animals, which is a greater harm than the waste itself. He also uses qiyas upon the rashid (mature person) who is not interdicted. However, an exception is noted: if the safih's actions cause general harm (like a quack doctor or insolent mufti), interdiction is allowed to repel the greater public harm. The passage concludes with a commentary that clarifies the meaning of 'khitab' (address) and the basis for legal capacity. The presentation follows the standard Hidayah structure: matn (base text) followed by expansion of each proof.

Key Terms
حجر (hajr): Legal interdiction preventing a person from disposing of his property due to incapacity or irresponsibility.
سفیه (safih): A free adult male who is wasteful with his property, spending on things with no valid purpose or benefit.
رشید (rashid): A mature person who manages his property wisely; used as the analogy for not interdicing the safih.
مخاطب (mukhatab): One who is addressed by divine commands; a person with legal capacity to receive obligations.

Accepting a Near-Pubescent's Claim of Puberty
Ruling on accepting the claim of a near-pubescent boy/girl about puberty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting a claim of puberty from a near-pubescent boy/girl, identify the condition of plausibility, and explain the qiyas with a woman's menstruation claim.

Core Idea
A near-pubescent's claim of puberty is accepted like a woman's menstruation claim—internal knowledge is trusted unless outward signs contradict.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hadaya addresses a practical scenario: when a boy or girl approaches puberty but their status is uncertain. The Hanafi school rules that their own statement is sufficient to establish adulthood, drawing an analogy to a woman's claim about menstruation. The key condition is that the apparent state—including age—must not contradict the claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actors and the claim
A boy or girl nearing puberty (murahiq) claims to have reached bulugh. The text explicitly considers this scenario.
The ages of 12 for boys and 9 for girls are established as minima in later fatwa works.
2
Note the default ruling
Their statement is accepted, and they are treated as adults in all legal rulings (e.g., obligation of prayers, zakah, marriage).
This is the primary hukm: the claim is credible.
3
Unpack the proof (qiyas)
The reason given is that puberty is an internal matter known only to the individual. This is analogous to a woman's claim of menstruation, which is accepted based on Quran 2:228.
The qiyas is khafiy (subtle) because the illa is personal knowledge of an internal state.
4
Apply the condition (al-zahir)
The claim is accepted only if the apparent state (zahir) does not contradict it. This means the person must be at an age where puberty is biologically possible (boy ≥12, girl ≥9) and no outward signs (e.g., complete lack of physical development) suggest otherwise.
If the apparent state belies the claim, the statement is rejected.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hadaya explains the ruling on accepting the claim of puberty from a boy or girl who is approaching the age of maturity. The matn states that if such a person's state is ambiguous and they claim to have reached puberty, their statement is accepted and they are treated as adults. The reason given is that puberty is an internal matter that can only be known by the individual themselves. The commentary adds the condition that the apparent state must not contradict the claim: for example, a boy under twelve or a girl under nine cannot be believed because biologically it is impossible. The ruling is supported by analogy (qiyas) to a woman's claim about menstruation, which is accepted based on the Quranic verse (al-Baqarah:228). This principle applies as long as the age is plausible (i.e., above the minimum age for possible puberty). The commentary references Fatawa Qadi Khan and Fatawa Zahiriyya for the minimum ages of plausibility: 12 for boys and 9 for girls.

Authorized Slave's Acknowledgment: Valid for Trade Debts
Ruling on authorized slave's iqrar with rational proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an authorized slave's acknowledgment of debts and distinguish between trade-related and non-trade acknowledgments.

Core Idea
An authorized slave's iqrar is valid only for trade-related debts to protect his business.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a practical question: can an authorized slave (servant given permission to trade) acknowledge debts or deposits? The answer is yes, but only for trade-related obligations. The reasoning is grounded in the need to facilitate commerce – if people could not rely on his word, they would avoid dealing with him. However, this permission does not extend to debts arising from non-trade causes, where he is treated like a legally incapacitated person (mahjur).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is the slave's iqrar (acknowledgment) of debts, wrongful possessions, and deposits. The general ruling is that it is valid (jā'iz). What conditions must be met?
2
Extract the condition of health vs. illness
The text distinguishes between iqrar during health (ṣiḥḥa) and during terminal illness (marad). During health, the acknowledgment is valid regardless of existing debts. During illness, health debts take priority – similar to a free man's iqrar.
This prioritization prevents fraud against creditors.
3
Identify the rationale (illah)
The illah given is that iqrar is an ancillary of trade (min tawābiʿ al-tijāra). Without validity, people would avoid transactions – causing harm to the slave's business and indirectly to the master.
This is a rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī): the rule is derived from the purpose of trade facilitation.
4
Distinguish trade-related vs. non-trade iqrar
The text states that iqrar for a debt not arising from trade (lā bi-sabab al-tijāra) is invalid. In that regard, the slave is like a mahjur (legally interdicted person) because he has no authority for such obligations.
This preserves the slave's limited capacity: he can only bind himself in trade matters.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Example: An authorized slave acknowledges he borrowed money for inventory. This is valid. If he acknowledges he caused property damage (ghuṣb) unrelated to trade, it is valid only if it happened during trade? The text says iqrar for ghuṣb is valid – likely because ghuṣb can occur in trade contexts. But for non-trade torts, perhaps different.
The text explicitly includes ghuṣb as valid; this may be because it often arises from trade dealings.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling on an authorized slave's acknowledgment (iqrar) of debts, wrongful possessions (ghusb), and deposits (wadi'ah). The ruling is that such acknowledgment is valid, provided it is made while he is healthy. The reason given is that acknowledgment is a necessary part of trade (tawabi' al-tijarah); otherwise, people would avoid transacting with him, causing harm to his business. There is no distinction between whether he already has debt or not, as long as the acknowledgment is during his health. If acknowledged during illness, then debts from health (i.e., ordinary trade debts) take priority. However, acknowledgment of a debt that arises from a non-trade cause (e.g., a tort or personal guarantee) is not valid because in that regard he is like a forcibly confined person (mahjur), i.e., not authorized. This shows the Hanafi concern for the smooth functioning of trade and the protection of the slave's business capacity.

Key Terms
اقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment of a liability or right to another.
محجور (mahjur): A person under legal interdiction, like a minor or insane; here, the slave in non-trade matters is analogized to a mahjur.
