Scope of Authorization: Type vs Item
Dispute on scope of authorization and distinction between type and item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between permission for a type (which generalizes authorization) and permission for a specific item (which is employment), and state the Hanafi ruling with its evidence.

Core Idea
Permission for a type authorizes all trades; permission for an item is employment, not authorization.

This passage examines a key dispute in Hanafi fiqh regarding the scope of authorization when a master permits his slave to engage in trade. Does permission for a specific type (e.g., cloth trade) restrict the slave to that type, or does it grant general authorization? The Hanafi school takes a broad view, while Zufar and al-Shafi'i hold a restrictive view. The text also clarifies a related distinction: if the master orders a specific item, it is not authorization at all but a command for service.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
The issue: when a master permits a specific type of trade, is the slave authorized only in that type or in all types? The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) says all types; Zufar and al-Shafi'i say only that type.
The same disagreement applies if the master forbids a type.
2
Analyze the opponents' proof
Zufar and al-Shafi'i argue that authorization is a delegation (tawkil) from the master, similar to agency (wakala). Just as a mudarib is restricted to the specified capital and trade, the authorized slave is restricted to the permitted type.
They analogize to mudaraba partnership.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school argues that authorization is not agency but a removal of interdiction (fakk al-hajr). The slave's own ownership (milk) becomes apparent, so he is free to trade in all types. Unlike an agent, the slave owns the profit and can use it for his own needs.
The slave's capacity is based on his own legal personality, not the master's delegation.
4
Distinguish the case of a specific item
If the master orders a specific item (e.g., 'buy this cloth'), it is not authorization but a service command (istikhdam). The slave does not become a madhun. If the master demands money or says 'become a dyer,' that is authorization because it requires earning.
This preserves the master's ability to order services without granting general trading authority.
5
Summarize the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Hanafi, the slave can engage in any trade if authorized for a type; under Zufar/Shafi'i, only the specified type. For a specific item, both agree the slave is not a madhun.
Practical consequence: the scope of the slave's trading activities.

If he gave permission for a specific type, he will be authorized in all types; statements of jurists: He said, 'If he permits him for a type without others, then he is authorized in all of them.' Zufar and Shafi'i said he is only authorized in that type, and the same disagreement applies if he forbids him from dealing in another type. Their proof is that authorization is a deputyship (tawkil) and delegation from the master, because he acquires authority from him, and the ruling (ownership) is established for the master, not the slave; thus the master can restrict him, so it is limited to what he specified, like a mudarib. He said: And if he permits him for a specific item, he is not an authorized slave, because it is employment (istikhdam). Or if he said to him, 'Become a dyer or a washerman,' because it is permission to buy what is necessary for both, which is a type, so he becomes authorized in all types.

Commentary
This passage deals with the scope of authorization (idhn) for an authorized slave. It begins with a dispute: if the master permits a specific type of trade (e.g., cloth trade), according to the Hanafi school (Imam Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad), the slave becomes authorized in all types of trade. Zufar and Shafi'i restrict it to that specific type. The proof from the opponents is that authorization is like agency (wakala) from the master, so it is limited to what is specified, like a mudarib in a partnership. The Hanafi proof is that authorization removes the hijr (interdiction) and reveals the slave's own ownership; thus, he is not restricted. The text then distinguishes a different case: if the master permits a specific item (e.g., 'buy this specific cloth'), the slave is not considered an authorized slave at all; it is simply a command for service (istikhdam), because the master needs that specific item. However, if the command is for earning (e.g., 'give me profit each month'), it becomes authorization. This distinction shows the subtlety of Hanafi fiqh in defining the scope of the slave's legal capacity.

Key Terms
اذن (idhn): Permission from the master to the slave to engage in trade, making him a madhun.
استخدام (istikhdam): Employment/service; when the master orders a specific purchase, it is not authorization but a service command.
ماذون (madhun): Authorized slave who can trade on his own account.
