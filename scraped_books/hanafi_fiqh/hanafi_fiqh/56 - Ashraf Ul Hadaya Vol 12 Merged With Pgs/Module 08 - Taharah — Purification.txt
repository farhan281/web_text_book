Definition and Ruling of Kitabah: The Contract of Gradual Manumission
Defines kitabah and its immediate effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of kitabah, identify its essential components (offer, acceptance, specified sum), and explain the Hanafi legal definition of a mukatab as 'free in action, slave in body'.

Core Idea
Kitabah grants immediate freedom of earnings but holds ownership until payment — a unique gradual emancipation.

The passage introduces the contract of kitabah (manumission by written agreement). We will examine the ruling from al-Quduri's matn, the legal definition from classical texts, and the linguistic analysis of the term 'mukatab'. Understanding this contract is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to gradual freedom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its parties
The kitabah contract involves a master (mukatib) and a slave (mukatab). The master stipulates a sum of money, and the slave accepts. Both participate in the writing, hence the pattern mufa'alah.
The master is mukatib (one who writes), the slave is mukatab (one written for).
2
Identify the essential stipulations
The master must stipulate a specific sum of money as the price of freedom. The slave must accept this offer. Without a specified sum or acceptance, no valid kitabah occurs.
The sum can be paid in installments; the contract is written.
3
State the legal consequence
Upon valid contract, the slave becomes a mukatab. According to the legal definition: 'freeing the hand immediately and the body upon payment.' This means the slave gains immediate control over his own earnings (yad) but remains a slave in terms of ownership (raqabah) until the sum is fully paid.
This dual status distinguishes kitabah from immediate 'itaq.
4
Understand the bilateral nature
The word 'kataba' from باب مفاعلة indicates mutual participation. The master writes the contract and grants freedom conditionally; the slave pays installments. Both contribute to the process.
This bilaterality is why the chapter is placed after ijarah (lease), not after 'itaq.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal concept of kitabah (contract of manumission) and states its basic ruling. The lesson type is a definition with ruling. The author first quotes the matn of al-Quduri: 'If a man makes a contract of kitabah with his male or female slave for a sum of money which he stipulates, and the slave accepts, then he becomes a mukatab.' This establishes that kitabah is a bilateral contract requiring offer, acceptance, and a specified consideration. The commentary then explains why this book comes after ijarah: both involve a contract where wealth is obtained in exchange for usufruct or service, and both require offer and acceptance. A critique of al-Kafi's arrangement is given - kitabah is more akin to ijarah than to 'itaq because it combines ownership of the slave's person with usufruct for another, similar to a lease. The term 'mukatab' is analyzed linguistically: it is the passive participle from kataba yukatibu kitabatan, meaning to free on condition of payment. The root meaning is 'to collect', from which 'kitab' (book) and 'katibah' (battalion) are derived. The legal definition from classical sources is: 'freeing the hand immediately, and the body upon payment of the wealth' - i.e., the slave gains immediate freedom to work and earn, but the actual emancipation occurs when the contracted sum is paid. The passage also notes that kitabah is an Islamic term not known in jahiliyyah, and the first mukatab was Abu Miyyah, a slave of 'Umar. Finally, the use of the pattern mufa'alah is explained: it indicates mutual participation, as both master (mukatib) and slave (mukatab) are involved in writing the contract and paying installments.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukātab): A slave who has entered into a kitabah contract with his master, becoming a 'mukatab' - he gains freedom of action immediately but remains a slave until he pays the agreed sum.
كتابة (kitābah): A contract between a master and his slave for the slave's freedom in exchange for a specified payment in installments, derived from the root 'to write' because the contract is written.

Ruling on Kitabah: Mandub Proof and Dispute
Provides Quranic and ijma evidence for permissibility and recommended status of kitabah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on kitabah (contract of manumission) and explain how the Quranic evidence supports it being mandub rather than wajib.

Core Idea
The Quranic command for kitabah is conditional on khayr, making it recommended (mandub) not obligatory (wajib).

In this passage, the author establishes that kitabah—a contract where a slave earns freedom by paying a sum—is permissible and recommended, not obligatory. The proof comes from the Quranic verse 'fakatibuhum in 'alimtum fihim khayran' and the consensus of the majority of fuqaha. We will unpack the evidence and the inter-madhhab dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Master granting kitabah to a slave who requests it and has khayr. The ruling (hukm level) is mandub (recommended).
The passage explicitly states 'amr nadb'.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is the Quranic verse: 'So write a contract for them if you know any good in them' (Surah al-Nur, 24:33).
This is a Quranic amr (command).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
According to Hanafi usul, the default in amr is wujub, but here the condition 'in 'alimtum fihim khayran' diverts it to nadb. If it were wajib, the condition would be redundant. Thus the command is for recommendation when the condition is met.
The condition is the qarinah that changes the meaning.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Dawud al-Zahiri and others hold the command is wajib. They rely on the apparent imperative without accepting the condition as a diversion.
The passage lists these opponents.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The majority of fuqaha, including the four imams (except one narration from Ahmad), support nadb. The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi).
The 'ijma among fuqaha' excludes the Zahiri minority.

The recommendation, however, is contingent upon knowing good (khayr) in the slave. 'Khayr' is defined as the slave not harming Muslims after emancipation. If the slave is likely to cause harm, it is better not to make him a mukatab, but if done, the contract is still valid. The condition of the slave's acceptance is also explained: since the contract imposes a financial obligation on the slave, his acceptance is necessary. The passage then provides additional proofs: a hadith narrated by Umm Salamah (about a mukatab who earns enough to pay should seclude himself from his mistress), and a general ijma of the Ummah on the legality of kitabah. Finally, the author notes that despite the majority view, some scholars (Dawud al-Zahiri and his followers, and others) consider the command obligatory. The passage quotes al-Kashshaf to show that even the majority of scholars consider it mandub, with some exceptions from al-Hasan and others. Thus the Hanafi position is firmly that kitabah is mandub, not wajib.

Key Terms
خير (khayr): In the context of kitabah, 'khayr' means that the slave is trustworthy and will not harm Muslims after being freed; it implies religious and worldly good, specifically trustworthiness and non-harmfulness.
ندب/استحباب (nadb/istihbāb): In this passage, 'nadb' is the legal category between mubah and wajib; an act is recommended but not obligatory. The command for kitabah is classified as nadb.
اجماع الفقهاء (ijmā' al-fuqahā'): The consensus of the jurists (fuqaha) as opposed to the consensus of all scholars (al-ummah). Here it is used to exclude the opinion of Dawud al-Zahiri, who is a jurist but outside the mainstream fuqaha.

Women's Right to Wala' and the Invalid Condition of Sa'iba
Ruling with proof on women's wala' and sa'iba condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why women are entitled to wala' when they free a slave and state the Hanafi ruling that any condition to negate wala' (sa'iba) is void.

Core Idea
Wala' is a fixed right of the emancipator, regardless of gender; a condition to negate it is void.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes that women acquire wala' (right of inheritance through patronage) when they free a slave, using both general principles and a specific prophetic practice. It also rules that the pre-Islamic custom of sa'iba (freeing a slave without wala') is invalid. We will examine the proofs and the underlying Hanafi reasoning.

1
Identify the Ruling and its Scope
The author first asserts that a woman who frees a slave becomes entitled to his wala'. Note that this is a ruling about the right of inheritance, not the act of freeing itself.
The ruling applies to any emancipator, male or female.

The author cites the general wording of the hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taqa' where 'man' is inclusive, and the specific incident of the daughter of Hamza (Umama) who freed a slave. The Prophet divided the estate between his daughter and the female patron, proving women's entitlement.

The Prophet's action of giving half of the estate to the female patron shows that wala' inheritance applies to women. The word 'man' in 'liman a'taqa' is general ('amm) and includes women per Hanafi usul.

Some earlier jurists restricted wala' to males, but the Hanafi school follows the general evidence. Al-Marghinani confirms the mufta bihi position that women acquire wala'.
The tarjih authority is al-Hidayah itself, based on the hadith of the daughter of Hamza.

Special Cases of Wala': Mukatab, Wasiyyah, Tadbir, Istilad, and Mahram
Expansion of wala' rulings to five special cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five special cases where wala' is established (mukatab, wasiyyah, tadbir, istilad, mahram) and apply the rule that wala' belongs to the one who caused the freedom.

Core Idea
Wala' is tied to the cause of freedom: the master who sets the cause (kitabah, tadbir, etc.) retains wala' even after death.

This passage extends the concept of wala' to several special scenarios where freedom is delayed or indirect. We will examine each case: the mukatab (slave under contract), the slave freed by bequest (wasiyyah), the mudabbar (slave promised freedom after death), the umm walad (slave mother), and the mahram relative who is freed upon ownership. The unifying principle is that wala' belongs to the master who initiated the cause of freedom.

Guided Path
1
List the Five Special Cases
The passage enumerates: (1) mukatab who pays the kitabah, (2) slave freed by wasiyyah, (3) mudabbar, (4) umm walad (istilad), (5) mahram relative owned.
Each case involves an indirect or deferred freedom.
2
Identify the Common Principle
In all cases, the master's action or declaration (kitabah, wasiyyah, tadbir, istilad, ownership of mahram) is the cause of freedom. The master retains wala' even if freedom occurs after his death.
The principle: wala' follows the cause (sabab).
3
Apply to the Mukatab Case
A mukatab who pays the consideration becomes free; his wala' belongs to the master, even if the master died before payment. The cause (kitabah) was set during the master's life.
This is established in the Book of Mukatab.
4
Apply to the Wasiyyah Case
If a master bequeaths that a slave be freed or purchased and freed after his death, the executor's act is like the master's act. The slave becomes free from the estate, and wala' belongs to the master's heirs (as part of the estate).
The estate is considered as continuing the master's ownership.
5
Apply to Tadbir, Istilad, and Mahram
Mudabbar and umm walad become free upon the master's death, and their wala' is for the master. Similarly, if one owns a mahram relative, that relative becomes free immediately, and wala' is for the owner.
These are all causes of freedom established by the master's own action.

Hajr: Interdiction in Statements vs. Actions
Explains that three causes of hajr apply only to statements, not actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that legal interdiction (hajr) applies only to statements (aqwal) and not actions (af'al), and outline the rational proof (dalil aqli) for this distinction.

Core Idea
Hajr only blocks statements, not actions, because actions physically exist while statements depend on legal recognition and intention.

In this passage from Ashraf al-Hadayah commenting on Al-Hidayah, we learn a key distinction in Hanafi law: the three causes of legal interdiction (minority, insanity, slavery) affect only a person's statements, not their actions. This is based on a rational argument: actions are undeniable because they exist in the physical world, whereas statements have no external reality and only gain legal effect through intention. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying rules of capacity and liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Find the core statement: the three causes of hajr impose interdiction only on statements, not actions. Note the types of statements affected (those involving benefit and harm).
2
State the Hanafi position
Summarize: hajr applies to aqwal (e.g., contracts) but not af'al (e.g., torts). Actions remain valid and binding even if the actor lacks capacity.
3
Locate the proof
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): actions exist by sense and observation, so they cannot be legally negated. Statements exist only by legal recognition and require intention.
This is a qiyas or istidlal from reality.
4
Analyse the proof
Explain why actions cannot be denied: if a person kills, the act is real; treating it as non-existent would be sophistry. For statements, they are signs of meanings; their legal effect depends on intention (qasd), which is absent in those under interdiction.
Consider the exception: statements that are 'pure harm' like divorce are void ab initio for minor/insane; 'pure benefit' like accepting a gift are valid without guardian.
5
Note the objections and responses
The author of Nata'ij objects to the two reasons given. First objection: insha'at (e.g., divorce) are creations, not signs, so the sign argument fails. Second: jesting statements are sometimes valid (e.g., divorce) despite lack of intention. The Hanafi response: these are exceptions due to the gravity of consequences; for those under interdiction, lack of capacity prevents any valid intention.
Despite objections, the Hanafi ruling remains mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling from Al-Hidayah regarding the effect of the three causes of legal interdiction (minority, insanity, slavery) on statements (aqwal) versus actions (af'al). The author explains that these causes only impose interdiction on statements, not actions. The reason is that actions are physically observable and cannot be denied—if a person kills, cuts, or destroys property, the act exists in reality and cannot be treated as non-existent. Statements, on the other hand, have no physical existence; their legal validity depends on being recognized by the Shari'ah, and such recognition requires intention (qasd). Therefore, statements made by a minor, insane person, or slave (regarding matters involving benefit and harm) may be suspended or invalid due to lack of valid intention. However, actions like killing or destroying property always entail liability regardless of intention, because the physical act is undeniable. The text then addresses an objection: if statements require intention, how can divorce, manumission, oath, or vow be valid even when made jokingly (hazl) by a sane adult? The response distinguishes that such statements are legally effective due to their serious consequences, but for those under interdiction, their lack of capacity prevents valid intention. The author presents two reasons: first, statements are signs (dalil) of meanings, and a sign can lag behind its meaning, so treating a statement as non-existent is possible; second, statements can be truthful or false, serious or jesting, so intention is necessary to determine validity. Actions, being identical with their meanings, cannot be undone. The objector (sahib al-Nata'ij) criticizes these reasons but the text implies the Hanafi position stands.

Key Terms
حجر (hajr): Legal interdiction preventing a person from validly performing certain acts due to lack of capacity or protection of rights.
قصد (qasd): Intention; a necessary condition for the legal validity of statements (aqwal) because statements are recognized by Shari'ah only when accompanied by intent.
فعل (fi'l): Physical action; exists externally by sense and observation, thus cannot be legally negated. Hajr does not apply to actions.
إنشاء (insha'): Constructive statement that creates a legal effect (e.g., divorce, sale). These are not mere reports but acts that change legal status.

Signs of Puberty and the Age Dispute in Hanafi Fiqh
Explains signs of puberty and the intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab age dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi signs of puberty and explain the difference between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's age rulings, applying the mufta bihi in the absence of signs.

Core Idea
When signs are absent, the Hanafi fatwa sets puberty at 15 for both, though Abu Hanifa held 18 for males and 17 for females.

This passage from al-Hajr discusses the legal signs of puberty and what happens when none appear. While Imam Abu Hanifa initially set distinct ages for males and females, the Sahibayn's view of 15 years for both became the relied-upon position. The text also engages with the Zahiri rejection of any age criterion, offering a classic Hanafi rebuttal based on custom and consensus.

1
State the Hanafi position on signs of puberty

2
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on age default
Abu Hanifa holds that if signs are absent, a male becomes baligh at 18 and a female at 17. The Sahibayn (and a narration from Abu Hanifa) set the age at 15 for both.
The text states 'fatwa is upon it' for the 15-year age.
3
Present the opposing Zahiri school's argument
Dawood al-Zahiri rejects any age limit based on the hadith: 'The pen is lifted from three: from the child until he has a wet dream.' He argues that only a wet dream (ihtilam) establishes puberty, not age.
The hadith is cited in the source as evidence.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Zahiri proof
The Hanafi response is that the hadith applies to the lifting of accountability before puberty; it does not negate age as a default criterion when signs do not appear. The age limit is based on custom and scholarly consensus (ijma).
The source does not elaborate further, but this is the standard Hanafi reasoning.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If a 16-year-old male shows no signs, according to the Hanafi mufta bihi he is baligh and must fulfill obligations. According to the Zahiri, he remains a child until he has a wet dream.
This difference affects accountability for prayer, fasting, and other acts of worship.

Minimum Age for Bulugh and the Precautionary Ruling
Discussion of minimum age of bulugh with weak narrations and precautionary ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the minimum age of bulugh for boys (18) and girls (17), explain the reliance on weak narrations and the Quranic verses, and apply the principle of precaution (ihtiyat) to determine legal capacity.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school sets bulugh at 18 (boys) and 17 (girls) as a precaution to ensure certainty.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah clarifies why the Hanafi school adopts 18 years as the minimum age for bulugh, relying on a weak narration from Ibn Abbas rather than the 15-year narration from Anas. The key is the principle of precaution (ihtiyat): at 18, the person is certainly baligh according to both narrations, whereas at 15 only according to one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling on minimum age of bulugh
State the ages: 18 lunar years for boys, 17 for girls (or earlier if physical signs appear). Note that this is the mufta bihi position.
The passage also mentions that for girls, one year is reduced because their growth is faster.
2
Examine the Quranic verses that mention ashadd
Look at Quran 6:152 ('until he reaches his full strength') and 46:15 ('until he reaches his full strength and reaches forty years'). These verses indicate that ashadd is a stage before 40, but the precise age is not given.
The Quranic dalala is zanni (speculative) regarding the exact age.
3
Analyze the conflicting narrations from Ibn Abbas and Anas
Ibn Abbas narrates that ashadd is between 18 and 30/40 years (weak chain). Anas narrates that at 15, the child becomes legally responsible (also weak). The Hanafis prefer Ibn Abbas's narration because it is more precautionary.
Both narrations are weak, but the Hanafi school follows the weaker one for caution.
4
Understand the Hanafi reliance on Ibn Abbas's narration for precaution
By adopting 18, a person meets the criteria of both narrations: at 18, they are baligh according to Ibn Abbas and also according to Anas (since 18 > 15). At 15, they are only baligh according to Anas, not Ibn Abbas. This ensures certainty.
The principle of tayaqqu (certainty) is invoked: the minimum age is chosen because it is certain that at that age, the child is baligh based on all available evidence.
5
Address the objection regarding certainty
An objection states that the longest period (e.g., 40) is more certain for actual maturity. The response: the ruling is about the limit of the prohibition on using the orphan's property, not about the attainment of maturity itself. The least age (18) is certain as the end of that prohibition, so it is used for precaution.
Taj al-Shari'ah and al-Kifayah answer this by citing the verse: the prohibition extends until ashadd, and the least interpretation is 18, which is certain.

Wine turned to vinegar returns free; tanned hide returned with compensation for added value.

3
Analyze the distinction (tathir vs adding property)
Takhlil removes the attribute of wine and returns the substance to its pure state; no new wealth is created. Tanning attaches a valuable material (e.g., bark) to the hide, increasing its value and creating a property right for the usurper.
This distinction is the key to applying the rule to other similar cases.
4
Address the objection (najis al-ayn)
The Shafi'i/Hanbali objection: wine is intrinsically najis, so takhlil does not purify. The Hanafi response: wine's essence is grape juice; the impurity is an accidental attribute. When the attribute is removed, the impurity goes away, just as a garment's filth is removed by washing.
This shows how the Hanafi school defends its qiyas by arguing that the 'illa remains valid.
5
Apply the method for estimating tanning increase
The increase is calculated by comparing the market value of the hide as undressed (slaughtered but not tanned) and as dressed (tanned). The difference is the amount the usurper must be paid.
The usurper also has a right of retention until paid, similar to a seller's lien.

If a Muslim usurps wine and turns it into vinegar, or usurps the hide of a dead animal and tans it, then the owner of the wine may take the vinegar without anything, and he takes the hide of the dead animal and returns to him what the tanning increased in it. What is meant by the first part: when he turns it into vinegar by moving it from sun to shade and from there to sun. And by the second part: when he tans it with something having value, like qarz (leaves of a tree) and 'afs (oak galls) and the like. And the difference is that this turning into vinegar is a purification for it, like washing a najis garment, so it remains in his ownership because no financial value is established by it. And by this tanning, a valuable property of the usurper is attached to the hide, like dye in cloth, so it is like that. Therefore, he takes the vinegar without anything, and he takes the hide and gives what the tanning increased in it. Its explanation: its value is considered as slaughtered undressed, and its value as dressed, so he guarantees the excess between them. And the usurper may keep it until he receives his right, like the right of retention in a sale.

Translation: If a Muslim's wine is usurped and made into vinegar, or a dead hide is usurped and tanned, the owner of the wine has the option to take the vinegar for free, and the owner of the hide takes it and gives what the tanning increased. The first case means making vinegar by moving it into the sun, etc. The second case means tanning with something valuable. The difference is that making vinegar is purification, like washing a dirty cloth, so it remains in the owner's ownership because no new value is created. Tanning attaches a valuable property to the hide, like dye in cloth. Hence vinegar is taken for free; hide is taken but compensation is given for the increase. The method: the value of the undressed hide and dressed hide are compared; the difference is guaranteed. The usurper may retain until paid, similar to retention in sale.
Explanation of the statement 'If he usurps...': The usurper took the wine and by placing it in the sun made vinegar; the owner can take it without paying. If he tanned the hide with bark, the owner takes it but pays the increase. Reason: making vinegar is purifying it, like washing a dirty cloth; no new value, so property remains with original owner. Unlike tanning, where valuable material is added, so compensation is due. According to Imam Shafi'i and Imam Ahmad, wine is not purified by turning to vinegar; hence returning it is not wajib; rather, paying its value is necessary. If wine turns by itself, there is consensus that it must be returned, and its value is not guaranteed.
Statement 'The difference is that...': The difference between the two cases is that making vinegar purifies the wine; he removed the impurity and the property of wine without adding anything of his own; it is like washing a dirty garment; similarly, the vinegar remains the owner's property.
Question: A garment is not intrinsically najis, but wine is intrinsically najis because it is inherently haram, as the Prophet said: 'Wine is forbidden in its essence.' Answer: The essence of wine is the same as grape juice; the attribute of wine made it najis; when the attribute is removed by takhlil, the impurity goes away. The attribute is accidental, like attributes of a person. The hadith is interpreted to mean that wine itself is haram, whether little or much. Hence he said after that: 'Intoxication from every drink.' 
Statement 'and its explanation...': The method of estimating the increase in the hide from tanning: consider the value of the hide as slaughtered undressed and then as dressed; the difference is what is guaranteed.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents a detailed ruling on two related cases of usurious (ghasb) transformation: wine turned into vinegar, and a dead animal's hide tanned. The ruling from the original matn (Hidayah) is that the owner of the wine can reclaim the vinegar for free, while the owner of the tanned hide can reclaim it but must pay for the increase in value caused by tanning. The commentary explains the reasoning: making vinegar is merely purification (tathir), like washing a najis garment—no new valuable property is added, so the original owner's property right continues unchanged. Tanning, however, attaches the usurper's valuable property (the tanning agent) to the hide, analogous to dyeing cloth; therefore, the usurper is entitled to compensation for the addition. The method to assess this is to compare the value of the hide before and after tanning. The usurper also has a right of retention (habs) until paid, similar to a seller's right in a sale. The commentary then addresses a dispute: Imam Shafi'i and Imam Ahmad hold that wine is not purified by turning to vinegar, so the usurper must pay its value instead of returning the vinegar. However, the Hanafi position is that takhlil (making vinegar) is a valid purification, so the vinegar remains the original owner's property. A potential objection is raised: wine is intrinsically najis (najis al-ayn), while a garment is not. The answer is that wine's essence is the same as grape juice; the attribute of 'wine-ness' is accidental. When this attribute is removed, the impurity is also removed, just as attributes of a person can change without altering the essence. Thus, the analogy with washing a garment stands. The passage concludes with the method for estimating the increase from tanning.

Key Terms
تخلیل (takhleel): The process of turning wine into vinegar by exposure to sun/shade.
دباغت (dibaghah): Tanning of a hide using astringent materials like qarz or 'afs to make it usable.
نجس العین (najis al-'ayn): Something that is intrinsically impure in its substance, like wine and pork.
غصب (ghasb): Unlawful usurpation of another's property without their consent.
مال متقوم (mal mutaqawwim): Property that has recognized financial value in Islamic law and is permissible to own.
