How a Qadi's Wala' Ruling Proves a Mukatab's Inability
Explaining how a qadi's wala' ruling proves a mukatab's inability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qadi rulings that do and do not prove a mukatab's inability, and explain why a wala' ruling in a dispute after the son's death is conclusive evidence of inability.

Core Idea
A qadi's ruling in an ijtihad matter becomes binding proof—here it resolves whether a mukatab died slave or free.

This passage from the Hidayah commentary clarifies a subtle point: not every qadi ruling about a mukatab's family proves his inability to pay kitabah. Only when the ruling directly hinges on the continuation or rescission of kitabah—such as a wala' dispute after death—does it become definitive. Understanding this provides deeper insight into the interplay of qada', ijtihad, and the unique status of the mukatab.

Guided Path
1
Review the mukatab's status
The mukatab is a slave under a kitabah contract: he owns his earnings and can be sued, but remains a slave until full payment. His ability to pay is crucial for freedom.
2
Contrast the two scenarios
The qadi's ruling on compensation for the son's crime does NOT prove inability, because it only confirms the requirement that the son be attached to the mother's masters. But if the son dies and a wala' dispute arises, the qadi's award of wala' to the mother's masters does prove inability.
3
Link wala' to kitabah status
Wala' depends on whether the mukatab died as a slave or free. If kitabah is rescinded, he dies a slave and wala' goes to the mother's masters. If kitabah continues, he dies free and wala' goes to the father's masters. Thus, the wala' ruling indirectly decides the kitabah's fate.
4
Recognize the ijtihad element
The effect of death on kitabah is a matter of ijtihad: Zayd ibn Thabit held it rescinds kitabah; other Companions disagreed. The qadi's ruling on wala' effectively chooses one view, and such rulings are binding by consensus.
5
Conclude the legal consequence
Because the qadi's ruling is conclusive in this ijtihad matter, awarding wala' to the mother's masters implies the mukatab died a slave—i.e., kitabah was rescinded due to inability to pay. Hence the ruling itself constitutes proof of inability.

Reversal of a Qadi's Interdiction: Fatwa vs. Judgment
Explains the ruling when a second qadi reverses the interdiction imposed by a first qadi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a fatwa and a judgment (qada) and apply the rule that a second qadi may reverse a first qadi's interdiction because it is a fatwa.

Core Idea
A qadi's interdiction is a fatwa, not a judgment, so another qadi may reverse it.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a practical question: Can a second qadi overturn an interdiction imposed by a first qadi? The answer hinges on a crucial distinction in Hanafi fiqh between a fatwa (non-binding legal opinion) and a qada (binding judgment). Understanding this distinction is essential for anyone involved in judicial or fatwa work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Determine what the passage rules: a second qadi may nullify the interdiction of a first qadi and release the interdicted person. This is valid (ja'iz).
2
Understand the Reasoning
The reason given is that the first qadi's interdiction is a fatwa, not a judgment. Why? Because there is no plaintiff (mudda'i) and defendant (mudda'a alayh). A proper judgment requires a dispute between parties.
This is the key element: no dispute means no judgment.
3
Contrast with a Judgment
If it were a judgment, it would be in a matter of ijtihad and generally must be enforced. But since it is a fatwa, it can be reversed. The commentary also discusses the scenario where the first qadi's ruling is affirmed (imda') by a subsequent qadi, making it irreversible.
Imda' (affirmation) attaches finality.
4
Apply the Principle
Apply this distinction to similar cases: any qadi's action that is purely protective and not resolving a dispute is a fatwa and can be revised by another qadi.
Examples: appointment of a guardian, temporary restrictions.

Commentary
This passage addresses a procedural ruling: whether a second qadi can reverse an interdiction imposed by the first qadi. The author of Ashraf al-Hidayah explains the Arabic text: if a qadi interdicted a squanderer and then the case goes to another qadi who nullifies that interdiction and releases the person, this is permissible. The reason is that the interdiction by the first qadi is a fatwa (legal opinion), not a final judgment (qada). It lacks the essential elements of a judgment: there is no plaintiff (mudda'i) or defendant (mudda'a alayh). Interdiction is a protective measure, not a dispute. Therefore, the second qadi can overturn it based on his own ijtihad. However, if the first qadi's action were considered a judgment, then it would be a matter of ijtihad and must be enforced; but here it is not. The commentator then discusses an objection: if the first qadi's interdiction is a judgment, then it cannot be overturned even by another qadi. He responds that for it to be a judgment, there must be a dispute; but interdiction is not a dispute, so it is merely a fatwa. He further states that even if we hypothetically consider it a judgment, it would be in a matter of ijtihad, and such judgments are enforceable but can be overturned only if they conflict with a clear text. However, the ruling here is that the second qadi can nullify it because the first interdiction was not a proper judgment. The passage concludes that if after interdiction the squanderer makes a transaction and the same qadi or another rules it void, and then the case goes to yet another qadi, that third qadi must enforce the annulment because the affirmation (imda') is attached. Thus, the ruling emphasizes the distinction between a fatwa and a judgment in the context of interdiction.

فتوى (fatwā): A non-binding legal opinion issued by a mufti or qadi in a matter not involving a dispute.
امضاء (imḍā'): The affirmation or enforcement of a previous ruling, making it irreversible.

The Judge's Interdiction: Fatwa Not Final Qada
Explains a judge's hajr as a fatwa and the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a judge's fatwa and qada in the context of hajr, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute, and apply the ruling that hajr by a judge is revocable.

Core Idea
A judge's interdiction is a fatwa, not a binding qada, because it lacks a plaintiff and defendant.

This lesson examines the legal nature of a judge's imposition of interdiction (hajr) on a safih. The passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah clarifies that such an act is a fatwa, not a final judicial verdict (qada). It also reveals an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether hajr can ever be qada. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping the limits of judicial authority and the possibility of review.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Evidence
The passage states that a judge's hajr is a fatwa, not qada, and cites Quran 2:282 as evidence for the guardian's authority over the safih.
The Quranic verse establishes the guardian's role, not directly the judge's fatwa.
2
Understand the Reason for Fatwa Status
The key reason given is the absence of a plaintiff (muqadi lahu) and a defendant (muqadi alayh), which are essential for a qada. Without litigation, the judge's act is merely a non-binding opinion.
This distinction is fundamental in Hanafi judicial theory.
3
Analyze the Hypothetical Qada Scenario
Even if we hypothetically consider the judge's hajr as qada, it is a qada subject to disagreement (mukhtalaf fih) and therefore requires enforcement by another judge to become binding.
This shows that hajr is not final like an undisputed qada.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that qada bil-hajr is not permissible; the Sahibayn hold it is permissible. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position for fatwa is that of the Sahibayn, as stated in Durr al-Mukhtar.
This ikhtilaf does not affect the operative ruling that hajr is a fatwa unless formally confirmed as qada.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya, a commentary on al-Hidaya, addresses the nature of a judge's imposition of interdiction (hajr) on a safih (a foolish person). It opens with the Quranic verse (2:282) that establishes the guardian's authority over the safih in financial matters. The text then notes that according to Durr al-Mukhtar, the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position for fatwa is that of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). The central ruling discussed is: if a judge imposes interdiction on a safih, and another judge subsequently overturns that interdiction and releases him, it is valid. The reasoning is that such a judge's act is a fatwa (legal opinion), not a final qada' (judicial verdict). This is because qada' requires a plaintiff and a defendant, which are absent here. The imposition merely affirms an already existing condition (foolishness). Even if we hypothetically consider it qada', the qada' itself is a matter of disagreement (ikhtilaf) within the Hanafi school: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that qada' of interdiction is not permissible, while the Sahibayn hold it is permissible. A judge's ruling in a disputed matter only settles the dispute if the ruling itself is not disputed. Therefore, such a qada' needs enforcement by another judge to become binding. Once a subsequent judge endorses the annulment, it becomes final and cannot be overturned. The passage thus clarifies the jurisdictional limits of a judge's interdiction: it is essentially a non-binding administrative measure until confirmed by a judicial process.

Key Terms
حجر (hajr): Legal restriction preventing a person from freely disposing of property due to foolishness or similar incapacity.
فتوى (fatwa): A non-binding legal opinion issued by a judge or mufti, in contrast to qada' (binding judgment).
قضاء (qada): A binding judicial verdict that resolves a dispute, requiring a plaintiff and defendant.
سفيہ (safih): A person who is foolish or imprudent, lacking sound judgment in financial matters.
مرافعہ (murafa'a): Referring a matter from one judge to another, often for review or appeal.

Qadi's Authority to Sell Debtor's Property When He Refuses (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qadi selling debtor's assets

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa denies the qadi the authority to sell a refusing debtor's property, and contrast it with the position of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The qadi may imprison but not sell; only the debtor chooses the method of repayment.

This passage addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: when a bankrupt debtor refuses to sell his property to repay creditors, may the qadi step in and sell on his behalf? The Sahibayn argue yes, by analogy to a husband who refuses to divorce. Abu Hanifa disagrees, offering three rebuttals centered on the debtor's alternatives and the purpose of imprisonment. Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of the limits of judicial authority in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
State the positions
Identify the ruling of the Sahibayn (qadi may sell) and Abu Hanifa (qadi may not sell, only imprison).
2
Understand the Sahibayn's analogy
The Sahibayn compare the debtor to a husband who must divorce his wife (mahbub and 'anain). In that case, if the husband refuses, the qadi divorces on his behalf. Similarly, since selling is wajib on the debtor, the qadi should sell if he refuses.
This is a qiyas (analogical reasoning).
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's first rebuttal
Abu Hanifa argues that the fear of talji'a (fraudulent sale by the debtor to avoid creditors) is hypothetical and cannot strip the debtor of legal capacity.
Talji'a is merely a possibility, not a certainty.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's second rebuttal
Repayment is the obligation, not selling per se. The debtor can raise money through borrowing, gifts, or begging, unlike the divorce case where separation is the only option.
The existence of alternatives breaks the analogy.
5
Analyze Abu Hanifa's third rebuttal
Imprisonment is meant to pressure the debtor to choose a method, not to enforce a specific sale. If sale were decreed, imprisonment would harm both creditor (delay) and debtor (unnecessary hardship).
Imprisonment is a means, not an end.
6
Conclude with mufta bihi
The text indicates that Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi). The qadi only imprisons, not sells.
The phrase 'قلنا' (we say) signals the adopted view.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the qadi's authority to sell a debtor's property when the debtor refuses to sell. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argue that since selling is wajib on the debtor to repay debt and he is imprisoned for it, if he refuses, the qadi should sell on his behalf, analogous to the case of a husband who refuses to divorce his wife when required (mahbub and 'anain). Core Idea
A debtor who falls ill in prison without a servant must be released to prevent death, for saving life outweighs debt collection.

This lesson deals with a specific scenario in qada (judicial rulings): what happens when an insolvent debtor (muflis) imprisoned for debt becomes ill? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling that balances the creditor's right and the debtor's life. The key factor is whether the debtor has a servant to care for him; if not, the judge must order his release to prevent certain death.

The relied-upon position for fatwa is the majority view: release if no servant. The text indicates this by presenting Abu Yusuf's view as a mere narration, implying the stronger position is release.
The judge should follow the majority view to avoid endangering life.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether the qadi's declaration of insolvency bars creditors from following the debtor, and apply the relied-upon view (Sahibayn) to a practical scenario.

Core Idea
A qadi's insolvency declaration does not automatically end the right of accompaniment; a dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn.

When a debtor is released from prison after being declared insolvent, can creditors still follow him to demand payment? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. This lesson unpacks the proofs and practical consequences of each view.

Guided Path

The passage begins by stating that after the debtor's release, the qadi does not become a barrier; creditors may accompany him (mulazamah) but cannot prevent his travel or transactions. Understand this as the baseline ruling.

The Prophet (PBUH) said: 'For the right-holder there is a hand and a tongue.' Hand means accompaniment, tongue means demanding payment. This hadith supports the creditors' right to follow the debtor.

Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the declaration of insolvency does not create a barrier because wealth can appear suddenly, and witnesses cannot truly prove lack of wealth. The Sahibayn hold that once the qadi declares insolvency, the creditors cannot follow unless they prove he has wealth.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4

For Abu Hanifa: dalil is the uncertainty of wealth (it comes and goes) and the limitation of witnesses' knowledge. For Sahibayn: the declaration is a valid judicial act that establishes poverty, entitling the debtor to respite.
Each side uses different reasoning about the certainty of the declaration.

Under Abu Hanifa, creditors can always follow. Under Sahibayn, after declaration they must prove wealth. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in later Hanafi fatwa is the Sahibayn's view.
The mufta bihi balances the debtor's need for respite with creditors' rights if they can show evidence.

Commentary
This passage discusses the situation after the debtor is released from prison. The ruling is that the qadi does not stand between the debtor and his creditors; rather, the creditors may follow the debtor (mutalazamah) but they cannot prevent him from conducting transactions or traveling. The basis is the hadith: 'For the right-holder there is a hand and a tongue' — the hand means following him, the tongue means demanding payment. The creditors may take the surplus of his earnings and distribute it equally among themselves. Then the passage presents a dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two students (Sahibayn: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). According to the Sahibayn, if the qadi declares the debtor insolvent, the creditors cannot follow him (meaning the qadi becomes a barrier) unless they prove he has wealth. In their view, the declaration of insolvency is valid, so poverty is established and the debtor deserves respite. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the declaration of insolvency is not effective because wealth can come and go at any time (from Allah), and witnesses cannot truly know the absence of wealth; therefore, the right of accompaniment is not nullified. The passage clarifies that proof of solvency (bayyinat al-yasar) outweighs proof of insolvency (bayyinat al-i'sar) because the default is hardship (i.e., the debtor is presumed poor).

Buyer as Adversary When Seller Is Absent
Intra-Hanafi dispute on buyer as adversary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf on whether the buyer can be sued as an adversary when the seller is absent, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
The buyer is not an adversary because the claim annuls the absent seller's contract.

This passage presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute in procedural law: when a seller is absent and the buyer denies a debt owed by a slave sold with permission, can the creditors sue the buyer? The ruling determines who is a proper adversary (khasm) in court. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the buyer is not an adversary, while Abu Yusuf dissents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and core issue
The scenario: a ma'dhun slave is sold, the seller (slave's master) is absent, and the buyer denies the slave's debt to creditors. The core issue is whether the buyer is a proper adversary for the creditors to sue.
The debt is established while the slave was with the master, and the master is responsible.
2
State the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad)
They say there is no litigation between creditors and buyer because the buyer is not a proper adversary. The claim would involve annulling the contract made by the absent seller, which is not permissible as it would be judgment against an absent person.
This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).
3
State Abu Yusuf's opposing view
Abu Yusuf holds that the buyer is an adversary because he claims ownership of the slave. Anyone who claims ownership is an adversary to anyone who disputes that ownership.
Abu Yusuf's view is based on the buyer's own assertion of ownership.
4
Analyze the arguments (dalil) for each side
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that the creditors' claim essentially demands annulment of the sale contract, which was made by the absent seller. Ruling against the buyer would indirectly rule against the absent seller, which is procedurally invalid. Abu Yusuf argues that the buyer's claim of ownership makes him a direct adversary regardless of the seller's absence.
The difference hinges on whether the subject of the claim is the contract (affecting seller) or the ownership (affecting buyer).
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If the buyer is not an adversary, creditors cannot get a judgment against him; they must wait for the seller's return to sue. If the buyer is an adversary, they can sue and recover directly from the buyer.
This affects the creditors' immediate remedy.

Commentary

The primary ruling: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say that the creditors cannot sue the buyer because the buyer is not the proper adversary; the debt is against the slave and the seller, and the buyer's denial means the dispute should be with the seller, who is absent. Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Defendant After Buyer Gifts and Disappears
Intra-Hanafi dispute on procedural defendant in a gift-after-purchase scenario

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Imam Abu Yusuf and the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) regarding who is the proper defendant in a dispute when a buyer has gifted property and disappeared, and explain the legal reasoning behind each view.

Core Idea
The dispute hinges on whether the claim implies rescission of the original contract or merely follows the buyer's ownership claim.

This passage from the Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi procedural dispute. When a buyer purchases property, gifts it to a donee, and then disappears, can the claimant (e.g., a creditor or pre-emptor) sue the donee directly? Imam Abu Yusuf says yes, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-tarifayn) say no. The disagreement turns on the nature of the claim and the principle of judgment against an absent party.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Factual Scenario
Recognize the key facts: a buyer purchases property, gifts it to a donee, and then disappears. A claimant (pre-emptor or creditor) appears. The legal question is: who is the proper defendant?
The gift is complete; the dispute is about procedural standing, not the gift's validity.
2
State the Two Positions
Imam Abu Yusuf: the donee can be the defendant. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad): the donee cannot be the defendant.
The majority view is the Zahir al-Riwayah position.
3
Analyze the Proofs
Abu Yusuf's proof: the buyer claimed ownership, so any disputant is his defendant (the ownership claim transfers to the donee). The majority's proof: the claim is effectively a demand to rescind the original sale contract, which would require judgment against the absent seller—impermissible.
The majority's proof invokes the principle that judgment against an absent person (qada' ala al-gha'ib) is not allowed.
4
Determine the Practical Consequence
Under Abu Yusuf, the case proceeds immediately against the donee. Under the majority, the case must wait until the buyer is present, potentially delaying justice.
This difference is the thamarah al-khilaf.
