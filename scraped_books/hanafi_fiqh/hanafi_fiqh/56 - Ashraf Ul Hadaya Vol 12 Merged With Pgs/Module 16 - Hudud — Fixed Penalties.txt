Coercion to Kufr: Outward Permission and Martyrdom
Rules on being forced to utter kufr or curse the Prophet under threat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on uttering kufr under coercion and distinguish between permissible compliance and meritorious martyrdom.

Core Idea
Outward kufr under threat of death is permissible if the heart remains firm; refusing and dying is heroic but not obligatory.

When a Muslim is forced to utter words of kufr or curse the Prophet under threat of death or loss of limb, Islamic law provides a critical distinction: outward compliance does not harm faith if the heart remains steadfast. This lesson explores the evidence from the hadith of Ammar ibn Yasir and Quran 16:106, and explains why the Hanafi school permits the utterance while still honoring those who choose martyrdom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Clearly define the act: being coerced to utter kufr or curse the Prophet (SAW). Determine the ruling for two scenarios – when the person complies while heart is firm, and when they refuse and are killed.
The passage discusses both the permissibility (jawaz) of outward utterance and the reward of martyrdom for refusal.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the primary proofs: the hadith of Ammar ibn Yasir (Prophet's approval) and the Quranic verse (16:106) which excepts the coerced.
The hadith is narrated with the phrase 'If they return, then return' (amr indicating permission).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how the proofs demonstrate permissibility. The explicit approval of outward utterance despite inner faith shows that the heart's conviction is what matters; outward words do not nullify iman.
The verse makes an exception for the coerced whose heart is firm, proving the act does not constitute kufr.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Identify that some scholars hold the stricter view: one must never utter kufr even under threat. Understand the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) – sin versus no sin.
The Hanafi position is that coercion lifts the obligation of outward display, while refusal is a virtuous azeemah.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that outward utterance is permissible with a firm heart; however, choosing martyrdom is a higher rank. Both are valid options.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf exists; the text presents this as a clear ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on coercion to commit kufr or curse the Prophet. It begins by establishing that mere imprisonment or beating does not constitute valid coercion for such severe acts; rather, a threat to life or limb is required. This is a fortiori (awla wa ahra) compared to drinking wine, where the same condition applies. If such a threat exists, the coerced person may outwardly utter words of kufr while maintaining inner faith, and there is no sin. The proof is the hadith of Ammar ibn Yasir, who was tortured and uttered kufr under compulsion; the Prophet (SAW) approved his action and said, 'If they return, return.' This incident is also the occasion of revelation for Quran 16:106. The reasoning is that faith resides in the heart (tasdiq), and outward display does not negate it, while refusing would lead to certain death. Therefore, it is permissible to comply. However, if a person endures and is killed without uttering kufr, they are rewarded as a martyr, exemplified by Khabib ibn Adiy, whom the Prophet called the master of martyrs. The difference from the previous case of eating haram is that here the prohibition of kufr remains even in coercion (it is not an exception), and abstention is azeemah for the sake of din, so refusal is praiseworthy and rewarded.

Key Terms
إكراه (ikrah): Coercion that overcomes a person's will, here requiring threat to life/limb to be valid.
مطمئن بالایمان (mutma'in bil-iman): Heart firmly established in faith, unaffected by outward utterance.
عزيمة (azimah): The stronger, preferable course: refusing to utter kufr even if it leads to death.

Coercion to Commit Zina and Application of Hadd
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether hadd is due on one coerced to commit zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on hadd for coerced zina, IDENTIFY the thamarah al-khilaf, and EXPLAIN the Hanafi tarjih that favours the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Coercion to zina splits the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa imposes hadd unless the ruler coerces; the Sahibayn drop it entirely.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether the fixed punishment (hadd) for zina can be applied when the act was committed under duress. The two positions — Abu Hanifa's conditional liability and the Sahibayn's absolute exemption — reveal how different readings of the relationship between coercion and divine rights lead to opposite practical outcomes. Mastering this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to weigh conflicting proofs and identify the mufta bihi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ikhtilaf
Recognize that the passage records a dispute within the Hanafi school between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Abu Hanifa holds that hadd is obligatory on the coerced unless the coercer is the ruler. The Sahibayn hold that hadd is not binding at all.
This shows that even within a single school, different conclusions can arise from different premises about the nature of hadd and coercion.
2
Examine each side's proof
Consider the underlying reasoning: Abu Hanifa views hadd as a right of Allah (haqq Allah) that cannot be dropped by the choice of the individual, but he makes an exception for the ruler's coercion because the ruler's authority can override. The Sahibayn analogize coercion to lack of consent, arguing that hadd requires intentional sin (qasd) and coercion removes that. They also rely on hadith: 'My ummah is forgiven for what they do under coercion.'
The proofs are aqli (rational) and naqli.
3
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is stark: under Abu Hanifa's view, the coerced person is punished (unless ruler); under Sahibayn, he goes free. This difference affects real cases of forced zina.
Understanding thamarah helps a mufti appreciate the weight of each opinion.
4
Identify the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi scholars (e.g., in Al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya) have preferred the view of the Sahibayn. Therefore, the relied-upon position for fatwa is that hadd is not imposed on the coerced.
The authority of Sahibayn is strong when they agree against Abu Hanifa, especially in hudud.

Coercion to Commit Zina: Hadd Under Duress
Discusses whether hadd is imposed on a person coerced to commit zina, with school differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether hadd is applied to a person coerced to commit zina and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Coercion to zina does not automatically exempt from hadd; Abu Hanifa imposes it unless the coercer is the ruler.

This lesson explores a nuanced case within hudud: when a person is forced to commit zina, is the hadd penalty still applied? The Hanafi school is divided: Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Zufar generally require hadd, making an exception only for coercion by a ruler, while the Sahibayn reject hadd in all coercive scenarios. By understanding the reasoning, you will appreciate how principles of consent, deterrence, and doubt (shubha) interact in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core question
What is the legal issue? Determine whether the hadd for zina applies when the act is committed under coercion. Note that the discussion is about the coerced person, not the coercer.
2
State the Hanafi positions
List the positions: Abu Hanifa (two views), Zufar, and the Sahibayn. Identify which is the later view of Abu Hanifa (mufta bihi).
The later view of Abu Hanifa excepts coercion by the ruler; his earlier view had no exception.
3
Analyze the reasoning
Understand why Abu Hanifa and Zufar consider hadd obligatory: pleasure indicates consent, so the act is voluntary. Why the ruler exception: fear of death creates a doubt (shubha) that blocks hadd. Contrast with Sahibayn: coercion completely negates volition.
The text notes that arousal can occur naturally, so pleasure is not always a sign of consent, but the majority view links it.
4
Extract the practical difference
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf: in a case of coercion by a private individual, Abu Hanifa and Zufar impose hadd; Sahibayn do not. In coercion by a ruler, all agree no hadd under the mufta bihi.
The earlier view of Abu Hanifa would impose hadd even with ruler coercion.

Hajr Applies Only to Statements: Rationale and Exceptions
Explains that hajr is in statements due to intent, but actions are real.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that hajr (legal restraint) applies only to statements, explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) for this rule, and identify the exception for hudud and qisas.

Core Idea
Actions are undeniable facts; statements derive effect from Shari'ah and require intent—hence hajr restricts only statements.

This passage establishes a fundamental principle in Hanafi fiqh: the three causes of legal restraint (minority, slavery, insanity) restrict only verbal legal acts, not physical actions. The reason is that actions exist as sensory realities, while statements depend on Shari'ah recognition and the actor's intent. However, when an action is linked to a ruling that can be repelled by doubts (such as hudud and qisas), the lack of full intent becomes a doubt that prevents application of those penalties. This lesson unpacks the rationale and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The passage states that the three causes (minority, slavery, insanity) necessitate hajr (legal restraint) only in statements (aqwal), not in actions (af‘al). Recognize that hajr restricts the legal effectiveness of verbal acts like sales, divorce, or manumission.
Hajr does not mean the person cannot act, but that their verbal declarations have limited legal effect.
2
Understand the rational proof (dalil aqli)
Actions are proven by sense and observation (hissan wa mushahadah)—they are undeniable realities. Statements, by contrast, are given legal effect by Shari'ah and require intention (qasd). Without intent, the statement lacks the necessary condition for validity.
This is a rational argument: you cannot deny a visible act, but a statement's legal existence depends on the law and the speaker's intention.
3
Identify the exception for hudud and qisas
If an action (e.g., killing, cutting off a hand) is linked to a ruling that can be repelled by doubts (shubuhat), like hadd penalties or qisas, then the absence of intent (due to legal incapacity) creates a doubt that prevents application of that ruling.
The act itself is still a fact, but the penalty is suspended due to the shubhah.
4
Distinguish among types of statements
Statements are further categorized: (a) purely beneficial (e.g., accepting a gift, embracing Islam) – valid from a child, slave, or insane without guardian's permission; (b) purely harmful (e.g., divorce, manumission) – void from the outset for child and insane, but not for slaves (whose statements are suspended on the master's right).
This demonstrates the nuanced application of hajr based on the nature of the statement.
5
Apply to hypothetical scenarios
Test your understanding: If a child destroys someone's property, is he liable? (Yes, because the action is real, so compensation is due. But no hadd or qisas.) If a child pronounces divorce, is it valid? (No, it is void.)
Refer to the commentary's examples for clarity.

قال وهذه المعانى الثلاثة تُوجب الحجر فى الاقوال دون الافعال لانه لا مرد لها لوجودها حسا ومشاهدة بخلاف الاقوال لان اعتبارها موجودة بالشرع والقصد من شرطه الا اذا كان فعلا يتعلق به حكم يندرىء بالشبهات كالحدود والقصاص فيجعل عدم القصد فى ذلك شبهة في حق الصبي والمجنون ترجمہ اور یہ تینوں باتیں واجب کرتی ہیں حجر کو اقوال میں نہ کہ افعال میں، کیونکہ ان سے چارہ نہیں ان کے موجود ہونے کی وجہ سے حسا اور مشاہدہ بخلاف اول کے۔ کیونکہ ان کے موجود ہونے کا اعتبار بذریعہ شرع ہے اور اعتبار کی شرط قصد ہوتا ہے۔ مگر یہ کہ ایسا فعل ہو جس سے کوئی ایسا حکم متعلق ہو جو شبہات سے اٹھ جاتا ہے۔ جیسے حدود و قصاص تو اس میں عدم قصد کو شبہ قرار دیا جائے گا صبی اور مجنون کے حق میں۔ تشریح قوله وهذه المعانى .... الخ اسباب ثلثہ مذکورہ یعنی صغرور قیت اور جنون صرف اقوال میں حجر کو واجب کرتے ہیں نہ کہ افعال میں۔ کیونکہ افعال کو مانے بغیر چارہ نہیں اسلئے کہ وہ محسوس اور مشاہد کے طور پر موجود ہوتے ہیں۔ چنانچہ اگر کسی نے ایک انسان کو قتل کر دیا یا اس کا ہاتھ کاٹ دیا یا کسی کی کوئی چیز بر باد کردی تو قتل وقطع اور نعل اتلاف کو کالعدم نہیں کہہ سکتے ورنہ اس کا نتیجہ یہ ہوگا کہ جس کو قتل کیا گیا ہے وہ مقتول نہ ہو اور جس کا ہاتھ کاٹا گیا ہے وہ مقطوع الید نہ ہو اور جو چیز برباد کی گئی ہے وہ متلف نہ ہو حالانکہ نیہ دخول فی السوفسطائیہ اور حقائق کا کھلا انکار ہے۔ یعنی ما تردد منها بين النفع والضرر كالبيع والشراء وأما الاقوال التي فيها نفع محض فالصبى فيها كا لبائع ولهذا صح منه قبول الهبة والاسلام ولا يتوقف على اذن الولى وكذا العبد والمعتوه وأما ما يتمحض منها ضرراً كالطلاق والعتاق فانه يوجب الاعدام من الاصل في حق الصغير والمجنون دون العبد ۱۲ بنايه
He said: And these three meanings cause legal restraint (hajr) in statements, not in actions, because there is no escape from them due to their existence by sense and observation, unlike statements because their existence is considered by Shari'ah, and intent is a condition for their consideration. Except if it is an action to which a ruling is attached that is repelled by doubts, like hadd penalties and qisas, then lack of intent is considered a doubt (shubhah) in the case of the child and the insane. Translation: These three things necessitate hajr in statements, not in actions, because there is no escape from their existence by sense and observation, unlike the former, because their existence is considered through Shari'ah and the condition for consideration is intent. However, if an action has a ruling that is repelled by doubts, like hudud and qisas, then lack of intent is considered a doubt for the child and insane. Commentary: The three mentioned causes—minority, slavery, and insanity—necessitate hajr only in statements, not in actions, because actions cannot be denied; they exist as sensed and observed. So if someone kills a person, cuts off a hand, or destroys property, one cannot say the killing, cutting, or destruction did not happen; otherwise it would lead to sophistry and open denial of realities. That is: what is between benefit and harm like sale and purchase; as for statements that are purely beneficial, the child is like an adult regarding them, hence his acceptance of gifts and Islam is valid without guardian's permission, as is the case for the slave and the ma'tuh. As for statements that are purely harmful, like divorce and manumission, they are entirely void from the start in the case of the minor and the insane, but not the slave. (12 Binaayah)

Commentary
This passage explains the scope of legal restraint (hajr) imposed by the three causes: minority, slavery, and insanity. The key principle is that hajr applies only to statements (aqwal), i.e., verbal legal acts, because statements derive their legal effect from Shari'ah and require intent (qasd). Actions, on the other hand, are physically observable realities that cannot be denied. If a child or insane person kills someone, the act of killing is a fact; it cannot be treated as non-existent. However, the exceptional case is where the ruling itself is one that can be repelled by doubts (shubuhat), such as hudud and qisas. In those cases, the lack of intent (absence of full legal capacity) creates a doubt that prevents the application of the punishment or retaliation. The commentary further distinguishes among statements: those that are purely beneficial (e.g., accepting a gift, embracing Islam) are valid from the child or insane without guardian's permission; those that are purely harmful (e.g., divorce, freeing a slave) are void outright for the child and insane but not for the slave (because the slave's statements are suspended on the master's right). This nuanced approach reflects the Hanafi methodology of balancing textual authority, rational analysis, and practical consequences.

Key Terms
حجر (hajr): Legal restraint: prohibition on the effectiveness of certain legal acts due to lack of capacity (minority, slavery, insanity). Applies to statements not actions.
شبهة (shubhah): Doubt: a legally recognized uncertainty that repels the application of hadd penalties and qisas.
قصد (qasd): Intent: the deliberate intention to bring about a legal effect. Required for the validity of statements.
