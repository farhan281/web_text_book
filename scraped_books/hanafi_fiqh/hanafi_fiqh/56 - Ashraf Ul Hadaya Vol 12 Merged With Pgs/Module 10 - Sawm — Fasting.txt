Kitaba by a Sick Person with a Term: Dispute Between Shaykhayn and Imam Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on kitaba by a sick person with a term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Shaykhayn and Imam Muhammad regarding the immediate payment in a sick person's kitaba with a term, and apply the one-third bequest rule to calculate the amount.

Core Idea
A term in a sick person's kitaba is limited to one-third of the estate, affecting the immediate payment amount.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the chapter of kitaba: a sick person makes his slave a mukatab for an amount exceeding the slave's value, with a payment term, then dies. The heirs reject the term, demanding immediate payment. The Hanafi school has an internal dispute between the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Imam Muhammad on how much the slave must pay now. We will explore the reasoning behind each view and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
The slave's value is 1000, kitaba amount is 2000 with a one-year term, the sick person dies leaving only this slave, heirs reject the term.
The text also discusses a variant where kitaba amount is less than value.
2
State the Shaykhayn's ruling and its basis
They rule: slave pays two-thirds of the full named amount (2000) immediately, i.e., 1333. They treat the term as a partial waiver of the right to immediate payment, and the waiver is limited to one-third of the entire named amount.
This is the Zahir al-Riwayah position.
3
State Imam Muhammad's ruling and its basis
He rules: slave pays two-thirds of his own price (1000) immediately, i.e., 667. He argues that the sick person's authority is only over the excess beyond the slave's value, so the term applies only to that excess.
The excess (1000) is like a bequest.
4
Analyse the qiyas to a sale above market value
The text draws an analogy: a sick person sells a house worth 1000 for 3000 on a one-year term. Same dispute applies. This reinforces the principle that the Shaykhayn look at the total named price, while Imam Muhammad looks at the actual value plus the excess.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The slave's immediate payment differs by 666 (1333 vs 667). This affects the slave's ability to pay and achieve freedom, and the heirs' immediate recovery.
The consensus case when kitaba amount is less than value is also discussed.

Commentary on his statement: 'And if a sick person made kitaba...' etc. A sick person made his slave, whose price is one thousand, a mukatib for two thousand with a term of one year, then he died. And he left no property except this slave. Meanwhile, his heirs do not accept the term and want to take the kitaba payment immediately. According to the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), the aforementioned slave will pay two-thirds of the kitaba price (two thousand dirhams) immediately, and will pay the remaining one-third over his term. If he cannot do so, the kitaba contract is terminated and he becomes a slave. His statement: 'And according to Muhammad...' etc. According to Imam Muhammad, he will pay two-thirds of his own price (one thousand) immediately, and will continue to pay the rest over the term. He says that when the master has the right to forgo the amount that exceeds his price, i.e., one thousand, then he also has the right to defer that amount. Therefore, the slave is obliged to pay two-thirds of his price immediately. This is like a sick person who gave his wife a divorce for one thousand dirhams with a promise of one year, and this is valid, because the sick person has the right to divorce his wife without compensation. His statement: 'For them, that all of the named amount...' etc. The Shaykhayn's proof is that here the compensation is the full two thousand dirhams, such that the rulings of compensation apply to the full two thousand, and the heirs' right is connected to the subject matter (mubdal), so it will also be connected to the compensation. And deferral (giving time) is in the meaning of relinquishing a right, i.e., as if something of the right is waived, so its consideration will be from one-third of the entire named amount. That is, the sick person's authority is only over one-third of the estate, so reducing by setting a term will be considered from one-third of the entire named amount. Unlike divorce, because in divorce the compensation fixed is not in exchange for property, so the heirs' right is not related to the subject matter, because she is his wife, so similarly it will not be related to her compensation. His statement: 'And an example of this...' etc. An analogy for the aforementioned original issue is that a sick person sold his house worth one thousand for three thousand with a term of one year and died, and the heirs did not accept the term. According to the Shaykhayn, the buyer will be told to pay two-thirds of the full price immediately and one-third at its term, otherwise the sale is annulled. According to Imam Muhammad, only one-third of the amount of the price will be considered, not more. So he will pay two-thirds of one thousand immediately and the rest at the term, because the heirs' right without the term is only to that extent. This is because the sick person's sale at the full price is valid, so selling at an excess was the sick person's own right, and similarly granting a term in the excess is also his right. His statement: 'And if he made kitaba for one thousand...' etc. And if the slave's price is two thousand dirhams and the sick person makes kitaba for one thousand, the rest of the issue remains as before. In this case, by consensus of the three imams, he will pay two-thirds of his price immediately or choose slavery. This is because here the sick person has granted a concession (muhabah) in both amount and term, so one-third is considered in both.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the kitaba contract made by a sick person (marid) who is in his death-illness (marad al-mawt). The ruling involves the interplay between the bequest limited to one-third of the estate and the effects of stipulating a term (ajal) in the kitaba. The Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) hold that when a sick person makes kitaba for a slave worth 1000 for 2000 with a one-year term, and then dies leaving only that slave, the heirs who do not accept the term can demand immediate payment of the full kitaba amount. However, since the sick person's actions are restricted to one-third of his estate (as a bequest), the deferral is treated as a partial waiver of the right. Thus, two-thirds of the full named amount (2000) must be paid immediately, and one-third is deferred. Imam Muhammad disagrees, arguing that the sick person's authority extends only to forgoing the excess over the slave's value (i.e., 1000), so the deferral applies only to that excess. The slave pays two-thirds of his own price (1000) immediately and defers the rest. The passage also compares this to a sick person's divorce with compensation (khula) on a term, and to a sale of property above market value. It concludes with a consensus ruling that if the kitaba amount is less than the slave's value (e.g., 1000 for a 2000 slave), the slave must pay two-thirds of his value immediately or revert to slavery.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has contracted with his master to earn his freedom by paying a specified amount.
محاباة (muhabah): A concession or discount in price or term in a transaction, often considered a gift or bequest.
تہائی (tiha'i): One-third; the limit of bequests from the estate after death.
میعاد (mi'ad): A term or period for payment, set in the kitaba contract.

Free Person Performing Kitaba on Behalf of a Slave
Ruling on a free person making kitaba for a slave, with two scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a free person pays a slave's kitaba without a condition, and explain the role of istihsan.

Core Idea
A free person's payment of a slave's kitaba frees the slave by istihsan, even without a condition.

In this lesson, we explore a specific scenario in the Hanafi fiqh of kitaba: a free person contracts with a slave master on behalf of the slave. The passage distinguishes between two cases: when the free person explicitly makes manumission conditional on his payment, and when he does not. The ruling uses istihsan to override a strict qiyas, highlighting a key Hanafi method of legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the contract
Note that the free person (deputy) contracts with the master (principal) for the slave's kitaba. The slave is absent.
2
Understand the first scenario: conditional payment
The free person says: 'Make your slave a mukatab on condition that if I pay you 1000 dirhams, he is free.' When the free person pays, the slave becomes free immediately by virtue of the condition.
The condition makes the payment the direct cause of manumission.
3
Understand the second scenario: no condition stated
If the free person does not mention any condition and pays, by qiyas the slave does not become free because the contract is suspended (mawquf) pending the slave's acceptance. But by istihsan, the slave becomes free because there is no harm to the absent slave.
Istihsan overrides qiyas based on absence of harm.
4
Analyze the slave's acceptance
If the slave is informed and accepts the contract, he becomes a mukatab (the contract becomes binding on him). The kitaba was suspended on his permission; his acceptance acts as authorization.
Acceptance is required for the contract to be binding on the slave.
5
Determine the free person's right to reimbursement
If the free person pays, he cannot recover the amount from the slave because he is a benefactor (mutabarri'), not a creditor.
The free person's payment is considered a gift unless he stipulated reimbursement.

Kitab al-Mukatab
A free person stipulated kitaba on behalf of a slave for one thousand dirhams. If he pays on his behalf, the slave becomes free. If the slave is informed and accepts, he becomes a mukatab.
He said: And if a free person makes kitaba on behalf of a slave for one thousand dirhams, then if he pays on his behalf, the slave becomes free. And if the slave is informed and accepts, he is a mukatab. The form of the issue is that the free person says to the master of the slave: 'Make your slave a mukatab for one thousand dirhams on the condition that if I pay you one thousand, he is free.' So the master makes kitaba on this basis, and he becomes free by his payment by virtue of the condition. And if the slave accepts, he becomes a mukatab, because the kitaba was suspended on his permission, and his acceptance is permission. And if he did not say 'that if I pay you one thousand, he is free' and then he paid, by qiyas he does not become free because there is no condition and the contract is suspended. By istihsan he becomes free, because there is no harm to the absent slave in making freedom contingent on the payment of the speaker. So it is valid with respect to this ruling, but is suspended with respect to the obligation of the one thousand on the slave. And it is said that this is the form of the issue of the Book. And if the free person pays the compensation, he cannot recover it from the slave, because he is a benefactor.

Commentary
This passage introduces a new sub-chapter (bab) concerning kitaba performed by a deputy (na'ib) – a free person acting on behalf of a slave. The main ruling distinguishes two scenarios: First, if the free person pays the kitaba amount from his own pocket, the slave becomes free immediately (by the condition). Second, if the slave is informed and accepts the contract, he becomes a mukatab, meaning the contract becomes binding on him and he is responsible for payment. The text also discusses the case where the free person, when contracting with the master, did not explicitly state 'if I pay you, he is free'. In that case, by strict analogy (qiyas), the slave does not become free because there is no condition linking payment to manumission, and the contract is suspended pending the slave's acceptance. However, by istihsan (juristic preference), the slave does become free upon payment because there is no harm to the absent slave if freedom is made contingent on the payment of the free person. The ruling also addresses that the free person cannot reclaim the paid amount from the slave, as he is considered a benefactor (mutabarri'). The commentary explains the structure: the author has completed rulings for the principal (the master) and now discusses rulings for the deputy (the free person acting for the slave).

Key Terms
کتابت (kitaba): A contract between a master and slave whereby the slave earns freedom by paying a specified amount.
موقوف (mawquf): Suspended; a contract that is conditional upon the acceptance of the slave, pending which it is not binding on him.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; a method of departing from a strict analogy (qiyas) in favor of a more equitable ruling.
متبرع (mutabarri'): A benefactor; one who pays on behalf of another without expectation of repayment.
