Priority of Nafaqah for Dependents over Creditors
Ruling on priority of nafaqah over creditors' rights.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the priority of nafaqah for children and distant relatives over creditors' rights and explain the reasoning based on necessity.

Core Idea
Necessity of dependents outweighs debt claims in Hanafi law.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a critical priority: when a debtor has limited wealth, must he first pay his creditors or feed his children? The Hanafi position, rooted in the principle of necessity (darurah), establishes that the maintenance of dependents takes precedence. Additionally, the wife's proper dower (mahr al-mithl) ranks equally with creditors, reflecting the same concern for essential support.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
The passage states that maintenance (nafaqah) of children and distant relatives must be given from the debtor's wealth before paying creditors.
2
Understand the reasoning
The reason given is that the essential need (asli zarurat) of dependents takes precedence over the right of creditors. This is a form of istihsan (juristic preference) based on necessity.
3
Note the effect of hajr
Legal restriction (hajr) due to insolvency does not nullify the obligation of nafaqah because the right of maintenance is established for others and cannot be invalidated by the debtor's restriction.
4
Apply the same principle to mahr
If the debtor marries, his wife becomes a partner with creditors in his wealth up to the amount of her proper dower (mahr al-mithl), meaning she shares equally. This further illustrates that certain familial obligations rank with debt claims.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah states the ruling that the maintenance (nafaqah) of children and distant relatives (dhu al-arham) takes priority over the debts owed to creditors. The reasoning (ta'lil) is that the essential necessity of dependents is superior to the claims of creditors. Additionally, the right of maintenance is established for others, so the legal restriction (hajr) of insolvency cannot negate it. Furthermore, if the insolvent person marries, his wife becomes a partner with the creditors in his wealth up to the amount of her proper dower (mahr al-mithl), meaning she ranks equally with them.

Key Terms
نفقہ (nafaqah): Maintenance, including food, clothing, and shelter for dependents.
حجر (hajr): Legal restriction on a person's capacity to transact due to insolvency or other reasons.
مہر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The proper dower equal to that given to women of similar status.
