Is Tanāṣur the Definition or Purpose of Wala'?
Definitional discussion with intra-Hanafi dispute and hadith evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school treats tanāṣur as the purpose of wala' rather than its legal definition, and cite the hadith 'mawlā al-qawm minhum' as evidence.

Core Idea
Wala' creates mutual support (tanāṣur) – this is its purpose, not its definition.

The opening of Kitab al-Wala' in al-Hidayah contains a phrase that has sparked a subtle but important interpretive debate. Does 'wa al-murad bihima al-tanāṣur' define what wala' means, or does it state what wala' is meant to achieve? The answer shapes how we understand the legal nature of wala' as a quasi-kinship bond. In this lesson, we will parse the commentary and the hadith evidence to settle the question.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested phrase
Locate the phrase 'wa al-murad bihima al-tanāṣur' in the Hidayah context. The author of 'Inayah interprets it as a legal definition. The author of Nata'ij objects, arguing that if it were a definition, tanāṣur would need to be qualified with 'causing inheritance and blood wit'.
The key is whether the phrase is descriptive (what wala' is) or teleological (what wala' is for).
2
Trace the commentator's preferred view
The commentator sides with Nata'ij, noting that al-Kafi and al-Mabsut use 'al-matlub' (the sought purpose) instead of 'al-murad' (the meaning). This indicates that the author of Hidayah intended to explain the objective of wala', not its lexical or legal definition.
3
Understand the historical context
Before Islam, Arabs used various bonds (kinship, alliance, etc.) for mutual support. The Prophet retained through wala' the function of tanāṣur, making it a legitimate means of creating solidarity.
4
Analyze the hadith evidence
The hadith 'mawlā al-qawm minhum' from multiple Companions shows that the client is considered part of the people. This declaratory statement establishes a legal bond of belonging and mutual rights, supporting the idea that wala' is a cause of tanāṣur.
The hadith is mutawātir ma‘nawī, strengthening its probative value.
5
Synthesize the legal significance
Tanāṣur is not the definition but the hikma (wisdom) or maqsad (purpose) behind wala'. This distinction clarifies that wala' retains its technical meaning (a bond causing inheritance and 'aql) while fulfilling the broader objective of mutual support.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional discussion and evidence segment from the opening of Kitab al-Wala'. The commentator clarifies a dispute about the meaning of the phrase 'wa al-murād bihamā al-tanāṣur' from the Hidayah. Some scholars, like the author of 'Inayah, take it as a legal definition, while the author of Nata'ij argues it is not the definition but the purpose or objective of both types of wala'. The commentator supports the latter view, citing that the Kafi and Mabsut use 'al-matlūb' (the sought purpose) instead of 'al-murād' (the meaning). The essence is that both wala' al-itaqah and wala' al-muwalat are intended to foster mutual support (tanāṣur). This is evidenced by pre-Islamic Arab customs where various bonds created support, and the Prophet retained mutual support through both types of wala'. The hadith 'mawlā al-qawm minhum' are then quoted from multiple Companions as proof that a client is considered part of the people, implying mutual rights and support. This hadith section serves as textual evidence (dalil naqli) for the principle that wala' creates a bond of mutual support and belonging.

Key Terms
ولاء (walā’): The bond of clientage or patronage that arises from emancipation or agreement, entitling the patron to inherit and liable for blood wit.
تناصر (tanāṣur): Mutual support and assistance; the intended purpose of both types of wala'.
مولى (mawlā): A client; one who is the recipient of wala' – either the manumittor (upper mawlā) or the freed slave (lower mawlā).
عتاقہ (‘itāqah): Emancipation; the act of freeing a slave, which establishes wala' al-itaqah.
موالات (muwālāt): A pact of mutual patronage between a freedman and another person, establishing wala' al-muwalat.

Verification of the Hadith on Wala
Critical refinement of a hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the authenticity of the hadith 'al-wala' luhmah' by analyzing its chains and identifying the Hanafi position on its reliability.

Core Idea
The hadith 'al-wala' luhmah' is accepted by Hanafis despite criticism because it is transmitted through multiple trustworthy chains.

In the previous lesson we learned that wala is not transferable. This proof is anchored in a specific hadith. However, hadith critics have raised questions about its chain. In this lesson we will walk through the verification process, examining the sources and resolving the objections to confirm the hadith's authority within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and its legal use
State the exact Arabic wording of the hadith: 'al-wala' luhmah ka luhmah al-nasab la yuba' wa la yuhab'. This hadith is used to prove that wala is a bond like lineage and cannot be sold or gifted.
The hadith directly supports the ruling that wala is not transferable.
2
Trace the chains of narration
The hadith comes from four Companions: Ibn Umar, Ibn Abi Awfa, Abu Hurairah, and Ali. Focus on the Ibn Umar chain, which has multiple paths. One key chain: Ibn Umar ← Abdullah ibn Dinar ← Ya'qub ibn Ibrahim (Abu Yusuf) ← Bishr ibn al-Walid.
Ibn Hibban recorded it in his Sahih, and al-Hakim declared it sahih al-isnad.
3
Evaluate the criticism
Some scholars like al-Bayhaqi and Abu Bakr al-Nishapuri claimed the hadith is not preserved in its raised (marfu') form; they said the words belong to a mursal narration from Hasan al-Basri. Understand their argument: the chain omitted Ubaidullah and the text was only known as a forbiddance (nahy) of selling and gifting wala, not the 'luhmah' wording.
This would weaken the hadith if proven, making it mursal rather than marfu'.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response relies on the fact that in Imam Muhammad's Kitab al-Wala, the hadith is narrated with a complete, unbroken chain: Abu Yusuf ← Abdullah ibn Dinar ← Ibn Umar. Al-Bayhaqi's apology explains that al-Shafi'i narrated from memory and accidentally dropped Ubaidullah, but the original chain in Kitab al-Wala is sound.
Multiple trustworthy narrators (Shu'bah, Sufyan al-Thawri, etc.) also narrated the meaning but with the wording 'nahy' (prohibition) rather than the explicit 'luhmah' comparison. The Hanafis accept the marfu' version due to the strength of the chain in Kitab al-Wala.
5
Conclude the Hanafi position
The Hanafi madhhab considers the hadith sahih (authentic) and relies on it as a dalil for the ruling that wala cannot be sold or gifted. The criticism is outweighed by the presence of a sound chain and the acceptance by major hadith masters like Ibn Hibban and al-Hakim.
This verification step is crucial because it establishes the authority of the proof used in the earlier ruling.

Commentary
This section critically examines the hadith 'al-wala' luhmah ka luhmah al-nasab' which is used as proof for the previous ruling. It documents multiple chains, starting with Ibn Umar through Abu Yusuf, and notes that it was authenticated by Ibn Hibban, al-Hakim, and al-Dhahabi. A criticism arose: the hadith might be mursal from Hasan al-Basri. Al-Bayhaqi and others considered it non-masculine (unpreserved) in its raised form. The Hanafi position, however, holds the hadith as authentic and relies on it, supported by the fact that it is found in Imam Muhammad's Kitab al-Wala with a complete chain. The passage thus provides the necessary hadith criticism to establish the authority of the proof.

Key Terms
تخریج (takhrij): The process of extracting and verifying a hadith from its sources.
اسناد (isnad): Chain of transmission for a hadith.
رفع (rafa): Attributing a statement or action directly to the Prophet (peace be upon him).
مرسل (mursal): A hadith where the Companion is omitted from the chain; often considered weak by many scholars but accepted by Hanafis under certain conditions.
صحیح الاسناد (sahih al-isnad): A hadith whose chain is authentic according to the criteria of hadith critics.

Verification of the Hadith: Al-Wala' Luhmah and its Ruling
Hadith verification with multiple chains and responses to criticisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that wala (clientage) cannot be sold or gifted, and evaluate the hadith evidence supporting this position by analyzing its chains and responses to criticisms.

Core Idea
Wala is like lineage: it cannot be sold or gifted — only inherited.

The hadith 'al-wala luhmah' is a foundational proof for the Hanafi ruling that clientage (wala) is a right that cannot be transferred by sale or gift, only by inheritance. This lesson delves into the detailed verification (takhrij) of this hadith, examining multiple chains of narration from the Companion Ibn 'Umar and addressing the objections raised by hadith critics. By the end, you will understand why the Hanafi school considers this hadith authentic and how it supports the legal ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hadith and its Legal Import
Recognize that the hadith 'al-wala luhmah' (clientage is like a bond of lineage) explicitly states that wala cannot be sold or given away. This forms the basis for the Hanafi ruling that wala is inheritable and not transferable by contract.
2
Trace the Multiple Chains of Ibn Umar's Narration
The passage presents four distinct chains for the hadith from Ibn Umar. Follow each chain: first through Bishr ibn al-Walid to Abu Yusuf; second through Ali ibn Sulayman from al-Shafi'i to Abu Hanifa; third through Damrah from Sufyan al-Thawri; fourth through Muhammad ibn Ziyad and Yahya ibn Sulaym. Note the narrators and the sources (Ibn Hibban, al-Hakim, al-Bayhaqi, al-Tabarani).
3
Review Objections Raised by Hadith Critics
Identify the main criticisms: al-Bayhaqi and al-Naysaburi claimed the hadith is not preserved (ghayr mahfuz) and is actually a mursal of Hasan al-Basri; al-Tabarani noted the uniqueness of Damrah in one chain; al-Daraqutni pointed to a possible mistake in the fourth chain regarding Isma'il ibn Umayyah.
4
Examine the Hanafi Responses Defending Authenticity
For each objection, the passage provides a rebuttal: the marfu' version is narrated from multiple companions; Damrah is a reliable jurist; the fourth chain is corroborated by another narrator; and the omission of 'Ubaydullah in one chain is due to narration from memory. Understand how these responses uphold the hadith's authenticity.
5
Conclude the Status of the Hadith and the Derived Ruling
After addressing all criticisms, the hadith is established as sahih (authentic) by multiple chains. Consequently, the Hanafi ruling that wala cannot be sold or gifted is firmly based on this prophetic statement. The ruling applies to all transactions involving wala.

Verification of the hadith 'al-wala luhmah'. From 'Abdullah. His statement 'al-wala luhmah' etc. comes from four Companions: Ibn 'Umar, Ibn Abi Awfa, Abu Hurairah, and 'Ali. Among them, the hadith of Ibn 'Umar is narrated through multiple chains. First chain: from Bishr ibn al-Walid from Ya'qub ibn Ibrahim (al-Imam Abu Yusuf) from 'Abdullah ibn Dinar from Ibn 'Umar who said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: 'Wala is like the flesh of lineage; it is not sold nor gifted.' Its takhrij was done by Ibn Hibban in the second section of his Sahih. Imam al-Shafi'i narrated it in his Musnad with this chain: Akhbara Muhammad ibn al-Hasan from Abu Yusuf al-Qadi - Ya'qub ibn Ibrahim from 'Abdullah ibn Dinar. Al-Hakim narrated it in al-Mustadrak via the chain of Imam al-Shafi'i and said: This hadith is sahih al-isnad, and the two shaykhs (Bukhari and Muslim) did not record it. Al-Dhahabi followed him in al-Talkhis. In the Musnad of Imam al-Shafi'i, the mention of 'Ubaydullah is omitted. Whereas in Imam Muhammad's Kitab al-Wala, it is explicitly from Abu Yusuf from 'Abdullah ibn Dinar from Ibn 'Umar. Al-Hafiz al-Bayhaqi in Kitab al-Ma'rifah apologized for this: that Imam al-Shafi'i narrated this hadith from Imam Muhammad from memory, hence the omission of 'Ubaydullah in the chain. Objection: al-Bayhaqi in Kitab al-Ma'rifah said this hadith is ghayr mahfuz (unpreserved). Abu Bakr al-Naysaburi said: the mentioned hadith is wrong, i.e., raising it to the Prophet (marfu') with these words is wrong; these words are from the mursal narration of Hasan al-Basri. Indeed, a group of reliable narrators like 'Abd al-Wahhab al-Thaqafi, Sufyan al-Thawri, Shu'bah, al-Dahhak ibn 'Uthman, Sufyan ibn Humaid, Sufyan ibn Bilal, Isma'il ibn Ja'far and others narrated: 'The Prophet forbade selling wala and gifting it.' Answer: This hadith is narrated mursal with these words and also established as marfu'. The mursal was recorded by al-Daraqutni via Yazid ibn Harun from Hisham ibn Hassan from al-Hasan from the Prophet. As for the marfu', it is narrated from the four Companions, and the marfu' hadith of Ibn 'Umar has multiple chains, one of which is the one mentioned above. Second chain: recorded by al-Hakim in Kitab Manaqib al-Shafi'i: from 'Ali ibn Sulayman al-Ahmimiy, from Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafi'i, from Muhammad ibn al-Hasan, from Abu Yusuf, from Abu Hanifah, from 'Abdullah ibn Dinar. According to al-Hakim, al-Shafi'i also narrated it from Muhammad from Abu Yusuf from 'Abdullah ibn Dinar. Third chain: from Damrah from Sufyan al-Thawri from 'Abdullah ibn Dinar from Ibn 'Umar. Recorded by al-Bayhaqi and al-Tabarani. Objection: al-Tabarani said that Damrah is unique in narrating this hadith with these words. Al-Bayhaqi said that this narrator was mistaken and that Muhammad ibn Ibrahim ibn Yusuf al-Faryabi narrated it from Damrah correctly, like the group of huffaz. Answer: Imam Muhammad said: Its narrator Damrah ibn Rabi'ah was a famous jurist of Palestine in his time, with no equal in the land of Syria. Ibn Sa'd said: He was trustworthy and reliable, and there was no one like him there. It is obvious that if such a person is unique in narrating a hadith, his uniqueness is not harmful. Fourth chain: from Muhammad ibn Ziyad, from Yahya ibn Sulaym al-Ta'ifi, from Isma'il ibn Umayyah, from Nafi', from Ibn 'Umar. Recorded by al-Tabarani in al-Mu'jam al-Awsat and by al-Bayhaqi in al-Sunan. In the copies of al-Mu'jam al-Awsat, it is Muhammad ibn Ziyad, and this is correct. In the hawashi of al-Sunan al-Kubra, it is written in the handwriting of al-Hafiz Abu al-Qasim ibn 'Asakir: 'Indeed it is Muhammad ibn Ziyad ibn 'Ubaydullah al-Ziyadi al-Basri, and he is the shaykh of Ibn Khuzaymah, not Abu Hassan al-Ziyadi as al-Bayhaqi said in al-Sunan.' Objection: Regarding the narrator Yahya ibn Sulaym, al-Bayhaqi said he is poor in memory and makes many mistakes. Answer: Yahya ibn Sulaym has a mutabi' (corroborator) in Muhammad ibn Muslim al-Ta'ifi, as his hadith was narrated by al-Hakim in al-Mustadrak. Objection: al-Daraqutni in al-'Ilal said that the mention of Isma'il ibn Umayyah in the chain is a mistake from Muhammad ibn Ziyad, because all others narrated it differently: from Yahya ibn Sulaym from 'Ubaydullah ibn 'Umar from Nafi' from Ibn 'Umar (may Allah be pleased with him). Answer: This is not a mistake, because it is possible that in this narration Yahya ibn Sulaym had two shaykhs (Isma'il ibn Umayyah and 'Ubaydullah ibn 'Umar), and he heard this hadith from both. Objection: 'Allamah al-Zayla'i wrote that I did not find the word 'yurath' (inherited) in any chain of the hadith. Answer: Al-Daraqutni in Kitab al-'Ilal mentioned a narration from Ayyub ibn Sulayman al-A'war from 'Abd al-'Aziz ibn Muslim al-Qasmali from 'Abdullah ibn Dinar from Ibn 'Umar (which includes that word).

Commentary
This section is a detailed hadith verification (takhrij) of the hadith 'al-wala luhmah' (clientage is like a bond of lineage). It begins by listing the companions who narrated it: Ibn Umar, Ibn Abi Awfa, Abu Hurairah, and Ali. The focus is on Ibn Umar's narration, which has multiple chains. The first chain goes through Bishr ibn al-Walid from Ya'qub ibn Ibrahim (Abu Yusuf) from Abdullah ibn Dinar from Ibn Umar. This chain is recorded by Ibn Hibban and Imam Shafi'i. Al-Hakim declared it sahih, and al-Dhahabi agreed. The text then addresses various objections from hadith critics like al-Bayhaqi, al-Tabarani, and al-Daraqutni. Each objection is answered by providing alternative chains or defending the reliability of narrators. For instance, al-Bayhaqi claimed the hadith is not preserved (ghayr mahfuz) and is actually a mursal of Hasan al-Basri. The response shows that it is narrated both as mursal and marfu', with the marfu' version coming from multiple companions. Similarly, the uniqueness of Damrah in one chain is defended by citing his reliability. The passage also clarifies the identity of narrators in chains where there was confusion. Ultimately, the hadith is established as authentic and authoritative for the rulings on wala (clientage) in Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
الولاء لحمة (al-wala' luhmah): Wala (clientage) is a bond like that of lineage (flesh and blood).
تخريج (takhrij): The process of tracing a hadith to its original sources and verifying its chains of narration.
طريق (tariq): A chain of narration (isnad) for a hadith.
صحيح الإسناد (sahih al-isnad): A hadith whose chain of narration is authentic.

Source Criticism in Hidayah: The Case of Habib
Textual criticism of Hidayah's citation of Habib's story

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of source criticism (naqd al-mutun) to evaluate the evidentiary basis of a historical narrative cited in al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
A Hanafi scholar verifies sources even in authoritative texts; not all Hidayah citations are from Sahih.

In this lesson, we examine a passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah where the commentator critiques the Hidayah's citation of the story of Habib ibn Adiyy. The Hidayah mentions four details about Habib to illustrate patience under coercion. The commentator, however, shows that none of these four appear in the Sahih Bukhari narration, and only some appear in a weaker source. This demonstrates the meticulous care Hanafi scholars take in verifying the sources behind legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four claims
Find the four specific matters the Hidayah attributes to the story of Habib: compulsion, crucifixion, title Sayyid al-Shuhada, and being the Prophet's companion in Paradise.
These are listed at the beginning of the passage.
2
Compare with the primary source (Sahih Bukhari)
Read the summary of Bukhari's narration provided by the commentator. Note which of the four claims are present or absent.
Bukhari's version is considered the most authentic source.
3
Identify the alternative source (Waqidi)
Observe that the commentator cites a narration from Waqidi's Maghazi that includes ikrah and crucifixion. Note the underlined words that indicate these details.
Waqidi is a later historian; his narration is weaker than Bukhari in terms of isnad.
4
Evaluate the title and the 'companion' claim
The commentator notes that the title 'Sayyid al-Shuhada' is authentically attributed to Hamza, not Habib, and that the statement about being a companion in Paradise is not specifically proven for Habib.
This shows the importance of checking specific ascription.
5
Understand the commentator's purpose
The commentator is refining the Hidayah's source use, not rejecting the ruling that ikrah can excuse. He clarifies the evidentiary weight of the story so that scholars know which parts are strongly supported.
This is a lesson in scholarly integrity and source hierarchy.

The Objection on 'Muflis' in Hajr and its Resolution
Addressing objection that 'muflis' is inappropriate in hajr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the chapter on hajr mentions 'muflis' despite the ruling also applying to wealthy debtors, and analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute over the independent basis of interdiction.

Core Idea
Interdiction for debt is an independent ground, not dependent on a declaration of insolvency.

This passage addresses a subtle objection: why does the chapter on hajr (interdiction) specifically mention the 'muflis' (insolvent debtor) when, according to the Sahibayn, interdiction can also be imposed on a wealthy debtor? The commentator unpacks the intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether hajr due to debt requires a prior judicial declaration of insolvency (qada' bil-iflas). Through careful analysis of the author's wording, we see that the preferred view treats hajr as an independent ground, thus resolving the objection. Understanding this dispute deepens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists reconcile text with legal reality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Note the objection: the text's mention of 'muflis' seems inappropriate because hajr can also apply to a wealthy debtor. This objection arises from the Sahibayn's view that hajr is permissible for the sake of creditors even on a wealthy person.
The objection is methodological: a terminology mismatch.
2

Understand that some mashayikh hold that hajr due to debt is dependent on qada' bil-iflas (judicial declaration of insolvency). The fear of talji'ah (legal stratagem) is not a sufficient reason to make hajr dependent on insolvency.

Determine the preferred (mufta bihi) view
The commentator concludes that the author's wording supports the independent view. Therefore, the mention of 'muflis' in the chapter is not flawed; rather, it is a specific case that does not exclude the general ruling on wealthy debtors.
This intra-Hanafi tarjih shows how later scholars (fatawa mutakhkhirin) resolved the dispute.

Silence as Implicit Authorization for a Slave
Inter-madhhab dispute on silence as authorization

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that a master's silence constitutes authorization for a slave to trade, and evaluate the opposing view of Imam Zufar and the three Imams.

Core Idea
Silence in a context requiring speech is consent – protecting third-party dealings from harm.

This passage tackles a key dispute in Islamic commercial law: can a master's silence when seeing his slave trade be taken as authorization? The Hanafi school says yes, to safeguard the interests of those who deal with the slave. Imam Zufar and the three Imams disagree. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi fiqh balances textual proof with public welfare (istihsan).

Guided Path
1
Identify the central issue
What is the legal question being debated? It is whether a master's silence upon seeing his slave engage in buying and selling establishes authorization for the slave to trade.
Focus on the precise act: the master's silence, not the slave's actions.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that silence in this context constitutes implicit authorization (idhn bi al-dalalah al-hal). The slave becomes a ma'dhun (authorized to trade) and his transactions are binding on the master.
The Hanafi proof is based on contextual indication (dalil al-hal) and the need to prevent harm to third parties.
3
State the opposing position
Imam Zufar (a Hanafi) and the three Imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) hold that silence does not establish authorization. They argue that the master is not obliged to prevent imaginary harm, and his silence could be due to anger, not consent.
This view treats silence as ambiguous and requires explicit verbal or written permission.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that people naturally interpret silence in such a situation as permission because the master would have objected if displeased. After debts are incurred, the master's excuse of anger is not accepted because he had the opportunity to speak.
The response relies on 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' and the prevalence of custom (urf) in commercial dealings.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Hanafi law, if a slave trades with the master's silent acquiescence, the transactions are binding on the master and third parties can claim debts from him. Under the opposing view, such transactions are not binding on the master unless explicit authorization exists.
This difference directly impacts the liability of the master and the security of market transactions.

The key example: if a master sees his slave buying and selling and remains silent, that silence is interpreted as consent, making the slave a ma'dhun (authorized slave). The rationale is to protect third parties who deal with the slave in good faith; if the master later denied authorization, people would suffer harm. The master could have stopped the slave if he disapproved. Imam Zufar and the three Imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) disagree, arguing that the master is not obliged to prevent imaginary harm and his silence might be due to anger. The Hanafi response is that people understand silence in such a context as permission; the master's after-the-fact excuse of anger is ineffective once dealings have occurred and debts are incurred. This shows the Hanafi emphasis on protecting market transactions and preventing harm to the public.

Key Terms
إذن (idhn): Permission granted by the master to the slave to engage in trade, making him a ma'dhun.
سكوت (sukut): Silence of the master in a situation where speech is expected; considered as consent in Hanafi fiqh.
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave authorized by his master to engage in trade within the scope of permission.

General Liability for Destruction or Defect of Usurped Property
Explains liability for destruction/defect and distinguishes from market fluctuation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a usurper's liability for destruction or defect of movable property and distinguish it from liability for market price fluctuation.

Core Idea
A usurper guarantees every part of the property; market drop is not a loss of a part.

This passage explains that if a usurped movable property is destroyed or becomes defective, the usurper must compensate for the value at the time of usurpation or the deficiency. However, if the property is returned in the same physical condition but its market price has fallen, there is no liability because market fluctuation does not involve loss of a physical part. The commentator also distinguishes this from the case of sale where liability is by contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is ghasb (usurpation) of movable property. The ruling: the usurper is liable (daman) for the value at the time of seizure if the property is destroyed, or for the deficiency if it becomes defective.
Liability arises from the act of ghasb itself, not from any subsequent action.
2
Understand the basis of liability
Through ghasb, every part of the property enters the usurper's daman (liability). When restitution in kind is impossible (e.g., destroyed part), price compensation becomes wajib.
3
Distinguish market fluctuation
If the property is returned intact but its market value has dropped, the usurper is not liable by consensus. A market drop is a change in demand, not a loss of a physical part.
Contrary to physical defect, which is a loss of a part.
4
Contrast with sale
In a sale contract, liability is by contract; if the seller defects the item before delivery, he is not liable for attribute defects because attributes are not compensated in sale. In ghasb, liability is by possession and the usurper's act.
This reinforces that ghasb liability is broader.

The Ribawi Exception: No Compensation for Defect in Usurious Goods
Explains the exception for ribawi goods where no compensation for qualitative defect is allowed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a usurper does not compensate for a qualitative defect in ribawi goods and identify the ruling on restitution without compensation for impurity.

Core Idea
In ribawi items, compensation for impurity would be riba; only the original measure is returned.

This passage addresses a special exception: if the usurped property is ribawi (e.g., wheat, silver) and the usurper mixes it with something (like water), he is not required to compensate for the lost purity. He simply returns the goods as they are. This is because ribawi transactions require equality in measure; taking extra would be riba. The commentator clarifies that Imam Quduri's phrase 'wa in naqasa fi yadihi' refers to non-ribawi goods; for ribawi goods, compensation for defect is not possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
The usurped property is ribawi (e.g., wheat, silver, gold). The usurper causes a qualitative defect by mixing it with another substance (e.g., water in wheat).
The defect is qualitative, not quantitative; the measure remains the same.
2
State the ruling
The usurper must return the property in its current state. He does not pay compensation for the decreased purity or quality.
He simply returns the mixed goods as they are.
3
Explain the rationale
In ribawi items, the Shari'ah requires equality in measure (kayl or wazn) when exchanging like for like. Taking compensation for impurity would involve an unequal exchange, which is riba.
Fully, pure and mixed are considered equal in measure; hence no extra can be taken.
4
Contrast with non-ribawi goods
For non-ribawi goods, compensation for defect is wajib because the property is under the usurper's daman for every part.
5
Note the practical effect
The usurper returns the mixed wheat; the owner cannot demand extra wheat to compensate for the water. The owner bears the loss of purity.
This prevents riba.
