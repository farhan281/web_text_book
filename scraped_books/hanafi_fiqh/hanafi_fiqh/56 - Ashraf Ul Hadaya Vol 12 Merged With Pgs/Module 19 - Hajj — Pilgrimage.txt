Rulings on Umrah, Hajj, and Wills for the Safih
Rulings on safih: umrah by istihsan, qiran allowed, badanah allowed, wasiyyah valid in third.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a safih may perform umrah, and explain the role of istihsan in resolving inter-madhhab disputes.

Core Idea
A safih is allowed one umrah by istihsan due to Shafi'i disagreement, even though umrah is not wajib in the Hanafi school.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses three practical issues for a safih (interdicted person under financial restriction): performing umrah, combining umrah with hajj (qiran), and making bequests during illness. The central legal question is whether the safih's financial limitation prevents him from engaging in devotional acts that involve expenditure. The ruling on umrah is particularly interesting because it involves an inter-madhhab dispute: although the Hanafi school holds umrah to be sunnah mu'akkadah (not obligatory), the Shafi'i school considers it wajib. This disagreement leads the Hanafi school to apply istihsan (juristic preference) to allow one umrah.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on umrah for the safih
Identify what the default ruling (qiyas) would be based on the Hanafi school's own classification of umrah. Since umrah is not obligatory, the safih would normally be prevented from spending on it, just as he would be prevented from performing nafl hajj.
Qiyas would also apply to any additional umrahs beyond one.
2
Identify the opposing school and its view
Recognize that Imam Shafi'i holds umrah to be wajib (obligatory) once in a lifetime. This creates an ikhtilaf (valid disagreement) among scholars.
The opposing school's view is not elaborated in the passage but is the basis for the istihsan.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response: istihsan
Understand that the Hanafi school applies istihsan to deviate from qiyas. The reason given is 'ikhtilaf al-ulama fi wujubiha' – the disagreement of scholars regarding its obligation. To avoid the risk of preventing an act that might be obligatory, the safih is not prevented from performing one umrah.
This does not change the Hanafi school's own position on umrah; it is a practical accommodation.
4
Apply the reasoning to qiran and badanah
Since the safih may perform one umrah and also may perform hajj individually, he is allowed to combine them (qiran). Similarly, he may drive a sacrificial animal (badanah) to avoid the area of dispute, following the view of Abdullah ibn Umar.
This shows that the principle of istihsan extends to related details.
5
Note the ruling on bequests (wasiyyah)
If the safih falls ill and makes bequests for charitable purposes, these are valid within one-third of his wealth. The reason given is that at the time of death, his interest shifts from preserving wealth to earning reward.
This ruling is independent of the umrah dispute and follows general Hanafi principles.

The سفيه and Acts of Worship: Umrah, Hajj, and Bequests
Rulings for the سفيه on Umrah, Hajj, and bequests with istihsān reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school allows a سفيه to perform one Umrah despite his interdiction, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
A سفيه is not prevented from one Umrah due to istihsan arising from a valid ikhtilaf on its obligation.

This lesson examines the boundary between interdiction and worship for the سفيه (prodigal placed under legal restriction). While the default qiyas would prevent him from any supererogatory act that wastes wealth, a significant scholarly difference on the obligation of Umrah creates an exception. The Hanafīs use istihsān (juristic preference) to permit one Umrah. The passage also covers Hajj al-Qirān, driving a badanah, and bequests, illustrating how necessity and ikhtilaf shape the rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default qiyas
By analogical reasoning, a سفيه should be prevented from spending on supererogatory acts like Umrah, just as he is prevented from supererogatory Hajj. Recognize that Umrah in the Hanafī school is sunnah, not obligatory.
Qiyas would prohibit; this is the starting point.
2
Understand the istihsan exception
The text states he is not prevented 'istihsānan li-ikhtilāf al-‘ulamā’ fī wujūbihā'. Because Imam al-Shāfi‘ī considers Umrah obligatory, there is a real possibility that preventing him would cause him to miss an obligation. The Hanafīs adopt the more cautious position via istihsān.
Istihsān overrides qiyas due to strong disagreement.
3
Apply the ruling to Hajj al-Qirān and badanah
He is not prevented from combining Hajj and Umrah (Qirān) because each alone is permitted. Driving a badanah (sacrificial camel/cow) is also permitted to avoid the dispute with Ibn ‘Umar who required it.
The same principle of avoiding ikhtilaf extends.
4
Examine the bequest ruling
If the سفيه falls ill and bequeaths up to one-third of his wealth for good deeds, it is valid. Reason: at death, his connection to wealth ceases, and the bequest brings reward. This is not prevented because it is a final act of devotion.
Bequests from a سفيه are allowed despite interdiction.
5
Contrast with the Shāfi‘ī position
Imam al-Shāfi‘ī holds Umrah is wājib, so a سفيه would be compelled to perform it, not merely allowed. The practical difference (thamarah al-khilāf) is that in the Hanafī school he may perform it once; for Shāfi‘īs, it is mandatory.
The ikhtilaf changes the ruling from permission to obligation.

Four Capacity Differences: The سفيه vs. the Discriminating Minor
List of four capacity differences between a سفيه and a discerning minor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four specific rulings where a سفيه differs from a discerning minor and explain the rationale behind each difference.

Core Idea
Despite similar interdiction, the سفيه has greater legal capacity in marriage, divorce, manumission, and tadbir than a discerning minor.

The text concludes with a comparison between two persons with limited capacity: the سفيه who attains majority but is prodigal, and the discriminating minor (صبي عاقل) who understands transactions. The author of Kifayat al-Muntaha notes they are equal in general rules but differ in four specific matters. Understanding these distinctions clarifies the Hanafi view that the سفيه's deficiency is in financial management, not personal status actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common ground
Both the سفيه and the discerning minor are under interdiction regarding property. Their transactions are restricted. But their capacity for personal actions differs.
Both need guardian approval for major financial dealings.
2
List the four differences
The text enumerates: (1) Authority of father/guardian, (2) Marriage, (3) Divorce and manumission, (4) Tadbir (contract to free slave after death).
These are all personal status (ahwal shakhsiyya) matters.
3
Analyze difference 1: Guardian authority
For a minor, the father or his executor can manage property without court order. For a سفيه, only the judge can authorize transactions; the father cannot act directly.
The سفيه's majority gives him some autonomy; his guardian is the judge.
4
Analyze difference 2: Marriage
The سفيه's marriage is valid, while the discerning minor's is not. Rationale: The سفيه is of age and can consent; the minor cannot.
Marriage is a personal act, not purely financial.
5
Analyze differences 3 & 4: Divorce, manumission, tadbir
The سفيه's divorce and manumission are valid; the minor's are not. Similarly, the سفيه's tadbir is valid; the minor's is not.
These are unilateral dispositions that the سفيه, being mature, can execute.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya addresses several rulings related to the سفيه (prodigal) who is under legal interdiction. The first part discusses the سفيه's performance of Umrah, Hajj, and related acts. By qiyas, a سفيه should be prevented from any voluntary act that wastes wealth, including a supererogatory Umrah. However, due to a significant difference among scholars (Imam al-Shafi'i holds Umrah is obligatory, while the Hanafis consider it sunnah), the Hanafis make an exception via istihsan: the سفيه is not prevented from performing one Umrah, as it is precautionary to avoid potential sin. For more than one Hajj (supererogatory), he is prevented because there is no such dispute. He is also not prevented from Hajj al-Qiran (combining Hajj and Umrah in one ihram) because he is allowed each individually, nor from driving a badanah (a camel or cow as a sacrificial animal) since it avoids the disagreement of Abdullah ibn Umar, who required such for validity. Regarding bequests, if a سفيه falls ill and bequeaths up to one-third of his wealth for charitable purposes, it is valid because at the point of death his interest shifts to the afterlife, and preventing him would deprive him of reward. The author notes that more details are in Kifayat al-Muntaha. The second part explains a comparison between two types of persons: a سفيه who attains majority and a discerning minor (صبي عاقل). They are generally equal in legal capacity but differ in four specific issues: (1) the father/guardian can manage the minor's property without court order but not the سفيه's; (2) the سفيه's marriage is valid while the minor's is not; (3) the سفيه's divorce and manumission are valid while the minor's are not; (4) the سفيه's tadbir (contract to free a slave after death) is valid while the minor's is not. These distinctions are based on the سفيه's maturity and accountability despite his financial irresponsibility.

Key Terms
سفیہ (safīh): A legally incompetent person due to prodigality, who wastes wealth even if of sound mind, and is placed under interdiction.
صبی عاقل (sabīy 'āqil): A discerning minor who understands the nature of transactions, though not yet an adult.
