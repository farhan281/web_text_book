Liability for Accidental Fire and Validity of Half-Profit Work
Gives two independent Hanafi rulings on liability and partnership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for accidental fire on rented land and explain the validity of half-profit work arrangements as a form of shirkat al-wujuh.

Core Idea
No liability for accidental harm without transgression; half-profit work is valid as credit partnership.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents two independent rulings from the Hanafi school. The first concerns liability for fire that spreads from rented land to another's property, distinguishing based on the wind condition at the time of lighting. The second validates a common practice among artisans—employing a worker who accepts jobs on a half-profit basis—classifying it as a form of credit partnership (shirkat al-wujuh). Both rulings illustrate key Hanafi principles: the role of transgression (ta'addi) in tort liability and the flexibility of contractual forms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios and their rulings
Read the passage and note the two distinct cases: (a) burning harvests on rented land causing fire damage to another land, and (b) a tailor/dyer seating a worker who offers work on halves. State the initial ruling for each case as given in the text.
The first ruling says 'no liability' under certain conditions; the second says 'it is permissible'.
2
Examine the liability condition for fire damage
Notice that the ruling distinguishes based on the wind condition at the time of lighting. If the winds were calm and then changed, there is no liability. If the winds were already turbulent when the fire was lit, the person is liable because he should have known the fire would spread. Identify the underlying principle: transgression (ta'addi).
The phrase 'غير متعد' (not transgressing) is key.
3
Trace the qiyas used in the fire ruling
The text analogizes the burning act to digging a well in one's own property. The original case (asl) is digging a well where no liability arises because there is no transgression. The new case (far') is burning harvests on rented land. The effective cause (illa) is the lack of transgression in the causation.
The qiyas is explicit: 'فاشبه حافر البئر في دار نفسه'.
4
Analyze the partnership arrangement
For the half-profit work arrangement, the text explains that it is valid because it is essentially a credit partnership (shirkat al-wujuh). One party uses his reputation (وجاهته) to attract customers, and the other uses his skill (حذاقته) to perform the work. Ignorance of the exact profit does not invalidate the arrangement because the profit is divided according to the agreed ratio.
The term 'شركة الوجوه' is a recognized form of partnership in Hanafi law.

Travel as an Excuse in Slave Hire Contracts
Ruling with proof on travel as udhr in slave hire.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on travel as a valid excuse (udhr) in a slave hire contract and apply the principle of harm (darar) to distinguish between hire of services and rental of property.

Core Idea
Travel excuses terminating a slave's city-service hire because it imposes un-stipulated hardship – but not for property rental.

This passage examines a specific case in Hanafi ijarah: when a change of circumstance—travel—gives the hirer the right to cancel the contract. The ruling hinges on whether the contract implicitly limits service to urban residence and on avoiding harm (darar) that was not agreed upon. The text contrasts slave hire with property rental to illustrate the principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contract and Its Implicit Scope
The contract is for a slave's service in the city (al-misr) or absolutely (mutlaq). Under Hanafi law, a mutlaq service contract defaults to urban residence.
This default is established in earlier sections of the text.
2
Recognize Travel as a Source of Harm
Travel imposes additional hardship (mashaqqah) and prevents the hirer from traveling if the contract remains binding. Neither was intended in the contract.
The term 'darar' (harm) is key: anything causing un-stipulated harm qualifies as udhr.
3
Apply the Ruling: Travel is an Excuse
Because the contract does not require travel service, forcing it would cause extra hardship. Therefore, the hirer may terminate the contract without penalty.
This applies whether the contract specifies 'city' or is absolute.
4
Contrast with Property Rental
If the owner of a rented house travels, the tenant can still benefit from the property. No harm arises, so travel is not an excuse for the owner. However, if the tenant travels, it becomes an excuse due to the owner's loss of rent without occupation.
The illah (cause) is the presence or absence of darar to the other party.

Liability for Fire Damage from Burning Stubble on Rented Land
Ruling with proof: liability for fire spread from burning stubble.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for fire damage caused by burning stubble on rented land and apply the condition of wind turbulence to determine liability.

Core Idea
No liability for permissible acts without transgression; but foreseeability of harm changes the ruling.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi tort ruling: if a tenant burns stubble on rented land and the fire spreads to a neighbor's property, is he liable? The answer hinges on whether the act was transgressive (ta'addi) and whether the wind was calm or turbulent. The ruling is grounded in a qiyas (analogy) to digging a well on one's own property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is burning stubble on rented or borrowed land. The general ruling: no liability if the fire spreads to another's land, provided the act is permissible and the wind is calm.
Ruling changes if wind is turbulent.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a qiyas: the act is analogized to digging a well in one's own house. In both cases, the act is permissible and without transgression, so no liability arises from the outcome.
The well analogy: if someone falls in, the digger is not liable.
3
Trace the illa (effective cause)
The illa is the absence of transgression (ghayr muta'addi). The act is permissible and done within one's rights, so causation (tasabbub) does not create liability.
Transgression is a necessary condition for liability in indirect causation.
4
Note the condition (wind)
The ruling is conditional: if at the time of burning the wind is calm, no liability. But if the wind is turbulent, the burner knows the fire will spread, so he is liable because he foresees the harm and acts despite it.
Foreseeability transforms a permissible act into a transgressive one.
5
Evaluate objections
Some object that fire is inherently more dangerous than a well. The Hanafi response: the key is transgression, not danger level. If the wind is calm, the burner does not foresee spread and is not transgressing.
This distinction preserves the principle that liability follows transgression.

Fire Liability in Rented or Borrowed Land
Ruling on liability for fire spreading from rented land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a person is not liable for fire damage from rented land and explain the legal reasoning (talil) based on direct vs. indirect causation.

Core Idea
No liability for fire damage from rented land if wind calm and fire permissible because transgression is absent.

Imagine you rent a field and burn crop residue. Sparks fly to your neighbor's barn. Are you liable? This lesson from Al-Hidayah addresses exactly this scenario. It introduces the crucial distinction between direct causation (mubāsharah) and indirect causation (sabab), and how the presence of transgression (ta'addi) flips liability. We will dissect the ruling, its condition (calm vs. turbulent wind), and the underlying principle that private use without transgression does not automatically entail compensation for harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Ruling
Read the base text: someone rents or borrows land, lights a fire that spreads to another's property. The initial ruling: no liability (ḍamān). Why? Because the act of lighting was permissible and there was no transgression.
The ruling assumes the fire was lit for a legitimate purpose (e.g., burning crop residue).
2
Understand the Legal Reasoning (Talil)
The commentary explains that the fire-lighter is a direct agent (mubāshir). For a direct agent, liability does not require transgression—however, the ruling says no liability. This is because the direct act is not attributed to the agent if the act is permissible and the damage is unforeseeable under normal conditions. The analogy is to digging a well in one's own house: if someone falls in, the digger is not liable because the act was permissible and within his property.
This is based on the Hanafi principle that mubāsharah coupled with a permissible act and no transgression does not create liability.
3
Examine the Exception: Turbulent Wind
Al-Marghinani adds that the ruling of no liability only applies when the wind is calm (sākin). If the wind is turbulent (hā'ija), the lighter becomes liable because he knows the fire will likely spread. This knowledge constitutes transgression (ta'addi) as he failed to prevent foreseeable harm.
The key is foreseeability: with calm wind, spread is not expected; with turbulent wind, it is.
4
Recognize the Qiyas (Analogy)
The qiyas is to digging a well in one's own property: permissible act, no transgression, no liability. Similarly, burning crop residue on one's own (or rented) land is permissible. Only when the act invites harm (like digging a well near a public road) does liability arise.
This is a qiyas shabah (analogy based on resemblance) with the well scenario.
5
Note the Clarification from Later Scholars
Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi and Sheikh al-Sighnaqi confirm that the word 'hādi'a' means calm, and the ruling depends on wind condition. This shows how the Ḥanafī school refines rulings by adding conditions that were implicit.
The condition of wind is not in the base matn but derived by later scholars.

Tailor Hiring a Person to Bring Work and Sharing Fees
Ruling on permissibility of a tailor hiring someone to solicit work and share earnings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers this arrangement a valid partnership (shirkat al-wujuh) while the Shafi'i school deems it invalid, and evaluate the role of istihsan.

Core Idea
A tailor hiring a person to bring work and sharing fees is valid as a partnership, not a lease.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common trade practice: a tailor or dyer hires someone to bring in customers and they split the earnings. By strict analogy (qiyas), this looks like a lease with unknown wages, which is invalid. But the Hanafi school overrides the analogy with istihsan, reclassifying it as a partnership based on prestige and skill. Let's unpack the reasoning and the Shafi'i objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the arrangement
A craftsman hires a person to solicit work and shares the fee equally. The hired person does not perform the craft; he uses his social standing to bring in customers.
2
Apply qiyas to the case
By strict analogy, this resembles a lease (ijara) with unknown compensation, which leads to gharar. Also, the craftsman's usufruct (skill) cannot be capital in a partnership. Thus, by qiyas, the contract is invalid.
This is the position of Imam al-Shafi'i.
3
Apply istihsan to override qiyas
Hanafi jurists rule by istihsan that the contract is valid. They recharacterize it as a partnership (shirkat al-wujuh), not a lease. The hired person contributes his prestige (wajahah), and the craftsman contributes his skill. In a partnership, unknown profit is permissible.
Istihsan is a Hanafi source of law that can override qiyas.
4
Examine the basis of istihsan
The author of Al-Hidayah calls it shirkat al-wujuh; commentators also call it shirkat al-sana'i' (crafts partnership). The hired person's contribution is his personal prestige, not capital or labor.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam al-Tahawi preferred qiyas over istihsan, but the majority of Hanafis follow istihsan. The mufta bihi is validity.

Validity of Hiring a Camel with Litter and Riders: Istihsan vs Qiyas
Ruling on validity of hiring a camel with litter and riders despite gharar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school validates the hire of a camel with a litter and two riders despite gharar, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i/Hanbali position.

Core Idea
The rider is known; the litter is subsidiary—custom removes uncertainty, making the contract valid by istihsan.

In hire contracts, uncertainty about the subject matter generally invalidates the agreement. Yet in practice, some uncertainty is unavoidable. This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a classic dispute: hiring a camel to carry a litter and riders to Mecca. The Shafi'i and Hanbali schools deem it invalid due to gharar, while the Hanafi school rules it valid by istihsan. The key is that the main object—the rider—is known, and the subsidiary litter's specifications are determined by custom. Understanding this distinction clarifies how Hanafi fiqh balances textual principles with practical custom.

Guided Path

Istihsan Over Qiyas in Ijarah: The Camel Litter Case
Ruling with proof via istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring a camel with a litter and two riders, explain the istihsan reasoning, and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Custom removes uncertainty in subsidiary terms, making a lease valid by istihsan despite apparent qiyas.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi ijarah case: hiring a camel to carry a litter (mahmil) and two riders to Mecca. While qiyas (analogy) suggests invalidity due to uncertainty about the litter's size, the Hanafi school upholds the contract using istihsan (juristic preference) grounded in custom. Understanding this case clarifies how Hanafi fiqh balances textual reasoning with practical custom ('urf).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is leasing a camel to carry a litter and two riders. The ruling is that the contract is valid (jaza) and the hirer is entitled to the customary litter.
The ruling departs from strict qiyas.
2
Locate the dalil (proof) and its type
The proof is istihsan, a departure from qiyas based on custom. No direct textual proof is cited; the reasoning is that the primary object (maqsud) is the rider, who is known, while the litter is subsidiary (tabi').
Istihsan is a recognized source of law in the Hanafi school.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal reasoning)
The key point: the rider is known, and any uncertainty about the litter is removed by referring to the customary norm ('urf). Since people generally know the size of a typical litter, the contract does not lead to dispute.
The subsidiary is governed by custom, which makes the contract determinate.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement) and opposing proof
Imam al-Shafi'i holds the contract invalid due to uncertainty (jahalah) that could lead to dispute. He applies strict qiyas without considering custom.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different usul al-fiqh.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih (preference)
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa). The reliance on custom to cure uncertainty reflects the school's pragmatic approach, preventing hardship in commercial transactions.
The rule is that uncertainty in subsidiary matters can be cured by custom if it does not lead to dispute.

Porter Eating Provisions on Journey: Permissibility and Custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a porter eating from provisions without stipulation, explain the role of custom (urf) in ijarah contracts, and contrast this with the Shafi'i/Hanbali requirement of an explicit condition.

Core Idea
A porter may eat from the journey provisions by custom, not condition — common practice binds the contract.

In this passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, the author discusses a classic scenario of ijarah (hire): a porter hired to carry goods on a journey. The question arises whether he may eat from the provisions without explicit permission. The Hanafi school relies on widespread custom (urf) to permit it, while other schools demand a stipulation. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning and the practical difference between the schools.

Guided Path
1
1. State the Hanafi position
Identify what the Hanafi school rules: the porter may eat from the provisions as much as he consumes and may replenish the deficiency, because he is entitled to carry a fixed amount over the whole journey. This is based on custom (urf).
The porter does not need an explicit condition in the contract.
2
2. Identify the opposing school's view
Imam al-Shafi'i and one narration of Imam Ahmad (Hanbali) require an explicit stipulation (shart) for the porter to eat; without it, it is not permissible. Another narration of Ahmad and Imam Malik follow custom, similar to Hanafi.
Custom-based and stipulation-based positions are the main dividing lines.
3
3. Follow the opponent's proof
The opponent argues that the contract does not mention eating, so by default it is not allowed. They require explicit permission for any benefit beyond the core obligation.
This reflects a stricter approach to contract interpretation.
4
4. Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the common custom of travelers (urf mushtarak) is so widespread that it effectively acts as an implied term. Just as travelers refill water, replenishing eaten provisions is customary. The absolute contract is interpreted in light of this custom.
Custom that is general and cannot be restricted (like this one) overrides the need for a stipulation.
5
5. Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: In the Hanafi school, the porter can eat without any condition; in the Shafi'i/Hanbali view, he cannot unless the contract explicitly allows it. However, if the contract includes a condition prohibiting eating, that condition is followed in all schools.
The same ruling applies to any other item (not just provisions) if it is customary.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses the ruling on a porter (hammal) who is hired to carry goods on a journey, including provisions (zad al-rah). The Hanafi position: the porter is entitled to eat from the provisions as much as he consumes during the journey, and he can also replenish (replace) what he eats, because he is entitled to carry a fixed amount. The author presents the inter-madhhab dispute: Imam al-Shafi'i and, in one narration, Imam Ahmad make it conditional — they require an explicit stipulation for the porter to eat. In a second narration from Imam Ahmad, and also Imam Malik, the ruling depends on custom: if it is customary for porters to eat from the provisions, then it is permissible; otherwise not. The author then clarifies a possible objection: how can the porter eat without a stipulation? The answer is based on 'urf (custom). Just as travelers customarily refill water, replenishing eaten provisions is also customary. This common custom (urf mushtarak) cannot be restricted, so the contract is interpreted according to custom. However, if there is an explicit condition to the contrary, that condition is followed. If the contract stipulates that the porter will replenish, he must do so by agreement. The text also notes that the same ruling applies to any other item besides provisions (e.g., other goods carried).

معتاد (muʿtād): What is customary or usual according to practice.
عرف مشترک (ʿurf mushtarak): A common custom that is widespread and not subject to restriction; it is deemed effective in contracts.

The Validity of an Authorized Slave Hiring Himself Out: Hanafi vs. The Three Imams
Inter-madhhab dispute on ijarah of slave's person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an authorized slave hiring himself out and explain the opposing view and the Hanafi response using analogy (qiyas).

Core Idea
An authorized slave's person is his capital; he may hire himself out as a trade, unlike selling or pledging himself.

This passage addresses a key point of contention: can an authorized slave contract to hire himself out? The Hanafi school permits it, while three other Imams prohibit it. Understanding the underlying principles – ownership of person versus capacity for trade – reveals the logical basis for each position and the practical consequence in Islamic commercial law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the authorized slave may hire himself out (ijarah). According to the Hanafis, this is valid (sahih).
The slave's person is considered his capital for trade.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The 'three Imams' (likely Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and a third, possibly from another school) hold it invalid. They argue the slave does not own his person, so he cannot contract over his benefits.
Benefits are subsidiary to the person (al-manafi' tabi'ah li al-dhat).
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The opponents' reasoning: since the slave lacks ownership of his person, he cannot authorize use of his person or its benefits. This is a syllogism based on the principle that one cannot transfer what one does not own.
This is a form of qiyas (analogy) from the inability to sell oneself.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis rebut by distinguishing: the slave's person is not owned by him, but it is the capital (ra's al-mal) with which he trades. The master's permission implies the slave may use his person for profit, except for actions that nullify the authorization (like selling or pledging himself).
Hiring does not destroy the capital; it generates profit.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi, the ijarah contract is valid and wages go to the slave (or master as per agency); under the opposing view, the contract is void from the outset.
This affects the permissibility of the transaction in practice.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute regarding whether an authorized slave can hire himself out (ijarah). The Hanafi position (followed by the commentator) is that he can, because his person is his capital (ras al-mal) in trade; he can use it for profit, except for actions that would nullify the authorization (like selling himself into slavery or pledging himself as pawn). The opposing view (the three Imams: Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and possibly Shafi'i) argues that he does not own his person, thus cannot contract over his benefits, as benefits are subsidiary to the person. The Hanafi response differentiates: the slave's purpose is trade, and his person is the means of profit; hiring is a form of trade that does not deprive the master of the slave. This shows the Hanafi emphasis on the slave's legal capacity as an agent for profit.
