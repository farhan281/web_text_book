Permissibility of Transferring Wala Before Paying Blood Money
Explains that the mawla can transfer wala to another before paying blood money; similar to a will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a mawla may transfer wala al-muwalat and explain why the contract is considered non-binding (ghayr lazim).

Core Idea
A muwalat contract is non-binding like a will—transferable or revocable until blood money is paid.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a key aspect of wala al-muwalat: its revocability before payment. The text explains that both parties can transfer or renounce the relationship because the contract is non-binding, analogous to a will. The commentary deepens the analogy, drawing parallels to agency dismissal. Let’s unpack the ruling and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Distinguish between the inferior mawla (the one who contracted muwalat) and the superior mawla (the patron). Understand who can transfer or renounce.
The inferior mawla is the person who becomes a client; the superior is the patron.
2
Locate the condition for transfer
Transfer is permitted only as long as no blood money ('aql) has been paid on behalf of the mawla. After payment, it becomes binding.
The text says 'as long as he has not paid blood money on his behalf'.
3
Recognize the legal nature
The contract is 'aqd ghayr lazim (non-binding), similar to a will (wasiyya). This justifies flexibility and revocability.
This is the key difference from a binding contract.
4
Examine renunciation by the superior mawla
The superior can renounce his wala, but must do so in the presence of the other party, like intentional dismissal of an agent.
Renunciation is a separate right with its own condition.
5
Distinguish unilateral contract by inferior without presence
If the inferior contracts with a third party without the knowledge of the original superior, this is a judicial rescission (faskh hukmi), analogous to implied dismissal in agency.
No presence is required because it’s considered a rescission, not a voluntary renunciation.

He said: And the mawla has the right to transfer from him his wala to another as long as he (the mawla) has not paid blood money on his behalf, because it is a non-binding contract, similar to a will. Similarly, the superior mawla has the right to renounce his wala due to its non-binding nature, except that it is stipulated in this (renunciation) that it be in the presence of the other, just as in the intentional dismissal of an agent. Commentary
This ruling addresses the transferability of wala al-muwalat before any blood money ('aql) has been paid. The mawla (the person who contracted muwalat, called the inferior mawla) can transfer his wala to someone else because the contract is not binding—it is similar to a will. The superior mawla (the other party) can also renounce his wala, but this must be done in the presence of the other party. The commentary explains that if the inferior mawla contracts muwalat with a third party without the knowledge of the original superior mawla, this constitutes a judicial rescission (faskh hukmi) analogous to the implied dismissal of an agent. Thus, the contract remains flexible until payment of blood money makes it binding.

Key Terms
العقد غير اللازم (al-'aqd ghayr al-lazim): A contract that is not binding, allowing rescission or transfer as long as no rights of others have become attached.

Key Takeaway

Binding Effect of Wala After Blood Money Payment and Prohibition for Mawla al-Itaqah
Ruling statement with expansion on binding effect and prohibition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that once blood money is paid, wala becomes binding and cannot be transferred, and explain why a mawla al-itaqah cannot enter into a muwalat contract.

Core Idea
Payment of blood money makes wala binding; wala al-itaqah is superior and prevents muwalat.

This lesson examines two rulings from the Hanafi school regarding wala (patronage). First, once the mawla has actually paid blood money on behalf of the new Muslim, the wala becomes binding and cannot be transferred to another. Second, a mawla al-itaqah (one who has emancipated a slave) cannot enter into a muwalat contract because his existing wala is binding and superior. These rulings highlight the importance of established rights and the hierarchical nature of wala relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage contains two separate rulings: one about the binding effect after paying blood money, and another about the prohibition for the mawla al-itaqah.
2
Analyze the reasoning for binding after payment
The text gives three reasons: (a) a right of another has attached, (b) the judge has ruled, (c) it is like consideration received. Understand each reason.
3
Extend the ruling to the mawla's child
Note that the same binding effect applies to the mawla's child, and if payment was made for the child, both father and child are bound.
4
Understand the prohibition for mawla al-itaqah
Recognize that wala al-itaqah is a binding (lazim) right; as long as it exists, the inferior wala of muwalat cannot appear.
5
Review conditions for valid muwalat
From the commentary: the new Muslim must be of unknown lineage and must not already have a wala (from emancipation or previous muwalat).

He said: And if he has paid blood money on his behalf, then he cannot transfer his wala to another because a right of another has become attached to it, and because the judge has passed a ruling on it, and because it is like consideration (iwad) he has obtained, similar to consideration in a gift. Commentary
This passage explains that once the mawla has actually paid blood money on behalf of the new Muslim, the wala becomes binding. It can no longer be transferred, because a right of the mawla has been established, the judge's ruling has been issued, and it is analogous to a contract where consideration has been received. The same applies to the mawla's child; they cannot transfer it either. If the mawla paid blood money for the new Muslim's child, then both the father and child are bound. The text then states a separate ruling: a mawla al-'itaqah (the person who has emancipated a slave) cannot enter into a muwalat contract because the wala of emancipation is binding and superior; as long as it exists, the inferior wala of muwalat cannot take effect. The commentary adds additional conditions: the new Muslim must be of unknown lineage, and he must not already have a wala (either from emancipation or previous muwalat).

Key Terms
مولى العتاقة (mawla al-'itaqah): The patron who has emancipated a slave, giving him wala al-'itaqah (patronage of emancipation).
الولاء (al-wala): Patronage relationship that creates inheritance and blood money liability between parties.
