Orientation to Volume 12: Ijarat and Ghasb
Title page and publication details.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two fiqh topics covered in Volume 12 of Ashraf ul Hadaya and state the significance of each in Hanafi law.

Core Idea
Volume 12 covers Ijarat (rentals) and Ghasb (usurpation) — two critical areas of property law in the Hanafi school.

This front matter introduces Volume 12 of Ashraf ul Hadaya, a well-known Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah. The volume spans two major sections of Hanafi fiqh: Kitab al-Ijarat (Book of Rentals/Hiring) and Kitab al-Ghasb (Book of Usurpation). Understanding the scope of this volume helps you navigate the broader Hidayah corpus and appreciate the depth of Hanafi property law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the volume and its commentary
Note that this is Volume 12 of Ashraf ul Hadaya, an Urdu commentary on the classical Hanafi text al-Hidayah. Recognise the title page and publication details as your entry point.
The author is likely Maulana Muhammad Hanif Gangohi; the original text is al-Hidayah by Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani.
2
Name the two fiqh books covered
From the title page, locate the phrases 'كتاب الاجارات' (Book of Rentals) and 'كتاب الغصب' (Book of Usurpation). These are the two sections included in this volume.
Volume 12 begins with Ijarat and ends with Ghasb, covering property transactions and wrongful appropriation.
3
Recall the basic subject of each book
Ijarat deals with contracts of hire and lease — rights and obligations of lessor and lessee. Ghasb deals with wrongful possession of another's property and the resulting liability.
Both are essential for understanding Hanafi muamalat (financial transactions).
4
Appreciate the volume's place in the curriculum
Al-Hidayah is a foundational text in Hanafi fiqh. Studying its commentaries, like Ashraf ul Hadaya, allows you to master classical rulings and their application.
The volume number (12) indicates this is part of a multi-volume work covering the entire Hidayah.

Introduction to the Book of Ijarah: Key Topics and Conditions
Publication info and table of contents for Kitab al-Ijarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main topics covered in the Hanafi Book of Ijarah and state the essential condition for the validity of an ijarah contract.

Core Idea
Ijarah requires known benefit and wage; its validity depends on certainty.

This lesson introduces the Book of Ijarah from Ashraf ul Hadaya. The table of contents provides a roadmap of the key rulings and disputes that will be covered. Understanding this overview will help you navigate the detailed discussions that follow. The central condition for a valid ijarah contract is that both the benefit (manfa'ah) and the wage (ujrah) must be known.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main topics
From the table of contents, list the major topics covered in Kitab al-Ijarah. Note: This includes definitions, validity conditions, when wage is due, various scenarios of hiring, and disputes with other schools.
Pay special attention to the chapter headings and the mention of Imam Shafi'i's viewpoint.
2
Recognize the essential condition
The table states: 'صحت اجارہ کیلئے منافع اور اجرت معلوم ہونا ضروری ہے' meaning for validity, benefit and wage must be known.
This is a fundamental requirement in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Note the dispute with Imam Shafi'i
The TOC mentions that Imam Shafi'i holds that wage becomes obligatory upon taking possession of the house, contrasting the Hanafi view that wage is not due from the contract alone.
Understanding this ikhtilaf will help in later detailed lessons.
4
Understand the structure
The chapter is divided into sections (abwab) each focusing on specific issues. This structure will guide further study.
The TOC serves as a map for the entire book.

Commentary
This passage is front matter and a table of contents for Volume 12 of Ashraf ul Hadaya, specifically the Book of Ijarah (Leasing). The publication information indicates a revised edition with editorial oversight by Khalil Ashraf Uthmani, printed in 2005 in Karachi. The table of contents lists topics covered in the Book of Ijarah, including: lexical and legal definitions of ijarah, its validity conditions (known benefit and wage), when the wage is due, different scenarios of hiring (workers, animals, land, houses), disputes with Imam Shafi'i regarding when the wage becomes obligatory, and various rulings on liability and detaining property for unpaid wages. The subsequent lessons in this book will delve into each of these topics in detail.

Key Terms
اجارہ (ijarah): A contract for the use of a benefit (manfa'ah) of an asset in exchange for a known wage (ujrah).
اجرت (ujrah): The wage or rental payment given in exchange for the benefit in an ijarah contract.

Overview of the Book of Ijarah (Hiring/Labour)
Table of contents listing chapter headings for the Book of Ijarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major topics covered in the Hanafi Book of Ijarah and explain how the structure guides the study of hiring contracts.

Core Idea
Ijarah governs all hiring and leasing; its validity hinges on known benefit and payment.

This lesson introduces the table of contents for the Book of Ijarah from Ashraf al-Hidayah. Rather than presenting a specific ruling, it provides a roadmap of the key discussions: from the definition and validity of ijarah, to disputes with other schools, void contracts, and liability of workers. Use this overview to orient yourself before diving into the detailed chapters.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the scope of ijarah
Ijarah covers hiring of services (labour) and leasing of assets (property, animals). The table of contents shows both types.
2
Note the conditions for validity
The first major heading stresses that benefit (manfa'ah) and payment (ujrah) must be known. This is a core Hanafi requirement.
Three methods of ascertaining benefit are listed.
3
Identify the payment-related disputes
The table includes a chapter on when payment becomes due, contrasting the Hanafi view (payment not due by mere contract) with Imam Shafi'i's view (due upon taking possession).
Also details on specific workers: baker, cook, tailor.
4
Scan the topics on void contracts and prohibitions
Chapters on void ijarah (fasid) and impermissibility of wages for religious duties (imamate, adhan, teaching Quran) highlight the ethical boundaries in Hanafi law.
Also includes prohibition of hiring for sinful purposes.
5
Review liability rules for hired workers
The final chapter ('Babal Daman al-Ajir') discusses types of workers and when they are liable for loss. This is a practical aspect of ijarah.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents (fihrist) for Volume 12 of Ashraf al-Hidayah, an Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah. It outlines two major books: the Book of Ijarah (hiring) and the Book of al-Mukatab (contract of manumission). The Ijarah section covers rulings on common hired workers (ajir mushtarak), private hired workers (ajir khas), hiring with conditions, hiring of slaves, disputes between parties, and termination of contracts due to defects or excuses. The Mukatab section addresses the validity of kitabah, conditions for freedom, payment of the kitabah price, and permissibility of various actions for the mukatab. The page numbers in brackets refer to the corresponding pages in the original work. This fihrist serves as a navigational aid for the reader.

Key Terms
اجیر مشترک (ajir mushtarak): A hired worker who is not exclusively employed by one hirer; he may work for multiple people and is responsible for damage caused by his work.
اجیر خاص (ajir khas): A hired worker who is exclusively employed by one hirer for a specific period; he is under the hirer's control and not liable for damage unless negligent.
مکاتب (mukatab): A slave who has entered into a contract of kitabah with his master to purchase his freedom by paying a specified sum over time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the main topics covered in Kitab al-Mukatab from the fihrist and explain the basic concept of kitabah.

Core Idea
Kitabah is a contract that allows a slave to earn freedom by paying installments.

This lesson introduces the Book of Mukatab from Ashraf al-Hidayah. The fihrist (table of contents) lists the key rulings and chapters covered in the volume. Understanding the structure helps you navigate the detailed legal discussions that follow. The central theme is the contract of kitabah, a unique mechanism for manumission.

Guided Path

Identify the subject
The fihrist is for Kitab al-Mukatab, the Book of the Mukatab. A mukatab is a slave who has entered a kitabah contract.
Kitabah is a contract of gradual freedom.

Scan the listed topics
Notice the major categories: the ruling on making a mukatab, conditions of payment, invalid kitabah (e.g., with wine or pork), what the mukatab is permitted to do (trade, travel, marry), marriage of slaves, purchase of relatives, intercourse with slave-girls, options for umm walad, tadbir, and joint slaves.

These terms appear throughout the chapter.

Recognize invalid conditions

Invalid kitabah has different consequences.

If a sick man makes his slave a mukatab for 2000 dirhams payable in one year, and the slave's price is 1000, then he dies leaving no property except this slave, and the heirs do not permit the term, the mukatab must pay 2000 immediately or be enslaved. Chapter on who may make a kitabah on behalf of a slave. If a free man makes a kitabah on behalf of a slave for 1000 dirhams, and he pays it, the slave becomes free. If the slave hears and accepts, he is a mukatab. If the slave makes a kitabah on his own behalf and on behalf of his absent master, and he pays it, both become free; the one who pays becomes free, and the master is compelled to accept. Chapter on kitabah of a jointly owned slave. If a slave is jointly owned by two people, and one gives his partner permission to make him a mukatab for 1000 dirhams and receive the kitabah amount, and he does so and takes possession of part of the 1000, then the slave becomes unable to pay, the money belongs to that partner.

Overview of Kitab al-Ghasb (Usurpation) in Hanafi Fiqh
Table of contents listing topics in Kitab al-Ghasb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key principles of usupation (ghasb) as outlined in the table of contents of Ashraf Ul Hadaya Volume 12.

Core Idea
Usurpation creates strict liability: the usurper must return the equivalent of measured/weighed goods or the value of other goods at the time of usurpation.

This lesson explores Kitab al-Ghasb from the table of contents. Usurpation (ghasb) is a major tort in Hanafi fiqh. The TOC highlights two key rules: liability for the equivalent (mithl) of measured/weighed goods, and liability for the value (qimah) of other goods. Understanding this distinction is fundamental to the chapter.

Guided Path
1
Locate Kitab al-Ghasb in the TOC
Find 'كتاب الغصب' in the TOC, which appears after the Ma'dhun section. Note its subtopics.
It begins with the linguistic and legal meaning of ghasb.
2
Identify the two categories of goods
The TOC explicitly mentions 'مکیلی یا موزونی' (measured or weighed) goods and 'جس کی مثل موجود نہیں' (goods without an equivalent). Note the different liabilities.
Measured/weighed goods require the equivalent (mithl); other goods require the market value at the time of usurpation.
3
Recognize the scope of the chapter
Although the TOC lists only a few entries, the full chapter likely covers definitions, possession, liability, and return of usurped property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main topics covered in Volume 12 of Ashraf al-Hidayah and state the opening ruling of Kitab al-Ghasb regarding liability for usurped property.

Core Idea
The table of contents maps the fiqh journey; usurpation begins with liability: mithl for fungibles, value for non-fungibles.

This lesson introduces you to the structure of Volume 12 of Ashraf al-Hidayah and the initial rulings of the chapter on usurpation (Kitab al-Ghasb). Understanding the volume's topics helps you navigate the text, while the opening ruling of Ghasb establishes a key principle of liability in Hanafi law.

Guided Path

Scan the Table of Contents
Identify the main sections listed, beginning with 'Detailed rules of the Ma'dhun' and ending with various subsections of Ghasb.
The fihrist serves as an overview of the volume’s content.
2
Locate the Start of Kitab al-Ghasb
Find the heading 'كتاب الغصب' and read the first few lines to see the opening ruling.
The opening is a concise statement of liability.
3
State the Initial Ruling
For usurped goods that are makil (measured) or mawzun (weighed), the usurper must return an equivalent (mithl). For other goods, he must pay the value on the day of usurpation.
This distinction between mithli and qimi is foundational.
4
Connect to the Ma'dhun Sections
Note that the volume begins with detailed Ma'dhun rules before Ghasb; these topics are connected in practice.
The Ma'dhun section deals with a permitted slave’s trade, which may involve usurpation issues.

Scope of the Chapter on Ijarah
Explaining the intended content of the chapter on ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the intended scope of the chapter on ijarah in Al-Hidayah and explain why invalid contracts are excluded.

Core Idea
The ijarah chapter covers only valid contracts and disputes; invalid ones have their own separate chapter.

This lesson clarifies what the chapter on ijarah (hire) actually contains according to Hanafi fiqh scholars. Understanding the scope prevents confusion when seeking rulings on different types of ijarah contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's stated intention
Read the passage to see what the commentator says the chapter intends to cover after preliminaries.
The chapter is said to describe valid and invalid forms initially.
2
Note the objection from the author of Nata'ij
The commentator mentions a flaw: the author of al-Hidayah did not include invalid contracts here.
This objection highlights a structural nuance.
3
Resolve the discrepancy
Understand that invalid ijarah is treated in a later chapter (Babal Ijarah al-Fasidah), so this chapter focuses only on valid contracts and lessee-lessor disputes.
This is a pedagogical choice for clarity.

Introduction to Interdiction for Profligacy: Ordering and Definition
Chapter introduction explaining ordering and definition of fasad as safah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school delays the chapter on interdiction for profligacy (hajar li al-fasad) after heavenly causes and define safah as the specific meaning of fasad in this chapter.

Core Idea
Safah (fiscal foolishness) is an acquired, disputed cause for interdiction, weaker than heavenly causes.

This passage opens the chapter on interdiction due to corruption (fasad) with a methodological note: why it is placed after the chapters on heavenly causes like insanity and minority. The commentator then clarifies that 'fasad' here means 'safah' (foolishness), a specific fiscal irresponsibility. Understanding this ordering and definition helps you see how Hanafi fiqh prioritizes stronger, agreed-upon causes over weaker, disputed ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of hajar causes
The passage contrasts 'heavenly' (samawi) causes—those not chosen by the person, like insanity—with 'acquired' (muktasab) causes, like safah. List examples of each from the text.
Heavenly causes are stronger in effect and agreed upon.
2
Understand the principle of precedence
The commentator gives two reasons for ordering: (1) heavenly causes are stronger (aqwa) in effect, so they come first; (2) they are agreed upon (muttafaq alayh) while safah is disputed (mukhtalaf fih). Apply this principle: stronger and agreed-upon topics precede weaker, disputed ones in fiqh arrangement.
This reflects a broader Hanafi method: prioritize what is certain and strong.
3
Define 'fasad' in this chapter
The passage explicitly states that here 'fasad' means 'safah' (foolishness), not general corruption. Explain in your own words how safah leads to interdiction.
Safah refers to intellectual lightness causing wasteful financial behavior.
4
Analyze the linguistic root of safah
The text provides linguistic evidence: 'safah' originally means lightness, as in wind bending trees. Quote one poetic example and explain how it relates to the legal meaning.
Linguistic analysis helps anchor the legal definition.

Chapter of interdiction for corruption. Translation: Chapter regarding compulsion due to corruption. Commentary
This passage introduces the chapter on interdiction (hajar) due to corruption or profligacy (fasad). The commentator explains why this chapter is placed after the previous ones: the earlier causes of interdiction (like insanity, minority, slavery) are heavenly (samawi) – i.e., they are not acquired by the person's choice and are more powerful in effect. The present cause is acquired by the person (muktasab) through his own actions, such as squandering wealth, and thus it is considered less severe and placed later. Additionally, the previous causes were agreed upon by scholars (muttfaaq alayh), while interdiction due to profligacy is disputed (mukhtalaf fih). The agreed upon matter takes precedence in order. The commentator then defines the term 'fasad' in this context: it refers to 'safah' (lightness or foolishness). Linguistically, 'safah' means lightness, as in the wind making trees bend. Examples from poetry illustrate this meaning. Thus, the chapter deals with interdiction against a person who is fiscally irresponsible due to a deficiency in intellect or judgment.

Key Terms
فساد (fasad): In this chapter, it means safah (profligacy/foolishness), not corruption in a general sense.
سفہ (safah): Lightness or foolishness, specifically intellectual lightness leading to wasteful and imprudent financial behavior.
سبب سماوی (sabab samawi): A cause of interdiction that is not acquired by the individual's choice, such as insanity, minority, or slavery.
