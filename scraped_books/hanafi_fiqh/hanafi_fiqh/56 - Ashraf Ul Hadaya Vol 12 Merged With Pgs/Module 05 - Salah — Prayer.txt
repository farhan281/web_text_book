Converting Umm Walad into Mukatab and Mukataba into Mudabbara
Ruling on two transformations with intra-Hanafi difference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on converting an umm walad into a mukatab and a mukataba into a mudabbara, and explain the Sahibayn's additional option.

Core Idea
An umm walad can become a mukatab; a mukataba can become a mudabbara, and the Sahibayn grant her a choice.

This passage addresses two related questions: Can a master make his umm walad a mukatab (enter a kitaba contract)? And can he convert a mukataba into a mudabbara (a slave promised freedom upon his death)? The rulings are straightforward, but the second scenario involves an intra-Hanafi difference: the Sahibayn add that the mukataba retains an option to choose her status.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on making umm walad a mukatab
Identify the ruling from the first line: it is permissible. Consider that umm walad has a special status (she becomes free upon master's death), but this does not prevent kitaba.
2
State the Hanafi position on making a mukataba a mudabbara
The passage says it is permissible. Note that this is the general ruling.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's additional detail
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) add that the tadbir is valid and the mukataba has an option: she may remain a mukatab or declare herself incapable (ajz) and become a mudabbara.
The exact phrase in the original is likely 'تدبیر صحیح' (the tadbir is valid).
4
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the Sahibayn's view, the mukataba gains a choice; under the general view (possibly Abu Hanifa's), there is no mention of an option—the tadbir simply takes effect. The lesson is that the Sahibayn grant the slave agency to choose her path.

Commentary
This passage first states that it is permissible for a master to make his umm walad into a mukatab (i.e., enter a kitaba contract with her) because her status as umm walad does not prevent a kitaba. Similarly, it is permissible to make a mukataba into a mudabbara (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death). The text then presents the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani): if the master converts a mukataba into a mudabbara, the tadbir (declaration) is valid, and the female slave is given the option to either remain a mukatab (continue the kitaba) or declare herself incapable (ajz) and become a mudabbara. Reward for Patience Under Coercion: Rational Proof and Quranic Exception
Rational proof that patience under coercion earns reward

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rational proof that patience under coercion earns reward and explain why the Quranic exception removes punishment, not prohibition.

Core Idea
Patience under coercion earns reward because the prohibition remains; the Quranic exception removes punishment, not permissibility.

This passage from Al-Hidayah provides a rational proof that a person who patiently endures coercion and refrains from uttering kufr deserves reward. The commentator then addresses an objection that the Quranic exception should make uttering kufr permissible, like eating carrion. The response hinges on a precise reading of the verse: the exception excludes the coerced from punishment, not from the prohibition. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the Hanafi position on 'azimah versus rukhsah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling being proved
The passage aims to prove that a coerced person who does not utter kufr earns reward. Recognize that this is a ruling about the virtue of patience and the superiority of 'azimah over rukhsah.
The ruling is not about permissibility but about reward.
2
Understand the rational argument
The argument: because the coerced person did not actually commit kufr, the prohibition against kufr remains operative. By refraining, they comply with the prohibition, which is an act of strong resolve ('azimah), which is superior to taking a concession.
3
Examine the objection
An objector claims that the Quranic exception 'illa man ukira' should make uttering kufr permissible, just as the exception in the carrion verse (Quran 6:119) makes eating carrion permissible. Identify the parallel structure claimed.
The objection relies on the apparent similarity of exceptions.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The commentator responds that the verse has an implicit structure: the threat of punishment is for those who disbelieve willingly. The exception only excludes the coerced from punishment, not from the prohibition. Thus, the prohibition remains, and uttering kufr is not made permissible.
The key distinction: removal of punishment vs. removal of prohibition.
5
Derive the practical consequence
The practical outcome: a coerced person may either utter kufr (taking the rukhsah, permissible) or refrain (acting with 'azimah, rewarded). The latter is superior, but the former is not sinful if done sincerely under coercion.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the Hidayah's statement 'and because the prohibition...' The commentator provides a rational proof that a person who patiently endures coercion without committing kufr deserves reward. The logic: since the prohibition against kufr remains operative (the person did not actually commit kufr), the person has fulfilled the legal obligation by refraining, thus earning reward. This is an act of 'azimah (firm resolve), which is superior to taking a rukhsah (concession) like eating carrion. The commentator then addresses an objection: why is uttering kufr not permissible under coercion like eating carrion? The answer involves a precise interpretation of the Quranic verse (16:106). The exception 'except one who is coerced while his heart is firm in faith' does not create permission to utter kufr; rather it removes the punishment from the person who is coerced but internally remains faithful. The removal of wrath does not imply the removal of the prohibition itself. This is similar to the case of a traveler or sick person: the cause for fasting (being healthy and resident) exists, but the obligation is deferred; the obligation remains but is delayed through a valid concession. Thus, the person who does not take the concession (i.e., does not utter kufr) is acting with 'azimah and is rewarded, whereas one who utters kufr under coercion, though sinful according to some, is not punished if his heart is firm, but that does not mean the act is permissible or rewardable.

عزیمت (azimah): In this context, acting with firm resolve by choosing the more stringent option (i.e., not uttering kufr despite coercion).
رخصت (rukhsah): A concession; a permissible alternative that eases the original prohibition under necessity or coercion.

Property Destruction under Coercion: Permissibility and Compensation
Ruling on property destruction under coercion with proof by necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when property destruction under coercion is permissible and identify who is liable for compensation according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Necessity can make another's property mubah, but compensation is from the coercer, not the coerced.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on destroying another's property under threat of death or bodily harm. The text distinguishes between the sin of the act and the liability for compensation, clarifying the role of the coerced as an instrument of the coercer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is destroying another's property when coerced by threat of killing or cutting a limb. The ruling is permissibility (mubah) due to necessity.
The necessity must be existential—saving one's own life.
2
Locate the condition for permissibility
The threat must be of death or loss of a limb, not lesser harm. The coerced must have no other means to avoid the threat.
The analogy is to the state of famine (makhmasa).
3
Determine who is liable for compensation
The owner can take compensation from either the coerced or the coercer, but the ultimate liability is on the coercer because the coerced acts as an instrument (ala) of the coercer.
Exception: acts like eating or speaking cannot be instruments.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah is a commentary expanding on the rulings of coercion (ikrah) in two contexts: property destruction and homicide. The author first addresses property: if one is coerced by threat of death or amputation to destroy another's property, it is permissible to do so because necessity (darurah) makes another's property mubah, analogous to a famine situation. The reward for patience is mentioned. The owner may claim compensation from either the coercer (mukrih) or the coerced (mukrah), but the coercer is ultimately liable since the coerced is considered an instrument (ala) of the coercer, as actions are attributed to the one who causes them. The exception is for acts like eating or speaking, where one cannot be an instrument for another. The second and major part concerns homicide under coercion: killing a Muslim is never permissible, even under threat, and the killer is sinful. The question is on whom qisas (retaliation) becomes wajib. The author enumerates four possible positions: (1) qisas on both coercer and coerced – held by the 'three imams' (likely referring to the founders of the other three schools: Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali); (2) qisas on neither – Imam Abu Yusuf; (3) qisas only on the coercer – the two sides (Tarafain: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani); (4) qisas only on the coerced – Imam Zufar. Each position is supported with proof. Zufar argues the coerced actually performed the killing, so he bears sin and qisas. Shafi'i adds causation (tasbib) on the coercer, making both liable. Abu Yusuf sees doubt on both sides due to sin on the coerced and incitement on the coercer, so qisas drops and diyah is paid from both. Tarafain argue that the coerced, preferring his life, becomes by nature an instrument (ala) of the coercer, so the act of killing is attributed to the coercer for qisas, but the sin remains on the coerced because he cannot be an instrument for aggression against his religion. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is that of Tarafain: qisas on the coercer only. The passage concludes with an analogy to coercion to emancipate or to a Magian slaughtering another's sheep.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah is a commentary expanding on the rulings of coercion (ikrah) in two contexts: property destruction and homicide. The author first addresses property: if one is coerced by threat of death or amputation to destroy another's property, it is permissible to do so because necessity (darurah) makes another's property mubah, analogous to a famine situation. The reward for patience is mentioned. The owner may claim compensation from either the coercer (mukrih) or the coerced (mukrah), but the coercer is ultimately liable since the coerced is considered an instrument (ala) of the coercer, as actions are attributed to the one who causes them. The exception is for acts like eating or speaking, where one cannot be an instrument for another. The second and major part concerns homicide under coercion: killing a Muslim is never permissible, even under threat, and the killer is sinful. The question is on whom qisas (retaliation) becomes wajib. The author enumerates four possible positions: (1) qisas on both coercer and coerced – held by the 'three imams' (likely referring to the founders of the other three schools: Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali); (2) qisas on neither – Imam Abu Yusuf; (3) qisas only on the coercer – the two sides (Tarafain: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani); (4) qisas only on the coerced – Imam Zufar. Each position is supported with proof. Zufar argues the coerced actually performed the killing, so he bears sin and qisas. Shafi'i adds causation (tasbib) on the coercer, making both liable. Abu Yusuf sees doubt on both sides due to sin on the coerced and incitement on the coercer, so qisas drops and diyah is paid from both. Tarafain argue that the coerced, preferring his life, becomes by nature an instrument (ala) of the coercer, so the act of killing is attributed to the coercer for qisas, but the sin remains on the coerced because he cannot be an instrument for aggression against his religion. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is that of Tarafain: qisas on the coercer only. The passage concludes with an analogy to coercion to emancipate or to a Magian slaughtering another's sheep.

آلہ (ala): Instrument: a tool or means through which an action is accomplished, analogous to a sword or fire acting by nature.
تسبیب (tasbib): Causation: being a cause of an act without directly performing it.
طرفین (tarafain): The two sides: referring to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a coerced convert's apostasy and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas in this case.

Coerced conversion is valid, but apostasy after it is not punished by death due to doubt.

Commentary
This passage contains two related discussions. The first is a response to an objection about coercion and belief change. The objector argues: if a disbeliever is forced to become Muslim and says the shahada, he is considered Muslim despite coercion, so coercion does not prevent belief change. The Hanafi response is that we do not know his inner state; there are two possibilities. However, we give precedence to Islam because of its inherent superiority and because we have verbal affirmation from him. The tongue is considered the interpreter of the heart, so external ruling follows. This is supported by Quran 3:83. The second discussion is about a person who was coerced into converting to Islam and then later apostatizes. According to qiyas (analogy with the hadith 'Whoever changes his religion, kill him'), he should be executed. But the Hanafi school applies istihsan (juristic preference) and does not execute him. The reason is that there is doubt (shubha) about whether his initial conversion was genuine. He may have uttered the shahada without inner conviction. Since capital punishment is prevented by doubt, he is not executed. This illustrates the principle that punishments are averted by doubts. Acceptance of Puberty Claim from a Muhaqiq
Ruling on accepting a muhaqiq's claim of puberty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting a muhaqiq's claim of puberty and identify the two conditions (minimum age and no outward contradiction).

Core Idea
A muhaqiq's claim of puberty is accepted unless outward signs contradict; minimum ages 12 for boys, 9 for girls.

When a child approaches puberty, the status of adulthood can be ambiguous. In such cases, the Hanafi school gives weight to the child's own statement about their bodily state, following the same principle applied to women's statements about menstruation. However, this acceptance is not absolute—it is conditioned by outward evidence and minimum age limits that prevent abuse.

The actor is a muhaqiq (boy or girl nearing puberty) and the act is a claim that they have reached puberty (bulugh).
The term muhaqiq comes from rahaqa al-hulm, meaning 'approached the dream' (nocturnal emission).
2
State the default ruling
The default ruling is that the claim is accepted, and the child is treated as a legally responsible adult (mukallaf) in all rulings—such as obligation of salah, sawm, and zakat.
The text says: 'their statement is accepted, and their rulings are those of adults.'
3

The claim is only accepted if external signs do not contradict it. If the child's appearance clearly shows immaturity, the claim is rejected.
The author says: 'and the outward does not contradict them.'
4
Identify the minimum age condition
Even if outward signs are ambiguous, if the child is below the minimum age of puberty in the Hanafi school (12 for boys, 9 for girls), the claim is not accepted because the outward (age) contradicts.
These minimum ages are cited from Fatawa Qadikhan and Fatawa Zahiriyyah.

The ruling is analogized to the ruling of a woman's statement about menstruation, which is accepted based on Quran 2:228. In both cases, the matter is known only to the person themselves, so their word is taken unless contradicted.

He said: When a boy or girl approaches puberty (rahaqa) and their matter of puberty becomes ambiguous, and they say 'I have reached puberty,' then their statement is accepted, and their rulings are those of adults, because it is a matter that is only known from them outwardly. So when they inform and the outward does not contradict them, their statement is accepted, just as the statement of a woman is accepted regarding menstruation. Translation: When a boy or girl is near puberty and it is difficult to know whether they are adult, and they say 'I have become adult,' then their statement is accepted and they are treated as adults, because this is something only known from them. So when they inform and the outward does not contradict, their statement is accepted, like the woman's statement about menstruation. Explanation... The phrase 'and the outward does not contradict them' is a condition: if the boy is less than twelve years old or the girl less than nine, and they claim puberty, they are not believed, because the outward contradicts. In Fatawa Qadikhan and Fatawa Zahiriyyah: the minimum age for a boy's claim to be accepted is twelve years, and for a girl nine; below that their claim is not accepted.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the Hanafi school regarding the acceptance of a claim of puberty from a child approaching puberty (muhaqiq). The original text in Arabic states: when a boy or girl is near puberty (rahaqa al-hulm) and their status of puberty is ambiguous, and they claim to have reached puberty, then their statement is accepted, and they are treated as adults. The reason given is that puberty is a matter that is only known from them apparently, so if they inform and the outward signs do not contradict, their word is accepted, just as a woman's word is accepted about menstruation. The commentary then explains the condition 'and the outward does not contradict them': this means that if the boy is below twelve years or the girl below nine years, their claim is not accepted because the outward signs of age contradict. The minimum ages (12 for boys, 9 for girls) are cited from Fatawa Qadikhan and Fatawa Zahiriyyah. The analogy to menstruation is based on the Quranic verse 'It is not lawful for them to conceal what Allah has created in their wombs' (2:228), which establishes that women's statements about their own bodies are accepted, and similarly the child's statement about their own puberty is accepted when it cannot be known otherwise.

Key Terms
مراهق (muhaqiq): A boy or girl who has approached the age of puberty (rahaqa al-hulm), i.e., is near physical maturity.
حلم (hulm): Puberty, specifically the occurrence of nocturnal emission (ihtilam) as a sign of physical maturity.
بلوغ (bulugh): The state of reaching puberty, after which one is legally responsible (mukallaf).
