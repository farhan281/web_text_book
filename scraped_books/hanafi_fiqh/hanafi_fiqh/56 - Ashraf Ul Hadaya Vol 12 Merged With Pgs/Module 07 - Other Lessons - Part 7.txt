Owner's Option When Tanned Hide Exists: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on owner's option

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the owner's option to abandon a tanned hide and take compensation, and identify the basis of each position.

Core Idea
When a usurped hide gains value through tanning, the owner's right to abandon it and claim compensation is disputed even within the Hanafi school.

In the law of ghasb (usurpation), when the usurper adds value to the property (e.g., by tanning a hide), the owner may have the option to abandon the property and claim its value. This lesson examines a specific scenario: a tanned hide that had no value before tanning. The Hanafi mashayikh disagree on whether the owner can leave it with the usurper and take compensation. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to navigate ikhtilaf within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A usurper tans a carcass hide using a valuable substance. The tanned hide now exists with the usurper. The owner wishes to leave the hide with the usurper and take its monetary value (tāwān) instead.
The hide had no value before tanning, unlike a dyed garment which had value before dyeing.
2
State the first position (no option)
Some mashayikh claim that by consensus, the owner has no such option because the hide had no value initially. They liken it to a destruction case.
This view appears to deny the option absolutely.
3
State the second position (dispute among founders)
Other mashayikh attribute the lack of option to Imam Abu Hanifa, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) grant the owner the option. Thus, the matter is disputed (mukhtalaf fih).
The reasoning: if the owner takes compensation, the usurper becomes unable to return the hide, so it is treated as destruction.
4
Analyze the compensation details
Among those who grant the option (Sahibayn's view), further ikhtilaf exists on the amount: some say the owner takes the full tanned hide's price and gives the tanning increase to the usurper; others say he takes only the untanned hide's price (negligible).
This mirrors the dispute in cases of destruction (itlāf).
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The passage does not specify a relied-upon position. The text is a later mashayikh discussion, so the mufta bihi is unclear. In practice, one should follow the preferred view of one's own tradition or the dominant fatwa in the locality.
No tarjih is given here.

Tanning with a Valueless Substance: Liability for the Tanned Hide
Ruling when hide is tanned with valueless substance like soil or sun

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a hide is tanned with a valueless substance and explain the majority proof for liability of the tanned hide.

Core Idea
Tanning with valueless substance gives owner free benefit; but if destroyed, the usurper pays for the tanned hide.

The previous lesson dealt with tanning using a valuable substance, where the owner had an option to pay or not. Now we turn to tanning with no-cost items like soil or sunlight. The rule for the benefit is simpler: the owner takes the hide free. However, a dispute arises among later Hanafi scholars (mashayikh) on what the usurper must pay if he destroys the tanned hide. The majority opinion, which is the relied-upon position, holds that the improvement (tanning) follows the substance, so liability includes the added value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The usurper tans the hide using a substance that has no market value, such as soil or sunlight. The hide is not destroyed initially.
2
Determine the benefit for the owner
Since the tanning cost nothing, the hide becomes free (muft) for the owner – the usurper has no claim for compensation.
This is analogous to washing a stolen garment: the owner gets it back without paying for the washing.
3
Recognize the ikhtilaf when the hide is destroyed
If the usurper destroys the tanned hide, the mashayikh differ: majority say he pays the value of the tanned hide; a minority say he pays only the value of an untanned hide.
The minority argue that the usurper himself created the improvement, so he should not be liable for it.
4
Analyze the majority proof
The majority hold that the attribute of tanning is subordinate (tabi‘) to the hide itself. Since liability for the original hide is established, it extends to the attribute as well.
This is based on the principle that one who is liable for the substance is also liable for its inseparable attributes.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The text indicates that the majority view (jamboor mashayikh) is the preferred and operative position for fatwa.
The authority weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin, as this is a later mas’alah not explicitly covered in Zahir al-Riwayah.

When a Usurper Turns Wine into Vinegar: Ownership and Compensation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on usurper's conversion of wine to vinegar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership of vinegar made by a usurper, and apply the mufta bihi ruling in a given scenario.

Core Idea
If a usurper turns wine into vinegar, ownership depends on whether the process is istihlak or mixture – Abu Hanifa says usurper owns it; Sahibayn and Muhammad differ.

This passage explores a fascinating intra-Hanafi dispute: a usurper takes wine and turns it into vinegar by adding salt or vinegar. Who owns the resulting vinegar? Is the owner entitled to compensation? The three founding imams of the Hanafi school – Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani – offer different answers. Their reasoning draws on analogies to tanning hide and the concept of istihlak (consumption) versus mixture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties
A usurper takes wine (khamr, ghayr mutaqawwim) from its owner and transforms it into vinegar (khall, mutaqawwim) by adding salt or vinegar. Determine who owns the vinegar and what compensation, if any, is due.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that the vinegar becomes the usurper's property and he owes nothing. His proof: wine is non-valuable property, and mixing is istihlak (destruction) of his own property (the additive). No liability for destroying non-property.
Wine is not mutaqawwim, so its destruction incurs no financial liability.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their qiyas
The Sahibayn analogize to tanning a hide: the owner may take the vinegar but must pay for the increase from the salt. They also hold the usurper is liable if he destroys the vinegar, unlike Abu Hanifa.
Qiyas on dabbgh al-jild: the hide owner gets the tanned hide but pays for tanning.
4
State Muhammad's distinction
Muhammad differentiates based on time and quantity: if vinegar results immediately (from adding vinegar), it is istihlak and usurper owns it; if delayed (little vinegar added), it is a mixture and shared proportionally.
Muhammad's principle: immediate change = istihlak; delayed = sharikat (partnership).
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf and later tarjih
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, usurper gets vinegar free; under Sahibayn, owner pays for salt increase; under Muhammad, depends on immediacy. Later mashayikh prefer the absolute view that owner takes vinegar for free, as the additive is consumed (mustahlak) in the wine.
This tarjih is established in Kifayat al-Muntaha.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a usurper who turns wine (khamr) into vinegar (khall) by adding salt or vinegar. The key legal issue: Is the resulting vinegar the property of the usurper or does the owner have a claim? Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the vinegar becomes the property of the usurper regardless of method, because the original wine (khamr) was not valuable property (ghayr mutaqawwim), and the mixing is considered a destruction (istihlak) of the wine, and one who destroys his own property owes no liability. The Sahibayn analogize to the tanning of hide: the owner may take the vinegar but must compensate the usurper for the increase from the salt. They also hold that if the usurper destroys the vinegar, he is liable. Imam Muhammad distinguishes between immediate vinegar (by adding vinegar) and delayed vinegar (by adding little vinegar): immediate vinegar is destruction and the usurper owns it; delayed vinegar is a mixture of two valuable substances (vinegar with wine becoming vinegar) and thus is shared. Abu Hanifa rules consistently that mixing is destruction and the usurper owns it entirely. The text then mentions that many later mashayikh prefer the absolute view that the owner can take the vinegar without giving anything, as the additive is consumed.

خل (khall): Vinegar; a valuable commodity derived from wine through a chemical change.
استہلاک (istihlāk): Consumption; a process that destroys the original substance, making it integible.
متقوم (mutaqawwim): Having legal value; property that can be bought, sold, and compensated.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding ownership and liability when a usurper adds vinegar to wine, and explain the legal reasoning (ta'lil) behind each view.

When a usurper adds vinegar to wine, the timing of transformation determines ownership: for Sahibayn, immediate gives ownership; for Abu Hanifa, mere mixing suffices.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a nuanced scenario: a usurper (ghasib) pours vinegar into a Muslim's wine, turning it into vinegar. Identify the Act and Parties
Identify that a usurper adds vinegar to wine to produce vinegar. Note the legal status of wine: non-muttaqawwim for Muslims.

Imam Muhammad distinguishes two scenarios: if transformation is immediate, the usurper owns the vinegar; if delayed, ownership is shared proportionally.

Sahibayn argue that when transformation is not immediate, the wine's identity persists, so mixing different types does not cause istihlak; the mixture is legally estimated as vinegar with vinegar. Abu Hanifa counters that wine is never property, so its transformation is irrelevant.
Estimation (taqdir) plays a key role in Sahibayn's argument.

If the usurper destroys the resulting vinegar, liability differs: Abu Hanifa says no liability; Sahibayn say liable only if transformation was delayed.
This shows how the ikhtilaf affects real-world outcomes.

Commentary
This passage deals with a usurper adding vinegar to wine to turn it into vinegar. The ruling depends on timing and the imam's principle. Imam Muhammad distinguishes: if the wine turns into vinegar instantly upon adding the vinegar, the usurper owns the vinegar without liability because he destroyed non-valuable wine. If it turns after a delay, ownership is shared proportionally, because the mixture is legally considered vinegar mixed with vinegar (estimation), and mixing like substances does not cause idhlal (consumption of identity). Imam Abu Yusuf agrees with Muhammad. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that regardless of timing, the usurper owns the vinegar without compensation, because for him mere mixing is idhlal (the wine is consumed in the process), and he destroyed his own valuable vinegar against a non-valuable base, so no liability arises. The passage further clarifies that if the usurper destroys the resulting vinegar, liability (ضمان) follows the ownership: if it becomes vinegar instantly, no liability (Muhammad); if after delay, liability. For Abu Hanifa, no liability in both cases.

خلط (khalat): Mixing two or more substances.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on destroying musical instruments and spilling alcoholic drinks. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the destroyer is liable for compensation and that such items can be sold. Liability for Breaking Musical Instruments and Spilling Wine: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for destroying instruments and spilling wine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on liability for destroying musical instruments and spilling wine and identify the mufta bihi view.

Later fatwa rejects liability for breaking lahwi instruments and spilling wine, adopting Sahibayn's view.

This passage from Kitab al-Ghashb presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding liability for breaking musical instruments and spilling wine. The ruling has practical implications for those engaged in commanding good. We will examine the proofs and the operative fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two positions
Read the matn: breaking a Muslim's barbat, drum, flute, or tambourine, or spilling sukra or minsaf. Note: Abu Hanifa says liable, sale valid; Sahibayn say not liable, sale invalid.

Examine the exception
The commentary clarifies that war drums and wedding tambourines are excluded by consensus: breaker is liable without dispute.

Abu Hanifa argues that items retain potential lawful use; value remains because corruption is from the user's free choice. He analogizes to singing slave girl, butting ram, etc. He also restricts 'changing by hand' to rulers. Later fatwa (multiple sources) adopts Sahibayn's view.
The tarjih is based on the majority of later Hanafi authorities.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the liability for destroying musical instruments and spilling wine. The matn states Abu Hanifa's position: breaking a Muslim's barbat (lute), drum, flute, or tambourine, or spilling his sukra (unfermented date juice) or minsaf (half-cooked wine) makes the breaker liable (daman), and such items are legally saleable. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, holding no liability and no validity of sale. The commentary clarifies that the disagreement applies specifically to instruments used for entertainment (lahw); the war drum and wedding tambourine are excluded by consensus — liability is agreed. Later fatwa (as quoted from several sources) adopts the Sahibayn's view: no liability. Definitions: sukra is raw date juice that has thickened; minsaf is wine cooked down to half. The Sahibayn's proof is twofold: these items are prepared for sin, thus they become non-valued (like khamr), and the destroyer is acting in command of good (amr bi al-ma'ruf), supported by hadith 'I was sent to break flutes and kill pigs' and 'Whoever sees an evil, let him change it with his hand.' Abu Hanifa counters that these items have potential for lawful uses, so they retain value; corruption is due to a free agent's act, not the item itself. He analogizes to a singing slave girl, a butting ram, a racing pigeon, etc., where value is assessed without considering the prohibited use. He also limits the 'hand' command to rulers. The operative ruling for fatwa is the Sahibayn's: no liability, no valid sale.

Key Terms
ضمان (daman): Legal liability to compensate for destruction of property.
سكر (sukr): Unfermented date juice that has become thick; not yet intoxicating.
منصف (minsaf): Wine cooked until half remains; considered intoxicating.

Liability for Usurping Items with Dual Use
Commentary expanding matn on liability for musical instruments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for destroying items usable for both lawful and unlawful purposes, explain the proof based on qiyas to a singing slave-girl, and apply the principle of valuation for lawful use to determine compensation.

Core Idea
An item's potential for lawful use establishes property value; misuse by the usurper does not negate liability.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a person destroys another's property that could be used for either lawful or unlawful purposes, such as a musical instrument. The Hanafi school holds that such items are still considered valuable property (amwal) because they can be put to lawful use, and therefore the destroyer is liable to pay compensation. The commentary explains Imam Abu Hanifa's rational proof (dalil 'aqli) using the analogy of a singing slave-girl, addresses an intra-Hanafi disagreement with the Sahibayn regarding the ruler's permission, and distinguishes the case from a Christian's cross.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is usurping (ghasb) or destroying items that can be used for both lawful and unlawful purposes (e.g., musical instruments). The ruling is that the usurper is liable (yadman) to pay the value (qimah) of the item.
The ruling applies only if the item is mal mutaqawwim (property with market value).
2
Analyze the Proof (Dalil)
Imam Abu Hanifa's proof is a qiyas to a singing slave-girl. Just as a slave-girl is valuable despite being usable for unlawful singing, these items are valuable due to their potential for lawful use. The illa (effective cause) is suitability for lawful benefit, which establishes property status.
The dalil is rational (aqli), not textual. The argument is that corruption is from the free agent's action, not the object itself.
3
Note the Intra-Hanafi Disagreement
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that if the usurper acts with the permission of the ruler to command good, he may not be liable. Abu Hanifa disagrees, stating that permission does not negate liability.
The commentary clarifies that commanding good by hand is only for rulers; others must use verbal forbiddance.
4
Apply the Valuation Principle
The value is assessed based on the item's worth for lawful purposes, not for its potential unlawful use. For example, a singing slave-girl is valued as a slave, not for her singing ability; similarly, a drum is valued as a container, not for its use in entertainment.
This principle is derived from the statement 'value is required based on not being suitable for play.'
5
Distinguish from Cross and Wine Cases
Unlike musical instruments, a Christian's cross and wine are items that non-Muslims are allowed to retain. Therefore, destroying a cross requires compensation including its value as a cross, whereas destroying a musical instrument does not include value for its unlawful use.
Imam Ahmad holds that no compensation is due for destroying a cross, which is a cross-madhhab difference.

Liability for Usurping Musical Instruments and the Dhimmi's Cross

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Value is owed for destroying a prohibited object, but a cross is mutaqawwim because dhimmis may keep it.

This passage addresses a key question in ghasb: is a usurper liable for destroying an object that is itself haram for Muslims? The Hanafi school distinguishes between items whose maintenance is forbidden (like musical instruments and wine) and items allowed for dhimmis (like the cross). Opening Cages and Ropes: When Usurpation Leads to Liability
Rulings on usurping drums, lutes, cross, and opening enclosures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that opening an enclosure does not create liability for the escape of an animal or slave, and contrast the positions of other schools.

Core Idea
Opening a cage or rope is not direct usurpation; Hanafis require direct control for liability.

This passage presents a recurring theme in ghasb: when does indirect causation create liability? The Hanafi school limits liability to direct acts of taking or destruction. Opening a rope or cage that leads to escape is not considered a direct act of usurpation, so no compensation is due. Other schools extend liability to causation. You will also learn an important benefit about returning the very same item unknowingly.

Liability for Usurped Umm Walad and Mudabbara
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for usurped slaves.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the liability for usurping an umm walad and evaluate the reasoning behind the intra-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
Umm walad is not mutaqawwim according to Abu Hanifa; Sahibayn disagree — critical for compensation.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability for usurping an umm walad versus a mudabbara. While the mudabbara's status as mutaqawwim is uncontested, the umm walad's legal value is debated between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two major disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi school's internal logic on the nature of property rights and the effect of restrictions on sale.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi positions
Identify what Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn say about liability when an umm walad or mudabbara is usurped and dies.
Abu Hanifa distinguishes between the two; Sahibayn treat them equally.
2
Identify the contested ruling
Focus on the umm walad: why is there disagreement? The mudabbara is agreed upon.
3
Examine the illa (legal cause)
Determine the basis for the ruling: the concept of mutaqawwim (legally valuable property).
Abu Hanifa says umm walad is not mutaqawwim because she cannot be sold; Sahibayn say she retains value.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf
Understand the practical difference: liability or no liability for the umm walad's value.
If mutaqawwim, compensation due; if not, no compensation.
5
Note authority weight and tarjih
Both positions are from Zahir al-Riwayah; later scholars often prefer Sahibayn's view.
The passage itself does not give tarjih.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability for usurping an umm walad (a female slave who has borne a child to her master) versus a mudabbara (a female slave who has been granted tadbir, i.e., freedom upon the master's death). The ruling is drawn from the matn of Al-Hidayah. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the usurper is liable for the value of the mudabbara but not the umm walad. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that he is liable for both. The reason ('illa) is the concept of mutaqawwim (legal monetary value). All agree that the mudabbara is mutaqawwim (her value can be assessed in exchange) because she is still owned and can be sold. However, the umm walad, according to Abu Hanifa, is not mutaqawwim because she cannot be sold (she becomes free upon the master's death, and during his life, her ownership is restricted). The Sahibayn argue that she retains some monetary value because she can still be owned and benefits can be taken from her. The passage refers to detailed proofs in the Book of Emancipation (Kitab al-Itq) from the same work. This is a purely Hanafi internal ikhtilaf, and the mu'tamad (relied-upon position) may vary; later Hanafi works often prefer the Sahibayn's view.

Key Terms
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child to her master; she cannot be sold and gains freedom upon the master's death.
مدبرة (mudabbara): A female slave who has been granted tadbir (suspensory emancipation upon the master's death); she can be sold during the master's life.
مالیة متقومة (maliyyah mutaqawwimah): Legal monetary value that can be assessed in compensation; property that is permissible to own and trade.

Compensation for Mudabbara and Umm Walad in Usurpation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding compensation for usurping a mudabbara and umm walad, and identify the rationale of each side.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa exempts the umm walad from compensation; the Sahibayn demand it for both slaves.

This passage reveals a critical difference within the Hanafi school on whether an umm walad – a slave-girl who has borne her master's child – is treated as fully owned property for purposes of compensation in usurpation. While Abu Hanifa restricts compensation to the mudabbara (a slave promised freedom at death), the Sahibayn extend it to the umm walad as well, citing her quantifiable market value. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of how ownership status affects liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the objects
The act is usurpation (ghasb) of two types of female slaves: a mudabbara (declared free upon master's death) and an umm walad (bore a child to her master). Recognize that both have special legal statuses affecting ownership.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that compensation is wajib only for the mudabbara, not the umm walad. This suggests he does not consider the umm walad as fully owned property (mutaqawwim) for compensation purposes.
The text refers to Abu Hanifa as 'the author' (صاحب).
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that compensation is due for both, because both have quantifiable market value (mutaqawwim). They argue that the value of the umm walad is also compensable.
The Sahibayn base their view on the market value being agreed upon for the mudabbara and extended to the umm walad.
4
Recognize the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Abu Hanifa, only the value of the mudabbara is paid; under the Sahibayn, both values are paid. This affects actual compensation in usurpation cases.
5
Note the authority weight
The passage does not specify the mufta bihi (operative opinion). Later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn, but the dispute remains valid.
The ruling is referenced from Kitab al-'Itq, indicating consistency across fiqh topics.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding compensation (ta'wan) for usurping (ghasb) a mudabbara (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death) and an umm walad (a slave-girl who bore a child to her master). The author (Abu Hanifa) holds that only the value of the mudabbara is compensable, not the umm walad, because the umm walad is not considered fully owned property. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argue that both have quantifiable market value and therefore compensation is due for both. They note that the value of a mudabbara is agreed upon as quantifiable, and they extend the same to umm walad. The commentator remarks that this ruling has already been mentioned in the Book of Emancipation (Kitab al-'Itq). This ikhtilaf is significant in practical cases of usurpation where both types of slave-girls are involved.

Key Terms
مدبرہ (mudabbara): A female slave whose master has declared that she will be free upon his death.
ام ولد (umm walad): A slave-girl who has borne a child to her master, thus gaining special status.
تاوان (ta'wan): Compensation or indemnity paid for usurped property.
متقوم (mutaqawwim): Having quantifiable monetary value according to shariah.
