Wala' al-Itaqah as Ta'sib and Patron's Inheritance Priority
Ruling on wala' as ta'sib with proof from hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that wala' al-itaqah is a form of asabah and explain the patron's rank in inheritance priority relative to lineage asabat and dhawi al-arham.

Core Idea
The patron inherits as the last residuary — after lineage asabat but before dhawi al-arham.

This lesson examines the institution of wala' al-itaqah — the bond of patronage by manumission. In Hanafi law, this creates a right of inheritance for the patron (mu'tiq) from his freed slave (mu'taq). The patron is treated as a residuary heir (asabah), but he is the 'last of the residuaries,' coming after all lineage asabat, yet he takes precedence over dhawi al-arham (distant relatives). Understanding this hierarchy is essential for correctly distributing the estate of a freed slave who dies without lineage residuaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Wala' al-itaqah is classified as ta'sib (residuary inheritance). The patron inherits from the freed slave as an asabah.
This is the foundational ruling.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith where the Prophet said to a man who freed a slave: 'If he dies and leaves no heir, you are his residuary.'
The hadith explicitly states the patron is a residuary.
3
Determine the patron's rank
The patron comes after lineage residuaries (asabat nasab) but before dhawi al-arham. The hadith's condition 'does not leave an heir' is interpreted as 'does not leave a residuary heir by lineage.'
Thus the patron is the last of the residuaries.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If the freed slave has a paternal uncle (asabah), the uncle inherits fully; the patron gets nothing. If no such residuaries exist, the patron takes the remainder after prescribed shares.
Practical application clarifies the hierarchy.

Commentary
This passage presents a foundational ruling from Kitab al-Wala' concerning the inheritance rights of a patron (mu'tiq) over his freed slave (mu'taq). It states that wala' al-itaqah is a form of ta'sib (residuary inheritance). This means the patron inherits from the freed slave as an asabah (residuary heir). The proof is a hadith where the Prophet told a man who bought and freed a slave: 'If he dies and leaves no heir, you are his residuary.' The patron's right of inheritance takes precedence over the paternal aunt ('ammah) and maternal aunt (khalah) – who are dhawi al-arham. The patron inherits after all residuary heirs by lineage (asabat nasab). If there are no such residuaries, the patron inherits the entire estate after prescribed shares (fara'id) are given. The passage also references that the Prophet made the daughter of Hamza inherit as a residuary in a particular case, showing that a residuary can exist even with a prescribed share. The principle is that the patron is the 'last of the residuaries'. The commentary explains that the hadith's condition 'does not leave an heir' means does not leave a residuary heir by lineage. Therefore, the patron is postponed after lineage residuaries but precedes dhawi al-arham. If the freed slave has no residuary by lineage, the patron takes the entire estate after prescribed shares, or the remainder if a prescribed share heir exists. The last line emphasizes that the residuary (asabah) is the one through whom mutual support of the lineage's household is achieved, and the patrons have triumph over other relations.

Key Terms
ولاء عقاقہ (wala' al-itaqah): Patronage by manumission, which creates a bond of inheritance between master and freed slave.
تعصيب (ta'sib): Residuary inheritance; the right to take the remainder of the estate after prescribed shares.
عصبہ (asabah): Residuary heir; male relatives through males who inherit the remainder after shares.
ذوی الارحام (dhawi al-arham): Distant relatives (non-asabat), such as maternal uncles, aunts, etc.

The Manumitter as 'Asabah: Evidence and Basic Hierarchy
Explanation of wala' al-itaqah as 'asabah with hadith and companion views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wala' al-itaqah, cite the primary hadith evidence, and explain why the manumitter is preferred over dhawil arham but postponed after blood 'asabah.

Core Idea
Wala' al-itaqah makes the manumitter an 'asabah — he inherits before uterine relatives but after blood agnates.

This lesson examines the key ruling on wala' al-itaqah (clientage by emancipation) in the Hanafi school. The manumitter is given a special status as an 'asabah for inheritance, a rule established by a clear hadith and the practice of the Companions. You will learn the precise hierarchy: the manumitter inherits only after all blood 'asabah are exhausted, but he takes precedence over dhawil arham (uterine relatives).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Concept
Wala' al-itaqah is a cause of 'asabah (ta'sib). Understand that the mu'tiq becomes an 'asabah for the freed slave.
This is a unique relationship not based on blood.
2
Examine the Primary Proof
The hadith: a man freed a slave and asked about inheritance. The Prophet said: 'If he dies and leaves no heir, his property is yours.' Note the key phrase 'lam yad'u waritha' (leaves no heir).
The hadith is mursal but accepted in the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the Daughter of Hamza Case
The Prophet gave the daughter of Hamza inheritance from her freed slave as an 'asabah, even though the freed slave had a daughter (a fixed-share heir). This shows the manumitter's 'asabah right is not blocked by fixed-share heirs.
The daughter takes her fixed share, and the manumitter takes the remainder as 'asabah.
4
Establish the Hierarchy
The manumitter is an 'asabah, so he inherits after blood 'asabah but before dhawil arham. If there is a blood 'asabah, he is postponed; if not, he inherits.
This is the mufta bihi position.
5
Apply to a Simple Scenario
Freed slave dies leaving a maternal aunt (dhawil arham) and no blood 'asabah. The manumitter inherits everything.
The maternal aunt is a uterine relative and does not block the manumitter.

Book of Wala'. Concerning the one who bought a slave and freed him: 'This is your brother and your freedman. If he thanks you, it is better for him and worse for you; if he is ungrateful, it is worse for him and better for you. And if he dies and leaves no heir, then you are his 'asabah.' And the Prophet (peace be upon him) gave the daughter of Hamza inheritance from her freedman as an 'asabah while there was an heir. So when the manumitter becomes an 'asabah, he is given precedence over the dhawil arham (uterine relatives). This is narrated from 'Ali. Then if the freed slave has any 'asabah by blood, that 'asabah takes precedence over the manumitter, because the manumitter is the last 'asabah. This is because the scholars said that in the Prophet's statement 'and he leaves no heir', the intended meaning is an heir who is an 'asabah, as evidenced by the second hadith. Thus the manumitter is postponed after the 'asabah heir, not after the dhawil arham. And if the freed slave has no 'asabah by blood, then his inheritance is for the manumitter. Its interpretation is that there is no such fixed-share heir (sahib fard) who has two statuses (i.e., both fixed share and 'asabah). And if such a fixed-share heir exists, then after his fixed share, the remainder is all for him, because he is an 'asabah. The reason is that the 'asabah is the one from whom mutual support comes due to the clan, and support is derived from the mawali themselves, and the 'asabah takes all the remainder. Explanation of the statement: 'And the wala' of emancipation... etc.' Wala' of emancipation is a cause of 'asabah that makes the manumitter an 'asabah. Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the ruling of wala' al-itaqah (clientage by emancipation) and its effect on inheritance. The author explains that wala' al-itaqah creates a legal 'asabah relationship: the manumitter (mu'tiq) becomes an 'asabah for the freed slave, meaning he inherits the freed slave's property if there are no blood 'asabah. The evidence is the hadith of the man who freed a slave and asked the Prophet about his status and inheritance; the Prophet said the freed slave is his brother and freedman, and if he dies without an heir, the manumitter gets the property. Additionally, the case of the daughter of Hamza is cited: the Prophet gave her inheritance as an 'asabah from her freed slave, even though the freed slave had a daughter (who is a fixed-share heir, not an 'asabah). Thus the ruling is established that the manumitter is an 'asabah. The hierarchy is detailed: if the freed slave has a blood 'asabah (like a son or brother), that 'asabah takes precedence over the manumitter. If there are only dhawil arham (uterine relatives like maternal aunts), the manumitter is preferred. The text addresses a potential objection: some companions, like 'Umar, 'Ali, and Ibn Mas'ud, reportedly gave inheritance to dhawil arham over mawali. The commentator reconciles this by explaining that the term 'dhawil arham' in those narrations refers to blood 'asabah, not the weaker uterine relatives. Thus there is no contradiction. Finally, the author discusses the phrase 'صاحب فرض ذو حال' (fixed-share heir with two statuses). Two interpretations are given: (1) a fixed-share heir who also qualifies as an 'asabah in some situations, like a father or grandfather, who takes the remainder as 'asabah after their fixed share; (2) a fixed-share heir who only has a fixed share and no 'asabah status, like a daughter, after whose fixed share the remainder goes to the manumitter. The second interpretation is preferred in al-Nihayah. The overall Hanafi position is clear: wala' al-itaqah creates a strong right of inheritance that places the manumitter after blood 'asabah but before dhawil arham and those with no blood relation.

Reconciling Companion Narrations and the Fixed-Share Heir Analysis
Reconciliation of companion views and analysis of fixed-share heir categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconcile the conflicting companion reports on wala' inheritance and apply the two interpretations of a fixed-share heir with two statuses (sahib fard dhu hal) to determine the manumitter's share.

Core Idea
The manumitter's 'asabah status is upheld despite companion disagreements, and the residue after fixed shares goes to him unless the fixed-share heir is also an 'asabah.

This lesson delves into two nuanced aspects of wala' al-itaqah. First, we address the apparent conflict between narrations from some companions who gave inheritance to dhawil arham over mawali, and the majority view that prioritizes the manumitter. The commentator reconciles these by reinterpreting the term 'dhawil arham' in the dissenting reports to mean blood 'asabah. Second, we analyze the concept of a 'fixed-share heir with two statuses' (sahib fard dhu hal), which determines whether the manumitter takes the residue after a fixed-share heir or is blocked by that heir's 'asabah claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conflicting Narrations
The passage presents two sets of narrations: (1) From Zayd ibn Thabit and majority that the manumitter takes precedence over dhawil arham. (2) From Umar, Ali, and Ibn Mas'ud that dhawil arham take precedence over the manumitter.
These are companion-level differences, not madhhab differences yet.
2
Apply Reconciliation (Tatbiq)
The author reconciles by interpreting 'dhawil arham' in the dissenting narrations to mean blood 'asabah (dhawu qarabat 'asabat), not the weak uterine relatives. Thus both sets agree that 'asabah (blood or by wala') take precedence over true dhawil arham.
This preserves the majority ruling.
3
Understand the Two Interpretations of Sahib Fard Dhu Hal
The phrase 'there is no fixed-share heir who is dhu hal' is interpreted two ways: (1) No fixed-share heir who is also an 'asabah (like a father or grandfather) — if such exists, he takes the residue after his fixed share. (2) No fixed-share heir who has only one status (like a daughter) — if such exists, after her fixed share, the residue goes to the manumitter.
The second interpretation is preferred in al-Nihayah.
4
Apply the Preferred Interpretation
If a fixed-share heir like a daughter (who is not also an 'asabah) exists, she takes her share and the manumitter takes the remainder. If a fixed-share heir like a father (who is also an 'asabah) exists, he takes his fixed share and also the remainder as 'asabah, blocking the manumitter.
This determines the manumitter's entitlement.
5
Summarize the Practical Outcome
The manumitter's right to the residue is only actualized when the remaining fixed-share heirs are not also 'asabah. If any such heir exists, the manumitter gets nothing.
This is a refinement of the basic hierarchy.

Women and Wala Inheritance: Sons Inherit, Daughters Do Not
Ruling with proof and expansion on women's wala inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wala inheritance for women and explain the concept of jarr al-wala with its two scenarios.

Core Idea
Women inherit wala only from direct emancipation or chain; otherwise, wala inheritance passes to the master's sons, not daughters.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on wala (inheritance through emancipation) concerning women. The central ruling is that when a freed slave dies without blood relatives, his inheritance goes to the master's sons, not daughters. However, women do have wala rights in specific cases: when they personally emancipate the slave or when they are part of a chain of emancipations (jarr al-wala). We will explore the hadith proof and the detailed application of these rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Base Scenario and Ruling
Read the first part of the passage: If the master dies first, then the freed slave dies without any agnates, who inherits? The text states: inheritance goes to the master's sons, not daughters. Understand the condition: the master must have predeceased the freed slave, and the freed slave must have no blood agnates.
This ruling is specific to wala inheritance, not general inheritance.
2
Analyze the Hadith Proof
The passage cites a hadith: 'There is nothing for women from wala except what they themselves emancipated...' Identify the operative negative phrase and note the exceptions. The hadith restricts women's wala to only three/four enumerated cases: direct emancipation, emancipation by their freed slave, kitaba, or dragging wala (jarr).
The hadith is transmitted by al-Bayhaqi and others; it is a Sunni authority.
3
Understand Jarr al-Wala (Dragging Wala)
The passage explains two scenarios of jarr al-wala: (1) a woman's slave marries a free woman with her permission, has children; when she frees the slave, the slave drags the children's wala to himself, and she drags it to herself. (2) A chain: woman frees slave A, slave A buys slave B, slave B marries a free woman and has children; when A frees B, B drags his children's wala, then A drags it, then the woman drags it. This shows how women can acquire wala indirectly.
Jarr al-wala is a distinctive Hanafi concept.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
A benefit (fa'idah) mentions that according to Zahir al-Riwayah, if the freed slave has only daughters, they do not inherit; the estate goes to bayt al-mal. But some later mashayikh gave fatwa to give it to the daughters due to the lack of proper bayt al-mal administration. Identify the two positions and note the practical difference.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
5
Connect to the Grandfather's Right
The passage ends with a related ruling: does a grandfather drag the wala of his grandson like a father? It cites authorities (Imam Shami, Qadi Shurayh, etc.) who affirm that he does. This expands the principle of jarr al-wala to the grandfather.
This demonstrates how usul principles are extended to similar cases.

Wala' Inherited by Nearest 'Asabah: Ruling and Rational Proof
General rule with rational proof and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that wala' goes to the nearest 'asabah, explain the rational proof based on attribution, and apply the priority ranking to given heirs.

Core Idea
Wala' follows the nearest agnatic heir, not just sons – proven by rational analogy and Companion practice.

This passage clarifies a common misconception about wala' inheritance. While many assume wala' is restricted to the manumittor's sons, the Hanafi school holds that it passes to the nearest 'asabah, with a clear priority ranking. The text provides a rational proof (qiyas) showing that wala' is attributed to the manumittor, and then cites the practice of the Companions that wala' goes to the 'elder' meaning the nearest relative. We will analyze this ruling and its applications.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling on wala'
Identify the general principle: wala' is not limited to the manumittor's sons; it goes to the nearest 'asabah (al-aqrab fal-aqrab).
This is the core Hanafi position.
2
Understand the rational proof
The proof: wala' is based on the manumittor's authority over the freed slave. This authority is attributed to the manumittor, so wala' should follow the same attribution. Since the manumittor's authority is not transmitted through inheritance like property, it goes to the one who succeeds him in providing support (nasr), i.e., the nearest 'asabah.
Contrast with lineage: lineage is through the father's bed, not the mother. Wala' attribution is different.
3

Work through the examples given: (a) father vs son: son is nearer, gets wala'. (b) grandfather vs brother: grandfather is nearer according to Abu Hanifa. (c) son of freed woman vs her brother: son inherits wala', but brother pays diyah. (d) son vs grandsons: son is nearer (sulbi).
The principle 'al-aqrab fal-aqrab' is applied consistently.
4
Recognize the Companions' authority
The rule is supported by the practice of Umar, Ali, Ibn Mas'ud and others, who gave wala' to the 'elder' (al-kabir), interpreted as the nearest relative.
This is a qawl sahabi, considered a strong proof in Hanafi methodology.

Inheritance of Wala: Closest Agnates and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains inheritance of wala among close male agnates with intra-Hanafi differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that wala passes to the closest agnate on the master's side and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding grandfather versus brother.

Core Idea
Wala belongs to the closest agnate on the master's side; the dispute is whether grandfather or brother is closer.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah deals with the inheritance of wala (patronage) from a freed slave. The core principle is that wala passes to the closest male agnate ('asabah) of the master. However, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute about ranking when the master leaves a grandfather and a brother. We will examine the agreed cases, the disputed case, and the reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed cases
First, note the cases where the Hanafi school agrees: if the master leaves a father and a son, wala goes to the son (closer agnate). If a freedwoman leaves a son and a brother, the son takes precedence. If the master leaves a son and grandsons, the son takes precedence. These all illustrate that wala follows the closest 'asabah.
The phrase 'both parties' likely refers to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
2
Locate the disputed case
The pivot of dispute is when the master leaves a grandfather and a brother. The text states Imam Abu Hanifa's view explicitly. The Sahibayn's view is implied by contrast and from other sources.
The dispute is only about this specific pairing; other pairings are agreed.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the grandfather takes wala over the brother because the grandfather is a closer agnate ('asbah) in the Hanafi hierarchy. He ranks the grandfather above the brother in 'asabah.
This is based on the principle that the grandfather inherits as an 'asabah when there is no father, and he is considered closer than a brother.
4
State the Sahibayn's position and reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the brother takes wala over the grandfather because they do not consider the grandfather an 'asabah in the presence of a brother. They give precedence to the brother as a full agnate.
The text does not elaborate their reasoning, but it is recorded in other Hanafi fiqh works.
5
Apply the principle to other cases in the text
The passage also covers liability for crimes: diyah for the freed slave's crime falls on the 'aqila (the master's tribal agnates, like the freedwoman's brother), not on the wala heir. This is a separate rule from inheritance of wala.
This shows that wala inheritance and diyah liability follow different principles: wala by closeness of lineage, diyah by tribal support.

Evidence for Wala' al-Muwalat Inheritance
Explaining the Quranic and prophetic proofs for wala' al-muwalat as a cause of inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two main proofs for wala' al-muwalat inheritance and evaluate the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute over the authenticity of the hadith.

Core Idea
Wala' al-muwalat inheritance is proven by Quran and Prophetic hadith, defended by Hanafi scholarship against criticisms of discontinuity.

In this lesson, we explore the textual evidence for wala' al-muwalat as a cause of inheritance according to the Hanafi school. The author of al-Hidayah presents a Quranic verse and a Prophetic hadith as proofs. We will examine each proof, the reasoning (wajh al-dalalah), and the subsequent intra-madhhab and inter-madhhab discussions, particularly the Shafi'i critique and the Hanafi response. Understanding this evidence is crucial for grasping the Hanafi position on a unique but valid cause of inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two primary proofs
Locate the Quranic verse (4:33) and the hadith of Tamim al-Dari cited in the text. Note that the Hanafi school uses both as evidence for inheritance through wala' al-muwalat.
2
Analyze the Quranic proof
Examine the verse 'And those with whom your right hands have made a pact, give them their share.' Understand the context: the previous verse discusses inheritance, and this verse is conjuncted to it, indicating that the share is by inheritance. The Hanafi interpretation is that this verse established inheritance through mutual pact when no closer heirs exist.
The verse is considered abrogated (naskh) by the kinship inheritance verse for those with close relatives, but remains operative for those without.
3
Analyze the hadith proof
Examine the hadith: 'He is the most entitled of people to him in his life and after his death.' The author explains that 'in his life' refers to the patron's responsibility for blood money ('aql), and 'after his death' refers to inheritance. Thus, the hadith establishes both duties.
4
Examine the Shafi'i objection
Note the Shafi'i stance that the hadith is not authentic due to a perceived break in the chain (munqati') and the weakness of certain narrators. Tirmidhi and al-Bukhari are cited as questioning its continuity.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
Understand the Hanafi defense: reliable narrators (Waki', Abu Nu'aym) explicitly confirm that Ibn Mawhab heard from Tamim directly. Even if there is an intermediary (Qubaysa), he is trustworthy and his 'an'anah is considered connected. Therefore, the hadith is valid evidence.
This response shows the Hanafi methodology in hadith criticism, prioritizing explicit confirmation of hearing over generic doubts.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the inheritance of wala' al-muwalat. The author begins by presenting the Hanafi evidence from the Quran: the verse 'And those with whom your right hands have made a pact, give them their share' (4:33). The Hanafi school understands this verse to establish a right of inheritance through a pact of muwalat, as it was revealed in that context and is conjuncted to the preceding verse about inheritance. The author clarifies that this ruling applies when the deceased has no other Islamic inheriting relative; otherwise, the verse of kinship takes precedence. Then the second proof is presented: the hadith of Tamim al-Dari where the Prophet (SAW) says the patron is most entitled to the client in life and death, indicating both the patron's responsibility for blood money ('aql) and the right to inheritance. The author then addresses criticisms regarding the authenticity of this hadith. Imam Tirmidhi and Imam Shafi'i considered it disconnected or weak. The Hanafi response is that explicit hearing has been confirmed by reliable narrators like Waki' and Abu Nu'aym, and even if there is an intermediary, Qubaysa is trustworthy. Thus, the hadith is valid evidence. The Hanafi position is that wala' al-muwalat is a legitimate cause for inheritance when there are no other heirs.

Priority of Heirs in Wala al-Muwalat
States that blood relatives, even distant ones, inherit before the mawla in muwalat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that blood relatives (including dhawu al-arham) take priority over the mawla in inheritance from a new Muslim, and identify the conditions required for a valid muwalat contract.

Core Idea
Blood kinship always trumps contractual patronage in inheritance, even if the relative is distant.

When a person with unknown lineage becomes Muslim, he may contract muwalat with a Muslim to establish inheritance and blood-money liability. However, this contract does not create a stronger tie than blood relation. This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies that any heir, even a paternal aunt or maternal aunt (dhawu al-arham), is preferred over the mawla.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Read the opening sentence: 'And if he has an heir, then he (the heir) is more entitled than him (the mawla).' This establishes the priority of blood relatives over the contractual patron.
Note that the passage explicitly mentions 'even if it is a paternal aunt or maternal aunt' to emphasize that all blood relatives, however distant, are preferred.
2
Understand the rationale
The text explains: 'because muwalat is their contract, so it does not bind others.' The contract only binds the two parties and cannot extinguish the rights of third parties (blood relatives).
This is a key principle: contracts create obligations only between the contracting parties.
3
Recognize the necessary conditions for the contract
The passage states that the muwalat contract must include conditions of mutual inheritance and liability for blood money ('aql). These obligations only become binding if explicitly stipulated.
The condition of inheritance and 'aql are considered wajibat (obligatory terms) for the contract to have legal effect.
4
Note the additional condition regarding the mawla's lineage
The mawla must not be an Arab. The reason: Arabs already have tribal support systems (tanasur bil-qaba'il) which are stronger than muwalat, so they do not need it.
This condition is specific to the Hanafi school; other schools may not have this restriction.
5
Consider the objection and response about explicit conditions
The commentary addresses an objection: some scholars (e.g., Ibrahim al-Nakha'i) held that the contract is valid without explicit conditions. The Hanafi response: those scholars considered these conditions implicit, so explicit mention is not always necessary, but for legal certainty the Hanafi school requires them.
This shows the importance of differentiating between validity (sihhah) and binding obligation (luzum) in Hanafi legal theory.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling that if a new Muslim who has entered into a muwalat (contract of patronage) has any heir, even a distant relative such as a paternal aunt or maternal aunt (dhawu al-arham), that heir takes priority over the mawla (the patron) in inheritance. The reason is that the contract of muwalat only binds the two contracting parties; it cannot override the rights of the heir who is entitled by birth. The text then notes that the contract must explicitly include conditions of mutual inheritance and liability for blood money ('aql). This is because these obligations only become binding if voluntarily agreed upon. Additionally, the mawla must not be an Arab because Arabs already have tribal support systems, making muwalat unnecessary. The commentary addresses an objection regarding whether the condition of inheritance and 'aql is required for validity, explaining that the absence of explicit mention in some narrations does not negate these conditions as they are inherently understood.

Key Terms
الموالات (al-muwalat): A contract of mutual patronage between two parties, typically a new Muslim of unknown lineage and a Muslim who agrees to be his patron.
ذوي الارحام (dhawu al-arham): Blood relatives who are not heirs in the Quranic or residuary categories, such as paternal aunts, maternal aunts, etc.
العقل (al-'aql): Liability for blood money (diyah) paid by the 'aqilah (patron) in cases of unintentional homicide.
