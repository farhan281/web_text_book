Mutual Oath and Rescission of Division on Claim of Non-Delivery
Ruling on mutual oath when one partner denies delivery of share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on mutual oath and rescission of division when one partner denies delivery of the other's share, and identify the underlying principle and proof.

Core Idea
When proof is lacking, mutual oath rescinds the division if the claimant never accepted receipt.

Partnership division can be annulled if one co-owner claims his share was never delivered to the agreed place and the other denies, provided the claimant never admitted receipt. This ruling protects against unfair division when delivery is disputed. Let's examine the conditions, procedure, and the hadith proof that justifies the mutual oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and ruling
What is the specific claim? One partner says his share was to be delivered to a certain place but was not delivered, and he has not received it. The other denies. The ruling: both swear oaths (tahāluf) and the division is rescinded.
The ruling applies only if the claimant never admitted receipt or acted to confirm division.
2
Trace the underlying principle
The claimant's statement is not contradictory because he never acknowledged receipt, so he never accepted the division. Therefore the division can be annulled.
If the claimant had admitted receipt or acted as if he accepted, the division would be binding.
3
Locate the proof (dalīl)
The hadith: 'The oath is upon the defendant in financial matters' (al-yamīn 'alā al-mudda‘ā 'alayhi). This means when the plaintiff has no witness, the defendant's statement with oath is accepted. Here both are plaintiffs and defendants, so both take oaths.
The dalīl is a hadith ahad, making it zannī al-thubūt, but it is applied by the Hanafī school.
4
Apply to the case
Since neither has proof, both swear. Their oaths conflict, so the division cannot stand and must be rescinded. The property is returned to its undivided state for a new division.
This ensures fairness when delivery is disputed.
5
Note the conditions for rescission
The claimant must not have admitted receipt or performed an act implying acceptance (e.g., taking possession, selling the share). The denial must be clear and the promise of delivery must have been part of the division agreement.
If the claimant admits receipt, he cannot later claim non-delivery.

Commentary
This passage contains a ruling from the Book of Division. It addresses a situation where one co-owner claims that his share was to be delivered to a specific location but that delivery did not occur, and he further claims he has not received his share. The other co-owner denies both the promise and the non-delivery. Since neither party has proof, they both take oaths (tahāluf), and as a result, the original division is rescinded. The principle behind this is that the claimant's statement is not contradictory: he never acknowledged receipt of the share, so he did not accept the division. When a party's claim is consistent and he never acted to confirm the division, the division can be annulled. The proof for the oath is the hadith that the defendant's statement with his oath is accepted when the plaintiff lacks evidence. This ruling ensures fairness when there is a dispute over the transfer of shares.

Key Terms
تحالفا (tahālufā): Both parties take an oath (mutual oath) regarding their claims.
فسخت القسمة (fusikhat al-qisma): The division is annulled/rescinded; the property is returned to its undivided state.
استيفاء (istīfā'): Receipt or taking possession of one's share.

Istihqaq of Part of Share: Rescission or Compensation?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on rescission of division upon istihqaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding the rescission of division upon istihqaq of part of a share, and explain the legal reasoning behind each position.

Core Idea
When part of a partner's share is reclaimed, the division stands with compensation per Abu Hanifa, but is rescinded per Abu Yusuf.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether a division (qisma) remains valid when a specific item within one partner's share is later claimed by a third party (istihqaq). The two positions reflect different views on the role of consent and the possibility of compensation. We will examine the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf and understand the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
The dispute is whether a division is rescinded when part of one partner's specific share is reclaimed by a third party.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
Abu Hanifa holds that the division is not rescinded (lam tufsakh). The affected partner recovers the value from the other partner's share.
The division contract is upheld; compensation remedies the loss.
3
State Abu Yusuf's ruling
Abu Yusuf holds that the division is rescinded (tufsakh al-qisma), requiring a new division.
The affected partner's consent is invalidated because the share is no longer whole.
4
Analyze the reasoning behind each position
Abu Hanifa: consent to the division as a whole; loss can be compensated without annulment. Abu Yusuf: consent was conditional on the entirety of the share; loss of part invalidates consent, and compensation from a different location may be disadvantageous.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the division stands and compensation is paid. Under Abu Yusuf, the property reverts to the undivided state and a new division is performed.
The practical difference affects the stability of property divisions.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement concerning the effect of a third party's claim (istihqāq) on a division. Suppose two partners divide property, and later it is discovered that a specific item in one partner's share actually belongs to a third party who reclaims it. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the division remains valid (lam tufsakh) despite the claim. The affected partner does not have the division rescinded; rather, he recovers the value of the lost item from his partner's share by way of compensation. The division is not rescinded because each party consented to the division as a whole, and the loss can be remedied by monetary compensation. Imam Abu Yusuf, however, argues that the division is rescinded. His reasoning is that the consent of the affected partner was based on the belief that his entire share was free of claims. When part of it is taken, his consent is invalidated, and he may suffer loss. Moreover, the compensation from the other partner's share comes from a different location, which may be inferior, causing further disadvantage. Therefore, the division is annulled and a new division is required. This dispute shows how different views on the condition of consent lead to different rulings on the validity of division.

Key Terms
استحق (istuḥiqqa): A third party establishes ownership of a specific item within a partner's share, reclaiming it from the division.
رجع بحصة ذلك (raja'a bihiṣṣati dhālika): The affected partner recovers the value of the claimed item from the other partner's share (recourse by compensation).
لم تُفسخ (lam tufsakh): The division is not rescinded; it remains valid despite the claim.
