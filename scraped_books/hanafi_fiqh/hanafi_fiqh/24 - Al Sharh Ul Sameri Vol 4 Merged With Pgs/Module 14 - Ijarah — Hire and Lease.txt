Dispute in Ijarah Before Service: Mutual Oaths and Rescission
Hanafi ruling on ijarah disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for resolving a dispute in an ijarah contract before any benefit is received.

Core Idea
When parties dispute an ijarah contract before service, both must take oaths, then rescind.

When two parties enter into an ijarah (lease/hire) contract and later disagree on its terms before any service has been rendered, a specific legal procedure applies. This short ruling from the Hanafi school outlines that the parties must exchange oaths and then annul the contract. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly managing contractual disputes in Islamic finance and daily transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and stage
First, confirm that the contract is an ijarah (lease or hire) and that the dispute arises before the lessee has received any benefit or the lessor has received the rent.
This stage is critical because the ruling changes after the benefit has been received.
2
State the dispute scenario
The parties disagree on a term of the ijarah—e.g., the rental amount, duration, or conditions—and neither can prove their claim.
The dispute must be genuine and unresolved.
3
Apply the Hanafi procedure
Both parties take an oath (yamin) in the presence of a judge or witnesses, swearing to their version of the contract. Then the contract is rescinded (taradd), i.e., annulled.
This mirrors the procedure for disputed sales.
4
Understand the legal effect
After rescission, the contract is void ab initio. Any deposit or advance must be returned, and neither party owes the other anything.
No further obligations remain.

Dispute in Ijarah Before Work: Tahaluf and Rescission
Ruling on ijarah dispute pre-performance with tahaluf and faskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of tahaluf to a disputed ijarah contract before work is performed and state the rescission ruling.

Core Idea
In an ijarah dispute before work begins, both parties take oaths and the contract is rescinded—just as in a sale.

Disputes can arise in any contract, including ijarah. But the timing matters: before any work is done, the contract is still executory. Hanafi fiqh provides a clear procedure: if both parties claim different wages without evidence, they both swear oaths (tahaluf) and the contract is rescinded (faskh). This lesson walks through that ruling and its proof through analogy to sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the ruling for a dispute in ijarah before any work is done? The text states that both parties take oaths and rescind the contract.
The ruling applies only before istifa' (receipt of benefit).
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The proof is an analogy (qiyas) to a sale (bay') dispute. The text explicitly says 'like in sale'.
The analogy is based on the similarity between benefit (manfa'ah) and commodity (mabi'), and wage (ujrah) and price (thaman).
3
Trace the effective cause (illa)
In both sale and ijarah, the subject matter and countervalue are in dispute, and neither party has evidence. This shared attribute justifies the same ruling.
The illa is jaliyyah (clear) because the parallel is straightforward.
4
Apply the ruling to the scenario
In the given example, the worker claims 10 dirhams, the hirer claims 5. Both are claimants and defendants. Since neither has evidence, both swear oaths and the contract is rescinded.
If one refuses to swear, the other's claim becomes binding.

Dispute in Ijarah After Full Receipt: No Mutual Oaths
Ruling on ijarah dispute after full receipt: no tahaluf, hirer's statement accepted with oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for disputes after full receipt in ijarah and explain the legal reasoning based on qiyas with sale of perishable goods.

Core Idea
After the benefit is consumed, rescission is impossible; the hirer's statement is accepted with oath.

When a worker has completed the job and received the wage, but a dispute arises about the amount, the Hanafi school provides a clear rule: no mutual oaths (tahaluf) are taken, and the hirer's statement is accepted with his oath. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this ruling, which relies on a qiyas (analogy) with a sale where the sold item has been consumed. Understanding this rule is crucial for correctly resolving post-execution disputes in ijarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
The passage deals with a disagreement after the worker has fully received the benefit (i.e., the work is done and the hirer has used it). The worker claims an excess wage; the hirer denies.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The ruling: no mutual oaths (tahaluf). The hirer's statement is accepted with his oath. This is because the defendant (hirer) denies a claim and the claimant (worker) lacks evidence.
This follows the general principle that the defendant's oath is decisive when the plaintiff has no proof.
3
Understand the qiyas (analogy)
The text draws an analogy with a sale. If the buyer receives goods and they are destroyed, returning them is impossible, so rescission cannot happen. Similarly, once the benefit (manfa'ah) is consumed, it is like a perished 'arad (perishable item).
Benefit is considered a 'arad because it perishes with use.
4
Apply the analogy to ijarah
In ijarah, after receipt, the benefit is consumed (perished). Therefore, rescinding the contract or ordering mutual oaths (which would lead to rescission) is impossible. The contract is already executed.
Thus, the procedural remedy is simply the hirer's oath, not tahaluf.

In such a case, rescinding the contract is impossible because restitution cannot be made. Therefore, the procedure is different: the worker claims additional wages, the hirer denies, and since the hirer is the defendant (he denies the extra claim), his statement is accepted with his oath. This follows the general rule that the defendant's oath is decisive when the plaintiff has no evidence.

Resolving Ijarah Disputes After Partial Work: Tahaluf and Hirer's Oath
Ruling on ijarah dispute after partial work

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for an ijarah dispute after partial work: rule on the completed part with the hirer's oath and on the remaining part with mutual oaths and rescission.

Core Idea
In ijarah, after partial performance, past is settled by hirer's oath, future by mutual oaths and rescission.

Disputes in ijarah (hire) often arise after the worker has completed part of the work. The Hanafi school provides a clear procedure: what has been done is treated like a completed sale, while what remains is still executory. This lesson walks you through that procedure with a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
There is an ijarah contract for a specified period (e.g., one month). The worker has performed part of the work (e.g., 15 days). The parties now dispute the wage amount.
The benefit of the completed work has been received and consumed.
2
Apply the rule to the completed part
For the work already done (past), no mutual oaths (tahaluf) are taken because the benefit is gone. Instead, the hirer's statement is accepted with his oath, as he is the defendant (mudda'a alayh).
This follows the principle of bayyinah (evidence) where the defendant's oath settles past obligations.
3
Apply the rule to the remaining part
For the unperformed part (future), both parties take oaths (tahaluf) and the contract is rescinded (faskh) for that portion. Each swears that the agreed wage was as they claim, leading to contradiction and thus rescission.
Since the subject matter is still executory, it can be annulled, unlike the consumed part.
4
Understand the rationale
The completed benefit is analogous to goods received in a sale: it cannot be returned, so no tahaluf. The hirer's statement is trusted because he is the one being asked to pay for what is already done.
This ruling is derived by qiyas (analogy) from the rules of sale after partial delivery.

The Qiyas Behind Ijarah Dispute Resolution: Analogizing to Sale
Qiyas analysis of ijarah dispute rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the qiyas (analogy) from sale to ijarah regarding dispute after partial performance, stating the asl, far', illa, and hukm.

Core Idea
Ijarah dispute after partial work is resolved like a sale after partial delivery because the delivered benefit is consumed and cannot be returned.

The Hanafi ruling for ijarah disputes after partial performance is not arbitrary; it is derived by qiyas from the well-established rules of sale. Understanding this analogy deepens your appreciation of Hanafi legal reasoning and allows you to apply the principle to other contracts.

Guided Path
1
Recall the sale rule for partial delivery
In a sale, if part of the goods are delivered and a dispute arises, the delivered part is not subject to tahaluf; the buyer's (hirer's analog) statement is accepted with oath. For the undelivered part, tahaluf and faskh apply.
This is the asl (original case) in the qiyas.
2
Identify the common illa (effective cause)
The common illa is that the delivered portion is completely received and consumed (or cannot be returned), while the undelivered portion is still executory. In both sale and ijarah, the benefit of the delivered part perishes upon receipt.
The illa is jaliyyah (clear) because consumption is evident.
3
Apply the qiyas
Since the illa is present in ijarah (partial work done, benefit consumed), the same hukm applies: for the completed work, no tahaluf, hirer's oath; for the remaining work, tahaluf and faskh.
The far' (new case) is ijarah after partial work.
4
Verify no countervailing objection
There is no valid objection that breaks the analogy. The Hanafi school confirms that the same principle holds across both contracts.
Objections based on the different nature of benefits vs. goods are overruled because both are considered 'mal' after receipt.

Ruling on Dispute After Full Performance in Ijarah
Ruling on mutual oath after full benefit received

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a dispute arises after full receipt of benefit in ijarah and explain why mutual oath is not performed.

Core Idea
After the benefit is fully consumed, the musta'jir's statement prevails; mutual oath is impossible because the contract is executed.

In ijarah (hire) contracts, disputes about wages can occur either before or after the service is completed. This lesson focuses on the post-performance scenario: once the worker has fully delivered the benefit, the contract is considered executed. The Hanafi school provides a clear rule that rescission through mutual oath (tahaluf) is no longer possible, and the hirer's (musta'jir) statement is accepted with his oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Condition for the Ruling
Recognize that this ruling applies only after the benefit has been fully received (istifa'). Before performance, mutual oath would apply.
The condition is explicitly stated: 'after full receipt' (بعد الاستيفاء).
2
Understand the Consequence
When the condition is met, the ruling is: no mutual oath (لا يتحالفان) and the musta'jir's statement is accepted with his oath (القول قول المستأجر مع يمينه).
3
Grasp the Rationale via Analogy
The text draws an analogy to a sale where the object has been received and then perished – rescission is impossible. Similarly, the benefit in ijarah is consumed, so the contract cannot be undone.
This is a qiyas: asl = sale after destruction of mabi', far' = ijarah after istifa', illa = consumption of subject matter.
4
Apply the Ruling to a Concrete Scenario
Consider an ajir who completed building a wall and claims 10 dirhams; the musta'jir says it was for 5 dirhams. No witnesses. After full performance, the musta'jir takes an oath and pays what he admits (5 dirhams).
The ajir cannot demand more because his claim is unproven, and the benefit is gone.
5
Distinguish from Pre-Performance Dispute
If the dispute occurred before the ajir started work, mutual oath would apply and the contract would be rescinded. This contrast highlights the critical role of istifa'.

Commentary
This is a ruling on disputes in ijarah (hire) after the benefit has been fully received. The passage states that when the ajir (worker) and musta'jir (hirer) disagree after the benefit (manfa'ah) has been fully received, they do not engage in mutual oath-taking (tahaluf). Instead, the musta'jir's statement is accepted with his oath. The reasoning is that the benefit has been consumed and it is akin to a sold item that has perished, making rescission impossible. The ajir claims an excess wage, and the musta'jir denies it; in the absence of evidence, the defendant (musta'jir) takes an oath and his statement prevails.

Key Terms
استيفاء (istifa'): Full receipt or complete performance of the benefit (manfa'ah) in an ijarah contract.
مستأجر (musta'jir): The person who hires another for service or benefit, paying wages.
أجير (ajir): The worker or person who provides service in exchange for wages (ujrah).

Dispute after Partial Performance in Ijarah
Ruling on mutual oath for remaining part and past part

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a dispute in ijarah after partial performance, distinguishing between the past and remaining parts, and apply the rule to a similar scenario.

Core Idea
For the remaining part of an ijarah, both parties take oaths and the contract is rescinded; for the consumed part, the hirer's word with oath prevails.

When an ijarah contract is partially performed and a dispute arises, the Hanafi school makes a critical distinction. The portion already consumed is treated differently from the portion still due. This lesson explores the ruling and its underlying logic, focusing on the mutual oath (tahaluf) for the remaining part and the defendant's oath for the past part.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the situation: an ijarah (hire) contract where the benefit has been partially received, and the parties disagree about terms (e.g., wage or duration).
This triggers the specific ruling.
2
Separate remaining and past parts
Divide the subject matter (manfa'ah) into two categories: the part already consumed (past) and the part still due (future). The ruling treats each differently.
The past part is considered as if the contract has been executed for that portion.
3
Apply mutual oath for remaining part
For the remaining part, since both parties are claimants and defendants simultaneously, they take mutual oaths (tahaluf). If oaths conflict, the contract is rescinded for that portion.
The rescission only affects the remaining part, not the whole contract.
4
Apply defendant's oath for past part
For the past part (consumed benefit), the musta'jir (hirer) is considered the defendant. His statement with an oath is accepted if the ajir (worker) cannot prove otherwise.
No mutual oath here because the benefit no longer exists.

Commentary
This is a ruling on ijarah disputes when only part of the benefit has been received. The passage differentiates between the past (already received) and future (remaining) parts. For the remaining part, because the benefit is still due, the parties take mutual oaths (tahaluf) and the contract is rescinded for that portion. For the past portion that has been consumed, no mutual oath is taken; instead, the musta'jir (hirer) is the defendant, and if he takes an oath, his statement is accepted. The commentary gives an example: a one-month contract where only 15 days were worked; for the remaining 15 days, mutual oath and rescission; for the completed 15 days, musta'jir's word with oath prevails.

Key Terms
تحالفا (tahālufā): Mutual oath-taking by both parties when they disagree and both are claimants and defendants.
معقود عليه (ma'qūd 'alayh): The subject matter of the contract; in ijarah, it is the benefit (manfa'ah).

Imam's Options When Conquering a City by Force
States the two options available to the imam after conquering a city by force

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two options available to the imam after conquering a city by force and explain the Quranic basis for the imam's discretion.

Core Idea
The imam may either distribute conquered land as spoils or leave it to inhabitants with jizya and kharaj.

This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling on what an imam may do with land conquered by force (anwatan). The text presents two valid options and cites a Quranic verse as proof. Understanding this ruling clarifies the imam's discretionary authority in matters of war booty and the treatment of conquered populations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? The imam's disposition of land conquered by force. The ruling is that he has a choice between two options.
The act occurs after conquest, not during.
2
Locate the Quranic dalil
The text cites Surah al-Hashr 59:7 regarding fay'. Identify the verse and note that it speaks of spoils from towns.
The verse is evidence for the imam's authority over distribution.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the verse prove the imam's discretion? The phrase 'فلله وللرسول' indicates that the imam (as deputy of the Prophet) has authority to decide the distribution.
The Hanafi analogy extends this to land conquered by force.
4
Note cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Compare with Imam al-Shafi'i's position that land must be distributed as spoils. The Hanafi school differs, allowing the second option.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different interpretations.
5
Evaluate the practical consequences
Consider the outcome of each option: distribution gives immediate benefit to fighters; leaving inhabitants produces ongoing tax revenue.
The imam chooses based on public interest (maslahah).

Commentary
This passage states a ruling of the Hanafi school regarding the disposition of conquered land taken by force ('anwatan). The imam has two options: (1) to divide the land as spoils among the fighters (ghanimin/mujahideen), or (2) to leave the original inhabitants on the land, imposing jizyah (poll tax) on them as dhimmis (protected non-Muslims) and kharaj (land tax) on the land itself. The author then notes that there is indication of this ruling in the Quran, citing Surah al-Hashr 59:7, which speaks of the distribution of war booty and the authority of the imam. This verse shows that the imam has discretion in distributing spoils, including land. The hashya following the verse appears to be misplaced but repeats the story of the 80 men from Mecca and the hadith of Badar captives, which are more relevant to the previous lesson on prisoners rather than this ruling.

Key Terms
عنوة (anwatan): By force, conquest through combat as opposed to sulh (peaceful surrender).
الجزية (al-jizya): Poll tax levied on non-Muslim dhimmis in exchange for protection and exemption from military service.
الخراج (al-kharaj): Land tax imposed on the land itself, usually on conquered agricultural lands, regardless of the owner's religion.
