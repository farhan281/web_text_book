Hoarding One's Own Produce or Imported Goods Is Not Ihtikar
States exception: hoarding own produce or imported goods is not ihtikar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hoarding one's own produce and imported goods, and distinguish it from prohibited ihtikar.

Core Idea
If you grow it or import it, you can store it — hoarding only applies to goods bought from the local market.

The passage draws a sharp line: not every act of storing food is the forbidden ihtikar. The Prophet forbade hoarding 'in the town', meaning buying from the same market and withholding. This lesson unpacks that exception and its basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What does the text rule about hoarding one's own produce or imported goods? (It is not ihtikar; it is permissible/mubah.)
The act is storing goods that one either produced or imported.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof is cited? The hadith: 'The Prophet forbade hoarding in the town.'
The hadith is from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the exception? The phrase 'in the town' restricts the prohibition to goods already in the local market, excluding one's own produce or imported goods.
The key is the restriction 'bi-al-balad'.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools may prohibit hoarding one's own produce if it causes harm, but the Hanafi school allows it based on the restricted scope of the dalil.
Cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.
5
Apply to a practical scenario
If a farmer stores his harvest, is he a muhtakir? No, because it is his own produce. If a merchant imports goods and stores them, also not ihtikar.
The decisive factor is the source of the goods.

Commentary
This passage gives an important exception: hoarding is only makruh when the goods are purchased from the local market. If a person stores the harvest from his own land, or imports goods from another region, it is not considered ihtikar. The evidence is the hadith 'The Prophet forbade hoarding in the town', meaning buying from the same town and hoarding. The reason is that the townspeople's right is attached to goods that are already in the market; one's own produce is not something they have a right to. The commentary also notes that this was the practice of Ibn Tawus, who kept food from his land for years waiting for high prices, and this was not prohibited.

Hoarding One's Own Produce vs. Market Goods: Key Distinction in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling with proofs on types of hoarding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between permissible and prohibited hoarding in the Hanafi school and apply the condition of harm to the public.

Core Idea
Hoarding is prohibited only when it harms the public by withholding market-bought food; one's own produce is not subject to this ruling.

This lesson examines a crucial distinction in Hanafi fiqh regarding hoarding (ihtikar). The text from Al-Sharh al-Sameri clarifies that not all withholding of grain is blameworthy. The key factors are the source of the goods (one's own land vs. market purchase) and the presence of harm to the community. We will explore the proofs from the practice of the Salaf and hadith, and understand how to apply this ruling in real-life scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the passage rules upon: hoarding one's own produce (ghallah al-day'ah) and goods imported from another city. The ruling: these are not considered ihtikar and are not disliked (makruh).
The act is withholding from sale, but the legal classification depends on source.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
Find the proofs cited: (1) the practice of Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib who hoarded date pits, leaves, and seeds (non-staple items); (2) the hadith of Abu Umama that the Prophet prohibited hoarding food; (3) the hadith of 'Ali prohibiting hoarding in the city.
The first athar shows that not all hoarding is prohibited; the hadiths limit the prohibition.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof proves the ruling)
The hadith prohibits hoarding food (al-ta'am). The Hanafi principle: a prohibition (nahi) indicates tahrim (unlawfulness) unless evidence reduces it to makruh tahrimi. Here, the context of harm makes it tahrimi only when harm occurs. The athar of Sa'id shows that hoarding non-staples (or one's own produce) does not cause harm, so it is not included in the prohibition.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly disagreement)
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that all hoarding of food is haram if it causes harm, and some Shafi'is extend to non-foods. The Hanafi school restricts the ruling to market-bought staples that cause harm to the public.
This difference affects the ruling on imported goods and own produce.
5
Determine the practical application
Apply the ruling: a farmer storing his own harvest is permissible; a merchant buying from the local market and withholding to raise prices is makruh tahrimi. The condition of harm must be present for the prohibition to apply.
If there is no harm (e.g., ample supply), even market hoarding may not be disliked.

Price Fixing by the Ruler is Not Recommended
Ruling with hadith proof on price fixing by the ruler

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on price fixing (tas'ir) and explain the hadith evidence used to derive it, including the ikhtilaf with other schools.

Core Idea
The ruler should not fix prices because the Prophet refused, affirming that Allah alone controls pricing.

This passage tackles a key economic fiqh issue: may the ruler intervene in market prices? The answer from the Hanafi school, based on the hadith of Anas, is that it is not recommended. We will analyse the hadith and the legal reasoning behind this ruling, including the differing views within and outside the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act of the ruler fixing prices (tas'ir). What is the hukm level according to the Hanafi school?
The text uses 'la yanbaghi' (not appropriate), which Hanafi jurists classify as makruh tanzih (disliked but not haram).
2
Locate the dalil
Which proof is cited? The hadith of Anas ibn Malik. Identify the source and the type of hadith (ahad).
The hadith is narrated by Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, and Ibn Majah. It is a hadith ahad, so it yields zanni certainty.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How exactly does this hadith prove the ruling? The Prophet's refusal to fix prices and his statement that Allah is the one who fixes prices indicates that this authority belongs to Allah alone, not to human rulers.
The imperative 'la tusa'ir' is not explicit but the act of refusal coupled with the theological statement shows disapproval.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Does any other school or even Hanafi scholars differ? Mention the Shafi'i position (allowed for maslahah) and the intra-Hanafi view of Abu Yusuf (allowed in necessity).
This is a mukhtalaf fih issue. The Hanafi school generally discourages tas'ir, but later scholars allow it in extreme cases.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Which position is the mufta bihi (relied upon)? The majority Hanafi position is that tas'ir is not permissible except when absolutely necessary to prevent severe hardship.
Al-Kasani in Bada'i' discusses the ikhtilaf. The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.

Selling Weapons During Fitnah
Ruling with hadith and athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling weapons during fitnah, identify the proof from hadith and athar, and explain why the sale remains valid despite the prohibition.

Core Idea
A sale of weapons during fitnah is valid but sinful—the prohibition targets the act, not the contract.

This lesson examines a critical issue from the chapter of sales: selling weapons during times of turmoil. The Hanafi school balances the severe consequences of arming potential adversaries with the Islamic principle that unlawful intent does not automatically invalidate a contract. You will learn the ruling, its evidence, and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal classification
The act is selling weapons to people involved in fitnah (rebels or enemy combatants). The Hanafi school classifies this as makruh tahrimi – prohibitively disliked but not haram in the sense of forbidden with invalidity.
The term fitnah here includes civil strife and war where weapons may be used against Muslims.
2

(Tirmidhi, chapter on prohibition of making wine into vinegar, no. 2395; Ibn Majah, chapter on cursing wine in ten aspects, p. 289, no. 3380) In this hadith, the winemaker and drinker are cursed. Also those who aid them are cursed. From this, it is understood that the aider incurs some sin. Therefore, one should avoid selling grape juice to such people. Principle: If one aids in a sinful thing from a distance, it is permissible, but one will be sinful commensurate with the aid. 'Al-asir' is juice, grape syrup. Footnote: (a) The Prophet cursed ten people regarding wine: the presser, the one for whom it is pressed, the drinker, its carrier, the one to whom it is carried, the server, its seller, the eater of its price, the buyer, and the one for whom it is bought.
Source pages p. 360

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Hazr wal-Ibaha (Book of Prohibitions and Permissions) discusses the ruling on selling grape juice to someone known to make wine. The author states that while selling grape juice to a winemaker is not inherently haram, it is makruh because it involves aiding sin. The hadith of ten people cursed regarding wine is cited, which includes the seller and the one who carries it, indicating that any direct assistance in the wine trade incurs sin. The author then distinguishes between direct and remote aid: if one aids from a distance (e.g., selling grapes without knowledge of its use), it is permissible, but if one knows the buyer makes wine, it is better to avoid. The footnote explains the hadith further.

Preferential Sale vs. Emancipation in Death Illness
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sequence of muhabat and itq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on the conflict between muhabat and itq during marad al-mawt, and explain why the Sahibayn's view is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Sequence decides under Abu Hanifa; emancipation always wins under Sahibayn.

When a terminally ill person makes a preferential sale (muhabat) and also emancipates a slave (itq), but the combined value exceeds one-third of the estate, a conflict arises: which transaction executes first? Imam Abu Hanifa treats the sequence as decisive, while his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, consistently give precedence to emancipation. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each position and identifies the operative ruling for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two actions and the context
The passage concerns two acts performed during marad al-mawt: a preferential sale (selling an item below market value out of kindness) and the freeing of a slave. Both are subject to the one-third limit of bequests.
Remember that muhabat is treated as a bequest for the excess amount.
2
State Abu Hanifa's two-case rule
According to Abu Hanifa: if muhabat comes first, it takes precedence over itq. If itq comes first, the two are considered equal (both execute proportionally within the one-third).
His reasoning: muhabat is a sale with consideration, thus more binding if earlier.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that itq always takes precedence, regardless of sequence.
Their reasoning: emancipation is an act of devotion that cannot be reversed, and it has a stronger claim than a mere sale.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Abu Hanifa, if muhabat is first, the purchaser acquires the slave; if itq is first, both are equal and the slave is partially freed. Under Sahibayn, the slave is fully freed first, and the preferential sale is scaled back or void.
The difference affects the rights of the slave, the purchaser, and other heirs.
5
Identify the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of the Sahibayn: emancipation always takes precedence. This is considered more precautionary and in line with the higher objective of freeing slaves.
Later Hanafi jurists have confirmed this tarjih.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about the order of validity when a dying person performs both a preferential sale (muhabat) and an emancipation (itq) during his death illness (marad al-mawt). Imam Abu Hanifa holds that if the preferential sale occurs first, it takes precedence over emancipation because it is a sale for a consideration, while emancipation is gratuitous. If emancipation occurs first, both are equal and each takes effect proportionally from one-third of the estate. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) maintain that emancipation always takes precedence regardless of sequence, because emancipation cannot be revoked and is an act of high virtue. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of the Sahibayn, as it is more precautionary and supported by practice.
