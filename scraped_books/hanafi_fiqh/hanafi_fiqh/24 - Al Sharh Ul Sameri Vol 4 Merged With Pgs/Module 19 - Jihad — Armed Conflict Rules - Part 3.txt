Hanafi Ruling on Capturing Families of Muslim Rebels
Ruling that rebels' families and property are not spoils

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking captives and property from Muslim rebels (bughat) and explain the evidence from the actions of Ali ibn Abi Talib.

Core Idea
Muslim rebels retain Islamic protections; enslaving their families is haram.

This lesson examines a key rule of Islamic warfare concerning Muslim rebels (bughat). The Hanafi school holds that rebels are not treated as disbelievers; their families and property remain inviolable. The passage cites the practice of Ali ibn Abi Talib during the Battle of the Camel as decisive proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The matn states that rebels' children are not to be taken captive and their property not divided.
2
Locate the primary proof
The commentator cites two athar from Ali: one on general conduct (no pursuit, no finishing off wounded, etc.) and one on captives specifically.
The second athar is the key evidence.
3
Analyze the second athar
Ali explicitly says 'ليس عليهم سبى' (there is no captivity for them) because 'إنما قاتلنا من قاتلنا' (we only fought those who fought us). This indicates the rebels' children retain their status as Muslims.
4
Determine the hukm
Based on this, the Hanafi ruling is that it is haram to enslave children of rebels and to distribute their wealth as spoils.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Commentary
This passage states a ruling concerning Muslim rebels (al-bughat). The matn declares that their children are not to be enslaved and their wealth is not to be distributed as spoils of war. The commentator supports this with two athar (traditions) from Ali ibn Abi Talib. The first athar establishes the broader conduct in fighting rebels: no pursuit of the fleeing, no killing of the wounded or prisoners, and those who remain in their homes or disarm are secure. The second athar specifically addresses the children of rebels: they are not to be taken as captives because the fight is only against those who actually fought. The ruling is based on the principle that rebels retain their Islamic rights; they are not treated as harbi kuffar.

Key Terms
سبى (saby): Taking captives for enslavement; in this context, taking rebel families as captives.
بغاة (bughat): Muslims who rebel against a legitimate Muslim ruler.

Using Rebels' Weapons: Hanafi Permissibility Based on Need
Figh ruling on using rebel weapons with proof from Ali's practice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on using weapons captured from Muslim rebels, explain the proof from Ali's practice, and distinguish it from the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Necessity justifies using rebel weapons, as Ali did at Siffin.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi fiqh of jihad: is it permissible for the Muslim army to use weapons confiscated from rebel prisoners to fight them? The source passage from a classical Hanafi text rules that there is no harm in doing so when the Muslims need them, citing the precedent of Ali during the Battle of Siffin. We will analyze the Hanafi position, the opposing view of Imam al-Shafi'i, and the legal reasoning that gives weight to necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? The act is using weapons taken from Muslim rebel prisoners to fight against those same rebels. The ruling is 'la ba's' = no harm = permissible (mubah) under the condition of need.
Note that the permissibility is conditional on the Muslim army needing the weapons.
2
Locate the Hanafi Proof (Dalil)
The proof is the practice (athar) of Ali during Siffin: he would take the mount and weapon of rebel captives, then release them on a pledge not to return. This shows taking is allowed, and by implication using is allowed.
This is a qawl al-sahabi, which is a recognised source in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand the Shafi'i Objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that using any Muslim's property without permission is prohibited, so rebels' weapons cannot be used.
The Shafi'i position is based on a general principle of inviolability of Muslim property.
4
Analyse the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response invokes necessity (darurah) and the precedent of Ali. The need to prevent rebellion and protect the army overrides the general prohibition in this specific context.
This is an application of istihsan (juristic preference) over strict qiyas.
5
Determine the Practical Difference (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
The practical consequence: Hanafi school permits using the weapons; Shafi'i school does not. The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi (relied upon) for fatwa.
The ikhtilaf remains valid; both schools respect property but differ on exceptional necessity.

And there is no harm in fighting with their weapons if the Muslims need them. He said: Ali, when a prisoner was brought to him on the day of Siffin, would take his mount and his weapon, take a pledge from him not to return, and set him free.

Commentary
This passage addresses the lawfulness of using weapons seized from rebel prisoners. The matn permits using them in battle if the Muslim army needs them. The commentator cites the example of Ali who, after capturing rebels in the Battle of Siffin, would take their horses and weapons and then release them after obtaining a pledge not to fight again. This practice shows that taking weapons is allowed; and if taking is allowed, using them is also allowed. The commentator then notes a disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i, who holds that using a Muslim's property without permission is not permissible even in this context. The Hanafi response, based on necessity and the precedent of Ali, is that the need to prevent further rebellion justifies the temporary use of their arms.

Key Terms
سلاح (silah): Weapons; in this context, arms captured from rebel prisoners.
دابة (dābbah): Mount (horse or camel); in the athar, Ali takes the rebel's mount as well.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
Hanafi fiqh permits using captured rebel weapons when the Muslim army needs them, based on Ali's practice and the principle of necessity.

The Imam's Duty to Hold Rebels' Wealth Until Repentance
Ruling with proof from Ali's athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on rebels' property, explain the evidence from the athar of Ali, and distinguish it from ghanimah.

Core Idea
Rebels' property is held, not confiscated; ownership remains until repentance.

When Muslim rebels are defeated, what happens to their property? The Hanafi school rules that the Imam does not distribute it as spoils nor return it immediately. Instead, he withholds it until the rebels repent, after which it is returned. This ruling is based on the practice of Ali ibn Abi Talib at the Battle of Nahrawan against the Kharijites. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its evidence, and the key distinction from the treatment of enemy combatants.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? The Imam's handling of rebels' property. Determine the hukm level: is it wajib, sunnah, etc.?
The matn uses imperative forms indicating obligation.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof is cited? An athar from Ali ibn Abi Talib during the Battle of Nahrawan. Identify the source and its chain.
The athar is recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does this athar prove the ruling? Ali announced that anyone recognizing their property could take it, showing that ownership remained with the rebels and was not expropriated.
This demonstrates that the property is merely held, not transferred.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Is this ruling agreed upon? The Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih; some schools treat rebels' property as spoils. Identify the practical difference.
The consequence is whether property is returned or distributed.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Why is the Hanafi position relied upon? The evidence from Ali's practice and the principle that rebels are Muslim owners who have not forfeited ownership.
The athar is considered a strong precedent.

Source Text

ويحبس الإمام أموالهم ولا يردها عليهم ولا يقسمها حتى يتوبوا فيردها عليهم. قال: لما جيء بعلي بما في عسكر أهل النهر قال من عرف شيئا فليأخذه، قال فأخذت الأقدر ثم رأيتها بعد قد أخذت.
And the Imam holds back their wealth, does not return it to them, and does not distribute it until they repent, then he returns it to them. He said: When the belongings of the camp of the people of Nahr were brought to Ali, he said: Whoever recognizes something, let him take it. He said: I took a cooking pot, then later I saw that it had been taken.
Source pages p. 334

Commentary
This passage explains the procedure for handling the property of Muslim rebels who have been defeated. The Imam is to seize their wealth but not distribute it as spoils, nor return it immediately. Instead, it is held until they repent. Upon repentance, the property is returned. The commentator supports this with an athar from the Battle of Nahrawan against the Kharijites. When Ali captured their camp, he announced that anyone who recognized their property could reclaim it. This demonstrates that after repentance or cessation of rebellion, the property goes back to its owners. The underlying principle is that rebels remain owners of their wealth; the state merely prevents them from using it to fund further insurrection.

Key Terms
حبس (habs): To withhold or detain property temporarily, not as confiscation.
أهل النهر (ahl al-nahr): The people of Nahr, referring to the Kharijites who were defeated at the Battle of Nahrawan.
أقدر (aqdar): Cooking pots (plural of qidr). In the athar, the narrator mentions taking a cooking pot.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi ruling: rebels' property is held until repentance, based on Ali's practice at Nahrawan.

Kharaj and Ushr Collected by Rebels
Imam does not re-collect kharaj and ushr already taken by rebels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on kharaj and ushr collected by rebels and explain why the Imam does not recollect them.

Core Idea
Taxes once collected by rebels fulfill the obligation; no double taxation.

Rebels who temporarily control territory may levy taxes. What happens when the Imam retakes control? This passage from the Siyar clarifies that the Imam does not recollect kharaj and ushr already taken, preventing hardship on the taxpayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actors and taxes
Recognize that the rebels (ahl al-baghy) have collected kharaj (land tax) and ushr (tithe) from the territory they conquered.
The taxes are from the legitimate Islamic treasury system.
2
State the primary ruling
The Imam does not take those taxes a second time from the people.
The passage explicitly says 'lam ya'khudhuhu al-imam minhum thaniyatan'.
3
Explain the rationale
The rebels' collection is deemed to have satisfied the tax obligation for that period; recollecting would cause double taxation.
This is a principle of preventing hardship (raf' al-haraj).
4
Address the secondary ruling
If the Imam seizes something from the rebels themselves, he does not return it to them.
This affirms the Imam's authority without benefiting the rebels.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
وما جباه على أهل البغي من البلاد التي غلبوا عليها من الخراج والعشر لم يأخذه الإمام منهم ثانية، فإذا أخذ الإمام منهم شيئا فلا يعيده عليهم.
And whatever kharaj and ushr the people of rebellion collected from the lands they conquered, the Imam does not take it from them a second time. And if the Imam takes something from them, he does not return it to them.
Source pages p. 334

Commentary
This passage addresses taxes collected by rebels during their occupation. The ruling states that if rebels have already collected kharaj (land tax) or ushr (tithe) from the areas they controlled, the Imam, upon reasserting authority, does not collect those taxes again from the taxpayers. The commentary explains that the collection by rebels is deemed to have fulfilled the obligation for that period. However, if the Imam himself collects something after regaining control, he does not return it to the rebels (since they are not entitled to it), but the taxpayers are not required to pay twice. This balances the rights of the Muslim treasury with the principle against double taxation.

Key Terms
خراج (kharaj): Land tax levied on non-Muslim subjects (or all landowners) in Islamic state.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
