Bequest for Hajj: Where Should the Proxy Start?
Ruling on bequest of Hajj and its execution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on executing a bequest for Hajj and identify the condition that determines the proxy's starting point.

Core Idea
A bequest for Hajj must be fulfilled from the testator's town if funds allow; otherwise from the nearest possible point.

When someone dies owing the obligatory Hajj and makes a bequest for it, the executor must arrange a proxy to perform it from the testator's estate. The Hanafi school prioritizes sending a proxy from the testator's own town, but practical constraints may require a closer starting point. This lesson unpacks the rule and its condition based on the bequest's sufficiency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The text rules on a bequest (wasiyya) for 'Hijjat al-Islam' – the obligatory Hajj. The ruling is that the executor must perform Hajj on behalf of the deceased.
Obligatory Hajj is fard 'ayn; if unpaid, it becomes a debt from the estate.
2
Determine the default procedure
The default is to hire a proxy (badal) from the testator's own town (min baladihi), and the proxy must travel riding (i.e., by customary transport). This assumes the bequest covers the expenses.
Riding means using a mount or vehicle, not walking, to match the testator's obligation.
3
Handle insufficient bequest
If the bequeathed funds do not suffice for a journey from the testator's town, then the proxy should be sent from the nearest point from which the journey is possible within the available funds.
This prioritizes fulfilling the Hajj rather than strictly adhering to the starting point.

Hajj for a Deceased Traveler: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on Hajj starting point

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position and the Sahibayn's position on the starting point of Hajj for a deceased who died en route, and explain the proof and thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Death cuts off the journey; the Hajj must restart from home, says Abu Hanifa, but Sahibayn count the completed travel.

When a person sets out for Hajj but dies on the way after making a will for Hajj to be performed on his behalf, a dispute arises within the Hanafi school: should the Hajj start from his home city or from the place of death? This lesson unpacks the proof each side uses and the practical financial consequence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what Abu Hanifa holds: the Hajj must be performed from the deceased's home city (balad). The journey is voided by death.
This is based on the hadith that deeds are cut off at death.
2
Identify the Sahibayn position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the Hajj should begin from the place where the person died, because the completed portion of the journey still carries reward.
They cite Quran 4:100 about emigrants.
3
Examine the proofs
Abu Hanifa uses the hadith 'When a human dies, his deeds are cut off...' to argue the travel is void. The Sahibayn use 'Whoever leaves his home emigrating... his reward has already fallen upon Allah' to argue the journey counts.
Note the distinction: one is general, the other is specific to hijrah.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa responds that the verse applies to hijrah, not ordinary travel, and the will itself specifies starting from home.
The will (wasiyyah) is key; it must be interpreted literally.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical effect
The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's. However, if the estate is insufficient for a full journey from home, the Sahibayn's view may be followed to reduce expense.
Thamarah al-khilaf: financial burden on the estate.

Whoever left his city to perform Hajj and died on the way, and made a will that Hajj be performed on his behalf, it shall be performed on his behalf from his city according to Abu Hanifa, and Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on them) said: It shall be performed on his behalf from where he died. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the location from which a deceased person's Hajj should be performed when they died en route after making a will. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the Hajj must be initiated from the deceased's home city (balad), because the journey is considered voided by death; the will's intent is to have Hajj from home. The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that the Hajj should commence from the place of death, since the deceased had already earned reward for the completed portion of travel. Abu Hanifa supports his view with the hadith that deeds are cut off at death—since the journey is a deed, it is cut off, so only home-based Hajj fulfills the will. The Sahibayn cite the Qur'anic verse (4:100) about emigrants who die en route receiving their full reward, implying that the completed travel counts, so the Hajj continues from that point. The practical outcome: if the estate can afford it, Abu Hanifa's opinion requires a new journey from home; if not, the Sahibayn's view allows starting from the death location.

Key Terms
وصية (wasiyyah): A will or testament directing that a certain act (like Hajj) be performed after death using up to one-third of the estate.
حج (hajj): The pilgrimage to Makkah; here, the act of performing Hajj on behalf of a deceased person (hajj al-badal).
