Continuous Weighing and Oaths on Piecemeal Collection
Ruling on continuous weighing not being piecemeal collection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'piecemeal collection' in the context of oaths and apply the Hanafi ruling that continuous weighing without other work does not break an oath.

Core Idea
Continuous weighed collection in one session is a single act—oath not broken.

An oath not to collect debt piecemeal seems straightforward, but what counts as 'piecemeal'? This lesson clarifies that multiple weighings in a single session without other work are considered one continuous act, so the oath remains intact. Understanding this nuance prevents unnecessary legal violations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the oath
The act is collecting a debt by weighing (e.g., wheat or silver). The oath prohibits piecemeal collection, meaning receiving the debt in separate parts.
2
Define 'piecemeal' in Hanafi legal context
Piecemeal (tafriq) requires an interruption that is not part of the collection process, such as engaging in other work or a time gap between receipts.
3
Recognize the exception for continuous weighing
If the collection occurs in one session and the only interruption is the act of weighing itself (e.g., due to scale capacity), it is not considered piecemeal.
4
Apply to the given example
A creditor swears not to collect 500 kg of wheat piecemeal. He weighs it in 5 batches in one sitting, with no other activities between weighings. This is valid—the oath is not broken.
5
Distinguish from true piecemeal
If the creditor pauses to eat, pray, or do any other work between weighings, it becomes piecemeal and breaks the oath.

Oath to Go to a Place: Timing of Breach at Death
Ruling on oath to go to a place until death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the precise moment when an oath to perform a future act is broken upon death and explain the Hanafi proof from the hadith of Hudaybiyyah.

Core Idea
An oath to do something is only broken at the last moment of life because until then, fulfillment remains possible.

When a person swears to perform an act but dies before doing it, at what exact moment does the oath break? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling that the breach occurs in the final instant of life, not earlier. The reasoning hinges on the possibility of fulfillment until death, and the proof is drawn from the Prophet’s open-ended promise about performing tawaf.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the legal consequence: the oath is broken (hanth) in the last part of life.
The ruling applies to any conditional oath to do something specific.
2
Understand the rationale
During life, there is always hope (rajaa) that the oath may be fulfilled. Only at death does certainty of impossibility arise.
This distinguishes the Hanafi view from those who might say the oath is broken at the moment of inability.
3
Locate the dalil
The hadith of Hudaybiyyah: the Prophet said 'We will perform tawaf' but did not specify a time.
The hadith is quoted partially in the source.
4
Extract the wajh al-dalalah
The absence of a time specification shows that a promise or oath remains in effect until the possibility ends—i.e., at death.
This supports the ruling that breach is only at the last moment of life.
5
Apply the ruling
The same principle applies to any oath to perform a specific act: the oath is only broken at the final instant of life.
This affects the calculation of expiation (kaffarah) timing.

Bringing the Claimed Object to Court
Ruling on producing the specific item in court or stating its value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a claimed item is in the defendant's possession and explain the procedure when the item is not produced.

Core Idea
The defendant must produce the exact object; failing that, he must state its value to allow the case to proceed.

This lesson deals with a procedural rule in qada (judicial proceedings) concerning the handling of a claimed object (al-'ayn). When the item is physically in the defendant's possession, the court requires its production to eliminate ambiguity. If the defendant refuses or is unable to bring it, the law does not leave the claimant without recourse—the value must be stated. This ensures the case can move forward even without the object's physical presence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
The ruling applies when the claimed object is an 'ayn (specific tangible item) and is in the possession of the defendant (fi yad al-mudda'a alayhi). Determine if this condition is met.
If the item is a debt or not in defendant's hand, different rules apply.
2
State the primary obligation
The defendant is compelled (kullifa) to bring the item to court so the claimant can point to it (yushira ilayha bi'l-da'wa). This avoids confusion about which item is being claimed.
The court may enforce this through legal pressure.
3
Apply the alternative
If the defendant does not bring the item (wa in lam yuhdir), he must mention its value (falyadhkur qimataha). The value allows the court to rule for compensation.
The value is the market price at the time of the claim.
4
Recognize the thamarah
The practical outcome: either the object is identified and returned, or its value is established for a monetary judgment.
This prevents the defendant from using non-production to delay the case.

Competing Claims to Property with Equal Evidence
Explaining the ruling when two claimants present evidence for the same property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two claimants present equal evidence for the same property and apply it to possession scenarios.

Core Idea
When evidence is equal and no possession, the property is divided equally.

This lesson addresses a classic dispute resolution: two claimants each produce valid evidence for the same property. The Hanafi ruling, grounded in Prophetic practice, requires equal division when both proofs are equal and no possession distinguishes them. Understanding when possession changes the outcome is key.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
Recognize that two claimants assert ownership of the same property (ayn) in the hand of a third party (or each has part). The core question: how does the judge rule when both present equal evidence?
The property is not in the hands of either claimant—this triggers the division rule.
2
State the Basic Ruling
If both claimants present evidence (bayyinah) and neither has possession, the judge divides the property equally between them.
This is the default ruling when evidence is equal and no preference factor exists.
3
Apply Possession Exception
If one claimant possesses the property, the evidence of the outsider (non-possessor) is given weight, not the possessor's. This overrides the equal division rule.
Possession creates a presumption of ownership, so the outsider must bring stronger evidence.
4
Examine the Prophetic Proof
The hadith of Abu Musa al-Ash'ari: two men each produced two witnesses for a camel; the Prophet (SAW) divided it equally. This establishes that equal evidence leads to equal division.

Core Idea
A deceased child's lineage need not be established, so the seller's claim is ineffective.

In Hanafi sale and slave law, if a female slave gives birth within six months of purchase, the buyer may claim paternity, establishing the child's lineage and the mother's umm walad status. If the child were alive, the seller's claim would be accepted: nasab is established to avoid illegitimacy, and automatically the mother becomes umm walad. Grasp the default: necessity (darura) overrides the finality of the sale.

Death changes the legal calculus – the child no longer needs a lineage.

Result: the seller's claim is not accepted. Nasab is not proven, the mother does not become umm walad, and the sale remains valid. The sale is not annulled because there is no pressing necessity to break it.

The reasoning is based on necessity (darura). Necessity exists only to protect a living child. Once that necessity vanishes, the default rule of contract finality applies. This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) that the Hanafi school employs.

The Umm Walad Sale Dispute: Full Refund vs. Child's Share Only
Intra-Hanafi dispute on refund amount after umm walad sale annulled

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on price return when a sold umm walad dies and apply the tarjih to determine the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
When an umm walad dies after sale, Abu Hanifa returns full price; Sahibayn only child's share because the mother was under guarantee.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the financial consequences of selling a slave woman who is later discovered to be an umm walad (mother of her master's child). The mother dies before the seller claims paternity of a child born within six months of the sale. While both parties agree the sale is annulled retroactively, they differ on how much of the price must be returned. The disagreement turns on whether the mother was held as a trust (amanah) or a guarantee (daman) in the buyer's possession.

Guided Path
1
Establish the case facts
The mother dies after the sale. The seller claims a child born to her within six months of the sale as his own. The child is alive.
The child's lineage is established to the seller, making the mother retroactively an umm walad.
2
Identify the legal effect on lineage and the sale
The child's lineage is established to the seller, and the mother becomes an umm walad. Consequently, the sale is annulled (the subject matter was not legally saleable).
Both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree on this part.
3
State the two positions on price return
Abu Hanifa: the seller must return the entire price. The Sahibayn: the seller must return only the share of the price attributable to the child, not the mother's share.
The child is alive and taken back by the seller.
4

Abu Hanifa views the mother as having been a trust (amanah) in the buyer's hands from the start (since the sale was void ab initio). Therefore, her death is not the buyer's loss. The Sahibayn view the mother as having been under guarantee (daman) because the sale was valid at the time; her death is the buyer's loss.
This difference arises from whether the contract is considered void ab initio or voidable upon discovery.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi jurists (e.g., al-Haskafi, Ibn Abidin) favor the Sahibayn's view as more practical and fair, avoiding unjust enrichment.
The mufta bihi is that the seller returns only the child's share.

Seller's Paternity Claim After Umm Walad's Death: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on price return after mother's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the return of the price when the seller claims paternity of a child after the mother's death in a void sale of umm walad, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
In a void sale of an umm walad, possession is a trust, so the buyer owes nothing for her death—full price is returned.

This passage presents a disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, over a specific scenario: a woman sold as a slave who is later discovered to be an umm walad dies, and then the seller claims paternity of her infant. The dispute centers on the nature of the buyer's possession in a void contract: is it a trust (amanah) or a guarantee (daman)? The practical consequence affects how much the seller must refund.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the contested ruling
Read the passage carefully. The scenario: a woman sold as a slave (who is actually an umm walad) dies; later the seller claims paternity of her child, born less than six months after sale. The question: must the seller return the full price or only part of it?
The child is taken back by the seller because paternity is established.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his rationale
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the seller must return the entire price. His rationale: the buyer's possession of the mother was a trust (amanah) because the sale was void (batil). Therefore, the buyer is not liable for her death.
Amanah means no liability for loss unless negligence.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their rationale
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the seller returns only the child's share of the price, not the mother's share. Their rationale: the buyer's possession was a guarantee (daman), meaning the buyer bore the risk of loss for the mother.
Daman means liability for loss even without fault.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
According to Abu Hanifa, the buyer receives a full refund. According to the Sahibayn, the buyer bears the loss of the mother's value and only recovers the portion allocated to the child.
This difference stems from the legal nature of possession in a void sale.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position and why
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's view. The sale of an umm walad is absolutely invalid, making the possession a trust. Hence the full price is returned.
This is the view adopted by later Hanafi authorities.

Claiming One Twin Establishes Both
Ruling statement on twin paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on claiming paternity of one twin and explain the legal reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
Claiming paternity of one twin automatically establishes paternity of both.

In Hanafi law, paternity (nasab) is a fundamental right that carries inheritance and lineage implications. This ruling from Kitab al-Da'wa addresses a specific scenario: when a man acknowledges only one of twin children. The legal principle is rooted in the biological reality that twins share the same father from a single act of conception. Understanding this rule clarifies how Hanafi jurisprudence ensures consistency in paternity claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and legal question
The act is a man claiming paternity of only one of two twin children born to a woman. The legal question: does this claim also affect the other twin?
The claim is a form of acknowledgment (iqrar), not a dispute.
2
State the ruling from the text
The text states: 'And whoever claims the lineage of one of the twins, his lineage is established from him for both of them.' Thus, the ruling is that both twins become the man's children in terms of paternity.
The pronoun 'them' in 'for both of them' refers to the twins.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The ruling is based on the impossibility of two different fathers for twins born from the same pregnancy. Since twins are conceived from a single act of intercourse, they must share the same biological father. Therefore, acknowledging one implies acknowledging the other.
This principle is consistent with the Hanafi maxim that the child belongs to the marriage bed (al-walad li al-firash).
4
Apply to a practical scenario
Consider a man who claims only one twin as his son. After this ruling, both twins inherit from him, bear his name, and have mutual inheritance rights with him.
The man cannot later deny the other twin once paternity is established.

The Imam's Duty to Appoint a Salaried Qasim
Opening ruling of Kitab al-Qismah with proof from Quran and Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the Imam should appoint a salaried qasim from the public treasury and identify the primary Quranic and Sunnah evidences for this ruling.

Core Idea
Partition of property is a public necessity; the state must fund a qualified divider to avoid corruption.

This passage opens the Book of Partition (Kitab al-Qismah) in a Hanafi manual. The first ruling establishes that the ruler (Imam) is responsible for providing a state-funded official to divide jointly owned property. The author cites multiple proofs: a Quranic verse implying division, the Prophet's division of Khaybar, and athar from the Companions. This reflects the Hanafi principle that essential public services must be funded by the treasury.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its legal force
The text uses 'yanbaghi' ('it is appropriate'). In Hanafi fiqh, this term, when applied to the Imam's duties, indicates a strong recommendation that is effectively obligatory (wajib) in light of public welfare.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author first cites Quran 4:8, which mentions 'al-qismah' (the division) as a known event, indirectly proving that division is a legitimate and established practice among Muslims.
The verse is used as indirect evidence (dalala zanni).
3
Examine the supporting proofs from Sunnah
Two hadith are presented: the Prophet's division of Khaybar into 36 shares, and his division of spoils at Dhu al-Hulayfah. These establish that partition was practiced by the Prophet and provide a model for equitable division.
The Khaybar hadith also shows setting aside part for public needs (nawā'ib al-nās).
4
Consider the practical application (athar)
Athar from Ibn Sirin and Umar show that paying a qasim a fee or salary is permissible and not bribery. Ibn Sirin distinguished between bribery for judgment and payment for estimation or division.
This refutes the misconception that any payment to a divider is corrupt.
5
Summarize the Hanafi position
The Imam must appoint a qualified qasim, pay his salary from the treasury, and ensure division is free for the parties. This prevents bias and ensures access to justice.
If the treasury cannot pay, alternative arrangements (e.g., volunteer qasim) may be allowed, but the Imam should strive to provide funding.

This passage from the Book of Division lists several rulings concerning the official divider (qasim). Core Idea

In Hanafi fiqh, when claimants come to a judge asserting ownership of a property without explaining how they acquired it, the judge has the authority to divide the property among them. Identify the legal basis
Possession is a strong external evidence (qarinah) of ownership. Since there is no judgment against an absent party (qada 'ala al-ghayr), the usual requirement for witnesses or detailed proof is waived.
This is a relaxation of normal evidentiary rules due to the circumstances.

Division When One Partner Benefits and the Other is Harmed
Ruling on compulsory partition when shares are unequal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partition when one co-owner benefits and the other is harmed, and apply the principle of preventing harm (la darar) to determine who can compel division.

Core Idea
Partition is not forced when it harms the smaller shareholder; only the one who benefits can compel it.

This passage addresses a common scenario in co-ownership where shares are unequal. Division may benefit one party but harm the other. The Hanafi school, prioritizing the prevention of harm, restricts the right to demand division. Let's explore the ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that one partner has a larger share that yields a usable portion after division, while the other's share is too small to be of practical use.
Example: two men owning three oxen; one gets two oxen (can plow), the other gets one (cannot plow alone).
2
Determine Who Benefits and Who is Harmed
The larger shareholder benefits from the division because he can use his portion productively. The smaller shareholder suffers harm because his portion is unusable.
Harm (darar) here means the inability to benefit from one's share as intended.
3
Apply the Principle of La Darar
The judge is appointed to prevent harm. Forcing division on the smaller shareholder would cause him harm; therefore, he cannot compel division. The larger shareholder, who is not harmed, can compel it.
This principle is derived from the maxim 'la darar wa la dirar'.
4
State the Ruling
If the larger shareholder requests division, the judge must order it. If the smaller shareholder requests, the judge must refuse.
Exception: If the smaller shareholder intends to sell his share for a fair price, division may be allowed.
5
Note the Underlying Rationale
The ruling balances the right to seek partition (based on ownership) with the duty to prevent harm. The smaller shareholder's right is suspended to protect him from his own potential loss.
The judge acts as guardian, preventing the smaller shareholder from harming himself.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Indivisible property cannot be forcibly divided; valuation and compensation preserve fairness.

This passage addresses a common fiqh scenario: co-owners of a property that would be ruined by physical division. The Hanafi school rules that such property cannot be divided without mutual consent, but provides an alternative: appraise the property and award it to one owner in exchange for payment. The author supports this with a hadith about freeing a jointly-owned slave, which establishes the principle of valuation (taqwim).

Guided Path
1
Identify the property type and ruling
The passage lists bathhouse, well, mill as examples of property that cannot be divided without mutual consent. State the ruling: forced division is invalid (batil).

2

The reason is that physical division would render the property useless (loss/dharar). The Hanafi principle: no forced division if it harms either party.

This resolves the deadlock without causing loss.

The author cites the hadith of freeing a share in a slave. When a co-owner frees his share and has wealth, the slave is valued and the other partners are compensated. By qiyas, the same method applies to any indivisible jointly-owned property.
The illa (effective cause) is the inability to physically divide without loss.

The commentator states: 'Where division would cause loss to both, division will not be done without mutual consent.' This is a key fiqh principle.
This principle applies beyond the three examples (e.g., a single parking space, a shared well).

Division When Absent Heir Possesses Property
Ruling on division when the absent heir has possession of the property or part of it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dividing property when an absent heir is in possession and explain the two proofs (qarinah and the prohibition of qada' 'ala al-gha'ib).

Core Idea
Possession by the absent heir blocks division: it is a qarinah against the present heirs' claim and any division would be judgment against the absent.

When an absent heir holds possession of the shared property, the judge must not divide it even if the present heirs request division. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its two rational proofs: circumstantial evidence (qarinah) and the fundamental principle that judgment cannot be passed against an absent party.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is the judicial division of property when an absent heir is in possession. The ruling is that division is not allowed; the judge must refuse.
2
Examine the first proof: qarinah
Possession by the absent heir is a qarinah (circumstantial evidence) that the property belongs to the absent heir or that the present heirs do not have a strong claim. If the present heirs were truly entitled, the property would not be solely in the absent's hands.
Qarinah is a valid basis for judicial decisions in Hanafi law.
3
Examine the second proof: prohibition of qada' 'ala al-gha'ib
Dividing the property would effectively be a judgment against the absent heir, which is not permissible. Hanafi law does not allow issuing rulings that affect an absent person's rights without representation.
This principle safeguards the rights of absent parties.
4
Conclude the legal consequence
Because of both proofs, the judge must leave the property undivided until the absent heir appears or until further evidence clarifies ownership.
The ruling is firm: no division is permitted.

Division When Only One Heir Is Present
Ruling on division when only one heir is present and seeks division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a single heir demands division and identify the two reasons (evidentiary and procedural) that prevent division.

Core Idea
A single heir cannot demand division because a claim requires both a plaintiff and a defendant.

In this lesson, we examine a procedural rule in inheritance distribution: if only one heir appears before the judge and demands division, the judge must refuse. The passage provides two rationales rooted in evidentiary law and the structure of litigation. Understanding this principle clarifies how Hanafi fiqh protects the rights of absent heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Note the factual setup: there are three heirs, but only one appears before the judge and demands division of the estate.
The other two are absent or unrepresented.
2
State the ruling
The judge must not divide the property. The text says: 'If one heir is present, do not divide.'
This is a procedural bar, not a substantive denial of the heir's right.

The single heir is compared to a single witness. In Hanafi law, one witness is insufficient to establish a claim (except in certain cases). Hence, his demand alone does not prove his right to division.

A valid lawsuit requires both a plaintiff (muddai) and a defendant (muddai alayh). With only one heir present, he can at most be a plaintiff, but there is no defendant. The judge cannot render a judgment against an absent person (qada' al-gha'ib).
If two heirs were present, one could be plaintiff and the other defendant (representing the estate).
5
Understand the consequence
Because there is no defendant, neither a decision nor division can occur. The judge must wait until all heirs are present or a legal representative is appointed for the absent.
This protects the rights of absent heirs and prevents unjust rulings.

Ruling on Pathway and Water Channel in Partition
Ruling with proof: partition with unspecified easement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on pre-existing pathways and water channels in a partition, distinguishing when diversion is required versus when the partition must be rescinded.

Core Idea
A pre-existing right of way or water channel not mentioned in a partition must be diverted; if impossible, the partition is void.

When co-owners divide jointly owned property, they may face existing easements like pathways or water channels crossing one share. The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: such rights are not automatically transferred unless stipulated. This lesson examines the legal consequences and the proofs from Quran and Sunnah that ensure justice and prevent harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the ruling
The subject is a partition (qismah) where a pathway (tariq) or water channel (masil) exists through one partner's share and was not mentioned in the partition agreement. The ruling: if diverting the pathway/channel to the beneficiary's own share is possible, the beneficiary must do so; if diversion is impossible, the partition is rescinded (faskh).
The ruling applies to both pathways and water channels.
2
Examine the condition of possibility
Determine what constitutes 'possible' diversion. It refers to physical feasibility without causing disproportionate hardship. If diversion is possible, the beneficiary must do it; failure to divert when possible would render the partition invalid due to ongoing harm.
The text assumes the beneficiary's current use causes harm to the other partner.
3
Analyze the proofs: Quranic verse and Hadith
The author cites Quran 6:136 which condemns the polytheists' unfair allocation of shares, implying that any division causing injustice is prohibited. The hadith 'la darar wa la dirar' (no harm and no reciprocation of harm) establishes the principle that causing harm invalidates the arrangement.
The hadith is a foundational legal maxim (qa'ida) in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Determine the consequence when diversion is impossible
If diversion is not possible, the harm caused by the continued use of the pathway or channel through the partner's share is unavoidable. The partition is therefore rescinded (faskh) because it perpetuates harm, violating the hadith principle.
Faskh means the contract of partition is declared void ab initio in effect.

Valuation of Lower and Upper Floors in Partition
Intra-Hanafi dispute on partition method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding the partition of multi-story buildings and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) in applying either method.

Core Idea
When utility differs, valuation (taqwim) overrides equal area measurement to ensure equitable partition.

When two partners own a building with a lower and an upper floor, how should they divide it? Is it enough to split the total area equally, or must each floor be valued separately? This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf on this question. The source passage from Al-Sharh al-Samiri presents the two positions and their underlying reasoning, helping you understand how differences in utility affect legal rulings in Hanafi partnership law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal problem
Understand that the issue is about the method of partitioning a shared building where floors differ in utility and value. This is a case of 'qisma' (partition) in Hanafi fiqh.
Partition can be by area (tawzi' bi al-misaha) or by value (tawzi' bi al-taqwim).
2
State the position of each Imam
Abu Hanifa: value each floor separately and divide by value. Abu Yusuf: both floors are houses of the same type, so equal area suffices.
Muhammad al-Shaybani's view is not mentioned; this is a two-way dispute.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil) behind each position
Abu Hanifa focuses on utility ('intifa') – the lower floor has greater utility and thus higher value. Abu Yusuf focuses on type (jins) – both are residential spaces, so they are legally equivalent.
The 'illa (effective cause) for Abu Hanifa is difference in utility; for Abu Yusuf, similarity in type.
4
Locate the evidence cited
The text cites a hadith about freeing a share of a slave to support valuation in general. Abu Yusuf uses analogy (qiyas) that since both are houses, they are like identical plots of land.
The hadith shows that when ownership shares differ, valuation is needed. Abu Yusuf argues that the floors are of the same kind, so area comparison is sufficient.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practically, under Abu Hanifa, a partner receiving 10 square yards of lower floor would receive more value than a partner receiving 10 square yards of upper floor. Under Abu Yusuf, equal area is equal in law.
This has real consequences in court rulings and inheritance partitions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Equal area does not guarantee justice; value must compensate for utility differences.

When two partners jointly own a building with an upper room and a lower house, can they simply split the area equally? The Hanafi school teaches that mere area equality is insufficient if the parts differ in value. This lesson examines the proof from hadith and the intra-school disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf on this issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
Recognize that the upper room (balakhana) and lower house are not equal in utility: the upper room lacks direct access to the street, cannot be used as a shop, and its floor is lost if the wall collapses. Therefore, equal area does not mean equal value.
The text describes these differences to show that value, not just area, matters.

The hadith about freeing a share of a slave is presented as proof that valuation (taqwim) is a legitimate method to equalize shares in joint ownership. The same principle is applied by qiyas to division of real estate.

3
Explain Abu Hanifa's position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the value difference must be compensated by adjusting the area: the partner receiving the lower-value upper room should get a larger area to equalize overall value.
He considers both external and internal qualities (bawatin al-khair).
4
Explain Abu Yusuf's position
Imam Abu Yusuf argues that since both are houses (of the same type), equality in area is sufficient; the difference in quality does not affect the division because both serve as residences.
He considers apparent equality (tasarruf zahiri) enough.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Imam Abu Hanifa: valuation is required. The hadith supports this, and the internal qualities are relevant. The text explicitly says 'division by value, and no consideration otherwise.'
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah and the text here prefer Abu Hanifa's view.

Disputed Delivery of Share to Specified Location
Ruling on mutual oaths and rescission in partition disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a partner claims his share was not delivered to the agreed location, and identify the condition that triggers tahaluf and rescission of the partition.

Core Idea
Non-delivery to an agreed place, without acknowledgment of receipt, triggers mutual oaths and rescission.

When partners divide property (qismah), they may agree on a specific location for delivery of each share. If after partition one partner claims the share was not delivered to that place and he has not acknowledged receipt, while the other denies, a legal impasse arises. This lesson examines the Hanafi solution: both parties take oaths (tahāluf) and the partition is rescinded, allowing a fresh division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claims
One partner (claimant) asserts that the share should have been delivered to a specific place (mawḍi‘) but was not, and does not acknowledge receiving it. The other partner (defendant) denies the claim or asserts delivery.
Both parties are simultaneously claimants and defendants regarding the delivery obligation.
2
Check for acknowledgment
The key condition is whether the claimant acknowledged receipt of the share. If he did not, his statement is not contradictory; he is effectively rejecting the partition from the start.
If he had acknowledged receipt, the ruling would differ (partition stands).
3
Determine evidentiary status
Neither party has bayyinah (clear evidence). Thus, the dispute cannot be resolved by standard proof.
Hanafi jurists apply the rule of tahāluf when each party is both claimant and defendant.
4
Apply tahaluf
Both parties take oaths. Since they are in a mutual claim regarding delivery, the oaths are administered to each.
The result: the partition is rescinded (فسخت القسمة) and the property is re-divided.
5
Extract the underlying principle
The ruling is based on the condition that the claimant's statement shows no contradiction (tanaquḍ) and he performed no act indicating acceptance of the partition.
Without these, the original division is considered not final.

A third-party claim on a specific item in a share does not rescind the entire partition; compensation through recourse suffices.

Under Abu Hanifa, the original partition stands and only compensation is exchanged; under Abu Yusuf, the parties must redivide the entire property.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the limits of coercion in consuming haram items. In Hanafi fiqh, normally, eating carrion or drinking wine is absolutely haram. However, under coercion (ikrah), it becomes permissible if the coercion involves a credible threat to one's life or a limb (such as threat of death or severe injury). The commentary clarifies that if the threat is only imprisonment or light beating, one should endure it and not consume haram. Only when there is genuine fear for life or limb is it permissible to consume carrion or wine.

Ruling on Making Peace with the Enemy
Imam may make peace if beneficial

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on peace with the enemy and identify the condition of communal benefit (maslahah) that determines its permissibility.

Core Idea
Peace with the enemy is permissible when it serves the Muslims' benefit; otherwise it is not.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on entering peace treaties with non-Muslims at war. The text from al-Sharh al-Sameri states that the imam may make peace if there is benefit (maslahah) for the Muslim community. We will study the Quranic and Prophetic proofs that establish this ruling, and clarify the condition that transforms peace from a mere option into a necessity when benefit is clear.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the imam making peace (sulh) with the people of war (ahl al-harb) or a party among them. The ruling is that it is permissible (mubah) if there is benefit, and not permissible if there is no benefit.
The ruling is conditioned on maslahah.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites two proofs: Quran 8:61 (and if they incline to peace, then incline to it) and the Sunnah of the Prophet at Hudaybiyyah, including the ten-year truce.
Both are textual proofs (dalil naqli).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Quranic imperative 'fajnah' indicates permissibility, not obligation, because it is conditioned on benefit. The Prophet's action at Hudaybiyyah demonstrates that benefit was assessed before agreeing to peace.
The Hanafi usul principle: al-amr yufidu al-idhn (command indicates permission) when accompanied by a condition.
4
Note the condition and its implications
The condition is benefit for the Muslims (maslahah). If benefit is present, peace is mubah; if absent, peace is not permissible. The imam must evaluate benefit based on his authority.
This is a matter of ijtihad for the ruler.
5
Evaluate cross-madhhab difference
Some schools restrict peace to situations of necessity (e.g., when Muslims are weak), but Hanafis allow it as long as there is benefit, even if the Muslims are strong.
The Hanafi position gives broader scope to the imam's authority.

Breaking a Peace Treaty: Notification Before Attack
Ruling on breaking a peace treaty with notification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on breaking a peace treaty and explain the requirement of notification (nabdh al-'ahd) based on Quran and Sunnah.

Core Idea
A peace treaty may be broken if beneficial, but only after notifying the enemy — otherwise it is treachery (ghadr).

Dealing with enemies under a peace treaty requires careful adherence to Islamic law. The Hanafi school teaches that while it may be beneficial to break the treaty, you must first inform the enemy. This lesson explains the ruling, the evidence from the Quran and hadith, and why notification is essential to avoid treachery.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its condition
First, identify the permissibility of breaking the treaty when beneficial, and note the condition: notification must precede any attack.
The condition is not optional — it is legally binding.
2
Examine the Quranic proof
Read Surah al-Anfal 8:58 and see how the command 'fainbadh ilayhim' (throw back to them) implies open notification.
The term 'nabdh' literally means to throw, indicating public renunciation.
3
Analyze the hadith evidence
Study the hadith about Mu'awiyah and 'Amr ibn 'Abasah. The Prophet ﷺ said: do not break a treaty until its term expires or you 'throw it back to them on equal terms'.
This hadith reinforces the notification requirement even if the treaty has not expired.
4
Distinguish permissible termination from treachery
Understand that attacking without notification is ghadr (treachery), which is haram. Notification turns the act into a legitimate termination.
The Quran says Allah does not love the treacherous.
