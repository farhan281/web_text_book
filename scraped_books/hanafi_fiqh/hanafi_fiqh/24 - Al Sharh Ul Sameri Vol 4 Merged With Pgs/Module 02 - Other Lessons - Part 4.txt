What Constitutes Entering a House in Oaths
Ruling on what constitutes entry for oath purposes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which parts of a house are considered part of it for the purpose of an oath not to enter, and apply the rule to roof, hallway, and arch.

Core Idea
The roof and hallway are part of the house; an arch outside when door closes is not.

In oaths not to enter a house, the precise definition of 'entry' matters. This lesson clarifies which structures count as part of the dwelling based on physical boundaries and a qiyas (analogy) with i'tikaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the oath
The person swore not to enter the house; now he performs an action that may be considered entry. Determine if the action involves a part of the house.
The oath is specific to 'entering' this house.
2
Classify the location
Is the location roof, hallway, or arch? Determine based on structural inclusion. The roof and hallway are structurally part of the house. The arch may or may not be part of the house depending on its position relative to the door.
The key test: when the door is closed, is the arch inside or outside?
3
Apply the rule for roof and hallway
If the person stands on the roof or enters the hallway, he breaks his oath because these are considered part of the house. The ruling is supported by analogy with i'tikaf: roof of mosque is part of mosque.
The roof is included even though it is not enclosed.
4
Apply the exception for the arch
If the arch of the door is such that when the door is closed, the arch is outside, then standing in that arch does not break the oath. The arch is not part of the house.
The arch is only considered part of the house if it is inside the door when closed.

This ruling hinges on the physical definition of the house: areas that are structurally within the boundary of the house count as part of it, while those that become outside when the door is closed do not.

Oath Not to Eat 'Roasted': Scope by Custom
Ruling that 'roasted' in oaths refers only to roasted meat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that oath terms are interpreted according to common custom ('urf) to determine whether a specific act breaks the oath.

Core Idea
In oaths, words are interpreted by common custom, not literal generality.

When someone swears not to eat 'roasted', does that include roasted vegetables? Common sense might say yes, but the Hanafi school looks at how the term is actually used. This passage from al-Sameri shows that custom restricts 'al-shiwa' to roasted meat. Understanding this principle is crucial for correctly applying oath rulings in daily life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath term
The person swears not to eat 'al-shiwa' (roasted). First, determine the precise term used in the oath.
The term is al-shiwa, not a general word for roasted food.
2
Determine the default interpretation
Apply the Hanafi principle that oath terms are interpreted according to the customary usage (urf) of the people, unless the swearer had a specific intention. Here, in Arab custom, al-shiwa means roasted meat.
Custom ('urf) is a recognized source in Hanafi fiqh for interpreting ambiguous terms.
3
Apply to the specific food eaten
The person eats roasted eggplant or carrots. Since custom does not include these under al-shiwa, the oath is not broken.
The ruling would differ if the person intended all roasted foods, but the default is custom.
4
Consider exceptions
If the swearer explicitly intended a broader meaning (e.g., 'anything roasted'), his intention would override custom. The ruling here is for cases where no specific intention is known.
This aligns with the general rule: al-umur bi-maqasidiha (matters are judged by intentions).

Ma'dhun's Oath: Custom vs Ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the ma'dhun's oath and explain why custom ('urf) overrides technical ownership in oath interpretation.

Core Idea
Oaths follow common usage, not legal technicalities.

This passage presents a nuanced ruling within the Hanafi school: a slave permitted to trade (ma'dhun) swears not to ride his master's mount, then rides the very mount he uses for trade. The disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions hinges on whether oaths are interpreted by literal ownership or by custom. We will dissect their arguments and identify the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling from Abu Hanifa: the ma'dhun does not break his oath. Note the key principle: oaths are interpreted by common usage ('urf).
Abu Hanifa says: people call it the slave's mount, not the master's.
2
Identify the opposing view and its basis
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: the slave and his property belong to the master, so the mount is the master's, and riding it breaks the oath.
Their argument rests on legal ownership, a literal interpretation.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa implicitly rebuts by asserting that oath intention is governed by what people commonly understand, not by legal technicalities.
This is a standard Hanafi usul principle: al-yaminu 'ala al-'urf.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference is whether the slave incurs kaffarah (expiation) for breaking the oath or not. Under Abu Hanifa, no kaffarah; under the Sahibayn, he would be liable.
This affects the slave's financial responsibility.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Imam Abu Hanifa, as seen in the commentary and later fatwa works.
Custom prevails over technical ownership in oath cases.

Oath Not to Enter: Roof, Vestibule, and Door Arch
Ruling on roof, vestibule, and arch concerning oath of entry

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The roof and vestibule are part of the house; an arch that stays outside when the door closes is not.

An oath not to enter a house raises the question: what exactly counts as 'entering'? Is standing on the roof or stepping into the porch enough? This passage from al-Sharh al-Samari clarifies the Hanafi position: the roof and vestibule are integral parts of the house, but an arch that lies outside when the door closes is not. The ruling is supported by custom and analogy with the mosque roof in i'tikaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its consequence
The passage defines which actions break an oath (cause hanth) and which do not. Focus on the three specific locations: roof (sath), vestibule (dehliz), and arch (taq al-bab).
2
Understand the classification of roof and vestibule
Both are considered part of the house. The roof is compared to the mosque roof in i'tikaf—climbing onto it does not nullify i'tikaf because the roof is part of the mosque. Similarly, the vestibule is an integral entrance area.
Custom ('urf) plays a key role: in the local dialect the vestibule is called 'asara and is considered part of the room.
3
Analyze the arch exception
An arch that, when the door is closed, lies outside the house does not count as entering. The key is the door's position relative to the arch. If closing the door leaves the arch exterior, standing there does not break the oath.
This ruling depends on the construction custom of the house.
4
Apply to similar cases
Use the same principle of customary integration to evaluate other attached structures (e.g., balconies, verandas). The Hanafi school relies on 'urf (custom) to define the boundaries of a house for oath purposes.

ومن حلف لايقبض دينه درهما دون درهم فقبض بعضه لم يحنث حتى يقبض جمیعه متفرقا [۲۷۲۷] (۸۴) کسی نے قسم کھائی کہ اپنا قرض ایک ایک درہم کر کے نہیں لے گا پھر قبضہ کیا بعض پر تو نہیں حانث ہوگا یہاں تک کہ قبضہ کرے تمام کو تھوڑا تھوڑا کر کے۔ تشریح قسم کھائی کہ تھوڑا تھوڑا کر کے قرض وصول نہیں کرے گا، پھر تھوڑے سے قرض پر قبضہ کیا تو ابھی حانث ہونے کا حکم نہیں لگائیں گے جب تک کہ سارے قرضوں کو تھوڑا تھوڑا کر کے وصول نہ کرے۔ جب سارے قرضوں کو تھوڑا تھوڑا ہی کر کے وصول کر لیا تب اس وقت حانث ہونے کا حکم لگائیں گے۔ یہ ہو سکتا ہے کہ ابھی جو تھوڑا سا لیا ہے وہ مقروض کو واپس کر دے اور پھر تمام قرضوں کو بیک وقت واپس لے۔ اس لئے ابھی حانث ہونے کا فیصلہ نہیں کریں گے۔ ہاں باقی قرضوں کو تھوڑا تھوڑا ہی لیا تو تمام قرضوں کو تھوڑا تھوڑا لینے کے بعد حانث ہونے کا فیصلہ کریں گے۔
And whoever swears not to collect his debt dirham by dirham, and then collects some of it, he does not break the oath until he collects all of it piecemeal. [2727] (84) Someone swore that he would not take his debt dirham by dirham, then he received part of it: he is not considered to have broken the oath until he receives all of it piecemeal. Explanation: He swore that he would not collect the debt little by little; then he took a small portion; the ruling of breaking the oath is not applied yet, until he collects all the debts little by little. When he collects all the debts little by little, then the ruling of breaking the oath is applied. It is possible that he might return what he has taken and then collect all at once. So the decision is postponed.

Commentary
This passage explains a ruling from the Book of Oaths. If a person swears that he will not collect his debt in installments (dirham by dirham), and then he takes only a part of the debt, he does not break the oath immediately. The reasoning is that he might return that partial payment and collect the entire debt in one lump sum. The oath is only broken once he has collected the entire debt piecemeal. The Hanafi position is that the oath is considered broken only when the action prohibited by the oath is completed. Here, the prohibited action is receiving the debt in installments; receiving a single installment does not complete the action. Therefore, the ruling is deferred until the entire debt is received in that manner.

Key Terms
حَنِثَ (hanitha): To break one's oath; to violate a solemn promise.
قَبْضَ (qabada): To take possession or receive payment.
