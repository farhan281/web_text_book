Marking the Quran with Verse Markers and Dots: From Makruh to Mustahsan
Ruling with evidence on verse markers and dots

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the original Hanafi ruling on ta'shir and nuqat in the Quran, explain the reason for its change, and apply the principle of changing fatwa due to changing circumstances.

Core Idea
A ruling can change when the need arises: originally makruh, now mustahsan for non-Arab Muslims.

You may have seen Quran copies with verse markers like '10' or '20' and diacritical dots. Were these always part of the Mushaf? This lesson explores how Islamic law addresses innovations in the physical text of the Quran, balancing preservation of tradition with practical needs. We will trace the Hanafi position from its original disapproval to its current recommendation, supported by athar from the Salaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its original ruling
The passage deals with two acts: placing ten-verse markers (ta'shir) and adding diacritical dots (nuqat). What was the original ruling mentioned?
Original ruling: makruh.
2
Locate the evidence (dalil)
The author cites three athar from the Salaf: Ibn Mas'ud, Muhammad (ibn Sirin), and 'Ata. What do they indicate?
They all disliked these additions, showing disapproval.
3
Understand the rationale behind the original ruling
Why was it makruh? Because early Arabs knew Arabic intuitively and did not need such aids. Adding them was seen as an unnecessary change to the Quran's pristine form.
The Quran was kept as it was during the Prophet's time.
4
Identify the change and its cause
Later, for non-Arab (ajami) convenience, these markers became necessary. The ruling changed from makruh to mustahsan (recommended).
This shift is based on istihsan (juristic preference) and the principle of changing fatwa with changing conditions.
5
Apply the current mufta bihi position
Today, it is considered mustahsan to add these aids in Quran copies, especially for learners. This is the operative fatwa.
This showcases flexibility within the Hanafi school.

Embellishing the Quran and Mosque with Gold: Ruling and Proofs
Ruling on decorating the Quran and mosque with gold water

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on decorating the Quran and mosque with gold water, identify the proofs used, and distinguish between moderate and excessive decoration.

Core Idea
Moderate embellishment of the Quran and mosque is permissible; excessive is makruh.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on using gold water to decorate the Quran and mosque. The author presents evidence from the practice of Caliph Uthman and the statement of Muhammad ibn Sirin, balanced against prophetic warnings against excess. By the end, you will understand the nuanced ruling and the evidence behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is embellishing the Quran and mosque with gold water. The initial ruling is 'la ba'sa' (no harm, i.e., permissible).
The ruling is not absolute; it is conditioned by moderation.
2
Locate the Dalil
Two proofs are cited: the practice of Uthman (carved stones in the mosque) and the statement of Ibn Sirin permitting Quran embellishment.
These are qawl sahabi and practice of the Salaf.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
Uthman's action shows that decorated building materials are permissible. Ibn Sirin's explicit permission for the Quran establishes the asl. Qiyas extends this to mosque decoration, since both are acts of veneration.
The illa is respect for the symbols of Allah.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
The author balances permission with warnings against excess (hadith on raising mosques high and Abu Dharr's warning). This creates a boundary: moderate is mubah, excessive is makruh.
Some schools (e.g., Maliki) discourage all decoration; Hanafis permit within limits.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi Tarjih
The mufta bihi is moderate decoration is permissible. Excessive decoration is makruh tahrimi due to the prophetic prohibition.
This balances respect with avoiding extravagance.

Ruling on Ta'shir and Nuqt in the Mushaf
States ruling on ta'shir and nuqt with supporting athars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ta'shir and nuqt in the Mushaf and identify the evidence that supports it.

Core Idea
Marking ten-verse sections and dotting letters in the Mushaf is disliked (makruh) as a later innovation.

The Mushaf is the physical book of the Qur'an, and Muslims have differed over practices that were not part of the original revelation. This lesson examines two such practices: marking every ten verses (ta'shir) and putting dots on letters (nuqt). The Hanafi school, drawing on early authorities, considers these actions disliked. We will explore the evidence and the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, note that the text explicitly states that ta'shir and nuqt are disliked (makruh). Understand that this is a negative recommendation, not a prohibition.
Ta'shir refers to marking 'ashr (ten) verses; nuqt refers to placing dots on ambiguous letters.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites athars from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, specifically reports from 'Ata' and others who considered these practices disliked. The evidence is from the practice of the Salaf (early generations).
The athars show that early scholars avoided these innovations.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The reasoning is that these markings were not present in the Prophet's era or the era of the Companions; they are later innovations (bida'). Since the Salaf did not do them, engaging in them is disliked.
The Hanafi principle is that anything new in worship that was not done by the Salaf is considered bid'a and thus makruh unless there is evidence of permissibility.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The text mentions that 'Ata' was stricter, but the Hanafi position is clear. Some later scholars may allow nuqt for clarity, but the relied-upon view remains karahah.
The primary ikhtilaf is between those who consider it permissible (for practical benefit) and those who consider it disliked.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The operative ruling in the Hanafi school is that ta'shir and nuqt are makruh tanzihi (disliked, not sinful). The evidence from the early generations outweighs modern convenience.
The authority_weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin, but the ruling is grounded in the practice of the Salaf.

Accepting the Word of a Slave or Child in Gifts and Permissions
Ruling with proof from hadith and athar on accepting testimony of slaves and children

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting the statement of a slave or child in matters of gift and permission, and distinguish it from formal testimony (shahadah).

Core Idea
A slave or child's word is accepted in gifts and permission because it is mere information, not formal testimony.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies a nuanced ruling: while the testimony of slaves and children is not accepted in formal legal matters that establish rights, their word is accepted in informal contexts like gifts and permission. This is because such statements are merely conveying a message, not giving court testimony. The ruling is supported by hadith and athar from the Salaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The matn states 'ويجوز' indicating permissibility (jawaz). Identify the act: accepting the word (qawl) of a slave (abd) and a child (sabi) in the context of gift (hadiyah) and permission (idhn).
The ruling is that it is permissible (mubah), not obligatory or recommended.
2
Locate the dalil
The commentator cites two proofs: (1) An athar from Anas ibn Malik relating to the testimony of a slave, and (2) the hadith of the slave woman regarding breastfeeding from Sahih al-Bukhari.
Both are ahad hadith, thus zanni al-thubut, sufficient for permissibility.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
In the hadith, the Prophet (saw) accepted the statement of a black slave woman that she had breastfed the man and his wife, which carries a legal consequence (marriage prohibition). This shows that her word is accepted in matters affecting rights, so a fortiori it is accepted in less significant matters like gifts.
The acceptance here is not formal shahadah but simply believing her news.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The passage mentions that in formal shahadah (testimony that establishes a legal right), the testimony of a slave or child is not accepted. Some scholars may restrict acceptance entirely, but Hanafis distinguish based on the nature of the information.
This distinction is key to understanding the Hanafi position.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The commentator presents the Hanafi view as the correct one, supported by the athar of Anas and the hadith. The mufta bihi position is that acceptance is permissible in such contexts.
The authority weight is zahir riwayah from the matn.

Ruling on Looking at a Non-Mahram Woman: Face and Hands
Ruling on permissible extent of looking at a non-mahram woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on looking at a non-mahram woman's face and hands, and identify the condition that changes the ruling to prohibition.

Core Idea
A man may look at a non-mahram woman's face and hands, but if he fears desire, it becomes haram.

This lesson addresses a common daily scenario: a man encountering a woman who is not a close relative. The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling that balances social necessity with modesty. We will examine the extent of permissible looking and the critical condition of desire.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is a man looking at a non-mahram woman. The text states it is not permissible except at her face and hands. This means the default ruling is prohibition for other body parts, but mubah for face and hands.
Note the exception: face and hands are exempt because they are needed for daily transactions and social interaction.
2
Apply the condition of desire
The text adds a condition: if the man is not secure from desire (shahwah), then even looking at the face becomes prohibited unless there is a necessity. This shows the ruling is contingent on the state of the looker.
The same applies to the hands if they are a source of attraction.
3
Examine the primary proof from the Quran
The author cites Quran 24:30-31. Men are commanded to lower their gaze, and women not to display adornment except 'what is apparent' (ma dhahara). The Hanafi interpretation, via Ibn 'Abbas, limits 'what is apparent' to the face and hands.
This interpretation is crucial: it defines the boundary between permissible and forbidden looking.
4
Confirm with the Prophetic hadith
The hadith of Asma bint Abi Bakr shows the Prophet turning away from her thin clothing and indicating that after puberty, only the face and hands may be seen. This supports the Quranic interpretation.
The hadith adds the element of maturity: the ruling applies to women past puberty.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh al-Sameri deals with the rulings on looking at and touching mahram women. It begins by clarifying the parts of a mahram woman's body that may be shown to her male mahram relatives, based on Qur'an 24:31. The author explains that the 'places of adornment' (maa yazhar min al-zinah) are those where jewelry is worn: the nose and ears (which include the face/head), neck (including collarbone), shins, and forearms. These parts may be uncovered and looked at. The back, belly, thigh, and chest (around the breast) are not places of adornment, so they may not be shown based on the verse. Additionally, these parts inherently arouse desire, so looking at them is prohibited for that reason as well. The author provides a practical reason: mahram women work alongside men, and strict covering of head, arms, and shins would cause hardship, so Shariah does not require covering those parts. An athar from Hasan ibn Ali and Husayn ibn Ali entering upon their sister Umm Kulthum while she was combing her hair supports that the head may be seen; by analogy, the shins and arms are also permissible to view. The passage then discusses touching: it is permissible to touch those parts that may be looked at, provided there is no desire. Evidence includes a hadith about turning a slave girl when purchasing, which implies touching, and an athar about Talaq applying oil to his mother's hair. However, if desire is present, even mahram women may not be touched, as per the hadith that all limbs commit zina in a sense when used with desire.

Key Terms
ذات رحم محرم (dhati rahim mahram): A female relative related by blood who is permanently forbidden for marriage (e.g., mother, sister, daughter, aunt, etc.).
شہوت (shahwat): Desire or lust; a controlling condition for permissibility of touching.
عورت ('awrat): The bodily parts that must be covered; in this context, the 'awrah of a woman relative to mahram is different from non-mahram.

Eunuch's Ruling in Looking at Foreign Women
Ruling with proof for eunuch's prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a eunuch looking at a non-mahram woman and identify the proof (athar) used.

Core Idea
A eunuch is legally a full man — castration does not change the prohibition of looking at foreign women.

The passage addresses a specific legal question: does a eunuch (a castrated male) have the same restrictions as a full man regarding looking at non-mahram women? The Hanafi school answers clearly: the eunuch is like the full man. The prohibition remains. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ruling
The subject is the eunuch (khasi) regarding looking at a foreign woman (ajnabiyya). The ruling is that he is like a full man (fahl): haram.
The text explicitly states: 'والخصى فى النظر الى الجنبية كالفحل'
2
Understand the basis of the analogy
The eunuch is considered a full man in all legal rulings (inheritance, prayer, etc.). Therefore, the same prohibition applies to looking at non-mahram women.
The commentary notes: 'وراثت نماز اور دیگر احکام میں خصی آدمی مکمل مرد کی طرح ہے'
3
Locate the proof
The author cites an athar from Ibn Abbas: 'Castration of animals is disfigurement (muthla)' and recites Quran 4:119.
The athar is from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
If castration is a prohibited disfigurement, it does not change the legal status; the eunuch remains a man. Thus, the prohibition of looking continues.
The proof is zanni al-thubut and zanni al-dalala but sufficient for the ruling.
5
Cross-madhhab note
Some scholars might consider eunuchs less harmful, but the Hanafi school firmly equates them to full men. The mufta bihi is haram.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

And the eunuch regarding looking at a foreign woman is like the full man. [3129] (29) The eunuch in looking at a foreign woman is like the full man: just as it is haram for a complete man (not a eunuch) to look at the 'awrah of a foreign woman, so too for a man who has been made a eunuch, it is haram to look at the 'awrah of a foreign woman. In inheritance, prayer, and other rulings, a eunuch is like a complete man, so in looking at a foreign woman he is also like a man. (2) He was originally a man; later he was castrated, so he still has the ruling of a man. There is an athar: from Ibn Abbas: 'Castration of animals is disfigurement (muthla)', then he recited: 'and indeed they will command them to change the creation of Allah' (4:119). This athar indicates that a eunuch is a type of disfigurement, and the ruling of disfigurement is the same as the original man.
Footnote (alif): Ibn Abbas said: Castration of animals is disfigurement. Then he recited: 'Satan will surely command them to change Allah's creation.'

Commentary
This passage clarifies the legal status of a eunuch (خصی) regarding the prohibition of looking at non-mahram women. The ruling is that a eunuch is exactly like a full man (فحل, literally stallion) in this matter. The author explains that just as a full man cannot look at the 'awrah of a foreign woman, a eunuch also cannot. The reason is that in all other legal rulings (inheritance, prayer, etc.), a eunuch is considered a man. Nothing changes simply because he has been castrated. The author supports this with an athar from Ibn Abbas equating castration to disfigurement (muthla), which does not change gender rulings. Thus, the prohibition remains.

Key Terms
خصی (khasi): A eunuch, a man who has been castrated.
فحل (fahl): A full, uncastrated male; used here as a full man.
