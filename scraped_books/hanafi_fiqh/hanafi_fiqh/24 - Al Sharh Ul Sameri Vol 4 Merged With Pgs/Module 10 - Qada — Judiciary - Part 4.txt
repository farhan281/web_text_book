Exception for Judge and Witness: Looking at Face Permissible Despite Desire
Exception to rule of looking at non-mahram women for judicial purposes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exceptional ruling for a judge and witness to look at a non-mahram woman's face even if desire is feared, and identify the condition (necessity of duty) that justifies it.

Core Idea
Necessity of justice permits judge and witness to look at woman's face, overriding general prohibition against desirous glances.

This lesson examines an important exception in Islamic judicial procedure: the permission for a judge and witness to look at a non-mahram woman's face even if they fear desire. The general rule prohibits looking with desire, but the necessity of fulfilling judicial and testimonial duties overrides this prohibition. We will explore the ruling, its evidence, and the extent of its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its exceptional ruling
What exactly is being permitted? To whom and under what circumstances?
Focus on the phrase 'ويجوز للقاضي وللشاهد'
2
Locate the condition triggering the exception
The intention to judge or testify is the key condition. Without that intention, the general prohibition applies.
The text says 'اذا اراد ان يحكم عليها' (when he intends to rule on her)
3
Analyze the evidence (dalil)
The hadith of 'zina of the eye' proves that looking with desire is sinful. However, necessity (darurah) overrides this prohibition.
The principle 'al-darurah tubihu al-mahzurat' is applied here via istihsan.
4
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
Imam al-Shafi'i does not allow looking even for necessity if desire is feared, highlighting the Hanafi reliance on public interest.
The difference is thamarah al-khilaf: practical impact on court procedure.
5
Apply the ruling with conditions
Ensure the intention is purely for duty, not pleasure. The looking must be limited to identification.
If the intention shifts to pleasure, the permission is lost.

Commentary
This passage presents an exception to the general ruling on looking at a non-mahram woman. For a judge (qadi) who needs to identify the woman in order to pass a judgment concerning her, and for a witness who needs to identify the woman to give testimony regarding her, it is permissible to look at her face even if he fears desire (shahwah). The necessity of performing his judicial or testimonial duty overrides the potential for temptation, provided his intention is purely for the duty. The text then reiterates the general principle: a woman should not display except her face and palms, but in cases of severe need (such as walking, feet may be uncovered). It also relates the hadith about the zina of the eye (looking with desire) and the hadith of Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) regarding not following a glance with another. These prove that looking with desire is sinful, but when necessity is severe, it is allowed. The commentary emphasizes that the judge and witness must intend their official duty, not pleasure.

Key Terms
قاضي (qadi): A judge who passes rulings in Islamic court. He needs to see the woman to identify her for a judgment.
شاهد (shahid): A witness giving testimony in court. He must identify the woman he is testifying about.
ضرورة (darurah): Necessity that overrides a prohibition. Here, the need for proper justice is considered a strong necessity.
