Conditions for Halal Hunting with Trained Animals
States the ruling and evidence for halal hunting with trained animals upon pronouncing Bismillah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for halal hunting with trained animals and derive each from the Quranic verse and hadith.

Core Idea
Three conditions make hunting halal: trained animal, Bismillah at release, and killing by wounding.

This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling on game caught by trained dogs and birds of prey. Based on Quran 5:4, hadith, and athar, the school sets precise conditions. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly applying hunting rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon (hunting with trained dogs/birds) and its legal classification (mubah, with conditions).
The ruling is permissive but conditional; if conditions are not met, the game is not halal.
2
List the three conditions
Work through the passage to extract the three required conditions: (1) the animal must be trained (mu'allam), (2) Bismillah must be pronounced at release, and (3) the animal must kill by wounding (jarih).
Each condition is individually necessary and collectively sufficient.
3
Trace the dalil for each condition
For each condition, identify the proof from Quran (5:4), hadith (Abu Tha'laba, Adi ibn Hatim), and athar (Ibn Abbas, Hammad, etc.).
The word 'الجوارح' indicates wounding; 'أمسكن عليكم' indicates catching for the hunter; 'اذكروا اسم الله' indicates Bismillah.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider what happens if a condition is missing: if the animal eats the game, it is not trained; if no Bismillah, the game is carrion; if killed by strangling, it is waqid and not halal.
These cases are explicitly mentioned in the passage and evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It states that if a person releases a trained dog, baz, or saqr upon game and pronounces Bismillah at the time of release, and the animal catches and wounds the game causing its death, then eating that game is halal. The passage then explains the definition of a trained dog: it must not eat the game after catching it three times. A bird of prey is considered trained if it returns when called. Evidence is provided from hadith: Ibn Abbas said if a dog eats from the game it is not trained. Hammad said if a bird plucks feathers or eats, it is still halal because its training is defined by returning when called. The Quranic verse (Surah al-Ma'idah 5:4) is quoted: 'They ask you what is lawful for them. Say: Lawful for you are good things and what you have taught of hunting animals... So eat of what they catch for you and mention the name of Allah upon it.' This verse indicates three conditions: the animal must be trained, it must wound (jarih), and Bismillah must be pronounced. The hadith of Abu Tha'laba al-Khushani confirms this. The hadith of Adi ibn Hatim about the mi'rad (arrow without feather) is used analogically: if the arrow hits with its edge, it kills by wounding, so it is halal; if it hits with its side and kills by crushing, it is not. Similarly, if a hunting animal kills by wounding, it is halal; if it kills by strangling without wounding, it is not.

Key Terms
الجوارح (al-jawarih): Predatory animals and birds that hunt by clawing and wounding, such as dogs, falcons, and hawks.
معلم (mu'allam): Trained hunting animal that has been taught to refrain from eating the game and to return when called.
وَقِيد (waqid): An animal killed by a blow without wounding, which is carrion and not halal.
معراض (mi'rad): An arrow without feathers, used for hunting.

Ruling When the Hunting Animal Eats from the Game
States the ruling on consuming game after the hunting animal has eaten from it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on consuming game from which a hunting animal has eaten, distinguishing between dogs/cheetahs and birds of prey, and cite the evidence (hadith of Adi ibn Hatim and athar of Jabir and al-Sha'bi).

Core Idea
If a dog or cheetah eats from the game, it is haram; if a falcon eats, it is halal.

Hunting with trained animals is permitted in Islam, but what happens if the hunting animal eats from the game? The Hanafi school draws a clear distinction based on the type of animal. This lesson explains the ruling and the evidence from the Quran, Sunnah, and the practice of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the ruling for two categories: dogs/cheetahs versus birds of prey (baz). If a dog or cheetah eats from the game, it becomes haram. If a baz eats, it remains halal.
The Arabic text explicitly says 'فلا يؤكل' for dog/cheetah and 'اكل' for baz.
2
Understand the rationale
The ruling is based on the Quranic phrase 'مما أمسكن عليكم' (what they catch for you). If the animal eats, it caught for itself, not for the hunter.
The hadith of Adi ibn Hatim confirms this: 'do not eat, for it caught for itself.'
3
Cite the evidence for dogs/cheetahs
The primary evidence is the hadith of Adi ibn Hatim narrated in Bukhari and Muslim. The Prophet ﷺ explicitly forbade eating if the dog eats from the catch.
The hadith is ahad (single-narrator) but its meaning is clear (qati' al-dalalah).
4
Cite the evidence for birds of prey
For the baz, the evidence is the athar (report) from Jabir and al-Sha'bi: 'Eat from the baz's game even if it ate.' This shows a distinction in Hanafi fiqh.
The baz is considered different because its training is more reliable and it does not catch for itself when it eats.

Commentary
This passage presents a distinction between dogs/cheetahs and birds of prey. If a dog or cheetah eats from the game, the game becomes impermissible because the animal caught for itself, not for the hunter, based on the Quranic phrase 'مما أمسكن عليكم' and the hadith of Adi ibn Hatim. However, for the baz (falcon), if it eats from the game, it remains halal. This is evidenced by the athar of Jabir and al-Sha'bi. The footnote provides the complete text of Quran 5:4 and the hadith of Abu Tha'laba and Adi. The mufti's opinion is that one should eat the baz's game even if it has eaten from it.

Key Terms
كلب (kalb): A trained hunting dog; in this context, refers to any canine predator that hunts by clawing.

Ruling on Saying Bismillah During Slaughter
Establishes Hanafi ruling on tasmiyah with proofs and refutes Shafi'i and Maliki positions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tasmiyah during slaughter, distinguish between intentional and forgetful omission, and evaluate the Shafi'i and Maliki counter-positions using the Quranic and hadith proofs.

Core Idea
Intentional omission of Bismillah renders meat carrion; forgetfulness is excused — this middle position distinguishes Hanafis from both Shafi'i and Maliki.

The act of saying 'Bismillah' (tasmiyah) during slaughter is a wajib act in the Hanafi school. However, what happens if the slaughterer forgets? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that forgetfulness does not invalidate the meat, while intentional omission makes it carrion. We will explore the proofs from the Quran and hadith, and contrast the positions of Imam al-Shafi'i (who permits in both cases) and Imam Malik (who forbids in both).

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two scenarios: intentional omission renders the meat carrion (maytah) and impermissible; forgetful omission does not affect permissibility.
2
Identify the opponent's view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that meat is permissible in both cases for Muslims and Ahl al-Kitab; Imam Malik holds it is impermissible in both cases.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Shafi'i relies on a hadith (Ibn Abbas) that the Muslim's heart contains Allah's name. Maliki relies on the general import of Quran 6:121.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that specific hadiths (from Ibn Abbas and Aisha) qualify the general Quranic prohibition, limiting it to intentional omission. The Shafi'i proof is not strong enough to override these specific hadiths.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Shafi'i: meat halal always; Maliki: meat haram always; Hanafi: halal if forgetful, haram if intentional.

Place of Slaughter and Required Veins
Explains the correct location and veins for valid Islamic slaughter, with an intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the precise location for slaughter (between halq and labbah) and the four veins that must be cut, and differentiate between the views of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the minimum number of veins required.

Core Idea
For meat to be halal, the cut must sever most of the four veins — Abu Hanifa: three; Sahibayn: at least one of three.

This passage from al-Sharh al-Sameri details the precise anatomical requirements for Islamic slaughter. It specifies that the cut must be made between the throat (halq) and the collarbone (labbah) and that four specific veins must be severed to ensure blood flow, which is the key criterion. The text then records a subtle but important disagreement within the Hanafi school: while all agree that cutting all four makes the meat halal, Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples differ on the minimum number required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the location of slaughter
The text specifies the cut must be between the throat (halq) and the collarbone (labbah). This is supported by an athar from Ibn Abbas.
The labbah is the depression above the chest bone.
2
List the four veins to be cut
The four veins are: halqum (windpipe), mari' (esophagus), and the two wadajan (carotid arteries). Cutting them ensures blood flow.
These are the 'awdaj mentioned in the athar.
3
Explain the underlying principle
The primary objective is complete blood flow, as proven by the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij: 'Eat that which causes blood to flow, except teeth and nails.'
This hadith is the basis for the requirement to cut these specific veins.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa holds that cutting most (three of four) suffices. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) require at least one of the three (halqum, mari', wadajan) to be cut.
The text does not give a preference; both views are recorded.
5
Determine the practical difference
If only two veins are cut, Abu Hanifa's view would rule the meat haram (since three are needed), while the Sahibayn's view might permit it if one of the three specified veins is among them.
This thamarah al-khilaf has implications for halal certification.

Commentary

Conditions for Permissibility of Carrion Under Coercion
Ruling on when carrion or wine becomes permissible due to coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions that make carrion or wine permissible under coercion and apply them to distinguish valid from invalid coercion.

Core Idea
Only a threat to life or a limb permits eating carrion; lesser coercion does not override the prohibition.

The Hanafi school carefully balances the prohibition of carrion and wine with the necessity of preserving life. This lesson examines the precise conditions under which coercion lifts the prohibition. The key distinction is between threats that endanger life or limb (which permit) and those that merely inconvenience (which do not). The Quranic verses on idṭirār provide the foundation for this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited acts and the ruling
The passage addresses eating carrion and drinking wine. State that the general ruling is prohibition (ḥarām), but coercion can make it permissible (mubāḥ) under specific conditions.
The base ruling is found in the Quran.
2
List the types of coercion that are insufficient
The text mentions imprisonment, beating, and chains as examples of coercion that do NOT permit the prohibited acts. Understand that these are considered lesser harms.
The harm from consuming carrion is considered greater than these.
3
Identify the sufficient level of coercion
The sufficient level is a threat to life (death) or to a limb (e.g., amputation). Additionally, there must be a dominant suspicion (ẓann ghālib) that the threat will be carried out.
Ẓann ghālib means high probability, not certainty.
4
Derive the ruling from the Quranic proof
The passage cites Surah Al-Baqarah 2:173 and Surah Al-Ma'idah 5:3. The key word is 'idṭirār' (forced by necessity). Hanafi scholars interpret this as life-threatening necessity, which includes fear for life or limb.
The condition 'ghayra bāghin wa lā ʿādin' prohibits exceeding necessity.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a scenario of coercion, determine whether the threat is sufficient. For example: a threat of imprisonment does not permit, but a threat of beheading does. The person must consume the prohibited item and cannot choose to die.
Sacrificing life or limb is not permissible if eating the carrion would save it.

This is a ruling on when coercion makes eating carrion or drinking wine permissible. The Hanafi position is that only a threat to life or a limb (e.g., death, amputation) lifts the prohibition. The commentary explains that 'necessity' here is interpreted as fear of death or loss of a limb. The text also clarifies that once the threat reaches that level, the person must consume the prohibited item; it is not permissible to endure the threat and die or lose a limb, as that would be self-destruction which is forbidden (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:195).

Sin of Enduring Coercion Instead of Eating Carrion
Ruling that one who endures a death threat and does not eat is sinful

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a person coerced under threat of death or limb loss must eat carrion or drink wine, and that refusing and being killed or injured is sinful, citing the Quranic prohibition of self-destruction.

Core Idea
Enduring a death threat and refusing to eat carrion is sinful because self-destruction is forbidden.

In this lesson, we examine a crucial extension of the ruling on eating carrion under coercion. While it is well known that one may eat carrion when forced, the Hanafi school clarifies that in such a situation, one must actually eat—doing otherwise and allowing oneself to be killed or harmed is a sin. This ruling is based on the Quranic command to preserve life and not throw oneself into destruction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The scenario involves a person who is coerced by a threat of death or loss of a limb to eat carrion or drink wine. If he refuses and the threat is carried out, the ruling applies.
The coercion must be credible and immediate.
2
Determine the effect of coercion
Valid coercion transforms the originally prohibited carrion or wine into permissible items for the coerced person. The Quranic verse (2:173) establishes that there is no sin on one who eats under necessity.
The permissibility is limited to the extent needed to save life or limb.
3
Recognize the obligation to consume
Once the item becomes permissible, consumption becomes obligatory (wajib) to preserve oneself. Refraining while knowing the threat will be carried out leads to sin.
This is because the prohibition of self-destruction overrides the desire to avoid the prohibited substance.
4
Identify the sin of refusal
If the person endures and is killed or injured, he is sinful. The sin is not for eating haram but for causing his own destruction when a lawful alternative was available.
The text uses the term 'āthim' (sinful) to describe this person.
5
Cite the Quranic proof
The ruling is supported by Quran 2:195: 'And do not throw yourselves into destruction with your own hands.' This verse prohibits self-destruction, which applies here.
This is a direct proof from the text's commentary.

(6) And if he endures until they carry out [the threat] and he does not eat, then he is sinful. Commentary: If he endures and does not eat carrion or drink wine despite a death or limb threat, and the coercer kills him or cuts his limb, then he is sinful. Because the Quran says 'there is no sin' on the one who eats under necessity, meaning the item became permissible. Once permissible, not eating and causing one's death is forbidden by Quran 2:195: 'And do not throw yourselves into destruction.' Thus, one must eat; doing otherwise is sinful.

Commentary
This is an extension of the previous ruling. Once carrion or wine becomes permissible due to a valid threat (death or limb), the person must consume it. If he chooses to endure the threat and is killed or injured, he is sinful because he willingly caused his own destruction. The proof is from Surah Al-Baqarah 2:195, which forbids self-destruction. The commentary explains that the previous Quranic verse (2:173) established that there is no sin in eating under necessity; thus the item is essentially permissible. Refusing to eat when it is permissible and thereby causing death or harm is a sin.

Key Terms
آثم (āthim): Sinful; one who commits a sin by failing to do what is obligatory (here, eating when permissible)
