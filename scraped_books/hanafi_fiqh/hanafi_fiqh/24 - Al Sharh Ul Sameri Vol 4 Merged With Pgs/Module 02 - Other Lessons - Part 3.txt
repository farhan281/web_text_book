Kaffarah for an Oath: Freeing a Slave
States the ruling and condition for kaffarah by freeing a slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the type of slave acceptable for kaffarah of an oath and explain the Quranic proof and the condition of severe disability.

Core Idea
For kaffarah of an oath, any slave except one with severe disability suffices, regardless of religion, gender, or age.

This lesson examines the ruling on freeing a slave as one option for expiation (kaffarah) of a broken oath. The Hanafi school specifies that the slave must be free from severe disability; otherwise, even a non-Muslim, female, or young slave is valid. We will also review the primary evidence from Surah al-Ma'idah and the analogy to zihar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is freeing a slave as part of kaffarah for an oath. The ruling is that it is one of the wajib options (wajib takhyiri).
The kaffarah is wajib upon a deliberate oath, not fard, because the Quran offers choices.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is Quran 5:89, which explicitly mentions freeing a slave as an option for kaffarah.
The verse also mentions feeding or clothing ten poor, or fasting three days if unable.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'tahrir raqabah' indicates obligation. The specific conditions of the slave (e.g., not severely disabled) are derived from the analogy to zihar rules, as mentioned in the text.
The text says 'the same slave suffices as required in zihar' and refers to proofs passed in the Book of Zihar.

Statement of kaffarah) (2664) (21) The kaffarah for an oath is freeing a slave. The same slave suffices as required in zihar. In kaffarah of zihar, a Muslim slave, a disbeliever, male, female, young, old – all such slaves suffice. It is not necessary that the slave be a believer as in kaffarah of murder. Minimum Garment for Kaffarah: Clothing Ten Poor
Explains the minimum garment for kaffarah clothing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimum garment required for kaffarah of an oath when clothing ten poor, and identify the evidence from the Companion's practice.

Core Idea
A garment covering navel to knees suffices for kaffarah because it meets the prayer standard.

This lesson examines the second option for expiating an oath: clothing ten poor people. The key question is what constitutes a sufficient garment. The text sets the minimum by analogy to prayer requirements and provides a precedent from the Companion Abu Musa al-Ash'ari, demonstrating that a garment covering from waist to feet is adequate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Clothing ten poor is a wajib option for kaffarah of an oath. If chosen, it must satisfy specific conditions.
The other options are feeding ten poor or freeing a slave.
2
Determine the minimum garment
The minimum garment is that which makes prayer valid for a man: covering from navel to knees (the 'awrah in prayer).
This is the lower limit; more is permissible.
3
Examine the evidence
The text cites the action of Abu Musa al-Ash'ari, who after an oath gave each poor person a garment from 'maq'id hijr' (waist to feet), confirming the sufficiency of such a garment.
This is not a direct Prophetic command but a valid precedent (athar).
4
Note cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school requires a full garment covering the entire body; the Hanafi view is more lenient, considering the prayer minimum as sufficient.
Both are legitimate positions within their respective usul.

The Quantity of Food in Kaffarah of an Oath: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on kaffarah feeding quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the quantity of food per poor in kaffarah of an oath, explain each school's proof, and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
In kaffarah of an oath, the Hanafi school requires half a sa' of wheat per poor; the Shafi'i requires only one mudd — the difference hinges on interpretation of the same Prophetic reports.

This passage addresses a key inter-madhhab dispute in kaffarah of an oath: how much food must be given to each poor person when the option of feeding is chosen? The Hanafi school, following Imam Abu Hanifa, prescribes half a sa' of wheat (or equivalent in dates/barley), while Imam al-Shafi'i prescribes one mudd (one quarter of a sa'). The difference stems from divergent interpretations of hadith and athar, as well as varying views on the equivalence of different food types. This lesson breaks down the proofs and the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position and its proofs
Identify the exact quantity (half sa' wheat or one sa' dates/barley) and list the hadith and athar cited: the hadith of the Prophet giving one sa' dates per poor (equivalent to half sa' wheat), and reports from 'Ali and Ibn 'Abbas.
Note the equivalence of dates to wheat in the Prophet's time.
2
State the Shafi'i position and its proofs
Identify the Shafi'i quantity (one mudd wheat) and list their proofs: the hadith of Aus giving fifteen sa' barley to sixty poor (one mudd per poor) and an athar from Ibn 'Abbas stating one mudd per poor.
A sa' is 4 mudds, so 15 sa' = 60 mudds, thus 1 mudd per poor.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i proof
Explain the Hanafi rebuttal: the barley in Aus's hadith was not the main food but a charity, and the standard equivalence of dates to wheat makes the mudd inapplicable to wheat. Also, the athar of Ibn 'Abbas is interpreted differently.
The Hanafi saw that the Shafi'i evidence does not directly address wheat.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
State the practical consequence: Hanafi gives more food (half sa' wheat) per poor than Shafi'i (one mudd). Then confirm that the Hanafi position is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.
Mufta bihi: the ruling applied in Hanafi fatwa.

Core Idea
In the Hanafi school, kaffarah is caused by breaking an oath, not by swearing.

This lesson addresses a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: when must the expiation for an oath be given? The Hanafi school holds that the expiation is only valid if given after breaking the oath, while Imam al-Shafi'i considers expiation given after the oath but before the breach to be sufficient. We will examine the proofs from both sides and understand the underlying legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling and its basis
Imam Abu Hanifa rules that expiation (kaffarah) is not valid if given before breaking the oath (hanth). The cause (sabab) of kaffarah is hanth, not the swearing itself. Therefore, no obligation exists until the oath is broken.
The author emphasizes that any payment before hanth is merely charity (sadaqah), not kaffarah.
2
Analyze the Hanafi proof: Hadith of Abu Huraira
The Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam) said: 'Whoever swears an oath and then sees something better, let him do what is better and expiate his oath.' The command to expiate comes after the command to do the better act, implying hanth precedes kaffarah.
The order (tartib) is understood as sequential in the Hanafi usul.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position and evidence
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that kaffarah is valid if given after swearing but before hanth. His evidence is the hadith of Abu Musa: '...I do not swear an oath but then see something better except that I expiate my oath and do what is better.' This appears to show expiation occurring before the better act (i.e., before hanth).
Shafi'i considers the oath itself as the cause, and hanth as a condition for obligation.
4

The Hanafi school responds that the order in Abu Huraira's hadith is explicit and unambiguous. The hadith of Abu Musa may be interpreted as expiation after hanth, or referring to a different oath previously taken.
The Hanafi tarjih prefers the explicit tartib.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If someone gives kaffarah after swearing but before breaking the oath, the Hanafi requires it to be repeated after hanth; the Shafi'i considers it sufficient. This affects whether additional expiation is needed.
The difference has real implications for a person's obligation.

Core Idea
In oaths, words are interpreted by common usage, not literal meaning – eating 'from a tree' means its fruit.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi ruling on oaths. When someone swears not to eat from a specific palm tree, does eating its fruit break the oath? What about eating the wood? The answer lies in customary understanding (urf). The Hanafi school holds that common usage determines the intended meaning, so 'eat from a tree' refers to its fruit, not the tree itself. This ruling teaches us how urf resolves linguistic ambiguity in legal contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath wording
The person swears: 'I will not eat from this palm tree.' Note that the object is the tree, not the fruit explicitly.

Distinguish literal vs. customary meaning
Literally, 'eat from the tree' could mean consuming the tree's wood, leaves, or fruit. Customarily, people understand it as eating the fruit because trees are not eaten; only their fruit is consumed.
Hanafi usul al-fiqh recognizes urf (custom) as a valid interpretive tool.

Apply the Hanafi principle of urf
In ambiguous expressions in oaths, the word is taken according to the common usage of the people (urf). The speaker's intention is inferred from what is customary, not from the literal dictionary definition.

If he eats the dates (fruit) of that specific palm tree, he breaks his oath and must perform kaffarah (expiation). If he eats the tree's wood, leaves, or bark (even if edible in theory), the oath is not broken.
The ruling applies only to that tree – eating fruit from another palm tree does not break the oath.
5
Consider practical implications
The same principle applies to other trees and plants: e.g., swearing not to eat from an apple tree means apples, and from a grapevine means grapes. The urf of the locality should be consulted.
If the tree has no fruit at the time of the oath, the oath is suspended until fruit appears, by most Hanafi scholars.

The condition 'except with his permission' is interpreted as requiring actual awareness.

Abu Yusuf holds that the swearer does not break his oath because permission exists objectively; knowledge is not a condition.
The condition is satisfied by the mere granting of permission.

Abu Hanifa emphasizes the common understanding; Abu Yusuf focuses on the literal existence.

Riding a Slave's Animal: Does It Break an Oath Not to Ride the Master's?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath-breaking by riding a madhun slave's animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether riding a madhun slave's animal constitutes breaking an oath not to ride the master's animal, and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Property rights vs. common usage: the Sahibayn's view prevails for caution in oaths.

This lesson examines a nuanced case within the Hanafi school regarding oaths. When a person swears not to ride so-and-so's animal, and then rides the animal of that person's madhun (trading-permitted) slave, does the oath break? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples differ based on whether legal ownership or linguistic usage governs the reference of the oath. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi methodology in oath-related rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the oath and the scenario
Identify the exact wording of the oath and the action taken. The oath is not to ride Zaid's animal, but the person rides the animal of Zaid's madhun slave.
The madhun slave is permitted to trade and owns property in his own right to some extent.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position and basis
Abu Hanifa rules that the oath is not broken (no hanth). His basis is common usage ('urf): in everyday language, the animal of a madhun slave is attributed to the slave, not the master. Therefore, riding it is not riding the master's animal.
This relies on the principle that oaths are interpreted according to common linguistic understanding.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position and basis
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that the oath is broken (hanth). Their basis is legal ownership: the slave himself is the master's property, so everything the slave owns ultimately belongs to the master. Thus, riding the animal is effectively riding the master's animal.
This relies on the principle that legal ownership governs the attribution of property for oath purposes.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifa, no expiation is required; according to the Sahibayn, expiation (kaffarah) is necessary. This is the key difference in application.
Expiation for breaking a valid oath involves feeding or clothing ten poor people or freeing a slave.
5
State the mufta bihi and why
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of the Sahibayn. The author indicates this by stating that the fatwa is upon their view, because property rights take precedence over usage and the oath is interpreted broadly to prevent abuse.
This follows the principle of ihtiyat (caution) in oaths.

However, the Sahibayn (Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) argue that since the slave is the property of the master, the animal is ultimately the master's property. Thus, riding it is effectively riding the master's animal, and the oath is broken. The difference lies in whether legal ownership or common linguistic usage determines the reference of the oath. The mufta bihi position is that of the Sahibayn, because property rights outweigh usage, and the oath is interpreted broadly to prevent abuse.

Key Terms
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave who has been given permission by his master to engage in trade; such a slave owns property in his own right to some extent.
دابة (dabbah): A riding animal, such as a horse, mule, or donkey.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, the Sahibayn's view that riding a madhun slave's animal constitutes riding the master's animal is the relied-upon position for oaths, emphasizing property ownership over linguistic usage.
