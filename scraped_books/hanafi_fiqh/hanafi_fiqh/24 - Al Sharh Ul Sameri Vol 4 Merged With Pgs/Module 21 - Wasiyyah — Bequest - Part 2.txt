Distribution of Bequest to Relatives (Aqarib)
Distribution rule for bequest to relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of closeness precedence to distribute a bequest among multiple relatives when the term 'aqarib' is used.

Core Idea
Closer relatives take precedence over distant ones in bequests to 'aqarib'.

This passage resolves a specific bequest scenario: a testator leaves a portion of his estate to 'aqarib' (relatives), and the surviving relatives are one paternal uncle and two maternal uncles. The Hanafi school applies the rule that closer relatives take precedence, and the plural form 'aqarib' implies at least two recipients. The commentary also cites a hadith establishing the maternal uncle as an eligible relative. Understanding this ruling sharpens your ability to handle distribution when general terms like 'aqarib' are used.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest and the relatives
The bequest is made to 'aqarib' (relatives). The surviving relatives are one paternal uncle (closer) and two maternal uncles (farther). Determine the hierarchy based on blood relationship.
Paternal uncle is an agnatic relative (asabah), maternal uncle is a uterine relative (dhu rahm).
2
Apply the principle of closeness
Closer relatives take precedence. Since the paternal uncle is the closest, he takes half of the bequest. The remaining half goes to the next category (maternal uncles).
This principle is derived from istihsan and the general rules of inheritance.
3
Distribute among multiple relatives in the same category
The two maternal uncles are equally distant, so they share the remaining half equally (each gets one quarter).
If there were only one maternal uncle, he would take the entire remaining half.
4
Trace the proof: the hadith and the rule of plural
The commentary notes that 'aqarib' (plural) in bequests implies at least two recipients. A supporting hadith states 'the maternal uncle is the heir of one who has no heirs,' establishing maternal uncles as eligible relatives.
Dalil: Hadith (Daraqutni, Ibn Abi Shayba) – 'Al-khal warithu man la waritha lahu.'
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools may define 'aqarib differently or apply different rules of distribution, leading to a different allocation.
Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih; the difference stems from interpretive methods.

Sahibayn's Broad Definition of Relatives for Bequest
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Sahibayn's definition of relatives in a bequest and explain how it differs from Abu Hanifa's position.

Core Idea
Sahibayn extend bequests to all blood relatives traced to the first Muslim ancestor; Abu Hanifa limits to close kin.

In this lesson, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the scope of the term 'aqarib (relatives) in a bequest. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) adopt a broad interpretation based on the general plural form, while Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly restricts it. Understanding the linguistic principle of 'amm (generality) is key to grasping the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed term
The term 'aqarib (relatives) in a bequest is the focus. The testator said 'to my relatives' without qualifying near or far.
This is a common scenario in wills.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
Sahibayn hold that 'aqarib includes every blood relative who can be traced back to the first ancestor in the testator's lineage who embraced Islam.
This includes distant cousins, etc., as long as they share the earliest Muslim ancestor.
3
Identify the opposing view
Imam Abu Hanifa (implied) limits 'aqarib to close relatives who are mahram or inheritors, based on customary understanding.
This view is not explicitly stated in the passage but is the known intra-Hanafi difference.
4
Trace the Sahibayn's dalil
Their evidence is linguistic: the word 'aqarib is a general plural (amm) without restriction. Therefore, it retains its general application.
They rely on the usul principle: al-amm yufidu al-umum until a mukhassis is found.
5
Consider the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is that under Sahibayn, distant relatives receive a share; under Abu Hanifa, only close relatives do.
This affects distribution of the bequest.

Bequest of One-Third When Two-Thirds Perish: Mental Segregation
Ruling on bequest with partial destruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of mental segregation to determine the legatee's share when part of a bequeathed asset is destroyed.

Core Idea
When a specific one-third of a uniform asset is bequeathed, loss falls on heirs, not the legatee.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: a man bequeaths one-third of his dirhams or goats to someone, but two-thirds of those assets are destroyed before distribution. Who bears the loss? The Hanafi school, relying on the priority of bequests over inheritance, holds that the legatee's share is mentally separated from the moment of bequest; therefore, the destruction affects only the heirs' portion. This contrasts with the view of Imam Zufar, who argues for proportional loss. We will examine the three principles underlying the Hanafi ruling and its practical application.

Commentary
This passage deals with a scenario where a bequest of one-third of a specific property (dirhams or goats) is made, but two-thirds of that property are destroyed before the bequest is fulfilled. Commentary
This passage is a legal ruling from the Book of Wills, presented with its proof and an intra-Hanafi dispute. The ruling addresses what happens when a testator bequeaths one-third of his property (or specific items) and part of that property is destroyed after the bequest but before execution. The Hanafi position (of Imam Abu Hanifa) is that if the bequeathed items are of a single genus (e.g., all dirhams or all goats), the legatee's share is mentally separated and deemed to have been set aside from the moment of bequest. Therefore, subsequent destruction of the heirs' portion does not affect the legatee's full claim to the remaining one-third. The proof is derived from Quran 4:12, which establishes that bequests take precedence over heirs' rights, and from the principle that the legatee's right is fixed at the time of bequest. The text then contrasts this with a case where the bequeathed items are of different types (e.g., garments of various kinds). In that scenario, the items remain jointly owned between legatee and heirs until physical division, so any destruction is borne proportionally by both. The author then cites a historical precedent (athar) from 'Ata to reinforce the point about different types. Importantly, Imam Zufar disagrees with the first ruling: he argues that since no physical division occurred, the destruction should affect both parties proportionally even with a single genus. However, the relied-upon Hanafi position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa, treating the share as mentally separated. This distinction hinges on the concept of 'unity of genus' versus 'difference in genus' – a key factor in determining whether mental separation is legally effective.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for executing a bequest of a fixed sum when the estate comprises both cash and debt, calculating the immediate payment and future collections.

Core Idea
When assets are different genera, the legatee shares proportionally in each, not just the easiest.

A bequest of a fixed amount, such as 1000 dirhams, becomes complicated when the testator leaves both cash and debts owed to him. The Hanafi school provides a precise method to ensure fairness between the legatee and the heirs. This lesson unpacks that method, grounded in the principle that cash and debt are different genera and must be shared proportionally.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest and asset types
Determine the bequest amount (e.g., 1000 dirhams) and categorize the testator's assets: cash (ʿayn) and debts (dayn).
2
Calculate the total estate and the one-third limit
The bequest is valid only if it does not exceed one-third of the total estate. Add cash and debts to get the total, then find one-third.
If the bequest exceeds one-third, heirs must approve the excess.
3
Apply the sharing principle across genera
Since cash and debt are different genera, the legatee cannot take all from cash. He shares each genus proportionally with the heirs. Thus, he receives one-third of the cash immediately and one-third of each debt as it is collected.
This prevents the legatee from depleting cash to the detriment of heirs.
4
Compute the amounts and finalize
Calculate one-third of the cash. If that plus one-third of debts (collected over time) reaches the bequest amount, the obligation is fulfilled. If not, the legatee continues to receive one-third of each debt collection until the bequest is completed.
The legatee's total does not exceed the bequest amount.

Bequest to and of an Unborn Child: Condition of Six Months
Ruling on validity of bequest to unborn child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequest to and of an unborn child and apply the condition of six-month delivery to determine validity.

Core Idea
A bequest to or of an unborn child is valid only if the child is born within six months.

This passage from the matn and its commentary addresses a unique area of wasiyyah: bequests involving an unborn child. The Hanafi school permits bequests to an unborn child and bequests of an unborn child (the child itself), but with a critical temporal condition. Understanding this condition and its underlying rationale is essential for applying the ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of bequest
Distinguish between 'bequest to the unborn' (wasiyyah li al-haml) and 'bequest of the unborn' (wasiyyah bi al-haml). The first is a bequest for the child's benefit; the second is bequeathing the child itself to another person (e.g., the child of a slave-girl).
2
State the condition for validity
Both types are valid only if the child is born within six months from the date of the bequest. This ensures the child existed in the womb at the time the bequest was made.
The minimum duration of pregnancy in Hanafi law is six months, based on Quranic and juristic reasoning.
3
Understand the rationale
The law aims to provide property for the child from birth. If the child is born after six months, there is uncertainty whether the child existed at the time of bequest; thus, the bequest is invalid to prevent doubt.
4
Examine the evidence
The text cites Quran 4:11 (inheritance for children) and a hadith: 'When a newborn cries, he inherits.' By qiyas, inheritance rules apply to bequests, as both involve transfer of property.
The hadith is from Abu Dawud and Ibn Majah; its transmission is zanni, but its legal application is well-established.

Commentary
This ruling addresses a situation where a testator bequeaths a slave-girl to someone, and after the testator's death but before the legatee accepts the bequest, the slave-girl gives birth to a child. The legatee then accepts the bequest. The ruling: if the combined value of the mother and child is within the one-third limit of the testator's estate (i.e., does not exceed one-third of the total estate), then both mother and child go to the legatee. For example, if the total estate is 9,000 dirhams, and the slave-girl is worth 2,000 and the child 2,000 (total 4,000), which is 1,000 more than the one-third (3,000), then the legatee gets assets worth 3,000 from the total of 4,000, i.e., a proportion of 3/4 of the mother and child. The commentator explains that the child is considered a part (juz') of the slave-girl and her increase (nama'), so the bequest of the mother includes the child unless explicitly excepted (as in the previous lesson). Since no exception was made, the child is included. The legatee has the right to accept the bequest, and at the time of acceptance, both mother and child exist, so both are included in acceptance.

Bequest ‘Between’ Two: Effect of One Legatee’s Death
Ruling on bequest using word 'between' when one legatee dead

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a bequest uses the word ‘between’ (bayn) and one named legatee predeceases the testator.

Core Idea
The word ‘between’ forces equal halves; the dead legatee’s half returns to the estate.

In this short passage from al-Quduri’s Mukhtasar, we encounter a subtle but crucial distinction. When a testator says “one-third of my wealth is between Zayd and Amr,” the word “bayn” (between) implies an equal division into two shares. If one of them dies before the testator, his share does not pass to the surviving legatee; instead, it reverts to the testator’s heirs. This lesson clarifies the ruling and the linguistic basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operative word
Locate the word “bayn” in the testator’s statement. Understand its legal implication: in Hanafi fiqh, ‘bayn’ in a bequest indicates equal division among the named parties.
The word ‘bayn’ forces a share-based division, not joint ownership.
2
Apply the rule of division
Since the one-third is to be divided ‘between’ Zayd and Amr, each is entitled to half of that one-third, i.e., one-sixth of the total estate.
No other division is possible unless the testator specifies otherwise.
3
Determine the fate of the deceased’s share
Because Zayd is dead at the time of the will, his half does not pass to Amr. It reverts to the testator’s estate, to be distributed among the testator’s heirs.
This contrasts with a direct bequest to each individually, where the deceased’s share might lapse differently.

Bequest of One-Third When No Wealth at Time of Will
Ruling on bequest when testator acquires wealth after making will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a bequest when the testator had no wealth at the time of the will but later acquires wealth, and identify the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
A bequest is valid based on wealth at death, not at the time of making the will.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: someone makes a will to give one-third of their wealth but owns nothing at that moment. Later, they acquire wealth and die. What is the ruling? The Hanafi school, relying on Quranic evidence, rules that the bequest takes effect on the wealth possessed at death. The legatee receives one-third of whatever the testator owns at death, even if that wealth was entirely acquired after the will was made.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and ruling
Identify the act (making a will for one-third) and the initial condition (no wealth at that time). Then state the ruling: the legatee gets one-third of what the testator possesses at death.
2
Locate the Quranic evidence
The passage implicitly refers to Quran 4:12: 'from what you leave after any bequest.' The word 'taraktum' (you leave) indicates that the bequest operates on what is left at death, not at the time of will.
The full verse includes mention of debts and bequests.
3
Explain the wajh al-dalalah
The key word 'taraktum' (you leave) refers to the estate at the moment of death. Therefore, the testator's possession at that time determines the bequest, regardless of earlier lack of wealth.
4
Apply to a new scenario
Suppose the testator had no wealth when making the will, later inherits $60,000, and dies with that amount after debts. The legatee receives $20,000 (one-third of $60,000).
Debts are paid first, then the bequest is calculated on the net estate.
