Liability of One Woman Retracting from Mixed Testimony
Ruling statement on proportional liability for one woman witness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the liability of one woman retracting from mixed testimony and explain the proportional reasoning using the Hanafi principle of two women equaling one man.

Core Idea
When one woman retracts from mixed testimony, she guarantees one-quarter — not half — because her share is half of the women's half.

This lesson examines a specific ruling on testimony retraction. In Hanafi law, for financial claims, the testimony of two women equals that of one man. When one woman retracts after judgment, her liability is proportional to her contribution. Understanding this fractional calculation helps you apply the principle of equivalence in testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the testimony configuration
One man and two women testify. In Hanafi fiqh, two women are equivalent to one man, so total testimony is treated as two halves: the man contributes half, the two women contribute the other half.
This is for financial claims only, not for hudud or other cases.
2
Determine one woman's share
The two women collectively own half of the testimony. Each woman owns half of that half, i.e., one quarter of the total testimony.
Thus, retraction by one woman means retraction of one quarter of the testimony.
3
State the liability consequence
Since she retracted one quarter of the testimony, she guarantees one quarter of the claimed amount. She must pay that portion to the affected party.
This liability arises only if the retraction occurs after judgment has been passed.

Liability of Both Women Witnesses Retracting from Mixed Testimony
Ruling statement on liability when both women retract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the liability when both women retract from a mixed testimony and calculate the amount each must guarantee.

Core Idea
When both women retract, their half of the testimony collapses; they jointly guarantee half the property, each paying one-quarter.

In cases where testimony consists of one man and two women, the women's two accounts serve as a single male witness. If both women retract, the entire female half of the testimony is voided, leaving the man's testimony intact but half the total testimony retracted. The Hanafi rule holds them jointly liable for half the property involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the testimony is mixed — one man and two women. The two women collectively represent one male witness.
This is the default Hanafi rule: two women equal one man in testimony.
2
Understand the retraction
Both women retract their testimony. Since they act as a unit, their retraction removes the entire female half of the testimony.
3
Determine the portion retracted
Total testimony is divided into two halves: one man's half and the women's half. The women's retraction eliminates their half, leaving the man's half intact.
Thus exactly half the testimony is retracted.
4
Apply liability
Because they retracted, the women must compensate for the loss caused. They jointly guarantee half of the claimed property.
Liability is joint — both are responsible for the full half, not each for half.
5
Conclude individual share
Since they are jointly liable for half, each woman's individual share is half of that half, i.e., one-quarter of the total property.
If the property is 1000, each pays 250.

Liability When One or Two of Three Witnesses Retract
Ruling on liability when three witnesses retract partially

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the liability of witnesses who retract their testimony in a property case when the original witnesses exceed the required two.

Core Idea
Only the retraction of two out of three witnesses creates liability; the extra third witness bears no responsibility.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: three witnesses testify for property, then one or two retract. The ruling hinges on the principle that only two witnesses are decisive for judgment. Understanding this principle helps you correctly assign liability when witnesses retract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
State the scenario: three witnesses testify for property. Then determine the ruling for each case: one retracts vs. two retract.
The source text provides the answer: no liability for one retractor; both retractors jointly liable for half the property when two retract.
2
Extract the underlying principle
What principle governs this ruling? From the athar, we learn that judgment is based on two witnesses; additional witnesses carry no extra weight. Therefore, a single retraction does not affect the judgment.
The principle is that only the minimum required number (two) matters. Retraction beyond that threshold nullifies the minimal testimony.
3
Apply the principle to the two-retraction case
When two of three retract, the remaining testimony is only one witness, which is insufficient. Thus both retractors are liable. The liability is half the property because two-thirds of witnesses retracted but only two were decisive; half is the proportion affecting the judgment.
The Hanafi rule: liability equals the portion of the judgment that is invalidated. Here it is half (since two out of two decisive witnesses retracted, but the third is extraneous).
4
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The passage cites an athar from 'Abd al-Rahman ibn Udhayna and from 'Ali that more than two witnesses do not give preference. This supports the principle that extra witnesses are irrelevant.
The athar is a Qawl Sahabi (saying of a Companion), which in Hanafi usul is a source of law but not as strong as Quran or mutawatir hadith.
5
Work through a numerical example
If the property is 20 dirhams, and two retract, they are jointly liable for 10 dirhams (half), each bearing 5 dirhams.
The example clarifies the arithmetic: half of 20 is 10, divided equally between the two retractors.

Liability on Retraction by Women in Mixed Witnesses
Ruling statement on liability for retraction by women in mixed testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the liability of a retracting woman witness when a man and two women testify together.

Core Idea
Two women equal one man in testimony; each woman's liability equals her quarter share of the total claim.

This lesson covers the Hanafi ruling on retraction by women witnesses in cases where one man and two women testify together. The key is understanding how testimony is divided: the man's testimony counts as half, and the two women together count as the other half, each woman representing a quarter. When a woman retracts, she is liable for her portion of the claim, not an equal share of the total. These rules apply in financial claims where two women are equivalent to one man.

Guided Path
1
Identify the composition of witnesses
In Hanafi fiqh, for financial claims, two women witnesses are equivalent to one man. Therefore, total testimony is divided: the man represents half (1/2) and the two women represent the other half (1/2), with each woman representing one quarter (1/4) of the claim.
This division is based on the principle that two women equal one man in testimony.
2
Determine the portion of the retracting witness
If one woman retracts, she is liable for the portion she represented – one quarter of the claim. If both women retract, they are jointly liable for half the claim (each individually quarter). The liability is proportional to the testimony weight.
The retraction must occur before the judge has ruled; otherwise, the liability may differ.
3
Apply the liability to the claim amount
Multiply the total claim by the fraction representing the retracted share. For one woman, liability = claim × 1/4. For two women, joint liability = claim × 1/2, each responsible for 1/4 individually.
The man's liability arises only if he retracts; he is not liable for the women's retraction.

Liability on Retraction: Mixed Testimony of One Man and Many Women
Ruling on retraction liability for multiple female witnesses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the liability shares when women retract in a mixed testimony case of one man and ten women.

Core Idea
Liability for retraction is proportional to the deficiency caused; no liability if sufficient testimony remains.

This ruling addresses a scenario with one male and ten female witnesses. The Hanafi school determines liability based on testimonial weight: one man equals two women. Only when retraction reduces the surviving evidence below the required minimum (two male equivalents) does liability arise, apportioned according to the missing portion.

Guided Path
1
Understand testimonial equivalency
For financial claims, the required minimum is two male witnesses or one man and two women. Thus, two women have the testimonial weight of one man; each woman has half a man's weight.
This equivalency is fundamental to calculating fractions.
2
Compute initial testimonial weight
With one man and ten women, total weight in male equivalents = 1 (man) + (10 women ÷ 2) = 6. The required weight for a valid testimony is 2 (two men or equivalent).
3
Assess deficiency after retraction
When some witnesses retract, calculate the weight of the remaining witnesses. If it is at least 2, no liability. If less, the deficiency (2 - remaining weight) is the fraction of the property owed.
Deficiency is measured relative to the required 2 male equivalents.
4
Apportion liability among retracting witnesses
The total liability (the deficiency) is split among all retracting witnesses in proportion to their individual testimonial weight.
Men and women contribute according to their weight: a man contributes 1, a woman contributes 0.5.
5
Apply to the three rulings
(9) Eight women retract: remaining = 1 man + 2 women = 2 male equivalents → no liability. (10) Nine women retract: remaining = 1 man + 1 woman = 1.5 → deficiency = 0.5 → liability = 0.5/2 = 1/4 on the nine women. (11) All retract: deficiency = 2 (full right). Man's share = 1/6, women's share = 5/6 (ratio of their contributions 1:5).
The fractions match the source.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Liability shares follow the equivalence: ten women count as five men, so a man is one-sixth of six 'testifying men'.

When witnesses retract, they become liable for the property lost due to their testimony. But how should liability be divided among a man and ten women? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions differ on the method of division. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each view and shows the practical calculation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue

2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his reasoning

This is a qiyas on the rule of testimony equivalence.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
Sahibayn: man pays 1/2, women pay 1/2. Their reasoning: the man's testimony is half the proof, and the women collectively (no matter how many) constitute the other half.
They treat the number of women as irrelevant beyond equivalence to one man.
4

Take a property valued at 48 dirhams. Under Abu Hanifa: man pays 8, women pay 40 (each woman 4). Under Sahibayn: man pays 24, women pay 24 (each woman 2.4 or 24 dirhams collectively).
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is clear in the numbers.
5
Check the authority and fatwa status
Both views are recorded in the Zahir al-Riwayah. The text does not declare a mufta bihi; both are considered valid. In practice, a mufti may choose based on circumstances.
This is a legitimate intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.

Liability for retraction only arises when the remaining witnesses fall below a full unit: one man and two women.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original testimony composition

Liability is proportional to the deficiency caused.

Liability Upon Retraction of Testimony in Marriage
Three rulings on witness liability after retraction in marriage testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness liability when retracting testimony about a marriage, and explain the two principles that justify no liability.

Core Idea
Witnesses face no liability for retracted marriage testimony as long as a mahr was specified – bud'ah loss is not compensable after marriage.

This passage presents a notable exception in Hanafi liability law: witnesses who retract testimony typically must compensate for any loss caused. However, in marriage cases, even if the mahr they testified to is below the market rate (mahr al-mithl), they are not liable. Why? Because the woman received some mahr in exchange for her sexual union (bud'ah), and the value of bud'ah is recognized only at the time of marriage, not afterward. This lesson unpacks three scenarios from the text and the two principles that justify the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Read the three sub-paragraphs (12-14). Note the differences: testimony about a woman with mahr equal/exceeding mahr al-mithl; testimony with mahr less than mahr al-mithl; testimony about a man marrying a woman with mahr al-mithl.
All three involve retraction (ruju') after consummation is implied.
2
Extract the ruling for each scenario
The ruling is the same in all cases: la daman (no liability). Write it down: witnesses are not liable for any shortfall or loss.
This is a departure from the general rule of witness liability.
3
Understand the two underlying principles
The commentary mentions two principles: (1) bud'ah (sexual union) is valuable only at the time of marriage, not after divorce or annulment; (2) when a specific mahr is agreed, it becomes the standard (asl), replacing mahr al-mithl. Thus, the woman has received consideration and suffered no legal loss.
These principles are critical for justifying the exception.

No Liability for Witnesses When Mahr Is Sufficient
Ruling with proof: retraction liability conditioned on loss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that witnesses are liable only for actual loss, and determine liability when retracting testimony about marriage dower.

Core Idea
Witnesses are liable only for actual loss; no loss means no liability.

This passage from the chapter on retraction of testimony clarifies a key principle: witnesses are not automatically liable upon retraction; liability depends on whether their testimony caused harm. The test case is marriage testimony where the dower equals or exceeds the woman's customary dower (mahr al-mithl). Understanding this rule helps you distinguish between situations that trigger compensation and those that do not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the passage and extract the central ruling: when two witnesses testify that a woman married for a dower equal to or more than her mahr al-mithl, then retract, they are not liable for any damages.
The ruling is stated explicitly in the first line.
2
Understand the Condition for Liability
General principle: witnesses who retract are liable only if their testimony caused a loss. Here, the woman received at least the equivalent of her mahr al-mithl, so no loss occurred.
Loss is measured by the difference between what the woman actually received and what she was entitled to.
3
Apply the Reasoning (Talil)
The reasoning is that the woman gave up her vulva (in marriage) and received a dower that is fair compensation. Since the dower is not less than mahr al-mithl, she has no claim of loss. Therefore witnesses are not liable.
This reasoning is based on the concept of actual harm (ḍarar).
4
Contrast with the Opposite Scenario
If the testified dower were less than mahr al-mithl, the woman would suffer a loss (the shortfall), and witnesses would be liable for that difference.
This contrast is implied by the condition 'equal or more'.
5
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Imam Shafi'i holds that witnesses are liable for the full dower if they retract after consummation, because the woman lost the vulva without receiving full compensation (the dower might be less than mahr al-mithl in his view). The Hanafi position focuses on actual loss: if she got mahr al-mithl, no loss.
The practical difference: under Shafi'i, liability may exist even when dower equals mahr al-mithl if the mahr al-mithl is considered insufficient for the loss of vulva.

No Liability for Deficient Mahr Testimony
Ruling when witnesses retract after testifying for marriage with less than mahr al-mithl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when witnesses retract after testifying for a marriage with a dower less than mahr al-mithl and explain the two principles that justify no liability.

Core Idea
Fixed dower is the benchmark, not mahr al-mithl, so no loss occurs.

This passage continues the discussion on witness retraction in marriage testimony. Here, the dower is specified but is lower than the woman's mahr al-mithl. The ruling is surprisingly that the witnesses are not liable for the deficiency. The reasoning rests on two key Hanafi principles: the valuation of the vulva at the time of marriage, and the primacy of a fixed dower over mahr al-mithl. Understanding this ruling clarifies the school's approach to loss assessment in marriage contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the witnesses testifying to a marriage with a dower less than mahr al-mithl and then retracting. The ruling is that they are not liable for the deficiency.

The proof is two principles: (a) the vulva is valuable at the time of marriage but not at the time of dissolution, and (b) once a specific dower is fixed, that becomes the basis, not mahr al-mithl.

The woman received something for her vulva at marriage (the fixed dower), so she suffered no actual loss. The witnesses' testimony only affected the initial setting; after the marriage is consummated, the vulva's value is no longer assessed separately.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school would likely impose liability because they value the vulva at the time of testimony, not at the time of marriage, thus viewing the deficiency as a real loss.
This difference stems from a different valuation principle.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi because it upholds the finality of the fixed dower and avoids retrospective valuation, which could disrupt settled marriages.
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.

Liability for Excess When Witnesses Retract
Ruling when witnesses retract after testifying for excessive dower

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ruling that witnesses who testify for a dower above mahr al-mithl and then retract are liable only for the excess, and calculate the amount owed in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Witnesses are liable only for the loss they caused: the excess over mahr al-mithl.

In marriage contracts, witnesses may testify for a specific dower amount. If they later retract and the husband has already paid an amount above the customary mahr (mahr al-mithl), the witnesses become liable for that excess. This lesson explains the legal reasoning and the precise limit of their liability—only the extra amount, not the entire dower.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Recognize that the case involves witnesses testifying for a dower greater than mahr al-mithl, then retracting after consummation. The ruling is that they guarantee only the excess (ziyādah) over mahr al-mithl.
The ruling applies only when the husband actually paid the dower based on their testimony.
2
Understand the ʿilla (effective cause)
The vulva's actual value is mahr al-mithl. The husband paid extra solely due to the testimony. The extra payment is a genuine loss caused by the witnesses, so they must compensate that exact loss.
The loss is not the full dower, only the inflated portion.
3
Distinguish from other scenarios
If the witnesses do not retract, the dower stands and they are not liable. If the husband has not yet paid, there is no loss yet. If the testimony was within mahr al-mithl, no excess exists.
The ruling is specific to retraction after payment of an excessive amount.
4
Apply the calculation
Given mahr al-mithl (M) and testified amount (T), the liability = T − M. For example, M=1000, T=1200 → liability = 200.
Ensure the testimony is for a fixed amount, not subject to uncertainty.

Witnesses who retract after a hand amputation are liable for the loss exactly proportional to their testimony.

This passage establishes a key Hanafi principle in the law of testimony: if witnesses retract after a hudud penalty has been executed, they bear financial responsibility for the harm caused. The ruling is based on an athar (effect) from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i and is analogically extended from the Quranic rule of equal compensation. The practical consequence is that each witness is liable for the portion of the diyah corresponding to their share in the testimony.

Liability is proportional to loss caused. The commentary explicitly uses qiyas: witnesses are liable for exactly the loss they cause, no more.

By qiyas, any witness who causes loss through retraction is liable for that exact loss, whether it is part of a hand or other bodily harm.
The principle is general.

Witnesses Guarantee Excess Mahr When Retracting
Ruling on retraction testimony exceeding mahr al-mithl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when witnesses retract after testifying to excessive mahr and calculate the amount they must guarantee.

Core Idea
Witness liability is limited to the actual loss they cause – the excess over mahr al-mithl.

This passage from a classical Hanafi text addresses a specific scenario: witnesses testify to a marriage mahr higher than the standard (mahr al-mithl) and later retract. The ruling clarifies that they are not liable for the entire mahr, only the excess. This illustrates the Hanafi principle of proportional liability based on the loss caused by false testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is witness testimony to a mahr greater than mahr al-mithl, followed by retraction after the court has acted. The ruling is that the witnesses must guarantee the excess amount.
2
Understand the loss calculation
The mahr al-mithl compensates the husband for the vulva (the benefit received). Any amount beyond mahr al-mithl is a pure loss caused by the false testimony. The witnesses are liable only for that loss.
In the example, mahr al-mithl = 1000, testified = 1200, so excess = 200.
3
Apply the rule to calculate liability
Given a specific scenario, subtract mahr al-mithl from the testified amount. The difference is the amount the witnesses must guarantee.
The husband retains the mahr al-mithl as proper compensation.
4
Note the condition for liability
Liability arises only if retraction occurs after the court has acted (e.g., the husband paid the mahr or it was decreed). Retraction before judgment voids the testimony and does not impose liability.
This is based on the general Hanafi principle regarding retraction.

Two conditional rulings on witness retraction after sale testimony.

Core Idea
Witnesses guarantee only the loss they cause — no loss, no liability.

This passage presents a clear principle: witnesses who retract after testifying about a sale are liable only if their testimony caused a financial loss. The key is the objective market value. If the testified price equals or exceeds the value, no loss occurred; if it is less, they must compensate the difference. This ruling reflects the Hanafi emphasis on actual damage as the basis for liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note the two conditions: testimony at/above market value vs. testimony below market value.
The ruling hinges on whether a loss occurred.
2
Determine loss occurrence
For each scenario, compare the testified price to the market value (al-qima). Loss = market value - testified price (if positive).
If testified price ≥ market value, loss = 0.
3
Apply the liability rule
If loss > 0, witnesses are jointly liable for that loss. If loss = 0, no liability.
Liability is upon retraction after the transaction is executed.
4
Compute the guaranteed amount
In the example: market value 5 dirhams, testified 4 dirhams → loss = 1 dirham, witnesses guarantee 1 dirham.
Only the shortfall is guaranteed, not the full price.

Ruling on witness liability for divorce testimony with retraction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Witnesses compensate only when their false testimony causes a net loss to the husband.

In Hanafi fiqh, witnesses who testify to a divorce and then retract may be liable for compensation. The key distinction is whether the divorce occurred before or after consummation. This ruling applies the principle that liability follows actual loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Determine whether the alleged divorce is pre-consummation or post-consummation. The ruling hinges on this timing.
Pre-consummation means before sexual intercourse; post-consummation means after.
2
Determine the husband's obligation
In pre-consummation, the husband pays half the mahr. In post-consummation, he pays the full mahr.
The mahr is the mandatory bridal gift.
3
Assess the net loss caused by the witnesses
In pre-consummation, the husband pays half mahr without receiving the marriage benefit (badaal), so there is a net loss. In post-consummation, he pays full mahr but receives the benefit, so no net loss.
Badaal refers to the benefit of consummation.
4
State the liability
When witnesses retract, they must compensate the loss they caused. For pre-consummation, they guarantee half the mahr. For post-consummation, they guarantee nothing.
The ruling is based on the principle that liability tracks actual financial harm.

Witness Liability After Retraction in Qisas Cases
Ruling on witness liability after retraction in qisas cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness liability when witnesses retract after a qisas execution and distinguish between direct and indirect causation.

Core Idea
A retracting witness pays diya but never qisas because the judge, not the witness, executed.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: witnesses testify to a crime requiring qisas, the judge carries out the death penalty, and then the witnesses retract their testimony. The ruling distinguishes between financial liability (diya) and corporal punishment (qisas) based on the principle of direct causation. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the law of evidence and retaliation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and events
Recognize the sequence: witnesses testify → judge executes → witnesses retract. The witnesses are the actors here, not the murderer.
The judge is the direct executor, not the witnesses.
2
Extract the twofold ruling
The text states two consequences: (1) witnesses must pay diya, (2) witnesses are not subject to qisas. Note both parts.
3
Understand the rationale for diya
Diya is wajib because the witnesses' testimony was the cause (sabab) of the execution. Even though they retracted, the harm occurred due to their initial statement.
Causation does not require direct action; indirect causation suffices for financial liability.
4
Understand the rationale for no qisas
Qisas requires direct, intentional killing (qatl amd). The witnesses did not strike the blow; the judge did. Their testimony was indirect, thus not qisas.
Hanafi principle: qisas demands direct agency (mubasharah), not indirect (tasabbub).
5
Generalize the principle
The underlying principle: liability for diya follows from causing harm through testimony, while qisas requires direct physical killing. This aligns with the broader Hanafi rule on causation.
Memorize this as a key example of the distinction between direct and indirect causation in jinayat.

Core Idea

In Hanafi testimony law, subordinate witnesses (shuhud al-far') act as transmitters of the original witnesses' testimony. Identify the act and ruling

The practical result is that the judgment stands, and the subordinate witnesses' claim is ignored. Other schools might allow such a claim under certain conditions, but the Hanafi position maintains consistency.
This is a ruling with procedural finality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the liability of muzakkūn upon retraction and explain the reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
Muzakkūn are liable if their tazkiyah directly caused a judgment, per Abu Hanifa; Sahibayn dissent.

In a criminal case of zina, the judge relies on character witnesses (muzakkūn) to validate the integrity of the principal witnesses before imposing rajm. What happens if the muzakkūn later retract their endorsement? Does their retraction trigger liability (diyyah)? This lesson unpacks a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on this precise question.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Understand the factual setting: four witnesses testified to zina; the judge sent investigators who vouched for the witnesses' integrity ('adalah); on that basis, the judge ruled rajm. Later, the character witnesses retracted their tazkiyah.

2
State Abu Hanifa's Position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the muzakkūn are liable for diyah because their tazkiyah was instrumental in the judgment. Without it, the witnesses would not have been accepted, and rajm would not have been imposed.

The dalil is rational (talil): the judge's acceptance of the principal witnesses depended entirely on the tazkiyah. Thus, the retraction of the tazkiyah nullifies the basis of the judgment, making the retractors liable as if they had contributed to a wrongful punishment.
This is a source of liability beyond mere testimony.

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that muzakkūn are not liable because they are analogous to witnesses of ihsan (who testify to a person's marital status). Those witnesses only state a quality, and liability follows the primary cause (the principal witnesses), not an accessory.
They emphasize that the retraction does not create a new cause of harm.

The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the muzakkūn must pay diyah if they retract; under the Sahibayn, they do not. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the text is Abu Hanifa's view, though the Sahibayn's opinion is noted for comparison.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf with established tarjih.

ويقبل في المعاملات قول الفاسق ولا يقبل في اخبار الديانات الا قول العدل. عن شريح انه كان يجيز شهادة الصبيان على السن والموضحة ويتأباهم فيما سوى ذلك. وجلد عمر ابا بكرة وشبل بن معبد ونافعا بقذف المغيرة ثم استتابهم وقال من تاب قبلت شهادته واجاز عبد الله بن عتبة وعمر بن عبد العزيز وسعيد بن جبير وطاؤس ومجاهد والشعبي. ولا تقبلوا لهم شهادة ابدا واولئك هم الفاسقون الا الذين تابوا من بعد ذلك واصلحوا.
And the statement of the fasiq is accepted in transactions, and in reports of religious matters only the statement of the 'adil is accepted. From Shuraih: he used to permit the testimony of children concerning a tooth and a head wound, and he would forbid them regarding anything other than that. And 'Umar flogged Abu Bakrah, Shibl ibn Ma'bad, and Nafi' for slandering al-Mughirah, then he gave them the opportunity to repent and said: 'Whoever repents, his testimony is accepted.' And 'Abdullah ibn 'Utbah, 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz, Sa'id ibn Jubair, Tawus, Mujahid, and al-Sha'bi permitted it. 'And never accept their testimony, and they are the wicked except those who repent after that and reform.' (Qur'an 24:4-5)

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof from the Hanafi school. The text distinguishes between mu'amalat (transactions, such as sale and purchase) and akhbar al-diyanat (religious reports, such as testimony for hail sighting or marriage). In mu'amalat, due to their frequency and the difficulty of always finding an 'adil person, the statement of a fasiq (sinner) is accepted. This is expedited by necessity. In diyanat, only the statement of an 'adil (righteous, trustworthy) person is accepted because such matters are rare and require reliability. The author supports this with the following proofs: 1) The athar of Shuraih, who allowed children's testimony for minor injuries (tooth and wound) but not for other matters. 2) The action of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, who flogged false accusers (qadhif) and then accepted their testimony after repentance, indicating that a fasiq who repents can have his testimony accepted. 3) The Qur'anic verses (24:4-5) which state that qadhifs are fasiqs whose testimony is never accepted unless they repent and reform. The Hanafi position is that a fasiq's testimony is not accepted in diyanat, but in mu'amalat it is accepted due to need, and if the fasiq has repented, it is accepted in all matters.

Key Terms
فاسق (fāsiq): One who commits major sins or persists on minor sins, thus lacking 'adalah (moral probity).
عدل ('adl): One who is morally upright, avoids major sins and does not persist on minor sins, and is trustworthy in their reports.
معاملات (mu'āmalāt): Transactions and dealings between people, such as sale, purchase, lease, etc.
ديانات (diyānāt): Matters of religion, such as witnessing for Islamic rituals, marriage, divorce, and other religious reports.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school accepts the testimony of a fasiq in mu'amalat out of necessity, but rejects it in diyanat due to the requirement of 'adalah.
