Refusal of Oath in Qisas: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nukul in qisas: limb vs life.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on nukul in qisas (Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn) and explain the reasoning based on property analogy vs doubt (shubha).

Core Idea
In qisas, refusal of oath leads to qisas for limbs but not for life (Abu Hanifa); Sahibayn impose diyah in both.

When a qisas claim lacks evidence, the defendant is asked to swear an oath. Refusal (nukul) creates a legal dilemma: does it imply confession or permission? Imam Abu Hanifa and his students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagree on the outcome, based on whether limbs are treated as property or whether doubt (shubha) prevents qisas. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each position and its practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the two contexts
The passage distinguishes between nukul in claims for limbs (below life) and for life. Note that the defendant has no evidence (bayyinah) against him.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and its rationale for limbs
For limbs, nukul makes qisas binding because limbs are analogous to property; refusal is interpreted as permission (bazl) to cut.
The comparison is to property claims where nukul establishes liability.
3
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and its rationale for life
For life, nukul does not automatically impose qisas; the defendant is imprisoned until confession or oath. Life cannot be surrendered like property.
Imprisonment coerces admission or oath, but qisas is not directly enforced.
4
State the Sahibayn's ruling and its rationale for both cases
The Sahibayn hold that in both cases nukul leads to diyah (financial compensation) because qisas is a single category and tacit confession is doubtful; doubt drops qisas but property (diyah) can still be fixed.
They view the tacit admission as having shubha regarding the type, preventing qisas but allowing diyah.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Abu Hanifa, limb qisas is directly enforceable; under Sahibayn, only diyah is payable. This affects whether retaliation or compensation is demanded.
The text does not give a tarjih; both positions are recorded.

So just as in property, he can substitute (i.e., if he refuses the oath, it is understood that the property is not yours but I give it to you), similarly for limbs, qisas is not due but he can say: I allow you to cut this limb for qisas. And if the claim is for qisas of life, life does not have the status of property; substitution does not apply. No one can say: qisas is not upon me but I allow you to kill me. So for qisas of life, an oath is administered, and if he refuses, qisas does not become binding immediately; rather the defendant is imprisoned until he either confesses to qisas or swears that it is not upon him. If he confesses, qisas becomes binding; if he swears and denies, qisas is dropped. The Sahibayn say: Qisas, whether for limb or for the whole life, is qisas, and qisas is dropped by doubt. When the defendant refuses the oath, it means he tacitly confirms the claim: I confess that qisas is upon me. And tacit confession has doubt regarding the type of qisas. Due to doubt, neither qisas of life nor qisas of limb is established. However, since he tacitly confirmed the claimant's statement, diyah is binding for life or limb. And because diyah is property, even if there is doubt in the confession, it becomes binding, because property can be established with doubt. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, limb in certain contexts has the status of property. According to the Sahibayn, limb does not have the status of property. Al-Arsh: diyah for limb, payment for limb.

Commentary
This passage deals with the legal procedure when qisas (retaliation) is claimed but the claimant lacks evidence. The defendant is asked to swear an oath. If he refuses (nukul), the ruling depends on the nature of the claim. For claims involving limbs (below life), Imam Abu Hanifa considers limbs analogous to property; refusal of oath is construed as permission (bazl) to cut the limb, so qisas becomes binding. For claims involving life, refusal does not automatically impose qisas because life cannot be surrendered; instead, the defendant is imprisoned until he either confesses or takes the oath. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that in both cases, refusal leads to diyah (financial compensation) because they view qisas as a single category and argue that tacit confession (dabzaban iqrar) is doubtful, and doubt prevents qisas but property (diyah) can still be liable. The passage type is an intra-Hanafi dispute, with the commentator expanding on the reasoning of each imam.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for intentional injury or murder.
ارش (arsh): Compensation for injury to a limb, a form of diyah.
نكول (nukul): Refusal to take an oath after being directed by the judge.
بذل (bazl): Voluntary surrender or permission, here meaning allowing one's limb to be cut in retaliation.
شبهة (shubha): Doubt or ambiguity that prevents application of severe penalties like qisas.

Hanafi Ruling on Witness Retraction After Qisas Execution
Inter-madhhab dispute on retracting witnesses after qisas execution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that retracting witnesses after qisas execution pay diya, distinguish it from the Shafi'i position, and identify the proofs (Quran 4:92, effect from Ibrahim) that support it.

Core Idea
Retracting witnesses pay diya, not qisas – their error is akin to erroneous killing.

When witnesses retract their testimony after a qisas execution, the question arises: should they face qisas themselves or merely pay blood money? The Hanafi school, drawing on the Quranic principle of diya for accidental killing and a report from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, rules that the witnesses owe diya. The Shafi'i school, citing a report from al-Hasan al-Basri, argues for qisas. This lesson unpacks the proofs and the Hanafi response, clarifying the operative ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling: retracting witnesses are liable for diya (blood money), not qisas (execution). Note the hukm level: wajib.
The text explicitly says 'wa bihi na'khudh' (and we take this).
2
Identify the opponent and their proof
Recognize that Imam al-Shafi'i holds qisas should be applied. Locate his evidence: the effect from al-Hasan that a retracting witness after execution is killed.
This is a standard ikhtilaf between the schools.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the Shafi'i reasoning: the witness's testimony caused the death, so retraction is akin to admitting false testimony, which may warrant qisas.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school treats the situation as an error (khata') because the witnesses did not intend the fatal outcome. They cite Quran 4:92 (diya for erroneous killing) and the effect from Ibrahim to support diya.
The Hanafi response distinguishes between intentional murder and mistaken testimony.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi, retracting witnesses pay financial compensation; under Shafi'i, they face execution. The Hanafi view is the mufta bihi.
This difference has serious legal consequences.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute about the ruling when witnesses retract their testimony after the court has executed the punishment. The Hanafi position, based on the Quran (4:92) and a report from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, treats the killing as erroneous (khata) because the witnesses did not intend the death but mistakenly testified. Hence, the retracting witnesses are liable for diya (blood money) not qisas (retaliation). The Shafi'i school, citing a report from al-Hasan al-Basri, analogizes the retracting witness to a murderer and thus imposes qisas. The Hanafi response is that the situation is closer to erroneous killing since the witnesses' testimony was not malicious; they may have been mistaken. Therefore, the Hanafi ruling stands: diya is obligatory, not qisas.

Key Terms
دية (diyah): Blood money, financial compensation paid to the victim's heirs in cases of homicide or bodily harm.
قتل خطاء (qatl khata'): Erroneous or accidental killing where there is no intent to kill.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliatory punishment, specifically execution in cases of intentional murder.

Killing Under Duress: Haram and Sinful
Ruling on killing another under threat of death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing another under duress and explain the proof from Surah al-Nisa 4:93.

Core Idea
Under duress, killing another is haram; one must accept death rather than kill.

Sometimes a person may be forced under threat of death to kill an innocent person. Does necessity make this permissible? The Hanafi school firmly holds that it does not. This lesson explains the ruling and the evidence from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act
The act in question: a person is forced (mukrah) by threat of death to kill another person.
Duress (ikrah) must be by threat of death or loss of limb.
2
State the Hukm
The ruling: it is haram for the coerced person to kill the innocent victim. He must patiently accept death and not kill.
The killer is sinful (athim) even though coerced.
3
Understand the Reasoning
Both lives are equally inviolable; one's own life does not take precedence over another's. Killing is absolutely prohibited.
No exception for duress in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Cite the Dalil
The proof is Surah al-Nisa 4:93, which threatens severe punishment for intentional murder. The prohibition is absolute.
The verse does not make an exception for duress.
5
Identify Consequences
If the coerced kills, he is sinful. Qisas (retaliation) is on the coercer if the killing was intentional.
The coercer bears the legal liability.

Qisas on the Coercer for Intentional Killing Under Duress
Ruling on qisas for intentional killing under duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on qisas for intentional killing under duress and apply it to distinguish liability of the coercer from the coerced.

Core Idea
In intentional killing under duress, qisas falls on the coercer, not the coerced killer.

This lesson introduces a foundational ruling of Hanafi criminal law: when a person is forced to kill another intentionally, the retribution (qisas) is not on the coerced killer but on the one who forced him. The text from al-Sharh al-Saghir makes this clear, and we will explore the reasoning and cross-madhhab difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Recognize the coercer (mukrih) and the coerced (mukrah) in the scenario. The killing must be intentional (amdan).
2
Determine the locus of qisas
According to the text, qisas is on the coercer. Understand that the coerced is not liable for qisas even though he performed the act.
The coerced is sinful but not subject to retaliation.
3
Understand the rationale
The coercer is the effective cause (sabab), while the coerced is a tool. Hanafi law attributes the act to the one who compelled it.
This is based on the principle that intention and causation matter more than the physical act.
4
Contrast with other schools
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i holds the direct killer liable. This is the key ikhtilaf.
The practical difference (thamarah) is that in Shafi'i law, the coerced may face qisas.

Qisas on the Coercer: Hanafi Position and Dispute with Zufar and Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on qisas for coerced murder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas for a coerced killer, explain the proofs from the athar, and evaluate the opposing views of Imam Zufar and Imam Shafi'i.

Core Idea
In coerced murder, the coercer bears qisas; the coerced is a tool.

This lesson examines a key dispute in Hanafi criminal law: who is liable for qisas when a person is forced to kill? The Hanafi school holds the coercer responsible. We will analyse the arguments from the Sahihayn (Abu Hanifa's companions) and the opposing views of Imam Zufar and Imam Shafi'i. By the end, you will understand why the coerced person is considered a tool rather than the agent.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on qisas for coerced murder
Identify the ruling: retaliation ('qisas') is upon the one who coerced the killing, not upon the coerced person. The coerced is likened to a tool used by the coercer.
The passage opens with 'Al-qisas 'ala alladhi akrahahu' – this is the relied-upon Hanafi view.
2
Identify the opposing views and their schools
Two opposing views are presented: Imam Zufar (within Hanafi school) holds that the actual killer (the coerced) is subject to qisas. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that both the coercer and the coerced are killed.
Zufar's view is based on the physical act of killing; Shafi'i combines cause and effect.
3
Follow the proofs for the opposing views
Zufar cites an athar from Ata (a tabi'i) that 'the killer is killed and there is nothing upon the commander'. Shafi'i's proof combines both athar: the killer for the act, the commander for being the cause.
The athar from Ata is recorded in Musannaf Abdurrazzaq.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response and its prioritised evidence
The Hanafi view is supported by the athar of Abu Hurairah where a master who commands a slave to kill is executed, and the slave is not. This athar is given precedence, and the coerced person is analogised to a tool – the one who uses the tool (the coercer) bears liability.
The Hanafi response is not explicitly stated in this passage but is derived from the earlier reasoning and the prioritisation of the Abu Hurairah athar.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the Hanafi view is followed, the coercer is executed and the coerced goes free. If Zufar's view is followed, the coerced is executed. If Shafi'i's view is followed, both are executed. Each results in a different outcome for the same scenario.
The Hanafi view is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the school.
