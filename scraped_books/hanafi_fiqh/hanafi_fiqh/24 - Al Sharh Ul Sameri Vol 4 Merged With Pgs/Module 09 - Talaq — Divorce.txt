Oath concerning a divorced wife
Ruling on oath concerning a woman after she is divorced

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when an oath refers to a woman identified as a wife and she is later divorced, and identify the legal principle that the oath applies to her person.

Core Idea
An oath referring to a person by attribute remains binding on that person even if the attribute changes.

In Hanafi fiqh, oaths are interpreted according to the intended object. This passage clarifies that when someone swears not to speak to 'the wife of so-and-so,' the reference is to the woman's very being (dhat), not the marital relationship. Therefore, even if she is divorced and ceases to be a wife, the oath remains in effect. Speaking to her after divorce constitutes a broken oath (hinnth), requiring expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its object
Read the oath carefully: 'I will not speak to the wife of so-and-so.' Note that the object is described by a relationship but points to a specific individual.
The phrase 'wife of so-and-so' identifies a particular woman; the marriage is a descriptor.
2
Determine the referent of the description
Analyze whether the oath is attached to the attribute ('wife') or the person herself. In Hanafi usul, descriptors that indicate identity refer to the entity (dhat), not the contingent attribute.
This is based on the principle that names refer to the essence (al-asmā' li al-dhawāt).
3
Apply the ruling on change of attribute
The woman's divorce removes the attribute 'wife' but does not change her person. Since the oath is on her person, the divorce does not affect the oath's applicability.
Contrast with an oath that explicitly conditions on the attribute, e.g., 'while she is your wife'; such cases may differ.
4
Conclude the hukm: hinnth
If the swearer speaks to the woman after divorce, he has broken his oath (hinnth). He must perform kaffarah (expiation) for a broken oath.
Kaffarah for an oath is feeding or clothing ten poor people, or freeing a slave; if unable, fasting three days.
5
Consider the role of intention
The Hanafi school generally adheres to the literal meaning unless the swearer had a specific intention (niyyah) that is plausible. If he intended only while she was a wife, that might affect the ruling, but the default is the literal meaning.
This nuance is for more advanced study; the ruling here assumes no contrary intention.

Definition and Proofs of Compulsion (Ikrah)
Defines ikrah and cites its textual proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ikrah and identify the Quranic verses and athar that establish its legal validity.

Core Idea
Ikrah is coercion by a capable threat; its recognition relies on ability not status.

This lesson introduces the concept of ikrah (legal compulsion) as defined in Hanafi fiqh. We will examine the precise definition, the two Quranic proofs cited by the author, and the athar from Umar and Shuraih that illustrate its scope. Understanding these foundational elements is crucial before moving into the intra-madhhab dispute over who can be a compeller.

Guided Path
1
1. Grasp the Definition
Read the definition: ikrah is forcing someone to act through a threat of harm from a person capable of executing it. Note the two key conditions: threat of harm, and capability of the threatener.
The text uses the example of a child threatening to hit – such a threat does not constitute ikrah because the child lacks capability.
2
2. Analyse the Quranic Verses
Examine Surah an-Nahl 16:106, which permits outward disbelief under compulsion while the heart remains firm. Also Surah an-Nur 24:33, which forbids forcing slave-girls into prostitution. Both verses implicitly recognise the effect of compulsion.
These verses are proof of existence (thubut) of the legal effect of ikrah, not a detailed ruling.
3
3. Study the Athar
The athar of Umar states that a person is not secure if starved, tied, or beaten. Shuraih declares imprisonment, beating, shackling, and threatening as forms of ikrah. These illustrate what constitutes adequate coercion.
These sayings expand the definition to specific actions that are considered coercive.

Who Can Compel? The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Compeller (Mukrih)
Explains the intra-madhhab dispute over who can be a compeller.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on who qualifies as a compeller (mukrih) and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The mufta bihi: any capable person, not just the ruler, can be a compeller.

This lesson unpacks a well-known difference within the Hanafi school regarding the qualification of the compeller (mukrih). Imam Abu Hanifa initially held that only the sultan (ruler) could effect legally recognised compulsion. His two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), held that any person capable of executing the threat – including a thief – qualifies. Later Hanafi authorities adopted the Sahibayn's view as the mufta bihi. We will examine the proofs and rationale for each position.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the Two Positions
First position (Imam Abu Hanifa's first opinion): Only the sultan (ruler) can be a compeller for ikrah. Second position (Sahibayn): any person capable of carrying out the threat, regardless of status.
The text mentions that some scholars later explained Abu Hanifa's view was based on the conditions of his time when thieves lacked power.
2
2. Examine the Proof for the Restrictive View
The proof is the athar of ash-Sha'bi: 'if the sultan compels him, it is valid; if thieves compel him, it is not.' This seems to limit valid ikrah to the ruler.
This athar is a qawl sahabi (saying of a companion) and is zanni al-thubut.
3
3. Analyse the Hanafi Response and the Tarjih
Later scholars responded that ash-Sha'bi's statement was specific to his time when thieves were not powerful. With changing circumstances ('urf), thieves and other powerful persons now qualify. The general principles from Quran and other athar (Umar, Shuraih) do not restrict the compeller to the ruler.
The tarjih is based on the applicability of the hukm to contemporary contexts; the later position is mufta bihi.
4
4. Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
In a scenario where a thief threatens to kill a man unless he divorces his wife, under Abu Hanifa's first view the divorce would be invalid (no ikrah from a thief). Under the mufta bihi, the divorce is valid due to ikrah.
This difference directly impacts the validity of acts like divorce, freeing slaves, and contracts performed under duress.

Divorce and Manumission Under Coercion: Validity
Ruling on the validity of divorce or manumission under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on divorce and manumission under coercion and explain the proof from the hadith of the three serious matters.

Core Idea
Divorce and manumission take effect immediately upon utterance, even under coercion or jest.

In Hanafi law, certain personal status acts are considered effective solely by their utterance, irrespective of the speaker's intent. This lesson examines the ruling when such acts are performed under coercion, clarifying why they remain valid despite the lack of free consent. The key distinction is between contracts of exchange, where coercion invalidates, and acts of personal status, where utterance alone suffices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage rules on two acts: divorce (talaq) and manumission (itq) when performed under coercion. The ruling is that both are valid and take effect immediately.
The act is sahih (valid) and effective.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is the hadith from Abu Hurairah: 'Three things, their seriousness is serious and their jest is serious: marriage, divorce, and return (raj'ah).' This is a hadith ahad, but accepted in Hanafi usul.
Hadith reference: Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal implication)
The hadith equates jest with seriousness for these acts, meaning intention does not affect validity. Coercion is analogous to jest—both involve a lack of genuine will. If jest is valid, coercion is even more so, as coercion involves a stronger motive.
The reasoning is qiyas a priori: ma la yadkhulahu al-naqd (acts that do not accept revocation) are effective by utterance alone.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly difference)
Other schools, particularly the Shafi'is, may not accept this analogy. They require full intent (niyyah) for divorce, and coercion negates intent. Thus they rule that a coerced divorce is not effective.
The thamarah al-khilaf is practical: under Shafi'i law, the wife remains married; under Hanafi, she is divorced.

Recovering Compensation from the Coercer
States that the coerced person recovers compensation from the coercer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that the coercer must indemnify the coerced for the slave's value and half the dower, and apply the underlying qiyas to analogous cases of coercion.

Core Idea
The coercer indemnifies the coerced for all losses directly caused by the coerced act.

When a person is forced to free his slave or divorce his wife, Hanafi law holds the act valid but places financial liability on the coercer. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the proof from the athar of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, and the analogical reasoning that extends the principle to new cases. Understanding this teaches the broader Hanafi approach to causation (ta'diyya) in financial obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the coerced manumission of a slave and the coerced divorce before consummation. The ruling: the coercer must pay the full market value of the slave and half the stipulated dower.
Note that the coerced act itself is valid (the divorce takes effect), but the financial loss falls on the coercer.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author cites an athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i (a tabi'i) that coerced divorce is valid, implying the coercer is liable. Additionally, a hadith about the co-owner freeing a slave is used as supporting evidence for the principle that the one who causes the loss must compensate.
The athar is from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba and Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof establishes the ruling)
From the athar: since coerced divorce is valid, the husband incurs the obligation to pay half the dower (if before consummation) and loses the marital benefit. The coercer caused this loss, so he must indemnify. Similarly, for the slave: the coerced man freed the slave unwillingly, so the coercer caused the loss of the slave's value.
The hadith about the co-owner freeing a slave establishes that the one who causes the loss (by freeing without permission) must pay the other co-owner's share. Analogously, the coercer is the effective cause.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
The Shafi'i school holds that coerced divorce is invalid, so no divorce occurs and no compensation arises. The Hanafi school, relying on the athar, maintains validity and thus liability.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Shafi'i law, the coercer would not be liable for the dower; under Hanafi, he is.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preferred position)
The Hanafi position is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and is relied upon for fatwa. The athar is considered strong evidence, and qiyas supports the conclusion.
The authority weight is zahir_riwayah, and the cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.

Commentary
This passage states a ruling of compensation in cases of coercion. Coercion in Divorce and Manumission
Ruling on coerced divorce and right to recover compensation from coercer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced divorce and manumission, explain the evidence, and apply the compensation rule.

Core Idea
Coerced divorce takes effect, but the coerced recovers losses from the coercer.

In this lesson, we examine a classic Hanafi ruling: a person forced to divorce his wife or free a slave—does the act count? The answer may surprise those familiar with other schools. We will explore the proof, the counter-argument from Imam Shafi'i, and the unique Hanafi compensation mechanism.

Commentary
This passage from the book of coercion (Kitab al-Ikrah) presents a ruling with its proofs and a rebuttal of the opposing school. The main ruling is that if a person is coerced into divorcing his wife or freeing a slave, such divorce or manumission is valid and takes effect according to the Hanafi school. The Hanafi response is that the coerced person actively chose to save his life by divorcing, so it is a voluntary act. Similarly, the coercer is the real cause of the loss. The passage also includes a mention of a partner freeing a jointly-owned slave, where the freeing partner must compensate the other partner, based on the hadith of Ibn Umar. The overall lesson reinforces the Hanafi principle that coercion does not invalidate the act of the coerced, but the coerced may seek recompense from the coercer.

Coerced Apostasy and the Marriage Bond
Ruling that coerced apostasy does not invalidate marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that coerced apostasy does not invalidate marriage, citing the Quranic verse 16:106 and the hadith of Ammar ibn Yasir.

Core Idea
Coerced utterance of kufr does not break the marriage bond — the heart is what counts.

This passage tackles a critical issue: does forced apostasy immediately dissolve the marriage? The Hanafi school, drawing from the Quran and the Prophet’s guidance to Ammar ibn Yasir, holds that true apostasy requires inner conviction. Therefore, a person coerced into uttering words of disbelief remains a Muslim in the eyes of the law, and his marriage stands. We will examine the proofs and the underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
What is the ruling on marriage when a person is coerced into apostasy? The passage states the wife does not separate.
The key is the distinction between external utterance and internal belief.
2
Analyze the primary proof: Quran 16:106
Verse 106 of Surah an-Nahl explicitly states that one who is forced while his heart is at peace with faith is not a disbeliever. This establishes the principle.
The verse is clear: coercion does not make one an apostate if the heart is firm.
3
Review the secondary proof: Hadith of Ammar ibn Yasir
The Prophet told Ammar that if forced again, he could repeat the words, indicating his faith and marriage remained intact.
This hadith is a practical application of the verse.
4
Derive the ruling on marriage
Since the person is not a true apostate, the marriage is not dissolved. The term 'tubin' (bainunah) does not apply.
This ruling is specific to Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage addresses a scenario where a person is coerced into uttering words of apostasy. The ruling is clear: such a coerced statement does not constitute actual apostasy, because true apostasy requires heartfelt conviction. Therefore, the marriage of a coerced individual remains valid, and his wife does not become divorced or separated. The primary proof is the Quranic verse 16:106, which explicitly excludes those who are coerced while their hearts remain firm in faith. This is known as ikrah on riddah, and it does not dissolve the marriage. The passage also references the well-known incident of Ammar ibn Yasir, who was tortured and forced to utter kufr; the Prophet (peace be upon him) affirmed his faith and told him that if coerced again, he could repeat the words outwardly. This hadith confirms that the marriage is not affected. The term 'tubin' (from 'bayn') implies complete separation; since coerced apostasy does not constitute actual riddah, such separation does not occur.

Key Terms
ردة (riddah): Apostasy from Islam, which normally dissolves the marriage immediately.
بائن (ba'in): A form of divorce that is irrevocable; the wife is immediately separated from the husband without possibility of return except through a new marriage (if during 'iddah, only if they are in iddah of a revocable divorce, but here it is instantaneous).

Ruling on Apostate Who Joins Dar al-Harb: Debts and Property
Establishes legal death of apostate who joins enemy territory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the legal status of an apostate who joins Dar al-Harb, and explain how his debts are paid and his property distributed, citing the athar evidence.

Core Idea
An apostate who joins enemy territory is legally dead; debts become due and estate goes to Muslim heirs.

This lesson covers a critical area of Hanafi fiqh: the treatment of an apostate who not only leaves Islam but also physically joins the enemy camp (Dar al-Harb). The ruling establishes legal death, which has immediate effects on debts, inheritance, and family relations. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly applying the law in cases of apostasy with flight to enemy territory, and it distinguishes the Hanafi position from other schools that wait for actual death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an apostate (murtadd) who flees to Dar al-Harb and is declared by the judge as having joined the enemy. The ruling is that he becomes like a dead person — his legal personality ends.
This legal death is retroactively applied from the moment of apostasy if he does not return.
2
State the Hanafi position on debt payment
Debts incurred during his Islamic period are paid from wealth earned during Islam. Debts incurred during his apostasy are paid from wealth earned during apostasy. Any deferred debts become immediately payable (hallat).
This mirrors the rule for a deceased person: debts must be settled before inheritance.
3
State the Hanafi position on property distribution
Wealth earned during Islam is distributed among his Muslim heirs. The wife receives her share and becomes divorced (ba'in). Mudabbar and umm walad slaves become free.
The apostate's own heirs are excluded because he is treated as a non-Muslim enemy.
4
Identify the proof (dalil)
The proof is an athar from 'Amir and Al-Hakam recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah. It describes the 'iddah of the wife and the division of inheritance, indicating immediate legal consequences.
The athar is a statement of a Companion (or tabi'i), considered hujjah in Hanafi usul.
5
Explain the wajh al-dalalah
The athar's command to divide his inheritance among his wife and Muslim heirs shows that his property is distributed immediately upon joining Dar al-Harb, which is only possible if he is legally dead.
The Hanafi school uses this to establish that apostasy with flight to enemy territory is equivalent to death for legal purposes.

Returning Apostate's Right to Reclaim Property from Heirs
Ruling on property rights of a returning apostate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on an apostate's right to reclaim property from heirs upon return and identify the condition of the property being bi-'aynihi.

Core Idea
An apostate returning to Islam reclaims only property still physically present with heirs; consumed property is lost.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: an apostate who flees to enemy territory and is legally declared dead by the judge, then later returns as a Muslim. The central question is whether he can reclaim the property his heirs have taken. The Hanafi ruling distinguishes between property that remains intact and property that has been spent, based on the legal fiction of death and the subsequent reversal of that status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the apostate's return to Islam after the judge's ruling of lahaq. The ruling is that he may take back what he finds of his property in the hands of his heirs, but only if it is still in its original state (bi-'aynihi).
2
Understand the key condition: property must be bi-'aynihi
The term bi-'aynihi means the property is still physically present and identifiable, not consumed or transformed. If the heirs have spent or sold it, the returnee has no claim against them.
This condition prevents unjust enrichment and protects transactions made by the heirs in good faith.
3
Recognize the legal effect of the judge's ruling
Once the judge declares that the apostate has joined dar al-harb, he is treated as legally dead. This validates the heirs' ownership and disposal of the property. They become true owners, not merely custodians.
The ruling of death is a legal construct; the actual return of the apostate reverses the effects prospectively, but past consumption remains valid.
4
Derive the consequence for consumed vs. preserved property
Because the heirs became owners, they are not liable for property they consumed. However, property still in their possession has not yet changed ownership fully; the returnee's dire need and the fact that he earned it justify returning it.
This is a balance between protecting the heirs' completed transactions and the returnee's basic needs.
5
Note the public policy reasoning
The commentary mentions the returnee's 'dire need' (اشد ضرورت) and that the property is 'his earnings' (کمایا ہوا). This highlights the policy of mercy and justice for the repentant Muslim.
The ruling encourages return to Islam by not completely stripping the convert of all past wealth.
