Land Measurement Units in Kharaj
Defines land measurement units and conversions for kharaj.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the equivalence between acre, decimal, and seker (chain) units and calculate the area of a plot in square yards or meters for kharaj assessment.

Core Idea
One acre = 4840 sq yards = 4046.856 sq m; 100 decimals = 1 acre; 10 seker length × 1 seker width = 1 acre.

The calculation of kharaj (land tax) requires accurate measurement of agricultural land. In the Hanafi tradition, the jurists relied on standardized units known in their regions. This lesson explains the practical units used by the author Mir al-Din for Jharkhand: acre, decimal, and the local 'seker' chain. Understanding these conversions is essential for determining the area subject to tax.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the units
Identify the three units: acre, decimal, and seker (chain). Note that 1 acre = 100 decimals, and 1 seker is a chain of length 22 yards (20.1168 m).
2
Convert between units
Use the formulas: area in square yards = length (yards) × width (yards). For acre, 1 acre = 4840 sq yd. Using seker: 10 seker long × 1 seker wide = 1 acre. Calculate in meters similarly.
3
Apply to kharaj
Given a plot's measurements in seker or yards, compute its area in acres and decimals. This area is the basis for kharaj assessment per the Hanafi rules of land tax.
The author provides these conversions for students to verify calculations.

Commentary
This passage is a practical study aid added by the author Mir al-Din to assist students in understanding the land measurement units used in kharaj (land tax) calculations. The author explains that an acre (accepted international unit) equals 4840 square yards or 4046.856 square meters. He then describes the local unit 'seker' (or 'jarib') used in Jharkhand, which is a chain of fixed length (20.1168 meters or 22 yards). Ten seker in length and one seker in width equals one acre. This information is essential for determining the area subject to kharaj and for calculating tax amounts based on productivity. The author emphasizes that students should verify calculations using a calculator.

ایکڑ (ekṛ): A unit of land area equal to 4840 square yards or 4046.856 square meters; used for kharaj assessment.

ڈسمل (dismal): A subunit of area; 100 decimals = 1 acre; 1 decimal = 40.4685 square meters.

Ruling on Marking Verses in the Quran
Ruling on marking verses and diacritics in the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marking verses in the Quran and explain how the ruling changed from makruh to mustahsan due to necessity.

Core Idea
The ruling on marking the Quran shifted from disliked to commendable due to necessity for non-Arab reciters.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on adding verse markers and diacritical points to the Quran. The passage explains the historical context and rationale behind the change in ruling. Understanding this demonstrates how the Hanafi school considers custom and necessity (darurah) in deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act Under Discussion
The passage discusses two related acts: marking every ten verses (al-taʿshīr) and placing diacritical points (nuqat). These are textual aids for recitation.
The act is not an act of worship but a preparatory or aid activity.
2
State the Original Ruling and Its Reason
Initially, the ruling was makruh (disliked) because early Muslims were fluent in Arabic and such aids were unnecessary. The act was seen as an unwarranted addition.
The basis for makruh is the athar (tradition) of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyya, and 'Ata'.
3
Explain the Change in Ruling and Its Cause
When non-Arab converts (ʿajam) needed help with correct recitation, the ruling changed to mustahsan (commendable). Necessity (darurah) justified the change.
This shows how Hanafi fiqh accommodates changing circumstances (taghayyur al-zaman wa al-makan).
4
Identify the Basis for the Ruling: Athar of the Salaf
The passage cites three reports: Ibn Mas'ud disliked al-taʿshīr; Muhammad disliked chapter headings with qaf/kaf; 'Ata' disliked al-taʿshīr. These serve as the primary evidence for the original ruling.
The absence of a direct Quranic or hadith proof indicates the ruling is based on practice of the Salaf.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement on the issue of marking verses and placing diacritical points in the Quran. The author states that it is makruh (disliked). He explains the historical context: early Muslims were fluent in Arabic and did not need such aids, so introducing them was seen as an unwanted innovation. However, as non-Arabs embraced Islam, such markings became necessary for correct recitation, and the ruling shifted to mustahsan (recommended). The proof for the initial ruling is the effect (athar) of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, Muhammad (ibn al-Hanafiyya?), and Ata', who all disliked it. The Hanafi understanding is that although initially makruh, the ruling changed due to necessity (darurah), and now it is commendable.

Key Terms
التعشير (al-taʿshīr): Marking every ten verses in the Quran.
الفواتح (al-fawātiḥ): Chapter headings or opening marks.
العواشر (al-ʿawāshir): Marks indicating groups of ten verses.
