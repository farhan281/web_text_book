Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Swearing the Defendant in Non-Financial Claims
Intra-Hanafi dispute on swearing defendant in non-financial claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the cases where the defendant is not sworn according to Abu Hanifa and explain the underlying principle of badhl vs iqrar.

Core Idea
In non-financial claims, Abu Hanifa does not swear the defendant because refusal is badhl (giving up), not iqrar (admission).

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement about whether a defendant must be sworn in claims involving marriage, lineage, slavery, and similar non-financial matters. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that no oath is administered at all, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) maintain that an oath should be required except in hudud and li'an. The difference stems from how each side interprets the defendant's refusal to take an oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cases where Abu Hanifa does not swear the defendant
From the passage, list the nine types of claims: marriage, return (rujoo'), return from ila', slavery, making a slave an umm walad, lineage, wala', hudud, and li'an.
The Sahibayn accept oaths in all except hudud and li'an.
2
State the two positions on nukool
Abu Hanifa views the defendant's refusal to swear as badhl (giving up the claim without admitting it). The Sahibayn view it as iqrar (admission of the claim).
The text explicitly says these two principles generate the nine cases.
3
Examine the dalil for badhl
Analyze the athar from Hazrat Umar about the tribe of Hudhayl, which illustrates the concept of badhl: the defendant gives up the claim out of respect for Allah's name.
The full narrative involves fifty men swearing; one ransoms his oath, but the principle of giving up the claim is key.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, a marriage claim fails if the defendant refuses to swear. Under the Sahibayn, the refusal itself establishes the marriage.
The practical outcome depends on which interpretation is followed.

Resolving Conflicting Marriage Claims in Hanafi Fiqh
Explaining that marriage claims cannot be split, and the woman's testimony is sought.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two men claim marriage to the same woman with equal evidence and explain why the woman's confirmation is sought.

Core Idea
When marriage claims conflict and evidence is equal, the woman's word decides.

Imagine two men each claim they are married to the same woman. The judge cannot accept both marriages because polyandry is invalid. How does a Hanafi court resolve this? This lesson examines the procedural ruling from the matn and its justification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim Scenario
Note that two men each claim nikah to the same woman and both present evidence (bayanah).
The judge must first verify the evidence is equal and shows no precedence.
2
State the Primary Ruling
The judge does not rule based on either evidence alone; instead, he refers to the woman's confirmation (tasdiq) for one of them.
This is the default when evidence is equally strong.
3
Understand the Rationale
A woman cannot be the wife of two men simultaneously, so she cannot be divided. With no preference between the evidence, the decision rests on her word as the marriage is consensual.
The passage explicitly states: 'فلا يمكن قسمتها ولا مرجح للبينتين'.
4
Recognize the Exception
If one evidence proves the earlier marriage date (سبق النكاح), then judgment is given for the earlier marriage.
Temporal priority provides a clear basis for preference (tarjih).

Resolving Mahr Disputes: Evidence, Preference, and Mutual Oaths
Rulings on mahr dispute: evidence, preference, and oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving a mahr dispute when evidence is presented or absent, and explain the qiyas analogy to sale contracts.

Core Idea
In a mahr dispute, the wife's evidence is preferred if both present; if none, mutual oaths fix mahr al-mithl without dissolving the marriage.

Disputes over the mahr amount are common in marriage. Hanafi fiqh provides a three-tier approach: if only one party produces evidence, their claim is accepted; if both do, the wife's evidence is preferred; if neither has evidence, both take oaths and the court determines mahr al-mithl. This lesson examines the rulings and the qiyas (analogy) to sale contracts that underpin them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three dispute scenarios
Recognize the three possible situations: one party has evidence, both have evidence, or neither has evidence.
The passage also includes a qualification from Al-Hidayah regarding the wife's evidence when mahr al-mithl is less than her claim.
2
Apply the evidence rule
When only one party presents bayyinah, judgment is given in their favor. When both present, the wife's evidence is preferred.
3
Analyze the oath scenario
According to Abu Hanifa, both take oaths without rescinding the marriage, and the court uses mahr al-mithl as a benchmark.
This differs from sale, where mutual oaths lead to rescission.
4
Trace the qiyas to sale
The reasoning analogizes the marriage's subject matter (بضع) and mahr (price) to a sale's commodity and price, making each spouse both a claimant and defendant.
5
Apply mahr al-mithl
If mahr al-mithl equals or is less than the husband's admission, his claim stands; if it equals or exceeds the wife's claim, hers stands.
The passage ends mid-sentence noting a case where mahr al-mithl is more than that.

Commentary
This passage provides the rulings for a dispute between spouses over the amount of mahr (dower). The text presents three scenarios. First, if only one party produces evidence (بينة), their evidence is accepted and judgment follows it. Second, if both produce evidence, the wife's evidence is preferred. Third, if neither has evidence, both take oaths (تحالف) according to Abu Hanifa, but the marriage is not dissolved; instead, the court determines مهر المثل (mahr of the like). The mahr al-mithl then serves as a benchmark: if it equals or is less than the husband's admission, the husband's claim is upheld; if it equals or exceeds the wife's claim, the wife's claim is upheld. The commentary explains the reasoning by drawing an analogy to the previous sale case: just as in a sale the subject matter (مبیع) and price (ثمن) are disputed, here the woman's vulva (بضع) is the subject matter and the mahr the price. Thus both spouses are simultaneously claimants and defendants, depending on which element is considered. The wife claims a higher mahr, making her the claimant regarding the price; the husband claims the vulva for a lower price, making him the claimant regarding the subject matter. This dual perspective leads to the rule that whichever party provides evidence wins, and if both provide evidence, the wife's evidence prevails because she is the real claimant (she seeks the excess). The commentary also notes a qualification from Al-Hidayah: the wife's evidence is preferred only when مهر المثل is less than what she claims, because she is proving something beyond the apparent norm.

Key Terms
مهر (mahr): The dower or bridal gift given by the husband to the wife in marriage, which becomes her exclusive property.
مهر المثل (mahr al-mithl): The mahr of the like: the amount customary for a woman of similar status, used when the specified mahr is disputed or invalid.
بينة (bayyinah): Evidence, typically two male witnesses or one male and two females, that proves a claim in court.
تحالف (taḥāluf): Mutual oaths taken by both parties in a dispute when neither has evidence; in marriage, it does not dissolve the contract.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when spouses dispute the mahr without evidence and apply the procedures of tahaluf and mahr al-mithl.

Core Idea
When spouses disagree on mahr without proof, both swear oaths; marriage stays valid, and the wife gets mahr al-mithl.

This passage addresses a common marital dispute: when no dower was fixed and spouses disagree on its amount. The Hanafi school provides a balanced method—both parties take oaths, and if both swear, the marriage remains sound and the wife receives her customary dower. This preserves the nikah while ensuring justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and its context
Recognize that the marriage occurred without a fixed mahr, and now the spouses disagree on the amount. The court must resolve this without evidence.
This is a common scenario in marriages where mahr is deferred or not specified.
2
Apply the rule of tahaluf
According to Abu Hanifa, when there is no evidence, each spouse takes an oath because each is both a claimant and a defendant. This is the procedure of mutual oath-taking.
Tahaluf is also used in sale and ijarah disputes.
3
Determine the consequence of the oaths
If one refuses to swear, judgment goes against that party. If both swear, the fixed mahr falls away, but the marriage is not annulled. Instead, mahr al-mithl becomes obligatory.
This distinguishes nikah from sale, where such a dispute would rescind the contract.
4
Calculate mahr al-mithl
The judge awards the customary dower based on the woman's family status—considering age, beauty, wealth, and lineage. This aligns with the principle that when no dower is fixed, the default is mahr al-mithl.
If the mahr al-mithl matches one spouse's claim, that claim is preferred; otherwise, the mahr al-mithl itself is awarded.
5
Cite the evidentiary basis
The ruling is supported by a hadith in Ibn Majah that judges use a predominant indicator (qarinah rajihah) when witnesses are absent.
This hadith provides the basis for tahaluf and awarding mahr al-mithl.

Dispute in Kitabah: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mutual oath in kitabah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on mutual oath in kitabah disputes and explain the underlying legal reasoning regarding the nature of the kitabah contract.

Core Idea
Kitabah's legal nature determines oath procedure: non-binding slave contract (Abu Hanifa) vs commutative contract (Sahibayn).

In this lesson, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning how to resolve a disagreement between a master and his mukatib over the amount of the kitabah payment. The core question is whether mutual oath (tahaluf) applies, which hinges on the legal characterization of the kitabah contract. Abu Hanifa views it as a non-binding arrangement due to the slave's status, while the Sahibayn analogize it to a sale contract. Understanding this dispute sharpens our grasp of how legal analogies (qiyas) and the nature of contractual obligations influence procedural rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
Master and mukatib disagree on the kitabah amount (master claims 1000, mukatib claims 500). The question is whose word is accepted and whether mutual oath applies.
The dispute is over the quantity of the kitabah wealth, not the fact of the contract.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and reasoning
No mutual oath; mukatib's oath taken because he is the defendant (master is claimant). Reasoning: kitabah is not a binding contract; mukatib is still a slave, his wealth belongs to master, so contract is not like sale.
Abu Hanifa emphasizes the slave's status and the non-binding nature of kitabah.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling and reasoning
Mutual oath applies and contract is rescinded. Reasoning: kitabah is a commutative contract like sale, with freedom as commodity and kitabah wealth as price; dispute over price triggers mutual oath.
The Sahibayn analogize kitabah to sale (bay').
4
Compare the two views
Identify the core difference: the nature of kitabah (non-binding vs binding commutative). Also note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf): under Abu Hanifa, mukatib's oath prevails; under Sahibayn, both oaths lead to rescission.
The practical outcome differs significantly.
5
Note the later tarjih
Although not in this text, the later Hanafi fatwa typically follows the Sahibayn view for stronger analogy with sale.
Mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's position.

Dispute over Household Items: Classification and Presumption
Ruling on ownership when spouses disagree

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify household items into three categories and apply the Hanafi presumption that common items belong to the husband in the absence of evidence.

Core Idea
Custom defines exclusive use; house ownership shifts common items to the husband.

When a husband and wife dispute over household items and neither has proof, the Hanafi school provides a clear classification based on customary usage and a default presumption. This ruling helps judges resolve such conflicts without unnecessary paperwork, relying on objective criteria and the known fact that the house belongs to the husband.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute context
Recognize that this ruling applies only when spouses disagree over who owns specific household items and there are no witnesses or other clear evidence (qarinah). The judge must decide without direct proof.
The ruling assumes the house is owned by the husband, which serves as a qarinah for the contents.
2
Classify each disputed item by customary use
Determine whether the item is exclusively used by men, exclusively used by women, or can be used by both. This is based on common practice (urf) in that time and place.
Examples: turban, men's shalwar (men); jewelry, women's clothing (women); phone, car (common).
3
Apply the default presumption for common items
For items that could be used by either spouse, the ruling gives them to the husband because the house is his. The principle is that ownership of the container (the house) indicates ownership of its contents.
This is a default; if the wife could provide a qarinah, the result might differ.

Coercion and the Validity of Marriage
Ruling on coerced apostasy and its effect on marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced apostasy and its effect on marriage, and identify the Quranic and hadith proofs that support it.

Core Idea
Coerced disbelief with a firm heart does not break the marriage.

Marriage is a sacred bond that can be dissolved by apostasy. But what if a person is forced to utter words of disbelief under duress? Does the marriage automatically break? In this lesson, you will learn the Hanafi position: as long as the heart remains firmly settled in faith, the marriage remains valid. We will examine the primary proofs from the Quran and Sunnah that establish this ruling, and understand the critical condition of inner conviction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the specific act being ruled upon? It is a person who is coerced into uttering words of disbelief (kufr). What is the hukm on their marriage? The source states 'the marriage will not be broken' – it remains valid (sahih).
The act is coerced speech, not actual apostasy.
2
Locate the dalil
What proofs does the author cite? First, Quran 16:106, which contains an exception for those coerced while their hearts are content with faith. Second, the hadith of Ammar ibn Yasir, where the Prophet (peace be upon him) permitted him to repeat such words under compulsion.
Both proofs are primary textual evidences.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How exactly does the Quranic verse prove the ruling? The exception clause (illa man ukriha wa qalbuhu mutma'in bil iman) removes the legal consequence of apostasy (and thus the dissolution of marriage) from one who is coerced. Since actual apostasy would nullify marriage, its absence means the marriage remains intact.
The exception is explicit and operative.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Is there disagreement? Some schools may hold that any utterance of kufr, even under coercion, dissolves the marriage. The Hanafi school, however, relies on the clear Quranic exception and the precedent of Ammar.
The Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih but firmly grounded.

A male slave is like a foreign man to his mistress; he may only see her face and hands.

This passage addresses the limits of a male slave's gaze towards his mistress. While some might assume that close familiarity would permit greater exposure, the Hanafi school firmly rules that the slave is legally a foreign man. The Ruling of 'Azl with a Slave Woman
Ruling on 'azl with slave woman without permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'azl with a slave woman and identify the proofs from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
'Azl with one's slave woman is permissible without her consent, based on the practice of the Companions and the statement of Ibn Abbas.

This passage addresses a specific aspect of marital intimacy: coitus interruptus with a slave woman. While 'azl with a free wife requires her consent in the Hanafi school, the rules differ for a slave woman. The author provides two proofs: a hadith showing general permissibility of 'azl, and an athar from Ibn Abbas specifically exempting the slave woman from consultation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the matn (ويعزل عن امته بغير اذنها) and note the explicit permission to practice 'azl without the slave woman's consent.
The ruling is مطلق الإباحة (absolute permissibility).
2
Examine the Hadith of Jabir
The hadith 'كنا نعزل والقرآن ينزل' shows that the Companions practiced 'azl during revelation without prohibition, establishing general permissibility.
This is a proof for permissibility of 'azl itself, not specific to slave women.
3
Examine the Athar of Ibn Abbas
The athar 'تستأمر الحرة في العزل ولا تستأمر الأمة' directly states that a free woman is consulted but a slave woman is not. This provides the specific distinction.
This athar is the basis for the different ruling for slave women.
4
Understand the Distinction
The difference is based on the rights and status of a slave woman versus a free wife in Hanafi fiqh. A slave woman does not have full ownership of her reproductive capacity.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school and reflects the legal capacity (أهلية) of a slave.

Ruling on 'Azl with a Free Wife
Ruling with proof from prophetic hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'azl with a free wife and cite the hadith of Umar as proof.

Core Idea
A man may not practice 'azl with his free wife without her permission.

This lesson examines the ruling for free wives, which is opposite to that for slave-girls. The proof is an explicit prophetic prohibition, emphasizing the wife's right to sexual satisfaction.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the act ('azl) and the subject (free wife). Note the ruling: it is prohibited (haram) without her permission.
Use the Arabic: 'ولا يعزل عن زوجته الا باذنها'.
2
Locate the proof
Find the primary dalil – the hadith from Umar: 'The Messenger of Allah forbade performing 'azl with a free woman except with her permission.'
This is a hadith with a chain, considered zanni al-thubut but reliable.
3
Explain the legal rationale
Understand that the wife has a contractual right to sexual satisfaction (haqq al-wat'i). Practicing 'azl without her permission denies her pleasure, which is her right.
The text also mentions that the husband should ensure the wife's satisfaction first.
4
Contrast with the slave-girl
Recall that a slave-girl has no such right, so permission is not required. This contrast highlights the importance of the woman's status in Islamic law.
This was covered in the previous lesson.
