Conditions for Hunting with Predatory Animals
First method: hunting with predatory animals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for hunting with a trained predatory animal and explain the training criterion for a dog.

Core Idea
A dog is trained if it leaves prey uneaten three times; if it eats, the prey is haram.

This lesson unpacks the first method of hunting using predatory animals like dogs and cheetahs. The Hanafi school establishes strict conditions based on the Quranic verse and the practice of the Companions. Understanding these conditions is crucial for determining the halal status of game caught by trained animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
List the conditions for hunting with a predatory animal: trained animal (mu'allim), saying Bismillah at release, and the animal not eating from the prey.
The first condition requires the animal to be trained; the second is verbal; the third concerns the animal's action after catching.
2
Understand the training criterion for dogs
A dog is considered trained if it catches prey three times and does not eat from it, leaving it for the owner. This is established by the athar of Ibn Abbas.
The three-time requirement ensures that the dog's behaviour is not coincidental.
3
Apply the rule: what if the animal eats?
If the predatory animal eats from the prey after catching it, the prey becomes haram for the owner. An exception is made if the prey is wounded in a way that constitutes emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari).
The exception applies when the animal wounds the prey, making it like a slaughter.
4
Connect to the Quranic proof
Surah al-Ma'idah 5:4 provides the basis: the imperative 'fakulu' indicates permissibility, 'mimma amsakna 'alaykum' implies the animal catches for the owner without eating, and 'wadhkuru isma Allahi' requires saying Bismillah.
The verse is the primary dalil, supported by athar.

Book of Hunting. (2) The training of a dog is that it leaves eating three times, and the training of a falcon is that it returns when called. Hunting with trained animals is permissible. The detail of hunting is that there are three methods: (1) By means of predatory animals such as a dog, cheetah. For hunting with them, there are three conditions. First, the dog must be trained. The method of training a dog is that it catches prey three times and does not eat its skin, flesh, bones, etc., but leaves it for the owner; then in the eyes of Shari'ah, the dog is considered trained. This is the method for training all predatory animals. The second condition is that the animal is released with Bismillah. The third condition is that the predatory animal does not eat from the prey after catching it. In such a case, if the animal catches the prey and the prey dies before being slaughtered, that prey is halal. And if the prey was alive and the owner caught it and had the opportunity to slaughter it but did not, then it is not halal. And if there was no opportunity to slaughter and the prey died, then it is halal. So if the predatory animal eats the prey after catching it, then it is not halal for the owner. And in one narration, it is that it wounded the prey somewhere, which becomes like emergency slaughter and not like strangulation. The second method of hunting is by means of a bird such as a falcon, hawk, etc. There are three conditions. One is that the bird is trained. The method of training it is that when released it goes for prey and when stopped it stops. Doing this three times makes it trained in the eyes of Shari'ah. Unlike the dog, the criterion for its training is not based on eating or not eating. The reason is that the bird is a wild animal; so if it comes back repeatedly when called and abandons its natural wildness, that is the sign of its training. And the dog is a domestic animal that comes around people. However, after catching prey it tries to eat, so if it abandons its natural habit and does not eat three times for the owner, that is the sign of its being trained. The second condition is that it is released upon the prey with Bismillah. Now if it eats from the prey, it is still halal for the owner. However, if after the prey comes into hand, there is an opportunity to slaughter it and he does not, then it is not halal for the owner. And if there was no opportunity to slaughter and it died, then the prey is halal. And according to one narration, the third condition is that it made a wound, because in the verse it says 'and what you have taught of hunting animals', and 'jawarih' means wounding. The third method of hunting is by means of an arrow or gun. There are three conditions. One is that the arrow is shot with Bismillah. The second condition is that the part of the arrow that hits the prey is sharp. If a non-sharp part hits and it dies, then the prey is not halal because it becomes mawqudhah (killed by a blunt object) which is forbidden in the verse. The third condition is that the prey is wounded in some part. The proofs for all these will come later. Definition: Mu'allim: trained; al-Jawarih: derived from jarh, meaning wounding; al-Fahd: cheetah; al-Bazi: a type of hunting bird. (2) And the training of a dog is that it leaves eating three times, and the training of a falcon is that it returns if called. It has passed above that the training of a dog is that it hunts three times and does not eat the prey in all three times. The dog wants to eat after hunting, and if it does not eat against its nature, then it has become trained. (2) There is an athar: from Ibn Abbas, he said: 'If a dog eats from the prey, it is not trained' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, etc.). From this athar it is known that a dog that eats from the prey is not trained. The restriction of three times is to ensure experience, because once or twice it might not eat coincidentally. As for the bird and falcon, their not being trained is that when called, it immediately returns. The reason is that the falcon and bird are wild. So when it returns upon being called, it has abandoned its nature and become trained and mu'allim. (2) There is an athar: from Hammad, he said: 'If the bird plucks feathers or eats, then eat, for its training is that it returns to you' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah). Another narration: from 'Amir and al-Hakam, they said: 'If you release your hawk or falcon, then call it and it comes to you, that is its knowledge. Then if you release it upon prey and it eats, then eat' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah). From this athar two things are known: One is that the training of a falcon and bird is that when called, it returns to you. The second thing is that eating or not eating the prey is not the criterion for the bird's training. Therefore, if the bird eats the prey, it is still halal to eat, because eating or not eating is not the criterion for learning. (3) Then if you release your trained dog or falcon or hawk upon prey, and at the time of release you say Bismillah upon it, then it catches and wounds the prey, and the prey dies, then eating it is halal. You release the trained dog or falcon or hawk upon prey. At the time of release, the owner says Bismillah upon it. Now the dog or falcon catches the prey and tears it, provided the dog does not eat from it, and the prey dies. Then even if it was not slaughtered, its eating is halal. According to the verse, the animal is trained. Bismillah was said upon it, so its tearing is an emergency slaughter. Therefore this much slaughter is sufficient for halalness. (2) In this issue there are three things: releasing a trained animal, releasing with Bismillah, and the animal tearing the prey so it dies. The proof for all three is present in the verse: 'They ask you what is made lawful for them. Say: Good things are made lawful for you, and what you have taught of hunting animals, training them as dogs, teaching them from what Allah has taught you; so eat of what they catch for you, and mention the name of Allah upon it.' Commentary
This passage from Al Sharh Ul Sameri is a commentary on the rules of hunting (Kitab al-Sayd). It opens by stating the general permissibility of hunting with trained animals, then provides a detailed breakdown of three methods with their conditions and proofs from Quran and athar. The author first discusses hunting with predatory animals such as dogs and cheetahs. The key conditions are: the animal must be trained (mu'allim), the hunter must say Bismillah when releasing it, and the animal must not eat from the prey. The training of a dog is established by it refusing to eat the prey three times, leaving it for the owner. If the animal eats, the prey becomes haram. An exception is made if the prey is wounded in a way that constitutes emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari). The second method is hunting with birds of prey like falcons and hawks. The training criterion differs: a bird is considered trained if it returns when called, regardless of whether it eats the prey. The condition of saying Bismillah still applies. If the bird eats, the prey remains halal, unlike a dog. The third method is hunting with arrows or firearms. The conditions are: saying Bismillah, the projectile must strike with a sharp part, and it must wound the prey. If the prey is killed by a blunt impact (mawqudhah), it is haram. The passage then presents supporting athar (reports from Companions and early scholars). The athar from Ibn Abbas states that a dog that eats from prey is not trained, establishing the three-time training requirement. The athar from Hammad and from 'Amir and al-Hakam clarify that a bird's training is indicated by returning when called, not by abstaining from eating. Finally, the author cites Quran 5:4 as the primary proof for the three conditions: the animal must be trained (mu'allim), the hunter must mention Allah's name, and the animal must catch and wound the prey, making its killing equivalent to slaughter. This verse is the foundational dalil for Hanafi rulings on hunting with trained animals.

Key Terms
معلم (mu'allim): A trained hunting animal that obeys commands and leaves prey for its owner.
جوارح (jawarih): Hunting animals that wound prey, derived from 'jarh' meaning wound.

Conditions for Hunting with Birds of Prey
Second method: hunting with birds of prey.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the training criterion for a bird of prey and distinguish it from that of a dog, and state the ruling when the bird eats the prey.

Core Idea
A bird is trained if it returns when called; it may eat the prey and still be halal.

This lesson covers the second method of hunting: using birds of prey such as falcons and hawks. The Hanafi school treats birds differently from dogs due to their wild nature. The training criterion is based on the bird's obedience to return when called, not on abstaining from eating. This has important implications for the permissibility of the prey.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions for birds of prey
There are three conditions: the bird must be trained (mu'allim), Bismillah must be said at release, and the bird must wound the prey. Unlike dogs, the bird may eat from the prey.
The third condition about wounding is based on the meaning of 'jawarih' (wounding).
2
Understand the training criterion for birds
A bird is trained if, when called, it returns to the hunter. This is based on athar from Hammad and from 'Amir and al-Hakam. The bird's wild nature means that obedience to return is the true sign of training.
The athar explicitly state that eating does not negate training for birds.
3
Apply: what if the bird eats the prey?
If the bird eats from the prey after catching it, the prey remains halal. This is because the training criterion does not involve abstaining from eating. The hunter is allowed to eat the prey even if the bird consumed part of it.
This is a clear difference from predatory animals.
4
Connect to the evidence
The athar from Hammad says: 'If the bird plucks feathers or eats, then eat, for its training is that it returns to you.' The athar from 'Amir and al-Hakam similarly state that if the bird comes when called, that is its training, and if it eats, still eat.
These athar are the basis for the Hanafi distinction.

Book of Hunting. (2) The training of a dog is that it leaves eating three times, and the training of a falcon is that it returns when called. Hunting with trained animals is permissible. The detail of hunting is that there are three methods: (1) By means of predatory animals such as a dog, cheetah. For hunting with them, there are three conditions. First, the dog must be trained. The method of training a dog is that it catches prey three times and does not eat its skin, flesh, bones, etc., but leaves it for the owner; then in the eyes of Shari'ah, the dog is considered trained. This is the method for training all predatory animals. The second condition is that the animal is released with Bismillah. The third condition is that the predatory animal does not eat from the prey after catching it. In such a case, if the animal catches the prey and the prey dies before being slaughtered, that prey is halal. And if the prey was alive and the owner caught it and had the opportunity to slaughter it but did not, then it is not halal. And if there was no opportunity to slaughter and the prey died, then it is halal. So if the predatory animal eats the prey after catching it, then it is not halal for the owner. And in one narration, it is that it wounded the prey somewhere, which becomes like emergency slaughter and not like strangulation. The second method of hunting is by means of a bird such as a falcon, hawk, etc. There are three conditions. One is that the bird is trained. The method of training it is that when released it goes for prey and when stopped it stops. Doing this three times makes it trained in the eyes of Shari'ah. Unlike the dog, the criterion for its training is not based on eating or not eating. The reason is that the bird is a wild animal; so if it comes back repeatedly when called and abandons its natural wildness, that is the sign of its training. And the dog is a domestic animal that comes around people. However, after catching prey it tries to eat, so if it abandons its natural habit and does not eat three times for the owner, that is the sign of its being trained. The second condition is that it is released upon the prey with Bismillah. Now if it eats from the prey, it is still halal for the owner. However, if after the prey comes into hand, there is an opportunity to slaughter it and he does not, then it is not halal for the owner. And if there was no opportunity to slaughter and it died, then the prey is halal. And according to one narration, the third condition is that it made a wound, because in the verse it says 'and what you have taught of hunting animals', and 'jawarih' means wounding. The third method of hunting is by means of an arrow or gun. There are three conditions. One is that the arrow is shot with Bismillah. The second condition is that the part of the arrow that hits the prey is sharp. If a non-sharp part hits and it dies, then the prey is not halal because it becomes mawqudhah (killed by a blunt object) which is forbidden in the verse. The third condition is that the prey is wounded in some part. The proofs for all these will come later. Definition: Mu'allim: trained; al-Jawarih: derived from jarh, meaning wounding; al-Fahd: cheetah; al-Bazi: a type of hunting bird. (2) And the training of a dog is that it leaves eating three times, and the training of a falcon is that it returns if called. It has passed above that the training of a dog is that it hunts three times and does not eat the prey in all three times. The dog wants to eat after hunting, and if it does not eat against its nature, then it has become trained. (2) There is an athar: from Ibn Abbas, he said: 'If a dog eats from the prey, it is not trained' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, etc.). From this athar it is known that a dog that eats from the prey is not trained. The restriction of three times is to ensure experience, because once or twice it might not eat coincidentally. As for the bird and falcon, their not being trained is that when called, it immediately returns. The reason is that the falcon and bird are wild. So when it returns upon being called, it has abandoned its nature and become trained and mu'allim. (2) There is an athar: from Hammad, he said: 'If the bird plucks feathers or eats, then eat, for its training is that it returns to you' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah). Another narration: from 'Amir and al-Hakam, they said: 'If you release your hawk or falcon, then call it and it comes to you, that is its knowledge. Then if you release it upon prey and it eats, then eat' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah). From this athar two things are known: One is that the training of a falcon and bird is that when called, it returns to you. The second thing is that eating or not eating the prey is not the criterion for the bird's training. Therefore, if the bird eats the prey, it is still halal to eat, because eating or not eating is not the criterion for learning. (3) Then if you release your trained dog or falcon or hawk upon prey, and at the time of release you say Bismillah upon it, then it catches and wounds the prey, and the prey dies, then eating it is halal. You release the trained dog or falcon or hawk upon prey. At the time of release, the owner says Bismillah upon it. Now the dog or falcon catches the prey and tears it, provided the dog does not eat from it, and the prey dies. Then even if it was not slaughtered, its eating is halal. According to the verse, the animal is trained. Bismillah was said upon it, so its tearing is an emergency slaughter. Therefore this much slaughter is sufficient for halalness. (2) In this issue there are three things: releasing a trained animal, releasing with Bismillah, and the animal tearing the prey so it dies. The proof for all three is present in the verse: 'They ask you what is made lawful for them. Say: Good things are made lawful for you, and what you have taught of hunting animals, training them as dogs, teaching them from what Allah has taught you; so eat of what they catch for you, and mention the name of Allah upon it.' Commentary
This passage from Al Sharh Ul Sameri is a commentary on the rules of hunting (Kitab al-Sayd). It opens by stating the general permissibility of hunting with trained animals, then provides a detailed breakdown of three methods with their conditions and proofs from Quran and athar. The author first discusses hunting with predatory animals such as dogs and cheetahs. The key conditions are: the animal must be trained (mu'allim), the hunter must say Bismillah when releasing it, and the animal must not eat from the prey. The training of a dog is established by it refusing to eat the prey three times, leaving it for the owner. If the animal eats, the prey becomes haram. An exception is made if the prey is wounded in a way that constitutes emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari). The second method is hunting with birds of prey like falcons and hawks. The training criterion differs: a bird is considered trained if it returns when called, regardless of whether it eats the prey. The condition of saying Bismillah still applies. If the bird eats, the prey remains halal, unlike a dog. The third method is hunting with arrows or firearms. The conditions are: saying Bismillah, the projectile must strike with a sharp part, and it must wound the prey. If the prey is killed by a blunt impact (mawqudhah), it is haram. The passage then presents supporting athar (reports from Companions and early scholars). The athar from Ibn Abbas states that a dog that eats from prey is not trained, establishing the three-time training requirement. The athar from Hammad and from 'Amir and al-Hakam clarify that a bird's training is indicated by returning when called, not by abstaining from eating. Finally, the author cites Quran 5:4 as the primary proof for the three conditions: the animal must be trained (mu'allim), the hunter must mention Allah's name, and the animal must catch and wound the prey, making its killing equivalent to slaughter. This verse is the foundational dalil for Hanafi rulings on hunting with trained animals.

Conditions for Hunting with Arrows and Firearms

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for hunting with a projectile weapon and explain why a blunt strike makes the prey haram (mawqudhah).

Core Idea
The projectile must wound with a sharp part; a blunt kill is mawqudhah and haram.

This lesson covers the third method of hunting: using arrows, guns, or other projectiles. The Hanafi school requires that the projectile strike with a sharp part that wounds the prey, as killing by blunt force is forbidden in the Quran. Understanding these conditions ensures that the meat is lawful.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions for projectiles
The three conditions are: saying Bismillah when shooting, the projectile striking with a sharp part (e.g., arrowhead), and the projectile wounding the prey in some part.
The second condition excludes blunt impacts like a club or a broadside hit.
2
Understand the prohibition of mawqudhah
Mawqudhah refers to prey killed by a blunt object without wounding. This is explicitly forbidden in the Quran (Surah al-Ma'idah 5:3 as part of carrion). The Hanafi school applies this to hunting arrows that hit with a non-sharp part.
The verse lists mawqudhah among prohibited meats.
3

The criterion is that the fatal hit must be from a sharp part. If the prey dies from the impact of a blunt part, it is haram. If both sharp and blunt parts hit, and the wound is fatal, it may be halal if the wound is considered the cause of death.
The text mentions that if a non-sharp part hits and the prey dies, it is haram.
4
Connect to the Quranic proof
The primary proof is the general permission in Surah al-Ma'idah 5:4 for hunting with trained animals and projectiles, combined with the prohibition of mawqudhah in 5:3. The condition of wounding is derived from the word 'jawarih' (wounding).

Commentary
This passage from Al Sharh Ul Sameri is a commentary on the rules of hunting (Kitab al-Sayd). It opens by stating the general permissibility of hunting with trained animals, then provides a detailed breakdown of three methods with their conditions and proofs from Quran and athar. The author first discusses hunting with predatory animals such as dogs and cheetahs. The key conditions are: the animal must be trained (mu'allim), the hunter must say Bismillah when releasing it, and the animal must not eat from the prey. The training of a dog is established by it refusing to eat the prey three times, leaving it for the owner. If the animal eats, the prey becomes haram. If the bird eats, the prey remains halal, unlike a dog. The third method is hunting with arrows or firearms. The conditions are: saying Bismillah, the projectile must strike with a sharp part, and it must wound the prey. If the prey is killed by a blunt impact (mawqudhah), it is haram. The athar from Hammad and from 'Amir and al-Hakam clarify that a bird's training is indicated by returning when called, not by abstaining from eating. This verse is the foundational dalil for Hanafi rulings on hunting with trained animals.

Ruling on apostate fleeing to dar al-harb with proof from hadith

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ilhaq declares the apostate legally dead for property: slaves freed, debts due, Muslim-era property to heirs.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in Hanafi fiqh: an apostate who flees to enemy territory and is declared 'ilhaq' (separation) by a judge. This declaration triggers specific consequences regarding his property, debts, and slaves. The ruling is derived analogically from the Prophet's treatment of Banu Qurayza, where those who returned to Islam were not punished and their property was restored.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
The apostate flees to dar al-harb (enemy territory) and a judge passes a ruling of ilhaq, officially separating him from the Muslim community.
Ilhaq is a formal declaration; without it, the apostate's status remains in limbo.
2
State the consequences of ilhaq
Upon ilhaq, the apostate's mudabbar (deferred-manumission slave) and umm walad (slave mother of his child) become free immediately, his debts become due, and his property acquired during his Muslim period is distributed to his Muslim heirs.
These rules treat ilhaq as equivalent to death for legal purposes.
3
Examine the dalil: Banu Qurayza hadith
The hadith (Bukhari 4028) mentions that among Banu Qurayza, those who joined the Prophet and returned to Islam were not killed nor their property confiscated.
The hadith is used to establish the principle that return to Islam restores property rights.
4
Apply the analogy
By analogy ('illa of returning to Islam and rejoining the community), if the apostate returns to Islam before ilhaq, his property is restored. If ilhaq has occurred, it is too late; the property is already distributed.
The analogy is drawn between the repentant Jews and the apostate who repents.

[۳۱۲۷) (۲۷) آدمی دیکھ سکتا ہے دوسرے کی باندی کا اتنا بدن جتناد یکھنا جائز ہے اپنی ذی رحم محرم عورتوں کا۔ ذی رحم محرم عورتوں کا ہنسلی کی ہڈی سے لیکر گھٹنے تک دیکھنا جائز نہیں باقی سر، بازو اور پنڈلی دیکھ سکتا ہے اسی طرح دوسرے کی باندی کا محرم کاہلی سےلیکر تک ہے اسی کا ہنسلی کی ہڈی سے لیکر گھٹنے تک دیکھنا جائز نہیں ہے، باقی سر، بازو اور پنڈلی دیکھ سکتا ہے۔ مملوکہ باہر کام کرنے نکلے گی تو ہر وقت سر پر چادر رکھنا مشکل ہو گا۔ اس لئے اس کے لئے گنجائش ہے کہ سر، بازو اور پنڈلی کھلی رکھے (۲) وہ ذی رحم محرم عورت کی طرح ہو گئی۔ البتہ جن اعضاء کو دیکھنا جائز ہے ان کو چھونا جائز نہیں۔ کیونکہ وہاں شہوت کاملہ ہے (۳) حدیث میں اس کا ثبوت ہے۔ عن عطاء قال قال رسول الله الا الله ان الامة قد القتل فروة رأسها (ب) دوسرے اثر میں ہے۔ عن ابراهیم قال تصلـى ام الولد بغير خمار وان كانت قد بلغت ستين سنة (ج) (مصنف ابن الي هية ۴۹۶۰ فی الامة تصلى بغیر خمار، ج ثانی ص ۱۴۱، نمبر ۶۲۲۶۶۲۳۴) اس حدیث اور اثر سے معلوم ہوا کہ باندی کا سر اور اسی پر قیاس کرتے ہوئے بازو اور پنڈلی ستر نہیں ہے۔
(ب) آپ نے فرمایا باندی سر کی اوڑھنی ڈال سکتی ہے
(ج) حضرت ابراہیم نے فرمایا ام ولد بغیر اور معنی کے نماز پڑھ سکتی ہے چاہے ساٹھ سال کی ہو چکی ہو
A man can look at another's female slave as much as he can look at his own mahram women. For mahram women, looking from the collarbone to the knee is not permissible; the rest (head, arms, shins) can be looked at. Similarly, for another's female slave, looking from the collarbone to the knee is not permissible; the rest can be looked at. A female slave going out for work will find it difficult to always cover her head, so she is allowed to keep head, arms, and shins uncovered. (2) She becomes like a mahram woman; however, touching what is allowed to look at is not permissible because of complete desire. (3) There is proof in hadith: from Ata who said: The Messenger of Allah said, 'The female slave may let down the covering of her head.' (b) Another athar: from Ibrahim who said: Umm walad may pray without a head covering even if she has reached sixty years. (Source: Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah) This hadith and athar show that the head of the female slave, and by analogy arms and shins, are not 'awrah.

Commentary
This passage is a legal ruling regarding the extent of 'awrah of a female slave owned by another man. The author explains that a man may look at such a female slave in the same areas as his own mahram relatives (e.g., mother, sister). For mahram women, the 'awrah is from the collarbone to the knee; areas outside this (head, arms, shins) are not 'awrah. Similarly, for another's female slave, the same limits apply. The reason given is practical: female slaves go out to work and cannot always cover their heads. The author then qualifies: although looking is permitted, touching those parts is not allowed due to the risk of desire. Proof is provided from a hadith where the Prophet allows a female slave to let down her head covering, indicating her head is not 'awrah. An athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i confirms that an umm walad (mother of a master's child) may pray without a head covering even at an advanced age. By analogy, arms and shins are also not 'awrah for a female slave.

Key Terms
ذی رحم محرم (dhi rahm mahram): Mahram relatives by blood with whom marriage is permanently prohibited.
باندی (bandi): Female slave (mamluka).
