Judge's Letter Rejected in Hudud and Qisas
Ruling that a judge's letter is not accepted in hudud and qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a judge's letter is not accepted in hudud and qisas according to the Hanafi school, and apply the hadith on warding off hudud to this ruling.

Core Idea
A judge's letter is rejected in hudud and qisas because these penalties are dropped by doubt.

This lesson addresses an important exception in judicial procedure: a judge's written communication to another judge is not accepted as evidence in cases involving hudud (fixed penalties) and qisas (retribution). The Hanafi school grounds this ruling on a hadith commanding that hudud be warded off as much as possible, and on the principle that such letters contain inherent doubt, which conflicts with the high evidentiary standard required for these severe punishments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
State the ruling: the judge's letter is not accepted in hudud and qisas cases. Scope: only these two categories of cases; in other matters the letter is generally accepted.
The ruling applies to both hudud and qisas, even though the primary hadith mentions only hudud.
2
Examine the primary dalil (hadith)
Find the hadith from Aisha (ra): 'Ward off the hudud from the Muslims as much as you can...' This is the core proof from the Sunnah.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for establishing the principle.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (wajh al-dalalah)
The command 'adra'u al-hudud' indicates a strong preference to avoid hudud. Accepting a judge's letter – which is second-hand and less certain – would go against this command by making it easier to impose hudud.
The rationale is that the letter introduces shubha (doubt), and hudud are suspended by doubts.
4
Analyze the secondary reason: shubha in the letter
Kitab al-qadi ila al-qadi inherently lacks direct testimony; it may be altered or misunderstood. This doubt (shubha) must prevent the application of hudud and qisas.
The Hanafi maxim: 'Hudud are dropped by doubts' (al-hudud tudra'u bi al-shubuhat).
5
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
The athar mentions 'some people' who allow the judge's letter except in hudud. The Hanafi position extends the prohibition to qisas as well, holding that the same rationale applies.
This highlights a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi view is based on analogy from hudud to qisas.

Coercion to Kill: Impermissibility and Sin
Ruling on coercion to kill with Quranic proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced killing, explain the Quranic proof, and apply the principle to a scenario.

Core Idea
Killing an innocent person is never permissible, even under threat of death.

This passage addresses a severe test: you are forced to kill an innocent person or be killed yourself. Hanafi law rules that you must not kill; you must endure your own death instead. Why? Because both lives are equally sacred. The Quran provides the definitive proof. Let's unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is killing another person under coercion (threat of death). The ruling is that it is impermissible (haram) and the person who kills is sinful.
Remember: the prohibition is absolute; no exception for duress.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites Quran 4:93 which threatens severe punishment for intentional killing of a believer.
The verse is qati al-thubut and qati al-dalalah.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The verse does not mention any exception for coercion. The threat of Hellfire shows the absolute prohibition. There is no justification for taking innocent life.
This is a strong proof because it is explicit and unconditional.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam Zufar (within Hanafi school) held a differing view, but the mufta bihi position is the one stated: the coerced person is sinful and must not kill.
Cross-madhhab, some may consider necessity as a factor, but Hanafi position is firm.
5
Apply the principle to a scenario
If a tyrant threatens to kill you unless you kill an innocent person, you must refuse and accept being killed. Your life is not more valuable than the other's.
This demonstrates equality of lives in Hanafi law.

Hadd for Coerced Zina: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on hadd for coerced male in zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the application of hadd to a coerced male in zina, explain the reasoning and evidence for each, and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view.

Core Idea
Coercion creates a doubt that drops the hadd penalty for zina in the relied-upon Hanafi view.

The question of whether a person forced into zina can be punished with the prescribed hadd penalty has been debated within the Hanafi school. Imam Abu Hanifa held that a male, whose sexual organ is under his control, cannot be truly coerced and thus remains liable for hadd unless the coercer is the sultan. However, his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), argued that coercion creates a doubt (shubhah) that drops the hadd entirely. This lesson unpacks their proofs, the principle of drooping hadd with doubts, and the final operative ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Positions
First, note that Abu Hanifa holds hadd wajib for a coerced male (unless by sultan), while the Sahibayn hold no hadd. Recognize this as an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
The agreement on the woman's case is unanimous – no hadd for the coerced female.
2
Analyze the Reasoning Behind Each View
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: the male has control over his organ and can resist, so he is not truly coerced. The Sahibayn's reasoning: coercion removes voluntariness, creating a shubhah that blocks the hadd.
The Sahibayn also rely on the principle 'al-hadd yudra' bi al-shubuhat' (hadd is averted by doubts).
3
Examine the Dalil – Quranic Verse and Hadith
The Quran (24:33) speaks of forgiveness for coerced slave girls, implying no punishment. The hadith of Umar shows he applied hadd to the coercer (a slave) but not to the coerced woman. Understand how these proofs support the Sahibayn.
Abu Hanifa's view is based on qiyas (analogy) that the male's control is akin to voluntary action.
4
Understand the Tarjih (Weighting) Process
Later Hanafi scholars preferred the Sahibayn's view because it aligns with the stronger principle of drooping hadd through doubts and is supported by clear textual evidence.
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's position; Abu Hanifa's view is considered marjuh (weaker) in practice.
5
Apply the Ruling to a Scenario
Given a situation of coerced zina, determine the hadd status of the coerced male according to the operative Hanafi view: no hadd.
Remember: the coercer, if found guilty of coercion, may face tazir but not hadd for the zina itself.

Coercion to Zina: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn on Hadd
Intra-Hanafi dispute on hadd for coerced zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi positions on hadd liability for a male coerced into zina, identify the proofs used by Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and state the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
A coerced man faces hadd per Abu Hanifa unless coerced by the sultan; Sahibayn drop it entirely due to shubha.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute over a critical question: does a man who is forced to commit zina incur the hadd penalty? Imam Abu Hanifa and his disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagree fundamentally. By the end, you will not only know the ruling but also the reasoning and proofs behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine whether a male coerced into zina is liable for the hadd punishment. Note that the act is zina, which normally carries hadd, but coercion introduces doubt (shubha).
The core question: does coercion negate the hadd entirely or only in specific circumstances?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that hadd is obligatory unless the coercion is by the sultan. Understand his reasoning: the instrument of zina is the man's own organ, so the act is attributed to him; only overpowering coercion from the sultan makes it fully involuntary.
Exception for sultan's coercion: considered analogous to a woman's situation.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that hadd is not binding under any coercion. Their reasoning: hadd is averted by doubts, and coercion itself creates a shubha because the act is not voluntary.
They cite Quran 24:33 and the practice of Umar.
4
Analyze the proofs
Examine the Quranic verse and the hadith cited. For Abu Hanifa, the verse about slave girls applies only to women; for the Sahibayn, it extends to all coerced persons. The hadith shows Umar flogged the coercer but not the coerced slave girl.
The dalal (indication) of the verse is zanni (open to interpretation).
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of the Sahibayn: hadd is dropped due to shubha. This is consistent with the general principle that hadd penalties are averted by doubts.
Later fatwa compilations adopt this view.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the application of the hadd penalty for zina when the perpetrator is coerced. اثر میں ہے۔ عن على انه اتى بمستورد الغجلى وقد ارتد فعرض عليه الاسلام فابی قال فقتله وجعل ميراثه بین ورثته المسلمين (الف) (مصنف ابن ابی شیبة ، ۳۲ ما قا توانی المرتد ما جاء فی میراثہ ، ج سادس، ص ۴۴۵، نمبر ۳۲۷۵۴ رستن سیبقی ، باب من قال في المرتد يستتاب مكانه فان تاب والا قتل ، ج امن، ص ۱۳۵۸، نمبر ۱۶۸۸۵) اس اثر میں ہے کہ حضرت علی نے مرتد پر اسلام پیش کیا اور اس کے شبے کو دور کیا۔ (۱۰۳) تین دن قید رکھا جائے گا ، پس اگر اسلام لائے تو ٹھیک ہے ورنہ قتل کر دیا جائے گا۔ تین دن اس لئے قید رکھا جائے گا تا کہ اتنے دنوں میں سمجھایا جا سکے اور مرتد کو سوچنے کا موقع مل سکے۔ امام ابوحنیفہ کے نزدیک تین دن کی مہلت دینا مستحب ہے ضروری نہیں۔ اثر میں ہے۔ عن على قال يستتاب المرتد ثلاثا (ب) (مصنف ابن ابی شیبۃ ،۳۰ ما قالوافی المرتد کم یستاب، ج سادس، ص ۲۴۴، نمبر ۳۲۷۴۷ سفن للبیہقی ، باب من قال حبس ثلاثه ایام، ج ثامن، ص ۳۵۹ نمبر ۱۶۸۸۷) اس اثر سے معلوم ہوا کہ تین دن تک مہلت دے۔
امام شافعی فرماتے ہیں کہ تین دن تک مہلت دینا ضروری ہے۔
حضرت عمر تین دن نہ کرنے پرسختی کی ہے۔ لما قدم على عمر فتح تستر وتستر من ارض البصرة سألهم هل من مغرية؟ قالوا رجل من المسلمين لحق بالمشركين فاخذناه، قال ما صنعتم به؟ قالوا قتلناه ، قال : قال افلا ادخلتموه بيتا واغلقتم عليه بابا و اطعمتموه كل يوم رغيفا ثم استبتموه ثلاثا . فان تاب والا قتلتموه ثم قال اللهم لم اشهد ولم آمر ولم ارض اذا بلغنی (ج) (مصنف ابن ابی شیبة ، ۳۰ ما قالوافی المرتد کم يستتاب، ج سادس، ص ۴۲۴۴، نمبر ۳۲۷۴ سفن للبیہقی ، باب من قال تحسبس ثلاثۃ ایام ، ج ثامن ، ص ۱۳۵۹ نمبر ۱۶۸۸۷)

And if a Muslim apostatizes from Islam, Islam is presented to him. If he has any doubt, it is removed for him. (103) He is imprisoned for three days. If he accepts Islam [then well and good], otherwise he is killed.
Source pages p. 322

Commentary
This passage deals with the procedure for handling an apostate (murtadd). The ruling: when a Muslim apostatizes, the authorities must first call him back to Islam. If he has any doubts or misconceptions, they are clarified. Then he is given a three-day grace period in custody. If he repents, he is freed; if not, he is executed. The author supports this with the athar of Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) who presented Islam to the apostate Mustawrid al-‘Ijli, and when he refused, he killed him and divided his inheritance among his Muslim heirs. The three-day period is established by the athar from Ali: 'The apostate is given three days to repent.' The author notes that according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the three-day waiting period is mustahabb (recommended), not obligatory. This contrasts with Imam al-Shafi'i, who considers it obligatory. The incident of Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) is quoted: when he heard that some people had killed an apostate immediately, he disapproved and said they should have confined him for three days, feeding him bread daily, and called him to repentance. He then said, 'O Allah, I was not present, I did not command, and I am not pleased when it reached me.' This shows the importance of granting the grace period.

Key Terms
مرتد (murtadd): A Muslim who apostatizes from Islam by word or action.
استتابة (istitabah): Calling the apostate to repent and return to Islam.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school mandates calling the apostate to Islam but considers the three-day imprisonment before execution as recommended, not obligatory, allowing flexibility in implementation.
