Bequest of an Unspecified Part (Juz)
Ruling on bequest of an unspecified part (juz) with discretion to heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a testator bequeaths an unspecified 'part' (juz) of his wealth and explain why the heirs determine the amount.

Core Idea
When the testator bequeaths a 'part' (juz), the amount is entirely at the heirs' discretion.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on a bequest where the testator uses the vague term 'juz' (a part) without specifying any quantity. Unlike the term 'sahm' (a share) which has a default meaning of 1/10, 'juz' leaves the amount completely open. The text explains that the heirs, as successors to the testator's authority, may give whatever they wish to the legatee, and that will constitute fulfillment of the bequest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key term
Recognize that 'juz' is an unspecified part, as opposed to 'sahm' which is a defined share. The word 'juz' can mean any amount, even a very small portion.
This distinction is crucial: 'sahm' defaults to 1/10, while 'juz' has no floor.
2
Understand the consequence of ambiguity
Since 'juz' has no minimum, the amount is not fixed. The heirs have authority over the estate and stand in the place of the testator after death.
The testator's intent was merely to give something, not to fix a specific share.
3
Apply the principle to the ruling
The heirs may give any amount, even a token amount, and the bequest is considered fulfilled. They are told: 'Give him whatever you wish.'
4
Distinguish from 'sahm'
Compare with the ruling on 'sahm': if the testator had said 'a share', the legatee would be entitled to at least 1/10. The difference is based on the linguistic default of each term.
This comparison reinforces the importance of precise wording in bequests.

Priority of Obligatory Bequests over Supererogatory
Ruling that obligatory bequests take precedence regardless of order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that obligatory bequests (fara'id) take precedence over supererogatory bequests, regardless of the order in which the testator mentioned them.

Core Idea
Obligatory acts are debts to Allah; they must be fulfilled before any voluntary bequests, even if mentioned later.

When a Muslim makes multiple bequests, some obligatory (like Hajj, Zakat, expiations) and some recommended (like voluntary charity), a common question arises: which should be fulfilled first? The Hanafi school rules that obligatory bequests take absolute priority, even if the testator wrote the voluntary bequest first. This lesson explores the legal reasoning and the proof from the practice of the Salaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The passage deals with a testator who makes several bequests, some of which are obligatory upon him (fara'id) and some are recommended (mustahabb). The question is the order of fulfillment.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi rule is that obligatory bequests are fulfilled first, regardless of the order in the will. This is because they are like debts owed to Allah.
The executor must follow this even if the testator explicitly wrote the voluntary bequest first.
3
Examine the dalil (proof)
The proof comes from two athar (reports from the Salaf). First, Az-Zuhri said: 'If a man has something obligatory, it is taken from the entire wealth.' Second, Al-Hasan and Tawus likened obligatory Hajj and Zakat to debt.
The second athar directly equates obligatory acts with debt, which in inheritance law is paid before any bequest.
4
Understand the legal reasoning (wajh al-dalalah)
Debt is given priority over bequests because it is a right that must be fulfilled from the estate. By analogy, obligatory acts are debts owed to Allah, so they must be executed before voluntary bequests.
This analogy is the basis for the rule.
5
Apply the ruling
When an executor encounters multiple bequests, he must first identify which are obligatory (fard/wajib) and fulfill them in any order (since all obligatory are equal). Only after that are voluntary bequests executed.
If there is not enough wealth, obligatory bequests take precedence over voluntary ones.

Order of Non-Obligatory Bequests
Ruling that non-obligatory bequests follow the testator's order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that non-obligatory bequests are executed in the order specified by the testator, and distinguish this from the rule for obligatory bequests.

Core Idea
Among voluntary bequests, the testator's order of mention is the operative priority.

When a testator makes multiple bequests, the order of execution depends on their legal nature. If some are obligatory (wajib), those automatically come first. But when all bequests are voluntary (mustahabb), the Hanafi school respects the testator's own ordering as a reflection of his priorities. This lesson explains that rule and its underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of the bequests
First, determine whether each bequest is obligatory (wajib) or non-obligatory (mustahabb). This ruling applies only when ALL bequests are non-obligatory.
Obligatory bequests include debts, zakat, and other religious duties that the testator failed to fulfill.
2
State the ruling on order
If all bequests are non-obligatory, the executor must execute them in the exact order the testator mentioned them. The first mentioned is executed first, then the second, and so on.
The imperative 'قدم' (give precedence) indicates a binding directive in Hanafi usul.
3
Contrast with obligatory bequests
If any bequest is obligatory, it takes precedence over all non-obligatory bequests, regardless of the order of mention. This ensures that religious duties are fulfilled first.
This distinction is covered in a previous lesson.
4
Understand the reasoning
The testator's order among voluntary acts reflects his personal priorities and intentions. Honoring that order respects his will and is consistent with the principle that voluntary acts are subject to the testator's discretion.
The Urdu commentary highlights that the testator may have considered some acts more important.

Mukatab's Will is Invalid: A Ruling with Proof
Ruling with proof: mukatab's will invalid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mukatab's will and identify the proofs that establish it.

Core Idea
A mukatab's will is invalid because he remains a slave until the last dirham is paid.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a mukatab who has almost paid off his kitaba but dies before full payment. Even if he leaves enough property, his will is not valid. The Hanafi school provides clear proofs from the sunnah and athar to establish that a mukatab, like any slave, lacks full legal capacity over his property. In this lesson, we will trace the reasoning and evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What action is being ruled upon? (a mukatab's will). What is the ruling? (invalid, haram).
2
Locate the primary proof
The hadith from Amr ibn Shu'ayb: the mukatab remains a slave until fully paid. This is the core evidence.
Hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit statement 'he is a slave' establishes that the mukatab's status remains slave, thus his property belongs to his master. Therefore, he cannot make a will.
4
Note the supporting evidences
The athar from Ibn Abbas and from al-Hasan strengthen the ruling: a slave cannot will, and a mukatab cannot give gifts without permission.
5
Summarize the practical consequence
Even if the mukatab leaves sufficient property, his will is void. The property goes to his master, not to his legatees.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on how to fulfill a legacy of 1000 dirhams when the testator's estate consists of both cash (`ayn) and outstanding debts (dayn). The ruling is that if one-third of the cash alone covers the 1000 dirhams, it is paid directly. If not, the legatee receives one-third of the cash immediately, and as debts are collected, he takes one-third of each collection until the full 1000 is reached. The commentary explains the underlying principle: cash and debt are treated as two distinct types of property. Before formal division, they remain jointly owned between the legatee and the heirs in proportion to their shares (one-third for the legatee, two-thirds for the heirs). Therefore, the legatee's one-third applies to both categories proportionally, not exclusively to one. The footnote from Ata reinforces this by showing that when a testator leaves different types of property (e.g., cash and a house), the one-third legacy is taken from each type proportionally, not from a single type. This ensures fairness and prevents harm to the heirs.

Key Terms
عين (ʿayn): Tangible, present property (cash or goods) in hand.
دين (dayn): Debt owed to the testator, not yet collected.
ثلث (thuluth): One-third; the maximum portion that can be bequeathed to non-heirs.

The Principle of Proportional Sharing Across Asset Types in Wills
Footnote explaining the principle via an athar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain, using the athar of Ata, why the legatee must receive a share from each type of asset rather than from a single type.

Core Idea
Different asset types remain jointly owned between legatee and heirs until formal division.

The ruling on mixed cash and debt is not arbitrary; it is based on a broader principle that when an estate contains different types of property, the legatee's one-third share is taken proportionally from each type. The athar of the tabi'i Ata ibn Abi Rabah illustrates this principle with a man who left property and a house. This lesson explores that foundational principle and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the athar
Read the narration from Ata: a man bequeaths one-third of his property and also leaves a house. Should the legatee get the house only?
The athar is from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba.
2
Extract the ruling from the athar
Ata rules that the legatee gets one-third from the property and one-third from the house, not the entire house as one-third.
This shows that the one-third applies to each asset category separately.
3
Generalize the principle
The underlying principle: before formal division, the legatee and heirs are co-owners of all assets in proportion to their shares. Therefore, the legatee takes a proportion from each type, not a whole type.
This prevents one party from monopolizing a liquid asset.
4
Apply to the cash/debt case
The same principle applies to cash and debt: the legatee takes one-third of the cash and one-third of the debt collections.
This ensures that the heirs retain two-thirds of the cash (liquidity).
5
Note the wisdom
This principle prevents injustice and ensures that both the legatee and heirs share the benefits and risks of each asset type.
It also preserves the liquidity for the heirs.

This passage presents a ruling on how to fulfill a legacy of 1000 dirhams when the testator's estate consists of both cash (`ayn) and outstanding debts (dayn). If not, the legatee receives one-third of the cash immediately, and as debts are collected, he takes one-third of each collection until the full 1000 is reached. Wills for a Fetus and Will of a Fetus (Wasiyyah li'l-Haml and bi'l-Haml)
Ruling with proof for wills involving an unborn child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wills involving a fetus and apply the six-month condition to determine validity.

Core Idea
A fetus can be a beneficiary or subject of a will if born alive within six months.

This lesson examines two types of wills related to an unborn child: a will that bequeaths property to the fetus (wasiyyah li'l-haml), and a will that bequeaths the fetus itself to another person (wasiyyah bi'l-haml). The Hanafi school upholds the validity of both, subject to a crucial condition confirming the fetus's existence at the time the will was made.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize the two distinct types of wills: (1) bequeathing property to the fetus, and (2) bequeathing the fetus itself (applies only if the fetus is owned, e.g., a slave-girl's child).
The Arabic terms distinguish them: al-wasiyyah li'l-haml (for the fetus) and al-wasiyyah bi'l-haml (of the fetus).
2
State the ruling and condition
Both wills are valid (jā'iz) provided the fetus is delivered alive within six months from the date the will was made. This condition ensures the fetus existed at the time of the will, since the minimum pregnancy term is six months.
The condition applies equally to both types.
3
Examine the Quranic proof
The verse 'Allah instructs you concerning your children' (4:11) is cited. The word 'children' is interpreted to include a fetus that has already come into existence in the womb, as it is a potential child entitled to inheritance and bequest.
The Hanafi principle: a viable fetus (one that will be born alive within six months) is considered a legal person for inheritance and bequest.
4
Examine the Prophetic proof and qiyas
The hadith 'When a newborn cries, he inherits' establishes inheritance upon live birth. By analogy (qiyas), a fetus that will be born alive within the minimum pregnancy period is similarly entitled to inherit and receive a bequest.
The crying is a sign of live birth; the Hanafi school uses this to extend inheritance rights to the unborn.
5
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
Some other schools require the beneficiary to be in actual existence at the time of the will. The Hanafi school, however, accepts potential existence based on the six-month presumption, allowing the will to be valid.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in differing views on legal personality of the fetus.

In inheritance (fara'id), the Quran clearly prescribes that a male receives twice the share of a female. Ruling on 'Bayn' in Bequests When a Legatee Dies Before the Testator
Ruling on bequest with 'between' when one legatee is dead

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the word 'bayn' in a bequest implies equal division, and determine the share of a surviving legatee when the other legatee dies before the testator.

Core Idea
When a bequest uses 'between', each legatee gets half; a predeceased legatee's share returns to the testator's heirs, not to the surviving legatee.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from the Hanafi school regarding bequests (wasiyya). The testator declares that a third of his wealth is 'between' two named individuals, but one of them is already dead. How is the bequest distributed? The answer hinges on the linguistic implications of the word 'bayn' and the principle of reversion to the estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key phrase
The testator says: 'A third of my wealth is between Zaid and Amr.' The word 'bayn' (between) is central. What does it indicate about division?
The phrase is 'ثلث مالی بین زید و عمر'.
2
Determine default division from 'bayn'
In Arabic and Hanafi usage, 'bayn' implies equal division among the named parties unless context dictates otherwise. So each legatee gets half of the bequeathed third.
This is a linguistic rule – the word itself allocates shares equally.
3
Apply the death scenario
Zaid is dead at the time of the statement. His share cannot go to him; it does not automatically transfer to Amr. The bequest is not a gift that passes to heirs of the legatee.
A bequest is not binding until acceptance; death before testator voids entitlement.
4
Trace the destination of the deceased's share
The share of the deceased legatee (half of the third) reverts to the testator's own heirs as part of the general estate. It does not increase Amr's share.
This is consistent with the principle that a bequest cannot create an inheritance for a non-heir beyond the testator's intent.
5
Conclude the surviving legatee's share
Amr receives only his original half of the bequeathed third – i.e., one-sixth of the total estate. The other sixth goes to the testator's heirs.
The ruling is: لعمر نصف الثلث – Amr gets half of the third.

Commentary
This ruling clarifies that the operative time for assessing a bequest is the moment of death, not the moment of utterance. A testator may bequeath a third of his wealth even if he currently owns nothing; the bequest is valid and will attach to whatever estate he leaves at death, up to a maximum of one-third. The commentary provides a concrete example: if the testator later earns 3,000 dirhams, the legatee receives 1,000 dirhams (one-third of the death-time estate). The proof is derived from the Quranic verse (Surah Nisa 4:12) where the phrase 'what you leave' (mā taraktum) is understood to refer to the estate at death. This is a fundamental principle of Hanafi bequest law: bequests are executed from the estate existing at death, regardless of the testator's financial state when the bequest was made.

ترکہ (tarkah): The estate left by the deceased at the time of death, after deducting burial expenses and debts.

The Rule of Radd: Returning Surplus to Quranic Sharers
States the ruling of radd with proof from Ali and addresses Shafi'i opposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on radd, identify its conditions, and explain the exclusion of spouses based on the athar of Ali.

Core Idea
Radd returns surplus inheritance to Quranic sharers except spouses — Ali's practice sets the rule.

This passage introduces the Hanafi rule of radd, a method of redistributing any surplus remaining after the fixed shares of the Quranic sharers have been allocated. The Hanafi school, following the practice of Ali ibn Abi Talib, returns this surplus to the sharers in proportion to their shares, but explicitly excludes the husband and wife. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the proof from the athar, and contrasts the Hanafi position with that of Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its conditions
The passage states that the surplus (al-fadil) above the prescribed shares of the sharers is returned to them in proportion to their shares, provided there is no residuary heir (asaba) and no dhawi al-arham. Also note the explicit exception of the husband and wife.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is an athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i that Ali ibn Abi Talib used to give radd to every sharer except the spouse. This is recorded in Mussanaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
The dalil is a sunnah ahad with zanni thubut but qati dalala due to the explicit exception.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit phrase 'except the husband and wife' (illa al-zawj wa al-mar'ah) in Ali's practice shows that radd is given to all other sharers. The exclusion is based on spouses not being blood relatives.
4
Note the dispute with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the surplus should go to bayt al-mal, not to the sharers. His evidence is an athar from Ibrahim that none of the Prophet's companions gave radd to spouses, and that Zayd ibn Thabit put the remainder in bayt al-mal.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that in Hanafi law, eligible sharers receive additional shares from the surplus, while in Shafi'i law, the surplus is not distributed to them.

Chapter of Radd. [3229 (1)] And the surplus over the prescribed shares of the sharers when there is no residuary, it is returned to them in proportion to their shares except to the husband and wife. Necessary note: In this chapter, besides radd, many issues are mentioned. Therefore, the chapter name is partial. The meaning of radd is to return. The sharers take their shares, then some shares remain, and there is no residuary and no dhawi al-arham, then the remaining shares are redistributed to the relatives and family sharers according to their shares; this is called radd. Commentary
This passage is a combined ruling and dispute from the chapter on radd in inheritance law. The author first states the Hanafi ruling: when there is a surplus after distributing the prescribed shares to the Quranic sharers (ashab al-furud), and there is no residuary heir (asaba), the surplus is returned to those sharers in proportion to their shares, except to the husband and wife. The term 'radd' literally means 'return', and here it refers to this redistribution. The basis for this ruling is an athar (report) from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i that Ali ibn Abi Talib used to practice radd for every sharer except the spouse. The author explains the logic: spouses are not blood relatives, so they do not share in the radd after taking their fixed shares. An example is given: a woman leaves husband and two daughters. Husband gets 1/4 (25), daughters get 2/3 (66.66), remaining 8.34 is given to the daughters as radd, not to the husband. Then the author presents the Shafi'i position: Imam Shafi'i holds that the surplus should go to the bayt al-mal (public treasury), not be returned to the sharers. Their evidence is an athar from Ibrahim that the companions, including Zayd ibn Thabit, did not give radd to spouses and put the remainder in bayt al-mal. The Hanafi school follows the practice of Ali, considering it stronger. This difference has practical consequences: in Hanafi law, surviving sharers (except spouses) get additional shares from the surplus; in Shafi'i law, that surplus is not distributed to them.

Key Terms
رد (radd): The legal rule of returning the surplus inheritance to the Quranic sharers (ashab al-furud) in proportion to their shares when there is no residuary heir (asaba) and no dhawi al-arham, excluding the spouses.
سهام (siham): Plural of sahm; the prescribed shares of the Quranic sharers, such as 1/2, 1/4, 1/6, etc.
عصبة (asaba): A residuary heir who takes the remaining inheritance after the prescribed shares are allocated; typically male agnatic relatives.
ذوي الأرحام (dhawi al-arham): Blood relatives who are not Quranic sharers nor residuaries, such as maternal uncles and aunts; they inherit only in the absence of sharers and residuaries (in some schools).

Prohibition of Selling or Gifting Wala'
Ruling with hadith evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling or gifting wala' and explain the hadith evidence that establishes the prohibition.

Core Idea
Wala' is a non-transferable right; selling or gifting it is haram and invalid.

Wala' (patronage right) is a unique legal bond established by manumission. Unlike property, it cannot be sold or gifted. This lesson examines a clear hadith prohibition and its application in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the sale (bay') or gift (hibah) of wala'. The ruling is that these transactions are prohibited (haram) and invalid.
The passage states this directly: 'ولا يباع الولاء ولايوهب'.
2
Locate the dalil
The evidence is a hadith from Abdullah ibn Umar: the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade the sale and gift of wala'.
This hadith is recorded in Sunan Abu Dawud, Book of Wala'.
3
Analyze the dalil's authority
The hadith is ahad (single transmission) → zanni al-thubut. However, its meaning is clear (qat'i al-dalalah) due to the explicit word 'نهى' (forbade).
In Hanafi usul, an explicit prohibition (nahy) indicates haram.
4
Apply Hanafi usul principle
The imperative tone of prohibition yields the ruling of haram. The act is sinful and the contract is void (batil).
There is no room for istihsan here; the dalil is clear.
5
Confirm school unanimity
All Hanafi authorities agree on the prohibition. There is no intra-school dispute.
This is a settled ruling within the madhhab.

Rule of 'Awl with Base 12 for Quarter and Third or Sixth
Rule for 'awl with base 12 for quarter and third/sixth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance shares when the quarter (rub') coexists with a third or a sixth, applying the 'awl rule to adjust the base.

Core Idea
When quarter meets third or sixth, the base starts at 12 and 'awls to 13, 15, or 17 when shares overflow.

This lesson introduces a core faraid rule: when one heir takes a quarter (1/4) and another takes a third (1/3) or a sixth (1/6), the base denominator is 12. However, the total shares often exceed 12, requiring 'awl (increase of base). You will learn the three possible 'awl outcomes (13, 15, 17) and how to compute them.

Guided Path
1
Identify heirs and their Quranic shares
List all inheritors and their fractional shares (e.g., wife = 1/4, two full sisters = 2/3, mother = 1/6).
Ensure shares are correct based on presence of other heirs.
2
Determine base denominator
Find the LCM of the denominators (4, 3, 6 → 12).
Use 12 as the base asl al-mas'alah.
3
Convert shares to base units
Convert each fraction to the base denominator: 1/4 = 3/12, 2/3 = 8/12, 1/6 = 2/12.
Each share is now expressed as a portion of 12.
4
Sum the shares and check for 'awl
Add the numerators: 3+8+2=13. Since 13 > 12, 'awl is required. Increase base to 13.
The base becomes the new denominator (13), so shares become 3/13, 8/13, 2/13.
5
Apply 'awl to other possible cases
The text notes that 'awl can also go to 15 or 17. This occurs with different combinations of heirs sharing quarter, third, and sixth.
Memorize the three 'awl numbers: 13, 15, 17.

[3258] (1) And if there is with the quarter a third or a sixth, then its base is from twelve, and it will 'awl to thirteen, fifteen, and seventeen. ... (6) If with the quarter there is a third or a sixth, then the original case will be from twelve and will 'awl towards thirteen, fifteen, or seventeen. Explanation: If with the quarter there is a sharer of a third or a sixth, the case proceeds from twelve. But sometimes the shares increase so they become thirteen instead of twelve, sometimes fifteen, sometimes seventeen. See below for details. The case of 'awl to thirteen: The deceased left a wife, two full sisters, and a mother. The wife gets a quarter, the two full sisters get two-thirds, and the mother gets a sixth. The case proceeds from twelve, but all shares together become thirteen, which is called 'awl.

Commentary
This passage presents a rule in faraid: when one heir takes a quarter (1/4) and another takes a third (1/3) or a sixth (1/6), the base denominator is 12 (LCM of 4,3,6). Depending on the combination of heirs, the sum of shares may exceed 12, leading to 'awl to a new denominator: 13, 15, or 17. The author then illustrates 'awl to 13 with an example: a wife (1/4 = 3/12), two full sisters (2/3 = 8/12), and a mother (1/6 = 2/12). Sum = 3+8+2 = 13, so base 'awls to 13. The shares become 3/13, 8/13, 2/13. This demonstrates how 'awl works when the quarter is present with fractional sharers.

Key Terms
ربع (rub'): Quarter (1/4) share, typically for spouse.
ثلث (thulth): Third (1/3) share, e.g., for uterine siblings or mother.
سدس (sudus): Sixth (1/6) share, e.g., for mother or grandfather.
عول ('awl): Increase of the base denominator when shares exceed the original base.
