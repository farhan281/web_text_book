Sharing a Sacrificial Animal: One Sheep for Household or Per Person?
Ruling on sharing sacrificial animals among multiple persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on how many persons a sheep, cow, or camel suffices for in udhiyah, and explain the difference with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
One sheep can be sacrificed with shared reward; cow and camel suffice for exactly seven persons.

This passage addresses a common question: Can multiple people share a single sacrificial animal? The Hanafi school provides flexibility through reward-sharing for sheep, while explicitly limiting cows and camels to seven participants. An inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i highlights differing interpretations of the evidence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what the Hanafi school says about the minimum number of persons per animal type.
The source mentions that one sheep is sufficient for one person, but reward can be extended to others; for cow/camel, it is for seven.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i and some scholars (Ibn al-Mubarak) have different rulings.
Shafi'i holds that one animal suffices for an entire household, impossible for two households. Ibn al-Mubarak restricts sheep to one person.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the hadith of Mukhnaf ibn Sulaym, which Shafi'i uses to argue obligation (wujub) per household.
The hadith states: 'على كل اهل بيت في كل عام اضحية' – which Shafi'i interprets as obligating one animal per household.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Understand how the Hanafi school interprets the same hadith and provides counter-evidence.
Hanafi treats the command as recommendatory (nadb) not obligatory, and cites the hadith of Aisha and Jabir to allow sharing across persons.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
See how the difference affects practice – when is a single animal sufficient?
Under Hanafi, one can sacrifice one sheep and gift reward to many; under Shafi'i, each household must have its own animal.

Commentary
This passage is an extended ruling with evidence (ruling_with_proof) on the number of persons for whom a sacrificial animal suffices, along with an inter-madhhab dispute with Imam Shafi'i. The author begins by stating the Hanafi position: one sheep suffices for one person or a household (by sharing reward), while a cow or camel suffices for seven persons. The evidence for sharing reward is the hadith of Aisha where the Prophet sacrificed a ram and included his family and entire ummah in the reward. For the cow and camel, the hadith of Jabir explicitly states that each is for seven. Then the author presents Imam Shafi'i's view: one animal suffices for an entire household regardless of size, but not for two households. Shafi'i supports this with the hadith of Mukhnaf ibn Sulaym that the Prophet made udhiyah annual upon every household. The Hanafi response is implicit: they accept the hadith but interpret it as recommended (sunnah) rather than obligatory, and they allow sharing reward across multiple households. The footnote clarifies that some scholars (like Ibn al-Mubarak) restrict a sheep to one person, but the Hanafi position is otherwise. This passage sets the fiqhi framework for collective sacrifice.

Udhiyah Not Obligatory on the Poor and Traveler
Ruling with proof that udhiyah is not wajib on poor and traveler.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on udhiyah for the poor and traveler and identify the dalil that supports the exemption.

Core Idea
Udhiyah is wajib only on the resident with financial capacity; the poor and traveler are exempt.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Saghir clarifies that udhiyah (sacrificial slaughter) is not obligatory on two categories: the poor (faqir) and the traveler (musafir). The author provides both hadith evidence and rational analogy. Understanding this ruling helps distinguish between obligations that are universally binding and those conditioned on specific circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two exempt categories and the general rule
The passage explicitly states that udhiyah is not wajib on the poor and the traveler. The general rule is that it is wajib on the one who has capacity (sa'ah) and is a resident.
Poor = one without nisab; Traveler = one on a journey ≥ 3 days.
2
Locate the primary hadith dalil
The hadith from Abu Hurayra (Ibn Majah) conditions udhiyah on 'sa'ah' (capacity/wealth). This implies that the poor are not obligated.
The hadith: 'Whoever has capacity and does not sacrifice, let him not come near our place of prayer.'
3
Understand the analogy (qiyas) for the traveler
The author analogizes to other obligations dropped during travel—fasting Ramadan, Friday prayer, and Eid prayers. Since these are dropped due to hardship, udhiyah is also dropped by analogy.
Qiyas is valid because the 'illa (effective cause) is hardship.
4
Recognize the athar as supporting evidence
An athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i explicitly grants a concession to the pilgrim and traveler not to slaughter. This strengthens the exemption.
Athar: 'He granted concession to the pilgrim and traveler not to slaughter.'
5
Note the practical consequence
If a poor person voluntarily performs udhiyah, it is valid and counts as such. The exemption does not forbid the act.
The ruling is about obligation, not permissibility.

Time of Udhiyah: Entry, Conditions, and Duration
Ruling on timing of udhiyah with evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when udhiyah becomes valid, explain the difference in ruling between city dwellers and villagers, and apply the timing conditions to determine when a person may slaughter.

Core Idea
Udhiyah time begins at Fajr on the 10th, but city dwellers must wait until after Eid prayer.

In this lesson, we examine the specific timing conditions for udhiyah. While the general entry time is Fajr of Yawm al-Nahr, a key distinction exists between city dwellers (ahl al-amsar) and villagers (ahl al-suwaad). Understanding this distinction is crucial to ensure the sacrifice is valid according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general entry time
The act is udhiyah (sacrificial slaughter). The general time begins at the break of true dawn (Fajr Sadiq) on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah.
This is the baseline ruling for all Muslims.
2
Distinguish between city dwellers and villagers
For city dwellers (ahl al-amsar), slaughter is not valid until after the Imam leads the Eid prayer. Villagers (ahl al-suwaad) may slaughter immediately after Fajr because Eid prayer is not obligatory upon them.
The term 'ahl al-suwaad' refers to rural inhabitants whose land appears black due to cultivation.
3
Locate the evidence for the condition
The hadith from Anas (Bukhari 5561, Muslim 1960) states: 'Whoever slaughters before the prayer, let him repeat.' This proves that slaughter before the Eid prayer for city dwellers must be repeated, so it is invalid. The athar from Ibn al-Mubarak (Tirmidhi 1508) grants concession to villagers to slaughter at dawn.
The imperative 'فليعد' indicates the need to repeat if done before the prayer for city dwellers.
4
Determine the duration of validity
Udhiyah is permissible for three days: the Day of Nahr (10th) and the two days following (11th and 12th). Sahih Muslim has 'all the days of tashriq are a time for sacrifice.'
If slaughter occurs after the 12th, it is not valid as udhiyah.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement with proof (ruling_with_proof) on the time for udhiyah. The author states that the time begins at the break of dawn (Fajr Sadiq) on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah (Yawm al-Nahr). However, for city dwellers (ahl al-amsar), slaughter is not valid until after the Imam has led the Eid prayer; if they slaughter before, it does not count as udhiyah and must be repeated. For villagers (ahl al-suwaad/rural areas), Eid prayer is not obligatory, so they may slaughter right after dawn. The evidence is the hadith from Anas that the Prophet commanded to repeat the slaughter if done before the prayer. An additional athar from Ibn al-Mubarak confirms that villagers are granted concession to slaughter at dawn. The passage also clarifies the term 'ahl al-suwaad' as rural people whose land appears black due to cultivation. Finally, the duration is stated: udhiyah is valid for three days — Yawm al-Nahr (10th) and the two following days (11th and 12th). This is the established Hanafi ruling.

Permissible Days for Udhiya: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Ruling on the time period for Udhiya with inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the days of Udhiya and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-evidence using the athar of Ibn Umar and the hadith of Jubayr ibn Mut'im.

Core Idea
Sacrifice is valid only on the 10th, 11th, and 12th of Dhul Hijja in the Hanafi school.

The time for offering the sacrifice (Udhiya) is a matter of slight difference between the schools. In this passage, the author states the Hanafi position clearly and then presents the Shafi'i view along with its proofs. Understanding this dispute trains you to weigh conflicting evidences—a key skill in fiqh. We will trace how the Hanafi school restricts the days to three, while the Shafi'i school extends to the 13th.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position on time
Locate the sentence that states the permissible days. The text says: 'قربانی دسویں، گیارہویں اور بارہویں کو جائز ہے' (Sacrifice is permissible on the 10th, 11th, and 12th).
The word 'جائز' means permissible, but the act of Udhiya itself is wajib. The time condition makes the act valid.
2
Extract the Hanafi proof
The author cites an athar from Abdullah ibn Umar: 'Sacrifice is two days after the day of sacrifice' (meaning 11th and 12th). This is a companion saying (qawl sahabi).
In Hanafi usul, qawl sahabi is a source of law when it does not contradict analogy.
3
Identify the opposing Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that sacrifice is allowed until the 13th, citing the hadith of Jubayr ibn Mut'im: 'All the Days of Tashreeq are for slaughter.' Days of Tashreeq include the 13th.
The hadith is general (amm) in scope.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds that the athar of Ibn Umar is specific (khass) and restricts the general hadith. Also, the practice of the Companions limited it to three days.
This is an application of takhsis al-amm bi al-qaul al-sahabi.
5
Determine the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical outcome): a Hanafi considers a sacrifice offered on the 13th invalid and must be repeated (or given as sadaqah), while a Shafi'i considers it valid.
This affects fatwa given to laypeople who may slaughter on the 13th.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Al-Sharh al-Samarqandi on the book of sacrifice (Udhiya). It covers two main rulings: (1) the time period for sacrifice, and (2) defects that invalidate the sacrificial animal. The author begins by stating the Hanafi ruling that sacrifice is permissible on the 10th, 11th, and 12th of Dhul Hijja, and not after. He then cites an athar from Abdullah ibn Umar to support this. He then presents the Shafi'i position that sacrifice is allowed until the 13th, supported by a hadith from Jubayr ibn Mut'im about all days of Tashreeq being for slaughter, and another from Ibn Abbas. The author does not explicitly prefer one view but notes that the best day is the first. The second part deals with defects. The author lists four types of animals that cannot be sacrificed: blind, one-eyed, lame (that cannot walk to the place of slaughter), and emaciated. He provides a principle: the animal should be sound, and if more than half (specifically two-thirds) is sound, it is considered acceptable. He cites a hadith from Bara' ibn 'Azib listing four defects: one-eyed, sick, lame, and emaciated. The author's commentary is methodical, combining textual proofs (Quran and Sunnah) with rational principles (e.g., what people would find defective). Four Defects that Invalidate the Sacrificial Animal
Ruling with proof on four major animal defects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four defects that render an animal invalid for Udhiya according to the hadith of al-Bara' ibn 'Azib, and explain the underlying principle.

Core Idea
An animal with a clear and obvious defect – blind, one-eyed, severely lame, or emaciated – is not valid for sacrifice.

The Prophet (saw) explicitly listed defects that disqualify an animal from being offered as a sacrifice. This lesson unpacks that hadith and the Hanafi application. We will see that the prohibition is not just about physical flaws, but about presenting a perfect gift to Allah.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith of al-Bara'
Find the hadith: 'أربع لا تجوز في الأضاحي...' – Four are not permissible in sacrifices: the one-eyed with clear defect, the sick with clear illness, the lame with clear limp, and the emaciated with no marrow.
This hadith is from Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi, graded as sahih.
2
Identify the four defects
List them: 1) العوراء – one-eyed, 2) المريضة – sick, 3) العرجاء – lame, 4) العجفاء/الكسراء – emaciated (or broken). The text also mentions العمياء (blind) which is even more severe.
Blindness is included by analogy (qiyas awla) because it is worse than a one-eyed condition.
3
Understand the principle behind the defects
The author states a principle: 'اللہ کے حضور میں ہدیہ دینا ہے تو ایسا ناقص نہ ہو کہ لوگ بھی پسند نہ کرتے ہوں۔' – One is giving a gift to Allah, so it should not be defective to the extent that people would also dislike it.
This ethical (akhlaqi) rationale supports the legal ruling.
4
Apply the principle to borderline cases
The defect must be clear (bayyin). Minor defects that do not detract from the animal's value are overlooked. The hadith uses the expression 'بين عورها' (clear in its one-eyedness) etc.
This distinguishes between major and minor defects.
5
Note the Hanafi position on 'sick'
The hadith includes 'المريضة بين مرضها' – the sick whose illness is clear. This covers any obvious illness, not just chronic ones.
A temporary illness that does not affect the meat quality may be excused, but the default is precaution.

Threshold for Partial Defects: Ear and Tail Cuts
Ruling on ear and tail cuts with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on threshold

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi threshold for partial defects (ear and tail cuts) and distinguish between the two-thirds (Abu Hanifa) and half (Sahibayn) positions.

Core Idea
If more than half the ear or tail is cut, the sacrifice is invalid; the sound part must be at least two-thirds.

After the major defects, the text addresses partial losses like ear cuts and tail cuts. The Hanafi school uses a threshold to separate acceptable from unacceptable. This lesson unpacks that threshold and the internal Hanafi difference on whether the benchmark is two-thirds or half. Understanding this helps in evaluating borderline cases.

Guided Path
1
Read the ruling on ear/tail cuts
The text says: 'اگر اکثر کان اور دم باقی ہوں تو جائز ہے' (If most of the ear and tail remain, it is permissible). Then it elaborates: if less than half is cut, it is permissible; if more than half is cut, it is not.
Note: 'most' here is interpreted as more than half.
2
Identify the hadith supporting this
The author cites a hadith from Ali (ra) mentioning muqabala and mudabara (ear cuts). The principle is that if the ear is cut such that the ear canal appears, it is invalid.
This hadith provides the basis for considering ear cuts as defects.
3
Understand the two principles
The author states two principles: (1) the animal should be presentable as a gift, and (2) the threshold: if more than half (two-thirds) is sound, it is acceptable; if less than two-thirds is sound, it is defective.
The two-thirds figure is derived from caution (ihtiyat).
4
Note the Sahibayn's view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that if more than half is sound, it is sufficient. This is based on the athar of Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib: 'Half or more is a defect.'
The text mentions this as their proof.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The author does not explicitly choose, but the precautionary approach favors the two-thirds threshold (Abu Hanifa's view). However, many later fatwas rely on the Sahibayn's half threshold as a concession (takhfif).
Practice may vary; a mufti should know both views.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Give at least one-third of your udhiyah meat in charity — a recommended balance from Prophetic guidance and Quranic division.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that it is recommended to give no less than one-third of the udhiyah meat in charity. We will see how the author derives this from the Prophet's hadith authorizing three uses for the meat — eat, feed, store — and from the Quranic verse dividing the sacrifice into three shares. Understanding this ruling helps you practice proper distribution during Eid al-Adha.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling

2

The hadith from Salamah bin al-Akwa' says: 'Eat, feed, and store.' This implies three categories, one of which is charity (feeding). The Hanafi scholars infer that each category is one-third.
The hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim.
3

Surah al-Hajj 22:36 says: 'So when they fall on their sides, eat of them and feed the contented and the beggar.' This also divides the meat into three portions: for oneself, for the visitor, and for the beggar (charity).
The term 'qani'' is the contented beggar, 'mu'tir' is the visitor.

Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Both sources list three distinct uses. The Hanafi combine them to conclude that the recommended division is one-third for oneself, one-third for storage/gifts, and one-third for charity. The minimum charity should not be less than one-third.
The recommendation is based on the practice of the Prophet and the Quranic indication.
5

While the Hanafi specify one-third, other schools may not prescribe a minimum. The Hanafi position is relied upon (mufta bihi) within the school, derived from zahir al-riwayah.
No intra-Hanafi dispute exists on this point.

A kitabi slaughtering the udhiyah is disliked but valid; Arab Christians are not ahl al-kitab.

This lesson addresses the ruling on a kitabi (Jew or Christian) slaughtering the udhiyah (sacrificial animal). The matn states it is makruh, but the flesh is halal if done. The commentary expands on this by clarifying the definition of ahl al-kitab, using athar from Umar and Ali to exclude Arab Christians. Understanding this nuance is critical for correctly applying the Hanafi position on slaughtered meat.

The Quantity of Food in Kafarat al-Yamin: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on the quantity of food in kafarat al-yamin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the quantity of food for each poor person in kafarat al-yamin, explain the Hanafi proofs, and compare it with the Shafi'i position, identifying the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
Half a sa' of wheat per poor person: the Hanafi quantity derives from the equivalence of one sa' of dates to half a sa' of wheat.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamiri examines the feeding option in the expiation for breaking an oath (kafarat al-yamin). The author presents the Hanafi ruling—that each poor person must receive half a sa' of wheat (or equivalent)—and supports it with multiple hadith and athar. Then the Shafi'i position of one mudd is introduced and answered. By studying this lesson, you will understand how the Hanafi school derives precise quantities from the Prophetic practice and how it responds to conflicting evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
What is the specific ruling being disputed? Focus on the quantity of food per poor person in kafarat al-yamin, not the broader feeding obligation.
The dispute is between Hanafi (half sa' of wheat) and Shafi'i (one mudd of wheat).
2
Extract the Hanafi ruling and its textual evidence
Note the Quranic verse (5:89) and three hadith/athar: the hadith of a wasq of dates (1 sa' per poor = half sa' of wheat), the athar of 'Ali (sa' barley or half sa' wheat), and the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas (half sa' wheat).
The Hanafi position is that the quantity is half a sa' of wheat, or one sa' of dates or barley as alternative grains.
3
Understand the Hanafi reasoning
Why does half a sa' of wheat become the standard? Because the Prophet gave a sa' of dates per poor person, and in the Prophetic era one sa' of dates was equivalent to half a sa' of wheat. The Hanafis use this equivalence.
This reasoning links the hadith of dates to the wheat quantity.
4
Identify the Shafi'i position and its proofs
The Shafi'i school holds that the quantity is one mudd of wheat per person. Their evidence: the hadith of Aws (15 sa' of barley for 60 poor = 1 mudd each) and an athar from Ibn 'Abbas (one mudd).
Note that the Shafi'i proofs involve barley, not wheat directly.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is implicit: the Shafi'i hadith uses barley, and the Hanafi position is based on wheat and dates. The athar of one mudd is interpreted as a concession or referring to barley. The thamarah al-khilaf is the practical difference: Hanafi gives twice the amount of wheat compared to Shafi'i.
The passage does not explicitly refute the Shafi'i proofs but presents them after the Hanafi view.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute over the quantity of food to be given to each poor person in the expiation for an oath (kafarat al-yamin). The author first presents the Hanafi position: based on the Quranic verse (5:89) which commands feeding ten poor people from the average of what one feeds one's family, Imam Abu Hanifah specifies that each poor person receives half a sa' of wheat, or one sa' of dates, or one sa' of barley. This is supported by multiple hadith and athar: a hadith from Ibn 'Abbas that the Prophet gave a sa' of dates per poor person, and that a sa' of dates was equivalent to half a sa' of wheat in the Prophetic era; an athar from 'Ali stating a sa' of barley or half a sa' of wheat; and another hadith from Ibn 'Abbas confirming half a sa' of wheat for those who cannot find dates. The passage then notes that Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the quantity is one mudd of wheat per person, citing a hadith where the Prophet gave fifteen sa's of barley to sixty poor people (each sa' equals four mudds, thus one mudd per person) and an athar from Ibn 'Abbas stating one mudd. The Hanafi response is implicit: the hadith cited by Shafi'i uses barley, not wheat, and the Hanafi position is based on equivalence with dates and wheat from other evidence. The author then continues with the ruling on the method of feeding: it should be a full day's meal, meaning feeding both morning and evening, as supported by athar from Tawus and Ibrahim. This ensures the feeding fulfills the requirement of 'average food'.

مد (mudd): A unit of volume equal to one-quarter of a sa'. Understood as a meal portion.
وسق (wasq): A unit of volume equal to 60 sa'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Khums is one-fifth of spoils reserved for Allah, the Prophet, his kin, orphans, poor, and wayfarers; the rest goes to the fighters.

This passage establishes the imam's duty in distributing war spoils. The key operation is extracting the khums (one-fifth) before dividing the remainder among the combatants. We will see the textual proof from Surah al-Anfal and the prophetic practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the imam dividing the spoils of war (ghanimah). The ruling is wajib: the imam must extract the khums and distribute the remaining four-fifths to the fighters.
This is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and agreed upon.
2
Locate the dalil
Two proofs are cited: the Quranic verse (8:41) which commands the khums and lists its five recipients, and an athar from Abu al-‘Aliyah describing the Prophet's practice.
The Quran is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalalah on the obligation.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'اعلموا' and the enumeration of recipients establish that the khums is obligatory and must be distributed to those categories. The athar confirms the practical division.
The Hanafi school takes the apparent meaning of the verse as binding.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
There is no intra-Hanafi dispute on this basic ruling. The khums allocation after the Prophet's time may differ slightly, but the principle remains.
The reliance is on the explicit text and established practice.

Division of Khums into Three Shares after the Prophet's Death
Ruling with proof on division of khums after the Prophet's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the division of khums after the Prophet's death and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
After the Prophet's death, khums is divided into three shares; the Prophet's share and relatives' share are dropped.

This lesson examines how the khums (one-fifth of war booty) is divided after the Prophet's death. The Hanafi school holds that only three shares remain—for orphans, the needy, and wayfarers—based on the consensus of the Companions and supporting hadith. We will explore the evidence and the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i, who maintains the original five shares.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage rules on the division of khums (one-fifth of war booty). The Hanafi ruling is that it is wajib to divide it into three shares.
The ruling is about distribution, not an act of worship.
2
Understand the change from five to three shares
During the Prophet's lifetime, khums was divided into five shares (Allah, Prophet, relatives, orphans, needy, wayfarers). After his death, the shares for the Prophet and relatives are dropped by consensus.
The remaining three: orphans, needy, wayfarers.
3
Examine the dalil: consensus and hadith
The evidence includes the consensus of the Companions (recorded in an athar from al-Hasan ibn Muhammad) and the hadith 'My heirs shall not divide a dinar...' (Bukhari).
The hadith implies the Prophet's share is charity, not inherited.
4
Note the ikhtilaf with Imam al-Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the verse (8:41) still requires a share for relatives. The Hanafi response is that the ruling was abrogated by practice.
Practical difference: Hanafi three shares, Shafi'i five.
5
See how poor relatives are treated
Poor relatives of the Prophet are included in the three categories and given priority; wealthy relatives receive nothing.
This shows the Hanafi position is not absolute exclusion of relatives.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the division of khums (one-fifth of war booty) after the death of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The text begins by stating that khums is divided into three shares: for orphans (yatama), the needy (masakin), and wayfarers (ibn al-sabil). This is a change from the Prophet's lifetime, when it was divided into five shares (including the Prophet's share and a share for his relatives). The author explains that the shares for the Prophet and his near relatives (dhu al-qurba) have been dropped due to their extinction and the consensus of the Companions. This is supported by an athar from al-Hasan ibn Muhammad and a hadith from Abu Hurayrah that the Prophet's inheritance is charity. The text also clarifies that poor relatives of the Prophet are included in the three categories and given priority over others, while wealthy relatives receive nothing. A difference with Imam al-Shafi'i is noted, who maintains that relatives still receive a share based on the Quranic verse. The passage provides proofs from hadith and athar, establishing the Hanafi position that khums is now divided into three shares, with the Prophet's share spent on Muslim interests.

Key Terms
خمس (khums): One-fifth of war booty set aside for certain recipients as specified in the Quran.
ذوى القربى (dhu al-qurba): The near relatives of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), specifically the Banu Hashim and Banu Abd al-Muttalib.
سهم (sahm): A portion or share of the khums allocated to a specific category.

Arabian Peninsula: All Land is 'Ushr Land
Ruling that all land of Arabia is 'ushr land and non-Muslims cannot reside permanently

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling regarding the land classification of the Arabian Peninsula and explain the legal reasoning based on the prohibition of non-Muslim permanent residence.

Core Idea
All of Arabia is 'ushr land because non-Muslims cannot reside there, making kharaj inapplicable.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamiri establishes a foundational Hanafi rule: every inch of the Arabian Peninsula is classified as 'ushr land, subject to a one-tenth tax on agricultural produce rather than the kharaj land tax. The reasoning hinges on the prohibition of permanent non-Muslim residence in Arabia, which precludes the application of kharaj. Let's explore the proofs and boundaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The author states that all land of the Arabs is 'ushr land. Note the reason: non-Muslims cannot reside permanently in Arabia.
2
Analyze the primary proof
The hadith 'Expel the polytheists from the Arabian Peninsula' is cited. How does this prove the land is 'ushr? Because kharaj is levied on lands owned by non-Muslims, and since they cannot own land, kharaj falls away, leaving 'ushr.
3
Explore the secondary proof
The hadith 'Two religions shall not remain in the land of the Arabs' reinforces the prohibition of non-Muslim residence.
4
Understand the geographical definition
The passage defines the boundaries of Jazirat al-Arab from al-'Udhayb to Hajar and from Mahrah to Syria. This determines which lands are included.

Commentary
This passage establishes a key Hanafi rule: all land of the Arabian Peninsula (Jazirat al-'Arab) is 'ushr land, meaning it is subject to a one-tenth tax on produce (like 'ushr on agricultural land) rather than kharaj (land tax). The reason is that non-Muslims cannot reside permanently in Arabia, so kharaj (which applies to lands owned by non-Muslims) is inapplicable. The author provides two hadith proofs: first, the command to expel polytheists from Arabia, which implies that no non-Muslims can own land there; second, the Prophet's statement that two religions cannot remain in the land of the Arabs. Since kharaj is only levied on lands held by non-Muslims, and such lands are not permitted in Arabia, all land there is 'ushr land. Kharaj Adjustment: Capacity and Calamities

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Kharaj is flexible: it must match land capacity and is waived in disasters.

In this lesson, we examine two key rulings from the Hanafi school on the land tax (kharaj). The first ruling establishes that kharaj must be set within the land's productive capacity and can be reduced if it becomes burdensome. The second ruling provides an exemption when a natural calamity destroys the crop. The proof comes from the practice of Caliph Umar (رضي الله عنه), who personally ensured that the tax did not exceed what the people could bear.

Validity of Payments to Rebel Rulers
Ruling with proofs on payments to rebel rulers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on zakat paid to rebel rulers and distinguish between judicial validity (qada'an) and religious duty (diyanatan).

Core Idea
Paying zakat, ushr, and kharaj to a rebel ruler fulfills the obligation judicially, but diyanan the owner must ensure proper distribution if misused.

The passage addresses a practical dilemma: when a rebel ruler (baghi) takes control of a territory and collects zakat, ushr, and kharaj, is the payer's obligation discharged? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position, which balances the duty of obedience to the ruling authority with the condition of tamlik (making the poor owners) for zakat. You will learn how the school distinguishes between judicial and religious validity, supported by hadith and athar from the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is paying zakat, ushr, and kharaj to the de facto ruler (even a rebel). The ruling for each is that judicially (qada'an) the obligation is fulfilled, but for zakat, diyanan (religiously) the payer must repeat it if the ruler misappropriates.
Focus on the distinction between judicial and religious validity.
2
Locate the dalil
The commentator cites three proofs: the hadith of Abu Dharr commanding obedience to any amir, and two athar from Ibn Umar and others directing that zakat be handed over to rulers regardless of their character.
The hadith and athar are given in the source.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith's command to listen and obey implies that paying dues to the amir is part of obedience, thus fulfilling the obligation. The athar emphasize that handing zakat to the ruler suffices judicially even if he misuses it.
Understand how 'obedience' is used as a legal basis.
4
Distinguish between qada'an and diyanan
The passage states that if the rebels spent zakat incorrectly (not giving to the poor), the owner must repeat it diyanan because the condition of tamlik was not met. But judicially, no repetition is required because the payment was made to the ruler.
This is a key Hanafi subtlety.
5
Differentiate between zakat and kharaj/ushr
For zakat, tamlik (making the poor owners) is a condition; if not fulfilled by the ruler, diyanan repetition is needed. For kharaj and ushr, no such condition exists; spending on public welfare suffices, so no repetition is required even if spent incorrectly.
The basis is the Quranic lam of tamlik in 9:60 for zakat.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proofs on the validity of payments (zakat, ushr, kharaj) made to rebel rulers (baghi) who have taken control of a territory. The commentator first states the core ruling: if the rebels collected these dues and spent them in their proper place (for zakat: giving to the poor; for ushr/kharaj: on public welfare), then the payer's obligation is fulfilled, and the imam (legitimate ruler) cannot take them again. If they did not spend correctly, then the payer must repeat the payment diyanan (in his private conscience) but not judicially (qada'an), because judicially the payment is considered fulfilled due to obedience to the ruler. The commentator supports this with three textual proofs: (1) The hadith of Abu Dharr commanding obedience to the amir even if he is a slave. (2) The athar of Ibn Umar and others directing that zakat be handed over to the ruling authorities regardless of their character. (3) Another athar of Ibn Umar emphasizing the same. Then he distinguishes between zakat and kharaj/ushr: For zakat, making the poor owners (tamlik) is a condition; if the rebels did not do that, the payer must repeat the zakat privately. For kharaj and ushr, no such condition exists; spending on public works suffices, so no repetition is needed. The commentator also notes that the Qur'anic verse (9:60) with the lam of tamlik supports the need for tamlik in zakat. The ruling is that judicially the obligation is discharged by payment to the ruling authority, but in private conscience, the payer must ensure the zakat reaches the deserving if the authority misappropriates it.

Key Terms
ديانة (diyānatan): In private conscience or religious duty, as opposed to judicial enforcement.
تمليك (tamlīk): The act of making the recipient the owner of the zakat, which is a condition for zakat validity in Hanafi fiqh.
باغي (bāghī): A rebel or insurgent group that takes control of a territory and establishes its own authority.

Zakat Paid to Rebels: Judicial vs Moral Obligation
Ruling on zakat collected by rebels without proper distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on zakat paid to rebels and explain the distinction between qada' (judicial) and diyanah (moral) obligations in this context.

Core Idea
Zakat requires ownership transfer (tamlik); without it, the payer must morally repeat it, though judicially it is valid.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: zakat given to a rebel ruler who collected but did not distribute it to the poor. The Hanafi school makes a critical distinction between the judicial validity of the payment and the moral duty to ensure the poor actually become owners of the zakat. Understanding this distinction is essential for fulfilling the religious obligation in corrupt or unjust states.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its judicial ruling
Recognize that the act is paying zakat to rebels who collected but did not distribute. Determine the judicial (qada') ruling: the zakat is considered discharged because the payment was made to the authority.
Judicially, the obligation is lifted once the payment is made, regardless of the recipient's misuse.
2
Locate the dalil for tamlik
The Quranic verse (9:60) is cited: 'Indeed the charities are for the poor, the needy...' The lam in 'lil-fuqara' is interpreted as lam al-tamlik, indicating that ownership must transfer to the recipients.
This interpretation is specific to Hanafi usul; other schools may view the lam differently.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The wajh (legal implication) is that the lam signifies transfer of ownership. Therefore, if the payer does not ensure that the poor become owners, the zakat is not morally fulfilled.
The payer is responsible for the outcome, not just the act of payment.
4
Apply the distinction to kharaj and 'ushr
Contrast with land taxes (kharaj, 'ushr): for these, tamlik is not required. Spending on public welfare like bridges suffices. Thus, the ruling is specific to zakat.
This contrast highlights the unique nature of zakat as a spiritual act requiring direct ownership transfer.
