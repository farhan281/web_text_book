Partial Permission of Heirs for a Bequest to an Heir
Ruling on bequest to an heir with partial heir permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a bequest to an heir when only some heirs give permission, and explain the reason based on the hadith and the principle of authority over one's own share.

Core Idea
A bequest to an heir is valid only to the extent of the share of the heirs who permit it.

Bequests (wasiyya) to an heir are generally invalid unless the other heirs grant permission. But what happens when only some heirs agree and others reject? The classical Hanafi text provides a clear answer: the bequest is partially valid, proportional to the share of the permitting heir. This lesson unpacks that ruling, its proof from hadith, and the legal rationale behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling from the hadith
The hadith states: 'A bequest to an heir is not valid unless the heirs give permission.' Recognize that permission is a condition for validity.
The hadith is a primary dalil (proof) for this ruling.
2
Understand the exception of partial permission
The passage explains that if some heirs permit and others reject, the bequest is valid only to the extent of the permitting heir's share. This is an application of the general rule to a specific scenario.
This is an expansion (expansion_probe) of the basic ruling.
3
Analyze the legal rationale (ta'lil)
The reason given: 'because the one who permits has authority over his own share.' Each heir can only dispose of his own portion of the inheritance, so their permission is limited to their share.
This principle is rooted in the concept of wilayah (authority) over property.
4
Apply the ruling to a concrete example
Imagine a testator leaves a bequest of 100 shares to an heir. Heirs A and B each have a 50-share inheritance. A permits, B rejects. The bequest is valid for A's 50 shares, invalid for B's 50 shares.
The bequest is not entirely void, nor entirely valid.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Quduri addresses the ruling of a bequest made to an heir. The default ruling, based on the hadith, is that a bequest to an heir is invalid unless all other heirs give their permission. The hadith (لا تجوز الوصية لوارث إلا أن يشاء الورثة) establishes this principle. The text then explains the scenario where some heirs permit and some reject. In such a case, the bequest is valid only in proportion to the share of the heirs who gave permission, and it is invalid with respect to the share of those who rejected. The reason is that each heir has full authority (wilayah) over his own share of the inheritance; thus, they can permit or reject the bequest only in relation to their own portion. This aligns with the Hanafi position that the permission of an heir is effectively a waiver or gift from his own share.

Key Terms
وصية (wasiyya): A bequest or will made by a person (testator) to transfer ownership of property after death.
وارث (warith): An heir entitled to a share in the deceased's estate by inheritance.
اجازت (ijazah): Permission given by heirs to allow a bequest to an heir.
مجیز (mujiz): The one who gives permission (the permitting heir).

Two Executors Appointed Jointly Cannot Act Alone
Ruling with proof on joint executors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when two executors are appointed jointly and explain the two reasons (illatayn) that prohibit individual sale or receipt.

Core Idea
Joint executors must act together; individual sale or receipt is invalid because the testator's trust is in their combined honesty.

When a testator appoints two executors (wasiyan) together, neither may sell the estate's property or take possession of debts alone. The Hanafi school bases this on two interconnected principles: the testator's reliance on their collective integrity and the legal nature of receipt as an exchange falling under guardianship. This lesson unpacks the ruling from al-Jami' al-Saghir and its underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and parties
The act is sale (bay') and receipt (taqāḍī). The parties are two jointly appointed executors. The ruling: neither may do these alone.
2
Extract the primary proof
The text from al-Jami' al-Saghir is the authoritative source. Note that the ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Unpack the first causal reason (illah)
The testator was satisfied with both together in receipt. This implies the condition of the testator defines the scope of authority.
4
Unpack the second causal reason
Receipt is like exchange (mubādalah), which falls under guardianship (wilāyah). Guardianship requires joint action.
5
Determine the consequence
If one executor acts alone, the sale or receipt is invalid (batil).

Commentary
This passage addresses the case where a testator appoints two executors together (jointly). The ruling is clear: neither executor may independently sell the testator's property or take possession of debts owed to the estate. The Arabic from al-Jami' al-Saghir states that one cannot sell or 'yaqtaḍī' (which here means to receive/take possession). The reason is twofold: first, the testator trusted both of them together, so the confidence is in their combined action; second, taking possession is akin to exchange (mubādalah), which falls under the category of guardianship (wilāyah), and guardianship requires joint action unless the testator specified otherwise. The commentary explains that this is because the testator was satisfied with the joint trust, and because taking possession inherently involves an element of exchange (especially when the debt is of a different type than money). Thus, one executor acting alone would exceed his authority. This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah via al-Jami' al-Saghir.

Key Terms
وصي (waṣī): An executor appointed by the testator to administer the estate after death.
تقاضي (taqāḍī): In this context, it means receiving or taking possession of a debt or property owed to the estate.
مبادلة (mubādalah): Exchange; the act of taking possession is considered a type of exchange between the estate and the debtor.
ولاية (wilāyah): Guardianship; a legal authority that normally requires joint action when two are appointed together.
