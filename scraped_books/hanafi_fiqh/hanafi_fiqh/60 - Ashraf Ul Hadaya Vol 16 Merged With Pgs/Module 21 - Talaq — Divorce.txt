Oath of Divorce or Emancipation Conditional on Child's Gender
Ruling when an oath of divorce or emancipation is tied to the birth of a male child, but the child appears as khuntha.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that an oath conditional on a child's gender does not take effect if the child is a khuntha until the gender is clear, and explain the principle that hanth is not established by doubt.

Core Idea
An oath's violation requires certainty; doubt suspends the consequence.

In Hanafi law, oaths of divorce or emancipation that are conditional on future events are taken seriously, but their effect is suspended until the condition is clearly fulfilled. This lesson addresses a common scenario: the condition is the birth of a male child, but the child is born with ambiguous genitalia (khuntha). The ruling clarifies that because the child's gender is uncertain, the oath is not violated until certainty is achieved. The principle behind this is that legal consequences cannot be based on doubt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its condition
A man makes an oath of divorce or emancipation contingent on the birth of a male child. Determine the exact wording and the condition that must occur.
e.g., 'If your first child is a son, you are divorced/ free.'
2
Observe the actual outcome
The woman gives birth to a khuntha (hermaphrodite), a child whose gender is not immediately apparent. Recognize that the condition (male child) has not been definitively confirmed.
Khuntha mushkil: gender unclear.
3
Apply the principle of hanth
The violation of an oath (hanth) requires certainty that the condition has occurred. Since there is doubt about the child's gender, hanth is not established.
Hanfi principle: 'Al-hanth la yathbutu bi al-shakk'.
4
Derive the ruling
The oath does not take effect (divorce or emancipation does not occur) until the child's gender becomes clear. The oath remains valid but its consequence is suspended.
The ruling is sahih; the oath is not invalidated.
5
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
Some other schools might consider external signs to determine gender immediately, but Hanafis require clarity to avoid doubt.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf (difference of opinion).

And whoever swears an oath of divorce or emancipation saying, 'If the first child you give birth to is a male,' and she gives birth to a khuntha (hermaphrodite), it does not take effect until the condition of the khuntha is clarified, because the violation (hanth) is not established by doubt. Translation: And whoever swears an oath of divorce or emancipation: 'If the child you bear is a male,' and she gives birth to a khuntha, then divorce or emancipation does not occur until the matter of the khuntha becomes clear, because hanth (violation of oath) is not established by doubt. Explanation: Zayd said to his wife or his slave-girl: 'If your first child is a male, then you are divorced/free.' The child born is a khuntha mushkil, whose gender is not yet clear. Then divorce or emancipation does not occur, because there is doubt about him being a male, and a person does not become a hanith (violator) due to doubt.

Commentary
This passage presents a straightforward ruling: If a man makes an oath contingent upon his wife or slave-girl giving birth to a male child, and she delivers a khuntha (hermaphrodite of ambiguous gender), the oath is not broken (and the divorce or emancipation does not occur) until the child's true gender becomes apparent. The legal principle is that an oath is only violated when the condition is certainly fulfilled. Since the child's being male is doubtful, hanth (violation) cannot be established. The ruling emphasizes that in Hanafi law, doubt (shakk) prevents the enforcement of consequences. This is consistent with the general principle that legal effects are not based on uncertainty. The child's gender may become clear later through signs like urination or physical development.

Key Terms
حنث (hanth): Violation of an oath; the occurrence of the condition that triggers the penalty or consequence.
خنثى (khuntha): Hermaphrodite; a person with ambiguous gender characteristics.
شك (shakk): Doubt; uncertainty about a fact or legal condition.

Conditional Divorce with Khuntha: The Rule of Certainty
Ruling on conditional divorce/emancipation with khuntha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional divorce/emancipation when a child is khuntha mushkil, explain the reasoning based on the principle of certainty, and apply it to similar cases.

Core Idea
Uncertainty blocks the breach of oath – doubt cannot trigger divorce or emancipation.

When a husband makes divorce or emancipation conditional on a specific outcome, such as the child being male, certainty of that outcome is required. The birth of a khuntha mushkil (ambiguous gender) introduces doubt, and in Hanafi law, legal consequences cannot rest on doubt. This lesson unpacks the ruling from the classical texts and shows how the principle of certainty (yaqīn) governs such oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the condition
Read the scenario: a man swears by divorce or emancipation that if his wife or slave-girl gives birth to a male child, the divorce or emancipation will take effect. Pinpoint exactly what is required for the condition to be fulfilled.
The condition is the birth of a male (ghulām).
2
Recognize the khuntha issue
The child is born as a khuntha mushkil – whose gender is not immediately clear. Note that this creates uncertainty about whether the condition (male) has been met.
Khuntha mushkil is a hermaphrodite whose gender is ambiguous.
3
Apply the rule that hanth requires certainty
The text states 'al-hanth la yathbutu bi al-shakk' – breach of oath is not established by doubt. Therefore, the divorce or emancipation does not occur until the khuntha's gender is clarified.
This is based on the legal maxim 'Certainty is not removed by doubt'.
4
Conclude the ruling
Until the gender is known, the conditional act is suspended. If later the khuntha is determined male, the divorce/emancipation takes effect from that moment. If female or remains ambiguous, it never occurs.

If he swears by divorce or emancipation that if the first child you give birth to is a boy, then she gives birth to a khuntha, it does not occur until the condition of the khuntha is clarified, because the breach of oath is not established by doubt. Translation: And whoever swears an oath of divorce or emancipation that the child you give birth to is a boy, then she gives birth to a khuntha, divorce or emancipation will not occur until the matter of the khuntha becomes clear, because breach is not established by doubt. Commentary: Zaid said to his wife or his slave-girl, 'If your first child is a boy, then you are divorced or you are free.' And the child born is a khuntha mushkil whose gender is not yet clear, so divorce or emancipation does not occur, because there is doubt about it being a boy, and a person does not incur breach of oath due to doubt.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the chapter of khuntha regarding conditional oaths. If a man makes divorce or emancipation conditional upon the child being a boy (ghulam), but the child is born as a khuntha mushkil (ambiguous hermaphrodite), the condition is not fulfilled. The reason is that the occurrence of the condition (the child being male) is doubtful, and legal consequences (breach of oath, occurrence of divorce or emancipation) cannot be based on doubt. The ruling applies until the gender of the khuntha becomes clear (e.g., through signs or later development). If it later turns out to be male, then the divorce/emancipation would take effect. This is based on the legal maxim: 'Certainty is not removed by doubt' and the principle that oaths are interpreted strictly.

Key Terms
حنث (hanth): Breach of an oath, incurring the consequence (divorce, emancipation, expiation).
خنثى (khuntha): Hermaphrodite; a person with ambiguous gender characteristics. 'Khuntha mushkil' is one whose gender is not determinable at birth.
