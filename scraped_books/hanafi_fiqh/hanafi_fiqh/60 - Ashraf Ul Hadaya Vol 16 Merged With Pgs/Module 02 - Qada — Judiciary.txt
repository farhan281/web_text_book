Navigating the Chapter of Qasamah Using the Table of Contents
Table of contents for Bab al-Qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use the table of contents (fihrist) of Bab al-Qasamah to locate specific topics including the definition of qasamah, the lawth requirement, and rulings for different locations where a body is found.

Core Idea
The table of contents is your roadmap to the chapter — every heading points to a specific legal discussion.

Before diving into the rulings of qasamah, a jurist must know the structure of the chapter. The table of contents (fihrist) in Ashraf al-Hidayah lists over forty sub-headings, from the literal meaning of qasamah to the ruling when a body is found between two villages. This lesson will teach you how to read this roadmap so you can quickly find any Hanafi ruling on qasamah when the killer is unknown.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chapter
Locate 'Bab al-Qasamah' at the top. This tells you that all following sub-headings belong to the chapter on oaths for unresolved homicide.
2
Scan the major divisions
Notice that the fihrist groups sub-headings thematically: definitions, evidence (Hanafi vs Shafi'i), locus of the body (neighbourhood, house, boat, market, etc.), and exceptional cases.
These groupings are not explicit but emerge from the headings.
3
Pinpoint a specific ruling
If you need, for example, the diyah ruling when a body is found in a shared house, scan for 'مشترک گھر' (shared house). The fihrist lists it as a sub-heading, so you can jump to that page.
4
Note the intra-madhhab differences
Look for headings mentioning 'امام ابو یوسف' or 'صاحبین'. These indicate places where Abu Hanifa's position differs from his disciples — a key feature of Hanafi fiqh study.
5
Use the TOC to preview ikhtilaf
The fihrist shows where the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on lawth is discussed. This helps you prioritise the most contested issues in the chapter.

Key Terms
قسامت (qasamah): Oaths taken in cases of suspected murder when the killer is unknown; a collective oath by fifty men from the neighborhood.
لوث (lawth): Strong circumstantial evidence or prevailing suspicion pointing to a specific group or person.
عاقلہ ('aqilah): The group responsible for paying diyah (blood money) on behalf of the killer, typically the paternal relatives.

Qasamah and Diyah: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute Over Specifying Defendants
Explanation of Abu Yusuf's non-Zahir al-Riwayah view and the istihsan counter-argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on qasamah when the plaintiff specifies some defendants and identify which position is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides qiyas: qasamah and diyah bind all inhabitants unless a specific outsider is accused.

When a dead body is found in a neighborhood, the institution of qasamah (fifty oaths) and diyah (blood money) applies. But what if the plaintiff (wali) names only some inhabitants? Does the obligation fall on the rest? Imam Abu Yusuf, in a non-Zahir al-Riwayah transmission, argued that qiyas drops the liability. However, the relied-upon Hanafi position employs istihsan to uphold the general application of the prophetic texts. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind both views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute's core
Recognize that the disagreement is about whether qasamah and diyah remain obligatory on the entire neighborhood when the plaintiff specifies only some defendants, or whether they drop for the rest.
2
State Abu Yusuf's non-Zahir al-Riwayah view
According to this transmission, qiyas dictates that if the plaintiff specifies some, the obligation drops for the remaining inhabitants. The plaintiff is then asked for evidence; if none, a single oath is taken from the specified defendant.
This is a non-Zahir al-Riwayah position, thus less authoritative.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's qiyas argument
The qiyas argument rests on the possibility that an outsider committed the killing. The text (nass) that established qasamah only covers the case where the body is found in a place attributable to the defendants and the claim is against them. Beyond that, original qiyas (no liability without proof) applies.
4
Present the istihsan position and its proof
The relied-upon Hanafi position holds that the texts regarding qasamah are absolute and general; they do not differentiate between claims against all, some, or unspecified. Therefore, the obligation is established by the text itself, not by analogy. Istihsan overrides qiyas.
This is the mufta bihi view.
5
Apply the distinction: outsider exception
If the plaintiff accuses a specific person outside the neighborhood, then the texts do not apply, and qiyas is also impossible because the original case (qasamah on the neighborhood) is itself contrary to analogy. Normal evidentiary rules follow: one oath, standard consequences.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the obligation of qasamah (oaths) and diyah (blood money) when a dead body is found in a neighborhood (mahalla) and the killer is unknown. The text first records a non-Zahir al-Riwayah transmission from Imam Abu Yusuf. In this view, analogical reasoning (qiyas) dictates that if the plaintiff (wali) specifies some defendants, then qasamah and diyah are dropped from the remaining inhabitants. Instead, the plaintiff is asked if he has evidence (bayyina); if not, the defendant takes only one oath, not fifty. This is because qiyas sees the possibility that the killing was committed by someone outside the neighborhood, and the text (nass) that established qasamah only applies when the dead body is found in a place attributable to the defendants and the claim is against them. Beyond that, the original principle of qiyas—no liability without proof—remains.

In contrast, the relied-upon position based on istihsan (juristic preference) holds that qasamah and diyah are obligatory on the inhabitants, regardless of whether the claim is against all, some, or unspecified individuals. The Istihsan argument is that the prophetic texts (nusus) are absolute and general; they do not differentiate between types of claims. Therefore, the obligation is established by the text itself, not by analogy. However, this exception is limited: if the plaintiff specifically accuses a person outside the neighborhood, then the texts do not apply, and qiyas is also impossible because the original case (qasamah on the neighborhood) is itself contrary to analogy. In that situation, the normal rules of evidence apply: one oath and the standard consequences for refusal.

The passage concludes by explaining the procedure when the claim is against an outsider. If the plaintiff has evidence, it is accepted. If not, a single oath is administered. If the defendant swears, he is free. If he refuses to swear and the claim is for property, the refusal establishes the claim; if for retaliation (qisas), the ruling depends on the differing opinions detailed in the Book of Claims. This demonstrates the Hanafi method of balancing textual authority (nass) with rational analogy (qiyas) and juristic preference (istihsan), and the hierarchy of sources.

No Qasamah When Killer is an Outsider
States ruling when killer is not from the locality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when the locality is not liable for qasamah or diyah, and explain the Hanafi reasoning based on actual/constructive killing and claim contradiction.

Core Idea
If the killer is known to be an outsider, the locality bears no liability for qasamah or diyah.

In the previous lessons, we learned that when a slain person is found in a locality and the killer is unknown, the locality may be liable for qasamah and diyah. This lesson addresses a crucial exception: when the killer is proven to be an outsider. We will see why the Hanafi school holds that the locality is exonerated, based on the principles of constructive liability and the necessity of a valid claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage deals with a slain person found in a locality, but the killer is from outside the locality. Identify the two key elements: actual killing (committed by the locality) and constructive killing (due to negligence or omission).
2
Apply the rule of constructive liability
Hanafi law holds the locality liable only if the killing is actual (committed by them) or constructive (due to their negligence). If the killer is an outsider, neither actual nor constructive killing applies to the locality.
Constructive killing occurs when the locality could have prevented the crime but failed, not merely because the body was found there.
3
Understand the role of the claim (da'wa)
The diyah obligation on the locality also requires a formal claim by the heir against them. If the heir claims against the outsider, they cannot simultaneously claim against the locality without contradiction.
The passage states that allowing both claims would entail contradiction (تناقض).
4
Conclude the ruling
Therefore, no qasamah or diyah is due on the locality. The conditions for liability are absent: no actual/constructive killing and no valid claim against them.

Fighters Need Evidence to Be Liable
States that accused fighters are not liable without bayyinah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that liability requires evidence (bayyinah), explain the underlying hadith principle, and apply it to a qasamah scenario involving a locality and accused fighters.

Core Idea
No liability without evidence: the burden of proof is on the claimant, not on the accused.

When a dead body is found in a locality, the heirs may accuse specific fighters of murder. But in Hanafi fiqh, the accused fighters are not automatically liable. They become liable only if the heirs produce legal evidence (bayyinah). This rule protects the accused from baseless claims and is rooted in the prophetic command: 'al-bayyinah 'ala al-mudda'i wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara'. The claimant's own accusation also serves to exonerate the locality, because the claimant's word is evidence against himself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the primary ruling: the accused fighters are not liable until the heirs establish bayyinah (evidence). Note that the locality is exonerated by the mere claim against the fighters.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith: 'al-bayyinah 'ala al-mudda'i wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara' (The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier). This is the proof that mere da'wa (claim) does not establish liability.
3
Understand the exception for the locality
The claimants' statement that the fighters are the killers is evidence against themselves. Therefore, the locality is exonerated without needing further proof. This is because the claimant's word is hujjah (evidence) against his own interest.
4
Apply the principle
In practice, if the heirs fail to produce two male witnesses (the standard bayyinah), the fighters walk free. The locality also remains exonerated. This protects both the accused and the community from frivolous accusations.

Dead Body in Camp: Liability on Nearest Tent
Ruling on qasamah/diyah liability based on possession in ownerless land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that possession (qabd) substitutes for ownership when determining liability for qasamah and diyah for a slain body found in a camp on ownerless land.

Core Idea
In ownerless land, liability for a slain body follows the nearest inhabited tent based on possession (qabd), not ownership.

When a murder occurs in a camp pitched on ownerless land (e.g., a desert or forest), the question arises: who bears liability for the diyah and qasamah? Ownership cannot determine liability because no one owns the land. The Hanafi school resolves this by substituting possession (qabd) for ownership: whoever has effective control over the spot where the body is found, or the nearest tent if outside, must pay the blood money and perform the oath. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this substitution and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the basic ruling
Read the passage and note: a camp is set up in ownerless land (mubah). A dead body is found either inside a tent or outside. The ruling assigns liability to the inhabitants of that tent or the nearest tent. Understand that this is a special case where ownership is absent.
The 'land' is not owned by anyone, so normal rules based on ownership (milk) do not apply.
2
Locate the effective cause (illa) of the ruling
The text states 'considering possession (qabd) when ownership (milk) is absent.' Here, possession (being inside or nearest to the tent) is made the basis for liability. Identify that qabd is a substitute for milk in this context.
This is a form of qiyas: the original basis for liability in owned property is ownership; here, possession stands in its place.
3
Distinguish between two sub-cases
Case 1: body found inside a tent → liability on the inhabitants of that specific tent. Case 2: body found outside all tents → liability on the inhabitants of the nearest tent. The principle of proximity (being nearest) applies when there is no exclusive possession.
The ruling is not arbitrary; it follows the logic that whoever has de facto control (or closest proximity) is most likely responsible or can best defend themselves.
4
Trace the Hanafi legal reasoning
The Hanafi position relies on a rational proof (dalil aqli): liability follows control over the locus (mahall). In ownerless land, the tent dwellers have exclusive or primary control over the area around their tent. This reasoning is consistent with the school's emphasis on qabd as a legal cause in liabilities.
This is a clear example of istihsan (juristic preference) or qiyas with a different effective cause.
5
Note the cross-madhhab disagreement
The passage is from a Hanafi text; other schools may not accept possession as a substitute for ownership in this context. Understand that this ruling is specific to the Hanafi madhhab and may differ in other schools (e.g., Shafi'i might require ownership or other conditions).

Core Idea
If the army fought the enemy, any corpse is presumed enemy-killed (hader); if on owned land, the owner bears diyah — but Abu Yusuf adds joint liability.

This passage from the Hidayah addresses two factors that change the application of qasamah and diyah in the context of an army camp. Understanding these nuances is crucial for correctly applying the principles of qasamah in military settings.

State the general rule

If Abu Hanifa's view is followed, the army is fully exempt; under Abu Yusuf, liability is shared. The mufta bihi (operative view) is not specified here.

Method of Paying Diyat from Government Stipends over Three Years

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paying diyat from government stipends ('ataaya) in installments and identify the conditions that determine whether the diyat is collected.

Core Idea
Diyat is collected from 'ataaya over three years, but the period adjusts to the stipend schedule.

In the Hanafi school, when accidental killing occurs, the diyat (blood money) is borne by the 'aqilah (killer's clan). For soldiers receiving annual government stipends ('ataaya), the payment is spread over three years as a concession for ease. This lesson explains the method, including scenarios where stipends are given in more or fewer years, and the crucial condition that the stipends must be for future years after the judge's ruling.

Guided Path
1

The act is the payment of diyat by the 'aqilah via the government stipends. The default ruling is that diyat is taken in three equal annual installments from the 'ataaya.

The passage gives three cases: (1) If stipends are given over more than 3 years (e.g., 6 years), diyat is spread proportionally (1/6 per year). The key is that the stipends must be for future years after the judge's ruling for the collection to apply.

Two conditions must be met: (a) the judge has ruled the diyat obligation on the diwan, and (b) the stipends are given in advance for future years. Past stipends are exempt.
This prevents collection from already-assigned funds.

If the actual obligation is one-third of a full diyat or less, it is taken in one year. If between one-third and two-thirds, the first portion is taken in year one, the rest in year two. The text gives a precise breakdown.

The proof is the sunnah (practice of Prophet and 'Umar). The Shafi'i school requires immediate payment, not deferment, but the Hanafi school adopts the three-year ta'jil for ease.

Ruling: Commanding a Child to Kill – Liability and Recourse
Ruling on diya liability and recourse in command-to-kill cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the initial liability for diya when a child is commanded to kill and distinguish the recourse depending on the mode of proof (bayyinah vs iqrar).

Core Idea
The child's 'aqila pays diya first; they recover from the commander or his 'aqila based on proof.

In Hanafi law, when a person commands a child to kill someone and the child carries out the killing, the liability for diya falls initially on the child's 'aqila. However, the 'aqila has a right of recourse against the commander or his 'aqila, depending on how the command is proven. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the legal act: a command to kill given to a child, followed by the child's killing. The ruling is that diya is wajib on the child's 'aqila initially.
2
Identify the initial liability
The child's 'aqila (paternal relatives) are made liable for the diya first. This is because the child is the direct agent of the killing.
The default rule: liability falls on the 'aqila of the direct killer.
3
Analyze the recourse rule
The child's 'aqila can reclaim the diya from the commander if the command is established: (a) by bayyinah (clear evidence) → recourse against commander's 'aqila; (b) by iqrar (confession) → recourse against commander's personal wealth.
Why different? Evidence against the 'aqila requires external confirmation; confession is binding only on the confessor.
4
Understand the payment terms
The repayment is made over three years, starting from the date of the judge's verdict. This is because diyat are generally paid in installments for ease (taysir).
5
Note the rationale
The ruling balances justice: the child's 'aqila pays initially to ensure the victim's family is compensated quickly, but they have recourse to the one who actually caused the killing through the command.
This is consistent with the Hanafi principle that direct causation (the child's act) takes precedence in liability, but indirect causation (the command) allows recovery.

Commentary
This passage presents a foundational principle (asl) along with four sub-rules (usul) that govern how liability for diya (blood money) transfers when circumstances change. The principle is attributed to Imam Muhammad, who listed various cases separately. The author of the commentary provides a unified framework. The four principles are:
1. If the killer's legal status changes (e.g., his wala' transfers due to manumission), the crime remains with the original 'aqila, regardless of whether a judge has already ruled. Example: a child born to a slave father and free mother commits a crime; the mother's 'aqila pays. Later the father is freed, so wala' transfers to the father, but the liability for the past crime stays with the mother's 'aqila.
2. If a hidden status becomes apparent (e.g., a child born from li'an, i.e., denied paternity, is later claimed), the crime transfers to the new 'aqila (the father's side), even if judgment was already passed.
3. If the killer's status remains unchanged but the 'aqila itself changes (e.g., the killer moves from one region to another), the ruling depends on timing of the judge's decision. If judgment was made against the first 'aqila, it stays; otherwise, it goes to the new 'aqila.
4. If the same 'aqila gains new members (e.g., a minor attains majority) or loses members (death), all members (old and new) share in the liability, except for payments already made. The new members do not pay for past installments already discharged.

Key Terms
جانی (jānī): The perpetrator of the homicide or injury; the killer.
ولاء (walā'): The bond of patronage that transfers upon manumission; the right of inheritance and liability for crimes shifts to the patron.
ملاعنہ (mulā'anah): A married couple who perform mutual imprecation (li'an) when the husband accuses the wife of adultery and denies paternity.
قضاء (qaḍā'): The judgment or decree of a qadi (judge).

Liability of Judge vs Executor in Estate Sales
Comparison of liability between executor and judge when selling estate property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the liability of a judge and an executor when selling estate property and state the Hanafi rationale based on public interest.

Core Idea
Judge sells without liability to protect public interest; executor is liable like an agent.

The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between a judge (qadi) and an executor (wasi) when they sell property from a deceased's estate. While the executor faces potential liability if the property is claimed by a rightful owner, the judge is completely exempt. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind this difference, which hinges on the vital principle of maslahah (public interest) and its role in overriding strict analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two roles compared
The passage contrasts the judge (and his agent) with the executor (wasi). Recognize that both may sell estate property, but their legal status differs.
2
State the ruling for each
For the judge: no liability (la 'ahdah 'alayhi). For the executor: he is liable, similar to a wakil (agent).
The judge's agent is like a messenger, also not liable.
3
Locate the rationale for the judge's exemption
The text says imposing liability would deter people from becoming judges, disrupting the judiciary and harming public interest (maslahah 'ammah). This is the core reason.
This is an application of istihsan based on maslahah.
4
Explain why the executor is treated differently
The executor is not a public servant but a private appointee. He is like an agent (wakil), and agents bear liability for their actions. There is no overriding public interest to exempt him.
The passage refers to the Book of Judiciary for this principle.
5
Note the practical consequence if the estate cannot cover the loss
If the estate is lost or insufficient, the executor cannot recover the compensation from elsewhere, just as any unpaid debt of the deceased remains uncollected.
This highlights the executor's personal risk.

Commentary
This passage contrasts the executor (wasi) with the judge (qadi) and his agent. It states that when a judge sells estate property and the price is lost, and the property is found to belong to another, the judge is not liable (la 'ahdah 'alayhi). The reason is that imposing liability on judges would deter qualified individuals from accepting judicial office, disrupting the public interest (maslahah 'ammah). The judge's agent is like a messenger, not personally liable. The executor, however, is like an agent (wakil) and thus can be liable. The passage further notes that if the estate is lost or cannot cover the compensation, the executor cannot recover from anywhere else, similar to any other debt on the deceased that goes unpaid. The commentary reinforces that this distinction is based on the principle of maslahah, a key consideration in Hanafi usul.
