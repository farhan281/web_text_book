Position of the Khuntha Mushkil in Congregational Prayer
Ruling on the positioning of a khuntha mushkil in jama'at

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state where a khuntha mushkil stands in congregational prayer and identify the valid placement as well as the precautionary repetition rule.

Core Idea
The khuntha mushkil must stand between the rows to avoid invalidating anyone's prayer.

In congregational prayer, the proper formation of rows is crucial. When a person of ambiguous gender (khuntha mushkil) joins, uncertainty arises about which row they belong to. This passage from al-Quduri provides the rule: they stand alone between the rows, and if they inadvertently stand with men or women, specific precautionary measures apply. Let's work through this ruling step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two concerns
Recognize that the khuntha could be male or female, so placing them in either row risks invalidating someone's prayer—if they stand with men and are actually female, the adjacent men's prayers are invalidated; if they stand with women and are male, their own prayer is invalidated.
2
State the recommended position
The khuntha stands between the row of men and the row of women, forming a separate row alone behind the imam. This avoids any risk of invalidation.
This is the sunnah placement.
3
Analyze the case of standing in the women's row
If the khuntha stands in the women's row, they should repeat their own prayer as a precaution (ahwat), because there is a possibility they are a man, which would invalidate their prayer.
The text says 'ahabb ilayya' – it is preferable, not obligatory.
4
Analyze the case of standing in the men's row
If the khuntha stands in the men's row, their own prayer is valid (even if female, their prayer is considered valid), but the men to their right, left, and directly behind them should repeat their prayer as a precaution, because of the possibility that the khuntha is a woman.
The khuntha's prayer is valid because the uncertainty about their gender does not affect their own intention.

The Position of the Khuntha Mushkil in Congregational Prayer
Ruling on the standing position of the khuntha mushkil and consequences of deviation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct default position for a khuntha mushkil in congregational prayer and determine when repetition (i‘ādah) is required for himself or others.

Core Idea
The khuntha mushkil must stand between men's and women's rows to avoid invalidating any prayer.

In congregational prayer, proper row formation is essential. For a person of ambiguous gender (khuntha mushkil), uncertainty about their sex creates a dilemma: standing with men might invalidate theirs if they are a woman, and standing with women might invalidate their own if they are a man. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling from al-Quduri’s Mukhtasar, which establishes a default middle position and spells out the consequences of deviation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the ruling context
The subject is the khuntha mushkil (ambiguous hermaphrodite) in congregational prayer. The ruling addresses where such a person should stand relative to men's and women's rows.
The ruling is based on the principle of iḥtiyāṭ (precaution) due to uncertainty.
2
Parse the default position and its rationale
The default position is between the rows of men and women. The rationale is dual: to avoid invalidating others' prayers if the khuntha is actually a woman (since a woman praying among men invalidates their prayer in the Hanafi school), and to avoid invalidating his own prayer if he is actually a man (since a man praying among women invalidates his prayer).
'Between' typically means a separate row, not merging with either.
3
Analyze the two exceptional scenarios
If the khuntha stands in the women's row, i‘ādah (repetition) is recommended for him due to the possibility that he is a man. If he stands in the men's row, his own prayer is valid, but those adjacent or directly behind him should repeat as a precaution.
The repetition for the khuntha in the women's row is mustaḥabb (recommended), not wajib, as expressed by 'I prefer' (أحب إلي).
4
Determine the effect on others
When the khuntha stands in the men's row, only those on his immediate right, left, and directly behind must repeat their prayers. Others in the row are unaffected. This is because proximity in the same row is the cause of invalidation if the khuntha is a woman.
The precautionary repetition is iḥtiyāṭan, not wajib, but it is strongly recommended to ensure validity.

Prayer of the Hermaphrodite: Head-Cover and Posture
Ruling on dress and posture for the khuntha mushkil in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the khuntha mushkil's dress and sitting posture in prayer, and differentiate between the obligations for a murāhiq and a bāligh.

Core Idea
Precaution requires the khuntha to cover and sit like a woman; repetition is wajib for a bāligh but only mustahabb for a murāhiq.

This lesson examines how the Hanafi school addresses the prayer of a person whose sex is ambiguous (khuntha mushkil). The ruling balances between two possibilities: if the khuntha is actually a woman, covering and sitting modestly are obligations; if a man, the same acts are merely recommended or permissible. The key distinction is whether the khuntha has reached puberty (bāligh) or is still an adolescent (murāhiq).

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The passage concerns the khuntha mushkil (hermaphrodite of ambiguous sex) during prayer. Determine the two acts under discussion: wearing a head-cover (qinā‘) and sitting posture (julūs).
Khuntha mushkil is a person with both male and female physical signs whose true sex cannot be determined.
2
State the preferred (mustahabb) position
Imam Muhammad states it is preferable (ahabba ilaynā) for the khuntha to pray with a head-cover and to sit like a woman. Understand the reasoning: if the khuntha is a woman, these are obligatory; if a man, he merely abandons a sunnah (sitting like a man) and wears an extra sunnah (head-cover), which is permissible.
The head-cover is fard for a free woman but sunnah for a man in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the ruling for prayer without a head-cover
If the khuntha prays without a head-cover, Imam Muhammad says he would order him to repeat the prayer due to the possibility of being a woman. This repetition is mustahabb (recommended) for the murāhiq.
'Ahabba ilaynā' indicates a preferred opinion, not a categorical obligation.
4
Distinguish murāhiq from bāligh
The tanbīh (notice) clarifies that the above recommendation applies only to the murāhiq (adolescent). For a bāligh (adult) khuntha, repeating the prayer without a head-cover becomes wājib (obligatory). The reason is that for an adult woman, covering is definitively obligatory; thus, the duty of precaution becomes stronger.
This distinction is based on the point at which acts of worship become fully binding.
5
Apply the ruling to the sitting posture
Similarly, the khuntha must sit like a woman in prayer. If he sits like a man, and he is in fact a woman, he commits a makrūh (disliked) act. If he is a man, he only leaves a sunnah, which is permissible. The preferred posture is thus the woman's sitting.
Woman's sitting: sitting on the left thigh with feet spread to the right. Man's sitting: sitting on the left foot with right foot upright.

Ruling on the Hermaphrodite's Position in Prayer Rows
Ruling on where khuntha stands and who repeats prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the preferred ruling for a khuntha mushkil standing in the women's or men's prayer row and explain the principle of ihtiyat applied.

Core Idea
A khuntha mushkil repeats prayer when in women's row; those around him repeat when he is in men's row.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on where a person of ambiguous sex (khuntha mushkil) should stand in congregational prayer and who must repeat their prayer. The text from Ashraf al-Hidayah provides a precautionary (ihtiyat) approach based on the possibility of being the opposite sex.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note the two cases: the khuntha standing in the women's row versus the men's row. The ruling differs for each.
2
Determine who must repeat
In the women's row, the khuntha himself repeats his prayer. In the men's row, those around him (right, left, and behind) repeat their prayers, while his prayer is complete.
The khuntha does not repeat when in men's row.
3
Understand the reasoning
The ruling is based on ihtiyat (precaution) due to the possibility (ihtimal) of the khuntha being of the opposite sex. If in women's row, he might be male, invalidating his prayer leading women. If in men's row, he might be female, invalidating the row for those adjacent.
The reasoning is talil: the probability drives the precaution.
4
Apply the preferred view
The text states the 'ahabb ilayya' (preferred to me) view: the khuntha repeats when in women's row, and others repeat when he is in men's row. This is the mufta bihi position.

Ruling on khuntha's prayer: headcover and sitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a khuntha's headcover and sitting posture in prayer, distinguishing between baligh and murahiq.

Core Idea
For a khuntha, precaution dictates: wear headcover and sit like women to avoid the worse possibility.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling for a khuntha (hermaphrodite) regarding his prayer attire and posture. The key is the possibility of being female, which triggers precautionary obligations and recommendations. The passage from Imam Muhammad's al-Mabsut provides the framework, distinguishing between a mature (baligh) and near-mature (murahiq) khuntha.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Recognize that the khuntha is classified as baligh (mature) or murahiq (nearly mature). The ruling differs for each.
2
Understand the headcover ruling
For baligh: wearing a headcover (qina') is wajib; omission requires wajib repetition. For murahiq: wearing headcover is mustahabb; omission requires mustahabb repetition.
3
Understand the sitting posture ruling
Both baligh and murahiq should sit like women in prayer. For baligh, sitting like men would be makruh tahrimi if female; for murahiq, it is lighter (abandoning a sunnah if male).
4
Grasp the underlying principle
The ruling is based on the possibility of being female and the need to avoid the more severe prohibition (makruh tahrimi). The precautionary principle leads to adopting the more covering option.
5
Note the practical consequences
For baligh: repetition is wajib. For murahiq: repetition is mustahabb, and if not done, the prayer is still valid.

Prohibitions for Khuntha: Jewelry, Silk, Khalwa, Travel, and Ihram Ruling
Rulings on prohibitions for khuntha and dispute on ihram clothing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibitions for a khuntha mushkil and explain the Hanafi dispute regarding ihram clothing, applying the principle of akhaff al-dararayn.

Core Idea
Uncertain sex leads to prohibitions; in ihram, Muhammad's lesser evil rule prevails: wear women's clothing.

This lesson examines the special rulings for a khuntha mushkil (a person with ambiguous genitalia) whose sex is unknown. Due to this uncertainty, certain actions are prohibited out of precaution. The passage also presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about what such a person should wear when entering ihram for Hajj, resolved by Imam Muhammad's application of the principle of choosing the lesser harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general prohibitions
List the actions that are makruh/haram for the khuntha mushkil during his lifetime: wearing jewelry/silk, exposing awrah, khalwa with non-mahram, traveling without mahram. Note that these are based on ihtiyat because his sex is unknown.
The prohibitions apply regardless of his actual sex because he might be male or female.
2
Locate the dispute regarding ihram
The passage then focuses on what the khuntha should wear when he enters ihram and has reached near puberty (murahiq). Two Hanafi imams differ: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf says 'I have no knowledge' (la ilma li) about his clothing. He reasons that if male, wearing sewn garments (makhit) is disliked; if female, not wearing them is disliked. So he gives no definite ruling.
Abu Yusuf's view highlights the epistemological dilemma.
4
State Muhammad's position and his dalil
Muhammad rules that the khuntha should wear women's clothing (i.e., sewn garments). His evidence (dalil) is based on weighing harms: abandoning sewn garments if the khuntha is actually a woman is a more severe prohibition (afhash) than wearing them if he is a man. This is the principle of akhaff al-dararayn (choosing the lesser evil).
Muhammad also notes that since the khuntha has not reached puberty, there is no sin (la shay'a 'alayhi).
5
Determine the tarjih and practical consequence
The text implies Muhammad's view is preferred because it resolves the uncertainty with a clear ruling. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf): under Muhammad's view, the khuntha wears women's clothing in ihram; under Abu Yusuf's view, there is no clear guidance.
In the Hanafi school, the mufta bihi is Muhammad's view.

Placement of Khuntha Mushkil in Funeral Prayer
Ruling on the order of placing biers when a man, khuntha, and woman are present for funeral prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of placing the biers when a man, khuntha, and woman are present for funeral prayer and explain the reasoning based on gender probability.

Core Idea
A khuntha is placed between the man and woman in funeral prayer due to gender ambiguity.

When multiple deceased are present for funeral prayer, their biers are arranged in a specific order that mirrors the rows of living congregational prayer. This passage from Al-Hidayah explains how to place a khuntha mushkil—a person whose gender is uncertain—alongside a man and a woman. The ruling derives from the principle of giving precedence based on the stronger probability of gender, while maintaining respect for both possibilities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three biers
Note that there are three deceased: a man, a khuntha mushkil, and a woman. They are to be prayed over together in one funeral prayer.
The khuntha mushkil is someone whose gender cannot be determined.
2
State the order of placement
The man's bier is placed nearest to the imam. The khuntha's bier is placed behind the man. The woman's bier is placed behind the khuntha.
This order is from the author's statement: 'the man is placed next to the imam, the khuntha behind him, and the woman behind the khuntha.'
3
Explain the reasoning for the khuntha's placement
The khuntha is placed behind the man because there is a possibility that the khuntha is a woman, and a woman is placed behind the man. The khuntha is placed ahead of the woman because there is a possibility that the khuntha is a man, and a man is placed ahead of a woman.
This is the wisdom: 'due to the probability that he is a woman and due to the probability that he is a man.'
4
Connect to the living arrangement analogy
Just as in the living congregational prayer, the khuntha would stand in the middle row (behind the men and ahead of the women), so too in the funeral prayer the bier is placed in the middle.
The author explicitly says: 'Just as in the state of life there was an order among them, so now too the same order will be adopted.'
5
Summarize the ruling
The final order is: man (closest to imam) → khuntha (behind man) → woman (behind khuntha). This respects both possibilities of the khuntha's gender.

Ruling on arranging three biers for funeral prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for ordering three funeral biers (man, khuntha mushkil, woman) and explain the underlying reasoning based on analogy to living prayer rows and the principle of ihtimal (probability).

Core Idea
Funeral bier order mirrors living prayer rows: man nearest imam, then khuntha, then woman — the khuntha's middle position balances possibility of being either gender.

In Hanafi fiqh, when multiple funeral biers are present for simultaneous prayer, their placement follows a specific order. This lesson addresses the case of three biers: a man, a khuntha mushkil (ambiguous hermaphrodite), and a woman. The ruling is based on analogy to the order of rows in congregational prayer during life. The khuntha is placed after the man due to the possibility that it is a woman, and before the woman due to the possibility that it is a man. Understanding this ruling illustrates how the Hanafi school uses rational analogy and probability to derive practical rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Three funeral biers are present: one man, one khuntha mushkil, and one woman. The congregation wants to pray over them together.
The khuntha mushkil is someone whose gender cannot be determined.
2
Recall the living prayer row arrangement
In congregational prayer, men stand in the front rows, then children and khuntha, then women at the back.
This order is established in hadith and practice.
3
Apply the analogy to bier placement
The same order is adopted for the biers relative to the imam: the man's bier is placed closest to the imam, then the khuntha's, then the woman's farthest.
The analogy is based on the principle that death does not change the social/ritual order.
4
Explain the reasoning for the khuntha's middle position
The khuntha is placed after the man because it could be a woman, and before the woman because it could be a man. This balances both probabilities.
This shows the use of ihtimal (probability) in Hanafi fiqh.

The order is: man's bier closest to imam, then khuntha's bier, then woman's bier at the end.

Behind [the man], and the woman behind the [khuntha]; thus the khuntha is placed after the man due to the probability that it is a woman, and is placed before the woman due to the probability that it is a man. Explanation: If three funerals happen to be present at one time—one man, one khuntha mushkil, and one woman—and they want to pray over them together, then what order should be adopted for placing the biers? It was said: Just as in life there was an order among them, so now the same order will be adopted. In life, just as the khuntha's row was in the middle, so now it will be in the middle. Here the man's bier will be placed closest to the imam, then the khuntha's, and then the woman's at the very back. The rest is clear.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the order of funeral biers when a man, a khuntha mushkil (ambiguous hermaphrodite), and a woman are present simultaneously for prayer. The principle stated is that the arrangement mirrors the order of rows in life: in congregational prayer, men stand in the front rows, then children and khuntha, then women at the back. Here, for funeral prayer, the same logic applies: the man's bier is placed nearest to the imam, the khuntha's in the middle, and the woman's at the end. The reasoning is based on the probability of gender: the khuntha is placed after the man because it could be a woman, and before the woman because it could be a man. The author emphasizes that this order follows the analogy of living arrangement. No explicit proof from Quran or Hadith is cited; the ruling is derived from rational analogy and established practice.
