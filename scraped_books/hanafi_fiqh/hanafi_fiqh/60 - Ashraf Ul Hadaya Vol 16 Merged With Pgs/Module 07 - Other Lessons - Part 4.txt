Testimony on Debt vs Bequest: The Dhimma-Estate Distinction
Explains why testimony regarding bequest after death is rejected due to shared interest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi fiqh accepts testimony of one creditor for another regarding a debt during the debtor's lifetime but rejects it after death, using the distinction between dhimma and estate.

Core Idea
Debt attaches to the dhimma; bequest attaches to the estate. Sharing creates suspicion, nullifying testimony.

This lesson explores a nuanced rule in Hanafi evidence law: testimony regarding a debt is accepted during the debtor's lifetime but rejected after death when it concerns a bequest. The rationale lies in how debt and bequest relate to the dhimma (legal personality) and the estate (tarka). Understanding this distinction sharpens your ability to apply principles of legal suspicion (tuhma) and shared interest (shirka).

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Formulate the ruling clearly: testimony of one creditor for another about a debt is accepted while the debtor is alive; after death, such testimony is rejected.
This is the mufta bihi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Identify the essential distinction
The key difference is that a debt is an obligation on the dhimma (which survives until death), whereas a bequest is directly linked to the estate. The dhimma can accommodate multiple rights without creating shared ownership in specific property.
Dhimma is not a physical thing; it is the legal capacity that accepts obligations.
3
Understand why dhimma prevents sharing
During life, the debt is 'in the dhimma' — an abstract obligation. If two creditors claim, the debtor owes each separately; there is no joint claim on a particular asset. Thus, no shared interest (shirka) arises, and testimony is free from suspicion.
Reason: the dhimma remains intact and can bear multiple rights without mixing.
4
Understand why estate creates sharing
After death, the dhimma ceases (it is 'corrupted' by death). The debt then attaches to the estate, a concrete pool of assets. If one creditor takes his share, the remaining assets are shared among the others. This creates shirka, which generates tuhma (suspicion) and invalidates testimony.
The proof: 'due to death the debt attaches to the estate because the dhimma is corrupted.'
5
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Consider a stranger who voluntarily pays the debt of a deceased debtor on behalf of one creditor. The payment benefits only that creditor; the other creditors do not share it. This illustrates that after death, rights become concretely attached to the estate, but voluntary payments do not create the same shared interest unless they come from the estate itself.
This reinforces the distinction: sharing arises from the estate, not from outside payments.

Mutual Testimony with Different Bequests
Ruling on mutual testimony with different bequeathed items.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mutual testimony with different bequests and explain why it is valid by identifying the absence of shirka.

Core Idea
When bequests are of different items, mutual testimony is valid because there is no shared interest (shirka) to create suspicion (tuhma).

This lesson examines a specific case from al-Jami' al-Saghir where two pairs of people testify about each other's bequests. At first glance, mutual testimony might seem questionable due to potential bias. However, the Hanafi school clarifies that when the bequests are for different items (a slave-girl and a slave), there is no shared interest, hence no suspicion. The ruling is accepted by consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling: validity of testimony
The text states the testimony is permissible (ja'iz) by consensus. This is the legal ruling for this scenario.
2
Determine the potential invalidator
In Hanafi law, testimony can be invalidated by tuhma (suspicion of bias) when the witness stands to benefit personally from the testimony.
Tuhma arises from shared interest or close relationship.
3
Examine the reasoning (ta'lil)
The text gives the reason: 'because there is no partnership (shirka)'. The bequests are of two different items (a slave-girl and a slave), so the two groups have no shared interest in the same property.
The absence of shirka removes the tuhma.
4
Confirm the consensus
The ruling is by consensus (bil-ittifaq), meaning there is no disagreement among Hanafi scholars on this point.

The ruling is that this mutual testimony is valid by consensus (bil-ittifaq). The reason is that the items bequeathed are different (a slave-girl versus a slave), so there is no shared interest (shirka) between the two groups. Without shared interest, there is no suspicion (tuhma) to invalidate the testimony. The commentary emphasizes that despite the bequests linking both groups, the distinct nature of the bequests prevents any partnership, making the testimony acceptable.

Testimony Void When Bequests Establish Partnership
Ruling on mutual testimony when bequests overlap

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when testimony is void due to establishing partnership (shirka) in bequests and apply the ruling to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
Testimony that creates partnership in the same bequest property is void due to suspicion of collusion.

In this passage from al-Hidayah, we examine two scenarios where testimonies about bequests are mutually supporting but create a shared interest. The key principle is that any testimony which establishes a partnership (shirka) between the testifier and the beneficiary is invalid. Understanding this ruling helps prevent fraudulent claims and ensures the integrity of inheritance proceedings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parties in each testimony
In both cases, there are two groups: one group testifies for the other, and vice versa. Label them Group A and Group B. Note what each group claims about the bequest.
Group A testifies that the deceased bequeathed to Group B; Group B testifies that the deceased bequeathed to Group A.
2
Determine the bequest content
In the first case, both testimonies claim a one-third share. In the second case, one claims a specific slave and the other claims a one-third share. Recognize that the slave is part of the one-third portion of the estate.
The value of the slave is included in the one-third of the estate.
3
Check for shared interest in the same property
In both cases, the testimonies create a claim over the same portion of the estate (the one-third). This establishes a partnership (shirka) in that property.
Even if the bequest appears different (slave vs. one-third), overlap exists because the slave is part of the one-third.
4
Apply the ruling: testimony void
Since the testimonies establish partnership, they are void (batil) due to the suspicion (tuhma) that the testimonies are collusive or self-serving.
The ruling is consistent across both cases.
5
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is that any testimony that creates a shared interest between the witness and the beneficiary invalidates the testimony. This protects against fraudulent claims.
This principle applies broadly in Hanafi fiqh to prevent conflict of interest.

Definition of Khuntha and Gender Determination by Urination
Defines khuntha and gives the primary ruling for gender identity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khuntha and state the Hanafi rule for determining its gender based on urination, citing the primary proof.

Core Idea
The khuntha's gender is determined by the organ from which he urinates—the primary organ is the source of urine.

The khuntha, or hermaphrodite, is a rare condition where a child is born with both male and female genitalia. This lesson presents the Hanafi definition and the primary ruling for gender identification, based on the prophetic directive to determine gender by urination. This ruling is foundational for inheritance and other legal matters.

Guided Path
1
Define the khuntha
Identify the terms: farj (vulva) and dhakar (penis). A child with both is called khuntha.
The term khuntha is used for both sexes.
2
Apply the urine test
Observe which organ the child urinates from. This determines the gender.
Only one organ is functional; the other is considered a defect.
3
State the ruling
If urine comes from the penis, the child is a boy. If from the vulva, the child is a girl.
This ruling applies to inheritance and other legal matters.
4
Identify the proof
The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'From where he urinates.' A similar narration is from Ali (may Allah be pleased with him).
The hadith is the primary textual evidence.
5
Understand the rationale
Urination indicates the primary and functional organ; the other is extra, like an extra finger.
This is a qiyas based on the hadith.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on using urine quantity to determine the gender of a khuntha, explain the reasoning of both sides, and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
Urine quantity is not a reliable gender indicator for a khuntha — Abu Hanifa's view prevails because anatomical width, not gender, determines flow.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Sahibayn) regarding whether the amount of urine discharged from each organ can determine the gender of a person with ambiguous genitalia (khuntha). The Sahibayn apply the maxim 'the majority takes the ruling of the whole', while Abu Hanifa argues that quantity depends on the width of the passage, not the true gender. Understanding this dispute highlights how Hanafi jurists weigh rational evidence (dalil aqli) and legal maxims in deriving rules for difficult cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
The issue is whether the quantity of urine from each organ determines the gender of a khuntha. The Sahibayn say yes; Abu Hanifa says no.
2
Understand the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn invoke the maxim 'al-akthar hukm al-kull' (the majority takes the ruling of the whole). They argue that greater output indicates that organ is the dominant and primary one, so gender should be assigned accordingly.
This is a qiyas-based application of a usul maxim.
3
Understand Abu Hanifa's counter-proof
Abu Hanifa counters that quantity is not based on a sound cause (sabab) but on physical dimensions. He gives the example: the male urethra is narrow, the female vagina is wide, so a wider passage will naturally discharge more urine regardless of true gender. Therefore, quantity is unreliable.
This is a dalil aqli (rational argument) based on anatomical observation.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
If one organ produces more urine, the Sahibayn would assign gender based on that organ (if it is male, the person is male). Abu Hanifa would not, leaving the person as khuntha mushkil unless other signs appear. If output is equal, both agree it is ambiguous.
The practical consequence directly affects inheritance, marriage, and religious obligations.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi works (e.g., Al-Hidayah, Fatawa Alamgiri) adopt Abu Hanifa's view. Thus, urine quantity is not considered as a criterion in the relied-upon position.
This is a tarjih based on the strength of Abu Hanifa's reasoning and later scholarly consensus.

This lesson introduces the foundational principle governing all rulings for a khuntha mushkil (ambiguous hermaphrodite). In the absence of certainty about gender, the Hanafi school prescribes that one must act with the utmost caution (ahwat) and reliability (awthaq) in every religious matter, and refrain from imposing any ruling whose basis is doubtful. This principle ensures that acts of worship are valid and obligations are not assumed without clear evidence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

When an ambiguous hermaphrodite (khuntha mushkil) who is not yet adult (murahiq) enters ihram, what should they wear? Both Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani agree that no sin is incurred due to non-adulthood, but they differ on whether a definitive instruction can be given. This lesson explores their arguments and the Hanafi approach to handling uncertainty in such ambiguous cases.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of khuntha mushkil
Recall that a khuntha mushkil has ambiguous gender signs; in Hanafi fiqh, precaution is taken to avoid haram. Note the general makruh rulings: wearing jewelry/silk, exposing the body, seclusion, and travel with non-mahram are all makruh to avoid possible haram.
These general rulings set the stage for the specific ihram issue.
2
Present the ihram dilemma
A murahiq khuntha mushkil enters ihram. The gender is unknown. For an adult male, stitched clothes are prohibited (haram) in ihram. For an adult female, unstitched clothes are prohibited. What should this child wear?
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf says: 'I have no knowledge of his clothing.' He cannot decide because if the khuntha is male, wearing stitched (like women's clothes) is makruh; if female, leaving stitched (i.e., wearing unstitched) is makruh. Muhammad al-Shaybani says: 'He will wear women's clothes (stitched).' His reasoning: For a man, wearing stitched clothes in ihram is prohibited, but that prohibition is less severe than a woman wearing unstitched clothes. Moreover, since the child is not adult, no sin is incurred. Therefore, giving a definite ruling (women's clothes) is procedurally safer.
Muhammad invokes the principle of choosing the lesser harm (akhaf al-dararayn).
5

The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf): Abu Yusuf leaves the khuntha without a clear directive, effectively allowing both options. Muhammad gives a specific ruling (women's clothes), removing ambiguity. The passage does not give a tarjih (preference) between the two.
This ikhtilaf is recorded but not resolved; later Hanafi works may have a preferred view.

Shroud a khuntha mushkil in five garments to fulfill the sunnah regardless of actual gender.

When a person of ambiguous gender (khuntha mushkil) passes away, the question arises: should the shroud be that of a man or a woman? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling, which opts for the safer approach – five garments – to cover both possibilities. The reasoning combines fulfilling the sunnah for women while allowing extra cloth for men.

The ruling applies specifically to a khuntha mushkil (hermaphrodite with unclear dominant gender).

The standard sunnah for a man is three garments; for a woman, five. Using five for the khuntha covers both possibilities.

The preferred and relied-upon opinion is five garments.

Shrouding of the Khuntha Mushkil in Five Garments

Shafi'i Qiyas on Gesture for the Temporarily Speechless
States the Shafi'i position allowing gesture for both types with an analogy to emergency slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Shafi'i argument for the validity of gesture for the temporarily speechless, identify the Hanafi response, and evaluate the qiyas used.

Core Idea
Inability alone does not justify gesture; the Shafi'i analogy to emergency slaughter fails because it is a textual dispensation, not a qiyas.

This lesson examines a key inter-madhhab dispute: does a person whose tongue is temporarily tied share the same ruling as a congenital mute regarding the validity of gesture (ishara) for making a will? The Shafi'i school says yes, based on the common cause of inability and an analogy to emergency slaughter of animals. The Hanafi school rejects this analogy and distinguishes the two cases. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to evaluate qiyas arguments and recognize where textual dispensations override analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Recall that in the Hanafi school, the gesture of a congenital mute (akhras) is valid immediately because his gestures are known and customary. For a temporarily speechless person (mu'taqil al-lisan), gesture is not initially accepted; his condition must persist long enough (e.g., a year or until death) for his gestures to become familiar.
The Hanafi school distinguishes based on the duration and familiarity of gestures.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that gesture is valid in both cases from the outset. The permissibility-granting factor is inability (ajz), which is equally present in both the mute and the temporarily speechless.
Shafi'i does not distinguish between original and temporary inability.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Shafi'i supports his view with a qiyas: just as emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari) is permitted for wild animals due to inability to perform normal slaughter, and also for feral domestic animals because the reason (wildness) is the same, similarly gesture is permitted for both types of speechless due to the common cause of inability.
The analogy compares two pairs: wild/feral animals and mute/temporarily speechless.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response rejects the qiyas. The permissibility of emergency slaughter for feral domestic animals is a dispensation (rukhsa) established by a specific text (nas), not by qiyas. In fact, qiyas would require it not to be permitted. Therefore, the analogy is invalid. Additionally, the temporarily speechless person is negligent for delaying the will, unlike the congenital mute.
The Hanafi school argues that a rukhsah cannot be extended by qiyas, and that the temporary person's negligence precludes the same leniency.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under the Hanafi school, the will of a temporarily speechless person is not valid through gesture unless his condition persists for a prolonged period (typically a year or until death). Under the Shafi'i school, it is valid immediately.
The difference affects real-life cases of sudden illness or accidents.

The Three Hanafi Differences Between the Congenital Mute and the Temporarily Speechless
Explains the three Hanafi differences between the congenital mute and the temporarily speechless, including the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the duration of imtidad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and explain the three Hanafi reasons for distinguishing a congenital mute from a temporarily speechless person, and apply the condition of imtidad (persistence) for the validity of gestures.

Core Idea
The temporary speechless is not like the mute until his gestures become known and his condition persists; the relied-upon view is persistence until death.

In this passage, Al-Hidayah outlines the Hanafi rationale for treating a congenital mute differently from a person who loses speech temporarily. The school builds on the principle that gesture-based communication is only valid when it is customary and known. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for issues of wills and testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Clearly define akhras (congenital mute) and mu'taqil al-lisan (temporarily speechless). The mute was never able to speak; the temporarily speechless lost speech due to a recent accident or illness.
The key difference is the origin and expected duration of the condition.
2
First difference: Gesture validity and imtidad
Gesture is only valid when it becomes customary and known. For the mute, this is true from the start. For the temporarily speechless, his gestures are not initially known. However, if his condition persists (imtidad) long enough for gestures to become known, he becomes like the mute.
There is ikhtilaf on the duration: some say one year, others (mufta bihi) say until death.
3
Second difference: Negligence (qusur)
The temporarily speechless is considered negligent because he could have made a will earlier while able to speak. The congenital mute has no such opportunity, so he is not at fault.
This fault justifies treating them differently in the early period.
4
Third difference: Permanent vs. temporary
The mute's condition is original and permanent (asli), while the temporary's condition is accidental and expected to end (aridi). Therefore, they cannot be analogized to each other.
This is a usul argument: qiyas between fundamentally different cases is invalid.
5
Practical consequence
The gesture of a temporarily speechless person is only valid after prolonged persistence. Until then, his gesture is not accepted for wills or testimony.

Commentary
This passage provides the Hanafi reasoning (talil) for distinguishing between the congenital mute (akhras) and the temporarily speechless (mu'taqil al-lisan). Three differences are given: (1) Gesture is only valid when it becomes customary and known; this is initially only true for the congenital mute, but if the temporary condition persists long enough (some say one year, others say until death), then his gestures become known and he is treated like the mute. (2) The temporarily speechless is negligent for delaying his will while he could speak; the congenital mute is not negligent. (3) The congenital condition is permanent, while the temporary condition is expected to end, so they cannot be analogized. The text also refutes Shafi'i's analogy to domestic animals that become feral, stating that the permissibility of emergency slaughter for feral animals is established by text (nas) against qiyas, so it cannot be used as a basis for qiyas in another case. The fatwa is that if the temporary condition persists until death, the person is treated like the mute.

Key Terms
معتقل اللسان (mu'taqil al-lisan): A person whose tongue is temporarily tied, unable to speak due to a recent accident or illness.
امتداد (imtidad): Persistence or prolongation of the speech impediment, which may eventually make the person like a congenital mute.
قصور (qusur): Negligence or fault; the temporarily speechless is considered qasir for delaying the will.
أخرس (akhras): Congenitally mute person, born without speech.

Refuting the Shafi'i Analogy: The Case of Feral Animals and the Rule of Qiyas with Text
Refutes the Shafi'i analogy between the temporarily speechless and feral domestic animals, establishing the usul principle that rulings based on text contrary to qiyas are not subject to analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school rejects the Shafi'i analogy between the temporarily speechless person and a domestic animal that turns feral, and identify the principle that something established by text contrary to qiyas cannot be used for analogy.

Core Idea
The permissibility of emergency slaughter for feral domestic animals is based on text, not qiyas, so it cannot analogize the speechless condition.

The passage concludes by addressing an analogy raised by Imam al-Shafi'i, who compared the temporarily speechless person to a domesticated animal that becomes wild. The Hanafi response illustrates a fundamental usul principle: rulings established by text (nass) that contradict qiyas cannot serve as a basis for further qiyas. This reinforces the Hanafi reliance on textual authority over analogical reasoning in certain cases.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Shafi'i analogy
Imam al-Shafi'i analogized the temporarily speechless (whose gesture validity is unclear) to a domesticated animal that turns feral (mutawahhish): just as emergency slaughter is allowed for the feral animal despite the rule requiring proper slaughter, so should gesture be allowed for the temporarily speechless.
The Shafi'i view extends the analogy to argue for validity of gesture.
2
Identify the Hanafi response
The Hanafi rebuttal: the permissibility of emergency slaughter for feral domestic animals is established by a specific text (nass) of the Prophet, which operates contrary to the normal qiyas. Therefore, it is an exception that cannot be analogized to other cases.
The principle: 'ma thabita bil-nass khilafan lil-qiyas la yuqasu 'alayhi'.
3
Apply the principle to the case
Since the feral animal ruling is an isolated exception based on text, it cannot be extended via qiyas to validate the gesture of the temporarily speechless. The school maintains its original ruling based on the three differences.
4
Recognize the broader usul lesson
This shows the Hanafi commitment to text over analogy when the text explicitly overrides qiyas. It also demonstrates how exceptions are not to be used as precedents for analogy.

Validity of the Mute's Contracts and Declarations
Ruling on the validity of mute's contracts through writing or recognized gesture

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of contracts (marriage, divorce, manumission, sale, purchase) and legal declarations (qisas, hadd) made by a mute person via writing or recognized gesture, and explain the rationale and the distinction between qisas and hadd.

Core Idea
The mute's writing or recognized gesture is valid for contracts and qisas, but not for hadd.

The Hanafi school recognizes the legal capacity of a mute person to perform contracts and make legal declarations through writing or clear gestures. This ruling, based on the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) sending written letters, equates writing to speech when speech is impossible. However, a critical distinction is made between qisas (a personal right) and hadd (a divine right), with hadd requiring the highest certainty and thus not being established by the mute's declaration alone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Person
Determine that the person is mute (akhras) and that his writing or gesture is recognized (ma'ruf). The act can be a contract (nikah, talaq, itq, bay', shira') or a legal claim/confession (qisas or hadd).
The mute's inability is permanent, unlike an absent person who can speak.
2
State the Ruling for Contracts
For contracts (marriage, divorce, manumission, sale, purchase), the mute's writing or recognized gesture makes the contract valid (sahih). This includes all transactions and family law agreements.
Gestures must be commonly understood in the context.
3
State the Ruling for Qisas
Qisas (retaliation) can be claimed by or against the mute through writing or gesture. If the mute confesses to qisas or accuses another, and the claim is proven, qisas is established.
Qisas is a private right of the victim or heirs.
4
State the Ruling for Hadd
Hadd punishments (e.g., for theft or adultery) are NOT established by the mute's declaration. Neither will the mute be subjected to hadd nor can he initiate a hadd claim against others.
Hadd is a right of Allah; doubts remove it.
5
Analyze the Proof
The proof is the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) who conveyed the message both verbally and in writing to absent people. Writing from a distant person is equivalent to speech of a present person because of inability. The mute's inability is even greater, making his writing even more compelling.
The proof is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted via istihsan and practice.

Three Categories of Writing and Their Legal Effect
Classification of writing into three levels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any piece of writing into one of three Hanafi categories (mustabīn marsūm, mustabīn ghayr marsūm, ghayr mustabīn) and state its corresponding legal effect.

Core Idea
Clear and customary writing has full legal force; unclear writing is legally silent.

In Hanafi fiqh, writing (kitabah) is not a monolithic category. The fuqaha have classified writing into three levels based on clarity and custom. Understanding this classification is essential because the legal effect of a written declaration—whether for divorce, sale, or other contracts—depends on which category it falls into. This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah lays out the three categories with clear examples.

Check clarity (ustubān vs ghayr ustubān)
Is the writing clear and legible (mustabīn) or not (ghayr mustabīn)? If not clear, it has no legal effect.
Ghayr mustabīn includes writing on air, water, or any impermanent medium.
3
Check custom (marsūm vs ghayr marsūm)
If the writing is clear, is it on a customary medium (marsūm) like paper, or on an unconventional medium (ghayr marsūm) like a wall or leaves?
Marsūm means writing that is customary for written documents.
4
Determine legal effect
If clear and customary → equivalent to speech, no intention needed. If clear but not customary → requires intention (niyyah) for its effect. If not clear → no legal effect.
The second category is like explicit metaphor (sarih al-kinnaya).

Commentary
This passage defines the three categories of writing (kitabah) for legal purposes. The first category is writing that is clear (visible) and customary (مستبين مرسوم). This writing has the same legal effect as spoken words for both present and absent people. The second is writing that is clear but not customary (مستبين غير مرسوم), such as writing on a wall or on leaves. This requires intention (niyyah) because it is like an explicit metaphor (صريح الكناية) — meaning it is understood as writing but the specific intention behind it matters. Without intention, its legal effect is not certain. The third is writing that is not clear (غير مستبين), like writing on air or water. This is like unheard speech and has no legal effect whatsoever. The classification helps determine when a written statement can serve as a valid declaration for contracts, divorces, etc. Only the first category is treated as fully equivalent to speech.

The Three Categories of Writing for a Mute Person
Classification of writing types with rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three categories of writing for a mute person and state the legal ruling for each category.

Core Idea
A mute's writing is like speech, but only clear addressed writing is fully binding without intention.

When a person cannot speak, writing or gesture becomes their voice. Not all writing, however, carries the same legal weight. The Hanafi school, based on the practice of the Prophet ﷺ, classifies writing into three levels depending on clarity and address. This lesson unpacks each category and its effect on contracts, claims, and hudud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Start by understanding the central rule: a mute's writing and gesture are valid for contracts (marriage, divorce, manumission, sale) and claims, except hudud. This is because writing substitutes speech for those unable to speak.
Hudud are rights of Allah and averted by doubts, so stricter proof is needed.
2
Learn the three categories of writing
The text breaks writing into: (1) clear with address (mastabin marsum) – sender and recipient identifiable, (2) clear without address (mastabin ghayr marsum) – legible but not addressed, (3) unclear (ghayr mastabin) – illegible, like on air/water.
Arabic terms: مستبین مرسوم, مستبین غیر مرسوم, غیر مستبین.
3
Apply the ruling to each category
For clear addressed writing: it is like speech, fully binding without need for intention. For clear unaddressed: it is like kinayah (metaphor) and requires the writer's intention to be effective. For unclear: it is like inaudible speech and has no legal effect.
Examples: a letter on paper with address vs. writing on a wall without address.
4
Examine the proof (dalil)
The proof is the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ sending letters to distant tribes. Since an absent person's writing equals speech, and a mute is even more unable to speak, his writing is at least as valid.
Dalil is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for this ruling.
5
Note exceptions and nuances
Writing or gesture is not accepted for hudud (acknowledgment or claim). Also, if a mute acknowledges qisas, it is valid, but hadd is not. This shows the school's care in distinguishing rights of Allah from rights of individuals.
The mirror principle: what applies to the absent applies to the mute, a fortiori.

The Validity of a Mute's Gesture in Huquq al-‘Ibad vs. Hudud
Explaining why a mute's gesture is valid for huquq al-‘ibad but not for hudud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mute's gesture and explain why it is accepted for huquq al-‘ibad but not for hudud, citing the underlying principles of need (hajah) and aversion of doubts (shubuhat).

Core Idea
A mute's gesture is valid for huquq al-‘ibad due to need, but not for hudud because they are averted by doubts.

In this lesson, we examine the second means of communication for a mute person: gesture (ishara). The Hanafi school holds that a well-known gesture is a valid proof (hujjah) for establishing rights of servants (huquq al-‘ibad) such as contracts and qisas. However, for hudud (fixed penalties), gesture is not accepted. Why? The reason lies in the nature of the rights involved and the principle that hudud are averted by doubts. Let's explore the reasoning from Al-Hidayah.

Analyze acceptance for huquq al-‘ibad
Huquq al-‘ibad (rights of servants) are not restricted to specific words; they can be established by any clear indication. Since the mute cannot speak, gesture becomes necessary.
Contracts like sale, marriage, divorce, and qisas fall under this category.

However, for hudud (fixed penalties), gesture is not accepted because hudud are rights of Allah (haqq Allah) and are averted by doubts (shubuhat). Even in the case of qadhf (false accusation of adultery), the mute's gesture is not sufficient for hadd because explicit slander (qadhf sarih) is a condition, and gesture cannot provide that explicitness. The translation in Urdu reiterates the same rationale.

حدود (ḥudūd): Fixed penalties prescribed for specific crimes (e.g., theft, zina, qadhf). They are rights of Allah and are averted by doubts.

The Mute's Gesture and Writing: Equality and Distinction
Expounding Imam Muhammad's ruling on the mute person's gesture and writing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that a mute person's gesture and writing are equally valid legal expressions and distinguish this from the case of the tongue-tied person (mu'taqil al-lisan) whose gestures are invalid.

Core Idea
A mute person's gesture and writing are equally valid; the tongue-tied person's gestures are invalid.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether a mute person's gesture remains valid when he can write. While some scholars argued that gesture is subsidiary and thus invalid when writing is possible, the author, citing Imam Muhammad's explicit text, affirms that both are equal substitutes for speech. The lesson also clarifies the critical distinction between a true mute (akhras), who lacks the speech organ, and the tongue-tied (mu'taqil), whose speech organ is intact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed issue
The act is the mute person's gesture (īmā') and writing (kitāba) as means of legal expression (e.g., for marriage, divorce, sale). The dispute is whether gesture is valid when the mute is able to write.
The dispute is within the Hanafi school; some earlier scholars held that gesture becomes invalid.
2
State the opposing view and its proof
Some Hanafi scholars argued that when the mute can write, gesture is not considered. Their proof: the principle 'necessity is measured by its extent' – since writing suffices, gesture (based on necessity) drops off.
They considered writing as the original means of expression for the literate mute.
3
Present the Hanafi rebuttal and proof
The author rebuts by citing Imam Muhammad's text in al-Jami' al-Saghir, which treats gesture and writing equally. Both are based on necessity; neither is original – speech is original. Therefore both are valid substitutes.
The author adds that gesture can be clearer in some aspects, and writing is a distant effect while gesture is a near effect (directly from the body).
4
Define the key terms: akhras vs. mu'taqil
Akhras is a person born mute or who has lost speech ability, lacking the instrument of speech. Mu'taqil al-lisan is a person whose tongue is tied (e.g., due to a stroke) but the speech organ is intact. For mu'taqil, gestures are invalid because he can potentially speak.
This distinction is crucial: the ruling applies only to akhras, not to mu'taqil.
5
Apply the ruling to practical scenarios
A mute person can lawfully contract marriage, divorce, or sale by a clear gesture or by writing. Both are equally binding. A tongue-tied person's gestures are invalid; he must wait until he can speak or use writing if possible but writing is not directly addressed in this passage.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is the equality of gesture and writing for the akhras.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute. The author explains that some Hanafi scholars incorrectly thought that when a mute person is able to write, his gestures are invalid. They based this on the principle 'necessity is limited by its extent' and argued that since writing (the original necessity) is possible, gestures (a subsidiary necessity) should be disregarded. Both are substitutes for speech due to necessity, and neither is inherently superior. The author further argues that from one perspective, writing is clearer; from another, gestures are closer to speech. Thus both are equal. The passage then clarifies the definition of 'mu'taqil al-lisan' (the tongue-tied). This refers to a person whose speech organ is intact but who is temporarily or permanently unable to speak. For such a person, gestures are not valid because the instrument of speech is present, unlike the entirely mute person (akhras) who lacks the instrument. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that both gesture and writing are valid for the mute, and the one whose tongue is tied (mu'taqil) cannot use gestures.

In this passage, the author presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute: whether meat from a mixed herd of halal-slaughtered and carrion animals can be eaten when the halal are more numerous. Al-Shafi'i says it is not permissible in a state of choice because taharri is a proof only for necessity, and here there is no necessity.

Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In practice, Hanafis allow eating in such cases, while Shafi'is do not, leading to differences when buying meat from unknown sources.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the permissibility of eating meat when there is a mixture of halal-slaughtered animals and carrion. The author first states the Hanafi ruling: it is permissible in any case to eat, after performing taharri (investigation) when the halal are predominant. He then introduces Imam al-Shafi'i's opposing view. Al-Shafi'i argues that in a state of choice (i.e., no necessity), it is not permissible to eat even if the halal are more numerous, because taharri is a proof only resorted to due to necessity, and here there is no necessity. The Hanafi response, presented in Arabic and explained in Urdu, is that predominance (ghalabah) takes the place of necessity (darurah) in conferring permissibility. The proof is an analogy to the markets of Muslims: despite the presence of prohibited, stolen, and usurped goods, purchasing is allowed relying on the predominant halal items. This is because the small amount of haram cannot be completely avoided, and disregarding it removes hardship (haraj), similar to the rules of small impurity and small exposure. However, if the halal and carrion are equal or carrion is predominant, permissibility does not apply due to lack of necessity or predominance. The commentator emphasizes that both necessity and predominance are causes for permissibility, and the ruling is based on the principle that difficulty necessitates ease.

Key Terms
تحری (taharrī): Investigation or attempt to determine the majority or likely halal status when halal and haram are mixed.
غلبہ (ghalabah): Predominance; the condition where halal items outnumber haram items, leading to permissibility.
ضرورت (darūrah): Necessity that makes prohibited things permissible to preserve life or avoid severe hardship.
حرج (haraj): Hardship or difficulty that necessitates leniency in the law, based on the principle 'hardship begets ease'.
قلیل نجاست (qalīl najāsah): A small amount of impurity that is excused and does not affect the validity of worship.
