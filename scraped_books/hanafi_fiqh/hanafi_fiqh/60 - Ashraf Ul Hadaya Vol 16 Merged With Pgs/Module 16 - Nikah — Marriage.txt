Dhimmi's Will to Build a Church: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute with qiyas and a distinction between building and will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on a dhimmi's will to build a church, explain the basis for the difference, and apply the distinction between building (like waqf) and bequeathing.

Core Idea
A dhimmi's will is valid if the act is pious in their own belief.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa validates a dhimmi's will to build a church because the dhimmi considers it an act of piety, while the Sahibayn invalidate it because building a church is disobedience in Islamic law. The passage also clarifies why building a church in one's lifetime does not transfer ownership, but a will to build does, using the concept of muhraz (exclusive dedication).

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed act
What exactly is the act under dispute? A dhimmi (non-Muslim subject) makes a will to build a church for an unspecified group of people. Is this will valid?
The will is for a specific purpose, not a general charity.
2
State the two Hanafi positions
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the will is valid (sahih). According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the will is invalid (batil).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze each school's proof
The Sahibayn argue that building a church is inherently a sin (ma'siyat) in Islam, and a will for sin is void. Abu Hanifa argues that for dhimmis, validity depends on the testator's belief: if the act is considered piety (qurbat) in their religion, the will is valid because we are commanded to leave them to their faith.
Abu Hanifa uses an analogy: if a dhimmi bequeaths for a mosque (true piety but disobedience in their belief), we invalidate it out of respect for their belief. So conversely, if they bequeath for a church (disobedience in Islam but piety in their belief), we validate it.
4
Understand the distinction: building vs. bequeathing
Why does Abu Hanifa treat a dhimmi building a church in his lifetime differently from a will to build? Because building alone does not transfer ownership; ownership transfers only when the property becomes exclusively dedicated to Allah (muhraz). Churches are not muhraz (they have rooms and human rights), so the builder retains ownership and it passes to heirs. But a will is a tool for ownership transfer; if the bequest is for a pious act according to the testator, it is valid and ownership transfers upon death, so it is not inherited.
This distinction resolves a potential objection against Abu Hanifa.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, the will is executed: the property is used to build the church and does not revert to the testator's estate. Under the Sahibayn, the will is void, so the property remains in the estate and is inherited by the testator's heirs.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute followed by a distinction between two cases. The issue: If a dhimmi (non-Muslim subject) makes a will to build a church or synagogue for an unspecified group of people, Imam Abu Hanifa considers the will valid, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) consider it invalid. The Sahibayn argue that such a will is inherently invalid because building a church is an act of disobedience in Islamic law, and a will for disobedience is void; executing it would promote falsehood. Abu Hanifa argues that validity depends on the testator's belief. Since the dhimmi believes building a church is an act of piety (qurba), and the Shariah commands us to leave them to their beliefs, the will is valid. He uses a qiyas: if a dhimmi bequeaths something that is actually piety (like a mosque) but in their belief it is disobedience, we invalidate it out of respect for their belief; similarly, if they bequeath something that is disobedience in our law but piety in their belief, we validate it out of respect for their belief. The commentary then addresses a potential objection: why does Abu Hanifa treat a dhimmi building a church in his lifetime differently from a will to build? The author explains the distinction: building itself does not transfer ownership; ownership only transfers when the property becomes exclusively dedicated to Allah (muhraz), like a mosque. Since churches are not genuinely dedicated (they contain rooms and are used for residence), the builder retains ownership, so the property is inherited. But a will is a legal instrument designed to transfer ownership; if the bequest is for something the testator believes to be pious, the will is valid and ownership transfers upon death, so it is not inherited. Thus, building (like waqf) is non-binding and does not transfer ownership, while a bequest is binding and does transfer ownership.

Key Terms
قربت (qurbat): Piety or an act that brings one closer to God according to one's own religious belief.
معصیت (ma'siyat): Disobedience or sin; an act that is contrary to God's commands.
محرز (muhraz): Dedicated property exclusively for Allah, free from human rights, like a mosque properly consecrated.

Legal Validity of the Mute's Contracts and Transactions
Ruling on mute's contracts with proof from analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for a mute person's contracts, explain the proof from the Prophet's practice, and apply the analogy to determine validity of acts.

Core Idea
A mute's contracts are valid through writing or known gesture—inability replaces speech.

This passage from Imam Muhammad's al-Jami' al-Saghir establishes a key principle: the mute (akhras) who can write or gesture enters into contracts just like a speaking person. The ruling covers marriage, divorce, manumission, sale, and purchase, and even qisas. But why? Because writing from a distant person is like speech from one who is near—and the mute's inability is even more evident. Let's unpack the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal acts listed
Read the passage and list all acts that are valid from the mute: marriage, divorce, manumission, sale, purchase, and qisas (both as claimant and defendant). Note the exception: no hadd punishment.
The text explicitly states 'لا يحد ولا يحد له'.
2
Identify the condition for validity
The mute's acts are valid only if he writes or makes a known gesture. This is the means of expression.
Gesture must be known (ma'ruf) to be understood.
3
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The proof is based on the Prophet's practice: he communicated by writing to absent people. Since the mute is unable to speak, writing (or gesture) stands in place of speech by analogy.
The 'illa is inability (al-'ajz).
4
Apply the analogy
The absent person writes due to inability to be present; the mute writes due to inability to speak. Thus, his writing carries the same legal weight as speech.
The text says 'al-mujawwiz fi haqq al-gha'ib al-'ajz wa huwa fi al-akhras azhar'.

قال واذا كان الاخرس يكتب كتابا او یومی ایماء يعرف به فانه يجوز نکاحه و طلاقه و عتاقة وبيعه و شرائه و يقتص له ومنه ولا يحد ولا يحد له اما الكتابة فلانها ممن ناى بمنزلة الخطاب ممن دنا الاترى ان النبي عليه السلام ادى واجب التبليغ مرة بالعبارة وتارة بالكتابة الى المغيب و المجوز فى حق الغائب العجز وهو فى الاخرس اظهر والزم. ترجمہ ہم خدا نے جامع صغیر میں فرمایا ہے اور جب کہ گونگا تحریر لکھتا ہو یا اشارہ کرتا ہو جس کو پہچان لیا جائے تو اس کا نکاح اور اس کی طلاق اور اس کا آزاد کرنا اور اس کا خریدنا جائز ہے اور اس کے لئے قصاص لیا جائے گا اور اس سے قصاص لیا جائے گا اور اخرس کو حد نہیں ماری جائے گی اور نہ اخرس کے لئے حد ماری جائے گی بہر حال کتابت پس اس لئے کہ کتابت اس شخص سے جو کہ دور ہو اس شخص کے خطاب کے درجہ میں ہے کہ جو قریب ہو گیا آپ نہیں دیکھتے کہ نبی کریم ہے نے فریضہ تبلیغ کو کبھی عبارت سے ادا کیا ہے اور کبھی غائبین کی جانب تحریر سے اور غائب کے حق میں جو جائز کرنے والی چیز ہے وہ عاجزی ہے اور عاجزی اخرس کے اندر زیادہ ظاہر اور یادہ پینٹ ہے۔
He said: And if the mute writes a book or gestures with a known indication, then his marriage, his divorce, his manumission, his sale, and his purchase are permissible, and qisas is taken for him and from him, and he is not subjected to hadd nor is hadd taken for him. As for writing, it is because writing from one who is far is like speech from one who is near. Do you not see that the Prophet (peace be upon him) fulfilled the duty of communication sometimes by expression and sometimes by writing to those who were absent. And the permissibility-granting factor for the absent is inability, and it is more apparent and binding in the case of the mute. Translation: The author said in al-Jami' al-Saghir: And when the mute writes a writing or gestures with a known indication, then his marriage, his divorce, his manumission, his sale and his purchase are valid, and qisas is taken for him and from him, and he is not subjected to hadd nor is hadd taken for him. As for writing, it is because writing from one who is far is in the status of speech from one who is near. Do you not see that the Prophet (peace be upon him) fulfilled the duty of communication sometimes by expression and sometimes by writing to those who were absent. And the permissibility-granting factor for the absent is inability, and it is more apparent and binding in the case of the mute.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement (from al-Jami' al-Saghir of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the legal capacity of a mute person (akhras) who can either write or make known gestures. The ruling covers: his marriage, divorce, manumission, sale, and purchase are all valid. He is subject to qisas (both as plaintiff and defendant), but he is not subjected to hadd punishment (e.g., for theft or slander) nor is hadd applied for his benefit (i.e., he cannot claim hadd for himself). The proof (dalil) given is that writing from a distant person is like speech from one who is near, analogizing to the Prophet's practice of communicating by writing to absent people. The underlying cause ('illa) is inability, which is clearest in the mute. Therefore, all transactions that require speech are valid through his writing or known gesture.

Key Terms
اخرس (akhras): A mute person who can write or make known gestures.
قول (qawl): In this context, the legal statement or expression required for contracts; replaced by writing for the mute.
قصاص (qisas): Retributive justice in cases of murder or bodily harm; the mute is subject to qisas both as victim and perpetrator.
