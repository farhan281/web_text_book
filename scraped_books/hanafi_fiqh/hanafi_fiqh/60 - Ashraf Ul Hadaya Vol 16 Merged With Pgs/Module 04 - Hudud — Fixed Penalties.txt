Qasamah: Hanafi Ruling and Shafi'i Dispute
Defines qasamah and contrasts Hanafi and Shafi'i rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on Qasamah and explain the two points of disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
In Qasamah, locals' oath drops qisas but not diyah—blood money remains wajib on the aqila.

Qasamah is a unique legal institution in Islamic criminal law for cases where a murder victim is found but the killer is unknown. The Hanafi school has a specific procedure involving collective oaths by the locality's men, which has implications for liability. This lesson will unpack the definition, the Hanafi procedure, and the key differences with the Shafi'i school on whether the claimant must swear and whether the oath absolves all financial responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Define Qasamah
Identify the linguistic and technical meanings of qasamah as presented in the text: linguistically 'oath', technically a collective swearing by fifty men from the locality due to a specific cause.
The technical definition includes four elements: cause, number (fifty), person (locals), and manner (specific oath phrase).
2
State the Hanafi Procedure
Outline the Hanafi ruling: when a body is found with signs of homicide, the heir selects fifty locals to swear they did not kill nor know the killer. If they swear, qisas is dropped but diyah becomes wajib on their aqila.
Qisas only applies if locals confess or clear evidence of intentional murder exists.
3
Identify the Two Disputed Points
Extract the two points of difference from the text: (1) whether the claimant (heir) can be required to swear an oath; (2) whether the locals' oath completely exempts them from liability.
Hanafi: claimant never swears, diyah remains. Shafi'i: claimant swears if lawth exists, and after locals swear no diyah.
4
Understand the Practical Consequences
Apply the differences to a real case: in Hanafi fiqh, the aqila pays diyah even after the oath; in Shafi'i, they are fully exonerated. Also, in Shafi'i, the claimant may be forced to swear if there is strong circumstantial evidence.
The thamarah al-khilaf is the financial liability of the aqila.

Chapter of Qasamah.
Linguistically, it means oath. And in Shari'ah, Qasamah is to swear by Allah Almighty on account of a specific cause, with a specific number of people, a specific person, in a specific manner.
If a murdered person is found in a locality and signs of [homicide] are present on him, and his killer is not known, and the heir of the murdered claims murder against all people of the locality, or against some unspecified, or against some specified ones—then, according to us, the heir has the right to select fifty men from the locality whomsoever he wishes and have them take the oath. They shall swear thus: 'By Allah, we did not kill him, nor do we know his killer.' If they take the oath, qisas is dropped from them, but diyah becomes wajib on their aqila, whether the heir claims intentional murder or erroneous murder. Summary: According to us, qisas is only established when the people of the locality refuse the oath and confess to the murder, or intentional murder is proven by clear evidence. This is the detail of our school.
Statements of other Imams regarding Qasamah.
Imam Shafi'i says: If the apparent situation is a witness in favor of the claimant—like the sword of one of them being bloodied, or enmity between the murdered and the people of the locality being apparent, etc.—then the oath is not taken from the people of the locality, but rather the oath is taken from the claimant. That is, the claimant swears fifty oaths. When the claimant swears that the people of the locality killed him, then if it is an erroneous killing, diyah is wajib; and in the case of intentional killing, there are two sayings according to him: according to one saying, qisas is wajib; according to the other, diyah is wajib. But if the claimant refuses to take the oath, then the oath is taken from the people of the locality. If they take the oath, then neither qisas nor diyah is upon them. And if they refuse the oath, then according to Imam Shafi'i there are two sayings: one saying holds qisas wajib, another holds diyah. And if there is no circumstantial evidence (lawth), then the oath is taken from the people of the locality in the same way, which is our madhhab. Summary: Our difference with Imam Shafi'i is in two places:
1. According to us, the oath is not taken from the claimant (heir); according to Imam Shafi'i, it is taken.
2. If the people of the locality take the oath, according to Imam Shafi'i they become free from liability—i.e., diyah is not wajib upon them—whereas according to us, diyah is wajib.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding Qasamah (collective oaths in cases of unsolved homicide). The author first defines Qasamah linguistically (oath) and technically (swearing by Allah in a fixed procedure due to a specific cause). He then presents the Hanafi ruling: when a murdered person is found in a locality with signs of foul play and the killer is unknown, the heir can demand fifty men from the locality to swear an oath that they did not kill him nor know who did. If they swear, qisas is dropped but diyah becomes obligatory on their aqila (male paternal relatives liable for blood money). The text emphasizes that qisas only applies if the locals refuse the oath and confess, or if clear evidence of intentional murder exists. The author then contrasts this with Imam Shafi'i's view, detailing two points of difference: (1) The Hanafi school does not make the claimant swear an oath, while Shafi'i does when there is circumstantial evidence (lawth); (2) After locals swear, Hanafis still impose diyah, while Shafi'is absolve them entirely. The passage concludes by summarizing the two key disagreements. Throughout, the author follows the classical method of stating the madhhab's ruling first, then the opponent's view, implicitly or explicitly indicating the preferred position.

Key Terms
القسامة (al-qasamah): A specific procedure of collective oaths taken by fifty men from a locality when a murder victim is found with no known killer.
دية (diyah): Blood money, a specified compensation paid by the aqila for unintentional homicide or in cases where qisas is not applied.
عاقلة (aqila): The male paternal relatives of the killer, who collectively bear the responsibility of paying diyah.

Ruling on Qasamah: Combining Oath and Blood Money in Blood Claims
Establishes the Hanafi ruling on qasamah and its application in three scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasamah, distinguish it from property oaths, and identify the three scenarios where it applies.

Core Idea
In qasamah, the oath is wajib alongside diyah; refusal to swear leads to imprisonment, not automatic payment.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on qasamah—the procedure of oaths and blood money in unresolved homicide cases. Unlike property disputes where the oath is a substitute for the claim, in cases of blood the oath is an independent obligation due to the sanctity of life. The text from Ashraf al-Hidayah clarifies that both the oath and diyah are wajib, and this ruling applies uniformly across three scenarios of claimants and defendants.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the legal act (qasamah) and its hukm: the oath and diyah are both wajib.
2
Locate the underlying reason (illah)
The text states the illah is 'the reverence of blood' (ta'zim al-dam). Understand why this makes the oath non-substitutable.
3
Contrast with property claims
Note the difference: in property, oath is a substitute; in qasamah, it is an original obligation.
4
Enumerate the three scenarios
The ruling applies when the wali sues (1) all people of the locality, (2) some unspecified individuals, or (3) specific individuals.
5
Identify the consequence of refusal
If a defendant refuses to swear, imprisonment until he swears; diyah alone does not drop the oath.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains the fundamental difference between the chapter of qasamah (oaths related to blood) and ordinary property claims. In property disputes, the oath is a substitute for the right; if the defendant refuses to swear, the claimant can simply take the property, and the oath drops. But in qasamah, because of the sanctity of blood, both the oath and the blood money (diyah) are wajib concurrently. If a defendant refuses to swear, he is imprisoned until he does so—the goal is not merely financial compensation but also establishing the truth through oaths. The passage then clarifies that this ruling applies in three scenarios: (1) when the legal guardian (wali) sues all the people of the locality where the corpse was found, (2) when he sues some unspecified individuals from that locality, and (3) when he sues specific named individuals. In all three, the procedure is the same: fifty oaths are required from the defendants, and they must also pay diyah. This is the established Hanafi position (Zahir al-Riwayah). The text quotes al-Quduri and al-Mabsut as supporting authorities.

Key Terms
القسامة (al-qasāmah): A procedure in blood claims where fifty oaths are sworn by the defendants (people of the locality) to deny responsibility for a homicide.
دية (diyah): Blood money paid by the defendants (people of the locality) to the heirs of the victim.

Abu Yusuf's Non-Zahir al-Riwayah Opinion on Qasamah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qasamah with qiyas argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Imam Abu Yusuf's non-Zahir al-Riwayah opinion on qasamah and compare it with the Zahir al-Riwayah position, identifying the role of qiyas in his reasoning.

Core Idea
In qasamah, Abu Yusuf uses qiyas to limit the text's scope, dropping the special ruling when specific individuals are accused.

This lesson introduces a non-Zahir al-Riwayah opinion of Imam Abu Yusuf, which applies qiyas to restrict the ruling. Understanding this intra-madhhab ikhtilaf helps you see how Hanafi scholars reasoned from texts and analogy, and why the established position prevails.

1
Identify the disputed scenario
The legal guardian accuses specific individuals from the locality of murder. Does the procedure of qasamah (fifty oaths) and diyah apply to the entire locality?
This is the key point of disagreement.
2
State the majority position (Zahir al-Riwayah)
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that qasamah and diyah are wajib on all people of the locality even if specific individuals are accused. This is mufta bihi.
The text (nass) is general.
3
Examine Abu Yusuf's qiyas-based argument
Abu Yusuf argues that the text only applies when the claim is against the entire locality. Otherwise, the general principle of evidence (bayyinah) applies. By qiyas, the special ruling of qasamah is confined to its textual scope.
He uses qiyas khafi (hidden analogy).
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
According to Abu Yusuf, the rest of the locality is not required to swear fifty oaths or pay diyah; only the accused swears one oath. According to the majority, fifty oaths and diyah are required from the people of the locality.
This is the practical consequence of the disagreement.
5
Understand the authority level
This is a non-Zahir al-Riwayah opinion (nawadir). The mufta bihi remains the Zahir al-Riwayah position. Abu Yusuf's view is not acted upon in fatwa.
Not all school opinions are operative.

Commentary

According to him, when the legal guardian accuses specific named individuals from the locality, the ruling should be based on qiyas. The reasoning is that the original text (nass) that established qasamah and diyah only applies when the claim is against the entire locality and the corpse is found in a place attributed to them. Beyond that, the general principle of qiyas applies, which requires evidence for a claim. The possibility exists that the killer is from outside. Hence, according to Abu Yusuf, the special ruling of qasamah is limited to its textual scope. However, the established Zahir al-Riwayah position (from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani) is that qasamah and diyah apply even when specific individuals are accused, as explained in the previous lesson. The commentary notes that this is Abu Yusuf's view, but the mufta bihi position is the one from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Qasamah Liability: House Owner vs. 'Aqilah (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Dispute on whether qasamah falls on house owner alone or also on 'aqilah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi disagreement on qasamah liability for a house owner and his 'aqilah, and apply the reconciled view based on the presence of the 'aqilah.

Core Idea
Qasamah liability on the house owner alone or shared with 'aqilah depends on their presence.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a dead body is found in a house, the qasamah (oaths and diyyah liability) may fall on the house owner alone or also on his 'aqilah (agnatic support group). This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Yusuf on one hand and Imam Abu Hanifa with Imam Muhammad on the other. We will explore their proofs and how later scholars reconciled conflicting narrations.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Note that two views exist within the school: one limits qasamah to the house owner, another includes the 'aqilah under certain conditions.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf, not a cross-madhhab dispute.
2
Identify the opposing views
Imam Abu Yusuf holds that qasamah is only on the owner. The other two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) hold that if the 'aqilah are present, they share it.
The 'tarafain' refer to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
Follow the proof for each position
Abu Yusuf analogizes to a locality case where the supporting clan is not included. The other two argue that protection of the spot is a duty shared by the 'aqilah when present.
Both use qiyas.
4
Analyse the reconciliation
The tanbeeh resolves conflicting narrations by conditioning the 'aqilah's liability on their presence, attributed to Imam Karkhi.
This harmonizes the two riwayat.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if 'aqilah are present, they share oaths and diyyah; if absent, only the owner is liable.
The mufta bihi follows the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about the scope of qasamah liability when a dead body is found in a house. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that qasamah is confined to the house owner alone, based on the specificity of the house to him, analogous to the rule that only the direct neighbors are liable in a locality case. The reconciliation, attributed to Imam Karkhi, is that the first applies when the 'aqilah are away, the second when they are present. This shows the nuance in Hanafi thought: the principle of collective responsibility (qasamah) is not absolute but conditioned by presence and capability.

Clarifying that qasamah is only obligatory on house owner if guardian claims murder against him

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that triggers qasamah obligation on the house owner and apply it to different accusation scenarios.

Core Idea
Qasamah is not automatic; it requires the guardian's accusation.

In some cases, a dead body is found inside a house, raising questions about who is liable. The Hanafi madhhab clarifies that qasamah (swearing of oaths and potential diyyah) does not automatically fall on the householder. A specific condition must be met: the victim's legal guardian must claim that the householder committed the murder. This short but crucial rule prevents injustice and ensures that liability follows direct accusation.

Diyyah in a Shared House: Equal Per Head
Ruling on diyyah when a murdered person is found in a shared house: equal per head regardless of shares.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for distributing diyyah when a murdered person is found in a shared house and explain the rationale using the analogy of shuf'ah.

Core Idea
Diyyah in a shared house is divided equally per head, not by ownership share, because all owners are equal in management and protection.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Imam Muhammad regarding liability for diyyah when a body is found on co-owned property. The passage clarifies that the burden falls equally on each owner, irrespective of their fractional ownership, drawing a parallel with the right of shuf'ah (preemption). We will dissect the proof structure and the underlying principle of equal responsibility.

1
Identify the scenario and the legal question
A murdered person is found in a house co-owned by multiple parties with unequal shares. The question is: how is the diyyah (blood money) distributed among the owners?
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The diyyah is divided equally among the heads (owners), not proportionally to ownership shares.
This is the ruling given by Imam Muhammad in the text.
3
Identify the proof (dalil)
The ruling is derived through qiyas on the established rule of shuf'ah, where the right of preemption is equal among co-owners regardless of share size.

Classify legal matters into rights of servants (huquq al-ibad) and rights of Allah (hudud). The gesture's validity depends on this distinction.
Huquq al-ibad include transactions, marriage, divorce, and qisas. Hudud are fixed punishments for offenses like theft and slander.
3
Follow the proof for huquq al-ibad
The proof is based on necessity and the nature of huquq al-ibad, which are not restricted to specific words. The text analogizes with bay' al-ta'ati (sale by mutual delivery) and nikah al-fuduli (marriage by unauthorized agent) where contracts are valid without words.
Analogy (qiyas) and istihsan are used.
4
Analyze the exception for hudud
Hudud are excluded because they are rights of Allah and are repelled by doubts (shubhah). Even a slight possibility that the gesture meant something else (e.g., confirmation instead of accusation) is enough to prevent hadd.
The principle 'hudud are repelled by doubts' is a major rule.
5
Note the compensatory nature of qisas
Qisas is considered a right of the servant because it has an element of compensation (iwadiyyah). Therefore, it can be established by gesture, similar to other compensatory transactions, despite possible doubt.
This distinguishes qisas from hudud.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof explaining the validity of a mute person's gesture in Hanafi law. The text states that out of necessity, the gesture of a mute is accepted as proof for matters concerning the rights of servants (huquq al-ibad), including civil transactions, divorce, and even qisas. The reason is that these matters are not confined to specific words and can be established through indications. However, hudud (quranic punishments) are an exception because they are rights of Allah, and the law repels hudud in the presence of any doubt. The text also distinguishes between hudud and qisas: qisas has an element of compensation (iwadiyyah), so it can be established despite doubt, similar to other civil transactions. Hudud, being purely deterrent, cannot. The author supports this with examples from testimony and confession. This lesson is particularly important for understanding the evidence of intent in Islamic law.

Key Terms

اخرس (akhras): Mute person, one who cannot speak.

حدود (hudūd): Quranically prescribed punishments.
قصاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliatory punishment for murder or injury, considered a right of the individual.

The Mute's Gesture as Valid Legal Proof for Huquq al-Ibad
Ruling on mute's gesture validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the gesture and writing of a mute (akhras) are valid proof for all huquq al-ibad except hudud, and explain the necessity-based reasoning.

Core Idea
A mute's gesture is a valid legal expression for all rights except Allah's fixed punishments.

This passage addresses the legal capacity of a mute person to communicate through gestures and writing. The Hanafi school grants it validity for all matters involving the rights of people (huquq al-ibad), but excludes the divinely prescribed punishments (hudud) due to the strict evidentiary requirements. The reasoning hinges on necessity (haja) and analogy to other non-verbal transactions. Let us unpack this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the core ruling: the gesture and writing of a mute are valid proof for all legal matters except hudud. Note the exception.
This applies to both the mute confessing or accusing.
2
Examine the Justification for Inclusion
The justification is necessity (haja) because the mute cannot speak. The author analogizes to bay' al-ta'ati (sale by mutual delivery) and nikah fuduli (marriage by an unauthorized agent), where verbal utterance is not required. A fortiori, the mute's gesture is even more fitting.
Necessity overcomes the usual requirement of speech.
3
Appreciate the Exception for Hudud
Hudud are excluded because any potential doubt (shubha) in the gesture's meaning—such as the possibility that the mute is confirming rather than accusing—prevents the application of the severe punishment. Hudud require certainty.
The broad Hanafi interpretation of shubha means even weak doubt drops hadd.
4
Confirm the Scope
The ruling covers all huquq al-ibad, including qisas (retaliation), as the passage later clarifies. It is not limited to financial transactions.
This expands the practical utility of the mute's gesture.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the legal capacity of a mute person (akhras) and the distinction between hudud (divinely ordained punishments) and qisas (retaliation). The commentator first establishes that the gesture and writing of a mute are valid proof for all legal matters except hudud, because these matters are huquq al-ibad (rights of people) and necessity (haja) demands acceptance. He analogizes to cases where verbal utterance is not required, like bay' al-ta'ati (sale by mutual delivery) and nikah al-fuduli (marriage by an unauthorized agent). Since the mute lacks speech, his gesture is even more fitting. Then he turns to the difference between hudud and qisas: both are punishments, but they differ in their nature. Qisas has the element of compensation (iwaḍiyya) because it is a restorative (jabir) for the victim's right, similar to other civil transactions (mu'awadat). Thus, it can be established even if there is doubt (shubha). For example, if witnesses testify to 'absolute killing' (qatl mutlaq) without mentioning intent, or the accused confesses to it, qisas becomes wajib despite the ambiguity, because the doubt does not prevent its establishment. In contrast, hudud are deterrents (zawajir) purely for Allah's right and have no compensatory element; therefore, even a slight doubt causes them to fall. The commentator provides examples: if someone confesses to 'unlawful intercourse' (wati' haram), which could include intercourse during menstruation (which is not zina), hadd is not applied. Similarly, if they confess to 'absolute killing', qisas applies because the default is intent. The commentator reiterates that qisas is like other huquq al-ibad, while hudud are different. He concludes by noting that the author of Nata'ij al-Afkar listed places where the author (of Hidayah) said qisas falls due to doubt, implying that the statement that qisas is established with doubt is not absolute but has exceptions. Overall, the passage establishes the Hanafi rule that the mute's gesture is a valid legal instrument for most matters, and that the strict evidentiary standard for hudud does not apply to qisas.

شبهة (shubha): Doubt or ambiguity in the evidence that prevents the application of hadd punishments.
عوضية (iwaḍiyya): The quality of being compensatory or a substitute; a characteristic of qisas that allows it to be established despite doubt.

Why Qisas Survives Doubt but Hudud Do Not: The Compensatory Nature of Qisas
Difference between hudud and qisas regarding shubha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why qisas is established despite shubha while hudud are not, and apply the principle of iwaḍiyya to similar cases.

Core Idea
Qisas is compensatory like civil rights, so it withstands doubt; hudud are deterrents and drop with any doubt.

This passage draws a critical distinction between two types of punishments in Islamic law: hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation). While both are severe, they operate under different evidentiary rules. The Hanafi school holds that qisas, like other civil rights, can be proven even when there is some doubt (shubha), whereas hudud require absolute certainty. The key is the concept of iwaḍiyya (compensation) inherent in qisas. Let us examine this difference through the examples given.

Two Narrations on the Mute's Written Confession: An Intra-Hanafi Analysis
Analyzing two narrations regarding a mute's written confession for qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two probabilities regarding the mute's written confession for qisas and identify the basis for distinguishing the mute from the absent person.

Core Idea
A mute's written confession is valid due to necessity; an absent person's is not—capacity for speech distinguishes them.

This passage from the Hidayah commentary examines an apparent conflict between two works of Imam Muhammad: Jami' al-Saghir accepts a mute's written confession, while Al-Mabsut rejects a similar confession from an absent person. The author, al-Marghinani, offers two ways to reconcile these positions: either they represent two narrations within the school, or the cases are distinguished by the mute's permanent inability to speak. Understanding this intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of how legal capacity ('ahliyyah) and necessity shape evidence law in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two primary sources
Note the two works by Imam Muhammad: Jami' al-Saghir (mute's writing valid) and Al-Mabsut (absent person's writing invalid). Recognize that both are Zahir al-Riwayah texts.
Jami' al-Saghir explicitly rules on the mute; Al-Mabsut rules on the absent person.
2
Recognize the conflicting positions
From Jami' al-Saghir, the mute's written confession is valid and qisas becomes wajib. From Al-Mabsut, the absent person's written confession is invalid. The conflict arises when the mute is analogized to the absent person.
The mute and absent person both use writing, but the mute cannot speak at all.
3
Follow the first probability: two narrations
If the mute is treated like the absent person, then there are two conflicting narrations within the Hanafi school: one accepting the mute's writing (Jami' al-Saghir), one rejecting it (by analogy from Al-Mabsut).
This view accepts that Imam Muhammad had two different opinions.
4
Follow the second probability: distinction based on capacity
Alternatively, the cases are distinguished because the absent person retains the capacity to speak when present, while the mute is permanently disabled. Thus the mute's writing is accepted out of necessity, and no conflict exists.
The basis for distinction is the presence or absence of the ability to speak (nutaq) in the future.

Gesture of a Mute is Valid Even if He Can Write
Establishing that a mute's gesture is valid evidence alongside writing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a mute's gesture and writing are equally valid as evidence and refute the objection based on the principle of necessity.

Core Idea
For the mute, gesture and writing are equal proofs of necessity—Imam Muhammad's text binds.

This passage tackles a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute: is a mute's gesture valid when he can write? Some argued that gesture is only a necessity-based proof, so if writing is possible, gesture falls away. But the author, citing Imam Muhammad's Jami' al-Saghir, shows that gesture and writing are equal alternatives, each with its own advantage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its context
The author states that a mute's gesture is valid even if he can write. This is a ruling about the means of legal proof (tariqa al-ithbat) for confessions and testimony.
Not a hukm taklifi (obligation/permission) but a ruling on evidentiary validity.
2
Locate the primary proof
The proof is Imam Muhammad's statement in Jami' al-Saghir: 'اشار او کتب' (he gestured or wrote), which treats both as equal. This is a text from the Zahir al-Riwayah.
Zahir al-Riwayah is the highest authority in the Hanafi school.
3
Understand the opposing view
Some Hanafi scholars argued that gesture is only valid out of necessity (darura), and when writing is possible, the necessity disappears. They rely on the principle 'الضرورة تتقدر بقدر الضرورة' (necessity is measured by its extent).
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
4
Analyze the refutation
The author rejects the opposing view because Imam Muhammad did not differentiate. Both gesture and writing are proofs of necessity since the original (speech) is absent. Moreover, gesture is closer to speech (direct bodily act), while writing has greater clarity—so they balance each other.
The refutation uses both textual authority and rational reasoning.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the opposing view, a mute who can write would not have his gesture accepted as evidence; under the relied-upon view, both are accepted equally.
The mufta bihi is the equality view.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof, establishing that a mute's gesture is valid as evidence even if he is capable of writing. The author refutes the assumption of some Hanafi scholars that gesture is only accepted when there is no alternative (e.g., the mute cannot write). The proof is from Imam Muhammad's statement in Jami' al-Saghir where he said 'اشار او کتب' (he gestured or wrote), treating both as equal. The reasoning: writing has clarity, gesture has closeness to speech; both are proofs of necessity since the original requirement is oral speech. The passage also includes a separate ruling about a person who is temporarily mute (ma'tuqil al-lisan) due to an accident: his gestures are not valid because his speech organ is intact, so he could potentially speak. This is based on the same principle that gesture is a substitute for speech when speech is impossible, not when it is merely temporarily hindered.

Key Terms
اشارة (isharah): Gestures made by a mute to communicate, considered valid as evidence out of necessity.
کتابة (kitabah): Writing by a mute, also valid as evidence. Both gesture and writing are equal in the Hanafi school.
معتقل اللسان (ma'tuqil al-lisan): A person whose tongue is tied or temporarily unable to speak due to an accident or ailment, but whose speech organ is intact.

Gesture of a Temporarily Mute Person is Invalid
Distinguishing the temporary mute from the permanent mute for gesture validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a permanently mute person (akhras) and a temporarily mute person (ma'tuqil al-lisan) and explain why the gesture of the latter is not accepted as evidence.

Core Idea
Only a permanent mute's gesture is valid; a person with an intact speech organ must use speech.

This short passage clarifies an important limitation: gesture is a substitute for permanent muteness, not for temporary loss of speech. If the speech organ is intact and the person may soon recover, gesture is not a valid proof. This follows the same logic that gesture is a necessity-based proof, and necessity is only established when speech is impossible in the long term.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of ma'tuqil al-lisan
Ma'tuqil al-lisan refers to a person whose tongue is temporarily tied (e.g., due to a stroke, accident, or swelling) but whose speech organ (the tongue and vocal cords) is still intact. He may be unable to speak for a day or two.
This contrasts with the akhras (permanent mute) whose speech organ is permanently damaged or absent.
2
State the ruling for ma'tuqil al-lisan
The author states that such a person's gesture is not valid as evidence, just like the person whose tongue is tied (from the earlier text in Jami' al-Saghir).
The ruling is derived by analogy from the case of a person with a tied tongue.
3
Understand the rationale
The organ of speech is present and functional. Therefore, the person has the potential to speak (even if temporarily hindered). Gesture is only a substitute when speech is permanently impossible, not when it is merely temporarily unavailable.
The necessity (darura) is not established because the impediment is temporary.
4
Distinguish from the permanent mute
The permanent mute has a permanent inability to speak; therefore, gesture and writing are valid substitutes. The temporary mute does not meet the threshold of necessity.
This distinction is crucial for proper application.
