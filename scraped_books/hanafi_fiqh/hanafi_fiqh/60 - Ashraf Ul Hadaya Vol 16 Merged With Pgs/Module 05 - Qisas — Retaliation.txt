Qasamah: The Hanafi Procedure and Inter-Madhhab Differences
Ruling on qasamah with Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasamah, explain its procedure, and compare it with the Shafi'i and Maliki positions, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
When a slain person is found in a locality, Hanafi law requires fifty men to swear; their oath waives qisas but diyah remains wajib on their aqilah.

This lesson addresses the procedure of qasamah (compurgation) in cases of unknown homicide. The Hanafi position is presented alongside the views of al-Shafi'i and Malik. Understanding this dispute clarifies how each school balances the rights of the victim's heirs with the protection of the accused community.

Guided Path
1
Define qasamah linguistically and technically
Understand the lexical meaning (oath) and the legal definition: a specific oath by specific persons due to a specific cause.
The cause is the presence of a slain person in a locality with unknown killer.
2
Outline the Hanafi procedure
The guardian selects fifty men from the locality; they swear they did not kill nor know the killer. Upon oath, qisas drops but diyah is wajib on their aqilah.
No requirement of lawth (circumstantial evidence) in Hanafi view.
3
Identify the Shafi'i and Maliki positions
Shafi'i: if lawth exists, the guardians swear fifty oaths and receive diyah from the accused. Maliki: if intentional killing, retaliation (qisas) applies.
Lawth includes signs pointing to a specific person, enmity, or testimony.
4
Contrast the role of lawth
Hanafi does not require lawth; the oath is always on the locality. Shafi'i/Maliki require lawth to shift the oath to the plaintiff and impose liability.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
In Hanafi, the locality's aqilah always pays diyah when the slain is found there. In Shafi'i, if lawth exists, the plaintiff swears and the accused pays; if no lawth, the locality swears and no one pays. In Maliki, intentional killing leads to retaliation.
The evidentiary threshold and who bears the burden of oath differ.

Chapter of Qasamah
Qasamah in language means oath, and in sacred law: an oath by Allah Almighty in a specific manner due to a specific cause with a specific number of specific persons in a specific way.
He said: (If the slain person is found in a locality and it is not known who killed him, then fifty men from among them chosen by the guardian are made to swear: 'By Allah, we did not kill him, nor do we know his killer.') And al-Shafi'i said: If there is lawth (suspicion/evidence), then the guardians swear fifty oaths and judgment is given for them to receive blood-money from the accused, whether the claim is for intentional killing or by mistake. And Malik said: Judgment is given for retaliation if the claim is for intentional killing, and that is one of al-Shafi'i's two opinions. And lawth according to them is that there be a sign pointing to a specific person as the killer, or an apparent circumstance testifying for the plaintiff such as open enmity, or the testimony of a just person or a group of unjust persons that the people of the locality killed him.
Explanation: It means that qasamah in language means oath, and in sacred law it is an oath by Allah Almighty in a specific manner due to a specific cause with a specific number of specific persons in a specific way. The specific cause: the presence of the slain in the locality. The specific number: fifty. The specific persons: sane, mature men or those who are responsible. The specific manner: that they be fifty men, and if they are not complete the oath is repeated. The summary of the issue: that whoever finds a slain person in a locality and does not know his killer, then the guardian may select fifty from the people of the locality and make them swear by Allah that they did not kill him nor do they know his killer. When they swear, retaliation is dropped from them and blood-money becomes obligatory upon their clan. This is our school. Al-Shafi'i and Malik differed in what has preceded.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the procedure of qasamah (compurgation) when a slain person is found in a locality and the killer is unknown. The author presents the Hanafi ruling first: the guardian selects fifty men from the locality and makes them swear that they did not kill the victim nor know his killer. If they swear, retaliation (qisas) is waived, but blood-money (diyah) is due on their clan (aqilah). This is the Hanafi position. Then he presents the views of Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik. According to al-Shafi'i, if there is lawth (suspicion or circumstantial evidence), the guardian himself swears fifty oaths, and upon that, the accused party pays diyah regardless of whether the claim is for intentional or mistaken killing. According to Malik, if the claim is for intentional killing, retaliation is inflicted on the accused; this is also one of al-Shafi'i's two opinions. The passage then defines lawth: a sign pointing to a specific person, open enmity, or testimony of a just person or a group of unjust persons. The commentary explains that the Hanafi position does not require lawth for qasamah; the oath is always on the locality's men. The dispute thus revolves around who takes the oath and its legal consequences. The Hanafi view is that the locality's men swear to exculpate themselves, and if they swear, they are not liable for retaliation but still must pay diyah. Shafi'i and Maliki impose liability on the accused only when lawth exists, and the oath is on the plaintiff. The practical result: in Hanafi fiqh, the locality's aqilah always pays diyah when the slain is found there and the killer is unknown, whereas in the Shafi'i school, if there is lawth, the plaintiff swears and receives diyah from the accused, otherwise the locality swears and no liability arises.

Key Terms
القسامة (al-qasamah): An oath in a specific manner, with a specific number of persons, in a case of homicide where the killer is unknown.
اللوث (al-lawth): Circumstantial evidence or suspicion pointing to the guilt of a specific person or group.
الولي (al-wali): The guardian or heir of the slain person.
العاقلة (al-ʿāqilah): The clan or group liable for paying blood-money on behalf of the killer.

The Hanafi Position on Qasamah: Oaths Drop Qisas but Not Diyah
Inter-madhhab dispute on qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the effect of oaths in qasamah and evaluate the Shafi'i objection using the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
In qasamah, the accused's oaths remove qisas but diyah remains obligatory.

This lesson introduces the core inter-madhhab dispute on qasamah. After fifty men from the locality swear oaths denying involvement, does diyah become obligatory or are they completely exonerated? The Hanafi school, relying on the practice of the Prophet and Umar, holds that diyah remains due. We will examine the proofs from both sides.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the opposing view
Read the matn (Quduri) and note the Hanafi ruling: diyah is obligatory after oaths. Then note al-Shafi'i's view that no liability remains.
2
Examine the Shafi'i proofs
Examine the two Shafi'i proofs: the hadith 'The Jews exonerate you by their oaths' and the general principle that an oath exonerates the defendant.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school cites the hadiths of Sahl and Ziyad, and the practice of Umar, where diyah and qasamah were combined. This shows that oaths do not remove diyah.
4
Interpret the Shafi'i hadith
The Hanafi response interprets the Prophet's statement as exoneration from qisas and imprisonment, not from diyah. Understand the principle of limitation.

Commentary
This passage presents a complete inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the effect of oaths (qasamah) in cases of suspected homicide. The Hanafi position, as stated by al-Quduri in the matn, is that when fifty men from the locality swear oaths denying involvement, the qisas (retaliation) is dropped but diyah (blood money) becomes obligatory upon them. The wali (heir) is not required to swear. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that no financial liability remains at all, neither qisas nor diyah. He presents two proofs: first, the hadith of Abdullah ibn Sahl where the Prophet said 'The Jews exonerate you by their oaths,' implying complete exoneration; second, a rational principle that in all other claims, the defendant's oath serves to exonerate, not to bind. The Hanafi response begins by asserting their own proof: the combination of diyah and qasamah in the hadiths of Sahl and Ziyad, and the practice of Umar, all indicate that the oaths do not remove diyah. They then address Shafi'i's first proof by interpreting the Prophet's statement to mean exoneration from qisas and imprisonment only, not from diyah. For the second proof, they explain that the oath in qasamah is prescribed for the specific purpose of revealing the truth about qisas; it exonerates only from the right for which it was required, namely qisas. The diyah, however, is not based on the oath but on two alternative grounds: either the apparent causation (the victim was found among them, so responsibility attaches) or their negligence in protecting the victim, analogous to unintentional killing. Thus, the oath drops qisas but diyah remains. The passage concludes with a separate ruling: if any of the accused refuses to swear, he is imprisoned until he does so. This is because in qasamah, the oath is required for its own sake to honor the sanctity of blood, and therefore it is combined with diyah. In financial claims, refusal (nukul) has a different effect because the oath is a substitute for the original right and can be replaced by payment, but here the diyah is not a substitute for the right to qisas; rather, both are distinct obligations.

Liability of 'Āqila Requires Proven Ownership
Ruling with proof via analogy to shuf'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for 'āqila liability when a dead body is found on property and apply the analogy to shuf'ah to justify the requirement of ownership.

Core Idea
Mere possession does not obligate the 'āqila; ownership must be proven by evidence.

When a dead body is found in a house, the initial assumption may be that the occupant's 'āqila is liable for blood money. However, the 'āqila may deny liability by claiming the occupant is not the owner. This passage from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani clarifies that the 'āqila is only liable if the occupant's ownership is established through proof. Possession alone is insufficient because it is ambiguous—it could be due to a loan, lease, or other arrangement. The ruling is supported by an analogy to the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah), which also requires ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the condition
The ruling is that the 'āqila is not liable unless witnesses testify that the occupant owns the house. This condition is the key point of the passage.
The ruling applies to cases where a dead body is found in a house and the 'āqila denies ownership.
2
Understand why possession is insufficient
The text states that possession (yad) is evidence of ownership but it is probable (muhtamal) because the occupant could be a tenant, borrower, or trustee.
Probability undermines certainty required for liability.
3
Examine the analogy to shuf'ah
The author analogizes to shuf'ah (pre-emption), where a claim based solely on possession is also insufficient; proof of ownership is required.
This is a qiyas: the illa is the ambiguity of possession.
4
Note the burden of proof
The claimant (usually the victim's family) must produce evidence (witnesses or other proof) to establish ownership.
If ownership is not proven, the 'āqila is not liable.
5
Recognize the broader principle
The principle is that mere possession is not a definitive proof of ownership for imposing financial obligations on third parties (like 'āqila).
This aligns with the general Hanafi caution in imposing liability.

Chapter of the Hermaphrodite. Explanation: Here arises the question: why is there a difference between qisas and hudud in that the writing and gesture of a mute person are valid for qisas but not for hudud, while apparently both are of the same status? The answer: there is a difference between them, and that difference is that in hudud, even a slight shubha (doubt) cannot be tolerated. For its proof, see: if witnesses testify that Zaid committed illegal intercourse, or if Zaid himself confesses to illegal intercourse, then in both cases the hadd of zina will not be imposed on Zaid, because intercourse during menstruation is also illegal intercourse, but it does not make hadd obligatory. Similarly, intercourse with a Magian slave-girl is also illegal, but it does not make hadd obligatory. And complete illegal intercourse is zina, yet even with such testimony or confession, hadd will not be obligatory. And if witnesses testify that Zaid killed Khalid, or Zaid himself confesses that 'yes, I killed Khalid', then in both cases qisas will be obligatory on Zaid, even though qisas is only obligatory in intentional murder, and the word 'intentional' does not appear here. Despite this, this doubt (shubha) has been considered, and there is a difference: if there is some doubt in hadd, hadd will not be established, but if there is a slight doubt in qisas, qisas can be established. The question remains: what is the reason that hadd is not established due to doubt but qisas is? The answer given is that in qisas there is an element of compensation, and it is a right of the servant (haqq al-'abd). That is, qisas is legislated to compensate for the fault committed by the killer. So just as the ruling applies to all other compensatory rights that are rights of the servant, that they are established even with doubt, thus qisas is also established even with doubt. As for hudud, they have no meaning of compensation; rather, hudud are deterrents. They are legislated so that people refrain from committing these acts. Therefore, distinguishing between them is necessary. Hence, qisas is established with doubt, but hudud are not established with doubt. This is the gist of the author's discussion. In other words, qisas is compensator (jabir) and hadd is deterrent (zajir); doubt can be borne in the compensator but not in the deterrent. The author here stated that qisas is established with doubt. The author of Nata'ij al-Afkar counted all the places where the author stated that qisas is dropped due to doubt. The summary of his words is that this is not evidence. And it is mentioned in Kitab al-Iqrar that writing from an absent person is not a proof in qisas that is obligatory upon him. It is probable that the answer here is the same, so there would be two narrations in this matter. And it is probable that it is different, because it is possible to obtain speech from the absent person insofar as he possesses the capacity for speech, but not so for the mute person due to the impossibility of obtaining speech because of the preventing affliction. Translation: And in al-Mabsut's Kitab al-Iqrar, it is stated that the writing of an absent person is not a proof in the qisas that is obligatory upon him. It is probable that the answer here (regarding the mute person) is the same, so there will be two narrations in the issue of the mute person. And it is probable that the mute person is different from the absent person, because for the absent person, access to his speech is possible in some ways due to the presence of his capacity for speech, but not so for the mute person because access to his speech is impossible due to the affliction that prevents speech. Explanation: The passage from al-Jami' al-Saghir has already passed, and the author of al-Hidayah has discussed it in detail. The summary is that if a mute person acknowledges and confesses qisas through writing or gesture, his confession is valid and qisas becomes obligatory upon him. But Imam Muhammad in al-Mabsut's Kitab al-Iqrar stated this issue: if an absent person confesses in writing that qisas is incumbent upon him, then his writing and written confession are not proof, and qisas will not be obligatory upon him. This is the partial issue from al-Mabsut. Now, the author comments on this, saying that considering this partial issue from al-Mabsut, there are two probabilities. The ruling of the mute person's writing is the same as that of the absent person's writing, i.e., if a mute person confesses qisas in writing, his writing is not a proof and qisas will not be obligatory upon him, even though the narration from al-Jami' al-Saghir is contrary to this. So it can be said that in the issue of the mute person, there are two narrations among the Hanafis: one mentioned in al-Jami' al-Saghir, and the other understood from the narration of al-Mabsut. The second probability is that this partial issue from al-Mabsut is only specific to the absent person, and the ruling for the mute person is the same as that in al-Jami' al-Saghir. Then there would not be two narrations in the issue of the mute person; rather, there is a difference between the mute person and the absent person. And in al-Mabsut, the issue of the absent [person] is mentioned, while in al-Jami' al-Saghir, the issue of the mute person is mentioned. The reason for the difference between the absent person and the mute person is that the absent person, after appearing, is capable of speech and verbal expression because he has the ability to speak, whereas the mute person does not have the ability to speak because he is considered disabled in his speech organ.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and an intra-Hanafi discussion. It begins by addressing the question of why a mute person's writing or gesture is accepted for qisas but not for hudud, despite both being legal claims. The author explains that hudud are dropped upon the slightest doubt (shubha), citing examples: if someone confesses to or is witnessed committing illegal intercourse (wat'i haram), the hadd for zina is not imposed because the confession or testimony does not specify the exact type of intercourse—it could be during menstruation or with a Magian slave-girl, which are not subject to hadd. In contrast, for qisas, a confession or testimony of killing suffices even if it does not specify intentional murder, because qisas is a compensatory right (haqq al-'abd) and is upheld despite doubt. The passage then distinguishes between 'jabir' (compensator) and 'zajir' (deterrent). The discussion then shifts to the issue of writing from an absent person. According to al-Mabsut's Kitab al-Iqrar, an absent person's written confession of qisas is not valid proof. The author of al-Hidayah considers two possibilities: either the same ruling applies to a mute person (creating two narrations within the Hanafi school), or the mute person is different because he permanently lacks speech, while the absent person can speak when present. This leads to the conclusion that for a mute person, the narration from al-Jami' al-Saghir (accepting his writing and gesture) is the relied-upon view, as it accounts for his permanent incapacity.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliatory punishment for homicide or bodily injury, considered a compensatory right (haqq al-'abd) in this passage.
حد (hadd): Fixed punishment prescribed by Shari'ah for certain crimes, considered a deterrent (zajir) and dropped upon doubt.
شبہ (shubha): Doubt or semblance that prevents the imposition of hadd, but does not prevent qisas in this context.
حق العبد (haqq al-'abd): A right belonging to an individual, as opposed to a right of Allah; qisas is classified as such.
جابر (jabir): Compensator; qisas is described as a compensator in this passage.
زاجر (zajir): Deterrent; hudud are described as deterrents in this passage.
