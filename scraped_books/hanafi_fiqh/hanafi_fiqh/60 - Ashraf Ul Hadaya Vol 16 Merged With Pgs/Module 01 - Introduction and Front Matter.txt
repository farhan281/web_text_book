Commentary
This passage is the front matter of the sixteenth volume of Ashraf ul Hadaya, an Urdu commentary (sharh) on the Hanafi fiqh classic Al-Hidayah by Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani. It provides the book title, its scope (from Bab al-Qasamah to miscellaneous rulings—'tasal fi ahkamihi wa masa'il shatta'), the author (Mufti Muhammad Yusuf Ahmad), and those who added headings. The presence of a permanent scholar for proofreading underscores the care taken in publishing Islamic texts. Students should note that this is a commentary, not a direct translation, meaning the author explains Hidayah's rulings with additional reasoning and context.

Key Terms
شرح (Sharh): A commentary that explains and expands upon a base text; Ashraf ul Hadaya is a sharh of Al-Hidayah.
باب القسامة (Bab al-Qasamah): The chapter on oaths related to homicide; the starting point of this volume.

دیت (diyah): Blood money or compensation paid to the heirs of a victim of accidental killing or injury.

This passage is the Table of Contents (Fihrist) for the Book of Wills (Kitab al-Wasiyyah) from Ashraf al-Bidayah, an Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah. It lists the topics and corresponding page numbers for the entire section on Islamic inheritance bequests. The central theme is the legal framework of wasiyyah in Hanafi fiqh, emphasizing limitations (one-third), the role of heir consent, and rules for revocation.

Key Terms
وصیت (wasiyyah): A bequest or will, a legal declaration by which a person disposes of his property after death, subject to Shari'ah limits.
ثلث (thuluth): One-third, the maximum portion of the estate that can be freely bequeathed without heir consent.

Definition and Etymology of al-Ma'aqil and 'Aql
Defines the term 'Ma'aqil' and explains why diyah is called 'aql

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the etymology of the term 'aql in Hanafi fiqh and identify the meaning of 'Ma'aqil' as the plural of 'Ma'qila' (diyah).

Core Idea
Blood money is called 'aql because it restrains bloodshed — the root meaning is 'to hold back'.

This lesson opens the chapter on al-Ma'aqil by clarifying the terminology. The author explains that 'Ma'aqil' is plural of 'Ma'qila', which itself is synonymous with diyah. The word 'aql is used for diyah because its linguistic root implies restraint — the payment stops revenge killings. Understanding this lexical connection is essential for grasping the legal structure of 'aqila responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the full phrase
The author states that the full title is 'Kitab Ahl al-Ma'aqil', but most authors drop the word 'Ahl' for brevity. Recognize the abbreviation.
This is a common practice in Islamic legal literature (ḥadhf al-muḍāf).
2
Define the key term
Determine that 'al-Ma'aqil' (المعاقل) is the plural of 'ma'qila' (معقلة), and that 'ma'qila' means diyah (blood money).
Note that the term appears in the chapter heading.
3
Explore the etymology of 'aql
The passage states that diyah is called 'aql because it restrains blood from being spilled. The root meaning of 'aql is 'to hold back' or 'to bind'.
This is a linguistic derivation (ishtiqāq) that ties the legal term to its effect.
4

One Executor Cannot Act Without Co-Executor's Consent: Joint Authority in Wasiyya
Ruling on multiple executors and refutation of Abu Yusuf's analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on multiple executors, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute, and apply the principle of qiyas ma'a al-fariq to distinguish between executorship and marriage guardianship.

Core Idea
Joint appointment implies joint authority: one executor cannot act alone without the co-executor's consent.

When a testator appoints two executors, the trust is placed in their collective judgment. This lesson examines the general rule that such executors must act jointly, the important exception made by Imam Abu Yusuf based on analogy to marriage guardianship, and the majority's refutation that distinguishes the two cases. Understanding this ruling is essential for anyone dealing with estate administration in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Understand the general rule
The established Hanafi position is that multiple executors must act jointly. The testator's trust is in the collective, not in each individually.
This is based on the testator's statement appointing both together.
2
Identify Imam Abu Yusuf's opposing analogy
Imam Abu Yusuf analogized executorship to co-guardianship in marriage, where one brother can perform the marriage without the other's consent.
He considered the common element of joint authority.
3
Analyze the Hanafi refutation
The commentators refute this as qiyas ma'a al-fariq (analogy with a difference). In marriage, the basis is kinship (equally complete in each), while in executorship it is the testator's choice (collective). Moreover, marriage is an obligatory right on the guardian, while executorship is a permissive right to act.
This difference makes the analogy invalid.
4
Apply the debt analogy
The text gives a financial analogy: if both owe a debt to one person, collecting the whole by one is invalid (like executor acting alone). If one owes a debt to both, paying it to one fulfills the right (like one brother marrying).
This clarifies the ruling with a concrete example.
5
Note exceptions for necessities
Certain acts of necessity (e.g., burial, feeding children) are exempt from the joint-decision rule. These are enumerated in al-Quduri.
Necessity overrides the general principle.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and an intra-Hanafi dispute. The subject is the authority of multiple executors (wasi). The established Hanafi position (from the commentators) is that if the deceased appoints two executors, neither may act alone without the other's consent. The reason is that the testator's trust is in their collective judgment, not individual, and one person does not equal two in capacity. Imam Abu Yusuf analogized this to co-guardians in marriage (nikah), where one brother can perform the marriage without the other. The commentators (the 'two sides'—presumably Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, or the later Hanafi authorities) refute this as qiyas ma'a al-fariq (analogy with a differentiating factor). In marriage, the basis of guardianship is equally inherent kinship; both are equally qualified, so acting alone suffices. In executorship, the basis is the testator's choice, which attaches to both collectively, not individually. Moreover, marriage guardianship is a right due from the guardian to the woman—it is incumbent; if one brother performs it, he fulfills the other's obligation. In executorship, there is no such incumbent duty; each executor has a right to act, but acting alone would usurp the other's authority. The passage then gives an analogy of debts: if Zaid owes a debt to both Khalid and Bakr, and Bakr pays Zaid's debt to Khalid, that is valid (like one brother marrying). But if both owe Zaid a debt, and Khalid collects both, he wrongfully takes Bakr's share (like one executor acting alone). This clarifies the ruling with a concrete financial example. Finally, the text anticipates exceptions: certain acts of necessity (kafan, feeding children, etc.) are exempt from the joint-decision rule, as they will be enumerated in al-Quduri. This is a typical Hanafi methodology: general principle first, then exceptions based on necessity (darurah).

وصی (wasi): An executor appointed by the testator to administer the will and estate.
ولایت (wilayah): Legal authority to act on behalf of another, here specifically the authority to dispose of the estate.
قیاس مع الفارق (qiyas ma'a al-fariq): An analogy where the original and the new case differ in a legally relevant aspect, making the analogy invalid.

And I, the slave in need of the mercy of my Master, the Self-Sufficient, Muhammad Yusuf ibn al-Azim al-Taulawi, servant of the Islamic University, Darul Uloom located in Deoband. Muhammad Yusuf al-Hafr [or: al-Hanafi?]. besturdubooks.wordpress.com
Source pages p. 304

Commentary
This is the colophon of the volume, indicating the completion of the fourth part of Al-Hidayah. It includes praise to Allah, blessings upon the Prophet Muhammad, and identification of the scribe or perhaps the author of the commentary: Muhammad Yusuf ibn al-Azim al-Taulawi, who describes himself as a servant of the Islamic University Darul Uloom in Deoband. The name 'al-Hafr' may be a misreading; in context it is likely 'al-Hanafi' indicating his madhhab affiliation. The website 'besturdubooks.wordpress.com' is a modern addition from the digitization.

Key Terms
خاتمة (khatima): Colophon; a concluding note in a manuscript providing details about its completion.
العبد المفتقر (al-'abd al-muftaqir): The slave in need; a humble self-reference used by scribes.
دار العلوم بديوبند (Darul Uloom Deoband): A prominent Islamic seminary in India, known for its Hanafi orientation.
