Testimony of Locality Residents Against an Outsider in Homicide
Dispute over accepting testimony from potential defendants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute over the admissibility of testimony from potential defendants and apply the principle of tainted potential opponent status to similar cases.

Core Idea
Testimony from potential defendants is tainted even after they are removed from the case.

When a murder occurs in a locality, the residents may become potential defendants due to their negligence. But what if they later testify against an outsider? This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether such testimony is acceptable, revealing deeper principles about bias and analogy in Islamic legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa: the testimony of two locality residents against an outsider in a murder case is not accepted. This is the mufta bihi (operative) position.
This is the baseline from al-Quduri's matn.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that the testimony is accepted because the potential opponent status (부عة) was nullified when the claimant accused someone else.
The Sahibayn are the two primary students of Abu Hanifa.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Sahibayn use qiyas (analogy) to a litigation agent (wakil) who is dismissed before the case: the agent can then testify freely. Similarly, the residents were once potential opponents but now are not, so their testimony is clear.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) based on removal of taint.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa responds by analogizing to an executor (wasi) who exits executorship: the wasi's prior connection still taints his testimony. The residents' initial negligence (taqsir) places them in the position of killers, so their testimony remains tainted even if formally excluded.
Abu Hanifa's analogy emphasizes enduring taint, not just procedural exclusion.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the testimony is rejected (Abu Hanifa), the case may rely on other evidence; if accepted (Sahibayn), the outsider may be convicted based on potentially biased testimony.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, leaning toward caution.

Residents of a locality gave testimony against a person from another locality that he killed. The ruling of the testimony, according to the statements of the jurists.
He (al-Quduri) said: When two from the people of a locality testify against a man not from them that he killed, their testimony is not accepted. This is according to Abu Hanifa. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: It is accepted, because they were liable to become opponents (of the case), and that liability was nullified by the claimant's accusation of killing against someone else, so their testimony is accepted, like an agent in litigation when he is dismissed before the litigation.
Translation: Al-Quduri said: When two people from the locality testify against someone who is not from them that he killed the victim, their testimony will not be accepted according to Abu Hanifa. The Sahibayn said: Their testimony will be accepted, because they were liable to become opponents, and due to the claimant's accusation of killing against someone else, that liability became void, so their testimony is accepted, like a litigation agent when he is dismissed before the litigation.
Explanation: Two people from a locality testified about a person not from that locality that he killed that victim. According to the Sahibayn, this testimony is accepted, and according to Imam Abu Hanifa, it is not accepted.
Qasamah
The proof of the Sahibayn is that the residents were liable to become opponents, but when the claimant made the accusation against someone else, their liability became void, so no doubt remains in their testimony. It is considered exactly like when someone has a litigation agent; he cannot be a witness for his client, but if the client dismissed him from agency before the litigation, then there is no doubt and his testimony is accepted. So here also.
The proof of Imam Abu Hanifa:
He (Abu Hanifa) stated: They are opponents because they are considered as killers due to the negligence emanating from them, so their testimony is not accepted, even if they exit from the list of opponents, like an executor when he exits from executorship after having accepted it, then testifies. The author (al-Marghinani) said: On these two principles, many issues of this kind are derived.
Translation: The proof of Abu Hanifa is that they are opponents due to the negligence that occurred from them, placing them in the position of killers, so their testimony is not accepted, even if they exit from the list of opponents, like an executor who exits from executorship after having accepted it, then gives testimony. The author said: And on these two principles, many issues of this type branch out.
Explanation: This is the proof of Imam Abu Hanifa. The summary is that these people definitely have negligence, because the incident occurred in their locality and they had an obligation to protect. Due to this negligence, they come into the category of killers and are opponents. But it so happens that now, because the claimant has accused someone else, these people are not in the list of opponents, but due to the initial doubt, their testimony is not accepted. They are like someone who was an executor for a child, but now the child has become an adult and exited executorship, and now he testifies for the child; still his testimony is not accepted. So the ruling is similar. In summary, now they are not opponents by agreement of both parties, but the Sahibayn analogize them to an agent, and Imam Abu Hanifa analogizes them to an executor. After that, the author (al-Marghinani) said that both are general rules upon which many issues of this kind are derived.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the admissibility of testimony from residents of a locality when a murder occurs. The matn, from al-Quduri, states the baseline ruling: testimony of two residents against an outsider is not accepted per Abu Hanifa. The Sahibayn disagree, arguing that since the claimant has accused someone outside the locality, the residents are no longer potential defendants (khusama'), and their testimony suffers no taint. They draw a qiyas (analogy) to a litigation agent who, if dismissed before the case, can testify. Abu Hanifa, however, maintains that the residents bear inherent negligence for the murder occurring in their midst, placing them in the position of defendants even if formally excluded; he analogizes to an executor (wasi) who, after his executorship ends, still cannot testify for the formerly ward because of his prior connection. The commentator notes that both analogies form general principles (qawa'id kulliyyah) from which many subsidiary issues are derived. The mufta bihi (operative position) is Abu Hanifa's, being more cautious.

Key Terms
المحلة (al-mahallah): The locality or neighborhood where the murder occurred.
شهادة (shahadah): Testimony given in court.
خصومة (khusumah): Litigation or opponent status.
وكيل (wakil): Agent or representative in litigation.
وصي (wasi): Executor of a will or guardian of minors.

Testimony Rejected When Witnesses Are Also Potential Defendants
Ruling on testimony from locality residents in homicide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the inadmissibility of testimony from locality residents in a homicide case and explain the legal reasoning based on cutting off liability.

Core Idea
Testimony from residents of the same locality is rejected because they are cutting off liability from themselves.

Imagine a murder in a neighbourhood. The victim's guardian accuses a specific person from the same locality. Two other residents come forward to testify against the accused. Should the court accept their testimony? This lesson, from the Hidayah, explains the Hanafi position: the testimony is not accepted because the witnesses themselves are under suspicion. We will unpack the reasoning and also consider a different view from Imam Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The claimant (wali) accuses a specific person from the same locality. Two witnesses from that locality testify against the accused.
All parties belong to the same mahallah.
2
State the ruling
The testimony is not accepted (la tuqbal al-shahadah).
This is the primary ruling.
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
Because the litigation (khusumah) stands against all residents of the locality. By testifying, the witness cuts off the accusation from himself, so he is considered accused (muttahem).
The witness has a personal interest in the outcome.
4
Note the intra-madhhab difference
Imam Abu Yusuf suggests an alternative: the witnesses should take an oath that they did not kill, and not add anything more.
This does not overturn the main ruling but offers a procedural alternative.
5
Apply the principle
In any case where the witness stands to benefit personally from testifying (by removing liability from themselves), their testimony is inadmissible.
This is a general principle in Hanafi shahadah law.

Commentary
This passage addresses a scenario where the claimant (wali) accuses a specific individual from the same locality, and two witnesses from that locality testify against the accused. The ruling is clear: the testimony is not accepted. The reason is that the witnesses are themselves potential defendants (khusama') in the case because the murder occurred in their locality, and by testifying they are cutting off the accusation from themselves. Thus, they are considered accused (muttahem) and their testimony is tainted. The text also reports a view from Abu Yusuf stating that the witnesses should take an oath: 'By Allah, we did not kill him,' and not add anything else, because they have informed that they know the killer. This suggests a procedural alternative where, instead of testimony, an oath might be used to clear them. The primary ruling remains that the testimony is inadmissible.

Testimony of Locality Residents After Claim Transfer: Wakil vs Wasi Analogy
Presents Sahibayn's and Abu Hanifa's proofs regarding witness testimony of locality residents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the competing qiyas of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the testimony of locality residents after transfer of claim, and apply the correct analogy to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Two qiyas, two outcomes: wakil's tuhma ends upon dismissal; wasi's tuhma persists—which analogy governs?

In qasamah cases, the entire locality is initially accused. When the claim is transferred to a specific person, can the other residents testify? The Sahibayn say yes, by analogy to a dismissed wakil (agent). Abu Hanifa says no, by analogy to a removed wasi (executor). The difference hinges on the nature of the prior responsibility: mere agency versus fiduciary duty. This lesson unpacks the qiyas and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Qiyas
First, clearly state the two analogies: Sahibayn analogize to a wakil bil khusumat dismissed before litigation; Abu Hanifa analogizes to a wasi removed after accepting executorship.
Both are qiyas jali (clear analogies) based on the removal of tuhma.
2
Extract the Illa in Each
For Sahibayn: the illa is removal of adversarial status (khusumat) before any active involvement. For Abu Hanifa: the illa is prior negligence (taqsir) that creates a persistent tuhma.
The nature of the prior relationship defines whether tuhma can be removed.
3
Evaluate the Strength of Each Analogy
Assess which asl (original case) is more similar to the locality residents: wakil or wasi? Consider that residents had a duty of protection (hirasa) akin to a wasi's duty to the orphan.
Abu Hanifa's analogy is stronger because residents' negligence in protection mirrors a wasi's responsibility.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence: if the court follows Sahibayn, the residents can testify; if it follows Abu Hanifa, their testimony is rejected. This can decide the outcome of a murder case.
The mufta bihi is context-dependent, but many later Hanafi texts prefer Abu Hanifa's view.
5
Reflect on the Expansion Principle
Al-Marghinani notes that from these two principles (wakil vs wasi) many sub-issues (furu') derive. Think of other scenarios where prior relationship affects testimony admissibility.
This passage is an expansion probe: the author uses the dispute to illustrate a broader usul pattern.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the testimony of residents of a locality when the guardian of the victim initially accused the entire locality but later transferred the claim to a specific person. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) analogize the situation to a legal agent (wakil bil khusumat) who, if dismissed before litigation, loses the tuhma (suspicion) and can testify. Similarly, the residents, once the claim is transferred away from them, are no longer adversaries and their testimony is acceptable. Imam Abu Hanifa, however, analogizes to an executor (wasi) who, even after being removed from executorship, remains tainted by the prior relationship and cannot testify for the minor. The author notes that these two principles (the wakil analogy and the wasi analogy) serve as foundational maxims from which many sub-issues (furu') of this genre derive. The Hanafi position presented is that the testimony is rejected, which is Abu Hanifa's view, though the mufta bihi (operative ruling) may depend on context. The authority weight: this is from Zahir al-Riwayah as it appears in the Hidayah commentary.

خَصْم (khasm): Adversary or opponent in a legal claim
تُّھْمَة (tuhma): Suspicion or accusation that impairs the credibility of a witness

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Testimony from locality residents is rejected due to tuhma — they remove themselves from the circle of suspects.

In qasamah, when the victim's guardian accuses a specific person from the locality, the testimony of two other residents against the accused is not accepted. This seems counterintuitive — why would local witnesses be disqualified? The answer lies in the principle of tuhma (self-interest). This lesson unpacks the ruling and its rationale, as well as an important nuance from Abu Yusuf regarding the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The guardian (wali) claims against a specific person from the locality. Two residents testify against that accused.
This is a qasamah case where all residents are initially considered as possible defendants.
2
State the ruling
The testimony is not accepted (ghayr maqbula).
This is the Zahir al-Riwayah position agreed upon by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani on this point.
3
Analyse the reason
The witness cuts off the litigation from himself — by testifying, he removes himself from the pool of suspects. This creates a tuhma (suspicion of self-interest).
Tuhma is a specific impediment to testimony, not general doubt about honesty.
4
Note Abu Yusuf's additional ruling
If these witnesses are to take the qasamah oath, they only swear 'We did not kill him' and do not add 'nor do we know the killer', because their prior testimony already declares they know the killer.
This is an intra-Hanafi nuance that does not contradict the main ruling.

Core Idea
Sale deed and guardianship deed must be separate to prevent false testimony.

When a guardian (wasi) sells property on behalf of an orphan, the documentation must be precise to avoid confusion. This passage from al-Hidayah explains the cautious method: writing the sale deed independently of the will or guardianship deed. Understanding this procedure ensures the contract's integrity and protects witnesses from inadvertently testifying falsely.

Testimony of Two Executors for a Third: Qiyas and Istihsan
Ruling with istihsan override

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the testimony of two executors for a third executor and explain the istihsan reasoning that overrides qiyas.

Core Idea
Two executors' testimony for a third is invalid by qiyas, but accepted by istihsan if the third claims it.

This passage from Imam Muhammad's al-Jami' al-Saghir addresses a nuanced scenario in testamentary law: can two already-appointed executors validly testify that the deceased appointed a third executor alongside them? The general rule (qiyas) rejects such testimony due to suspicion of self-interest, but Imam Muhammad introduces an istihsan exception that changes the outcome when the third party steps forward to claim the role. Understanding this interplay between strict analogy and juristic preference is essential for grasping how Hanafi fiqh balances procedural integrity with the deceased's intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling (qiyas)
State the ruling the text gives 'in qiyas': the testimony of the two executors for a third is invalid (batil) because they are suspect (tuhmah) – they benefit by adding a co-executor to share responsibility.
The suspicion arises from self-interest, a standard ground for rejecting testimony.
2
Locate the istihsan exception
Find the condition under which the testimony becomes acceptable: 'except if the person for whom the testimony is given claims it.'
The third party must actively claim the executorship for the testimony to be considered.
3
Understand the istihsan rationale
Examine the basis: the judge has inherent authority (wilayah) to appoint executors. The testimony merely informs the judge of the deceased's wish, relieving him of the burden of independent investigation. The actual legal effect (establishment of executorship) is achieved through the judge's appointment, not the testimony alone.
This reduces the risk of fraud – the testimony is not the sole cause of the appointment.
4
Compare qiyas and istihsan
Note that the qiyas ruling treats the testimony as tainted by suspicion in all cases, while istihsan differentiates based on the third party's claim. The istihsan overrides the qiyas because the judge's authority provides a stronger consideration (that the deceased's wish be honoured) compared to the mere potential for self-interest.
This is a classic example of istihsan as a source of law in the Hanafi school.
5
Apply the mufta bihi ruling
State the operative ruling for fatwa: if the two executors testify and the third person claims the executorship, the testimony is accepted (sahih) and the judge may appoint the third executor. If the third person does not claim it, the testimony is rejected.
This is the position from Zahir al-Riwayah, attributed to Imam Muhammad.

Testimony Disqualified by Self-Interest
Ruling on disqualification of testimony due to self-interest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony that brings direct benefit to the witness and explain why self-interest invalidates the testimony.

Core Idea
Testimony tainted by self-interest is rejected – even true testimony cannot benefit the witness.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: two sons testify that their deceased father appointed a certain person as executor, but that person denies the appointment. Imam Muhammad rules that the testimony is rejected. The basis is not a defect in the witnesses' character but the benefit they stand to gain – having an executor protects their inheritance. This lesson explores how self-interest (tuhmah) renders testimony inadmissible in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the ruling: The testimony of the two sons is invalid (batil).
The text says 'the will is invalid' meaning the purported appointment is not established.
2
Locate the reason (illah)
Find the reason given in the text: the sons draw benefit (naf') by appointing a guardian for the estate.
The Arabic phrase 'يجَرَّانِ إِلَى أَنْفُسِهِمَا نَفْعًا' directly expresses self-interest.
3
Understand the legal principle
Recognise that the presence of any direct personal benefit to a witness disqualifies their testimony, even if they are otherwise upright and competent.
This is a manifestation of the maxim 'al-mar'u mu'akhadh bi tuhmathi nafsihi' (a person is held accountable for their own suspected bias).
4
Distinguish from similar cases
Note that if the executor admitted the appointment, the sons' testimony would be unnecessary, and the executor's acceptance would establish the will. Here, the denial triggers the rule.
The previous lesson likely covered the case where the executor affirms, making this a contrasting scenario.
5
Apply to other contexts
Consider how the same principle applies when heirs testify about a debt owed to the deceased – if they stand to inherit, their testimony is rejected for self-interest.
This ruling is not limited to executors but extends to any testimony where the witness benefits directly.

Mutual Testimony for Different Bequests: Valid by Consensus
Ruling on mutual testimony for different bequests

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mutual testimony when bequests are of different items and explain why it is valid by consensus.

Core Idea
Different bequests, no shared interest – testimony valid by consensus.

In the previous lesson, we saw that mutual testimony regarding a single bequest raises suspicion of collusion and is rejected. Here, Imam Muhammad presents a contrasting scenario where two pairs of people testify for each other about different slaves. The ruling is that this testimony is valid by consensus. The key legal insight is that when the bequests are distinct, there is no shared interest (shirkah), so no suspicion (tuhma) attaches. This lesson examines the reasoning behind this consensus ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling scenario
Read the passage and identify the two sets of witnesses and beneficiaries. Note that each set testifies about a different slave (a slave girl vs. a slave).
The bequests are of different items, not a single property.
2
State the hukm (legal ruling)
The passage explicitly says the testimony is valid (jāz) by consensus (bil-ittifāq). Determine the hukm level: it is sahih (valid) and there is no ikhtilaf.
Consensus here refers to the Hanafi school, not necessarily all madhahib.
3
Analyze the underlying reason (illah)
The passage gives the reason: 'lā shirkah, falā tuhmah' (no partnership, so no suspicion). Explain why the absence of partnership removes the potential for bias.
If there were a partnership, the witnesses would have a personal stake, tainting their testimony.
4
Contrast with a case of shared interest
Consider the opposite scenario where both pairs testify about the same slave. In that case, each witness would be testifying for their own benefit (since they each claim a share), creating suspicion. Contrast this with the current ruling.
The distinction hinges on whether the subject of the bequest is the same or different.
5
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Take a modern example: A and B testify that the deceased bequeathed his car to C and D; C and D testify that the deceased bequeathed his house to A and B. Determine the ruling using the same logic.
The items are different, so no partnership; testimony is valid.

Commentary

Acceptance of Khuntha's Claim When Not Ambiguous (Khuntha Ghayr Mushkil)
Ruling on accepting a khuntha's claim when not ambiguous

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a khuntha's claim of being male or female is accepted and explain why his statement is deemed credible.

Core Idea
If a khuntha is not established as mushkil, his claim of gender is accepted because he is the best authority on his own condition.

This lesson covers the second ruling from the passage: when the khuntha's gender was not previously determined as ambiguous (ghayr mushkil), his claim to be male or female is accepted. We examine the reasoning that his self-knowledge and trustworthiness (amana) outweigh any external doubt, provided there is no contrary evidence.

The text says: 'because he knows his own condition better than others.' The person is considered trustworthy (amin) about his own state.
There is no contrary evidence to override his claim.
3
Apply the legal principle
In Hanafi law, when there is no conflicting evidence, a person's statement about their own condition is accepted (al-amin yu'amin).
The khuntha is seen as reliable regarding his own body.
4
Contrast with the mushkil case
The difference hinges on the presence of evidence (dalil). Without evidence to the contrary, the claim is accepted; with evidence, it is rejected.

Ruling on Gesture vs Writing for a Mute Person Capable of Both
Establishes that gesture is valid even if capable of writing, contrary to some Hanafi scholars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a mute person's gesture when capable of writing and explain the proof from Jami' al-Saghir.

Core Idea
Gesture and writing are equal proofs for a mute person; both are valid even if capable of writing.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute: some scholars argued that a mute person's gesture is only accepted due to necessity, so if he can write, the necessity is removed. The author refutes this by citing Imam Muhammad's Jami' al-Saghir, where gesture and writing are treated as equal. Let's explore the reasoning and the authority behind the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue and the two positions
The issue is whether a mute person's gesture is valid if he can write. One position (some Hanafis): not valid. The relied-upon position (Imam Muhammad): valid.
Note that this is an intra-Hanafi disagreement.
2
Examine the opposing argument
Some Hanafis argue that gesture is a proof of necessity, so if writing is possible, necessity is removed, invalidating the gesture.
3
Study the Hanafi proof from Jami' al-Saghir
Imam Muhammad explicitly says 'ashara aw kataba' (he gestured or wrote), treating them as alternatives. This text is a primary source from Zahir al-Riwayah.
The phrase is quoted in the source.
4
Understand the reasoning for equality
Both are proofs of necessity because the mute person cannot speak. Writing has clarity, gesture is closer to speech. They complement each other, so they are equal.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from Imam Muhammad's Jami' al-Saghir. It addresses a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: some Hanafi scholars thought that if a mute person is capable of writing, his gesture is not valid because gesture is only a proof of necessity (darurah) and there is no necessity if he can write. Therefore, they are equal in legal validity, and either can be used to establish qisas or other claims.
