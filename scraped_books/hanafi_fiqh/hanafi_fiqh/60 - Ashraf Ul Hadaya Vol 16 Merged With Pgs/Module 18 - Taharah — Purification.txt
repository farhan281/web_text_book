Gender Determination of Khuntha by Precedence of Urination
Ruling with proof on khuntha gender determination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on khuntha gender determination by identifying the organ from which urine first exits and stating the evidence from the athar of Ali ibn Abi Talib.

Core Idea
If a khuntha urinates from both passages, the first exit determines gender — precedence indicates the original organ.

When a child is born with ambiguous genitalia (khuntha), certain legal rulings depend on gender. The Hanafi school provides a clear criterion: if urine exits from both passages, observe which organ releases urine first. That organ is considered the original (asli) and determines the child's gender for all legal purposes. This ruling is based on a tradition from Ali ibn Abi Talib, preserved by Abd al-Razzaq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the situation
Confirm that the child is a khuntha (possessing both male and female organs) and that urine is exiting from both passages.
If urine exits from only one passage, no ambiguity arises.
2
Observe precedence
Carefully note from which organ the first urine exits. This is the asbaq (preceding) organ.
The other organ’s urination is considered a defect or illness.
3
Apply the ruling
Declare the gender based on the asbaq organ: if first urine came from the male organ, the child is male; if from the female organ, female.
This ruling applies to all subsequent legal matters (e.g., inheritance, prayer, marriage).
4
Cite the proof
The ruling is supported by an athar (tradition) from Ali ibn Abi Talib, recorded by Abd al-Razzaq in al-Musannaf.
The tradition directly links the first urination to the original organ.
5
Understand the rationale
Precedence serves as a decisive indicator: the organ that urinates first is the sound, original channel; the other is merely a defect.
This reasoning is based on direct observation and common sense.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on how to determine the gender of a khuntha (hermaphrodite) who passes urine from both the male and female passages. The ruling is based on the precedence (sabq) of urination. The text supports this with an athar (tradition) from Ali ibn Abi Talib, recorded by Abd al-Razzaq in his Musannaf. The reasoning is that the organ from which urine first exits is the original, healthy organ, while the other is considered a defect or illness. This is a straightforward ruling: if urine comes first from the male organ, the child is male; if first from the female organ, female. The commentary emphasizes that this precedence is a decisive factor and serves as a second proof (the first being the initial urination from a single passage mentioned earlier) that the preceding organ is the primary one.

Key Terms
خنثی (khuntha): A person who has both male and female reproductive organs or ambiguous genitalia.
اسبق (asbaq): The one that comes first; here, the organ from which urine exits first.
عضو اصلی (udw asli): The original/primary organ, as opposed to a secondary or defective one.

Gender Determination via Puberty Signs for the Khuntha
Rulings on how physical signs of puberty determine a khuntha's gender.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the male and female signs of puberty that determine the gender of a khuntha and apply the ruling when signs are absent or conflicting.

Core Idea
Beard and flat chest = male; breasts and menses = female; if no sign or conflict, khuntha remains mushkil.

This passage from Quduri's Mukhtasar explains how the physical signs of puberty resolve the ambiguous gender of a khuntha (hermaphrodite). In Hanafi law, these signs are the primary evidence for assigning male or female status, which affects inheritance, marriage, and ritual obligations. Understanding these signs is essential for applying the correct rulings.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the khuntha's ambiguity
A khuntha at birth has both male and female characteristics. Puberty often brings clear physical signs that settle the gender.
2
List male signs
From the passage: beard growth, ability to have intercourse with women (wusul ila al-nisa'), nocturnal emission (ihtilam) like a man, and flat chest (non-protruding breasts).
These indicate the individual is biologically male.
3
List female signs
From the passage: developing breasts like a woman, lactation (milk in breasts), menstruation (hayd), pregnancy (haml), and possibility of vaginal penetration.
These indicate the individual is biologically female.
4
Apply the ruling
If any single male or female sign appears, the khuntha is definitively classified as male or female. If no sign appears, or signs conflict (e.g., beard and menstruation), the khuntha remains mushkil (problematic) until further clarification.
The ruling is immediate; waiting for additional signs is not required.

Commentary
This passage from Quduri's Mukhtasar, explained in the commentary, provides the Hanafi rulings on determining a khuntha's gender at puberty. Male signs include: beard growth, ability to have intercourse with women (wusul ila al-nisa'), nocturnal emission (ihtilam) like a man, and a flat chest (as men have flat chests, not protruding breasts). If any of these signs appear, the khuntha is definitively classified as male or female accordingly. If none of these signs appear, or if signs conflict (e.g., beard and menstruation together), the khuntha remains a mushkil (problematic) and further investigation or waiting is needed. This is based on the default that signs of puberty are the most reliable indicators, as they reflect true biological development.

علامات النساء (alamaat al-nisa): Signs of women: breasts, milk, menses, pregnancy, vaginal receptivity.

Ruling on Circumcision of Khuntha Mushkil
Ruling on circumcision arrangement for khuntha mushkil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on circumcision of a khuntha mushkil and explain the precautionary arrangement using a slave girl and bayt al-mal.

Core Idea
A slave girl circumcises the khuntha to avoid haram, as her sight is permissible regardless of sex.

The circumcision of a khuntha mushkil (hermaphrodite with indeterminate sex) presents a unique challenge: it is makruh for a man or woman to perform it due to the uncertainty of awrah. The Hanafi school provides a careful solution that upholds both the obligation of circumcision and the limits of looking. This lesson explains the ruling and its application using the public treasury when needed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
Recognize that the khuntha's sex is unknown, so a man or woman performing circumcision risks seeing awrah without necessity, making it makruh.
Makruh, not haram, because the risk is potential, not certain.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that a slave girl belonging to the khuntha (or purchased by him or the imam) should perform the circumcision.
The slave girl is his property, so looking at him is permissible for her.
3
Explain the financial arrangements
If the khuntha has wealth, he purchases a slave girl himself. If not, the imam purchases one from bayt al-mal (public treasury). After circumcision, the slave girl is sold and the price returned to the treasury.
The treasury is used because it is prepared for the needs of Muslims.
4
Summarize the rationale (talil)
The reasoning is precaution (ihtiyat) to avoid any possibility of haram. The slave girl is lawful to see him whether he is male or female.
This solution balances the wajib of circumcision with the prohibition of looking at awrah.

Commentary
This passage provides the ruling on the circumcision of a khuntha mushkil. Since his sex is ambiguous, it is makruh for a man (who might see a woman's awrah) or a woman (who might see a man's awrah) to perform the circumcision. The solution is to purchase a slave girl who will perform the circumcision, as she is his property and can lawfully see him regardless of his sex. If the khuntha has wealth, he buys the slave girl; if not, the imam purchases one from the public treasury (bayt al-mal) for this necessity. After circumcision, the slave girl is sold and the money returned to the treasury. This shows the application of ihtiyat (precaution) and the use of bayt al-mal for essential needs.

Key Terms
بیت المال (bayt al-mal): The public treasury of the Muslim community, used for public needs.

Funeral Rulings for a Deceased Khuntha Mushkil
Rulings on washing, shrouding, and funeral prayer for a deceased khuntha mushkil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four funeral rulings for a deceased khuntha mushkil and explain the underlying principle of avoiding prohibition due to uncertainty.

Core Idea
When gender is uncertain, funeral rites default to caution: tayammum instead of ghusl, and placing the khuntha between male and female in prayer.

A khuntha mushkil is a hermaphrodite whose gender cannot be determined. Upon death, standard funeral rites such as ghusl and shrouding become problematic because the washer's gender must be opposite to the deceased, but the khuntha's gender is unknown. Hanafi fiqh resolves this by replacing ghusl with tayammum and arranging the funeral prayer in a middle position. This lesson presents the four key rulings from the classical Hanafi text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the khuntha mushkil scenario
The passage applies to a khuntha who died before their gender became clear. Understand that uncertainty (ishkal) is the operative factor.
The ruling differs if gender becomes clear before death.

No man or woman may wash the deceased because of the possibility that the washer is of the opposite gender, which is prohibited. Since definitive ghusl cannot be performed, tayammum with pure earth is substituted.
A slave girl is not purchased for washing because ownership ceases with death.
3
Rule on attending washing
A living khuntha mushkil (baligh or near-pubescent) should not attend the washing of a man or woman, due to the same possibility of opposite gender.
This preserves the prohibition of looking at the private parts of the opposite gender.
4

Covering the khuntha's grave (tasjiyah) is recommended (mustahab). If the khuntha was actually a woman, covering is an obligation; if a man, covering does no harm.
This is an application of the maxim: act on the safer possibility when there is doubt.

When a man, a woman, and a khuntha are prayed over together, the man is placed nearest the imam, the khuntha behind him, and the woman behind the khuntha. This balances the probabilities: delayed from man in case female, advanced over woman in case male.
This arrangement is specific to the funeral prayer, not daily prayers.

Ruling on washing a deceased khuntha mushkil with explanation of prohibition and alternative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a deceased khuntha mushkil and explain why tayammum is performed instead of ghusl.

Core Idea
A deceased khuntha mushkil cannot be washed by either gender; tayammum replaces ghusl.

When a person dies, it is obligatory to wash their body (ghusl). But what if the deceased's gender is ambiguous (khuntha mushkil)? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that prohibits both men and women from washing such a body, substituting tayammum with pure earth to avoid the possibility of a forbidden act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
Recognize that the deceased is a khuntha mushkil—a person whose gender remained ambiguous throughout life and at death.
Khuntha mushkil is distinct from a resolved khuntha; the ambiguity persists.
2
Apply the prohibition
Understand that ghusl by either men or women is haram because of the possibility that the deceased is of the opposite gender, and washing the opposite gender is forbidden.
The prohibition stems from the rule against gazing and touching the opposite gender's awrah.
3
Substitute with tayammum
Since ghusl cannot be performed, the body is purified by tayammum—striking pure earth and wiping the face and hands of the deceased.
Tayammum serves as a valid substitute when ghusl is not possible.
4
Note the tanbih (warning)
A slave girl cannot be purchased to wash the deceased because ownership ends with death; this contrasts with the living khuntha where a slave girl could be bought for circumcision.
The distinction is based on cessation of ownership after death.

Khuntha Mushkil Attending Ghusl of Deceased
Ruling on khuntha mushkil attending ghusl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a khuntha mushkil must not attend the washing of any deceased person and explain the rationale based on gender ambiguity.

Core Idea
A khuntha mushkil cannot attend any ghusl because of the possibility of opposite gender, making attendance haram.

This lesson covers the first ruling in the passage: a khuntha mushkil (whether adult or nearly adult) is prohibited from being present during the washing of a deceased man or woman. The key reason is the inherent ambiguity of the khuntha's gender, which could lead to a violation of modesty rules (looking at the 'awrah of the opposite gender). Understanding this ruling helps clarify how Islamic law handles cases of uncertainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject: khuntha mushkil
The ruling applies specifically to a khuntha mushkil – a hermaphrodite whose gender is ambiguous and cannot be determined. The passage specifies 'baligh or murahiq' (close to puberty).
Khuntha ghayr mushkil (determinable) is not included; if gender can be resolved, different rules apply.
2
Identify the act: attending the ghusl of a deceased person
The act in question is being present during the washing of the dead body, which involves exposing the 'awrah.
Ghusl of the deceased typically requires privacy and only same-gender attendants.
3
Apply the ruling: prohibition
The text states 'لا يحضر' – he/she must not attend. This is a prohibition, making the act haram. The reason is the probability (ihtimal) that the deceased may be of the opposite gender to the khuntha.
Even though the khuntha's own gender is unknown, the possibility of opposite gender creates a scenario where looking at the 'awrah of the opposite gender could occur, which is prohibited.

Covering the Grave of a Khuntha Mushkil
Ruling on covering khuntha's grave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that covering the grave of a khuntha mushkil is recommended (mustahabb) and explain the reasoning that if the khuntha is female, it fulfills an obligation, and if male, it causes no harm.

Core Idea
Covering the grave of a khuntha mushkil is recommended because it fulfills an obligation if female and causes no harm if male.

This lesson addresses the second ruling in the passage: whether the grave of a khuntha mushkil should be covered with a cloth (tasjiyah). Typically, covering the grave is obligatory for women to preserve modesty and not required for men except in exceptional circumstances (like extreme weather). For a khuntha mushkil, the ruling is that it is mustahabb (preferred) to cover the grave, as a precautionary measure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act: covering the grave (tasjiyah)
This refers to placing a cloth over the grave, a practice normally associated with women to cover the 'awrah of the deceased.
For men, covering is not customary unless there is a valid excuse like heat, cold, or rain.
2
State the ruling: it is 'more loved' (mustahabb)
The text says 'فهو أحب' meaning it is more beloved/preferable. This indicates a recommended (sunnah) action, not obligatory.
Mustahabb means it is rewarded to do but not sinful to leave.
3
Consider the female possibility: if khuntha is female, covering is wajib
If the khuntha is actually female, covering the grave is an obligation to preserve her modesty, similar to any woman.
Women's graves are covered as a protection of 'awrah even after death.
4
Consider the male possibility: if khuntha is male, covering causes no harm
For a male, covering the grave is not normally required, but it does not cause any harm. In fact, if there is an excuse like weather, covering is permissible. The ambiguity of khuntha is a greater excuse.
The text mentions that covering is harmless for a man, drawing analogy to covering due to heat/cold.
5
Conclusion: prefer covering due to precaution
Since covering can fulfill an obligation (if female) and is at worst harmless (if male), it is recommended to do it as a precautionary measure (ihtiyat).
This is an application of the legal maxim: 'Precaution in doubtful matters is recommended.'
