Qasama and Diya When a Wounded Person Dies After Being Moved: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qasama liability when a wounded person dies after being moved.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding qasama liability when a wounded person dies after being moved and identify the key factor (sahib firash) that determines attribution of death.

Core Idea
A bedridden status (sahib firash) transforms a wound into killing, triggering qasama; without it, doubt prevents liability.

This passage presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf on the liability for qasama and diya. The scenario: a person is wounded in a tribe or neighborhood, then moved home and dies from the wound while bedridden. The central question is whether the tribe remains responsible for the death. Abu Hanifa argues yes, because continuous bedridden status ensures the death is attributed to the wound. Abu Yusuf argues no, because the wound itself is not a killing and moving the victim breaks the causal link. This lesson will dissect their arguments and the legal principle of causal attribution (ittisal al-mawt).

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Understand the facts: wounding occurs in tribe A, victim is moved home, dies from wound while bedridden. What is the legal question?
The question is on whom qasama and diya fall.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa holds qasama and diya are on the tribe. His proof: when death is connected to the wound, it becomes killing. The victim's bedridden status (sahib firash) ensures attribution.
Key phrase: al-jarh idha ittasala bihi al-mawt sara qatlan.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position and proof
Abu Yusuf says no qasama/diya because only a wound occurred (ma dun al-nafs), and qasama applies to killing, not wounds. He analogizes to cases where victim is not bedridden.
Qasama is for murder, not for wounding.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the tribe pays diya and takes oaths; under Abu Yusuf, they are not liable.
This affects blood money obligations.
5
Consider the role of doubt
Abu Hanifa's condition: if victim was not bedridden, doubt arises, so no liability. This shows the importance of certainty in criminal liability.
Certainty (yaqin) is not overridden by doubt (shakk).

If someone is wounded in a neighborhood and someone picks him up and brings him home, then the wounded person dies from the pain of that wound, on whom are qasama and diya? Sayings of jurists and each one's proof. He said: Whoever is wounded in a tribe and then transferred to his family and died from that wound, if he remained bedridden until death, then qasama and diya are on the tribe. This is the saying of Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf said: There is no qasama and no diya, because what occurred in the tribe or neighborhood is less than killing (wound) and there is no qasama in it, so it becomes like when he is not bedridden. And [Abu Hanifa] has [the proof] that when death is connected to the wound, the wound becomes killing, and for this reason retaliation becomes obligatory. If he was bedridden, it is attributed to him; if not, it is possible that death was from other than the wound, so it is not binding due to doubt. Commentary: Zayd was wounded in a tribe or neighborhood, then he was lifted and transferred to his home, and he remained continuously bedridden until he died from that wound. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, qasama and diya are obligatory on the people of that tribe or neighborhood. According to Imam Abu Yusuf, qasama and diya are not obligatory at all. Abu Yusuf's proof is that in this neighborhood only wounding occurred, and qasama and diya are obligatory in murder, not in other than murder. So just as in the case where he is not bedridden and dies, by consensus there is no qasama and diya, similarly here qasama and diya will not be obligatory. Imam Abu Hanifa's proof is that when the wound becomes the cause of death, the wound is given the status of killing. This is why if the killer is known and identified, retaliation is obligatory. Therefore, here qasama and diya will be obligatory. If he remained continuously bedridden and died, death is attributed to this wound. But if he was not bedridden, then probabilities arise: it may be that he died from the wound or from something else, so doubt arises, and due to doubt, qasama and diya are not obligatory.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding the liability for qasama and diya when a person is wounded in a tribe/neighborhood, then taken home, and dies from the wound while bedridden. Abu Hanifa rules that qasama and diya are upon the tribe because the wound, when followed by death, is considered killing (qatl). He argues that if the victim remained bedridden (sahib firash) until death, the death is attributed to the wound, so the tribal responsibility is triggered. Abu Yusuf disagrees, holding that only a wound occurred in the neighborhood, and qasama applies only to killing, not to wounds. He compares it to the case where the victim is not bedridden — in that case, there is consensus that no qasama applies. The key point of divergence is whether the victim's bedridden status allows attribution of death to the wound. Abu Hanifa says yes, because the continuous connection (ittisal) between wound and death while bedridden establishes causation. Abu Yusuf says no, because the wound itself is not a killing, and moving the victim breaks the connection. The passage then provides the reasoning of both imams in detail.

Key Terms
صاحب فراش (ṣāḥib firāsh): A wounded person who remains bedridden under care until death, indicating that the wound was the direct cause of death.
مادون النفس (mā dūn al-nafs): An act less than killing, such as wounding, which does not trigger qasama or diya for murder.
اتصال الموت (ittiṣāl al-mawt): The direct connection between the wound and death, such that death follows without interruption.

Liability of a Person Carrying a Wounded Person Who Later Dies
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability of carrier when wounded dies after being moved

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on the liability of a carrier of a wounded person with ramaq, and explain the qiyas (analogy) used by Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Possession (yad) over a wounded person can create liability like a neighborhood's collective responsibility.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability of a person who carries a wounded individual (still having a spark of life, ramaq) to his home, after which the wounded person dies within a day or two. Abu Yusuf holds that the carrier is not liable, while Abu Hanifa, through qiyas, holds that he is. The key is the legal analogy between the carrier's possession (yad) and the neighborhood's responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Read the passage: a wounded person with ramaq is carried to his home and dies after a day or two. Ramq means the last spark of life; the person is not yet dead but death is imminent.
The presence of ramaq is critical; if there were no ramaq, the person would be considered dead, and the ruling might differ.
2
State the Two Positions
Note the two positions: Abu Yusuf says the carrier is not liable (damin). Abu Hanifa (via qiyas) says the carrier is liable. The phrase 'في قياس قول أبي حنيفة' suggests that Abu Hanifa's explicit view may be inferred by analogy.
The passage does not give the proof for Abu Yusuf; it refers to an earlier discussion.
3
Analyze the Qiyas Structure
Identify the components of the qiyas: the original case (asl) is a wounded person found in a neighborhood (mahalla), where the neighborhood is liable. The new case (far') is a wounded person in the carrier's possession. The effective cause (illa) is possession/control (yad).
The illa is khafiyyah (hidden analogy), extracted via tanqih al-manat.
4
Understand the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa's view, the carrier must pay diya (blood money). Under Abu Yusuf's view, he does not.
The passage does not specify which view is mufta bihi (relied upon for fatwa).
5
Reflect on the Methodology
Notice that this is an expansion of a principle from a previous case (the tribe issue). The carrier's yad is treated as equivalent to the mahalla, creating liability for the person in his custody.
This shows how Hanafi fiqh uses qiyas to extend rulings to new scenarios.

This case extends the earlier principle: the carrier takes the place of the neighborhood in terms of liability.
