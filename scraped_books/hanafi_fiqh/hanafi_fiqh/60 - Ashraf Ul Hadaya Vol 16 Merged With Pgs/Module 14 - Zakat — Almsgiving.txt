Priority between Zakah and Hajj: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on priority of Zakah vs Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on priority between Zakah and Hajj and explain the reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
Zakah involves the right of servants; Hajj involves wealth and person—two different strengths lead to two valid narrations.

When a person dies and their estate can only cover one obligation due to the one-third limit, a dispute arises: should Zakah or Hajj be performed first? The Hanafi school has two narrations: one prioritizes Zakah because it involves the right of the poor, the other prioritizes Hajj because it involves both wealth and physical effort. Understanding this dispute is crucial for executors and muftis dealing with bequests.

Guided Path
1
Contextualize the dispute
Understand that this priority question arises in the context of wasiyya (bequests) when the estate's one-third is insufficient to fulfill both outstanding Zakah and Hajj. The executor must decide which to perform first.
The dispute is about the order of fulfillment, not about the obligation itself—both are fard.
2
Identify the two narrations
Recognize that there are two narrations within the Hanafi school: (1) Zakah is given priority, (2) Hajj is given priority. Note that the text mentions that many authorities attribute the first view to Imam Muhammad, while the Hidayah follows the second.
The second narration is from Abu Yusuf and also attributed to Muhammad by some.
3
Extract the proofs for each
For the first narration: Zakah involves the right of the servants (huquq al-‘ibad) in addition to the right of Allah, making it more deserving. For the second narration: Hajj involves both wealth (mal) and person (nafs), while Zakah involves only wealth, so Hajj is stronger (aqwa).
The proofs are based on the nature of the obligations, not on textual evidence.
4
Expand the hierarchy: Zakah/Hajj over kaffarat, kaffarat over sadaqat al-fitr
The text then states that Zakah and Hajj both take priority over expiations (kaffarat) because of the severe warnings (wa‘id) associated with them. Then kaffarat (for killing, zihar, oath) take priority over sadaqat al-fitr because their obligation is established by the Quran (qat‘i al-thubut) while sadaqat al-fitr is from hadith (zanni al-thubut).
This shows a hierarchy based on strength of evidence and severity of warning.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: the executor must choose which narration to follow. The text indicates that the Hidayah adopts the view that Hajj comes first, so in practice many follow that, but the ikhtilaf remains valid.
Local fatwa practice may vary; a mufti should be aware of both positions.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the priority between Zakah and Hajj when the estate is insufficient for both. The commentator presents two narrations: one from Abu Hanifa (via a narration of Abu Yusuf and also attributed to Muhammad al-Shaybani by some) that Hajj is given priority, and another from Muhammad al-Shaybani (as reported by many authorities) that Zakah is given priority. The reasoning for each is provided: the first view argues that Zakah involves the rights of the poor (right of servants), making it more pressing; the second argues that Hajj involves both wealth and physical effort, making it more comprehensive and thus stronger. Then the passage explains that both Zakah and Hajj take precedence over expiations because of the stronger warnings in the Quran and hadith about neglecting them. Finally, expiations for killing, zihar, and oaths take precedence over sadaqat al-fitr because their obligation is established by the Quran (which is definitive) whereas sadaqat al-fitr is established by hadith (which is conjectural). The commentary follows the author's order, explaining the two narrations, their proofs, and the subsequent priorities.

Key Terms
زکوۃ (zakāt): Obligatory alms on wealth, involving the right of the poor (right of servants).
حج (hajj): Pilgrimage to Mecca, obligatory once in a lifetime with ability in wealth and person.
کفارہ (kaffārah): Expiatory act for certain sins, such as killing, zihar, or breaking an oath.
صدقہ فطر (ṣadaqat al-fiṭr): Charity given at the end of Ramadan, obligatory according to consensus.

Priority Rules for Obligations and Bequests
Expands priority rules among wajibat and voluntary bequests

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the priority rules among sadaqat al-fitr, udhiyah, nadhr, kaffarat, and voluntary bequests, and identify the basis for each priority (consensus, strength of proof, testator's intention).

Core Idea
Priority among religious obligations is determined by the strength of their proof: consensus takes precedence over disagreement.

When a testator's estate can only fulfill part of the bequests, a priority system must be applied. This passage, expanding on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, establishes a hierarchy: sadaqat al-fitr (the obligatory charity at the end of Ramadan) takes precedence over udhiyah (the sacrificial animal) because the former's obligation is agreed upon, while the latter's obligation is disputed. By analogy, other obligations are ranked by the strength of their evidence. This lesson walks through the reasoning and the full hierarchy, including voluntary bequests where the testator's own ordering is decisive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base priority rule
State the rule: sadaqat al-fitr is given precedence over udhiyah. Understand why: there is consensus (ijma) on the wajib status of sadaqat al-fitr, while udhiyah is subject to ikhtilaf (Abu Hanifa says wajib, the Sahibayn say sunnah mu'akkadah).
Consensus creates stronger obligation than a disputed ruling.
2
Extend the analogy to other obligations
The same principle (qiyas) applies: among other wajibat, those with stronger proof take priority. For example, sadaqat al-fitr is preferred over nadhr (vows) because the former is imposed directly by Allah, while the latter is self-imposed.
Obligations from divine command are stronger than those from human undertaking.
3
Compare nadhr and expiations with udhiyah
Nadhr and expiations (kaffarat) have consensus on their obligation, unlike udhiyah. Therefore, nadhr and kaffarat take precedence over udhiyah. Udhiyah itself takes precedence over supererogatory (nafl) acts.
Consensus on obligation elevates priority over disputed acts.
4
Rank expiations among themselves
Expiations have an internal hierarchy: kaffarat al-qatl (for killing) is strongest, requiring freeing a believing slave; then kaffarat al-yamin (for oath) because it involves disrespect to Allah's name; then kaffarat al-zihar (for zihar) which is a personal prohibition.
The strength of expiation is judged by the severity of the sin and the requirements of the act.
5
Handle voluntary bequests (tatawwu'at)
For bequests that are not obligatory but voluntary, the testator's own indication of priority is decisive. If he lists one first, that implies it is more important and it takes precedence, even without explicit wording.
The testator's ordering is treated as if he said explicitly 'this is more important'.

Commentary
This passage expands on the priority rules for bequests when the one-third of the estate is insufficient. The commentator starts with the rule that sadaqat al-fitr (obligatory charity at Eid al-Fitr) takes precedence over udhiyah (sacrificial animal) because all jurists agree on the obligation of sadaqat al-fitr, whereas there is disagreement on the obligation of udhiyah (some consider it wajib, others sunnah mu'akkadah). The same analogy applies to other obligations: those with stronger evidence take precedence. Then the commentator explains a hierarchy: sadaqat al-fitr over nadhr (vows) because the former is imposed by Allah directly, while nadhr are self-imposed; nadhr and expiations over sacrifice due to consensus; sacrifice over supererogatory acts. Among expiations, expiation for killing is strongest because it requires freeing a believer slave; then expiation for oath over expiation for zihar due to the element of disrespect to Allah's name. Finally, for purely voluntary bequests (not obligatory), the order of priority follows the testator's expression of importance, which can be inferred from the sequence in which he writes them.

قربانی (qurbānī / uḍḥiyah): Sacrificial animal slaughtered on Eid al-Adha.
نذر (nadhr): Vow to perform an act of worship, making it obligatory.
