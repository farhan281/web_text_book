Executor's Acceptance of Hawala for Orphan's Property
Ruling on executor's acceptance of hawala for orphan's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which an executor may accept a hawala on behalf of an orphan and explain the rationale based on the executor's fiduciary duty.

Core Idea
An executor may only accept hawala for an orphan if it is more beneficial than not accepting.

This passage from al-Jami' al-Saghir addresses a crucial aspect of an executor's (wasi) authority over an orphan's property. The hawala (transfer of debt) is a common financial transaction, but accepting it on behalf of an orphan is not automatic. The ruling hinges on a single decisive criterion: the benefit (khayr) of the orphan. This lesson unpacks that condition and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Fiduciary
The act is the executor (wasi) accepting a hawala (transfer of debt) on behalf of an orphan. The executor is a fiduciary with a duty to protect the orphan's interests.
Hawala is a contract where a debtor transfers the obligation to a third party. The executor is accepting the third party's liability.
2
Recognize the Conditional Nature
The ruling is not absolute: it is conditioned on whether the acceptance is 'khayr' (better) for the orphan. If the hawala is more advantageous than not accepting, it is permissible (ja'iz). If not, it is impermissible.
'Khayr' here means objectively more beneficial in terms of security, convenience, or value.
3
Analyze the Criterion of Benefit
The text states that accepting hawala is permissible when it is 'more advantageous due to the guardianship of consideration' (bi wilayat al-nazariyyah). This means the executor must evaluate the circumstances: is the transfer of debt actually improving the orphan's position?
The executor's authority (wilayah) is limited by the orphan's best interest; it is not unlimited.
4
Apply to Hypothetical Scenarios
Consider two scenarios: (1) The original debtor is insolvent, but the transferee is wealthy and reliable => hawala is beneficial and allowed. (2) The original debtor is solvent and trustworthy, the transferee is less reliable => refusing hawala is better, so acceptance is not allowed.
The executor must compare the original debtor and the transferee.

Executor Accepting Hawala for an Orphan: Conditions for Permissibility
Ruling on executor accepting hawala for orphan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which an executor may accept a hawala on behalf of an orphan and apply the principle of wilayat nazariyyah to evaluate permissibility.

Core Idea
Accepting hawala for an orphan is permissible only if the new debtor is solvent—the executor must preserve, not risk, the orphan's wealth.

This passage from al-Jami' al-Saghir of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani addresses the delicate fiduciary duties of an executor (wasi) managing an orphan's estate. When a debtor offers to transfer his debt to a third party (hawala), the executor must decide whether to accept. The ruling hinges on whether the transfer benefits the orphan. This lesson unpacks the textual ruling and the underlying rationale rooted in the executor's guardianship authority (wilayat nazariyyah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the hawala contract
Clarify who is the original debtor (Khalid), the new debtor (Bakr/muhtal alayh), and the executor. Understand hawala as a transfer of obligation from one debtor to another with consent.
Hawala frees the original debtor once the new debtor accepts.
2
Extract the conditional ruling
The passage states permissibility (jawaz) only if it is 'khayr al-yatim' (better for the orphan). It contrasts two scenarios: new debtor wealthy vs original debtor wealthy.
The condition is not general; it depends on benefit.
3
Analyze the rationale: wilayat nazariyyah
The executor's authority is not absolute; it is bound by the orphan's welfare. 'Wilayat nazariyyah' means guardianship with consideration of benefit, not mere authority.
This principle restricts the executor's actions to those that serve the orphan's interest.
4
Apply the condition to the fact pattern
In the given scenario, if the new debtor (Bakr) is wealthy, acceptance is beneficial (speedy collection). If the original debtor (Khalid) is already wealthy, acceptance may cause delay or risk, hence impermissible.
The measure of benefit is solvency of the new debtor.
5
Seal the ruling
Conclude: acceptance is permissible only when the new debtor is solvent. If the original debtor is already solvent, accepting hawala is impermissible due to potential waste (tadiyyah).
This ruling illustrates the Hanafi emphasis on protecting orphan property.

What is the ruling if the executor accepts hawala (transfer of debt) regarding the orphan's property?

He (Muhammad) said: And when the executor accepts hawala for the orphan's property, if it is better for the orphan, it is permissible; meaning if the new debtor (muhtal alayh) is wealthy, because this is guardianship with consideration (wilayat nazariyyah). And if the original debtor (asl al-madyun) is wealthy, it is not permissible, because in it there is waste (tadiyyah) of the orphan's property in some ways.

Translation: Muhammad said in al-Jami' al-Saghir: And when the executor accepts hawala concerning the orphan's property, then if this acceptance of hawala is better for the orphan, it is permissible. And that is when the muhtal alayh is wealthy, because this is guardianship based on consideration. And if the original debtor is wealthy, then it is not permissible, because in it there is waste of the orphan's property in some ways.

Explanation: The orphan has a debt on Khalid. When the executive demanded the debt from Khalid, Khalid made a hawala to Bakr, meaning Bakr will pay my debt, and Bakr accepted it. So if the executor accepts this hawala, is it permissible or not? He (the imam) said that there is a detail: if the muhtal alayh is wealthy, then in it there is benefit for the child, i.e., the debt will be collected quickly. And if Khalid himself is wealthy, then accepting this hawala is not permissible, because when the muhtal alayh is not wealthy, payment will be delayed. And delay is in a way loss and waste. And the executor's guardianship is based on kindness, and if there is no benefit for the child, there is no good in adopting that course.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jami' al-Saghir deals with hawala (transfer of debt) by an executor on behalf of an orphan. Hawala is a contract where a debtor (Khalid) transfers his obligation to a third party (Bakr) who accepts. The key issue is whether the executor may accept such a transfer. The ruling is conditional: acceptance is permissible only if it benefits the orphan. The measure of benefit is the wealth of the new debtor (muhtal alayh). If the new debtor is wealthy, the debt is likely to be paid sooner, which is beneficial. If the original debtor is already wealthy, then transferring to an unknown or less wealthy party may cause delay or risk. Hence it is not permissible. The commentary explains that the executor's authority is founded on care for the orphan's welfare (wilayat al-nazar). Therefore any action that potentially wastes the orphan's property is forbidden. This ruling illustrates the Hanafi principle of prioritizing the orphan's interest in financial transactions.

Key Terms
حوالہ (hawala): A transfer of debt from one person to another with the consent of all parties
ولایت نظری (wilayat nazariyyah): Guardianship based on consideration of welfare, not just authority
