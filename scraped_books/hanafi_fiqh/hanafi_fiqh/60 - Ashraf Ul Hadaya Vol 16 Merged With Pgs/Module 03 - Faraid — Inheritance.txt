Introduction to Kitab al-Ma'aqil: Blood Money Liability
Table of contents for blood money rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key scenarios where qasama and diya apply, and distinguish between the roles of the 'āqila, property owner, and neighborhood in Hanafi law.

Core Idea
Location of a corpse determines who bears qasama and who pays diya.

This lesson provides an overview of the Hanafi rules on blood money (diya) and the oath procedure (qasama) as outlined in the table of contents of Kitab al-Ma'aqil. You will learn how the location where a murdered person is found—whether on an animal, in a house, in a boat, or in a public place—affects liability. These rules reflect the Hanafi school's concern for communal responsibility and precise allocation of financial compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key liability holders
From the table of contents, note the three main parties that may bear qasama or diya: the 'āqila (agnatic group), the property owner, and the neighborhood (people of the locality). Each scenario assigns liability differently.
2
Categorize scenarios by location
Group the listed cases by where the body is found: private property (house, land), public spaces (market, road, mosque), natural environments (forest, river), and shared areas (boat, shared house).
3
Distinguish qasama from diya
Qasama is the oath-taking procedure to establish liability; diya is the actual blood money payment. In many scenarios, qasama falls on one party (e.g., owner) and diya on another (e.g., his 'āqila).
4
Note intra-Hanafi differences
The contents mention opinions of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Be aware that some rulings may differ among them, as indicated in the headings like 'Proof of Imam' and 'Proof of the two companions'.
5
Understand the rationale
The last headings refer to why diya is on the 'āqila and include a 'rational proof'. This underpins the school's reasoning: the 'āqila shares liability as a form of solidarity.

Key Terms
عاقلہ (‘āqila): The agnatic relatives (tribe or group) who are liable to pay blood money on behalf of a member who commits unintentional homicide.
قسامت (qasāma): A procedure where fifty oaths are taken to establish liability for murder when no direct evidence exists.
دیت (diya): Blood money paid as compensation for homicide or bodily harm.
معاقل (ma‘āqil): Plural of 'āqila; the groups responsible for paying blood money.

Dispute Between Debt and Deposit Claimants in an Estate
Ruling on debt vs deposit claims in deceased's estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on priority between a debt claimant and a deposit claimant in a deceased's estate, and explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A deposit is linked to specific property; a debt is a mere liability. Hence, the depositor has priority.

When multiple claimants approach a deceased's estate, determining who gets priority is critical. This lesson tackles a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: if one person claims a debt and another claims a deposit from the deceased, who prevails? Imam Abu Hanifa gives the depositor priority because the deposit is a specific asset, while the Sahibayn treat both claims equally. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to analyze competing rights in an estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimants and their claims
The passage presents two claimants: one for a debt (qard) and one for a deposit (wadi'ah). Note that both claim the same amount (1000 dirhams) from the deceased's estate.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the deposit claim is stronger (aqwa) than the debt claim. Therefore, the depositor takes the specific property in full.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani consider both claims equal in strength. The estate is shared proportionally between the two claimants.
4
Analyze the reasoning
Abu Hanifa's dalil: deposit is tied to the 'ayn (specific property), while debt is merely a liability on the dhimmah (abstract obligation). The Sahibayn see both as rights against the estate, hence equal.
The key distinction is the link to specific property versus general liability.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on a conflict of claims to a deceased's estate. When one claimant asserts a debt (qard) and another asserts a deposit (wadi'ah), the school differs. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the deposit claimant has priority over the debt claimant for the specific property, because the deposit is tied to the 'ayn (specific property) while debt is a liability on the dhimmah (abstract obligation). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) consider both claims equal in strength, so they share the estate proportionally. The commentary follows the author's order: first the Arabic ruling, then its translation, then an explanatory example with Zayd. The reasoning for Abu Hanifa's position is that the deposit has a direct link to the identical property, whereas debt is merely a legal obligation. This is a standard Hanafi principle: the owner of a specific property has a stronger right than a general creditor.

Key Terms

قرض (qard): A debt; a loan of fungible items that must be repaid in kind or value.

Dividing the One-Third Among Multiple Bequests
Explaining proportional distribution of the one-third among bequests

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule for dividing the one-third among multiple bequests and apply it to a scenario involving both charitable and personal bequests.

Core Idea
Each bequest is treated separately within the one-third; charitable acts each receive their own share.

This passage explains the method of distributing the one-third of an estate when there are multiple bequests—some for acts of worship (rights of Allah) and some for individuals (rights of servants). The Hanafi jurists rule that the one-third is divided proportionally among all bequests, and each charitable act (qurbat) is treated as a separate bequest, not combined into one. This ensures that each intended act gets its share, following a priority order that respects the strength of the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The one-third is divided among all bequests, regardless of whether they are for Allah or for a servant. This is the starting principle.
2
Separate charitable acts from personal bequests
From the divided shares, the portions that correspond to charitable acts (qurbat) are identified. These include acts like Zakah, Hajj, and expiations.
Personal bequests are to individuals and are treated as rights of servants.
3
Apply the priority order among charitable acts
The shares allocated to charitable acts are then spent on them in a specific order: Zakah first, then Hajj, then expiations, etc. This order is derived from the strength of obligation.
The priority order is established earlier in the text; this passage assumes it.
4
Treat each charitable act as separate
All charitable bequests are not combined into one category. Each act is independent, so each receives its own share proportionally. The reasoning is that each act is intended independently for the pleasure of Allah.
This distinguishes the Hanafi view from a possible view that treats all charitable bequests as one.
5
Apply to an example
Consider a person who bequeaths for Zakah, Hajj, expiations, and to a friend. The one-third is divided into four equal shares. The three charitable shares are then allocated to Zakah, Hajj, and expiations according to the priority order.
If the total from the one-third is insufficient, the priority order ensures the most obligatory acts are fulfilled first.

Dhimmi's Church/Synagogue Built During Life Becomes Inheritance
Ruling on a dhimmi's place of worship built during life becomes inheritance upon death

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A dhimmi's place of worship built during life is inherited by agreement; the dispute is only about legal reasoning, not outcome.

Having covered the bequests of Muslims, the author now turns to those of dhimmis (non-Muslim protected subjects) because they are subordinate to Muslims in transactional matters. The first case addresses what happens when a dhimmi builds a church or synagogue during his lifetime and then dies. While the ruling is unanimous—the building is inherited—the underlying legal reasoning differs between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Event
Recognize that a dhimmi builds a church or synagogue during his lifetime. The key event is his death, which triggers the inheritance.
The act is not a bequest (wasiyyah) but a construction during life.
2
State the Unanimous Ruling
The building becomes inheritance (miraath) and is distributed among the heirs by agreement (bil-ittifaq).
There is no dispute on the final outcome.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Reasoning
Abu Hanifa considers the building akin to a waqf. Since in his view waqf is not binding (lazim), it is inheritable like any property.
For Abu Hanifa, waqf is revocable and does not remove ownership.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's Reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that building a place of false worship is an act of disobedience (ma'siyah), so a valid waqf cannot be established. Thus the building remains owned property and passes to heirs.
Their principle: waqf of a sin is invalid.
5
Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Despite different usul, the practical result (inheritance) is identical. This is an example of ikhtilaf without practical consequence.
Such differences occur when two proofs lead to the same ruling.

Executor's Recourse After Void Partition
Ruling statement with commentary on executor's recourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for executor's liability when a slave from a minor's share is sold and later reclaimed, and explain the chain of recourse from executor to minor to other heirs.

Core Idea
Executor recovers from minor's property due to agency; minor recovers from other heirs because partition is void.

This passage from al-Jami' al-Saghir addresses a delicate scenario in inheritance distribution: after partition, the executor sells an asset from a minor's share, but the asset is later reclaimed or the sale price is lost. Who bears the loss? The Hanafi ruling traces liability through agency and the voiding of partition, ensuring fairness among heirs.

Guided Path
1
Scenario Setup
Identify the initial conditions: The executor divided the inheritance. Among the heirs there is a minor. In the minor's share there is a slave. The executor sells that slave and takes possession of the price.
This is a valid sale because the executor acts as agent for the minor.
2
Triggering Events
Two possible events occur: (1) The price perishes in the executor's hands, or (2) The slave is claimed by a rightful owner (e.g., a missing heir). Either event creates liability.
The executor must compensate the buyer for the price.
3
Executor's Recourse
Because the executor acted as the minor's agent (wakil), he can recover the compensation from the minor's property. The loss is not personal.
This is based on the principle that an agent's liability shifts to the principal.
4
Minor's Recourse
Since the partition is voided by the claim of ownership (the slave was not truly the minor's), the minor can recover his loss from the other heirs proportionally.
The partition is invalidated retroactively, so shares must be recalculated.
5
Underlying Principle
The ruling references the Book of Division (Kitab al-Qisma): if after partition something appears in which someone's right is proven, the partition is void.
This ensures that no heir bears a loss that should be shared by all.

Ruling on executor's recourse after istihqaq

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The executor recovers from the minor's property; the minor recovers from co-heirs because the division is vitiated.

This lesson addresses a detailed scenario in estate administration: an executor divides an inheritance, a slave falls to a minor heir, the executor sells it and takes the price, then the price is lost and a third party proves rightful ownership (istihqaq) of the slave. The Hanafi school, based on a statement from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir, provides a precise chain of financial recourse that respects the principles of agency and the invalidation of a flawed division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and sequence of events

When the slave is reclaimed, the sale is invalidated. The executor as seller must return the price to the buyer. But the price is lost, so the executor is liable to pay the buyer from his own funds.
This liability arises from the invalid sale.
3

Since the executor acted as agent (wakil) for the minor in selling the slave, he can recover the amount he paid to the buyer from the minor's property.
The executor is 'amil' (agent) for the minor.

The original division of the estate is vitiated (muntasid) because a share was later found to belong to another. Therefore, the minor can recover from the other heirs a portion proportional to the value of the slave that was wrongly taken from his share.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the inheritance of a khuntha mushkil. The scenario: a father dies leaving a son and a khuntha mushkil (no other heirs). Three views are given. Imam Abu Hanifa treats the khuntha as a female, giving the son two-thirds (2/3) and the khuntha one-third (1/3) — essentially treating the khuntha as a daughter. Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the khuntha gets half of a male's share and half of a female's share, a view attributed to al-Sha'bi. However, they differ on the mathematical implementation: Imam Muhammad calculates a base of 12, giving the son 7 and the khuntha 5; Abu Yusuf calculates a base of 7, giving the son 4 and the khuntha 3. This difference arises from how they apply the half-male-half-female principle in the context of a son's residuary share. The passage then notes that if the khuntha's gender becomes clear later, the inheritance will be redistributed accordingly. This ruling is an example of how Hanafi jurists handle ambiguity in inheritance by applying cautious and inclusive calculations, but the actual practice follows the view that best protects the khuntha's rights until clarity.

Shrouding the Khuntha Mushkil
Ruling on the shrouding of an ambiguous hermaphrodite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended number of shroud garments for a khuntha mushkil and explain the rationale based on caution and analogy.

Core Idea
Shroud the khuntha in five garments like a woman to ensure the sunnah is fulfilled.

When a khuntha mushkil dies, we cannot be sure whether to shroud him as a man (three garments) or a woman (five garments). The Hanafi school applies the principle of ihtiyat (caution) and analogy to life clothing. In this lesson, you will learn the recommended practice and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify default sunnah
Recall that the sunnah for a man is three shrouds, for a woman five, based on common practice in life.
2
Understand the analogy
The ruling is based on analogy to life: women wear five garments, men three. Analogy transmits this to the shroud.
This is qiyas based on 'urf (custom).
3
Apply caution (ihtiyat)
For the khuntha, we cannot determine sex. If we shroud as a woman and it is a man, extra garment is permissible; if we shroud as a man and it is a woman, we miss a sunnah. Hence, caution dictates five garments.
The principle: al-ihtiyat fi ibadat.
4
Conclude the ruling
Therefore, the khuntha mushkil is shrouded in five garments, like a woman.
This is the mufta bihi for khuntha mushkil.

Inheritance of the Khuntha Mushkil with a Son
Intra-Hanafi dispute on inheritance shares of a khuntha mushkil with a son.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance shares for a khuntha mushkil when the deceased leaves a son, according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa treats the khuntha as a daughter, giving her 1/3; Sahibayn use a complex average, but the fatwa follows Abu Hanifa.

When a person dies leaving a son and a khuntha mushkil, the inheritance shares differ among the Hanafi imams. This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi dispute, the arithmetic methods, and the relied-upon position for fatwa. Understanding this will sharpen your faraid skills and illustrate how the school resolves ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
The deceased leaves two heirs: a son and a khuntha mushkil (ambiguous hermaphrodite). No other heirs.
This is the specific case in the text.
2

Abu Hanifa treats the khuntha as a female in inheritance. Thus the estate is divided into 3 shares: son gets 2, khuntha gets 1 (i.e., son 2/3, khuntha 1/3).
This is the simplest division.
3

The Sahibayn follow al-Sha'bi: the khuntha gets half of a male's share plus half of a female's share. Since a son's share when alone is the whole, and a daughter's share when alone is 1/2 (if no son), the khuntha's share when alone would be 1/2 + 1/4 = 3/4? Actually the text says for the khuntha alone: 3/4. Then the son's share when alone is 1. Their combined rights are in ratio 4:3, giving son 4/7, khuntha 3/7 (Abu Yusuf).
This is the 'half male + half female' principle.
4
Imam Muhammad's derivation
Imam Muhammad averages the two scenarios: if khuntha is male, estate split equally (1/2 each); if female, son gets 2/3, khuntha 1/3. Scaling to 12, male scenario: 6 each, female: 8 and 4. Average: son 7, khuntha 5 out of 12.
This gives son 7/12, khuntha 5/12.
5

The text explicitly states that the fatwa is on Abu Hanifa's view: son 2/3, khuntha 1/3.
This is the operative ruling in the school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi imams' methods for calculating the khuntha's inheritance share and apply Imam Abu Hanifa's method to a new case.

Core Idea
When sex is uncertain, inheritance follows certainty: the khuntha gets only the minimum female share (Abu Hanifa).

This lesson examines the different rational methods used by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani for calculating the share of a khuntha (hermaphrodite) when inheriting with a son. Their disagreement stems from how to handle the doubt about the khuntha's sex: should we be conservative, proportional, or average possibilities? The lesson walks through each method and the underlying reasoning, ultimately settling on the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
State the common principle
All three imams agree that the khuntha receives half of a male's share and half of a female's share. The dispute is how to apply this when calculating the actual share with other heirs.
The base rule comes from the principle of giving the khuntha a share that is the average of male and female, but the imams interpret 'average' differently.
2
Understand Imam Abu Yusuf's method
He compares the shares each heir would get if alone: son gets 4/4 (all), khuntha gets 3/4. Then he divides proportionally: son's entitlement is 4 parts, khuntha's 3 parts, so total 7 parts. Thus son gets 4/7, khuntha gets 3/7.
This method treats the khuntha as an entity with a fixed entitlement (3/4) regardless of the other heir.
3
Understand Imam Muhammad's method
He considers two scenarios: if khuntha is male, each gets 1/2; if female, khuntha gets 1/3 and son gets 2/3. He takes the smallest number with both halves and thirds (6), then averages the khuntha's shares from both scenarios: (3+2)/2 = 2.5, leading to fraction. He doubles to 12, giving khuntha 5 and son 7.
This method explicitly averages the two possibilities, then resolves the fraction to whole shares.
4
Understand Imam Abu Hanifa's method
He applies the principle 'wealth is not obligatory by doubt' (al-mal la yajib bi al-shakk). The certain minimum is the female's share (1/3). Since the khuntha's male status is doubtful, only the female's share is given; the rest (2/3) goes to the son.
This is the most cautious and is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the school.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The text explicitly states 'الفتوى على قول أبي حنيفة' (the fatwa is on the saying of Abu Hanifa). Thus, in practice, the khuntha receives 1/3 and the son 2/3.
This overrides the other two methods, though they remain as valid scholarly opinions within the madhhab.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute presenting the different methods of calculating the inheritance share of a khuntha (hermaphrodite) when there is also a son. The three imams — Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani — each propose a method based on their reasoning about certainty and doubt. The passage first explains that the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) follow the principle that the khuntha gets half of a male's share and half of a female's share, but they differ in the calculation. Imam Abu Yusuf's method is simpler: he takes the shares when each heir is alone and divides proportionally. The son alone gets all (4/4), the khuntha alone gets 3/4, so together the son gets 4/7 and the khuntha 3/7. Imam Muhammad's method considers two possibilities: if the khuntha is male, each gets half; if female, the khuntha gets 1/3 and the son 2/3. He then finds a common base (6) and averages the shares, leading to a broken number, so he doubles to 12, giving the khuntha 5 and the son 7. Imam Abu Hanifa's method is the most cautious: he takes the certain minimum, which is the female's share (1/3), and gives that to the khuntha, and the remainder to the son. This results in the khuntha getting 1/3 and the son 2/3. The passage notes that the fatwa is on Imam Abu Hanifa's view, meaning his method is the relied-upon one in issuing legal rulings.

Key Terms

Imam Muhammad's Method for Khuntha Inheritance
Imam Muhammad's rational proof for sharing inheritance with a hermaphrodite

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance share of a khuntha using Imam Muhammad's tansif method and compare it to the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
The uncertain excess share is halved (tansif) to resolve khuntha inheritance.

In this lesson, we explore Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's rational method for distributing inheritance when an heir is a khuntha (hermaphrodite) and their sex is unknown. This intra-Hanafi dispute illustrates how different Imams handle uncertainty in faraid calculations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the possible scenarios
Determine what the shares would be if the khuntha is male (equal shares with the son) or female (son receives double the daughter's share).
Male scenario: each gets 1/2; Female scenario: son gets 2/3, khuntha gets 1/3.
2
Determine the certain minimum share
The lower of the two possible shares (the female share) is guaranteed, as the khuntha is entitled to at least that.
In base 6, certain minimum = 2 shares out of 6.
3
Identify the uncertain excess
The difference between the male and female shares is the uncertain portion.
Excess = male share (3) - female share (2) = 1 share out of 6.
4
Apply tansif (halving) to the uncertain excess
The uncertain excess is split equally between the two possibilities, adding half of it to the certain minimum.
Khuntha receives 2 + 0.5 = 2.5 shares out of 6.
5
Resolve fractions by scaling
If the result is fractional, double the base to convert it into whole numbers.
Base doubled to 12: khuntha gets 5, son gets 7.

Commentary

The certain minimum for the khuntha is the female share (two in base six). The extra share is uncertain, so it is halved. This results in a fractional amount; doubling the base removes the fraction, giving final shares of five for the khuntha and seven for the son out of twelve. This method is a rational application of taking the certain and splitting the uncertain remainder.

This lesson works through three specific cases from the text to illustrate this principle in action.

Guided Path
1
Identify All Heirs
List every surviving heir mentioned in the case, including the khuntha. Note their relationship to the deceased.
For the khuntha, do not yet decide gender; treat them as a generic relative (e.g., sibling).
2
Determine Two Possible Scenarios
For each possible gender of the khuntha (male or female), assign the corresponding legal status. For example, if the khuntha is a sibling, as a male they become 'asabah; as a female they become a prescribed heir with a fixed share.
The gender changes both the share and the category (fard vs 'asabah).
3
Calculate the Shares Under Each Scenario
Using standard faraid rules, compute the distribution for each gender assumption. Determine the base number (asl al-masalah) and check if 'awl is needed.
Focus on what the khuntha receives in each scenario, not just the total.
4
Compare the Khuntha's Shares
Compare the amount (in shares or fractional value) the khuntha would receive as male vs. as female. Identify which is smaller.
The smaller share is the assured distribution; any larger share would be uncertain and thus invalid.
5
Adopt the Status with the Lesser Share
Finalize the distribution using the gender that gives the khuntha the lesser share. This becomes the operative ruling for the estate.
If both scenarios give the same share, either can be used, though the text suggests preferring the status that makes the khuntha an 'asabah if equal.

Commentary
This passage presents three inheritance cases involving a khuntha (hermaphrodite). The Hanafi method here is to assign the khuntha the status that results in the lesser share (i.e., the more disadvantageous) because that is the certain outcome. In the first case, the khuntha is treated as a brother, which excludes him entirely (since siblings are excluded by father or son, but here only spouse and mother? Actually it says 'nothing remains' so he gets nothing). The second case involves a husband, mother, and khuntha. If the khuntha is considered a brother, he takes the residue as 'asabah (1/6). If considered a sister, she would get half as a prescribed share, leading to 'awl from 6 to 8. The assured method is to take the lesser share (brother) so it's valid. The third case involves a wife, children, and khuntha (originally said 'two full brothers and real sister' but actually the khuntha is the heir). The khuntha is treated as a brother, taking the residue after wife (1/4) and children (1/3). If treated as a sister, she would get a prescribed share, increasing her portion. Again, the lesser share is chosen. The author notes that the principles of deriving these cases are covered in the Siraji lesson.

Key Terms
خنثى (khuntha): A hermaphrodite person whose gender is ambiguous.
عول (awl): Proportional reduction of shares when total prescribed shares exceed the estate.
عصبة ('asabah): Residuary heir who takes the remainder after prescribed shares.

Key Takeaway
When an heir's gender is ambiguous, always calculate both possibilities and adopt the status that gives the khuntha the smaller share, as that is the only assured distribution.
