Freeing a Khuntha Slave Under General Manumission Statements
Ruling on manumission of a khuntha slave by general statements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a khuntha slave is freed by general manumission statements and explain the reasoning based on legal certainty (yaqeen) vs. doubt (shakk).

Core Idea
A khuntha slave is freed only when the master's statement covers both genders with certainty.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses how general manumission statements apply to a khuntha mushkil (a hermaphrodite slave whose gender is unknown). The ruling hinges on the Hanafi principle that legal effects require certainty. When the master specifies only one gender, doubt prevents inclusion; but when both genders are mentioned, certainty is achieved and the slave is freed immediately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage considers two cases: (1) the master says 'every male slave of mine is free' or 'every female slave of mine is free', and (2) the master says both statements together. In both cases, he owns a khuntha slave.
The khuntha is 'mushkil' – its gender is not ascertainable by humans.
2
Analyze the ruling for a single-gender statement
If the master says only 'every male slave' or only 'every female slave', the khuntha is not freed. The reason is doubt: it is uncertain whether the khuntha falls under the specified gender. Hanafi law requires certainty for legal effects like emancipation.
The principle: 'Legal effects cannot be based on doubt.'
3
Analyze the ruling for combined statements
If the master says both 'every male slave' and 'every female slave' are free, the khuntha is freed immediately. Because the khuntha is certainly either male or female in Allah's knowledge, the combined statements cover both possibilities with certainty, removing doubt.
The khuntha is not exempt from being either gender; it is one of them in reality.
4
Understand the underlying reasoning (talil)
The passage provides a legal cause (illa): doubt (shakk) prevents inclusion when only one gender is specified. Certainty (yaqeen) is achieved when the statement covers both genders, because the khuntha necessarily belongs to one of the two categories.
This reasoning is consistent with the Hanafi emphasis on yaqeen in matters of obligation and legal effects.
5
Note the cross-madhhab implication
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may consider the khuntha freed by a single statement based on probability. The Hanafi position requires strict certainty, leading to a different practical ruling.
The difference highlights the Hanafi deference to yaqeen over ghalabat al-zann.

And if he said: 'Every slave of mine is free' or he said: 'Every slave-girl of mine is free' and he owns a khuntha slave, he is not freed until his matter becomes clear, for the reason we mentioned. Translation: And if a man said: 'Every male slave of mine is free' or said: 'Every female slave of mine is free' and he has a khuntha slave, that khuntha will not be free until his matter becomes clear, for the same reason we explained. Commentary: Zaid has a slave who is a khuntha mushkil, whose gender is not clear. And Zaid said: 'Every male slave of mine is free' — then the khuntha is not freed because his being a male slave is doubtful. Similarly, if Zaid said: 'Every female slave of mine is free' — this one is also not freed because his being a female slave is also doubtful. But if the master said both: 'Every male slave and female slave of mine is free' — then the khuntha slave becomes free because the khuntha is not devoid of one of these two categories; he is either male or female. Even if it is not apparent to us, in the knowledge of Allah he is one of them. And in Tafsir al-Jalalayn: 'Al-khuntha al-mushkil, it is either male or female in the knowledge of Allah.'

Commentary
This passage deals with a khuntha slave owned by a master who makes a general manumission statement. If the master says 'every male slave of mine is free' and he has a khuntha mushkil, the khuntha is not freed because it is uncertain whether he falls under 'male slave'. Similarly, if he says 'every female slave', the khuntha is not freed because it is uncertain whether she falls under 'female slave'. However, if the master says both 'every male slave and every female slave are free', then the khuntha is freed because it is certain that the khuntha is either male or female, and thus he is included in one of the two groups. The ruling is based on the principle that legal effects cannot be based on doubt, but when the statement covers both possibilities, certainty of inclusion is achieved. The passage cites Tafsir al-Jalalayn to emphasize that a khuntha is essentially either male or female in Allah's knowledge, even if humans cannot determine.

Key Terms
عتق (itiq): Emancipation or freeing of a slave.
خنثى (khuntha): A hermaphrodite; in fiqh, a person with both male and female genitalia or ambiguous gender.
تيقن (yaqeen): Legal certainty; the highest degree of conviction required for legal effects.
شك (shakk): Doubt; uncertainty about a legal fact.
