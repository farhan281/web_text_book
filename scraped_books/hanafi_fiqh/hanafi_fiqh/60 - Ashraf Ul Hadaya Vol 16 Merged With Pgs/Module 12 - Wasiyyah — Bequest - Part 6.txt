Executor's Authority to Appoint Successor Executor: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Resolving dispute over executor's right to appoint successor executor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on an executor's authority to appoint a successor executor and explain the key difference in legal reasoning that leads to the divergent rulings.

Core Idea
An executor's authority is not like an agent's; it transfers and can be delegated to ensure the testator's wishes are fulfilled.

This passage addresses a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding whether an executor (wasi) can appoint a successor executor who will manage the original testator's estate. The core issue is the scope of the successor's authority: does it extend to both the first executor's own estate and the original testator's? Understanding this requires distinguishing executorship from agency, as the Shafi'i school analogizes the two while the Hanafi school argues they are fundamentally different.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the dispute
Identify the parties: Zayd (original testator), Khalid (first executor), Bakr (successor executor). The dispute is whether Bakr becomes executor for both Zayd's and Khalid's estates or only Khalid's.
The Hanafi position is that Bakr manages both estates; Shafi'i says only Khalid's.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i analogizes executorship to agency (wakala). Just as an agent cannot appoint a sub-agent without the principal's consent, an executor cannot appoint a successor for the testator.
The common cause claimed is that in both cases the principal (testator/principal) consented only to the original appointee's judgment.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof is based on transferred guardianship (wilayat). The executor acts by a transferred authority, similar to how a grandfather succeeds a father's authority in marriage matters. The testator, knowing the executor might die, implicitly consents to the appointment of a successor.
The key distinction: the testator is dead and cannot act, so delegation is necessary to fulfill his wishes.
4
Compare the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Hanafi law, the successor manages both estates; under Shafi'i, the successor only manages the first executor's estate, leaving the original testator's estate unmanaged unless a new executor is appointed by the judge.
This practical difference determines who is responsible for distributing the original testator's assets.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The commentator (Al-Marghinani) upholds the Hanafi position as the relied-upon view, citing the strength of the analogy with grandfather's guardianship and the flaw in the Shafi'i analogy.
The principle of 'qiyas ma'a al-fariq' (invalid analogy due to fundamental difference) is central.

Zayd had made Khalid his executor. Now before his death, Khalid made Bakr his executor, and that is permissible. The question is: will Bakr be executor only over Khalid's estate or over both Khalid's and Zayd's estates? There is a difference of opinion. The Hanafi school's position is that Bakr will be executor over both estates. Imam al-Shafi'i says that Bakr will be executor only over Khalid's estate, not Zayd's. Imam al-Shafi'i analogized this to agency; if Zayd made Khalid his agent and Khalid made Bakr his agent, then Bakr's agency is only with respect to Khalid, not Zayd. Similarly, it should be with executorship. In summary, Imam al-Shafi'i analogized executorship to agency, so there must be a common effective cause ('illah) between the analogized case (executorship) and the analogue (agency). He said that the common cause is that in both cases, Zayd consented to Khalid's judgment, not to Bakr's. Our (Hanafi) proof: [Arabic phrase] Translation: Our proof is that an executor acts by virtue of a transferred authority; therefore, he has the right to appoint another executor, like the grandfather. Do you not see that the authority that was established for the testator transfers to the executor in matters of property, and to the grandfather in matters of person? Then the grandfather stands in the place of the father in that which was transferred to him. Similarly, the executor. Explanation: This is our proof: the executor has the authority to appoint an executor, and this second executor will be the executor of both the first testator and the second testator, because the authority that the testator had transfers to the executor, and the executor acts under that authority. And the testator had the right to appoint an executor; similarly, the executor has the right to appoint an executor. The grandfather stands in the place of the father: the father had the authority to contract marriage for his children, and that authority transferred to the grandfather. So just as the father could appoint someone to stand in his place, the grandfather can also appoint someone. Similarly, in matters of property, the testator had authority, and the executor received that authority; the grandfather received it, and it is permissible for the grandfather to appoint a substitute. So the executor also has the authority to appoint a substitute in that which he has authority over. Further clarification of the aforementioned proof: [Arabic phrase] Translation: And this is because executorship is the appointment of another to one's place in that which one has authority. At the time of death, the testator had authority over both estates; therefore, the second executor will take the place of the first in both. And because when the testator sought assistance in this, knowing that death may overcome the executor before completing his purpose—which is to rectify what was neglected—he consented to the executor appointing another, unlike the agent, because the principal is alive and can achieve his purpose himself, so he would not consent to the appointment of another by the agent. Explanation: This is a further clarification. They say that the meaning of appointing an executor is that the testator, in something over which he has authority, appoints someone to take his place. And when the executor dies, he had authority over both estates—his own and the testator's—so the second executor will be placed in the rank of the first and given authority over both estates. Then the second point: the testator's purpose in appointing an executor is to seek assistance to rectify what was neglected regarding property, even though he knows that the executor might die before completing this purpose. Despite this, he appointed him, indicating that he is pleased with the executor's act of appointing another executor. As for the case of agency, it is different because the principal is alive and can achieve his purposes himself. So when he appointed Khalid as his agent, he was not content that Khalid would appoint Bakr as his agent or make Bakr his executor at death. Therefore, there is a difference between executorship and agency; analogizing executorship to agency is an analogy with a difference.

Commentary
This passage addresses an inter-madhhab dispute. The scenario: Zayd appoints Khalid as his executor (wasi). Before Khalid's death, Khalid appoints Bakr as his own executor. The question is over which estates Bakr becomes executor. The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and his school) is that Bakr becomes executor for both Zayd's and Khalid's estates. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, holding that Bakr is only executor for Khalid's estate, based on an analogy with agency (wakala). The Shafi'i argument: just as an agent cannot appoint a new agent to act for the original principal without consent, an executor cannot appoint a successor executor for the original testator. The Hanafi response: the analogy fails because the situations are fundamentally different. The testator, knowing the executor might die before completing the task, implicitly consents to the executor appointing a successor. In contrast, a principal appointing an agent is alive and can act personally, so consent to a sub-agent is not implied. The Hanafi proof is based on the concept of transferred guardianship (wilayat). The executor's authority comes from the testator and allows him to appoint another, just as a grandfather succeeds a father's authority in marriage matters. The text includes a detailed Arabic argument and its Urdu explanation, emphasizing that the executor's role is to complete the testator's unfinished work, and the testator's awareness of possible death implies consent to a successor.

The Executor's Authority in Dividing the Estate: Protecting the Legatee's Right
Ruling with proof on executor's division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an executor's division of the estate with the legatee versus with the heirs, and explain the underlying reason based on succession.

Core Idea
An executor's division that preserves the legatee's share is valid; any division harming the legatee is invalid.

This lesson examines a key ruling from the Hidayah regarding the executor's role in dividing the estate after the testator's death. The passage distinguishes between two scenarios: division with the legatee against the heirs, and division with the heirs against the legatee. Understanding this ruling clarifies the executor's fiduciary duty to prioritize the bequest (wasiyya) over the interests of the heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of division
Recognize that the passage describes two alternative actions: the executor dividing the estate with the legatee (excluding the heirs) and the executor dividing the estate with the heirs (excluding the legatee).
The term 'muqasamah' refers to partition of the estate.
2
State the ruling for each division
The first division (with the legatee) is ruled valid; the second (with the heirs) is ruled invalid. Note the clear contrast: 'jā'izah' vs 'bāṭilah'.
This is a definitive ruling from the matn.
3
Extract the legal reason (illah)
The text gives the reason: the heir is the successor (khalīfah) of the deceased, responsible for defects, while the executor is also a successor but in a different capacity. The legatee's right must be protected.
The reasoning implies the executor cannot act against the legatee's interest.
4
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Consider if the executor pays the legatee from a specific asset. The division must not reduce the legatee's entitlement. Any collusion with heirs to diminish the bequest is void.
The muftā bihī is that the executor cannot divide with heirs to the detriment of the legatee.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the matn of the Hidayah regarding the executor's authority to divide the estate. The key point: when an executor makes a division of the estate, it is valid if it is with the legatee (the person entitled to a bequest) against the interests of the heirs. However, if the executor makes a division with the heirs against the legatee, it is invalid. The reason given is that the heir is the successor of the deceased (خليفة الميت) in the sense that any defect in the estate, such as a hidden defect in a purchased item, comes back to the heir (i.e., the heir is responsible for defects). The executor is also a successor of the deceased, but in a different capacity. The Urdu commentary adds that the executor is the caliph and deputy of the testator, meaning he holds the testator's powers. The ruling implies that the executor must prioritize fulfilling the bequest (wasiyya) over the heirs' shares, and any division that dilutes the legatee's right is invalid.

Proof of Executor's Authority to Appoint Another Executor
Elaborates proof for executor's delegation authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi proof that an executor (waṣī) may appoint another executor and distinguish it from the case of an agent (wakīl) using the concept of implied consent.

Core Idea
An executor can appoint a successor because the testator's consent is implied; an agent cannot because the principal is alive and can act personally.

In Hanafi law, an executor (waṣī) appointed in a will has the authority to appoint another executor to manage the estate after his own death. This authority is implied from the testator's circumstances. But what about an agent (wakīl) appointed by a living person? The rule differs. This lesson unpacks the proof from the Hidayah commentary that distinguishes between the two, showing how implied consent operates in each case and warning against a flawed analogy (qiyās ma'a al-fāriq).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal institutions
First, note that the passage compares two relationships: the testator-executor (al-mūṣī-al-waṣī) and the principal-agent (al-muwakkil-al-wakīl). Both involve one person appointing another to perform tasks, but their legal effects differ after the appointer's death.
The executor's role continues after the testator's death; the agent's role ends upon the principal's death.
2
Locate the implied consent in executorship
The testator knows his executor might die before completing the estate management. Yet he appoints him anyway, which implies consent (riḍā) to the executor appointing a successor. This is the core proof for the ruling.
The text states: 'he became satisfied with the appointment of another by his executor' (ṣāra rāḍiyan bi-īṣā'ihi ilā ghayrihī).
3
Contrast with agency: the living principal
In agency, the principal (muwakkil) is alive and can achieve his objectives himself. Therefore, no implied consent exists that the agent may delegate to another. The agent's authority is strictly limited to what the principal explicitly grants.
The principal can always revoke or modify the agency; he is not bound by the agent's delegation.
4
Understand the qiyas ma'a al-fariq
The passage warns against analogizing executorship to agency. Although both involve delegation, the presence of death in executorship creates a different legal basis. Comparing them as equivalents would be a flawed analogy (qiyās ma'a al-fāriq).
This distinction is typical of Hanafi istihsan, where a ruling is derived from underlying rationale rather than strict qiyas.

Ruling on Executor's Division between Heirs and Legatee
States the ruling and proof for division of assets by the executor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of division by the executor and explain why one is valid and the other invalid based on the Hanafi caliph principle.

Core Idea
The executor can only represent the heirs (caliphs), not the legatee.

The executor (waṣī) serves as the testator's deputy, but not all parties in the estate share the same status. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the heir, who is a caliph (successor) of the deceased, and the legatee, who is merely a beneficiary. This lesson unpacks the ruling that the executor's division from the legatee to the heirs is valid, but the reverse is invalid, and examines the rational proof behind this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of division
The passage mentions two scenarios: (1) the executor divides from the legatee (al-mūṣā lahu) on behalf of the heirs, and (2) the executor divides from the heirs on behalf of the legatee. Note which is declared valid or invalid.
Focus on the Arabic phrases 'مقاسمة الوصى الموصى له عن الورثة' and 'مقاسمته الورثة عن الموصى له'.
2
State the ruling for each
The first division (from legatee to heirs) is valid (jā'iz); the second (from heirs to legatee) is invalid (bāṭil). Record this clearly.
The ruling is about the validity of the legal act of division (muqāsama).
3
Understand the caliph principle
The reason given: the heir is a caliph of the deceased, and the executor is also a caliph; the legatee is not. Caliphs can represent each other, but a caliph cannot represent a non-caliph. Thus the executor can act for the heir but not for the legatee.
Caliph here means a legal successor who steps into the rights and obligations of the deceased.
4
Examine the proof via examples
The passage provides examples from returns due to defects and cases of deception (ghurūr). Just as an heir can return a defective purchase because he stands in the deceased's place, so too the executor can act for an absent heir. These examples illustrate the caliph's authority.
The proof is aqli (rational) based on the logic of representation.
5
Apply to the absent heir scenario
If the executor divides while the heir is absent, and the property perishes, the heir cannot later claim a share from the legatee. This shows the binding effect of the division.
The division is valid and final for the absent heir once executed by the executor properly.

Ruling with proof on executor's authority based on caliph status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the executor's division of the estate in the absence of the heir versus the legatee, and explain the rationale based on the caliph concept.

Core Idea
The executor may divide on behalf of an absent heir because both are caliphs of the deceased; the legatee is not a caliph.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah examines a key distinction in Islamic inheritance law: the executor's authority to divide the estate when the heir is absent versus when the legatee is absent. The ruling hinges on whether the absent party is considered a 'caliph' (representative) of the deceased. The legatee receives property by a new cause (the bequest), so he is not a caliph, and thus the executor cannot act on his behalf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The text presents two cases: the executor divides the estate when the heir is absent, and when the legatee is absent. Note the ruling for each.
The heir's absence is from the estate division; the legatee's absence is from receiving his bequest.
2
Understand the basis: the caliph concept
The key term is 'khalifah' (representative). Both the heir and the executor are khulafa' of the deceased, meaning they stand in his place regarding rights and obligations.
The legatee is not a khalifah because his ownership arises from a new cause (sabab jadid).
3
Examine the proof
The text gives examples: the heir can return defective property bought by the deceased, and can be considered a deceived party (maghrur) in transactions. These rights show that the heir stands in the deceased's place.
The executor also stands in the deceased's place, so he can act on behalf of the heir.
4
Apply the principle to the legatee
Since the legatee is not a caliph, the executor cannot represent him. Therefore, any division made on behalf of the absent legatee is invalid.
The legatee's acquisition is independent of the deceased's representative chain.
5
Consequences of valid vs invalid division
If the division is valid (for heir), the heir cannot later claim against the legatee's share. If invalid (for legatee), the legatee retains his right to the bequest from the remaining estate.
The text later discusses the liability of the executor for loss.

Book of Bequests. Ahmad said in al-Jami' al-Saghir: The executor's division on behalf of the heirs against the legatee is valid, and the executor's division on behalf of the legatee against the heirs is invalid, because the heir is the caliph (representative) of the deceased, to the extent that the heir can return (the property) due to a defect, and the heir can be made to accept return due to a defect, and the heir becomes a deceived party (maghrur) due to the deceased's purchase. And the executor is also the caliph of the deceased. So the executor can be an opponent on behalf of the heir when the heir is absent. So division on behalf of the heir is correct, to the extent that if the heir appears while the property in the executor's hand has perished, the heir has no right to become a partner with the legatee. Explanation: The executor is the caliph and deputy of the deceased. Therefore, regarding the heirs of the testator, the executor will have authority over those matters that the testator had authority over. Hence, the executor can divide the estate on behalf of the testator's heirs, but he cannot divide as a deputy of the legatee. Because the heir and the executor are both caliphs of the deceased, while the legatee is not a caliph of the deceased. So it will be permissible for the executor to become the representative of the other caliph (i.e., the heir), but he will not have the right to become the representative of the legatee. So they say: because the heir is the caliph of the deceased. Now he gives the proof for this: If the deceased bought something and died, and that thing reached the heir, and the heir found a defect that was present at the seller, then the heir has the right to return that thing to the person from whom the deceased took it. And if the deceased sold something and died, and the buyer found a defect that was present at the seller, and the seller has died but his heir is present, then the buyer has the right to return it to the heir, because the heir is the caliph of the deceased. And if the deceased bought a slave-girl and died, and she came into the share of his heir, and he had intercourse with her and a child was born, then someone claimed ownership of the slave-girl and proved it, then the child will be free, but the price of the child must be paid. Because if the deceased were alive and this incident occurred, the same ruling would apply. So in the case of the heir, the same ruling applies, because here the seller deceived the deceased, otherwise he would not have consented that his son be a slave of someone. Therefore, he is considered deceived, and the ruling of the deceived is applied to him, and the child is declared free by payment of value. In any case, the heir and the executor are both caliphs of the deceased. And the principle is that whoever stands in the place of someone else also owns his disposals. Therefore, in the absence of the heir, the executor will have the right to divide on behalf of the heir. So when the executor divided, his division is correct. Therefore, if the heir comes later, and at that time the property that the executor had taken for him in the division has perished, then the heir will have no right to become a partner with the legatee. The legatee, however, is not a caliph of the deceased in any respect, because he became owner by a new cause. For this reason, the legatee cannot return (due to defect), nor can return be made upon him, nor does he become deceived by the testator's purchase. So the executor will not be the caliph of the legatee at the time of the legatee's absence, to the extent that if the property set aside for him with the executor perishes, he will get one-third of the remainder, because the division is not effective. Explanation: The executor is the caliph of the deceased, but the legatee is not the caliph of the deceased, because the legatee acquired the bequeathed property not by an old cause but by a new cause, i.e., on the basis of the bequest. Therefore, the legatee cannot be considered the caliph of the testator. Hence, the legatee cannot return to the testator's seller due to a defect, nor can the testator's buyer return to the legatee due to a defect. Similarly, the legatee cannot be considered deceived regarding the slave-girl; hence the child of the legatee in the mentioned scenario will not be free, even if he pays the value. So when the legatee is not the caliph of the deceased, then in the absence of the legatee, the executor will not be the caliph of the legatee. Therefore, if the executor divided in the absence of the legatee and the legatee's share perished with the executor, since this division will not be effective and this partition will not be valid, when the legatee comes, he will get one-third of the remaining estate. Ruling on the executor's partition for the legatee in his absence: However, the executor will not be liable (damān) because he is a trustee (amīn) in it, and he has authority (wilāyah) to preserve the estate. So it becomes as if some part of the estate perished before division; then for the legatee there will be one-third of the remainder, because the legatee is a partner of the heir. Whatever perishes from the common property perishes under partnership, and whatever remains remains under partnership. Explanation: The executor made a division for the legatee in the absence of the legatee; the executor had no right to this division. Therefore, if it perishes, it will be considered as if no division took place, and the legatee will get one-third of the remainder. But the question is: will the executor be liable for the perished property? He said: he will not be liable, because the executor is a trustee, and he has the authority to preserve the estate.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof discussing the executor's (wasi) authority to divide the estate in the absence of either the heir or the legatee. The central principle is that the wasi is the caliph (representative) of the deceased (mayyit), just as the heir is also a caliph. Therefore, when dealing with the heirs, the wasi can act on their behalf in dividing the estate because both the heir and the wasi share the capacity of representing the deceased. However, the legatee (musa lahu) does not hold this position; the legatee's ownership of the bequeathed property arises from a new cause (sabab jadid) — the bequest itself — not from being a successor of the deceased. Consequently, the legatee does not have the same rights as the heir, such as the right to return defective property purchased by the deceased or being considered a deceived party (maghrur) in transactions involving the deceased. The text provides proofs through examples: an heir can return a defective item bought by the deceased because he stands in the deceased's place; similarly, a buyer can return a defective item to the heir of a deceased seller. In contrast, the legatee cannot do so. Therefore, if the wasi performs a division in the absence of the legatee, and the share allocated to the legatee perishes while in the wasi's custody, that division is void (ghayr nafidh). The legatee retains his original right to one-third of the remaining estate (unless the bequest exceeds one-third, but that is not discussed here). The wasi is not liable for the loss because he is a trustee (amin) with authority to preserve the estate, not a guarantor. The loss is treated as if it occurred before the division, and the partnership between the legatee and the heirs remains intact.

Key Terms
وصی (wasī): The executor appointed by the testator to manage the estate and execute the bequest.
موصی لہ (mūṣā lahu): The legatee, i.e., the person to whom a bequest is made.
خلیفہ (khalīfah): Representative or successor who stands in the place of another regarding rights and obligations.
مغرور (maghrūr): A deceived party who has been misled in a transaction, entitled to compensation.

Consequences of Invalid Division and Executor's Liability
Consequences of invalid division and executor's non-liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the legal effects when an executor divides the estate in the absence of a legatee, and determine the executor's liability for loss.

Core Idea
If the executor divides on behalf of an absent legatee, the division is void; the legatee still gets his share from the remainder, and the executor is not liable as a trustee.

This lesson continues the discussion on the executor's authority, focusing on what happens when he performs an invalid division for an absent legatee. The text clarifies that the division is not effective, and the legatee retains his right to the bequest from the remaining estate. Additionally, the executor is not liable for any loss because he is a trustee (amin) over the estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The executor divides the estate for an absent legatee, and the property set aside for the legatee perishes while in the executor's custody.
This scenario highlights the practical consequence of the invalid division.
2
Determine the status of the division
The division is invalid (batil) because it was done on behalf of a non-caliph. The text says 'al-qismah lam tanfudh' (the division is not effective).
The invalidity is not due to the executor's fault but due to the legatee's lack of representative status.
3
Analyze the effect on the legatee's right
Since the division is void, the legatee's original right to the bequest remains intact. He is entitled to his share (e.g., one-third) from whatever remains of the estate.
The legatee is considered a partner with the heirs; loss is borne proportionately.
4
Examine the executor's liability
The executor is not liable (la yadman) for the loss because he is a trustee (amin) over the estate. His status as amin gives him authority to preserve the estate, but not to guarantee it.
The executor's liability only arises if he is negligent or exceeds his authority in a way that causes loss.
5
Understand the rationale: partnership
The legatee and heirs are partners in the estate. Any loss before a valid division is shared proportionally. The invalid division does not change this partnership.
This is analogous to a situation where part of a common property perishes before division.

خصما عن الوارث اذا كان غائبا فصحت قسمة عليه حتى لو حضر وقد هلك ما في يد الوصى ليس له ان يشارك الموصى له
ترجمہ: احمد نے جامع صغیر میں فرمایا ہے اور وصی کا بٹوارہ کرنا موصی لہ سے ورثا کی جانب سے جائز ہے اور وصی کا بٹوارہ کرنا ورثا سے موصی لہ کی طرف سے باطل ہے اس لئے کہ وارث میت کا خلیفہ ہے یہاں تک کہ وارث رد کر دیگا عیب کی وجہ سے اور وارث پر عیب کی وجہ سے رد کیا جائے گا اور وارث مغرور ہو گا مورث کے خریدنے کی وجہ سے اور وصی بھی میت کا خلیفہ ہے تو وصی وارث کی جانب سے خصم ہو سکتا ہے جب کہ وارث غائب ہو تو وارث پر بٹوارہ کرنا صحیح ہے یہاں تک کہ اگر وارث حاضر ہو حالانکہ جو مال وصی کے قبضہ میں تھا وہ ہلاک ہو چکا ہے تو وارث کو حق نہیں ہے کہ وہ موصی لہ کا شریک ہو۔
تشریح: وصی میت کا خلیفہ اور نائب ہوتا ہے لہذا موصی کے ورثا کے حق میں وصی کو ان امور کا اختیار ہوگا جن کا اختیار موصی کو تھا لہذا وصی موصی کے ورثا کی جانب سے ترکہ کا بٹوارہ کر سکتا ہے لیکن موصی لہ کا نائب بن کر بٹوارہ کرنا چاہے تو نہیں کر سکتا۔ اس لئے کہ وارث اور وصی دونوں میت کے خلیفہ ہیں اور موصی لہ میت کا خلیفہ نہیں ہے لہذا یہ تو وصی کیلئے جائز ہوگا کہ وہ دوسرے خلیفہ میت نمائندہ ہو جائے لیکن یہ حق نہ ہوگا کہ موصی لہ کا نمائندہ ہو جائے تو فرماتے ہیں اسلئے کہ وارث میت کا خلیفہ ہے۔
اب آگے اس کی دلیل بیان فرماتے ہیں کہ اگر مورث نے کوئی چیز خریدی اور وہ مر گیا یہاں تک کہ وہ چیز وارث کو ملی اور وارث نے اس میں کوئی ایسا عیب پایا جو بائع کے پاس سے اس میں موجود ہے تو وارث کو حق ہوگا کہ اس چیز کو اس شخص پر رد کر دے جس سے مورث نے اس کو لیا تھا۔
اور اگر مورث نے کوئی چیز فروخت کی تھی اور وہ مر گیا اور مشتری نے اس میں کوئی ایسا عیب پایا جو بائع کے پاس سے ہے اور بائع مر چکا ہے لیکن اس کا وارث موجود ہے تو مشتری کو حق ہوگا کہ اس کو وارث پر رد کر دے کیونکہ وارث میت کا خلیفہ ہے۔ اور اگر مورث نے کوئی باندی خریدی اور مر گیا اور وہ اس کے وارث کے حصہ میں آگئی لہذا اس نے اس سے وطی کی اور بچہ پیدا ہو گیا پھر کسی نے اس باندی میں استحقاق کا دعویٰ کیا اور اس دعوے کو ثابت کر دیا تو اس کا بچہ آزاد ہوگا البتہ بچہ کی قیمت دینی ہوگی کیونکہ اگر مورث خود زندہ ہوتا ہے اور اس کے ساتھ یہ واقعہ پیش آتا تو بھی یہی حکم تھا لہذا وارث کی صورت میں بھی یہی حکم ہوگا کیونکہ یہاں بائع نے مورث کو دھوکہ دیا ورنہ وہ اس بات پر راضی نہ ہوتا کہ اس کا بیٹا کسی کا غلام ہو اس لئے اس کو مغرور مان کر مغرور کا حکم اس پر جاری کر دیا گیا ہے اور بچہ کو بالقیمت آزاد قرار دیا گیا ہے۔ بہر حال وارث اور وصی دونوں میت کے خلیفہ ہیں، اور اصول یہ ہے کہ جو کسی کے قائم مقام ہوتا ہے وہ اس کے تصرفات کا بھی مالک ہوتا ہے لہذا وارث کے غائب ہونے کی صورت میں وصی کو حق ہوگا کہ وارث کی جانب سے بٹوارہ کرے۔ لہذا جب وصی نے بٹوارہ کیا تو اس کا بٹوارہ کرنا صحیح ہے لہذا اگر وارث بعد میں آیا اور اس وقت آیا جب کہ وصی کے ہاتھ میں وہ مال جو وصی نے اس کے لئے بٹوارہ میں لیا تھا ہلاک ہو گیا ہے تو اب وارث کو یہ حق نہ ہوگا کہ وہ موصی لہ کا شریک ہو جائے۔

وصی میت کا خلیفہ ہے نہ کہ موصی لہ
اما الموصى له فليس بخليفه عن الميت من كل وجه لانه ملكة بسبب جديد ولهذا لا يرد بالغيب ولا يرد عليه ولا يصير مغرورا بشراء الموصى فلا يكون الوصى خليفة عند غيبته حتى لو هلک ما افرز له عند الوصى كان له ثلث ما بقى لأن القسمة لم تنفذ
ترجمہ: بہر حال موصی لہ وہ کسی اعتبار سے میت کا خلیفہ نہیں ہے اس لئے کہ موصی لہ اس کا سبب جدید کی وجہ سے اس کا مالک ہوا ہے اسی وجہ سے موصی لہ رد نہیں کر سکتا اور نہ موصی لہ پر رد کیا جا سکتا ہے اور نہ موصی لہ موصی کے خریدنے کی وجہ سے مغرور ہوگا تو وصی موصی لہ کا خلیفہ نہ ہوگا موصی لہ کے غائب ہونے کے وقت یہاں تک کہ اگر وہ مال ہلاک ہو گیا جو اس کے لئے جدا کیا گیا تھا وصی کے پاس تو موصی لہ کے لئے مابقی کا ثلث ہوگا اس لئے کہ بٹوارہ نافذ نہ ہوگا۔
تشریح: وصی تو میت کا خلیفہ ہے لیکن موصی لہ میت کا خلیفہ نہیں ہے کیونکہ موصی لہ نے جو موصیٰ بہ حاصل کیا ہے اس کا کوئی قدیم سبب نہیں ہے بلکہ ایک جدید سبب ہے یعنی وصیت کی بنیاد پر موصی لہ نے موصیٰ بہ حاصل کیا ہے تو موصی لہ کو موصی کا خلیفہ قرار نہیں دیا جا سکتا لہذا عیب کی وجہ سے موصی لہ نہ تو موصی کے بائع پر رد کر سکتا ہے اور نہ موصی کا مشتری موصی لہ پر عیب کی وجہ سے واپس کر سکتا ہے اسی طرح موصی لہ باندی کے سلسلہ میں مغرور قرار نہیں دیا جا سکتا لہذا موصی لہ کا بچہ صورت مذکور میں آزاد نہ ہوگا اگر چہ وہ قیمت بھی ادا کرے تو جب موصی لہ میت کا خلیفہ نہیں ہے تو موصی لہ کی عدم موجودگی کے وقت وصی موصی لہ کا خلیفہ نہ ہوگا۔
لہذا اگر وصی نے موصی لہ کی عدم موجودگی میں بٹوارہ کر لیا تھا اور موصی لہ کا حصہ وصی کے پاس ہلاک ہو گیا تو چونکہ یہ بٹوارہ نافذ نہیں ہوگا اور نہ یہ تقسیم نافذ ہوگی لہذا موصی لہ جب آ جائے تو اس کو مابقی ترکہ کا ثلث ملے گا۔

وصی کا موصی لہ کی عدم موجودگی میں اس کیلئے تقسیم کا حکم
غير ان الوصى لا يضمن لانه امين فيه وله ولاية الحفظ فى التركة فصار كما اذا هلك بعض التركة قبل القسمة فيكون له ثلث الباقى لان الموصى له شریک الوارث فیتوی ماتوی من المال المشترك على الشركة ويبقى ما بقى على الشركة
ترجمہ: علاوہ اس بات کے کہ وصی ضامن نہ ہو گا اس لئے کہ وہ تو اس میں امین ہے اور وصی کو ترکہ کی حفاظت کی ولایت ہے پس ایسا ہو گیا جیسے جب کہ ترکہ کا کچھ حصہ تقسیم سے پہلے ہلاک ہو جائے تو موصی لہ کے لئے باقی کا ثلث ہو گا اس لئے کہ موصی لہ وارث کا شریک ہے تو مال مشترک میں سے جو ہلاک ہو گا وہ شرکت پر ہلاک ہو گا اور جو باقی رہے گا وہ شرکت پر باقی رہے گا۔
تشریح: موصی لہ کی عدم موجودگی میں وصی نے جو موصی لہ کے لئے بٹوارہ کیا ہے تو وصی کو اس بٹوارہ کا حق نہیں تھا لہذا اگر ہلاک ہو جائے تو ایسا سمجھا جائے گا گویا کہ بٹوارہ نہیں ہوا اور موصی لہ کو مابقی کا ثلث ملے گا۔ لیکن سوال یہ ہے کہ کیا وصی اس ہلاک شدہ مال کا ضامن بھی ہوگا تو فرمایا کہ ضامن نہ ہوگا کیونکہ وصی امین ہے اور اس کو ترکہ کی حفاظت کا اختیار ہے۔
Book of Bequests. Ahmad said in al-Jami' al-Saghir: The executor's division on behalf of the heirs against the legatee is valid, and the executor's division on behalf of the legatee against the heirs is invalid, because the heir is the caliph (representative) of the deceased, to the extent that the heir can return (the property) due to a defect, and the heir can be made to accept return due to a defect, and the heir becomes a deceived party (maghrur) due to the deceased's purchase. And the executor is also the caliph of the deceased. So the executor can be an opponent on behalf of the heir when the heir is absent. So division on behalf of the heir is correct, to the extent that if the heir appears while the property in the executor's hand has perished, the heir has no right to become a partner with the legatee. Explanation: The executor is the caliph and deputy of the deceased. Therefore, regarding the heirs of the testator, the executor will have authority over those matters that the testator had authority over. Hence, the executor can divide the estate on behalf of the testator's heirs, but he cannot divide as a deputy of the legatee. Because the heir and the executor are both caliphs of the deceased, while the legatee is not a caliph of the deceased. So it will be permissible for the executor to become the representative of the other caliph (i.e., the heir), but he will not have the right to become the representative of the legatee. So they say: because the heir is the caliph of the deceased. Now he gives the proof for this: If the deceased bought something and died, and that thing reached the heir, and the heir found a defect that was present at the seller, then the heir has the right to return that thing to the person from whom the deceased took it. And if the deceased sold something and died, and the buyer found a defect that was present at the seller, and the seller has died but his heir is present, then the buyer has the right to return it to the heir, because the heir is the caliph of the deceased. And if the deceased bought a slave-girl and died, and she came into the share of his heir, and he had intercourse with her and a child was born, then someone claimed ownership of the slave-girl and proved it, then the child will be free, but the price of the child must be paid. Because if the deceased were alive and this incident occurred, the same ruling would apply. So in the case of the heir, the same ruling applies, because here the seller deceived the deceased, otherwise he would not have consented that his son be a slave of someone. Therefore, he is considered deceived, and the ruling of the deceived is applied to him, and the child is declared free by payment of value. In any case, the heir and the executor are both caliphs of the deceased. And the principle is that whoever stands in the place of someone else also owns his disposals. Therefore, in the absence of the heir, the executor will have the right to divide on behalf of the heir. So when the executor divided, his division is correct. Therefore, if the heir comes later, and at that time the property that the executor had taken for him in the division has perished, then the heir will have no right to become a partner with the legatee. The legatee, however, is not a caliph of the deceased in any respect, because he became owner by a new cause. For this reason, the legatee cannot return (due to defect), nor can return be made upon him, nor does he become deceived by the testator's purchase. So the executor will not be the caliph of the legatee at the time of the legatee's absence, to the extent that if the property set aside for him with the executor perishes, he will get one-third of the remainder, because the division is not effective. Explanation: The executor is the caliph of the deceased, but the legatee is not the caliph of the deceased, because the legatee acquired the bequeathed property not by an old cause but by a new cause, i.e., on the basis of the bequest. Therefore, the legatee cannot be considered the caliph of the testator. Hence, the legatee cannot return to the testator's seller due to a defect, nor can the testator's buyer return to the legatee due to a defect. Similarly, the legatee cannot be considered deceived regarding the slave-girl; hence the child of the legatee in the mentioned scenario will not be free, even if he pays the value. So when the legatee is not the caliph of the deceased, then in the absence of the legatee, the executor will not be the caliph of the legatee. Therefore, if the executor divided in the absence of the legatee and the legatee's share perished with the executor, since this division will not be effective and this partition will not be valid, when the legatee comes, he will get one-third of the remaining estate. Ruling on the executor's partition for the legatee in his absence: However, the executor will not be liable (damān) because he is a trustee (amīn) in it, and he has authority (wilāyah) to preserve the estate. So it becomes as if some part of the estate perished before division; then for the legatee there will be one-third of the remainder, because the legatee is a partner of the heir. Whatever perishes from the common property perishes under partnership, and whatever remains remains under partnership. Explanation: The executor made a division for the legatee in the absence of the legatee; the executor had no right to this division. Therefore, if it perishes, it will be considered as if no division took place, and the legatee will get one-third of the remainder. But the question is: will the executor be liable for the perished property? He said: he will not be liable, because the executor is a trustee, and he has the authority to preserve the estate.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof discussing the executor's (wasi) authority to divide the estate in the absence of either the heir or the legatee. The central principle is that the wasi is the caliph (representative) of the deceased (mayyit), just as the heir is also a caliph. Therefore, when dealing with the heirs, the wasi can act on their behalf in dividing the estate because both the heir and the wasi share the capacity of representing the deceased. However, the legatee (musa lahu) does not hold this position; the legatee's ownership of the bequeathed property arises from a new cause (sabab jadid) — the bequest itself — not from being a successor of the deceased. Consequently, the legatee does not have the same rights as the heir, such as the right to return defective property purchased by the deceased or being considered a deceived party (maghrur) in transactions involving the deceased. The text provides proofs through examples: an heir can return a defective item bought by the deceased because he stands in the deceased's place; similarly, a buyer can return a defective item to the heir of a deceased seller. In contrast, the legatee cannot do so. Therefore, if the wasi performs a division in the absence of the legatee, and the share allocated to the legatee perishes while in the wasi's custody, that division is void (ghayr nafidh). The legatee retains his original right to one-third of the remaining estate (unless the bequest exceeds one-third, but that is not discussed here). The wasi is not liable for the loss because he is a trustee (amin) with authority to preserve the estate, not a guarantor. The loss is treated as if it occurred before the division, and the partnership between the legatee and the heirs remains intact.

Key Terms
وصی (wasī): The executor appointed by the testator to manage the estate and execute the bequest.
امین (amīn): A trustee who is entrusted with property without being a guarantor; liable only for negligence or breach.
موصی لہ (mūṣā lahu): The legatee, i.e., the person to whom a bequest is made.
خلیفہ (khalīfah): Representative or successor who stands in the place of another regarding rights and obligations.

The Executor is the Deceased's Representative, Not the Legatee
Ruling on the legal status of legatee vs executor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the legatee is not considered a representative of the deceased in Hanafi law and identify the legal consequences that follow from this principle.

Core Idea
The legatee acquires ownership through a new cause (the will), not as a substitute for the deceased.

This passage clarifies a fundamental distinction in Hanafi inheritance law: the executor acts as the deceased's representative, but the legatee does not. The reason lies in the nature of the legatee's ownership—it arises from a fresh legal cause (the will itself), not from the deceased's own position. Understanding this distinction is essential because it determines rights of return for defects, liability for deception, and the validity of division when the legatee is absent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
Recognize that the executor (wasi) manages the estate on behalf of the deceased, while the legatee (musa lahu) is a beneficiary who receives a bequest.
The executor is appointed by the deceased; the legatee is designated in the will.
2
State the central ruling
The text explicitly states that the legatee is not a representative (khalifa) of the deceased in any respect. The executor is the representative, but the legatee is not.
3
Understand the rationale: new cause of ownership
The legatee's ownership is based on a 'new cause' (sabab jadid)—that is, the will itself. Unlike an heir who inherits the deceased's legal position, the legatee acquires an independent right.
This is the key legal argument (talil) for the ruling.
4
Derive the corollaries
From this principle, follow: (1) the legatee cannot return a defective item to the deceased's seller, (2) a buyer from the deceased cannot return a defect to the legatee, (3) the legatee is not considered deceived (maghrur) in a sale by the testator, and (4) the executor is not the legatee's representative during absence.
Each corollary reinforces the idea that the legatee stands independently.
5
Apply to the case of division in absence
If the executor divides the estate in the legatee's absence and the legatee's allocated share is lost, the division is invalid. The legatee still receives one-third of the remaining estate upon return.
The loss falls on the estate, not the legatee, because the separation was not effective.

Executor's Liability for Lost Share After Unauthorized Division
Ruling on executor liability with analogical reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on executor liability when a legatee's share is lost after an unauthorized division and explain the legal reasoning based on the executor's status as amin and the partnership principle.

Core Idea
An executor is not liable for loss after unauthorized division because he remains an amin, and the loss is shared proportionately between legatee and heirs.

When an executor divides the estate without the legatee's presence, the division is not valid. If the legatee's share is subsequently lost, a key question arises: is the executor liable? This passage clarifies that the executor is not liable, drawing on the principles of trusteeship (amanah) and partnership (shirkah). We will unpack the legal reasoning and the supporting proof from Jami' al-Saghir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the executor dividing the legatee's share in the legatee's absence, followed by the loss of that share. The ruling is that the executor is not liable (la yadman), and the legatee receives one-third of the remaining estate.
The ruling is from Jami' al-Saghir.
2
Analyze the dual basis for non-liability
First, the executor is an amin (trustee) with authority to preserve the estate, so he is not liable for loss without negligence. Second, the legatee is considered a partner (sharik) with the heirs in the one-third share, so any loss is borne proportionately.
The partnership principle ensures the loss is shared, not borne entirely by the legatee.
3
Trace the analogy (qiyas)
The ruling is analogous to loss before division: in both cases, the executor is not liable, and the legatee takes one-third of the remainder. The common illa (effective cause) is that the executor is an amin with authority, and the property remains joint between legatee and heirs.
This analogy is explicitly stated in the text: 'so it is as if some of the estate was lost before division'.
4
Confirm the source of authority
The passage cites Imam Muhammad's Jami' al-Saghir, which belongs to the Zahir al-Riwayah, the most authoritative layer of Hanafi transmission. This confirms that the mufta bihi (operative position) is exactly as stated.
No intra-Hanafi disagreement is recorded on this point.

Bequest for Hajj: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Lost Funds
Intra-Hanafi dispute on performing Hajj from bequest when designated funds are lost

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad regarding a bequest for Hajj when designated funds are lost, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of each view.

Core Idea
When Hajj bequest funds are lost, the obligation to perform Hajj depends on how loss is attributed to the testator vs. executor.

This passage from al-Jami' al-Saghir presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about a bequest for Hajj where the funds set aside for Hajj are lost after the executor divides the estate. The three leading imams — Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad — disagree on whether the bequest must still be fulfilled from the remainder of the estate. Understanding their differing arguments reveals how key principles of agency (wakalah) and property division (qismah) are applied in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common scenario
The executor divides the estate and sets apart funds for Hajj; those funds are lost. Determine what happens to the obligation to perform Hajj from the bequest.
The loss occurs after division, not before.
2
State Imam Muhammad's position
Imam Muhammad holds that the bequest lapses entirely; nothing is taken from the remainder. His reasoning: the testator himself, if he had set aside funds and lost them, would void the bequest; the executor stands in his place, so the same rule applies.
This is the ruling from al-Jami' al-Saghir.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf distinguishes: if the lost amount exhausted the entire one-third, nothing is reclaimed from the remainder; otherwise, the full one-third is taken from the remainder to perform Hajj.
The condition is whether the lost property 'consumes' the one-third.
4
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that Hajj must be performed from the remaining one-third of the estate, regardless of where the loss occurred — whether in the executor's hand or the appointed pilgrim's hand.
His view is based on the bequest being an obligation upon the estate that is not extinguished by loss.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the heirs must fund Hajj from the remainder; under Muhammad, the bequest is void and no Hajj is performed; under Abu Yusuf, the outcome depends on whether the lost amount equals the one-third or not.
This practical difference may affect the executor's liability and the heirs' rights.

Once the judge validly divides, the legatee's share is considered delivered. If the property is later lost (e.g., stolen or destroyed) before the legatee returns, the legatee has no claim against the heirs.

Who bears the loss? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling and its underlying reasoning, grounded in the principles of contractual responsibility and unjust enrichment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal question
Read the passage and understand the facts: an executor sells a slave as per the will, takes the price, the price is lost, then the slave is claimed by a third party. The question is: who must compensate the buyer?
The slave is mustahaqq (belongs to another); the price is no longer in the executor's possession.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The ruling is that the executor is liable (dhamin) to return the price to the buyer, even if the price was lost in his possession.
This is the mufta bihi position: the executor must pay from his own property.
3
Analyze the first reasoning: the executor is the contracting party
The passage states that the executor is the 'aqid (contracting party). In Hanafi fiqh, contractual obligations and warranties fall on the person who makes the contract, not on the testator. Therefore, the buyer's claim for return of the price lies against the executor.
This is based on the general principle that the agent who acts as principal in a contract bears personal responsibility.
4
Analyze the second reasoning: the buyer's intent was conditional on delivery
The buyer paid the price only with the understanding that he would receive the slave. Since the slave was not delivered (due to the claim), the buyer did not get what he paid for. The executor, as seller, took the buyer's property without his true consent, making restitution wajib (obligatory).
This reasoning aligns with the Hanafi concept of daman (liability) arising from taking property without owner's consent.
5
Conclude the liability and its basis
Thus, the executor is liable because he both contracted as seller and took the price for a consideration that could not be given. The liability is classified as wajib (obligatory), not merely recommended.
The passage does not discuss the executor's recourse against the true owner of the slave; only the buyer's right to recover the price from the executor.

Executor's Recovery from Testator's Estate: Full Estate or One-Third?
Ruling on executor recovery from testator's estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on executor recovery from the testator's estate and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad, applying the principle that debt is paid from the entire estate.

Core Idea
Compensation due to the testator's deception is a debt, paid from the entire estate—not a bequest limited to one-third.

This passage introduces an intra-Hanafi dispute over whether an executor who paid compensation to a buyer due to the testator's deception can recover from the estate. The analysis explores the initial and final positions of Abu Hanifa, the alternative view of Imam Muhammad, and the principle that classifies such recovery as a debt rather than a bequest. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi approach to agency and liability in estate matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
The executor sold a slave that turned out to belong to another. The executor paid compensation to the buyer. Now he wants to recover this amount from the testator's estate.
2
State the initial Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa initially said no recovery because the executor's liability arose from his own possession. He later retracted. Imam Muhammad holds recovery is limited to one-third of the estate.
The later position of Abu Hanifa is the mufta bihi.
3
Analyze the reasoning behind each position
Abu Hanifa's final view: the executor is an agent (wakil) of the testator, so he can recover like any agent. Muhammad's view: the sale was by virtue of the will, so recovery is limited to one-third (the limit for bequests).
The key is whether the compensation is a debt or a bequest.
4
Resolve the dispute using Hanafi principles (ghurur, dayn)
Zahir al-Riwayah holds that the testator's deception (ghurur) made the compensation a debt (dayn) on the testator. Debts are paid from the entire estate, not just one-third.
This principle is supported by the generality of the hadith 'The debt of Allah is most worthy of being fulfilled.'
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Abu Hanifa's final view, the executor recovers the full amount even if it exhausts the estate. Under Muhammad's view, if the estate is large, recovery is capped at one-third; if smaller, it's limited to that.
The mufta bihi (Abu Hanifa) gives the executor full recovery, protecting the executor from personal loss.

Guardian's Sale of Absent Adult's Movable Property
Ruling on guardian's sale of absent adult's movable property with istihsan justification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a guardian's sale of an absent adult's movable property and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas.

Core Idea
A guardian may sell movable property to prevent loss, but not real estate, based on preservation need.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Jami' al-Saghir: the authority of a guardian to sell property of an absent adult. The key is that the guardian's role is preservation, not ownership, so only at-risk property may be sold. The Hanafi school uses istihsan to relax the strict analogy.

Guided Path
1
State the Ruling
Identify the ruling: the guardian's sale of movable property is permissible, but real estate is not.
The ruling is from Imam Muhammad in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Understand the Analogy (Qiyas)
Recognize that qiyas would deny any sale authority because the father has no true ownership over an adult child.
Qiyas compares the guardian to the father.
3
Apply Istihsan
See that the Hanafi school abandons qiyas for istihsan: movable property risks spoilage, so selling it preserves its value.
Istihsan is juristic preference based on public interest.
4
Differentiate Movable and Immovable
Real estate is inherently preserved; no need for sale. The guardian's preservation authority only covers movable goods.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school.

Ruling that guardian is not permitted to engage in trade with the estate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a guardian trading with estate property and explain the legal basis (hifz vs tijarah).

Core Idea
A guardian is appointed for preservation, not profit — trade with the estate is haram.

This lesson addresses a fundamental limitation on a guardian's authority: the estate is entrusted for safekeeping, not for entrepreneurial activity. Imam Muhammad explicitly rules that any trade with the estate property is impermissible, as it exceeds the guardian's mandate. The underlying principle is that the testator's intent is preservation (hifz), not commercial growth (tijarah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is the guardian engaging in trade (buying, selling, investing) with the estate property. The ruling is that it is impermissible (haram).
This is a clear-cut prohibition from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (illah)
The guardian's mandate is limited to preservation (hifz). The testator appointed the guardian to keep the property safe, not to deploy it in commerce. Trade requires a different authorization.
The Arabic phrase 'al-hifz duna al-tijarah' encapsulates this distinction.
3
Recognize the consequence of violation
If the guardian trades, he becomes a transgressor (mut'addi) and is personally liable for any loss. Any profit earned must be returned to the estate; the guardian cannot keep it.
The liability is based on exceeding the permitted scope of agency.
4
Distinguish when trade might be allowed
If the testator explicitly permits trade in the will, the guardian is authorized to do so. The default ruling, however, is strict prohibition.
This exception aligns with the principle of respecting the testator's expressed wishes.

A guardian appointed by a brother stands in the brother's place, inheriting his authority over the absent major's property.

The passage presents the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) equating the authority of a guardian appointed by a brother with that of a guardian appointed by the father over an absent major. This ruling extends the principle of substitution (qa'im maqam) to guardians appointed by other relatives such as the mother or uncle, as long as the appointer himself possesses authority over the property in question.

Executor vs Grandfather: Who Manages the Minor's Property?
Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on executor vs grandfather priority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the priority between an executor and a grandfather in managing a minor's property, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
The executor is preferred because the father's appointment transfers guardianship, and his choice indicates better care for the children.

When a father dies, he may appoint an executor (wasi) to manage his minor children's inherited property. But what if the children's paternal grandfather is also alive and claims authority? This passage from al-Jami' al-Saghir presents a sharp difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. Let's explore how each school derives its position and why the Hanafi school gives priority to the executor.

2

Recognize that Imam al-Shafi'i holds the opposite view: the grandfather is more entitled. Understand his basis: the Law places the grandfather in the father's place during his absence.
The Shafi'i proof is that the grandfather inherits and acts as natural guardian.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Analyze the Shafi'i argument: since the grandfather is the father's substitute, he should have authority over the property, just as the father would.
This is a general substitution argument.
4

Examine the Hanafi rebuttal: the father's appointment transfers his specific guardianship to the executor. The father's choice, despite knowing the grandfather exists, proves the executor is better for the children.
The key point is that the appointment creates a stronger, specific right over the general substitution.
5

This difference affects inheritance and guardianship arrangements.

Grandfather as Default Guardian When Father Appoints No Executor
Ruling that grandfather stands in place of father when no executor appointed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for guardianship of minor children when a father dies without appointing an executor, and identify the grandfather as the default guardian with full authority including marriage.

Core Idea
When a father dies without an executor, the grandfather automatically steps into his guardianship role.

This passage establishes a fundamental default rule in Hanafi inheritance and guardianship law. When a father passes away without designating an executor (wasī) for his minor children, the paternal grandfather (jadd) assumes the father's position as guardian. The reason given is the grandfather's closeness and compassion—he is the nearest relative and most affectionate. Consequently, he gains all guardianship rights, including the authority to contract marriages for the minors, a right that no other executor would have. However, this default rule is superseded if the father did appoint an executor; then the executor takes precedence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The rule applies when a father dies and has not appointed an executor (wasī) for his minor children. If an executor was appointed, the rule does not apply.
2
State the Default Guardian
In the absence of an executor, the paternal grandfather automatically becomes the guardian (qā'im maqām al-ab).
3
Understand the Rationale
The grandfather is considered the closest relative (aqrab) and most compassionate (ashfaq) toward the children, making him the best default choice.
4
Note the Scope of Authority
The grandfather gains full guardianship, including the right to contract marriage (wilāyat al-nikāḥ) for the minors—a right that no other executor would have.
5
Recognize the Exception
If the father did appoint an executor, that executor is given precedence over the grandfather in all matters of disposition.
This exception is based on the father's explicit choice, which overrides the default.

Testimony of Two Executors About a Third Co-Executor
Ruling on testimony of executors about a third co-executor with qiyas and istihsan analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the testimony of two executors about a third co-executor, distinguish the qiyas and istihsan positions, and apply the exception when the third party claims.

Core Idea
Testimony of executors about a third executor is invalid due to self-interest, but accepted by istihsan if the third party himself claims.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the law of wills: when two already-appointed executors testify that the deceased also appointed a third person. At first glance, their testimony seems suspect because they gain an assistant. Hanafi law provides a nuanced ruling, balancing qiyas (strict analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference). Understanding this ruling illustrates how the Hanafi school employs istihsan to achieve practical justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is the testimony of two executors that the deceased appointed a third executor. The default ruling (by qiyas) is that this testimony is invalid (bāṭil) due to self-interest (tuhma).
The executors benefit by gaining an assistant, so their testimony is suspect.
2
Understand the illa (effective cause)
The illa for rejection is tuhma: the executors are proving an assistant for themselves, so they have a personal interest in the outcome.
Tuhma is a recognized cause to reject testimony in Hanafi law.
3
Contrast the qiyas outcome
Qiyas (strict analogy) applies this illa uniformly, so the testimony is always invalid regardless of circumstances.
The qiyas view treats the case the same as any other where witnesses have self-interest.
4
Identify the istihsan exception
The text states an exception: if the third person himself claims the executorship, then the testimony of the two executors is accepted. This is by istihsan.
Istihsan overrides the qiyas to achieve a more just outcome.
5
Analyze the wajh al-istihsan (basis of juristic preference)
The judge has independent authority to appoint executors without needing the executors' testimony. When the third party claims, the executors are merely supporting his claim, not initiating a benefit for themselves. The tuhma is removed.
The judge's authority removes the suspicion because the testimony is not necessary for the appointment.

Commentary
This passage begins a new section within the chapter on wills, focusing on the rules of testimony (shahādah) specifically related to wills. The author first presents the general ruling: if two already-appointed executors testify that the deceased also appointed a third person as an executor with them, their testimony is invalid (bāṭil). The reason is that they are accused of self-interest (tuhma) because by proving a third executor, they are gaining an assistant who could share the burden or support their decisions, thus they benefit from the testimony. The practical question is then reiterated: two executors testify that the deceased appointed a third; is this testimony acceptable? The answer: generally no, but yes if the third party himself claims it.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony of two executors regarding a third executor and explain how tuhma and istihsan determine its validity.

Core Idea
Testimony of executors about a co-executor is invalid due to suspicion unless the third claims, then istihsan prevails.

In Hanafi law, the testimony of witnesses is generally rejected if they have a personal interest in the matter. But what if the witnesses are already appointed executors and they testify that the deceased appointed another person as co-executor? Does their interest disqualify them? This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from Imam Muhammad where the outcome depends on whether the third person himself claims the executorship. The interplay between qiyas (analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference) is central.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling under qiyas
Recognize that the act is testimony of two existing executors about a third. According to qiyas, the testimony is invalid due to tuhma (suspicion) because they benefit by having a co-executor.
Tuhma arises from securing a partner in executorship.
2
Examine the istihsan exception
Note that the ruling changes when the third person himself claims executorship. By istihsan, the testimony becomes acceptable. Understand the rationale: the judge has independent authority to appoint executors, so the testimony only saves the judge effort, not proving the appointment itself.
Istihsan overrides qiyas because the judge could have appointed without testimony.
3
Map the two scenarios
Apply the rule: if the third does not claim, testimony rejected (batil). If the third claims, testimony accepted (sahih) by istihsan. Write the concrete example from the commentary.
Example: Zaid appointed Khalid and Bakr; they testify about Sajid.
4
Understand the evidence for istihsan
The judge's authority (wilaya) to appoint executors independently is key. Since the appointment is ultimately by the judge, the witnesses' testimony does not create a right for themselves, reducing suspicion.
The burden of determination is removed from the judge.

Testimony of Two Sons About Father's Appointment of Executor
Ruling on the invalidity of testimony by heirs regarding executor appointment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the testimony of heirs regarding the appointment of an executor and identify the reason (tuhma) that invalidates it.

Core Idea
Testimony tainted by personal benefit is invalid, even from otherwise credible witnesses.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Imam Muhammad's al-Jami' al-Saghir: the testimony of two sons that their father appointed an executor is invalid if the alleged executor denies. The passage illustrates the Hanafi principle that any indication of personal gain ('naf') creates suspicion ('tuhma') against the witness, rendering the testimony inadmissible. Understanding this ruling requires grasping how Hanafi fiqh safeguards the integrity of testimony by excluding even partial interests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is the testimony of two sons that their father appointed a specific person as executor. The ruling is that this testimony is invalid (batil).
The invalidity is absolute, meaning the testimony carries no legal weight.
2
Identify the Condition Triggering Invalidity
The condition is that the alleged executor denies the appointment. Additionally, the sons have a personal interest – they benefit from having an executor manage the estate.
If the executor had affirmed, the ruling might differ, but here the denial combined with the sons' interest creates tuhma.
3
Locate the Proof (Dalil)
The proof is rational: the sons draw benefit to themselves (yajurrani ila anfusihima naf'an) by securing a guardian for the estate. This is stated in the text.
The proof is a direct observation, not based on a specific Quranic verse or hadith.
4
Understand the Legal Reasoning
The benefit (naf) makes the sons 'mutahim' (suspected) in their testimony. Under Hanafi witness law, anyone who stands to gain from the outcome of their testimony is disqualified.
This is a general principle: testimony must be free from even the appearance of self-interest.
5
Consider Implications and Edge Cases
If the sons had no personal benefit (e.g., they were not inheritors), the testimony might be admissible. Also, if multiple unrelated witnesses testified, the ruling could be different.
The text only covers the scenario where the executor denies and the sons are heirs.

Testimony of Executors for a Major Heir: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on executors' testimony for major heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the testimony of executors for a major heir regarding estate property, and state the reason for the disagreement.

Core Idea
When major heirs exist, executors lose authority over the estate; Sahibayn accept their testimony, Abu Hanifa restricts it.

This lesson examines a specific dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the admissibility of testimony given by two executors on behalf of a major heir. The key factor is whether the testimony concerns property from the deceased's estate or other property, and whether the executors have any interest or suspicion (tuhma) that could affect their credibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
The act is two executors (wasiyan) testifying on behalf of a major heir (warith kabir). The property in question may be from the deceased's estate or from other sources.
A major heir has full legal capacity, unlike a minor.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds: if the testimony concerns property that is part of the deceased's estate, it is invalid (la yajuz); if it concerns property outside the estate, it is valid (yajuz).
The distinction is based on potential conflict of interest.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) hold that the testimony is valid in both cases, whether the property is from the estate or not.
They see no suspicion because executors have no authority over the estate when heirs are major.
4
Analyse the Sahibayn's evidence
The Sahibayn argue: since heirs are major, the executors have no authority of disposal (wilayat al-tasarruf) over the estate. Therefore, there is no tuhma (suspicion) that could undermine their testimony.
The absence of authority eliminates the risk of self-interest.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if Abu Hanifa's view is followed, the testimony is accepted only for non-estate property; if the Sahibayn's view is followed, it is accepted for both.
Later Hanafi fatwa may prefer the Sahibayn's view due to lack of suspicion.

Testimony that creates mutual benefit between witnesses is void—the appearance of collusion disqualifies even if the facts are true.

In Hanafi fiqh, the integrity of testimony is paramount. A witness must be free from any accusation of bias (tuhmah). This lesson examines a specific case where two parties testify for each other in bequests, creating a partnership (shirkah) that invalidates both testimonies. The ruling emphasizes that even if each testimony is factually correct, the mutual benefit renders them inadmissible.

The second example involves one party testifying about a slave and the other about one-third of the wealth; but since the slave is part of the one-third (as it comes from the bequest), the same partnership arises. Thus, the principle is that any testimony that establishes mutual benefit (shirkah) is inadmissible. The Will of a Mute Person: Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on Gesture Validity
Inter-madhhab dispute with proof and response

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on a mute person's will via gesture, state the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate the Hanafi response using the concepts of habitual gesture, negligence, and textual basis.

Core Idea
A mute's will is valid only if gestures are habitual; temporary speech loss does not suffice until gestures become known.

This passage presents a classic Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on whether a person who cannot speak can make a valid will via gesture. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a congenital mute (akhras) and a person with a temporary speech impediment (mu'taqal al-lisan). The Shafi'i school treats both equally, arguing that the underlying cause (inability to speak) is the same. We will examine the proofs and the specific conditions that the Hanafis require for the gesture to be legally effective.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on the mute's will
Identify the ruling from al-Jami' al-Saghir: the gesture of a congenital mute (akhras) is valid for a will if the gesture is habitual and understood by people. For a temporary speech impediment, it is not valid unless the condition persists and gestures become known.
The key condition is that the gesture must be 'ma'hudah ma'lumah' (habitual and known).
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection and their analogy
Imam al-Shafi'i holds both cases valid equally. His proof: the reason (illah) is incapability to speak, which exists in both. He draws an analogy to wild animals: both originally wild and tame animals that become wild permit emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari) because of the same incapability.
The analogy is a qiyas khafi, comparing speech incapability to wildness.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response: three distinctions
Hanafi jurists respond with three points: (1) Gestures are only reliable if customary and known, which is true only for a congenital mute; (2) The person with temporary loss is negligent for delaying the will until speech ceased, unlike the mute; (3) The temporary condition is likely to disappear, so it does not warrant the same ruling as a permanent condition. Additionally, the ruling on wild animals is based on nass (text), not analogy, so the Shafi'i analogy is invalid.
These distinctions show why the Hanafi school does not accept the qiyas.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Hanafi ruling, if a person suddenly loses speech, his gestured will is invalid until his gestures become habitual. Under Shafi'i, the will is valid immediately.
This affects estate planning and the validity of bequests.
5
Note the authority weight and mufta bihi
The Hanafi position is from Zahir al-Riwayah (al-Jami' al-Saghir) and is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the madhhab. The Shafi'i view is acknowledged as a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The text presents the Hanafi view as the madhhab without naming a specific tarjih authority, but the presentation implies it is the operative position.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafis and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the validity of a will made by a person who cannot speak. The ruling originates from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's al-Jami' al-Saghir. The Hanafi position is that a will made via a gesture (like nodding the head) is valid only for a person who is congenitally mute (akhras), provided the gesture is habitual and understood. For a person who temporarily loses speech (mu'taqal al-lisan) due to a sudden ailment, the gesture is not valid unless the condition persists and he develops known gestures—then the mashayikh liken him to a mute. Imam al-Shafi'i holds both cases valid equally, arguing that the rationale (illah) is the inability to speak, which exists in both. He draws an analogy: just as emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari) is permissible for both originally wild animals and tame animals that became wild due to the inability to perform voluntary slaughter, similarly here. The Hanafi jurists counter by pointing to three distinctions: (1) Gestures are only reliable if customary and known—this is true for a congenital mute whose gestures people know, but not for a sudden case; (2) The person with a temporary condition is negligent for delaying the will until speech ceases, unlike the mute who had no choice; (3) The temporary impediment is likely to go away, so it does not warrant the same ruling as a permanent condition. Finally, the Hanafis note that their ruling on wild animals is based on textual evidence (nass), not analogy, so the Shafi'i analogy is invalid. The mufta bihi position is the Hanafi one.

Key Terms
اخرس (akhras): A person who is congenitally mute, born without the ability to speak, and whose gestures are habitual and understood by people.
معتقل اللسان (mu'taqal al-lisan): A person whose tongue is tied or speech is suddenly blocked due to a temporary cause; he was previously able to speak.
اشارة معهودة معلومة (isharah ma'hudah ma'lumah): Gestures that are habitual and known to people; they are reliable for legal declarations like a will.
