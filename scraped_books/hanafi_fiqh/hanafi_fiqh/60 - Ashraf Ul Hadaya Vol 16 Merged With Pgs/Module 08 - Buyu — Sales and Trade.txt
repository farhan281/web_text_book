Diyyah Liability for a House Before Possession: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diyyah liability in a sale before possession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on diyyah liability for a purchased house before possession, and explain how the differing bases (possession vs. ownership) determine the liable 'aqilah in absolute sales and sales with khiyar.

Core Idea
Liability follows the authority to protect: Abu Hanifa says possession; Sahibayn says ownership.

This lesson explores a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: if a body is found in a house that has been sold but not yet handed over, whose 'aqilah (agnatic group) must pay the diyyah? Abu Hanifa and his two major disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), disagree on the fundamental criterion for liability. Understanding this disagreement clarifies how the Hanafi school approaches protective liability and the role of possession versus ownership.

Guided Path
1
State the unanimous principle
All agree that diyyah liability requires that the person had the authority (wilayah) to protect the premises and neglected that duty. Identify this shared premise.
This principle is stated in the commentary: 'All agree that liability is wajib when a person has authority of protection and he abandons protection.'
2
Identify the point of disagreement
The dispute is over what gives a person the authority to protect: physical possession (qabd) or ownership (milk). Determine each imam's criterion.
Abu Hanifa: possession. Sahibayn: ownership or ultimate ownership in khiyar cases.
3
Apply the criterion to an absolute sale (no khiyar)
For a sale without any option, work out which 'aqilah is liable according to each view. Abu Hanifa: seller's 'aqilah (seller has possession). Sahibayn: buyer's 'aqilah (buyer has ownership).
4
Apply the criterion to a sale with khiyar
When a party has an option (khiyar), ownership is suspended. Abu Hanifa looks at current possession; Sahibayn look at who will be the final owner after the option period.
Example: Seller has Khiyar al-Shart → possession remains with seller, so Abu Hanifa holds seller liable; Sahibayn hold the eventual owner.
5
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn argue that protection authority is derived from ownership, using the analogy of a depositary (mudi') who has possession but is not liable for diyyah. Also they cite sadaqat al-fitr which follows ownership. Understand this reasoning and how Abu Hanifa might respond.
The text states the Sahibayn's proof explicitly.

And the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: If there is no khiyar, then it is on the 'aqilah of the buyer; and if there is khiyar, then it is on the 'aqilah of the one for whom the house becomes (i.e., who ultimately owns it). This is according to Abu Hanifa. And the two (sahibayn) said: If there is no khiyar, then the diyyah is on the 'aqilah of the buyer; and if there is khiyar, then the diyyah is on the 'aqilah of the one for whom the house remains. Explanation: All agree that liability is wajib when a person has authority (wilayah) of protection and he abandons protection. Now the question is: when is protection acquired? There is disagreement. According to Abu Hanifa, the basis of the authority of protection is possession (qabd). According to the sahibayn, if immediate ownership exists, liability is on the owner; if ownership is not immediate, then on whoever ownership is proven later. Now that this principle is understood, listen: Zaid bought a house but had not yet taken possession when a murdered was found in it. On whom is the diyyah wajib? According to Abu Hanifa, since the basis of liability is possession, the diyyah is on the seller's 'aqilah because possession is with the seller. According to the sahibayn, the diyyah is on the buyer's 'aqilah because according to them the basis of liability is ownership, and the buyer has acquired ownership. If one of the parties has taken an option (khiyar), then on whom is liability? According to Abu Hanifa, it is on the one in whose hand the house is at that time. According to the sahibayn, since liability is based on ownership, which is not yet finalized, they said: the diyyah is on the one for whom the house ultimately becomes: if it remains with the seller, then on his 'aqilah; if it goes to the buyer, then on his 'aqilah. The proof of the sahibayn: because liability for murder is based on deficiency in protection, which is only wajib on one who has the authority of protection, and authority is acquired through ownership. For this reason, diyyah is on the 'aqilah of the owner of the house, not on the depositary. And ownership belongs to the buyer before possession in an absolute sale, and in a sale with option, the final ownership is considered, as in sadaqat al-fitr.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about who bears the diyyah liability when a body is found in a house that has been sold but not yet delivered to the buyer. The fundamental difference is the basis of liability: Abu Hanifa considers actual possession (qabd) as the criterion for the authority to protect the premises, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider ownership (milk) as the basis. Accordingly, if there is no option (khiyar) in the sale, Abu Hanifa holds the seller's 'aqilah liable because the seller still has possession; the Sahibayn hold the buyer's 'aqilah liable because ownership has transferred. If there is an option, the liability follows the current possessor according to Abu Hanifa, while according to the Sahibayn it follows the final owner after the option is resolved. The Sahibayn provide proof: liability is linked to deficiency in protection, which requires authority; authority is obtained through ownership, not mere possession, as seen in the case of a depositary (mudi') who has possession but is not liable for diyyah. The commentary explains the principles and applies them to concrete cases.

Key Terms
قبضہ (qabd): Taking physical possession or control of the property.
خیار (khiyar): Option or right to cancel the sale within a specified period.
عاقلہ ('aqilah): The clan or group of agnates responsible for paying diyyah.

Liability During Sale with Option: Possession vs Ownership
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability basis: possession vs ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the basis of liability (ḍamān) in khiyār al-sharṭ and apply Imam Abu Hanifa's rule to determine liability in various scenarios.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, when an option is involved, liability follows possession, not ownership.

This lesson examines a critical intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two distinguished students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The question: when a sold item is destroyed during the option period, who bears the loss? The answer hinges on whether liability is based on ownership or physical possession. We will explore both proofs and understand why the Hanafi school ultimately adopted Imam Abu Hanifa's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
Clearly state the legal question: what is the basis for liability (ḍamān) when a sold item is destroyed during a sale with an option condition? Is it ownership (milk) or possession (yad)?
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; all three imams agree on the general principle of liability but differ on the effective cause.
2
Examine the Sahibayn's position and proof
Understand the proof of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani: they argue that authority for protection (wilāyat al-ḥifẓ) derives from ownership, so liability follows ownership. Support their claim with examples from wadī‘ah (deposit) and ṣadaqat al-fiṭr.
Their proof is based on qiyas (analogy).
3
Analyze Imam Abu Hanifa's proof
Study Imam Abu Hanifa's reasoning: ability to protect comes from physical possession (yad), not ownership. One can protect without ownership (e.g., a borrower) but cannot protect without possession. In a sale with option, the person with effective possession is liable.
He uses a direct observation (al-ayā) as the basis for the effective cause.
4
Apply the rule to specific scenarios
Work through the scenarios given in the text: (a) buyer has possession and option, (b) seller has option but buyer has possession, (c) no possession transferred yet. Determine liability under each view.
Focus on the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
5
Identify the mufta bihi position and its justification
Conclude that the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is Imam Abu Hanifa's: liability is based on possession. Understand why this view is considered stronger (e.g., it gives greater weight to actual control).
This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and is affirmed in later works like al-Hidayah.

Expressions That Do Not Revoke a Will: Haram/Riba and Postponement
Ruling on whether certain testator expressions constitute will revocation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between expressions that constitute revocation of a will and those that do not, applying the Hanafi principle that a description (wasf) implies the existence of the described thing.

Core Idea
Calling a will 'haram' or 'riba' does not revoke it; revocation requires explicit annulment like 'batil' or 'tark'.

When a testator makes a will, certain later words may or may not revoke it. The Hanafi school examines the literal meaning of the expressions used. This lesson unpacks the ruling on describing a will as 'haram' or 'riba', and on postponing or abandoning the will, based on the principle that a description presupposes the continued existence of the described object.

2
Classify Each Expression
Determine whether each phrase is a description (wasf) or a declaration of nullity (batil) or abandonment (tark).
Wasf implies existence; batil/tark imply non-existence.
3
Apply the Legal Principle
For wasf: the description implies the existence of the described thing, so the will remains. For batil and tark: these indicate the will has ceased to exist.
The principle is that a description (wasf) presupposes the subject.
4
Contrast with Exceptions
Note that 'batil' and 'tark' do constitute revocation, while 'haram/riba' and 'ta'khir' do not.
Postponement is like deferring a debt – not cancellation.
5

Summarize which expressions are revocation and which are not.
Revocation: batil, tark. Not revocation: haram/riba, ta'khir.

Deathbed Transactions: Validity and One-Third Limit
Ruling statement with commentary on deathbed transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on deathbed manumission, muhabah, and hibah, and explain why they are limited to one-third of the estate.

Core Idea
A deathbed gift is like a bequest – valid but capped at one-third because the heirs' right has attached.

What happens when a person on their deathbed frees a slave, sells property below value, or makes a gift? Are these acts valid from the entire estate? In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position from Quduri's matn and its commentary. The key insight: these acts are valid but restricted to one-third of the estate, just like bequests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and the ruling
List the three acts covered: manumission ('itq), sale with favor (muhabah), and gift (hibah). The author states they are 'ja'iz' (valid) and 'mu'tabar min al-thulth' (considered from the third).
The variant wording 'huwa wasiyyah' does not change the ruling.
2
Understand the one-third limitation
All three acts are limited to one-third of the estate. They compete with other bequests within that third. This reflects the heirs' right that attaches during deathbed illness.
The limitation applies even though these are immediate (munajjaz) acts, not contingent on death.
3
Distinguish from true bequests
A bequest (wasiyyah) is an obligation after death. These acts are not bequests; they are immediate. But they are treated like bequests for the one-third rule because of the heirs' right.
The variant wording ('it is a bequest') is figurative, not literal.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Suppose a person in marad al-mawt frees a slave worth 1,000 and later dies leaving an estate of 3,000 with no other bequests. The manumission is effective from one-third (1,000), so the slave is free. If there was another bequest, they share proportionally.
The freed slave becomes a legatee of sorts.

Executor's Liability and Recourse in Failed Slave Sale
Ruling on executor's liability and intra-Hanafi dispute on recourse from the estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the views of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the executor's recourse from the estate and apply the mufta bihi ruling (full recourse from entire estate).

Core Idea
An executor's recourse for a lost price after a reclaimed slave is a debt, not a will benefit, so it's paid from the entire estate.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a scenario where a testator wills his slave to be sold for charity. The executor sells the slave, but the price is lost and the slave is reclaimed. The executor must compensate the buyer, but can he recover from the estate? Abu Hanifa changed his position, and he and Muhammad al-Shaybani differ on the basis of the recourse. Understanding this dispute clarifies that the executor's claim is a debt, not a bequest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the liability
Understand the facts: testator wills slave sale, executor sells and loses price, slave is reclaimed. The executor is liable to the buyer because he took his money without delivering the slave.
2
State the initial problem: can the executor recover from the estate?
Since the executor acted on behalf of the testator, he might seek reimbursement. The Hanafi masters have differing views.
3
Examine the dispute: Abu Hanifa's initial and later views
Abu Hanifa first denied recourse because the executor became liable by his own act. Later he allowed recourse from the entire estate, treating it as a debt.
4
Examine Muhammad al-Shaybani's view
Muhammad restricts recourse to one-third of the estate, because he considers it a matter of the will (wasiyyah).
5
Analyze the tarjih and legal basis
The relied-upon view (zahir al-riwayah) is Abu Hanifa's later view: the recourse is based on ghurur (deception/implied guarantee), which creates a debt, and debts are paid from the entire estate.

Commentary
This passage addresses a scenario where a testator wills that his slave be sold and the price given as sadaqah to the poor. The executor sells the slave, takes the price, but the price is lost (e.g., destroyed or stolen) in his possession. Later, the slave is reclaimed (e.g., it turns out the slave belonged to someone else, so the sale is void). The question is: who is liable to the buyer for the price? The ruling states that the executor (as the seller/contractor) is liable to return the price to the buyer because the buyer paid in exchange for the slave, which he did not receive. The executor took the buyer's money without valid delivery, so he must return it. However, since the executor acted on behalf of the testator, he has the right to recover the loss from the testator's estate. This is analogous to an agent (wakil) who incurs a loss in executing the principal's instructions. Regarding the extent of recovery, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the executor can only recover from one-third of the estate because his recourse is based on the will (wasiyyah), and the will can only affect up to one-third of the estate. Abu Hanifa initially held that the executor cannot recover at all because he became liable by his own act of taking possession (qabd). Later, he retracted and held that the executor can recover from the entire estate (all property). The relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah) is that the executor recovers on the basis of ghurur (deception or implied guarantee/tort), and this amount is a debt upon the deceased, which is paid from the entire estate without the one-third restriction. This reflects the principle that debts take precedence over bequests.

Key Terms
استحقاق (istihqaq): Reclamation: a third party proves ownership of the sold slave, invalidating the sale.
ضمان (daman): Liability: the executor becomes liable to compensate the buyer for the lost price because the sale failed.
غرور (ghurur): Deception or implied guarantee; here it means the executor is responsible because he induced the buyer to pay by offering the slave, which turned out not to be deliverable.
رجوع (ruju'): Recourse: the executor's right to claim reimbursement from the estate for the loss he incurred.

Executor's Restriction on Excessive Fraud vs. Ma'dhun's Capacity
Ruling on validity of executor's sale with excessive fraud versus ma'dhun child/slave/mukatab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the legal basis for limiting an executor's transactions with ghban fahish from the capacity of a ma'dhun, and state the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
An executor is bound by the ward's interest; a ma'dhun acts as owner – the difference determines validity of fraudulent sales.

The executor of a minor's estate is a fiduciary, not a free agent. The Hanafi school strictly prohibits the executor from selling at an excessively low price (ghban fahish), allowing only minor discrepancies unavoidable in trade. But what about a minor who has been granted permission to trade (ma'dhun)? The school splits: Abu Hanifa views the ma'dhun as acting by virtue of ownership, fully capable even at a loss; the Sahibayn see excessive fraud as a prohibited donation. This lesson unfolds the reasoning and its practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage compares two types of persons: the executor (wasi) of a minor's estate, and the ma'dhun child, ma'dhun slave, or mukatab. The executor is a representative; the ma'dhun acts for himself.
Executor = niyabah; ma'dhun = ownership.
2
Note the ruling for the executor
The executor's sale and purchase are not valid except within the range of customary taghabun (ghaban yasir). Ghban fahish invalidates the transaction because it harms the ward.
Ruling: la yajuz with ghban fahish.
3
Understand the basis for the executor
The executor acts by legal representation (niyabah shar'iyyah) and must consider the ward's interest. He is restricted to actions that benefit the ward, so he cannot sell at a loss.
Basis: niyabah and consideration (nazar).
4
Apply Abu Hanifa's view on the ma'dhun
According to Abu Hanifa, the ma'dhun child, slave, or mukatab may validly sell with ghban fahish because they act by virtue of ownership (malikiyyah). Permission (idhn) lifts the restriction (hajr), giving them full capacity to dispose of their property.
Key: they act as owners, not representatives.
5
Contrast with the Sahibayn's view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that selling with ghban fahish is a donation (tabarru') without necessity, and these persons are not qualified for donations. Therefore, such sales are invalid.
Dispute: ownership vs. donation analysis.

Emancipation of a Khuntha Slave: Single vs. Dual Declaration
Ruling on khuntha in emancipation oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when a khuntha slave is freed by a general emancipation declaration and apply the muhmal principle to determine inclusion.

Core Idea
A khuntha slave is freed only when the master's declaration covers all possible genders.

This short but powerful ruling from Al-Hidayah addresses a critical nuance in emancipation oaths. When a master declares only male or only female slaves free, a khuntha slave (of ambiguous gender) is not included. But if he declares both, the khuntha is freed because at least one description applies. Understanding this hinges on the concept of muhmal (unspecified).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The master says either 'every male slave is free' or 'every female slave is free' (single declaration), or he says both phrases together (dual declaration).
Focus on the difference in outcome.
2
Apply the khuntha's gender ambiguity
A khuntha is of unknown gender. A single declaration cannot cover him because we lack certainty that he belongs to that gender.
Certainty is required for legal consequences.
3
Understand the term muhmal
Muhmal means 'left unspecified'. A single declaration leaves the khuntha's gender unspecified, so he is not covered. But both declarations together cover all possibilities.
The opposite of muhmal is mubayyan (specified).
4
Distinguish the rule
The khuntha is freed only in the dual declaration because of certainty that one of the two descriptions applies. This is not a contradiction; it is a logical consequence.
This ruling shows the importance of wording in legal declarations.

Permissibility of Buying from Muslim Markets Despite Prohibited Items
Explains permissibility in Muslim markets via abundance and necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principles of kathrat (abundance) and darurat (necessity) to determine the permissibility of transactions in mixed markets, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
When lawful goods dominate, the small amount of unlawful is overlooked due to necessity and hardship.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a practical concern: can Muslims buy from markets where prohibited items are also sold? The author explains that the Hanafi school permits it based on the dominance of lawful goods and the impossibility of avoiding the small unlawful portion. This ruling is contrasted with Imam Shafi'i's stricter analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
State the question: Is it permissible to buy goods from Muslim markets when some items sold are prohibited (haram, stolen, usurped)?
This is a case of mixed lawful and unlawful in the market.
2
State the Hanafi position and its basis
Hanafi: Permissible because permissible goods are abundant and dominant (kathrat/ghalabah) and avoiding the small amount of unlawful is impossible without hardship (darurat).

Imam al-Shafi'i would hold it impermissible by strict analogy (qiyas) because of the presence of unlawful items.
Opposing school: Shafi'i.

5
Determine the practical difference
Thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafis allow buying without investigation; Shafi'is require caution or avoidance.
This ruling removes hardship.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory expansion from the commentary on the chapter of al-Khuntha (hermaphrodite) from Ashraf al-Hidayah. It addresses the permissibility of purchasing goods from Muslim markets despite the known presence of prohibited items such as haram, stolen, or usurped goods. The author summarizes that two factors—abundance (kathrat/ghalabah) and necessity (darurah)—serve as grounds for permissibility. The ruling is that it is permissible to buy from Muslim markets because permissible items are dominant and abundant. This is contrasted with the position of Imam al-Shafi'i, who (by analogy) would not permit it due to the presence of unlawful items. The author draws an analogy with an earlier case of goats where a small amount of dead meat was overlooked after the goat moved (taharruf), allowing permissibility. The reason given is that it is impossible to avoid such small amounts, and requiring avoidance would cause hardship (haraj). This follows the principle that small amounts of impurity or exposure are overlooked. However, if the lawful and unlawful are equal in quantity, or if the unlawful dominates, then permissibility is not granted because neither necessity nor overwhelming abundance exists. Thus, the Hanafi school uses istihsan (juristic preference) based on general prevalence (umum al-balwa) to permit what would otherwise be problematic.

Key Terms
کثرت (kathrat): Abundance or dominance of permissible items in the market.
ضرورت (darurat): Necessity that relaxes the normal prohibitions, either actual or constructive through general prevalence.
غلبہ (ghalabah): Dominance of lawful over unlawful, a condition for permissibility.
قلیل (qalil): A small amount that is excused because it cannot be easily avoided.
حرج (haraj): Hardship or difficulty that triggers leniency in the law.
