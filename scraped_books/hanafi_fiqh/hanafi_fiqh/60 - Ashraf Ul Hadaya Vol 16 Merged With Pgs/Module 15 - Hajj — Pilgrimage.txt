Starting Point for Proxy Hajj: Home City or Place of Death?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on proxy Hajj start point

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the starting point of proxy Hajj when the testator dies en route, explain the proofs of each side, and apply the ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
The intention of the journey determines whether proxy Hajj starts from home or the place of death.

When a person sets out for Hajj, dies on the way, and has willed that Hajj be performed for him, a question arises: should the proxy start from the deceased's home city or from where he died? The Hanafi school has a legitimate internal difference on this point. This lesson explores the reasoning of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and the practical implications of their views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic scenario and the point of disagreement
Read the matn and commentary to understand the case: a person leaves his city intending Hajj, dies en route, and wills proxy Hajj. The dispute is about the starting point.
Note that the journey must be solely for Hajj, not for trade.
2
State the position of Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar
They rule that the proxy must start from the testator's home city. Their proof: the will is to perform Hajj from his city, fulfilling the obligation as it was originally due.
3
State the position of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad)
They rule that the proxy starts from the place where the testator died. Their proof is istihsan: the journey was an act of piety, so part of the distance has been covered as worship, making the testator like a resident of that place.
This is contrasted with a trade journey, where the journey is not pious, so the proxy must start from home.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The difference affects the cost and logistics: starting from the home city means a longer journey, while starting from the death location may reduce expenses and effort.
Both positions lead to a valid proxy Hajj; the dispute is only about the departure point.
5
Consider the fatwa position
The commentator does not explicitly prefer one view, ending with 'Wallahu a'lam'. However, the Sahibayn's view based on istihsan is widely accepted in practice, though both are valid.
If a mufti is asked, he may choose either opinion, but should be consistent with his school's tarjih.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the starting point for proxy Hajj when the testator dies en route to Hajj and has made a will for Hajj. The matn from al-Quduri states the basic ruling: according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Zufar, the proxy Hajj must be performed from the testator's hometown (بلده). According to Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn), it should be performed from the place where the testator died, and this is based on juristic preference (istihsan). The same difference applies if the proxy himself dies during the journey; then a second proxy is sent, and the same disagreement on starting point arises. The commentary then gives the proofs for each side. The Sahibayn argue that since the testator had already begun a journey specifically for Hajj (an act of piety), part of the distance has already been covered as worship, and its reward is with Allah. Therefore, it is as if the deceased become a resident of that place; the proxy should start from there. This is unlike a trade journey, which is not an act of piety. So for a trade journey, the proxy must start from the home city. Imam Abu Hanifa argues that the will itself directs that Hajj be performed from his city, as the obligation was originally from his city. It must be fulfilled in the same manner as it was obligatory. The commentator leaves the issue with 'And Allah knows best', indicating that both views are recognized, though the fatwa position may follow the predominant view.

Key Terms
صاحبین (sahibayn): Refers to Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, the two principal students of Imam Abu Hanifa.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference used by the Sahibayn to depart from the strict analogy (qiyas) in favor of a ruling that considers the pious nature of the journey.
قربة (qurbah): An act of piety or worship that brings one closer to Allah.

Hajj Will for One Who Dies En Route: Location of Performance
Ruling on where to perform Hajj for a deceased who died en route after a will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on where to perform Hajj for a deceased who died en route after a will, and identify the evidence for each position.

Core Idea
The dispute hinges on whether the journey itself is an act of worship or merely a means to the hajj.

A person who sets out for Hajj but dies on the way—if he had made a will for Hajj to be performed on his behalf, from where should the Hajj be performed? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement. Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar hold that it must be from the deceased's hometown, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that it should be from the place where he died. This difference arises from a deeper question: Did the journey itself constitute an act of worship that changes the obligation? Let's unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
A person leaves his hometown intending Hajj, dies en route, and had willed that Hajj be performed on his behalf. The question is: from where must the substitute Hajj be performed?
2
State the two positions
Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar: from the hometown. Sahibayn: from the place of death, by istihsan.
Both positions are recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's evidence
The journey with intention of Hajj is an act of worship (qurbah). The obligation to cover that distance is fulfilled, and reward is secured. Thus, one may start from the death location as if the deceased were a resident there.
They contrast this with a trade journey, which is not a qurbah.
4
Understand Abu Hanifa's evidence
The will is interpreted to mean performance from the hometown, because the original obligation arose there. Performing from anywhere else would be a dissimilar performance.
The text sides with Abu Hanifa (wa lahu).
5
Note the practical consequence
If the death location is far from the hometown, performing from there is cheaper; from hometown may require longer travel and higher cost. The estate is affected accordingly.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the location from which Hajj must be performed on behalf of a person who died en route after making a will for Hajj. The ruling is from the Book of Wills (Kitab al-Wasaya). The base matn (al-Quduri) states the positions: according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Zufar, the Hajj must be performed from the deceased's hometown. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), it should be performed from the place where the deceased died, by way of istihsan (juristic preference). The same disagreement applies if the person was performing Hajj on behalf of another and died en route.

The commentary then presents the evidence (dalil) for each position. The Sahibayn argue that the deceased's journey was an act of worship (qurbah) from the start, so the obligation of traveling that portion is fulfilled and its reward is secured. Therefore, the Hajj should start from that place, as if the deceased were a resident there. This differs from a journey for trade, which is not an act of worship, so the Hajj must be from the hometown. Abu Hanifa's evidence is that the will is understood to mean Hajj from the hometown, as that is how the obligation was originally due. The author of al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani) sides with Abu Hanifa, as indicated by the phrase 'wa lahu' (and for him).

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): A method of juristic preference that allows deviation from an analogical ruling for a stronger reason; here the Sahibayn use istihsan to rule that Hajj be performed from the place of death.
قربة (qurbah): An act of worship that brings one closer to Allah; the pilgrim's journey is considered an act of worship, not a worldly undertaking.

Hajj Badal for a Deceased Who Died En Route: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on Hajj badal location

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa's and Sahibayn's positions on the starting point for Hajj badal and identify the key factor (qurbat vs non-qurbat) that determines the ruling.

Core Idea
The intention and completion of the Hajj journey determine residency for Hajj badal.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about where to perform Hajj on behalf of a deceased who died after beginning a journey for Hajj. The disagreement hinges on whether the incomplete journey counts as an act of worship (qurbat). Understanding this dispute clarifies how intent and religious merit affect the execution of a will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core question
What exactly is the dispute about? The starting point for Hajj badal when the deceased died on the way to Hajj, not in his original city.
The dispute is within the Hanafi school between Abu Hanifa and his two main students.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa: Hajj must be performed from the deceased's original city. His proof: the journey was not qurbat because Hajj was not completed; it is like a trade journey.
His reasoning is based on qiyas (analogy) with a trade journey.
3
State Sahibayn's position and proof
Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): Hajj must be performed from the place of death. Their proof: the completed portion of the journey is an act of worship (qurbat), so the deceased is considered a resident of that place.
Their reasoning is based on istihsan (juristic preference) considering the religious merit achieved.
4
Apply the ruling to the agreed trade journey scenario
Both agree: if the journey was purely for trade, Hajj must be performed from the original city, because the journey had no religious value.
This consensus highlights the pivotal role of qurbat.

The context is a will (wasiyyah) where the deceased instructed that Hajj be performed on his behalf. The key question is: should the Hajj be performed from the deceased's original city (where he resided before the journey) or from the point where he died (having started the journey)?

Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the Hajj must be performed from the deceased's original city. His reasoning is that since the deceased died before completing Hajj, his journey is not considered a valid commencement of Hajj (it was not an act of worship that is counted). Therefore, it is as if he never left, and the will should be executed from his original city, just as if he were at home. This is analogous to the case of a merchant who dies on a trade journey—his journey has no religious merit, so Hajj is performed from his hometown. Abu Hanifa's approach is based on qiyas (analogy) and the principle that the will is executed according to what was originally required.

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) disagree. They argue that when the deceased set out with the intention of Hajj, the portion of the journey he completed is an act of worship (qurbat). That portion has already fulfilled part of his obligation, and its reward is attained. Therefore, the Hajj should be performed from the point where he died, as if he were a resident of that place. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) and consideration of the actual benefit achieved.

Both sides agree that if the journey was purely for trade (not for Hajj), the Hajj must be from the original city, as no religious benefit occurred. The passage ends with 'Wallahu a'lam' (Allah knows best), indicating that both views are transmitted without a definitive preference stated here.

وصیت (wasiyyah): A will or testament directing the disposition of one's property or performance of certain acts after death.
حج بدل (hajj badal): Hajj performed on behalf of a deceased person who was obligated to perform Hajj but died before doing so.
قربت (qurbat): An act of worship or piety that brings one closer to Allah.

Imam Muhammad rules that a khuntha mushkil in ihram wears women's clothing because the harm of a woman being uncovered outweighs the harm of a man wearing sewn garments.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a khuntha mushkil who enters ihram while still a minor (murahiq). Since ihram rules differ sharply between men and women—men wear unsewn wraps, women wear sewn clothes—the ambiguous gender creates a dilemma. Guided Path

State the scenario
