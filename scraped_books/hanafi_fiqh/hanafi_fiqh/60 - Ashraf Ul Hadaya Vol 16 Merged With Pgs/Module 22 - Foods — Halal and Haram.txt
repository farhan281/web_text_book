Taharrī for Mixed Slaughtered and Dead Goats: Optional and Necessary Contexts
Stating the ruling on taharrī for mixed slaughtered and dead goats in optional and necessary conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating from a mixed batch of slaughtered and dead goats, distinguish between optional and necessary contexts, and apply the conditions for taharrī.

Core Idea
In doubt, taharrī guides to the lawful; necessity makes even the forbidden permissible temporarily.

What happens when a pile of goats contains both halal slaughtered and haram dead ones, and you cannot tell which is which? Imam Muhammad gives a clear ruling based on majority and taharrī. But when hunger threatens your life, the rules shift. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act is eating from a mixed batch of slaughtered (halal) and dead (haram) goats. Determine whether the situation is one of optionality (non-necessity) or necessity (life-threatening hunger).
The ruling differs fundamentally between the two contexts.
2
Apply the taharrī rule in optional context
If the situation is non-necessity, check the majority. If slaughtered goats are more than dead ones, you may perform taharrī (reasoning to identify the likely slaughtered) and eat those indicated. If dead are more or equal, no taharrī is allowed and none may be eaten.
Taharrī is a method of ijtihad based on signs or preponderance.
3
Apply the necessity exception
In a genuine necessity (e.g., starvation risking death), all goats become permissible to eat regardless of numbers or taharrī, because what is certainly forbidden (dead) is allowed in necessity, so the probable permissible is even more so.
Necessity is strictly defined: must be life-threatening.
4
Note the recommendation of taharrī even in necessity
Even in necessity, if one has the ability to perform taharrī (e.g., time, light), it is recommended to do so because taharrī may lead to certainty of eating lawful meat. However, if the severity of hunger prevents it, the obligation of taharrī drops.
The text says: 'he will perform taharrī because it is a way that leads him to the slaughtered in some sense, so he will not leave it without necessity.'

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on mixed slaughtered and dead goats (or sheep) when it is impossible to distinguish them. It is based on Imam Muhammad's text in al-Jami' al-Saghir. The ruling distinguishes between two conditions: optional (non-necessity) and necessity (darūra). In an optional situation, if the slaughtered ones are in the majority, one may perform taharrī (independent reasoning to determine which are likely slaughtered) and eat those that one's taharrī indicates are slaughtered. If the dead ones are in the majority or the numbers are equal, then taharrī is not permitted and none may be eaten. In a state of necessity (e.g., extreme hunger where one would die without eating), all become permissible to eat regardless of the numbers. However, even in necessity, if one has the ability and time to perform taharrī, it is recommended to do so because it may lead to certainty of eating lawful meat. The ruling is based on the principle that what is certainly forbidden becomes permissible in necessity, so the probable permissible is even more so. The text is an expansion of the matn, explaining the rationale.

Key Terms
تحري (taharrī): An endeavor or reasoning to determine the most likely permissible object when there is doubt, using signs or majority.
ضرورة (darūra): A state of necessity that permits otherwise prohibited acts, such as eating carrion to prevent death.
مذبوحة (madhbūḥa): Slaughtered according to Islamic law; halal meat.

Mixed Slaughtered and Dead Goats: Ghalabah and the Shafi'i Dispute
Ruling on eating mixed slaughtered/dead goats with taharri, necessity, and Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on eating mixed slaughtered and dead goats, identify the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate the Hanafi response based on ghalabah and removal of hardship.

Core Idea
When halal and haram mix, the majority (ghalabah) determines permissibility, like Muslim markets.

This lesson examines a classic inter-madhhab dispute: when slaughtered and dead goats are mixed indistinguishably. The Hanafi school permits eating after performing taharri (effort to identify the slaughtered) if the slaughtered are in the majority, even in normal conditions. The Shafi'i school restricts this to dire necessity. We will unpack the proofs, focusing on the Hanafi principle of ghalabah and the analogy to Muslim markets, and see how the ruling changes in necessity.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling as given in the text: when slaughtered are more, taharri then eating is allowed; when dead are more or equal, not allowed. Note the exception of necessity where all are permissible.
The ruling applies in normal (ikhtiyar) conditions.
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that taharri is a 'necessary proof' used only in necessity, and since there is no necessity here, eating is not permitted even if slaughtered are majority.
The objection focuses on the nature of taharri as a last resort.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school replies that ghalabah (preponderance) is like necessity because avoiding a small amount of haram is impossible, causing hardship. They cite the example of Muslim markets where some haram goods exist but eating is allowed based on the majority.
This is an istihsan based on removal of hardship.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis eat after taharri in normal conditions when slaughtered are majority; Shafi'is do not. In necessity, both allow absolutely.
This directly affects daily life in mixed meat markets.

Commentary
This passage presents a complete inter-madhhab dispute from Ashraf al-Hidayah, an Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah. The issue is when slaughtered and dead goats are mixed indistinguishably. The passage begins with the ruling from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir: if the majority are slaughtered, one performs taharri (attempt to identify based on signs or guesswork) and may eat; if the dead are majority or equal, one does not eat. This applies in normal (ikhtiyar) conditions. In necessity (idtirar), all are permissible because even a known dead animal is halal in necessity. Taharri is still recommended if possible. Then Imam al-Shafi'i's objection is presented: in normal conditions, even if slaughtered are majority, taharri is a forced proof used only in necessity, so eating is not allowed. The Hanafi response follows: ghalabah (preponderance) is like necessity because avoiding a small amount of haram is impossible, causing hardship (haraj). The analogy is made to Muslim markets where some haram goods exist but eating is permitted based on the majority being halal. Thus, the Hanafi position relies on istihsan based on removal of hardship. The ruling differs: Hanafis allow with taharri if majority are slaughtered; Shafi'is do not allow at all in normal conditions.

Key Terms
تحرّي (taharrī): Effort to identify the permissible (slaughtered) animal through signs or guesswork.
ضرورة (ḍarūrah): Life-threatening necessity that permits normally prohibited acts.
غلبة (ghalabah): Preponderance or majority of halal over haram in a mixed set.
