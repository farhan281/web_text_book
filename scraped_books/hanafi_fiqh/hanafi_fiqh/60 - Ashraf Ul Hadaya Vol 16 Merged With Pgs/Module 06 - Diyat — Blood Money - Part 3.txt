Diyah on Bayt al-Mal When Killer Has No 'Aqila
Ruling on diyah liability when killer lacks 'aqila

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Sahibayn's position on diyah liability when the killer lacks an 'aqila, explain the qiyas used, and distinguish the relied-upon view from the shadhdh narration.

Core Idea
When the killer has no 'aqila, the Muslim community (Bayt al-Mal) assumes the diyah.

In Hanafi fiqh, the 'aqila (agnatic relatives) typically bear the diyah for accidental killing to ease the burden on the killer. But what happens when the killer has no 'aqila? The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule that the diyah falls on Bayt al-Mal—the public treasury. They draw an analogy with inheritance: just as a Muslim with no heir leaves his wealth to Bayt al-Mal, so too the liability of a killer with no 'aqila is taken up by the community. Abu Hanifa has a rare (shadhdh) view that the killer remains personally liable, but the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of the Sahibayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core case and ruling
The passage presents the case of a killer with no 'aqila. The Sahibayn rule that the diyah wajib on Bayt al-Mal. Identify this as the mufta bihi position.
The ruling is said to be from 'our companions' i.e., Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
2
Understand the reasoning: community as helper
The proof is that the Muslim community (jamā‘ah al-muslimīn) are the helpers (ahl nuṣratihi) of every Muslim, and no one is more entitled than another. This establishes collective responsibility.
This is a general principle of mutual support in Islam.
3
Trace the qiyas (analogy)
The Sahibayn draw an analogy: just as the inheritance of a Muslim with no heir goes to Bayt al-Mal, so the liability (gharāmah) of a killer with no 'aqila is borne by Bayt al-Mal. The illah (effective cause) is that the community is the supporter.
Asl: Muslim dies heirless. Hukm asl: inheritance to Bayt al-Mal. Far': killer with no 'aqila. Hukm far': diyah on Bayt al-Mal.
4
Note the shadhdh narration from Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa is reported to hold that the diyah is on the killer's own wealth. His reasoning: the basic principle is that the killer is personally liable, and the 'aqila's liability is a concession (takhfīf); when no 'aqila, the original principle returns.
This narration is explicitly called 'shādhah' (rare/weak) in the text, so it is not the operative view.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) for fatwa is that of the Sahibayn: diyah on Bayt al-Mal. The shadhdh narration does not affect the operative ruling.
This is confirmed by the text's emphasis on the weakness of the opposing narration.

Diyah for Son of Mula'anah: Reclaim After Father's Acknowledgment
Ruling on diyah for son of li'an and reclama after father's acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on diyah for a son of mula'anah and apply the rule of reclama when the father later acknowledges paternity.

Core Idea
Father's later acknowledgment shifts diyah liability to his 'aqila, who reimburse the mother's 'aqila.

This lesson addresses a unique case in Hanafi fiqh: the diyah (blood money) for a child born through li'an (mutual imprecation). Initially, the child's lineage is only from the mother, so her 'aqila is liable. However, if the father later retracts his denial and acknowledges the child, the li'an is invalidated retroactively, making the father's 'aqila ultimately responsible. Understanding this ruling ensures justice between the two families and clarifies the temporal aspect of payment.

Guided Path
1
Identify initial liability
Understand that after li'an, the child's nasab is from the mother only, so the mother's 'aqila bears the diyah if the child causes harm.
Li'an severs inheritance and diyah obligations from the father.
2
Understand the effect of ikhdhab
When the father retracts his denial (ikdhāb), the li'an is nullified, and the child's lineage is established from the father from the beginning.
Ikhdhab means the father acknowledges paternity.
3
Determine the consequence for diyah
Since the child was always from the father, the father's 'aqila should have paid the diyah. Hence, the mother's 'aqila can reclaim what they paid.
The principle: 'water returns to its source' (al-ma'u ya'udu ila aslihi).
4
Identify the time frame for repayment
The father's 'aqila must reimburse over three years from the date of the judge's ruling in favor of the mother's 'aqila.
This allows the father's 'aqila to pay in installments.

Diyah for Son of Mukatib: Shift of Wala and 'Aqila Liability
Ruling on diyah for son of mukatib when kitabah is paid after mother's 'aqila pays

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the conditions under which diyah liability shifts from the mother's 'aqila to the father's 'aqila when a mukatib's kitabah is paid after the initial diyah payment, and identify the key role of wala.

Core Idea
Payment of kitabah retroactively establishes freedom, shifting wala and diyah liability to the father's 'aqila.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in Hanafi diyah law concerning the son of a mukatib. When the son commits a crime and the mother's 'aqila pays the diyah, but later the father's kitabah is paid, a retroactive shift in wala occurs. Understanding this shift is crucial for correctly determining liability and avoiding erroneous fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and sequence
Identify the mukatib (father), his free son, the son's free mother, and the respective 'aqila groups. Note the order: crime committed → mother's 'aqila pays diyah → later, kitabah is paid from the mukatib's estate.
The mother was a freed slave, so her 'aqila includes her former master's tribe.
2
Understand wala and its relation to 'aqila
Recall that wala (patronage) follows the free parent. Since the father is a slave at the time of the crime, the son's wala is with the mother's side. The 'aqila responsible for diyah is the group that holds the wala.
In Hanafi law, wala belongs to the manumitter or the free father.
3
Recognize the retroactive effect of kitabah payment
When the mukatib dies leaving enough wealth to pay the kitabah, his estate is liable. The payment of kitabah is deemed to occur at the moment of death, retroactively establishing his freedom from that point.
This is a specific Hanafi rule for mukatib.
4
Apply the wala shift
Once the father's freedom is established retroactively, the son's wala shifts from the mother's side to the father's side from the time of the father's death. This is because wala follows the free parent, and the father is now considered free at death.
The shift is based on qiyas to the case of ibn al-mula'anah.
5
Determine the outcome
Since the wala shifted, the diyah liability shifts to the father's 'aqila. The mother's 'aqila can reclaim the amount they paid from the father's 'aqila.
The father's 'aqila are his 'asabah (agnatic heirs).

Recovery of Diya After Mukatib's Kitaba: Retroactive Wala

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When kitaba is paid, wala transfers retroactively, making the father's aqila liable for the son's diya, not the mother's.

This lesson examines a nuanced case from the chapter of 'Aqila. A mukatib dies leaving enough property to pay his kitaba, but before payment, his free son commits a homicide and the mother's aqila pays the diya. Later, the kitaba is paid from the estate. The Hanafi ruling holds that the mother's aqila can recover the diya from the father's aqila because the son's wala transfers retroactively to the father's side upon payment of the kitaba. This ruling illustrates a key principle about the nature of wala and how it affects liability for blood-money.

The son is free because his mother is free; the father's status as a mukatib does not affect the son's freedom.

The son commits a crime (jīnāya). The mother's aqila pays the diya. Subsequently, the mukatib's estate pays the kitaba. The key is that the kitaba payment occurs after the diya payment.

Upon payment of the kitaba, the mukatib is deemed free from the moment of his death (because the kitaba is paid from his estate). Therefore, his son's wala (clientage) transfers from the mother's side to the father's aqila retroactively, as if the father had been free at the time of the crime.

The recovery is from the father's aqila, not from the mukatib's estate or the son directly.

Liability for a crime does not automatically follow changes in the killer's status; it depends on the nature of the change and timing of judgment.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents a foundational principle (aṣl) that governs the transfer of liability for diya from one 'āqila to another. As for the claim of the child of li'an, the crime transfers to another 'aqila, whether the qadi's verdict has been issued or not. And if the condition of the killer did not change but the 'aqila changed, then the time of the verdict is considered. So if the diyah was already decreed against the first 'aqila, then it does not transfer to the second 'aqila. And if the diyah was not decreed against the first 'aqila, then the diyah is decreed against the second 'aqila. And when the 'aqila is one (i.e., unchanged), then whatever increase or decrease occurs, all are included in the ruling of the crime before and after the verdict, except with respect to the amount that has already been paid. So whoever has strengthened this principle with deliberation, then he will be able to derive the ruling for the analogous and opposite cases that arise. Explanation: From here, the author (Imam Muhammad) has stated some scattered particulars and issues. However, if the general rule (qa'idah kulliyyah) here is committed to mind, then understanding these issues and their analogues will be easy. So the author stated four principles: 1. If the condition of the killer changes (i.e., his ruling changes, like in the case of wala' of a child), then the wala' of that child transfers to the father, but the crime does not transfer because the principle is that in case of change of the killer's status, the crime remains with the first 'aqila, whether the qadi has decreed the diyah or not. Hence, if a person's father is a slave and his mother is free, and he commits a crime, then the mother's 'aqila pays the diyah. Then if the father is emancipated, the wala' transfers to the father but the crime does not transfer. And in the case of mukatib (a slave with a contract of manumission), the crime transfers because at the time of death he was free (i.e., before the crime occurred). 2. If the hidden status of the criminal becomes apparent, then the crime does not stay with the first 'aqila but transfers to the second 'aqila, whether the qadi has decreed or not. Like the case of the child of li'an, where there is an emergence of a hidden status, so the crime transfers to the community of the person who conducted li'an. 3. If the condition of the criminal does not change but there is a change (of 'aqila), then the time of the verdict is considered. If the qadi has not yet decreed the diyah against the first, then it is wajib upon the second. If he has decreed against the first, then it is fixed and only the first is liable, as in the issue of the people of Kufa and Basra that passed. 4. If there is no change in the 'aqila but there is an increase or decrease (e.g., one of them becomes adult or dies), then the ruling is that after the qadi's verdict and before it, all are included in the ruling. However, the amount that has been paid is excluded. So what has been paid is done; no one else will share in it. Then the author said: This is a subtle point; whoever ponders over it and commits it to mind will be able to derive the rulings for analogous and opposite cases that arise.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents four foundational principles (usul) governing the transfer of liability for diyah (blood money) among the 'aqila (the group responsible for paying diyah on behalf of a killer). The commentator, Allamah Ashraf al-Din, is explaining a section from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's original text. The core insight is that liability shifts or stays based on whether the killer's legal status changes, whether a hidden status becomes apparent, whether the 'aqila itself changes before a verdict, or whether the 'aqila merely changes in composition through increase or decrease of its members. The first principle: if the killer's status changes (e.g., a child's wala' shifts due to father's emancipation), the crime liability remains on the original 'aqila—it does not transfer. Example: a boy has a slave father and free mother; crime liability is on mother's 'aqila. If father later becomes free, wala' transfers to father but diyah remains on mother's 'aqila. The second principle: if a hidden status becomes manifest (e.g., a child born from li'an later proves lineage to the father), liability transfers to the new 'aqila regardless of whether a qadi had already ruled. The third principle: if the killer's status is unchanged but the 'aqila changes (e.g., he moves from one city to another), the timing of the qadi's verdict determines liability. If a verdict has been given against the first 'aqila, they remain liable; if not, the new 'aqila is liable. The fourth principle: if the same 'aqila merely changes in size (a child reaches adulthood, or a member dies), all present members share equally in paying the diyah, except that amounts already paid by specific individuals are not redistributed. The author emphasizes that mastering these four principles enables the jurist to derive rulings for all analogous cases that may arise.

Key Terms
عاقلہ ('āqilah): The group of male agnates (asabah) who collectively pay diyah (blood money) for unintentional homicide or injuries committed by a member of their group.
دیت (diyah): The financial compensation (blood money) paid by the 'aqila of a killer to the heirs of the victim in cases of unintentional homicide or certain injuries.
لاعن (mulā'in): A husband who performs li'an (mutual imprecation) against his wife accusing her of adultery, resulting in the negation of the child's lineage from him.
