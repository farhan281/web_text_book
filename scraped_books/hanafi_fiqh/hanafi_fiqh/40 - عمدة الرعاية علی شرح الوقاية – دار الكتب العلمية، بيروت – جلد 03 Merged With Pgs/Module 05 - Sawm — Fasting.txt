Effect of Qada' and Nadhr Fasts on Khalwah Confirming Mahr
Discusses whether qada' and nadhr fasts and prayer prevent khalwah from confirming mahr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether khalwah during a qada' or nadhr fast confirms the full mahr, and explain the basis of the two riwayat within the school.

Core Idea
Khalwah during qada' or nadhr fast does not prevent confirmation of full mahr.

In Hanafi marriage law, certain conditions may prevent khalwah from confirming the full mahr. Among these are five definite preventers: illness preventing intercourse, fasting Ramadan, ihram, menstruation, and postpartum bleeding. But what about making up missed fasts (qada') or vowed fasts (nadhr)? This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains the disagreement within the Hanafi school and identifies the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five definite preventers
Recall the five factors that definitely prevent khalwah from confirming mahr: illness preventing intercourse, Ramadan fast, ihram, hayd, nifas.
2
Recognize the two riwayat on qada' and nadhr fasts
There are two transmitted reports: one says they prevent (like Ramadan fast), the other says they do not (like supererogatory fast).
3
Understand the reasoning behind each view
The preventing view analogizes qada' to Ramadan because both are obligatory and breakable only with sin. The non-preventing view differentiates because breaking qada' only requires making up one day (no kaffarah), so the prohibition is weaker.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
Later authorities (e.g., al-Khaniyah) state the most correct (asahh) is non-prevention, and this is the operative ruling for fatwa.
5
Extend the analogy to prayer
The text equates prayer to fasting. Although al-Bahr argues that all prayers should absolutely prevent because invalidating any prayer is unlawful, the matn implies the same two-reports scenario applies.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah addresses the effect of a spouse's fasting or praying on the validity of khalwah in confirming the mahr. The matn states: 'Or a person fasting a qada' fast (in the most correct view) and a vowed fast (in one narration), and with one of the five previously mentioned – no. And prayer is like fasting, whether fard or nafl.' The commentator explains there are two reports (riwayat) regarding whether fasting qada' and vowed fasts prevent khalwah from confirming the full mahr. According to al-Khaniyah, the most correct position (al-asahh) is that they do not prevent, because the obligation of qada' fast is similar to supererogatory fast in that breaking it only requires making up one day, which is minor. In the other report, they do prevent, analogizing qada' to the performance (ada') of Ramadan fast. The relied-upon view is non-prevention. For prayer, the commentator from al-Bahr argues that invalidating any prayer without excuse is unlawful, so it should absolutely prevent khalwah, regardless of whether it is fard or nafl. However, the text of the matn equates prayer to fasting, implying the same two-reports scenario. The five factors that definitely prevent khalwah from confirming mahr are: illness preventing intercourse, Ramadan fast, ihram, hayd (menstruation), and nifas (postpartum bleeding).

Key Terms
قضاء (qada'): Making up a missed obligatory fast.
نذر (nadhr): A vowed fast that becomes obligatory upon making the vow.
رواية (riwayah): A transmitted report or narration from the founding imams regarding a ruling.

Key Takeaway
Within the Hanafi school, khalwah during a qada' or nadhr fast does not prevent full mahr, as these fasts are not among the explicit legal preventers.

Fasting as Expiation for Zihar: Consecutiveness and Prohibited Days
Ruling on two-month consecutive fast conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the two-month expiatory fast for zihar and apply the rule of consecutiveness (wala') to determine when a restart is required.

Core Idea
A single break — even by accident — nullifies the consecutiveness of expiatory fasts.

When a person performing zihar cannot free a slave, the alternative is fasting two consecutive months. However, not any two months suffice: they must be free of Ramadan and the five prohibited days of Eid and Tashriq. Moreover, the consecutiveness is strict: any break, even from illness or an obligatory fast, forces a restart.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its context
Recognize that this passage addresses the second alternative in zihar expiation: fasting when freeing a slave is impossible.
The ruling is from Quran 58:3-4.
2
State the core conditions
The fast must be for two consecutive lunar months, excluding Ramadan and the five prohibited days.
Consecutive means no break of a full day.
3
Analyze the requirement of consecutiveness (wala')
Understand what constitutes a break: intentionally or unintentionally breaking the fast for a full day (from dawn to sunset) nullifies the sequence.
A break of less than a full day (e.g., a few hours) does not break consecutiveness.
4
Identify the five prohibited days
List them: Eid al-Fitr (1 Shawwal), Eid al-Adha (10 Dhul-Hijjah), and the three days of Tashriq (11-13 Dhul-Hijjah). Fasting on these is haram, so they cannot be counted.
If one of these days occurs during the two months, the fast is invalid and must be restarted.
5
Examine the case of Ramadan entering
Ramadan is obligatory and cannot be used for expiation. If Ramadan enters during the expiatory fast, one must break for Ramadan and restart the two months after Eid.
The sequence is broken even if one does not intend to break.
6
Apply to edge cases
Illness, travel, or fasting on prohibited days inadvertently all require restart. The text emphasizes that even unintentional breaks destroy consecutiveness.
The only exception is if the break is less than a full day (e.g., breaking a few hours before sunset).

Commentary
This passage states the ruling for the fasting alternative in zihar expiation when one cannot free a slave. The conditions are: (1) The fast must be for two months consecutively (mutatabi'ayn), meaning no break of a full day or more; breaking for less than a full day (e.g., a few hours) does not break consecutiveness. (2) The months must not include Ramadan, because fasting Ramadan is obligatory and cannot be used for expiation, nor can one fast voluntary days during Ramadan. (3) The five prohibited days for fasting are: the day of Eid al-Fitr (1 Shawwal), the day of Eid al-Adha (10 Dhul-Hijjah), and the three days of Tashriq (11, 12, 13 Dhul-Hijjah). Fasting on these days is haram, so they cannot be counted. If any of these days occur during the two-month period, the sequence is broken and one must restart. The commentary clarifies that 'consecutively' means no break of twenty-nine days (the maximum number of days between two lunar months). If one fasts without lunar months (e.g., by calendar) and breaks after fifty-nine days, that does not count because the intention of two months requires lunar months. Also, if Ramadan enters during the fast, one must stop because Ramadan is obligatory, and one cannot combine it with expiation fast; thus the sequence is broken and restart is required. The Fatawa Hindiyyah adds that even an unintentional break due to illness or travel requires restart; similarly, if one fasts on prohibited days, those days do not count and the fast is invalid, requiring restart.

Key Terms
ولاء (wala'): Consecutiveness; fasting two months without any break, even for a single day.
الأيام الخمسة المنهي صومها (al-ayyam al-khamsah al-manhi sawmuha): The five days when fasting is prohibited: Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the three days of Tashriq.
شهر رمضان (shahr ramadan): The month of Ramadan, which is obligatory fasting; it cannot be included in the two-month expiation fast.

Key Takeaway
