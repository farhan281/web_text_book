Options of the Silent Partner in Partial Emancipation and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains the choices of a co-owner when a partner frees his share in a slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three options for the silent partner according to Abu Hanifa, explain the disagreement of the Sahibayn, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Emancipation is divisible: the silent partner retains ownership in his share and may free it.

When a co-owner frees his share of a jointly-owned slave, the silent partner is not left without recourse. The Hanafi school presents a range of options, but the Imams differ on which ones are available. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya expands on the matn and highlights the intra-madhhab dispute, grounding each position in either the hadith or the principle of divisible emancipation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the silent partner's options
The passage explains that when one co-owner frees his share of a jointly-owned slave, the other partner (silent partner) has several legal options. List the three main options mentioned.
Options: free his own share, demand istis‘ā’ (slave earns the value), demand surety (from the wealthy freeing partner).
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Abu Hanifa includes the option to free his own share; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) exclude this option. Identify which options are contested.
The contested option is freeing the silent partner's own share.
3
Trace the proofs
The Sahibayn rely on the hadith 'Whoever frees his share... his manumission is from his wealth... if not, the slave earns.' They interpret it as exhaustive. Abu Hanifa relies on the principle that emancipation is divisible (yatjazza’u) – the silent partner's ownership remains, so he may free his portion.
Understand how each dalil leads to the respective ruling.
4
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf
What practical difference does it make whether the silent partner can free his share? How does walā' (patronage) distribute under each view?
Under Abu Hanifa, if silent partner frees, walā' is shared; under Sahibayn, walā' goes entirely to the freeing partner if he pays surety.

(And the one who frees if he makes him surety, and he may recourse upon the slave, and both said: He has the right of surety from the wealthy freeing partner). Essentially, if the slave's share is freed without compensation, and the second share is freed with compensation, which is the earning money; then if the slave pays the amount of the other's share value, he becomes free upon the freeing partner, so his patronage is for him; if he chooses surety and takes from the freeing partner the amount of his share, the patronage all goes to the freeing partner because he owned the other's share by paying the surety, so the emancipation became fully upon him. This is all according to Abu Hanifa. [1] His saying: "if he makes him surety"; it may be from 'tadmin' (making surety), so the implicit raised pronoun refers to the other, and the explicit attached pronoun refers to the freeing partner, meaning: if the other takes from the freeing partner a guarantee of his share and makes him a guarantor. And it may be from 'daman' (surety) as a past tense verb; then the raised pronoun refers to the freeing partner and the explicit to the other, meaning: if the freeing partner gives the surety and pays it to his partner. [2] His saying: "and he may recourse upon the slave"; means in the case of surety, the freeing partner who paid the surety can recourse upon the slave for the amount he paid because the freeing partner has taken the place of the silent partner by paying the surety to him; and the silent partner could have demanded earning from him for the value of his share; so likewise that is for the one who takes his place; he may demand earning and take from him the amount he paid. This is contrary to the case if the slave earns for the silent partner when he chooses demanding earning, as the slave does not recourse upon the freeing partner for anything because he earns for the release of his own neck. Thus in 'al-Hidayah' and its commentaries. [3] His saying: "and both said... etc."; their statement is supported by the hadith: 'Whoever frees a share of his in a slave, his manumission is from his wealth if he has wealth; if he has no wealth, the slave is made to earn.' Narrated by the Imams of the Sunnah. It indicates that if the freeing partner is wealthy, then his silent partner has the right of surety; if poor, he has only the right of demanding earning from the slave, not otherwise. So he does not have the right of demanding earning when the freeing partner is wealthy, nor the right of surety when he is poor. Abu Hanifa agreed with them regarding not having surety when he is poor, but he gave the other partner a choice between demanding earning and freeing his share, based on his principle that emancipation is divisible, so the freeing partner's freeing his share does not make the entire slave free; thus the other may free his share because his ownership remains in it and slavery remains in the slave.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the options available to a co-owner (sharīk) when one of their jointly-owned slaves is partially emancipated. The matn states that the silent partner can either demand the slave work for his share (istis‘ā’), or demand that the freeing partner (mu‘tiq) provide surety (ḍamān) for the value of the freed share if he is wealthy, but not if poor. In both cases, the clientage (walā’) goes to both if the silent partner also frees or demands work. The commentary expands: The silent partner is given a choice among three options: (1) free his share immediately or conditionally, (2) demand the slave earn the value of his share, or (3) demand surety from the wealthy freeing partner. Additional options include kitāba (contractual manumission) or ṣulḥ (settlement) with the freeing partner or slave, and tadbīr (postponed manumission), but then earning is due immediately. If the master dies, earning drops if it came from the third category. The silent partner cannot leave the status quo. Then the commentary discusses the meaning of wealth: it is yasār al-taysīr (ability to pay), not outright wealth, and it is assessed at the time of emancipation. The text then moves to a dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that when the freeing partner gives surety, he may recourse upon the slave for the amount paid, because he stands in the shoes of the silent partner who could have demanded work. The two companions (Sahibayn – Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ: they limit the silent partner to only surety (if the freeing partner is wealthy) or istis‘ā’ (if poor), relying on the hadith from Bukhari and Muslim. Abu Hanifa agrees on no surety if poor, but adds the option for the silent partner to free his share, based on his principle that emancipation is divisible (yatjazza’u) – thus the silent partner’s ownership remains, so he may free his portion. This is the core difference: under Abu Hanifa, the silent partner has three options; under Sahibayn, only two (surety or istis‘ā’). The text concludes by stating Abu Hanifa’s view as the presented position.

Key Terms
استسعاء (istis‘ā’): The act of the silent partner demanding that the slave work for his share's value, i.e., the slave earns money to pay the share.
تضمين (taḍmīn): Making the freeing partner a guarantor (ḍāmin) for the value of the silent partner's share, to be paid if wealthy.
ولاء (walā’): The legal relationship of patronage and inheritance between the manumitter and the freed slave.
شقص (shiqṣ): A share or portion of a jointly-owned slave.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, the silent partner whose co-owner frees his share has three options: free his own share, demand istis‘ā’, or demand surety; the Sahibayn limit to the latter two.
