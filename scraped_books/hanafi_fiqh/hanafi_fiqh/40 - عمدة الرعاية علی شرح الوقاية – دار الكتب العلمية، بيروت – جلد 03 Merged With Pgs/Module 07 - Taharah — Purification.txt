Breastfeeding by a Co-wife: Legal Consequences and Proof
Ruling on breastfeeding by co-wife and its legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a wife breastfeeds her co-wife, determine the mahr consequences for both women, and identify the required testimony for proving breastfeeding.

Core Idea
Breastfeeding a co-wife makes both women haram to the husband and triggers specific mahr rules with possible recovery.

This passage presents a nuanced scenario in Hanafi fiqh: a man has two wives, one adult and one infant. When the adult breastfeeds the infant, the marriage becomes invalid for both. The ruling also details financial rights and the conditions for the husband to recover the mahr he paid. Finally, it specifies the evidentiary standard for proving such breastfeeding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the basic ruling
Note that the case involves two wives, one adult (kabīrah) and one infant (radīʿah). The adult breastfeeds the infant within the breastfeeding period. The immediate legal effect: both wives become ḥarām to the husband.
The relationship created by radāʿ causes legal impediments (maḥramiyyah).
2
Work out the mahr consequences
For the adult wife: if the marriage was not consummated, she gets no mahr because the separation (furqa) occurred before consummation. For the infant wife: she receives half the mahr because the separation is not due to any fault on her part.
This follows general rules of mahr when separation occurs before consummation.
3
Analyse the husband's right of recourse
The husband may recover the mahr he paid to the infant wife from the adult (breastfeeding) wife if she intentionally caused the corruption (qasada al-fasād). Intent is defined: she was an adult, sane, not coerced, aware of the marriage and that breastfeeding ruins it, and did not act out of necessity (e.g. saving a life).
If she acted without such intent (e.g. to satisfy hunger), the husband cannot recover.
4
Determine the proof required for breastfeeding
The text states that the proof of breastfeeding is the same as for financial claims: two male witnesses, or one man and two women. A single woman's testimony is insufficient, even if she is the one who breastfed and even if the testimony is given before or after the marriage.
This is a Hanafi position; other schools may differ on the minimum testimony for radāʿ.
5
Summarise the legal consequences
Bring together: (1) Both wives become ḥarām, (2) Adult wife gets no mahr, infant gets half, (3) Husband may recover from the adult if she intended corruption, (4) Breastfeeding is proven by the standard financial testimony (two men or one man and two women).

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on a specific scenario: a man has two wives, one adult and one infant (رضيعة). The adult wife breastfeeds the infant wife during the period of breastfeeding. The ruling states that both women become haram to the husband. The reasoning is that the infant girl becomes his daughter (if the milk is from him) or the daughter of his wife with whom he has consummated marriage, and the adult woman becomes the mother of his daughter or the mother of his betrothed. Regarding financial consequences: the adult wife receives no mahr if she has not been consummated, because the separation (فرقة) occurred before consummation. The infant wife receives half the mahr because separation occurred before consummation and not due to any fault of hers. The husband may sue the breastfeeding wife for the mahr he paid to the infant wife if the breastfeeding wife intentionally caused the corruption of the marriage—meaning she was an adult, sane, not coerced, aware of the marriage and that breastfeeding would cause corruption, and did not do it to satisfy hunger or save a life. If she had no such intent, he cannot recover. The proof (حجة) for the breastfeeding itself is the same as for financial claims: the testimony of two men or one man and two women. The testimony of a single woman, even if she is the one who breastfed, is not sufficient, whether given before or after the marriage.

Key Terms
رضاع (radāʿ): Breastfeeding a child by a woman other than the mother, which can create legal relationships of mahram.
ضرَّة (ḍarra): Co-wife, a woman sharing the same husband.
مهر (mahr): The bridal gift given by the husband to the wife, obligatory in marriage.
فساد (fasād): Intentional corruption or harm caused by an act, here referring to the breastfeeding wife's deliberate act to ruin the marriage.
شهادة (shahāda): Testimony, legal evidence. For breastfeeding, the required evidence is the same as for financial claims: two men or one man and two women.

Tayammum for Completing Idda and Forgotten Limb in Ghusl
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tayammum and ruling on forgotten limb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether tayammum alone completes idda and apply the distinction between forgetting a full limb vs a part in ghusl.

Core Idea
Tayammum's completeness in idda: Sahibayn require a prayer, Muhammad argues tayammum is absolute purification.

This passage from the commentary on al-Hidayah presents two nuanced sub-rulings related to a woman's idda when water is unavailable. First, if she performs tayammum instead of ghusl, does that automatically end her waiting period? The Hanafi school itself splits on this. Second, if she performs ghusl but forgets a body part, the validity depends on whether she forgot a whole limb or just a small area. Let's examine the reasoning behind these distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal issues
The passage covers two distinct rulings: (1) the effect of tayammum on completing the idda period, and (2) the validity of ghusl when a part is forgotten. Note that they are connected by the context of a woman in idda who cannot use water.
The first issue is a pure intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the second is a settled Hanafi position with a clear rationale.
2
Analyse the dispute on tayammum
The matn states the woman performs tayammum and prays. The commentary clarifies that this is the view of the Sahibayn (Muhammad and Abu Yusuf). According to Imam Muhammad alone, the idda is completed by tayammum without prayer. Identify the reasoning: Muhammad considers tayammum an absolute purification when water is unavailable; the Sahibayn see it as a mere concession valid only for the specific act of prayer.
3
Evaluate the evidenciary basis
Muhammad's argument: tayammum in the absence of water establishes the same rulings as ghusl, so it alone ends the idda. The Sahibayn argue: tayammum is inherently impure and only becomes effective as a purification when used for an obligatory act like prayer. Which principle do you find stronger?
This is a rational (qiyas) dispute about the nature of tayammum.
4
Apply the forgotten limb ruling
The second ruling: if a woman forgets to wash a full limb (e.g., arm) in ghusl, the ghusl is invalid and the idda continues, allowing raj'a. But if she forgets less than a limb (e.g., finger), the ghusl is valid. The rationale: small parts dry quickly, so one cannot be certain water did not reach; a full limb retains moisture longer, so certainty of omission exists. This distinction is defended against those who would treat both equally.
This shows Hanafi attention to empirical certainty (yaqin) in purification rules.

Commentary
This passage addresses two sub-rulings related to the completion of idda. First, if a woman has no access to water, she may perform tayammum. The matn states that if she performs tayammum and prays, the idda is considered complete. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the tayammum only confirms the end of idda if she actually performs a prayer with it, because tayammum is of lower status than ghusl and only becomes effective when used for an obligatory act. However, according to Imam Muhammad alone, the idda is completed by the mere act of tayammum, without the condition of prayer, because tayammum in the absence of water is a full purification equal to ghusl in its effect. The second sub-ruling: if the woman performs ghusl but forgets to wash a complete limb (e.g., an arm), the ghusl is invalid and hence the idda is not complete, so the husband may still perform raj'a. However, if she forgets to wash less than a full limb (e.g., a finger), the ghusl is considered valid because such small areas dry quickly, so it is not certain that water did not reach. The commentary defends this distinction from those who might claim both should be treated the same.

الاغتسال (al-ghusl): The ritual full-body wash required after menstruation, janabah, etc.
العضو (al-'udw): A complete limb (e.g., arm, leg) as opposed to a part of it (e.g., finger).

Ruling on Forgetting to Wash a Limb in Ghusl for 'Iddah
Commentary on tayammum and ghusl 'iddah rulings with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether tayammum alone completes 'iddah and distinguish the ruling on forgetting a limb versus less than a limb in ghusl.

Core Idea
Forgetting a whole limb invalidates ghusl, but less than a limb does not—and tayammum's purification power is disputed among Hanafi masters.

This passage from a sharh on Al-Hidayah delves into two interconnected rulings concerning a woman in her 'iddah. First, it addresses the validity of the husband's revocation (ruj'ah) when the wife forgets to wash part of her body in her ghusl. Second, it introduces an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Muhammad and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding whether tayammum alone is sufficient to complete the 'iddah or requires the actual performance of prayer. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying Hanafi law in cases of divorce and purification.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on forgetting a limb
Identify the ruling from the text: if a woman forgets to wash a complete limb, the ghusl is invalid, so the 'iddah is not considered finished, and the husband may revoke the divorce. If she forgets less than a limb, the ghusl is valid, the 'iddah ends, and revocation is not possible.
The key distinction is based on certainty: less than a limb dries quickly, so water probably reached it; a whole limb is certain to have been missed.
2
Identify the dispute on tayammum
Note that the text mentions two positions: Imam Muhammad holds that tayammum alone (without prayer) completes the 'iddah, while the Sahibayn require that the woman actually prays with the tayammum for it to count.
Both views are from major authorities within the Hanafi school.
3
Follow the Sahibayn's proof
Understand their argument: tayammum is inherently impure (mutanajjis) and only considered purification out of necessity, which is realized only in the act of prayer. Therefore, tayammum alone does not establish the same rulings as ghusl for non-prayer purposes like completing 'iddah.
They rely on the limited scope of tayammum as a substitute.
4
Analyze Imam Muhammad's response
Imam Muhammad argues that tayammum in the absence of water is an absolute purification (ṭahārah muṭlaqah), just like ghusl, so it lifts janabah completely. Hence, the 'iddah is completed immediately upon performing tayammum.
He views necessity as fully enabling the same rulings.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Imam Muhammad's view, the husband can revoke immediately after the wife’s tayammum (even if she hasn't prayed); under the Sahibayn, he must wait until she actually prays with that tayammum. The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi, so both are recognized.
Later Hanafi works may lean toward the Sahibayn as a precaution.

The Mother's Right to Custody and the Female Custody Hierarchy
Ruling with proof on mother's custody and female hierarchy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the mother's primary right to custody, explain the hadith proof, and list the order of female custodians after the mother.

Core Idea
The biological mother has the strongest right to custody; after her, custody goes through maternal relatives.

Custody (ḥaḍānah) is a fundamental right of the mother, rooted in the Prophet's ﷺ hadith. This lesson explores the mother's entitlement, the condition that she not be forced against her will, and the detailed hierarchy of female relatives who inherit this right after her. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for applying Hanafi family law in cases of divorce or separation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its conditions
Read the opening statement: 'Custody belongs to the mother without coercion, whether divorced or not.' Note that the mother is the primary custodian but cannot be compelled to accept custody unless no other suitable custodian exists.
The term 'biological mother' excludes foster mothers.
2
Locate the dalil and its wajh al-dalalah
The author cites the hadith: 'You have more right to him as long as you do not marry.' Analyze how this proves the mother's precedence: the Prophet ﷺ explicitly gives her right over the father, conditional upon not marrying a stranger.
The hadith is aḥad (zannī al-thubūt) but accepted in fiqh.
3
Trace the hierarchy of female custodians
After the mother, custody passes to: (1) maternal grandmother (even ascending), (2) paternal grandmother, (3) full sister, (4) maternal half-sister, (5) paternal half-sister, (6) maternal aunt. Understand the principle: maternal relatives are preferred because the mother's compassion is strongest.
The order is based on the strength of blood ties through women.
4
Note disqualifying factors
The passage lists factors that forfeit custody: apostasy, immorality (adultery, singing, theft, wailing), unreliability (leaving the child often), or being a slave when the child is free. These remove the custodian from the hierarchy.
Marrying a stranger also forfeits custody until divorce from that stranger.

Categories of Words for Emancipation: Sarih, Mulhaq, and Kinayah
Defines sarih, mulhaq bi al-sarih, and kinayah for emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sarih, mulhaq bi al-sarih, and kinayah in Hanafi emancipation law, and state the ruling for the word 'mawla'.

Core Idea
Emancipation words: sarih (no intention), mulhaq (no intention), kinayah (intention needed).

This passage from 'Umda al-Ri'ayah breaks down the three categories of words used to effect emancipation in Hanafi fiqh. The main focus is on the word 'mawla' – is it explicit (sarih) or something else? Understanding these distinctions is crucial to know when a master's utterance legally frees a slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage defines sarih (explicit), mulhaq bi al-sarih (annexed to explicit), and kinayah (metonymy). Note the criterion: whether intention (niyya) is required.
Sarih does not need intention; mulhaq is like sarih in not needing intention but is not originally placed for emancipation; kinayah requires intention.
2
Recognize examples of each category
Sarih: 'anta hurr', 'anta mu'taq', etc. Mulhaq: 'anta mawlay', 'wahabtu laka nafsaka'. Kinayah: 'anta sayyidi', 'anta ma'bud'.
The passage lists several examples for sarih and mulhaq.
3
Analyze the word 'mawla'
Though polysemous (over 20 meanings), when addressed to a slave, only 'emancipator' fits, so it is treated as mulhaq bi al-sarih and does not require intention.
This is the dominant Hanafi view, but al-Uthqani considered it kinayah needing intention.
4
Connect to the Shia dispute
The passage uses the same polysemy to refute the Shia argument that 'mawla' in the hadith means caliph. Without a contextual indication (qarina), it cannot mean caliph.
This is an example of how lexical analysis impacts theological claims.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some Hanafi scholars (e.g., al-Uthqani) hold that 'mawla' requires intention because of its ambiguity, but the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that it is mulhaq.
The text records this disagreement and reinforces the majority view.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umda al-Ri'ayah is a commentary expanding upon the categories of words used to effect emancipation (itq) in Hanafi fiqh. The author begins by clarifying the classification: there are three types—sarih (explicit), kinayah (metonymy), and mulhaq bi al-sarih (annexed to explicit). The sarih words do not require intention (niyya); they effect emancipation regardless of what the speaker intended, as long as the literal meaning is clear. Examples include 'anta hurr', 'mu'taq', 'atiq', and derivatives. The mulhaq bi al-sarih also does not require intention, but it is not originally placed for emancipation; it is annexed due to common usage. Then the passage focuses on the word 'mawla' (patron/master). The text states that 'mawla' is mulhaq bi al-sarih, not sarih, because it is polysemous. The author explains that if a master says to his slave 'anta mawlay' or 'ya mawlay', emancipation occurs without intention because in the context of a slave, only the meaning of 'emancipator' fits. However, this view is challenged by the fact that 'mawla' has many meanings (helper, friend, etc.). The author then uses this to refute the Shia argument for 'Ali's caliphate based on the hadith 'Man kuntu mawlahu...'—arguing that without a qarina, the word cannot be taken as 'leader'. The passage concludes with a note that some scholars like al-Uthqani hold that 'mawla' requires intention, emphasizing its ambiguity.

Key Terms
صريح (sarih): A word that is placed for the meaning of emancipation and does not require intention to effect it.
كناية (kinayah): A word that requires intention (niyya) to effect emancipation, e.g., 'anta ma'bud' etc.
ملحق بالصريح (mulhaq bi al-sarih): A word that is not originally sarih but is treated like sarih because it does not require intention, like 'mawla' and 'wahabtu laka nafsaka'.
مولى (mawla): A polysemous word. In the context of emancipation, when used by a master to his slave, it means 'emancipator' and is treated as mulhaq bi al-sarih.

The Grammatical Phenomenon of Al-Jar bil Jiwari in Legal Texts
Explains the grammatical phenomenon of al-jar bil jiwari as a response to a potential objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of al-jar bil jiwari in Arabic texts and explain its significance in Hanafi legal interpretation.

Core Idea
Al-jar bil jiwari allows a word to take a genitive case due to proximity, affecting how Quranic verses are parsed and rulings derived.

The commentator addresses a grammatical objection: why is 'mahram' majrur (genitive) when it is an adjective of 'dhi rahim' which is mansub (accusative)? He explains this as 'al-jar bil jiwari' (case by adjacency), a known phenomenon in Arabic. This is illustrated with two Quranic examples: 'wa arjulakum' in the verse of wudu and 'yawmin' in a verse about punishment. Understanding this rule helps Hanafi scholars interpret ambiguous grammatical constructions to derive precise legal meanings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical issue
The reader might question why 'mahram' is in genitive case (majrur) when it describes 'dhi rahim' which is accusative (mansub). Normally, adjectives agree in case with the noun they modify.
The objector assumes 'mahram' is an adjective of 'dhi rahim'.
2
Understand the rebuttal: al-jar bil jiwari
The commentator explains that the jar (genitive) here is due to adjacency, not grammatical agreement. 'Mahram' takes the case of the preceding word because of close proximity and metaphorical connection.
This phenomenon is called 'al-jar bil jiwari' or 'jar ala al-jiwar'.
3
Analyze the first Quranic example: Q5:6
In the verse of wudu, 'wa arjulakum' (and your feet) is recited with kasra (genitive) in some Qira'at, even though 'arjul' is a conjunction of 'wujuhakum' (faces) which is nasb (accusative). The genitive reading is attributed to adjacency with 'ru'usikum' (heads) which is majrur.
This shows the jar bil jiwari is recognized in the Quran.
4
Analyze the second Quranic example: Q40:33
In the phrase 'yawmin' (a day) in 'I fear for you the punishment of a Day', 'yawmin' is majrur even though it is an adjective of 'adhab' (punishment) which is added to it, and should be mansub in that construction. The jar is due to adjacency with a preceding majrur word.
This further supports the permissibility of jar bil jiwari.
5
Apply the concept to the text at hand
Therefore, 'mahram' being majrur is not an error; it is a valid example of al-jar bil jiwari, and the meaning is clear: it refers to the mahram relative.
This grammatical analysis is part of the Hanafi method for clarifying legal terms.

Classification of Guarantees in Tadbir and Emancipation
Explaining the classification of guarantee in partial emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi classification of guarantee for tadbir (commutative) and itq (tort) and explain why each is classified as such.

Core Idea
The guarantee of tadbir is commutative because the slave was transferable; the guarantee of itq is tort because the slave was already non-transferable.

In this passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, the commentator explains the two types of guarantee that arise when one co-owner makes a slave mudabbar and another emancipates his share. The key distinction depends on whether the slave was transferable at the time of the act. This classification determines the legal consequences for compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acts: tadbir and itq
Tadbir is the act of making a slave mudabbar (deferred emancipation); itq is immediate emancipation. The passage examines the guarantee arising from each act when performed by different co-owners.
These are two distinct legal acts affecting the same slave.
2
Determine transferability at the time of each act
At the time of tadbir, the slave was fully owned and transferable (qinn). At the time of itq, the slave was already mudabbar and thus non-transferable. This difference is the key to classifying the guarantee.
Non-transferability is a result of tadbir.
3
Classify the guarantee based on transferability
Since the mudabbar's act (tadbir) occurred when the slave was transferable, his guarantee is commutative (daman al-mu'awada) — he must compensate as if acquiring ownership. The emancipator's act occurred when the slave was non-transferable, so his guarantee is a tort (daman al-jinaya) — he compensates for damage without acquiring ownership.
Commutative guarantee is the default; tort only when commutative is impossible.
4
Analyze the dispute with al-Hidayah regarding status at emancipation
Al-Hidayah states that at the moment of emancipation the slave is considered a mukatab or free based on the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. The commentator corrects this, arguing that the status free/mukatab is only after emancipation, not at the moment. Therefore, the slave remains mudabbar at the time of itq.
This correction ensures the guarantee classification remains consistent.
5
State the resulting ruling
The mudabbar can claim from the emancipator one-third of the slave's value as mudabbar (since the emancipator destroyed the mudabbar's share). The guarantee of the mudabbar is commutative; the guarantee of the emancipator is tort.
The amount of compensation is derived from the value of the slave as mudabbar.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on emancipation discusses the types of guarantee (ضمان) arising when one of several co-owners makes a slave mudabbar (deferred emancipation effective after the owner's death) and another co-owner emancipates his share immediately. The key legal point is the classification of the guarantee. The guarantee of the mudabbar (ضمان التدبير) is considered a commutative guarantee (ضمان المعاوضة) because at the time of tadbir, the slave was transferable from one ownership to another. The guarantee of the emancipator (ضمان العتق) is a tort guarantee (ضمان جناية) because at the moment of emancipation, the slave was already mudabbar and therefore not transferable. The principle is that commutative guarantee is the default, and deviation to tort guarantee only occurs when commutative is impossible. The mudabbar can claim from the emancipator one-third of the slave's value as a mudabbar, since the emancipator's act of emancipation destroyed the mudabbar's share. The value of a mudabbar slave is two-thirds of his value as an unrestricted slave (qinn), because the benefit of sale is lost through tadbir, while the benefits of intercourse and service remain. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the basis: in al-Hidayah it says the slave at the time of emancipation is considered a mukatab or free depending on the two principles (Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), but the commentator corrects this, stating that the status changes only after emancipation, not at the moment. Another dispute exists over the exact value: the preferred view is two-thirds, but a second view holds it is half. The passage includes a concrete example with three partners and a slave worth 27 dirhams to illustrate the calculation: the silent partner receives 9 dirhams from the mudabbar, and the mudabbar receives 6 dirhams from the emancipator.

Key Terms
ضمان المعاوضة (daman al-mu'awada): A guarantee arising from a commutative contract or transaction where the subject is transferable, making the guarantor liable to compensate with the intent of acquiring ownership.
ضمان الجناية (daman al-jinaya): A guarantee arising from a tort or wrongful act that causes loss, without an exchange or transfer of ownership.
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whose emancipation is deferred until after the owner's death, making him non-transferable during the owner's lifetime.
قن (qinn): An unrestricted slave fully owned, with all benefits (including sale, use, and proceeds) available to the owner.

Valuation of the Mudabbar Slave: Two-Thirds vs Half
Explaining the valuation of a mudabbar slave and the intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the value of a mudabbar slave as two-thirds of the qinn value and explain the two opinions within the Hanafi school regarding this valuation.

Core Idea
The value of a mudabbar slave is two-thirds of a qinn, because the benefit of sale is lost while service and intercourse remain.

This part of the passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah delves into the valuation of a mudabbar slave for the purpose of compensation. The author explains the reasoning behind the two-thirds valuation based on the benefits of the slave, and also presents an alternative half valuation. The intra-Hanafi dispute is crucial for understanding which valuation is applied in practice.

Guided Path
1
List the three benefits of slave ownership
The passage identifies three benefits: intercourse (wat'), service (istikhdam), and sale (bay'). Intercourse applies only to female slaves; the other two apply to both genders.
Some authorities substitute sale with 'profit through sale' and add 'payment of debts after death'.
2
Determine which benefits are lost through tadbir
Tadbir makes the slave non-transferable, thus the benefit of sale is completely lost. The benefits of intercourse and service remain because the owner can still use the slave in those ways.
The loss of sale reduces the value by one-third, leading to a two-thirds value.
3
Explain the two-thirds valuation
Since one of three benefits is lost, the value becomes two-thirds of the original qinn value. This is the most correct view, supported by al-Kafi and others.
For a female slave, the calculation is simpler; for a male, the better view uses only two benefits (service and profit), leading to the same result.
4
Explain the alternative half valuation
The second opinion holds that the value is half because tadbir removes the benefit of the physical self (sale), but the benefit of the substitute (hire/rent) remains. Thus, only one of two main benefits is lost.
This view is also reported and some say the fatwa is upon it.
5
Apply the valuation to a concrete example
Using the example: a slave worth 27 dirhams as qinn. Under two-thirds, mudabbar value = 18 dirhams; under half, = 13.5 dirhams. The compensation the mudabbar receives from the emancipator is one-third of this mudabbar value (i.e., 6 dirhams under two-thirds).
The example also illustrates the distribution among three partners.
