Degrees of Firash and Christian's Umm Walad
Classifies firash and rules on Christian's umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three degrees of firash and explain why a Christian's umm walad who converts to Islam must perform sa'y.

Core Idea
Firash has three levels; each level determines how lineage is established or denied. A Muslim umm walad of a Christian master must perform sa'y to gain freedom.

In this passage, the author defines three types of matrimonial bed (firash) based on the woman's status: weak (slave), medium (umm walad), and strong (free married). He then applies these principles to a specific case: a Christian's umm walad who embraces Islam. Let's examine the legal reasoning and the resulting obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three degrees of firash
List weak, medium, and strong, and note the woman's status for each: slave, umm walad, free married.
Weak: slave; child's lineage needs master's claim. Medium: umm walad; lineage established but can be denied. Strong: free married; lineage established, requires li'an to deny.
2
Understand how lineage is established for each
For weak, only by claim. For medium, automatically but denied by denial. For strong, automatically and cannot be denied without li'an.
Li'an is the mutual imprecation when husband accuses wife of adultery.
3
Examine the Christian's umm walad case
When a Christian's umm walad becomes Muslim, she cannot remain a slave to a disbeliever. She is made a mukataba and must work (sa'y) to pay her value.
This preserves the master's property right while respecting the Muslim woman's dignity.
4
Analyze the reasoning behind sa'y
The Hanafi school imposes sa'y as a form of kitabah to reconcile the rights of the Muslim woman and the Christian master. Abu Yusuf disagreed, holding immediate freedom.
The relied-upon position is sa'y due to the hadith and to protect the master's right.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof that classifies the concept of 'firash' (the matrimonial bed) into three levels—weak, medium, and strong—based on the type of relationship between the man and the woman. The weak firash is that of a slave-woman (concubine): her child's lineage is not established unless the master explicitly claims the child. Upon claiming, she becomes an umm walad (mother of the master's child), which is the medium firash. In the medium firash (umm walad), lineage is established automatically without a claim, but the master can negate it by denying paternity. The strong firash is that of a married free woman (manakiha): lineage is established automatically, and denial requires li'an (mutual imprecation in court). Then the passage addresses the case of a Christian's umm walad who embraces Islam: she must perform sa'y (work to pay her value to the master) and becomes free upon payment. The underlying reasoning is that a Muslim woman cannot remain in the ownership of a disbeliever due to the ignominy, so she is freed via kitabah (contract of manumission). The ruling is from the Hanafi school, based on the principle of removing hardship from the Muslim and considering the rights of the master.

Key Terms
الفراش (al-firash): The matrimonial bed; the marital relationship based on which lineage is established.
أم ولد (umm walad): A slave-woman who has borne a child to her master and the master has acknowledged the child.
مدبرة (mudabbara): A slave promised freedom upon the master's death.
سعاية (sa'y): Working to earn and pay the value to the master as part of a kitabah contract.

Ruling on Umm Walad of a Dhimmi Who Becomes Muslim
Commentary on the ruling of umm walad of a dhimmi who converts to Islam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when the umm walad of a dhimmi becomes Muslim, explain the reasoning of istihsan, and apply the concept of sa'ayah to a similar scenario.

Core Idea
A dhimmi's umm walad who becomes Muslim must perform sa'ayah to earn her freedom, balancing removal of humiliation with master's compensation.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary addresses a unique situation: a non-Muslim's slave-concubine (umm walad) accepts Islam. The ruling balances the obligation to free a Muslim from the humiliation of being owned by a non-Muslim with the master's right to compensation. The solution is sa'ayah, a form of compulsory kitabah, which ensures justice for both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their statuses
Who is the master (dhimmi) and who is the umm walad? What triggers the ruling? The umm walad becomes Muslim while her master remains non-Muslim.
Note that the ruling applies to any dhimmi, not just Christians, though the text mentions Christian as an example.
2
Determine the sequence of the ruling
First, the master is invited to Islam. If he accepts, nothing changes. If he refuses, the umm walad must perform sa'ayah.
Sa'ayah means she works to earn her market value and pays it to the master.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The ruling is based on istihsan: it is wajib to remove humiliation (dhull) from a Muslim woman owned by a non-Muslim. Sale is not possible because an umm walad cannot be sold, so emancipation is the only option. But to avoid harming the master, she compensates him through sa'ayah.
The Hanafi scholars consider both sides: the humiliation removal and the master's property rights.
4
Recognize exceptional features
If she fails to complete sa'ayah, she is not returned to slavery. If the master dies before full payment, she becomes free without paying because she is an umm walad.
This shows the ruling favors her freedom while protecting the master's compensation.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn 'وأم ولد النصراني إذا أسلمت تسعى'. The commentator first clarifies a related point about paternity negation for children of an umm walad versus a married woman, noting that for an umm walad, the master's denial negates lineage without li'an, unlike a married woman. He then distinguishes four types of marital beds (firash) in terms of strength. The main topic begins: the ruling applies generally to any dhimmi's umm walad who accepts Islam. The master is invited to Islam. If he converts, she remains his umm walad unchanged. If he refuses, she must perform sa'ayah – i.e., work to earn her value – and upon payment becomes free. This sa'ayah is considered a debt on her. The reasoning is istihsan (juristic preference): it is obligatory to remove the humiliation (dhull) of a Muslim woman being owned by a non-Muslim. Since she cannot be sold (as umm walad is unsellable), emancipation is the only option, but it would harm the master by taking his property without compensation. Therefore, the solution is to make her like a mukatabah (a slave under a contract of manumission), so she becomes free in hand (hand freedom) while the master gets his property's value. The commentator notes that if she fails to pay, she is not returned to slavery – the obligation remains. If the master dies before full payment, she becomes free without payment because she is an umm walad. The passage ends by addressing an objection: according to Abu Hanifah, the umm walad is not considered a commodity (ghayr mutaqawwimah), so how can she be valued? The response, referenced from al-Hidayah and al-Binayah, is that the dhimmi master considers her valuable according to his own belief, and the ruling is based on that.

Paternity Claim for a Jointly Owned Slave-Girl
Ruling on paternity claim for a jointly owned slave-girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paternity claim for a jointly owned slave-girl and calculate the compensation due to the co-owner.

Core Idea
Claiming paternity from a jointly owned slave-girl establishes lineage but requires compensation of half value and half 'uqr.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Hidayah: when one co-owner claims paternity of a child born to a jointly owned slave-girl, several legal consequences follow. The child's lineage is established, the slave-girl becomes an umm walad, and the claimant must compensate his partner. We will break down the conditions, the compensation amounts, and an exceptional case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal effect
The claim of paternity by a co-owner of a jointly owned slave-girl results in three effects: (1) the child's lineage is established from the claimant, (2) the slave-girl becomes his umm walad, (3) he must pay compensation to the other co-owner.
The act is the claim (da'wa) – a verbal assertion that triggers these consequences.
2
Determine the conditions for the claim to be effective
The slave-girl must be jointly owned by two or more persons, and the child must be born during the joint ownership. The claimant must be one of the owners.
If the slave-girl is solely owned by the claimant, the ruling differs (not covered here).
3
Calculate the compensation due
The claimant must pay two components: (a) half the market value of the slave-girl (to compensate the co-owner for their share), and (b) half the 'uqr (dower equivalent) for the intercourse that produced the child. He does NOT pay the value of the child.
'Uqr is the compensation for sexual intercourse with a slave-girl, analogous to mahr for a free woman.
4
Apply the exception when the co-owner is the claimant's son
If the other co-owner is the claimant's son, the 'uqr is waived; only the value of the slave-girl is due. This is because of the special relationship and rules governing intercourse with a son's slave-girl.
This exception is derived from al-Hidayah and highlights how kinship modifies obligations.

Compensation for Istīlād of a Jointly-Owned Slave-Girl
Explains the ruling and rational proof for a shared umm walad case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on istīlād of a shared slave-girl and explain the rationale based on the indivisibility of lineage.

Core Idea
Half the value and half the 'aqr are due, not the child's value, because paternity requires full ownership at conception.

This passage from the commentary on al-Wiqaya addresses a specific legal scenario: a man causes istīlād (motherhood) of a slave-girl that he jointly owns with another. The base text rules that he must pay half her value and half her 'aqr, but not the child's value. The commentary unpacks the reasoning, grounding it in the necessity of establishing paternity and the indivisibility of lineage. By studying this, you will see how the Hanafi school uses rational proofs (dalīl 'aqlī) to derive compensation rules in cases of shared property and unlawful intercourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is istīlād? What exactly does the base text rule in the shared slave-girl case?
The base ruling: he must pay half her value and half her 'aqr, not the child's value.
2
Understand the premise of partial ownership
At the time of intercourse, the man owns only half the slave-girl. Lineage is established in that half.
Coincidentally his property (half).
3
Apply the principle of indivisibility of lineage
Lineage cannot be partial – a child cannot be from two fathers. Therefore, to fully establish paternity, the man must also own the other half at conception.
Ownership of partner's half becomes necessary at the moment of conception (al-‘ulūq).
4
Determine the compensation owed
He must pay half the value to acquire the partner's share, and half the 'aqr because his intercourse occurred partly in the partner's property (since ownership was not yet complete at the moment of intercourse).
The child's value is not separate because the child is part of the mother's value.
5
Contrast with the case of the father and his son's slave-girl
In that case, the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father' is interpreted metaphorically to permit use, so the father becomes owner just before intercourse, making intercourse lawful and thus no 'aqr due.
This contrast highlights that the key factor is ownership at the time of intercourse.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a ruling from the base text (al-Wiqaya) regarding the case where a man causes a jointly-owned slave-girl to become an umm walad (i.e., she gives birth to his child). The ruling states: he must pay half her value and half her 'aqr (compensation for intercourse), but not the value of the child. The author explains the reasoning: lineage is established in the half he owns, and due to the indivisibility of lineage, he must also own the other half to fully establish paternity. Therefore, he becomes owner of the partner's half at the moment of conception, and must compensate by paying half the value. Additionally, because his intercourse occurred partly in his partner's property (since ownership was not yet complete at the time of intercourse), he must pay half the 'aqr, which is the compensation for unlawful intercourse. The child's value is not owed separately because the child is considered part of the mother's value; once he owns the mother, he owns the child. The commentator then contrasts this with the case of a father having intercourse with his son's slave-girl. Based on the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father,' the father is allowed to use his son's property, and this includes intercourse with the son's slave-girl. But for the intercourse to be lawful, the father must own the slave-girl before intercourse. The hadith is interpreted not literally (as transferring ownership), but as permitting use, so the father becomes owner just before intercourse, thus no 'aqr is due. This contrasts with the shared slave-girl case where no such permission exists, so 'aqr is obligatory. The passage also notes that the obligation of payment does not depend on the man's wealth, because it is compensation for a forbidden act. The assessment of value and 'aqr is at the time of conception, as clarified in al-Fath.

Key Terms
استيلاد (istīlād): Causing a female slave to become an umm walad (mother of a child) by having intercourse and acknowledging the child.
عقر ('aqr): Compensation for unlawful intercourse, assessed as the dower (mahr) of a woman of similar status.
علوق ('ulūq): Conception; the moment when the sperm meets the egg and pregnancy begins.

Ruling on Co-owner's Intercourse: Haram with 'Uqr vs Father's Lawful Intercourse
Ruling with proof: co-owner's intercourse vs father's intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on intercourse with a jointly owned slave woman, explain why it differs from a father's intercourse with his son's slave girl, and apply the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father' to determine when 'uqr is due.

Core Idea
A co-owner's intercourse is haram and requires 'uqr due to partial ownership of another; the father's is halal and no 'uqr due to a proof permitting benefit.

In classical Hanafi fiqh, the permissibility of intercourse with a slave woman depends on ownership. This lesson examines two contrasting cases: a co-owner and a father. The source passage uses a hadith and careful reasoning to show why one is forbidden with compensation and the other is lawful without. Understanding this distinction reveals how Hanafi jurists interpret proof texts and apply the principle of ownership at the moment of conception.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two scenarios
Identify the two legal situations: (1) a co-owner of a jointly owned slave woman has intercourse with her; (2) a father has intercourse with his son's slave girl. State the ruling for each.
The passage contrasts these to highlight the different outcomes.
2
Analyze the hadith proof
Examine the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father' (ant wa maluka li-abik). Understand why it is not taken literally—it cannot mean the father owns his son outright. The Hanafi interpretation is that it permits beneficial use when needed.
This is a classic example of majaz (metaphorical interpretation) to avoid absurdity.
3
Understand the timing of ownership
Note that ownership for the purpose of lineage (istilad) is established at the moment of conception (al-'uluq), not at the start of intercourse. This timing determines whether intercourse occurred in the property of another or in one's own property.
In the father's case, ownership is established just before intercourse; in the co-owner's case, only after conception.
4
Derive the rulings on 'uqr and child value
Apply the timing principle: For the father, intercourse is lawful, so no 'uqr is due and no hadd. For the co-owner, intercourse is forbidden (haram), so 'uqr is obligatory, but no value for the child because the child is conceived free and belongs to the father (the claimant).
The child is free because lineage is attributed to the father who claims.
5
Address the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on number of claimants
Recognize that when both co-owners claim the child together, there is disagreement: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow any number; Imam Muhammad limits to three; Zufar limits to five. The passage records this but does not prefer one view.
This is a minor ikhtilaf within the school; the thamarah (practical effect) is only relevant when many co-owners exist.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umda al-Ri'aya is a commentary expanding on the ruling about co-ownership of a slave woman and the implications of intercourse and paternity claims. The passage type is a 'ruling with proof' as it uses a hadith and reasoning to establish the law.

The text begins by contrasting the case of a father having intercourse with his son's slave girl with the case of a co-owner having intercourse with a jointly owned slave girl. For the father, the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father' is interpreted metaphorically: the father does not become absolute owner, but rather is permitted to benefit from his son's property when needed. This includes intercourse with the son's slave girl, and to make it lawful, the father becomes owner just before intercourse. Hence, no compensation ('uqr) is due. In contrast, when a co-owner has intercourse with the jointly owned slave girl, there is no such evidence to make it lawful. The intercourse occurs in property partly belonging to another (the other co-owner), so it is forbidden, and compensation is obligatory.

The text further explains that ownership of the child for the purpose of establishing lineage (istilad) arises at the moment of conception, not at intercourse. Therefore, the intercourse itself is in mixed ownership, but the child is conceived only after intercourse begins, and ownership of the child's mother for the purpose of paternity arises just before conception. Consequently, the child is born free and is attributed to the father who claims him; no compensation for the child's value is due because the child was never owned by the other partner.

Finally, the passage discusses the scenario where both co-owners claim the child simultaneously. The ruling is that the child's lineage is established from both, but with a condition: according to Imam Muhammad, paternity can be established from up to three claimants; according to Zufar, up to five. If one claims before the other, the first claimant is preferred. This intra-Hanafi disagreement is noted.

العقر (al-'uqr): Compensation payable for unlawful intercourse, calculated based on the slave woman's value and the loss of her value due to childbirth.
الاستيلاد (al-istilad): Claiming paternity of the child of one's slave woman, which makes the child free and establishes lineage.
العلوق (al-'uluq): Conception; the moment when the slave woman becomes pregnant.

Mutual acknowledgment (iqrar) of paternity makes a slave woman an umm walad for both partners, with shared rights and obligations.

The matn states the core ruling: she is an umm walad for both, each owes half the 'aqr (compensation for intercourse) to the other, they offset these debts, the child inherits from each as a full son, and each inherits from the child as a single father (i.e., the paternal inheritance is divided equally between them). On 'she is an umm walad for both,' it explains that her service is shared equally (alternate days) because istilad does not affect the right to service. Regarding emancipation at death: when one partner dies, she becomes free by both agreements, with no liability from the deceased's estate; Abu Hanifa says no sa'yah (earning to pay for freedom) is required, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say she must earn half her value for the surviving partner. If one partner frees her during his lifetime, again Abu Hanifa says no liability or sa'yah, while the Sahibayn say he must pay if wealthy, or she earns if he is poor. On the 'aqr: each owes half the 'aqr to the other because each man's intercourse occurred in the other's property. On taqas: they offset their mutual debts so no money changes hands. On inheritance: the child inherits from each as a full son because each acknowledged paternity, and acknowledgment is binding. But when the child dies, both inherit as a single father, splitting the share equally because lineage is indivisible and the true father is unknown. The footnote clarifies that the dower (mahr) is proportional to ownership, but paternity and inheritance are equal because lineage cannot be divided. This is a Hanafi analysis based on the principle of iqrar and the indivisibility of nasab.

Core Idea
Master claims child from mukatab's slave: pays 'aqr, lineage established, but woman not umm walad due to partial ownership.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in Hanafi slave law where a master claims paternity of a child born to a slave woman owned by his mukatab (a slave under contract of manumission). The ruling balances the master's right to the mukatab's earnings with the limits of his ownership, leading to a unique outcome: the child's lineage is established and the master pays compensation, but the woman does not become an umm walad. The commentary uses the analogy of the 'deceived one's child' (walad al-maghruur) to clarify the legal principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components of the ruling
From the text: the claimant must pay 'aqr (compensation for intercourse), the child's lineage is established, and the child is free upon payment of its value. The slave woman does not become an umm walad. The ruling is conditional on the mukatab's confirmation (tasdiq).

The commentary explains: the master has only a right to the mukatab's earnings, not full ownership of the slave woman. Intercourse occurred in non-ownership, which normally mandates hadd or 'aqr. Hadd is dropped due to shubha (doubt of ownership), so 'aqr becomes obligatory. This is a form of istihsan.
The principle of dar' al-hudud bi al-shubuhat applies.

The analogy highlights partial ownership scenarios.

Dispute on Establishing Lineage of a Mukatab's Slave-Girl's Child
Intra-Hanafi dispute on requirement of mukatab's confirmation for nasab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over the requirement of the mukatab's verification for establishing lineage and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The mukatab's right over his earnings creates an impediment to the master's claim of lineage unless the mukatab confirms or the master later owns the child.

In Hanafi fiqh, lineage (nasab) from a master to a child born to his slave property is generally established by the master's claim. However, when the slave mother belongs to a mukatab (a slave under contract of manumission), a disagreement arises among the Hanafi imams. This lesson examines the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf on whether the mukatab's verification is required, and the underlying reasoning based on ownership rights over acquisitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The core question is whether the master's claim alone suffices to establish lineage of a child born to his mukatab's slave-girl, or whether the mukatab's verification (tasdiq) is required.
This is a dispute about a condition for nasab.
2
State the majority position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad)
They hold that the mukatab's verification is required; without it, lineage is not established unless the master later comes to own the child.
The key reason: the master does not own the mukatab's earnings at the time of need.
3
State Abu Yusuf's opposing position
Abu Yusuf holds that the master's mere claim suffices, drawing an analogy with a father's claim over his son's slave-girl's child, where no verification from the son is needed.
He argues that the mukatab's acquisition is ultimately the master's, just as a son's earnings belong to the father.
4
Analyze the proofs (talil)
The majority distinguishes based on ownership rights: the master lacks the right to dispose of the mukatab's earnings, while the father has full ownership of his son's earnings. Hence the analogy fails.
This is a qiyas ma' al-fariq (analogy with a clear difference).
5
Determine the practical consequence and mufta bihi
Under the majority view, lineage is only established with the mukatab's confirmation or if the master later owns the child. The mufta bihi is the majority position, as supported by the commentator's 'lana' (our proof).
