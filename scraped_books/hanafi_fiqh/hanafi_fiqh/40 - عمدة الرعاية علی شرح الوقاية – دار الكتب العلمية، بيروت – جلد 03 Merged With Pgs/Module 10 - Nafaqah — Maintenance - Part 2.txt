Dividing Maintenance by Hypothetical Inheritance Shares
Ruling with proof: maintenance proportioned to inheritance shares

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle of dividing maintenance among relatives according to their hypothetical inheritance shares and apply it to calculate shares in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Maintenance is divided by potential inheritance shares, not actual inheritance.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rule that when multiple relatives are obligated to provide maintenance (nafaqah) for a needy person, the amount each pays is proportioned to the share they would inherit if the supported person had died. The passage presents the rule, illustrates it with two examples, and addresses an objection about mahram relatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the Hanafi position: the maintenance of a needy relative (e.g., a child) is divided among his/her eligible relatives according to the shares they would hypothetically inherit.
This applies to relatives who are not direct descendants or spouses.
2
Understand the Basis
The measure is not actual inheritance but 'ahliyyat al-irth' (capacity to inherit). The text calls this 'اعتبار أهلية الإرث'.
Even if a relative is barred from inheriting (e.g., by a closer heir), the potential share is used.
3
Work Through the First Example
A man dies leaving three sisters (full, consanguine, uterine). Their inheritance shares are 3/5, 1/5, 1/5. Thus, the maintenance of his child is divided in the same proportions.
The full sister pays three-fifths, the consanguine one-fifth, the uterine one-fifth.
4
Examine the Second Example and Objection
A man has a maternal uncle and a paternal cousin. The cousin is not a mahram, but he is included because the criterion is eligibility for inheritance, not mahram status.
The maternal uncle would inherit if no closer heir exists, so he bears maintenance.
5
Grasp the Key Distinction
The rule uses hypothetical inheritance (أهلية الإرث) not actual inheritance (حقيقة الإرث). This is a Hanafi-specific practical measure.
This avoids unfairness when actual inheritance is unequal due to exclusion rules.

No Maintenance with Difference of Religion: General Rule and Exceptions
Stating the rule and its exceptions with talil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on maintenance between relatives of different religions and identify the three exceptions, explaining the reasoning (talil) behind each.

Core Idea
Maintenance between Muslim and non-Muslim relatives is not obligatory except for spouse, ascendants, and descendants.

This passage from 'Umda al-Ri'aya expands on a key ruling in Hanafi fiqh: difference of religion (ikhtilaf din) prevents the obligation of maintenance (nafaqah) between relatives, with three crucial exceptions. Understanding these exceptions and their underlying reasons is essential for applying the law correctly, especially in diverse societies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Read the first phrase: 'There is no maintenance with difference of religion'. This establishes the default: if two relatives have different religions (one Muslim, one non-Muslim), the Muslim has no obligation to provide maintenance to the non-Muslim, and vice versa.
This applies to all relatives except those explicitly named.
2
Note the three exceptions
After the rule, the text says 'except for the wife, ascendants, and descendants.' These are the only relationships where maintenance is still obligatory despite difference of religion.
Ascendants: parents, grandparents, etc. Descendants: children, grandchildren, etc.
3
Understand the reasoning for the wife
The wife's maintenance is based on the marriage contract and her confinement (habs) for the husband's right. This obligation arises from the contract, not from religious unity, so it remains even if the wife is non-Muslim.
This is a contractual obligation, not a kinship-based one.
4
Understand the reasoning for ascendants and descendants
Maintenance for parents and children is based on birth ties (wilada), which are independent of religion. The natural bond of lineage obligates support, regardless of the religious difference.
This reasoning contrasts with other relatives (e.g., siblings) where maintenance is based on sila (kinship ties), which is not required towards non-Muslims.
5
Distinguish difference of religion from sectarian differences
The commentator clarifies that if two people are both Muslims (e.g., Sunni and Shia) but their sectarian differences do not amount to kufr (disbelief), it is not considered ikhtilaf din. Therefore, maintenance between them is still obligatory.
This distinction prevents misuse of the rule for intra-Muslim differences.

Father Selling Son's Movables for Maintenance: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on father's sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the father selling the son's movables for his own nafaqah and apply the Hanafi position to a new scenario.

Core Idea
A father may sell his absent son's movables for his own maintenance — Abu Hanifa's istihsan overrides the Sahibayn's qiyas.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a father's authority over his adult son's property. The issue is whether the father can sell his son's movable goods to cover his own nafaqah when the son is absent. The matn follows Abu Hanifa's permission, while the Sahibayn argue by qiyas that guardianship ends at puberty. The commentary explains the basis and the limitation to movables and absence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the first sentence to determine what the father is permitted to do. Note the conditions: only movables, only when the son is absent, only for the father's own maintenance.
The ruling is from Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn disagree.
2
Note the dispute and its basis
The Sahibayn argue by qiyas: since guardianship ends at puberty, the father cannot sell the son's property just as he cannot sell immovables. Abu Hanifa uses istihsan, prioritizing the father's need and the son's duty to provide nafaqah.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
3
Understand the dalil of each side
The Sahibayn's dalil is qiyas jaliy: analogy from the absence of guardianship. Abu Hanifa's dalil is istihsan: an exception based on the father's need and the son's obligation.
The commentary points to a longer justification in the sharh.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and practical consequence
The matn follows Abu Hanifa, and the commentary supports this as the relied-upon position. The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa the sale is valid; under the Sahibayn it would be invalid unless the son authorizes it.
The ruling applies only to movables and only when the son is absent.

Nafaqah Obligations: Poor vs. Rich Relatives
States exceptions to nafaqah obligation based on wealth and poverty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whom a poor person and a rich person must support, identify the exceptions for wife and descendants, and explain the rationale based on contract and lineage.

Core Idea
A poor person only owes maintenance to his wife and children; a rich person only to his wife.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies a key Hanafi rule on maintenance (nafaqah). The general principle is that nafaqah is not obligatory except for specific relatives. The text distinguishes between a poor person (faqir) and a rich person (ghaniy), each with only a narrow set of obligations. Understanding these exceptions prevents common errors in calculating family support obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of obligors
The ruling divides people into two financial categories: the poor (faqir) and the rich (ghaniy). The poor person has no surplus wealth beyond basic needs; the rich person possesses nisab or surplus.
These terms are defined in Hanafi fiqh with specific thresholds.
2
State the general negative rule
The text begins with 'لا على الفقير إلا' and 'ولا لغني إلا', meaning 'No maintenance is obligatory upon the poor except...' and 'No maintenance from the rich except...' This establishes that nafaqah is not a general obligation; it is limited to the listed exceptions.
3
List the exceptions for the poor person
For a poor person, nafaqah is wajib only for his wife (zawjah) and his descendants (furūʻ). Descendants include children and grandchildren of both genders. He does not owe maintenance to parents, siblings, or other relatives.
The exception for the wife is unconditional; the exception for descendants is also unconditional regardless of their wealth.
4
List the exception for the rich person
For a rich person, nafaqah is wajib only for his wife. He does not owe maintenance to his descendants, parents, or any other relative. This is a stricter limitation.
The text says 'ولا لغني إلا لها' – no maintenance from the rich except for her (the wife).
5
Understand the rationale for the exceptions
The wife's maintenance is based on the marriage contract (ṣila bi'l-ʻaqd) and her confinement for his benefit. Descendants' maintenance is based on lineage (wilādah). Both grounds are independent of religion or wealth. For other relatives (like siblings), maintenance is based on kinship ties (ṣilah), which is not obligatory if the relative is a disbeliever or if the obligor is poor.
The commentary also notes that differences in sect (e.g., Sunni vs Shia) within Islam do not affect these obligations.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed Hanafi ruling on the father's authority to sell his adult son's property for maintenance (nafaqah). The source text (matn) states: 'The father may sell his son's movable property for his maintenance, but not real estate, and not for a debt other than maintenance.' - The father may sell the son's movable property only for the son's mandatory maintenance, not for any other debt the father may have against the son.
- The sale must be limited to the amount needed for maintenance (not more).
- For a minor or insane son, the father may sell any property (including real estate) for maintenance, by consensus.
- For an adult son, the ruling differs: movable property can be sold for maintenance, but not real estate.

1. The evidence only justifies sale for preservation, not for the purpose of spending on maintenance.

The distinction between maintenance and other debts remains because maintenance is uniquely obligatory before judgment and is need-based.

The passage then defends that selling movable property is itself preservation, even if the father later spends the price for himself, because the primary act of sale saves the property from potential loss. The creditor's right to take his due from the debtor's property when found (zafaran) is a general principle, but here it is limited to maintenance due to the special nature of nafaqah.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the ruling that a father may sell his son's movable property for the purpose of maintenance, but not immovable property. The commentator begins by stating the ruling: selling movable property is a form of preservation (حفظ), while immovable property is inherently protected and thus not sold. When the father sells movable property, the price is of the same type as his right to maintenance, so he spends it on that. The commentator then raises an objection: the discussion is about permissibility of selling for maintenance, not for preservation; if preservation were the rationale, then selling for any debt would be allowed. The correct rationale, says the commentator, is that the father has guardianship (ولاية) to take ownership of his son's property when in need, based on a textual evidence (النص الوارد). This guardianship gives him authority to sell movable property for his son's maintenance. Immovable property, however, can be used without selling (e.g., for agriculture), so there is no necessity to sell it. The commentator also addresses a potential contradiction: if the father had another debt against his son besides maintenance, would he also be allowed to sell? The answer is no, because the specific authority is for maintenance, not for any debt. Finally, the commentator discusses the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father,' clarifying that it does not mean actual ownership but rather permission to dispose when necessary, emphasizing that the purpose is to encourage filial service and allow parental disposal in necessity.

Mother Cannot Sell Child's Property for Her Maintenance
Ruling that mother cannot sell child's property, with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the mother lacks authority to sell the child's property for her own maintenance and identify the hadith proof and its interpretive basis.

Core Idea
Only the father has authority to sell the child's property; the mother cannot, as per the hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father.'

In Hanafi family law, the father holds a unique protective authority (wilaya nazariyya) over his child's property, including the power to sell. This lesson examines the ruling that the mother, along with other relatives and even the judge, cannot sell the child's property to cover her own maintenance, even when maintenance is obligatory on the child. The proof is a well-known hadith, but its interpretation hinges on understanding the nature of the father's authority as protective rather than proprietary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the parties
Pinpoint the exact ruling: it is prohibited (haram) for the mother to sell the child's property for her maintenance. Note that this applies to the mother, not the father. Also note that the same prohibition extends to other relatives and the judge.
The child in question is a minor or absent wealthy child.
2
Locate the primary proof
The proof is the hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father' (أنت ومالك لأبيك). Identify it as a Sunnah ahad, narrated by Jabir ibn Abdullah and others.
The hadith is sound (sahih) as per Ibn Majah and others.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Understand how the hadith proves the father's authority. The phrase 'wa maluka' indicates that the father has a right over the child's property, not literal ownership. In Hanafi usul, this is interpreted as establishing wilaya (guardianship) for the father, exclusive to him.
The imperative form is not literal ownership; otherwise the child himself would be owned, which is impossible.
4
Understand the principle of nazariyya
The father's authority is 'nazariyya' – based on care and consideration for the child's welfare. This means the father must act in the child's best interest; selling real estate is generally harmful (ijhaf) to the child, so the father would not sell without need.
The mother lacks this protective authority; her selling would be purely for her own benefit.
5
Apply the rule to other parties
Recognize that the absence of authority extends to all others: children of the child, other relatives, and the judge. The permissibility of sale is subsidiary to wilaya, which only the father (or grandfather) possesses.
If the father is absent or unable, the grandfather inherits the authority.

Depositary's Liability for Spending on Parents without Permission
Ruling that a depositary must guarantee spending on parents, but parents themselves are not liable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling regarding a depositary who spends a child's property on the child's parents without permission, and explain why the parents themselves are not liable when they spend on themselves.

Core Idea
A depositary who spends a child's property on parents without permission must guarantee it; but parents taking their due maintenance are not liable.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a child is absent, and his property is held by a depositary (trustee). If the depositary spends that property on the child's parents without the child's or a judge's permission, is he liable? The Hanafi ruling is clear: yes, he must guarantee the amount. However, if the parents themselves have the property and spend it on their own maintenance, they are not liable because they have a legal right to maintenance from their child. This distinction hinges on authority and rights, supported by a hadith about the father's authority over the son's wealth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary scenario
Consider an absent child's property held by a depositary (muda'). The depositary spends it on the child's parents without permission from the child or a judge.
The scenario also extends to spending on the child's wife and children, and to a debtor of an absent person.
2
Determine the rule for the depositary
The depositary is liable (must guarantee the amount) because he has no authority to dispose of the property without the owner's permission. This is the judicial ruling (qada').
Exception: if a judge orders the spending, the depositary is not liable because the judge has general authority.
3
Contrast with the parents' situation
If the parents themselves have the child's property and spend it on themselves, they are not liable. Same for wife and children.
Reason: maintenance of ascendants, descendants, and spouse is obligatory, so they may take their due from the property.
4
Understand the religious dimension (diyanah)
Even when the depositary is liable in court (qada'), he is not sinful if he intended reform (diyanah). Allah knows intentions.
This distinction between qada' and diyanah is a nuanced point in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Analyze the hadith evidence
The hadith 'you and your wealth belong to your father' is cited. It is interpreted as establishing the father's authority over the son's wealth, not ownership, because the son is not owned by the father.
This authority applies only to the father, not the mother or other relatives, as per the text.

Commentary
This passage presents two related rulings: (1) If a depositary (or debtor) of an absent person spends that person's property on the absent person's parents, wife, or children without the permission of the owner or the judge, the depositary is liable (must guarantee) the amount spent. (2) However, if the parents themselves (or wife/children) have the son's property in their possession and spend it on themselves, they are not liable. The reason for the distinction is that the depositary has no authority over the property; his spending is a wrongful disposal. But the parents, wife, and children have a right to maintenance from the son, and this right is established even without a court order, so when they take their due from his property, they are collecting what is lawfully theirs. The text also notes that if the judge orders the depositary to spend, the depositary is not liable because the judge has general authority. Furthermore, if the depositary ends up paying the guarantee to the owner, he cannot reclaim from the parents because by paying he has acquired ownership of the amount spent, as if he paid from his own pocket voluntarily. The hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father' is cited again to emphasize that it establishes authority, not ownership, and it applies only to the father, not the mother or other relatives.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement from a super-commentary on al-Wiqāyah, presenting a ruling with its proof and intra-Hanafi clarification. It opens with the general rule: a depositary of an absent son's property is liable (ḍamin) if he spends it on the son's parents without the son's permission or a judge's order. This is because the depositary has no inherent right to dispose of the deposited property. Similarly, if he spends on the son's wife or children without authorization, he must guarantee. The same applies to a debtor of an absent creditor who pays the debt to someone else without permission. However, if the judge orders the expenditure, the judge's authority substitutes for the owner's permission, and thus no guarantee arises. The commentary distinguishes between judiciary (qaḍā') and religious conscience (diyānah): if one spends with pious intent but no proof, he is not liable before Allah. After guaranteeing (i.e., compensating the owner), the depositary cannot reclaim the amount from the recipients, as he is deemed to have transferred ownership voluntarily. The second part addresses the exception for parents: when the parents themselves spend their absent son's money on their own sustenance, they are not liable to guarantee, even without permission. The rationale is that maintenance of ascendants and descendants, as well as the wife, is wajib (obligatory) independent of any judicial decree—it is a pre-existing obligation upon the son. Hence, if they gain access to his wealth and take from it to fulfill their right, it is permitted. This exception does not extend to other relatives. The author then cites a hadith from several sources, including the famous 'You and your wealth belong to your father.' The text explains that this hadith, taken literally, seems to give the father absolute ownership, but by consensus (ijmā') its outward meaning is diverted, because the phrase 'you' includes the son's person, which cannot be owned. Hence the hadith actually establishes the father's authority (wilāyah) over the son's wealth for need, not outright ownership. This authority is not extended to the mother or other relatives.

Expiry of Court-Ordered Maintenance for Relatives
Critical refinement of lapse rules for non-wife maintenance and slave nafaqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the lapse of court-ordered maintenance for relatives (non-wife) and identify the conditions under which borrowing creates a debt.

Core Idea
Court-ordered maintenance for relatives lapses after a month unless the judge authorized borrowing.

In Hanafi fiqh, maintenance for relatives is tied to immediate need. Once a judge decrees maintenance, it must be taken within a reasonable time or the order expires. This lesson clarifies the time limit, the exception of judicial borrowing, and how the rule differs from wife maintenance. It also defines which slaves are entitled to maintenance from their master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on lapse
State that if a judge decrees maintenance for relatives other than a wife, and a period passes without the maintenance being taken, the decree lapses. The reason: maintenance is for immediate need, so after time passes, the need is presumed fulfilled.
This is the default rule.
2
Note the critical refinement of time length
From al-Bazdawī's transmission, the lapse applies only if the period is long (more than a month). If less than a month, the maintenance does not lapse; otherwise the decree would be meaningless.
Short period defined as less than a month.
3
Understand the borrowing exception
If the judge authorizes borrowing (istiqdāḍ) and the relative actually borrows money to cover maintenance, the borrowed amount becomes a debt on the absent liable person. Mere court order without actual borrowing does not create a right of recourse.
This is unlike the wife's maintenance where the decree alone suffices.
4
Distinguish wife maintenance
Wife's maintenance does not lapse with time; she can claim arrears at any time. The difference is due to the contractual nature of marital maintenance.
See al-Fatḥ and al-Khāniyah.
5
Define slave maintenance scope
The master must provide maintenance for all slaves whose usufruct belongs to him, including male, female, umm walad, mudabbar, and those bequeathed service. The mukātab (slave under contract of manumission) is excluded because he owns his own usufruct.
