Interpreting 'كل عبد لي حر' in Wills: The Haqiqah-Majaz Solution
Resolves the dilemma of combining haqiqah and majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that a testator's statement 'every slave of mine is free' applies to slaves owned at death, and evaluate the refined argument that avoids combining haqiqah and majaz.

Core Idea
In wills, the word 'my' refers to ownership at death, not at utterance, so no contradictory meanings arise.

When a testator says 'every slave of mine is free', does it include slaves he hasn't yet acquired? The apparent dilemma is that interpreting the phrase literally limits it to present slaves, while extending it to future slaves would require a figurative reading. This passage critiques one attempted solution and offers a more refined one based on the principle that the will's effect is deferred to death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
A testator declares 'every slave of mine is free' in his will. He currently owns some slaves and may acquire more before death. The question is whether the statement applies to slaves owned at the time of utterance or also to those acquired later.
The ruling in wills is based on ownership at death, not at utterance.
2
Understand the Objection
If the statement is interpreted as covering both present and future slaves, it would combine the literal meaning (haqiqah) for present slaves and the figurative meaning (majaz) for future slaves in one utterance. In Hanafi usul, this is generally not permissible under a single cause.
The objection arises from the principle that a word cannot be used in both its literal and figurative senses simultaneously without a valid reason.
3
Examine the Earlier Response
Al-Hidayah attempts to answer by saying the statement serves two different causes: immediate emancipation (making present slaves mudabbar) and a bequest for future slaves. However, this is problematic because the same sentence cannot have two distinct legal effects concurrently.
The flaw is that the utterance itself must be assigned a single legal nature at the moment of speech.
4
Learn the Preferred Solution
The better answer is that the statement 'every slave of mine is free' only applies to slaves owned at the time of utterance. At death, a new hypothetical declaration is imagined, effectively renewing the statement to apply to all slaves then owned. This avoids combining haqiqah and majaz because there is only one declaration at a time.
The effect is that the testator can sell slaves acquired after the will because they are not mudabbar until death.
5
Appreciate the Legal Implication
Under this interpretation, slaves acquired after the will are not made mudabbar (which would prevent sale), so the testator retains full power of disposition over them until death. This resolves a practical issue in estate management.
The ruling supports the permissibility of selling such slaves before the testator's death.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of the earlier commentary on the chapter of wills. The issue is how to interpret a testator's statement 'every slave of mine is free' when he owns slaves at the time of the will and may acquire more before death. The author argues that the statement in a will applies only to slaves owned at death, not at the time of utterance, because wills are based on ownership at death. The objection is that interpreting 'every slave of mine' as referring to both present and future slaves forces a combination of the real meaning (haqiqah, present slaves) and the metaphorical meaning (majaz, future slaves) in one utterance, which is not allowed. The response in al-Hidayah suggests that the statement serves two different legal purposes: one is an immediate emancipation (for present slaves, making them mudabbar) and the other is a bequest (for future slaves). However, this is problematic because the same sentence cannot have two different legal effects simultaneously. The preferred answer is that the statement only applies to current slaves when spoken, and when the testator dies, a hypothetical new declaration is imagined applying to all slaves then owned. Thus no combination of haqiqah and majaz occurs.

Key Terms
الحقيقة والمجاز (al-haqiqah wa al-majaz): Haqiqah is the literal, original meaning of a word; majaz is the figurative, transferred meaning. In usul al-fiqh, a word can only be used in one sense at a time unless there is a qarina (contextual indication) allowing multiple meanings.
الإيجاب (al-ijab): The act of declaring or imposing a legal obligation or effect. Here it refers to the declaration of emancipation (ijab al-'itq) or the declaration of bequest (ijab al-wasiyyah).
تقديراً (taqdiran): An estimated or hypothetical consideration. In legal reasoning, it means treating something as if it exists for the purpose of applying a ruling, even though it does not actually occur. Here it refers to the virtual renewal of the declaration at death.

Mudabbar's Freedom: One-Third Rule and Wasiyyah Analogy
Explanation that tadbir is like a will, so the mudabbar is freed from one-third of the master's estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that tadbir is treated as a wasiyyah and apply the one-third rule to various scenarios of mudabbar emancipation.

Core Idea
Tadbir is like a will: the mudabbar is freed from one-third; any excess must be earned by work.

This passage establishes a crucial analogy in Hanafi fiqh: tadbir (the master's declaration that a slave will be free upon his death) is legally equivalent to a wasiyyah (bequest). Since a wasiyyah can only take effect from one-third of the testator's estate, the same limit applies to the mudabbar. The commentary then walks through several scenarios—what happens if the mudabbar's value is less than, equal to, or exceeds one-third, and how debts affect the outcome. Understanding these rules clarifies the boundary between voluntary post-death obligations and the rights of heirs and creditors.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the analogy
The author states that tadbir is like a wasiyyah because both are obligations that take effect after death. This analogy is the key proof (dalil) for applying the one-third limit.
The technical term is 'tashbih' (analogy) leading to 'ilhaq' (joining the ruling).
2
Apply the one-third limit
Once the analogy is established, the one-third rule from wasiyyah applies. If the mudabbar's value is within one-third, he is fully freed upon the master's death; if it exceeds, he is freed proportionally from one-third and must work (sa'a) to earn the remaining portion.
The working (saya'ah) is a unique Hanafi institution to complete the freedom.
3
Consider the case of no other property
If the master leaves only the mudabbar as wealth, then one-third of the mudabbar is freed and he must work for two-thirds for the heirs.
This is because the one-third is taken from the total estate, which is just the mudabbar himself.
4
Prioritize debts over bequests
If the master has debts that consume the estate, the creditor's right takes precedence. The mudabbar must work for the entire value to pay the debt, because the bequest (tadbir) cannot take effect until debts are settled.
This aligns with the general principle that debts are paid before bequests.
5
Note the exception: murder by the mudabbar
If the mudabbar kills his master, the bequest is voided, and he must work for the full value for the heirs. This is analogous to a killer not inheriting, and unlike umm al-walad whose freedom is not a bequest.
This exception reinforces that tadbir is treated as a will; a will is invalid for a killer.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof by analogy. It establishes that tadbir is treated as a wasiyyah (bequest) because both are obligations that take effect after death. Therefore, the mudabbar is freed only from one-third of the master's estate. The commentary provides a detailed breakdown: if the mudabbar's value is within one-third, he is fully freed; if it exceeds, he works (sa'a) for the excess to earn his freedom. If the master dies with no other property, the mudabbar works for two-thirds (for the heirs) and is freed from one-third. If the master has debts, the creditors are paid first, and the mudabbar must work to pay the debt if there is no other estate. The commentary [4] gives an important exception: if the mudabbar kills his master, he cannot benefit from the bequest (analogy to a killer not inheriting), so he must work for the full value for the heirs. This contrasts with umm al-walad (mother of a child), whose freedom is not a bequest and takes effect immediately, so she is freed even if she kills her master.

Key Terms
وصية (wasiyyah): A bequest or will; an obligation that takes effect after death, limited to one-third of the estate.
سعى (sa'a): The share of a mudabbar that remains unfreed must be earned by him through work (saya'ah) to complete his freedom.
ثلث المال (thuluth al-mal): One-third of the master's estate at death, the maximum portion from which a wasiyyah or tadbir can take effect.

Tadbir: Posthumous Emancipation and Its Legal Effects
Explains when tadbir is valid and its legal effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid tadbir and determine whether a mudabbar must perform sa'yah (work to earn his value) based on the master's estate.

Core Idea
Tadbir is a bequest, not immediate freedom — its conditions determine the slave's obligation after the master's death.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains the rulings of tadbir (posthumous emancipation). It addresses the key question: when does a mudabbar have to work to earn his freedom, and when is he free without payment? The answer depends on whether the master left other property, owes debts, or tied the tadbir to a specific condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic rule
Tadbir has the ruling of a bequest (wasiyyah). Its effect occurs after death.
This is the foundational principle that governs all tadbir rulings.
2
Understand the sa'yah obligation
If the master dies leaving no other property, the mudabbar works for two-thirds of his value for the heirs. If the master has debts that consume all his wealth, the mudabbar works for his full value to pay creditors.
If there is other property, it is used first; the mudabbar only works to cover any shortfall.
3
Distinguish conditional tadbir
When the master says 'if I die in this journey' or 'from this illness,' the slave is not a mudabbar because the condition is not certain to occur; thus the master can still sell him.
The cause (sabab) is not concluded immediately due to uncertainty.
4
Note the exception for near-certain conditions
If the condition is practically certain (e.g., an 80-year-old saying 'if I die within 100 years'), the slave is a full mudabbar because the condition is effectively inevitable.
The key is the likelihood of occurrence: uncertain vs. predominant.

[0] And he becomes free if his condition occurs, like the emancipation of a mudabbar. [1] His saying: 'and he becomes free' – meaning the emancipation of a restricted mudabbar occurs upon the existence of his condition such as his death on that journey or from that illness, not because he is a mudabbar, but because his emancipation is tied to a condition, so it becomes effective upon the existence of the condition like all other conditional statements. [2] His saying: 'and likewise... etc.' – an indication that the mention of sale is incidental and exemplary. [3] His saying: 'and he intended uncertainty' – not absolute essential possibility, for the occurrence of a restriction that is also predominantly occurring is essentially possible as well, yet the emancipation tied to it is an absolute mudabbar as previously detailed.

Commentary
This is a commentary on the rulings of tadbir (posthumous emancipation). The passage begins by stating the basic ruling: If a master declares a slave mudabbar and has no other property, the slave must work to earn two-thirds of his value for the heirs; if the master has debts, the slave must work for the full value to pay creditors. The commentary then clarifies conditional tadbir: when the master says 'If I die in this journey or from this illness' etc., the slave is not considered a mudabbar because the condition is not certain to occur, so the master can still sell him. The author explains that tadbir takes the effect of a bequest (wasiyyah) because its effect occurs after death. An important distinction is made between a mudabbar who kills his master and an umm walad who does: the mudabbar must pay his value to the heirs (since he forfeits the bequest), while the umm walad becomes free without payment because her emancipation is not a bequest but direct from motherhood. The commentary also discusses the subtlety of conditions that are 'ordinarily possible' versus those that are inevitable: if the condition is such that it is predominantly likely to occur (e.g., an old man saying 'if I die within 100 years'), the slave is a full mudabbar because the condition is effectively certain.

Key Terms
تدبير (tadbir): A form of emancipation where a master declares that a slave will be free upon the master's death.
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave who has been made tadbir (will become free upon master's death).
سعاية (sa'yah): The requirement for a slave to work and earn money to pay a certain sum for his freedom or for the heirs.
وصية (wasiyyah): A bequest or will made by a person to take effect after death.
