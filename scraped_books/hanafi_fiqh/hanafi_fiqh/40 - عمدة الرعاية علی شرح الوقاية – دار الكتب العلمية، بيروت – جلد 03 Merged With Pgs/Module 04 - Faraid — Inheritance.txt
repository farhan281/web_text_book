Understanding Hajb: Total and Partial Preclusion in Inheritance
Defining hajb as total or partial preclusion from inheritance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hajb, distinguish between hajb hirman (total preclusion) and hajb nuqsan (partial preclusion), and apply the definition to examples from Hanafi inheritance law.

Core Idea
An heir can be completely excluded (hajb hirman) or have their share reduced (hajb nuqsan) by a closer relative.

Inheritance in Islam is governed by fixed shares, but not all heirs always receive their full entitlement. The presence of certain relatives can block or reduce the shares of others. This lesson introduces the concept of hajb, which is the mechanism by which preclusion occurs, and distinguishes its two types: total and partial. Understanding this is essential before learning specific inheritance calculations.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the linguistic and technical meaning of hajb
Start with the literal meaning: hajb means 'prevention'. In inheritance law, it refers to preventing an heir from receiving all or part of their share because another heir exists.
The term is used only in the context of inheritance.
2
Identify the two types: hajb hirman and hajb nuqsan
Hajb hirman is total exclusion—the heir gets nothing. Hajb nuqsan is reduction—the heir gets a smaller share than they would otherwise. Memorize the definitions and the Arabic terms.
Hajb hirman always involves a 'blocker' who is a closer relative in the same line.
3
Analyze the examples provided in the text
The text gives two clear examples of hirman: the son's son is excluded by the son, and the brother is excluded by the father. For nuqsan: the mother's share drops from 1/3 to 1/6 when a son or two siblings survive. Understand why these relatives cause preclusion.
These examples are foundational for building a mental map of inheritance priorities.
4
Classify new scenarios as hirman or nuqsan
Apply the definition to other common inheritance situations, such as: a son excludes a grandson (hirman), a husband's share is reduced by children (nuqsan). Practice until you can explain why.
This step bridges definition to application.

Commentary
This passage defines the term hajb (preclusion) as used in inheritance law. Linguistically, it means prevention. Technically, it refers to the situation where the presence of one heir prevents another heir from receiving all or part of their inheritance. The commentary distinguishes two types: hajb hirman (total preclusion), where an heir is completely excluded, e.g., a son's son is excluded by a son, or a brother is excluded by a father; and hajb nuqsan (partial preclusion), where an heir's share is reduced, e.g., the mother normally gets one-third, but if there is a son or two siblings, she gets one-sixth instead. The passage ends with a note that the text 'قُدِّمَ الجزء' (the part is given priority) refers to the arrangement of inheritance, and according to al-Fara'id al-Sirajiyya, there are four categories (which are not detailed here). This is a foundational definition for understanding inheritance calculations in Hanafi law.

Key Terms
حجب (hajb): Preclusion from inheritance, either total (hirman) or partial (nuqsan), due to the presence of another heir.
حجب حرمان (hajb hirman): Total exclusion from inheritance, e.g., son's son excluded by son.
حجب نقصان (hajb nuqsan): Reduction in share, e.g., mother's share reduced from 1/3 to 1/6 in presence of children or siblings.

Definition of 'Asaba and Its Categories
Defining 'asaba and its subtypes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'asaba and distinguish between 'asaba bi-ghayrihi and 'asaba ma'a al-ghayr.

Core Idea
An 'asaba is a male agnatic heir; females become 'asaba only through or alongside male agnates.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya establishes the critical definition of 'asaba in Hanafi inheritance law. Understanding who qualifies as an 'asaba is essential for determining residual heirs after prescribed shares are allocated. The text also introduces two special categories: 'asaba bi-ghayrihi and 'asaba ma'a al-ghayr, which describe how female prescribed-share holders can become residuary heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of 'asaba
Look for the phrase 'ذكر يتصل بلا توسط أنثى' (a male connected without an intervening female). This is the core definition.
The definition excludes relatives with female links, like uterine siblings.
2
Understand the condition of no intervening female
Note that if a female enters the lineage, the relative is not an 'asaba. Examples: children of the mother (uterine siblings) and maternal grandfather.
The text explains why full brothers are still 'asaba despite the mother's link: paternal kinship suffices.
3
Distinguish 'asaba bi-ghayrihi from 'asaba ma'a al-ghayr
'Asaba bi-ghayrihi: a female prescribed-share holder becomes 'asaba due to a male 'asaba (e.g., daughter with son). 'Asaba ma'a al-ghayr: a female becomes 'asaba alongside another female (e.g., sister with daughter), but neither is an 'asaba alone.
The key difference: in bi-ghayrihi, the accompanying male is an 'asaba; in ma'a al-ghayr, both are prescribed-share holders.
4
Apply the definitions to examples
Work through the examples: daughter with son becomes 'asaba bi-ghayrihi; sister with daughter becomes 'asaba ma'a al-ghayr. Note that uterine sister does not become 'asaba with daughter.
This distinction affects who inherits the residue after Quranic shares.

Rules of Hajb and Precedence Among 'Asaba
Explaining hajb and order of 'asaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hajb, distinguish between hirman and nuqsan, and apply the order of precedence among 'asaba heirs.

Core Idea
Exclusion (hajb) prevents an heir from all or part of inheritance; among 'asaba, preference is by degree then by strength of blood.

This section of 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains the two types of hajb and the systematic order of 'asaba heirs. The Hanafi school classifies 'asaba into four categories based on proximity to the deceased, and further ranks them by strength of kinship (full vs. half-blood). A hadith from Ali radiAllahu anhu confirms the precedence of full siblings over consanguine siblings.

Guided Path
1
Define hajb linguistically and terminologically
Linguistically, hajb means prevention. Terminologically, it is preventing a specific person from all or part of their inheritance due to another person.
The text gives examples: son's son excluded by son (hirman), mother reduced from 1/3 to 1/6 (nuqsan).
2
Distinguish hajb hirman from hajb nuqsan
Hajb hirman excludes the heir completely; hajb nuqsan reduces the share.
The text uses the terms 'حرمان' and 'نقصان'.
3
List the four categories of 'asaba
According to al-Fara'id al-Sirajiyya: (1) part of the deceased: sons and their descendants; (2) root: father and grandfather; (3) part of the father: brothers and their descendants; (4) part of the grandfather: uncles and their descendants.
The order is based on proximity to the deceased.
4
Apply the two-tier preference: degree then strength
First, prefer the closer degree (e.g., son over brother). If degrees are equal, prefer the stronger relationship (full blood over half-blood).
The text quotes the hadith: full siblings inherit before consanguine siblings.
5
Identify the dalil for strength preference
The hadith of Ali: 'قضى رسول الله أن أعيان بني الأم يتوارثون دون بني العلات...' establishes that full siblings take precedence over half-siblings.
The dalil is ahad but relied upon.

Ruling on lineage establishment with heirs' acknowledgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a widow's child's lineage is established without complete evidence, and apply the ruling when the heirs acknowledge the birth.

Core Idea
A widow's child born within two years gets lineage from the deceased if the heirs acknowledge — no full proof needed.

This lesson examines a special Hanafi ruling that establishes the lineage of a child born to a widow after her husband's death, even without the usual complete evidence (two men, or one man and two women). The key is that the deceased's heirs acknowledge the birth. We will trace the conditions, the underlying rationale, and a subtle textual difference between al-Wiqaya and al-Hidayah that clarifies the legal logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the specific case: a woman in the death 'iddah (4 months 10 days) gives birth within two years of her husband's death. The heirs (inheritors of the deceased) acknowledge the birth.
The time limit is two years, which is the Hanafi maximum pregnancy period.
2
Recall the default rule
Normally, establishing lineage requires complete evidence (hujja tamma) — two men or one man and two women. Understand why this case is exceptional.
Default rule is from general principles of evidence.
3
Understand the exception
The heirs' acknowledgment substitutes for complete evidence. This is due to the likelihood that the child is from the deceased and the difficulty of providing proof after death.
The acknowledgment must be explicit.
4
Analyze the textual nuance
Note the wording difference: al-Wiqaya uses 'and' (wa) the heirs acknowledged, while al-Hidayah uses 'or' (aw). The commentator suggests 'and' might be a scribal error or means 'with'; the intended meaning is that the heirs' acknowledgment alone suffices, not in combination with another condition.
The 'or' reading aligns with the legal logic that either visible pregnancy/confession OR heirs' acknowledgment can establish lineage.
5
Apply the additional condition
From the commentary, the widow must not be a minor; otherwise, different rules apply (birth within 10 months 10 days).
This is an additional condition mentioned by al-'Ayni.

Ruling on inheritance from a deceased who acknowledged a son and his mother

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the inheritance rights of an acknowledged son and his mother, and identify the conditions under which the mother inherits.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a child as one's son implies a valid marriage, making both inherit — unless the mother's freedom or Islam is unknown.

This lesson examines a key rule in Hanafi inheritance law: when a deceased person acknowledges a child as his son, both the child and his mother (claimed as wife) inherit, provided the mother is known to be free and Muslim. The child's paternity is established by acknowledgment, but the mother's claim to marriage requires that she be a free Muslim woman, otherwise she may be a concubine (umm walad) who does not inherit.

First scenario: woman known free and mother of child → both inherit. Second: woman's freedom unknown, heirs contest → she does not inherit unless she proves marriage.

Determine conditions for mother's inheritance
Mother must be known free, Muslim, and impliedly married via acknowledgment of paternity.
If any condition missing, she does not inherit.
3
Explain why child always inherits
Paternity is established by acknowledgment, which is binding; child inherits as son independent of mother's status.
Child's inheritance is unconditional.
4
Analyze the legal reasoning
Acknowledging paternity normally requires a valid marriage (nikah sahih); other possibilities (irregular marriage, concubinage) are disregarded unless evidence shows otherwise.
The default is valid marriage, not irregular.

When co-owners deny manumission, wala' is suspended until agreement; sa'aya depends on the manumitter's solvency.

Recognize the Sahibayn's principle
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that manumission of a part (by one co-owner) is manumission of the whole slave. This is a key principle underlying the rulings.
This contrasts with Abu Hanifa's view that manumission of a part only frees that portion.
3
Apply the principle to denial
Since both deny, the true manumitter is unknown. Consequently, wala' (patronage) cannot be assigned to either, and is suspended until they agree on who freed the slave. If they never agree and the slave dies, wala' goes to the public treasury.
Wala' is the right to inherit from the freed slave.
4
Understand sa'aya implications
Sa'aya (the slave's obligation to earn compensation) is only imposed if the manumitter is insolvent. Since the manumitter is unknown, detailed rules apply: the slave's affirmation of each partner triggers sa'aya differently depending on wealth status.
If both partners are wealthy, no sa'aya; if both insolvent, sa'aya for full value; if one wealthy and one insolvent, sa'aya only for the wealthy partner's share.
5
Contrast with Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa holds that wala' belongs to both partners equally, and sa'aya is always required regardless of wealth. This simplifies the rules but differs from the dominant school position.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is on the Sahibayn's position.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the chapter of partial manumission, specifically dealing with two scenarios. The first scenario is when two partners jointly own a slave, and each denies having freed the slave. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), the wala' (right of patronage) is suspended until they agree on who freed the slave. If they never agree and the slave dies, the wala' goes to the public treasury. According to Abu Hanifa, however, the wala' belongs to both partners equally. The second scenario involves each partner conditionally freeing the slave on a future event, but the events are contradictory (e.g., one says 'if X enters,' the other says 'if X does not enter'). If the condition's occurrence is unknown, the slave is half-free and must perform sa'aya (work to compensate) for his value to both partners according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad; but according to Muhammad alone, he must perform sa'aya for his entire value. This is because the partner who broke his condition (al-hanith) is unknown, so sa'aya cannot be dropped for either. The commentary also clarifies that the rulings on sa'aya depend on the financial status of the partners: if both are wealthy, no sa'aya; if both insolvent, sa'aya for full value; if one wealthy and one insolvent, sa'aya only for the wealthy partner's share. The commentator emphasizes that these details are according to the Sahibayn's positions; Abu Hanifa's view is different and simpler.

السعاية (al-sa'āyah): The compulsory work or earning that a partially freed slave must perform to compensate his master for the unfreed portion.
عتق البعض ('itq al-ba'd): Manumission of a part of a slave, i.e., when one co-owner frees his share.

Conflicting Conditional Manumissions: Determining Sa'aya with Unknown Conditions
Ruling with proof: sa'aya in conflicting conditional manumissions with unknown outcome.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the sa'aya obligation when two partners conditionally free a slave with contradictory conditions and the outcome is unknown, and contrast the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
With conflicting conditions and unknown outcome, the slave is half-free and must earn compensation; the amount differs between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

This lesson examines a scenario where two partners each conditionally manumit a slave on contradictory future events, such that only one condition can be true. If the outcome is unknown, the slave becomes half-free, but the obligation to earn compensation (sa'aya) varies based on which partner's condition actually occurred. The Hanafi masters Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ on the extent of sa'aya, and we will explore their reasoning and the role of the partners' wealth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contradictory conditions
Two partners each attach the slave's freedom to a condition that cannot both be true, e.g., one says 'if X enters town tomorrow' and the other says 'if X does not enter'. The slave's freedom depends on which condition occurs.
2
Determine the outcome if the condition is known
If it is known that the condition of one partner occurred (e.g., X entered), that partner's share is freed; the other's share remains enslaved. No sa'aya is needed for the freed share because the manumitter is known.
The slave is half-free, half-enslaved.
3
Analyze the case with unknown condition
If it is unknown whether the condition occurred (e.g., no one knows if X entered), then half the slave is definitely free (since one condition must be true), but the identity of the manumitter is unknown. The slave must perform sa'aya to compensate the unfreed portion.
The slave is half-free, and must earn compensation for that half to both partners.
4
Apply the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
According to Abu Yusuf, the slave performs sa'aya for half the value to both partners (since the freed half is owned by both potentially). According to Muhammad, the slave performs sa'aya for the full value to both partners, because the partner whose condition did not occur (the hanith) is unknown and cannot be exempted from sa'aya.
Muhammad's reasoning: the one against whom sa'aya is dropped is unknown, so it cannot be dropped.
5
Incorporate the wealth factor
The obligation of sa'aya is further modified by the partners' wealth: if both are wealthy, no sa'aya; if both insolvent, sa'aya as above; if one wealthy and one insolvent, sa'aya is only for the wealthy partner's share (since the insolvent's share does not require compensation).
Wealth of the manumitter prevents sa'aya according to the Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the chapter of partial manumission, specifically dealing with two scenarios. The first scenario is when two partners jointly own a slave, and each denies having freed the slave. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), the wala' (right of patronage) is suspended until they agree on who freed the slave. If they never agree and the slave dies, the wala' goes to the public treasury. According to Abu Hanifa, however, the wala' belongs to both partners equally. The second scenario involves each partner conditionally freeing the slave on a future event, but the events are contradictory (e.g., one says 'if X enters,' the other says 'if X does not enter'). If the condition's occurrence is unknown, the slave is half-free and must perform sa'aya (work to compensate) for his value to both partners according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad; but according to Muhammad alone, he must perform sa'aya for his entire value. This is because the partner who broke his condition (al-hanith) is unknown, so sa'aya cannot be dropped for either. The commentary also clarifies that the rulings on sa'aya depend on the financial status of the partners: if both are wealthy, no sa'aya; if both insolvent, sa'aya for full value; if one wealthy and one insolvent, sa'aya only for the wealthy partner's share. The commentator emphasizes that these details are according to the Sahibayn's positions; Abu Hanifa's view is different and simpler.

Conditional Emancipation with Ambiguous First-Born: The Ruling of Half-Freedom
Ruling on conditional emancipation with unknown first-born

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a conditional emancipation statement is followed by birth of twins of unknown order, and explain the reasoning behind the partial freedom for the mother and daughter.

Core Idea
Doubt in the condition frees half of the mother and daughter; the son remains a slave.

A master tells his slave-girl: 'If your first child is a son, you are free.' She gives birth to a son and a daughter simultaneously, and the order cannot be determined. What happens? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi reasoning: because freedom depends on an uncertain condition, the law resolves the doubt by granting partial freedom to the mother and daughter, while requiring them to compensate the master for the remaining half of their value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional emancipation statement
The master says: 'If the first child is a son, you are free.' The condition is the first-born being a male. The slave-girl later gives birth to both a son and a daughter.
This is a valid conditional emancipation (ta'liq al-itaq) in the Hanafi school.
2
Analyze the two possible scenarios
Scenario 1: The son is born first – then the condition is fulfilled, so the mother is free, and the daughter (born while the mother is free) is also free. Scenario 2: The daughter is born first – then the condition is not fulfilled, so the mother remains a slave, and the daughter (born to a slave mother) is also a slave.
The son is never freed because his birth is the condition for the mother's freedom, not his own.
3
Apply the principle of doubt
Since the order is unknown, the law cannot give full freedom to both, nor keep them both in slavery. Instead, half of the mother and half of the daughter are freed, and they must work (sa'y) to pay the master for the remaining half of their value.
This is a compromise based on the uncertainty. The son remains a full slave in both scenarios.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Muhammad, in al-Kaysaniyyat, holds that no one is freed in this case; instead, the mother is made to swear an oath. However, the established position (zahir al-riwaya) is the half-freedom ruling from al-Hidayah.
The majority view (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad in his later works) is that half-freedom and payment are the correct outcome.

Clarifying Ambiguous Emancipation: Intercourse as Evidence and Testimony Rulings
Dispute on intercourse as clarification and ruling on testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Sahibayn) regarding the use of intercourse to clarify ambiguous emancipation, and state the ruling on testimony for ambiguous emancipation.

Core Idea
Intercourse indicates ownership, clarifying which slave was intended; testimony requires a specific claimant, so ambiguous testimony is invalid.

If a man says 'One of my two slave-girls is free' and then has intercourse with one, does that clarify which one was intended? And if two witnesses testify that he emancipated one slave ambiguously, can that testimony be accepted? This lesson explores these two questions, presenting the Hanafi position and the intra-madhhab dispute on intercourse, and the clear ruling on testimony.

Guided Path
1
Define ambiguous emancipation and the issue of clarification
Ambiguous emancipation ('itq mubham) is when a master says 'One of my slaves is free' without specifying. The question is how to determine which one is free. One method is by examining the master's subsequent conduct.
The Hanafi school accepts ambiguous emancipation as valid.
2
Present the dispute on intercourse as clarification
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that intercourse does not clarify, because emancipation is a non-physical act, so intercourse cannot indicate intent. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that intercourse does clarify, because intercourse is only permissible with one's property; therefore, having intercourse with one slave-girl proves she is still his property, so the other slave-girl must be the one intended for freedom.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf. The mufta bihi is the view of the two companions.
3
Understand the proof of the two companions
The proof is rational: intercourse is only halal in ownership. If the master had intercourse with one slave-girl, it shows she remains his property, so she cannot be the free one. Therefore, the other slave-girl is the one intended for freedom. This is a form of inference (istidlal) based on a necessary correlation.
The commentator adds that if this were not accepted, intercourse would be occurring with a non-owned woman, which is forbidden.
4
State the ruling on testimony for ambiguous emancipation
If two witnesses testify that a man emancipated one of his two slaves ambiguously, without specifying which, the testimony is invalid according to Abu Hanifa (except in a will). The reason: testimony in rights of people (huquq al-'ibad) requires a prior claim (da'wa) from a specific claimant. Here, the potential claimant (the freed slave) is unknown, so no claim can be made, and the testimony fails.
If the emancipation were specific (e.g., 'Zayd is free'), the slave himself would be the claimant.
5
Apply the tarjih and practical consequences
For the intercourse issue, fatwa is given according to the two companions, as stated in al-Hidayah, al-Fath, and al-Bahr. For testimony, the ruling is that ambiguous testimony is invalid, which is the established position.
The practical consequence: if a master has intercourse with one of two slave-girls after an ambiguous statement, the one he had intercourse with remains a slave, and the other is free.

Ruling on ambiguous emancipation: If a man says to his slave-girl, 'If the first child you give birth to is a son, you are free,' and she gives birth to both a son and a daughter without knowing which was first, half of the mother and half of the daughter are freed. According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), intercourse in ambiguous emancipation is also a clarification, because intercourse is only permissible in ownership, so it indicates that the one with whom intercourse occurred is his property, and thus she is not intended by the emancipation. And according to the two companions, intercourse with each of them is forbidden. This is the preferred view per al-Fath and al-Bahr, and fatwa is given according to their view, not Imam Abu Hanifa's, as in al-Hidayah, due to caution. The commentator explains: The permissibility of intercourse is specific to ownership, so it indicates continued ownership and specifies the other as intended. The detailed ruling: If a man says to his slave-girl, 'If the first child you give birth to is a male, you are free,' as long as she has not given birth, she remains a slave. If she gives birth to a male, she is free; if she gives birth to a female, she remains a slave. If she gives birth to both without knowing which was first, half of the mother and half of the daughter are freed, and they both must work to pay for the remaining half. According to Muhammad in al-Kaysaniyyat, no one is freed in this case, but rather the mother is made to swear an oath. The son is a slave in both cases. As for testimony: If two witnesses testify that a man emancipated one of his two slaves ambiguously, the testimony is invalid according to Abu Hanifa, except in a will. The commentary states: Testimony in rights of people is based on a prior claim; here there is no claimant because the specific slave is unknown. If the emancipation was specific, the slave himself would be the claimant. The narrator in al-Nihaya affirms that the correct view is that the one who denies the condition (that the son was born first) is believed. Then it explains that the mother and daughter each are freed in one scenario but not in the other, so half is freed.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'āyah covers two main issues in ambiguous emancipation (al-ʿitq al-mubham). First, the case where a master conditions his slave-girl's freedom on the first-born being a son, but she gives birth to twins of unknown order. The ruling is that half of the mother and half of the daughter are freed, while the son remains a slave. The reasoning: if the son was first, both mother and daughter (as a dependent) are free; if the daughter was first, no freedom occurs; thus, due to the doubt, half of each female is freed. Imam Muhammad in a rare transmission holds that no one is freed and the mother must swear. Second, regarding testimony: if two witnesses ambiguously testify that one of two slaves was emancipated, the testimony is invalid because there is no specific claimant. Abu Hanifa's position is presented, but the preferred view (mufta bihi) follows the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding the use of intercourse as a clarifying factor in ambiguous emancipation. The commentator emphasizes the rational proof: intercourse indicates ownership, thus clarifying which slave-girl was intended for freedom.

Key Terms
الوطء (al-waṭʾ): Sexual intercourse with a slave-girl, which in this context is argued to indicate ownership and thus clarify ambiguous emancipation.
الشهادة (al-shahādah): Testimony in court; here, it refers to two witnesses testifying that a man emancipated one of his slaves ambiguously.
المدعي (al-muddaʿī): The claimant in a legal case; necessary for testimony to be accepted.
