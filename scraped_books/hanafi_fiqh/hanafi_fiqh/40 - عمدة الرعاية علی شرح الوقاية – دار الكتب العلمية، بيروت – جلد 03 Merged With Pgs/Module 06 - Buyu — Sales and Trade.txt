Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Implied Gift in Conditional Emancipation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on legal effect of 'اعتقته عني'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the opposing views of Abu Yusuf and the two shaykhs on the legal effect of a woman's statement 'اعتقته عني' and evaluate the Hanafi response based on iqtiḍā'.

Core Idea
When a woman says 'free him for me', her ownership of the slave is implied by necessity, and possession can be dropped.

This passage delves into an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal implication (iqtiḍā') of a woman's statement when she asks a master to free a slave on her behalf. The core issue is whether her statement alone establishes ownership via an implied gift, or whether possession (qabd) is essential. Abu Yusuf argues that possession cannot be dropped, while the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) maintain that legal necessity allows dropping the possession condition, similar to how acceptance is dropped in mutual exchange (ta'āṭī). Understanding this debate clarifies how Hanafi jurists prioritize textual implication and practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario and its legal dispute
A woman says to a slave's master 'اعتقته عني' (free him for me). The master frees him. Who owns the slave? Does emancipation come from her or the master? What happens to her marriage if she is married to the slave?
This sets the stage for the ikhtilaf.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position and its reasoning
Abu Yusuf holds that possession is required for a gift; without possession, the woman does not own the slave, so emancipation is from the master, patronage goes to the master, and her marriage is valid.
His argument: possession is a condition that cannot be dropped.
3
State the two shaykhs' position and its reasoning
The two shaykhs hold that ownership is established by implication (iqtiḍā') to correct speech, and possession can be dropped just as acceptance is dropped in mutual exchange. Thus emancipation is from the woman, and her marriage is invalidated.
Their argument: istihsan based on necessity.
4
Analyze the key argument: analogy to ta'āṭī and the distinction between condition and pillar
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad analogize dropping possession to dropping acceptance in sale by mutual exchange. Abu Yusuf responds that acceptance is a pillar (rukn) that can be dropped, whereas possession is a condition (shart) that cannot. The Hanafi response counters that possession can be dropped by legal implication in this specific context due to necessity.
This is the core of the qiyas khafi.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and the thamarah al-khilaf
The two shaykhs' view is upheld as mufta bihi. The practical consequence: the marriage is invalidated, and patronage goes to the woman.
The commentator's tarjih supports this.

Commentary
This passage contains an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) regarding the legal characterization of a woman's statement 'اعتقته عني' (free him for me) when she says it without mentioning a price. Abu Yusuf argues that the statement implies a gift requiring possession (qabd) for ownership to be established, and since possession is a condition that cannot be dropped, the emancipation does not take effect from her (instead from the master). The two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) respond using istihsan: since the statement can be interpreted as implying a gift, and since it is necessary to correct the speech of a rational person, ownership is established by implication, and possession can be dropped just as acceptance is dropped in sale by mutual exchange (ta'ati). They further argue that possession in a gift can never be dropped in ordinary circumstances, but here by way of legal implication (iqtiḍā') it can be dropped because the situation demands it. The ruling: the woman owns the slave by implied gift, then emancipates him, so the patronage (walā') goes to her, and her own marriage is invalidated because she owns herself as a slave? Actually, the text says her marriage is invalidated (يَفسد نكاحها) because she becomes the owner of her husband? Wait, the ruling is: if a woman says to the master of a slave 'free him for me', and the master frees him, then the slave becomes free, and the woman must pay the value? No, in the dispute, the consequence is that the marriage of the woman to the slave (if she is married to him) is invalidated because she has become his owner? The text says: 'ويفسد نكاحها' – her marriage is invalidated. This is because if the woman owns the slave, she cannot have him as a husband. The author explains that in the case where the slave is her husband, the marriage is invalidated because she now owns him. Abu Yusuf avoids this by not establishing ownership via gift without possession. The two shaykhs rule that ownership is established and the marriage is invalidated. The passage then concludes with a comparison to mutual exchange (ta'ati) where acceptance is dropped, but possession cannot be dropped in a gift. The final position: the two shaykhs' view is upheld (since the commentator supports them). This is a nuanced debate on the legal construction of speech and the role of conditions in contracts.

Key Terms
اقتضاء (iqtiḍā'): Legal implication: deriving a necessary legal consequence from a statement without explicit mention.
تعاطي (ta'āṭī): Mutual exchange: a sale without verbal offer and acceptance, e.g., giving money and taking goods.

Multiple Sales of a Slave for Maintenance Debt
Ruling with example on selling a slave multiple times for maintenance debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling a slave multiple times for maintenance debt and distinguish it from other debts.

Core Idea
A slave can be sold repeatedly for maintenance debt, but only once for other debts.

This passage clarifies a nuanced ruling in Hanafi sales law: a slave who owes maintenance (nafaqah) to his wife can be sold multiple times to cover each new obligation, while for any other debt, the slave is sold only once. The author of 'Umda al-Ri'aya corrects a previous commentator's misunderstanding, emphasizing that the second sale is for a new debt, not a remainder. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the rule correctly.

Guided Path
1
Classify the debt type
Identify whether the debt is maintenance (nafaqah) or another type. This determines the ruling on multiple sales.
Maintenance debt arises from the financial support owed to a wife.
2
Analyze the example
Consider a slave who marries with his master's permission, the judge imposes maintenance, and a debt of 1000 dirhams accumulates. He is sold for 500 (his value). If the debt was maintenance, the slave can be sold again; if other, not.
The buyer must be aware of the maintenance debt.
3
Recognize the commentator's error
Earlier commentators implied that the second sale was for the leftover remainder of the first debt. The author of 'Umda corrects this: the second sale is for a new maintenance obligation that arises after the first debt is paid.
'Umda's correction avoids confusion in application.
4
Apply the principle
For any new maintenance debt that arises after a previous sale, another sale is permitted. For non-maintenance debts, only one sale is allowed regardless of remaining balance.
The new obligation must be a fresh debt, not a remainder.

Father's Sale of Son's Movable Property for His Own Maintenance
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on father selling son's movable property for his maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a father selling his adult son's movable property for his own maintenance and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A father may sell his absent adult son's movable goods for his own maintenance — Abu Hanifa's istihsan overrides the qiyas of the Sahibayn.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the scope of paternal guardianship over an adult son's property. While guardianship generally ends at puberty, Imam Abu Hanifa makes an exception for the father's own maintenance needs, allowing him to sell the son's movable goods when the son is absent. The Sahibayn disagree, applying strict qiyas. Understanding this ruling reveals how istihsan operates within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position and its conditions
Identify exactly what Abu Hanifa permits: the father may sell his adult son's movable property (not real estate) for his own maintenance, but only when the son is absent. The father may take only as much as needed.
The matn simply says 'the father sells' – the commentary expands the conditions.
2
Identify the opposing view and its basis
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) prohibit the sale entirely. Their dalil: guardianship ends at puberty, so by qiyas, the father cannot sell any property of the adult son, just as he cannot sell real estate or property for other debts.
The text says 'this is the qiyas' – meaning the Sahibayn follow the strict analogy.
3
Trace the Hanafi response (istihsan)
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: maintenance is a special obligation that is wajib before adjudication, unlike other debts. Moreover, movable goods are perishable, so selling them is an act of preservation (hifz) – this is the basis for istihsan overriding qiyas.
The commentary explains the distinction in the brackets about 'maintenance is not like other debts'.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
Under Abu Hanifa's view, the sale is valid and the father may use the proceeds. Under the Sahibayn, the sale is invalid and the son can reclaim the property. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, as recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
The mufta bihi is stated in the analytical toolbox.

Manumission for a Consideration: Ruling and Suretyship
States the basic ruling on 'itq 'ala mal and its implications for suretyship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on manumission for a consideration and explain why suretyship is valid for its payment but not for kitabah.

Core Idea
In 'itq 'ala mal, freedom is immediate, the payment is a valid debt—suretyship is valid, unlike kitabah.

This passage from al-Hidayah's commentary explains the distinction between manumission for a consideration ('itq 'ala mal) and the kitabah contract. It establishes that when a master frees a slave for a payment and the slave accepts, the slave is freed immediately and owes the payment as a valid debt. This type of debt is enforceable by suretyship, unlike the kitabah payment which can drop.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two forms
Distinguish between manumission using 'ala (upon) and ba (for). Both indicate immediate compensation in this context, not condition.
The contextual indication (qarinat al-hal) shows the master intends immediate freedom with payment, not a conditional suspension.
2
Understand acceptance conditions
The slave must accept the full payment in the same session. Partial acceptance is disputed: Abu Hanifa rejects it, Sahibayn accept it.
This follows the principles of mu'awadah (counter-exchange) where mutual consent in the same session is required.
3
Classify the debt as valid
The payment becomes a dayn sahih (valid debt) because it does not drop except by payment or discharge. This makes suretyship (kafalah) permissible.
A valid debt is one that cannot be extinguished without the creditor's consent or full payment.
4
Contrast with kitabah
In kitabah, the payment is not a valid debt because it can drop if the slave becomes unable to pay. Thus suretyship is not valid for it.
This is a key practical difference: the master in kitabah cannot have a surety for the debt.
5
Apply the ruling
Given a valid manumission for a consideration, a third party can act as surety for the freed slave's debt. The debt is enforceable like any debt of a free person.
This ruling is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school according to zahir al-riwayah.

Seven Differences Between the Mu'allaq and Mukatab
Lists seven differences between mu'allaq and mukatab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the mu'allaq slave and the mukatab by stating the seven differences and applying them to novel scenarios.

Core Idea
The mu'allaq slave's freedom depends solely on his own payment; the mukatab's freedom is contractual and gradual.

In Hanafi fiqh, a master may condition a slave's freedom on the slave's payment of a sum. This creates a status distinct from the mukatab (slave under kitabah contract). Though similar on the surface, the rulings differ in seven key areas, from acceptance and saleability to the effect of death and third-party payment. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying the law in cases involving conditional manumission.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish acceptance requirement
Identify that the mu'allaq does not require the slave's acceptance, unlike the mukatab whose kitabah contract requires acceptance.
The mu'allaq's freedom is a unilateral conditional suspension; the mukatab's freedom is contractual.
2
Analyse effect of rejection
Note that if the mu'allaq refuses, the condition remains and he is not freed; rejection does not nullify. For the mukatab, rejection nullifies the kitabah.
Rejection has different legal consequences.
3
Examine saleability
The mu'allaq can be sold before the condition occurs; the mukatab cannot be sold without his consent.
This protects the mukatab's partial freedom.
4
Evaluate third-party payment
If someone else pays on the mu'allaq's behalf, he is not freed because the condition is his own payment. For the mukatab, third-party payment frees him.
The condition is personal to the mu'allaq.
5
Consider effect of master's death
If the master dies and the mu'allaq pays the heirs, he is not freed because the condition (payment to the master) is lost. The mukatab would be freed by payment to heirs.
The condition is tied to the master's lifetime.
6
Check session requirement
If the condition is phrased with 'in' (إن), the mu'allaq must pay in the same session. The mukatab has no such session requirement.
The word 'in' imposes a time constraint.
7
Analyse death leaving wealth
If the mu'allaq dies leaving enough to pay, he is not ruled free. The mukatab is ruled free in his last moment if he leaves enough.
The mu'allaq remains a slave; the mukatab becomes free.

When a slave buys himself for a specific item that perishes, the value of the slave is owed—not the value of the item.

Conditional Manumission: When a Slave-Girl Refuses Marriage After Being Freed
Ruling on conditional manumission with refusal to marry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a slave-girl is freed on condition that she marry the commander and she refuses, and explain why no compensation is due, distinguishing the case from divorce.

Core Idea
In manumission, stipulating compensation on a third party is invalid, so the slave-girl is freed unconditionally and cannot be compelled to marry.

This lesson examines a distinct ruling: a man says to his slave-girl, 'I have freed you for a thousand on condition that you marry me,' and she refuses. The text from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies that she is freed, owes nothing, and cannot be forced to marry. This ruling is contrasted with divorce, where similar stipulations on a third party are valid. Understanding this distinction highlights the different legal nature of manumission and divorce in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Read the passage: a commander says to a slave-girl, 'I have freed you for a thousand on condition that you marry me.' She refuses.
The 'thousand' is a compensation (jual) promised by the commander, not the slave-girl.
2
State the Ruling
The slave-girl is freed, she is not compelled to marry, and the commander owes nothing.
She becomes owner of herself, so she has choice.
3
Understand the Basis
The ruling is based on the principle that in manumission, stipulating compensation on a third party (the commander) is not permissible, unlike in divorce.
Al-Hidayah is quoted: 'Whoever says to another: Free your slave for a thousand upon me, nothing is incumbent upon him, and the manumission occurs on behalf of the commanded.'
4
Contrast with Divorce
Contrast with a similar statement in divorce: 'Divorce your wife for a thousand upon me' - the commander is liable for the thousand and the divorce occurs on behalf of the husband.
The difference: stipulating compensation on a third party is valid in divorce but not in manumission.
5
General Principle
Manumission is an act of piety that cannot be made conditional on a financial burden on a third party, whereas divorce can involve such conditions.
This reflects the higher sanctity of freeing a slave.

Ruling on Mudabbar: Prohibition of Sale and Permissibility of Use
Ruling on mudabbar and interpretation of conditional tadbir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sale and use of a mudabbar and explain the rationale (talil) distinguishing permissible from prohibited transactions.

Core Idea
A mudabbar cannot be sold because it extinguishes his right to freedom, but use and hire are permissible as they do not transfer ownership.

The mudabbar is a slave promised freedom upon the master's death. This lesson explores the legal restrictions that protect that future right. The Hanafi school draws a sharp line: transactions that transfer ownership are void, while those that only affect benefits are allowed. The rationale is rooted in preserving the slave's claim to freedom while acknowledging the master's retained ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of acts
List the prohibited acts (sale, gift) and the permissible acts (use, hire, marriage, intercourse) concerning the mudabbar as stated in the text.
Prohibited acts are those that transfer ownership (milk al-raqabah).
2
Understand the talil (reason) for prohibition
Explain why sale and gift are prohibited: they nullify the slave's right to freedom after the master's death. Use the text's statement that ownership of the person remains with the master, but the right to freedom cannot be voided.
The talil is that selling would 'nullify the right that the slave has acquired of freedom after the master's death.'
3
Analyze the conditional tadbir dispute
Examine the intra-Hanafi disagreement: some scholars consider a mudabbar restricted if a condition is mentioned (e.g., 'if I die within a year'), while the chosen view treats such conditions as perpetual, making the tadbir absolute.
The preferred view (mufta bihi) is that the tadbir is absolute (mutlaq).
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a scenario where a mudabbar is leased or where his child is born, state the ruling and justify using the principles from the text.
The child of a female mudabbar is also a mudabbar.

Sale of the Mudabbar: Hanafi vs Shafi'i and the One-Third Rule
Inter-madhhab dispute on sale of mudabbar and one-third rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the sale of a mudabbar, explain the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i proof, and apply the one-third rule to various estate scenarios.

Core Idea
A mudabbar cannot be sold; he is like a bequest, freed from only one third of the estate.

The sale of a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death) is a point of contention between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. This lesson unpacks the proofs each school uses and the practical consequences of their differing positions. We also explore how the one-third rule operates when the mudabbar's value exceeds that portion of the estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi Position
State clearly that the Hanafi school prohibits the sale or gift of a mudabbar and that upon the master's death, the slave is freed from one third of the estate.
This ruling is based on the analogy (qiyas) between tadbir and a bequest (wasiyyah).
2
Examine the Shafi'i Proof
Review the hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) sold a mudabbar for a man with no other property. Understand why the Shafi'is consider this a valid counterproof.
The hadith is found in the six major collections.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Response
Learn the two Hanafi interpretations: (1) the mudabbar in that hadith was a 'restricted' (muqayyad) mudabbar, not absolute; (2) the sale was of services (khidmah) not of the person (raqabah).
Abu Ja'far explicitly states the sale was of service only, as recorded by al-Daraqutni.
4
Apply the One-Third Rule
Work through the three scenarios: (a) mudabbar's value ≤ 1/3 of estate → fully freed; (b) value > 1/3 → freed proportionally, slave works for the rest; (c) estate only has the mudabbar → freed 1/3, works for 2/3.
If the master has debts, creditors take precedence, and the slave must work to repay the full debt before any freedom.
5
Understand the Practical Difference (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
Note that the Hanafi prohibition makes a mudabbar non-transferable, affecting estate planning and transactions, while the Shafi'i permission allows the master to sell the slave if needed.
Both schools accept the one-third limitation, but only Hanafis forbid sale during the master's lifetime.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh regarding tadbir (the declaration that a slave will be freed upon the master's death). وبيع إن قال له : إن مت في سفري، أو مرضى هذا، أو إلى سنة، أو نحوها مما يمكن غالباً [0] قوله : وبيع؛ بصيغة المجهول، يعني إن علّق عتقه بموته مقيداً بقيد لا يجب وقوعه غالباً لا يكون مُدبَّرا ، فيجوز بيعه وهبته وغير ذلك من التصرفات ؛ وذلك لأنَّ السبب لم ينعقد في الحال ؛ للتردّد في وقوع الموت على تلك الصفة، بخلاف ما إذا عَلَّقَ عتقه بموته مطلقاً، فإنه كائن لا محالة ، فينعقد سبباً في الحال، ويمتنع إخراجه من ملك إلى ملك. [٦]قوله : مما يمكن ؛ أي من القيود التي يكون وقوعها ممكناً متردّداً بين أن يكون وبين أن لا يكون لا غالب الوقوع واحترز بـه عـن قـول المولى : وهو ابن ثمانين سنة مثلاً : إن مت إلى مئة سنة فأنت حرّ، فإنه يكون مدبّراً مطلقاً، كما مرّ.
And he is sold if he said to him: 'If I die on this journey of mine, or this illness, or within a year,' or similar things that are generally possible. [0] His saying: 'and he is sold' in the passive voice, meaning if he attached his freedom to his death restricted by a qualifier whose occurrence is not normally obligatory, then he is not a mudabbar, so it is permissible to sell him, give him as a gift, and other dispositions. This is because the cause (sabab) is not established immediately due to the doubt (taraddud) in the occurrence of death in that manner. Contrary to if he attached his freedom to his death unconditionally, for it is necessarily occurring, so the cause is established immediately, and it is forbidden to remove him from ownership. [6] His saying: 'that are generally possible' meaning from the qualifiers whose occurrence is possible, fluctuating between being and not being, not predominantly occurring. By this he excluded the master's statement, when he is, for example, eighty years old, 'If I die before a hundred years, you are free,' for then he is an absolute mudabbar, as has passed.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement that clarifies what constitutes a valid tadbir. The matn states that if the master says 'If I die in this journey/illness/within a year' etc., the slave is not a mudabbar and can be sold. The commentary [0] explains that because the condition is not normally certain to occur (the death is not certain to happen in that specific manner), the legal cause (sabab) for tadbir is not established at the moment of the declaration. Therefore, the slave remains in normal ownership and can be sold or given. In contrast, unconditional tadbir ('You are free when I die') makes the cause immediate because death is certain. Commentary [6] provides a further nuance: if the condition is highly likely (e.g., a very old man saying 'if I die before 100' — which is almost certain), it is still considered absolute tadbir because the death is likely to occur within that term. So the key is whether the condition's occurrence is 'ghalib' (predominant/normal) or merely 'mumkin' (possible with doubt).

Key Terms
تدبير مقيد (tadbir muqayyad): Tadbir that is conditional on a specific event that is not certain to occur, such as 'if I die on this journey'.
سبب (sabab): The legal cause that triggers a ruling; here the cause for tadbir is the declaration linked to certain death.
غالب الوقوع (ghalib al-wuqu'): An event that is predominant in occurrence; if the condition is ghalib, it is treated as absolute tadbir.

Checkpoint
A master says to his slave: 'If I die in this illness, you are free.' The master recovers. Which of the following is correct according to the Hanafi school?
A) The slave is a mudabbar and cannot be sold.
B) The slave is not a mudabbar and can be sold.
C) The slave is a mudabbar only if he is over 80 years old.
D) The sale requires the slave's consent.

Key Takeaway
A conditional tadbir with an uncertain condition does not make the slave a mudabbar; the master retains full ownership and can sell.
