Prohibition Through Breastfeeding (Rada')
Explains that all previously listed female relatives are also prohibited through breastfeeding.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that breastfeeding creates the same marriage prohibitions as blood lineage and identify the Quranic and prophetic proofs for this ruling.

Core Idea
Breastfeeding establishes the same marriage prohibitions as blood lineage.

This lesson examines the core principle that breastfeeding (rada') creates legal prohibitions for marriage identical to those of lineage. The passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah illustrates this rule by explaining that all previously listed female relatives (mothers, daughters, sisters, aunts, etc.) are equally prohibited when the relationship comes through breastfeeding. The proof is drawn directly from the Quran and the Sunnah, making this a clear ruling with strong textual basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Marriage to a female relative through breastfeeding. The ruling is haram (prohibited).
The ruling applies to all categories of mahram by lineage.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites Quran 4:23 and the hadith 'Whatever is prohibited through lineage is prohibited through breastfeeding.' Identify these as the primary proof.
The hadith is reported by al-Tabarani and in the two Sahihs.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the dalil prove the ruling? The Quran explicitly mentions foster mothers and sisters; the hadith generalizes to all relations. The imperative of prohibition is clear.
Both dalil have qati' thubut and dalala, giving certainty.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Is there disagreement? The text does not mention intra-Hanafi or inter-madhhab ikhtilaf here. The ruling is muttafaq alayh among all four schools? Actually, Shafi'i require five specific feedings, but the prohibition as such is agreed. The text doesn't discuss details, so we note that the basic principle is unanimous.
Further details on how many feedings or age limits come in later sections.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Which position is mufta bihi? The Hanafi position is that breastfeeding within the first two years establishes prohibition. The text follows the well-known view.
No tarjih needed as it is the standard Zahir al-Riwayah position.

Dispute on Conditionals: Marrying a Slave Woman Despite Capacity for Free Woman
Hanafi-Shafi'i usul dispute on conditional statements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the implication of conditional statements and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument based on mafhum al-mukhalafah.

Core Idea
A conditional links the ruling to its condition; it does not automatically negate the ruling when the condition is absent.

This lesson examines a classic usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools concerning the interpretation of conditional statements in the Quran. The immediate legal issue is whether a free man who has the financial means to marry a free woman may instead marry a slave woman. The Shafi'i school relies on the principle of mafhum al-mukhalafah to argue that the conditional in Q.4:25 implies prohibition when the condition (lack of capacity) is absent, while the Hanafi school rejects this inference and permits the marriage. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi method of interpreting legal texts and the rejection of mafhum al-mukhalafah as a binding proof.

Guided Path
1
State the Legal Issue
Identify the specific question: Is it permissible for a free man who has financial capacity (tawl) to marry a slave woman?
The dispute centers on the interpretation of Q.4:25: {وَمَن لَّمْ يَسْتَطِعْ مِنكُمْ طَوْلًا}.
2
Identify the Shafi'i Proof
Understand the Shafi'i argument: The conditional (ta'liq bi al-shart) necessitates the absence of the ruling when the condition is absent. Thus, the verse implies that if one has tawl, marrying a slave woman is prohibited.
This is based on mafhum al-mukhalafah (inferential meaning of opposition).
3
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi school argues that the verse is silent on the case where tawl exists. The conditional only links the ruling to the condition; it does not imply negation. Additional reasons: the specification (takhsis) may be for praise/blame, not negation, and the ruling can have multiple causes.
Hanafis do not accept mafhum al-mukhalafah as a binding proof in this context.
4
Determine the Practical Difference
Note the thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi ruling: permissible (mubah) but better to marry a free woman; Shafi'i ruling: prohibited (haram).
This difference has real consequences for fatwa.
5
Apply the Principle to Other Conditionals
Generalize: The Hanafi view on conditionals applies across fiqh. For example, a command like 'If you are impure, purify' does not imply purification is not required when pure.
This is a foundational usul principle distinguishing Hanafi from Shafi'i methodology.

Commentary
This passage presents a classic usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i on the interpretation of conditional statements (ta'liq bi al-shart). The immediate legal issue is whether a free man who has the financial capacity (tawl) to marry a free woman may instead marry a slave woman. The Shafi'i position, based on the principle that a condition implies the negation of the ruling when the condition is absent (al-ta'liq bi al-shart yujibu al-'adam 'inda 'adam al-shart), holds that the Quranic verse {وَمَن لَّمْ يَسْتَطِعْ ...} (Q. 4:25) implies permissibility of marrying a slave woman only when one lacks capacity for a free woman, and thus prohibition when one has capacity. The Hanafi position, however, argues that the verse is silent on the case where capacity exists; the conditional merely links the ruling to the condition without negating it when the condition is absent. The Hanafi school therefore permits marrying a slave woman even when one has the means to marry a free woman, based on the general permissibility of marriage (Q. 4:24: {وَأُحِلَّ لَكُم مَّا وَرَاءَ ذَٰلِكُمْ}). The commentary in the text references the dispute over the principle of mafhum al-mukhalafah (inferential meaning of opposition), which the Hanafis reject in many contexts. The passage then provides the Hanafi refutation: that specifications (takhsis) have many other possible motivations (such as praise, blame, emphasis) besides negating the ruling, and thus the conditional does not necessarily indicate negation. Moreover, even if the condition were a cause ('illa), a ruling can be established by multiple causes, so the absence of one cause does not necessitate absence of the ruling. Thus, the Hanafi position is that marrying a slave woman remains permissible, though it is considered preferable (awla) not to do so when one can marry a free woman.

Key Terms
طول (tawl): Financial capacity or ability to marry a free woman, including the dowry (mahr) and maintenance (nafaqah).
التعليق بالشرط (al-ta'liq bi al-shart): The linking of a legal ruling to a condition such that the ruling is mentioned when the condition is present.
مفهوم المخالفة (mafhum al-mukhalafah): The inferential understanding that a ruling specified for a particular condition implies its opposite when the condition is absent.

The Effect of Fasting and Prayer on Valid Khalwa
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khalwa validity during worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on whether a wife's state of fasting or prayer prevents valid khalwa, and identify the intra-madhab dispute and the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Fasting and prayer do not invalidate khalwa — the wife's submission suffices for full mahr.

This passage delves into an intra-Hanafi debate: if a wife is fasting (qada' or nadhr) or praying during the marriage seclusion, is the khalwa still valid such that the full mahr becomes obligatory? The matn and commentary reveal two narrations within the school, with the soundest view holding that these states do not prevent. We will unpack the legal reasoning and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
Determine the legal question: does a wife's act of worship (fasting or praying) that prohibits intercourse prevent valid khalwa and thus reduce the mahr to half?
The five permanent impediments (illness, Ramadan fast, ihram, menstruation, postpartum) are agreed to prevent khalwa; fasting and prayer are debated.
2
State the Zahir al-Riwayah position
In the apparent riwayah, supererogatory fasting does not prevent khalwa. For obligatory fasting (qada', kaffarah, nadhr) there are two narrations, but the soundest (al-asahh) says it does not prevent.
Al-Khaniyyah and al-Binaayah attest to the soundest view being non-prevention.
3
Analyze the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa, on the soundest narration, holds that fasting and prayer do not prevent. In another narration, they do prevent. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ on the case of mahbub (severe illness) but not on the general rule.
The ikhtilaf is about the effect of worship that prohibits intercourse, not about the validity of the wife's submission.
4
Apply the mufta bihi ruling
The relied-upon position is that khalwa is valid regardless of fasting or prayer. Therefore, full mahr is wajib. The thamarah: in Hanafi law, a husband does not lose the right to full mahr if he secludes with his wife while she is fasting.
This differs from the Shafi'i school where khalwa would be invalid and only half mahr due.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of a woman being in a state of fasting or prayer on the validity of khalwa (valid seclusion with the husband) after marriage. The main issue: does being a fasting woman (whether for qada' of missed fasts or for a vow) or being engaged in prayer (obligatory or supererogatory) prevent the khalwa from being valid, thereby affecting the obligation of the full mahr? The matn states: 'Or a woman fasting qada', according to the soundest view, and nadhr in one narration, and with one of the five previously mentioned (impediments), no. And prayer is like fasting, whether obligatory or supererogatory.' The five impediments referred to earlier are: illness preventing intercourse, Ramadan fast, ihram, menstruation, and postpartum bleeding. The commentary then explains that the khalwa of such women (i.e., those with the five impediments) is valid and full mahr becomes obligatory, because the wife's duty is to deliver herself to the extent she can, and she has done so. Then it mentions a dispute about the mahbub (one suffering from a severe, love-induced illness) – Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that such a person is even more incapacitated than the sick, but the ruling is tied to the soundness of the organ, so she is like the sick. The footnotes elaborate: in the Khaniyyah, there are two narrations concerning fasting qada', expiations, and vows: one prevents, one does not; the soundest is non-prevention. Supererogatory fasting does not prevent. Al-Bahr suggests that obligatory fasting should prevent by agreement because it is prohibited to break it. But the relied-upon position is that it does not prevent. For prayer, the same principle applies: although invalidating prayer is sinful, it does not prevent valid khalwa. The reason is that the impediment here is not a physical barrier but a prohibition against intercourse, which does not render the wife legally unavailable; she can still be delivered in the sense of being available for the marriage. The hadith and legal principles indicate that the right of the husband is established by the contract, and the wife's obligation is to submit herself. Her being in a state of worship does not negate that submission; she is still considered to have been delivered, hence full mahr is due. The distinction between obligatory and supererogatory worship is not in the effect on khalwa but in the severity of sin. The overall ruling: the Hanafi school, on the soundest view, considers khalwa valid even if the wife is fasting (any type) or praying, as long as there is no physical bar to intercourse. This is based on the principle that the wife's duty of submission is fulfilled by her presence and readiness, even if intercourse is temporarily forbidden due to her state of worship.

Kinayah Expressions for Emancipation and the Concept of Riqq
Analysis of kinayah phrases for manumission and definition of riqq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three kinayah expressions for emancipation and explain why they require intention (niyyah) according to the Hanafi school, and distinguish between riqq and milk.

Core Idea
A master's ambiguous phrase about ownership or authority is not a valid manumission without clear intention.

In Hanafi fiqh, manumission (itq) is a solemn act that requires explicit language. However, sometimes a master uses indirect phrases that could imply emancipation but also other meanings. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah examines three such phrases and clarifies that they are kinayah (metaphorical) and only effect emancipation if accompanied by intention. The text also provides a precise legal definition of riqq (slavery) to distinguish it from ownership (milk). Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying rulings on manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three kinayah phrases
The passage lists three phrases: 'la milk li 'alayka', 'la sabil li ilayki/ 'alayki', and 'la riqq li 'alayka'. Classify each as kinayah and note the reason given for ambiguity.
The first phrase may imply lack of ownership due to sale, gift, or emancipation. The second indicates lack of access or authority. The third uses 'riqq' to intend 'milk'.
2
Understand why each phrase is kinayah
For each phrase, the author explains that it has multiple possible meanings beyond emancipation. This plurivocality prevents immediate manumission; intention is required.
Hanafi usul defines kinayah as a word that requires niyyah to specify the intended meaning.
3
Distinguish between riqq and milk
The passage gives detailed definitions: riqq is a legal disability (ajz shar'i) established as a punishment for disbelief; milk is a legal connection granting exclusive disposal. They can exist independently or together.
Riqq without milk: a captive in dar al-harb. Milk without riqq: owned commodities. Both: a purchased slave.
4
Apply the ruling: necessity of intention
From the analysis, derive that using any of these phrases does not automatically free a slave. The master must have intended itq. Otherwise, the slave remains in bondage.
This is the mufta bihi position; it is wajib to intend emancipation when using kinayah.

Conditional Emancipation: Takhliya, Partial Payment, and Ijbar
Expands rulings on takhliya, partial payment, and ijbar for conditional emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on conditional emancipation upon payment, distinguishing between valid payment through takhliya and invalid partial payment, and evaluate the dispute between Abu Hanifa and Zufar regarding compulsion (ijbar).

Core Idea
Full payment via takhliya frees the slave; partial payment does not, and the master is compelled to accept—based on istihsan not qiyas.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah expands on the rulings of conditional emancipation (ta'liq al-'itq bi al-ada). The key issues are: what constitutes valid payment (takhliya), whether partial payment triggers freedom, and whether the master can be forced to accept payment. The Hanafi school answers these using a blend of textual analysis and istihsan, distinguishing the mu'allaq (conditional slave) from the mukatab (kitaba slave).

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act
Determine the type of emancipation: conditional upon payment. The master says: 'If you pay me X, you are free.' The slave is a 'mu'allaq 'itqahu bi al-ada'.
This is not a kitaba contract; payment is a condition, not a price.
2
Understand takhliya as valid payment
Recognize that placing the money where the master can easily take it (takhliya) is legally equivalent to actual receipt. The master is deemed a receiver (qabid) by law.
This applies only to valid considerations (e.g., lawful currency). Invalid considerations like wine require actual acceptance.
3
Distinguish full vs partial payment
The condition is full payment (ada al-kull). Partial payment does not fulfill the condition, so the slave is not freed. This holds even if the master remits part of the debt.
Contrast with kitaba: remission is possible there because it is a commutative contract.
4
Analyze the ijbar (compulsion) dispute
The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and majority) holds that the master is compelled to accept full payment. This is implemented by deeming the master a receiver via takhliya. Zufar opposes, arguing by qiyas with oaths.
The proof: istihsan based on the purpose of the transaction—it is like a commutative contract at the end.
5
Compare with kitaba
Note the practical differences: partial remission works in kitaba but not here; takhliya works for both but only leads to freedom here with full payment; ijbar applies to mu'allaq but not to kitaba according to Zufar.
These distinctions are crucial for accurate fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is an expansion on the rulings of conditional emancipation (ta'liq al-'itq bi al-ada) from the text of al-Wiqayah. The author explains that if a master says 'if you pay me a sum, you are free,' payment is achieved by takhliya—placing the money where the master can easily take it—even if the master does not physically take it. The master is deemed a receiver by law, and the slave becomes free. This applies to valid considerations (e.g., lawful money). If the consideration is invalid (e.g., wine), the master is not deemed a receiver unless he willingly takes it. The passage then distinguishes between the mu'allaq (conditional slave) and the mukatab (writing contract slave). For the mu'allaq, partial payment does not effect emancipation because the condition is full payment. If the master remits part of the debt, emancipation is not achieved, as remission is not possible here. For the mukatab, remission of part of the kitaba money followed by payment of the rest does lead to emancipation, because kitaba is a commutative contract. The author also discusses the ruling of ijbar (compelling the master to accept). Hanafi scholars (al-Hidayah) hold that if the slave brings the full payment, the judge compels the master to accept and the slave is freed. This compulsion is implemented by deeming the master a receiver via takhliya. Zufar opposes this, arguing by qiyas that since it is a conditional oath, the master cannot be forced, similar to other oaths. The Hanafi response uses istihsan: considering the purpose, it is like a commutative contract at the end, thus the master is compelled. This reasoning also explains why the slave is not subject to sale or his earnings to the master before payment, and emancipation does not extend to a child born before payment. The author concludes that the master is deemed a receiver in both cases (full and partial payment) but emancipation only occurs with full payment. Some earlier mashayikh held that in partial payment the master is not compelled to accept, but the relied-upon position is that he is compelled only when full payment is made.

Key Terms
تخلیة (takhliya): The act of removing obstacles to taking possession and placing the money such that the master can take it without hindrance; the master is deemed a receiver by law.
إجبار (ijbar): Compelling the master to accept payment; implemented by deeming him a receiver through takhliya.
معلق عتقه بالأداء (mu'allaq 'itqahu bi al-ada): A slave whose emancipation is conditionally attached to payment of a sum by the master's statement.
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave under a kitaba contract, where emancipation is achieved upon paying the agreed price.
حط (hatt): Remission by the master of part of the debt in kitaba or conditional emancipation.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, conditional emancipation upon payment is a hybrid: a conditional statement in wording but a commutative transaction in purpose, leading to ijbar (compulsion) and the rule that only full payment via takhliya achieves freedom.
