Sick Person's Iqrar for Heir Invalid Without Consent
Ruling on a sick person's acknowledgment for an heir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a terminally ill person's acknowledgment to an heir and explain the hadith evidence that supports it.

Core Idea
A sick person's acknowledgment for an heir is void unless all other heirs confirm it.

The Quduri matn presents a clear rule that protects the rights of all heirs. When a person is terminally ill, any acknowledgment of debt or property in favor of an heir is invalid unless the other heirs confirm it. This lesson examines the ruling and its evidence from hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is iqrar (acknowledgment) made by a sick person for someone who would inherit from him. The ruling is batil (void).
The matn explicitly states 'bāṭil'.
2
Locate the Evidence
Two hadith are cited. The first from Ibn Abbas: 'No will is valid for an heir unless the heirs wish.' The second from Ja'far: 'No will for an heir nor acknowledgment of a debt.'
Both hadith are ahad (single narration), thus zanni al-thubut.
3
Analyze the Analogy
The Hanafi school analogizes acknowledgment to a will. Both involve transferring wealth or rights to an heir. Since a will for an heir is prohibited without consent, the same applies to acknowledgment.
The second hadith explicitly mentions 'no acknowledgment of a debt,' strengthening the analogy.
4
Note the Exception
The ruling is void unless the other heirs confirm the acknowledgment (tasdiq). Confirmation must be voluntary and without coercion.
The exception is mentioned directly in the matn: 'illā an yuṣaddiqahu baqiyyatu al-waraṯati'.
5
Understand the Wisdom
The ruling prevents a sick person from favoring one heir over others through a false acknowledgment, which would undermine the Quranic inheritance distribution.

Commentary
This lesson states a clear ruling from the matn: when a person is terminally ill, any acknowledgment he makes in favor of a legal heir (someone who would inherit from him) is invalid unless the other heirs confirm it. The reason is that such an acknowledgment could be a fraudulent attempt to give extra rights to one heir over others, similar to bequeathing to an heir, which is generally prohibited. The hadith from Ibn Abbas equates this to a will for an heir, which requires consent of other heirs. The second hadith explicitly says 'no will for an heir nor acknowledgment of a debt,' reinforcing that an acknowledgment of debt to an heir is treated like a bequest and thus invalid without consent. This ruling protects the rights of all heirs.

Key Terms
وارث (warith): Heir, someone entitled to inherit from the deceased.
تصديق (tasdiq): Confirmation or affirmation by the other heirs that the acknowledgment is true.
وصية (wasiyyah): Will or bequest. The hadith prohibits wasiyyah for an heir without consent.

Iqrar for a Triply Divorced Wife During Illness
Ruling on iqrar for a triply divorced wife during terminal illness with hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a terminally ill man's iqrar to his triply divorced wife is limited to the lesser of the debt and her inheritance share, and state the proof from the hadith.

Core Idea
In terminal illness, an iqrar to a divorced wife is restricted to the lesser of the debt and her inheritance to prevent fraud.

This passage from Al-Quduri addresses a scenario where a husband divorces his wife irrevocably (three times) during his terminal illness (marad al-mawt), and later acknowledges a debt in her favor. Hanafi law restricts the wife's recovery to the lesser of the debt and her inheritance share, preventing the husband from using a false iqrar to harm other heirs. The hadith condemning harm (idrar) in wills serves as the proof. We will unpack the ruling, its rationale, and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Pinpoint the exact situation: a husband divorces his wife three times during terminal illness, then acknowledges a debt, then dies. The ruling: she gets the lesser of the debt and her inheritance.
The wife still inherits because the divorce occurred during illness to prevent disinheritance.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites a hadith from Ibn Abbas: 'Causing harm in the will is among the major sins.' (Sunan al-Daraqutni). This is the proof.
The hadith is about wills, but the principle of prohibiting harm extends to iqrar.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith prohibits harmful wills. By analogy, an iqrar that would harm other heirs is also prohibited. Hence the limitation to the lesser amount prevents harm.
The Hanafi usul principle: al-amr bi al-nahy indicates prohibition; here the prohibition implies the iqrar is only valid to the extent it does not harm.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some schools may allow the full debt if proven genuine. The Hanafi school restricts regardless to block fraudulent claims.
The Hanafi ruling is mufta bihi due to the risk of idrar.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position: she receives the lesser of the debt and her inheritance share. This balances her right with protection of other heirs.
This is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is the standard Hanafi fatwa.

Commentary
This ruling addresses a situation where a husband divorces his wife irrevocably (three times) during his terminal illness, and afterwards acknowledges a debt in her favor. Since she is no longer his wife due to the triple divorce, she does not inherit from him? Actually, in Hanafi law, a divorced woman during terminal illness may still inherit if the husband divorced her to deprive her of inheritance; but here the text says 'her inheritance from him' implying she does inherit? The ruling says she gets the lesser of the debt acknowledged and her inheritance share. This prevents the husband from favoring his ex-wife through a false debt while still ensuring she gets something. The hadith cited condemns causing harm in wills, indicating that such an acknowledgment could be a harmful act, so it is restricted. Thus, if the debt is larger than her inheritance, she only takes the inheritance amount; if smaller, she takes the debt. This balances potential fraud with her right.

Key Terms
إضرار (idrar): Causing harm, especially to heirs through fraudulent declarations.
الأقل من الدين ومن ميراثها (al-aqall min al-dayn wa min mīrāthihā): The lesser amount between the acknowledged debt and her inheritance share.

Rulings on the Missing Person: Guardian, Maintenance, and Marriage Bond
States initial rulings for a missing person: guardian, maintenance, no separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the missing person's marriage and explain both the standard position and the exceptional fatwa in times of fitna.

Core Idea
A missing person's wife remains his wife indefinitely — no separation unless fitna necessitates the Maliki view.

This lesson examines the initial rulings of the Book of the Missing Person (Kitab al-Mafqud). When a man disappears without news of his whereabouts or status, the Hanafi school mandates a guardian to preserve his property and provide for his family. Most crucially, the marriage bond is preserved until certainty of death or divorce arrives. We will explore the proofs from hadith and companion practice, and understand the exceptional fatwa that may be issued in times of fitna according to Imam Malik's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the missing person and the judge's duties
Define who is a mafqud (missing person) and list the three obligations of the judge: appoint a guardian to preserve property, collect debts, and spend on wife and children from the missing person's wealth.
The judge's appointment of a guardian is wajib (obligatory).
2
Extract the core ruling on the marriage
State the explicit ruling: 'no separation is made between him and his wife.' Understand that the marriage remains valid and the wife cannot remarry as long as the husband's death is not confirmed.
This is the default Hanafi position from the matn and hadith.
3
Examine the hadith proof and its legal implication
Analyze the hadith from Mughira ibn Shu'ba: 'The wife of the missing person is his wife until news reaches her.' Recognize that the ruling is derived from the imperative to preserve the marriage until definitive news (death or divorce) arrives.
The hadith is a strong dalil for the Hanafi position.
4
Compare the Maliki position and the Hanafi response
Understand Imam Malik's view that the wife may be separated after a four-year waiting period plus 'idda. Note the Hanafi response: this is allowed only in times of fitna (widespread tribulation) and is adopted as a mufta bihi exception.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is whether the wife can remarry before confirmed death.
5
Understand the conditional fatwa (mufta bihi)
Learn that in normal circumstances, the Hanafi ruling applies. However, if fitna prevails, the mufti may issue a fatwa according to Imam Malik's view to prevent harm to the wife. This shows the flexibility of the Hanafi school.
The authority_weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin; the tarjih authority is the mufti's discretion based on fitna.

Book of the Missing Person. He (the author) said - may Allah have mercy on him - When a man disappears and his whereabouts are not known, and it is not known whether he is alive or dead, the judge appoints someone to preserve his property, manage it, collect his rights, and spend on his wife and children from his wealth, and no separation is made between him and his wife. Evidence: (1) Hadith for proof of 'When a man disappears...' from Mughira ibn Shu'ba: The Messenger of Allah said: 'The wife of the missing person is his wife until news reaches her.' ... (2) Saying of the Companion for proof of 'spend on his wife and children from his wealth' that Ibn Abbas and Ibn Umar discussed the wife of the missing person and said: She waits four years, then observes the waiting period of death, then they mentioned maintenance, and Ibn Umar said: She has maintenance because she keeps herself available for him. ... (3) Hadith for proof of 'no separation between him and his wife'... (4) Saying of the Companion for proof of 'no separation' from Ali: 'Regarding the wife of the missing person when he returns and she has remarried, she is his wife; if he wishes he divorces, if he wishes he keeps her, and she is not given a choice' ... Principle: No separation between the missing person and his wife according to Imam Abu Hanifa. However, in times of fitna, fatwa is given according to Imam Malik's view, i.e., after four years of waiting and 'idda, the woman is given the option to remarry.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's matn begins the Book of the Missing Person (Kitab al-Mafqud). It defines the rulings for a person who has disappeared without any knowledge of his location or whether he is alive or dead. The ruling: The judge (qadi) must appoint a trustee or guardian to preserve the missing person's property, manage his affairs, collect any debts owed to him, and provide for the maintenance of his wife and children from that wealth. Critically, the marriage remains intact; the qadi does not separate the wife from the missing husband. This is based on the hadith from Mughira ibn Shu'ba that 'the wife of the missing person is his wife until she receives news' and on the practice of the Companions. The Urdu commentary adds that according to Imam Abu Hanifa, separation is never ordered, but during times of widespread fitna (tribulation), the mufti may issue a fatwa according to Imam Malik's view, which allows the wife to remarry after a four-year waiting period and the 'idda of death. This shows the flexibility of the Hanafi school in adapting to changing circumstances.

Key Terms
المفقود (al-mafqud): A person whose location is unknown and whose life or death is uncertain.
تربص (tarabbus): Waiting period for the wife of a missing person before she can take any action.
