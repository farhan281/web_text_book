Introduction to Al-Quduri Maa Ahadithih Wa Usoolih
Introduces the book's methodology and features

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the distinctive methodology of this work and identify its three key components: the Al-Quduri matn, hadith proofs, and usul al-fiqh principles.

Core Idea
This work uniquely provides three hadith proofs and legal principles for every Hanafi ruling in Al-Quduri.

The opening pages of this book introduce a unique resource for Hanafi fiqh study. Unlike a bare matn or a simple translation, this work aims to equip the student with both textual evidence (hadith) and jurisprudential reasoning (usul) for each ruling. Understanding this methodology will help you appreciate how Hanafi fiqh is rooted in both transmitted sources and systematic legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text
Recognize that the base text is Al-Mukhtasar Al-Quduri, a core Hanafi matn authored by Imam Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Quduri (d. 428 AH). This matn summarizes Hanafi fiqh rulings.
Al-Quduri is a standard beginner text in the Hanafi school.
2
Understand the methodology
Note that for each mas'ala (ruling), the book provides three prophetic hadith as evidence. The front matter explicitly states: 'for each and every mas'ala there are three ahadith.'
This emphasizes the textual foundation of Hanafi rulings.
3
Recognize the inclusion of usul
For most mas'ala, the book also explains the usul (legal principles) behind the ruling. This bridges transmitted evidence (hadith) with jurisprudential reasoning (usul al-fiqh).
Hanafi usul includes tools like qiyas, istihsan, and urf.
4
Appreciate the pedagogical value
This work is designed as a comprehensive study aid: it not only presents rulings but also substantiates them with proof texts and explains the methodological derivation. This deepens understanding and strengthens one's ability to apply fiqh.
The book is intended for students who want both knowledge and reasoning.

This is the opening front matter of the book 'Al-Quduri Maa Ahadithih Wa Usoolih'. The author introduces the work as a commentary on the classical Hanafi matn 'Al-Mukhtasar Al-Quduri' (commonly known as Al-Quduri). This lesson introduces the unique methodology of the book 'Al-Quduri with his Hadith and Usool'. Introduction
Features of this book:
1. It contains the text of Al-Quduri with diacritical marks, so that reading the text becomes easy.
2. For every single issue there is a verse, or a hadith, or a saying of a Companion, or a saying of a Successor.
3. It clarifies which verse it is, which hadith it is, which Companion's saying it is, and which Successor's saying it is, so that the strength and weakness of the issue may be known.
4. All these hadiths are taken from only 13 books, which are the earliest books.
5. The statements of scholars are a great thing, but given the subject matter, they have not been used as evidence.
6. For most of the text, principles (usool) are stated so that understanding the issue becomes easy.
7. A glossary is also given for understanding difficult words.
8. The book is written very simply, suitable to be placed before a teacher and taught in a classroom.

Commentary
This passage is the introduction (مقدمه) of the book 'Al-Quduri with his Hadith and Usool'. It outlines eight distinct features of the work. The author emphasizes that the matn (original text) of Al-Quduri is presented with full diacritics (إعراب), which aids in correct recitation and understanding. Each legal ruling is supported by evidence from one of four categories: a Qur'anic verse, a hadith, a saying of a Companion (qawl al-sahabi), or a saying of a Successor (qawl al-tabi'i). This allows the reader to assess the strength of each ruling. Notably, all hadiths are sourced from only thirteen early Hadith collections, ensuring authenticity. The author deliberately avoids using later scholarly opinions ('ibarat al-'ulama) as primary evidence, sticking to primary sources. Additionally, the book often explains the underlying legal principles (usool) to make the rulings easier to comprehend, and includes a glossary for difficult terms. The overall presentation is simple and classroom-friendly.

Key Terms
قدوری (Al-Quduri): Refers to the matn (text) of Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, a foundational Hanafi fiqh primer.
قول صحابی (qawl al-sahabi): A statement or legal opinion attributed to a Companion of the Prophet Muhammad, used as a source of evidence.
قول تابعی (qawl al-tabi'i): A statement or legal opinion attributed to a Successor (a follower of the Companions), used with lesser authority than a Companion.

Commentary
This is the introduction (مقدمه) to the edition of Al-Quduri titled 'Al-Quduri Ma'a Ahadithih wa Usoolih'. The author or editor lists eight features of this edition. First, the text (متن) of Quduri is presented with full diacritics (اعراب) to facilitate correct reading and recitation. Second, for each ruling, a citation is provided from the Qur'an, authentic hadith, the saying of a Companion (صحابی), or a Successor (تابعی). Third, the source is explicitly identified so that the user can assess the strength (قوت) or weakness (ضعف) of the evidence. Fourth, all hadith are drawn exclusively from thirteen early collections, ensuring reliability. Fifth, though the 'ibarat al-'ulama (expressions of scholars) are valuable, they are not used as direct proof in this work; this keeps the focus on primary textual evidence. Sixth, for most rulings, the underlying usul (principles) are clarified, aiding comprehension. Seventh, a brief lexicon is provided for difficult words. Eighth, the language is kept simple, making it suitable for a classroom setting where the teacher can use it as a direct teaching text. This introduction shows a pedagogical approach: ease of reading, evidential grounding, and clarity without sacrificing scholarly depth.

Key Terms
متن (matn): The core text of Al-Quduri, containing the fiqh rulings without commentary.
اعراب (i'rab): Diacritical marks indicating case endings, placed on the text to aid correct pronunciation and grammatical understanding.
اصول (usul): Principles of Islamic jurisprudence (usul al-fiqh) that are explained alongside the rulings.

The Hanafi Methodology of Attaching Hadith to Fiqh Rulings
Explaining the motivation and method behind compiling hadith evidence for Hanafi rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the motivation behind the compilation of 'Quduri ma'a Ahadithihi wa Usoolihi' and identify the hierarchical sources (hadith, qawl sahabi, qawl tabi'i) that determine the legal weight of a Hanafi ruling.

Core Idea
Hanafi fiqh is not based on analogy alone; every ruling is supported by Prophetic hadith or Companion/Follower sayings.

This introduction to 'Al-Quduri ma'a Ahadithihi wa Usoolihi' reveals the painstaking effort to anchor every Hanafi ruling in textual evidence. Driven by a directive from Maulana Ashraf Ali Thanwi, the compiler worked to provide three hadiths per ruling, using newly available hadith collections. The result not only refutes the claim that Hanafis rely solely on qiyas but also gives students a clear framework: a ruling supported by a hadith is Sunnah, while one supported by a Companion or Follower saying is Mustahab.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the historical context
In the early 20th century, major Hanafi works like al-Hidayah and al-Quduri lacked systematic hadith references. Critics alleged that Hanafis relied solely on rational analogy (qiyas). This prompted a need to compile hadith evidence for every ruling.
2
Understand the directive of Thanwi
In 1943, Maulana Ashraf Ali Thanwi instructed his nephew, Maulana Zafar Ahmad Uthmani, to attach hadith references to every ruling in al-Hidayah, to satisfy both scholars and critics.
This directive was driven by a long-standing desire (inspired by a dream) to see Hanafi fiqh fully grounded in hadith.
3
Note the initial challenge and partial achievement
Maulana Zafar Ahmad Uthmani authored I'la' al-Sunan in 17 volumes, focusing on al-Hidayah. However, key hadith collections like Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah and Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq were still unpublished manuscripts, limiting his work.
These two collections are critical because they contain abundant sayings of Companions and Followers, which are foundational for many Hanafi rulings.
4
See the solution: utilizing newly published sources
When the Musannaf works became available, the present compiler (the author of this work) used them to extract qawl sahabi and qawl tabi'i, and also to find additional hadiths. This enabled him to provide three evidences for each ruling in both al-Hidayah and al-Quduri.
Using sources accepted by all madhahib (e.g., Bukhari's teachers) ensured cross-madhhab acceptability.
5
Learn the pedagogical benefit: distinguishing Sunnah from Mustahab
With the evidence now visible, students can immediately see whether a ruling is based on a hadith (making it Sunnah) or on a Companion/Follower saying (making it Mustahab). This helps in understanding the legal weight and authority of each ruling.
This distinction is crucial for fatwa and personal practice.

In compliance with this command, Hadhrat Maulana Zafar Ahmad Uthmani authored I'la' al-Sunan in seventeen volumes. Bukhari benefited from these two books to prepare his book Sahih al-Bukhari; both these persons are teachers of Bukhari. Since these two books were not available and were very voluminous, Hadhrat Maulana Zafar Ahmad Uthmani could not benefit from them, and due to this he could not set many hadiths. In my time, both these books had been published and become available, so I benefited greatly and brought almost all sayings of the Companions and sayings of the Followers from there. Because many of our detailed issues are based on sayings of the Companions and sayings of the Followers, it became very easy to verify every issue. The benefit was that evidence from sayings of the Companions and sayings of the Followers came for many issues of Hidayah, and hadiths also came. The result was that three hadiths were set for every issue of Hidayah, which is called 'Hidayah ma'a Hadithihi wa Usoolihi'. And the desire of Hadhrat Maulana Ashraf Ali Thanwi was fulfilled. The great benefit of setting three hadiths for every issue of the entire Quduri is that our students, while studying Quduri, will see the hadiths and also memorize them, and they will be satisfied which hadith is for which issue and where. Then they will also know whether there is a hadith for it, or a saying of a Companion, or a saying of a Follower. And this is a great success. Previously, hadiths were not set on Quduri, so finding hadith was very difficult; now it has become easy. All praise to Allah.

Commentary
This passage is the introduction (مقدمه) of the work 'Al-Quduri ma'a Ahadithihi wa Usoolihi'. It explains the motivation behind compiling hadith evidence for Hanafi rulings. The author begins by recounting a directive from Hadhrat Maulana Ashraf Ali Thanwi to his nephew, Maulana Zafar Ahmad Uthmani, to attach hadith to every ruling in al-Hidayah. The purpose was to demonstrate that Hanafi fiqh is rooted in authentic sources, not merely analogy (qiyas). At that time, major Hanafi works like al-Mabsut, al-Asl, Jami' al-Saghir, etc., did not include hadith references. Maulana Zafar Ahmad Uthmani compiled 'I'la' al-Sunan' in 17 volumes, focusing on Hidayah. However, due to the unavailability of important hadith collections like Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah and Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq (which were still in manuscript form), he could not fully achieve the goal. Later, when these two monumental works were published, the present author (the compiler of this book) utilized them extensively to extract qawl sahabi (sayings of Companions) and qawl tabi'i (sayings of Followers), which form the basis of many detailed Hanafi rulings. By providing three proofs (hadith, qawl sahabi, or qawl tabi'i) for each ruling in both Hidayah and Quduri, the compiler fulfilled Thanwi's wish and refuted the allegation that Hanafis lack hadith support. The introduction also highlights a pedagogical benefit: students can now identify whether a ruling is based on a hadith (making it Sunnah) or on a Companion/Follower saying (making it Mustahab), thereby understanding its legal weight. The work simplifies the study of fiqh by combining rulings with their textual evidence.

Key Terms
قول صحابی (qawl sahabi): A statement or legal opinion attributed to a Companion of the Prophet Muhammad (صلى الله عليه وسلم).
قول تابعی (qawl tabi'i): A statement or legal opinion from a Follower (Tabi'i), i.e., a Muslim who met a Companion but not the Prophet.
قدوری (Quduri): A foundational Hanafi legal text (matn) authored by Abu al-Husayn Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Quduri (d. 428 AH). It is one of the earliest and most concise works on Hanafi fiqh.
ھدایہ (Hidayah): A major Hanafi fiqh work by Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani (d. 593 AH), which is a commentary on his own earlier work, Bidayat al-Mubtadi. It is a key reference for Hanafi fatwa.

Methodology of Al-Quduri: Textual Evidence for Hanafi Rulings
Preface explaining the inclusion of hadith, sahabi, and tabi'i narrations for each mas'ala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the purpose of including hadith, qawl sahabi, and qawl tabi'i in Al-Quduri, and distinguish between sunnah and mustahabb based on the source of evidence.

Core Idea
Hanafis do not rely solely on qiyas; this preface provides the textual evidence strategy to prove contrary.

This preface introduces the methodology of the book 'Al-Quduri ma'a Ahadithihi wa Usoolihi'. The author explains why he includes three narrations for each mas'ala: to show that Hanafis have strong textual support and to help students and scholars determine the legal weight of each ruling. Understanding this preface is key to appreciating how Hanafi fiqh engages with primary sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Motivation
The author states the purpose: to provide hadith, qawl sahabi, and qawl tabi'i for each mas'ala, fulfilling the wish of Maulana Ashraf Ali Thanwi and refuting the accusation that Hanafis only use qiyas.
This is a response to Ahl al-Hadith critics.
2
Understand the Classification of Rulings
If a ruling is based on a hadith, the action is categorized as sunnah; if based on qawl sahabi or qawl tabi'i, it is mustahabb. This distinction helps determine the strength of the ruling.
In Hanafi fiqh, sunnah is stronger than mustahabb.
3
Recognize the Source Limitations
The author uses only 12 early authoritative sources: the six canonical hadith collections, two musannafs, Musnad Ahmad, Tabarani, Dar Qutni, and Quranic verses. This ensures authenticity and wide acceptance.
All schools recognize these books as reliable.
4
Appreciate the Clear Labelling
The author explicitly labels each narration as hadith, qawl sahabi, or qawl tabi'i, unlike vague terms like 'athar'. This makes it easier for students to identify the type of evidence.
Full references are provided for each narration.
5
Reflect on the Benefits for Students
Students can now memorize hadith alongside the mas'ala, know the exact source, and confidently determine the ruling's weight. This was a major improvement over previous works.
The author considers this a great success.
