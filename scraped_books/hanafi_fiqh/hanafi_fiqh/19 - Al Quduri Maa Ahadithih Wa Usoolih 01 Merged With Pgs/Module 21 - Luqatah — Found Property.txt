Found Property as a Trust and Condition of Witness
Definition of found property as trust and its condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for found property and identify the condition that makes it a trust, and apply the announcement duration rule for items under ten dirhams.

Core Idea
Found property is a trust, not ownership — but only if the finder declares his intent with witnesses.

This lesson opens the Hanafi book of Luqata (found property). The first ruling clarifies the legal nature of found property: it is a trust (amānah) in the finder's hands, not ownership. However, this trust status is conditional — the finder must take witnesses to confirm his intention to preserve and return. The text also establishes a threshold for the announcement period: items worth less than ten dirhams require only a few days of announcement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that found property (luqata) is a trust (amānah) in the hands of the finder. This means the finder does not own it but is entrusted to preserve and return it.
This is the default legal classification.
2
Identify the condition for trust status
Trust status is conditional: the finder must take witnesses (ishhād) that he is taking the item to preserve and return it to its owner.
Without ishhād, the finder becomes liable (ḍāmin) for the item if damaged or lost.
3
Identify the announcement duration threshold
If the found item is worth less than ten dirhams, the announcement period is a few days (the exact number is specified elsewhere).
Items worth ten dirhams or more require a full year of announcement.

Commentary
This opening from the Book of Luqata (found property) establishes two key rulings. First, found property is a trust (amānah) in the finder's hands, not an ownership, provided the finder declares witnesses that he intends to preserve and return it. This protects the finder from liability if the item is lost without negligence. Second, if the found item is worth less than ten dirhams, the finder need only announce it for a few days (the number is specified later in the text, but here it says 'for days'). The Hanafi school derives the amānah status from the hadith of the Prophet (ṣallallāhu ʿalayhi wa sallam) and differentiates between valuable and insignificant items.

Key Terms
اللُّقَطَة (al-luqata): Found property that is not livestock (which has separate rulings)
أَمَانَة (amānah): A trust; the finder is entrusted to preserve it, not own it
عَشَرَةِ دَرَاهِمَ (ʿasharati dirāhim): Ten dirhams; a threshold for announcing duration

Luqata as a Trust and Announcement Periods
States ruling on luqata as trust and its announcement period for small value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on luqata, explain the conditions for it to be a trust, and apply the announcement period for items worth less than ten dirhams.

Core Idea
Luqata is a trust only when the finder declares his intention to preserve and return it.

The finder of a lost item must not assume ownership immediately. Instead, the Hanafi school prescribes a protective regime: the item becomes a trust in the finder's hands if he explicitly states his intention to return it. The period of announcement varies with the item's value, and for low-value items, only a few days are required. This lesson unpacks the legal rule and its prophetic proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is picking up a found item (luqata). The ruling is that it becomes a trust (amanah) under specific conditions: the finder must declare his intention to preserve and return it. If he does not declare, it is not a trust.
2
Distinguish the announcement periods
If the item is worth ten dirhams or more, it must be announced for a full year. If less than ten dirhams, only a few days (e.g., three) are required. This distinction is based on the hadith about Ya'la ibn Murrah, though it is considered weak.
Weak hadith does not preclude practice; the principle of convenience for small items is accepted.
3
Examine the proofs
Multiple hadith are cited: the main one from Ubayy ibn Ka'b (Bukhari) establishes the obligation to announce and preserve. Others emphasize witnessing and the one-year rule for valuable items. The weak hadith from Ya'la provides the exception for small value.
The Hanafi principle that a command (amr) indicates obligation (wujub) is applied to the command 'announce it'.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars require witnessing for the luqata to be a trust. The Hanafi school accepts the declaration without witnesses, though witnessing is recommended.
The other position is based on the hadith from 'Iyad ibn Himar.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from Al-Quduri's matn on the Book of Luqata. The author states that a found item (luqata) is considered a trust (amanah) in the finder's hands, provided that the finder explicitly declares his intention to take it for preservation and return. The ruling then differentiates based on value: if the item is worth less than ten dirhams, the finder announces it for a few days (a period specified in the hadith as three days for trifling items, or up to six). The text then provides multiple hadith proofs: the first from Suwayd ibn Ghafalah stressing announcement and preservation; the second from 'Iyad ibn Himar emphasizing witnessing and not concealing; the third from Zaid ibn Khalid about announcing for a year for valuable items; and a specific hadith from Ya'la ibn Murrah on the three/six day rule for small items. The commentary notes the weakness of the last hadith. Finally, a usul (principle) in Urdu defines luqata and clarifies that picking it up is permissible (ja'iz) if intending to return, and obligatory (wajib) if there is fear of loss.

Ruling on Valuable Luqata: Year-Long Announcement and Charity
States the ruling for valuable luqata with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a luqata worth ten dirhams or more, explain the obligation of a year-long announcement and subsequent charity, and describe the owner's option if they appear after charity.

Core Idea
Valuable luqata: announce a year, then give in charity; owner retains a choice.

This lesson addresses what a finder must do when the found item (luqata) is worth ten dirhams or more. The Hanafi school prescribes a year-long announcement, then charity, and grants the owner a unique choice if they appear later. We will examine the ruling, its proofs, and the wisdom behind the school's position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the value threshold
Note that the ruling applies to items worth ten dirhams or more (عشرة فصاعدًا). This triggers a full year of announcement.
Items under ten dirhams have a different rule covered earlier.
2
Describe the year-long announcement
The finder must announce the luqata for a full year (حولاً كاملاً). This is wajib according to Hanafi usul, derived from the command in the hadith.
The announcement should be made in places where the owner is likely to search.
3
State the charity requirement
If after one year the owner does not appear, the finder must give the item in charity (تصدق بها). This is an obligation, not an option.
The charity is a disposal of the item on behalf of the owner.
4
Explain the owner's option after charity
If the owner appears after the charity has been given, they have a choice: either approve the charity (and gain reward) or hold the finder liable for the value. This is based on the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab.
The finder does not own the item; they are a trustee.
5
Recognize the proofs cited
The text provides multiple dalils: the hadith of Zaid ibn Khalid al-Juhani (command to announce for a year), the hadith of 'Iyad ibn Himar (collecting and witnessing), and the practice of Ali and Umar (charity with owner's choice).
Hanafi relies on a combination of ahad hadith and qawl sahabi.

Rulings on Luqata: Expenses, Hal/Haram Equality, and Claimant Evidence
Matn and hadith proofs for luqata rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on luqata expenses, the equality of Hal and Haram, and the evidence requirement for claimants, and apply these rulings to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Luqata in Hal and Haram are equal: announce, spend with judge's permission as a debt, and require evidence from claimant.

This lesson covers three essential rulings from Al-Quduri's matn on lost-and-found items (luqata). The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines: the finder may recover expenses with judicial permission, the sacred precinct (Haram) receives the same treatment as outside it, and a claimant must bring proof. These rulings are supported by multiple hadith, which we will examine to understand the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sub-rulings
Read the matn and note the three distinct rulings: (1) expenses as a debt, (2) equality of Hal and Haram, (3) claimant evidence. Each is a separate legal issue.
The matn states these sequentially; the commentary expands them.
2
Analyze the conditions for each ruling
For expenses: finder needs judge's permission; expense becomes debt on owner; finder can withhold item. For Hal/Haram: same rules apply (announce for a year, then give in charity). For claimant: must bring evidence (bayyinah) before receiving the item.
No explicit mention of announcement period in matn; the Urdu conclusion clarifies one year.
3
Examine the hadith proofs
The text provides hadith evidence for each ruling: Aisha's instruction to benefit for Haram luqata; prohibition of taking pilgrim's luqata; general rule that evidence is on claimant (Ibn Abbas, Abu Huraira). Understand how each hadith supports the Hanafi position.
Some hadith are presented as proofs for the opponent (Shafi'i) but are interpreted by Hanafis to support equality.
4
Understand the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that luqata in the Haram cannot be taken by the finder at all (except for a person announcing it). Hanafis argue that it is the same as outside the Haram. The hadith of Aisha and Ibn Abbas are used to support the Hanafi view.
The difference lies in the interpretation of 'nor is its luqata permissible except for one who announces it'.
5
Synthesize the complete legal framework
Combine all rulings: a finder must announce the item (not explicitly in matn but implied), can spend with permission, and must give charity after a year. The owner must repay expenses. Claimant must provide evidence. The same applies to Haram.
This framework is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.

وجه: (١) قول التابعي لثبوت وَإِنْ كَانَ الْمُلْتَقِطُ غَنِيًّا لَمْ يَجُزْ عَنْ نَافِعٍ، أَنَّ رَجُلًا وَجَدَ لُقَطَةً فَجَاءَ إِلَى عَبْدِ اللَّهِ بْنِ عُمَرَ فَقَالَ لَهُ: إِنِّي وَجَدْتُ لُقَطَةً، فَمَاذَا تَرَى؟ فَقَالَ لَهُ ابْنُ عُمَرَ: «عَرِّفْهَا»، قَالَ: قَدْ فَعَلْتُ، قَالَ: «زِدْ»، قَالَ: قَدْ فَعَلْتُ، قَالَ: «لَا آمُرُكَ أَنْ تَأْكُلَهَا، وَلَوْ شِئْتَ لَمْ تَأْخُذْهَا» (سنن بيهقي، باب اللقطة بأكلها الغني والفقير إذا لم تعترف بعد تعريف سنة، نمبر 12063)
وجه: (٢) قول الصحابية لثبوت وَإِنْ كَانَ الْمُلْتَقِطُ غَنِيًّا لَمْ يَجُزْ جَاءَتِ امْرَأَةٌ إِلَى عَائِشَةَ فَقَالَتْ: إِنِّي وَجَدْتُ شَاةً، قَالَتِ: «اعْلِفِي، وَاحْلُي، وَعَرِّفِي» ثُمَّ عَادَتْ إِلَيْهَا ثَلَاثَةَ مَرَّاتٍ فَقَالَتْ: «أَتُرِيدِينَ أَنْ آمُرَكِ بِذَبْحِهَا؟» (مصنف عبدالرزاق، كتاب اللقطة، نمبر 18634)
اصول: لقطہ پانے والا اگر غریب اور نادار ہو تو صحیح قول کے مطابق تشہیر مدت کے بعد اس سے فائدہ اٹھانا درست ہے، البتہ مالدار کے لئے امام کے اجازت کے بغیر جائز نہیں ہے۔
Then if he (the claimant) gives its description, it becomes permissible for the finder to hand it over to him, and he is not compelled to do so in judiciary. And he should not give the lost item in charity to a rich person. And if the finder is rich, it is not permissible for him to benefit from it.

Proof: (1) Hadith to establish: 'Then if he gives its description, it becomes permissible for the finder to hand it over' – 'Then if its owner comes, otherwise benefit from it.' He said: 'I benefited from it, then I met him later in Mecca, and he said: I don't know whether it was three years or one year.' (Bukhari, chapter: If the owner informs of the sign, hand it over, Kitab al-Luqata, no. 2426/Muslim, Kitab al-Luqata, no. 1723)
Proof: (2) Hadith to establish: 'If he gives its description, it becomes permissible for the finder to hand it over' – 'If someone comes who informs you of its number, its container, and its string, then give it to him.' (Muslim, Kitab al-Luqata, no. 1723)
Proof: (1) Hadith to establish: 'He should not give the lost item in charity to a rich person' – from Abu Huraira: The Messenger of Allah was asked about the lost item, he said: 'The lost item is not permissible (for the finder to keep without announcement). Whoever finds something should announce it for a year; if its owner comes, he should return it; if not, he should give it in charity.' (Darqutni, Kitab al-Ridha', no. 4389/Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, Kitab al-Luqata, no. 18623)
Proof: (1) Statement of a Tabi'i to establish: 'If the finder is rich, it is not permissible' – from Nafi: A man found a lost item and came to Abdullah ibn Umar and said: I found a lost item, what do you think? Ibn Umar said: 'Announce it.' He said: I did. He said: 'Increase.' He said: I did. He said: 'I do not order you to eat it; if you wished, you would not have taken it.' (Sunan al-Bayhaqi, chapter: The lost item is eaten by the rich and poor after a year of announcing if not recognized, no. 12063)
Proof: (2) Statement of a female companion to establish: 'If the finder is rich, it is not permissible' – A woman came to Aisha and said: I found a sheep. She said: 'Feed it, shear it, and announce it.' Then she returned to her three times and said: 'Do you want me to order you to slaughter it?' (Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, Kitab al-Luqata, no. 18634)
Principle: If the finder is poor and needy, according to the correct saying, after the period of announcement, benefiting from it is permissible; but for the rich, it is not permissible without the permission of the imam.

Commentary
This passage continues the rulings on luqata from al-Quduri. The first ruling: If the claimant correctly describes the lost item (e.g., its container, string, number), the finder may hand it over, but the judge does not compel him to do so in court. This is based on the hadith where the Prophet told the finder to hand it over if the claimant describes the container and tie. The second ruling: The finder should not give the luqata in charity to a rich person; rather, after announcement, it should be given to the poor, as per the hadith 'give it in charity'. The third ruling: If the finder himself is rich, he is not allowed to benefit from the item even after announcement, unless the judge permits. This is supported by the statement of Ibn Umar, who refused to allow the rich finder to eat the lost item. The concluding Urdu note clarifies that if the finder is poor, the correct view allows him to benefit after a year of announcement; for a rich finder, it requires the judge's permission.

Key Terms
الْعَلَامَة (al-'alamah): The distinctive signs or description of the lost item (e.g., number, container, tie) that the claimant provides.
لُقَطَة (luqata): Lost property picked up by someone else.

Use of Found Items: Poor vs Rich Finder
Ruling on use of found item based on finder's wealth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a poor finder's use of a found item and the rich finder's obligation to give charity to poor relatives, and explain the dalil from the hadith.

Core Idea
Found items: poor finder may use; rich finder must give to poor relatives.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents the Hanafi ruling on luqata (found items). It distinguishes between a poor finder, who may benefit from the item, and a rich finder, who may not use it personally but may give it in charity to his poor father, son, or wife. The commentary provides the hadith evidence from the incident of Zayd ibn Khalid al-Juhani and notes the Shafi'i position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for a poor finder
Read the matn: 'Wa in kana faqiran fala ba'sa an yantafi'a biha' — the poor finder is permitted to benefit from the found item.
The text uses 'fala ba'sa' indicating permissibility (mubah).
2
Identify the ruling for a rich finder
The matn says a rich finder may not use it but may give it as charity to certain poor relatives: his father, son, or wife.
The phrase 'yatashaddaqa biha' indicates giving in charity, not personal use.
3
Locate the dalil (hadith)
The commentary cites the hadith of Zayd ibn Khalid about a lost sheep and camel, and the additional phrase 'and if not, then spend it.'
The hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim.
4
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The Hanafi school interprets the phrase 'and if not, then spend it' as permission for a poor finder to use the item; a rich finder is not addressed by this permission.
The Shafi'i school uses the general permission 'it is for you' to allow the rich finder to keep it.
5
Note the ikhtilaf with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i considers the hadith as allowing the finder (regardless of wealth) to keep the found sheep. The Hanafi school restricts this based on the finder's need.
The practical difference is that a rich Hanafi must give the item away, while a rich Shafi'i may keep it.
