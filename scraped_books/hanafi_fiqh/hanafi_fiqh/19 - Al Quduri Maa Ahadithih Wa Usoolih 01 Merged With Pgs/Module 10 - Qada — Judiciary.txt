Mortgagee Sues for Destruction of Pawn
Ruling on standing and substitution in pawn destruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the mortgagee is the proper plaintiff when a third party destroys a pawn and explain that the compensation replaces the pawn as security.

Core Idea
The mortgagee sues for destruction; the recovery becomes a new pawn.

This short ruling from Al-Quduri clarifies a key procedural point in the law of mortgage (rahn). When a third party destroys the pawned item, the mortgagee—not the pledgor—has the right to sue for compensation. This reinforces the mortgagee's possessory interest and ensures the debt remains secured even after the original pawn is gone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Determine who the mortgagee (pledgee) and pledgor are, and that the destroyer is a stranger (ajnabi).
The pawn is in the mortgagee's possession.
2
Identify the plaintiff
The text explicitly states that the mortgagee is the plaintiff (al-khasm) in claiming compensation.
This is because the mortgagee has a direct interest in preserving the security.
3
Determine the consequence
The compensation (value) taken from the destroyer becomes a new pawn in the mortgagee's hand, replacing the original.
The debt remains secured by this substitute.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position is that only the mortgagee sues; some other schools may allow the pledgor to sue instead.
This is a mukhtalaf fih issue.

Commentary
This ruling addresses the situation where a third party destroys the pawned item. Since the mortgagee has a possessory right and an interest in the pawn, he is the one to sue the destroyer for damages. The compensation (value of the item) then replaces the original pawn and becomes a new pawn in the mortgagee's possession, securing the debt until payment.

Key Terms
الْخَصْمُ (al-khaṣm): The plaintiff or proper party to bring the claim.
تَضْمِينِهِ (taḍmīnihi): Holding him liable for compensation; claiming damages.

Interdiction (Hajr) Due to Debt: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on interdiction for debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on hajr for debt and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa denies interdiction for debt; Sahibayn affirm it to protect creditors.

This passage from al-Quduri presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a judge can restrict an insolvent debtor's legal capacity (hajr). Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the correct ruling in financial disputes between creditors and debtors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disagreement
The act is judicial interdiction (hajr) for an insolvent debtor (muflis) upon creditors' request. The disagreement is whether the judge should interdict or only imprison.
Hajr restricts legal capacity; habs (imprisonment) does not.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa says: no interdiction. The judge imprisons the debtor indefinitely until he sells his property. If the debt and property are the same type (e.g., both dirhams), the judge can pay directly without consent. If different, the judge sells the property.
The debtor retains capacity to contract; his sales and acknowledgments are valid.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say: upon request, the judge interdicts, preventing any disposition (sale, acknowledgment) that could harm creditors. If the debtor refuses to sell, the judge sells and distributes the proceeds.
The debtor's legal capacity is restricted; his detrimental acts are void.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, a debtor's acknowledgment of a new debt is valid; under Sahibayn, it is not effective against creditors. Also, the debtor can still make gifts or sales under Abu Hanifa, but not under Sahibayn.
This affects how judges handle debtor assets.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi jurists (al-Kasani, al-Haskafi) adopt the Sahibayn's view for practicality and creditor protection. It is now the relied-upon position in most Hanafi courts.
The matn records Abu Hanifa's view, but fatwa follows the Sahibayn.

Source Text

وَقَالَ أَبُو حَنِيفَةَ: لَا أَحْجُرُ فِي الدَّيْنِ ، وَإِذَا وَجَبَتْ الدُّيُونُ عَلَى رَجُلٍ وَطَلَبَ غُرَمَاؤُهُ حَبْسَهُ، وَالْحَجْرَ عَلَيْهِ لَمْ أَحْجُرْ عَلَيْهِ وَإِنْ كَانَ لَهُ مَالٌ لَمْ يَتَصَرَّفْ فِيهِ الْحَاكِمُ وَلَكِنْ يَحْبِسُهُ أَبَدًا حَتَّى يَبِيعَهُ . فَإِنْ كَانَ دَيْنُهُ دَرَاهِمَ وَلَهُ دَرَاهِمُ قَضَاهَا الْقَاضِي بِغَيْرِ أَمْرِهِ 19 وَإِنْ كَانَ دَيْنُهُ دَرَاهِمَ وَلَهُ دَنَانِيرُ، أَوْ عَلَى ضِدِّ ذَلِكَ بَاعَهَا الْقَاضِي فِي دَيْنِهِ 20 وَقَالَ أَبُو يُوسُفَ وَمُحَمَّدٌ : إِذَا طَلَبَ غُرَمَاءُ الْمُفْلِسِ الْحَجْرَ عَلَيْهِ حَجَرَ الْقَاضِي عَلَيْهِ وَمَنَعَهُ مِنْ التَّصَرُّفِ، وَالْبَيْعِ، وَالْإِقْرَارِ حَتَّى لَا يَضُرَّ بِالْغُرَمَاءِ وَبَاعَ مَالَهُ إِنِ امْتَنَعَ مِنْ بَيْعِهِ وَقَسَمَهُ بَيْنَ غُرَمَائِهِ
And Abu Hanifa said: I do not impose interdiction for debt. When debts become due upon a man and his creditors request his imprisonment and interdiction upon him, I do not impose interdiction upon him. And if he has property, the judge does not dispose of it, but he imprisons him forever until he sells it. Then if his debt is dirhams and he has dirhams, the judge pays it without his order. And if his debt is dirhams and he has dinars, or the opposite, the judge sells them for his debt. And Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: When the creditors of an insolvent person request interdiction upon him, the judge imposes interdiction upon him and prevents him from disposing, selling, and acknowledging (debt) so that he does not harm the creditors. And he sells his property if he refuses to sell it, and divides it among his creditors.
Source pages p. 352

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri covers the Hanafi ikhtilaf on interdiction (hajr) due to debt. Abu Hanifa's position is that the judge does not interdict the debtor (i.e., does not restrict his legal capacity to contract), because interdiction removes capacity and is a severe measure. Instead, the judge can imprison the debtor indefinitely until he sells his property voluntarily to pay the debt. If the debtor's property is of the same type as the debt (e.g., dirhams for dirhams), the judge can seize it directly without the debtor's consent. If different (e.g., dirhams debt but dinars property), the judge sells the property. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) disagree: they allow full interdiction upon the creditors' request. The judge prevents the debtor from any disposition (sale, acknowledgment) to protect creditors, and if the debtor refuses to sell, the judge sells and distributes the proceeds. The Hanafi school follows Abu Hanifa's view in principle but the Sahibayn's view is considered more practical for courts and is often adopted in fatwa due to ease.

Key Terms
الْحَجْر (al-ḥajr): Judicial restriction on a person's legal capacity to dispose of property due to debt or other reasons.
الْمُفْلِس (al-muflis): An insolvent person whose debts exceed his assets.
الْإِقْرَار (al-iqrār): Acknowledgement of debt or obligation; under Sahibayn, the interdicted debtor's iqrar is not effective against creditors.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
The intra-Hanafi dispute on hajr for debt hinges on whether protecting creditors justifies removing the debtor's legal capacity; the later practice adopts the Sahibayn's view.

Judicial Procedure for Claiming Shuf'a
Steps a pre-emptor must follow when filing a claim before the judge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the steps a pre-emptor must follow when claiming shuf'a before a judge and identify when the buyer is made to swear an oath.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor must prove his ownership in court; if he fails, the buyer swears an oath.

This passage from al-Quduri describes the legal procedure for claiming shuf'a (right of pre-emption) in court. It clarifies the burden of proof on the pre-emptor and the role of the judge in administering an oath to the buyer when proof is lacking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sequence of actions
The pre-emptor presents himself to the judge, claims the purchase, and demands shuf'a. This initiates the procedure.
2
Understand the judge's initial inquiry
The judge asks the defendant (the buyer) about the pre-emptor's ownership that gives the right of shuf'a.
The defendant is the person who purchased the property.
3
Analyze the defendant's response
If the defendant admits the pre-emptor's ownership, no further proof is needed. If he denies, the pre-emptor must provide evidence (bayyinah).
Admission settles the claim; denial shifts the burden.
4
Determine the outcome of evidence
If the pre-emptor declines to provide evidence or cannot provide it, the judge makes the buyer take an oath.
The oath is 'by Allah that he did not buy the property' (in a way subject to shuf'a).
5
Recognize the legal effect of the oath
The oath protects the buyer; without proof, the pre-emptor's claim is not upheld. The procedure ends here.
If the buyer refuses to swear, the pre-emptor's claim may be accepted.

وَإِذَا تَقَدَّمَ الشَّفِيعُ إِلَى الْقَاضِي فَادَّعَى الشَّرَاءَ وَطَلَبَ الشُّفْعَةَ سَأَلَ الْقَاضِي الْمُدَّعَى عَلَيْهِ فَإِنْ اعْتَرَفَ بِمِلْكِهِ الَّذِي يَشْفَعُ بِهِ، وَإِلَّا كَلَّفَهُ إِقَامَةَ الْبَيِّنَةِ فَإِنْ نَكَلَ، أَوْ قَامَتْ لِلشَّفِيعِ بَيِّنَةٌ سَأَلَهُ الْقَاضِي فَإِنْ عَجَزَ عَنْهَا أَسْتَحْلَفَ الْمُشْتَرِي بِاللَّهِ مَا ابْتَاعَ
And when the pre-emptor presents himself to the judge and claims the purchase and demands shuf'a, the judge asks the defendant. If he admits to the ownership by which the pre-emptor claims shuf'a, [then fine]. Otherwise, the judge requires him to provide evidence. If the pre-emptor declines [to provide evidence] or evidence is established for the pre-emptor, the judge asks him. If he is unable [to provide evidence], the judge makes the buyer swear by Allah that what he bought [is not subject to shuf'a].
Source pages p. 384

Commentary
This passage outlines the judicial procedure for claiming shuf'a. The pre-emptor must first prove his ownership of the property that gives him the right. The judge interrogates the defendant (the buyer or the seller). If the defendant admits the pre-emptor's ownership, no further proof is needed. If he denies, the pre-emptor must provide evidence. If he cannot, the buyer is asked to take an oath that he did not purchase the property (or that he purchased it without the pre-emptor's right attaching). The procedure ends with the oath of the buyer if the pre-emptor fails to prove his case.

Key Terms
تَقَدَّمَ (taqaddama): To present oneself before the judge; to file a case.
بَيِّنَة (bayyinah): Evidence; proof, usually witness testimony.
نَكَلَ (nakala): To decline or refuse to take an oath or provide evidence.
أَسْتَحْلَفَ (astaḥlafa): To ask someone to take an oath; to administer an oath.
