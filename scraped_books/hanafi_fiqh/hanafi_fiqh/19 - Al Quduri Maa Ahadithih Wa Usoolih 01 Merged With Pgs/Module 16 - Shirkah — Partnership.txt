Definition and Types of Partnership (Shirkah)
Defines partnership and divides it into two major types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify partnerships into shirkat al-amwal and shirkat al-uqud, and state the conditions for a valid mufawadah partnership according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Partnership is either co-ownership or contract; only contractual partnership allows agency without permission.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar opens the Book of Partnership by laying out the foundational classification. It distinguishes between mere co-ownership (shirkat al-amwal) and true contractual partnership (shirkat al-uqud). The latter has four subtypes, with mufawadah being the most comprehensive, requiring absolute equality between partners. Understanding this classification is essential before delving into the detailed rulings of each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two broad categories
Note the author's division: shirkat al-amwal (co-ownership by inheritance or purchase) and shirkat al-uqud (contractual partnership). Recognize that shirkat al-amwal is not a true partnership contract.
This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh.
2
Analyze shirkat al-amwal
Understand that in co-ownership, each owner is like a stranger to the other's share. No disposition of the other's share is allowed without permission.
This rule upholds individual ownership rights.
3
List the four types of shirkat al-uqud
Memorize the four types: mufawadah (unlimited/equal), 'inan (limited), partnership of craftsmen (sana'i), and partnership of credit (wujuh). The passage then elaborates on mufawadah.
Only mufawadah and 'inan are the main commercial partnerships.
4
Extract conditions for mufawadah
The conditions: both partners must be free, Muslim, adult, and sane. Inequality in these aspects makes mufawadah invalid. Also note the inherent agency and suretyship.
These conditions ensure mutual trust and capacity.

Book of Partnership. He (the author) said - may Allah have mercy on him: Partnership is of two types: partnership of properties and partnership of contracts. As for partnership of mufawadah, it is that two men partner and they become equal in their capital, their right to transact, and their liability for debts. It is valid between two free, Muslim, adult, sane men, and it is not valid between a free man and a slave, nor between a minor and an adult, nor between a Muslim and a non-believer, and it is concluded on the basis of mutual agency and mutual suretyship.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar opens the Book of Partnership by providing a foundational definition and classification. The author states that partnership (shirkah) is of two broad types: (1) Shirkat al-Amwal (partnership of properties) and (2) Shirkat al-Uqud (partnership of contracts). Shirkat al-Amwal is not a true contractual partnership but rather co-ownership arising from inheritance or joint purchase. In such co-ownership, neither partner can use or dispose of the other's share without explicit permission; each is considered a stranger regarding the other's portion. This falls under the general principle of ownership rights. Shirkat al-Uqud, the second type, is a contractual partnership created by offer and acceptance. It has four sub-types: (a) Mufawadah (unlimited partnership with full equality), (b) 'Inan (limited partnership where contributions and authority may differ), (c) Shirkat al-Sana'i (partnership of craftsmen where each contributes skill), and (d) Shirkat al-Wujuh (credit partnership where partners buy on credit and sell using their reputations). The text then details Mufawadah, defining it as a partnership where two free, adult, sane Muslim men contribute equal capital, have equal authority to transact, and are equally liable for debts. The conditions for its validity are: both must be free (not slaves), Muslim (not non-Muslims), adult (not minors), and sane. It is invalid if there is inequality in freedom, age, or religion. The contract of Mufawadah inherently includes mutual agency (wakalah) and mutual suretyship (kafalah), meaning each partner can act on behalf of the other and is a guarantor for the other's obligations.

Key Terms
شَرِكَةُ الْأَمْلَاكِ (shirkat al-amlāk): Joint ownership of property that occurs without a partnership contract, e.g., inheritance or joint purchase.
شَرِكَةُ الْعُقُودِ (shirkat al-'uqūd): A partnership established by a contract (offer and acceptance) for the purpose of trade or profit, with shared capital and profit.
مُفَاوَضَة (mufāwaḍah): A type of partnership where partners are equal in capital, authority to transact, and liability, and each acts as agent and surety for the other.

Key Takeaway
Partnership in Hanafi fiqh is either co-ownership (shirkat al-amwal) or contractual (shirkat al-uqud); the latter has four types, and mufawadah requires full equality of partners in capital, authority, and liability.

Scope of Joint Ownership and Liability in Mufawadah
Defines scope of joint ownership and liability in Mufawadah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the scope of joint ownership and debt liability in Shirkat al-Mufawadah and identify the exceptions for personal household expenses.

Core Idea
In Mufawadah, trade purchases are joint; personal household expenses remain individual; debts from valid partnership are mutual.

This passage from Al-Quduri lays down the foundational rules of Shirkat al-Mufawadah. It answers two critical questions: what becomes joint property, and who is liable for debts? Understanding these boundaries is essential for correctly applying Mufawadah and distinguishing it from other partnership forms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling on purchases
Note that 'whatever each of them purchases is on the partnership'. This establishes automatic joint ownership for trade acquisitions.
This is the general rule; any purchase made by one partner for trade purposes is joint property.
2
Locate the exception
The text explicitly excludes 'food of his family and their clothing'. These personal household expenses remain the individual partner's responsibility.
The exception prevents unfair imposition of personal necessities on the partnership.
3
Identify the debt liability rule
Debts incurred by one partner 'as a substitute for what is valid for partnership' make the other partner a guarantor. This means debts arising from valid partnership transactions are jointly binding.
This creates mutual suretyship (kafalah) inherent in Mufawadah.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school does not accept automatic mutual debt liability without explicit stipulation. This highlights the unique Hanafi conception of Mufawadah as an unlimited partnership.
The ikhtilaf turns on whether partnership inherently implies full agency.
5
Apply to a scenario
Consider a partner who buys wheat: if for resale, it's joint; if for his family's bread, it's personal. Similarly, a debt for purchased inventory is joint, but a personal loan is not.
The key is the purpose of the transaction: trade vs. personal.

The rule is that any purchase made by one partner for trade purposes is automatically joint property. Two exceptions are mentioned: family food and clothing for their dependents. Additionally, any debt incurred by one partner as compensation for valid partnership transactions is jointly binding; the other partner is guarantor. This establishes the mutual agency and suretyship that characterize Mufawadah.

Effect of Inheritance or Gift on Mufawadah
Ruling on inheritance/gift in mufawadah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the effect of a partner receiving non-liquid property on a Shirkat al-Mufawadah and identify the resulting partnership type.

Core Idea
Non-liquid inheritance or gift nullifies mufawadah, reverting it to 'inan.

This passage from Al-Quduri addresses a common scenario in partnerships: what happens when a partner inherits or receives property that is not suitable as partnership capital? The Hanafi school has a clear rule to protect the nature of mufawadah, which requires full equality and fungible capital. Let's parse the ruling and its underlying logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger event
What event causes the change? The text mentions inheritance or gift of property that is not valid for partnership.
Only property that is not suitable as capital (e.g., real estate, merchandise) triggers nullification.
2
Determine the effect on mufawadah
The mufawadah is voided (batil) upon such property coming into the partner's possession.
3
Identify the new partnership form
The partnership automatically becomes 'inan, a limited partnership.
In 'inan, partners are agents only, not guarantors for each other.

Valid Capital for Partnership: Currency and Bullion Exception
Ruling on permissible partnership capital

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the types of capital valid for Hanafi partnership and explain the exception for bullion.

Core Idea
Partnership capital must be liquid currency; bullion is allowed only if customary.

This passage continues with a fundamental rule: a valid partnership contract requires capital that is a medium of exchange. The Hanafi school excludes commodities and non-fungible assets. However, an exception is made for regions where people trade with gold or silver bullion. Understanding this requirement is essential for drafting valid partnership agreements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Partnership is only contracted with dirhams, dinars, and current fulus (copper coins). These are monetary units.
Dirhams and dinars are silver and gold coins; fulus must be current (accepted in the market).
2
Recognize the exclusion
It is not valid with anything else (e.g., goods, real estate, livestock).
This is the general prohibition.
3
Identify the exception
If people customarily transact with bullion (tibr and nuqrah), partnership with those is valid.
Bullion is uncoined gold/silver; it becomes valid by 'urf (custom).

Partnering with Commodities and the Rules of Shirkat al-'Inan
Procedure and rules for shirkat al-'inan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the procedure for forming a shirkat al-'inan with commodities and identify the core legal features of the 'inan contract.

Core Idea
'Inan is based on agency, not guaranty, and allows flexible capital and profit sharing.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out the essential rules for shirkat al-'inan, the most common form of partnership. It covers a special procedure when partners contribute commodities instead of cash, and then clarifies the legal nature of 'inan: mutual agency without automatic suretyship. It also affirms that partners can contribute unequal capital and yet agree on equal or unequal profit shares. Let's parse the text step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue: partnership with commodities
The passage begins by addressing a scenario where partners intend to form a partnership using 'urud (commodities/goods) rather than cash. The first question is how to legally transform their individual properties into jointly-owned partnership capital.
Commodities are not homogeneous like cash; the Hanafi solution is a pre-contract sale.
2
Follow the required procedure
The text prescribes: each partner must sell half of his own property to the other in exchange for half of the other's property. This creates co-ownership of the entire combined pool. Only after this exchange do they contract the partnership as 'inan. Why? Because partnership requires the capital to be owned jointly from the start.
This exchange is a necessary condition (shart) for the validity of the partnership when commodities are used.
3
Identify the legal nature of 'inan
The text then states: 'This partnership is based on agency (wakalah) without suretyship (kafalah).' This means each partner is the agent of the other for trading — they can buy and sell on behalf of the partnership — but neither is automatically a guarantor for the other's personal debts incurred outside the scope of agency.
Contrast with mufawadah, where both agency and guaranty are present.
4
Recognize the flexibility in capital and profit
The passage then lists three permissions: (1) disparity in capital is valid (one can contribute more, the other less). (2) They may contribute equal capital but agree on unequal profit shares. (3) Each partner may contribute only part of his wealth to the partnership and keep the rest outside.
Profit sharing is based on mutual agreement, not necessarily proportional to capital — a uniquely Hanafi position.
5
Note the cross-reference to mufawadah
The final sentence states that 'inan is valid only with what we have explained that mufawadah is valid with. This refers back to an earlier condition: the capital must be in currency (dirhams and dinars) or, if commodities, must first be converted through the exchange procedure above.
This ties the rules of 'inan to the same fundamental conditions as mufawadah regarding the nature of capital.

Partnership with Mixed Currencies and Capital Perish
Rulings on partnership with different currencies and capital perish

Learning Objective

This passage from Al-Quduri lays out foundational rules for partnership (shirkah) capital. Sulh by One Partner on a Joint Debt
Ruling on options for co-creditor when partner settles share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the options available to a non-settling partner when his co-partner settles a shared debt through sulh, and identify the exception that nullifies the option.

Core Idea
A partner who settles his share of a joint debt does not bind his co-owner; the other partner retains a choice to pursue the debtor or take part of the settlement.

When two people jointly own a debt, each has an undivided share. If one partner makes a sulh (settlement) with the debtor for his portion, the other partner is not forced to accept that settlement. Instead, he is given options to protect his share. This lesson explains those options and the conditions under which they apply.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that the debt is jointly owned by two partners. Each partner has an undivided half share.
The ruling applies only to joint ownership of debt, not to other forms of partnership.
2
Action of One Partner
One partner makes a sulh (compromise) with the debtor for his own share, accepting a cloth (or other commodity) in lieu of his share of the debt.
The settlement is valid for his share only.
3
Options of the Other Partner
The non-settling partner has two options: (1) pursue the debtor for his half of the original debt, or (2) take half of the cloth (the settlement item) from the settling partner.
The cloth becomes jointly owned because it replaces the debt share of the settling partner.
4
Exception and Additional Rules
The option disappears if the settling partner guarantees to pay the non-settling partner a quarter of the debt (i.e., full compensation for his share). Also, if the non-settling partner collects part of his share directly from the debtor, the settling partner can share in that collection. If the settling partner uses his share to buy a commodity for himself, he may be held liable for a quarter of the debt.
These rules protect the co-owner's interest and prevent one partner from acting to the detriment of the other.

وَإِذَا كَانَ الدَّيْنُ بَيْنَ شَرِيكَيْنِ فَصَاحَ أَحَدُهُمَاعَنْ نَصِيبِهِ عَلَى ثَوْبِ فَشَرِيكُهُ بِاخْيَارِإِنْ شَاء تَبِعَ الَّذِي عَلَيْهِ الدَّيْنُ بِنِصْفِهِ وَإِنْ شَاءَ أَخَذَنِصْفَ الثَّوْبِ إِلَّا أَنْ يَضْمَنَ لَهُ شَرِيكَهُ رُبِّعَ الدَّيْنِ وَلَوْ اسْتَوْفَى نِصْفَ نَصِيبِهِ مِنْ الدَّيْنِ كَانَ لِشَرِيكِهِ أَنْ يُشَارِكَهُ فِيمَا قَبَضَ ثُمَّ يَرْجِعَانِ عَلَى الْغَرِيمِ بِالْبَاقِي وَإِنْ اشْتَرَى أَحَدُهُمَا لِنَفْسِهِ بِنَصِيبِهِ مِنْ الدَّيْنِ سِلْعَةَ كَانَ لِشَرِيكِهِ أَنْ يُضَمِّنَهُ رُبُعَ الدَّيْنِ كَانَ لِشَرِيكِهِ أَنْ يُضَمِّنَهُ رُبُعَ الدَّيْنِ
And when a debt is between two partners, and one of them settles from his share on a cloth, then his partner has a choice: if he wishes, he can follow the debtor for his half of the debt; if he wishes, he can take half of the cloth, unless his partner guarantees to him a quarter of the debt. And if he collects half of his share of the debt, his partner has the right to share with him in what he received, then they both go back to the debtor for the remainder. And if one of them buys for himself with his share of the debt a commodity, his partner has the right to hold him liable for a quarter of the debt.

Commentary
This passage provides rules for when a debt is owned jointly by two partners, and one partner makes a sulh agreement with the debtor for his share, accepting a cloth as payment. The non-settling partner is given an option: he can either pursue the debtor for his half of the original debt, or he can take half of the cloth from the settling partner (since the cloth is now jointly owned if the sulh was valid). However, if the settling partner guarantees to pay him a quarter of the debt (i.e., his partner gets his full share), then the option disappears. Similarly, if the partner collects part of his share from the debtor directly, the other partner can share in that collection. If the partner uses his share of the debt to buy a commodity for himself, the other partner can hold him liable for a quarter of the debt, implying the transaction is treated as if he purchased on behalf of both. These rules protect the interests of the co-owner of the debt.

Key Terms
شَرِيك (sharīk): A partner who jointly owns a debt. Each partner has an undivided share in the debt.
خِيَار (khiyār): An option or choice given to the non-settling partner in this context.
صُلْح (sulh): A settlement or compromise agreement, often used to settle a debt or dispute.

Key Takeaway
A partner's settlement on a joint debt does not bind his co-owner; the co-owner has a choice to either pursue the debtor or share the settlement proceeds, unless the settling partner indemnifies him.
