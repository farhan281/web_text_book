Iqrar for a Stranger and Subsequent Claim of Paternity
Ruling on iqrar to stranger then claiming paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a terminally ill person acknowledges a debt to a stranger and then claims paternity, and explain why the acknowledgment becomes void.

Core Idea
A paternity claim overrides a prior iqrar to a stranger because lineage takes priority over financial debt.

This lesson deals with a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: a person in death-illness acknowledges something to a stranger, then later declares that stranger is his son. How does the law reconcile the two? The passage provides a clear ruling: the paternity is established and the acknowledgment becomes void. We will explore the rationale behind this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial act
The person makes an iqrar (acknowledgment of a right or debt) to an ajnabi (stranger) during his death-illness.
Ajnabi means not a relative or spouse.
2
Identify the subsequent claim
Later, the same person says 'he is my son' (huwa ibni).
This is a claim of lineage (nasab).
3
Determine the legal outcome
The lineage is established (thabata nasabuhu minhu) and the original iqrar becomes void (batala iqraruhu lahu).
The paternity claim supersedes the financial acknowledgment.
4
Analyze the rationale
If the person is truly his son, he was never a stranger; the iqrar was based on a false premise. Additionally, nasab carries stronger legal weight than financial debt.
This prevents fraud and ensures consistency in declarations.
5
Apply the condition
Note that the iqrar was made during marad al-mawt (death-illness). This affects the presumption of the validity of the iqrar, but the rule applies generally to any iqrar to a stranger followed by paternity claim.
The condition emphasizes the gravity of the situation.

Source pages p. 362

Commentary
This passage presents two related rulings. First, if a terminally ill person acknowledges a debt or right to a stranger, and later claims that the stranger is his son, his lineage is established (provided it is plausible), but the original acknowledgment becomes void. The reason is that the acknowledgment of paternity implies that the child is his own, so the debt or right acknowledgment is superseded because one cannot be both a creditor and a child with inheritance rights. Second, if a man acknowledges something to a foreign (non-mahram) woman, then marries her, the acknowledgment remains valid. Marriage does not retroactively invalidate the prior acknowledgment because marriage is a new relationship; the debt or right was established before marriage. These rulings protect against fraud and ensure consistency in declarations.

Key Terms
أجنبي (ajnabi): A stranger, someone who is not a close relative or spouse.
نسب (nasab): Lineage, paternity; legal relationship through birth.
مرض الموت (marad al-mawt): Death-illness; a terminal condition that affects the validity and interpretation of legal acts.

Iqrar for a Woman and Subsequent Marriage
Ruling on iqrar to woman then marrying her

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a man acknowledges something to a foreign woman and then marries her, and explain why the acknowledgment remains valid.

Core Idea
Marriage does not invalidate a prior iqrar; the debt remains due even after marriage.

This lesson addresses a complementary scenario: a man acknowledges something (e.g., a debt or a gift) to a non-mahram woman, and later marries her. Does the marriage retroactively void the acknowledgment? The passage clearly states it does not. We will explore the reasoning behind this rule, which contrasts with the first case where a paternity claim voids the iqrar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial act
A man makes an iqrar (acknowledgment) to an ajnabiyyah (a woman who is not his mahram).
Ajnabiyyah means a woman unrelated and not his wife.
2
Identify the subsequent act
Later, he marries her.
Marriage changes her status from ajnabiyyah to wife.
3
Determine the outcome
The original iqrar does not become void (lam yabtul iqraruhu laha).
The debt or right acknowledged remains valid.
4
Analyze the rationale
Marriage does not retroactively alter the fact that at the time of iqrar, she was a stranger. The iqrar was valid when made and continues after marriage.
This prevents a person from escaping a valid debt by marrying the creditor.
5
Contrast with first case
Unlike the paternity claim which voids the iqrar because it reveals the person was never a stranger, marriage does not negate the initial relationship.
The key difference: paternity goes to the root of identity; marriage is a new contract.

Rules of Revocation in Gifts (Hibah)
Rulings on when a donor may revoke a gift, with exceptions and proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on revocation of a gift and identify the exceptions and bars that determine its permissibility.

Core Idea
Revocation of a gift to a stranger is allowed but disliked; exceptions bar revocation.

Gifting is a commendable act, but what if the donor later wishes to take back the gift? The Hanafi school provides a detailed framework balancing the donor's rights and the donee's expectations. This lesson unpacks the core rule and its key exceptions based on the classical text of al-Quduri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and its hukm
The default rule: a donor may revoke a gift given to a stranger (non-mahram), but this act is disliked (makruh tanzihan). Understand that the dislike is removed in specific circumstances.
Note: 'disliked' means legally permissible but morally discouraged.
2
Recognize the three exceptions that remove dislike
Revocation is no longer disliked if: (1) the donor receives compensation from the donee, (2) the gift has undergone an inseparable increase (e.g., building, planting), or (3) either party dies. In these cases, revocation becomes permissible without dislike.
Inseparable increase examples: dyeing cloth, giving birth in livestock.
3
Identify the categorical bars to revocation
Two types of donees are protected: (1) blood relatives who are mahram (unmarriageable kin), and (2) spouses. Gifts to them can never be revoked, regardless of circumstances.
Evidence for spouses: Quran 4:4 and practice of early Muslims.
4
Analyze the compensation rules
Compensation ('iwad) can be given by the donee or a third party. Once accepted by the donor, the right of revocation is lost. If half the gift is reclaimed, the compensation is reduced proportionally; if half the compensation is reclaimed, the donor may revoke only after returning the remaining compensation.
This converts the gift into a sale-like transaction.
5
Understand the procedural requirement for revocation
Revocation is not valid unless both parties agree or a judge rules accordingly. The donee's possession or consumption of the gift does not automatically forfeit the donor's right (except through the exceptions).
The judge's role is to enforce legal limits on revocation.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Hibah presents the Hanafi position on revocation (ruju') of gifts. The matn (core text) begins by stating the default rule: a donor may revoke a gift given to a stranger (ghayr mahram), but this is disliked unless one of three exceptions applies. The first exception is when the donor receives compensation ('iwad) in exchange. The second is when the gift undergoes an inseparable increase (ziyadah muttasilah), such as planting crops in gifted land, dyeing a garment, building on a house, or a slave giving birth. The third is the death of either party. The commentary then provides evidence: a hadith prohibiting revocation except for a father, and a hadith comparing the revoker to a dog returning to its vomit. The commentary also cites sayings of Tabi‘in (Tawus, al-Sha'bi, Sufyan) explaining that consumption or alteration of the gift bars revocation. A principle in Urdu clarifies that after compensation, the gift becomes like a sale and cannot be revoked. The text then adds that leaving the possession of the donee (e.g. sale) also bars revocation. Two categorical exceptions follow: a gift to a mahram relative cannot be revoked, nor can a gift between spouses. The evidence for spousal gifts is Quran 4:4 and a narration from Ibrahim al-Nakha‘i and Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz. The passage then details compensation rules: if the donee offers compensation and the donor accepts, the right of revocation is lost. Even if a third party voluntarily compensates on behalf of the donee, the right is lost. If part of the gift is reclaimed by a rightful owner, the compensation is reduced proportionally. If part of the compensation is reclaimed, the donor may only revoke after returning the remaining compensation. Revocation must be by mutual consent or a judge's ruling. Finally, if the gifted item is destroyed or rightfully claimed, the donee has no recourse against the donor. Overall, this passage establishes a balanced framework: revocation is generally allowed but restricted in specific situations to prevent harm and encourage generosity.

Key Terms
هبة (hibah): A voluntary transfer of ownership of a property without compensation (gift).
رجوع (ruju'): The act of a donor taking back the gifted property, revoking the gift.
عوض (iwad): Compensation given in exchange for the gift, often monetary or property.
ذو رحم محرم (dhu rahim mahram): A blood relative whom one cannot marry due to close kinship (e.g., parents, children, siblings).

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi law, revocation of a gift to a stranger is permissible but disliked, and is barred by compensation, inseparable increase, death, transfer, or if the donee is a mahram relative or spouse.
