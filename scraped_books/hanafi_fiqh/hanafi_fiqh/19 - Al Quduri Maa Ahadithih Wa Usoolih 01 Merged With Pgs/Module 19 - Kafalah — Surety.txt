Kafalah Bil-Nafs: Definition, Ruling, and Expressions
Definition and ruling on two types of kafalah with expressions and proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of kafalah according to Al-Quduri, explain the ruling and obligation of kafalah bil-nafs, and identify the valid expressions that form the contract.

Core Idea
Kafalah is valid; the guarantor must produce the person, and specific wording creates the contract.

Kafalah (suretyship) is a vital contract in Islamic finance and personal dealings. This lesson from Al-Quduri defines its two types, focuses on suretyship for the person (kafalah bil-nafs), and lays out the ruling and necessary expressions. We will examine the proofs from Quran and Sunnah that establish its permissibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of kafalah
The text divides kafalah into two: for the person (bil-nafs) and for property (bil-mal). Note that the lesson focuses on the first type.
Kafalah bil-mal is covered in later lessons.
2
State the ruling and obligation for kafalah bil-nafs
The ruling is 'ja'iz' (permissible). The guarantor's primary obligation is to produce the guaranteed person (ihdar al-makful bihi).
This is a binding obligation, not optional.
3
Recognize the contractual expressions
The contract is formed by specific Arabic phrases, such as 'takaffaltu bi-nafsi fulan' or 'damintuhu laka'. Familiarize yourself with the list given in the text.
Equivalent phrases in other languages may also be valid if they convey the same meaning.
4
Examine the textual proofs
The author cites Quranic verses (Surah Yusuf 12:72 and Surah Al Imran 3:37), hadith (e.g., 'al-za'im gharim'), and the practice of the Companions (Umar taking a guarantor).
Each proof demonstrates either the permissibility or the obligation.
5
Evaluate the evidence
Analyze how each proof supports the ruling: the word 'za'im' in the verse indicates a guarantor; the hadith explicitly states liability; the companion practice shows early acceptance.
This strengthens the Hanafi position that kafalah is a valid contract.

Commentary
This passage opens Kitab al-Kafalah in Al-Quduri. It defines kafalah as a contract of suretyship divided into two types: kafalah bil-nafs (suretyship for the person) and kafalah bil-mal (suretyship for property/debt). The first type is valid, and upon the surety (al-damin) is the obligation to produce the person for whom he stood surety (al-makful bihi). The contract is formed by specific expressions: 'I guarantee the person of so-and-so' or similar phrases referring to the body, soul, etc. Various synonymous phrases are also valid: 'I guarantee him for you', 'he is upon me', etc. The text then provides multiple proofs: Quranic verses (Surah Yusuf where the brothers say 'I am a guarantor for it', and Surah Al Imran where Zakariyya guarantees Maryam), hadith (including 'the guarantor is liable'), and the practice of the Companions (Umar taking a guarantor). The Urdu note summarizes that kafalah is undertaking payment of a debt if the debtor fails to pay.

Key Terms
كَفَالَة (kafālah): Suretyship; a contract where one person undertakes to produce another or pay a debt.
ضَامِن (ḍāmin): The surety/guarantor who is obligated to produce the person or pay the debt.
مَكْفُول (makfūl): The person for whom surety is given (in kafalah bil-nafs).

Time and Place of Delivery in Personal Suretyship
Ruling on time and place of delivery in kafalah bil nafs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid delivery of a guaranteed person and apply the ruling on place of delivery to determine when the guarantor is discharged.

Core Idea
Delivery must occur where the creditor can litigate; wilderness delivery does not discharge.

This lesson from Al-Quduri's matn on suretyship clarifies the guarantor's obligations regarding when and where to deliver the debtor. The ruling hinges on ensuring the creditor's ability to pursue legal action, reflecting the contractual nature of kafalah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the stipulation of time
If the contract specifies a delivery time, the guarantor must produce the person at that time upon demand.
2
Examine the consequence of failure
If the guarantor fails, the judge may imprison him, showing the obligation is enforceable.
3
Analyze place of delivery
The guarantor is discharged if he delivers in a place where litigation is possible. Delivering in the judge's assembly or market suffices; wilderness does not.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri's matn on suretyship contains several key rulings of the Hanafi school. The first ruling clarifies that when a guarantor stipulates a specific time for delivering the person, he must produce the person at that time upon demand. If he fails, the judge can imprison him, reflecting that the obligation is enforceable. The place of delivery matters: if the guarantor delivers the person in a place where the creditor can litigate against the debtor, the guarantor is freed from liability. Delivering in the judge's court or market suffices, but a wilderness does not, as litigation is impossible there. Second, the death of the debtor (makful bihi) discharges the guarantor, but death of the creditor does not—this is because the obligation of the person dies with the debtor, while the debt remains. Third, if the guarantor makes a conditional guarantee that he will be liable for the debt if he fails to bring the debtor, he must pay the debt if he fails, but he is not freed from the original personal guarantee until delivery. The hadith proofs include the famous story of Abu Qatada and the Prophet's statement that he is responsible for the debts of believers. The Urdu usool notes summarize principles: the guarantor must deliver where litigation is possible; if the principal liability ends, the secondary ends; in property matters, imprisonment is the final sanction, while in hudud and qisas it is flogging; and both guarantor and guaranteed are liable.

Key Terms
كفالة (kafalah): A contract of guarantee where one person becomes liable for another's presence or debt.
مكفول به (makful bihi): The person whose presence or debt is guaranteed.
مكفول له (makful lahu): The beneficiary of the suretyship (creditor).
كفيل (kafil): The guarantor who assumes liability.

Effects of Death and Conditional Suretyship
Ruling on death and conditional suretyship in kafalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the effect of death of the principal and creditor on the guarantor's liability, and state the ruling when a guarantor makes a conditional promise to pay the debt.

Core Idea
Death of the principal discharges the personal guarantee; conditional liability for debt is enforceable.

This lesson continues the matn of Al-Quduri on suretyship, addressing how death affects the contract and what happens when a guarantor stipulates he will pay the debt if he fails to produce the principal. These rules derive from Prophetic hadith, including the famous statement of the Prophet taking responsibility for debts of believers.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish death of principal vs creditor
Death of the guaranteed person discharges the guarantor of the person (kafalah bil nafs). Death of the creditor does not discharge the guarantor.
2
Examine conditional suretyship
If the guarantor says: 'If I do not bring him by such time, I am liable for his debt,' then if he fails to bring, he must pay the debt.
3
Link to Prophetic hadith
The Prophet's statement 'I am nearer to every believer than himself; whoever leaves a debt, it is upon me' is used as evidence that such conditional liability is valid.

Commentary

Kafalah bi al-Nafs in Hudud and Qisas: Invalidity and Proof
Ruling on suretyship for person in penal matters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on kafalah bi al-nafs in hudud and qisas, identify the hadith proof, and distinguish between the positions of Hammad and al-Hakam regarding the surety's liability upon the principal's death.

Core Idea
You cannot guarantee to produce a person for a fixed punishment or retaliation – such suretyship is invalid and prohibited.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on guaranteeing the physical presence of an accused person (kafalah bi al-nafs) in cases of hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation). The school holds that such a guarantee is not permissible, based on a direct prophetic prohibition. We will also explore two early opinions on the surety's liability if the principal dies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is kafalah bi al-nafs (suretyship for the person) in the specific context of hudud and qisas. The ruling according to Abu Hanifa is that it is not permissible (la tujiz), meaning invalid and not binding.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith: 'La kafalah fi hadd' (No suretyship in a hadd). This hadith is narrated by Amr ibn Shu'ayb from his father from his grandfather.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'la' negates the genus, implying a general and comprehensive prohibition. 'Kafalah' in this context refers to suretyship for the person, and 'hadd' includes hudud and qisas. Thus, the hadith prohibits such suretyship entirely.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Early scholars Hammad and al-Hakam disagreed on a subsidiary issue: if the surety guarantees a person and that person dies, is the surety liable? Hammad said nothing is due; al-Hakam said the surety is liable.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon position is the prohibition, as it is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and supported by the hadith. The subsidiary dispute does not affect the main ruling.

Suretyship With or Without the Principal's Order
Ruling on suretyship with or without order

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Suretyship is valid even without the principal's order — the surety's voluntary undertaking suffices.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position that suretyship (kafalah) is valid whether the principal debtor requests it or not. The key legal question is whether the principal's consent is a condition for the surety's obligation. Through the Quranic verse and the hadith of Abu Qatadah, we see that the surety can voluntarily guarantee a debt without prior authorization, and this is accepted in Islamic law.

1
Identify the act and its ruling

Locate the dalil
Two proofs are cited: a Quranic verse (Surah Yusuf 12:72) where the herald says 'I am a guarantor for it', and a hadith where Abu Qatadah voluntarily guaranteed the debt of a deceased person without being asked.
The Quranic verse shows an example of a guarantor; the hadith directly addresses the issue of consent.
3

In the hadith, Abu Qatadah said 'Pray over him, O Messenger of Allah, and I will take on his debt' without any request from the deceased. The Prophet accepted this and prayed, showing that a voluntary guarantee is valid.

The dispute is about the right of recourse, not the validity of the guarantee itself.

The hadith is considered strong evidence for the Hanafi view.

The Quranic proof is from Surah Yusuf where the herald announces a camel-load reward for finding the missing cup, and the speaker adds 'and I am a guarantor for it,' which is a conditional promise (the finding is the condition). The hadith proof shows the Prophet himself taking responsibility for the debts of deceased believers, which is a conditional statement (if they die in debt, he pays). The Urdu principle clarifies that such conditions must be appropriate; legally relevant conditions are valid, irrelevant ones are not. The second ruling deals with a dispute over the amount guaranteed. If the creditor claims a thousand and the guarantor admits less, the guarantor's word is taken with oath because he is the defendant (mudda'a 'alayh). If the principal debtor acknowledges more than what the guarantor admits, that acknowledgment does not bind the guarantor—it only affects the principal's own liability. The hadith proof is the famous principle that the burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the defendant. The Urdu principle reinforces that a mere acknowledgment after oath does not establish liability against the guarantor.

Rulings on guarantee with and without the principal's order and the right to recover

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a guarantor recovers only if the principal ordered the guarantee; without order, it is a gift.

This lesson presents the Hanafi position on guarantee (kafalah) with a clear distinction: the validity of the guarantee is independent of the principal's consent, but the right to recover the paid amount depends on whether the principal ordered the guarantee. We will explore the proof texts from the Quran and Sunnah that support these rulings.

Discharge, Conditional Discharge, and Invalid Guarantees
Rulings on discharge of guarantor, conditional discharge, and invalidity of guarantees for hudud and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi rulings on discharge from guarantee and identify which obligations can be guaranteed.

Core Idea
Discharge of the principal frees the guarantor; the reverse does not hold.

This lesson covers three axiomatic rules from the Hanafi school on guarantees (kafalah). Understanding these rules is essential for correctly applying the law of suretyship, especially the interplay between principal and guarantor, the nature of discharge, and the scope of guaranteeable rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rulings
Read the passage and list the three distinct legal statements: (1) discharge of principal → guarantor free; (2) discharge of guarantor → principal not free; (3) discharge cannot be conditional; guarantee invalid for hudud/qisas.
Memorize these as axioms.
2
Analyze the first ruling: principal's discharge frees guarantor
If the creditor releases the original debtor or takes payment from him, the debt is extinguished, so the guarantor is automatically released. The guarantor's obligation is accessory.
3
Analyze the second ruling: guarantor's discharge does not free principal
The principal's debt is primary; the guarantor is merely a surety. Releasing the surety does not affect the principal's liability.
Common mistake: thinking release of guarantor also releases principal.
4
Understand the prohibition of conditional discharge
Discharge (bara'ah) must be immediate and unconditional. It is a contract of forfeiture that does not tolerate ta'liq (suspension on a future event).
Applies to ibra' in general.
5
Identify guaranteeable rights
Only rights that can be collected from the guarantor (financial debts) can be guaranteed. Hudud and qisas are corporal punishments that cannot be transferred or executed on a guarantor; thus guarantee is invalid.
Financial compensation (diyah) can be guaranteed if it replaces qisas.

Commentary
This passage presents three distinct rulings. First, if the creditor releases the principal debtor from the debt (ibra') or obtains satisfaction from him (e.g., by taking payment or collateral), the guarantor is automatically discharged because the debt is extinguished. However, the reverse does not hold: if the creditor releases the guarantor, the principal debtor remains liable—this is because the principal's liability is primary, and the guarantor is merely a surety. Second, a discharge from guarantee cannot be made conditional on a future event; it must be immediate and unconditional. This is analogous to the rule that ibra' (release) is a contract of forfeiture that does not admit ta'liq. Third, guarantee is only valid for rights that can be collected from the guarantor. Hudud (e.g., punishment for theft) and qisas (retaliation for homicide) are personal corporal punishments that cannot be transferred or executed on a guarantor; therefore, one cannot guarantee that such a punishment will be carried out. However, financial compensation (diyah) can be guaranteed.

Guarantee in Sale and Hire: Price vs. Object, Specific vs. Unspecified
Rulings on guarantee for the buyer's price, seller's object, and hired animal load

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on guarantee for the price and object of sale and for loads on hired animals, and apply the underlying principle of the guarantor's ability to fulfill.

Core Idea
Guarantee is valid only for obligations the guarantor can personally fulfill — not for specific objects.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents two contrasting guarantee scenarios that reveal the Hanafi school's core principle: guarantee (kafalah) is limited to financial obligations (mal madmun) that can be collected from the guarantor. The first scenario distinguishes guaranteeing the buyer's price (valid) from guaranteeing the seller's object (invalid). The second extends the same logic to hired animals: if the animal is specific, the guarantee for its load is invalid; if unspecified, it is valid. A hadith and a general principle reinforce this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two pairs of rulings
Read the first part: guarantee for the buyer's price is valid; guarantee for the seller's object is invalid. Then the second part: hiring a beast for carrying: if the beast is specific, guarantee for the load is invalid; if unspecified, it is valid.
Note the parallel structure: valid when the obligation is a debt, invalid when it is a specific item.
2
Understand the key distinction: thaman vs. mabee'
The price (thaman) is a monetary debt in the dhimma, which the guarantor can pay. The sold object (mabee') is a specific 'ayn (tangible item) that only the seller can deliver; the guarantor cannot force a substitute on the buyer.
This distinction is fundamental to Hanafi kafalah.
3
Understand the hire distinction: specific vs. unspecified animal
If the hired animal is specified (mu'ayyanah), the guarantor cannot provide the identical animal if it becomes incapacitated, so the guarantee is invalid. If the animal is unspecified (e.g., any animal of a certain type), the guarantor can provide a similar one, so the guarantee is valid.
The same logic applies: the guarantor must be able to produce the equivalent (mithl).
4
Extract the general principle and its basis
The Urdu principle states: 'Where one can give the equivalent from his side, he can be a guarantor; where he cannot, he cannot.' This is the ratio legis. The hadith of the Israelite loan shows that guarantee for a loan (a debt) is permissible, but here it is used to show that guarantee for a specific object is not analogous.
The hadith is cited as proof (dalil) but the principle is derived from the school's reasoning.

Core Idea
Release of the principal automatically releases the surety, but not vice versa; conditional releases are invalid.

This passage from Al-Quduri outlines key principles in the contract of suretyship (kafalah). Understanding these rules is essential for correctly applying the law of guarantees in Hanafi fiqh. The rulings are straightforward but have important practical consequences.

Guided Path

Identify the three rulings
Read the passage and note the three distinct legal statements: (1) If the creditor releases the principal or recovers the debt from him, the surety is released. (2) If the surety is released, the principal is not released. (3) Conditional release is invalid.
The third ruling is a prohibition.
2
Understand the ancillary nature
The surety's obligation is dependent on the principal's debt. Therefore, when the principal's obligation ends (by release or payment), the surety's obligation automatically ends.
This is the logic behind the first rule.

The release of the surety does not affect the principal because the surety is not the original debtor. The principal remains liable independently.
Contrast this with the first rule.

Invalidity of Kafalah for Kitabah Debt
Ruling with proof from 'Ata' and 'Ali

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on kafalah for kitabah, explain the legal reason (illa) for its invalidity, and cite the proofs from 'Ata' (a Tabi'i) and 'Ali (a Sahabi).

Core Idea
Suretyship is invalid when the underlying debt is contingent on the slave's freedom.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh: the invalidity of suretyship (kafalah) for the debt owed by a mukatab (a slave under a kitabah contract). The Hanafi school holds that such a suretyship is void from its inception, whether the surety is free or a slave. The reason is that the mukatab's debt is not a fixed liability; if the mukatab defaults, he reverts to slavery and the debt disappears, making the surety liable for something that may cease to exist. This ruling is supported by statements from the Tabi'i 'Ata' ibn Abi Rabah and a hadith from Caliph 'Ali. Understanding this case clarifies a key principle: kafalah requires a definite, unconditional obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The text rules on 'kafalah bi mal al-kitabah' (suretyship for the kitabah debt). The ruling is that it is not valid (la tajuz), i.e., the contract is batil.
The ruling applies whether the surety is free or a slave—no distinction.
2
Locate the proofs
Two proofs are cited: (1) The opinion of 'Ata', narrated by Ibn Jurayj, explaining the reason: if the mukatab becomes insolvent, he returns to slavery and owns nothing, so the debt becomes void. (2) A statement from 'Ali that if a mukatab misses two consecutive installments, he is returned to slavery.
These are dalil naqli (transmitted evidence) from a Tabi'i and a Sahabi.
3
Understand the illa (legal reason)
The illa for invalidity is the contingency of the debt. The mukatab's obligation is conditional on his continued status; if he fails to pay, he is re-enslaved and the debt is nullified. A surety cannot guarantee something that is not a fixed, unconditional liability.
This illustrates a broader Hanafi principle: kafalah requires a known, assured debt (dayn thabit).
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Some later scholars allowed kafalah for kitabah under certain conditions, but the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is invalidity. The practical effect is that a surety cannot be held liable for a mukatab's unpaid installments; if someone pays on behalf of the mukatab, it is considered a voluntary gift, not a binding surety.
This protects the surety from an obligation that may vanish.

Invalidity of Kafalah for a Deceased with No Assets (Abu Hanifa)
Ruling with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that suretyship for a deceased with debts and no assets is invalid and explain the proof from the hadith of Abu Qatada.

Core Idea
Suretyship for a deceased with no assets is invalid because the debt is unenforceable.

This lesson presents a specific ruling from Imam Abu Hanifa: if a person dies with debts and leaves no assets, any suretyship (kafalah) offered on their behalf is invalid. The ruling is based on the principle that an obligation must be enforceable for a guarantee to be valid. The hadith of Abu Qatada, where the Prophet initially refused to pray over a deceased debtor until Abu Qatada assumed the debt, serves as the primary proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is kafalah (suretyship) for a deceased person who has debts and left no assets. According to Abu Hanifa, this kafalah is invalid (batil).
The Arabic phrase 'lam tasihh al-kafalah' indicates invalidity.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Salamah ibn al-Akwa', where the Prophet initially refused to lead the funeral prayer for a deceased debtor until Abu Qatada assumed responsibility for the debt.
The hadith is narrated by Bukhari and Tirmidhi.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's refusal indicates that the debt was an obstacle. Only after Abu Qatada guaranteed the debt did the Prophet pray. Abu Hanifa argues that this permission was specific to that situation; generally, suretyship for a debt that cannot be enforced (due to no assets) is invalid.
The hadith shows an exception, not a general rule.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i and others consider the hadith as evidence that such kafalah is valid. They argue that the Prophet accepted the guarantee, so it establishes validity.
The Hanafi school restricts the application to the specific case.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon Hanafi position is that the kafalah is invalid, because the debt is not due from the deceased's estate (since there is none). The hadith is interpreted as a concession (rukhsah) for that particular companion, not as a general precedent.
The later Hanafi masters like Ibn Abidin uphold this view.
