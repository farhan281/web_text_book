This ruling is agreed upon (muttafaq alayh) among the madhahib; there is no significant disagreement.

The statement directly forbids eating the meat. The Hanafi school accepts the fatwa of a Successor as a proof when it is widespread and not contradicted.
The ruling is consistent with the principle that a muhrim cannot lawfully slaughter game.

Core Idea
A muhrim may eat game from a non-muhrim if not involved; Haram game requires compensation.

In this lesson, we examine a clear ruling from the Hanafi school: a muhrim (one in ihram) may eat the meat of game that was hunted and slaughtered by a non-muhrim, provided he did not direct or order the hunt. The proof comes from a well-known hadith of Abu Qatada. Additionally, a special rule applies for game within the Haram — compensation is due even if the hunter is non-muhrim. Understanding these distinctions helps avoid common confusion about hunting restrictions during ihram.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its hukm
Identify the act (muhrim eating game) and determine its legal category: mubah (permissible) under conditions.
The text says 'la ba'sa' meaning no harm, i.e., permissible.
2
Identify the condition for permissibility
The muhrim must not have guided (dalla) the hunter nor ordered (amara) him to hunt. If he did either, eating becomes haram.
The condition is explicit: 'idha lam yadulluhu al-muhrimu wa la amarahu'.
3
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Abdullah ibn Abi Qatada, where the Prophet (ﷺ) allowed muhrim companions to eat game hunted by Abu Qatada, a non-muhrim.
Dalil type: Hadith ahad, sahih.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's explicit command 'Kulu' (Eat) indicates permission. The context shows the muhrim did not assist or direct.
The permission is unconditional in the hadith, but other evidence restricts it to non-involvement.
5

If the game is from the Haram (sacred precinct), even if hunted by a non-muhrim, compensation is due. The ruling for the muhrim is not stated here, but it implies he still may not eat without compensation? Actually, the text says 'jaza' (compensation) is required, meaning the hunter must pay. The muhrim's eating is subject to the same condition? The passage focuses on the compensation obligation, implying the game is not lawful unless compensation is paid. For the muhrim, it remains problematic. We note the distinction.
The Haram has additional sanctity; even non-muhrims are prohibited from hunting there, and if done, compensation is required.

Ruling on Cutting Vegetation of the Haram
Ruling that cutting grass or trees of the Haram requires paying their value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cutting Haram vegetation and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Cutting Haram vegetation requires paying its value — not a fixed animal.

The sanctity of the Haram (Makkah and Madinah) extends to its vegetation. This lesson examines the Hanafi position that whoever cuts grass or a tree that is not owned or cultivated must pay its current market value. We will explore the textual proofs and compare with the Shafi'i school, which prescribes fixed animal compensations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The passage rules on cutting grass (hashish) or trees of the Haram that are not owned or cultivated. The obligation is to pay the value (qimah) of what was cut.
The compensation is wajib, not just recommended.
2
Locate the primary proof
The author cites a hadith from Abu Shurayh (Bukhari no. 1832) that prohibits cutting trees in the Haram. This prohibition implies that compensation is due for violation.
The hadith is used as a basis for the sanctity, though it does not explicitly state the compensation amount.
3
Analyze the opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i is quoted with a fixed compensation: a sheep for a small tree, a cow for a large tree. The Hanafi school responds that such fixed penalties are not established from reliable texts in their view.
The Hanafi use istihsan and general principles of damage compensation.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi, one pays the market value; under Shafi'i, one pays a specific animal. The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi (operative) in the Hanafi school.
This affects pilgrims and residents of the Haram.

Qarin's Double Blood Obligation
Ruling with proof on qarin's double blood obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the number of bloods owed by a qarin for violations and identify the exception when only one blood is due.

Core Idea
A qarin owes double penalties — two bloods for each violation that costs one for a mufrid.

This passage clarifies the legal obligation of a qarin (one combining Hajj and Umrah) when he commits an act that would require a dam for a mufrid. The author establishes the general rule of double penalty and then notes a specific exception when the qarin passes the miqat without ihram.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two categories
Recognize that the passage concerns a qarin (performing Hajj and Umrah together) and a mufrid (performing only Hajj). The act in question is any violation that would require one dam for the mufrid.
The qarin's ihram is considered to cover both pilgrimages.
2
State the general rule
The general rule: for every such violation, the qarin owes two dams — one for his hajj and one for his umrah. This is explicitly stated in the text.
3
Note the exception
There is one exception: if the qarin passes the miqat without ihram and then enters ihram for both, he owes only one dam. This exception is mentioned after the general rule.
The reasoning: since he entered ihram after the miqat, his ihram is considered as one act.
4
Understand the legal support
The text cites a statement from Imam Malik that whoever combines Hajj and Umrah should sacrifice two sacrifices. This supports the Hanafi view of double obligation.
The exact wording in the source is: قَالَ مَالِكٌ: وَمَنْ قَرَنَ الْحَجَّ وَالْعُمْرَةَ ... يُهْدِي هَدْيَيْنِ...
5
Recognize the inter-madhhab dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that a qarin owes only one dam, considering his ihram as a single unit. The Hanafi school responds that the two intentions require separate compensations.
This difference has practical implications: a Hanafi qarin must sacrifice two animals, while a Shafi'i qarin sacrifices one.

Marriage and Dower of a Safih
Ruling on a safih's marriage and dower limitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the validity of a safih's marriage and specify which part of the dower is valid and which is void.

Core Idea
A safih's marriage is valid, but any dower exceeding the proper mahr is void.

The Hanafi school recognizes that even a safih (a person with poor financial judgment) can marry out of need, but his wasteful tendencies are checked by limiting the dower to mahr al-mithl. This lesson explains the ruling and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the safih's marriage. The text states 'جَازَ نِكَاحُهُ' meaning it is valid (jā'iz).
Validity here means the marriage contract is legally effective.
2
Locate the condition on dower
If a dower is specified, only the amount equal to mahr al-mithl is valid; any excess is void.
This restricts the safih's financial waste.
3

Sulh on a Claim of Slave Ownership

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sulh here is not a sale of a free person but a manumission contract disguised as settlement.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Book of Sulh presents a unique case: a claimant alleges that another man is his slave, but instead of pursuing the claim in court, they agree to a settlement where the alleged slave pays property. The ruling establishes that such a sulh is valid and legally equivalent to manumission for a consideration ('itq 'ala mal). This means the alleged slave becomes free, and the claimant's ownership is permanently terminated, regardless of the truth of the original claim.

The Hanafi Ruling on Sulh (Settlement) of Debts: Waiver vs. Exchange
Legal principles and examples for sulh on debts, with hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of isqat to determine whether a sulh on a debt is valid or invalid, and state the ruling for each of the five examples given in the source.

Core Idea
Sulh on a debt is a waiver, not a sale — so changing the type or timing of payment is invalid because it resembles riba.

When a creditor agrees to accept less than what is owed, is that a sale of the debt? The Hanafi school says no — it is a partial waiver (isqat). This distinction is crucial because if the settlement were treated as an exchange, it could fall into riba. In this passage from Al-Quduri, you will learn the general rule and its application through concrete examples, each backed by hadith or companion practice. By the end, you will be able to spot the difference between a valid waiver-based sulh and an invalid exchange-based one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Read the first sentence: 'Everything that is subject to a sulh while it is owed under a debt is not considered an exchange; rather it is considered that he has collected part of his right and dropped the rest.' This is the Hanafi foundation.
The key contrast is between 'mu'awada' (exchange) and 'isqat' (waiver).
2
Apply the principle to valid examples
Consider the first two examples: (1) 1000 good dirhams for 500 defective dirhams, (2) 1000 dirhams for 1000 delayed dirhams. In both, no change in type or addition; only a reduction or postponement. They are valid because they involve waiver or deferral of the same right.
The validity hinges on the absence of riba: staying within the same genus and not creating a benefit from delay.
3
Apply the principle to invalid examples
The next three examples are invalid: (3) settling for dinars payable in a month, (4) settling a delayed debt for immediate payment with a reduction, (5) settling black dirhams for white dirhams. These change the type, timing, or quality, turning the settlement into an exchange (mu'awada) which is subject to riba rules.
Even if the creditor agrees, the contract is invalid because it resembles a sale of debt.
4
Examine the proofs
Three proofs are cited: the hadith of Ka'b (Prophet gestured to accept half, showing waiver is permitted), the hadith of Jabir (Prophet suggested taking fruit for debt but the Jew refused, showing that changing the subject is not enforceable), and the saying of Zayd ibn Thabit (prohibiting a reduction for early payment, as it resembles riba). Each proof reinforces the rule that sulh must be a waiver, not an exchange.
The hadith of Ka'b is a direct approval (taqrir) of partial waiver.
5
Formulate the usul al-fiqh maxim
The Urdu line at the end summarizes: 'Where there is isqat (waiver), sulh is permissible; where there is mu'awada (exchange), it is impermissible.' Commit this maxim to memory.
This maxim is your tool for analyzing any sulh on debt scenario.
