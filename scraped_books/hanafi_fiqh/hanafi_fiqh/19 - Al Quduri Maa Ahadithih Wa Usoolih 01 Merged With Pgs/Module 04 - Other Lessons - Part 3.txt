Usurper's Liability for Usufruct: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Ruling that usurper does not guarantee usufruct unless it causes a decrease

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a usurper's liability for usufruct and explain the key difference with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Usufruct is not guaranteed; only a decrease in value triggers liability.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents a foundational Hanafi principle regarding the liability of a usurper (ghasib). The ruling states that the usurper does not owe compensation for the usufruct (benefits) of the usurped property unless his use causes a physical decrease. This contrasts with the Shafi'i position, which holds the usurper liable for the value of the usufruct regardless of decrease.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Read the Arabic and translation. Identify the core rule: the usurper does not guarantee (yadman) the usufruct (manafi') of the usurped property.
2
Identify the exception
The text adds 'unless it decreases by his use, then he pays the decrease.' Note that liability attaches only to the decrease, not to the usufruct itself.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
In Hanafi usul, usufruct is considered a non-entity at the time of usurpation, created moment by moment, and thus not subject to guarantee. The decrease is a tangible harm that can be measured.
This is based on the nature of manafi' as araj (accidents).
4
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that usufruct has value and must be compensated even if no decrease occurs. The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is that under Hanafi law the usurper does not pay rent; under Shafi'i he does.
5
Apply the ruling
Consider examples: a usurped house lived in without damage → no rent. A usurped car used causing wear (e.g., tyre damage) → pay for the decrease in value.

Commentary
This is a key Hanafi principle: the usurper is not liable for the usufruct (manafi') of the usurped property during the period of usurpation. For example, if he lives in a house or rides an animal, he does not have to pay rent or hire. This is because the usufruct itself is not considered a guarantee in Hanafi law (as opposed to the Shafi'i view). However, if the use causes a physical decrease in the property (e.g., wear and tear), he must pay for that decrease, not for the usufruct per se.

Key Terms

Liability for Destroying a Dhimmi's Wine or Pig
Muslim's liability for destroying dhimmi's wine/pig, but not a Muslim's

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for destroying a dhimmi's wine or pig and explain the juristic reasoning (istihsan) behind it.

Core Idea
A Muslim compensates a dhimmi for wine/pig, but not a Muslim – dhimmi property is protected.

This lesson examines a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh regarding the property of Muslims and dhimmis. While wine and pigs hold no legal value for Muslims, they are considered valuable property for dhimmis. The ruling highlights how the principle of protecting dhimmi property (dhimma) overrides the general rule that impure substances are not compensable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the ruling carefully: first, a Muslim destroys a dhimmi's wine or pig; second, a Muslim destroys a Muslim's wine or pig.
2
Determine the ruling for each
In the first case, the Muslim must pay the value (daman). In the second case, no liability (la yadman). Note the opposite outcomes.
3
Locate the dalil (hadith)
The text cites the hadith of Salamah ibn al-Akwa' about the Prophet's order to spill wine at Khaybar. Identify the hadith and its relevance.
The hadith is about spilling wine, not about compensation, but it shows wine is not valuable property for Muslims.
4
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The command to spill wine indicates it is not property (mal mutaqawwim) for Muslims. Hence, no compensation between Muslims. For dhimmis, the ruling is derived from istihsan: protecting their property.
5
Recognize the istihsan basis
Hanafi jurists use istihsan (juristic preference) to give dhimmis' wine/pig the status of valuable property, even though it is not so for Muslims, because their faith permits it.

Commentary
This ruling distinguishes between the property of a dhimmi (non-Muslim under Muslim protection) and that of a Muslim. Wine and pigs are not considered valuable property (mal mutaqawwim) for Muslims, so destroying them incurs no liability between Muslims. However, for a dhimmi, these items are considered valuable because they are legally permitted for them. Therefore, a Muslim who destroys a dhimmi's wine or pig must pay their market value. The hadith from Khaybar supports the principle that wine must be destroyed (spilled), but it does not address dhimmi property. The ruling is based on juristic reasoning (istihsan) that dhimmi's property is protected as property even if not so for Muslims.

Key Terms
الذمي (al-dhimmi): A non-Muslim living under Muslim rule with a pact of protection
الخمر (al-khamr): Wine; intoxicating drink

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, a Muslim who destroys a dhimmi's wine or pig must compensate, but not if the owner is Muslim, due to the protected status of dhimmi property.

Foundation of deposit: amanah, no daman, and conditions of liability
Opening of Kitab al-Wadi'ah: deposit is a trust, no liability unless modification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a deposit is a trust, identify the conditions that make the depositee liable, and explain the evidence from Quran, Hadith, and Companion practice.

Core Idea
A deposit is a trust: no liability unless the depositee violates the trust by improper custody.

This lesson opens the Hanafi Book of Deposit (al-Wadi'ah). The foundational principle is that a deposit (wadi'ah) is a trust (amanah), meaning the depositee is not liable for loss unless he exceeds proper bounds. The passage states the basic rule, then cites multiple proofs from Quran, Hadith, and Companion practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
State the rule: 'al-wadi'atu amanah' – the deposit is a trust. The depositee is not liable if it perishes.
The ruling is based on the nature of the contract as gratuitous safekeeping.
2
List the conditions that trigger liability
Identify the actions that make the depositee liable: keeping the deposit with someone outside his household (ghayrihim) or redepositing it (awda'aha).
Also note the exception: if fire breaks out, handing it to a neighbor does not incur liability.
3
Examine the cited proofs
The passage cites: (1) Quran 4:58 – command to render trusts; (2) Hadith 'man udi'a wadi'atan fala daman 'alayh'; (3) Companion statements from Abu Bakr, Umar, and practice of the Prophet through Aisha's hadith.
Each proof establishes that the deposit is a trust without liability unless negligence.
4
Understand the legal reasoning
The proofs collectively show that the depositee is an amin (trustee), not a damin (guarantor). Liability arises only when he acts as a muta'addi (transgressor) by changing the agreed custody.
This aligns with the Hanafi principle that an amanah does not entail daman.

Commentary
This is the opening of the Book of Deposit (Wadi'ah). The foundational ruling is that a deposit is a trust (amanah), so the depositee is not liable for loss unless he commits negligence or transgression. The depositee may personally keep the deposit or allow his household members to do so. However, if he entrusts it to someone outside his usual circle (e.g., a stranger) or redeposits it with another, he becomes liable for any loss. An exception: if a fire breaks out in his house, he may pass it to a neighbor to save it, and he is not liable for that transfer. The text then cites multiple proofs: the Quranic verse commanding fulfillment of trusts, several hadiths confirming no liability for the depositee, and statements of the Companions (Abu Bakr, Umar) and narrator (Shuraih) supporting the same. The hadith from Aisha shows the Prophet's own arrangement for returning deposits during the Hijrah, confirming the permissibility of the depositee keeping the property personally.

The Deposit as a Trust: Principle and Applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default liability rule for a deposit (wadi'ah) in Hanafi law, identify the conditions that trigger liability, and explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding joint deposits.

Core Idea
A deposit is a trust: no liability unless the depositary transgresses, mixes, denies, or withholds.

This passage from Al-Quduri establishes the foundational principle of deposit (wadi'ah) in Hanafi jurisprudence: it is a trust (amanah) in the depositary's hands, so he is not liable for loss without his fault. The author then provides multiple proofs from the Quran, Sunnah, and Companion practice, and explores specific scenarios where liability does arise. Finally, he presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on handling joint deposits.

Book of Deposit. He (the author) said - may Allah have mercy on him - The deposit is a trust in the hand of the depositary; if it perishes, he does not guarantee it. And the depositary may keep it himself and those in his employ. If he keeps it with others or re-deposits it, he guarantees it, unless a fire occurs in his house and he hands it over to his neighbor. Evidence: (1) The verse for establishing that the deposit is a trust in the hand of the depositary: 'Indeed, Allah commands you to render trusts to whom they are due and when you judge between people to judge with justice. Indeed, Allah is excellent in what He instructs you.' (Surah an-Nisa, 4:58). Evidence: (2) The hadith for establishing that the deposit is a trust in the hand of the depositary: From 'Amr ibn Shu'ayb, from his father, from his grandfather, who said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: 'Whoever is entrusted with a deposit, there is no liability upon him.' (Sunan Ibn Majah, Book of Deposit, no. 2401; Sunan al-Daraqutni, Book of Sales, no. 2934). Evidence: (1) The saying of a Companion for establishing that the deposit is a trust in the hand of the depositary: From Jabir that Abu Bakr would not impose liability for a deposit. (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, on Mudarabah, Aariyah, and Wadi'ah, no. 21467). Evidence: (2) The hadith for establishing that the deposit is a trust in the hand of the depositary: From 'Amr ibn Shu'ayb, from his father, from his grandfather, from the Prophet (peace be upon him) who said: 'There is no liability on the borrower who is not negligent, nor on the depositary who is not negligent.' (Sunan al-Daraqutni, Book of Sales, no. 2961). Evidence: (3) The saying of a Companion for establishing that the deposit is a trust in the hand of the depositary: 'Umar ibn al-Khattab said: 'The loan is in the position of the deposit, and there is no liability in it unless he transgresses.' (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, Book of Loan, no. 14785). Principle: The principle of trust is that if it perishes without transgression, there is no compensation upon him. Or if it is on a ship and he fears drowning, so he transfers it to another ship, he does not guarantee it. If the depositary mixes it with his own property until it becomes indistinguishable, he guarantees it. If the owner demands it and he withholds it from him while able to deliver it, he guarantees it. If it mixes with his property without his action, he is a partner with its owner. If the depositary spends some of it, then returns the like and mixes it with the remainder, he guarantees all of it. When the depositary transgresses in the deposit, such as if it was an animal and he rode it, or a garment and he wore it, or a slave and he employed him, or he deposited it with someone else, then he removes the transgression and returns it to his hand, the liability ceases. If the owner demands it and he denies it to him, he guarantees it; if he returns to confessing, he is not freed from liability. The depositary may travel with the deposit, and if it has a burden and expense according to Abu Hanifa... And when two men deposit with a man a deposit, then one of them comes demanding his share, nothing is given to him until the other comes, according to Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: His share is given to him. He then deposited it without the permission of its owners, so he has guaranteed it. Evidence: (1) The saying of a Companion for establishing that if the depositary mixes it with his property until it becomes indistinguishable, he guarantees it: From Anas ibn Malik, he said: I was entrusted with wealth, so I placed it with my own wealth, and it perished among my wealth. The matter was raised to 'Umar, and he said: 'You are trustworthy in my view, but it perished among your wealth, so you guarantee it.' (Sunan al-Kubra lil-Bayhaqi, Book of No Liability on the Trusted, no. 12704). Evidence: (1) The saying of a Companion for establishing that if the owner demands it and he withholds it from him: 'Umar ibn al-Khattab said: 'The loan is in the position of the deposit, and there is no liability in it unless he transgresses.' (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, Book of Loan, no. 14785). Evidence: (1) The hadith for establishing that the depositary may travel with the deposit: That Hakim ibn Hizam, a companion of the Messenger of Allah, would stipulate to the man when he gave him money for a partnership that he should not place his money in a moist liver, nor carry it by sea, nor descend with it into a valley bed; if he does any of that, then he has guaranteed my money. (Sunan al-Daraqutni, Book of Sales, no. 3033). Evidence: (1) The saying of a Companion for establishing that when two men deposit with a man a deposit: From Hanash that two men... Principle: If he denies the trust in front of the owner, the trust is terminated; if he denies it in front of someone else, it is not terminated.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from Al-Quduri's matn on deposits, with commentary providing textual evidence. The opening statement establishes the foundational principle: a deposit (wadi'ah) in Hanafi law is a trust (amanah) in the depositary's hand. As a trust, the depositary is not liable for loss or destruction unless he commits an act of transgression (ta'addi) or negligence (taqsir). This is supported by multiple proofs: the Quranic verse commanding the rendering of trusts, several authentic hadith expressly stating no liability for the trusted depositary, and the practice of the Companions Abu Bakr and 'Umar. The passage then outlines specific scenarios: if the depositary keeps the deposit with someone other than his usual employees or re-deposits it, he becomes liable unless there is an emergency (fire, fear of sinking ship). Mixing the deposit with his own property so it cannot be distinguished makes him liable; if mixing occurs without his action, he becomes a co-owner proportional to his property. Spending part of the deposit and replacing it with similar property, then mixing, makes him liable for the entire amount. Transgression (using the deposit) creates liability, but if he ceases and returns it, liability ends. Denial of the deposit upon the owner's demand creates liability that is not removed by later confession. The depositary may travel with the deposit, but this may incur costs according to Abu Hanifa. Finally, the passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: if two people jointly deposit an item and one demands his share, Abu Hanifa refuses to give anything until both are present, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad give him his share. The commentary underscores that the underlying principle is that a trust is not guaranteed unless there is transgression or denial to the owner.

Key Terms
الْوَدِيعَةُ (al-wadi'ah): Property placed in the custody of another for safekeeping without compensation; a trust in Hanafi law.
أَمَانَةٌ (amanah): An item held in trust; the depositary is not responsible for its loss unless he commits ta'addi (transgression).
الْمُودَعُ (al-mudi'): The person with whom the deposit is left; the depositary.
الضَّمَانُ (al-daman): Liability to compensate for loss or damage; guarantee.

Key Takeaway
A deposit in Hanafi law is a trust; the depositary is not liable for loss without transgression, denial, or mixing, and this principle is supported by the Quran, Sunnah, and Companion practice.

Rulings on Deposits with Multiple Trustees and Conditions
Rulings on deposits with two trustees and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on deposits with multiple trustees and identify when a trustee becomes liable for violating conditions on safekeeping and delivery.

Core Idea
A trustee is liable only when deviation reduces protection; same courtyard safe, different courtyard not.

This lesson examines key rulings from the Book of Deposits in al-Quduri's matn. You will learn how trustees should handle divisible and indivisible deposits, the effect of conditions on liability, and a famous case decided by Umar ibn al-Khattab that applies the Quranic command to return trusts. These rules are foundational for understanding the Hanafi position on breach of trust.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and rulings
The passage covers three scenarios: (1) deposit with two trustees for divisible property, (2) deposit with conditions on location, (3) deposit with condition on delivery to only one of two co-owners. Identify the ruling for each.
Focus on the hukm (liability or not) not just the action.
2
Analyze the conditions for liability
For location conditions, note that moving to a different house in the same courtyard does not incur liability, but moving to a different courtyard does. Why? Because the degree of protection is similar within the same property.
This is key: liability depends on whether the deviation reduces security.
3
Apply the story of Umar
Study how Umar judged the woman who gave the deposit to one partner alone. She was held liable because she could not prove the recipient was entitled (the other partner had not died). This shows that violating a condition (delivery only when both come) triggers liability.
Note the procedural aspect: she had no evidence (bayyinah).
4
Connect to the Quranic proof
The woman quotes verse 4:58 to remind Umar to judge justly. The underlying principle is that trusts must be rendered to their rightful owners; deviation from conditions is a breach.
This is the dalil naqli supporting the rulings.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Deposit (Kitab al-Wadi'ah) presents a set of rulings from the matn of al-Quduri. First, when a depositor entrusts divisible property to two trustees, neither may simply transfer the whole to the other; they must divide it, and each is responsible for his half. For indivisible property, one may keep it only with the owner's permission. Second, if the depositor specifically prohibits delivery to the trustee's wife, and the trustee violates this condition, he is not liable (because a spouse is considered a trusted party). Third, if the depositor specifies a particular house for safekeeping, moving it to another house in the same courtyard does not incur liability, since the degree of protection is similar. However, moving it to a different courtyard (a separate property) constitutes a breach and makes the trustee liable. The story of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) demonstrates the principle that a trustee who violates conditions — here, delivering the deposit to one partner without the other — may be held liable. Umar's judgment indicates that if the trustee cannot prove the recipient was entitled, she is liable. The Quranic verse (4:58) is cited as the underlying principle for the obligation to return trusts. The passage establishes the Hanafi position that the trustee's liability depends on explicit conditions and the nature of the deviation.

Definition and Liability of 'Ariyah (Loan for Use)
Definition and basic rulings of 'ariyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'ariyah, identify valid offer expressions, explain the lender's right of revocation, and apply the rule that the borrower is not liable for loss without transgression.

Core Idea
Ariyah grants usufruct without compensation; the lender may revoke anytime, and the borrower is not liable if the item perishes without his fault.

This lesson opens the Book of Loans (Ariyah) in al-Quduri's matn. The text provides a clear definition, lists the phrases that create a valid contract, explains the lender's right to reclaim the item at will, and establishes the borrower's liability only for transgression. Understanding these fundamentals is essential before studying more complex rulings on borrowed property.

Guided Path
1
Define 'Ariyah
Identify the core definition: 'ariyah is the ownership of usufruct (manafi') without compensation (iwad). Note that it does not transfer ownership of the object itself.
Distinguish from hibah (gift of the object) and ijarah (lease with rent).
2
List Valid Expressions
Identify the phrases that form the contract: 'a'artuka, at'amtu, manah'tu, hamaltuka, akhdamtuka, sakhna, 'umra. Understand the condition that these must not intend hibah.
These expressions indicate permission to use, not transfer of ownership.
3
Understand the Lender's Right to Revoke
The text states the lender may revoke the 'ariyah at any time (mata shaa). Recognize that this makes the contract non-binding (ghayr lazim) in the Hanafi school.
This is a point of ikhtilaf with other schools, notably Shafi'is who consider it binding once possession is taken.
4
Apply the Trust Liability Rule
The borrowed item is a trust (amanah). If it perishes without transgression (ta'addi) by the borrower, he is not liable. If there is transgression, he must compensate.
Transgression includes misuse, negligence, or exceeding the agreed terms.

It is valid through specific verbal expressions such as 'I lend you' or 'I give you this land for use' etc., provided the intent is not a gift. The lender (mu'ir) has the right to revoke the loan at any time because it is a non-binding contract. The borrowed item is an amanah (trust); therefore, if it is destroyed without the borrower's transgression (ta'addi), he is not liable. The borrower cannot postpone return after demand. However, he may sub-lend the item to another if the benefit does not vary with the user (i.e., the item's utility is not person-specific). Special rule: giving coins (dirhams/dinars) or measured/weighed items as aariyah is actually considered a qard (loan for consumption) because these items are fungible; ownership of the corpus is transferred, not just usufruct. Lastly, borrowing land for building or planting is valid, implying the borrower may make permanent structures. The text then supplies several hadith proofs: (1) The Prophet borrowing a horse from Abu Talhah (Bukhari) – shows permissibility; (2) The verse 'and they withhold the small kindness' (al-Ma'un) is cited as proof; (3) Hadith 'the loan is to be returned' (Abu Dawud) indicates it is not guaranteed; (4) Hadith that aariyah is not guaranteed unless terms are violated (Abd al-Razzaq). The Urdu notes add that in an unrestricted aariyah of an animal, the borrower may choose the rider, but once chosen cannot change, and different riders matter.

وَجْهٌ (١): قَوْلُ التَّابِعِيِّ لِثُبُوتِ وَيَجُوزُ الْتِقَاطُ الشَّاةِ وَالْبَقَرَةِ وَالْبَعِيرِ: أَنَّهُ سَمِعَ ابْنَ شِهَابٍ يَقُولُ: "كَانَتْ ضَوَالُّ الإِبِلِ فِي زَمَانِ عُمَرَ بْنِ الْخَطَّابِ لَهُ إِبِلًا مُؤَبَّلَةً تَتَنَاتَجُ لَا يَمَسُّهَا، حَتَّى إِذَا كَانَ زَمَانُ عُثْمَانَ بْنِ عَفَّانَ أَمَرَ بِمَعْرِفَتِهَا وَتَعْرِيفِهَا ثُمَّ تُبَاعُ، فَإِذَا جَاءَ صَاحِبُهَا أُعْطِيَ ثَمَنَهَا" (سنن بيهقي، بَابُ الرَّجُلِ يَجِدُ ضَالَّةً يُرِيدُ رَدَّهَا عَلَى صَاحِبِهَا لَا يُرِيدُ أَكْلَهَا، نمبر 12080، مصنف عبدالرزاق، كِتَابُ اللُّقَطَةِ، نمبر 18597)

وَجْهٌ (٢): الْحَدِيثُ لِثُبُوتِ وَيَجُوزُ الْتِقَاطُ الشَّاةِ وَالْبَقَرَةِ وَالْبَعِيرِ: عَنْ زَيْدِ بْنِ خَالِدٍ الْجُهَنِيِّ قَالَ: «جَاءَ أَعْرَابِيٌّ إِلَى النَّبِيِّ صلى الله عليه وسلم فَسَأَلَهُ عَمَّا يَلْتَقِطُهُ... قَالَ: يَا رَسُولَ اللَّهِ، فَضَالَّةُ الْغَنَمِ؟ قَالَ: لَكَ أَوْ لِأَخِيكَ أَوْ لِلذِّئْبِ، قَالَ: ضَالَّةُ الْإِبِلِ؟ فَتَمَعَّرَ وَجْهُ النَّبِيِّ صلى الله عليه وسلم فَقَالَ: مَا لَكَ وَلَهَا، مَعَهَا حِذَاؤُهَا وَسِقَاؤُهَا، تَرِدُ الْمَاءَ وَتَأْكُلُ الشَّجَرَ» (بخاري، بَابُ ضَالَّةِ الْإِبِلِ، 2427، مسلم، 1722)

وَجْهٌ (٣): قَوْلُ التَّابِعِيِّ لِثُبُوتِ فَإِنْ أَنْفَقَ الْمُلْتَقِطُ عَلَيْهَا بِغَيْرِ إِذْنِ الْحَاكِمِ: عَنْ إِبْرَاهِيمَ قَالَ: «لَوْ أَنَّ رَجُلًا الْتَقَطَ وَلَدَ زِنًا فَأَرَادَ أَنْ يُنْفِقَ عَلَيْهِ وَيَكُونَ لَهُ عَلَيْهِ دَيْنٌ فَلْيُشْهِدْ، وَإِنْ كَانَ يُرِيدُ أَنْ يَحْتَسِبَ عَلَيْهِ فَلَا يُشْهِدْ» قَالَ أَبُو حَنِيفَةَ: أَقُولُ أَنَا لَيْسَ بِشَيْءٍ إِلَّا أَنْ يَفْرِضَهُ لَهُ عَلَيْهِ السُّلْطَانُ (مصنف عبدالرزاق، بَابُ وَلاءِ اللَّقِيطِ، نمبر 16188)

وَجْهٌ (٤): قَوْلُ التَّابِعِيِّ لِثُبُوتِ فَإِنْ أَنْفَقَ الْمُلْتَقِطُ عَلَيْهَا بِغَيْرِ إِذْنِ الْحَاكِمِ: أَنَّهُ سَمِعَ ابْنَ شِهَابٍ يَقُولُ: "كَانَتْ ضَوَالُّ الإِبِلِ فِي زَمَانِ عُمَرَ بْنِ الْخَطَّابِ إِبِلًا مُؤَبَّلَةً تَتَنَاتَجُ لَا يَمَسُّهَا، حَتَّى إِذَا كَانَ زَمَانُ عُثْمَانَ بْنِ عَفَّانَ أَمَرَ بِمَعْرِفَتِهَا وَتَعْرِيفِهَا ثُمَّ تُبَاعُ، فَإِذَا جَاءَ صَاحِبُهَا أُعْطِيَ ثَمَنَهَا" (سنن بيهقي، 12080، مصنف عبدالرزاق، 18597)

أصول: لقطہ پانے والے نے حاکم کی اجازت سے خرچ کیا تو وہ خرچ مالک پر قرض ہو گا۔

وَإِنْ كَانَ الْأَصْلَحُ الْإِنْفَاقَ عَلَيْهَا أَذِنَ الْحَاكِمُ فِي ذَلِكَ وَجَعَلَ النَّفَقَةَ دَيْنًا عَلَى مَالِكِهَا فَإِذَا حَضَرَ الْمَالِكُ فَلِلْمُلْتَقِطِ أَنْ يَمْنَعَهُ مِنْهَا حَتَّى يَأْخُذَ النَّفَقَةَ مِنْهُ وَلُقَطَةُ الْحِلِّ وَالْحَرَمِ سَوَاءٌ وَإِذَا حَضَرَ رَجُلٌ وَادَّعَى أَنَّ اللُّقَطَةَ لَهُ لَمْ تُدْفَعْ إِلَيْهِ حَتَّى يُقِيمَ الْبَيِّنَةَ

وَجْهٌ (٥): قَوْلُ الصَّحَابِيَّةِ لِثُبُوتِ وَلُقَطَةُ الْحِلِّ وَالْحَرَمِ سَوَاءٌ: أَنَّ امْرَأَةً سَأَلَتْ عَائِشَةَ فَقَالَتْ: إِنِّي أَصَبْتُ ضَالَّةً فِي الْحَرَمِ وَإِنِّي عَرَّفْتُهَا فَلَمْ أَجِدْ أَحَدًا يَعْرِفُهَا فَقَالَتْ لَهَا عَائِشَةُ: اسْتَنْفِعِي بِهَا (شرح معاني الآثار، بَابُ اللُّقَطَةِ وَالضَّوَالِّ، رقم 6085)

وَجْهٌ (٦): دَلِيلُ الشَّافِعِيِّ الْحَدِيثُ لِثُبُوتِ وَلُقَطَةُ الْحِلِّ وَالْحَرَمِ سَوَاءٌ: عَنِ ابْنِ عَبَّاسٍ رضي الله عنهما أَنَّ رَسُولَ اللَّهِ صلى الله عليه وسلم قَالَ: «... وَلَا تَحِلُّ لُقَطَتُهَا إِلَّا لِمُنْشِدٍ» (بخاري، بَابٌ: كَيْفَ تُعَرَّفُ لُقَطَةُ أَهْلِ مَكَّةَ، 2433)

وَجْهٌ (٧): الْحَدِيثُ لِثُبُوتِ اللُّقَطَةُ أَمَانَةٌ إِذَا أَشْهَدَ الْمُلْتَقِطُ: عَنْ عَبْدِ الرَّحْمَنِ بْنِ عُثْمَانَ التَّيْمِيِّ أَنَّ رَسُولَ اللَّهِ صلى الله عليه وسلم «نَهَى عَنْ لُقَطَةِ الْحَاجِّ» قَالَ أَحْمَدُ: قَالَ ابْنُ وَهْبٍ: يَعْنِي لُقَطَةَ الْحَاجِّ يَتْرُكُهَا حَتَّى يَجِدَهَا صَاحِبُهَا (سنن أبي داود، بَابُ التَّعْرِيفِ بِاللُّقَطَةِ، 1719)

وَجْهٌ (٨): الْحَدِيثُ لِثُبُوتِ وَإِذَا حَضَرَ رَجُلٌ وَادَّعَى أَنَّ اللُّقَطَةَ: عَنِ ابْنِ عَبَّاسٍ أَنَّ النَّبِيَّ صلى الله عليه وسلم قَالَ: «لَوْ يُعْطَى النَّاسُ بِدَعْوَاهُمْ لَادَّعَى نَاسٌ دِمَاءَ رِجَالٍ وَأَمْوَالَهُمْ وَلَكِنَّ الْيَمِينَ عَلَى الْمُدَّعَى عَلَيْهِ» (مسلم، بَابُ الْيَمِينِ عَلَى الْمُدَّعَى عَلَيْهِ، 1711)

وَجْهٌ (٩): الْحَدِيثُ لِثُبُوتِ وَإِذَا حَضَرَ رَجُلٌ وَادَّعَى أَنَّ اللُّقَطَةَ: عَنْ أَبِي هُرَيْرَةَ عَنِ النَّبِيِّ صلى الله عليه وسلم قَالَ: «الْبَيِّنَةُ عَلَى مَنِ ادَّعَى وَالْيَمِينُ عَلَى مَنْ أَنْكَرَ إِلَّا فِي الْقَسَامَةِ» (سنن الدارقطني، كِتَابُ الْحُدُودِ وَالدِّيَاتِ وَغَيْرُهُ، 3190)

وَجْهٌ (١٠): دَلِيلُ الشَّافِعِيِّ الْحَدِيثُ لِثُبُوتِ وَإِذَا حَضَرَ رَجُلٌ وَادَّعَى أَنَّ اللُّقَطَةَ: عَنْ زَيْدِ بْنِ خَالِدٍ الْجُهَنِيِّ قَالَ: «جَاءَ أَعْرَابِيٌّ إِلَى النَّبِيِّ صلى الله عليه وسلم فَسَأَلَهُ...» (بخاري، 2427)

أصول: حرم اور حل دونوں لقطے کا ایک ہی حکم ہے کہ ایک سال تک تشہیر کرے بعد ازاں صدقہ کر دے۔
Book of Found Property. It is permissible to pick up the sheep, the cow, and the camel. If the finder spends on them without the permission of the judge, then he is a volunteer (donor). And if he spends by his (the judge's) command, then that is a debt upon its owner. And when that is raised to the judge, he looks into it: if the animal has a benefit, he hires it out and spends on it from its hire; and if it has no benefit and he fears that the expenses will consume its value, he sells it and orders the preservation of its price. (Proof 1: Statement of the Tabi'i for establishing permissibility... Proof 2: The hadith... etc.) Principles: If the finder spends with the judge's permission, that expense becomes a debt on the owner. And if it is most beneficial to spend on it, the judge permits that and makes the expense a debt upon its owner. When the owner appears, the finder may withhold the item from him until he takes the expense from him. And the found property of the lawful area and the sacred area are the same. And when a man appears and claims that the found property is his, it is not given to him until he produces evidence. (Proofs continue)... Principles: The ruling for found property in the Haram and Hal is the same: announce for one year, then give in charity.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Luqata (Book of Found Property) presents the core Hanafi rulings on finding lost animals (sheep, cow, camel). The author, al-Quduri, states that picking them up is permissible. The key legal distinction is regarding expenses: if the finder spends money on the animal without a judge's permission, it is considered a voluntary donation (tabarru'), and he cannot claim reimbursement from the owner. However, if he spends with the judge's order, it becomes a debt on the owner. The judge has the authority to decide the best course: if the animal has benefit (e.g., can be hired), he may rent it out and use the income for upkeep; if not and the expenses might exceed its value, he may sell it and keep the proceeds safe. The proofs (wujuh) include the practice of Caliph Umar and Uthman (as reported by Ibn Shihab) and a hadith from Zayd ibn Khalid about the Prophet's response to a Bedouin regarding lost sheep and camels. The Prophet permitted taking sheep but discouraged taking camels because they can survive on their own. This indicates that sheep (and by extension, cows) can be picked up, while camels are best left due to their self-sufficiency. Another proof from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i about a foundling child is applied analogically to animals: spending without authorization is a donation unless the ruler mandates it. The text then moves to the judge's role in managing the found animal. It also equates the ruling for luqata in the non-sacred (al-Hil) and sacred (al-Haram) areas; both require announcement for a year. Finally, it addresses a claimant: if someone comes forward, he must provide evidence (bayyinah) before the item is handed over, based on the hadith 'The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the defendant.'

Key Terms
لُقَطَة (luqata): Found property, specifically lost items or animals that have no known owner at the time of finding.
ضَالَّة (dalla): A lost animal, especially one that wanders away from its owner.
مُلْتَقِط (multaqit): The person who finds and takes possession of a lost item (the finder).
تَبَرُّع (tabarru'): An act of voluntary donation; spending without expectation of repayment.
الْحِل وَالْحَرَم (al-hil wa al-haram): The non-sacred area (outside the sanctuary of Makkah) and the sacred area (the Haram of Makkah).
الْبَيِّنَة (al-bayyinah): Proof, evidence, or testimony required to establish a claim.

Reward for Returning a Runaway Slave: The Hanafi Ruling and Its Proofs
Ruling on reward for returning a runaway slave with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the reward (ju'l) for returning a runaway slave, identify the proofs from the Sunnah and the sayings of the Companions, and apply the ruling to scenarios involving distance and value.

Core Idea
Forty dirhams for three days' journey: the reward for returning a runaway slave is fixed by the Sunnah.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents the Hanafi ruling on the reward due to a person who returns a runaway slave to his master. The ruling specifies a fixed amount for a distance of three days or more, and proportional compensation for shorter distances. The text also addresses the case where the slave's value is less than the fixed reward, and the liability (or lack thereof) if the slave runs away again from the returner. The proofs include a hadith from Ibn Mas'ud and a statement of 'Ali, establishing both the reward and the non-liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? Returning a runaway slave. What is the ruling? The reward (ju'l) is wajib upon the master when the slave is returned from a distance of three days or more.
The reward is forty dirhams for three days or more.
2
Locate the dalil
Which proof establishes the amount? The hadith of Abu 'Amr al-Shaybani from Ibn Mas'ud, who explicitly stated the reward as forty dirhams per slave.
This is a sunnah ahad, zanni al-thubut, but accepted in practice.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith indicate the amount? The explicit phrase 'arba'una dirhaman min kulli ra's' is taken as a binding precedent for the reward when the distance is three days or more.
The Hanafi school considers this as a fixed amount, not a matter of estimation.
4
Note the ikhtilaf and tarjih
Some scholars like Shuraih differed on liability if the slave runs away again. The Hanafi school follows the saying of 'Ali that the returner is not liable unless he trespassed.
The mufta bihi position is as stated in the matn.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Runaway Slaves states the ruling on the reward for returning a runaway slave. The author, al-Quduri, rules that if a slave runs away and someone returns him from a journey of three days or more, the master must pay the returner forty dirhams. If the distance is less, the reward is proportional (e.g., one dinar per day). If the slave's value is less than forty dirhams, the returner receives the value minus one dirham — a safeguard to prevent the reward from exceeding the slave's worth. If the slave runs away again from the returner, the returner is not liable provided he did not trespass. The author recommends taking witnesses when capturing the slave to clarify intention. If the runaway is a pledged slave (rahn), the reward is upon the pledgor (mrqin = murhi? unclear). The text then provides proofs: (1) a hadith from Abu Hurayra about covering water vessels (though its relevance is unclear; perhaps a copyist error), (2) a hadith from Abu 'Amr al-Shaybani giving forty dirhams per slave, (3) a practice of 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz giving a dinar per day up to four, (4) a saying of 'Ali that the returner is not liable if the slave runs away again, opposing Shuraih's view. The Urdu principle explains that the reward is calculated per day's journey.

Reviving Dead Land (Ihya al-Mawat)
Ruling on reviving dead land, ownership conditions, and intra-Hanafi dispute on permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of dead land, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the requirement of the imam's permission for ownership, and apply the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Reviving dead land can grant ownership, but the need for the imam's permission divides the Hanafi school.

In this lesson, we examine the rules governing dead land (mawat) in Hanafi fiqh. The text defines mawat, sets conditions for ownership through revival (ihya), and presents a key internal disagreement: Abu Hanifa requires the imam's permission, while the Sahibayn do not. Understanding this ikhtilaf and its resolution is essential for applying the ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Define Dead Land
Identify the three conditions for land to be considered mawat: uncultivable, ownerless, and distant from habitation.
The distance is measured by audibility of a shout from the edge of the inhabited area.
2
State the Ruling on Ownership
Whoever revives mawat with the imam's permission becomes owner.
This is agreed upon.
3
Analyze the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Contrast Abu Hanifa's requirement of permission with the Sahibayn's unconditional ownership.
The opposing school is within the Hanafi school itself.
4
Examine the Evidence and Thamarah
Review the hadith cited and understand how each side interprets it. Identify the practical consequence of the different views.
The thamarah al-khilaf affects ownership rights in communities.
5
Apply the Mufta Bihi and Related Rules
Note the later tarjih favoring the Sahibayn, and also handle the rule on tahjir (enclosure) and dhimmi ownership.
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view.

Harim of a Canal in Another's Land: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on canal harim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on the harim of a canal in another's land and apply the Sahibayn's view to determine the automatic right to a pathway.

Core Idea
A canal in another's land: no harim without proof? Or automatic pathway for maintenance?

This passage from al-Quduri presents a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on whether a canal owner automatically acquires a harim (protected zone) when the canal runs through someone else's property. Understanding this dispute clarifies how Hanafi fiqh balances property rights with practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the two positions
The subject is a canal (nahr) located in another person's land. State clearly what each imam rules: Abu Hanifa says no harim unless proof; Sahibayn say a musannah (pathway and mud-place) is automatic.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa relies on the principle that no one can use another's property without a clear right. A harim would impose on the landowner's rights without evidence.
His position protects property inviolability.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's reasoning
The Sahibayn argue from necessity: a canal requires maintenance and cleaning, so a minimal pathway and mud-place are essential accessory rights. They draw analogy (qiyas) to the harim of wells.
They cite the hadith on well harim as support.
4
Examine the hadith evidence
The hadith specifies harim measurements for wells and springs. The Sahibayn use it analogically for canals. Abu Hanifa might restrict it to wells on one's own land.
The hadith is ahad, thus zanni al-thubut.
5
Assess the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the canal owner must present evidence of a prior right to claim a harim. Under the Sahibayn, a minimal harim is granted automatically for access and maintenance.
Most later fatwas adopt the Sahibayn's view for practicality.

Four Forms of Muzara'ah According to Sahibayn
Enumeration of four muzara'ah forms with validity rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four forms of muzara'ah according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, identify which is invalid, and explain the principle that seed ownership determines crop ownership.

Core Idea
Seed ownership determines crop; other inputs are hire, enabling three valid forms.

This passage from Al-Quduri, explained by later commentators, presents the Sahibayn's (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) detailed classification of muzara'ah (sharecropping) contracts. While Imam Abu Hanifa generally considered muzara'ah with a share of produce invalid due to gharar, his two disciples distinguished four specific arrangements based on who provides the seed. Three are deemed valid because the seed owner clearly owns the crop and others are hired; the fourth is invalid due to uncertainty in dividing the produce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four parties and contributions
In muzara'ah, there are four potential inputs: land, seed, oxen (or plow), and labor. The text specifies who provides each in each form.
The fourth form is invalid because the seed and labor are together vs. land and oxen, causing ambiguity.
2
Apply the seed-ownership rule
In Hanafi fiqh, the owner of the seed owns the crop. All other contributions are considered as hire (ujrat). This principle is derived from hadith and is the key to validity.
If the seed is with one party, that party's ownership of the crop is clear; others get fixed compensation.
3
Compare the four forms
Examine each of the four forms listed: (1) land+seed vs labor+oxen: valid; (2) land vs labor+oxen+seed: valid; (3) land+seed+oxen vs labor: valid; (4) land+oxen vs seed+labor: invalid. Note which party owns the seed in each.
In the invalid form, the seed is not with the land/oxen owner nor solely with the other, leading to jahalah.
4
Understand the proof from hadith
The hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij (Abu Dawud) shows the Prophet ruling that the seed owner gets the crop and the landowner gets expenses. This supports the principle.
Also, the hadith from Mujahid (in al-Asl) explicitly allocates crop to seed owner and gives fixed rent to others.

So we took our crops and gave him back his expenses. (Abu Dawud 3399) Proof (2) for the four types: From Mujahid: Four persons shared on the covenant of the Prophet (peace be upon him). One said: I provide seed, another: I provide labor, another: I provide the plow, another: I provide the land. He ruled: The owner of the plow gets a named rent, the worker gets a dirham each day, all the crops belong to the owner of the seed, and the landowner is excluded. (al-Asl, Kitab al-Muzara'ah, no. 526) Proof (3) for the four types: From Rafi' ibn Khadij: The Messenger of Allah said: 'Whoever sows in a people's land without their permission, he has nothing of the crop, and he gets his expenses.' (Abu Dawud 3403) Principles: (1) The one with the seed owns the grain; the other party's contribution is like hired on rent, so this form is valid. (2) The one with labor, seed, and oxen takes the land on rent for grain, so this form is valid. (3) The one with land, seed, and oxen hires the worker, so this form is valid.

Commentary
This passage expands on the Sahibayn's position by enumerating four possible forms of muzara'ah contract based on who contributes land, seed, oxen, and labor. Three are valid (ja'iz) and one is invalid (batil). The valid forms are: (1) Land and seed from one party, labor and oxen from the other. (2) Land only from one party, all else from the other. (3) Land, seed, and oxen from one party, labor from the other. The invalid form is: Land and oxen from one party, seed and labor from the other. The Urdu usul principles clarify the reasoning: in the valid forms, the ownership of the produce is clear and the other contributions are treated as hire (ujrat), avoiding gharar. The hadith from Rafi' ibn Khadij and the case from Mujahid support this division. The proof from Abu Ja'far (a tabi'i) shows the practice of the Sahaba, which the Sahibayn rely on. Note that the text also includes a proof that might seem to support Abu Hanifa (the companion's saying) but it is listed here as a proof for the Sahibayn's classification? Actually the text says 'وجه (۳) قول الصحابي لثبوت قال أبو حنيفة...' but then quotes Abu Ja'far's statement about the practice of the Muhajirun, which actually supports the permissibility. This may be a misplaced reference or the author is showing that even the Sahibayn use this practice to support their view. The lesson is a parsing exercise because it lists four distinct cases with clear rulings.

الفَدَّان (al-faddān): Plow (with oxen). In the hadith, the plow owner gets a fixed rent.
