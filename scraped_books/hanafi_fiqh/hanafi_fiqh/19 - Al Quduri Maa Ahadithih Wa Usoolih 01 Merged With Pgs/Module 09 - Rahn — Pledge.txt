Formation and Conditions of Rahn (Pledge)
Defines rahn contract and its prerequisites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the pillars and conditions of a valid rahn contract and identify the role of possession in making it binding.

Core Idea
Rahn is concluded by offer and acceptance, but becomes binding only upon possession.

This lesson introduces the foundational rules of the pledge (rahn) contract as taught by Imam al-Quduri. You will learn how the contract is formed, what makes it binding, and the key conditions for its validity. We will also examine the primary textual evidence from the Quran and Sunnah that supports the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the formation elements
The text states that rahn is 'concluded by offer and acceptance.' What are the specific words or actions that constitute offer (ijab) and acceptance (qubul)? Identify these as the pillars (arkan) of the contract.
Offer comes from the pledgor, acceptance from the pledgee.
2
Understand the role of possession (qabd)
The text says the contract is 'completed by possession.' Distinguish between the initial conclusion (offer+acceptance) and the perfection/completion (by qabd). Until possession, the pledgor has an option to withdraw. This is a key Hanafi principle.
Possession is not a pillar but a condition for bindingness.
3
Examine the condition of a guaranteed debt
The rahn is only valid for a 'guaranteed debt' (dayn madmun). What does this mean? A debt that is certain and enforceable. The phrase 'guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt' explains the liability of the pledgee if the pledged item is lost.
This is a unique Hanafi rule: the pledge covers the debt to the extent of the lesser amount.
4
Analyze the Quranic evidence
The author cites Surah al-Baqarah 2:283: '...then a pledge in hand.' How does this verse support the requirement of possession? The phrase 'maqbudah' indicates that the pledge must be physically received. In Hanafi usul, the imperative form implies obligation.
The dalala (indication) is zanni, but the thubut (transmission) is qati.
5
Analyze the hadith evidence
Two hadith are presented: (1) the Prophet pledging his armor to a Jew, and (2) the hadith 'the pledge is for what it contains.' How do these establish the permissibility and liability rules? The first shows the act of the Prophet (practice), the second indicates that the pledgee bears responsibility for the pledged item.
The hadith are from reliable sources (Bukhari, Darqutni, Bayhaqi).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from al-Quduri's matn on the contract of rahn (pledge/mortgage) along with its proofs. The author begins by defining the formation of the pledge contract: it requires both an offer (ijab) from the pledgor and acceptance (qubul) from the pledgee, and it is perfected (tamm) only by physical possession (qabd). The pledgor retains an option to deliver or withdraw until possession occurs. Once possession is taken, the pledge enters the liability (daman) of the pledgee, meaning the pledgee is responsible for it. Then he states two important conditions: (1) the pledge is only valid for a guaranteed debt (dayn madmun) - that is, a debt that is established and can be recovered; (2) the pledge is guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt - if the pledge is destroyed, the pledgee's claim is satisfied to that extent. After the matn, the commentary presents several evidences: the Quranic verse from Surah al-Baqarah (283) which mentions 'a pledge in hand,' indicating the requirement of possession; the hadith of Aisha about the Prophet pledging his armor for food; the practice of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz requiring separation and marking for gifts; and two hadiths establishing that the pledgee is liable for the pledge once in his possession: the hadith of the pledged horse where the Prophet said 'your right is gone' (indicating the pledgee loses the debt if the pledge perishes without his fault? Actually the Hanafi position is that the pledge is a trust in the hand of the pledgee, but here the hadith suggests liability? The commentary reconciles by stating that the pledge is in the liability of the pledgee meaning he is responsible for it, and if it is destroyed, his right to the debt is extinguished. The Urdu section defines the key terms: rahin (pledgor), murtahin (pledgee), and marhun (pledged item).

Key Terms
الرَّهْنُ (ar-rahn): The contract of pledge or mortgage where property is given as security for a debt.
الْإِيجَابُ (al-ijab): The offer made by the pledgor to pledge the property.
الْقَبُولُ (al-qubul): The acceptance by the pledgee of the pledge.
الْقَبْضُ (al-qabd): Physical possession or taking control of the pledged item by the pledgee.
ضَمَان (daman): Liability; the pledgee becomes liable for the pledge once in his possession; if destroyed, he loses the debt proportionally.

Destruction of the Pledge: Scenarios and Proofs
Explains the rule if the pledge is destroyed in the pledgee's hand.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios when a pledge is destroyed in the pledgee's hands and apply the corresponding Hanafi rulings, while identifying the supporting evidence from hadith and the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

Core Idea
When a pledge perishes, the debt is satisfied up to its value; excess is a trust, deficiency is recoverable.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses a common and delicate issue: what happens if the pledged item is destroyed while in the pledgee's custody? The ruling hinges on the relative values of the pledge and the debt. The matn lays out three clear scenarios, each with a distinct legal outcome. We will also examine the key proofs—a hadith of the Prophet ﷺ and a statement of Umar ibn al-Khattab—that underpin the Hanafi position, which treats the pledge as being under the pledgee's liability (daman) rather than a mere trust.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Read the matn and list the three possibilities: (1) value equals debt, (2) value exceeds debt, (3) value is less than debt.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For scenario 1: the debt is considered fully paid (mustawfiyan hukman). For scenario 2: the debt is paid, and the excess value is a trust (amanah) with the pledgee. For scenario 3: the debt is reduced by the value of the pledge, and the pledgee recovers the remainder.
Understand the legal fiction: the pledgee is deemed to have received the debt 'hukman'.
3
Analyze the hadith proof
Look at the hadith 'La yughallu ar-rahn, lahu ghanmuhu wa alayhi ghurmuhu.' Identify the phrase 'wa alayhi ghurmuhu' (upon him are its losses) as the basis for the pledgee's liability (daman). Discuss its thubut and dalala.
Hanafis classify this hadith as ahad and zanni in meaning, but it suffices for a ruling.
4
Examine the companion saying of Umar
Umar's statement provides a practical precedent: if the pledge is lost and its value is less than the debt, the pledgee returns the balance; if more, he is a trustee. This supports the view that the pledgee is liable for the debt portion but not for the excess.
The 'excess as trust' means the pledgee is not liable for its loss unless negligent.
5
Evaluate the practical implication (thamarah)
The key difference from other schools: the Hanafi view is that the pledge is in daman (liability) for the debt, whereas others consider it a trust entirely. This affects who bears the loss if the pledge perishes without negligence.
This thamarah al-khilaf is crucial for financial transactions.

Commentary
This passage continues the matn of al-Quduri and provides the ruling on the destruction of the pledged item while in the pledgee's possession. Three scenarios are given: (1) If the value of the pledge equals the debt, the pledgee's debt is considered fully satisfied legally (though the item is gone). (2) If the value is more than the debt, the excess over the debt remains as a trust (amanah) in the pledgee's hands; he is not liable for it beyond the debt, and if it perishes without his fault, he does not owe the excess to the pledgor. (3) If the value is less than the debt, the debt is reduced by the amount of the value, and the pledgee can claim the remaining debt from the pledgor. The commentary then gives supporting evidences: a hadith from Abu Hurairah stating that the pledge does not become 'locked' (la yughallu) and that the pledgee enjoys its profits but bears its losses (like a type of liability). This hadith is used to argue that the pledgee is liable for the pledge. Also the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab is cited: if the pledge is lost, and its value is less than the debt, the pledgee returns the balance; if more, he is a trustee (meaning he is not liable for the excess). These proofs support the Hanafi position that the pledge is in the pledgee's liability (daman) but he does not own it.

Key Terms
مُسْتَوْفِيًا (mustawfiyan): The pledgee is considered to have received his debt in full, i.e., the debt is extinguished when the pledged item of equal value perishes.
الْفَاضِلُ (al-fadil): The excess value of the pledge over the debt. This excess is considered a trust (amanah) with the pledgee.
أَمَانَةٌ (amanah): Trust; the excess value is a trust, not subject to liability for loss unless by transgression.

Invalid Rahn: Undivided Share and Amanah Debts
Lists invalid and valid rahn subjects with a dispute on musha'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which items cannot be validly pledged in the Hanafi school and explain the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute regarding the pledge of an undivided share.

Core Idea
For a pledge to be valid, it must be physically separable and the debt must be guaranteed.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lists the invalid and valid forms of rahn. It highlights a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding pledging an undivided share (musha'). The Hanafi school requires full possession (qabd) which is impossible for an undivided share, while the Shafi'i school relies on a hadith that may allow it. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly structuring pledges.

Guided Path
1
List the invalid pledges
Read the passage and identify all items that cannot be pledged according to the Hanafi school: undivided share, fruit without trees, crops without land, land without crops, and trust debts (deposits, loans, mudarabah, partnership property).
2
Understand the rationale for prohibition
For each prohibited item, deduce the underlying principle. Undivided shares cannot be physically possessed. Trust debts are not guaranteed (dain madmun). Incomplete separation of fruit or crops from the land also prevents full possession.
3
Analyze the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on musha'
The Hanafi school relies on the Quranic verse requiring a 'pledge in hand' (maqbudah) to argue that possession must be complete. The Shafi'i school uses a hadith about pledging land which may imply that undivided shares can be pledged. Note the practical consequence of this difference.
4
Apply the ruling to valid cases
Identify the exceptions: pledge for capital of salam, price of sarf, and muslam fihi are valid because these debts are guaranteed. Also note the rule about perishing in the contract session.
5
Evaluate the rule of placing with an adl
Understand that if both parties agree to place the pledge with a trustworthy third party, it is permissible, and neither party can unilaterally retrieve it. This ensures mutual security.

Manumission of a pledged slave is effective, but the pledgee's right is protected via substitution or the slave's work.

When a pledger frees a slave that has been pledged as collateral, the manumission is valid and takes effect immediately. Learning Objective

Core Idea
The pledgee, not the pledger, sues for damages—the compensation becomes a new pledge.

When a pledged item is destroyed by a stranger, who has the right to claim compensation? The Hanafi school gives this right exclusively to the pledgee because he holds possession and has a direct interest. The compensation he receives itself becomes a new pledge. This lesson explains the ruling and its scriptural proofs.

Guided Path

State the case and the ruling
Identify the scenario: a third party destroys the pledged item. Then state the Hanafi ruling: the pledgee is the plaintiff, he takes the compensation, and it becomes a new pledge in his hand.

The pledgee has a right over the pledge by virtue of possession. The third party's destruction harms that right. The pledgee is therefore entitled to sue and recover value.
This follows the maxim that possession gives standing.
3

The hadith of al-ʿAbbās shows that conditions protecting one's interest in a contract are valid (as approved by the Prophet). Analogically, the pledgee's right to claim compensation protects his interest.

The hadith 'lā ḍarar wa lā ḍirār' prohibits causing harm. The third party's destruction is harm, so he must compensate. The pledgee is the one harmed, so he sues.

The hadith of ʿAmr ibn Yathribī forbids taking a brother's property unless given willingly. The compensation ensures the pledgee's right is not lost without his consent.
The compensation replaces the destroyed pledge, preserving the pledgee's security.

Rulings on liability for injuries and expenses on pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on liability for injuries to and expenses of a pledged item, and explain the proofs from hadith.

Core Idea
Pledger bears his own torts and maintenance; pledgee's tort reduces his debt; pledge's tort is uncompensable.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents a comprehensive set of rules governing liability and expenses when an item is pledged as security for a debt. The rulings distinguish between injuries caused by the pledger, the pledgee, and the pledged item itself, and also allocate the costs of storage, herding, and maintenance. The proofs are drawn from two hadith that establish risk-bearing principles. By working through this passage, you will understand how Hanafi fiqh balances the interests of both parties in a pledge contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three parties and the pledge
The passage involves the pledger (rāhin), the pledgee (murtahin), and the pledged item (rahn). Understand their roles: pledger is the debtor-owner, pledgee is the creditor-possessor.
2
Categorize each ruling on tort liability
Three scenarios: (1) Pledger injures the pledge → compensable (maḍmūna) – he must pay damages. (2) Pledgee injures the pledge → reduction from his debt by the amount of injury. (3) The pledge injures the pledger, pledgee, or their property → uncompensable (hadar).
Note the logic: the pledgee's injury reduces debt because he already has a security interest.

Allocate the expenses
Rent of storage place (ujrat al-bayt) is on pledgee (since he benefits from possession). Herder's wage (ujrat al-rāʿī) and general maintenance (nafaqa) are on the pledger (owner).
The distinction: possession triggers storage cost; ownership triggers upkeep.

First hadith: a pledged horse died in the pledgee's hand – Prophet said pledgee's right is lost (risk on him). Second hadith: 'The pledge is not to be withheld; its owner enjoys benefits and bears losses.' This supports that maintenance and herder's wage are on the pledger.
Both hadith have zanni thubut and dalala, but are accepted in Hanafi school.

Ruling on the status of offspring of a pledge with proportional distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the offspring of a pledged item and apply the proportional redemption method when the original pledge is destroyed.

Core Idea
The offspring of a pledge is part of the pledge and redeemed proportionally.

This ruling from al-Quduri addresses the status of produce or offspring (ghull/namāʾ) generated by a pledged item. While the offspring belongs to the pledger, it is automatically included in the pledge. If the original pledge is destroyed but the offspring remains, a proportional calculation determines how much of the debt is discharged and how much must be paid to redeem the offspring.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The ruling concerns the offspring (ghull) or increase (namāʾ) that arises from the pledged item after the contract of rahn.
Examples: fruit from a tree, milk from an animal, foal from a mare.
2
State the general principle
The offspring is considered part of the pledge (rahman maʿa al-aṣl) and belongs to the pledger (lil-rāhin).
It is not owned by the pledgee, but is pledged as security.
3
Analyze the two scenarios
Scenario 1: Offspring perishes → no compensation, because it is part of the pledge and loss is on the owner. Scenario 2: Original perishes, offspring remains → offspring can be redeemed by paying its proportional share of the debt.

4
Detail the proportional redemption method
The debt is divided based on the value of the original at the time of possession (yawm al-qabḍ) and the value of the offspring at the time of redemption (yawm al-fikāk). The share of debt corresponding to the original is discharged; the share for the offspring must be paid to free it.
Formula: Portion for original = (Value_original at possession) / (Total value) × Debt; Portion for offspring = (Value_offspring at redemption) / (Total value) × Debt.
5

The hadith: 'La yughlaq al-rahn; li-ṣāḥibihi ghunmuhu wa-ʿalayhi ghurmuhu' — the owner gets the benefit (ghunm) and bears the loss (ghurm), indicating that offspring (a benefit) belongs to the pledger and is part of the pledge.
The hadith is from Sunan al-Daraqutni and is ahad, thus zanni al-thubut.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Rahn presents a series of rulings regarding the pledge (rahn) contract from the Hanafi perspective. The text begins by addressing a scenario with multiple pledgors: each is liable for his share of the debt, and if one repays, the entire pledge remains with the other until full recovery. It then covers a conditional sale where the buyer agrees to pledge an item for the price but refuses; he is compelled to deliver, and the seller may either accept the situation or cancel the sale, unless the buyer pays immediately or provides an equivalent pledge. The pledgee (murtahin) is permitted to keep the pledge with himself and his immediate family/household; storing it with others or depositing it with a third party without necessity constitutes a breach (ta'addi) and makes him liable for the value. If the pledgee voluntarily returns possession to the pledgor as a loan (i'arah), the pledge leaves the pledgee's liability; if it perishes with the pledgor, the pledgor loses nothing, but the pledgee may repossess it at any time, restoring liability. Upon the pledgor's death, his executor must sell the pledge and pay off the debt; if no executor exists, the judge appoints one to ensure the right is enforced. The proof cited is the well-known hadith 'The pledge is not forfeited' (la yughlaq al-rahn), establishing that the pledgee does not automatically own the pledge by default; it remains the pledgor's property, with profits for him and losses upon him. The Urdu principles (usul) further clarify: any increase from the pledge is a trust; keeping it with non-household is misconduct; a person's own property perishing in his possession incurs no liability; and if a right is at risk, the judge must protect it.

Conditional Sale with Agreement to Pledge (Rahn)
Ruling on conditional sale with pledge agreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the seller's options when a buyer refuses to deliver an agreed pledge and apply the exceptions of immediate payment or substitute pledge.

Core Idea
The seller may compel the pledge or cancel the sale unless the buyer substitutes payment or another pledge.

This lesson covers a sale made conditional on the buyer giving a pledge for the price. The Hanafi school provides the seller with a choice: enforce the pledge, accept its absence, or rescind the sale, unless the buyer pays immediately or offers an equivalent pledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional agreement
The seller sells an item on the condition that the buyer pledges a specific thing for the price. This is a valid condition in the Hanafi school.
2
Determine the buyer's refusal
If the buyer refuses to deliver the agreed pledge, he is compelled to do so by the judge.
Compulsion indicates the condition is binding.
3
Analyze the seller's options
The seller may either accept the abandonment of the pledge (i.e., waive the condition) or rescind the sale. These are the seller's rights.
The seller is not forced to continue without the pledge.
4
Apply the exceptions
The seller's right to rescind is blocked if the buyer pays the price immediately or delivers the value of the agreed pledge as a replacement pledge. In these cases, the sale remains valid.
The substitute must be a pledge of equivalent value.

Custody, Misconduct, and Lending the Pledge (Rahn)
Rulings on custody, misconduct, and lending of the pledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the pledgee's duties in custody, identify misconduct and its consequences, and explain the effect of lending the pledge to the pledgor.

Core Idea
The pledgee must keep the pledge with his household; misconduct triggers ghasb liability; lending it to the pledgor ends liability until repossession.

This lesson covers three interrelated rulings on the pledgee's responsibilities: proper custody, consequences of misuse, and the effect of voluntarily returning possession to the pledgor. Understanding these safeguards is essential for protecting both parties' rights.

Guided Path
1
Define permissible custody
The pledgee may keep the pledge himself, his spouse, his child, and his servant who lives with him. This is the standard of care.
These are considered trustworthy.
2
Identify impermissible custody and liability
If the pledgee keeps the pledge with anyone outside his household (e.g., a stranger) or deposits it with a third party, he becomes liable (dhamin) for its value.
This is because he breached the standard of care.
3
Recognize misconduct (ta'addi)
If the pledgee misuses the pledge (e.g., uses it without permission), he is liable for the full value as in ghasb (usurpation).
Misconduct includes any unauthorized use or abuse.
4
Analyze the effect of lending to the pledgor
If the pledgee voluntarily lends the pledge to the pledgor and the pledgor takes possession, the pledge leaves the pledgee's liability. If it perishes in the pledgor's hands, no compensation is due. The pledgee may reclaim it at any time; liability returns upon repossession.
This is a special rule: the pledge becomes a trust in the pledgor's hands.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Rahn presents a series of rulings regarding the pledge (rahn) contract from the Hanafi perspective. The text begins by addressing a scenario with multiple pledgors: each is liable for his share of the debt, and if one repays, the entire pledge remains with the other until full recovery. It then covers a conditional sale where the buyer agrees to pledge an item for the price but refuses; he is compelled to deliver, and the seller may either accept the situation or cancel the sale, unless the buyer pays immediately or provides an equivalent pledge. The pledgee (murtahin) is permitted to keep the pledge with himself and his immediate family/household; storing it with others or depositing it with a third party without necessity constitutes a breach (ta'addi) and makes him liable for the value. If the pledgee is guilty of misconduct, he must pay full compensation as if he had usurped (ghasb) the property. If the pledgee voluntarily returns possession to the pledgor as a loan (i'arah), the pledge leaves the pledgee's liability; if it perishes with the pledgor, the pledgor loses nothing, but the pledgee may repossess it at any time, restoring liability. Upon the pledgor's death, his executor must sell the pledge and pay off the debt; if no executor exists, the judge appoints one to ensure the right is enforced. The proof cited is the well-known hadith 'The pledge is not forfeited' (la yughlaq al-rahn), establishing that the pledgee does not automatically own the pledge by default; it remains the pledgor's property, with profits for him and losses upon him. The Urdu principles (usul) further clarify: any increase from the pledge is a trust; keeping it with non-household is misconduct; a person's own property perishing in his possession incurs no liability; and if a right is at risk, the judge must protect it.

الضَّمَان (al-daman): Liability for compensation; being accountable for the value of property.
الْغَصْب (al-ghasb): Usurpation; wrongful taking or use of another's property.
الإِعَارَة (al-i'arah): A loan of an object for use, without compensation.

Sale of Pledge After Death of Pledgor and the Prophetic Proof
Ruling on sale of pledge after pledgor's death and the hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the procedure for selling a pledge upon the pledgor's death and explain the significance of the hadith 'la yughlaq al-rahn' as a foundational proof for pledge rulings.

Core Idea
Upon the pledgor's death, the pledge must be sold by the executor or judge, and the hadith confirms the pledge is never forfeited.

The final part of this passage addresses what happens when the pledgor dies: the pledged property must be sold to repay the debt, with the judge ensuring this if no executor exists. The passage then cites the key hadith that underpins the entire law of pledge in the Hanafi school: the pledge is not forfeited but must be liquidated fairly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation after pledgor's death
Upon the pledgor's death, the executor (wasi) is obligated to sell the pledge and use the proceeds to repay the debt.
This is to honor the debt and return any surplus to the heirs.
2
Determine the procedure if no executor exists
If the deceased pledgor did not appoint an executor, the judge (qadi) appoints one specifically for this task.
The judge acts as protector of rights.
3
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith from Abu Hurairah (ra) states: 'The pledge is not forfeited, and the pledge belongs to the one who pledged it; for him its profit, and upon him its loss.' This hadith is a key proof for many pledge rulings.
It establishes that ownership remains with the pledgor.

Derive the legal principles (usul)
The Urdu principles explain: (14) increase of the pledge is a trust, not deducted from debt; (15) keeping with non-household is misconduct; (17) loss in own possession incurs no liability; (18) judge supervises when right is at risk.
These principles summarize the rulings.
