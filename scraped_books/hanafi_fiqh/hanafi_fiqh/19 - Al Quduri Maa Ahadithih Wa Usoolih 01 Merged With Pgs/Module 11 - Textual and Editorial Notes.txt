Hadith of the Sacrifice: Proof for Confession Including Quality
Hadith proof for confession including quality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a confession of debt with a specified quality (e.g., counterfeit) is valid and explain how the hadith of the Prophet's sacrifice provides analogical support.

Core Idea
A comprehensive confession includes all specified qualities, like the Prophet included all parts of the sacrifice.

This lesson examines the first of two hadith cited as scriptural proof in Kitab al-Iqrar. The hadith recounts the Prophet's instruction to distribute all parts of the sacrificial animals. The Hanafi jurists use this to analogically support that when someone confesses a debt 'from the price of goods' and specifies the coins as counterfeit, the confession is valid and includes that quality. We will trace the wajh (aspect of proof) to understand how this hadith grounds the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling Being Proved
Recall the previous ruling: if someone says 'I owe him one thousand from the price of goods, and they are counterfeit' – the Hanafi position is that the confession is valid and the quality (counterfeit) is accepted. Focus on the element of comprehensiveness: the confession includes all stated conditions.
The ruling is about acknowledging a debt with a specific quality that affects the obligation.
2
Examine the Hadith Text
Read the hadith: The Prophet ordered Ali to distribute all parts of the sacrificial animals – meat, skins, and coverings – and not to give anything to the butcher. Note the comprehensiveness: everything is included.
The hadith is from Sahih al-Bukhari (no. 1717).
3
Locate the Wajh (Aspect of Proof)
The author marks 'wajh' to indicate the link. The common element is comprehensiveness: just as the Prophet's instruction included all parts of the sacrifice, so too does a confession that mentions a quality include that quality in the obligation. This is an analogical reasoning (qiyas) from the hadith to the fiqh ruling.
The analogy: the 'comprehensiveness' of the Prophet's command (including all parts) mirrors the 'comprehensiveness' of the confession (including the specified quality).
4
Assess the Proof Strength
The hadith is sahih (qati' al-thubut) but its dalala (indication) is zanni – it does not directly command the ruling but supports it through analogy. This is an example of istidlal (inferential proof) in Hanafi fiqh.
The Hanafi school accepts such analogical support (qiyas) as valid for deriving rulings.

Commentary
This passage cites two hadith as legal proofs (wajh) for earlier rulings. The first hadith about the Prophet's sacrificial animals is cited to support the ruling that when someone confesses to a debt of goods and says the coins are counterfeit, the quality is not accepted – perhaps by analogy, since the Prophet included all parts of the animal (meat, skin, cover) in the distribution, showing that a comprehensive statement includes details. The second hadith about Safiyyah is cited to support the ruling on conditional confession (load of so-and-so), perhaps showing that specifying identity (like the Prophet specifying Safiyyah) prevents ambiguity. The hadith are from Sahih al-Bukhari and serve as scriptural foundations for the Hanafi positions in Kitab al-Iqrar.

Key Terms
وجه (wajh): Aspect or point of proof linking the hadith to the ruling.
بدنة (badanah): Sacrificial camel or cow used in Hajj.

Hadith of Safiyyah: Proof for Conditional Confession with Specified Person
Hadith proof for conditional confession requiring specification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the hadith of Safiyyah supports the Hanafi ruling that a conditional confession (e.g., 'the load of so-and-so is owed by me') is valid when the load is specified as belonging to a known person.

Core Idea
Specifying the person removes ambiguity, just as the Prophet specified Safiyyah to avoid misunderstanding.

The second hadith cited in this passage concerns the Prophet specifying that the woman with him was Safiyyah, his wife. The Hanafi jurists use this to support the ruling that a conditional confession – such as 'the load of so-and-so is owed by me one thousand dirhams' – is valid when the load is attributed to a specific individual. The key is that ambiguity is removed by clear identification. This lesson unpacks the wajh (aspect of proof) and its application in the law of iqrar.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Ruling Being Proved
From the earlier text, the ruling is about someone saying 'the load of so-and-so is owed by me one thousand dirhams.' The Hanafi position is that such a confession is valid provided the load and the person are clearly identified (not ambiguous).
The ruling concerns a conditional confession where the debt is linked to a specific person's property.
2
Examine the Hadith
Read the hadith: Safiyyah visited the Prophet during i'tikaf. Two Ansari men passed and the Prophet told them 'It is only Safiyyah' to remove any suspicion. The Prophet specified her identity to prevent misunderstanding.
Hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari (no. 2035).
3
Identify the Wajh (Link)
The author marks 'wajh' – the aspect is the importance of specifying a person to remove ambiguity. Just as the Prophet clarified Safiyyah's identity, a confession that attributes a debt to a specific person (e.g., 'the load of Zaid') removes ambiguity and is thus valid.
The common element is the removal of uncertainty (ibham) through clear specification.
4
Assess the Implication
If the load is attributed to an unspecified person (e.g., 'someone's load'), the confession may be invalid due to uncertainty. The hadith reinforces that ambiguity must be avoided.
This aligns with the Hanafi principle that gharar (uncertainty) invalidates contracts and confessions.
