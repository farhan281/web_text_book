The Recommended Procedure for Washing the Deceased
Rulings on turning and massaging the deceased with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah steps for washing the deceased and apply the ruling that post-wash discharge does not necessitate repeating ghusl or wudu.

Core Idea
Gentle massage is sunnah; discharge after washing only needs spot-cleaning, not a full repeat.

Washing the deceased is a communal obligation, but within it are recommended actions that bring reward and ease. This passage details the proper turning, belly massage, and the ruling if impurities emerge after completion. The Hanafi school provides a practical, lenient view supported by the practice of the Prophet and his Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah acts
List the physical actions described: turning on left side, washing, turning on right side, washing, sitting up, supporting, and gently massaging the belly.
These are sunnah, not fard.
2
Locate the dalil for the procedure
The hadith of Umm 'Atiyya and the narration from Sulayman b. Musa provide the basis. Identify what each proof establishes.
Focus on the explicit commands and descriptions.
3
Understand the ruling on discharge
The text states that if something comes out after washing, only that spot is washed; no repeat of ghusl or wudu. Note the athar supporting this leniency.
This is a point of ikhtilaf with other schools.
4
Compare with other madhahib
Some schools require full ghusl if najasah emerges after washing. The Hanafi position eases the burden on the family of the deceased.
The evidence: the athar from Hammad and al-Hasan.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a case: after finishing ghusl, the washer notices fluid from the deceased. State the Hanafi ruling and cite the source.
Answer: wash the area, no repeat.

Commentary
This passage details the physical steps of washing the deceased and the ruling on discharge. The deceased is washed on his left side until the water penetrates to the side touching the ground, then on the right side similarly. Then he is sat up and supported, and the belly is gently massaged to expel any remaining filth. If any impurity emerges after washing, only that spot is washed; the entire ghusl and wudu are not repeated. The proofs include the hadith of Umm 'Atiyya (starting with the right side), a detailed narration from Sulayman b. Musa describing the procedure including covering the face and using a rag for private parts, and athar from Ibrahim, Hammad, and al-Hasan confirming the gentle massage and non-repetition of ghusl. The Urdu note adds that the water may be boiled with sidr or ashnan, but alternatives like soap are permissible. This shows the Hanafi school's focus on thorough cleaning while preventing undue hardship.

The Hanafi Procedure for Washing the Deceased
Describes the procedure for washing a deceased person with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Hanafi steps for washing a deceased person and identify the key proofs from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
The method of ghusl al-mayyit is a sunnah prescribed by the Prophet and the Salaf, balancing care and modesty.

Washing the deceased is a collective obligation (fard kifayah) upon the community. This lesson presents the recommended (sunnah) procedure preserved in the Hanafi school, derived from authentic hadith and the practice of the early Muslims. You will learn the order of washing, the handling of the body, and the importance of maintaining dignity throughout.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling category
Understand that washing the deceased is fard kifayah, but the specific method described is sunnah muakkadah. This sets the authority level.
2
Sequence the washing steps
The text gives a clear order: lay on left side and wash, then right side, then sit up and gently press the belly, then dry and apply perfume. Recognize the logic and evidence for each.
3
Examine the key hadith proof
The hadith from Umm 'Atiyyah instructs to start with the right side and the places of wudu. How does this support the steps described?
The hadith is the primary dalil for the order.
4
Assess the rulings on impurities
If something exits after washing, only that spot is washed; the ghusl and wudu are not repeated. This is based on athar from the Tabi'un.
5
Apply the final steps of drying and perfuming
Drying the body and applying hanut (perfume) to the beard/head and kafur (camphor) on prostration points are sunnah, supported by the practice of the Companions.

Drying, Perfuming, and Additional Rulings for Ghusl al-Mayyit
Describes the procedure for washing a deceased person with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the sunnah acts of drying and perfuming the deceased and state the Hanafi rulings on managing impurities and using alternatives in washing.

Core Idea
After washing, drying and perfuming the deceased are sunnah acts that honor the deceased and follow prophetic practice.

The final stages of ghusl al-mayyit involve drying the body and applying perfume (hanut) to the beard and head, and camphor (kafur) to the prostration points. These acts are recommended to honor the deceased and emulate the Prophet's instruction. This lesson also addresses the permissibility of using soap or other cleansers and the prohibition of combing hair or cutting nails.

Guided Path
1
Understand the purpose of drying and perfuming
These acts are performed after washing to prepare the body for shrouding. They are considered sunnah based on the practice of the Prophet's Companions.
2
Examine the evidence from the text
The hadith of 'Abdullah ibn 'Amr recounts his father's instruction to dry and perfume the body. This serves as the primary proof for the sunnah.
3
Apply the ruling on using alternative cleansers
The text allows using jujube leaves or wash grass in water. If unavailable, substitutes like soap are permissible. This is based on the principle of necessity and ease.
This is a practical facilitation for modern contexts.
4
Recognize the prohibitions
It is not permissible to comb the deceased's hair or cut nails/hair. The body should be left as is, preserving dignity.
This distinguishes the treatment of the dead from the living.

Commentary
This passage provides the Hanafi procedure for washing the deceased (ghusl al-mayyit), which is a fard kifayah. The text describes the recommended method (sunnah), supported by hadith and athar. The washer begins by placing the deceased on his left side and washing until water reaches the area beneath, then repeats on the right side. Then the deceased is propped up and the belly is gently massaged to expel any remaining impurities. If anything exits, it is washed but the ghusl or wudu is not repeated. After washing, the body is dried, perfume (hanut) is applied to the beard and head, and camphor is placed on the prostration points (masajid). The Urdu notes explain that water may be boiled with jujube leaves or soap, and it is mustahabb to perfume but not to comb hair or cut nails.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri on the sunnah method of shrouding a man. It opens the chapter on shrouding. Five garments are sunnah; three suffice in need.

The Prophet ﷺ personally provided five garments for a woman's shroud (Abu Dawud). Also, Umar ibn al-Khattab instructed five.
The hadith and companion practice establish the sunnah.

Commentary

Additional Shrouding Rulings: Hair, Nails, and Fumigation
Rulings on hair handling, prohibition of cutting nails, and fumigation of the shroud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the rulings on the deceased woman's hair (place on chest, not combed), the prohibition on cutting nails/hair, and the sunnah of fumigating the shroud in odd numbers.

Core Idea
The deceased woman's hair is placed on her chest, not combed or cut.

Beyond the number of garments, there are specific sunnah acts and prohibitions in preparing the shroud. The Prophet's ﷺ practice and the Companions' instructions guide how to handle the hair, avoid cutting nails, and fumigate the cloth.

Guided Path
1
Handle the hair
The woman's hair is placed on her chest, not left loose. It is not combed or styled. The text from Umm Atiyyah says they plaited the hair and put it behind her.
This distinguishes her from the man.
2
Prohibition on cutting nails/hair
The deceased's nails or hair should not be cut. Hammad said: 'If he were uncircumcised, would you circumcise him?' emphasizing that altering the body after death is not allowed.
This applies to both men and women.
3
Fumigation of the shroud
The shrouds are fumigated with incense (like frankincense) an odd number of times (once, thrice). The hadith says: 'When you fumigate the deceased, do it in odd numbers.'
This is a sunnah act.
4
Tying the shroud if needed
If there is concern that the shroud may come undone, it can be tied. After shrouding, the funeral prayer is performed.
The tying is for practical necessity.

The Ruling and Conditions of I'tikaf in the Hanafi School
Al-Quduri's matn on i'tikaf with hadith proofs and rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of i'tikaf, identify its conditions and invalidators, and explain the evidence from Quran and Hadith.

Core Idea
I'tikaf requires fasting and intention; it is recommended but becomes an emphasized communal sunnah in the last ten days of Ramadan.

I'tikaf (spiritual seclusion in the mosque) is a beloved act of worship in the Hanafi school. This lesson unpacks its ruling, conditions, and invalidators as presented by Imam al-Quduri, along with the proofs from the Quran and Sunnah. Understanding these details ensures that this act of devotion is performed correctly and accepted by Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The matn states that i'tikaf is mustahabb (recommended). Note that this is the default ruling, but the commentary adds that in Ramadan it becomes sunnah mu'akkadah ala al-kifayah.
2
List the conditions (shurut) for valid i'tikaf
From the hadith and usul note: fasting (sawm) is a condition, along with intention (niyyah) and staying in a mosque (preferably a congregational mosque).
The hadith 'La i'tikafa illa bi-sawm' explicitly makes fasting a condition.
3
Understand the invalidators (mufsidat)
Intercourse, touching or kissing with desire, and leaving the mosque without a valid excuse (e.g., natural need, Friday prayer) invalidate i'tikaf and require qada.
The Quranic verse (2:187) and hadith from Aisha support these prohibitions.
4
Review the evidence (dalil)
The Quranic verse (2:187) and multiple hadith from Aisha, Ubayy ibn Ka'b, and others establish the ruling and conditions. The commentary provides specific references.
The evidence is naqli (text-based) and qat'i in thubut but zanni in dalala.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider a person who begins i'tikaf in Ramadan without fasting. According to the Hanafi school, his i'tikaf is invalid because fasting is a condition. He must make it up with fasting.
This shows the practical consequence of missing a condition.

Commentary
This passage is from Al-Quduri's chapter on I'tikaf (spiritual seclusion in the mosque) combined with its proof-text commentary. The author begins by defining i'tikaf as 'staying in the mosque with fasting and intention' and declares it mustahabb (recommended). The commentary then provides multiple evidences from the Quran and hadith to establish the rule. The Quranic verse (2:187) prohibits intimate relations during i'tikaf, indicating its validity. The hadith of Aisha shows the Prophet's practice of i'tikaf in the last ten days of Ramadan, establishing its sunnah status. A second hadith from Aisha lists the sunnah practices for the mu'takif: abstaining from visiting the sick, attending funerals, touching or having relations with women, and leaving only for unavoidable needs; it also conditions i'tikaf with fasting and requires a congregational mosque. Another hadith explicitly states 'No i'tikaf except with fasting.' The Urdu 'usul' section clarifies that in the Hanafi school, i'tikaf in Ramadan is a sunnah mu'akkadah ala al-kifayah – an emphasized communal obligation. If one person in a neighborhood performs it, the obligation is lifted for all. However, for both Ramadan i'tikaf and any obligatory i'tikaf (e.g., due to a vow), fasting and intention are prerequisites. The matn then states the prohibitions: intercourse, touching with desire, and kissing invalidate i'tikaf and require its repetition (qada). The mu'takif may leave the mosque only for a human need (like using the toilet) or for Friday prayer. The commentary provides further evidence: a tabi'i saying for recommendation (from Ya'la), a hadith allowing i'tikaf in any mosque with an adhan and imam, the same hadith from Aisha and the Quranic verse for prohibitions, Ibn Abbas's statement that intercourse necessitates restarting i'tikaf, a hadith on entering the mosque for i'tikaf after Fajr, and a hadith that the Prophet would not enter his house except for need. The final usul note explains that leaving for natural needs and Friday is permissible, and i'tikaf is valid even in a mosque where Friday is not held, as long as it has the five daily prayers.

Prohibitions and Permissible Exits in I'tikaf
Ruling with proof: prohibitions and exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibitions during i'tikaf, identify what nullifies it, and explain the permissible exits from the mosque according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Sexual contact and ejaculation from intimacy nullify i'tikaf; leaving the mosque is only for essential needs or Friday prayer.

I'tikaf is a spiritual retreat in the mosque, but it comes with strict rules to preserve its sanctity. This passage from Al-Quduri lays out the clear prohibitions on sexual activity and the limited exceptions for leaving the mosque. Understanding these boundaries is essential for anyone performing this act of worship, as violating them can nullify the i'tikaf entirely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their ruling
Read the first sentence: 'Sexual intercourse, touching, and kissing are forbidden for the mu'takif.' These are explicitly prohibited (haram) during i'tikaf.
The prohibition is derived from the general command in the Qur'an and Sunnah.
2
Determine the nullification condition
The text adds: 'If he ejaculates due to kissing or touching, his i'tikaf is invalidated and he must make it up.' So the nullifier is ejaculation (inzal) resulting from intimate contact.
Mere touching or kissing without desire or without ejaculation does not nullify; only when it leads to inzal.
3
Analyze the permissible exits
The text states: 'He may not leave the mosque except for the call of nature or for Friday prayer.' These are the only two exceptions during a supererogatory i'tikaf.
If the Friday prayer is not held in the same mosque, leaving for it is obligatory.
4
Connect to the proof
The primary proof is Qur'an 2:187: 'And do not approach them (your wives) while you are secluded in the mosques.' This general prohibition is understood to include all forms of sexual intimacy.
Hanafi usul treats the imperative as general and binding.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) allow leaving for more reasons, but the Hanafi position is strict: only these two exceptions.
The Hanafi view relies on the apparent meaning of the verse and hadith.

The Three Causes of Legal Interdiction and Their Rulings
Defines the three causes of hajr and their effect on contracts and actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three causes of hajr and explain the Hanafi ruling on contracts of a minor, slave, and insane person.

Core Idea
Hajr protects the incapable: minors, slaves, and the insane have limited legal capacity; their contracts are suspended or void.

This lesson introduces the foundational concept of legal interdiction (hajr) in Hanafi fiqh. You will learn the three causes that restrict a person's financial capacity and how the school distinguishes between suspended and void transactions. The hadith evidence anchoring these rulings is also examined.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three causes
From the matn, extract the three grounds for hajr: minority (sighar), slavery (riqq), and insanity (junun). Note that these are exclusive in the Hanafi school.
Other schools add categories like safah (foolishness), but the Hanafi limit is these three.
2
Determine the effect on contracts
For the minor and slave, contracts are suspended (mawquf) – valid only if the guardian/master ratifies. For the insane, contracts are absolutely void (batil) under all circumstances.
Suspension means the contract is neither valid nor invalid until ratification or annulment.
3
Distinguish between statements and actions
Hajr applies only to statements (aqwal) like contracts, divorces, and acknowledgments. For actions causing damage (itlaf), both minor and insane are liable for compensation.
The slave's statements are valid regarding himself but not binding on his master's property.
4
Examine the hadith evidence
The 'pen is lifted' hadith removes religious accountability for minors and the insane, which extends to financial capacity. The other hadiths specify that divorce and sale require ownership, limiting the slave's capacity.
The hadith from Ibn 'Abbas is the primary dalil; the other two reinforce specific applications.
5
Apply the ta'lil (reasoning)
The underlying principle is that transactions depend on intellect ('aql). Those lacking complete intellect (minor, insane) or incomplete ownership (slave) cannot act independently.
This connects to the usul maxim: 'Al-umur bi-maqasidiha' – acts are judged by their purposes.

Commentary
This passage is the opening section of the Kitab al-Hajr (Book of Interdiction) from al-Quduri's matn, along with supporting hadith and usul (principles) from the commentary. The author first states the three causes that necessitate legal interdiction (hajr): minority (sighar), slavery (ruq), and insanity (junun). These are the three classical grounds in Hanafi fiqh for restricting a person's legal capacity to conduct financial transactions. The ruling is clear: a minor cannot act without his guardian's permission; a slave cannot act without his master's permission; and an insane person (who has completely lost his mind) has no capacity whatsoever—his transactions are absolutely invalid. The passage then explains the consequences: if a minor or insane person does make a sale or purchase, the guardian has the option to ratify it (if it is beneficial) or annul it. This is the Hanafi principle of 'aqd mawquf (suspended contract). Importantly, interdiction applies only to statements (aqwal) like contracts, divorces, and manumissions—not to actions that cause damage. For torts (atbaf), both the minor and insane are liable for compensation. The slave is treated differently: his statements (like sale, divorce, acknowledgment) are valid regarding himself (i.e., they affect his own rights and obligations) but not binding on his master's property. The hadith proofs include the well-known 'lifting of the pen' hadith, which establishes that minors and the insane are not held accountable for their religious obligations; a parallel is drawn to their financial transactions. Another hadith states that all divorces are valid except that of the insane, reinforcing that only the insane's statements are completely void. For the slave, the hadith 'no divorce except in what you own' implies that a slave can divorce his wife because he owns her in the sense of having a right to divorce? Actually, the Hanafi interpretation: the slave's divorce is valid and affects himself because he 'owns' his marital bond with his own person; but it does not affect his master's property. The usul (principles) in Urdu at the end summarize key takeaways: transactions depend on intellect; the divorce of a minor and insane person is invalid; and if a minor husband divorces, the judge may annul the marriage based on maslahah (public interest).

الصغر (al-sighar): Minority, the state of being a child before reaching puberty.
الرق (al-riqq): Slavery, the status of being owned as property by another.
الجنون (al-junun): Insanity, loss of reason and intellect.
الولي (al-wali): Guardian, the person legally responsible for managing the affairs of a minor or incapacitated person.

Accidental Mixing of Deposit Creates Partnership
Ruling when deposit mixes without depositary's act

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a deposit mixes with the depositary's property without his action and identify the resulting legal relationship as partnership (shirkat al-milk).

Core Idea
Accidental mixing creates joint ownership, not liability.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in the law of deposit (wadi'ah): what happens when the deposited property accidentally mixes with the depositary's own property, without any deliberate act by the depositary? The Hanafi ruling is clear and concise: the depositary becomes a partner (sharik) with the owner in the mixed property. This reflects the principle that without ta'addi (transgression), the trust (amanah) remains intact.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario

The phrase 'min ghayri fi'lihi' (without his action) is decisive. If the mixing results from the depositary's deliberate act, the ruling changes to liability. Here, the absence of his act preserves the trust.

The text says: 'فَهُوَ شَرِيكٌ لِصَاحِبِهَا' (he is a partner with its owner). Memorise this exact wording.
4
Recognise the consequence: partnership
The depositary becomes a co-owner (sharik) in the mixed property. Ownership is shared proportionally based on the value of each party's contribution. This is a form of shirkat al-milk (joint ownership by mixing).

This lesson examines a concise ruling from al-Quduri's matn that addresses a common scenario: the depositary uses part of the amanah but then tries to restore it by returning an equivalent amount. The passage states: 'He is liable for all' (damina al-jami'a). This means he must compensate the owner for the full original deposit.
Liability covers both the spent portion and the remainder, as well as the replacement that is now mixed.

Returning an equivalent does not nullify the liability because the original amanah was violated. The mixing makes the replacement indistinguishable, but even if kept separate, the transgression is not undone.
This ruling protects the owner's right strictly.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mawat according to Hanafi fiqh and state the ruling on reviving dead land with and without the imam's permission.

Core Idea
Mawat is unowned, unproductive land distant from habitation; reviving it with the imam's permission grants ownership.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi definition of mawat (dead land) and the basic ruling that reviving it with the imam's permission yields ownership. The passage establishes the distance criterion and clarifies the consequence of reviving without permission according to Imam Abu Hanifa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of mawat
The text defines mawat as land not benefitted from due to lack of water or excess water, with no owner or unknown owner, and distant from the village such that a voice from the edge of inhabited area is not heard.
This distance criterion is unique to the Hanafi school.
2
State the ruling with permission
If one revives mawat with the imam's permission, he owns it. The hadith from Bukhari (man a'mara ardan laysat li ahadin fahuwa ahaqqu) supports this.
3
State the ruling without permission according to Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa holds that reviving without the imam's permission does not grant ownership. The hadith from Jabir in Musnad Ahmad is cited as evidence.
This is the view recorded in the matn.
4
Note the presence of ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree with Abu Hanifa; their view and the mufta bihi will be covered in the next lesson.
This intra-Hanafi dispute is a key feature of the text.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof passage from the book of Reviving Dead Lands (Kitab Ihya al-Mawat). A dhimmi also gains ownership through revival just like a Muslim. The hadith cited include: “Whoever cultivates land that does not belong to anyone is more entitled to it” (Bukhari), supporting the concept; and “Whoever revives dead land, it is his” (Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud) which is general without requiring imam's permission. Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Permission and the Rule of Tahjir
Explains intra-Hanafi dispute and tahjir rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding revival without permission, state the mufta bihi view, and apply the three-year rule for tahjir.

Core Idea
Imam Abu Hanifa requires imam's permission for ownership; Sahibayn grant ownership; the mufta bihi follows Sahibayn, and tahjir gives only a three-year priority.

While the previous lesson established the basic ruling, this lesson delves into the key intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two companions on whether reviving without the imam's permission yields ownership. The passage also covers the rule of tahjir (enclosing) and the equal right of dhimmis in revival.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Abu Hanifa says no ownership without permission; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say ownership is granted. This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf recorded in the text.
The Sahibayn's view is considered the stronger due to the general hadith.
2
Understand the evidence for each side
Sahibayn rely on the general hadith 'Man ahya ardan mayyitatan fahiya lahu' without condition. Abu Hanifa interprets it as requiring legitimacy (permission) based on the hadith from Bukhari indicating priority rather than absolute ownership.
The text provides multiple hadith to support each position.
3
Recognize the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., Al-Marghinani) adopt the view of the Sahibayn for practical ease. This is the operative view for fatwa.
Thus, in practice, reviving without permission grants ownership.
4
Apply the tahjir rule
If someone only encloses land but does not cultivate it within three years, the imam can reclaim and reassign it. This prevents hoarding and encourages actual development.
The three-year limit is a key safeguard.

Prohibition of Reviving Land Near Settlements
States the rule on reviving near inhabited areas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reviving land close to inhabited areas and explain the rationale based on public benefit.

Core Idea
Land near settlements is reserved for common use; reviving it is prohibited.

This lesson covers the first prohibition in the passage: land that is close to a village or town cannot be revived as private property. The Prophet declared a sacred area around crops, and this principle protects communal pasture and threshing grounds.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is reviving (ihya) land that is close to an inhabited area. The ruling is that it is not permissible (la yaju).
The term 'al-'amir' refers to a settled, inhabited area.
2
Understand the rationale
Such land is left as pasture (mar'a) for the villagers and as a place for their harvests (matrah li hisadihim). This protects communal rights.
3
Note the dalil
The hadith of Ikrimah: the Prophet declared a sacred area (hurma) for crops measured by a bowshot (ghalwah bi sahm). This indicates a protected zone around cultivation.
The exact distance of a bowshot is between 300 and 400 cubits according to some scholars.
4
Recognize the principle
The Urdu principle states that places of public benefit (rafah 'amm) cannot be owned by individuals because they are the right of all Muslims.

Harim (Protected Zone) Around Wells and Springs
States the harim measurements for different water sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi measurements for the harim of different water sources and explain the purpose of the harim.

Core Idea
The harim protects the water source; its size depends on the type of well or spring.

This lesson focuses on the second part of the passage: the protected zone (harim) around wells and springs. The harim prevents encroachment that could damage the water source. The size varies: 40 cubits for a livestock watering well, 60 for an irrigation well, and 300 for a natural spring. The evidence comes from hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of water sources
Three categories: well for livestock watering (al-'atan), well for irrigation (al-nadih), and natural spring (al-'ayn).
'Atan is where animals stand to drink; nadih involves drawing water for crops.
2
Memorize the harim measurements
For 'atan: 40 cubits. For nadih: 60 cubits. For 'ayn: 300 cubits.
Cubit (dhira') is approximately 0.5 meters, so 40 cubits ≈ 20m, etc.
3
Understand the rationale
The harim ensures that the water source is not harmed by nearby digging or construction. Larger wells and springs need larger buffer zones.
4
Note the evidence
Hadith from Ibn Majah: 'Whoever digs a well has forty cubits as a resting place for his livestock.' Hadith from Daraqutni/Bayhaqi gives specific measurements for different wells.
These hadith are ahad, but accepted in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Apply the ruling
If someone digs a well, he owns the harim and others cannot dig within that boundary.
The passage also says: 'Whoever wants to dig a well in its harim is prevented.'

Reviving Abandoned Riverbeds and Mawat Land
States the rule for reviving abandoned riverbeds

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions under which an abandoned riverbed can be revived and determine ownership.

Core Idea
If water will return, the bed cannot be revived; if not, it is mawat and can be owned with imam's permission.

This lesson covers the third part of the passage: land that was once a river or stream but has dried up. The ruling depends on whether the water is likely to return. If it is likely, the land cannot be revived because it may again be useful as a watercourse. If not, it is treated as dead land (mawat) and can be revived with the imam's permission, provided it is not within the harim of an inhabited area.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Land that a river (al-furat) or a water course (al-rijlah) has abandoned, and the water has turned away from it.
'Furat' refers to a river or large stream; 'rijlah' is a smaller water channel.
2
First condition: likelihood of water return
If the water is likely to return (yaju' 'awduhu ilayhi), then reviving the land is not permissible (la yaju').
The land retains potential as a watercourse.
3
Second condition: no return expected
If the water will not return, then the land is like mawat (dead land).
Mawat can be revived to become private property.
4
Additional condition: not harim
Even if water won't return, if the land is within the harim of an inhabited area, it cannot be revived (as per lesson 1).
5
Procedure for reviving
The reviver must obtain permission from the imam (ruler or his deputy). Then he becomes the owner.
This is the standard rule for reviving mawat.

It is not permissible to revive what is close to inhabited land; it should be left as pasture for the villagers and as a place for their harvests. Whoever digs a new well in the wilderness has its harim (protected area). If it is for livestock watering, its harim is forty cubits; if for irrigation (nadih), sixty cubits; if it is a spring, its harim is three hundred cubits. Whoever wants to dig a well in its harim is prevented from it. As for (land) that a river or water course has abandoned and water has turned away from it: if the water is likely to return to it, it is not permissible to revive it; if the water is not likely to return to it, then it is like mawat, if it is not a harim of an inhabited area, and whoever revives it with the imam's permission owns it. (Then the author lists hadith and a statement of al-Zuhri.) Principle: Reviving a place of public benefit does not make one an owner because it is the right of all Muslims.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on reviving dead land, focusing on two restrictions: (1) land close to an inhabited area cannot be revived because it is reserved as pasture and dumping ground for the villagers. This is to protect communal rights. The hadith of Ikrimah mentions that the Prophet (peace be upon him) declared a sacred area for crops 'a bowshot' which scholars estimated between 300 to 400 cubits. This distance is to prevent encroachment. (2) The second part details the harim (protected zone) around wells and springs. Harim is an area around the water source that cannot be dug or interfered with to protect the water supply. For a well used for livestock (atn), the harim is 40 cubits from the well; for a well used for irrigation (nadih), 60 cubits; for a flowing spring, 300 cubits. Hadith from Ibn Majah and others support these measurements. Additionally, if a river or stream changes course, the abandoned bed is considered like mawat if the water is not expected to return, but can be revived only with imam's permission and provided it is not part of someone's harim. The Urdu principle summarizes that public welfare land cannot be privately owned. The hadith references include a statement from al-Zuhri (a tabi'i) who reports that people held the harim of springs to be 500 cubits, showing some variation in practice.

Key Terms
الحریم (al-harim): Protected area around a well or spring; here it refers to the protected zone of an inhabited area.
المیات (al-mawat): Dead land that is not owned and can be revived with imam's permission.

The Harim of Wells and Reviving Dead Land
Ruling with proof on harim and revival.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reviving land near inhabited areas and specify the harim measurements for different water sources.

Core Idea
Reviving land near villages is prohibited; the harim of a well protects its water and ownership.

This passage from Al-Quduri establishes key principles of land ownership through revival (ihya) and the protected perimeter (harim) around water sources. Understanding these rules is essential for determining who owns what in barren lands and preventing disputes over water access. The passage also provides specific measurements derived from the Prophet's teachings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon and what the legal classification is (e.g., permissible, prohibited). The passage rules on reviving land near inhabited areas and digging wells within a harim.
Focus on the prohibition: 'It is not permissible to revive what is near inhabited land.'
2
Locate the dalil
Find the proof cited for the ruling. The passage includes several hadith, such as the one from Abdullah ibn Mughaffal and Abu Hurairah, and the statement of Umar.
The hadith specify the harim measurements.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how the hadith prove the rulings. The explicit mention of measurements indicates that the harim is defined by these numbers, and the imperative context implies obligation.
Note that the hadith are ahad (single narrations), so the ruling is based on zanni evidence.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools differ on harim sizes. The Hanafi position is based on these hadith and is the operative ruling for fatwa.
For example, the Shafi'i school has different measurements.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, the harim prevents others from digging within the perimeter, protecting the well owner's rights. Also, land near settlements is reserved for common use.
The Imam's permission is required for reviving distant dead land.

Book of Reviving Dead Land. It is not permissible to revive what is near inhabited land, and it is left as pasture for the people of the village and as a dumping ground for their harvests. Whoever digs a new well in the wilderness has its harim. If it is for animals to drink, its harim is forty cubits; if for irrigation, sixty cubits; if it is a spring, its harim is three hundred cubits. Whoever wants to dig a well in its harim is prevented from it, except if he establishes proof regarding that. Water that leaves the river or canal and turns away from it: if it is possible that it will return to it, its revival is not permissible; if it is not possible for it to return to it, it is like dead land when it is not harim of an inhabited area. Whoever revives it with the Imam's permission owns it. And Umar said: Whoever revives a dead land, it is his. I heard Ikrimah say: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: 'Indeed, Allah has made for cultivation a sanctity of a bowshot.' From Abdullah ibn Mughaffal, from the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Whoever digs a well, for him is forty cubits as a resting place for his livestock.' From Abu Hurairah, he said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: 'The harim of a new well is twenty-five cubits, the harim of an ancient well is fifty cubits, the harim of a flowing spring is three hundred cubits, and the harim of a spring for cultivation is six hundred cubits.' And al-Zuhri said: I heard the people saying: The harim of springs is five hundred cubits.

Commentary
This passage presents the rulings on reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat) and the associated concept of harim (protected perimeter) around water sources. The passage type is a ruling with proof. It begins with a key ruling: it is not permissible to revive land that is close to an inhabited area because such land is reserved for the common benefit of the villagers as pasture and dumping ground. This is based on the hadith that cultivation has a sanctity of a bowshot (ghalwah bi-sahm), which the scholars interpret as about 300-400 cubits from the edge of the village. Then the passage details the harim of wells: a newly dug well in the wilderness grants its owner a harim of forty cubits if for livestock drinking, sixty cubits if for irrigation, and three hundred cubits for a spring. These measurements are derived from the hadith of Abdullah ibn Mughaffal (forty cubits) and Abu Hurairah (varying numbers). The passage then discusses water channels: if water leaves a river or canal and turns away but could return, the land cannot be revived because it is still part of the original water source; if it cannot return, it becomes dead land that can be revived with the Imam's permission. The author also mentions Umar's saying that reviving dead land grants ownership, which is a general principle. The proofs include several hadith and the statement of al-Zuhri. This passage establishes the Hanafi position that the harim is a right of ownership for the one who digs the well, preventing others from digging within that perimeter to avoid harm.

Key Terms
إِحْيَاءُ الْمَوَاتِ (ihya' al-mawat): Reviving dead land – bringing barren, uncultivated land into productive use.
حَرِيم (harim): The protected perimeter around a well, spring, or canal that belongs to its owner and cannot be intruded upon.
بِئْرٌ عَادِيَّةٌ (bi'r 'adiyyah): An ancient well, one that has existed from old times, as opposed to a newly dug well (badi).
