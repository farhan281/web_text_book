Definition and Conditions of Mudarabah
Opening definition with condition and proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of mudarabah and identify the condition regarding the type of capital, citing the evidence from the Sunnah and practice of the Companions.

Core Idea
Mudarabah is a partnership of capital and labor, but only valid with gold, silver, or currency – not commodities.

This lesson opens the Book of Mudarabah from al-Quduri, defining the contract and its essential condition. We will examine the Hanafi definition, the requirement that capital be monetary, and the evidence from the Sunnah, tabi'un, and Companion practice that supports this ruling. By understanding the precise conditions, you will be able to distinguish valid mudarabah contracts from invalid ones and know the consequences when conditions are violated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and parties
Read the first line: mudarabah is a contract of partnership (shirkah) where one party (rabb al-mal) provides capital and the other (mudarib) provides labor. Note that it is a type of shirkah but distinct because only one contributes capital.
The definition emphasizes that it is a partnership, not a loan or agency.
2
Locate the condition on capital
Find the statement: 'mudarabah is not valid except with the type of wealth regarding which we explained that partnership is valid.' Recall that in Hanafi fiqh, partnership (shirkah) is valid only with gold, silver, and currency (dirhams/dinars). Thus, commodities are excluded.
This condition is a major point of difference with other schools.
3
Examine the primary proof (hadith)
Read the hadith of Urwah al-Bariqi: the Prophet gave him a dinar to buy a sheep; he bought two sheep, sold one for a dinar, and returned with a sheep and a dinar. The Prophet's approval shows the permissibility of mudarabah, where one provides capital and the other trades.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for permissibility.
4
Review the supporting evidence
Consider the saying of al-Hasan that the mudarib is a trustee (amin) – this explains liability rules. Then the practice of Ibn Umar with Abu Musa – a clear example of mudarabah with state funds. Finally, Ibrahim's dislike of mudarabah with cloth limits capital to gold and silver.
These evidences reinforce both the permissibility and the capital condition.
5
Understand the consequence of invalid capital
Note Sufyan's comment: if commodities are given, the worker gets wages (ujr al-mithl), not profit. This is the thamarah (practical effect) of violating the condition.
The contract becomes fasid (invalid) and is treated as an ijarah.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of Kitab al-Mudarabah in Al-Quduri. It defines the mudarabah contract and states its essential condition regarding the type of capital, followed by evidence from hadith, sayings of tabi'in, and practice of companions. The author begins by stating: Mudarabah is a contract of partnership (shirkah) where one party provides capital and the other provides labor. This is similar to a partnership but with a distinction that one partner contributes only money and the other only work. Then he adds a condition: mudarabah is only valid with the same type of wealth that is valid for partnership, meaning gold, silver, and currency (dirhams and dinars). This excludes commodities and goods. The author then provides four evidences: (1) A hadith from Urwah al-Bariqi where the Prophet gave him a dinar, and he engaged in a transaction that resulted in profit, showing the permissibility of mudarabah. (2) A statement of al-Hasan al-Basri that the mudarib is a trustee, meaning he is not liable for loss unless he violates terms. (3) The incident of Abdullah and Ubaydullah, sons of Umar, who received capital from Abu Musa al-Ash'ari on a mudarabah basis, and Umar later accepted it, showing companion practice. (4) A statement from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i that he disliked mudarabah with cloth, limiting it to gold and silver, which is followed by Sufyan's comment that if one gives commodities, the worker gets wages instead of profit. Finally, the Urdu text defines key terms: Rabbi al-Mal (capital owner), Ra's al-Mal (capital), Mudarib (worker), and Ribh (profit).

Key Terms
مضاربة (mudarabah): A contract of partnership where one party provides capital and the other provides labor, with profit shared as agreed.
رب المال (rabb al-mal): The owner of the capital in a mudarabah contract.
رأس المال (ra's al-mal): The capital provided for mudarabah, which must be gold, silver, or currency.
مضارب (mudarib): The worker or agent who manages the capital in a mudarabah contract.
ربح (ribh): The profit earned from the mudarabah venture.

Mudarib Purchasing Relatives and Slaves
Rulings on mudarib buying relatives and slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a mudarib purchasing relatives of the capital owner and apply the condition of profit to determine the permissibility of purchasing a self-freeing slave.

Core Idea
Mudarib cannot buy owner's relatives; buying self-freeing slave depends on profit.

Mudarabah is a trust-based partnership where the mudarib acts as an agent. To protect the capital owner's interests, certain purchases are prohibited. This lesson covers two specific cases: buying relatives of the capital owner and buying a slave who would be set free on the mudarib. Understanding these restrictions is crucial for avoiding invalid transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two prohibited categories
Read the Arabic text and list the two categories of people the mudarib is not allowed to purchase with mudarabah capital: (1) relatives of the capital owner (father, son, etc.), (2) anyone who would be set free upon being owned by the mudarib (e.g., his own relatives).
The text uses 'من يعتق عليه' meaning those who would become free if owned.
2
Understand the ruling for each category
For the first category, the purchase is absolutely prohibited; if done, the mudarib is deemed to have purchased for himself, not for the mudarabah. For the second category, the ruling depends on whether the mudarabah capital has profit.
3
Apply the condition of profit presence
If profit exists in the fund, the mudarib may not purchase a self-freeing slave; if he does, he must compensate the capital. If no profit exists, the purchase is permissible. If the slave's value exceeds the price, the mudarib's share becomes free and the slave must work to earn the remaining value.
Profit refers to actual profit earned in the mudarabah venture, not just the existence of capital.
4
Determine the liability of the mudarib
When the mudarib violates the prohibition, he is personally liable: for the first case, he must pay from his own money (the slave is his); for the second case with profit, he must compensate the mudarabah fund for the capital spent.
If no profit and permissible, no liability unless value exceeds price—then partial emancipation occurs.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar states two rulings regarding what the mudarib (agent-manager) may purchase with mudarabah capital. First, the mudarib may not purchase the capital owner's ascendants (father) or descendants (son), nor anyone who would become free by being owned by the capital owner (i.e., his own relatives whom he cannot own). If he does so, the purchase is for himself, not the mudarabah, meaning he must pay from his own money and cannot take from the mudarabah fund. Second, regarding purchasing a slave who would be set free on the mudarib himself (e.g., his own relative): if the mudarabah capital has profit, the mudarib is prohibited from such purchase; if he does it anyway, he must compensate the mudarabah for the capital spent. If there is no profit, he may purchase; if the slave's value exceeds the purchase price, the mudarib's share of the slave becomes free (the slave is partially emancipated), and the mudarib does not owe anything to the capital owner. The slave must then work to earn the remaining value of his freedom. This is a nuanced rule based on the principle of trust and avoiding conflict of interest.

Key Terms
مضارب (mudārib): The agent-manager in a mudarabah partnership who invests the capital in return for a share of profit.
رب المال (rabb al-māl): The owner of the capital in a mudarabah partnership.
يعتق عليه (ya‘tiq ‘alayh): A person who would be set free immediately if owned by the mudarib, such as a close relative.

Sub-Mudarabah: Permission and Profit Distribution
Rulings on sub-mudarabah: permission and profit distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a mudarib gives capital to a second mudarib with and without permission, and calculate profit distribution in various scenarios.

Core Idea
A mudarib cannot subcontract without permission; profit is divided as per original contract and any excess is borne by the first mudarib.

This lesson examines the rules of sub-mudarabah—when the first mudarib entrusts the capital to a second mudarib. The central condition is the capital owner's permission. Without it, the second mudarib cannot act until profit appears, and the first mudarib becomes liable. With permission, the profit distribution must protect the capital owner's agreed share, and any deviation may require the first mudarib to compensate from his own pocket.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its permission condition
Determine whether the first mudarib has permission from the capital owner to appoint a second mudarib. This is the key condition that changes the ruling.
2
Analyse the scenario without permission
Without permission, the second mudarib cannot transact until profit appears. Once profit appears, the first mudarib becomes liable for the capital to the capital owner.
The first mudarib is not liable immediately upon giving the capital, only after profit is earned.
3
Analyse the scenarios with permission and different wordings
When the capital owner grants permission, the wording of his instruction affects profit distribution. Three wordings are given: general sharing ('between us halves'), specific to first's effort ('whatever Allah grants you'), and fixed share for owner ('I get half of anything').
4
Calculate profit distribution in each scenario
For each wording, distribute the profit among capital owner, first mudarib, and second mudarib according to the text. The key is that the capital owner always gets his agreed share (usually half), and the first mudarib's share is what remains after giving the second his promised portion.
If the first mudarib promises the second more than the owner's share, the first must compensate the shortfall.
5
Handle the case where the first mudarib exceeds authority
If the first mudarib stipulates a share for the second that exceeds the owner's entitlement, the owner still gets his half, and the first mudarib must guarantee the difference from his own wealth.

How Mudarabah Ends: Death and Revocation
Rulings on mudarabah termination through death and revocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios of mudarabah termination and apply the specific conditions under which the mudarib may continue transacting after revocation.

Core Idea
Death voids mudarabah instantly; revocation limits further action depending on awareness and asset type.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents the key rulings on when a mudarabah contract ends. Two events terminate it: death of either party, and revocation by the capital owner. The precise effects of revocation turn on two variables: whether the mudarib knows of the revocation, and whether the capital is still in goods or already in cash. Understanding these conditional rules protects both parties' rights during the wind‑down.

Guided Path
1
Identify termination triggers
Note the two events that end a mudarabah: death of either party (capital owner or mudarib) and revocation (azl) by the capital owner. Death voids the contract automatically.
2

If the mudarib transacts before learning of the revocation, those transactions are valid. If he learns of it, his authority is restricted.
Hanafi position: knowledge is required for revocation to limit actions.
3
Apply asset-type condition after knowledge
After knowing of revocation: if capital is goods (urud), the mudarib may sell them but may not buy anything new. If the capital is already cash (dirhams/dinars), he may not transact at all.
Goods: sell only. Cash: no transactions allowed.
4
Understand the rationale
The mudarib's agency is gradually curtailed to protect the owner. He is allowed to liquidate existing goods (converting to cash) but cannot reinvest, ensuring the owner recovers his capital.

Settlement of Debts and Losses in Mudarabah
Rulings on debts and losses upon mudarabah termination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for debt collection after mudarabah termination and explain how losses are allocated between profit and capital.

Core Idea
Losses hit profit first, not capital; mudarib is not liable for accidental losses.

When a mudarabah ends, unresolved debts and losses must be settled clearly. This passage from al-Quduri lays out two key rules: the mudarib's duty to collect debts hinges on the existence of profit, and any loss in the mudarabah assets is deducted from profit first, protecting the capital. The mudarib, as a trustee, bears no personal liability for such losses unless negligent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios for debt collection
Look at the condition: if profit exists, the judge compels collection; if no profit, the mudarib is not forced to collect personally.
The ruling treats profit as a threshold for mandatory action.
2
Determine the mudarib's obligation
With profit, debt collection is mandatory (al-ijbar); without profit, the mudarib can simply appoint the capital owner to collect.
The mudarib is not required to do free work when no profit is realized.
3
Understand the loss allocation rule
Any loss (halak) of mudarabah assets is first deducted from profit. Only if profit is exhausted does the loss affect capital.
The profit buffer protects the principal investment.
4
Recognize the mudarib's non-liability
If the loss exceeds profit, the mudarib is not personally liable (la daman) for the excess, as long as he did not transgress (ta'addi).
This preserves the mudarib's role as an agent-trustee, not a guarantor.

Dismissal of the Mudarib and Transaction Validity
Ruling on the mudarib's transactions when unaware of dismissal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the mudarib's transactions after dismissal without knowledge, and apply the distinction based on capital type.

Core Idea
A dismissed mudarib who remains unaware retains authority; upon knowledge, capital type limits further action.

When a capital owner dismisses a mudarib, the mudarib's authority does not end immediately. The law protects transactions made in ignorance of the dismissal. But once the mudarib learns, the nature of the capital—goods versus cash—determines what he can still do. Profit Return and Mudarib Authority in Mudarabah
Rulings on returning distributed profit and mudarib authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on returning distributed profit when loss occurs in an ongoing mudarabah and distinguish when return is not required, and identify the mudarib's permissible actions.

Core Idea
Distributed profit must be returned to cover loss if the contract continues, but not after termination and restart.

When a mudarabah suffers a loss after profit has been distributed, the parties must return the distributed profit to restore the capital owner's principal—if the contract remains in effect. But if the contract was terminated and a new one started, earlier profits are not touched. This lesson unpacks these scenarios and also clarifies the scope of the mudarib's authority in trade.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct cases: (1) profit distributed while mudarabah is still in effect, then loss occurs; (2) profit distributed, then mudarabah terminated, then new contract, then loss.
The ruling differs based on the continuity of the contract.
2
Apply the rule for scenario 1
In an ongoing mudarabah, any distributed profit must be returned to the principal until the capital owner recovers his full capital. Only after that is any surplus shared again. If even after returning all profit the principal is not restored, the mudarib owes nothing.
The mudarib does not guarantee the principal beyond the profit already taken.
3
Apply the rule for scenario 2
If the mudarabah was terminated after profit distribution and later a new contract is formed, a subsequent loss does not require returning the earlier profit. The new contract is independent.
Termination severs the link between profit and future loss.
4
Analyze the mudarib's authority
The mudarib may sell for cash or credit—these are trade-related acts. But he may not use mudarabah funds to marry slaves, as that is not related to trade.
Authority is limited to customary commercial activities.
5
Connect to the dalil
The hadith from Ali (ra) establishes that loss is on the capital and profit is by agreement. This supports the rule that the mudarib does not bear loss beyond the profit he took, and the profit distribution is subject to agreement.
The dalil is a Qawl Sahabi with zanni thubut and dalala.

Mudarabah: Profit Distribution and Subsequent Loss
Rulings on profit distribution and loss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three rulings regarding profit distribution and subsequent loss in mudarabah and apply them to new scenarios.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, profit is provisional until the capital is fully recovered; loss is always borne by the capital owner.

This passage from al-Quduri covers three key scenarios in mudarabah. First, what happens if profit is distributed but the capital is later lost while the contract is still active? Second, what if the contract is cancelled and then renewed and then loss occurs? Third, what actions is the mudarib allowed to take with the mudarabah capital? Understanding these rules is essential for proper partnership accounting and for distinguishing between trade-related and non-trade-related acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Read the text and list the three rules: (1) profit divided and then capital lost in an ongoing mudarabah; (2) profit divided, contract cancelled, then renewed and capital lost; (3) permissible actions for the mudarib.
2
Apply the first rule
For the first scenario: the partners must return the profit to the capital owner until his capital is fully recovered. Any surplus after restoration is shared as profit. If loss exceeds profit, no liability on the mudarib.
Profit is provisional.
3
Distinguish the second and third rules
