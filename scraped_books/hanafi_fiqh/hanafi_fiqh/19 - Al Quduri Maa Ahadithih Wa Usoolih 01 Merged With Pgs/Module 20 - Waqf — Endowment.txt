Ownership in Waqf: Abu Hanifa's View
Ruling that waqf does not transfer ownership from the waqif except by court order or death condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on ownership in waqf and explain the primary evidence (dalil) from the hadith of Umar.

Core Idea
Waqf does not transfer ownership from the waqif unless a judge rules or death condition — Abu Hanifa's distinct view.

This passage from al-Quduri opens the Book of Waqf with a uniquely Hanafi position: the founder of a waqf does not lose ownership of the property. According to Abu Hanifa, the waqif retains ownership until a judge declares the waqf binding or the waqif makes it conditional upon death. This view differs from the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), who hold that ownership transfers immediately upon declaration. The text then provides multiple proofs, including the well-known hadith of Umar's waqf of his Khaybar land.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the first sentence: 'لا يَزُولُ مِلْكُ الْوَاقِفِ عَنْ الْوَقْفِ عِنْدَ أَبي حَنِيفَةَ' – the waqif's ownership does not leave the waqf according to Abu Hanifa.
This is the core ruling.
2
Identify the Exceptions
Note the two exceptions: 'إِلَّا أَنْ يَحْكُمَ بِهِ حَاكِمٌ أَوْ يُعَلَّقَهُ بِمَوْتِهِ' – unless a judge rules it or the waqif attaches it to his death.
This shows Abu Hanifa's view is not absolute; there are conditions that transfer ownership.
3
Locate the Primary Dalil
The text cites the hadith of Umar: the Prophet said 'إن شئت حبست أصلها وتصدقت بها' – if you wish, keep the corpus and give its produce in charity.
This is a sunnah mutawatir hadith (Bukhari & Muslim).
4
Understand the Legal Link (Wajh al-Dalalah)
The hadith instructs Umar to 'keep the corpus', implying that the corpus remains in Umar's possession and control. If ownership had transferred, the Prophet would not prohibit sale, gift, or inheritance.
Abu Hanifa uses this to argue that the waqif retains ownership.
5
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
The Sahibayn disagree: they interpret the same hadith as showing that the corpus is held back from the owner's full disposal (i.e., ownership is transferred but some restrictions apply). The mufta bihi position is the Sahibayn's view.
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Hidayah) prefer the Sahibayn's view.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Waqf with the distinct Hanafi position of Imam Abu Hanifa: the donor (waqif) does not lose ownership of the waqf property; it remains in his legal possession. Exceptions occur if a judge declares it waqf or if it is made conditional upon death (like a will). This differs from the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) who hold that ownership transfers immediately. The text then provides multiple proofs: (i) a Quranic verse about bequests, (ii) the famous hadith of Umar's waqf of his Khaybar land, (iii) the hadith of Sa'd ibn 'Ubadah giving charity for his mother, (iv) the hadith about three ongoing deeds, and (v) a statement from a tabi'i. The concluding principle explains the etymology: waqf means 'holding back' property for the benefit of the poor, and it is a valid institution.

Key Terms
الْوَقْف (al-waqf): The holding back of property from ownership (tamlik) and dedicating its usufruct for charitable purposes.
الْوَاقِف (al-wāqif): The person who establishes a waqf.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ownership Transfer in Waqf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership transfer in waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the moment of ownership transfer in waqf and identify the practical consequence (thamarah) of each view.

Core Idea
Ownership transfer in waqf is disputed: immediate utterance vs. appointment of trustee vs. judge's ruling.

Within the Hanafi school, the three major imams disagree on a fundamental question: at what moment does the waqif's ownership leave the endowed property? This affects whether the waqf is immediately binding or requires further steps. We will examine the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani, along with their proofs.

Guided Path
1
State the three Hanafi positions
List the views of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on when ownership transfers. Abu Hanifa requires a judge's ruling or death condition; Abu Yusuf says by mere utterance; Muhammad requires appointment of a trustee and delivery.
These are all valid positions within the school.
2
Identify the proofs for each view
Examine the dalil cited for Abu Yusuf (hadith of the mosque) and infer the proofs for Abu Hanifa (hadith of Umar's endowment) and Muhammad (need for delivery as in other transactions).
The passage provides one hadith explicitly; others are implied.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response to Abu Yusuf's proof
Consider how supporters of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad might respond to the mosque hadith: perhaps it is specific to mosques or does not establish a general rule.
The text does not provide rebuttals, so we must reason from usul.
4
Determine the thamarah (practical difference)
Work out what happens if a person says 'this is waqf' without appointing a trustee or judge: under Abu Yusuf it is immediately binding; under Muhammad it is not until delivery; under Abu Hanifa it is not binding unless a judge is involved.
This difference impacts whether the waqf can be revoked or sold.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
State that the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa, as it is the established madhhab view.
This is the position used for fatwa today.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute. It presents the positions of the three major Hanafi imams on the moment when ownership transfers from the waqif in a waqf. Abu Hanifa's view (stated earlier) is that ownership does not transfer unless a judge rules or it is attached to death. Here, Abu Yusuf says ownership transfers immediately upon uttering the words of waqf (by mere statement). Muhammad al-Shaybani says ownership does not transfer until the waqif appoints a trustee (wali) and hands over the property to him. The commentary provides a hadith as proof for Abu Yusuf's view: when the Prophet built the mosque, Banu al-Najjar donated the land without demanding compensation, implying that the ownership transferred immediately upon their statement of donation. For Muhammad, the proof is implied (not quoted directly in the text but the text later provides a hadith for the ruling that after the waqf is valid, it leaves the waqif's ownership; this likely supports Muhammad's view that delivery is required). The passage then states a common ruling: once the waqf is valid (according to any of the three views), it leaves the ownership of the waqif but does not enter the ownership of the beneficiary (i.e., the beneficiary does not own the corpus). The Urdu note adds the practical implication: the endowed property cannot be sold or gifted by either the waqif or the beneficiary. The mufta bihi (relied upon) position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa, as it is the established madhhab position.

Key Terms
الْوَلِي (al-wali): In this context, the trustee or administrator of the waqf, to whom the property is handed over by the waqif.
الْمَوْقُوفُ عَلَيْهِ (al-mawquf 'alayhi): The beneficiary of the waqf, i.e., the person or group for whose benefit the waqf is established.

Waqf of Undivided Share (Musha') – Abū Yūsuf vs. Muhammad
Intra-Hanafī dispute on waqf of undivided share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abū Yūsuf and Muhammad al-Shaybānī regarding waqf of musha‘, identify the dalīl used by each, and explain why Abū Yūsuf’s view is the muftā bihī.

Core Idea
Waqf of an undivided share is valid in the Hanafī school—Abū Yūsuf’s view is relied upon for practice.

The validity of endowing a share of jointly owned property (al-mushā‘) has been a point of debate within the Hanafī school. Two great imāms, Abū Yūsuf and Muhammad al-Shaybānī, differed on this issue, each supported by a hadith. This lesson unpacks their arguments, the evidentiary strength, and why the school ultimately adopted Abū Yūsuf’s position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue and the two positions
The issue is whether endowing an undivided share (mushā‘) is valid without physical separation. Abū Yūsuf holds it is valid; Muhammad holds it is invalid until the share is separated.
2
Examine Abū Yūsuf’s proof
The hadith of the Prophet building the mosque: Banū al-Najjār donated a wall (part of a larger property) without dividing it. This indicates that undivided shares can be given for a cause like waqf.
The wall was part of a property, yet the Prophet accepted it for a mosque.
3
Examine Muhammad’s proof
The hadith of Abū Talḥah: He divided his garden among relatives before giving charity. This implies division is necessary for gifts, and by analogy for waqf.
This case was a gift (hiba), not a waqf; different contractual rules apply.
4
Understand the tarjīḥ (preference)
The school adopted Abū Yūsuf’s view because his proof is directly about a mosque (waqf-like), and it facilitates charitable endowments. The mosque hadith is stronger in context.
The muftā bihī position is Abū Yūsuf’s.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of waqf of musha' (shared/undivided property). Abu Yusuf holds that it is permissible to endow a share of jointly owned property without separating it. Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that it is not permissible until the share is physically separated or specified. The commentary provides two hadith as proofs. For Abu Yusuf: the hadith of building the mosque where the Banu al-Najjar donated a wall (which is part of a larger property) without first dividing it. For Muhammad: the hadith of Abu Talhah who, when he wanted to give charity, divided his garden among his relatives, implying that division was necessary before the endowment could be effective. The Urdu note repeats the general principle that endowed property cannot be sold or gifted by either party. The mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Yusuf, as waqf of undivided share is considered valid and is commonly practiced. The dispute highlights the tension between immediate validity (Abu Yusuf) and the requirement of delivery and specification (Muhammad).

Key Terms
الْمُشَاع (al-musha'): Undivided or shared property, where the ownership is common and not physically separated. In waqf, it refers to endowing a share of such property.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the completion of waqf (delivery requirement) and the validity of waqf of an undivided share, and identify the mufta bihi positions within the school.

Core Idea
Delivery to a guardian completes waqf; waqf of an undivided share is valid by the stronger opinion.

This passage addresses two key issues in Hanafi waqf law: when ownership leaves the founder, and whether an undivided share can be endowed. The school’s founders differ on both, and later authorities have chosen relied-upon positions. Understanding these disputes is essential for issuing correct fatwas on endowments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two issues and the Imams' positions
The passage presents two separate disputes: (1) the condition for ownership transfer (delivery vs. mere utterance), (2) validity of waqf al-musha'. Identify each Imam's view for both issues.
Abu Yusuf says utterance suffices; Muhammad requires delivery. On musha', Abu Yusuf allows, Muhammad forbids. Abu Hanifa agrees with Muhammad on delivery and with Abu Yusuf on musha'.
2
Locate the dalil for each dispute
The hadith of Umar ibn al-Khattab (land at Khaybar) serves as general proof for waqf rules. The hadith of the Banu Najjar (wall donated for mosque without partition) specifically supports permissibility of musha' waqf.
The hadith of Abu Talha distributing palm trees without dividing land also supports musha'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
In Umar's hadith, the Prophet instructed 'habista aslaha' (withhold the original), implying physical retention and management—hence delivery to a guardian. In the mosque hadith, the Prophet accepted a wall without prior partition, showing musha' is permissible.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The three imams differ: Abu Yusuf vs Muhammad on both issues, with Abu Hanifa siding with Muhammad on delivery and with Abu Yusuf on musha'. Their reasoning varies based on different hadith interpretations.
The disagreement is within the school and all positions are recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
5

Later Hanafi authorities have resolved: delivery is required (following Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) for the waqf to be complete; waqf of musha' is permissible (following Abu Yusuf). These are the operative rulings for fatwa.
Knowledge of mufta bihi prevents contradictory rulings.

Perpetuity Condition in Waqf: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on perpetuity condition in waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition of perpetuity in waqf and explain the difference between the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A waqf must ultimately benefit a never-ending class; otherwise it is invalid according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

The validity of a waqf depends on the nature of its beneficiaries. Can you designate a waqf exclusively for your children, or must it eventually go to a perpetual class like the poor? This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute on this question, examining the proofs and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the legal act being discussed? It is the creation of a waqf (endowment). The ruling is conditional: according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the waqf is valid only if the ultimate beneficiaries (jihah) are perpetual.
The jihah must not cease; it must be a class like 'the poor' or 'mosques' that will always exist.
2
Distinguish the positions
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Yusuf holds that if the founder names a terminating class (e.g., 'my children'), the waqf is still valid. After that class ends, the property goes to the poor by default.
Abu Yusuf's view is more flexible, but it is not the mufta bihi.
3
Examine the dalil
The hadith about Umar's waqf is cited as evidence. Umar endowed a property that first benefited his daughter Hafsa for life, then a family administrator, and ultimately the poor. Abu Yusuf sees this as supporting the validity of temporary beneficiaries with a default to the poor.
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad interpret the hadith differently, requiring the ultimate class to be perpetual from the outset.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: perpetuity of the ultimate class is a condition for validity. This is the more cautious view and is maintained in Zahir al-Riwayah.
Some later jurists adopted Abu Yusuf's view for practical flexibility, but the madhhab's fatwa is on the requirement of perpetuity.

Commentary
This passage addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute on the requirement of perpetuity in waqf. Waqf of Movable Property and Its Exceptions
Ruling on waqf of real estate vs. movables, with exceptions allowed by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi ruling on waqf of movable property and identify the exceptions allowed by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Movable property cannot be made waqf unless attached to land or dedicated to jihad.

Waqf is a foundational institution in Islamic law, typically tied to immovable property. But what if someone wants to endow livestock, tools, or weapons? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi default position and the key exceptions introduced by the two leading disciples of Abu Hanifa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
Read the first sentence: 'waqf of real estate is valid, and waqf of what is transported and moved is not permissible.' This is the baseline Hanafi rule.
The term 'manqulat' includes all movables.
2
Note the two exceptions
Abu Yusuf permits waqf of a farm with its cows and workers if they are slaves of the owner. Muhammad permits waqf of horses and weapons specifically ‘in the path of Allah’ (jihad).
Each exception rests on a different reasoning: attachment to land for Abu Yusuf, religious purpose for Muhammad.
3
Examine the primary proof
The hadith of Khalid ibn al-Walid shows the Prophet approving his withholding (habis) of armor and equipment for jihad. This supports Muhammad's exception.
The hadith is ahad (single narrator) but accepted in Bukhari and Muslim.
4
Understand the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa (not quoted here) strictly limits waqf to real estate. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad expand it. The mufta bihi (operative fatwa) accepts both exceptions.
Later Hanafi jurists, such as al-Kasani, also allow other movables if needed for charitable purposes.
5
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Consider: can one endow a car for the mosque’s errands? Under the exceptions, if the car is attached to a property (e.g., stored in the mosque) or used for jihad (e.g., ambulance for fighters), it may be allowed.
Modern analogies require careful istihsan and tarjih.

This is based on the practice of Khalid ibn al-Walid, who withheld his armor and equipment for jihad, and the hadith where the Prophet approved of a camel designated as habis for jihad but allowed temporary use for Hajj. The general principle is that waqf of movables is not valid unless they are so attached to real estate that they become like part of it (per Abu Yusuf) or are specifically dedicated to a religious purpose like jihad (per Muhammad). The mufta bihi position leans toward Muhammad's view, allowing waqf of movables for pious causes.

الكراع (al-kurā'): Horses (specifically for cavalry) used in jihad.
حَبِيس (habīs): Synonym for waqf; a property withheld for charitable use.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Waqf requires perpetual direction; movables can be endowed only if attached to land or for jihad.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute on the conditions for completing a waqf (endowment). And if the waqif makes the proceeds of the waqf for himself or makes the administration to himself, it is permissible according to Abu Yusuf. And if he builds a mosque, his ownership does not cease from it until he separates it from his ownership in its way and permits people to pray in it. When one person prays in it, his ownership ceases according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. Abu Yusuf said: His ownership ceases by his saying 'I made it a mosque.' And whoever builds a watering place for Muslims, or a khan for wayfarers, or a ribat, or makes his land a graveyard, his ownership does not cease from that according to Abu Hanifa unless a judge rules over it. Abu Yusuf said: His ownership ceases by the statement. Muhammad said: When people drink from the watering place, and stay in the ribat and the khan, and bury in the graveyard, the ownership ceases.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the moment when the ownership of the waqif ceases for certain types of waqf. The first part deals with a waqif retaining benefit or administration: Abu Yusuf permits it, implying the waqf is valid despite the condition. The second part focuses on mosques: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require that (1) the property be physically separated from the waqif's other property, (2) the waqif permits people to pray, and (3) at least one person prays. Only then does ownership cease. Abu Yusuf holds that mere verbal declaration ('I made it a mosque') suffices. The third part covers public facilities (siqaya, khan, ribat, maqbara): Abu Hanifa requires a judicial decree to remove ownership; Abu Yusuf again suffices with statement; Muhammad requires actual use by the public (drinking, staying, burying). This shows a range of positions from strict (Abu Hanifa) to lenient (Abu Yusuf) with Muhammad in between. The relied-upon position varies by context, but in fatwa, Abu Hanifa's view on mosques and Muhammad's on facilities are often preferred (though not stated here).

Key Terms
الْوِلاَيَةُ (al-wilāyah): The authority to administer the waqf property.
الْمَسْجِدُ (al-masjid): A mosque, which is a waqf dedicated for worship and owned by Allah.
الرِّبَاطُ (al-ribāṭ): A hostel or fort for travelers or those engaged in religious retreat.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on When Waqf Ownership Transfers
Intra-Hanafi dispute on waqf completion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on waqf completion and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of each.

Core Idea
A waqf's completion depends on its type: verbal declaration alone is insufficient for Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents a critical intra-Hanafi dispute on when waqf ownership leaves the endower. While Abu Yusuf considers a mere declaration sufficient, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require additional steps — either a court ruling or actual public use. Understanding this dispute is essential for determining the bindingness of waqf and the rights of the waqif. We will examine each position and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the property types
The passage distinguishes between a masjid and other charitable endowments (siqaya, khan, ribat, cemetery). Note that different rules apply to each.
This distinction is crucial because the conditions for completion vary.
2
State the three positions
For each property type, state what Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad require for ownership to transfer from the waqif.
Abu Hanifa: mosque → prayer; others → court ruling. Abu Yusuf: verbal declaration suffices. Muhammad: actual use by the public.
3
Analyze the proofs
Examine the hadiths cited: the camel hadith (permissibility of waqif benefiting), the well hadith (waqif retains a right like others), and the story of Umar's horse (once given, cannot be taken back). Consider how each supports the positions.
The camel hadith shows the waqif may still benefit, indicating ownership is not fully severed until the waqf is 'complete'.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
What is the practical difference? Under Abu Yusuf, a verbal waqf is immediately binding and irrevocable. Under Abu Hanifa, it is revocable until the condition (prayer or court ruling) is fulfilled.
This affects inheritance, creditors' rights, and the ability of the waqif to change his mind.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Which view is preferred in later Hanafi fatwa? The text does not specify, but later practice often follows Abu Hanifa for non-masjid endowments, while for mosques the condition of prayer is sometimes relaxed.
Knowledge of the mufta bihi is essential for issuing a fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the completion of waqf. It first presents the ruling on a waqif reserving benefit or control for himself: according to Abu Yusuf, this is permissible. Then it addresses the case of building a mosque: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require that the waqif formally separate the property, grant public access, and at least one person prays there for ownership to transfer. Abu Yusuf holds that mere declaration ('I have made it a mosque') suffices. For other charitable endowments (siqaya, khan, ribat, cemetery), Abu Hanifa requires a court ruling; Abu Yusuf again relies on verbal declaration; Muhammad requires actual use by the public. The text then provides hadith proofs: the Prophet allowing riding a sacrificial camel (indicating permissibility of minor benefit), the hadith of buying a well and donating its water, and the story of Umar's horse. Finally, the Urdu usul notes sum up the three positions: Abu Hanifa requires qada (court judgment), Abu Yusuf requires only utterance, Muhammad requires commencement of use. This dispute affects validity: under Abu Hanifa, a verbal waqf is not binding until court confirmation; under Abu Yusuf, it is binding immediately.

Key Terms
غَلَّة (ghallah): The produce, income, or yield generated by the waqf property (e.g., rent, fruit).
وِلَايَة (wilayah): Custodianship or supervisory authority over the waqf property.
قَضَاء قَاضِي (qada al-qadi): The ruling of a judge that completes the waqf, according to Abu Hanifa.
