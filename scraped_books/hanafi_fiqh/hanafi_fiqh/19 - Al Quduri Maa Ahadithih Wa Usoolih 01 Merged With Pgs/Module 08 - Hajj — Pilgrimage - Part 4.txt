Ruling on Time of Slaughter for Hady
Specifies when different types of hady must be slaughtered

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the time of slaughter for voluntary, tamattu', and qiran hady and explain the Quranic proof used to restrict the time to Yawm al-Nahr.

Core Idea
The hady of tamattu' and qiran must be slaughtered on Yawm al-Nahr alone — timing is a wajib condition.

This passage from Al-Quduri specifies when different categories of hady (sacrificial animals brought during Hajj) must be slaughtered. The ruling distinguishes between voluntary/tamattu'/qiran hady and other types. Understanding this distinction is critical because violating the timing, while still valid, may incur sin. The author cites Quranic evidence to ground the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is slaughtering hady. For voluntary, tamattu', and qiran hady, the ruling is that it is not permissible (la yajuz) except on Yawm al-Nahr. This makes the time a wajib condition.
The word 'لا يجوز' indicates prohibition; slaughtering outside this time is sinful but the sacrifice remains valid.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is Quran 22:28-29, specifically the phrase 'on known days' (ayyam ma'lumat).
The verse commands mentioning Allah's name over cattle, and that is tied to the known days.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Hanafi school interprets 'known days' as the days of sacrifice (10th-13th Dhul Hijjah) but further specifies Yawm al-Nahr for voluntary/tamattu'/qiran hady based on practice of the Prophet and companions.
The verse also includes eating and feeding the poor, which was discussed in the previous lesson.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school allows slaughter of tamattu' and qiran hady throughout the days of tashreeq (10th-13th), not just Yawm al-Nahr.
Both schools use the same verse but different interpretations of the time scope.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (relied upon) and is considered Zahir al-Riwayah. The restriction to Yawm al-Nahr is based on the practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the Sahaba.
This position is authoritative for Hanafi fatwa.

Ruling on Place of Slaughter for Hady
States that all hady must be slaughtered within the Haram.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that all hady must be slaughtered within the Haram and identify the Quranic proof (Surah al-Baqarah 2:196) that establishes this.

Core Idea
All hady, obligatory or voluntary, must be slaughtered within the Haram of Makkah.

This lesson clarifies a key ruling in Hajj fiqh: where must the hady (sacrificial animal) be slaughtered? The Hanafi school, relying on the Qur'an and the practice of the Salaf, holds that the Haram is the exclusive place. Understanding this ensures your sacrificial offering is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Extract the core hukm from the text: it is not permissible to slaughter hadaya except in the Haram. This applies to all types of hady.
The word 'الهَدَايَا' is general, covering both obligatory and voluntary. No distinction is made.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The primary proof is Quran 2:196: 'And do not shave your heads until the hady reaches its place of sacrifice.' The Hanafi interpretation is that 'place of sacrifice' refers to the Haram.
The verse is from Surah al-Baqarah, but the source incorrectly cites it as 2:195. The correct reference is 2:196.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'يَبْلُغَ الْهَدْيُ مَحِلَّهُ' (the hady reaches its place) implies that the hady must be physically present in the Haram before shaving, thus the slaughter must occur there.
The dalala (indication) is zanni, as the verse does not explicitly say 'slaughter in the Haram', but it is understood from context.
4
Consider the secondary proof
The second proof is a statement from Imam Malik (cited as a Tabi'i, though he is later) requiring obligatory hady to be at Makkah, referencing the Quranic phrase 'hadyan baligha al-Ka'bah'.
This secondary proof reinforces the primary, showing consistency in the Salaf's practice.
5
Note the scope and cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi ruling applies universally to all hady. Some other schools allow voluntary hady to be slaughtered outside the Haram, but this is not accepted in the Hanafi school.
The cross-madhhab difference is real; the Hanafi position is based on the general evidence.

Sharing a Camel or Cow Among Seven Persons in Sacrifice
Ruling that a camel and cow each suffice for seven people in sacrifice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a camel or cow can be shared by up to seven persons in sacrifice and identify the primary proof from the Prophet's action during the Farewell Pilgrimage.

Core Idea
A camel or cow suffices for seven persons in sacrifice, as proven by the Prophet's practice.

This lesson addresses the permissibility of joint participation in sacrificial animals. The source states explicitly that a single camel or cow each suffices for seven persons. The proof is drawn from the Prophet's action during his Farewell Pilgrimage, where he slaughtered numerous camels and shared with Ali, indicating that one animal can serve multiple persons.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
Read the first sentence: 'The camel and the cow each suffice for seven persons' — this is the core ruling.
The matn uses the word 'yujzi'u' meaning 'suffices' or 'is valid for'.
2
Locate the hadith of Jabir
The source cites the hadith as proof. The Prophet slaughtered 63 camels, then gave to Ali to slaughter the rest, and made him share in his hady. He also ordered a piece from each camel to be cooked together.
The hadith is from Muslim and Abu Dawud.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's action of taking a piece from each camel and cooking them together shows that the camel was considered common. His sharing with Ali demonstrates joint participation, implying that one camel can serve multiple persons.
This is a clear indication of permissibility for sharing.
4
Conclude the ruling
Based on this proof, the Hanafi school holds that a camel or cow can be shared by up to seven persons. This ruling is consistent with the practice of the Prophet.
The ruling is considered muttafaq alayh among schools.

Eating from the Meat of a Shared Sacrifice
Clarification on which sacrifices can be eaten

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between types of sacrifice (hady) regarding the permissibility of eating the meat, applying the statement of Ibn Umar.

Core Idea
Meat from a voluntary or tamattu' hady may be eaten, but not from a vow or game compensation.

This lesson examines a secondary ruling within the same passage: when is it permissible to eat from the sacrificial meat? The Companion Ibn Umar clarifies that for certain types, such as penalty for game (jaza' al-sayd) or a vowed sacrifice (nadhr), the meat must be given entirely in charity, while for others, including tamattu' hady, one may eat and feed others. This ruling applies directly to the shared camel or cow from the previous lesson.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories in Ibn Umar's statement
Ibn Umar mentions three types: (1) jaza' al-sayd (compensation for game), (2) al-nadhr (vow), and (3) 'other than that' (ma siwa dhalik).
The categories are exclusive.
2
Understand the ruling for each category
For categories 1 and 2, one does not eat from them; they must be given in charity. For category 3 (other), one may eat.
This is a clear distinction based on the type of sacrifice.
3
Note the opinion of Ata' on tamattu'
Ata' specifically says that from the hady of tamattu', one may eat and feed others, confirming that tamattu' falls under 'other'.
Tamattu' hady is a type of voluntary sacrifice during Hajj.
4
Apply to the shared camel or cow
Since the shared sacrifice in the previous lesson is for hady of tamattu' or udhiyah (Eid sacrifice), it falls under 'other than that', so the meat may be eaten.
This means groups sharing a camel or cow can eat from it.

Chapter of Hady. And it is permissible to give in charity from what is upon the poor of the Haram and others, and it is not obligatory to announce the hadya. And the better in camels is nahr, and in cows and sheep is dhabh. And it is more proper that the person slaughters them with his own hand if he is good at that. Evidence (1): the verse to establish the permissibility of giving to the poor of the Haram: 'Eat from them and feed the distressed poor' (Quran, Surah al-Hajj 22, verse 28). Evidence (2): the statement of a tabi'i to establish that slaughter of hadya is not permitted except in the Haram. Malik said: 'The one who is obliged to offer hady for killing game, or has hady due for other than that, then his hady is only at Mecca, as Allah said: {hady reaching the Kabah}' (Muwatta of Imam Malik: Chapter of Compilation of Hady, no. 164). Evidence (1): the verse to establish that the better in camels is nahr and in cows [sheep] the slaughter: 'So pray to your Lord and sacrifice' (Quran, Surah al-Kawthar 108, verse 2). Evidence (2): the hadith to establish that the better in camels is nahr and in cows: from Anas (may Allah be pleased with him) said: The Prophet (peace be upon him) prayed zuhr in Medina four... and the Prophet (peace be upon him) sacrificed with his own hand seven standing camels, and sacrificed in Medina two horned rams with black and white markings (Bukhari: Chapter of Slaughtering the Hady Standing, no. 1714/Abu Dawud: Chapter of How to Slaughter the Hady, no. 1767). Evidence (3): the hadith to establish that the better in camels is nahr and in cows: from Abu Hurayra that the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) slaughtered on behalf of those of his wives who performed umrah a cow among them (Abu Dawud: Chapter on Hady of Cows, no. 1751). Evidence (1): the hadith to establish that it is more proper that the person slaughters them with his own hand: from Anas, he said: 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) sacrificed two horned rams with black and white markings, and I saw him placing his foot on their sides, saying Bismillah and Allahu Akbar, and slaughtered them with his own hand' (Bukhari: Chapter of Whoever Slaughtered the Udhiyah with His Own Hand, no. 5558/Muslim: Chapter of It Is Recommended to Slaughter the Udhiyah Oneself without Delegation and Saying Bismillah and Takbir, no. 1966). Principle: For hady whose time is specified, like tamattu', qiran, and naqli hady, it is not correct to do them before their time. Principle: The meat of any type of hady can be distributed to all kinds of poor and needy. And whoever leads a hady camel and is compelled to ride it, he may ride it, and if he is not in need of it, he should not ride it. And if it has milk, he should not milk it, and he sprinkles its udder with cold water until the milk stops. Evidence (1): the hadith to establish that whoever leads a hady camel and is compelled to ride it: I heard Jabir ibn Abdullah asked about riding the hady, and he said: I heard the Prophet (peace be upon him) say: 'Ride it in a proper manner when you are in need of it until you find another mount' (Muslim: Chapter on Permissibility of Riding a Hady Camel for One Who Needs It, no. 1324/Abu Dawud: Chapter on Riding the Hady, no. 1761). Evidence (1): the hadith to establish that if it has milk, he should not milk it and he sprinkles its udder with water: that Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) informed him: 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) ordered him to supervise his hady and to distribute all of his hady, its meat, skins, and covers, and not to give anything from it as the butcher's wage' (Bukhari: Chapter: One Gives in Charity the Hides of Hady, no. 1717/Muslim: Chapter on Charity with the Meat of Hady, Its Skins, and Its Covers, no. 1317). Evidence (2): the statement of a tabi'i to establish that if it has milk, he should not milk it and he sprinkles its udder with cold water until the milk stops: a man from Hamdan heard Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) asked about a man who bought a cow to sacrifice with it and it gave birth, so he said: 'Do not drink its milk except surplus' (Sunan al-Kubra for al-Bayhaqi: Chapter on the Milk of the Hady Camel Should Not Be Drunk, no. 10210). Principle: The hady animal becomes charity, and charity cannot be given to anyone as wages.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with its hadith proofs, covers several rulings on hady (the sacrificial animal brought to the Haram). The text opens with the chapter heading 'باب الهدي'. The first ruling states that it is permissible to distribute the meat of hady to the poor of the Haram and also to poor people outside the Haram. This is based on the Quranic verse in Surah al-Hajj which commands to eat from the animals and feed the poor, showing that distribution is not limited to the Haram. The text then notes that it is not obligatory to announce the hady (i.e., to make it known). The second ruling specifies the preferred method of slaughter: for camels (بدن), the method is نحر (stabbing the neck while standing), and for cows and sheep, the method is ذبح (cutting the throat). Evidence includes the Quranic command 'فَصَلِّ لِرَبِّكَ وَانْحَرْ' (pray and sacrifice) and multiple hadiths where the Prophet performed nahr for camels and dhabh for cows/sheep. The third ruling states that it is more virtuous for the person to slaughter the animal with his own hand if he is proficient, as the Prophet himself slaughtered his udhiyah with his own hand. The text then provides Urdu principles: that specified hady (for tamattu', qiran, or nadhr) cannot be done before its time, and that meat from any hady can be given to all poor. The passage continues on the next page with additional rulings. The fourth ruling: the collars (خلال) and halters (خطام) of the hady animal should also be given in charity, and the butcher's wage should not be paid from the hady itself. This is based on the hadith where the Prophet ordered that all parts of the hady be distributed, including the skins and covers, and not to give anything to the butcher from it. The fifth ruling: if a person is leading a hady camel and is compelled to ride it due to need, he may ride it; but if he can manage without riding, he should not do so. This is based on the hadith where the Prophet permitted riding the hady when needed. The sixth ruling: if the hady animal has milk, one should not milk it; instead, sprinkle cold water on its udder to dry up the milk. This is based on the hadith and the statement of Ali that the milk of a sacrificial animal should not be consumed except what is surplus (i.e., if the young is not taking it). The final Urdu principle summarizes that the hady animal becomes charity, and charity cannot be used to pay wages. All rulings are from the Hanafi school, with proofs from the Quran and authentic Sunnah. No internal Hanafi dispute is mentioned here.

Key Terms

بدن (budn): Camels (plural of badanah).
نحر (nahr): Method of slaughtering a camel by stabbing the lower neck while standing.
ذبح (dhabh): Method of slaughtering cows and sheep by cutting the throat.

Accessories, Riding, and Milk of Hady
Subsidiary rulings on the treatment of hady animals: collars, halters, butcher's wage, riding, and milk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on giving the collar and halter of a hady animal in charity, the prohibition of paying the butcher from the meat, the permissibility of riding the animal when necessary, and the prohibition of milking it except for surplus.

Core Idea
The hady animal is entirely charity; its collars and halters are included, the butcher cannot be paid from it, riding is allowed only when necessary, and milk is not for personal use.

This lesson continues the chapter on hady from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, covering what accessories of the animal should be given in charity, how to handle the butcher's wage, when riding the animal is permitted, and the rule regarding its milk. Each ruling is grounded in hadith and the principle that the hady becomes charity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four subsidiary rulings
Read the text: (1) give collars (خلال) and halters (خطام) in charity, (2) do not give butcher's wage from it, (3) ride only when compelled, (4) do not milk but sprinkle cold water to dry the milk. Note the imperative form.
These rulings clarify that nothing from the hady should be used for personal benefit except in genuine need.
2
Analyse the proofs for each
Find the hadith for each ruling. For (1) and (2), the hadith of Ali (رضي الله عنه) commands distribution of all parts. For (3), the hadith of Jabir permits riding when needed. For (4), the same hadith plus the saying of Ali (رضي الله عنه) prohibits consuming milk except surplus.
The hadith for (1) and (2) is the same; it emphasises that nothing should be withheld from charity.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The Urdu principle states: 'The hady animal becomes charity, and charity cannot be given as wages.' This explains why the butcher cannot be paid from the meat – it is already given in charity.
This principle also governs the collars and halters – they are part of the charity.
4
Distinguish between necessity and preference
Riding is only allowed when compelled (اضطر). Otherwise, it is not allowed. Similarly, milking is prohibited; only surplus milk (if the young does not take it) may be consumed.
The concession of riding and surplus milk are exceptions for necessity.

Chapter of Hady. And it is permissible to give in charity from what is upon the poor of the Haram and others, and it is not obligatory to announce the hadya. And the better in camels is nahr, and in cows and sheep is dhabh. And it is more proper that the person slaughters them with his own hand if he is good at that. Evidence (1): the verse to establish the permissibility of giving to the poor of the Haram: 'Eat from them and feed the distressed poor' (Quran, Surah al-Hajj 22, verse 28). Evidence (2): the statement of a tabi'i to establish that slaughter of hadya is not permitted except in the Haram. Malik said: 'The one who is obliged to offer hady for killing game, or has hady due for other than that, then his hady is only at Mecca, as Allah said: {hady reaching the Kabah}' (Muwatta of Imam Malik: Chapter of Compilation of Hady, no. 164). Evidence (1): the verse to establish that the better in camels is nahr and in cows [sheep] the slaughter: 'So pray to your Lord and sacrifice' (Quran, Surah al-Kawthar 108, verse 2). Evidence (2): the hadith to establish that the better in camels is nahr and in cows: from Anas (may Allah be pleased with him) said: The Prophet (peace be upon him) prayed zuhr in Medina four... and the Prophet (peace be upon him) sacrificed with his own hand seven standing camels, and sacrificed in Medina two horned rams with black and white markings (Bukhari: Chapter of Slaughtering the Hady Standing, no. 1714/Abu Dawud: Chapter of How to Slaughter the Hady, no. 1767). Evidence (3): the hadith to establish that the better in camels is nahr and in cows: from Abu Hurayra that the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) slaughtered on behalf of those of his wives who performed umrah a cow among them (Abu Dawud: Chapter on Hady of Cows, no. 1751). Evidence (1): the hadith to establish that it is more proper that the person slaughters them with his own hand: from Anas, he said: 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) sacrificed two horned rams with black and white markings, and I saw him placing his foot on their sides, saying Bismillah and Allahu Akbar, and slaughtered them with his own hand' (Bukhari: Chapter of Whoever Slaughtered the Udhiyah with His Own Hand, no. 5558/Muslim: Chapter of It Is Recommended to Slaughter the Udhiyah Oneself without Delegation and Saying Bismillah and Takbir, no. 1966). Principle: For hady whose time is specified, like tamattu', qiran, and naqli hady, it is not correct to do them before their time. Principle: The meat of any type of hady can be distributed to all kinds of poor and needy. Chapter of Hady: And he gives in charity its collars and halters, and does not give the butcher's wage from it. And whoever leads a hady camel and is compelled to ride it, he may ride it, and if he is not in need of it, he should not ride it. And if it has milk, he should not milk it, and he sprinkles its udder with cold water until the milk stops. Evidence (1): the hadith to establish that he gives in charity its collars and halters: that Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) informed him: 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) ordered him to supervise his hady and to distribute all of his hady, its meat, skins, and covers, and not to give anything from it as the butcher's wage' (Bukhari: Chapter: One Gives in Charity the Hides of Hady, no. 1717/Muslim: Chapter on Charity with the Meat of Hady, Its Skins, and Its Covers, no. 1317). Evidence (1): the hadith to establish that whoever leads a hady camel and is compelled to ride it: I heard Jabir ibn Abdullah asked about riding the hady, and he said: I heard the Prophet (peace be upon him) say: 'Ride it in a proper manner when you are in need of it until you find another mount' (Muslim: Chapter on Permissibility of Riding a Hady Camel for One Who Needs It, no. 1324/Abu Dawud: Chapter on Riding the Hady, no. 1761). Evidence (1): the hadith to establish that if it has milk, he should not milk it and he sprinkles its udder with water: that Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) informed him: 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) ordered him to supervise his hady and to distribute all of his hady, its meat, skins, and covers, and not to give anything from it as the butcher's wage' (Bukhari: Chapter: One Gives in Charity the Hides of Hady, no. 1717/Muslim: Chapter on Charity with the Meat of Hady, Its Skins, and Its Covers, no. 1317). Evidence (2): the statement of a tabi'i to establish that if it has milk, he should not milk it and he sprinkles its udder with cold water until the milk stops: a man from Hamdan heard Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) asked about a man who bought a cow to sacrifice with it and it gave birth, so he said: 'Do not drink its milk except surplus' (Sunan al-Kubra for al-Bayhaqi: Chapter on the Milk of the Hady Camel Should Not Be Drunk, no. 10210). Principle: The hady animal becomes charity, and charity cannot be given to anyone as wages.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with its hadith proofs, covers several rulings on hady (the sacrificial animal brought to the Haram). The text opens with the chapter heading 'باب الهدي'. The first ruling states that it is permissible to distribute the meat of hady to the poor of the Haram and also to poor people outside the Haram. This is based on the Quranic verse in Surah al-Hajj which commands to eat from the animals and feed the poor, showing that distribution is not limited to the Haram. The text then notes that it is not obligatory to announce the hady (i.e., to make it known). The second ruling specifies the preferred method of slaughter: for camels (بدن), the method is نحر (stabbing the neck while standing), and for cows and sheep, the method is ذبح (cutting the throat). Evidence includes the Quranic command 'فَصَلِّ لِرَبِّكَ وَانْحَرْ' (pray and sacrifice) and multiple hadiths where the Prophet performed nahr for camels and dhabh for cows/sheep. The third ruling states that it is more virtuous for the person to slaughter the animal with his own hand if he is proficient, as the Prophet himself slaughtered his udhiyah with his own hand. The text then provides Urdu principles: that specified hady (for tamattu', qiran, or nadhr) cannot be done before its time, and that meat from any hady can be given to all poor. The passage continues on the next page with additional rulings. The fourth ruling: the collars (خلال) and halters (خطام) of the hady animal should also be given in charity, and the butcher's wage should not be paid from the hady itself. This is based on the hadith where the Prophet ordered that all parts of the hady be distributed, including the skins and covers, and not to give anything to the butcher from it. The fifth ruling: if a person is leading a hady camel and is compelled to ride it due to need, he may ride it; but if he can manage without riding, he should not do so. This is based on the hadith where the Prophet permitted riding the hady when needed. The sixth ruling: if the hady animal has milk, one should not milk it; instead, sprinkle cold water on its udder to dry up the milk. This is based on the hadith and the statement of Ali that the milk of a sacrificial animal should not be consumed except what is surplus (i.e., if the young is not taking it). The final Urdu principle summarizes that the hady animal becomes charity, and charity cannot be used to pay wages. All rulings are from the Hanafi school, with proofs from the Quran and authentic Sunnah. No internal Hanafi dispute is mentioned here.

خطام (khitam): The halter or rope for leading the animal.
جزار (jazzar): The butcher; the one who slaughters and prepares the meat.

Charity from the Hady: Nose Ring, Halter, and Butcher's Wages
States rulings on charity from hady and prohibition of butcher's wage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on disposing of the nose ring and halter of the hady animal and explain why paying the butcher from the animal is prohibited.

Core Idea
The entire hady animal and its accessories are charity; the butcher cannot be paid from it.

In this lesson, we examine the first part of the matn from Al-Quduri's chapter on Hady. The text establishes that the nose ring (khilal) and halter (khitam) of a sacrificial animal must be given in charity, and that the butcher's wages may not be taken from the animal itself. These rulings are based on the Prophetic command to distribute the entire animal and not to pay the butcher from it, as narrated by Ali ibn Abi Talib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
The matn states: 'وَيَتَصَدَّقُ بِخِلَالِهَا وَخِطَامِهَا وَلا يُعْطِي أَجْرَ الْجَزَّارِ مِنْهَا'. Determine what is required (charity) and what is prohibited (paying butcher).
The nose ring (khilal) and halter (khitam) must be given in charity; the butcher's wages must come from elsewhere.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The hadith from Ali (Bukhari 1717, Muslim 1317) is cited: the Prophet ordered distribution of all parts and prohibited giving anything for butchering.
The hadith text is: 'أن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم أمره أن يقوم على بدنه، وأن يقسم بدنه كلها، حومها وجلودها وجلالها، ولا يعطي في جزارتها شيئًا.'
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'يقسم' implies obligation to distribute, and 'لا يعطي' indicates prohibition. Since the animal becomes charity, all its parts must go to the poor.
Hanafi usul: al-amr yufidu al-wujub, but here the context of charity makes it sunnah muakkadah; the prohibition is clear (haram).
4
Note cross-madhhab differences
Some schools may allow retaining accessories or paying the butcher from the animal, but the Hanafi school strictly prohibits both based on the explicit hadith.
The Hanafi position is supported by zahir al-riwayah and is the mufta bihi.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The ruling is established from sound hadith and is not overridden by istihsan. The Hanafi position is relied upon.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf reported.

Riding and Milking the Hady: Permissibility and Restrictions
States rulings on riding and milking the hady animal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain when it is permissible to ride a hady animal and why milking it is prohibited unless necessary.

Core Idea
A hady animal may be ridden only out of necessity; its milk may not be used by the owner.

This lesson covers the second part of the matn, dealing with riding and milking the sacrificial animal. The text states that if a person is driving a hady camel and is forced to ride it due to necessity, he may do so; otherwise, he should not. Similarly, if the animal has milk, the owner must not milk it for his own benefit; instead he should sprinkle the udder with cold water to dry up the milk. These rulings are supported by hadith and reports from the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acts and their default ruling
The matn mentions two acts: riding the hady camel and milking it. The default rule: riding is prohibited unless necessary; milking is prohibited entirely.
The text says: 'فَاضْطَرَّ إِلَى رُكُوبِهَا رَكِبَهَا وَإِنْ اسْتَغْنَى عَنْهَا لَمْ يَرْكَبْهَا' and 'وَإِنْ كَانَ لَهَا لَبَنْ لَمْ يَحْلُبْهَا'.
2
Extract the condition for riding
Riding is permitted only when the person is compelled (in need) to ride; otherwise, it is not permitted. This shows a necessity-based exception.
The condition is 'اضْطَرّ' (forced). Without necessity, riding is not allowed.
3
Locate the dalil for each ruling
For riding: the hadith of Jabir (Muslim 1324) reports the Prophet said, 'Ride it with kindness, when you are forced to it, until you find another mount.' For milking: the athar of Ali (Bayhaqi 10210) prohibits drinking its milk except the surplus.
The hadith explicitly links permission to necessity; the athar shows prohibition of using the milk.
4
Determine the hukm level for each case
Riding without necessity is haram; riding with necessity is mubah. Milking the animal for personal use is haram; the milk must be left or given in charity if surplus.
Hanafi: The animal is consecrated to Allah; using it for personal benefit is unlawful.

Hady Perishing or Defective: Voluntary vs Obligatory
States rulings when a sacrificial animal perishes or becomes defective on the way

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on replacement of a perished hady and describe the prescribed procedure for a voluntary camel that perishes.

Core Idea
Voluntary hady: no replacement, only slaughter and mark; obligatory hady: must be replaced.

When a sacrificial animal (hady) perishes en route to Makkah, the ruling depends on whether it is voluntary (tatatuwwu') or obligatory (wajib, e.g., due to a vow). This lesson from Al-Quduri's matn explains the key distinction and the prescribed actions. Understanding this difference prevents unnecessary replacement obligations for voluntary offerings.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between voluntary and obligatory hady
Determine whether the hady is a voluntary offering or an obligatory one (e.g., due to a vow). This is the key factor.
2
Apply the replacement rule
If voluntary, no replacement is required; if obligatory, a replacement must be provided.
3
Describe the procedure for a perished voluntary camel
The owner slaughters it, dyes the sandal in its blood, strikes its side, and prohibits consumption for himself and the rich.
4
Describe the procedure for a perished obligatory camel
The owner replaces it and may dispose of the dead animal as he wishes.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri addresses what happens when the hady animal perishes or becomes defective. The key distinction is between voluntary (tatatuwwu') and obligatory (wajib) hady. If a voluntary animal dies en route, the owner is not required to replace it. However, if it is obligatory (e.g., due to a vow or as part of a required pilgrimage), the owner must provide a replacement even if the original had a major defect. Garlanding of Hady: Which Animals Are Marked
States which types of hady are garlanded and which are not, with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which types of hady are garlanded in the Hanafi school and explain the rationale for excluding expiatory penalties.

Core Idea
Voluntary and pilgrimage-related hady are garlanded; expiatory penalties are not, because concealment is preferred.

The practice of placing a garland (taqlid) on a sacrificial animal marks it as a hady. Al-Quduri's matn specifies that three types are garlanded: voluntary offerings, the hady of tamattu', and the hady of qiran. Two types are not: the blood penalty for being prevented (dam al-ihsar) and penalties for violations (dam al-jinayat). This lesson explores the evidence and reasoning behind this distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its categories
First, recognize that the ruling distinguishes between types of hady: voluntary (tatawwu'), mut'ah, qiran on one side; dam al-ihsar and dam al-jinayat on the other.
The matn gives a clear list: garlanding is done for the first three, not for the last two.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The proof comes from Aisha's hadith describing how she wove garlands for the Prophet's hady and he placed them. This shows the practice for voluntary and similar hady.
The hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim, indicating it is a sound basis.
3
Understand the usul rationale
The usul note explains that dam al-ihsar and dam al-jinayat are penalties (jinayat) and must be concealed. Garlanding is a form of publicity, so it is not done for expiatory sacrifices.
This reasoning is specific to the Hanafi school; other schools may differ.
4
Apply the ruling to scenarios
Practice classifying different types of hady: a pilgrim who performs tamattu' sends a sheep – it should be garlanded. A pilgrim who commits a violation and owes a dam – it should not be garlanded.
The key is whether the sacrifice is an offering (qurbah) or an expiation (kaffarah).

Garlanding the Hadi: Which Offerings Are Adorned and Why
States the ruling on which offerings are garlanded and provides hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which types of hadi are garlanded and which are not, and explain the legal basis for this distinction using Hanafi proofs.

Core Idea
Garlanding expresses devotion; penalty blood is concealed because it stems from a sin.

Garlanding the sacrificial animal (taqlid al-hady) is a sunnah act that distinguishes voluntary and Hajj-related offerings from penalty blood. The Hanafi school, relying on the practice of the Prophet (PBUH) as reported by Aisha (RA), mandates that the hadi of tatabbu', mut'ah, and qiran be garlanded, while dam al-ihsar and dam al-jinayat are not. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The text rules on 'garlanding' (taqlid) the hadi. Determine which types of hadi are covered: voluntary, mut'ah, qiran (garlanded) vs. dam al-ihsar and dam al-jinayat (not garlanded).
The ruling for garlanding is sunnah muakkadah; for not garlanding penalty blood, it is a requirement (wajib).
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites three hadith from Aisha (RA) describing the Prophet's practice. Identify the key evidence: the Prophet himself garlanded the hadi and sent it with Abu Bakr.
The hadith are from Bukhari and Muslim, establishing the Sunnah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the dalil prove the ruling? The Prophet's consistent action (fi'l) of garlanding indicates recommendation (sunnah). The absence of garlanding for penalty blood is derived from the principle of concealing sin.
The Hanafi principle: 'al-jurm yukhfa' (sin is concealed).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Ibn Abbas (RA) held that the hadi of qiran is not garlanded. Aisha explicitly corrected him, providing direct evidence. The Hanafi school follows Aisha's testimony.
This is a legitimate Companions' disagreement; the stronger proof is adopted.
5
Apply the usul rationale
Why are penalty animals not garlanded? Because they are expiation for a sin (ihsar or jinayat). Garlanding would publicize the sin, contradicting the recommendation to conceal it. Voluntary and Hajj offerings are acts of devotion, so adorning them is appropriate.
This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh.

Principles: The blood of ihsar and injuries is not garlanded because it is a sin, and sin is to be concealed, and garlanding publicizes it. Vocabulary: yugallidu: he puts a garland or string around the neck.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proofs concerning the practice of garlanding sacrificial animals (taqlid al-hady). The matn from Al-Quduri states that one garlands the hadi for voluntary offerings (tatabbu'), for tamattu' (mut'ah), and for qiran (combined Hajj and Umrah), but one does not garland the blood required for ihsar (being prevented from completing Hajj) or for jinayat (violations during Ihram). The author then adduces three hadith proofs from the practice of the Prophet (PBUH) as reported by Aisha (RA). The first hadith explicitly states that the Prophet sent his hadi with a garland. The second from Umm al-Mu'minin confirms that she made garlands from wool. The third shows that the Prophet garlanded sheep while remaining in a non-muhrim state. The usul note explains why penalty blood is not garlanded: because it is a consequence of a sin, and sins should be concealed, whereas garlanding publicizes the animal. The principle is that voluntary and expiatory offerings are distinguished; only the former are adorned to show devotion.

Key Terms
يقلد (yugallidu): To put a garland (qiladah) around the neck of the sacrificial animal.
هدي (hady): The animal sent to the Haram as a sacrifice, especially during Hajj and Umrah.
دم الإحصار (dam al-ihsar): The blood (sacrificial animal) required when one is prevented from completing Hajj or Umrah.
