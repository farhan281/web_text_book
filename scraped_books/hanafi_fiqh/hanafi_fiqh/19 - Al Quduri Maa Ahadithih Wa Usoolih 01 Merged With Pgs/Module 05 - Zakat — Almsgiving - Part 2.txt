Gift of a Slave-Girl with Exception of Her Unborn Child
Ruling on hibah with exception and comparison to sadaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hibah with an impossible exception and explain why the exception is void, and distinguish it from hibah of a divisible undivided share.

Core Idea
An invalid exception in hibah does not void the gift; the gift stands, the exception is void.

In the Hanafi school, the validity of a gift (hibah) hinges on the possibility of the subject matter. When a donor attempts to exclude something that cannot be separated—like an unborn child from its mother—the exclusion is legally impossible. This lesson examines the ruling from Al-Quduri, where a gift of a slave-girl with an exception of her unborn child is valid, but the exception is void. We also learn that sadaqah (charity) follows the same rules regarding possession (qabd), and that gifting an undivided share (mushaa') that is divisible is not permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hibah (gift) of a slave-girl with an exception (istithna') of her unborn child. The ruling is that the gift is valid (sahih) and the exception is void (batil).
The unborn child is part of the mother and cannot be separately owned at the time of gift.
2
Understand the reason for the exception being void
The exception is impossible because the unborn child is not a separate entity capable of independent ownership; it is legally considered part of the mother. Therefore, the exception is invalid, but the main gift remains unaffected.
This is based on the legal maxim: 'What is impossible as an exception does not affect the validity of the contract.'
3
Compare with sadaqah
The text states that sadaqah (charity) is like hibah in that it is not valid without possession (qabd). This means both require actual delivery for the transfer of ownership to be binding.
Possession is a condition (shart) for the completion of both hibah and sadaqah in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Apply the prohibition of gifting mushaa'
The text adds that hibah of an undivided share that is divisible (e.g., half of a house that can be partitioned) is not permissible. This is because it leads to uncertainty (gharar) regarding the exact portion gifted.
If the share is indivisible, such as half of a small room, some Hanafi scholars allow it.
5
Connect to the general principle of gharar
The prohibition of uncertain transactions (gharar) underlies these rulings. The exception of an unborn child creates uncertainty, but since it is impossible, the main gift is free of uncertainty. The mushaa' case involves uncertainty in the subject matter itself.
For the exception, the hadith evidence (not in the text but in the commentary) prohibits certain types of gharar.

The Irrevocability of Sadaqah After Possession
Ruling on irrevocability of sadaqah after qabd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that sadaqah cannot be revoked after qabd and explain the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
Taking back sadaqah after possession is like returning to vomit — forbidden and invalid.

This lesson unpacks the first ruling from the passage: the absolute prohibition on revoking a charitable gift once the recipient has taken possession. The Hanafi school bases this on a powerful prophetic analogy that likens such an act to consuming one's own vomit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is returning sadaqah after the recipient has taken possession (qabd). The ruling is that it is not valid (la yasih) and thus haram.
The term 'la yasih' indicates legal invalidity; the act does not transfer ownership back.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith of Ibn Abbas (ra): 'The one who takes back his gift is like the one who returns to his vomit.' (Bukhari 2621, Muslim 1622)
This hadith is ahad but accepted by the Hanafi school as proof for prohibition.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The simile indicates strong disapproval and prohibition. The Hanafi school analogizes sadaqah to hibah, and since the hadith forbids taking back hibah, it applies to sadaqah as well.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) allow return in certain cases such as if the recipient is ungrateful. The Hanafi school holds the prohibition as absolute.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that returning sadaqah after qabd is absolutely prohibited and invalid.
This is the zahir al-riwayah position.

Fulfilling a Vow to Give Charity (Nadhr)
Rulings on nadhr to give charity, including types and concessions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rulings on a nadhr to give charity, including what must be given and the allowance to keep family expenses.

Core Idea
A vow to give charity creates a wajib obligation; if vowing all property, one may keep necessities but must later replace them.

This lesson covers the second and third sentences of the passage: the obligations that arise from a nadhr (vow) to give charity. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a general vow to give charity and a vow to give one's entire property, providing a concession for essential living expenses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of nadhr
The text mentions two scenarios: 1) 'vows to give charity from his wealth' (general), and 2) 'vows to give charity from his property' (all property).
'Wealth' (mal) is general; 'property' (milk) means ownership of all assets.
2
Determine the obligation for a general nadhr
For a general vow, one must give from the type of wealth on which zakat becomes due (e.g., gold, silver, trade goods, livestock).
This links the vow to zakatable categories, but the obligation is from the nadhr, not zakat itself.
3
Apply the ruling for a nadhr of all property
If a person vows to give all his property (milk), he must give all of it, but with a concession: he may keep what he needs for himself and his dependents until he earns.
4
Explain the replacement obligation
Once he earns money, he must give the equivalent of what he kept. The original vow remains binding and must be fulfilled eventually.
5
Note the underlying principle
The Shariah does not intend to cause hardship; it balances the binding nature of vows with the practical need to sustain oneself and one's family.

Two Foundational Principles: Sadaqah Distribution and Ownership Inclusivity
Two principles: distribution of charity and ownership inclusiveness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the two Hanafi principles regarding sadaqah distribution and ownership, and APPLY the exception to the distribution rule.

Core Idea
Charity must be distributed; but if division destroys it, giving whole is valid. Ownership includes all property.

This lesson presents two concise legal principles (usul) from the Hanafi commentaries on al-Quduri. They address when charity items must be divided among recipients and what 'ownership' encompasses. Though brief, these principles guide many rulings in zakat, vows, and gifts.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the first principle: charity without distribution is not permissible
State the default rule: sadaqah must be distributed among recipients. If not, the act is not valid (ghair ja'iz).
This is the general rule; exceptions exist as per next step.
2
Note the exception: if division destroys the item, there is no harm
When dividing the charity item would cause it to lose value or be wasted (e.g., breaking a single item into pieces), then it is permissible to give it whole without dividing.
This prevents waste, a key Hanafi consideration.
3
Understand the second principle: ownership includes all types of wealth
When one owns a category of property (e.g., 'all my wealth'), it encompasses every item within that category. This affects vows and charity where specification is needed.
The term 'milkiyyah' covers both tangible and usufruct rights.
4
Apply both principles together
Consider a scenario: someone vows to give all his wealth as sadaqah. The second principle means it includes everything. The first principle requires distribution, but if giving each item separately would waste them (e.g., a perishable bundle), the exception applies.
These principles often interact in practical cases.

Commentary
These are two juristic principles (usul) from the commentary on al-Quduri. The first states that when giving charity, the item should be distributed to the recipients; but if dividing it would cause it to be wasted (e.g., a divisible item that loses value), then it is permissible not to divide. The second principle clarifies that ownership encompasses all forms of property, which relates to rulings on vows and charity where 'all property' means everything owned.

صدقہ (sadaqah): Voluntary charity given for the sake of Allah.
