The Hanafi Ruling on Iqrar of Paternity and Relatives
Ruling on iqrar of paternity and iqrar for relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid iqrar of paternity and identify which relatives can be acknowledged by a man or woman, applying Hanafi fiqh principles.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment of paternity is valid if the child is plausible and confirms; iqrar for close relatives is always permissible.

This passage from Al-Quduri addresses two related issues: the establishment of lineage through acknowledgment (iqrar) of paternity, and the permissibility of acknowledging close relatives. The ruling on paternity requires both plausibility and the child's confirmation, even if the acknowledger is ill. For relatives, the law facilitates natural familial bonds. We will explore the legal reasoning and the prophetic precedent.

Guided Path
1
Define iqrar and its legal effect
What is iqrar? It is a declaration that establishes legal consequences. Here, it establishes lineage and inheritance.
2
Identify conditions for paternity iqrar
Two conditions: (1) the child must be plausible (yūladu mithluhu li-mithlihi), (2) the child must confirm (ṣaddaqahu). The acknowledgment is valid even if the acknowledger is terminally ill.
The term 'yūladu mithluhu' means the child could be born from someone like the acknowledger in age and health.
3
Examine the permissibility of iqrar for relatives
A man can acknowledge parents, child, wife, and mawla. A woman can acknowledge parents, husband, and mawla. These are accepted because they are natural and unlikely fraudulent.
Mawla includes freed slaves and clients with wala' relationship.
4
Connect the proof
The hadith of Ali shows that even in a disputed case, paternity can be determined by evidence, including acknowledgment. This supports the Hanafi position that iqrar is a strong proof.
The hadith also illustrates the use of lots as a last resort.

And whoever acknowledges a boy who would be born from someone like him, and he has no known lineage, that he is his son, and the boy confirms it – his lineage is established, even if he is sick, and he shares with the heirs in the inheritance. And the acknowledgment of a man is permissible for parents, and child, and wife, and mawla. And the acknowledgment of a woman is accepted for parents, and husband, and mawla. Commentary
This passage combines two sub-rulings. First, if a person acknowledges a boy as his son, and the boy (who is plausible to be his child) confirms it, the child's lineage is established even if the acknowledger is terminally ill, and the child shares in inheritance. The hadith about Ali's ruling on disputed paternity supports the principle of establishing paternity through acknowledgment and evidence. Second, it states that a man may validly acknowledge parents, children, spouse, and mawla (client/freed slave), and a woman may acknowledge parents, husband, and mawla. These are accepted because such acknowledgments are natural and unlikely to be fraudulent. The usul principle reminds that acknowledgment is valid as long as there is no suspicion of harming others.

Key Terms
مولى (mawla): A client, freed slave, or ally with whom one has a relationship of inheritance or loyalty.
قرعة (qur'a): Lots, a method of deciding disputed paternity when evidence is equal.

Sulh Among Heirs to Exclude One
Ruling on heirs paying one heir to exclude him from the estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on heirs paying one heir to exclude him from the estate and explain the proof from the companion's saying.

Core Idea
Sulh among heirs to exclude one is valid regardless of the amount paid, even if less than the share.

When multiple heirs inherit an estate, sometimes they prefer to buy out one heir's share rather than partition the property. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on such a transaction, which is permissible even if the paid amount is less or more than the actual share. The proof comes from a companion's practice, emphasising the flexibility of sulh in inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is heirs paying one of themselves to exclude him from the estate. The ruling is that it is valid (ja'iz) for real estate and goods.
The text explicitly mentions 'aqar (real estate) and 'urud (goods), implying that the ruling applies to non-liquid assets.
2
Understand the condition regarding the amount
The validity does not depend on the amount being equal to the heir's share; it can be little or much.
This is a departure from the general principle of justice in division but is allowed because the heir consents.
3
Recognise the nature of the transaction
This is a sulh (amicable settlement) contract, not a sale or gift. The heirs negotiate a mutual agreement to transfer the heir's share.
Sulh is a broad category in Hanafi fiqh, allowing flexibility in settling disputes and divisions.
4
Examine the proof (dalil)
The proof cited is a saying of a companion (Qawl al-Sahabi), specifically from Umar ibn Abi Salama from his father, indicating that such practice was known and approved.
Qawl al-Sahabi is a secondary source of evidence in Hanafi usul, especially when there is no conflicting text.

Commentary
This passage addresses a common scenario in inheritance: some heirs wish to buy out another heir's share by giving him cash or property. The ruling states that this is valid for real estate (aqar) and goods ('urud), regardless of whether the amount paid is little or much, as long as the excluded heir agrees. The basis is that this is a sulh contract, and the heirs are free to negotiate the price. The proof is from a companion (Umar ibn Abi Salama from his father), though the full narration is not provided here. This ruling facilitates the practical division of estates without forced sale.

Key Terms
تَرِكَة (tarika): The estate left by a deceased person, including all assets and liabilities.
أَخْرَجُوا أَحَدَهُمْ (akhraju ahadahum): Excluding one of the heirs, i.e., buying out his share from the estate through a sulh.

Determining Gender of a Khuntha – The Urination Test and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on gender determination for a khuntha with intra-Hanafi dispute on quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi method for determining the gender of a khuntha using urination, explain the point of disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding consideration of quantity (kathra), and apply the ruling to a practical case.

Core Idea
For a hermaphrodite, gender is determined by precedence of urine flow; when equal, Abu Hanifa ignores quantity, the Sahibayn consider it.

This passage from al-Quduri presents the legal test for identifying the gender of a hermaphrodite (khuntha), a critical issue for inheritance (faraid) and other personal status rulings. The matn outlines the urination test, and the commentary provides supporting narrations while also highlighting a fine-grained disagreement within the Hanafi school: when urine flows from both organs equally in precedence, does the quantity (kathra) matter? Abu Hanifa says no, while his two disciples differ. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to weigh evidence and identify the mufta bihi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine what the passage is ruling upon: the method for assigning gender to a child born with both male and female genitalia (khuntha).
2
State the Hanafi position
The matn gives a clear rule: if urine flows from the penis, the child is male; from the vulva, female; if from both, precedence (sabq) decides.
3
Identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement
When both streams start at exactly the same time, do we consider the volume of urine from each? The matn states 'no consideration to quantity according to Abu Hanifa.' The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) are reported to consider quantity.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
4
Examine the proofs
The passage cites several narrations: an athar from Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib (who only mentions precedence), a hadith from Ibn Abbas (weak chain), and an athar from Ali. Abu Hanifa relies on the first, which ignores volume.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa's view, if precedence is equal, the gender remains indeterminate until another sign appears. Under the Sahibayn's view, the gender would be decided by the organ producing more urine.

Commentary
This passage defines a hermaphrodite (khuntha) as a child born with both male and female organs. The matn gives a method for determining gender: if urine flows from the penis, it is male; from the vulva, female; if from both, look at which stream precedes; if equal in precedence, Abu Hanifa says quantity (how much from each) is not considered. The commentary provides various proofs: a narration from Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib about using the faster stream, a hadith from Ibn Abbas (though the chain includes a weak narrator, Muhammad ibn al-Sa'ib al-Kalbi), and an athar from Ali. The commentary also includes a misplaced hadith about returning lost property (luqata). The principles at the end state that a khuntha is one whose gender is unknown, and the stronger sign determines the ruling.

Key Terms
الخُنْثَى (al-khuntha): A person born with both male and female genitalia.
السَّبْق (al-sabq): The precedence of urine flow from one organ over the other.
الْكَفْرَة (al-kathra?): Probably 'al-kathra' meaning quantity or abundance of urine from each organ.

Inheritance of a Khuntha with a Son
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khuntha's share with a son

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance shares of a khuntha when inheriting with a son according to both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the operative ruling (mufta bihi) for a khuntha mushkil.

Core Idea
The khuntha's share is either 1/3 (Abu Hanifa) or 5/12 (Sahibayn) — a cautious compromise prevails in fatwa.

When a person dies leaving a son and a khuntha (hermaphrodite of ambiguous gender), the Hanafi school has two internal opinions on how to allocate the estate. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each position and guides you through the calculations so you can apply the correct ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
State the heirs and the core issue
Identify the two heirs: son and khuntha. The dispute is whether the khuntha is treated as a daughter or receives a compromise share due to ambiguity.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa treats the khuntha as a daughter unless signs indicate otherwise, so the son gets two shares and the khuntha gets one (ratio 2:1). Calculate the fractions: son 2/3, khuntha 1/3.
This is based on the default rule in the Quran (son gets twice a daughter's share).
3
Understand the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad give the khuntha half of a male's share (1/2) and half of a female's share (1/3), averaging to 5/12. The son gets the remainder: 7/12. Base is 12.
This is a precautionary principle (ihtiyat) to avoid incorrectly assigning the share of the wrong gender.
4

Write both distributions and note the difference: Abu Hanifa uses base 3, Sahibayn use base 12. The Sahibayn's method gives the khuntha a larger share (5/12 vs 1/3 = 4/12).
The difference is 1/12 of the estate.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
For a khuntha mushkil (no clear signs), the Sahibayn's view is preferred because it is more cautious. If signs appear, the khuntha is treated as that gender and normal inheritance applies.
Al-Quduri presents both, but later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Hidayah) favor the Sahibayn.

Presumption of Death After 120 Years: The Missing Person's Legal Status
States the rule that a missing person is presumed dead after 120 years from birth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for when a missing person (mafqud) is presumed dead, distinguish between inheritance from him and inheritance by him, and cite the evidence from the text.

Core Idea
A missing person is presumed alive for his own estate until age 120, but dead for inheriting from others.

The fate of a missing person can complicate inheritance and marriage. While some early scholars used a shorter four-year period from the wife's complaint, the Hanafi school sets a fixed maximum age of 120 years from birth as the point at which a judge may rule death. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its rationale, and the practical consequences for heirs and the wife.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Locate the condition in the text: when the missing person completes 120 years from birth. This triggers the judge's declaration of death.
The 120-year mark is calculated from birth, not from disappearance.
2
Identify the consequences
Three effects: the wife observes 'idda, the estate is divided among heirs alive at that time, and those who died earlier do not inherit from him.
The estate is divided only once, at the moment of presumed death.
3
Understand the dual presumption
For his own estate, he is considered alive until 120 years (so no one inherits earlier). For inheriting from others, he is considered dead from the time of disappearance (so he cannot inherit from someone who died during his absence).
This asymmetry prevents delays in other estates while respecting his own estate.
4
Locate the evidence
The text cites athar (narrations) from Ali (presumption of death only after definite news), and from Umar and Uthman (four-year period). The Hanafi school chose 120 years based on ijtihad and the principle of certainty.
The evidence is used to support the ruling, though the 120-year limit is a Hanafi construction.
5
Note the cross-madhhab disagreement
Some early scholars (Umar, Uthman) and later Maliki school used a four-year period from the wife's complaint. The Hanafi school rejects this as arbitrary and adopts 120 years as a fixed maximum age.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf on the method of determining death.
