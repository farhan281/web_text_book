Scope of Agency: General Rule and Exception for Hudud and Qisas
Ruling on the scope of agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that any valid personal act may be delegated, and identify the specific exception for executing hudud and qisas when the principal is absent.

Core Idea
Any act you may do personally, you may delegate—except executing hudud and qisas in the principal's absence.

Wakalah (agency) is a flexible contract in Hanafi fiqh, allowing delegation of many legal acts. This lesson covers the foundational rule from al-Quduri: everything you can do yourself, you can appoint an agent to do. However, there is a critical exception: the execution of hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation) cannot be delegated when the principal is absent from the court. Understanding this exception illuminates the Hanafi emphasis on personal presence in matters involving rights of Allah (huquq Allah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Find the statement that every contract a person may validly make for himself may be delegated to an agent. This is the foundational principle of wakalah.
The rule is 'kullu aqdin jaza an ya'qidahu al-insanu linafsihi jaza an yuwakkala bihi'.
2
Note the permissible types of delegation
The text explicitly includes: litigation (khushumat) concerning any rights, proving those rights, and execution (istifa) of rights. Understand that these cover most legal claims and enforcement actions.
3
Spot the exception
Look for 'illa fi al-hudud wa al-qisas' – except for hudud and qisas. Agency is not valid for their execution.
4
Identify the condition of the exception
The text adds 'ma'a ghaibat al-muwakkil 'an al-majlis' – when the principal is absent from the court. If the principal is present, agency may be valid. Focus on this condition.
Presence of the principal is key; the exception applies only when he is absent.
5
Reflect on the wisdom
Hudud are rights of Allah (huquq Allah) requiring strict procedural conditions; qisas involves personal rights but execution demands the victim or his heir's presence. The Hanafi school thus requires personal attendance for these capital penalties.
Other schools may differ; this ruling is specific to Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri begins the Book of Agency with a general rule: any transaction that a person may validly execute for himself may also be delegated to an agent. This includes contracts, sales, purchases, etc. Specifically, agency is permitted for litigation (khushumat) to claim or defend rights, for establishing proof, and for executing rights (e.g., taking possession, collecting debts). However, there is an important exception: agency is not valid for the execution of hudud (e.g., lashing for adultery, amputation for theft) and qisas (retaliation for murder or injury) if the principal is not present at the court session. The reason is that these punishments are considered to be rights of Allah that require personal attendance or specific conditions. The proofs include the Quranic verse where the People of the Cave sent one with silver to buy food, indicating delegation; the hadith where the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) sent Ali to distribute camels; and several narrations showing that Companions appointed agents for lawsuits. The Urdu usul note reiterates the general principle that agency is permissible for any act one can do oneself.

Key Terms
وَكَالَة (wakālah): A contract whereby one person appoints another to perform a legal act on his behalf, which the appointer is capable of doing himself.
خُصُومَة (khuṣūmah): Litigation or dispute; in this context, it refers to legal proceedings in court to establish or defend rights.
حَدّ / خُدُود (ḥadd / ḥudūd): Fixed punishments prescribed by Islamic law (e.g., for theft, fornication, false accusation, drinking alcohol); these are rights of Allah (huquq Allah) that cannot be delegated for execution in the principal's absence.
قِصَاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliation for murder or bodily harm; the right of the victim or his heirs to demand equal punishment.

The Scope of Sulh: Permissibility and Limits
States the scope of sulh: permissible in property, benefits, injury; not in hudud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the types of claims in which sulh is permissible according to the Hanafi school and explain why sulh is invalid in claims involving hadd punishments.

Core Idea
Sulh is valid in property, benefits, and injury claims but invalid in hudud because they are rights of Allah.

This lesson examines a foundational ruling from al-Quduri regarding the permissibility of sulh (amicable settlement). The text clearly delineates where sulh is acceptable and where it is not, with particular attention to the distinction between civil claims and divinely mandated punishments. By studying the proofs, you will understand why sulh in hudud is prohibited and how the school applies similar reasoning to marriage claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is sulh (amicable settlement). State the general permissibility ruling and then the specific exclusions.
The text states it is permissible in property, benefits, and jinaayat, but not in hudud.
2
Locate the dalil
Identify the two proofs cited: the Quranic verse on qisas (2:178) and the hadith of al-Rubayyi'. Understand how each supports the ruling.
The verse shows that financial compensation can replace retaliation; the hadith shows actual practice.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how each proof indicates permissibility. For the verse, the allowance of diyah demonstrates that injury claims can be settled monetarily. For the hadith, the parties' forgiveness after qisas confirms sulh in jinaayat.
The hadith also serves as evidence for sulh in intentional injury.
4
Analyse the marriage sulh scenarios
Apply the general rule to the two hypotheticals: why is the man's claim against a woman valid (like khul'), but the woman's claim against a man invalid?
The asymmetry arises because the man's claim resembles khul' (divorce initiated by wife with compensation), whereas the woman's claim does not mirror any recognised contract.
5
Identify the exception: hudud
Note that hadd punishments are excluded entirely. The hadith of the Makhzumiyyah woman reinforces that no intercession or sulh is allowed once a hadd case reaches the ruler.
Hadd are rights of Allah (huquq Allah) that cannot be waived by individuals.

Commentary
This passage continues the matn of al-Quduri on the scope of sulh. The ruling states that sulh is valid in claims involving property, benefits (usufruct), and both intentional and unintentional injuries (jinaayat), but is not permissible in claims for hadd (fixed corporal punishments). The reason is that hadd punishments are rights of Allah that cannot be compromised; sulh in such cases would undermine the enforcement of divine limits. Two examples are given: (1) If a man claims a woman is his wife and she denies, a sulh where she pays him to drop the claim is valid because it resembles khul' (divorce by redemption). (2) The reverse—a woman claiming marriage and a man paying her to drop it—is invalid because the man's claim is not like khul'; he has no right to claim marriage from her in that manner. The proofs include Quran 2:178, which allows forgoing retaliation in murder cases for compensation, showing that financial settlement replaces retaliation. The hadith of al-Rubayyi' shows that even after a crime, forgiveness and compensation are possible. The hadith of the Makhzumiyyah woman emphasizes that hadd punishments cannot be compromised through intercession or settlement. The Urdu principles at the end summarize: after a sulh, its legal category is determined by the closest match (e.g., sale, lease, khul'), and in qisas, the victim may accept property in lieu of retaliation.

Key Terms
جِنَايَة (jināyah): A crime causing bodily harm, including both intentional and unintentional acts.
حَدّ (ḥadd): A fixed punishment prescribed by Allah for specific crimes (e.g., theft, zina, drinking alcohol).
خُلْع (khulʿ): Divorce initiated by the wife in return for compensation paid to the husband.
قِصَاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliation in kind for bodily harm, permissible when not waived.
