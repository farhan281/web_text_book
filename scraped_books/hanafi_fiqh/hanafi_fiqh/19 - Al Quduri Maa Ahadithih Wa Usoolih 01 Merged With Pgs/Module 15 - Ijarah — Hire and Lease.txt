Definition and Conditions of Ijarah
Definition of ijarah with conditions of validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ijarah according to Hanafi fiqh and state its two essential conditions for validity.

Core Idea
Ijarah is a contract on usufruct, not on the asset itself — both the usufruct and the wage must be known to avoid uncertainty.

This lesson introduces the contract of ijarah (hire/lease), a fundamental transaction in Hanafi fiqh. Unlike a sale where the substance ('ayn) is exchanged, ijarah deals with benefits (manafi'). Al-Quduri's concise definition captures the essence and the prerequisites for validity. Understanding these conditions is crucial to distinguish valid from invalid hire contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the essential elements of the definition
Break down the definition: 'aqd 'ala al-manafi' bi-'iwad. Note that ijarah is a contract ('aqd), its subject is usufruct (manafi'), and it requires consideration ('iwad).
This distinguishes ijarah from sale, which is on a corporeal substance.
2
State the two conditions for validity
The text explicitly says: 'لا يصح حتى تكون المنافع معلومة والأجرة معلومة' — both the usufruct and the wage must be known (ma'lum) at the time of contract to avoid gharar.
Unknown usufruct or wage renders the contract invalid (fasid).
3
Apply the analogy with sale
The last line teaches that any item valid as a price (thaman) in sale is valid as a wage (ujrah) in ijarah. This includes cash, goods, or services, provided they are permissible.
This extends the rules of valid consideration from sale to ijarah.
4
Distinguish ijarah from sale
Reflect: ijarah transfers the benefit (manfa'ah), not ownership of the asset. The asset itself remains with the owner. This affects liability and termination rules.
Key difference: in ijarah, the risk of destruction of the asset is on the owner, not the hirer.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri defines the contract of ijarah (hire/lease) in Hanafi fiqh. It states that ijarah is a contract ('aqd) on manafi' (usufruct/benefits) in exchange for a 'iwad (consideration/wage). The definition distinguishes ijarah from sale: sale is on the substance ('ayn), ijarah is on benefits. The contract requires both the usufruct and the wage to be known (ma'lumah) to avoid gharar (uncertainty). Additionally, the text draws an analogy with sale: anything that can serve as a price (thaman) in a valid sale can serve as a wage (ujrah) in ijarah. This establishes the permissibility of using various types of currency or goods as payment. The proofs from Quran and Sunnah are provided in the footnotes, including the story of Musa (peace be upon him) hiring himself to Shu'ayb, the hadith of the Prophet warning against withholding an employee's wage, and other ahadith emphasizing fair payment.

Key Terms
إجارة (ijarah): A contract on known benefits (manafi') for a known consideration ('iwad).
منافع (manafi'): Usufruct or benefits derived from an asset or labor.
عوض ('iwad): Consideration, the payment or compensation in exchange for the usufruct.
أجرة (ujrah): The wage or rent paid in ijarah.

The Definition of Ijarah and Its Evidences
Definition of ijarah with Quranic and Hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijarah, identify its core conditions (known benefit and known rent), and cite the Quranic and Hadith proofs that establish its permissibility.

Core Idea
Ijarah is a contract on known benefits for known compensation—valid by Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma.

This lesson opens the Book of Ijarah in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The author defines ijarah as a contract on benefits (manafi') in exchange for compensation ('iwad). He then lays down two essential conditions: both the benefit and the rent must be known at the time of contract to avoid gharar (uncertainty). The rest of the passage marshals multiple proofs from the Quran and Sunnah. Mastering this definition and its textual foundation is critical before studying the detailed rulings of ijarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and its components
Break down al-Quduri's definition: ijarah is a contract ('aqd) on benefits (manafi'), not the substance ('ayn), in exchange for compensation ('iwad). Note how this differs from sale (bay'), which is a contract on the substance itself.
The term 'manafi'' is plural of manfa'ah meaning usufruct or service.
2
Extract the conditions of validity
The text states: 'ولا يصح حتى تكون المنافع معلومة، والأجرة معلومة' — the contract is not valid unless both the benefits and the rent are known. This condition prevents gharar (uncertainty), which invalidates contracts.
Knowledge is required in both the subject matter and the compensation.
3
Examine the Quranic evidence
The verse from Surah al-Qasas (28:27) recounts Musa (AS) agreeing to serve Shu'ayb (AS) for eight years in exchange for marriage. The phrase 'على أن تأجرني' means 'on condition that you hire yourself to me', showing the permissibility of hiring services for a known duration and compensation.
This is a primary textual proof for the validity of ijarah.
4
Examine the Hadith evidence
The passage cites four hadiths: (1) Hadith qudsi: Allah will oppose three on Judgment Day, including one who hires but does not pay. (2) Command to pay wages before sweat dries. (3) The Prophet paid a cupper with food. (4) The hadith on salam is used by analogy: ijarah also requires specification of measure, weight, and term to avoid gharar.
These hadiths establish both the validity and ethical obligations in ijarah.
5
Synthesize the proofs
The combination of Quran, multiple authentic hadiths, and the absence of valid opposition establishes ijarah as a permissible contract by default. The Hanafi school relies on these proofs to derive detailed rulings throughout the chapter.
The authority here is Zahir al-Riwayah — the six core books of Imam Muhammad.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Ijarah (hire/lease) from al-Quduri's matn. The author defines ijarah as a contract on benefits (manafi') in exchange for compensation ('iwad). This distinguishes it from sale (bay'), which is a contract on the substance ('ayn) of a commodity. Ijarah is valid only if both the benefit and the rent are known (ma'lumah) at the time of contract, to prevent gharar (uncertainty). Whatever can serve as a price (thaman) in a sale can also serve as rent (ujrah) in ijarah—thus gold, silver, currency, and any fungible or non-fungible goods that are valid as consideration in a sale are valid as rent. The author then provides multiple evidences: first, the Quranic verse from Surah al-Qasas where Musa (AS) agrees to serve Shu'ayb (AS) for eight years in exchange for marriage; second, the hadith qudsi listing three types of people Allah will oppose, including an employer who does not pay wages; third, the hadith emphasizing prompt payment of wages; fourth, the hadith of the cupper showing that paying for services (hijamah) with food (sa' or mudd) is permissible; fifth, the hadith about salam (advance payment) which establishes that beneficial contracts require specification of measure, weight, and term—this is analogically applied to ijarah. The overall proof is that ijarah is a valid contract established by Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma'.

Key Terms
إجارة (ijarah): A contract on benefits (manafi') in exchange for compensation ('iwad).
منفعة (manfa'ah): The usufruct or benefit derived from an asset or service, as opposed to the substance ('ayn).
أجرة (ujrah): The compensation (rent or wage) paid in exchange for the benefit in an ijarah contract.
عوض ('iwad): Consideration or compensation given in exchange for something in a contract.

Modes of Specifying Benefits in Ijarah
Defines how benefits become known and outlines permissible uses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three methods by which benefits become known in an ijarah contract and apply the Hanafi rule restricting certain professions in rented premises.

Core Idea
Benefits in ijarah must be known by duration, specification, or designation; unknown benefits void the contract.

In this passage, al-Quduri explains how the benefit (manfa'ah) in an ijarah contract must be clearly defined to avoid gharar (uncertainty). He outlines three methods to make the benefit known and then discusses the permissible and prohibited uses of rented houses, shops, and land. The rulings are supported by hadith and the practice of a Tabi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three methods of specifying benefits
Read the passage and list the three ways benefits become known: by duration (muddah), by specification (tasmiyah), and by designation (ta'yin/indication).
Each method corresponds to a different type of ijarah.
2
Analyze each method with examples
For each method, note the examples given: duration for renting houses/land, specification for hiring a worker or animal to perform a specific task, designation for pointing to a particular object to be transported.
Understand how each example avoids uncertainty.
3
Apply the rule on permissible uses
Identify the general rule: a lessee may use a rented house/shop for any purpose unless it causes damage. Then list the three explicitly prohibited professions (blacksmith, fuller, miller).
These are prohibited because they damage the structure.
4
Recognize the rights of the agricultural lessee
Note that when land is rented for cultivation, the lessee automatically has the right to irrigation (shurb) and pathway (tariq).
These are necessary for the land's benefit.
5
Connect the evidence to the rules
Review the cited hadith and athar: the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij supports ijarah for cash, the Khaybar hadith supports specification and land rights, and the athar of al-Hasan supports designation.
These evidences establish the validity of the three methods.

Commentary
This passage details how the benefits (manafi') in an ijarah contract become known (ma'lumah) to avoid gharar. There are three methods: (1) By duration (muddah): for contracts like renting a house for dwelling or land for cultivation—any fixed period suffices, whether short or long. (2) By specification (tasmiyah): when the task is clearly described, such as dyeing a specific cloth, tailoring it, or transporting a known amount to a known destination. (3) By designation (ta'yin) and indication: pointing to a specific object, e.g., 'carry this (particular) food to that place.' Then the text discusses renting houses and shops: the lessee may use them for any lawful purpose without specifying, except for blacksmithing, fulling (laundering), and milling, because these activities cause damage to the property (walls, floors). Renting land for cultivation is valid, and the lessee automatically gets the rights of irrigation (shurb) and pathway (tariq) as necessary for the land's benefit. Various hadith and a practice of a Tabi'i (al-Hasan al-Basri) are cited as evidence: (a) the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij shows that renting land for cash (gold/silver) is permissible, though renting for a share of produce (muzara'ah) was originally prohibited to avoid uncertainty—this is the basis for the permissibility of ijarah with fixed rent. (b) The Khaybar transaction proves that contracting for a share of produce is valid in musaqah and muzara'ah, but that is a separate contract from ijarah. (c) The story of al-Hasan shows that if a person hires a donkey by custom without explicit terms, the customary compensation (half dirham) is binding, supporting designation as a valid method of specification. (d) The Khaybar hadith also supports the lessee's right to water and pathway.

Key Terms
مدة (muddah): A fixed duration of time specified in the ijarah contract, e.g., one month, one year.
تسمية (tasmiyah): Specifying the exact nature of the work or service, e.g., dyeing a cloth, carrying a known weight.
تعيين (ta'yin): Designating a specific object by pointing, e.g., 'this food' or 'this donkey'.
حداد (haddad): A blacksmith; his work is prohibited in a rented house because it damages walls via fire and hammering.
شرب (shurb): The right to water for irrigation of agricultural land.

Ways Usufruct Becomes Known in Ijarah Contracts
Al-Quduri lists three ways the usufruct becomes known.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three methods by which the usufruct becomes known in an ijarah contract and apply them to classify a given contract as valid or invalid.

Core Idea
The usufruct in ijarah must be known — by duration, specification, or pointing — otherwise the contract is void.

In ijarah, the subject matter is not the physical object but its usufruct (manafi'). Since usufructs are intangible, the law requires them to be made known to both parties to avoid uncertainty (gharar). Al-Quduri outlines three recognized methods: duration, specification, and physical indication. Understanding these will help you assess the validity of any rental or hire contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three methods
List the three ways the usufruct becomes known: (1) by specifying a duration (muddah), (2) by describing the work or service (tasmiyah), and (3) by physically pointing to the subject (ta'yin wa isharah).
Each method corresponds to a type of ijarah: e.g., renting a house for a month, hiring a tailor to stitch a specific garment, or hiring someone to move 'this' pile of wheat.
2
Understand the ruling of validity
The contract is valid (sahih) if the usufruct is known by any of these methods. If the usufruct remains unknown, the contract is fasid (void due to uncertainty).
The Hanafi school is relatively flexible; other schools may require more specification.
3
Apply to the exceptions
Even when the usufruct is known, certain activities in a rented house may be prohibited by default (e.g., blacksmithing, fulling, grinding) because they damage the property. The lessee must explicitly agree to such uses.
For land, the crop must be specified unless the lessee reserves the right to choose ('whatever I wish').

Conditions for Leasing Land and Open Ground
States the ruling and proofs for leasing land with specification of crop and open ground for building or planting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid land lease contract and explain the Hanafi proof for its permissibility.

Core Idea
A land lease requires specifying the crop or giving discretion; the Prophet allowed it but preferred giving land freely.

Leasing land for cultivation or building is a common transaction, but its validity depends on clarity of the subject matter. In this passage, Al-Quduri lays down the essential condition that the lessee must specify the crop or reserve the right to choose, and he extends the ruling to open ground for building or planting. The Hanafi school supports this with the Prophet's hadith and Ibn Abbas's practice, countering the Shafi'i position that prohibits such leases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core condition
The contract is not valid (la yajuz) until the lessee specifies what he will plant, or says 'on condition that I plant whatever I wish.' This eliminates uncertainty (gharar) in the subject matter.
The condition applies only to leasing land for cultivation; for open ground, the contract is permissible without specifying the building or tree type.
2
Examine the scope of permissibility
The passage then states that hiring open ground (saha) for building or planting trees is permissible. Also, leasing agricultural land includes ancillary rights to water and pathway for the lessee.
These ancillary rights are implicit in the contract to make the lease beneficial.
3
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The first proof is the hadith where the Prophet said giving land as a gift is better than taking a known fee, implying the fee is permissible but less virtuous. The second proof is Ibn Abbas’s statement explicitly permitting leasing white land year to year.
The Hanafis interpret the hadith of prohibiting khabar (sharecropping) as referring to a different arrangement, not absolute prohibition.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i held that leasing land for cultivation is invalid based on a hadith prohibiting khabar al-ard. The Hanafi response is that this hadith is either abrogated or recommendatory, and the explicit permission in other narrations takes precedence.
This difference is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi position is mufta bihi.

Obligation to Remove Structures After Lease Expiry and Owner's Options
Ruling on removal of structures and plants after lease ends, and owner's option to pay value or keep them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default obligation of the lessee after lease expiry and explain the two options available to the landowner under Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The lessee must remove structures after lease ends; the owner may pay value or keep them.

When a lease of land for building or planting expires, the lessee's right to use the land ends. The default rule is restoration of the land to its original empty state. However, the landowner has the option to compensate the lessee for the structures or agree to leave them in place, creating a co-ownership arrangement. This lesson examines the ruling and its evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Obligation
Read the first part of the passage: 'When the term expires, it is incumbent upon him to remove that and to deliver it empty.' The lessee's default duty is removal (qal') of all structures and trees.
This is wajib (obligatory) because it is a contractual duty.
2
Analyze the Owner's Options
The passage states two exceptions: (1) the owner pays the value of the items as if uprooted (qimah maqlū'an) and takes ownership, or (2) the owner agrees to leave the items, resulting in shared ownership (building belongs to lessee, land to owner).
The owner's choice is a right; he cannot be forced to accept either option.
3
Understand the Legal Basis
The hadith 'Muslims are bound by their conditions' validates any mutual agreement. The hadith 'Property is not lawful except with consent' protects the lessee's property rights, requiring compensation if the owner retains the structures.
These hadith are ahad (single-chain) but accepted in Hanafi fiqh for establishing rules of contracts.
4
Apply to a Scenario
Consider a farmer who leases land for five years to plant an orchard. At lease end, he must uproot the trees unless the landowner chooses to pay their scrap value or agrees to let them remain. In the latter case, the trees remain the farmer's property but the land is the owner's.
The valuation for uprooted value is lower than standing value because of removal costs.

A hire contract binds use; specifying a user restricts it; deviation incurs liability for damage—even an extra rider on an animal.

This passage addresses practical issues in hire contracts for movables like animals and garments. The core distinction is between unrestricted hire, where the hirer may let anyone use the item, and restricted hire, where a specific user is named. The rulings also differentiate between movables (which vary with the user) and real estate (which does not). Additionally, taking an extra rider on a hired animal triggers specific liability. Understanding these rules is essential for proper application of ijara in daily transactions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of ajīr (worker) and state the Hanafi ruling on the liability of the common worker for goods in his possession, including the intra-madhhab disagreement.

Core Idea
The common worker's liability divides the Hanafi school: trust vs. guarantee.

Not all workers are alike under Hanafi law. This passage splits them into two categories – the common worker who serves multiple clients and the private worker hired exclusively. The key legal question is whether the common worker is liable for accidental loss of goods. Our source presents two rival positions within the school, along with the settled rule for direct work-related damage. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to navigate this disagreement and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of worker
Read the first line to find the two categories: ajīr mushtarak (common) and ajīr khāṣṣ (private). The common worker is defined as one who is not entitled to wages until he completes the work (like a washer or dyer).
The private worker is not further detailed here, but contrasts with the common worker in terms of being hired for a fixed period.
2
Locate the key dispute on liability
The passage states that according to Abu Hanifa and Zufar, the goods in the common worker's hand are a trust (amānah) – no liability without negligence. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, they are guaranteed (maḍmūn) – the worker is liable for loss or damage.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; both sides are from the school's founders.

Understand the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
Later Hanafi authorities (like Al-Hidayah) adopt the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad – that the common worker is liable – as a precaution and to protect people's property.
This is the position relied upon in fatwa.

The second half of the passage states that if the worker directly causes damage through his work (tearing, slipping, rope breaking, ship sinking), he is liable. However, this liability does not extend to human life – he is not liable for people who drown or fall.
This exception is unanimous among the Hanafis.
5
Apply the rule to a practical scenario
Suppose a dyer (common worker) receives cloth and it is accidentally burned in a fire not caused by his work. Under Abu Hanifa, he is not liable; under the mufta bihi, he is liable unless he proves a cause beyond his control. But if the cloth tears because of his vigorous beating, he is liable in all views.
The key is whether damage arises from the work itself or from external factors.

Types of Hired Workers and the Liability of the Common Ajir
Classification of hired workers and intra-Hanafi dispute on liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a common hired worker (ajir mushtarak) and a specific hired worker (ajir khas), and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability of the common ajir for goods in his custody.

Core Idea
The common ajir's possession is a trust; he is not liable for loss without negligence.

This lesson explores the classification of hired workers in Hanafi fiqh, focusing on the common ajir who works for multiple employers. A key dispute arises between Abu Hanifa and Zufar (who deem goods as amanah, so no liability) and Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (who hold the ajir liable). We will examine the proofs and the practical consequence of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Define the two types of ajir
First, understand the definitions: The common ajir (ajir mushtarak) works for several people and earns wage only after doing the work. The specific ajir (ajir khas) works exclusively for one employer and earns wage by making himself available. Examples: fuller (qassar) and dyer (sabbah) are common ajirs.
This classification affects liability.
2
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa & Zufar)
For the common ajir, the goods in his hands are a trust (amanah). If they perish without his fault, he is not liable. This is the view of Abu Hanifa and Zufar.
The proof is the action of 'Ali (RA) who did not hold the common ajir liable.
3
Identify the opposing view (Sahibayn)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the common ajir is liable (madhmun) for goods that perish in his hands.
Their proof would be that the ajir is entrusted with work and should guarantee the goods, like other contractors.
4
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is clear: if goods are lost, according to Abu Hanifa the ajir pays nothing; according to the Sahibayn he compensates the owner.
This difference is significant for daily transactions.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Although not explicitly stated in the passage, the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school (Zahir al-Riwayah) is that of Abu Hanifa and Zufar – no liability. The commentary includes both views, but the established corpus leans towards amanah.
Later Hanafi jurists like al-Marghinani reinforce this.

Liability for Property Damage Caused by a Hired Worker
Ruling on liability for property damage with proof from 'Umar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a hired worker is liable for property damaged by his work, and identify the proof from the action of 'Umar (RA).

Core Idea
A hired worker compensates for property damaged by his work, even without negligence.

This lesson focuses on the Hanafi rule that a hired worker (ajir) must compensate for property damage that occurs as a direct result of his work, even if he did not intend harm. The text gives examples like a fuller tearing cloth, a porter slipping, a muleteer's rope breaking, and a ship sinking from overloading. The key evidence is the practice of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, who held a dyer liable for damage. However, note that this liability applies only to property—human life is exempt, as covered in the next lesson.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the general ruling
Identify the hired worker's activity (e.g., dyer, porter, muleteer, boatman) and the ruling that damage to property caused by that work is liable (madhmun).
The Arabic phrase 'مَضْمُونٌ' means the worker must financially compensate.
2
Examine the examples given
Study the specific examples: tearing cloth from pounding, porter slipping, rope breaking, ship sinking from overloading. These illustrate that the damage is incidental to the work itself, not caused by negligence or intent.
The examples help clarify the scope of liability.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is from 'Umar's action: he held a dyer liable for damage caused by his work. This is a qawl sahabi (statement of a Companion), which is a source of law in Hanafi usul.
The reference is Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq.
4
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The proof shows that the worker is liable even without negligence, because the damage arose from the work itself. This establishes the principle that the ajir is responsible for incidental harm to property.
This is a straightforward application of the general rule.

Exception: No Liability for Harm to Human Life
Ruling on exemption of human life from worker's liability, with proofs from Shurayh and 'Amir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi exception that a hired worker is not liable for harm to human life caused incidentally by his work, and cite the proofs from Shurayh and 'Amir.

Core Idea
Human life is sacred; incidental harm to life does not trigger financial liability.

This lesson covers the exception to the general rule of worker liability: harm to human beings. Even if a boatman's overloading causes a ship to sink and passengers drown, or a camel driver's animal causes a fall, the worker is not liable for the human life. This is because humans are not property, and liability for life only arises from intentional killing or direct causation. The proofs come from the fatwas of Shurayh and 'Amir, who explicitly ruled no liability for drowning or burning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exception
The text states 'except that he is not liable for human beings'. This is an explicit exclusion from the general liability rule.
Note the phrase 'إِلَّا أَنَّهُ لَا يَضْمَنُ بِهِ بَنِي آدَمَ'.
2
Understand the scenarios
The scenarios involve drowning in a ship or falling from an animal. These are incidental to the worker's activity (overloading, driving).
The harm is not intentional, but a result of the work.
3
Examine the proofs
The proofs are statements of Shurayh and 'Amir: Shurayh did not hold the boatman liable for drowning or burning; 'Amir said the camel owner is not liable for drowned passengers.
These are qawl tabi'i (statements of Successors), which are a source of law in Hanafi usul.
4
Derive the principle
Human life is inviolable and cannot be valued as property. Therefore, incidental harm to life does not create a financial obligation (daman) on the worker.
Liability for life is only through diyah or qisas for intentional or quasi-intentional acts.

Liability of the Specific Employee (Ajir Khas) for Property in His Custody
Ruling with proof from the practice of Shurayh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the specific employee (ajir khas) is not liable for property that perishes in his hand or from his work, and identify the exception of transgression.

Core Idea
The specific employee holds property as a trust (amanah) — no liability unless he transgresses.

The specific employee (ajir khas) is distinguished by entitlement to wages merely through availability, not actual work. The passage delivers a critical ruling: such an employee is not liable for property that perishes in his custody or through his work. This is based on the principle that his possession is a trust (amanah), not a guarantee. The proof is cited from the practice of Shurayh, a renowned judge, who held that a hirer is not liable unless he transgresses.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of ajir khas
The specific employee is hired for a fixed period; he becomes entitled to wages by making himself available, even if he does no work. Examples: a servant hired for a month, a shepherd employed for a season.
Contrast with ajir mushtarak (common employee) who is hired for a specific task and entitled only upon completion.
2
Identify the default ruling
The passage states: 'There is no liability on the specific employee for what perishes in his hand nor for what perishes from his work.' This is the default position.
The phrase 'what perishes in his hand' includes items entrusted to him; 'what perishes from his work' includes damage caused by the work itself.
3
Recognize the exception
Liability only arises if the employee transgresses (ta'addi). The proof from Shurayh says the hirer is not liable unless he exceeds the time limit.
Transgression can be exceeding time, boundaries, or acting negligently beyond customary limits.
4
Examine the proof from Shurayh
Shurayh's statement: 'There is no liability on the hirer (mustakri), but if he transgresses and exceeds the time and it perishes, then both the hire and liability apply.' This establishes the principle that liability is tied to transgression.
This is a qawl sahabi (statement of a companion-level figure) used as evidence.
5
Apply to real examples
Apply the rule to a servant handling household items: he is not liable if a glass breaks unless he was negligent or acted beyond his instructions. Similarly, a shepherd is not liable if a sheep dies of natural causes.
The key is to check for any transgression (ta'addi) in the employee's conduct.

Chapter on Void Lease (Ijarah Fasidah): Conditions that Invalidate
General principle and examples of void lease conditions with hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which conditions render a lease contract fasid (void) in the Hanafi school and explain the ruling using the analogy to sale and the hadith proofs.

Core Idea
A lease with a void condition is fasid — defective but correctable.

This passage opens the chapter on void leases. The author establishes the principle: conditions that contradict the Shari'ah or the nature of the contract invalidate the lease, just as they invalidate a sale. Two examples illustrate this: hiring a slave for service with or without a travel stipulation, and hiring a camel for a howdah and two riders. You will learn to recognize such void conditions and the hadith proofs that support this ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
The text begins: 'Lease is invalidated by conditions just as sale is.' This analogy means that any condition that would make a sale fasid also makes a lease fasid.
Recall that in Hanafi fiqh, sale becomes fasid if there is a condition that contradicts its nature, e.g., giving a gift.
2
Analyze the first example: hiring a slave for service
If you hire a slave for service, you cannot travel with him unless you explicitly stipulate that in the contract. Travel is outside the default service scope.
The hadith 'Muslims are bound by their conditions' supports that stipulations must be honored.
3
Analyze the second example: hiring a camel for howdah
Hiring a camel to carry a howdah and two riders to Makkah is permissible. The hirer receives the customary howdah. It is better if the camel driver sees the howdah to avoid dispute.
The proofs include the hadith of Hind and the Quranic verse (4:6) about dealing with reasonableness.
4
Identify the hadith proofs
The author cites hadiths forbidding the price of a dog, prostitute's earnings, fortune-teller's fees, and the stud fee of a stallion. These show that contracts with invalid earnings or uncertain benefits are prohibited, analogically applying to void lease conditions.
The analogy (qiyas) is that if such earnings are invalid in sale, similar conditions in ijarah also invalidate.
5
Note the epistemological status
These proofs are ahad hadiths (zanni al-thubut). The Hanafi school relies on this analogy despite other schools possibly differing.
The ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and the mufta bihi position is that such conditions invalidate the lease.

Ruling on indefinite monthly rental with istihsan

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An indefinite monthly lease is valid only for the first month; occupancy validates subsequent months.

This lesson examines a classical Hanafi ruling from Al-Quduri on Ijarah. When a tenant rents a house at a fixed monthly rate without specifying the total number of months, the contract is initially valid for the first month but corrupt (fasid) for later months due to uncertainty. However, if the tenant actually occupies the property for even an hour of the second month, the contract becomes fully valid for that month, and the landlord cannot evict until the month ends. This unique Hanafi position balances the prohibition of gharar with practical necessity, using the concept of possession (qabd) to cure the defect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the first part of the text: 'وَمَنْ اسْتَأْجَرَ دَارًا كُلَّ شَهْرٍ بِدِرْهَم فَالْعَقْدُ صَحِيحٌ فِي شَهْرٍ وَاحِدٍ فَاسِدٌ فِي بَقِيَّةِ الشُّهُورِ'. Identify the act (hiring a house per month without specifying total months) and the ruling: valid (sahih) for the first month, corrupt (fasid) for the remaining months.

2
Locate the dalil and its application
The source margin cites a hadith from Muslim 1530: the Prophet forbade selling a pile of dates of unknown quantity for a known measure. This establishes that uncertainty (jahalah) in the countervalue is prohibited. By qiyas, uncertainty in rental duration is also prohibited, making the contract fasid for indefinite periods.
The hadith is ahad, so its dalala is zanni, yet it is sufficient for the Hanafi ruling.

Read the exception: 'إِلَّا أَنْ يُسَمِّيَ جُمْلَةَ شُهُورٍ مَعْلُومَةٍ فَإِنْ سَكَنَ سَاعَةً مِنْ الشَّهْرِ الثَّانِي صَحَ الْعَقْدُ فِيهِ'. The contract becomes valid for the second month if the tenant occupies (sakana) even for an hour. This is a form of istihsan: actual possession with the lessor's permission removes the uncertainty and validates the lease for that month.
The lessor cannot evict during that month; the tenant's right to stay is confirmed.

Consider a modern tenant who signs a lease for $500 per month with no fixed end date. Under Hanafi fiqh, the first month's tenancy is valid. If the tenant stays past the first month, the contract becomes valid for the second month, and the landlord cannot ask them to leave mid-month. This protects the tenant's expectation while maintaining the prohibition on contractual uncertainty.
This is distinct from a month-to-month tenancy with notice period, which has an implied duration.

Prohibition of Hiring for Singing and Wailing
Ruling with proofs prohibiting hiring for singing and wailing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring for singing and wailing and identify the three proofs cited by al-Quduri.

Core Idea
Hiring for sinful acts like singing and wailing is invalid ijarah.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses the validity of ijarah contracts where the object is sinful. The ruling is clear: hiring someone to sing or wail is not permissible. Why? Because the subject matter itself is forbidden, making the contract invalid. The author supports this with a hadith, a statement from a Tabi'i, and a Quranic verse. Let's examine each proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Subject
The phrase 'وَلَا يَجُوزُ الِاسْتِنْجَارُ عَلَى الْغِنَاءِ وَالنَّوْحِ' states the prohibition of hiring for singing (ghina') and wailing (nawh). Determine the hukm level (haram) and note that the contract is invalid.
Istinjar is from naajara, meaning to hire. The wage (ujrah) in such contracts is not permissible.
2
Analyze the First Proof (Hadith)
The hadith from Ibn Mas'ud: 'He is not of us who strikes the cheeks, tears the collars, and calls with the calls of Jahiliyyah.' This explicitly condemns wailing at calamities, proving it is a major sin. Since hiring for sin is forbidden, the ijarah is invalid.
The hadith is from Sahih al-Bukhari. Its chain is sound. The condemnation shows the act is haram, thus the contract is fasid/batil.
3
Analyze the Second Proof (Tabi'i Statement)
Ibrahim al-Nakha'i (a prominent Tabi'i) is reported to have disliked the wage of the wailer and female singer. This demonstrates the practice of the Salaf and provides support from early authorities.
Although this is a qawl of a Tabi'i, it is not a primary proof but serves as an additional legal precedent (amal ahl al-madina or qawl sahabi-adjacent).
4
Analyze the Third Proof (Quranic Verse)
The verse from Surah Luqman (31:6) condemns 'buying idle talk to mislead from the way of Allah'. Early mufassirun interpreted 'lahw al-hadith' as singing. Thus, singing for entertainment that distracts from Allah is prohibited.
This verse is general but applied specifically to singing. It strengthens the prohibition of taking payment for such activities.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Leasing an Undivided Share
Intra-Hanafi dispute on leasing undivided share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on leasing an undivided share and apply the mufta bihi view to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Leasing an undivided share is permissible in the Hanafi school despite Abu Hanifa's restriction.

This passage introduces an internal Hanafi disagreement (ikhtilaf) regarding leasing an undivided share of joint property (mushah). Abu Hanifa holds that such a lease is valid only when the lessee is a co-owner, while his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), consider it permissible even to a third party. Understanding this difference is essential for correctly applying Hanafi fiqh in property transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Read the Arabic text carefully. It first states Abu Hanifa's view with an exception, then the Sahibayn's view. Note that the ruling differs based on who the lessee is.
The key phrase is 'إِلَّا مِنْ الشَّرِيكِ' – except from the partner.
2
Parse the exception in Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa's ruling is not absolute: he permits leasing an undivided share specifically to a co-owner (sharik). Identify why the co-owner is treated differently: the partner already has access to the property.
3
Compare the Sahibayn's view
The Sahibayn remove the exception entirely. They consider the lease valid to anyone, relying on the principle that benefits can be delivered through joint access.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
4
Determine the practical consequence
Consider a scenario: A owns an undivided half of a house with B. A wants to lease his share to C (a stranger). Under Abu Hanifa, this is invalid; under the Sahibayn, it is valid. The mufta bihi allows it.
This thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of difference) highlights why the Sahibayn's view is preferred for ease of transactions.

Hiring a Wet Nurse: Payment by Known Wage or Food and Clothing
Ruling with proofs on hiring a wet nurse for known wage or food and clothing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring a wet nurse, identify the two permissible forms of payment, and explain the evidence for each.

Core Idea
Hiring a wet nurse is permissible; payment can be by known wage or by food and clothing, the latter upheld by Abu Hanifa upon prophetic and companion precedent.

This lesson examines a classical Hanafi ruling from al-Quduri on hiring a wet nurse (istinjar al-zuhr). The core ruling is that such hiring is permissible. However, a subtle intra-madhhab difference arises: while all agree on payment with a known wage, Imam Abu Hanifa alone permits payment with food and clothing. The passage provides three proofs: a verse from Surah al-Talaq, the practice of Abu Hurayrah, and the story of Prophet Musa. Understanding this ruling clarifies the role of evidence types and the scope of scholarly disagreement within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hiring a wet nurse (zuhr). The ruling is ja'iz (permissible). Recognize that the permissibility itself is agreed upon across the madhhab.
The term 'istinjar' refers specifically to hiring for breastfeeding services.
2
Distinguish the two payment options
The first option is a known wage (ajrah ma'lumah), which is uncontested. The second option is payment in food and clothing (ta'amiha wa kiswatiha), which is upheld only by Abu Hanifa (at least in this passage).
The source hints at an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: the two students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) may disagree.
3
Analyze the Quranic evidence
The verse Surah al-Talaq 65:6 commands: 'give them their wages.' The imperative (fa'tuhunna) indicates obligation to pay, which implies the permissibility of hiring in the first place.
This proof supports the general permissibility of hiring with a wage, not specifically the food/clothing option.
4
Analyze the hadith evidence
Two hadiths are cited: (1) Abu Hurayrah worked for food and clothing, showing that payment in kind is acceptable. (2) The Prophet Musa hired himself for food and marriage, establishing a prophetic precedent. Both strengthen Abu Hanifa's position.
These are ahad hadith, so their authority is zanni, but they suffice as supporting evidence for a permissible ruling.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi difference
The phrase 'according to Abu Hanifa' implies that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani may not accept the food/clothing option. The mufta bihi in the school is that both forms are permissible, as later tarjih often adopts Abu Hanifa's view.
The practical consequence: a judge or mufti may follow either opinion, but the established fatwa leans towards permissibility.

Rent Obligation in an Invalid Lease: Possession vs. Use
States ruling on rent due upon possession and effect of usurpation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when rent becomes obligatory in an invalid lease (ijarah fasidah) and apply it to scenarios of possession and usurpation.

Core Idea
In a fasid lease, rent is due upon possession — not use — but lost if property is usurped.

This passage from Al-Quduri addresses a key Hanafi rule regarding invalid leases (ijarah fasidah). While the contract is defective, it still produces some legal effects. The central point is that rent becomes obligatory immediately upon the lessee taking possession of the property, regardless of actual use. However, this obligation is contingent on the lessee retaining possession; if the property is taken by a usurper, the rent obligation falls. The ruling is supported by reports from the Successors Ibn Tawus and al-Thawri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Legal Category
Determine what type of contract is being discussed. The passage deals with 'al-ijarah al-fasidah' — an invalid lease. Understand that in Hanafi fiqh, a fasid contract is not completely void but has certain enforceable effects.
Fasid differs from batil; batil produces no legal effects.
2
State the Ruling Clearly
From the matn: 'Wa idha qabada al-musta'jiru al-dara fa'alayhi al-ujrah.' The rent is wajib (obligatory) upon the lessee once he takes possession.
This applies even if he does not reside in the property ('wa in lam yaskunha').
3
Locate the Proof (Dalil)
The author cites two reports from the Successors (Tabi'un): Ibn Tawus and al-Thawri. Ibn Tawus relates that his father would make the hire obligatory when a man set out to Makkah, even if he died before reaching it. Al-Thawri said regarding a hirer who died en route: 'It is according to calculation.'
These are qawl al-tabi'i, a weak source but accepted as supplementary proof.
4
Analyse the Wajh al-Dalalah (How the Proof Supports)
The key phrase from Ibn Tawus is 'yūjibu al-kirā'a' — making rent obligatory. This indicates that possession alone (setting out on the journey) triggers the obligation, not the completion of the journey. Similarly, al-Thawri's statement implies rent is calculated proportionally from the start of possession.
The Hanafi usul principle at the end reiterates: 'Rent begins to be binding merely by taking possession.'
5
Note the Exception: Usurpation
The matn adds: 'Fa in ghasabaha ghasibun min yadihi saqatat al-ujrah.' If a usurper seizes the property from the lessee's hand, the rent falls. This is because the lessee no longer has the benefit of possession.
This shows that continuous possession is necessary for the rent obligation to persist.

Defect and Ruin Dissolve the Invalid Lease
Explains lessee's right to cancel for harmful defect or dissolution due to ruin/water cutoff

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for lease cancellation due to defect and automatic dissolution due to impossibility, and explain the proof from the hadith al-kharaj bi al-daman.

Core Idea
A harmful defect gives the lessee the right to cancel; total impossibility dissolves the lease automatically.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn on hire (ijarah) outlines two situations where an otherwise valid lease can be terminated. The first is a lessee's option to cancel when a defect makes the dwelling harmful. The second is automatic dissolution when the property is ruined or a vital utility (water) is cut off, making performance impossible. The hadith 'profit follows liability' is cited to justify the lessee's right to return the property without penalty.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between two termination scenarios
Note that the passage presents two distinct cases: a lessee's right to cancel (fasakh) for a harmful defect, and automatic dissolution (infisakh) for ruin or water cutoff. Understand the difference: one requires an action by the lessee, the other happens by operation of law.
2
Identify the condition for the lessee's right to cancel
The defect must be harmful to dwelling (yaḍurru bi al-sukan). It is not a minor defect but one that makes dwelling harmful.
3
Analyze the proof from hadith
The hadith 'al-kharaj bi al-daman' is cited. The lessee enjoys the benefit (kharaj) of the property but also bears the risk (daman). If a defect arises, the lessee can return the property and reclaim rent because the risk was with him.
The hadith is sahih and recorded by Abu Dawud.
4
Understand automatic dissolution
The lease dissolves automatically (infasakhat) without any action when the subject matter is destroyed (house ruins) or essential utility (water for mill or estate) is cut off. This is based on impossibility of performance.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some schools require a court order to dissolve for such impossibility, but Hanafis allow automatic dissolution. This difference affects whether the lessee must continue paying rent until court decision.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi due to practicality.

Death and Lease: The Hanafi Distinction (Li-Nafsihi vs Li-Ghayrihi)
Ruling on effect of death on lease contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on the effect of a party's death on a lease, apply the distinction between contracting for oneself and for another, and cite the two hadith proofs.

Core Idea
When a lessee dies, the lease dissolves only if he contracted for himself; if for another, it survives.

This passage from Al-Quduri addresses a practical scenario in lease contracts: what happens if one party dies? The Hanafi school distinguishes based on whether the deceased contracted in his own right (li-nafsihi) or as a representative for another (li-ghayrihi). Two hadith proofs are presented: one indicating that personal deeds cease at death, and another from the Khaybar sharecropping arrangement that survived the Prophet's death because it was for the community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the matn: death dissolves a lease only if the deceased party had contracted for himself (li-nafsihi). If he contracted for another (e.g., as guardian or agent), the lease continues.
The verb 'infasakha' means automatic dissolution, not requiring court action.
2
Examine the first proof (Hadith on cessation of deeds)
The hadith 'When a human dies, his deeds cease except from three...' is used to argue that ongoing contractual obligations for personal benefit also cease, as they are part of one's 'amal'.
This proves that personal leases dissolve at death.
3
Examine the second proof (Khaybar incident)
The Prophet gave Khaybar to the Jews on half-share. After his death, Abu Bakr and Umar continued the arrangement without renewing it, showing that a contract for the community's benefit (others) survives the death of a party.
This supports the exception: leases contracted for others are not dissolved.
4
Apply the distinction
Given a scenario, determine whether the lease was for the deceased's personal benefit or for another. Then state the ruling.
E.g., a guardian leasing for an orphan: the lease continues after the guardian's death.

Commentary
This passage presents a nuanced Hanafi ruling on the effect of death in lease contracts. The matn states the general rule: if a party dies and the lease was contracted for himself (i.e., in his own right), the contract is dissolved (infasakha) upon his death. However, if the lease was contracted for another (e.g., a guardian leasing on behalf of an orphan, or an agent), then death does not dissolve the contract because the party acted as a representative, not a principal. The two hadith proofs: the first from Abu Huraira (the 'three deeds' hadith) is used to imply that only ongoing charities survive death; ordinary contracts like leases for personal benefit cease. The second from Ibn Umar about Khaybar: the Jews cultivated Khaybar on a sharecropping basis (muzara‘ah) with the Prophet. After his death, Abu Bakr and Umar continued and did not renew the contract, indicating that the original contract remained valid because it was for the benefit of the community (a general contract), and thus not dissolved. This indirectly supports the distinction: since the contract was made for the Muslims (a general entity), it survived the Prophet's death. This is an application of the rule.

Option (Khiyar) in Lease Contracts – Validity and Proof
Declares the validity of stipulating an option in a lease contract.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on stipulating an option in a lease contract and explain the proof from hadith and qiyas.

Core Idea
Stipulating an option in lease is valid by analogy to sale, supported by the khiyar hadith.

This passage from Al-Quduri declares that a lessee or lessor may stipulate an option period to confirm or rescind a lease. This ruling may seem surprising because lease is generally a binding contract. The Hanafi school bases this on the established khiyar in sale and extends it by qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The matn states that stipulating khiyar is valid (sahih) in ijara. This is the core legal pronouncement.
Note: 'yasihhu' indicates validity (sihha), not mere permissibility.
2
Primary Proof – Hadith of Khiyar in Sale
The first proof cites the hadith that the two parties to a sale have an option until they separate or the sale is made conditional. This hadith demonstrates that khiyar is permissible in sale.
The hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim, thus authentic (sahih).
3
Qiyas – Analogical Extension to Lease
Lease (ijara) is a type of exchange contract (mu‘awadah) similar to sale. The effective cause (illa) for allowing khiyar in sale is the need for deliberation in exchange contracts. Since lease shares this illa, the ruling is extended by qiyas.
This qiyas is jaliy (clear analogy) because both involve transfer of usufruct or ownership for a price.
4
Duration – Three Days Limit
The second hadith specifies that the option period is three days. This establishes the maximum duration for khiyar al-shart in Hanafi fiqh.
The three-day limit is a sahih hadith recorded by al-Daraqutni.
5
Cross-Madhhab Comparison
Other schools (e.g., Shafi‘i) generally do not allow khiyar in lease, considering lease binding from the moment of contract. The Hanafi position offers flexibility.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is that Hanafis allow a cooling-off period, Shafi‘is do not.

Chapter of Invalid Lease. (32) The condition of option is valid in a lease. (33) And the lease is rescinded due to excuses: such as one who rents a shop in the market to trade in it, then his wealth is lost; and such as one who leases a house or a shop, then becomes bankrupt and debts become incumbent upon him which he cannot pay except from the price of what he leased, then the judge rescinds the contract and sells it for the debt. And one who rents a beast to travel on it, then it occurs to him to refrain from the travel, then it is an excuse; but if it occurs to the carrier to refrain from the travel, then it is not an excuse. Proof (1) for the validity of condition of option in lease: from Ibn Umar that the Prophet said: 'The two contracting parties have the option in their sale as long as they have not separated, or the sale is an option [contract].' Proof (2): from Ibn Umar that the Prophet said: 'The option is three days.' Proof (1) for the rescission of lease due to excuses: from Abu Sa'id al-Khudri that the Prophet said: 'No harm and no reciprocating harm; whoever harms, Allah will harm him; and whoever causes hardship, Allah will cause hardship upon him.' Proof (2): from Abu Hurairah that the Prophet said: 'Whoever cancels a sale for a Muslim, Allah will cancel his sin.' Principles: When a defect arises such that benefit is not possible, the lease can be broken. A major defect makes the lease invalid. If a defect arises in the lease and one is still benefiting, then only the amount of rent for that [period] is due. Due to a major defect, the lease can be rescinded.

Commentary
This passage is from the chapter on invalid lease (ijārah fāsidah). It covers two rulings: (1) the validity of stipulating an option (khiyār) in an ijarah contract, and (2) the termination of the lease due to valid excuses (a‘dhār). The author presents examples: if a lessee loses his capital, or if a lessor becomes bankrupt and the property must be sold to pay debts, the judge may cancel the lease. Also, if a person rents an animal for travel then decides not to travel, it is an excuse for the lessee (because the purpose is frustrated), but not for the carrier (because the carrier's change of mind is not a hardship for the lessee). The ruling on option in lease is supported by two hadith: the general hadith on khiyār al-majlis (option of the session) and the hadith on a three-day option. The termination due to excuses is supported by the hadith 'La darar wa la dirar' (no harm and no reciprocating harm) and the hadith encouraging the cancellation of sales (iqālah). The usul notes in Urdu explain that a major defect (ayb kathīr) renders the lease invalid; if a defect arises but the lessee continues to benefit, only the proportional rent is due; and the lease can be rescinded due to a major defect.

Key Terms
الإجارة الفاسدة (al-ijārah al-fāsidah): A lease contract that is defective in some condition or element, but not completely void (bāṭil). According to the Hanafi school, a fāsid contract is one that is valid in essence but defective in description, and it can be rectified by removing the defect or, if not possible, it may be rescinded.
الخيار (al-khiyār): Option: the right of one or both parties to confirm or rescind a contract within a specified period. In this passage, it refers to the validity of stipulating an option in an ijarah contract.
الأعذار (al-a‘dhār): Excuses: valid reasons that allow one party to terminate the lease contract without penalty. Examples given: loss of capital, bankruptcy, change of travel plans for lessee.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school validates option clauses in leases and allows termination for genuine excuses to prevent harm, based on hadith and the principle of 'no harm'.
