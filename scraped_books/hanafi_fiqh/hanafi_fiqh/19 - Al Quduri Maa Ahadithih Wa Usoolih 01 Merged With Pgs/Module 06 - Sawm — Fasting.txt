Intention Rules for Fasting: Time-Specific and Debt-Based Fasts
Ruling with proofs on types of fasting and intention timing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of fasting in Hanafi fiqh and apply the correct intention timing for each category.

Core Idea
Intention for time-specific obligatory fasts can be made up to zawal; debt-based fasts require intention from night.

This passage from Al-Quduri opens the Book of Fasting by classifying fasting into obligatory and voluntary, and within obligatory, into time-specific and debt-based. The key distinction is the timing of the intention (niyyah). The author then supports each ruling with Quranic verses and hadith, including the famous hadith of Hafsa that is often cited for intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories and subcategories
Read the first lines: fasting is two types (obligatory and voluntary). Obligatory is further divided into time-specific (Ramadan, specific vows) and debt-based (qada, absolute vows, expiations).
2
State the intention timing for each subcategory
For time-specific obligatory, intention can be from the night or up to zawal. For debt-based, intention must be from the night. For voluntary, intention is allowed before zawal.
3
Locate the proofs
Find the hadith of Hafsa (man lam yujmi'...) and the hadith of Aisha (asking for food). These support the distinction between debt-based and voluntary fasts.
4
Understand the Hanafi reconciliation
The hadith 'no fasting for one who does not intend before dawn' is applied specifically to debt-based fasts, not to time-specific obligatory fasts, based on the practice of the Companions and other evidence.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school requires intention from night for all obligatory fasts, including Ramadan. The Hanafi school permits intention up to zawal for time-specific obligatory fasts.

الْأَصْلُ: صحیح بات یہی ہے کہ چاند دیکھ کر ہی روزے شروع کرے اور چاند دیکھ کر ہی روزے ختم کرے اور عید کرے۔ ہاں اگر موسم ابر آلود ہو تو تیس دن مکمل کرلے۔
The Book of Fasting. Fasting is of two types: obligatory and voluntary. The obligatory is of two types: one that is tied to a specific time, like the fast of Ramadan and a specific vow; it is permissible to fast it with intention from the night, and if he did not intend until morning, the intention between him and the zawal (midday) suffices. Then he mentioned the proofs: The first verse: [2:183]; the second verse: [2:185]; the first hadith: ...; the third verse: [22:29]; the second hadith: from Hafsa ...; the third hadith: from Salamah ibn al-Akwa' ...; Principle: Fasting is refraining from eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse; it is the third great pillar of Islam. The second type is what is established as a debt, like making up Ramadan, an absolute vow, and expiations; it is not permissible to fast it except with intention from the night. And all voluntary fasting is permissible with intention before zawal. Then he mentioned the proofs: The hadith: from Hafsa ...; the hadith: from Aisha ...; And it is fitting for people to seek the crescent on the twenty-ninth day of Sha'ban; if they see it, they fast; if it is cloudy upon them, they complete the count of Sha'ban as thirty days, then fast. And whoever sees the crescent of Ramadan alone, he fasts even if the imam does not accept his testimony. If there is an obstruction in the sky, the imam accepts the testimony of one trustworthy person regarding sighting the crescent, whether man or woman, free or slave. Then he mentioned the proofs: The hadith: from Abdullah ibn Umar ...; the hadith: from Rib'i ibn Hirash ...; The principle: The correct practice is that one should start fasting only after seeing the crescent, and end fasting and celebrate Eid only after seeing the crescent; yes, if the weather is cloudy, complete thirty days.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on fasting in Al-Quduri. It begins by categorizing fasting into obligatory (wajib) and voluntary (nafl). The obligatory is further divided: (1) tied to a specific time (e.g., Ramadan, specific nadhr) – here intention (niyyah) can be made from the night, and if missed until morning, it suffices if made before zawal (midday). (2) established as a debt (fi al-dhimmah) – like qada of Ramadan, absolute nadhr, and kaffarat – here intention must be from the night; daytime intention does not suffice. For voluntary fasts, intention before zawal is sufficient. The proofs are then cited: Quranic verses commanding fasting and mentioning Ramadan (2:183, 2:185), a hadith where a Bedouin asks about obligations and the Prophet mentions Ramadan (Bukhari, Muslim), verses on fulfilling vows (22:29), and hadiths emphasizing intention before dawn from Hafsa and the permissibility of breaking a voluntary fast from Aisha. The second part covers crescent sighting: people should seek the new moon on the 29th of Sha'ban; if seen, fast; if obscured, complete 30 days. If a single reliable person sees the crescent, he must fast individually even if the imam rejects his testimony. If the sky is cloudy, the imam accepts the testimony of one trustworthy person, male or female, free or slave. Proofs include the hadith of Ibn Umar (fast after sighting, complete 30 if cloudy) and the hadith of two Bedouins testifying to the imam about sighting Shawwal. The Urdu usul notes summarize that fasting is the third pillar and that sighting is essential.

Key Terms
الصَّوْمُ (al-sawm): Refraining from eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse from dawn to sunset with intention.
ضَرْبَانِ (darban): Two types: obligatory and voluntary.
النِّيَّةُ (al-niyyah): The intention in the heart to perform a specific act of worship. For fasting, it is the determination to fast.
الزَّوَالَ (al-zawal): Midday, when the sun passes its zenith.

Key Takeaway

Crescent Sighting and Testimony in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling with proofs on crescent sighting procedures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules for determining the start of Ramadan, including individual duty when sighting the crescent alone, and the acceptance of testimony by the imam when the sky is cloudy.

Core Idea
Sighting the crescent is essential: if seen alone, one must fast; if cloudy, one trustworthy witness suffices.

The second part of the Kitab al-Sawm passage from Al-Quduri deals with the practical determination of the beginning of Ramadan. It instructs believers to seek the crescent on the 29th of Sha'ban, and when it is obscured, to complete a 30-day month. The text also addresses the scenario of a lone sighting and the acceptance of testimony when visibility is obstructed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the recommended action on the 29th of Sha'ban
The text says 'يَنْبَغِي لِلنَّاسِ أَنْ يَلْتَمِسُوا الْهِلَالَ' – people should seek the crescent on the 29th of Sha'ban.
This is a recommended (sunnah) practice.
2
Understand the default ruling if the crescent is not seen
If the crescent is not visible, complete 30 days of Sha'ban based on the hadith of Ibn Umar.
The principle: a lunar month can be 29 or 30 days; if in doubt, complete 30.
3
Handle the case of a lone sighting
If a person alone sees the crescent, they must fast even if the imam does not accept their testimony.
This is an individual obligation; the community is not bound by a single sighting in clear conditions.
4
Determine the acceptance of testimony when the sky is cloudy
When the sky is cloudy (with an obstruction), the imam accepts the testimony of one trustworthy person, male or female, free or slave.
This is a concession due to difficulty in multiple witnesses.
5
Locate the proofs
The hadith of Ibn Umar (complete 30 days if cloudy) and the hadith of Rib'i ibn Hirash (two Bedouins testifying about the end of Ramadan).
These hadiths are the basis for the ruling.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on fasting in Al-Quduri. It begins by categorizing fasting into obligatory (wajib) and voluntary (nafl). The obligatory is further divided: (1) tied to a specific time (e.g., Ramadan, specific nadhr) – here intention (niyyah) can be made from the night, and if missed until morning, it suffices if made before zawal (midday). (2) established as a debt (fi al-dhimmah) – like qada of Ramadan, absolute nadhr, and kaffarat – here intention must be from the night; daytime intention does not suffice. For voluntary fasts, intention before zawal is sufficient. The proofs are then cited: Quranic verses commanding fasting and mentioning Ramadan (2:183, 2:185), a hadith where a Bedouin asks about obligations and the Prophet mentions Ramadan (Bukhari, Muslim), verses on fulfilling vows (22:29), and hadiths emphasizing intention before dawn from Hafsa and the permissibility of breaking a voluntary fast from Aisha. The second part covers crescent sighting: people should seek the new moon on the 29th of Sha'ban; if seen, fast; if obscured, complete 30 days. If a single reliable person sees the crescent, he must fast individually even if the imam rejects his testimony. If the sky is cloudy, the imam accepts the testimony of one trustworthy person, male or female, free or slave. Proofs include the hadith of Ibn Umar (fast after sighting, complete 30 if cloudy) and the hadith of two Bedouins testifying to the imam about sighting Shawwal. The Urdu usul notes summarize that fasting is the third pillar and that sighting is essential.

Acceptance of Testimony for Sighting the Ramadan Crescent and Time of Fasting
Ruling on crescent sighting testimony and fasting time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for accepting crescent sighting testimony in clear vs cloudy conditions, define the fasting time from dawn to sunset, and cite the textual proofs for each.

Core Idea
Testimony acceptance depends on sky conditions; fasting begins at true dawn and ends at sunset.

This lesson from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar clarifies two foundational rulings of fasting: how the start of Ramadan is confirmed and what determines the daily fast period. The Hanafi school distinguishes between clear and cloudy skies for testimony, and defines the fasting time with precise Quranic and Prophetic evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and identify the two distinct rulings: one about the acceptance of crescent sighting testimony, and another about the time boundaries of the daily fast.
The passage begins with the testimony rule and then shifts to the fasting time.
2
Analyze the testimony rule
Note the condition based on sky clarity: if the sky is clear ('illa), a large number of witnesses is required for knowledge; if cloudy, a single just witness suffices.
The hadith of the Bedouin and the statement of Ata support the cloudy scenario.
3
Examine the proofs for testimony
Identify the proofs: the hadith from Abu Dawud on single witness in cloudy conditions, and the saying of Ata (tabi'i) that in clear conditions, multiple witnesses are needed.
The Daraqutni hadith relates to breaking the fast, showing two witnesses are needed for iftar.
4
Define the fasting time
Extract the definition: fasting time is from the rising of the true dawn (al-fajr al-thani) until sunset. Note the distinction from false dawn.
The term 'al-fajr al-thani' is key; the hadith of Samurah warns against the first false light.
5
Examine the proofs for fasting time
Locate the Quranic verse (2:187) and the hadith of Umar and Samurah that establish the time boundaries.
The verse uses the metaphor of white and black thread; the hadith clarify the practical signs.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with its hadith commentary, addresses two connected rulings: the evidence required for confirming the Ramadan crescent, and the precise timing of the daily fast. The passage first states a general rule: when the sky is clear (no cloud cover), the testimony of one or two individuals is insufficient; rather, a large number of people must have seen the crescent such that their report yields certain knowledge. However, when the sky is cloudy (i.e., there is an 'illa or obstruction), the imam may accept the testimony of a single reliable witness, as demonstrated by the hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) accepted the word of a single Bedouin. The passage then defines the fasting time as from the rising of the 'second dawn' (al-fajr al-thani, the true dawn) until sunset, citing Quran 2:187 and numerous hadith, including one emphasizing that the spread of light (not the initial whiteness) marks the true dawn. The commentary clarifies that the witness must be Muslim, sane, adult, possessing eyesight, and just ('adl), but the judge or committee is not bound to accept any particular testimony; the matter rests on their ijtihad. The two Urdu principles at the end emphasize that even if one sees the moon and the judge rejects the testimony, that individual should still fast (if it is Ramadan) out of personal certainty. This reflects the Hanafi principle that individual certainty creates personal obligation, even if not legally proven in court.

Key Terms
الهلال (al-hilal): The crescent moon that marks the beginning of a lunar month, specifically Ramadan or Shawwal.
الشهادة (al-shahadah): Testimony given before a judge or authority regarding the sighting of the crescent.
الفجر الثاني (al-fajr al-thani): The true dawn, which is the horizontal light spreading along the horizon, distinct from the false dawn.

Definition of Fasting and Actions That Do Not Invalidate It
Defining fasting as abstinence with intention and listing actions that do not break the fast.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of fasting and list the actions that do not break the fast, citing the hadith evidence for forgetful acts and involuntary occurrences.

Core Idea
Forgetfulness and involuntary acts do not break the fast; only intentional eating, drinking, or intercourse does.

This foundational lesson from Al-Quduri's matn clarifies what fasting means in Islamic law and what preserves its validity. Understanding these rulings removes unnecessary scruples and ensures the fast is observed correctly. The passage defines fasting as intentional abstention and then addresses common scenarios that raise doubt.

Guided Path
1
Define fasting according to the Hanafi school
The passage explicitly states: 'Fasting is abstaining from eating to its end with intention.' Identify the two key elements: the physical act of abstaining (imsak) from dawn to sunset, and the mental act of intention (niyyah).
The phrase 'to its end' implies the full day from true dawn to sunset.
2
Identify the three major nullifiers
From the definition and context, the nullifiers are eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse. The passage then addresses these: if done intentionally, they break the fast.
The text focuses on the exception: forgetfulness does not nullify.
3
Understand the ruling on forgetfulness
The passage states that if the fasting person eats, drinks, or has intercourse forgetfully (nasiyan), he does not break his fast. This is supported by the hadith: 'If he forgets and eats and drinks, let him complete his fast, for Allah fed him and gave him drink.'
The command 'let him complete' (fa-lyutimma sawmahu) indicates the fast remains valid.
4
List the involuntary and non-consuming actions that do not break the fast
The passage lists: sleeping and having a wet dream, looking at a woman and discharging, applying oil, cupping, using kohl, and kissing. For each, it provides evidence from hadith or companion practice.
These actions are not considered direct ingestion or intentional sexual act.
5
Note the evidence for each ruling
The passage supplies hadith for each case: wet dream (hadith: 'the one who has a wet dream does not break his fast'), cupping (the Prophet was cupped while fasting), kohl (Anas ibn Malik used kohl while fasting), kissing (the Prophet kissed while fasting).
These evidences establish that such acts do not invalidate the fast.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri presents the core definition of fasting and lists various actions that do not invalidate it. The definition states that fasting (sawm) is abstention (imsak) from eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse from dawn to sunset, combined with the intention (niyyah). The mention of 'to its end' (ila akhirihi) implies the full day. The text then provides a series of rulings: if a fasting person forgets and eats, drinks, or has intercourse, the fast remains valid and no qada' (make-up) or kaffarah (expiation) is required, based on authentic hadith. Similarly, actions that occur involuntarily or are not considered direct consumption do not break the fast: a wet dream during sleep, looking at a woman with discharge, applying oil, cupping (hijama), using kohl (surma), and kissing (without emission). Each of these is supported by hadith or the practice of the Companions. The passage emphasizes that the fast's validity hinges on intention and abstention from the three main nullifiers (eating, drinking, sexual intercourse) knowingly; forgetfulness is pardoned. The Urdu principle at the end reiterates the definition of fasting in simple terms. This reflects the Hanafi position that the fast is not broken by actions that do not involve direct intentional ingestion or sexual act, in accordance with the principle of easing the fast.

Key Terms
الصوم (al-sawm): Fasting: abstaining from eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse from dawn to sunset with intention.
الإمساك (al-imsak): Abstention or refraining; the physical aspect of fasting.
ناسياً (nasīyan): Forgetfully; doing an action without intention or recollection of fasting.

Core Idea

Core Idea
Missed fasts require qada only if the person had time after the excuse ended.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses a critical question: If a person dies while still sick or traveling, are they still liable to make up the missed fasts? The answer hinges on whether they had an opportunity to fast after the excuse ended. The matn clearly distinguishes two scenarios, supported by Quran and hadith.

Guided Path

Identify the two scenarios
The matn mentions two situations: (1) die while still in the condition of sickness/travel, (2) recover or settle, then die. Determine the ruling for each.
The key is whether the excuse persisted until death.

For scenario 1: no qada incumbent. For scenario 2: qada is incumbent (لزِمَ) to the extent of the period of health/residence after recovery/settlement.

The verse 2:185: 'فَعِدَّةٌ مِّنْ أَيَّامٍ أُخَرَ' – the obligation to fast other days only applies after the excuse ends. If death occurs before that opportunity, no liability.

The principle is that liability (qada or fidyah) only arises when the person had the time to make up the fasts after the excuse ceased. If they die before that window, there is no obligation.
This is consistent with the Quranic emphasis on ease (يُسْر) and not hardship.

Feeding for Deceased with Missed Ramadan Fasts
Ruling and proof on feeding for deceased with missed fasts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on feeding for a deceased who owes missed Ramadan fasts and identify the evidence, conditions, and points of disagreement with other schools.

Core Idea
A will obligates feeding for missed fasts; without a will, the Hanafi school does not require it.

The obligation of fasting is a debt owed to Allah. If a person dies before making up missed Ramadan fasts, what happens? This lesson examines the Hanafi position based on Al-Quduri's matn: if the deceased made a will, the guardian must feed the poor on his behalf. We will explore the evidence from the hadith and understand why only feeding applies, not fasting on behalf of the deceased.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the feeding (it'am) on behalf of a deceased who owes missed Ramadan fasts. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) if the deceased made a will, and not wajib without a will.
The ruling depends on the presence of an explicit will (wasiyyah).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author cites a hadith from Ibn Umar: 'Whoever dies and owes fasting of a month, let food be given on his behalf in place of each day.' Identify the hadith and its chain.
The hadith is ahad (single-chain), thus zanni al-thubut.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah (evidentiary link)
The imperative 'فليطعم' (let be fed) indicates obligation. In Hanafi usul, al-amr yufidu al-wujub. However, the context of the hadith and other evidence restrict this obligation to cases where the deceased made a will.
The Hanafi school combines the hadith with the principle that no one bears the burden of another, unless specified by will.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
The Shafi'i school allows fasting on behalf of the deceased based on other hadith (e.g., 'Whoever dies owing a fast, his guardian may fast for him'). The Hanafi school rejects this qiyas, sticking to the apparent meaning of the hadith cited.
Intra-Hanafi disagreement exists: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow feeding without a will if heirs consent.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that feeding is only obligatory if the deceased willed it; otherwise, it is not required. This is the position in Zahir al-Riwayah.

Ruling for Traveler Returning or Purification During the Day
Stating ruling for traveler returning and menstruating woman purifying during day, with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that a traveler who returns home or a menstruating woman who becomes pure during the day should refrain from eating for the remainder of the day, and identify the evidence.

Core Idea
Respect for the sacred time: one must abstain after becoming eligible to fast, even if the day is already partially over.

This lesson covers a courteous ruling: a traveler who arrives home from a journey or a woman whose menses ends before sunset should, as a sign of respect, abstain from eating, drinking, etc. for the rest of the day, even though they are not required to fast the full day. The evidence from the Ashura hadith shows this practice from the Prophet.

Guided Path
1
Identify the individuals and the act
Two cases: a traveler returns home during the daytime; a menstruating woman becomes pure (menstruation ends) during the day. They are commanded to 'imsaak' (refrain) for the remainder of the day.
This applies to both after having eaten or not.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is from a hadith about Ashura: the Prophet commanded those who had already eaten to fast the rest of the day, and those who hadn't eaten to fast fully. This establishes the principle of refraining for the remainder of the day.
The hadith is from Bukhari.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's command to fast the rest of the day implies that a person who becomes capable of fasting during the day must observe the remainder. This is analogized to the traveler/purification case via qiyas.
The traveler returns home (becomes non-traveler) and the menstruating woman becomes pure (able to fast); both become eligible mid-day.
4
Determine the hukm
The ruling is mustahabb (recommended), not wajib, according to the Hanafi school. The text says 'amsaka' (they refrain) but it is a recommended act of respect.
Some might think it's obligatory, but the Hanafi position is that it is recommended, as the original rukhsah (concession) for travel and menstruation remains.
5
Note the thamarah
The practical effect: if they do not refrain, they are not sinning, but it is better to refrain. The day is not counted as a valid fast because they already broke it earlier.
They still must make up the fast later if they were originally traveling or menstruating.

Ruling for Mistaken Break of Fast
Stating ruling for mistaken eating/drinking with two proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that a person who mistakenly eats thinking dawn hasn't come or breaks fast thinking the sun has set must make up the day but owes no kaffarah, and identify the evidence.

Core Idea
Error excuses from expiation but not from making up the day.

This lesson addresses a common error: someone accidentally eats or drinks before dawn thinking it is still night, or breaks fast early thinking the sun has set. Even though the mistake is understandable, the fast is invalid. However, because it was unintentional, no expiation (kaffarah) is required—only qada'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the error
Eating/drinking under a mistaken belief: either thinking dawn hasn't come (and it has) or thinking the sun has set (and it hasn't).
The text covers both scenarios.
2
Locate the dalil
Two proofs: a hadith from Asma (Bukhari) about breaking fast on a cloudy day and then the sun appearing; the Prophet ordered qada'. And a statement from Umar (Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq) that emphasizes qada' without kaffarah.
The hadith is explicit about qada' only.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet did not mention kaffarah, only qada'. This shows that kaffarah is only for intentional violations. Umar's statement reinforces that qada' is easy, implying no kaffarah.
Silence about kaffarah in a context where it would be mentioned indicates it is not required.
4
Determine the ruling
Qada' (making up the day) is wajib; kaffarah is not wajib. The fast is invalid because the act of eating occurred.
The hukm level for qada' is wajib; the absence of kaffarah is due to the excuse of error.
5
Note the thamarah
If someone intentionally breaks the fast, kaffarah is required (in addition to qada'). This distinction is crucial.
The same applies if someone mistakenly thinks the sun has set but actually hasn't: they break fast, then discover their mistake.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of rulings (masa'il) regarding fasting. The first ruling states that menstruation obligates breaking the fast and later making it up (qada'), as proven by the hadith where the Prophet indicates that menstruating women are deficient in religion because they neither pray nor fast during menses, and must make up the fasts (not prayers). The second ruling covers the traveler returning home or a menstruating/post-childbirth woman becoming pure during the day: they must refrain from eating, drinking, etc. for the remainder of the day to honor the sacred time, even though they are not obligated to fast that day (since they had broken earlier due to valid excuse). This is supported by the Ashura hadith where the Prophet commanded those who had already eaten to abstain for the rest of the day. The third ruling addresses someone who mistakenly eats thinking dawn hasn't come, or breaks fast thinking the sun has set, then discovers the truth: he must make up that day but owes no kaffarah (expiation) because the error excused him from the severe penalty. The proof from Asma shows that when the sun reappeared after cloudy weather, the Prophet ordered qada' without kaffarah. Umar's statement reinforces this. The final Urdu note summarizes a principle: once a person becomes able to fast (e.g., traveler returns, menstruation ends), they must observe the remainder of the day as a fasting person, showing respect for the day.

Testimony for the Crescent of Eid al-Fitr
Detailing conditions for accepting witnesses for the crescent of Shawwal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for accepting testimony of the crescent of Shawwal and distinguish between cloudy and clear sky requirements.

Core Idea
The crescent of Shawwal is confirmed by a large group (clear sky) or two witnesses (cloudy); individual sighting does not break fast.

This passage from Al-Quduri lays out the rules for accepting testimony for the crescent of Eid al-Fitr. The key distinction is whether the sky is clear or obstructed, as this determines the number of witnesses required. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the Hanafi position on the start of Shawwal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling
The passage begins by stating that a person who sees the crescent of Shawwal alone does not break his fast. Recognize that individual sighting is not sufficient to establish the day of Eid.
This is a precautionary obligation (wajib) to avoid breaking fast prematurely.
2
Distinguish sky conditions
The ruling divides into two scenarios: when there is an obstruction (illah) in the sky (clouds, dust) and when the sky is clear. Identify which scenario applies in the case.
The Arabic term 'علة' (illah) here means obstruction, not the usul term for effective cause.
3
Apply the witness requirement for cloudy sky
If the sky is obstructed, the testimony of two men or one man and two women is accepted. This is derived from the hadith of the two Bedouins who testified before the Prophet.
The requirement is two witnesses; women's testimony is accepted in combination with a man.
4
Apply the witness requirement for clear sky
If the sky is clear, only the testimony of a large group (jam'u kathir) that produces certainty (ilm) is accepted. Note that no fixed number is given; it must be such that the report is definitive.
Abu Yusuf specified fifty men, comparable to the oath of qasamah; others say majority of the neighborhood.
5
Evaluate the evidence
The passage cites the hadith 'The fast is on the day you fast…' to emphasize the collective nature. Revisit the proofs: the two-Bedouin hadith for cloudy, the statement of Ata for clear, and the references from Radd al-Mukhtar.

Commentary
This passage addresses the rules for accepting the sighting of the crescent of Shawwal (Eid al-Fitr). The first ruling: a person who alone sees the crescent should not break his fast because the ruling is based on general public sighting, not individual certainty. The hadith from Abu Huraira emphasizes that fasting and breaking are collective acts ('the fast is on the day you all fast'). The hadith of the two Bedouins in cloudy conditions shows acceptance of two male witnesses. The references from Radd al-Mukhtar and Abu Yusuf indicate that in clear skies, the required number is large, e.g., fifty men or the majority of the neighborhood. I'tikaf: Recommended Seclusion in the Mosque
Definition and ruling of i'tikaf as mustahabb with proof from Quran and hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of i'tikaf, identify its ruling (mustahabb / sunnah mu'akkadah in Ramadan), and cite the primary proofs (Quran and hadith) that establish it.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is a recommended retreat in the mosque with fasting, established by Quran and Sunnah, and becomes a communal sunnah in Ramadan.

This passage opens the chapter on i'tikaf, defining it as a recommended act of staying in the mosque with fasting and intention. The author then provides the key evidence from the Quran and multiple hadiths, establishing its basis and conditions. The Urdu note clarifies that in Ramadan it becomes a sunnah mu'akkadah 'ala al-kifayah. You will learn not only the ruling but also how the proofs support the specific conditions required for a valid i'tikaf.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition from the matn
Read the author's statement: 'Al-i'tikafu mustahabb, wa huwa al-lubthu fi al-masjid ma'a al-sawmi wa niyyati al-i'tikaf.' Identify the three essential components: staying in the mosque, fasting, and intention.
The definition is concise; each component is a condition.
2
Identify the ruling level
The matn says 'mustahabb' (recommended). The Urdu note elevates it to 'sunnah mu'akkadah 'ala al-kifayah' for the last ten days of Ramadan. Note the distinction: outside Ramadan it remains mustahabb.
Sunnah mu'akkadah 'ala al-kifayah means if one person in the locality does it, the obligation is fulfilled for all.
3
Analyze the Quranic proof
The verse (2:187) mentions 'while you are in retreat in the mosques'. It prohibits certain acts during i'tikaf, thereby implying its permissibility and recommended nature. Consider how the prohibition only makes sense if the act is already established.
The verse does not command i'tikaf but regulates it, indicating it is a recognized practice.
4
Examine the hadith proofs
Several hadiths are cited: Aisha's report of the Prophet's regular i'tikaf in the last ten days, Ubayy ibn Ka'b's hadith about making up a missed year with twenty days, and Aisha's statement listing the sunnah of the mu'takif including conditions like no i'tikaf without fasting and only in a congregational mosque.
These hadiths provide the basis for the conditions and the emphasis on the last ten days.
5
Synthesize the Hanafi position
Combine the evidence: i'tikaf is recommended, but for Ramadan it becomes a communal sunnah. The conditions (fasting, congregational mosque, intention) are derived from the hadith. Recognize that the Hanafi school requires fasting for any i'tikaf that is obligatory or sunnah mu'akkadah, while nafl i'tikaf may be performed without fasting.
This analysis shows how the school uses both Quran and Sunnah to derive the ruling and conditions.

Leaving the Mosque During I'tikaf: The Dispute Between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on brief exit from mosque during i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on exiting the mosque during i'tikaf, identify their proofs, and determine the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A deliberate exit without excuse invalidates i'tikaf (Abu Hanifa); the Sahibayn require it to exceed half a day.

I'tikaf requires staying in the mosque for worship. But what if the mu'takif steps out briefly? Does a short errand break the retreat? This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement where Abu Hanifa holds that any deliberate unnecessary exit, no matter how short, spoils i'tikaf, while his two chief students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, allow a brief absence of less than half a day. We will trace the proofs from hadith and see which view is relied upon in the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling and the two positions
State the common scenario: a person in i'tikaf leaves the mosque for a short time (sa'ah) without a legitimate excuse. Identify the hukm according to Abu Hanifa and according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
The Arabic word sa'ah means any brief period, not necessarily exactly an hour.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof from hadith
Examine the first hadith from 'A'isha: the sunnah forbids the mu'takif from going out except for unavoidable needs. Understand how this hadith implies that any unnecessary exit is prohibited and thus invalidates.
The phrase 'except for what is unavoidable' shows that only true necessities excuse an exit.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof and reasoning
The Sahibayn rely on qiyas: if necessary exits are allowed, then brief non‑necessary exits might be tolerated by analogy. They also cite the hadith of the Prophet passing by a sick person without stopping, arguing that mere passing does not break i'tikaf.
The Sahibayn set a time limit of half a day, drawing from the idea that a short absence is not true departure.
4
Examine Abu Hanifa's response to the Sahibayn's evidence
Abu Hanifa argues that the Prophet's passing by was incidental, not a deliberate exit for a need. The first hadith's explicit prohibition overrides any qiyas.
The principle that 'al-ibaha al-asliyyah' (original permissibility) is overridden by a specific textual command.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical consequence
State which position is preferred in the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa's) and why. Explain the thamarah al-khilaf: a brief exit for a non‑necessity invalidates i'tikaf according to the mufta bihi.
The Sahibayn's view is weaker and not followed in practice; fatwa is on Abu Hanifa's position.

Vowed I'tikaf: Days Include Nights and Must Be Consecutive
Ruling with proof from tabi'un sayings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on vowed i'tikaf of days, identify that nights are included and consecutiveness is required even without stipulation, and explain the proof from the sayings of the tabi'un.

Core Idea
A vow to observe i'tikaf for days automatically binds the mu'takif to full 24-hour periods consecutively.

Al-Quduri presents a clear rule: when a person makes a vow (nazr) to perform i'tikaf for a specified number of days, those days are not counted by daylight only. The nights must be included, making each day a full day-and-night unit. Moreover, the days must be consecutive even if the person did not explicitly stipulate consecutiveness. The passage then provides two proofs from the sayings of the tabi'un to support this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act is being ruled upon? It is vowed i'tikaf (i'tikaf al-ayyam al-mawjab). The ruling is that it becomes obligatory to perform it with its nights and consecutively.
This is a ruling on the nature of the obligation, not on the validity of the vow itself.
2
Identify the two inherent conditions
The two conditions are: (1) inclusion of the nights (layali), meaning the mu'takif must remain in the mosque for the entire night as well as the day; (2) consecutiveness (tatabu'), meaning the days must be observed back-to-back without interruption.
These conditions apply even if the person did not explicitly stipulate them in the vow.
3
Locate the dalil
The author cites two athar (sayings) from tabi'un: one from 'Ata' and one from Ya'la ibn Umayyah, recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
These are considered proofs (dalil) in the Hanafi school, even though they are not from the Quran or hadith of the Prophet (ﷺ).
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
From 'Ata': He explicitly says that i'tikaf confined to daytime only is not valid i'tikaf. This shows that nights are an integral part. From Ya'la: He shows that even a short period can be called i'tikaf, but when vowed for a number of days, it must be full days including nights.
The second athar supports the principle that i'tikaf can be for any duration, but the vow for days creates a specific obligation.
5
Note cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
The Hanafi school holds that consecutiveness is required even without stipulation, while the Shafi'i school does not require it unless explicitly stated. This difference has practical consequences.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi.
