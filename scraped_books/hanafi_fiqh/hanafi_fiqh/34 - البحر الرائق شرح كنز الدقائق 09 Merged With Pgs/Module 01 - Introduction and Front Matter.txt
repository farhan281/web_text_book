Definition and Conditions of Jinaaya in Hanafi Fiqh
Opening chapter defining jinaaya, its proofs, pillar, and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define jinaaya linguistically and technically, identify its pillar (qatl) and condition (equivalence), and explain why equivalence is waived for multiple perpetrators in qisas.

Core Idea
Jinaaya is a prohibited act against life or limb; its punishment is qisas, but the condition of exact equivalence is waived for joint killing to preserve the benefit of retaliation.

This lesson introduces the foundational concepts of Kitab al-Jinaayat. The author first explains why this chapter is placed after ar-Rahn, then provides a comprehensive definition of jinaaya, its pillar, conditions, and ruling. You will learn why equivalence (mumathala) is required in principle but waived in cases of multiple perpetrators killing one person, based on Quranic evidence and juristic necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of jinaaya
Distinguish the linguistic definition (any harmful act) from the technical Hanafi definition (a prohibited act against human life or limbs). Note that property crimes are classified as ghasb, not jinaaya.
The passage explicitly contrasts jinaaya with ghasb and with pilgrim's hunting violations.
2
Identify the pillar (rukn) of jinaaya
Recognize that killing (qatl) is the pillar – an act of the servant by which life departs according to habit.
Qatl is defined broadly, covering intentional, accidental, and quasi-intentional forms in later discussions.
3
State the condition (shart) for qisas
Equivalence (mumathala) is the condition for executing qisas, derived from Quran 6:160: 'And whoever comes with an evil deed will not be requited except the like thereof.'
This condition ensures justice: neither deficiency nor excess.
4
Explain the exception for joint killing
The condition of equivalence is waived for lives when multiple perpetrators act together. The text argues this is because joint killing is common, and strict equivalence would make qisas impossible, defeating its purpose.
The exception does not apply to limbs; joint maiming is rare, so equivalence remains for limbs.
5
Determine the ruling (hukm) of jinaaya
The ruling is the obligation of qisas (for intentional killing), diyah (blood money for accidental), and sin. This is wajib, established by the qat'i text of Quran 2:178.
The author notes that the rulings themselves are legislated, not the prohibited acts.

As for its pillar, it is killing, which is an act attributed to servants by which life departs according to habit. As for its condition, it is equivalency and equality in execution because equivalency is conditioned in the requital of evil deeds and the compensation for aggressions, by His saying: 'And whoever comes with an evil deed will not be requited except the like thereof' (6:160). And because imposing less would be a deficiency toward the wronged, and imposing more would be injustice toward the wrongdoer—deficiency is not legislated and injustice is forbidden, so fairness and justice lie in imposing equivalency. If we were to consider equivalency in the locus of actions, it would lead to opening the door of transgression and closing the door of retaliation—what benefit would there be in legislating retaliation? Commentary
This passage opens Kitab al-Jinaayat with a discussion of why it is placed after ar-Rahn. The author clarifies that both topics deal with preservation: ar-Rahn preserves wealth, and jinaaya preserves life. Since wealth is a means for life, means precede ends. He then refutes an objection that jinaaya is inherently prohibited while its rulings are legislated. The passage provides definitions: linguistically, jinaaya is any harmful acquisition; legally, it is a prohibited act against life or limb. The technical usage among Hanafi jurists restricts it to acts against humans (lives and limbs), while acts against property are called ghasb. Seven aspects are outlined: legislation, cause of obligation, linguistic definition, legal definition, pillar (qatl), condition (equivalence in execution), and ruling (obligation of qisas, diyah, and sin). The condition of equivalence is explained: it is required in principle by Quran 6:160, but waived in cases involving multiple perpetrators for lives due to necessity (to prevent failure of qisas), while it remains for limbs.

Key Terms
الجناية (al-jinaaya): A prohibited act that affects lives and limbs of humans (technically), or any harmful act (linguistically).
القتل (al-qatl): An act of the servant by which life departs, the pillar of jinaaya.

Definition of 'Aqila and the Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on Who Bears Blood Money
Opening definition and dispute on the 'Aqila

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definitions of 'Aqila in the Hanafi school and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i position on who constitutes the 'Aqila.

Core Idea
The 'Aqila are those who bear blood money: in the Hanafi school, the people of the military register; in the Shafi'i school, always the agnatic relatives.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq introduces the Book of 'Aqila. It clarifies that the correct title should refer to the people bearing blood money, not the blood money itself. It gives the linguistic derivation and presents the Hanafi legal definition, followed by a dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i. Understanding this definition is crucial because it determines who is liable when an accidental killing occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic root
Look for the derivation of 'Aqila from 'aql (to prevent). Understand why supporters are called 'Aqila: they prevent the shedding of blood.
The linguistic meaning underpins the legal concept.
2
Distinguish literal from legal meaning
Literally, 'Aqila refers to supporters; legally, it is the group liable for blood money (diyah). Note that the passage warns against confusing 'Ma'aqil' (blood money) with 'Awaqil' (the bearers).
Accurate terminology is essential in fiqh.
3
State the Hanafi legal definition
The Hanafi school holds that the 'Aqila are the people of the diwan (military register) if the killer is among them; otherwise, his agnatic relatives (asaba) are liable.
This is the majority Hanafi position (Zahir al-Riwayah).
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i definition
Imam al-Shafi'i rules that the 'Aqila are always the agnatic relatives, regardless of the diwan. Understand the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).
The dispute affects liability in societies with or without a diwan.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi view
Al-Tahawi held that the obligation originally falls on the killer's wealth; the liability of the 'Aqila is a customary concession ('urf) contrary to qiyas.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle of limiting exceptions to their textual basis.

Commentary
This passage begins the Book of 'Aqila in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. The author first clarifies the terminology: 'Ma'aqil' is a plural of 'Ma'qilah' meaning blood money (diyah), but the intended topic here is actually about the 'Aqila—those who bear the blood money. Thus the more accurate title would be 'al-'Awaqil' (plural of 'Aqilah). The passage then provides a seven-point outline of the subject. Linguistically, 'Aqila is derived from 'aql (prevention), as these supporters prevent the shedding of blood. Legally, the Hanafi position is that the 'Aqila are the people of the diwan (military register) if the killer is one of them. If the killer has no diwan, the obligation falls on his agnatic relatives. This differs from al-Shafi'i, who holds that the 'Aqila are always the agnatic relatives. The passage also reports al-Tahawi's opinion that originally the blood money is on the killer's property, but the Law made an exception for the 'Aqila as a concession. This concession is limited to those specifically mentioned (people of the diwan or clan); for others, the analogy of the killer bearing it himself remains. This illustrates a key Hanafi usul principle: exceptions to qiyas are confined to their specific textual basis.

Commentary
This passage is the table of contents for the volume. It lists the major books (كتاب) and their subdivisions (باب, فصل) with page numbers. The volume covers criminal law (جنايات), blood-money (ديات), injuries, wills (وصايا), hermaphrodites (خنثى), and inheritance (فرائض). The inclusion of 'كتاب المعاقل' (the book on the 'aqila) is a distinctive Hanafi topic dealing with collective responsibility for blood-money. The table helps the reader navigate the text.

Key Terms
الجنايات (al-jināyāt): Crimes, specifically homicide and bodily harm that may entail retaliation or blood-money.
الديات (al-diyāt): Blood-money; financial compensation paid to the victim or heirs for homicide or bodily injury.
المعاقل (al-ma‘āqil): The 'aqila; a group of male agnatic relatives or tribesmen who collectively pay blood-money on behalf of a killer who acted by mistake.
الوصايا (al-waṣāyā): Testamentary bequests; a disposition of property that takes effect after death.
الخنثى (al-khunthā): A hermaphrodite; a person with ambiguous genitalia whose sex is uncertain.
الفرائض (al-farā’iḍ): Inheritance shares ordained by the Quran and Sunnah; the science of inheritance distribution.
