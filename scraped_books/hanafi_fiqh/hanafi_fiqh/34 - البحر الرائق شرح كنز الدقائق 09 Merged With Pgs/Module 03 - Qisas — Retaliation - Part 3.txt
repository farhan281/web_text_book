This lesson presents a transmitted ruling from Abu Yusuf on a scenario in intentional homicide where two heirs exist and one makes a settlement (sulh) with the killer's side. The ruling clarifies that the settlement only affects the settling heir's share, leaving the other heir's claim fully intact. Understanding this principle is essential for applying the Hanafi rules of qisas and diyat in multi-heir situations.

A settlement for a wound does not necessarily cover death—Abu Hanifa voids it; the Sahibayn deduct one-third.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether a sulh (reconciliation) for an accidental wound remains valid after the victim's death. The dispute turns on whether the settlement is interpreted to cover only the wound or to include future complications. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping how the Hanafi school treats contractual agreements in the context of evolving circumstances.

State the scenario

The key point of ikhtilaf: does the settlement cover only the existing wound or also potential death? The Sahibayn interpret the settlement broadly; Abu Hanifa interprets it narrowly.

Note the practical difference
The 'aqila pays either full diya (Abu Hanifa) or diya reduced by one-third with the wounder recovering his payment (Sahibayn).
This affects the amount the 'aqila must pay.

Liability After Qisas for a Limb When the Victim Dies
Inter-madhhab dispute on liability after qisas for a limb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on liability when a retaliated limb injury leads to the original victim's death, explain the Sahibayn's opposing view, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Executing qisas for a limb does not automatically exempt from liability for resulting death; shubha (doubt) triggers diyah.

This lesson examines a nuanced qisas scenario: A cuts B's hand, B retaliates by cutting A's hand, but B later dies from the original wound. The central question is whether A is liable for B's death. The Hanafi school is split: Abu Hanifa holds A liable for diyah, while Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and al-Shafi'i say no liability. We will trace the arguments, analogies, and the Hanafi rebuttal, clarifying the legal principle at work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and the Core Dispute
Read the passage and identify the factual situation: a hand cut, retaliation by cutting the injurer's hand, then the original victim dies from the infection. The dispute is whether the retaliator is liable for the death.
The passage begins with 'if he cut his hand and he retaliated for it, and the first died'.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position and Reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that the retaliator is liable for diyah because his right was only over the limb, not over life. The infection reveals the injury was fatal, so he did not fully execute his right. Qisas would be obligatory but drops due to shubha, leaving diyah.
Key phrase: 'the execution of the first right does not necessitate dropping his right to killing'.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position and Their Analogies
Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and al-Shafi'i hold no liability, arguing that executing the right to cut exhausts the claim. They analogize to the imam cutting a thief's hand, phlebotomy, cupping, circumcision, and a person asking another to cut his hand—where infection does not create liability.
Their key argument: prevention of sirayah is beyond human capacity, so liability would close the door of qisas.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Rebuttal
The Hanafi text rebuts the analogies: those cases involve obligations on the imam (hadd) or consent, whereas private qisas is a personal right. The retaliator acts voluntarily and must ensure the act does not kill; otherwise, he is liable.
The rebuttal emphasizes that the retaliator's assumption was wrong—he thought he had a right only to the cut, not to kill.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
According to Abu Hanifa, the 'aqila (tribal guarantors) of the retaliator must pay diyah. According to the Sahibayn, no payment is due. This affects how qisas claims are settled in practice.
The passage does not state a mufta bihi, but the commentator defends Abu Hanifa's view.

Forgiveness of the murder does not automatically waive liability for the hand injury.

Liability for Hand Qisas After Forgiveness: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Explains two rulings on qisas with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a guardian cuts the killer's hand and then forgives, and explain the ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A guardian who cuts the killer's hand and then forgives owes diyah because his right was only over the life.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents two related cases on qisas for limb and life. The first establishes that exercising qisas for a hand does not forfeit the right to qisas for life if the victim dies. The second delves into a dispute: if a guardian cuts the killer's hand and then forgives, is he liable for diyah? Abu Hanifa says yes; the Sahibayn and al-Shafi'i say no. We will unpack the reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage first discusses a victim of hand-cutting who retaliates by cutting the offender's hand but dies from his wound; the offender is killed for murder. The second scenario is a guardian of a murder victim who cuts the killer's hand and then forgives him.
Both involve the intersection of qisas for limb and life.
2
Extract the principle from the first scenario
The victim's exercise of qisas for the hand does not indicate waiver of his right to qisas for life. His act was based on the assumption he had a right, not as a discharge.
Abu Yusuf dissents, but the Hanafi position is that the right remains.
3
Locate the ikhtilaf in the second scenario
The dispute is whether the guardian, after cutting the killer's hand and then forgiving (waiving qisas for the life), owes diyah for the hand. Abu Hanifa says yes; the Sahibayn and al-Shafi'i say no.
The text states 'This is according to the Imam' (Abu Hanifa).
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's argument
Abu Hanifa argues the guardian had no independent right over the hand; his right was only over the life. Taking the hand was without right, so diyah is due. Qisas for the hand would be the analogy but is dropped due to shubha, leaving diyah.
Shubha here is the uncertainty whether the guardian's act was a legitimate part of qisas.
5
Analyze the Sahibayn's counterargument
The Sahibayn argue that the guardian was entitled to take the hand as subsidiary to qisas for the life. Forgiveness after taking does not create liability, analogous to cases where a hadd penalty leads to death without liability (e.g., cutting a thief's hand).
The Hanafi response is that hadd is obligatory upon the Imam, not the private individual.

Re-establishing Testimony for Qisas When an Heir Is Absent
Intra-Hanafi dispute on re-establishing testimony for qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the position of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on re-establishing testimony for qisas when an heir is absent, and explain the underlying proof for each view.

Core Idea
Each heir must independently prove qisas because it is a right of the heir, not the deceased.

When a person is killed and has two heirs, one present and one absent, and the present heir brings testimony, can the killer be executed in the absent heir's absence? What happens when the absent heir returns? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples differed on this precise question. This lesson unpacks their proofs and shows how the Hanafi school resolved the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
A man is killed, leaving two adult sane heirs. One heir is present, the other absent. The present heir brings testimony (bayyinah) of the killing. Question: Is the killer executed now? When the absent heir returns, must the testimony be repeated?
The ruling distinguishes between intentional killing (qisas) and accidental killing (khata) or debt.
2
State the Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa)
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the killer is not executed until the absent heir returns, and then the testimony must be re-established (i.e., brought again before the judge) before qisas can be carried out.
This is the ruling of the matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq).
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that re-establishment is not necessary. Once the present heir's testimony is accepted, it suffices for all heirs, and when the absent heir returns, the original testimony stands.
They also agree that the killer is imprisoned in the interim.
4
Analyse the proofs
Imam Abu Hanifa relies on the Quranic verse (al-Isra 33) which grants 'authority' (sultan) to the heir, implying qisas is a right of the heir, not the deceased. Therefore each heir is an independent claimant needing their own proof. The Sahibayn argue that qisas is a right of the deceased that transfers to the heirs by inheritance, so one heir's proof establishes it for all.
The author reconciles the apparent inconsistency in Abu Hanifa's view by noting he treats qisas as a right of the heir for testimony purposes but also as a right of the deceased for other purposes (e.g., forgiveness).
5
Determine the thamarah and tarjih
The practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifa, if the absent heir returns, the testimony must be repeated; according to the Sahibayn, it is not necessary. The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's, as it is more cautious in blood matters (al-ihriyaz fi al-dima).
This is the mufta bihi position.

Killer Proves Forgiveness by Absent Heir
Ruling with rational proof on evidence for absent heir's forgiveness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the procedural ruling when a killer proves forgiveness from an absent heir and explain why re-testimony is not required upon the absent heir's return.

Core Idea
Proving the absent heir's forgiveness transfers the right from qisas to money for that share.

This lesson addresses a nuanced scenario in qisas: a killer has multiple heirs to the victim, one of whom is absent. If the killer can prove that the absent heir has forgiven him, what happens to the present heir's claim? The text from Kanz al-Daqa'iq, explained by Ibn Nujaym, provides a clear procedural outcome: the present heir becomes the sole opponent, and the testimony does not need to be repeated when the absent heir returns. This rule illustrates the Hanafi approach to separating rights among heirs and the efficiency of legal procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key parties
Identify the killer, the present heir, and the absent heir. Understand that each heir has an independent right to qisas or diyah.
The absent heir's right is not extinguished by absence.
2
Understand the killer's claim
The killer brings evidence (bayyinah) that the absent heir has forgiven him. This forgiveness converts the absent heir's share of qisas into a financial claim (diyah).
Forgiveness only affects the forgiving heir's share.
3
Analyze the effect on the present heir
Because the absent heir's qisas right is gone, only the present heir retains a right to qisas. The present heir becomes the sole opponent (khasim) in the case.
The killer's claim is now against the present heir's remaining qisas right.
4
Explain why re-testimony is unnecessary
The killer proved a fact (forgiveness) that affects the present heir's right. Since the present heir was present during the proof, the evidence is binding on him. When the absent heir returns, the same evidence need not be re-produced because it was already established against the present heir.
The principle: the proof was against the present heir's interest, so it stands.
5
Summarize the ruling
The qisas is dropped, and the case converts to a financial claim (diyah) solely against the present heir's share. The absent heir's return does not revive the need for new evidence.
This ruling is specific to Hanafi fiqh; other schools may differ.

One Heir Forgives and the Other Kills Without Knowledge
Ruling when one guardian forgives and the other kills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the rulings for a guardian who kills after forgiveness, based on his knowledge or ignorance of the prohibition, and explain the role of shubha in substituting diyah for qisas.

Core Idea
Knowledge of prohibition triggers qisas; ignorance with plausible analogy triggers shubha and diyah.

In Hanafi qisas law, multiple guardians (awliya') share the right to demand retribution. If one guardian forgives the killer, the right to qisas is not entirely extinguished—the remaining guardian may still have a claim. However, if that remaining guardian kills the defendant, his liability depends on whether he knew that the forgiveness made killing unlawful. The text presents two distinct rulings and grounds them in the concept of shubha (legal doubt), supported by a precedent from Caliph 'Umar consulting Ibn Mas'ud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two guardians exist. One forgives the killer. The other guardian kills the killer.
This is the essential fact pattern.
2
Determine the guardian's knowledge state
Does the killing guardian know that the forgiveness has made killing the defendant unlawful? The ruling bifurcates based on this knowledge.
Knowledge here means awareness that the act of killing is now prohibited.
3
Apply the ruling for known prohibition
If he knows the prohibition, his act is purely unlawful (tamahhuda haraman). Qisas is imposed on him, and he also receives half the diyah from the original killer's estate.
The diyah component arises because he is still a guardian entitled to blood-money.
4
Apply the ruling for ignorance with shubha
If he did not know of the prohibition, whether he knew of the forgiveness or not, he is only liable for full diyah from his own property, not qisas. His ignorance is excused by shubha.
The shubha comes from an analogy: shared rights are not nullified by one party's release, so he assumed the right to kill remained.
5
Understand the reasoning via analogy
The guardian's mistaken belief is based on a valid qiyas: in other shared rights (e.g., debts), one creditor's release does not void the other's claim. This plausible analogy creates doubt (shubha) that prevents qisas.
Shubha arises from the strength of the analogy, not mere ignorance of the law.
6
Note the precedent from 'Umar
The text reports that 'Umar himself was uncertain about this issue and consulted Ibn Mas'ud. Even a Companion's doubt confirms the validity of shubha in this case.
This precedent strengthens the Hanafi position that shubha is legitimate.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi ruling regarding the exercise of qisas by multiple guardians. When one wali pardons the killer, the other wali's right is not automatically extinguished. However, if the second wali kills the killer without knowing that the pardon made the killing unlawful, he is excused from qisas due to a legal doubt (shubha) arising from analogy to shared property rights. The text also references a historical consultation by Umar (RA) with Ibn Mas'ud (RA).

The text distinguishes between the wali knowing the killing is unlawful (after the pardon) vs. not knowing.

Shubha is a key concept in Hanafi penal law for mitigating penalties.

Testimony of Striking and Death: Conditions for Qisas
Ruling on the conditions for testimony to prove intentional killing (qisas).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for testimony to establish qisas when the victim dies after being struck, and explain why the instrument must be wounding.

Core Idea
Testimony of striking with a wounding instrument that leads to death establishes qisas because wounding implies intent.

This lesson deals with the proof of intentional killing through witness testimony. The Hanafi school requires specific details for testimony to establish qisas: the witnesses must testify that the accused struck the victim with a wounding instrument (jarīḥ) and that the victim remained bedridden until death. This requirement ensures that the testimony points to intentionality, not mere accident. The commentators discuss the interpretation of the absolute wording in al-Jami' al-Saghir to ensure consensus on this point.

Guided Path
1
Identify the required content of testimony
The witnesses must testify that the accused struck the victim with a wounding instrument and that the victim became bedridden and died from that injury.
They do not need to explicitly state 'intentional'; the nature of the instrument implies intent.
2
Understand the purpose of a wounding instrument
A wounding instrument (like a knife) is typically used intentionally to cause harm, whereas a non-wounding instrument (like a stick) could be used accidentally.
This distinction is crucial to prove ‘amd (intent).
3
Examine the interpretive comment
The commentator interprets the absolute (mutlaq) statement in al-Jami' al-Saghir to require a wounding instrument, in order to reach consensus with other views.
This shows the Hanafi method of reconciling texts through interpretation.
4
Address the objection
If witnesses only testify to striking with a wounding instrument, how can qisas be proven without explicit testimony of intent? The answer: striking with a wounding instrument indicates intentionality because one does not accidentally wound someone with a sharp object.
This reasoning is attributed to Shaykh al-Islam Khawahirzada.

Basic Scenarios of Conflicting Admissions and Denials in Qisas
Initial rulings on liability for qisas with conflicting admissions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for each scenario when one accused confesses and the other denies, based on the nature of the claim and the confession.

Core Idea
A confessor is not liable for qisas if the other denies, unless the claim is specific and his confession is complete.

This lesson examines the foundational rulings from the Hanafi school when two persons are accused of homicide and their admissions conflict. You will learn how the law responds when one admits and the other denies, and how the guardian's characterization of the killing affects liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the responses
First, determine what the guardian claims (intentional killing or error) and how each accused responds (confession or denial).
Confession to 'amd triggers qisas; confession to khata' triggers diya. Denial shifts the burden.
2
Apply the general analogy
The default rule (qiyas) is that if one denies, nothing is adjudged against the confessor because his admission alone does not prove sole liability.
This is stated at the beginning: 'والقياس أن لا يقضى عليهما بشيء'.
3
Note where qisas is imposed
If the guardian claims intentional killing against both, and one confesses and the other denies, the confessor is killed because the claim is specific to each.
The text says: 'ولو ادعى العمد عليهما فأقر أحدهما وجحد الآخر القتل قتل المقر'.
4
Distinguish error vs intentional
If one confesses to intentional and the other to error, the intentional killer is executed; the erroneous one's denial of partnership does not protect the intentional killer.
The case where the erroneous one denies partnership: 'وأنكر شركة الخاطئ قتل العامد'.
5
Learn the effect of the guardian's later statement
If the guardian initially says he does not know who cut the limb, he loses his right to qisas, even if he later clarifies, unless the clarification is before the judge's ruling.
This introduces the dispute between Zufar and the mashayikh, which is covered in Lesson 2.

The Guardian's Ignorance and Later Clarification: Zufar vs Mashayikh
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the guardian's later clarification

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Once the guardian says 'I do not know,' his right to qisas is lost even if he later clarifies, according to the majority.

This lesson focuses on a specific legal nuance: when the guardian initially claims ignorance about who caused the injury, can he later change his statement and regain the right to retaliate? The Hanafi school is divided. You will analyze the arguments of Zufar and the majority mashayikh, and understand why the majority view is preferred.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
A man's hand and foot are cut, and he dies. One person confesses to cutting the hand intentionally. The guardian says 'I do not know who cut the foot.' What is the effect on the confessor's liability?
The text presents this scenario and immediately gives the ruling: the guardian loses the right to kill the confessor.
2
Identify Zufar's position
Zufar holds that if the guardian later clarifies (e.g., says 'You also cut the foot'), his clarification is valid and he regains the right to kill the confessor.
Zufar's view is based on the idea that ignorance can be resolved.
3
Analyze the majority's counter-argument
The mashayikh distinguish based on timing: if the guardian clarifies before the judge rules that his right is voided, it is valid. But after the judge's ruling, the clarification is ineffective.
The judge's ruling creates a legal certainty (thubut) that the right is lost.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The majority view is relied upon in fatwa. Therefore, the guardian's later clarification is ineffective after the judge rules.
This is the operative ruling.
5
Consider the implications
This ruling protects the defendant from being subjected to qisas after a definitive judgment has been passed, upholding legal finality.
It also shows how shubha (doubt) once established cannot be easily removed.

Partial Confession and Istihsan: Half Diya for Contributing to Death
Rule on partial confession leading to half diya by istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school imposes half diya on a confessor who admits to part of a fatal injury by istihsan, and contrast it with the strict analogy (qiyas) that would impose nothing.

Core Idea
Istihsan imposes half diya on a partial confessor because his act contributed to death, even though strict analogy says no liability.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the end of the passage: a man confesses to cutting one of two fatal wounds, but the guardian's claim is uncertain. Strict analogy would result in no liability, but the Hanafi school applies istihsan to require half diya. You will understand the reasoning behind this departure from qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Understand the facts
A man with his hands cut off dies. The guardian claims one defendant cut one hand intentionally, another defendant cut the other hand intentionally. One defendant confesses to cutting the left hand intentionally, but says he does not know who cut the right hand, except that he knows the right was cut intentionally. The guardian says he does not know who cut the right, but knows it was intentional.
The key is that the confessor admits to only one of the two wounds.
2
Identify the strict analogy (qiyas)
Since the confessor only admitted to part of the fatal injuries, and the guardian cannot prove he caused the whole death, strict qiyas would impose nothing: 'والقياس أن لا يلزمه شيء من الدية'.
This is because full liability requires certainty (yaqin) that the confessor caused the death.
3
Recognize the istihsan departure
Istihsan imposes half diya because the confessor's act contributed to the death, and it is unfair to leave the guardian with nothing. The school prefers to give some compensation rather than none.
The text says: 'عليه نصف الدية استحساناً'.
4
Analyze the illah (effective cause)
The illah for istihsan is that the confessor's admission of causing a part of the death, combined with the certainty that both wounds were intentional, creates a strong probability (ghalabat al-zann) that he is partially responsible.
This is a khafiyyah (subtle) illah.
5
Distinguish from other scenarios
If the confessor's statement implied that his act alone caused death (e.g., he says 'I cut the left hand and he died from it alone'), then no diya at all is due because his statement exculpates him from the death.
The text gives a contrasting scenario: 'ولو قال المدعى عليه قطعت اليد اليسرى ومات منها خاصة لا شيء على المقر'.

Conflicting Testimony on a Head Wound: When Witnesses Disagree on Death
Intra-Hanafi dispute on accepting testimony for qisas when witnesses differ on death vs recovery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling when witnesses agree on a wound but disagree on death or recovery, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding this ruling.

Core Idea
Agreement on the wound proves it; qisas follows even if witnesses differ on death.

In cases of intentional head wounds (muḍiḥa) that allegedly cause death, witnesses may agree on the wound but disagree on whether the victim died or recovered. This lesson examines how the Hanafi school handles such conflicting testimony, focusing on a well-known intra-madhhab dispute: does the acceptance of testimony for qisas on the wound come only from the Sahibayn or also from Abu Hanifa?

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
The plaintiff claims that the defendant committed an intentional muḍiḥa causing death. The defendant denies. Two witnesses testify: one confirms the death, the other confirms the wound but says the victim recovered. What is the ruling?
2
State the majority ruling
According to the majority of mashayikh (and as per the Sahibayn), the testimony is accepted for the wound, and qisas (retaliation) is adjudged for the muḍiḥa. The dispute about death does not invalidate the common element.
Qisas for a muḍiḥa means the perpetrator receives a similar wound on the same part of the head.
3

Some mashayikh say this ruling is only the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, while others say it is the view of all three (including Abu Hanifa). Understand the two positions and their reasoning.
Abu Hanifa reportedly would reject the testimony entirely because the contradiction on a material fact (death vs recovery) makes the whole testimony unreliable.
4

The passage gives two more cases: (a) if both witnesses disagree on the wound's outcome (one says recovery, the other fatal progression), testimony is rejected; (b) if one says death and the other says recovery, but the claim is death, the testimony is accepted for the wound and arsh (compensation) is awarded from the perpetrator's property.
Financial compensation (arsh) is a lower evidentiary burden than qisas.

The text specifies that the acknowledged son must be an adult (kabir) and must actively claim the right to qisas. The brother's acknowledgment alone is not enough.

When both conditions are met, the acknowledged son has the right of qisas against the killer. This means he can demand execution or waive it for blood money (diya).

Explanation of liability rules and intra-school dispute.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Liability for harm by a sent animal requires driving, except when hunting justifies attribution without it.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explores the nuanced Hanafi rules on liability for damage caused by animals that are sent or incited by a person. Core Idea
Pardon by one co-owner extinguishes all qisas rights but whether a monetary substitute arises is disputed.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents an intra-Hanafi dispute. When a slave jointly owned by two persons kills a relative of both (e.g., their mother), and one owner pardons the qisas, what happens to the other owner's right? Abu Hanifa holds that the entire right drops, while Abu Yusuf argues the pardon converts the other owner's share into a monetary claim. Understanding this difference illuminates Hanafi principles on the nature of qisas as a right and the limits of ownership.

Understand the nature of the right to qisas
Qisas is a right of the victim's heirs (here the owners are also heirs). Being jointly owned does not negate the right over the slave; the right exists despite ownership. The Hanafi school considers qisas a personal right that can be pardoned.
The right is not a property right but a claim for retribution.

The monetary substitute (diyah) is the right of the master, but the master cannot claim money from his own slave; thus, the right to money does not transfer to the co-owner.

4
Apply Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf: The right to qisas is not destroyed by ownership. Upon pardon, the other owner's share of qisas converts into a right to money. The pardoning owner must pay half of his own share (quarter of diyah) to the other owner, or ransom the slave accordingly.
Abu Yusuf views the right as divisible; the pardon only extinguishes the pardoning owner's share, leaving the other owner's share intact but transformed.
5
Analyze the tarjih and thamarah al-khilaf
The text does not specify the mufta bihi, but later Hanafi practice often follows Abu Yusuf in such cases. The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the non-pardoning owner receives nothing; under Abu Yusuf, he receives quarter diyah from the pardoning owner.
This difference affects how muftis advise in joint slave qisas cases.

Likewise if the slave belongs to a relative of both and he is [worth] a quarter of the blood money, because the right to qisas was established for them as a right, or if he killed his master and they inherited him, all is void. Abu Yusuf said: The one who pardoned pays half of his share to the other if he wishes, and if he wishes, he ransoms [the slave] in the slave prorata, because ownership does not negate the right of the master to qisas over the slave. So when one pardons, the other's share of half transforms into money. [Abu Yusuf's reasoning:] It is not spread over the entire slave; so half of it is in his share and half in his partner's share. What falls in his share is dropped because the master cannot claim money from his own slave, and what falls in his partner's share is established, which is half of half, i.e., one quarter. So he pays half of his share or ransoms him with a quarter of the blood money. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a jointly owned slave who kills a relative of both owners (e.g., their mother or brother). The question is: if one owner pardons the qisas (retribution) against the slave, what effect does that have on the rights of the other owner? According to Abu Hanifa, the pardon by one owner invalidates the entire right to qisas, so the other owner cannot claim anything beyond his original share in the slave. He does not receive any money from the pardoning owner or from the slave. Abu Yusuf disagrees. He argues that the right to qisas is not negated by ownership; it is a right established over the slave. When one owner pardons, that owner's share of the right converts into a monetary claim against the other owner's share. Since the right to qisas was joint, after pardon, the other owner's right to qisas transforms into a right to money. However, this money is not spread over the entire slave; rather, the proportion due from each owner's share is considered. The pardoning owner owes half of his share (which is half of the slave) to the other owner. This amount is half of the other owner's right, which was half the qisas. So the pardoning owner must pay half of his own share or ransom the slave with a quarter of the blood money (since half of half is one quarter). Abu Hanifa's reasoning is that the money becomes the right of the master (the owner). But since the master cannot claim money from his own slave, the right to such money does not pass to the heirs. Hence, the other owner cannot claim anything from the pardoning owner beyond his original share. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated here, but later texts often prefer Abu Yusuf's view in transactions. The authority weight is Zahir al-Riwayah as it appears in the core books.

دية (diyah): Blood money, monetary compensation paid to the victim or heirs in lieu of qisas.
عبد (abd): Slave, a human being owned as property; has limited legal capacity.

Liability After Qisas and Pardon: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah after qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on diyah liability when qisas is exacted and then pardon occurs, and explain the reasoning behind each position.

Core Idea
If a hand is cut as qisas and pardon occurs, Abu Hanifa holds diyah is due; the Sahibayn hold nothing — the dispute turns on whether the act is a guaranteed qisas or a completed punishment.

This passage from al-Kafi presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute. When a guardian cuts off the hand of a killer as qisas, then pardons him, and the wound later causes death, the question arises: is the guardian liable for the hand? Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ. This lesson unpacks their proofs and the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the core dispute
The scenario: a guardian cuts off the hand of a killer as qisas, then pardons him. After pardon, the wound may spread or cause death. The dispute is whether the guardian must pay diyah for the hand.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his proof
Abu Hanifa: diyah is wajib for the hand. His proof: the cutting is akin to a garanty (damān) because the spreading cut is more severe than the initial confined cut.
He analogizes to a case where fingers are cut and then the hand is pardoned.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: nothing is due. Their proof: once qisas is exacted, no further liability; pardon after the act does not create obligation.
They view the act as a completed punishment.
4
Examine the talil (reasoning) in the text
The text uses analogy: the fingers and palm are like the limbs of the self (al-asabi' wa al-kaff ka atraf al-nafs). Therefore, if one cuts fingers and pardons the hand, he does not warrant the value of the fingers. Similarly, if the hand cut spreads, it is more severe, so liability does not arise.
This analogy supports the Sahibayn's position against diyah.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the cutter must pay diyah for the hand. Under the Sahibayn, nothing. Later Hanafi fatwa often follows the Sahibayn.
The dispute is relevant when the killer later dies from the wound or demands compensation.

Commentary
This passage from al-Kafi discusses a scenario where a person (the guardian of a victim) takes qisas by cutting off the hand of the killer before the victim dies, and then later the offender dies from the wound or from another cause. The dispute is whether the person who cut the hand is liable for the death. According to Abu Hanifa, if the hand is cut and then the person who cut it pardons the killer, the cutter must pay blood money (diyah) for the hand. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say he owes nothing. The reasoning: if the cut spreads and causes death, that is more severe, so analogically he should be liable. However, if the wound spreads (sara) after pardon, there is no liability? The text clarifies: if the wound spreads (e.g., leads to death), that is more severe than the initial cut, so liability does not arise. The analogy is to a case where one cuts off someone's fingers (for which qisas is applicable) and then pardons the hand—he does not warrant the value of the fingers because the fingers and palm are like the limbs of the soul (i.e., the whole body). For the specific scenario of cutting the hand and then the victim recovers (bara'a), the ruling is disputed. Similarly, if he cuts the hand and then slits the throat before recovery, he is liable for the full qisas for killing. But if he slits the throat after recovery (i.e., after the hand wound has healed), the dispute applies. The authority weight is Nawadir (from al-Kafi, which is not part of the zahir al-riwayah).

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retribution for intentional injury; here applied to cutting the hand of the killer.
دية (diyah): Blood money for the hand cut according to Abu Hanifa.
ساري (sārī): Spreading wound; the text mentions 'al-qat' al-sari' (the spreading cut) as more severe.
مقتصر (muqtasar): Confined wound; the initial cut that does not spread.
