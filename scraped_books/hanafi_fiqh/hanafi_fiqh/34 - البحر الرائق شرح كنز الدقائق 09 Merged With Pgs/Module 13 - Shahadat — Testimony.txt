Testimony in Murder: Absent Heir Requires Re-establishment of Evidence
Ruling on testimony in murder with absent heir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on re-establishing evidence for qisas when an absent heir returns, and distinguish Abu Hanifa's view from the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A present heir cannot execute qisas alone; if an absent heir returns, evidence must be re-established according to Abu Hanifa.

When a murder victim has multiple heirs, and one is absent, the present heir cannot unilaterally have the killer executed. This lesson examines the rule that the evidence must be re-established when the absentee returns, and explores the disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Understand the context: a murder victim has two adult heirs, one present and one absent. The present heir brings valid testimony (bayyinah).
The absent heir is not present for the litigation.
2
State the initial ruling
According to all Hanafi scholars, the killer cannot be executed based on the present heir's testimony alone; qisas is not decreed until the absent heir returns.
This is due to the absent heir's right to participate.
3
Explain the consequence when the absent heir returns
According to Abu Hanifa, the evidence must be re-established (i.e., the witnesses must testify again) before qisas can be carried out. The Sahibayn hold that re-establishment is not required.
This is the main point of ikhtilaf.
4
Note the consensus exceptions
If the killing was by mistake (khatā') or debt-related (dainan), there is consensus that re-establishment is not needed, because qisas is not applicable.
In such cases, only diyah or other compensation applies.
5
Understand the practical implications
If the witnesses are unavailable on the absent heir's return, Abu Hanifa's position may delay or prevent qisas, while the Sahibayn's position allows immediate execution.
This difference affects the outcome in real cases.

Commentary
This passage begins a new chapter on testimony in murder cases. The author (Ibn Nujaym) explains why this chapter follows the chapter on murder rulings: testimony is subsidiary to the substantive law. The core ruling is from the matn: when there are two heirs (guardians) of a murder victim, one present and one absent, the present heir cannot have the killer executed based solely on his own testimony (or the evidence he brings) because the absent heir has a right to participate. If the absent heir later returns, the evidence must be re-established in court; the initial testimony is not sufficient for both to execute qisas. This is according to Abu Hanifa (Imam). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they hold that re-establishment is not required; the original testimony suffices for qisas when the absent heir returns. The passage also notes two exceptions where re-establishment is not required by consensus: if the killing was accidental (khatā') or a debt-related killing (dainan), because in those cases there is no qisas. Additionally, there is consensus that the accused killer may be imprisoned based on the present heir's evidence due to suspicion, and that qisas is not carried out before the absent heir appears because the present heir cannot alone execute the qisas (which requires the consent or presence of all heirs). The disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn is about whether the evidentiary process must be repeated when the absentee returns.

Key Terms
شهادة (shahādah): Testimony or evidence (bayyinah) brought in a murder case to prove the crime.
وليّ (waliyy): Guardian or heir of the victim who has the right to demand qisas or diyah.
بينة (bayyinah): Legal evidence, typically testimony of witnesses, required to prove a claim in court.

Two Heirs Testify to Pardon of Third Heir: Invalid Due to Self-Benefit
Ruling on two heirs testifying about a third's pardon, with nuances and sub-cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on two heirs testifying that a third heir has pardoned the killer, distinguish intentional from erroneous killing, and apply the principle that self-benefiting testimony is invalid.

Core Idea
Testimony that brings direct benefit to the witness is rejected, even if it covers a third party's action.

What happens when two of three heirs claim that the third heir already pardoned the killer? Their testimony may seem neutral, but the law sees it differently: it effectively transfers the right to blood money into their own hands. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the reasoning behind it, and the important exception for erroneous killing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the default ruling
Three heirs; two testify that the third pardoned the killer. The default Hanafi ruling is that their testimony is invalid.
The passage begins with 'وإن شهد وليان بعفو ثالثهما لغت'.
2
Understand the reason: jarr al-naf' (bringing benefit to oneself)
The testimony converts the shared right to qisas into blood money, which immediately benefits the testifying heirs. Since they stand to gain, their testimony is self-serving and rejected.
The text says 'لأنهما يجران لأنفسهما نفعاً'.
3
Note the restriction for erroneous killing and the analogy to debt
The commentator cites al-Muhit: in erroneous killing, if the testifying heirs have not yet received their shares of diyah, their testimony may be accepted because they do not benefit immediately. An analogy from al-Mabsut on debt cases reinforces this: two creditors testifying that a third discharged his share is invalid only after the testifiers have received their own shares.
The restriction is 'قيد حسن لا بد منه' – the commentator prefers it.
4
Apply the principle to the sub-case of delaying execution for a fee
Two witnesses testify that an heir delayed the killer's execution for a fee. This is not a valid pardon nor valid payment because delaying a right does not extinguish it. However, if the fee was taken on condition to pardon for one day, it becomes a valid settlement (sulh): the pardon is valid, the condition is void.
Analogy to divorce or manumission for a temporary condition.

Commentary
This passage addresses a scenario with three heirs. Two heirs testify that the third has pardoned the killer. Their testimony is generally invalid because it benefits them: it converts the shared qisas into money, effectively giving them immediate control over their shares of the blood money. Their claim is effectively a pardon from themselves as well. However, there is a nuance from al-Muhit: if the killing was erroneous (khatā'), and the two testifying heirs have not yet received their shares of the diyah, then their testimony might be accepted because they are not benefiting themselves (they don't have a claim to the diyah yet). The commentator prefers this restriction. He then analogizes to a case from al-Mabsut: three creditors, and two testify that the third discharged his portion of a debt; the testimony is not accepted because it cuts off the partnership and benefits the testifiers. Then a new case: two witnesses testify that the legal heir delayed the killer's execution for a specific period for a fee. This is not a pardon, nor is the fee valid because delaying a right does not extinguish it, and accepting payment for delay is invalid. However, if the heir took a fee on condition that he pardon the killer for one day, this is considered a valid settlement (sulh) because the pardon is valid (it extinguishes the qisas for that day), and the condition is void. The analogy is to a man saying to his wife or freeing his slave on a condition that is void but the core act is valid.

Key Terms
العفو (al-afw): Pardon: waiving the right to qisas, which may be unconditional or with compensation (blood money).
الصُّلح (al-sulh): Settlement: an agreement to drop qisas in exchange for compensation, often blood money.
الجر (al-jarr): Bringing benefit (to oneself): a cause for rejecting testimony because the witness gains an advantage.

Conditions for Qisas: Testimony of Beating with a Sharp Tool
Conditions for qisas when witnesses testify to a beating causing death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for qisas based on testimony of beating and explain why a sharp tool is required to infer intentional homicide.

Core Idea
Qisas requires proof of intentional killing; testimony of a beating with a sharp tool allows the inference of intent.

The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra’iq explains a key condition for qisas when witnesses testify that a beating caused death. The matn states that if the victim remained bedridden until death, qisas is imposed. However, the commentators qualify that the beating must be with a sharp tool. This qualification ensures that the act was capable of causing death intentionally, thus establishing the element of intent necessary for qisas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
The matn says that testimony of beating leading to bedridden death results in qisas. What is the surface ruling?
Surface ruling: qisas is wajib.
2
Note the commentator's qualification
The commentator adds that the beating must be with a sharp tool. Why is this necessary?
To infer intentionality; a sharp tool is more likely to be used intentionally.
3
Address the objection
Even with a sharp tool, the beating could be accidental. How do the commentators resolve this?
They say the witnesses testify that the accused intentionally struck the victim.
4
Understand the consensus
The qualification is made to achieve consensus on the issue, as explained in al-Kifayah.
The absolute wording in al-Jami' al-Saghir is interpreted to require a sharp tool.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, for qisas to be obligatory, the testimony must specify a sharp tool to allow the inference of intentional killing.
If the tool is not sharp, qisas is not established.

Conflicting Testimony on Essential Details Voids the Evidence
Conflicting details in murder testimony nullifies the evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that contradictory testimony on time, place, or weapon in a murder case is void and identify which discrepancies are material.

Core Idea
If witnesses contradict on time, place, or weapon, their testimony is void — consistency is essential for reliability.

In Hanafi criminal procedure, the reliability of testimony is paramount. This passage establishes a clear rule: if two witnesses in a murder case differ on key details — the time, place, or instrument of death — their testimony is void. Even if one witness is uncertain about the weapon, the testimony collapses. The commentator extends this to four witnesses by analogy, underscoring the principle that material inconsistency renders testimony unreliable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is giving testimony in a murder case. The ruling: void if witnesses contradict on time, place, or weapon.
2
Recognize the material facts
Time, place, and weapon are considered essential; any discrepancy on these voids the testimony.
3
Understand the logical extension
The author mentions two witnesses; the commentator notes it would be clearer to state for four, but it is understood by analogy.
If two witnesses' testimony is voided upon disagreement, then four witnesses' testimony is also voided when they differ.
4
Differentiate from minor discrepancies
The text implies that only major, material contradictions void testimony, not every minor detail.
For example, a disagreement on the color of the killer's shirt would not void testimony.

Differences in Testimony: Invalidation and Istihsan Exceptions
Analyzes the invalidation of testimony due to disagreement and the istihsan exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule that disagreement on time, place, or instrument invalidates testimony and explain the istihsan exceptions for close spatial or instrumental similarity.

Core Idea
Disagreement on time, place, or instrument invalidates testimony unless the differences are negligible, where istihsan overrides strict qiyas.

In homicide cases, the law requires precise testimony. Al-Quduri states the general rule: if two witnesses differ on essential details—time, place, or instrument—their testimony is void. The commentator expands with critical exceptions by istihsan, where minor discrepancies (e.g., opposite sides of the same house) do not invalidate. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, the qiyas vs. istihsan tension, and the detailed categories for weapons and ignorance of the instrument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Read the matn: testimony is invalid if witnesses differ on time, place, or instrument. Note that agreement is a condition for acceptance.
The rule applies to pairs of witnesses; the extension to four witnesses is a fortiori.
2
Understand the reasoning behind invalidation
The killing is a single event; difference in time/place/instrument implies separate incidents, so the judge must deem one witness lying. Also, the rulings differ (e.g., diya vs. qisas) based on the instrument, making the subject matter divergent.
Qiyas (analogy) would reject any disagreement.
3
Recognize the istihsan exceptions
If the places are very close (e.g., two sides of a small house), or the instruments are similar (e.g., sword and qisas weapon), testimony is accepted despite disagreement. This is by istihsan, not qiyas.
Istihsan applies when strict qiyas would lead to injustice or impracticality.
4
Analyze the categories of instrument agreement
The commentator breaks down instrument cases: (a) both agree on sword with intention/mistake, (b) both agree on a small stick (diya only), (c) large stick (Abu Hanifa treats like sword), (d) one specifies, the other does not know – invalid.
Each case has a different legal outcome; the instrument determines the ruling.
5

In cases where witnesses agree on the killing but are ignorant of the instrument, qiyas rejects the testimony, but istihsan accepts it and imposes diya on the killer's property.
This demonstrates the Hanafi preference for practical justice over strict analogy.

Acceptance of Heir's Evidence in Homicide: Hanafi Ruling
Ruling on accepting the heir's evidence that another caused the death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting an heir's testimony in homicide cases and explain the basis for depriving a proven killer of inheritance.

Core Idea
An heir's testimony is accepted even against a co-heir if credible, and can deprive the proven killer of inheritance.

In Hanafi law, evidence (bayyinah) is crucial for establishing guilt and determining inheritance rights. A common question is whether an heir—who has a personal interest in the outcome—can be allowed to testify. This lesson examines two rulings from al-Muntaqa and Abu Yusuf that clarify when an heir's testimony is accepted and how it affects the inheritance of a proven perpetrator.

The second scenario is attributed to Abu Yusuf.
2
Understand the ruling on acceptance
In both scenarios, the heir's evidence (bayyinah) is accepted by the court. The acceptance is not automatic but subject to credibility, yet the text indicates it is accepted.
The ruling contradicts a possible assumption that the heir's self-interest disqualifies the testimony.

In the second scenario, the evidence proves that the second son caused the wound by error. As a result, he is deprived of his share of the inheritance (hirman). This follows the general principle that a killer does not inherit from the victim.

The acceptance of the heir's testimony serves justice by preventing a murderer from benefiting from his crime. The ruling is consistent with the Hanafi position that a killer, whether intentional or by error, is excluded from inheritance.
This principle is grounded in the hadith: 'The killer does not inherit.'

إقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment of a right or fact; here, saying 'I have no right' is an iqrar that is binding.

Key Takeaway
Later evidence is accepted after denial of proof due to possible forgetfulness, but not after a binding acknowledgment of no right.
