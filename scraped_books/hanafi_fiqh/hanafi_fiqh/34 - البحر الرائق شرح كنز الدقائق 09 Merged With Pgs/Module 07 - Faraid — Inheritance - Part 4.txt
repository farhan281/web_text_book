Division When Origins Agree or Differ and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains method of division when ancestors agree or differ, and presents dispute between Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Muhammad al-Shaybani's and Abu Yusuf's methods for dividing inheritance among Dhawi al-Arham when origins agree or differ, and explain the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between their approaches.

Core Idea
When origins differ, Muhammad counts branches but uses origin's gender; Abu Yusuf uses branch gender directly.

In Hanafi faraid, inheritance among distant relatives (Dhawi al-Arham) requires careful tracing through ancestors. This passage presents the core methodology: if all ancestors through whom the living relatives trace have the same gender, division is per capita with males taking double. If they differ, Muhammad al-Shaybani and Abu Yusuf diverge on whether to use the ancestor's gender description or the living relative's. Understanding this dispute clarifies the nuanced Hanafi approach to inheritance through multiple lines.

Guided Path
1
Identify when origins agree or differ
Determine if all ancestors (asl) through whom the living relatives trace have the same gender (all male or all female). If they agree, division is per capita among the living relatives. If they differ, a different method applies.
Origins are those through whom the relative traces, even if they are not themselves ancestors (like siblings).
2
Apply Muhammad's method when origins differ
Take the count from the living branches (furūʿ) but the gender description (male/female) from the differing ancestor (the womb that differs). Divide the property on that ancestor's level first, then further subdivide down to the living heirs, treating each male ancestor as having male descendants and each female as female descendants.
This ensures the ancestor's gender influences the share distribution.
3
Apply Abu Yusuf's method
Always divide per capita among the living branches, regardless of whether ancestors agree or differ. Use the actual genders of the living relatives, males taking double.
Abu Yusuf and al-Hasan ibn Ziyad hold this view.
4
Handle multiple directions (jihāt)
If a relative inherits through more than one direction, they inherit by each direction. Muhammad considers directions at the origin level; Abu Yusuf considers them at the branch level. The grandmother is an exception according to Abu Yusuf – she inherits only by one direction because her entitlement is by the name 'grandmother', not by multiple kinship paths.
The text states that the distant relative inherits by two directions in the correct view of Abu Yusuf.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The author declares Muhammad's view as more correct (aṣaḥḥ) and the more famous narration from Abu Hanifa. Therefore, the operative ruling for fatwa follows Muhammad's method.
Tarjih is based on the stronger narration and internal consistency.

The Seven Denominators and Rules for Combining Fractions in Inheritance
Explanation of the seven denominators and their mixing rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the seven denominators used in Hanafi faraid and apply the rules of tawafuq and tabayun to determine the common denominator when multiple fractional shares mix.

Core Idea
Combining inheritance fractions requires checking for agreement or disparity between denominators.

In inheritance calculations, each heir's share is expressed as a fraction with a specific denominator. When multiple fractions appear together, you cannot simply add them; you must find a single denominator that accommodates all. This lesson explains the seven standard denominators and the systematic rules—based on mutual relationships like tawafuq (agreement) and tabayun (disparity)—to derive the correct common denominator. Mastering these rules ensures accurate distribution of the estate.

Guided Path
1
List the Seven Denominators
Identify the denominator for each fraction: half (2), third (3), quarter (4), sixth (6), eighth (8), two-thirds (also 3), and the combined results 12 and 24.
Two-thirds uses the same denominator as third.
2
Determine if Shares Mix
Check whether only one fraction appears or multiple. If only one, use its denominator directly. If multiple, proceed to the next step.
3
Classify the Relationship Between Denominators
For each pair of denominators from different families, check if they are mutadakhilah (one is multiple of the other), mutamathilah (equal), muwafiqah (sharing a common factor), or mubayanah (coprime).
4
Apply the Appropriate Rule
If mutadakhilah, use the larger denominator. If mutamathilah, use one. If muwafiqah, multiply the factor (wafq) of one by the whole other. If mubayanah, multiply the two numbers. Combine results iteratively if more than two fractions.

Commentary
This passage explains the principles of the seven denominators used in inheritance calculations (faraid). The denominators are: 2 (half), 4 (quarter), 8 (eighth), 3 (third), 6 (sixth), 12, and 24 (when fractions mix). When a single share appears alone, its denominator is its 'name' (e.g., eighth from eight), except for half which is from two. When shares mix, two cases arise: either like mixes with like (e.g., both from the same family of shares), then the smallest denominator covers all larger ones (e.g., eight covers eighth and its multiples). Or different families mix (e.g., half with sixth), then we find the least common multiple by checking for tawafuq (agreement) or tabayun (disparity). If there is tawafuq, multiply the agreed factor of one by the whole other; if tabayun, multiply the whole of one by the whole other. Examples: half and sixth agree by half, so 1/2 of 2 (factor) times 6 = 6; quarter and sixth also agree by half, giving 12; third and two-thirds have no agreement, so 3×8=24. The seven denominators are fixed, and no inheritance case involves more than four shares or five heir groups, and division never involves more than four groups having fractional parts.

Key Terms
مخرج (makhraj): The denominator of a fractional share in inheritance calculations.
مماثلة (mamathalah): Equality between two numbers, e.g., 6 and 6. In such case, one suffices for the other.
موافقة (muwafaqah): Two numbers sharing a common factor other than 1, e.g., 2 and 6 share 2.
مباينة (mubayanah): Two numbers that are coprime, e.g., 3 and 8. Their product becomes the common denominator.
متداخلة (mutadakhilah): One number is a multiple of the other, e.g., 2 and 8. The larger serves as denominator.

The Four Relationships for Tashih in Inheritance Calculation
Introduction to tashih and the four number relationships for faraid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four number relationships (tamathul, tadakhul, tawafuq, tabayun) and apply the correct multiplier to adjust the origin when a group's shares do not divide evenly in a Hanafi faraid case.

Core Idea
Tashih corrects fractional shares by multiplying the origin using one of four number relationships.

In inheritance calculations, it often happens that the assigned share of a group of heirs does not divide evenly among them. For example, 5 shares to be divided among 4 heirs leaves a fraction. Tashih (correction) is the method to find a new whole denominator so each heir gets an integer. The key is knowing whether the number of heads and their shares are equal, one is a multiple of the other, they share a common factor, or are coprime. This lesson explains these four relationships and how each dictates the multiplier.

Guided Path
1
Identify when tashih is needed
Determine that the shares of a group of heirs (e.g., 3 sons, 2 daughters) do not divide evenly among them. This is the kasr (break) that triggers tashih.
If shares divide evenly, no tashih is required.
2
Determine the relationship between heads and shares
Compare the number of heads (individuals in the group) and the total shares they are due. Classify as tamathul (equal), tadakhul (one is a multiple of the other), tawafuq (share a common factor), or tabayun (coprime).
For example, heads=6, shares=10: 6 and 10 share a factor of 2 → tawafuq.
3
Apply the corresponding multiplier rule
For tamathul: use either number. For tadakhul: use the larger number (but note special handling when heads > shares). For tawafuq: multiply the factor (wafq) of one number by the origin. For tabayun: multiply the two numbers together.
The multiplier is then applied to the origin (asl al-masalah or its 'awl).
4
Multiply the origin by the chosen multiplier
The origin is the base denominator of the case. Multiply it by the multiplier obtained from step 3 to get the new corrected denominator.
This new denominator ensures all shares become integer.
5
Distribute the units
Multiply each heir's original share by the multiplier and divide by the new denominator to get the integer number of units each receives.
The distribution should now be whole numbers.

The 'Awl of Base Numbers 12 and 24
Ruling statement on 'awl limits for base 12 and 24 with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the 'awl for base numbers 12 and 24 in Hanafi faraid and identify valid 'awl denominators for each base.

Core Idea
Base 12 'awls only to 13, 15, 17; base 24 only to 27 — never beyond.

In inheritance calculations, sometimes the sum of prescribed shares exceeds the estate's base denominator. The Hanafi school resolves this by increasing the denominator proportionally, a process called 'awl. This lesson focuses on the two most common bases, 12 and 24, and their maximum 'awl limits. Mastering these limits is essential for accurate distribution in many standard cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base number
Determine the least common denominator of the heirs' shares. For most common combinations, the base is 12 or 24.
Base 12 arises from shares like 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6; base 24 includes eighths.
2
List heirs and their prescribed fractions
Write each heir's Quranic or residuary share as a fraction over the base.
Only Quranic shares (fard) can cause 'awl; residuary shares (asabah) absorb the surplus.
3
Sum the numerators
Add all numerators. If the sum exceeds the base, 'awl is required; the sum becomes the new denominator.
The sum represents the total parts after 'awl.
4
Check against the maximum allowed 'awl
Verify that the new denominator does not exceed the maximum for that base: 17 for base 12, 27 for base 24.
If the sum exceeds the maximum, the case cannot occur in Hanafi faraid (except Ibn Mas'ud).
5
Apply the 'awl proportionally
Each heir's share is now represented with the new denominator; their portion of the estate is their numerator over the new denominator.
This preserves the prescribed ratios among the heirs.

The Four Relationships in Tashih (Correction of Fractions)
Ruiling statement on the four relationships used in tashih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four numerical relationships (tamathul, tadakhul, tawafuq, tabayun) between heads and shares, and apply the correct multiplication factor to adjust the estate base (asl al-mas'alah) so that all shares become whole numbers.

Core Idea
Correcting fractions uses four relationships: equal, factor, common divisor, coprime.

When after assigning shares to groups of heirs, the shares do not divide evenly among the individuals, we need to 'correct' (tashih) the base number by multiplying it. This lesson introduces the four possible relationships between the number of heads (individuals) in a group and the number of shares they receive collectively. These determine whether we multiply by the full number of heads, by a factor, or by a proportional part.

Guided Path
1
Identify the group with broken shares
After calculating the initial estate base (asl al-mas'alah) and assigning shares to groups, check if any group's total share does not divide evenly among its members. That group is 'broken'.
If all groups divide evenly, no tashih is needed.
2
Compare heads and shares
For each broken group, compare the number of heads (individuals) to the number of shares they receive. Determine which of the four relationships holds: tamathul (equal), tadakhul (smaller divides larger), tawafuq (share a common factor), or tabayun (no common factor).
Use the Euclidean algorithm or inspection to find the common factor for tawafuq.
3
Determine the multiplier for the group
Apply the rule: if tamathul, multiply by 1 (or just one of the numbers). If tadakhul, use the larger number. If tawafuq, use the proportional part (wafq) = heads divided by the common factor. If tabayun, use the full number of heads.
The multiplier is applied to the original estate base.
4
Handle multiple groups
If there are multiple broken groups, combine their multipliers using the same relationships among the multipliers themselves. For tamathul among groups, take one. For tadakhul, take largest. For tawafuq, multiply the wafq of the first by the wafq of the second, etc. Finally, multiply the combined multiplier by the original base to get the corrected base.
The corrected base (mukhrij) is the smallest number that allows all shares to be distributed as whole numbers.

Tashih with Multiple Heir Groups: Tamathul, Muwafaqah, Mubayanah
Hanafi faraid methodology for tashih with multiple heir groups

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the three Hanafi methods of tashih (tamathul, muwafaqah, mubayanah) to correct fractional shares when multiple heir groups are present, calculating the correct multiplier step by step.

Core Idea
When fractions hit multiple groups, adjust through tamathul, muwafaqah, or mubayanah to give every heir whole shares.

Dividing an estate often leaves fractional shares that cannot be split evenly among heirs in a group. The Hanafi school uses tashih—systematic adjustment of the base number—to ensure whole numbers. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks the rules for multiple groups: when head counts are equal (tamathul), when they share a factor (muwafaqah), and when they are coprime (mubayanah). We'll work through the examples to see how the multipliers are derived.

Guided Path
1
Identify each heir group and its share
List all groups of heirs (e.g., wives, sisters, grandmothers). Note the fraction of the estate each group receives and the number of individuals (heads) in that group.
2
Check divisibility of shares by heads
For each group, see if the share (in numerator units) divides evenly by the number of heads. If it does, no tashih needed for that group. If not, move to step 3.
If all groups divide evenly, the base number is already corrected.
3
Analyze relationships between head counts of non‑dividing groups
Among groups where shares do not divide, compare their head counts. Determine if they are equal (tamathul), share a common factor (muwafaqah), or are coprime (mubayanah). This determines the multiplier between groups.
For three or more groups, pairwise analysis is required.
4
Apply the appropriate multiplier method
For tamathul groups, pick one group's head count. For muwafaqah, use the wafq (head count divided by common factor). For mubayanah, multiply the full head counts. Then combine multipliers stepwise.
If some groups are equal and others different, handle equals first.
5
Multiply the overall multiplier by the estate's base number
The result from step 4 becomes the new corrected base. Multiply it by the original base (and 'awl if any) to get the final corrected base from which all shares are whole numbers.
Verify by recomputing each heir's share: it should be an integer.

Calculating Radd When a Spouse Is Present
Procedural explanation of radd calculation with spouse present

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Give the spouse's fixed share first; the remainder is divided among radd heirs, adjusting by multiplying heads if not evenly divisible.

In Hanafi faraid, when a spouse inherits alongside others who are entitled to return (radd), the spouse is first given their fixed share using the smallest denominator. The remaining surplus is then distributed among the radd heirs by heads. Sometimes the remainder does not divide evenly, requiring us to adjust the base of the estate. This lesson walks through both scenarios using clear examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs and their categories
Determine which heirs are entitled to radd (e.g., daughters, sisters) and which are not (the spouse). Only the spouse does not receive radd in Hanafi law.
All radd heirs here are of one gender (e.g., all daughters).
2
Assign the spouse's fixed share from its least denominator
Choose the smallest denominator for the spouse's share. For example, husband's 1/4 has denominator 4; wife's 1/4 also denominator 4. This becomes the base number for the first step.
The spouse receives exactly their Quranic share, nothing more.
3
Subtract the spouse's share to find the remainder
From the base denominator, subtract the spouse's portion. For husband with denominator 4, he takes 1 share, leaving 3 shares for the radd heirs.
The remainder is the surplus to be distributed among radd heirs.
4
Check divisibility of the remainder by the number of radd heads
If the remainder divides evenly by the number of radd heirs (e.g., 3 ÷ 3 = 1), the base is correct and each radd heir gets that quotient. If not, proceed to the next step.
If evenly divisible, the case is solved.
5
Adjust if remainder is not divisible: congruence or multiplication
If the remainder does not divide evenly, check for congruence (wafq) between the remainder and the number of heads. If they share a common factor, multiply the part of agreement by the spouse's denominator. If not (coprime), multiply the full number of heads by the spouse's denominator to form a new base.
Then recalculate the spouse's share from the new base and distribute.

Non-radd Heir with Multiple Types of Radd Heirs
Faraid calculation: non-radd heir with radd heirs of multiple types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate inheritance shares when a spouse (non-radd) is present with radd heirs of two or more types, using the method of multiplying the radd heirs' combined shares by the spouse's denominator.

Core Idea
When multiple radd groups exist, treat their shares as a single multiplier to adjust the base, then distribute according to their respective shares.

This lesson extends the previous one: now the radd heirs are not all of one type but belong to different categories (e.g., grandmothers and uterine sisters). Their shares are not equal per head but predetermined by their class. After giving the spouse their fixed share, we must distribute the remainder among the radd groups proportionally. The text shows how to find the least common base that gives integer shares for all, using the radd heirs' combined 'siham' (shares). We'll work through the examples step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the spouse and all radd groups
List the spouse (non-radd) and the different categories of radd heirs (e.g., grandmothers, uterine sisters). For each radd group, determine their share fraction (e.g., 1/6 each for grandmothers? Actually multiple grandmothers share 1/6 among them; uterine sisters share 1/3? etc.).
In the example: wife, 4 grandmothers, 6 uterine sisters. Grandmothers share 1/6 among them? Actually 4 grandmothers all get 1/6 total? The text says 'sihamuhunna thalatha' meaning their shares are 3? The details may be simplified.
2
Compute the radd heirs' combined shares (siham)
For each radd group, determine the number of shares they collectively receive from the residue. This may require a preliminary calculation for the radd groups alone without the spouse. The total shares of radd heirs become the multiplier.
In the text, for the case of 4 wives, 9 daughters, 6 grandmothers, the radd shares are: daughters 4, grandmothers 1, total 5.
3
Give spouse's share and find remainder
Take the spouse's share from its smallest denominator (e.g., wife's 1/8 from 8, husband's 1/4 from 4). The remainder (denominator minus spouse's share) is the numerator to be distributed among radd heirs.
For the example: wives get 1/8, so denominator 8, remainder 7.
4
Check if remainder divides onto radd heirs' combined shares
If the remainder (e.g., 7) divides evenly by the total radd shares (e.g., 5), then that denominator (8) works. If not, multiply the radd shares by the spouse's denominator.
7 does not divide by 5, so multiply radd shares (5) by spouse's denominator (8) = 40. This becomes the new base.
5
Distribute shares from the adjusted base
From the new base (40), give the spouse their proportion (1/8 of 40 = 5). Then give each radd group its share of the remainder (7) multiplied by their respective shares. For daughters: 4 × 7 = 28; for grandmothers: 1 × 7 = 7.
Alternatively, the text describes multiplying each radd group's shares by the remainder and then adjusting by the multiplier from step 4.

Method of Manasikha: Handling Multiple Deaths Before Division
Explains the ruling on inheriting from an heir who died before division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of manasikha to calculate inheritance shares when heirs die before estate division, distinguishing between muwafaqa and mubayana cases.

Core Idea
Estate recalculation after multiple deaths is simplified by chaining corrections using agreement or disparity between numbers.

When an heir dies before the estate is divided, the distribution becomes more complex. The Hanafi school provides a systematic method to handle such cascading deaths, known as manasikha. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the key rules: how to multiply the first and second corrections based on whether the share in hand and the new fardiyya share a common factor (muwafaqa) or are coprime (mubayana). It also introduces the concept of incomplete manasikha, where intermediate deaths are ignored if the heirs and their modes of inheritance remain uniform.

Guided Path
1
Identify the situation
Determine that we have a case of manasikha: one or more heirs died before the estate of the first deceased was distributed. Note the two fardiyyas (estate calculations) involved.
2
Apply the first death
Correct the first estate (first fardiyya) to distribute shares among the first heirs. Then identify the share of the second deceased from this first estate.
This share is 'what is in his hand'.
3
Check relationship between share and second fardiyya
Compare the second deceased's share from the first estate with his own fardiyya (second correction). Determine if there is muwafaqa (common factor >1) or mubayana (coprime).
This step is crucial to know how to combine the two corrections.
4
Multiply corrections appropriately
If muwafaqa: multiply the agreement factor (wafq) of the second correction by the entire first correction. If mubayana: multiply the entire second correction by the entire first correction. The product becomes the common denominator for the whole case.
The agreement factor is the second correction divided by the common factor.
5
Distribute final shares
Multiply the shares of each heir of the first deceased by the second correction (or its wafq) to get their final shares in the common denominator. For heirs of the second deceased, multiply their shares in the second fardiyya by the share of the second deceased from the first estate (or its wafq).
Heirs who inherit from both deceased require a double multiplication as described.

Finding Shares from Tashih: The Ratio Method
Explains the method to find shares from tashih with an example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the share of each group of heirs from the corrected base (tashih) and determine each individual's share using the ratio of shares to heads.

Core Idea
The share of each group from tashih equals their original share multiplied by the correction multiplier.

In faraid, after finding the asl al-mas'alah and assigning shares, we sometimes need to correct the base (tashih) when shares do not divide evenly among the heirs. Al-Bahr al-Raiq explains the method to extract the final shares from the corrected base, using the example of a wife, father, mother, and twenty daughters. This lesson walks you through the procedure step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source and its purpose
The passage consists of a matn (from Kanz al-Daqa'iq) and a commentary. The matn states the general rule: to find each group's share from the correction, multiply their original share by the multiplier used to correct the base. The commentary clarifies and provides an example.
The 'multiplier' here is the agreement factor of heads (wafq al-ru'us) or the whole number used to adjust the base.
2
Extract the general rule from the matn
The matn says: 'The lot of each group from the correction is known by multiplying what each has from the original case by what you multiplied into the original case.' Thus, if you multiplied the original base by a factor t, then each group's final share = (original share) × t.
The total of all final shares equals the corrected base.
3
Understand the ratio method for individuals
For an individual's share, find the ratio of the group's shares (from the original base) to the number of heads in that group. Then apply that ratio to the corrected total for the group. This ensures each person receives a fair portion.
The passage emphasizes that each group is considered independently; do not mix ratios across groups.
4
Work through the given example
The example: a deceased leaves a wife (1 head), father (1), mother (1), and 20 daughters (20 heads). Original base 27. Shares: wife 3, father 4, mother 4, daughters 16. Daughters' shares (16) and heads (20) share a common factor of 4, so the agreement factor of heads = 20/4 = 5. This is the multiplier. Corrected base = 27 × 5 = 135. Each group's final share = original share × 5: wife 15, father 20, mother 20, daughters 80. Total = 135.
The source text contains a copyist error ('three multiplied by twenty-five gives eighty'); the correct calculation is as above.
5
Verify and apply the method
Check that the sum of final shares equals the corrected base. To find each daughter's individual share, use the ratio: original shares of daughters (16) / heads (20) = 4/5. So each daughter gets 4/5 of the group's final share? Actually, easier: corrected total for daughters = 80, divided by 20 heads = 4 per daughter. The ratio method gives the same: original share per daughter (16/20 = 0.8) applied to corrected total per group? The passage says 'apply a similar ratio from the multiplied amount' – meaning the ratio of original shares to heads gives the fraction of the corrected group total per individual.
In practice, after finding each group's final share, divide by their number of heads for per-capita shares.

The Proportional Method of Estate Distribution
Explains the proportional calculation method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the proportional method to calculate the shares of heirs and creditors from an estate using the rule of three (four proportional numbers).

Core Idea
Estate distribution solved by proportion: heir's shares to tashih equals his portion to estate; similarly for creditors.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a straightforward mathematical method for dividing the estate when debts exist. It builds on the principle of proportional numbers, treating each creditor's debt like an heir's share and total debt like the tashih. Mastering this method allows you to compute exactly what each heir and each creditor receives, even when the estate amount differs from the tashih or total debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the known numbers
Determine the tashih (total shares of heirs), the total debt, the total estate, each heir's share count, and each creditor's debt amount.
These four numbers are the base for the proportion.
2
Set up the proportion for an heir
For a given heir, the ratio of his shares (from tashih) to the tashih equals the ratio of his unknown portion from the estate to the total estate.
This is a/b = c/d where a = shares, b = tashih, c = portion, d = estate.
3
Solve for the heir's portion
Multiply the heir's shares by the total estate and divide by the tashih. The result is his share from the estate.
Formula: (shares × estate) ÷ tashih.
4
Apply the same for a creditor
For a creditor, the ratio of his debt to the total debt equals the ratio of his unknown share from the estate to the total estate.
Treat debt like shares and total debt like tashih.
5
Simplify using agreement factor if present
If there is a common divisor (muwafaqa) between the estate and tashih (or between estate and total debt), use the agreement factor (wafq) to reduce numbers before multiplying and dividing.
Wafq = estate ÷ g.c.d. with tashih or total debt.

The mathematical basis is the rule of three (four proportional numbers): the ratio of each heir's shares to the total tashih equals the ratio of what they get from the estate to the total estate. Similarly for creditors: the ratio of each debt to total debt equals the ratio of what they get from the estate to the total estate. By knowing three of these numbers, you can solve for the unknown (the actual amount received). This proportional method ensures fair distribution according to the shares prescribed by fiqh.
