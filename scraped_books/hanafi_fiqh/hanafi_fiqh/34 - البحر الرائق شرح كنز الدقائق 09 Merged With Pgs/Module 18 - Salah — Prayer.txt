Will to Free a Slave: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn on Lost Sum
Dispute on validity of will for manumission with lost sum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi intra-madhab dispute regarding a will for manumission when part of the specified sum is lost, and apply the underlying principle to distinguish between pure and mixed acts of worship.

Core Idea
A will for manumission fails if the specified sum diminishes—Abu Hanifa insists on exact identity; Sahibayn analogize to Hajj.

When a testator leaves a specific sum to buy and free a slave, what happens if part of that sum is lost? While we might think 'any slave will do', Abu Hanifa and the two leading disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ sharply. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind each position and the deeper principle about the nature of manumission as an act of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the dispute
The testator says: 'Buy a slave with this hundred dirhams and free him after my death.' One dirham is lost. Abu Hanifa rules the will void; Sahibayn rule it is executed with the remaining 99 dirhams.
The dispute is about the validity of the will when the exact sum is not fully available.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the will specifies a particular slave (to be bought with that exact sum). Executing it with a cheaper slave changes the beneficiary, which is not allowed.
He analogizes to a bequest to a specific person: if part is lost, the remainder goes to that person, but here the slave is not yet identified.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
They analogize to a will for Hajj: if the bequest for Hajj is reduced, Hajj is still performed with the remainder. Manumission is also an act of piety (qurbah), so it should be fulfilled as much as possible.
They consider manumission as a pure act of worship like Hajj, where the beneficiary is Allah.
4
Trace the underlying principle
The text states that the dispute is built on whether manumission is a right of Allah (like Hajj) or a right of the slave. Abu Hanifa leans to the latter, so the specific slave matters; Sahibayn see it as Allah's right, so any slave suffices.
This deeper issue determines the ruling.
5
Note the practical consequence and the nuance of fixed vs. fractional bequests
Under Abu Hanifa the will fails entirely; under Sahibayn it is executed with the remainder. If the bequest is expressed as a fraction (e.g., one-third) rather than a fixed sum, and the estate is smaller, all agree the will is void due to uncertainty.
The text distinguishes between fixed sum (dispute arises) and fractional share (no dispute).

Commentary
This passage discusses a will in which the testator says: 'Buy a slave with this hundred dirhams and manumit him after my death.' If even one dirham of the specified hundred is lost before the purchase, Abu Hanifa holds that the will is completely void. His reasoning is that the testator specified a particular sum and a particular slave (since the slave is to be bought with that exact sum), so buying a cheaper slave is executing the will for someone not intended, which is impermissible. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) analogize to Hajj: if one bequeaths for Hajj and part of the money is lost, the Hajj is performed with the remainder, because it is a pure act of worship (qurbah mahdah) where the beneficiary is Allah, not a specific person. They extend this to manumission, which is also a pious act. Abu Hanifa distinguishes manumission from Hajj: manumission benefits a specific slave, so the identity matters. The passage also notes that this dispute rests on a deeper issue: whether manumission is a right of Allah (like Hajj) or a right of the slave. The text concludes with a nuance: if the bequest is expressed in terms of a share (e.g., one-third) rather than a fixed sum, then if the estate turns out smaller than expected, the will is void according to all, because the subject matter (the slave) is not specified and the will becomes doubtful.

Key Terms
قربة محضة (qurbah mahḍah): A pure act of drawing near to Allah, with no worldly interest; here used for Hajj as opposed to manumission.
بخلاف الحج (bikhilāf al-ḥajj): Contrary to (the case of) Hajj, where the will is valid despite loss of some money.

Conditional Manumission and Charity in Terminal Illness
Ruling on conditional manumission and charity in terminal illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a conditional bequest of manumission and charity in terminal illness and identify the evidentiary requirement for the beneficiary.

Core Idea
A will combining manumission and charity is valid, but the recipient must prove possession at death.

This passage from Al-Muntaqa addresses a scenario where a man on his deathbed makes a conditional declaration: if he dies from that illness, his slave woman is free, and whatever she has in her possession becomes charity for her. The ruling affirms the validity of this as a charity but imposes a key evidentiary requirement. This lesson breaks down the ruling, its conditions, and the burden of proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the conditional statement
The testator says during his terminal illness: 'If I die from this illness, then so-and-so (a female slave) is free, and whatever is in her hand is charity upon her.' Recognize that this is a conditional declaration (will) contingent on death.
2
Understand the legal consequences
Upon the testator's death from that illness, the slave woman becomes free. Simultaneously, the items in her possession become a charitable bequest to her, not simply her own property by right.
The combination of manumission and charity is permissible.
3
Note the evidentiary requirement
The slave woman must provide evidence (al-bayyinah) that the items were in her possession on the day the testator died. This prevents false claims and protects the estate.
Hanafi law typically requires two male witnesses or one male and two females.
4
Recognize the one-third rule context (implicit)
Although the text does not mention it, all bequests in terminal illness are subject to the one-third rule: the total value of bequests (including manumission and charity) cannot exceed one-third of the estate unless heirs consent.
This ruling applies only if the bequests are within the one-third limit.
5
Apply the ruling to a similar scenario
Consider a testator who says: 'If I die, my slave Khalid is free and the horse he rides is charity for him.' The same rule applies: Khalid becomes free and may receive the horse, but must prove he had possession on the day of death.

Rulings for the Ambiguous Hermaphrodite in Prayer
Specifies how the problematic hermaphrodite should stand in congregational prayer and related rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for the ambiguous hermaphrodite (mushkil) in congregational prayer and explain the reason for each ruling.

Core Idea
The ambiguous hermaphrodite must stand between rows — precaution governs where lives uncertain.

This passage addresses a unique scenario in Hanafi fiqh: how should a person whose sex cannot be determined (mushkil) pray in congregation? Because standing in the wrong row could invalidate his or others' prayer, the law prescribes a special standing position between the men's and women's rows. It also outlines the consequences of standing in the wrong row and recommends additional precautions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The passage concerns the 'mushkil' — a hermaphrodite whose sex remains ambiguous after applying signs.
This is not a general hermaphrodite but one that cannot be resolved.
2
Understand the problem
If a man stands in the women's row, his own prayer is invalidated. If a woman stands in the men's row, the prayers of adjacent men are invalidated. The mushkil could be either, so any row assignment risks invalidation.
The risk is mutual: either self-invalidity or harming others.
3
Note the primary ruling
The mushkil must stand between the men's and women's rows, not aligning with either.
Imperative 'fa yaqifu' indicates obligation (wajib).
4
Recognize the consequences of standing in the wrong row
If a baligh (adult) mushkil stands in the women's row, his prayer is invalid (fasid). If a murahiq (adolescent) does so, it is recommended to repeat. Standing in the men's row invalidates the prayer of adjacent persons.
The distinction between baligh and murahiq is important.
5
Apply the precautionary principle
The principle 'yu'khadhu bi al-ahwat fa al-ahwat' (take the most precautionary) extends to those adjacent: the persons on the right, left, and behind the mushkil are required to repeat their prayers as a precaution.
Also, it is recommended for the mushkil to wear a veil (qina') due to the possibility of being female.

Commentary
This passage gives the practical rulings for a hermaphrodite who remains ambiguous (mushkil) after applying signs. In congregational prayer, he/she stands between the men's and women's rows, not aligning with either, to avoid invalidating his/her own prayer (if standing in women's row as a man) or others' prayers (if standing in men's row as a woman). If the ambiguous person is an adult (baligh) and stands in the women's row, his prayer is invalid; if a minor (murahiq), it is recommended to repeat. The general principle is to take the most precautionary measure (al-ahwat fa al-ahwat), meaning that everyone around (right, left, behind) should also repeat their prayer out of caution, due to the possibility that the ambiguous person is a woman and thus invalidated the prayers of those adjacent (since a woman cannot lead men). Also, it is recommended for the ambiguous person to wear a veil (qina') because of the possibility of being a woman (to cover hair). These rules illustrate how fiqh handles uncertainty through precaution.

Key Terms
صف (saff): Row in congregational prayer.
قناع (qina'): Veil covering the head and face, worn by women.
مشكل (mushkil): A hermaphrodite whose sex cannot be determined due to ambiguous signs.

Practical Rulings for the Ambiguous Khuntha
Rulings on prayer, dress, circumcision, travel, and companionship for the ambiguous khuntha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the precautionary rulings for an ambiguous khuntha in prayer, dress, social interaction, and circumcision, and apply the principle of ahwat to derive the correct practice.

Core Idea
For the ambiguous khuntha, the ruling is precaution: always act to avoid potential sin or invalidity.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the fiqh of a person whose gender cannot be determined — the khuntha mushkil. Since every act could be for a male or female, the Shari'ah prescribes the most cautious course (ahwat) to ensure validity and avoid transgression. The rulings cover prayer, dress, travel, companionship, circumcision, and the difference between life and death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the khuntha's status
Determine if the khuntha is mushkil (ambiguous) after all signs fail. Precaution applies only when ambiguity is unresolved.
If gender becomes known later, rulings change.
2
Apply precaution in prayer
Stand between rows, wear qina', sit like woman. Distinguish between adolescent (recommended repetition) and adult (obligatory qina', invalid without it).
The principle is: the prayer must be valid regardless of true gender.
3
Apply precaution in dress and ihram
Wear sewn garments (mukhayyat) in ihram, avoid jewelry and silk, cover properly. Precaution against being female and uncovering 'awrah.
Muhammad's view: wearing sewn garments is more precautionary.
4
Apply precaution in social interactions
Avoid khalwa (seclusion) with non-mahram of either gender. Travel only with a mahram appropriate for both possibilities.
If a mahram is unavailable, travel in a group.
5
Apply precaution in circumcision
Prefer a female slave or a mahram of the appropriate gender. A female slave's view is permissible regardless of the khuntha's true gender.
During life, the khuntha can own property to buy a slave.
6
Understand the life-death distinction
After death, no washing due to uncertainty; tayammum is performed. During life, the khuntha can own property and perform acts that require gender-specific rules.
The difference is based on the capacity to own and the need for certainty.

The Necessity of Specification (Ta'yin) in Niyyah for Missed Acts
Detailed discussion on specification in niyyah for multiple missed prayers and fasts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for specification in niyyah for missed prayers and fasts, and apply it to distinguish when specification is required and when it is not.

Core Idea
Specification in intention is required for different genera of worship, but not within a single genus like the month of Ramadan.

When a person has multiple missed prayers or fasts, they may wonder exactly how to intend for each one. The Hanafi school provides a clear principle: specification (ta'yin) is needed only when the missed acts belong to different genera (jins). This lesson unpacks that rule, its proof from the Quran and reason, and a practical way out for those with a large number of missed prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the number of missed units
Determine whether the missed acts are prayers, fasts, or expiations, and whether they are of the same type or different types.
For prayers, even the same prayer from different days counts as different genera.
2
Determine whether the acts belong to the same genus or different genera
Acts are of the same genus if they share the same cause (sabab). For example, all days of Ramadan fasting arise from one cause: witnessing the month. For prayers, each day's prayer has a distinct cause (the time).
Different genera require specification; same genus does not.
3
Apply the ruling: specification required for different genera, not for same genus
If the missed acts are of different genera, you must intend which specific act you are performing (e.g., 'I am making up the Zuhr of Thursday'). If they are of the same genus, intending the genus alone (e.g., 'I am making up a missed Ramadan fast') suffices.
For multiple missed prayers from different days, you must specify each one.
4
Consider the role of order (tartib) and the relied-upon view
Some scholars (in Al-Muhit) held that specification in prayer is only needed to maintain order; if order is waived due to many missed prayers, simple intention suffices. However, the relied-upon view (with Qadikhan) is that specification is required regardless, because it is about distinguishing genera.
The final fatwa follows the view that specification is mandatory for different genera even when order is not required.

Priority of the Learned Young Man Over the Ignorant Elder
Ruling with proof on priority of knowledge over age in imamah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on who is more deserving to lead prayer between a learned young man and an ignorant elder, and cite the Quranic and Sunnah proofs for this ruling.

Core Idea
Knowledge, not age, qualifies one to lead prayer; the learned are inheritors of the prophets.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a common question in choosing an imam: should age or knowledge prevail? The author, Ibn Nujaym, asserts that a learned young man may lead over an ignorant elder. He supports this with clear Quranic verses and the hadith that scholars are prophets' heirs. Understanding this ruling clarifies a key criterion for imamah in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The author states that a learned young man may step forward over an ignorant elder because he is better (afḍal). Recognize that the ruling is about precedence in leading prayer.
2
Locate the primary Quranic proof
The author cites Quran 39:9: 'Are those who know equal to those who do not know?' This verse establishes the clear superiority of knowledgeable persons over ignorant ones.
This proof is qaṭ‘ī al-thubūt and qaṭ‘ī al-dalāla in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the secondary proofs
The author also uses Quran 4:59, interpreting 'uli al-amr' as scholars, and the hadith that scholars are inheritors of prophets. These reinforce that knowledge commands authority.
The interpretation of 'uli al-amr' as scholars is the sounder opinion (aṣaḥḥ al-qawlayn).
4
Understanding the practical implication
The ruling means that when choosing an imam, religious knowledge is the primary criterion, superseding age. The congregation should select the most knowledgeable person available.
This does not negate other qualifications but establishes knowledge as decisive.
