Hunting Prey: Apostasy of the Shooter Makes the Game Halal
Ruling on the permissibility of eating game when the shooter apostatizes before the arrow hits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the permissibility of game when the shooter's religious status changes between shooting and impact, and explain why the moment of shooting is decisive.

Core Idea
The permissibility of game depends on the shooter's state at the time of shooting, not at the time of impact.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq concerning hunting. The key is understanding that the act of shooting is considered the method of slaughter (dhabh), so eligibility (ahliyyah) is assessed at the moment the arrow is released, not when it strikes. This principle has a surprising effect: apostasy after the shot does not render the game haram, while repentance before impact does not make it halal if the shooter was an apostate at the time of shooting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being assessed? Shooting game. What is the ruling? If the shooter is Muslim at the time of shooting, the game becomes halal. If he apostatizes after shooting but before impact, it remains halal. If he is an apostate at the time of shooting but becomes Muslim before impact, it does not become halal.
The ruling is about change in the shooter's religious status between shooting and impact.
2
Isolate the decisive moment
According to the text, the considered matter (mu'tabar) is the state at the time of shooting (ḥālat al-ramy), not the time of impact or death. This is because the shooting itself is the act of dhabh.
The arrow's flight and impact are merely consequences; the hunter's eligibility is fixed at the moment he performs the dhabh.
3
Understand the rationale
Shooting is likened to slaughter; it is under the hunter's control (yaqdiru). The hitting is not under his control after release. Therefore, the conditions of the hunter are considered when he does the act, not when the animal dies.
This aligns with the Hanafi principle that the effective cause (illa) of permissibility is the intentional act of dhabh by a qualified person.
4
Contrast with a common misconception
Some might assume that the state at death matters. But the ruling explicitly reverses this: apostasy after the shot does not harm, and becoming Muslim after an apostate's shot does not help.
Memorize the maxim: The solution is by the apostasy of the shooter, not by his Islam (i.e., apostasy after shooting does not nullify, but Islam after an apostate's shot does not validate).

Commentary
This ruling concerns the effect of the shooter's apostasy on the permissibility of consuming the game. According to the Hanafi school, the key moment is when the shooter releases the arrow, not when it strikes. If the shooter is a Muslim at the time of shooting, the game becomes halal (provided proper intention is present). If he apostatizes before the arrow hits, it is still halal because the act of shooting—the effective cause (dhabh)—occurred when he was a Muslim. Conversely, if he was an apostate at the time of shooting but becomes Muslim before impact, it does not become halal because the act was invalid at its inception. The rationale: the shooter's eligibility (ahliyyah) is evaluated at the time of the dhabh, not at the time of death.

Key Terms
الردة (al-riddah): Apostasy from Islam (act of leaving the faith).
الذكاة (al-dhakāh): The method of slaughter that makes animals permissible to eat; here, shooting is considered a form of dhakāh.

Hanafi Ruling on Mixed Slaughtered and Dead Meat
Inter-madhhab dispute on mixed meat with rebuttal of analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on eating mixed meat when the slaughtered portion preponderates, and explain why the analogy with mixed cloths for prayer is rejected.

Core Idea
Preponderance of permissible meat allows taharrī and eating; the cloths analogy fails due to the difference between choice and necessity.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Rai'iq presents the Hanafi ruling on a mixture of halal slaughtered meat and carrion when the identities are unknown. The Hanafi school permits eating after taharrī if the slaughtered meat is more, relying on the principle of ghalaba (preponderance) to remove hardship. An objection from Imam al-Shafi'i and an analogy with mixed pure and impure garments for prayer are addressed and refuted by clarifying that the cloths case operates under necessity, not choice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling on mixed meat
Locate the rule: if slaughtered meat is more, perform taharrī and eat; if dead meat is equal or more, abstain. Note the condition of preponderance.
This is the core ruling (hukm) in dispute.
2
Understand the Hanafi proof (dalil)
The proof is that preponderance (ghalaba) takes the place of necessity (darura). Small amounts of the prohibited are overlooked to avoid hardship, just as small impurities on the body or garment are excused. Cite the example of markets where some goods may be stolen, yet consumption is allowed based on the apparent (zahir).
The dalil is aqli (rational) and based on the principle of removing hardship (raf' al-haraj).
3
Recognize the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that in a state of choice (ikhtiyar), eating from the mixture is not permissible because of the doubt. He does not accept the preponderance argument.
This sets up the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
4
Analyze the cloth analogy and the initial response
An objector draws an analogy: a traveler with two garments (one impure, one pure) can perform taharrī and pray even if they are equal. Why not allow taharrī for meat in the same way? The initial response claims that rulings for cloths are lighter because prayer is permitted even with certainty of impurity due to necessity.
This response is later critiqued by the author (Ibn Nujaym?).
5
Understand the author's critique and the real distinction
The author (sahib al-Bahr) argues that the cloth case as described is actually a state of choice (ikhtiyar), not necessity. The permission for taharrī in cloths is only in choice, and in necessity, consumption is outright permitted without taharrī. The real difference is that the sheep meat case is one of choice, and taharrī is allowed only when the permissible preponderates. The cloths analogy is thus invalid because it improperly conflates the two states.
The author also notes that the claim of cloths being 'lighter' is not absolute; the prayer in impurity is only valid in necessity, not in choice.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the permissibility of eating mixed meat (slaughtered halal meat and carrion) when the identities are uncertain. The Hanafi ruling is that if the slaughtered meat is more, one may perform taharrī (strive to ascertain) and eat, but if the dead meat is equal or more, one must abstain. Imam al-Shafi'i opposes this in non-emergency situations. The Hanafi proof is that the preponderance of the permissible takes the place of necessity, and a small amount of impurity is overlooked to avoid hardship, as in cases of mixed property in markets. An objection is raised based on the analogy of cloths: if a traveler has two garments, one pure and one impure, and cannot distinguish, he performs taharrī even if they are equal, so why not allow taharrī for sheep meat? The initial answer claims that rulings for cloths are lighter because prayer is permitted even with certainty of impurity due to necessity. However, the author critiques this: the permission for taharrī in cloths is only in a state of choice (ikhtiyar), not necessity. In necessity, consumption is allowed outright. The real difference is that the sheep meat case is a state of choice, while the cloth case (as presented) is also a state of choice, but the ruling for cloths is actually from a state of necessity? The author resolves by saying that the taharrī for cloths is in a state of choice, and in necessity, consumption is permitted without taharrī. Thus, the two issues are distinct in their contexts. The author also explains the second point: the claim that cloths are lighter is not absolute; the prayer in impurity is only valid in necessity, not in choice. Therefore, the analogy fails. The passage also includes three other rulings as examples or as part of the argument: 1) wrapping a wet impure cloth in a dry pure cloth does not cause impurity if the moisture does not flow (with a refinement from al-Marghinani about direction); 2) a sheep's head smeared with blood becomes pure when burned (istihalah by fire), so broth is permissible; 3) a ruler may assign kharaj to the landowner, but not 'ushr. These illustrate the principles of necessity, istihalah, and the distinction between rights of Allah and rights of people.

الميتة (al-mayta): Carrion, meat from an animal that died without proper slaughter, which is najis and haram.
التحري (al-taharrī): A method of determining the correct ruling by using personal reasoning when evidence is ambiguous, often by choosing the most likely option.
الغلبة (al-ghalaba): Preponderance, majority; when the permissible part is more than the forbidden, it is treated as akin to necessity.

Eating Game Caught by Trap Without Slaughter: Hanafi Ruling
Ruling on permissibility of eating game caught by trap without slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on game caught by a trap without direct human slaughter and explain the condition for lawful game.

Core Idea
Game is halal only if a human directly slaughters or wounds it—a trap does not fulfill this condition.

This lesson examines a specific hunting scenario: a trap set with tasmiyah, but the animal is found dead later. The Hanafi school holds it is not permissible to eat because the condition of direct human action is missing. We will explore the reasoning and the analogy to prohibited animals from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act: setting a trap (minjal) in the desert to catch a wild donkey, saying tasmiyah, then finding the animal dead the next day. Ruling: It is not permissible to eat.
2
Locate the condition
The condition for lawful game is that a human must directly slaughter the animal or wound it in a way that causes death while the animal is alive. The matn states: 'because the condition is that a human slaughters it or wounds it.'
This condition is derived from Quranic principles on halal meat.
3
Understand the analogy
The commentator compares the trapped animal to those killed by a blow (natiḥah) or fall (mutaraddiyah), which are explicitly prohibited in Quran 5:3. The common factor: no direct human action caused the death.
Saying tasmiyah does not substitute for the condition of human action.
4
Apply the ruling
Even if the animal is found dead immediately after being trapped, it is still not lawful. The condition is absent regardless of timing.

Disliked Parts of a Sheep: Makruh and Haram
Ruling on disliked and forbidden sheep parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for each of the seven mentioned parts of a sheep and explain why blood is haram while the others are makruh.

Core Idea
Blood is haram; the other six parts are makruh tanzihi — the soul's aversion makes them impure.

The Hanafi school provides a specific list of sheep parts that are either disliked (makruh) or forbidden (haram) to eat. This distinction is based on a Prophetic hadith and the general principle that Allah prohibits impure things. In this lesson, you will learn the precise ruling for each part and the reasoning behind the difference between haram and makruh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parts and their rulings
Read the list: vulva, testicles, gland, bladder, gall, poured-out blood, male organ. Note that according to Abu Hanifa, the first six are makruh tanzihi and the seventh (blood) is haram.
Makruh tanzihi means disliked but not sinful; haram is strictly forbidden.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The matn cites a hadith narrated by al-Awza'i from Wasil ibn Mujahid: the Prophet disliked these parts. Understand that the Prophet's karahah (dislike) indicates makruh in Hanafi usul.
Hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), so it cannot establish haram.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof indicates the ruling)
The hadith uses كره (disliked). In Hanafi usul, karahah from the Prophet yields makruh, not haram. For blood, Abu Hanifa relies on Quran (5:3) which forbids blood explicitly, making it haram.
The Quranic verse on blood is qat'i, thus haram.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (difference within the school)
Abu Hanifa specifically says 'Blood is haram, and the six are makruh.' The Sahibayn likely agree; no difference recorded in this text.
Other schools may consider some of these halal or haram; the Hanafi position is based on the hadith and the principle of khaba'ith.
5
Evaluate the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The relied-upon position is that the six parts are makruh tanzihi and blood is haram. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
No tarjih needed as it is clear from the matn.

Prayer (Salat) Only for Prophets and Angels
Prohibition and exception for sending prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sending salat upon non-prophets and apply the exception of followership (tba').

Core Idea
Salat is an exaltation only for the sinless; for others, ask forgiveness.

This lesson addresses a common practice: sending blessings upon pious individuals. The Hanafi school restricts the formal prayer of 'salat' exclusively to prophets and angels, except when it follows a mention of the Prophet. Understanding the rationale behind this ruling clarifies the precise meaning of salat in Islamic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act is sending salat (e.g., 'salli 'ala') upon someone who is not a prophet or angel.
Salat linguistically includes mercy, but technically it is an exaltation.
2
State the default ruling
The text says 'لا يصلي' (do not pray upon), indicating prohibition as an independent act.
The ruling is haram because it implies an honor only due to those sinless.
3
Explain the exception
It is permissible if done tba'an (followingly), e.g., 'Salli 'ala Muhammad wa 'ala aalihi' includes companions.
The exception preserves the honor while allowing natural expressions.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Some Hanafi scholars debate whether 'Allahumma irham Muhammad' (asking mercy) is allowed; some say permissible, others consider it redundant.
The relied-upon view is to stick to the salat formula.
