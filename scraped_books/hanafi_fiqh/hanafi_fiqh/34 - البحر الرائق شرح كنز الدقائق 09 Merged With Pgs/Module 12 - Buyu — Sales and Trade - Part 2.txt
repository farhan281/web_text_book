Father's Guarantee in Sale of Orphan's Property
Ruling on invalidity of father's guarantee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that the father's guarantee in the sale of his minor child's property is invalid, and explain the underlying reason.

Core Idea
The father's guardianship does not include authority to guarantee debts from the sale.

While a father is the natural guardian of his minor child, his authority over the child's property is limited. This lesson examines why his guarantee of the buyer's payment in a sale of the child's property is not valid, contrasting it with the qadi's trustee who can guarantee under judicial order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
The father sells the minor's property. He then guarantees the price from the buyer.
2
Extract the ruling
The text states 'la damanahu' – his guarantee is not valid.
The ruling is clear: the father's guarantee is void.
3
Reason for invalidity
The father, as a natural guardian, has authority to sell for the child's benefit, but not to personally guarantee the buyer's debt. Guaranteeing creates a personal liability that exceeds his guardianship.
The father cannot be compelled to guarantee; if he does voluntarily, it is a personal obligation unrelated to his role.
4
Contrast with the qadi's trustee
Unlike the qadi's trustee, who acts under delegated judicial authority, the father's authority is inherent but limited. The trustee's guarantee becomes valid through the qadi's order; the father has no such external authorization.
This distinction highlights the Hanafi principle that guardianship types differ in scope.

Source Text

إذا باع أمين القاضي مال الصغير بأمر القاضي وقبض المشتري المبيع ولم يسلم الثمن حتى أمر القاضي الأمين أن يضمن الثمن عن المشتري فضمن صح ضمانه، وكذلك الجواب لنفسه من وصي يصح عن في أمين القاضي. والأب إذا باع مال الصغير وضمن الثمن عن المشتري لا ضمانه.
If the qadi's trustee sells the minor's property by the qadi's order, and the buyer takes possession of the sold item but does not hand over the price until the qadi orders the trustee to guarantee the price from the buyer, and he guarantees it, his guarantee is valid. And if the father sells the minor's property and guarantees the price from the buyer, his guarantee is not valid.

Commentary
This passage deals with guarantees in sales of orphan property. When the qadi's amin (trustee) sells and the buyer fails to pay, the qadi can order the amin to guarantee the price. This guarantee is valid because the amin acts under judicial authority. However, if the father (as guardian) sells and then guarantees the buyer's payment, his guarantee is not valid. The reason is that the father's role as guardian does not include the authority to guarantee debts arising from the sale; such a guarantee would be a personal obligation exceeding his guardianship. The text also mentions an unclear analogy involving an executor, but the key distinction is between judicial and natural guardianship.

Key Terms
ضمان (daman): Guarantee; assuming responsibility for another's debt.
أب (ab): Father; natural guardian of a minor child.

Executor's Sale of Estate Property Without Creditor Presence
Ruling on the validity of executor's sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the executor's sale of estate property in the absence of creditors, explain the qiyas-based proof, and apply the principle that creditors' rights attach to value not form.

Core Idea
An executor may sell estate assets to pay debts even without creditor consent — creditors' rights are in the value, not the form.

This lesson examines a key ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq on the authority of an executor. The passage addresses whether the executor can validly sell a slave from the estate when the creditors are not present. The Hanafi school affirms validity based on analogy to the testator himself and the nature of creditors' rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the executor selling a slave from the estate in the absence of the creditors. The ruling from the matn is that it is valid (sahih).
The ruling is stated as part of the matn: 'بيع الوصي عبداً من التركة بغيبة الغرماء' – the sale is valid.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is qiyas (analogy). The executor stands in place of the testator (qa'im maqam). If the testator himself sold the slave during his life, even in terminal illness without creditors present, it would be valid. Therefore, the executor's sale is also valid.
The commentator explicitly draws this analogy: 'لأن الوصي قائم مقام الموصي'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The illa (effective cause) is representation: the executor fully represents the testator in estate management. Additionally, creditors' right is in the monetary value (al-mal) not the physical form (al-sura). Sale does not destroy value; it replaces one form (slave) with another (price), preserving the creditors' claim.
The text states: 'لأن حق الغرماء يتعلق بالمال لا بالصورة والبيع لا يبطل المالية لأنه أخلف شيئاً وهو الثمن'.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school disagrees: they hold that sale without creditor presence or court approval is invalid. This is because they view creditors' right as attached to the specific asset.
Cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf_fih.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (relied upon for fatwa) because of the strength of the qiyas and the value-centric view of creditors' rights.
The relied-upon position is from Kanz al-Daqa'iq, a zahir al-riwayah text.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
قال رحمه الله : ( وبيع الوصي عبداً من التركة بغيبة الغرماء) أي يصح بيع الوصي عبداً لأجل الغرماء لأن الوصي قائم مقام الموصي ولو تولاه بنفسه حال حياته يجوز بيعه، وإن كان مريضاً مرض الموت بغير محضر عن الغرماء فكذا الوصي لقيامه مقامه، وهذا لأن حق الغرماء يتعلق بالمال لا بالصورة والبيع لا يبطل المالية لأنه أخلف شيئاً وهو الثمن
He (author of Kanz) said, may Allah have mercy on him: And the executor's sale of a slave from the estate in the absence of the creditors, i.e., it is valid for the executor to sell a slave for the sake of the creditors, because the executor stands in the place of the testator, and if the testator himself undertook it during his life, the sale would be valid, even if he was terminally ill without the presence of the creditors. Similarly, the executor, because he stands in his place. This is because the right of the creditors attaches to the property, not to its form, and the sale does not nullify the monetary value because it replaces it with the price.
Source pages p. 322

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof regarding the validity of an executor's sale of a slave from the estate when the creditors are absent. The matn states the sale is valid. The commentator explains that the executor acts as the representative of the testator (qaim maqam al-musi). If the testator himself sold the slave during his lifetime, even in his terminal illness, without the creditors being present, the sale would be valid. Therefore, the executor, who takes his place, may also do so. The underlying rationale is that the creditors' right is in the value (mal) of the property, not in the specific physical form (sura). The sale does not destroy the value; it merely converts one form of asset (the slave) into another (the price). Hence, the creditors' rights are preserved. This principle allows the executor to liquidate assets to pay debts without waiting for all creditors to be present.

Key Terms
غرماء (ghurrama): Creditors of the deceased whose claims are against the estate.
قائم مقام (qa'im maqam): Standing in the place of; executor is the testator's representative with full authority for necessary actions.
المالية (al-maliyya): Monetary value; the sale does not destroy the property's value because it is replaced by price.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
An executor may sell estate assets to pay debts even without creditor consent because creditors' rights attach to the value, not the specific form, and the sale preserves value by exchanging it for price.

Executor Selling Slaves for Debt: Loss and Reclamation
Ruling on liability when a sold slave is reclaimed and the price is lost

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an executor can recover losses from creditors or heirs after a sold slave is reclaimed or the price is lost, and apply the distinction between specific and general instructions.

Core Idea
An executor's liability depends on whether the instruction was specific or general.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rai’iq explores a common scenario in estate management: an executor sells slaves to pay debts, but the sale goes awry—either the price is lost or the slave is reclaimed by its true owner. Who bears the loss? The Hanafi ruling hinges on the nature of the instruction given by the creditors or heirs. Let's trace the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Start with the basic case: the executor sells the deceased's slaves, takes the price, then it is lost, or the slaves die before delivery. The buyer reclaims the price from the executor. The question is whether the executor then recovers from the creditors.
The key is that the executor acts as an agent for the creditors.
2
State the general legal principle
The text states: 'Whoever works for another and suffers loss in it, he recovers from the principal.' This means an agent can recoup losses from the principal if acting under their authority.
This principle is the basis for the rulings.
3
Distinguish specific vs. general instruction
If the creditors gave a general order—'sell the deceased's slaves and pay us'—the executor cannot recover because he acted on his own general authority. But if they specified a particular slave—'sell this slave'—they become liable.
The rationale: specific instruction creates a direct command, making the principal liable.
4
Apply to heirs and minors
If heirs are adults, they are treated like creditors. If they are minors, the executor cannot recover from them because minors are not legally capable of giving binding instructions.
Minors are not liable for directions; the executor bears the loss.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
رجل مات وعليه دين فباع وصيه رقيقه للغرماء وقبض الثمن فضاع عنده أو مات بعض الرقيق في يد الوصي قبل أن يسلم إلى المشتري فالمشتري يرجع بالثمن على الوصي ويرجع به الوصي على الغرماء لأنه في البيع عامل للغرماء، ومن عمل لغيره ولحقه فيه ضمان رجع به على المعمول له ولو استحق العبد ورجع المشتري بالثمن على لم يرجع الوصي بالثمن على الغرماء إلا أن يكون الغرماء أمروه ببيعه، وكذلك لو قال الغرماء له بع رقيق الميت واقضنا لم يرجع عليهم ولو كانوا قالوا بع عبد فلان هذا بالثمن عليهم لأنهم عينوه إلا أن يكون الثمن من دينهم فلا يرجع عليهم بأكثر من دينهم . ولو قال له بع هذا العبد فإنه لفلان فقال الوصي لا أبيعه ثم باعه ثم استحق وقد ضاع الثمن رجع به الوصي على الغريم ولو لم يكن على الميت دين ولكن الوصي باع الرقيق للورثة الكبار فهم في جميع هذا الوجوه كلها بمنزلة الغرماء، وإن كانوا صغاراً لم يرجع عليهم في الاستحقاق.
A man died with debt. His executor sold his slaves to the creditors and took the price, and the price was lost with him, or some of the slaves died in the executor's hand before delivering to the buyer. Then the buyer recovers the price from the executor, and the executor recovers it from the creditors, because he is acting for the creditors in the sale. Whoever works for another and suffers loss in it, he recovers from the principal. If the slave is reclaimed (by its true owner) and the buyer recovers the price from the executor, the executor does not recover it from the creditors unless the creditors ordered him to sell it. Similarly, if the creditors said to him, 'Sell the deceased's slaves and pay us,' he does not recover from them. If they said, 'Sell this slave of so-and-so,' he recovers from them because they specified it, unless the price is from their debt, then he does not recover from them more than their debt. If someone said to him, 'Sell this slave, for it belongs to so-and-so,' and the executor said, 'I will not sell,' then he sold it, then it was reclaimed, and the price was lost, the executor recovers it from that person (the one who instructed). If the deceased had no debt, and the executor sold the slaves to the adult heirs, they are in all these cases like the creditors. If the heirs are minors, he does not recover from them in the case of reclamation.

Commentary
This passage details the legal consequences when an executor sells the deceased's slaves to pay off debts, and after the sale, the slave is claimed by a rightful owner (istihqaq) or the price is lost or the slave dies. The general principle: the executor is an agent for the creditors, so if the sale is rescinded due to reclamation, the buyer gets his price from the executor, who then turns to the creditors for compensation. However, if the creditors merely gave a general order to sell without specifying the particular slave, the executor cannot claim from them; the loss falls on him because he acted on his own authority. But if the creditors specified the exact slave to be sold, they are liable. Similarly, if an unrelated third party directed the executor to sell a slave falsely claiming it was theirs, the executor can recover from that party. When the sale is to adult heirs instead of creditors, the same rules apply; but if the heirs are minors, the executor cannot recover from them because minors are not liable for directions. The key is the principal's liability: the executor recovers from whoever gave a specific command, but not from a general authorization.

Key Terms
استحقاق (istiḥqāq): Reclamation of the sold property by a rightful owner, nullifying the sale.
غرماء (ghuramā'): Creditors of the deceased; the executor sells to pay them.
وصي (waṣī): Executor of the deceased's will; in this context, manages the estate and pays debts.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
An executor can recover losses from the principal only if a specific instruction was given; general authorization places the loss on the executor.

Buyer's Recourse When Judge Sells and Price Lost
Ruling on buyer's recourse when judge sells and price lost

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct party from whom the buyer recovers the price when the judge sells a reclaimed slave for creditors and the price is lost.

Core Idea
The buyer recovers from the creditors, not the judge, because the judge acts as their agent.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Islamic commercial law: the judge sells the slaves of a deceased person to pay creditors, but the price is lost before it is distributed, and the slave is later reclaimed by its true owner. The ruling clarifies the buyer's right of recourse. Understanding this ruling clarifies the legal position of the judge as an agent and the liability of the creditors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties involved
Recognize the roles: judge (seller), creditors (beneficiaries), buyer (purchaser), and the true owner of the slave (reclaimer).
2
Trace the sequence of events
The judge sells the slave, the price is lost while with the judge, and then the slave is reclaimed by its rightful owner.
3
State the ruling
The buyer recovers the price from the creditors, not the judge.
4
Understand the legal basis
The judge is acting as an agent for the creditors; therefore, the creditors are considered the effective sellers. The buyer's contract is with them, so they bear the loss.
Key Arabic phrase: كأنهم نالوا البيع بأنفسهم

Source Text

ولو باع القاضي رقيق الميت للغرماء فضاع الثمن عنده ثم استحق الرقيق رجع المشتري بالثمن على الغرماء لا على القاضي لأنهم بمنزلة بيع الغرماء كأنهم نالوا البيع بأنفسهم.
If the judge sold the deceased's slaves to the creditors, and the price was lost with him, then the slave was reclaimed, the buyer recovers the price from the creditors, not from the judge, because they are like the sale of the creditors as if they made the sale themselves.

Commentary
This ruling addresses a scenario where the qadi (judge) sells the deceased's slaves on behalf of the creditors. If after the sale, the slave is reclaimed by its true owner, and the price has been lost with the judge, the buyer cannot sue the judge for the price. Instead, he must recover it from the creditors. The reason is that the judge, in this context, acts as an agent for the creditors, and they are considered the direct sellers in effect. This contrasts with the executor who, if he acts on his own authority, may be liable; but the judge's involvement transfers liability to the creditors because his authority comes from them or from the court in their benefit. The principle is that the principal (creditors) bear the loss when the agent's act is legally effective.

Key Terms
القاضي (al-qāḍī): The judge who sells the deceased's property to pay creditors; he acts as an agent for the creditors.
الغرماء (al-ghuramā'): The creditors of the deceased, in whose favour the judge sells the slaves.
الرقيق (al-raqīq): The slaves belonging to the deceased, sold by the judge.

Executor's Sale and Purchase: Permissible Price Variance and Self-Dealing Rules
Ruling on executor's transactions with price variance and self-dealing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid executor transaction and distinguish the intra-Hanafi positions on self-dealing.

Core Idea
An executor must avoid gross fraud; self-dealing is only allowed if beneficial, and later Hanafis ban it entirely.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the fiduciary limits on an executor's commercial transactions. It distinguishes between ordinary price fluctuation and gross fraud, and critically addresses the controversial issue of self-dealing, where Abu Hanifa and his disciples differed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the executor's role and primary duty
Recognize that the executor acts as a fiduciary (wali) for the orphan, bound to protect the orphan's interest. This duty restricts the executor's freedom in pricing.
The opening phrase 'aw bay'uhu wa shira'uhu bima yataghaban' sets the baseline.
2
Distinguish between ghabn yasir and ghabn fahish
Understand that slight price variation (taghabun) is unavoidable and thus tolerated; gross fraud (ghabn fahish) is prohibited because it undermines the protective purpose of guardianship.
The text uses 'la yajuz' for gross fraud and 'yajuz' for ordinary fluctuation.
3
Compare the executor's restriction with other agents
Note that a slave, authorized minor, or mukatab may transact with gross fraud according to Abu Hanifa because they act by ownership or permission, not strict fiduciary duty.
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree, considering such transactions as donations.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on self-dealing
Examine Abu Hanifa's view (allowed if apparent benefit), the Sahibayn's view (absolutely prohibited), and the later Hanafi fatwa (prohibited).
The text explicitly states the positions and the preference of later scholars.
5
Note the stricter ruling for judge-appointed executors and the father's privilege
Observe that a judge-appointed executor (wasi al-qadi) can never deal with himself, while the father may buy from his minor child's property at fair price.
This gradation reflects different levels of judicial oversight and natural affection.

أو بيعه وشراؤه بما يتغابن أي يجوز بيع الوصي وشراؤه بما يتغابن الناس في مثله، ولا يجوز بما لا يتغابن الناس لأن الولاية نظرية ولا نظر في الغبن الفاحش بخلاف اليسير لأنه لا يمكنه التحرز عنه ففي اعتباره انسداد باب الوصاية بخلاف العبد والصبي المأذون لهما في التجارة والمكاتب حيث يجوز بيعهم وشراؤهم بالغبن الفاحش عند أبي حنيفة لأنهم يتصرفون بحكم المالكية والإذن فك الحجر والصبي يتصرف بحكم النيابة الشرعية نظراً فيتقيد بموضع النظر وعندهما لا يملكونه لأن التصرف بالغبن الفاحش تبرع وهم ليسوا من أهلهما ولا ضرورة إليه وهذا إذا تبايع الوصي للصغير مع الأجنبي. وأما إذا اشترى شيئاً من مال اليتيم لنفسه أو باع شيئاً منه من نفسه جاز عند أبي حنيفة إذا كان فيه منفعة ظاهراً وهو أن يبيع ما يساوي خمسة عشرة بعشرة ويشتري ما يساوي عشرة بخمسة عشرة، وإن لم يكن فيه نفع فلا يجوز. وعلى قول محمد وأظهر الروايات عن أبي يوسف أنه لا يجوز بيعه من نفسه بكل حال. هذا في وصي الأب، وأما وصي القاضي فلا يجوز بيعه من نفسه بكل حال لأنه وكيل وللأب أن يشتري شيئاً من مال الصغير لنفسه إذا لم يكن فيه ضرر على الصغير بأن كان بمثل القيمة والغبن يسير. وقال المتأخرون من أصحابنا : لا يجوز
Or his sale and purchase with what is considered ordinary ghabn (i.e., market rate) - that is, the executor's sale and purchase is permissible if it involves a price that people generally accept for similar items, and not permissible if it involves what people do not accept (i.e., gross fraud), because the guardianship is based on consideration and there is no consideration in gross fraud, unlike slight fraud because he cannot avoid it, so considering it would close the door of executorship. This is unlike a slave or a minor authorized in trade and a mukatab, where their sale and purchase with gross fraud is permissible according to Abu Hanifa because they act by virtue of ownership and permission lifts the restriction; the minor acts by virtue of legal representation and is restricted to consideration. According to them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), they do not possess that right because transacting with gross fraud is a donation, and they are not eligible for it and there is no necessity to it. This is when the executor transacts for the minor with a stranger. As for when he buys something from the orphan's property for himself or sells something to himself, it is permissible according to Abu Hanifa if there is apparent benefit, for example he sells something worth fifteen for ten, or buys something worth ten for fifteen. If there is no benefit, it is not permissible. According to Muhammad and the more apparent narrations from Abu Yusuf, his sale to himself is absolutely impermissible. This applies to the executor appointed by the father. As for the executor appointed by the judge, his sale to himself is absolutely impermissible because he is an agent. The father may buy something from his minor child's property for himself if there is no harm to the child, i.e., at fair market price with slight ghabn. Commentary
This passage deals with the executor's transactions on behalf of an orphan. It distinguishes between two levels of price variance: ghabn yasīr (slight discrepancy) which is unavoidable and thus tolerated, and ghabn fāḥish (gross fraud) which is not allowed because it wastes the orphan's property. The executor, being a fiduciary, must act prudently. However, the rule differs for other parties: a slave or a minor authorized in business, or a mukatab, can transact with gross fraud according to Abu Hanifa because they own the property and their permission lifts the restriction; but Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold they cannot because it's essentially a gift. Importantly, the executor is prohibited from buying from or selling to himself unless there is a clear benefit (buying dear or selling cheap, i.e., at an apparent profit for the orphan). If no such benefit, it is not allowed. The strictest view is for the executor appointed by the judge, who can never transact with himself. The father of the orphan has a little more leeway: he can buy from his child's property at fair price. The later Hanafis (mutākhkhirūn) reportedly completely prohibit any self-dealing. This shows the careful gradation of fiduciary duties based on the nature of the guardianship.

Key Terms
الغبن الفاحش (al-ghabn al-fāḥish): Gross fraud or excessive discrepancy from market price; prohibited for executor because guardianship requires protection.
التغابن (al-taghābun): The mutual concession in price that people accept customarily; slight fluctuations are allowed.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school strictly regulates executor transactions: ordinary price variance is tolerated, but gross fraud is prohibited, and self-dealing is banned by the later authoritative fatwa.

Permissible and Impermissible Overpricing by an Executor
Explaining the limits of overpricing in executor's transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between slight and gross overpricing in an executor's transactions and state the conditions under which each is permissible.

Core Idea
An executor must avoid gross overpricing because guardianship requires care; slight overpricing is tolerated as unavoidable.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the limits of overpricing (ghabn) when an executor sells or buys for an orphan. It distinguishes between permissible slight overpricing and prohibited gross overpricing, and details intra-Hanafi differences on self-dealing and real estate.

Guided Path
1
Define the two types of overpricing
Identify the categories: slight overpricing (ghabn yasir) which people overlook, and gross overpricing (ghabn fahish) which is not overlooked.
The text uses 'ما يتغابن الناس' and 'ما لا يتغابن الناس'.
2
Understand the rationale for tolerating slight overpricing
The executor cannot avoid slight overpricing; insisting on exact value would make executorship impossible.
This is based on the principle of 'al-wilaya al-nazariyya' (guardianship requires consideration).
3
Contrast the executor with a slave/minor permitted to trade
A slave or minor trader can transact with gross overpricing according to Abu Hanifa because they act as owners, not as guardians.
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree, considering it a donation they are not eligible for.
4
Analyze the dispute on self-dealing
Abu Hanifa permits self-dealing with apparent benefit; the Sahibayn prohibit it absolutely. Identify the positions and their bases.
The text also distinguishes father's executor vs. judge's executor.
5
Apply the later scholars' restriction on real estate
Real estate sale is only allowed in three specific cases: debt on deceased, double price offer, or orphan's need. This is the relied-upon fatwa.
Al-Sadr al-Shahid endorses this view.

قال رحمه الله : (أو بيعه وشراؤه بما يتغابن) أي يجوز بيع الوصي وشراؤه بما يتغابن الناس في مثله، ولا يجوز بما لا يتغابن الناس لأن الولاية نظرية ولا نظر في الغبن الفاحش بخلاف اليسير لأنه لا يمكنه التحرز عنه ففي اعتباره انسداد باب الوصاية بخلاف العبد والصبي المأذون لهما في التجارة والمكاتب حيث يجوز بيعهم وشراؤهم بالغبن الفاحش عند أبي حنيفة لأنهم يتصرفون بحكم المالكية والإذن فك الحجر والصبي يتصرف بحكم النيابة الشرعية نظراً فيتقيد بموضع النظر وعندهما لا يملكونه لأن التصرف بالغبن الفاحش تبرع وهم ليسوا من أهلهما ولا ضرورة إليه وهذا إذا تبايع الوصي للصغير مع الأجنبي. وأما إذا اشترى شيئاً من مال اليتيم لنفسه أو باع شيئاً منه من نفسه جاز عند أبي حنيفة إذا كان فيه منفعة ظاهراً وهو أن يبيع ما يساوي خمسة عشرة بعشرة ويشتري ما يساوي عشرة بخمسة عشرة، وإن لم يكن فيه نفع فلا يجوز. وعلى قول محمد وأظهر الروايات عن أبي يوسف أنه لا يجوز بيعه من نفسه بكل حال. هذا في وصي الأب، وأما وصي القاضي فلا يجوز بيعه من نفسه بكل حال لأنه وكيل وللأب أن يشتري شيئاً من مال الصغير لنفسه إذا لم يكن فيه ضرر على الصغير بأن كان بمثل القيمة والغبن يسير. وقال المتأخرون من أصحابنا : لا يجوز للوصي بيع عقار الصغير إلا أن يكون على الميت دين أو يرغب المشتري فيه بضعف الثمن أو يكون للصغير حاجة إلى الثمن. قال الصدر الشهيد وبه يفتى وأطلق المصنف في البيع والشراء فشمل العروض والعقار وما يخاف عليه الفساد وغير ذلك وتقدم حكم العقار، وإذا كانت الورثة كلهم صغاراً وسيأتي حكم تصرفه، وإذا كانوا كباراً أو مختلطين وإذا ادعى رد الوديعة .
He (the author) said: (Or his buying and selling with what people overlook) i.e., the executor's buying and selling is permissible with the amount of overpricing that people overlook in similar transactions, and it is not permissible with what people do not overlook because guardianship is based on consideration, and there is no consideration in gross overpricing, unlike slight overpricing because he cannot avoid it, so considering it would close the door of executorship. Unlike a slave and a minor who are permitted to trade, and a mukatab, where their buying and selling with gross overpricing is permissible according to Abu Hanifa because they act by virtue of ownership and permission, and permission removes the restriction; the minor (executor) acts by virtue of legal deputyship as a consideration, so he is restricted to what is considerate. According to them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), they do not possess it (the capacity for such transactions) because transacting with gross overpricing is a donation, and they are not eligible for it, nor is there necessity. This is when the executor transacts for the minor with a stranger. As for if he buys something from the orphan's property for himself or sells something from it to himself, it is permissible according to Abu Hanifa if there is an apparent benefit, i.e., he sells what is worth fifteen for ten and buys what is worth ten for fifteen. If there is no benefit, it is not permissible. According to Muhammad and the most apparent narrations from Abu Yusuf, his selling to himself is absolutely not permissible. This is regarding the executor appointed by the father. As for the executor appointed by the judge, his selling to himself is absolutely not permissible because he is an agent. And the father may buy something from the minor's property for himself if there is no harm to the minor, such as at a fair price and slight overpricing. The later scholars among our companions said: It is not permissible for the executor to sell the minor's real estate unless the deceased has debt, or the buyer desires it at double the price, or the minor has a need for the price. Al-Sadr al-Shahid said: And this is the fatwa. The author (of Kanz) made the permission general in buying and selling, so it includes movable property and real estate and what is feared to spoil, etc. The ruling on real estate has preceded.

Commentary
This passage discusses the limits of overpricing (ghabn) in the executor's transactions. It distinguishes between slight overpricing, which is tolerated because it is unavoidable, and gross overpricing, which is prohibited because guardianship requires looking out for the orphan's interest. It contrasts this with the cases of a slave or minor permitted to trade, who can transact with gross overpricing according to Abu Hanifa because they act as owners. The passage then discusses self-dealing: buying from or selling to oneself is allowed by Abu Hanifa only if there is clear benefit (like buying low and selling high), but Muhammad and the more authentic view of Abu Yusuf prohibit it absolutely. For a judge-appointed executor, self-dealing is never allowed. Finally, it gives a ruling from later scholars (mutakkhirin) that real estate should not be sold except for compelling reasons, and this is the relied-upon fatwa.

Key Terms
الغبن الفاحش (al-ghabn al-fahish): Overpricing that exceeds the customary tolerance in similar transactions
الولاية النظرية (al-wilaya al-nazariyya): Guardianship that requires the guardian to act in the best interest of the ward

Three Categories of Executor's Claims
Classifying claims made by the executor into three categories based on acceptance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify an executor's claim into one of three categories and determine whether the executor is believed based on his authority.

Core Idea
An executor is believed on claims within his authority; the dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on kharaj payment shows the boundary.

The executor of a minor's estate often makes claims about past expenses. Hanafi fiqh classifies these claims into three categories to determine if the executor is believed. This lesson walks through the categories and the reasoning behind each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the passage and note the three categories: believed by agreement, not believed by agreement, and disputed.
The categories depend on whether the claimed act is within the executor's authority.
2
Analyze the first category
Examine examples: spending on slaves, buying property, hiring to retrieve a runaway. These are acts the executor is authorized to do, so he is believed.
Authority is based on what benefits the minor.
3
Analyze the second category
Examine examples: spending from his own money for reimbursement, paying for a relative. These are not authorized, so he is not believed.
The executor cannot unilaterally create liability for the minor's estate.
4
Analyze the third category
Focus on the dispute: paying kharaj for ten years. Abu Yusuf believes the executor, Muhammad does not. Understand the reasoning.
The difference hinges on whether paying kharaj is seen as a benefit to the orphan's land.
5
Apply the classification to new scenarios
Given a claim, determine which category it falls into and whether the executor is believed.
Use the principle of 'musallat 'alayh' (authorized acts).

Then his cases are three categories: a category in which he is believed by agreement, a category in which he is not believed by agreement, and a category in which they differed. As for the first: if the executor says, 'Your father left slaves and I spent on them', or says, 'I bought slaves and paid the price then they died', he is believed because he confessed to something he is authorized to do by law, for he is authorized over what involves improvement of the minor and spending on him and his slaves to the extent of their need, which is improvement, so he is believed. If he says, 'I bought from so-and-so the slaves that are in his hand and paid the price', and the possessor denies, he is believed regarding the orphan but not regarding the possessor, because he is authorized to buy and sell and increase the orphan's wealth, for that is improvement so that expenses do not annihilate it. If he says, 'I hired a man to retrieve a runaway slave', he is believed by agreement because hiring is an act he is authorized to do by law, for it involves improvement and preservation of the orphan. As for the second category: if he says, 'I spent from my own wealth to claim reimbursement from you', he is not believed. Similarly, if he says, 'I consumed property (by paying damages) so I paid its guarantee, and I spent on your brother who was chronically ill', he is not believed because he confessed to something he was not authorized to do, for he is not authorized to spend from his own wealth nor to spend from the orphan's wealth on his relatives before a judge's decree. As for the third category: if he says, 'Your slave ran away and I paid a reward for the runaway and paid the kharaj of your land for ten years', and the heir says 'You only paid for one year', the executor is believed according to Abu Yusuf contrary to Muhammad. Similarly, if they dispute and the land is unfit for cultivation because water has overtaken it, and the orphan says it was like that and the executor says it was cultivable, then there is the same difference. In the first case, if the land was cultivable at the time, the executor is believed by consensus after they have spent for the period of the owner because the executor confessed to something he is not authorized by law, for that is not within his authority, and authorization is realized for an act of another, so he is not believed, just as if he said, 'Your slave committed a crime and I ransomed him for such-and-such' or 'He consumed someone's property and I paid its guarantee from your wealth', he is not believed. So this, according to Abu Yusuf, that he confessed to something he is authorized by law regarding his wealth because it is a substitute for the orphan's property, and in return he takes compensation counted for him or benefit, for he cannot cultivate without kharaj, so kharaj is a substitute for his property to correspond to it, and likewise improving his land, and the executor is authorized to dispose of the orphan's property if there is improvement and benefit. If the executor brings a man to the judge and says, 'This man returned the orphan's runaway slave, so a reward is due to him, and in my hand is this orphan's property, so I give it to him', will the judge believe him? It is said this is also subject to the difference, and it is said he is not believed (if he claims spending from his own wealth). So Abu Yusuf needs to distinguish between them. The distinction is that there he claimed the reward was due in his own wealth for someone else, and he is not authorized to claim someone else's right in the orphan's property; here he claimed that the reward was from the orphan's property and did not claim the reward from his own wealth at the moment, so he was authorized to dispose of the orphan's property to revive and improve it.

Commentary
This passage categorizes the claims of an executor regarding expenses into three categories. Category 1: Claims about actions he is authorized to perform (spending on slaves, buying property, hiring) – he is believed by agreement. Category 2: Claims about actions he is not authorized to perform (spending his own money to reimburse, spending on relatives) – he is not believed. Category 3: Disputed claims like paying kharaj for many years – Abu Yusuf believes him, Muhammad does not. The text provides reasoning based on whether the claimed act falls within the executor's authority. It ends with an example about claiming a reward for returning a slave and distinguishes between claiming the reward from his own pocket (not allowed) vs. from the orphan's property (allowed).

Guardian's Trading with Orphan's Property: Refinement of the Prohibition
Refinement of the ruling on guardian trading with orphan's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a guardian trading with an orphan's property and distinguish between trading for personal benefit and for the orphan's benefit.

Core Idea
A guardian may trade with orphan's property for the orphan's benefit, not for personal gain.

The text from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq refines a general prohibition. At first glance, it says the guardian must not trade with the orphan's property. However, the author clarifies that this means trading for the guardian's own benefit. Beneficial trade like mudaraba is permitted. This distinction is crucial for those managing orphans' assets.

Guided Path
1
State the apparent ruling
Read the matn: 'He shall not trade with his property.' This is the general statement.
The wording is absolute.
2
Identify the problem with the absolute reading
If taken absolutely, it contradicts other texts allowing the guardian to trade for the orphan's benefit, such as mudaraba.
The text itself raises this objection.
3
Apply the refinement from Qadikhan
The prohibition is restricted to trading for the guardian's own benefit. Trading for the orphan's benefit is permissible.
This is the mufta bihi position.
4
Distinguish types of guardians
Fathers and grandfathers have absolute authority; guardians appointed by brothers, uncles, or mothers have limited authority similar to the original guardian.
This affects the scope of permissible trading.
5
Note the evidence from al-Mabsut
The guardian may take the orphan's property for mudaraba (trade for the orphan's benefit), but may not hire himself out to the orphan.
Self-hiring is prohibited because caring for the orphan is his duty.

Commentary

Guardian's Authority to Sell Movable and Immovable Property of an Absent Adult
Ruling on guardian selling property of an absent adult ward, with exception for real estate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a guardian's sale of an absent adult's movable vs. immovable property, explain the istihsan reasoning, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding debt.

Core Idea
Guardian may sell movables by istihsan, but not real estate unless for debt or destruction; Abu Hanifa permits all real estate sale when debt exists; Sahibayn limit to debt amount.

In this passage, the commentator explains that a guardian (waṣī) appointed for an absent adult may sell the adult's movable property by istihsan, but not real estate. The default analogy would forbid all sales, but preservation of wealth justifies the exception for movables. Real estate is inherently preserved, so no sale is needed unless there is a debt or risk of destruction. An intra-Hanafi dispute arises when the deceased owed a debt: Abu Hanifa allows selling all real estate, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad restrict sale to the debt amount. The passage also addresses mixed heirs and destruction cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Basic Ruling
Read the opening lines: the guardian may sell anything except real estate ('aqār) for an absent adult. Note that this is a departure from the father's authority (who cannot sell real estate of an adult son).
The rule is based on istihsan, not qiyas.
2
Analyze the Istihsan Reasoning
The text states that the qiyas would forbid the guardian from selling even movables, but istihsan permits it because preservation of wealth (ḥifẓ al-māl) is easier through sale of perishable movables.
Real estate is inherently preserved (maḥfūẓ bi-nafsihī), so no exception is needed.

Silence as Tacit Acknowledgment: Relative's Presence at Sale
Ruling on relative's silent presence at a sale as acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a relative's silence at a sale constitutes legal acknowledgment and state the mufta bihi view on hearing a later claim.

Core Idea
Silence at a sale can forfeit a claim, but the most sound view hears claims from non-relatives.

The Hanafi school grapples with a delicate question: when does silence speak louder than words? If a relative stands by silently while real estate is sold, does that forfeit any future claim to ownership? This lesson examines the default ruling—claim not heard—and the intra-madhhab dispute that refines it, culminating in the operative view that the claim is heard except possibly for very close relatives.

Guided Path
1
State the Default Hanafi Ruling
Identify the ruling given in the matn: if a relative is present and aware of the sale, his later claim of ownership is not heard. Understand that this is based on the principle that silence in a context where one would naturally speak is treated as acknowledgment.
The ruling aims to prevent fraudulent claims that harm buyers.
2
Identify the Two Positions Within the Hanafi School
Note that there is a dispute among later Hanafis: (1) Abu al-Layth and the mashayikh hold that even a relative's son or wife cannot claim after silent presence. (2) Al-Khulasa states the most sound view is that the claim is heard from both relatives and non-relatives.
The dispute hinges on whether silence is always acknowledgment or only for those with a very close relationship.
3
Examine the Reasoning Behind Each Position
For the first position: presence and silence amount to implicit iqrar (acknowledgment) because the relative would have objected if he had a right. This closes the door to corruption. For the second position: silence is not a universal indicator; it may be due to shyness or other reasons, so the claim should be heard and evaluated on its merits.
The reasoning involves the maxim 'al-sukut fi ma'rad al-hajah bayan' (silence in a context of need is a statement).
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi (Operative Ruling)
According to the text, the most sound view (al-asahh) is that the claim is heard from both relative and non-relative. This is the position relied upon for fatwa today.
The tarjih authority is al-Khulasa, a later Hanafi work.
5
Apply the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
In practice, if a non-relative present at a sale later claims ownership, the court will hear his claim under the mufta bihi view. If the claim is from a close relative (son, wife), some judges might still apply the stricter view, but the official fatwa is to hear it.
This shows the importance of knowing the mufta bihi in changing contexts.

Agency for Wife's Divorce and Conditional Agency
Ruling on wife's agency for divorce and conditional agency with double dismissal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why appointing one's wife for her own divorce is considered an oath and not a revocable agency, and describe the mechanism of conditional agency with double dismissal.

Core Idea
Appointing one's wife for her own divorce is an oath, not a true agency; it is irrevocable. Conditional agency can be created and terminated by successive dismissals.

This part of the passage addresses two nuanced issues: first, the special case where a husband appoints his wife to divorce herself — this is treated as an oath, giving her authority that the husband cannot revoke. Second, complex conditional agency where the appointment is conditioned on dismissal from a prior agency. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly structuring agency contracts and avoiding unintended consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on wife's agency for divorce
The author rules that if a husband says to his wife 'I appoint you for your own divorce,' he cannot later dismiss her. The wife can divorce herself.
2
Understand the reasoning
This is not a true agency because the wife acts for herself, not for the husband. It is treated as an oath (yamin) — a suspension of divorce on her action. Oaths are generally revocable, but here the husband cannot revoke because it is 'tamlik' (transfer of ownership) of the divorce decision to her.
The text says: 'li-annahu yamin min jihatihi' — it is an oath from his side.
3
Distinguish from normal agency
In normal agency, the agent acts for the principal and the principal can revoke. Here, the wife acts for herself, so she is not an agent in the technical sense. Contrast with a third party who could be an agent for divorce.
The author says: 'al-wakil huwa alladhi ya'malu li-ghayrihi wa hiya 'amilatun li-nafsiha' — the agent works for another, she works for herself.
4
Analyze conditional agency: first formulation
If the principal says 'I appoint you on condition that whenever I dismiss you, you are my agent,' he must utter dismissal twice: first to revoke the definitive agency, then to trigger the conditional one. The conditional becomes a new agency upon dismissal.
The text: 'in 'azalahu in'azala 'an al-wakala al-munajjizah thumma tanajjazat al-mu'allaqah'.
5
Analyze alternative formulation
Another valid phrasing is 'Whenever I dismiss you, you are my agent.' Here, the principal can say in the dismissal: 'I have revoked the suspended agency and dismissed from the definitive agency.' The author prefers the first method (double dismissal).
The author says: 'al-awwal awjah' — the first is more appropriate.

Possession in Conciliation for Debt Exchange

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In conciliation for debt-for-debt exchange, immediate possession is necessary to avoid riba.

Conciliation (sulh) is a flexible instrument for settling disputes, but when the consideration is itself a debt, it transforms into a sale or currency exchange. This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies that immediate possession (qabd) becomes a condition in such cases, drawing on the Prophetic prohibition of 'al-kali' bi al-kali'' (debt for debt). Understanding when possession is required is essential for valid transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is conciliation (sulh) where the consideration is a debt (e.g., dirhams for dinars). The ruling: possession is a condition for validity.
If the consideration is a specific tangible asset, possession is not a condition.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith prohibition of 'al-kali' bi al-kali'' (debt for debt). This is the evidence for the condition.
The hadith is ahad, so its transmission is zanni, but its meaning is clear.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The prohibition indicates that exchanging debts with any deferment is invalid. Since sulh for debt-to-debt implies deferment unless possession occurs immediately, it is analogous to selling a debt for a debt.
The analogy transforms sulh into a sale (bai) or currency exchange (sarf), which require immediate possession.
4
Distinguish the two scenarios
If the conciliation is for a specific in-kind asset (ayn), it is not a debt, so immediate possession is not required because the asset is already determined.
Even if the asset is ribawi (e.g., barley for wheat), the contract is valid without immediate possession because it is a specific exchange.
5
Note the practical implication
A creditor may not accept a deferred debt as settlement for another debt. The parties must exchange immediately hand-to-hand to avoid riba al-nasi'ah.
This protects against uncertainty and delay inherent in riba.

Allah Most High says: 'And do not approach the property of the orphan except in the best manner' (Quran 6:152). This passage is a ruling with proof from the Quran, discussing the conditions for a father's compromise on behalf of his minor child. Validity of Sale Under Sultan's Confiscation
Ruling on sale validity under confiscation pressure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a sale when the seller is under duress from confiscation, and distinguish between coercion to sell and mere pressure.

Core Idea
A sale is valid if the seller acts by choice, even if external pressure exists — coercion must be on the act itself.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies a nuanced fiqh point: when the sultan confiscates a person's wealth without ordering a sale, and the person then sells the property, the sale is valid in the Hanafi school. The key distinction is that the coercion (ikrah) does not target the act of selling itself; the seller exercises free choice (ikhtiyar) in the transaction. The commentator supports this with an analogy of a debtor selling property to repay a debt after imprisonment, and adds an important case from al-Fatawa about voluntary entry into the sultan's debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the matn: 'If the sultan confiscates his wealth without specifying sale, and he sells his property, the sale is valid.' The ruling is explicit: the sale is sahih (valid).
2
Analyze the Reason for Validity
The commentator explains that the person was not coerced into selling; he sold by choice (bi-ikhtiyarihi). The confiscation is external pressure, but it does not directly force the act of sale. Understand the distinction: the duress (ikrah) is on the outcome (loss of wealth), not on the transaction itself.
In Hanafi usul, ikrah mulji' (overwhelming coercion) that directly forces a specific act invalidates that act. Here, the sultan did not say 'sell'; he simply took money. The owner decides to sell voluntarily.
3
Apply the Debtor-Creditor Analogy
The commentator draws a parallel: if a creditor imprisons a debtor, and the debtor sells property to pay the debt, the sale is valid. The coercion is on repayment, not on the sale. Confirm that the same principle applies here.
This analogy reinforces that the presence of pressure does not automatically invalidate a contract; the court looks at the direct cause of the act.
4
Examine the al-Fatawa Addition
The passage adds: if someone voluntarily puts himself in a position where he owes the sultan (e.g., by taking a state loan), and then the sultan pressures him to sell, it is still not coercion because he accepted the risk from the beginning. Identify the element of voluntary assumption of risk.
This extended case shows that consent can be implicit when one knowingly enters a situation of potential pressure.

No Liability for Snatching a Debtor from a Creditor
Ruling with analogies on non-liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a debtor is forcibly removed from a creditor's hand, and apply the qiyas jaliy principle to analogous cases.

Core Idea
If the debtor runs away of his own free will, the snatcher is not liable — the loss is caused by the debtor, not the snatcher.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh: when someone forcibly removes a debtor from the creditor's custody, the snatcher is not liable for the debt. The author supports this with analogies (qiyas jaliy) from related cases: untying a slave's shackle, pointing out a thief, and holding someone back from an enemy. The common thread is that the loss results from the free choice of an intervening agent, not from the action of the person. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify this ruling and its underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Ruling
Read the first part of the passage: 'And if he takes a female debtor and a person snatches her from his hand, he does not guarantee.' The ruling is clear: the snatcher is not liable (la yadman) for the debt. Note that the debtor is described as 'female' (gharima), but the rule applies to any debtor.
The term 'gharima' indicates a female debtor, but the ruling is general.
2
Examine the First Analogy: Untying a Slave's Shackle
The author compares the snatcher to someone who unties a slave's shackle, leading to the slave's escape. The untier is not liable because the slave's flight is his own free act. Both cases involve a free agent whose choice breaks the causal chain to the original actor.
This is a clear qiyas jaliy: the 'illa is the free agency of the immediate cause of loss.
3
Examine the Second and Third Analogies
The passage adds two more analogies: (1) pointing out a thief – the theft is attributed to the thief, not the informant; (2) holding a fleeing person until the enemy kills him – the holder is not liable. Both reinforce that indirect causation (tasabbub) does not create liability when a free agent directly causes the loss.
All analogies share the same 'illa: the loss is caused by a free agent, not by the secondary actor.
4
Extract the General Principle and Apply
The underlying rule is that liability (daman) only attaches to the person whose action directly causes the loss, provided the immediate cause is a free agent. Apply this to new scenarios: e.g., if someone unlocks a debtor's cell and the debtor walks out, the unlocker is not liable for the debt.
This principle is limited to cases where the direct cause is a free agent; if the debtor were an animal or a child, the ruling might differ.

Ruling on Conditioning a Prize in Horse Racing
Explains when a prize condition is permissible or prohibited in racing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between permissible one-sided prize conditions and prohibited two-sided gambling in horse racing, and apply the Hanafi ruling using dalil and istihsan.

Core Idea
A prize from one side is permissible; from both sides it is gambling – except when a third party equalizes the risk.

In Hanafi fiqh, horse racing with prizes is a practical matter, especially for training horses for jihad. The key legal question is whether the arrangement constitutes gambling (qimar). This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the distinction between one-sided and two-sided prize conditions, using the hadith of the Prophet's practice as a basis for istihsan, and also introduces the device of a third party (muhallil) to permit a two-sided condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for each scenario
State the ruling for two-sided, one-sided, and with a third horse. Note that two-sided is haram, one-sided is ja'iz by istihsan, and the third-party arrangement is ja'iz if the third horse is equally competitive.
The prohibition of two-sided is based on qimar (gambling).
2
Examine the dalil
Read the hadith of Ibn Umar: the Prophet raced horses and bet. Understand that the bet was from one side (the Prophet offered a prize). This supports permissibility of one-sided conditions.
Dalil is a hadith ahad with zanni thubut and zanni dalala.
3
Understand the role of istihsan
Qiyas would prohibit even one-sided conditions because ownership is linked to risk (khatar). But istihsan overrides qiyas due to the prophetic practice and the public benefit of training horses for jihad.
Istihsan is a Hanafi principle that prefers stronger evidence over qiyas.
4
Check conditions for validity
The one-sided condition is valid only if the distance is bearable for the horse and each horse has a realistic chance of winning. Similarly, for the third-party device, the third horse must be kufu' (equal) to the others.
These shurut prevent exploitation and ensure the contest is not fixed.
5
Analyze the third party device
When two parties both put up a prize, they can introduce a third party (muhallil) who is not liable for loss in any scenario. This removes mutual risk and makes the arrangement permissible.
The third party must be equally competitive, not a guaranteed loser.
