Choice Between Arsh and Qisas for Defective Limb
Explains the ruling where the victim can choose between full arsh or qisas when the offender's limb is defective.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the victim's choice when the offender's limb is defective, apply the condition of defect at the time of cutting, and explain the Hanafi proof (qiyas) and rebuttal to the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
When qisas cannot be equal, victim chooses full arsh or defective qisas — no extra compensation for deficiency.

In cases of intentional injury, qisas demands exact equivalence. But what if the offender's hand is already crippled or missing fingers? Can the victim still demand qisas, or must he accept compensation? This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq explains the rule: the victim may choose between full arsh or qisas on the defective limb. If he chooses qisas, he forfeits any claim for the deficiency, a ruling defended by a subtle analogy to defective goods in sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the choice
The passage deals with intentional cutting where the offender's limb (hand, finger) is defective (crippled or missing fingers) and the victim's limb is whole. The victim is given a choice between full arsh (blood money) or qisas (retaliation) on the defective limb.
2
Analyze the condition
The defect must exist at the time of cutting. If the limb becomes defective later, the victim does not have this choice. Also note that 'crippled' (shalla) includes both usable and non-usable hands.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof (dalil)
The Hanafi proof is a qiyas (analogy) to defective goods in a sale: accepting defective quality forfeits the claim for full quality. Similarly, the deficiency in the limb is a mere description (wasf) not separately compensable; by choosing qisas, the victim accepts the deficiency.
The dalil is aqli (rational) based on qiyas khafi.
4
Compare with the Shafi'i view and note the practical consequence
Shafi'i holds that the victim can exact qisas on the sound part and claim arsh for the defective part because exact equivalence is impossible. The Hanafi rebuttal is that the deficiency cannot be separated from the limb. The practical difference: Hanafi victim gets either full arsh or imperfect qisas; Shafi'i victim gets qisas plus arsh for the deficiency.

As for the second, it is when the head of the wound (ra's al-shajj) of the offender is larger, meaning it encompassed what is between the two horns of the victim; in exacting what is between the two horns of the offender, there is an increase over what he did; and in exacting the amount of his right, he does not afflict the offender with the same blemish as the victim suffers; thus he is given a choice. Then if he chooses qisas, he starts from whichever side he wishes, because his right is in that specific location, so he may choose. If the victim's head wound is larger, he also has a choice to ensure complete exacting. In al-Sirajiyya: and the thumb is not cut for the index finger.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn from Kanz al-Daqaiq. The ruling: If the offender's limb used for retaliation (e.g., hand) is defective (crippled or missing fingers) while the victim's limb is normal, the victim has a choice: (a) accept qisas on the defective limb (which would not be equivalent in degree of harm) or (b) take full arsh (blood money). The condition is that the defect must exist at the time of cutting; if it develops later, no choice. The commentator refines the term 'shalla': it includes both a crippled hand that can still be used and one that is completely useless; ideally, the text should have specified 'useful cripple' to avoid ambiguity. The Hanafi proof (dalil) against al-Shafi'i: the remaining part is a 'description' (wasf) that cannot be separately compensated, analogous to accepting a defective item in a sale. If the defective hand falls off before the victim chooses, his right to qisas is void (no arsh either) because the right was specifically in that hand. Al-Shafi'i's view: arsh is still due because impossibility of exact qisas reveals that the true right was arsh. Hanafis rebut: in case of multiple obligations (like nafs for qisas and another crime), the one takes precedence, but here the right is singular. For head wounds (shijaj), if the offender's wound (ra's) is larger than the victim's, the victim may choose qisas (which will inflict a larger wound) or take arsh. If the victim's wound is larger, he also has a choice to ensure full retaliation. Finally, an additional rule from al-Sirajiyya: the thumb is not cut for the index finger (i.e., in qisas, only the exact finger that was cut is subject to qisas, not an analogous one).

Key Terms
أرش (arsh): Fixed compensation for a specific bodily injury, essentially synonymous with diyah for parts.
شلاء (shalla'): Crippled or paralyzed hand that is either partially or fully dysfunctional.
شاج (shajj): A head wound that reaches or exposes the bone.

Specification of Horsemen: Taqyeed as a Structural Tool
Clarifying why the ruling specifies horsemen despite applying to pedestrians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi scholars specified 'horsemen' in the collision ruling and evaluate the differing interpretations of this specification.

Core Idea
Specification is not accidental; it defines the chapter's scope — pedestrian collisions belong elsewhere.

This passage presents a critical refinement by the author of al-Bahr al-Raiq on a point of textual interpretation. Earlier commentators argued that specifying 'horsemen' in the collision ruling was not beneficial since the ruling applies to pedestrians too. The author disagrees, showing that the specification is a deliberate taqyeed that confines the ruling to the chapter's subject: injuries involving animals. Understanding this clarifies how Hanafi scholars use textual context to limit general statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the commentaries being discussed
Recognize that the author cites al-Nihayah, al-Inayah, and al-Mi'raj, all explaining the specification as incidental.
These are standard commentaries on al-Hidayah or Kanz al-Daqaiq.
2
Understand the commentators' reasoning
They argue the ruling is general for all collisions, but specification is due to frequency among horsemen.
They see the taqyeed as non-beneficial (غير قائدة) because the ruling applies equally.
3
Analyse the author's objection
The author finds this 'forced' (تعسفات) and provides a different rationale: the chapter's topic (animal injuries) excludes pedestrian collisions.
The author emphasizes that pedestrian collision is 'خارج عن مسائل هذا الباب'.
4
Conclude the legal implication
The specification is a meaningful taqyeed that keeps the ruling within the proper fiqh chapter.
This shows that textual context can override the default presumption of generality.

Commentary

The Hanafi Definition of Neighbor (Jār) in Bequests
Definition of neighbor with proofs and inclusive ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of neighbor in a bequest, identify the evidence from hadith, and distinguish which individuals are included or excluded.

Core Idea
A neighbor is defined by sharing a mosque locality, not by physical adjacency.

This lesson focuses on the core Hanafi ruling for who qualifies as a 'neighbor' (jār) in a bequest. The author of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains that the definition is based on the hadith about the neighbor of the mosque, and uses istihsan to include both adjacent and non-adjacent residents. We will also examine the Shafi'i objection and the Hanafi response, as well as the inclusion of various categories of people.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational definition
Read how the author defines the neighbor: anyone who resides in the same mosque locality. Note the use of 'urfan wa shar'an' (customarily and legally).
The mosque locality is the key criterion.
2
Examine the primary evidence
The hadith 'No prayer for the neighbor of the mosque except in the mosque' is cited. Understand that it is interpreted to mean anyone who hears the adhan. Also note the secondary use of istihsan.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut.

Analyze the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i limits the neighbor to forty houses in each direction based on another hadith. Recognize the Hanafi response: that hadith is weak.
This is an inter-madhhab dispute.
4
List the inclusive categories
The author states that the term includes the resident and owner, male and female, Muslim and dhimmi. Note the exception for slaves according to Sahibayn and for married women.
The mukatib slave is treated differently from other slaves.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, explaining the definition of 'neighbor' (jar) for bequests. The author begins by stating that a neighbor is anyone who resides in the same locality and shares the local mosque, because both customarily and legally anyone in that category is called a neighbor. The proof is the hadith 'No prayer for the neighbor of the mosque except in the mosque,' which is interpreted as applying to anyone who hears the call to prayer. Defines 'sihr' and 'khatan' for bequests, including conditions and customary variations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'sihr' and 'khatan' for bequests, identify the condition of subsisting marriage at death, and apply the role of custom in determining the scope of these terms.

Core Idea
In-laws in bequests are only mahram relatives from the spouse, and only if the marriage exists at death.

A bequest to 'my in-laws' or 'my akhtan' raises the question: who exactly is included? Hanafi fiqh provides a precise definition rooted in the Prophet's practice, distinguishing between mahram and non-mahram relatives. This lesson unpacks the definitions of sihr and khatan, the crucial condition of marriage at the testator's death, and how custom (urf) influences the scope of these terms in different regions.

Guided Path

The proof for sihr is a hadith (Sunnah ahad); for khatan, the basis is custom (urf) as recorded in Imam Muhammad's books.
2

The text emphasizes that the in-law relationship is only valid for bequest if the connecting woman is the testator's wife (or in revocable iddah) at the moment of death. An irrevocable divorce severs the relationship.

Sihr (in-laws) includes only mahram relatives from the wife. Khatan (akhtan) includes husbands of mahram relatives and, in the Kufan custom, also the mahram relatives of those husbands.

The text notes that in some lands, 'khatan' only refers to the husband of a daughter or mahram relative, excluding the husband's relatives. The general principle is that custom determines the meaning unless the testator's intention indicates otherwise.
Imam Muhammad's definition is preferred because it aligns with the broader custom at the time of the madhhab's formation.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The preferred view is that of Imam Muhammad: sihr includes all mahram relatives from the wife; khatan includes husbands of mahram relatives and their mahram relatives. Later custom (Al-Halwani) narrowed sihr to wife's parents, but the madhhab follows Muhammad.
Local custom may be considered if the testator's usage is evident.

In other lands, the term khatan is applied to the husband of the daughter and the husband of every mahram relative from him, but not to the mahram relative of these husbands. Consideration is given to custom. In al-Kafi: Free and slave, near and distant, are equal, and the word includes all. He said: The akhtan are not from the side of the testator’s father, meaning that if the testator’s wife has a daughter from another husband and she has a husband, that daughter’s husband is not a khatan. So if someone bequeaths to his in-laws from the wives of the testator, she is his in-law; thus Muhammad mentioned in the book, and others have preceded. Acting on what Muhammad mentioned is better because it conforms to custom. Only those who are in-laws at the time of death enter under the bequest, because the consideration is the state of death. This is only when the woman through whom the in-law relationship is established is his wife at death or in the waiting period of a revocable divorce. If she is irrevocably divorced with three or one irrevocable pronouncement, then it is not so. Similarly, in the matter of akhtan, only those at the time of his death enter under the bequest, which requires that the marriage between his mahram relatives and their spouses exists at the death of the testator.

Commentary
This passage defines 'sihr' (in-laws by marriage) and 'khatan' (sons-in-law/in-laws of wife's side) for bequest purposes. The author begins with the matn 'And his in-laws are every mahram relative from his wife,' providing the proof from the Prophet's freeing of Safiyya's mahram relatives, who were called his in-laws. This definition (including only mahram relatives) is the choice of Imam Muhammad and Abu Ubaydullah, while al-Sihah defines in-laws as the wife's family unrestricted. Al-Qarafi distinguishes between nasab (blood relatives one cannot marry) and sihr (relatives one can marry, like cousins), citing the Quran. Ibn Abbas' view that sihr includes seven prohibited relations from the wife's side is presented as the correct linguistic view. Consequently, in-laws include every mahram relative from the wife, such as the husband's father's wife, son's wife, etc. A crucial condition is that the in-law relationship is established only if the connecting woman is in a valid marriage at the time of death (or in revocable waiting period); a bain (irrevocable) divorce severs it. Al-Halwani notes that in the testator's customary context, in-laws are all mahram relatives from the testator's wives, but in later custom ('urfuna) it is limited to the wife's parents. The passage then discusses 'akhtan' (plural of khatan): according to Imam Muhammad, it includes husbands of mahram relatives (daughters, etc.) and also mahram relatives of those husbands. However, the teachers note this is the Kufan custom; in other lands, khatan is only the husband of one's daughter or mahram relative. The ruling is that custom is decisive. The text also clarifies that step-relations by a previous marriage do not count: the husband of the testator's wife's daughter from another man is not a khatan. The preferable view is Muhammad's definition because it aligns with common custom. Finally, the time of death is the determinant: only those who are in-laws at the moment of death (i.e., the connecting marriage is intact) enter the bequest.

Key Terms
صهر (ṣihr): In-laws: every mahram relative from one's wife (e.g., wife's father, mother, etc.) or from one's husband's side according to some; the Hanafi school limits it to mahram relatives from the wife, established by the Prophet's treatment of Safiyya's family.
ختن (khatan): A son-in-law or in-law from the wife's side; in Kufan custom, it includes both the husband of a mahram relative and the mahram relatives of that husband; in other lands, it is limited to the husband of the daughter or mahram relative.
عرف (ʿurf): Custom; used to determine the meaning of terms like 'sihr' and 'khatan' in bequests. The text states that consideration is given to the custom of the locality (al-'ibratu li-l-'urf).
