Executor's Hawala for the Orphan: Conditions and Validity
Ruling on executor's hawala for orphan's property, with conditions and intra-Hanafi differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions for an executor's hawala (debt transfer) using orphan's property and determine its validity based on benefit and solvency.

Core Idea
Executor's hawala for orphan's property is valid only if the transferee is more solvent, ensuring benefit to the orphan.

Fiduciary duties require an executor to act in the orphan's best interest. Debt transfer (hawala) carries risk; the law permits it only when it improves the orphan's position by shifting the debt to a more reliable debtor. This lesson examines the precise conditions and exceptions derived from Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
State the basic rule: the executor's hawala with orphan's property is valid (sahih) only if it is 'better' (khayr) for the orphan. Determine what 'better' means here.
'Better' is defined as the transferee being more solvent (amlā') than the original debtor.
2
Analyze the rationale
The justification is guardianship based on consideration (wilāyah naẓariyyah): the executor must prioritize the orphan's welfare. If the first debtor is more solvent, transferring the debt wastes the orphan's asset.
The risk includes the transferee's insolvency, denial, or lack of evidence, with no recourse to the first debtor if the judge does not allow revocation.
3
Examine the case of equal solvency
Al-Maḥbūbī holds it is not allowed, unlike selling at equal value. Al-Asbījābī distinguishes based on the debt's origin.
This conflict is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilāf.
4
Apply the second distinction: debt origin
If the debt arises from the deceased's own transaction, the rule is as above. If it arises from the executor's loan (e.g., the executor borrowed orphan's money), hawala is allowed even if harmful, but the executor is liable.
Abu Yusuf disagrees on the harmful scenario—he forbids it outright.

وصح احتياله بماله لو خيرا له) أي يجوز احتيال بمال اليتيم إذ كان فيه خيراً بأن يكون الثاني أملاً إذ لولاية نظرية، وإن كان الأول أملأ لا يجوز لأن فيه تضييع مال اليتيم على بعض الوجوه وهو على تقدير أن يحكم بسقوطه حاكم يرى سقوط الدين إذا مات الثاني مفلساً أو جحد الحوالة أو لم يكن له عليه بينة ولا يرى رجوع الدين على الأول. وقوله «لو خيراً» أنه احتياله إذا كان الثاني خيراً من بين يصح الأول ولم يبين حكم ما إذا كانوا سواء ففي الذخيرة واختلف الناس فيه؛ ذكر المحبوبي أن كان الثاني مثل الأول لا يجوز بخلاف بيعه مال اليتيم بمثل قيمته حيث يجوز والحوالة لا تجوز . قال الإمام الأسبيجابي في شرح الطحاوي : اعلم أن للوصي أن يأخذ الكفيل بدين الميت لأن الكفالة لا توجب براءة الأصيل، ولو احتال بماله وأخذ الكفيل بشرط براءة الأصيل فإنه ينظر إن كان ذلك خيراً لليتيم فإنه يجوز إذا كان المحال عليه أملأ حتى لو أدرك وقال أخذ الدين فليس له أن يفسخ الحوالة، وإن لم يكن أملاً من المحيل فإنه لا يجوز هذا إذا ثبت الدين بمداينة الميت، وأما إذا ثبت بمداينة الوصي فإنه يجوز سواء كان خيراً لليتيم أو شراً له إلا أنه إذا كان خيراً له فإنه يجوز بالاتفاق حتى إنه أدرك وأراد أن ينقض ذلك ليس له ذلك، وإن كان شراً له جاز ذلك ويضمن الوصي لليتيم عندهما، وعند أبي يوسف لا يجوز إذا كان شراً.
(And his hawala with his property is valid if it is better for him) i.e. it is permissible for the executor to effect hawala (assignment of debt) with the orphan's property if there is goodness in it, i.e. if the second party is more solvent (amlā'), because the guardianship is based on consideration. If the first (the original debtor) is more solvent, it is not allowed because it entails wasting the orphan's property in some ways, namely if a judge rules that the debt is dropped due to the second party's insolvency or denial or lack of evidence, and the judge does not allow the claim to revert to the first. His statement 'if it is better' means his hawala is permissible if the second party is better (more solvent) than the first. He did not clarify the ruling if they are equal. In al-Dhakhira: People differed. Al-Mahbubi said: if the second is like the first, it is not allowed, unlike selling the orphan's property at its value where it is allowed; the hawala is not allowed. Imam al-Asbijabi said in Sharh al-Tahawi: Know that the executor may take a guarantor for the deceased's debt because guarantee does not discharge the principal. If he does hawala with his property and takes a guarantor on condition of discharging the principal, then it is considered: if it is beneficial for the orphan, it is allowed if the transferee is more solvent, so that if the orphan reaches majority and says 'I take the debt,' he cannot cancel the hawala. If the transferee is not more solvent, it is not allowed. This applies if the debt was established through the deceased's own debt. If the debt was established through the executor's own debt (i.e., the executor had loaned to the orphan's estate?), then it is allowed whether it is beneficial or harmful for the orphan, except that if it is beneficial, it is allowed by consensus and the orphan cannot rescind; if it is harmful, it is allowed but the executor is liable for the orphan. According to Abu Yusuf, it is not allowed if harmful.

Commentary
This passage discusses the permissibility of an executor using hawala (transfer of debt) with the orphan's property. The main matn states that hawala is valid if it is 'better' for the orphan. The commentator elaborates: 'better' means the person to whom the debt is transferred (the transferee) is more solvent than the original debtor, because guardianship requires acting in the orphan's best interest. If the original debtor is more solvent, hawala is not allowed as it risks loss. The text also addresses the case when both are equally solvent: according to al-Mahbubi, it is not allowed, unlike selling the orphan's property at its value which is allowed. Imam al-Asbijabi further distinguishes between debts arising from the deceased's own transactions and debts arising from the executor's own dealings (e.g., the executor had loaned the orphan's money). In the latter, the executor may be liable if it turns out harmful. This shows the sublety of fiduciary duties: the executor must prioritize the orphan's interest, and any transaction that carries unnecessary risk is prohibited.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-ḥawāla): Assignment/transfer of a debt from one debtor to another; the orphan's claim is transferred to a new debtor.
أملأ (amlā'): More solvent; the transferee must be wealthier or more trustworthy than the original debtor for the hawala to benefit the orphan.
ولاية نظرية (wilāyah naẓariyyah): Guardianship based on consideration and insight; the executor must act in the orphan's best interest.

Key Takeaway
Executor's hawala for orphan's property is valid only when the transferee is more solvent than the original debtor, ensuring a clear benefit; otherwise it is invalid, with exceptions for debts originating from the executor's own dealings where liability applies.
