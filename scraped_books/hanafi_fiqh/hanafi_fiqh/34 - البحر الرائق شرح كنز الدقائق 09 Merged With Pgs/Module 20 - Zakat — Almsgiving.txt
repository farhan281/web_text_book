Priority of Obligatory Acts Over Voluntary in Bequests for Allah's Rights
Ruling on preferring obligatory acts over voluntary ones when executing bequests for Allah's rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for prioritizing bequests for Allah's rights and apply it to scenarios involving obligatory versus voluntary acts.

Core Idea
When bequests for Allah compete, obligatory acts (fara'id) take precedence over voluntary ones (nawafil).

A testator bequeaths both unpaid Zakat (obligatory) and voluntary charity from the permissible one-third. If the one-third is insufficient or heirs do not consent, which bequest should be executed first? The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: obligatory acts are given priority over voluntary ones. This lesson examines the ruling, its rationale, and the details from Imam Muhammad's al-Asl regarding distribution among multiple voluntary bequests.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between rights of servants and rights of Allah
The passage first separates bequests for people (rights of servants) from bequests for Allah. For people, the testator's order of priority is followed; for Allah, a different rule applies.
This distinction is crucial: the rule of order applies only to rights of servants.
2
Identify the rule for bequests for Allah
For bequests for Allah, obligatory acts (fara'id) like Hajj, Zakat, and expiations are given precedence over voluntary nafl acts, because the obligatory is more important.
The rationale: 'al-fard ahammu min al-nafl' – the obligatory is more important.
3
Apply proportional distribution when both are voluntary
If all bequests for Allah are voluntary and are specific (mu'ayyan) – e.g., charity to a specific poor person and freeing a specific slave – they share proportionally (tahass) according to their values, regardless of the testator's order of mention.
This is from al-Asl: 'innahuma yatahaassan wa la yubda' bi ma bada'a bihi al-mayyit'.
4
Recognize an exception: inability to execute
If the specific bequest cannot be fulfilled – e.g., the owner of the slave refuses to sell, or the slave dies – that part of the bequest fails.
This is a practical limitation mentioned at the end of the passage.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on prioritizing bequests when the one-third is insufficient or heirs do not consent. It distinguishes between bequests for people (rights of servants) and bequests for Allah (rights of Allah). For bequests for people, the strongest claim is given precedence (e.g., debt over gift). For bequests for Allah, obligatory acts (fara'id) are given precedence over voluntary ones, because the obligatory is more important. However, the passage from al-Asl clarifies that if all bequests are voluntary (nawafil) and are specific (mu'ayyan), they share proportionally; the testator's own order of priority is not followed. An example: a bequest to give one hundred to a specific poor person and another to free a specific slave. If the slave's owner does not sell the slave for the allocated share, or the slave dies, then inability occurs and the bequest fails for that part. The principle is that the testator's order of priority is only relevant for bequests to people; for bequests to Allah, proportional distribution is applied.

Key Terms
فرائض (fara'id): Obligatory acts that are fard, such as Hajj and Zakat, that are bequeathed.
نفْل (nafl): Voluntary acts of worship, like extra charity or freeing a slave for reward.
نسمة (nasamah): A specific slave (lit. 'soul'), used in context of manumission bequest.
تحاص (tahass): Proportional distribution when multiple bequests compete for limited one-third.

Priority Between Zakat and Hajj Bequests: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on bequest priority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the priority between bequests for zakat and hajj when the third is insufficient, explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and apply the principle of 'most important first' to similar cases.

Core Idea
When bequests conflict, priority goes to the most important—Abu Hanifa says zakat first; the Sahibayn say hajj first.

When a dying person bequeaths more than one-third, only the third is executable. If multiple bequests are obligatory (like zakat and hajj), which one gets paid first? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement: Abu Hanifa prioritizes zakat over hajj, while his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) reverse the order. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical implications of this ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict scenario
Recognize the specific situation: bequests for zakat and hajj when the testator's third is insufficient to cover both fully.
The classification of bequests into ranks (obligatory, expiatory, etc.) is also key.
2
State the general Hanafi rule
If bequests are equal in strength, follow the testator's order; if no order, the most important first based on typical practice.
This is derived from the maxim 'بدئ بما بدأ به' and the apparent intention of the testator.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa: zakat has precedence because it involves the right of the servant (haqq al-'ibad), which cannot be waived.
4
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: hajj has precedence because of the stronger Quranic warning (e.g., 'من كفر' regarding abandoning hajj).
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, zakat bequest is fully executed first; under the Sahibayn, hajj is first. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq supports Abu Hanifa.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, discusses the order of priority among bequests (wasaya) when the testator's third is insufficient to cover all bequests. The passage begins by stating the general rule: if bequests are equal in strength (i.e., all are obligatory or all voluntary), we follow the order in which the testator mentioned them. This is because his ordering indicates his intention about what is more important. An example is given: a testator bequeaths 20,000 dirhams to various recipients and also says 'the remainder for the poor,' but his third is only 9,000 dirhams. Since the heirs do not consent, each legatee gets a proportional share: 9/20 of each bequest is executed, and 11/20 is void. The author then cites al-Natifiy's classification of obligatory bequests into four ranks: (1) what Allah has eternally made obligatory like zakat and hajj; (2) expiations; (3) what one makes obligatory on himself by vow; (4) voluntary acts. The discussion then focuses on the priority between zakat and hajj. There is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa holds that zakat is given precedence over hajj, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that hajj is given precedence. Both agree that zakat and hajj are given precedence over expiations because of the severer warnings in the Quran for neglecting them. The author quotes verses about hoarding wealth and about the consequences of not performing hajj. The passage then concludes with the rule that if the bequests are not categorized by strength, precedence is given based on what the testator started with or what is customarily considered more important. The commentary clarifies that this principle applies only when the testator did not explicitly specify an order, and it is based on the apparent intention of a sick person who usually mentions the most important first.

Key Terms

ثلث (thuluth): The one-third of the estate that is disposable through bequests.
كفارة (kaffarah): An expiatory obligation incurred by the testator during life, such as for breaking an oath, dhihar, or accidental killing.

بدئ بما بدأ به (budi'a bima bada'a bihi): The principle that one begins with what the testator began with when bequests are equal in strength.

Bequest for Charity: Giving the Exact Item or Its Value
Rulings on bequests for charity regarding whether to give the exact item or its value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether the executor of a charitable bequest may give the value of the specified item, and explain the evidence and the preferred view.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school permits giving the value instead of the specified item in charitable bequests, preferring flexibility over strict literal adherence.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses a common practical question: if a testator bequeaths a specific item for charity (e.g., a garment or a sum of money), may the executor give something else of equivalent value? The text presents multiple scenarios and narrations, revealing an intra-madhhab disagreement. The commentator ultimately prefers the view that giving the value is permissible, relying on the principle of istihsan (juristic preference) and several transmitted cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The central question is whether a charitable bequest must be fulfilled with the exact item specified, or whether the executor may substitute its value (price or equivalent).
This is a recurring issue in wills and endowments.
2
Analyze the reported opinions
The passage reports several scholars: Ibn Muqatil permits substitution; Muhammad ibn Salama insists on the exact item; Khalaf allows multiple options (give the item, sell it and give the price, or give the value and keep the item). Note the authority of each.
Muhammad ibn Salama's view is stricter; Khalaf's is more flexible.
3
Examine the supporting precedents
The commentator cites precedents from al-Ziyadat and Nawadir: e.g., if the bequest was to sell a slave and give the price, the executor may give the slave itself. This supports the permissibility of giving value.
These cases establish that the intention is charity, which can be fulfilled in multiple ways.
4
Understand the tarjih (preference)
Both al-Faqih Abu al-Layth and the author of al-Nawazil adopt Khalaf's view. The commentator states 'wa bihi na'khudh' (with it we take), indicating the mufta bihi position.
The preferred view is that giving the value is permissible, based on later fatwa practice.
5
Apply the ruling to similar cases
The passage extends the ruling: if the bequest is of a third of wealth, houses, or slaves, the executor may sell and give the price. Also, if the testator made a vow (nadhr) to give a specific item, the ruling is similar.
This shows the general principle: the objective is charity, not the specific form.

Bequest for Charity to Specified Recipients: Liability and Fatwa
Rulings on bequests for charity when a specific person or group is designated

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequests for charity to specified recipients, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute, and apply the mufta bihi position of permissibility.

Core Idea
Deviating from a specified charity recipient may incur liability, but the fatwa allows it.

When someone bequeaths charity to a specific person or group, can the executor give to others? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position through multiple scenarios: a specific poor person, geographic groups, vows, and number of recipients. You will see how the early authorities held the executor liable, but later fatwa adopted permissibility—a key illustration of how fatwa evolves within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Determine whether the bequest is to a specific person (e.g., Zayd) or a group (e.g., poor of Makkah), and whether it is a bequest (wasiyya) or a vow (nazr).
The ruling differs based on whether the testator himself acts or commands an agent.
2
State the base ruling in Zahir al-Riwayah
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, if the executor gives to someone other than the specified recipient, he is liable (daman). This applies whether the testator is alive or dead.
The passage states this for specific poor person, but also for groups like 'poor of Kufa'.
3
Identify the ikhtilaf and the later fatwa
Abu Yusuf has a narration that allows giving to others. Later authorities (the fatwa) adopt permissibility: 'والفتوى على الجواز في هذه المسائل'.
The text also notes that for geographic groups, it is best to give within the specified area but giving elsewhere is permissible.
4
Apply the ruling to vow vs. bequest
If the testator himself made a vow (e.g., 'upon me to give to so-and-so'), he may change the recipient if acting personally; but if he commands an agent, the agent is liable for deviation.
This distinction highlights that the agent must follow instructions strictly.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The operative ruling for fatwa is permissibility: the executor may give the charity to other needy persons without liability. However, he should try to follow the testator's wishes as much as possible.
This is based on the principle of facilitating charity and avoiding hardship.
