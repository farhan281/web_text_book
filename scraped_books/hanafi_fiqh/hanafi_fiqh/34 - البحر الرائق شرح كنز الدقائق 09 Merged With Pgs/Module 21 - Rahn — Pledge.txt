Pledging Minor's Property for Father's Debt and Borrowing from Minor's Wealth
Rulings on father's pledge of minor's property and borrowing from the minor's wealth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a father's pledge of his minor child's property for his own debt, calculate the compensation when the pledge is destroyed, and identify the disputed opinions on borrowing from the minor's wealth.

Core Idea
A father can pledge his minor's property for his own debt but must compensate the child; borrowing is disputed.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses the delicate intersection of guardianship and property rights. It affirms the validity of a father pledging his minor's property for his own debt, but imposes clear liability rules. It also highlights an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether the father can borrow from or spend the minor's wealth on himself. Understanding these limits is crucial for guardians and those dealing with minors' estates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on pledge validity and compensation structure
The text states that the father's pledge of the minor's property for his own debt is valid according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. Then it explains the compensation if the pledge is destroyed in the pledgee's possession.
The compensation depends on the value of the pledge relative to the debt.
2
Apply the compensation rule to two scenarios
If the value of the pledge equals or is less than the debt, the father guarantees the full value. If the value exceeds the debt, he guarantees only the debt amount, not the surplus.
The excess is considered a loss borne by the minor, not the father.
3
Identify the dispute on borrowing from minor's wealth
Shams al-Aimma (in Sharh Kitab al-Rahn) allows the father to borrow from the minor's wealth for himself. Shaykh al-Islam (in his commentary) forbids it.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, with no clear mufta bihi.
4
Note the additional narration on spending from minor's wealth
Shams al-Aimma al-Halwani narrates via Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Yusuf from Abu Hanifa that the father may spend from his son's wealth on himself.
This suggests a more permissive view, but it is from nawadir.
5
Determine the practical implications and caution
Given the dispute and the rarity of the permissive narration, the cautious position is to avoid borrowing or spending from the minor's wealth except for the minor's own needs.
The father must always act in the minor's best interest.

Commentary
This concluding passage discusses three related matters. First, if a father pledges his minor child's property as security for his own debt, and the pledge is destroyed in the pledgee's possession, the father must compensate the child. The compensation is equal to the value of the pledge if that value is equal to or less than the debt; if the value exceeds the debt, the father only guarantees the debt amount, not the surplus. This is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (عندهما). Second, there is a dispute about whether the father can borrow from his child's wealth for himself. Shams al-Aimma (likely al-Sarakhsi) states he can, while Shaykh al-Islam (al-Halwani or another) says he cannot. Third, al-Halwani narrates via Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Yusuf from Abu Hanifa that the father may spend from his son's wealth on himself. This indicates a more expansive view of the father's authority to use the minor's property for his own maintenance, at least according to this narration. The lack of full agreement shows caution is needed.

Key Terms
رهن (rahn): Pledge or mortgage of property as security for a debt.
استقرض (istaqrada): To borrow money, here from the minor's wealth.
