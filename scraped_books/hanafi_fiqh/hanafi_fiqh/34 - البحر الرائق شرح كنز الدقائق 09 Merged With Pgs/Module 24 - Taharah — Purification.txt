Gesture and Writing of the Mute: Hanafi Position and Shafi'i Dispute
Ruling on mute's gesture and writing, with Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi ruling on the legal validity of a mute's gesture from that of a person with a temporary speech impediment, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument.

Core Idea
A mute's gesture is like speech because it is customary; a temporary speech impediment is not.

This lesson addresses a classic fiqh dispute: can a mute person's gesture or writing substitute for spoken expression in legal acts? The Hanafi school affirms this for the permanently mute, while the Shafi'i school equates any speech-impaired person. Understanding the basis of each view sharpens your grasp of how custom ('urf) and analogy (qiyas) operate in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the opening sentence: the mute's gesture and writing are like verbal expression for all legal acts (marriage, divorce, sale, etc.).
2
Compare the mute vs. the locked tongue
Note the exception: a person with a temporary speech impediment (mu'taqal lisan) is not included. His gestures are not valid unless the condition becomes prolonged.
Duration estimates vary: one month to six months.
3
Understand the Shafi'i objection and Hanafi response
Imam al-Shafi'i argues no difference: both cannot speak. The Hanafi response: the mute's incapacity is permanent, making his gestures customary; the locked tongue may recover, so his gestures are unreliable.
4
Examine the proof from hadith
The Prophet (ﷺ) indicated the month with his fingers, demonstrating that gesture can be a valid expression.
This is a zanni proof used for analogy.
5
Classify the types of writing
Writing is of three levels: clear and formal (binding), clear but informal (needs corroboration), and unclear (void). Understand each.

Commentary
This passage opens the section on miscellaneous issues (masa'il shatta). The author states the ruling: the gesture (ima') of a mute person and his writing are equivalent to verbal expression (bayan) for all legal transactions—marriage, divorce, sale, etc.—unlike a person with a temporary speech impediment (mu'taqal al-lisan). The text then presents the Shafi'i view that there is no difference between the two. The Hanafi response: the gesture of a mute is customary (ma'hudah) since his condition is permanent, whereas the one with a locked tongue may recover, so his gestures are not reliable unless the impediment becomes prolonged. Various durations are given for when a temporary condition becomes permanent: one month, six months, etc. If the condition lasts until death, according to a narration from Abu Hanifa, his gestures are valid. The fatwa is on this view. The passage distinguishes between congenital and temporary muteness. Then it discusses the types of writing: clear and formal (with heading and signature) is binding; clear but informal (on walls) requires corroboration; unclear (on air/water) is void. An important nuance: the mute's writing is a proof for everything except hudud penalties, but the commentator notes this exception is problematic because the general proof would seem to include hudud. The text ends abruptly with a reference to al-Hidayah on retaliation.

Ruling on Wet Impure Cloth Wrapped in Dry Pure Cloth
Ruling on impurity transmission through moisture with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which impurity transfers from a wet impure object to a dry pure one, and apply the Hanafi principle of flowing moisture to similar cases.

Core Idea
Impurity transfers only when moisture visibly separates—mere contact does not render the pure impure.

When a wet impure cloth is wrapped in a dry pure one, does the pure become impure? The Hanafi school distinguishes based on whether moisture actually transfers. In this passage, the author explains the condition and then extends the rule to other scenarios through qiyas, showing the practical application in daily situations like sleeping on a mattress or walking on wet ground.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the basic ruling and its condition.
Read the opening sentence: wrapping a wet impure cloth in a dry pure cloth; if moisture appears on the pure cloth but does not drip, the pure cloth does not become impure. The condition is that moisture must separate or be absorbed in a perceptible amount.
This is the primary hukm.
2
2. Trace al-Marghinani's elaboration on which cloth is wet/dry.
Al-Marghinani provides a distinction: if the dry cloth is pure and the wet is impure, the dry takes a little impurity; if the dry is impure and the wet is pure, the wet does not take impurity. This refinement clarifies the rule based on which object is the source of moisture.
This is an expansion of the basic ruling.
3
3. Understand the underlying principle (illa).
The common factor is that impurity transfers only when moisture from the impure source flows to the pure object in a tangible way (separation or absorption). Mere vapor or temporary dampness is insufficient.
This is the operative cause (illa) for qiyas.
4
4. Apply the principle to Qadikhan's fatwas.
Qadikhan applies the same logic: dried semen on a mattress, if sweat causes moisture to transfer to the body, then impurity transfers; walking on wet ground with a dry foot also transfers impurity because the wet ground moistens the foot. Conversely, if no moisture transfer occurs, purity remains.
These are analogical extensions (qiyas) of the original case.

Commentary

Purification by Fire: Istihalah in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling with proof on istihālah by fire

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on istihalah by fire and apply it to similar cases of transformation.

Core Idea
Fire purifies by consuming or transforming impurity—istihalah is a valid method of taharah in the Hanafi school.

Can a burnt sheep's head be used for soup if the blood has been consumed by fire? The Hanafi school says yes, and even extends this principle to ovens and baker's tools. In this lesson, we examine the proof and scope of purification by istihalah (transformation), a key concept in Hanafi taharah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the first sentence: a sheep’s head coated with blood is burned until the blood disappears, then soup is made. The ruling: permissible (jāz). What hukm level is this?
The ruling is permissibility (ibāḥah)—the meat is halal because the impurity is removed.
2
Locate the proof (dalīl)
The author says burning is like washing (ka-l-ghusl). Why? Because fire consumes the impurity or transforms it into ash. This is an analogy (qiyās) to other cases of istihālah.
The proof is not a direct text but an analogy based on the effective cause: complete transformation.
3
Examine the analogical reasoning
Three analogies are given: wine turning to vinegar, pig becoming salt in a salt-pan, and manure burning to ash. Identify the common element: total change of substance (istihālah).
The original impurity no longer exists in its form; a new pure substance emerges.
4
Apply the principle to other cases
The passage extends the ruling: if an oven becomes impure, fire purifies it so bread does not become impure. Similarly, baker's tools are purified by fire. How does this map to the same istihālah logic?
Fire burns away the impurity or changes its nature.
5
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The Hanafi school accepts istihālah as purification. Some other schools require washing with water even if the substance is transformed. Why the difference? The Hanafi position gives weight to the complete removal of impurity.
This affects many practical cases, e.g., purification of cooking utensils.

Commentary

The Hanafi school accepts istihalah as a method of purification, unlike some schools that require washing with water. This ruling is based on the principle that impurity is a quality that can be removed by changing the substance.

Comprehensive rulings on circumcision and medical practices

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on circumcision, explain its timing based on capability, and identify the permissibility of related medical procedures for children, animals, and pregnant women.

Core Idea
Circumcision is a strongly emphasized Sunnah in Hanafi fiqh, so important that the Imam may fight a town that abandons it.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq covers a cluster of rulings on circumcision and medical ethics. The central legal question is the status and timing of male circumcision, but the text also addresses female circumcision, medical treatments for minors and animals, and precautions for pregnant women. The underlying principle is that causing pain is only allowed when beneficial, and the Sunnah of circumcision brings both spiritual and physical benefit.

صبي حشفته ظاهرة بحيث لو رآه انسان ظنه مختوناً ولا تقطع جلده ذكره إلا بتشديد ترك كشيخ أسلم وقال أهل النظر لا يطيق الختان) لأن قطع جلده لتنكشف الحشفة فإن كانت الحشفة طاهرة فلا حاجة إلى القطع، وإن كان يواري الحشفة يقطع الفضل، ولو ختن ولم تقطع الجلدة كلها ينظر؛ إن قطع أكثر من النصف يكون ختاناً لأن للأكثر حكم الكل، وإن قطع النصف فما دونه لا يعتد به لعدم الختان حقيقة وحكماً، والأصل أن الختان سنة كما جاء في الخبر وهو من شعائر الاسلام وخصائصه حتى لو اجتمع أهل بلد على تركه يحاربهم الأمام فلا يترك إلا للضرورة وعذر الشيخ الذي لا يطيق ذلك ظاهر فيترك قال رحمه الله : ( ووقته سبع سنين) أي وقت الختان سبع سنين ، وقيل لا يختن حتى يبلغ لأن الختان للطهارة ولا طهارة عليه قبله فكان ايلاماً قبله من غير حاجة، وقيل أقصاه اثنا عشر سنة، وقيل تسع سنين، وقيل وقته عشر سنين لأنه يؤمر بالصلاة إذا بلغ عشراً اعتياداً وتخلفاً فيحتاج إلى الختان لأنه شرع للطهارة، وقيل إن كان قوياً يطيق ألم الختان يختن وإلا فلا وهو أشبه بالفقه وقال أبو حنيفة : لا علم لي بوقته ولم ير وعن أبي يوسف ومحمد فيه شيء وأن المشايخ اختلفوا فيه. وختان المرأة ليس بسنة وإنما مكرمة للرجال في لذة الجماع وقيل سنة، والأصل أن ايصال الألم إلى الحيوان لا يجوز شرعاً إلا لمصالح تعود إليه، وفي الختان إقامة السنة وتعود إليه أيضاً مصلحته لأنه جاء في الحديث «الختان سنة يحارب على تركها وكذا يجوز كي الصغير وربط قرحته وغيره من المداواة، وكذا يجوز ثقب اذن البنات الأطفال لأن فيه منفعة للزينة، وكان يفعل ذلك من وقته إلى يومنا هذا من غير نكير والحامل لا تفعل ما يضر بالولد ولا ينبغي لها أن تحتجم ما لم يتحرك الولد فإذا تحرك فلا بأس ما لم تقرب الولادة فإذا قربت فلا تحتجم لأنه يضره. وأما الفصد فلا تفعله مطلقاً ما دامت حبلى لأنه يخاف على الولد منه، يجوز فصد البهائم وكيها وكل علاج فيه منفعة لها، وجاز قتل ما يضر من البهائم كالكلب العقور والهرة إذا كانت تأكل الحمام والدجاج لإزالة الضرر ويذبحها ولا يضر بها لأنه لا يفيد فيكون معذباً لها بلا فائدة
A boy whose glans is apparent such that if a person saw him he would think he is circumcised, and the skin of his penis is not cut except with difficulty, like an old man who embraced Islam and the people of insight said he cannot bear circumcision. Because cutting his skin is to uncover the glans; if the glans is pure [already exposed], there is no need to cut. And if it covers the glans, he cuts the excess. And if he was circumcised but the whole skin was not cut, it is considered: if he cut more than half, it is circumcision because the majority has the ruling of the whole; if he cut half or less, it is not counted due to lack of circumcision in reality and ruling. The basis is that circumcision is Sunnah as came in the report, and it is among the rituals and characteristics of Islam, such that if the people of a town agree to abandon it, the imam fights them; it is not abandoned except for necessity. And the excuse of the old man who cannot bear it is apparent, so it is left. He (the author) said: 'And its time is seven years.' That is, the time of circumcision is seven years. And it is said: he should not be circumcised until he reaches puberty, because circumcision is for purity and there is no purity required before him, so it would be painful before him without need. And it is said: the maximum is twelve years; and it is said: nine years; and it is said: its time is ten years because he is commanded to pray when he reaches ten as a habit and prevention, so he needs circumcision because it is legislated for purity. And it is said: if he is strong and can bear the pain of circumcision, he is circumcised; otherwise not, and this is more in line with fiqh. Abu Hanifa said: I have no knowledge of its time and did not see anything from Abu Yusuf and Muhammad about it, and the scholars differed over it. Female circumcision is not Sunnah; rather it is an honour for men in the pleasure of intercourse, and it is said it is Sunnah. The basis is that causing pain to an animal is not permissible legally except for benefits that revert to it. In circumcision, there is the establishment of the Sunnah and also benefit to him, because it came in the hadith: 'Circumcision is a Sunnah, and fighting is waged upon its abandonment.' Similarly, cauterizing a child, tying his wound, and other treatments are permissible. Similarly, piercing the ears of young girls is permissible because there is benefit in adornment, and it has been done from his time to our day without disapproval. A pregnant woman should not do what harms the child; she should not be cupped as long as the child has not moved; if it moves, there is no harm as long as childbirth is not near; if it is near, she should not be cupped because it harms him. As for phlebotomy, she should not do it at all while pregnant because the child is feared from it. Phlebotomy of animals, cauterizing them, and any treatment that benefits them is permissible. Killing harmful animals such as a biting dog or a cat that eats pigeons and chickens is permissible to remove harm, and it should be slaughtered and not harmed [unnecessarily] because that is not beneficial and would be torturing it without benefit.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding circumcision. The passage first addresses a boy whose glans is already exposed, so circumcision is not necessary because the purpose (exposure of the glans) is already achieved. Cutting would cause unnecessary pain. Then it discusses the timing of circumcision: the preferred time is seven years, but there are varying opinions; the most fiqh-consistent view is to consider the child's ability to bear pain. Abu Hanifa expressed no fixed time. Female circumcision is not a Sunnah in the Hanafi school but merely an honour; some consider it permissible but not recommended. The passage then extends the principle of causing pain to animals and humans: it is only allowed for a benefit. Thus, cauterization, piercing ears for adornment, and medical treatments are permissible. Pregnant women should avoid harmful procedures. Finally, killing harmful animals is allowed if done properly, not for torture.

Key Terms
الختان (al-khitan): Circumcision, specifically of males, and optionally of females as an honour.
مكرمة (mokrama): An honour or noble act, not a religious requirement.
الفصد (al-fasd): Phlebotomy; bloodletting from a vein.
