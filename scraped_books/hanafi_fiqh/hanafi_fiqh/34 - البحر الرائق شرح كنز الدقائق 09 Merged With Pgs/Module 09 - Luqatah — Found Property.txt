Ruling on Killing a Night Intruder
Axiomatic ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq on killing a night thief

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing a night intruder who steals and identify the conditions that trigger the rule.

Core Idea
Killing a night thief who escapes with stolen goods incurs no liability.

This lesson presents a clear ruling from the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq: if a person enters a house at night, takes stolen goods, and the owner chases and kills him, there is no financial or penal liability. The justification lies in the special vulnerability of nighttime when help cannot be summoned. This is an axiomatic ruling that students should commit to memory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act (dakhala laylan wa akhraja al-sibraqah) and the ruling (la shay'a 'alayhi: no liability)?
2
Note the key conditions
The intruder entered at night, took stolen goods (sibraqah), and the owner followed and killed him.
3
Understand the rationale
Night entry makes defense more permissible; the intruder is an aggressor endangering his own life.
4
Memorize the ruling
Use the Arabic phrase: 'wa man dakhala 'alayhi ghairuhu laylan fa akhraja al-sibraqah fa attaba'ahu fa qatalahu fa la shay'a 'alayhi.'

Commentary
This passage gives the ruling for a specific scenario: if a person enters a house at night, steals property (sabraqah or subraqah), and the owner follows him and kills him, the owner is not liable for any punishment or compensation. This is because the night intruder is an aggressor and a thief, and the owner's right to defend his property is particularly strong at night, when help is not readily available. The final broken phrase 'li tawahhuma nafsan' is unclear, but it might mean 'because he (the intruder) endangered his own life' or due to presumptions of intent. This is a straightforward ruling statement from the matn.

Key Terms
السبرقة (al-sibraqah/al-subraqah): Stolen goods (a type of plunder or which is hidden/secretly taken).
دخل ليلاً (dakhala laylan): To enter at night, often with criminal intent, making the intruder vulnerable to deadly force.

Ruling on Finding a Stray Animal and Conditions for Guarantee
Ruling on the liability of one who finds a stray animal and his intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a finder of a stray animal becomes liable (damin) according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Liability depends on intention, ability, and whether the animal belongs to a villager or outsider.

The ruling on finding a stray animal (luqatah) is nuanced: not every finder becomes liable. The passage distinguishes based on the finder's intention, the time of day, the animal's origin, and the extent of movement. It also presents the view of Qadi Ali al-Sughdi, which the author endorses as correct. This lesson will guide you through these conditions so you can determine liability in real-life scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the finder's intention
Note whether the finder intends to return the animal or to prevent the owner from getting it. This affects the initial ruling.
If intention is to prevent owner, immediate guarantee.
2
Distinguish daytime scenario
If the animal is taken during the day, the ruling depends on ownership: does it belong to a villager or an outsider?
Nighttime scenario is not covered here.
3
Apply the rule for outsiders
If the animal is from outside the village, it is luqatah. The finder must announce it (ishhad) if possible; if unable, he is excused.
Failure to announce when able leads to guarantee.
4
Apply the rule for villagers
If the animal belongs to the village, the finder is a guarantor from the moment he takes it, regardless of intention.
Immediate liability due to ease of locating owner.
5
Incorporate al-Sughdi's ruling on driving
If the finder drives the animal only enough to remove it from his property, he is not liable; driving beyond that makes him a usurper (ghasib).
This adds a condition on the extent of movement.

Source Text

منه كان نيته عند الأخذ أن يمنعه من صاحبه يضمن وإن كان نيته أن يرد إلا أنه لم يقدر لم يضمن فقيل : إن كان ذلك بالنهار ؟ قال : إن كان لغير أهل القرية كان لقطة، فإن ترك الإشهاد مع القدرة عليه يضمن، وإن لم يجد شهوداً يكون عذراً وإن كان لأهل القرية فكما أخرجه يكون ضامناً. وقال القاضي علي السغدي وإن وجد في زرعه دابة فساقها بقدر ما يخرجها عن ملكه لأن يكون ضامناً فإذا ساق وزاد وراء ذلك القدر يصير غاصباً بالسوق والصحيح ما قاله القاضي علي السغدي
From him: If his intention at taking [the animal] was to prevent it from its owner, he guarantees. If his intention was to return it but he was unable, he does not guarantee. It was said: If that was during the day? He said: If it belongs to someone not from the village, it is a stray. If he leaves bearing witness while able to do so, he guarantees; if he does not find witnesses, it is an excuse. If it belongs to the village people, then he is a guarantor from the time he takes it. And Qadi Ali al-Sughdi said: If he finds an animal in his field and drives it to the extent of removing it from his property, he does not become a guarantor; if he drives it beyond that extent, he becomes a usurper by driving. The correct is what Qadi Ali al-Sughdi said.
Source pages p. 135

Commentary
This is a ruling on the guarantee (damān) of a person who finds a stray animal (lāqiṭ). The passage distinguishes based on intention and ability. If the finder intends to keep the animal away from its owner, he is liable. If he intends to return it but is unable (e.g., the owner is unknown or absent), he is not liable. However, if he takes the animal during the day, the ruling depends on whether the animal belongs to a villager or an outsider. For an outsider's animal, it is considered a stray, and the finder must seek witnesses if possible; otherwise he is excused. For a villager's animal, the finder is immediately liable because he could notify the owner. Qadi Ali al-Sughdi adds that if a person finds an animal in his field and drives it only enough to remove it from his property, he is not liable; but if he drives it further, he becomes a usurper (ghāṣib). The author affirms that al-Sughdi's view is correct.

Key Terms
لقطة (luqaṭah): A found object (here an animal) that is lost but not in the finder's ownership.
ضامن (ḍāmin): Liable/guarantor for the value of the animal if lost or damaged due to negligence.

Judge's Authority to Lend Entrusted Property
Ruling on permissibility of lending under custodianship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a judge lending property of an absent person, orphan, or found item, and explain why it is permissible for the judge but not for the father, executor, or finder, except after announcement.

Core Idea
The judge may lend because he can recover; others cannot unless they gain recovery power.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the Hanafi fiqh on who may lend property entrusted to them. The judge enjoys a special authority to lend the wealth of an absent person, an orphan, or a found item, while the father, executor, and finder are generally prohibited. The key is the ability to recover the loan (istikhlāṣ), which the judge possesses but others lack. However, there is an exception for the finder after proper announcement, as he may then even give the item as charity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the actors
The act is lending (qard) property belonging to another. The actors considered are the judge (qāḍī), the father (ab), the executor (waṣī), and the finder (multaqiṭ).
2
State the default ruling for each actor
The judge may lend; the father, executor, and finder may not. This is the basic distinction drawn in the text.
The ruling for the judge is one of permission (jawāz), not obligation.
3
Identify the decisive factor
The text gives the reason: the judge is 'able to recover' (qādir ʿalā al-istikhlāṣ). He can enforce repayment through his judicial authority. The others lack this power.
The ability to recover ensures that preservation (ḥifẓ) of the property is not lost by lending.
4
Analyze the exception for the finder
After the announcement period, the finder may lend the found item to the poor. This is because at that point he could even give it as charity, so lending is more cautious and still beneficial.
This shows that once the item becomes essentially the finder's trust with disposal rights, lending is permissible.
5
Understand the underlying principle
Lending must not lead to waste (taḍyīʿ). If the lender cannot recover the loan, the property is at risk of being lost without benefit. The judge's authority prevents waste; the others' inability would cause waste.
This aligns with the broader Hanafi principle of preventing harm (ḍarar).

Commentary
This passage states a ruling: A judge (qadi) may lend out the money of an absent person (gha'ib), a minor (tifl), or a found object (luqtah) because the judge has the authority to recover the loan if needed. This is contrasted with the father (ab), executor (wasi), and finder (multaqit), who cannot lend such property because they cannot effectively recover it; lending would be a waste. However, an exception: if the finder has announced the lost item for the prescribed period and no owner came, he may lend it to the poor, because he could even give it as charity, so lending is even more appropriate.

Key Terms
لقطة (luqṭah): A lost item found by someone.
استخلاص (istikhlāṣ): The ability to recover or retrieve something.

Key Takeaway
