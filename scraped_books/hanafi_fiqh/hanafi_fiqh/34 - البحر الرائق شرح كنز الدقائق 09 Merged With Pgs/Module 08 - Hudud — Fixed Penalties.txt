Shibh al-'Amd: Quasi-Intentional Killing in Hanafi Fiqh
Inter- and intra-madhhab dispute on shibh al-'amd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shibh al-'amd, state its ruling (no qisas, diyah on 'aqilah), explain the proof from hadith, and distinguish the Hanafi position from both the Shafi'i and intra-Hanafi views on intention and kaffarah.

Core Idea
Shibh al-'amd is not intentional killing: no qisas, diyah on the 'aqilah, and disputed kaffarah.

Shibh al-'amd (quasi-intentional killing) occupies a middle ground between pure error and intentional murder. This lesson unpacks its definition, the primary proof from the hadith of the two women of Hudhayl, and the key differences between the Hanafi school and others. We also explore the internal Hanafi debate regarding certain lethal methods and whether expiation (kaffarah) is obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Understand the classification
Recognize that shibh al-'amd requires intent to perform the act (e.g., striking) but not intent to cause death. This distinguishes it from both error (no intent) and intentional killing (intent to kill).
2
State the Hanafi ruling and its basis
According to Abu Hanifa, shibh al-'amd does not entail qisas (retaliation). The blood money (diyah) is payable by the 'aqilah (tribal group). The primary proof is the hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) ruled for diyah on the 'aqilah for a killing that was not clearly intentional.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i considers repeated blows and methods like throwing from a height as indicative of intent to kill, thus requiring qisas. The Hanafi response emphasizes that the killer intended the act alone, not death.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ from Abu Hanifa on certain methods (e.g., throwing from a mountain, drowning), viewing them as 'amd requiring qisas. Understand the basis of this disagreement.
5
Analyze the kaffarah dispute
The passage records a debate on whether kaffarah (expiation) is obligatory in shibh al-'amd. Some texts attribute to Abu Hanifa that kaffarah is not wajib because the sin of the blow is complete. Others argue that kaffarah is attached to the killing (which is mistaken) and thus required. Understand the reasoning on both sides.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute and also includes an intra-Hanafi dispute. It discusses shibh al-'amd (quasi-intentional killing) as defined by the Hanafi school. The author begins by citing a hadith about two women fighting, which serves as a proof for the ruling that the diyah (blood money) for a fetus is a slave (ghurrah) and that the woman who killed is killed in retaliation. He then reports another hadith with a similar incident from Hudhayl. Hamal ibn Malik's objection is addressed by the Prophet's statement 'This is from the brothers of the soothsayers,' indicating that the questioner's objection is invalid. The passage then focuses on shibh al-'amd: if a person strikes another repeatedly or in a manner that is not clearly intentional to kill, Abu Hanifa rules that it does not entail qisas (retaliation) because it is not pure intentional killing (qatl 'amd). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ on the matter of successive blows (muwalat). Al-Shafi'i considers such acts as intentional and thus qisas is required. The passage then gives examples of acts that are shibh 'amd according to Abu Hanifa but intentional according to the Sahibayn (e.g., throwing from a mountain, drowning). The discussion then moves to the obligation of kaffarah (expiation) in shibh al-'amd. Some texts indicate kaffarah is obligatory because it is erroneous in one aspect. However, the author reports from the Hidayah that according to Abu Hanifa, kaffarah is not obligatory because the sin is complete, and kaffarah is a form of leniency only for pure error. The counter-argument is that the sin is the blow (which was intentional), not the killing (which was unintended), and kaffarah is attached to killing, not the blow, so it is required. The passage also confirms that diyah (blood money) is on the 'aqilah (tribal insurer) because it is considered erroneous in some respect, and the killer is thus excused and leniency is applied. The killer is deprived of inheritance from the victim, even more so than in pure error because of the intent in the act. Overall, shibh al-'amd is treated like error except concerning sin and the aggravated nature of the diyah.

Key Terms
شبه العمد (shibh al-'amd): Quasi-intentional killing where the killer intended the act (e.g., striking) but did not intend to cause death; it is distinct from pure error (khata') and pure intent ('amd).
القود (al-qawd): Retaliation/retribution in kind; here referring to qisas for intentional killing.

Qisas for a Mukatab: Certainty of Entitlement vs. Ambiguity of Cause
Commentary on the ruling for a mukatab killed intentionally

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas for a mukatab in both estate-and-heir scenarios and explain the Sahibayn's argument for overriding cause ambiguity.

Core Idea
Certainty of entitlement overrides ambiguity of cause – qisas is wajib when the master is definitely the heir.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning qisas for a mukatab (a slave under manumission contract). When a mukatab is killed intentionally, does the master always deserve qisas? The answer depends on two factors: whether the mukatab left an estate (wafā’) and who his heirs are. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and Imam Muhammad differ on the role of cause ambiguity. We will unpack their proofs and the basis for the mufta bihi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The commentary considers two cases: (a) the mukatab leaves an estate (property sufficient to fulfill the kitaba) and his only heir is his master; (b) the mukatab leaves no estate and has an heir other than the master. In scenario (b) all agree on the ruling; the dispute centers on scenario (a).
The term wafā’ here means the estate left covering the kitaba debt.
2
State the positions on scenario (a)
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that qisas is obligatory. Imam Muhammad holds that qisas is not obligatory because the cause of entitlement is ambiguous.
Imam Muhammad's view is a rare transmission (nawādir), while the Sahibayn's is in the six books (Zahir al-Riwayah).
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn argue that the master is definitely the entitled party on both possible causes (guardianship if the mukatab died free, ownership if he died a slave). The cause is not desired for itself but for its ruling (entitlement), and that ruling is certain. Mere difference in cause does not harm.
They use istihsan: certainty of entitlement overrides hypothetical ambiguity.
4
Analyze Imam Muhammad's proof
Imam Muhammad argues that the cause of entitlement is uncertain: did the master claim by guardianship (if the mukatab was free at death) or by ownership (if he was still a slave)? This ambiguity prevents qisas because the right is not firmly established. He compares it to a case where a statement creates doubt about ownership.
He holds that difference in cause is like difference in the entitled person, so the right drops entirely.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The mufta bihi (operative position) is that of the Sahibayn. Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah prefers their view because it is in Zahir al-Riwayah and because certainty of entitlement should not be lost due to ambiguity in the cause. The school relies on this position for fatwa.
This is a consistent Hanafi principle: certainty is not overridden by doubt (al-yaqīnu lā yazūlu bi al-shakk).

Key Terms

قصاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliatory punishment for intentional killing or wounding, equal to the harm inflicted (life for life).

Killing a Thief in Defense of Property
Ruling on killing a thief in defense of property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing a thief in defense of property and identify the condition that makes the killing subject to qisas.

Core Idea
No liability for killing a thief if recovery of property is impossible otherwise; liability if shouting would have sufficed.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position on killing a thief who has taken your property. The key distinction is between situations where you can recover your property by less lethal means (shouting for help) and where you cannot. The Prophet's hadith on defending one's property provides the foundational proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on killing a thief who has taken property. Determine the hukm: when is it permissible to kill without liability, and when does qisas become obligatory?
2
Locate the dalil
Find the proof cited: the hadith 'qatil duna malik' (fighter in defense of his property) and the principle that one may prevent with killing initially.
3
Understand the condition
The ruling hinges on whether the victim could have recovered the property by shouting. If shouting would have made the thief drop the property, then killing is not justified and qisas applies.
4
Distinguish from the usurper case
The text contrasts the thief with a usurper (ghasib): killing a usurper may still require qisas because one can seek help from Muslims and the judge.

باب القصاص فيما دون النفس
لما فرع من بيان القصاص فيما دون النفس شرع في بيان القصاص فيما دون النفس لأن الجزء يتبع الكل . قال رحمه الله تعالى : ( يقتص بقطع اليد من المفصل وإن كانت يد القاطع . أكبر وكذا الرجل ومارن الأنف والأذن لقوله تعالى : والجروح قصاص [المائدة : ٤٥] أي ذو قصاص لقوله تعالى : والسن بالسن [المائدة : ٤٥] والقصاص ينبني على المماثلة فكل ما أمكن فيه رعاية المماثلة يجب فيه القصاص وما لا فلا. وقد أمكن في هذه الأشياء التي ذكرناها ولا عبرة بكبر العضو لأنه لا يوجب التفاوت في المنفعة، وإذا قلنا أن المدار عن التساوي في المنفعة فلا تقطع اليمنى باليسرى ولا الصحيحة بالشلاء، ولا يد المرأة بيد الرجل، ولا يد الحر بيد العبد. وقيد بقوله من المفصل لأنه لو قطع ذلك من غير المفصل لا قصاص فيه. وفي النوادر روى الحسن عن أبي حنيفة رحمهما الله تعالى أنه إذا قطع شحمه اذنه يقتص منه، وإن قطع نصف إذنه وكان يقدر أن يقتص مثل ذلك اقتص منه لأن شحمه الأذن لها حد معلوم وللأذن مفاصل معلومة، فإذا قطع منها شيء يعلم أن القطع من أي المفصل أمكن القصاص، وكذلك إذا قطع غضروف الأذن قطعاً يستطاع فيه القصاص اقتص منه يعمل ذلك بحديدة أو بغير حديدة. وإن جذب أذنه فانتزع شحمته لا قصاص فيه وعليه الأرش في ماله، وإن كان أذن القاطع سكاً أي صغيرة الخلقة وأذن المقطوع صحيحة كبيرة كان بالخيار إن شاء ضمنه نصف الدية، وإن شاء قطعها على صغرها. وكذلك لو كانت أذن القاطع مقطوعة أو خرماء أو مشقوقة كان المقطوع بالخيار وإن كانت الناقصة هي المقطوعة كان له حكومة عدل لا قصاص فيه. وفي نوادر ابن سماعة عن محمد : ولو قطع المارن وهو أرنبه الأنف ففيها القصاص، وإن قطع من أصله لا قصاص عليه لأنه عظم وليس بمفصل ولا قصاص في العظم. قال أبو حنيفة : لو قطع ذكره من أصله أو . من الحشفة اقتص منه لأنه أمكن استيفاؤه وعلى سبيل المساواة إذ له حد معلوم فأشبه اليد من الكوع . قال رحمه الله :
والأذن والعين أن ذهب ضوءها وهي قائمة وإن قلعها لا والسن وإن تفاوتا وكل شجة

Chapter of Retaliation for What is Less than Life. Retaliation is enacted by cutting the hand from the joint even if the hand of the cutter is larger, and likewise the foot and the soft part of the nose. Theft: if he follows him and kills him, there is nothing upon him due to his saying, peace be upon him: 'The fighter in defense of his property', meaning for the sake of his property, and because he has the right to prevent him by killing initially, so likewise he has the right to recover it by killing finally if he is unable to take it from him. And if he knew that if he shouted at him he would drop his property, yet he killed him, then retaliation is obligatory upon him because his killing is without right, and he is like the owner of a usurped property if he kills the usurper where retaliation is obligatory because he is able to repel him by seeking help from Muslims and the judge, so his inviolability is not lost unlike the thief and the one who is not repelled by shouting. And God knows best. Chapter of Retaliation for What is Less than Life. When he completed the explanation of retaliation for less than life, he began to explain retaliation for less than life because the part follows the whole. He, may God have mercy on him, said: (Retaliation is enacted by cutting the hand from the joint even if the hand of the cutter is larger, and likewise the foot and the soft part of the nose and the ear due to His saying: 'And wounds are retaliation' [5:45] i.e., subject to retaliation, due to His saying: 'And the tooth for the tooth' [5:45]. Retaliation is based on similitude, so whatever similitude can be observed therein, retaliation is obligatory, and what cannot, then not. And it is possible in these things which we have mentioned, and no consideration is given to the size of the organ because it does not cause disparity in benefit. And if we say that the criterion is equality in benefit, then the right is not cut with the left, nor the healthy with the paralyzed, nor the hand of a woman with the hand of a man, nor the hand of a free man with the hand of a slave. And he restricted it by saying 'from the joint' because if he cuts it from other than the joint, there is no retaliation in it. And in al-Nawadir, al-Hasan narrated from Abu Hanifa, may God have mercy on them, that if he cuts the earlobe, retaliation is taken from him; and if he cuts half of his ear and he is able to take retaliation like that, retaliation is taken from him because the earlobe has a known limit and the ear has known joints. So if he cuts something from it that is known from which joint the cutting is, retaliation is possible. Likewise, if he cuts the cartilage of the ear in a way that retaliation is possible, retaliation is taken from him, done with an iron tool or without. And if he pulls his ear and tears off his earlobe, there is no retaliation in it and he owes the arsh (compensation) from his wealth. And if the ear of the cutter is small in size and the ear of the victim is healthy and large, he has the choice: if he wishes, he may guarantee half the diya, and if he wishes, he may cut it despite its smallness. Likewise, if the ear of the cutter is cut or perforated or split, the victim has the choice. And if the deficient ear is the victim's, he is entitled to a just compensation (hukumat 'adl), no retaliation. And in Nawadir Ibn Sam'ah from Muhammad: If he cuts the maran (soft part of the nose), then there is retaliation in it; and if he cuts from its root, there is no retaliation upon him because it is bone and not a joint, and there is no retaliation in bone. Abu Hanifa said: If he cuts his penis from its root or from the glans, retaliation is taken from him because it is possible to exact it in a manner of equality, since it has a known limit, so it resembles the hand from the wrist. He, may God have mercy on him, said: And the ear and the eye: if its light goes while it remains, but not if it is plucked out, and the tooth even if they differ, and every head wound in which similitude can be realized. For His saying: 'And the eye for the eye' [5:45]. That is, if he strikes the eye and removes its light while it remains, retaliation is obligatory because it is possible by heating a mirror for it and placing wet cotton on his face and tying his other eye, then bringing the mirror close to his eye, unlike if it is plucked out where retaliation is not taken from him due to the impossibility of observing similitude. This incident occurred in the time of Uthman, may God be pleased with him, and he consulted the Companions, and Ali, may God be pleased with him, said: Retaliation is obligatory, and he explained the possibility of exacting it by the method we mentioned. Then here he did not consider largeness or smallness, so he carried out retaliation in every case by exacting the whole. And he considered the head wound: if it encompassed the head of the victim while it did not encompass the head of the assailant, then he gave the victim the choice: if he wishes he may retaliate and take according to his wound, and if he wishes he may take the arsh for that, because the defect that befell him is greater, for the encompassing wound between its two edges is more than the non-encompassing wound. Unlike cutting a limb, for the defect therein does not differ, nor does its benefit differ, so only retaliation is possible due to equality in every aspect. And if it is plucked out, it is not obligatory where similitude is impossible, for we have no power to do to him as he did without increase or decrease, so for this reason retaliation is not obligatory. And in al-Hidayah: If he plucks out a tooth from its root, the second is plucked out as a similitude. The author of al-Kafi and the general commentators of the book said in this context: If he plucks out a tooth from its root, his tooth is not plucked out as retaliation because observance of similitude is difficult, perhaps similitude is spoiled, but it is filed with a file to the place of the root of the tooth, and the commentator attributed it to al-Mabsut. I say: Their style of writing here is a matter of amazement, for none of them addressed what was mentioned in the book, neither by rejection nor acceptance, rather they mentioned the issue contrary to what was mentioned in the book, and it was the custom of the commentators to address what is in the book either by acceptance or rejection, as if they never saw it. Yes, the statement I quoted here from the author is not mentioned in some copies, but it occurs in many copies, not such that none of the commentators would be aware of it. How, when the author of al-Wiqayah took it and mentioned it in his text where he said: 'And no retaliation in bone except in the tooth: so it is plucked out if it was plucked out, and filed if it was broken.' And what he took from the text of al-Wiqayah is al-Hidayah, as its author stated, and likewise it is mentioned in many texts. Then the investigation here is: if he plucks out another's tooth, should his tooth be plucked out as retaliation, or should it be filed with a file until it reaches the flesh? There are two narrations, as clarified in al-Muhit al-Burhani where he said: If the injury is by breaking part of the tooth, some of the tooth of the breaker is taken with a file to the extent of what was broken of the other's tooth, and this is by agreement. And if the injury is by plucking out a tooth, al-Quduri mentioned that the tooth of the plucker is not plucked out, but the tooth of the plucker is filed with a file until it reaches the flesh and the rest falls off, and Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi inclined to this. And Shaykh al-Islam mentioned in his commentary that the tooth of the plucker is plucked out, and Muhammad referred to this in al-Jami' al-Saghir. Al-Bahr al-Rai'iq, vol. 9, page 3.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary (sharh) on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, covering the chapter on qisas for injuries less than life. It begins with rulings from the matn: retaliation is enacted by cutting the hand from the joint, the foot, and the soft part of the nose. It then addresses the case of killing a thief in defense of property: if a thief enters and one kills him while he is unable to retrieve his property otherwise, no liability; but if shouting would have sufficed, then killing makes one liable for qisas. The author then repeats the chapter heading and begins expansion. The core principle is that qisas is based on exact similitude (mumathalah). For limbs with joints, cutting from the joint ensures equality; size does not matter because benefit is equal. Exceptions: the right hand is not cut for the left, a healthy hand not for a paralyzed one, a woman's hand not for a man's, a free man's not for a slave's. Cutting from beyond the joint invalidates qisas. Then follow details from al-Nawadir: cutting earlobe, half ear, cartilage, pulling and tearing earlobe – each with specific rules. The choice for the victim when the cutter's ear is smaller or defective: he may take arsh or cut despite difference. For the nose, if the soft part (maran) is cut, qisas applies; if from the root (bone), no qisas, only arsh. Abu Hanifa extends qisas to the penis because it has a known limit. Then the matn continues with the eye: if vision is destroyed while the eye remains, qisas is possible by using a heated mirror and wet cotton; if the eye is plucked out, no qisas. This was established by 'Ali's ruling during 'Uthman's caliphate. For head wounds (shajjah), if the wound on the victim is more extensive than on the assailant, the victim may choose qisas for the extent or take arsh. The commentator then discusses a conflict: according to al-Hidayah, if a tooth is plucked out, the assailant's tooth is plucked out in retaliation. But other commentators say it is filed (yubrad). The author of al-Bahr al-Rai'iq expresses amazement that earlier commentators ignored the text of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. He clarifies that there are two narrations: al-Quduri and al-Sarakhsi say file, while Shaykh al-Islam and Muhammad (in al-Jami' al-Saghir) say pluck. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated here, but the author leans toward plucking as it agrees with al-Hidayah.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for bodily injury, as prescribed in Islamic law.
سارق (sariq): Thief who takes property secretly.
غاصب (ghasib): Usurper; one who takes property openly and refuses to return.

Qisas for Limbs and Body Parts
Rulings on qisas for limbs and body parts with conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for qisas on limbs and body parts and apply the principle of mumathalah.

Core Idea
Qisas for limbs requires exact similitude in benefit; cutting must be from the joint.

This lesson covers the core rules for qisas on body parts like hands, feet, ears, nose, and eyes. The key principle is mumathalah (exact similitude). The passage details where qisas is possible and where compensation (arsh) substitutes. We will work through the conditions and exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The matn states: retaliation is enacted by cutting the hand from the joint, even if the cutter's hand is larger. The same applies to foot and nose soft part (maran). The proof is the Quranic verse 'And wounds are retaliation' (5:45).
2
Understand the criterion: mumathalah
Qisas is based on similitude. It is obligatory only when exact similitude is possible. If not, compensation (arsh/diyah) applies.
3
List conditions for qisas on limbs
Must cut from joint; size difference is ignored because benefit is equal. Exceptions: no qisas between right and left, healthy and paralyzed, male and female, free and slave.
4
Analyze specific cases (ear, nose, eye)
For ear: earlobe has known limit, so qisas is possible for cutting earlobe. For nose: only the soft part (maran) qualifies; bone does not. For eye: if vision is destroyed but eye remains, qisas is possible by using a heated mirror and wet cotton. If the eye is plucked out, no qisas.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary (sharh) on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, covering the chapter on qisas for injuries less than life. It begins with rulings from the matn: retaliation is enacted by cutting the hand from the joint, the foot, and the soft part of the nose. It then addresses the case of killing a thief in defense of property: if a thief enters and one kills him while he is unable to retrieve his property otherwise, no liability; but if shouting would have sufficed, then killing makes one liable for qisas. The author then repeats the chapter heading and begins expansion. The core principle is that qisas is based on exact similitude (mumathalah). For limbs with joints, cutting from the joint ensures equality; size does not matter because benefit is equal. Exceptions: the right hand is not cut for the left, a healthy hand not for a paralyzed one, a woman's hand not for a man's, a free man's not for a slave's. Cutting from beyond the joint invalidates qisas. Then follow details from al-Nawadir: cutting earlobe, half ear, cartilage, pulling and tearing earlobe – each with specific rules. The choice for the victim when the cutter's ear is smaller or defective: he may take arsh or cut despite difference. For the nose, if the soft part (maran) is cut, qisas applies; if from the root (bone), no qisas, only arsh. Abu Hanifa extends qisas to the penis because it has a known limit. Then the matn continues with the eye: if vision is destroyed while the eye remains, qisas is possible by using a heated mirror and wet cotton; if the eye is plucked out, no qisas. This was established by 'Ali's ruling during 'Uthman's caliphate. For head wounds (shajjah), if the wound on the victim is more extensive than on the assailant, the victim may choose qisas for the extent or take arsh. The commentator then discusses a conflict: according to al-Hidayah, if a tooth is plucked out, the assailant's tooth is plucked out in retaliation. But other commentators say it is filed (yubrad). The author of al-Bahr al-Rai'iq expresses amazement that earlier commentators ignored the text of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. He clarifies that there are two narrations: al-Quduri and al-Sarakhsi say file, while Shaykh al-Islam and Muhammad (in al-Jami' al-Saghir) say pluck. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated here, but the author leans toward plucking as it agrees with al-Hidayah.

Key Terms
مماثلة (mumathalah): Exact similitude between the injury inflicted and the retaliation, in degree and place.
شجة (shajjah): A head wound; its subtypes determine the compensation.
مارن (maran): The soft, fleshy part of the nose (not the bone).

The Tooth Plucking Dispute: File or Pluck?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on retaliation for tooth plucking.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two Hanafi narrations on retaliation for tooth plucking and explain the preferred view according to the author.

Core Idea
For a plucked tooth, two views: file the assailant's tooth down or pluck it out; the author favors plucking.

This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the proper method of qisas for a tooth that has been plucked out. The text al-Hidayah states that the assailant's tooth should be plucked out. However, many commentators argue for filing (byard) instead. We will analyze the arguments and the author's tarjih.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
The problem: If someone plucks out another's tooth from the root, how should retaliation be carried out? Should the assailant's tooth be plucked out or filed down?
2
Identify the two positions
Narration 1 (Al-Quduri, Al-Sarakhsi): Do not pluck, but file the assailant's tooth to the level of the victim's gum. Narration 2 (Muhammad, Shaykh al-Islam): Pluck out the tooth in kind.
3
Follow the evidence for each
File view: Exact similitude (mumathalah) is difficult to achieve with plucking because teeth may differ; filing is easier to control. Pluck view: Literal application of 'tooth for tooth'; the victim's tooth was completely removed, so the assailant's should be completely removed.
4
Examine the author's tarjih
The author (Ibn Nujaym) notes that al-Hidayah and al-Wiqayah support the plucking view. He criticizes earlier commentators for ignoring the text of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. He leans toward plucking as the mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary (sharh) on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, covering the chapter on qisas for injuries less than life. It begins with rulings from the matn: retaliation is enacted by cutting the hand from the joint, the foot, and the soft part of the nose. It then addresses the case of killing a thief in defense of property: if a thief enters and one kills him while he is unable to retrieve his property otherwise, no liability; but if shouting would have sufficed, then killing makes one liable for qisas. The author then repeats the chapter heading and begins expansion. The core principle is that qisas is based on exact similitude (mumathalah). For limbs with joints, cutting from the joint ensures equality; size does not matter because benefit is equal. Exceptions: the right hand is not cut for the left, a healthy hand not for a paralyzed one, a woman's hand not for a man's, a free man's not for a slave's. Cutting from beyond the joint invalidates qisas. Then follow details from al-Nawadir: cutting earlobe, half ear, cartilage, pulling and tearing earlobe – each with specific rules. The choice for the victim when the cutter's ear is smaller or defective: he may take arsh or cut despite difference. For the nose, if the soft part (maran) is cut, qisas applies; if from the root (bone), no qisas, only arsh. Abu Hanifa extends qisas to the penis because it has a known limit. Then the matn continues with the eye: if vision is destroyed while the eye remains, qisas is possible by using a heated mirror and wet cotton; if the eye is plucked out, no qisas. This was established by 'Ali's ruling during 'Uthman's caliphate. For head wounds (shajjah), if the wound on the victim is more extensive than on the assailant, the victim may choose qisas for the extent or take arsh. The commentator then discusses a conflict: according to al-Hidayah, if a tooth is plucked out, the assailant's tooth is plucked out in retaliation. But other commentators say it is filed (yubrad). The author of al-Bahr al-Rai'iq expresses amazement that earlier commentators ignored the text of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. He clarifies that there are two narrations: al-Quduri and al-Sarakhsi say file, while Shaykh al-Islam and Muhammad (in al-Jami' al-Saghir) say pluck. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated here, but the author leans toward plucking as it agrees with al-Hidayah.

Dispute: Is Qisas a Right of the Heirs or the Deceased?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the nature of qisas rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the nature of qisas, explain the proofs of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the practical consequence of their disagreement.

Core Idea
Qisas is a right of the heirs directly, not inherited from the deceased.

In this passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, the commentator examines an intra-Hanafi dispute: Is the right of qisas (retaliation) a right that belongs to the heirs directly, or does it originally belong to the deceased and then pass to the heirs by inheritance? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on this question, and their disagreement has practical implications for how the heirs must proceed in court. Let's unpack the arguments.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
Clearly articulate what Abu Hanifa holds vs. the Sahibayn. Abu Hanifa: qisas is a right of the heirs directly, established by khilafa. Sahibayn: qisas is initially a right of the deceased, then inherited by the heirs.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proofs
Identify the Quranic verse (17:33), the analogy with a slave accepting a gift, and the argument from the validity of the heir's pardon before the victim's death.
These proofs emphasize that qisas is an act (fi'l) that benefits the living, not the deceased.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proofs
They argue that qisas is like other rights (property, debt) that the deceased owned; the heir's pardon is only valid by istihsan, not qiyas.
This suggests that the right originally belongs to the deceased.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
How does the text rebut the Sahibayn? It points to the explicit Quranic text and the nature of qisas as an act that cannot be owned by the deceased, plus the analogy of the slave.
The commentator weighs in favor of Abu Hanifa.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If one heir is present and another absent, Abu Hanifa requires the present heir to re-prove the case when the absent heir returns, because the right belongs to all heirs jointly. The Sahibayn do not require this.
This test shows which view is more practical in court.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) on the nature of qisas (retaliation). The core question: is qisas a right of the heirs or of the deceased? Imam Abu Hanifa argues that qisas is not inherited because it is established only after death for the purpose of vindication (tashaffi) and vengeance (dark al-tha'r), and the deceased is not the subject of such feelings. Instead, qisas is established for the heirs directly by way of khilafa (succession) due to the cause that was incepted for the deceased. He analogizes to a slave accepting a gift: ownership immediately goes to the master, not to the slave. Similarly, qisas is 'ownership of the act' (milk al-fi'l) in the locus of the victim's body after death, which cannot be imagined from the deceased. The sahih (valid) pardon of the heirs before the victim's death supports this because they are pardoning their own right. The verse (al-Isra' 17:33) 'Whoever is killed unjustly, We have given his heir authority' is explicit that qisas is for the heir. The Sahibayn, however, hold that qisas is initially a right of the deceased, and then transfers to the heirs through inheritance like property. This difference has practical consequences: for example, if one heir is present and the other absent, Abu Hanifa requires the present heir to re-establish evidence after the absent heir returns, because the right belongs to all heirs and the present one cannot fully represent the absent one in a right that is of the heirs themselves. The Sahibayn do not require this, as they consider the right originally of the deceased and thus the present heir can represent the absent one fully. The commentator (probably Ibn Nujaym) notes that both sides have valid points, but the weight of evidence favors Abu Hanifa's position, as the Sahibayn's reliance on the validity of the heir's pardon during the victim's life is problematic because it is only valid by istihsan, not qiyas.

Key Terms
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliation: taking the life of the murderer as a punishment for intentional homicide (qatl 'amd).
الخلافة (al-khilafah): Succession: the heirs stand in the place of the deceased for certain rights that were incepted for him but not fully owned, such as qisas.
التشفي (al-tashaffi): Vindication or satisfaction: the emotional relief the victim's family feels when qisas is carried out.

Killer Proves Absent Heir's Pardon: Evidence Binds All
Ruling on testimony when killer proves absent heir's pardon

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a killer proves the pardon of an absent heir and explain why the evidence is not repeated.

Core Idea
Proving an absent heir's pardon makes the present heir a representative; evidence binds all heirs.

When multiple heirs exist and some are absent, the killer may present evidence that an absent heir has pardoned him. This lesson clarifies that such evidence is sufficient to drop qisas and convert the present heir's share to blood money, and it need not be re-proven when the absent heir appears. The key is the principle of representation (khasm) in evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the situation: a qisas case with multiple heirs, some present and some absent. The killer brings witnesses that an absent heir has pardoned him.
2
Understand the legal effect
The present heir's right to qisas drops and converts into a monetary claim (mal) because the pardon proves that the victim's blood has been waived.
The killer's claim is a right against the present heir, not a claim against the absent one.
3
Apply the principle of khasm
The present heir becomes a representative (khasm) for the absent heir in this matter, so the evidence against him also binds the absent heir.
This prevents the need to re-prove the pardon when the absent heir appears.
4
State the ruling
The evidence need not be repeated; the judgment based on it stands for all heirs.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Pinpoint the exact legal scenario: a testator bequeaths manumission of a slave, then dies; the slave commits a tort (jinayah) and is handed over (daf‘) to the guardian (wali al-jinayah). The ruling: the will becomes void (batil).

The reason is that the right of the victim's guardian takes precedence over both the testator's right and the legatee's right. The testator's ownership of the slave continues until the handover; upon handover, the slave leaves the testator's ownership, so the will cannot be executed.
The Hanafi principle: haqq al-ghurrama' (rights of creditors) precedes.

The ruling is analogous to the executor selling the slave after death for a debt. In both cases, the slave leaves the estate, voiding the will.

Qadhf and the Khuntha Mushkil: Gender Ambiguity and Hadd Punishment
Rulings on qadhf when the accuser or accused is a khuntha mushkil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on qadhf when the accused is a khuntha mushkil before and after puberty, and explain the analogy to majbub and ratqa.

Core Idea
The hadd of qadhf is suspended for a khuntha mushkil until gender signs appear, because absence of intercourse ability blocks ihsan.

This passage from al-Asl addresses an edge case in the law of qadhf: what happens when the accused (or accuser) is a person whose gender is ambiguous? The Hanafi jurists reason that the hadd punishment depends on the presence of ihsan, which requires the ability to have lawful intercourse. A khuntha mushkil whose gender is unknown may be a female who cannot have intercourse (like a ratqa), so no hadd is imposed until gender is clarified. Conversely, if the khuntha is the accuser, they are treated as an adult capable of accusing, and hadd applies.

1

Consider the two roles: the khuntha as the accused (object of qadhf) and as the accuser (subject of qadhf). The ruling depends on puberty and gender signs.
The khuntha mushkil is one whose gender cannot be determined by external signs.
2
Apply the condition of ihsan for the accused
For qadhf to incur hadd, the accused must be muhsan: free, adult, Muslim, and capable of lawful intercourse. The khuntha before puberty is not muhsan. After puberty, without gender signs, ihsan is uncertain because the khuntha could be female like a ratqa (who cannot have intercourse), thus not muhsan.
The text argues that the possibility of being a ratqa blocks hadd.
3

If the khuntha accuses someone of zina, hadd is imposed on the khuntha because they are like an adult majbub or ratqa, and such persons are subject to hadd when they accuse.
The analogy shifts: the khuntha is treated as a capable adult for the purpose of being liable for qadhf.
4

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on qisas, diyah, apostasy, jizya, and qasama for a khuntha mushkil, and distinguish the intra-Hanafi dispute on minor apostasy.

Core Idea
A khuntha's ambiguous gender suspends many capital and penal rulings, including qisas and apostasy execution.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses the unique legal situation of the khuntha mushkil (a person whose gender remains unclear even after puberty). Because gender determines many penal and ritual rulings, the Hanafi jurists apply special rules that suspend or modify typical punishments and obligations. You will explore these rules and then zoom into the famous intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a minor khuntha's apostasy is valid and punishable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the khuntha's legal status
A khuntha mushkil is someone whose gender is unknown. This ambiguity affects all rulings that depend on gender, such as qisas, diyah, hadd penalties, and inheritance.
The rulings apply differently whether the act is by or against the khuntha.
2
Understand the qisas rule
If someone cuts the hand of a khuntha before clarity, no qisas on the cutter because equivalence of gender is uncertain. But if the khuntha kills someone, qisas applies because the victim is known.
Qisas for injury requires equal status; unclear gender removes that.
3
Analyze diyah liability
When the khuntha injures another accidentally, the 'aqilah pays arsh. After puberty, if the injury is intentional, the arsh is from the khuntha's own wealth.
This distinguishes accidental (on 'aqilah) from intentional (on self).
4
Examine apostasy rulings and the intra-Hanafi dispute
For a minor khuntha, Abu Yusuf says apostasy is invalid (no effect), while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say it is valid but no killing. After puberty, apostasy is valid but execution is suspended due to the possibility of being female.
The dispute hinges on whether a minor can form valid intent for apostasy.
5
Note other rulings
The khuntha receives only a small portion (radhkh) in booty, no jizya/kharaj is imposed until gender is clear, and qasamah (oaths for murder) does not apply.
All these follow the principle of suspending definitive rulings when gender is unknown.

Commentary
This lesson continues the discussion by focusing on the exception: the gestures and writing of a deaf-mute are not valid in cases of hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation). The author quotes from Al-Hidayah that the reasoning is that qisas, though a right of the servant, is legislated as a compensatory measure and can be established despite shubha (ambiguity), whereas pure hudud (like zina, theft) are purely deterrents for Allah's right and cannot be established if there is shubha. However, the author (Ibn Nujaym) critically objects that this contradicts other statements in the same work, such as in the Book of Kafalah, Shahadat, Wakalah, and Jinayat, where qisas is consistently grouped with hudud as matters that are dropped by shubha. He also points out that hadd al-qadhf (false accusation) is not a pure right of Allah; it includes a servant's right, yet it is still not established by shubha. Therefore, the distinction based on 'purity' fails. Finally, he addresses a sub-point from the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding whether gesture and writing are both considered, citing al-'Inaya and al-Jami' al-Saghir to conclude that gesture is only valid if the deaf-mute cannot write; if he can write, then writing takes precedence, and gesture is not considered. This lesson thus clarifies the school's nuanced position and exposes apparent contradictions (which are resolved elsewhere).

Key Terms
الحدود (al-hudud): Fixed punishments prescribed by Shari'a for specific crimes (e.g., zina, theft, qadhf).
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliation, specifically for murder or bodily harm, a right of the servant.
الشبهة (al-shubhah): Ambiguity or doubt that prevents the application of a penalty.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi ruling that a deaf-mute's gesture and writing are not accepted in hudud and qisas is based on the principle that these penalties are dropped by shubha, and internal contradictions in Al-Hidayah are resolved by consistently applying this principle.
