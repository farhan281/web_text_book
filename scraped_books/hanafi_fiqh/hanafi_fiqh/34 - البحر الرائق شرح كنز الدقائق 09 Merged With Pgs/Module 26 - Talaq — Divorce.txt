Divorce Using Persian Kinaya Phrases: The Role of Intention
Ruling on Persian divorce phrases and the role of intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Persian kinaya, informative, and denial phrases in divorce pronouncements, and state when intention affects the ruling.

Core Idea
In Persian kinaya phrases, intention is essential; in direct reports of divorce, it is not; in denial phrases, intention cannot make it effective.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains the Hanafi ruling on divorce using Persian expressions. It distinguishes three types: kinaya (ambiguous) phrases that require intention, informative phrases that effect divorce regardless of intention, and denial phrases that never effect divorce even if intended. Understanding this classification is crucial for correct application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category
First, determine whether the phrase is kinaya (ambiguous), informative (a report of occurrence), or denial (expressing refusal). The text provides examples: 'داده كيرا' is kinaya; 'داده است و کرده است' is informative; 'داده أنكار' is denial.
Kinaya phrases include words like 'داده' (given) but require context.
2
Check intention requirement
For kinaya phrases, the husband's intention is necessary for divorce to occur. For informative phrases, intention is not required. For denial phrases, intention is irrelevant – divorce does not occur.
The ruling follows the principle that kinaya is ambiguous; intention clarifies it.
3
Apply the ruling
Based on the category and intention (if needed), determine if divorce takes effect. If kinaya and intended → divorce; if informative → divorce regardless; if denial → no divorce.
The distinction hinges on whether the phrase is a statement of fact or ambiguous.

Husband's Ambiguous Persian Response to Talaq Request

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Persian kinayah in talaq requires intention; without niyyah, no divorce occurs.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq tackles a practical question: when a wife requests divorce in Persian and the husband replies with a string of ambiguous words, what is the legal effect? The commentator explains that such phrases are kinayah (metaphorical), not sarih (explicit). Therefore, the divorce only takes effect if the husband intended it. Understanding this distinction prevents incorrect assumptions about the finality of a marriage based on unclear exchanges.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wife's request
The wife says 'مرا طلاق ده' (give me a divorce). Note that the request itself does not create any legal effect; it is the husband’s response that is at issue.

2

The full phrase combines words meaning 'given', 'assume', 'done', 'let it be possible'.
3
Classify the response as kinayah
The commentator explicitly states that these words are *kinayah* (ambiguous expressions) for divorce, not *sarih* (clear). This classification triggers the requirement of intention.
Kinayah includes any expression that does not explicitly say 'I divorce you' but implies it.
4
Apply the rule: intention determines effect
For kinayah, divorce occurs only if the husband intended talaq at the moment of utterance. If he had no such intention, the marriage remains intact. The passage states: 'فإن نوى بهذه الألفاظ الطلاق وقع، فإن لم ينو لا يقع'.
The rule is consistent with the general Hanafi principle that ambiguous words require niyyah.
5
Determine the default outcome
In the absence of evidence of intention, we assume no divorce occurs. The burden of proving intention would lie on the wife if she claims talaq took effect.
This protects the marriage from unintended dissolution.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A hypothetical statement cannot effect talaq, even with intention.

This lesson examines a specific Persian phrase that a husband might use. The key is whether the phrase is a report (khabar) or a hypothetical. The Hanafi school holds that only declarative utterances can effect talaq, and intention cannot make a non-declarative statement valid.

Classify the utterance type

Compare with 'dada ast' which is a report.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule for talaq utterances
Talaq falls by sarih (explicit) words without intention, or by kinayah (ambiguous) words with intention. But this phrase is not even kinayah; it is a supposition, which is outside the scope of talaq utterances.
Intention alone cannot make a non-declarative statement effective.
4
Conclude the ruling
No talaq occurs, even if the husband intended divorce when saying it.
The phrase is not a valid vehicle for talaq.

Saying 'hilah zanan kun' is kinayah for triple talaq because it implies iddah, which only follows an irrevocable divorce.

The Irrevocable Delegation: Husband's 'Agency' to Wife for Self-Divorce
Ruling on the irrevocability of the husband's delegation to his wife for self-divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a husband cannot revoke his wife's delegation to divorce herself, and distinguish this from ordinary agency.

Core Idea
A husband's delegation to his wife for self-divorce is an oath, not revocable agency.

This short passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq deals with a peculiar case: a husband says to his wife 'I appoint you to divorce yourself.' At first glance, it seems like a standard agency (wakalah). But the Hanafi ruling clarifies it is not. The husband cannot revoke it because the legal nature is that of a suspended divorce (ta'liq al-talaq), akin to an oath (yamin). Understanding this distinction requires careful parsing of the terms and the underlying principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Husband's Action
The husband appoints (wakala) his wife to divorce herself. Note that the verb 'wakala' is used, but the legal effect is not that of a standard agency.
The literal meaning suggests agency, but the ruling treats it as an oath.
2
State the Ruling on Revocation
The passage says: 'He does not have the right to revoke her' (la yamliku 'azlahu). So the husband cannot cancel this delegation.
This is the main ruling.
3
Analyze the Reason Given
The reason is 'because it is an oath from his side' (li-annahu yamin min jihatihi). The 'meaning of oath' is that divorce is suspended (ta'liq) upon her action.
The husband's statement is treated as a suspensive condition.
4
Consider the Opposing View (Abu Yusuf)
The text includes 'Yusuf said: No' (Yusuf qala: la). Abu Yusuf apparently held that the husband can revoke because retraction (ruju') from an oath is valid.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference.
5
Resolve the Distinction: Wife vs. Stranger
The author argues that the wife is not a true agent (wakil) because she works for herself, not for the husband. A stranger would be a true agent and the husband could revoke. Hence, the delegation to the wife is irrevocable.
This clarifies why the oath-like nature overrides the normal rules of agency.

Coercion in Khul': Divorce Valid, Compensation Void
Ruling on coerced khul' and financial consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced khul' and explain why the divorce is valid but the compensation is not due.

Core Idea
Coercion invalidates consent for compensation but not for the divorce itself.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a nuanced point: when a husband compels his wife to accept khul', the talaq takes effect immediately, but the wife is not liable to pay the agreed compensation. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the act of divorce, which is effective even under duress, and the financial obligation, which requires genuine consent. Let's unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dual components of khul'
Khul' comprises a divorce (talaq) and a financial consideration ('iwad). Each component has its own conditions and ruling.
2
Apply the Hanafi principle on coerced divorce
In Hanafi law, talaq al-mukrah (divorce under duress) is valid and effective. The pronouncement alone suffices; intention or free will is not required for its occurrence.
This differs from Shafi'i and Maliki schools.
3
Isolate the condition for the compensation
The financial obligation in khul' requires the wife's free consent (rida). Coercion negates this condition, so the compensation is not enforceable.
Consent is a condition (shart) for the 'iwad, not for the talaq.
4
Derive the combined ruling
Thus, the divorce occurs (sahih) but the wife owes no compensation (batil from her side). The coerced khul' is partially effective.
The passage explicitly states: 'the divorce occurs and the money is not dropped' – i.e., the compensation is not due.
