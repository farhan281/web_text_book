Differing on Place, Time, or Instrument of Killing
Ruling on testimony when witnesses differ on specifics

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on testimony when witnesses agree on murder but differ on specifics, and identify when istihsan permits acceptance.

Core Idea
Testimony is rejected if witnesses differ on place, time, or weapon—unless the difference is minor, accepted by istihsan.

Witnesses who agree that a murder occurred but disagree on the location, time, or instrument pose a problem for the judge. The Hanafi school holds that such disagreement generally invalidates the testimony because each circumstance creates a distinct legal event. However, jurists like Khawar Zada introduced an exception: if the differences are minor—like two sides of the same room—the testimony may be accepted by istihsan. This lesson unpacks the principle and its exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The passage states that when witnesses differ on place, time, or instrument, testimony is not accepted.
This is the default ruling.
2
Understand the reasoning
Murder is a unique event; differing specifics imply distinct events, so the judge deduces one witness is lying.
Logical impossibility of one murder occurring in two places or times.
3
Note the exception by istihsan
If places are close (e.g., a small room) or instruments similar, testimony is accepted by istihsan.
This exception is from later commentators (Khawar Zada, al-Asbijabi).
4
Distinguish major vs minor differences
The text qualifies that only 'large' differences invalidate; minor ones do not.
The original matn was absolute, but the commentary restricts it.
5
Apply the framework
Given a scenario with witness disagreement, determine if the difference is material (reject) or minor (accept).
Consider the closeness of place and similarity of instrument.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, dealing with testimony in murder cases. It opens by stating the general rule: if witnesses agree on the fact of murder but differ on the specific place, time, or instrument, their testimony is not accepted. The reasoning is that murder is a unique event; each specific circumstance (place, time, weapon) constitutes a different legal event with different rulings (e.g., qisas vs. diyah). Therefore, each witness is testifying to a different killing, so there is no agreement on the same subject. The judge must conclude that at least one is lying, since the same killing cannot have occurred in two different places or times. However, an exception exists if the places are very close, like two sides of a small room: then the testimony is accepted by istihsan (juristic preference). Similarly, differing on the instrument may be overlooked if the instruments are similar. The passage then notes that the author's absolute statement about place is qualified to large differences. Khawar Zada's commentary on al-Asl provides the example of a small room where the minor difference in location does not invalidate testimony. Al-Asbijabi adds the example of one witness saying 'sword' and the other saying 'qisas' (a type of weapon) — if both are seen as similar, the testimony may be accepted.

Key Terms
شهادة (shahāda): Testimony given by witnesses in court to establish the occurrence of a crime.
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference that allows departing from strict qiyas for a preferable ruling, here accepting testimony despite minor differences.
آلة (āla): Instrument or weapon used to commit the murder.

Valid Testimony in Murder Cases: Agreement on Instrument and Intent
Expansion of subcases on witness testimony regarding murder weapon and intent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony when witnesses agree or disagree on the weapon and intent, and apply the istihsan exception to cases of unknown intent.

Core Idea
Agreement on the essential weapon and intent determines qisas or diyah; istihsan accepts uncertain testimony, qiyas rejects it.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq expands on several subcases of witness testimony regarding a murder weapon and whether the killing was intentional or accidental. The ruling depends on whether witnesses agree on the type of weapon and whether they specify intent. The author contrasts strict qiyas with a more lenient istihsan approach that accepts testimony even when details are unknown, and highlights a difference between Abu Hanifa and his two companions on large sticks.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main subcases
The passage categorizes testimony based on whether witnesses agree on the instrument (e.g., both say sword or both say stick) or disagree, and whether they specify intent or remain silent.
There are three subcases: agreement on instrument, disagreement on instrument, differing basis of testimony.
2
Analyze agreement on a lethal instrument (sword)
If both testify that the killing was with a sword and specify 'intentional', qisas is imposed. If they say 'mistake', diyah on the 'aqila. If they remain silent or say 'we don't know', the testimony is accepted by istihsan and diyah is on the killer's own wealth.
Qiyas would reject testimony when intent is unknown, but istihsan prevails.
3
Analyze agreement on a stick (different sizes)
If the stick is small, testimony leads to diyah regardless of intent for both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. If it is large and typically lethal, Abu Hanifa treats it like a sword (qisas if intentional), while the text suggests his companions may differ.
The distinction hinges on whether the instrument is 'qatil' (lethal) or not.
4
Recognize when testimony is void
Testimony becomes void (batil) if the witnesses disagree on the instrument (e.g., one says 'sword', other says 'I don't know'), or if one testifies from eyewitness and the other from confession, because the subject matter differs.
Disagreement on the basis also renders testimony void.
5
Apply the intra-Hanafi difference on large stick
For a large stick that typically kills, Abu Hanifa's ruling mirrors that of a sword, while the Sahibayn may impose diyah only. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
The mufta bihi is the text's presentation of istihsan as accepted.

Testimony with Unknown Instrument
This passage explains the Hanafi istihsan ruling on defective testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when witnesses affirm murder but do not know the weapon, and explain the istihsan argument that accepts the testimony and imposes diyah on the killer's wealth.

Core Idea
When witnesses say 'we don't know the weapon', testimony is accepted and diyah is due from the killer's wealth, because the absolute killing is proven and doubt wards off qisas.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq tackles a classic penal conundrum: witnesses positively identify a killer but cannot specify the instrument. By strict qiyas, such testimony should be rejected as incomplete. However, the Hanafi school, via istihsan, accepts it and imposes the minimum financial penalty (diyah) on the killer. The reasoning is both pragmatic and principled, invoking the noble objective of avoiding excessive punishment in cases of doubt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize the case: two witnesses testify that the accused killed the victim, but when asked about the murder weapon, they say 'we do not know with what he killed him.'
The instrument is unknown, which raises uncertainty about the legal classification of the killing (intentional vs. quasi-intentional vs. accidental).
2
State the Qiyas Ruling
By analogy (qiyas), the testimony should be rejected entirely because the killing is unknown—without specifying the instrument, the legal implications (qisas or diyah) cannot be determined. The witnesses appear negligent.
Qiyas is the default methodology; the text presents it as the counterpoint.
3
State the Hanafi Position (Istihsan)
By istihsan (juristic preference), the testimony is accepted and the killer must pay diyah (blood money) from his own personal wealth (not from the 'aqila).
This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school.
4
Understand the Istihsan Reasoning
The witnesses' testimony establishes an absolute killing (qatl muṭlaq), which is legally actionable. The minimum consequence of such an absolute claim is diyah. Their ignorance of the instrument is interpreted not as negligence but as a deliberate attempt to ward off (dar') the application of qisas, which is recommended in penal matters when doubt exists.
The principle 'al-umūr bi-maqāṣidihā' (actions are judged by intentions) supports this interpretation.
5
Connect to the Hadith on Reconciliation
The author analogizes to a hadith that permits lying for the sake of reconciliation between people. If the Law allows a lie for a lesser good (peace), then allowing incomplete testimony to avoid wrongful execution is even more deserving.
The hadith: 'He is not a liar who reconciles between two people and says something good or conveys something good.'

Conflicting Testimonies Among Heirs: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conflicting heir testimonies in murder.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding conflicting testimony among heirs in murder cases, and apply the principle of tahātur (cancellation) to determine diyah and inheritance.

Core Idea
When heirs accuse each other of murder, Abu Hanifa accepts both testimonies; Sahibayn cancel them or prioritize the heir's testimony against a co-heir.

In cases where a murdered man's only heirs accuse each other, the acceptance of conflicting testimonies leads to different outcomes for diyah and inheritance. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents the views of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani, with practical consequences for qisas and diyah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Determine who are the heirs (walis) and who is accused. In the first scenario, there are two heirs: Abdullah and Zaid. One accuses the other; the other accuses a stranger.
2
Classify the testimonies
Note which testimony is against a co-heir and which is against a stranger. This classification determines how the Sahibayn prioritize.
Testimony against a co-heir is treated differently.
3
Apply Abu Hanifa's rule
Abu Hanifa accepts both testimonies. Each accused pays half the diyah (if intentional) or the 'aqila pays (if accidental). Inheritance is divided accordingly.
The practical result: both accused pay, and both heirs inherit a share.
4
Apply Sahibayn's rule
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad prioritize the testimony of the heir against his co-heir over the testimony against a stranger. The co-heir is subject to qisas or diyah; the stranger's testimony is invalid. If both heirs accuse each other, the testimonies cancel (tahātur) and no diyah is due.
Cancellation means no penalty, inheritance divided equally.
5
Consider inheritance implications
Under Abu Hanifa, inheritance is divided based on the shares after diyah payments. Under Sahibayn, if testimonies cancel, inheritance is divided equally among the heirs.
The text shows differing opinions on the fractions.

Resolving Conflicting Evidence in Khuntha Cases
Detailed rules on conflicting evidence and precedence in khuntha cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving conflicting evidence in khuntha cases by analyzing timing, dates, and prior judgments.

Core Idea
Evidence without precedence cancels; earlier evidence prevails if dated or if judgment is passed.

When conflicting evidence arises about a khuntha's gender or marital status, Islamic law provides specific procedural rules to ensure justice. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq outlines how a qadi must prioritize evidence based on timing and dates, and when to postpone judgment until the khuntha reaches puberty. Understanding these rules is essential for any student of Hanafi qada.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claims and evidence
Determine what each party claims (e.g., gender, marriage) and what evidence they present. Note the nature of the khuntha (alive or deceased, prepubescent or adolescent).
2
Determine if evidence is presented together or sequentially
Establish whether both parties brought their evidence at the same time or one before the other. This affects the application of the rules.
3
Check for dates and temporal precedence
If both evidences are presented together, check if they have dates. If undated or same date, apply tahātur (both cancel). If one has an earlier date, that evidence prevails.
If there is a date priority, it overrides simultaneous presentation.
4
Apply sequential rules
If evidence is presented sequentially: (a) if the second comes before judgment on the first, treat as simultaneous (apply tahātur or date rule); (b) if judgment has already been passed on the first, do not accept the second.
A prior judgment strengthens the first evidence.
5
Apply the special rule for living khuntha
If the khuntha is still alive and has not reached puberty, the qadi must postpone all decisions until the khuntha reaches puberty and his/her gender becomes clear. No evidence is accepted during this time.
This differs from the post-death scenario where decisions are made based on evidence.

Core Idea
A mute's gesture is valid even if he can write—the Hanafi position offers him a choice, not a necessity.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute about the legal force of a mute's gesture (ishara) when he is capable of writing. Possession Proof for Real Estate and Qadi's Jurisdiction
Explains possession proof for real estate and intra-Hanafi dispute on qadi's jurisdiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on possession proof for real estate and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether qadi's jurisdiction is based on place or persons.

Core Idea
For real estate, possession is not apparent; a qadi's jurisdiction hinges on the parties, not the place.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks two related qada rulings: the requirement to prove possession for real estate, and the basis of a qadi's authority. Understanding these rules is critical for any litigation involving land and the scope of a judge's power.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its conditions
The passage states that real estate does not leave the hand of the possessor until the plaintiff proves possession. This is a condition (shart) for the validity of the claim. The plaintiff must present evidence or the judge must have personal knowledge.
Movable property is treated differently because possession is visible.
2
Distinguish movable property
For movables, the hand is observable, so no proof of possession is needed. For real estate, due to the nature of land, proof is required to avoid collusion (tuhmah al-muwada'ah).
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school in some aspects.
3
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on qadi's jurisdiction
Some early Hanafi scholars consider the place (mصر) a condition for the qadi's authority, analogizing to Friday and Eid prayers which require a city. Others consider the people (أهل) as the criterion.
The matn states 'real estate not in the jurisdiction of the judge – his judgment is not valid,' but the commentary expands on the dispute.
4
Analyze the proofs and tarjih
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani reject the place condition. The relied-upon view (as in al-Khulasa) is that the people are what matter. The analogy to prayers is not perfect because judgment follows persons, not location.
This is the mufta bihi position and the practice of qadis today.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If a qadi judges a case involving parties from his jurisdiction but concerning real estate outside, his judgment is valid according to the preferred view, but invalid according to the other. This affects the enforceability of judgments.
The practical consequence is significant for cross-district property disputes.

Finality of a Qadi's Judgment: No Retraction After Delivery
Explains the finality of a qadi's judgment and its exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a qadi cannot retract his judgment after it is based on valid evidence, and distinguish the exception when a contrary text emerges.

Core Idea
A qadi's judgment based on valid evidence is final and cannot be retracted by a change of opinion.

In judicial proceedings, finality is essential for the rights of litigants. This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains that once a qadi passes judgment on the basis of valid evidence and a valid claim, he cannot later retract it, even if he changes his mind or claims deception. The ruling is supported by prophetic practice and analogy with testimony, ensuring that judgments are stable unless a clear textual source was overlooked.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the qadi's retraction of his judgment. The ruling is that the retraction is invalid and the judgment stands.
The matn explicitly says 'لا يعتبر والقضاء ماض' (it is not considered and the judgment is effective).
2
Locate the dalil
The commentary cites a mursal hadith reported by al-Sha'bi that the Prophet did not reverse his judgments even when later Quranic revelation contradicted them.
The hadith is: 'كان رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم يقضي بالقضاء ثم ينزل القرآن بعد ذلك بخلافه فلا يرد قضاءه'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
If the Prophet did not overturn his own judgments after direct revelation, a qadi cannot overturn his own judgment simply by a change of ijtihad. The dalil is stronger (a minori ad maius).
The ijtihad-based judgment becomes a 'shari'ah' for that case, binding until abrogated by a text.
4
Analyze the qiyas
The commentary draws an analogy: just as testimony once connected to the judgment cannot be retracted because the plaintiff's right is attached, likewise the judgment itself cannot be retracted.
The illa (effective cause) is the attachment of the plaintiff's right.
5
Note the exception from al-Muhit
If a qadi judged by ijtihad in a case with no text, then later discovers a text contrary to his ruling, the judgment is overturned because the text was always present but hidden. This differs from a mere change of ijtihad.
The distinction: ijtihad in a textless case is valid and final; ijtihad that overlooks an existing text is invalid.

Ruling on validity of testimony from hidden witnesses who hear a confession

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Certainty is the foundation of testimony; hearing alone is insufficient unless knowledge eliminates doubt.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the validity of testimony from witnesses who were hidden to hear a confession. The key issue is whether hearing alone, without seeing the speaker, suffices for certainty. The text provides the general rule, its proof from Quran and Sunnah, and an exceptional case where certainty is achieved by other means.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what action is being ruled upon and its legal classification. Here, the act is witnessing a confession by hidden witnesses. The ruling: valid if they both see and hear the confessor; invalid if they only hear, unless certainty is established.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the evidence cited by the author. The passage quotes Quran 43:86 from al-Zukhruf and the hadith 'When you know like the sun...'

Explain how the dalil yields the ruling. The hadith requires certainty ('like the sun'); hearing alone does not provide certainty because voices can resemble each other, so it is possible the voice belongs to another person.

The exception removes the requirement of seeing by establishing certainty through alternate means.

Consider the ruling's foundation. The Hanafi position prioritizes certainty (yaqin) and uses istihsan to allow the exception, balancing strict proof with practical necessity.
This demonstrates the Hanafi approach to shahadah: testimony must be based on certain knowledge.

And if they heard his speech but did not see him, their testimony is not valid, because voices resemble one another, so it is possible that the confessor is someone else, except if they entered the house and knew that there is no one else besides them, then they sat at the door and there is no other entrance to the house, then a man entered and they heard the confession of the entrant but did not see him at the time of the confession: because certainty is attained for them in this situation, so it is permissible for them to testify against him.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on the validity of testimony from witnesses who were hidden to hear a confession. The general rule is that testimony is valid if the witnesses both see and hear the confessor, but if they only hear without seeing, it is invalid due to the possibility of confusion with another person's voice. The proof is the Quranic verse requiring knowledge and the hadith commanding testimony only when certain. An exception is made when the witnesses have ensured that no one else is present and there is only one entrance, so they have certainty that the voice belongs to the specific person. In that case, they may testify even without seeing him at the moment of confession.

Judge's Authority to Lend Property of Absent and Minor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a judge may lend the property of an absent person, a minor, and lost property for their benefit, and explain the underlying principle of judicial guardianship.

Core Idea
A judge may lend property of those who cannot manage it for their benefit — guardianship implies authority to preserve and grow.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents a key principle of judicial authority: the qadi may lend the property of an absent person, a minor, and lost property. This is not a license for arbitrary use; rather, it is grounded in the judge's role as guardian (wali) over those who lack legal capacity. The ruling reflects the Hanafi commitment to maslahah (public/private benefit) within the boundaries of trusteeship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its subjects
The act is lending (iqraḍ). The subjects are the property of three categories: an absent person (ghā'ib), a minor child (ṭifl), and a lost item (luqaṭah).
The judge is the actor; the beneficiaries are the owners of these properties.
2
Recognize the implied condition of benefit
The permission is conditioned on benefit (maṣlaḥah) for the owner. The judge must act as a guardian, not for personal gain.
The text implies this because the judge's authority is derived from his guardianship over those who cannot manage their own affairs.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The judge has general guardianship (wilāyah) over those who lack legal capacity (e.g., minors, absentees, found property). This includes managing and investing their property for their welfare.
This is a core Hanafi doctrine: the judge stands in the place of the guardian.
4
Note scope and limitations
The ruling is not absolute; it is subject to judicial discretion and must be exercised with safeguards. Other schools may restrict such lending, but Hanafis allow it when beneficial.
The judge must ensure proper documentation and trusteeship.

Liability Under Coercion by the Sultan
Ruling on liability under sultan's coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a coerced payer is not liable and identify when the owner may claim from the coercer or the willing recipient.

Core Idea
Coercion (ikrah) transfers liability from the coerced payer to the coercer.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a situation where a person holding another's property is forced by the sultan to give it to a third party under threat of severe harm. The key legal question is: who bears the loss if the property is given under duress? The text gives the Hanafi ruling, distinguishing based on whether the recipient acted willingly or was also coerced.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
Recognize that the payer holds another's property, the sultan threatens severe harm (e.g., cutting off hand), and the payer complies. This is the scenario of coercion (ikrah).
The threat must be severe enough to overwhelm will.
2
Determine the payer's liability
The text states the payer is not liable (la yadman). Coercion negates his responsibility because he acted under duress.
Liability shifts from the coerced actor.
3
Analyze the liability of the coercer and recipient
If the recipient (taker) was a free agent (not coerced), the owner may choose to hold either the coercer or the taker liable. If the taker was also coerced, only the coercer is liable.
The owner's option exists when the taker accepts willingly.
4
Apply the ruling to variations
Consider cases where the taker knew about the coercion or acted in bad faith – the ruling remains: liability on coercer or taker if taker willingly participated.
The key is the taker's free choice.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement on coercion (ikrah). The scenario: a person holds another's property (e.g., as a trustee or finder). The sultan threatens him with severe harm unless he gives the property to someone else. He complies under threat. The ruling: the payer is not liable for the property because he acted under coercion (mukrah). If the recipient was also coerced, then liability is solely on the coercer. This shows that coercion negates civil liability for the coerced party.
