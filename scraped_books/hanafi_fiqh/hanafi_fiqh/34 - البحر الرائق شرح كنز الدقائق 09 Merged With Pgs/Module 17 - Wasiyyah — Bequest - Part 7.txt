Qadi's Appointment: Wakil vs Wasi
Ruling on qadi's appointment of executor and distinction between wakil and wasi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the scope of authority of a wakil and a wasi when appointed by the qadi, and state the default authority in each case.

Core Idea
A qadi's word choice matters: 'wakil' preserves, 'wasi' manages the estate fully.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies a critical distinction in estate management: the difference between a wakil (agent) and a wasi (executor) when appointed by a qadi. While both are appointed to handle an estate, their default powers differ dramatically. Understanding this distinction prevents misuse of authority and ensures proper administration of orphans' and absent persons' estates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two appointments
The qadi may say either 'j'altuka wakilan' or 'j'altuka wasiyan'. Recognize that each term carries a specific legal effect.
2
Determine the authority of a wakil
The wakil's default authority is limited to preservation of assets unless explicitly authorized to buy and sell.
3
Determine the authority of a wasi
The wasi has full management authority (bi-amri nafs al-qadi) without needing further specification.
4
Note the exception for the father's appointment
The father's appointment of a wasi cannot be restricted, unlike the qadi's appointment which can be limited to specific types.

Division of Estate by Executor: Acting for Heirs vs Legatee
Ruling on executor's division with legatee vs heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the valid and invalid forms of the executor's division (qisma) and explain why the direction matters based on the legal concept of khalifa.

Core Idea
The executor's division is valid only when he acts on behalf of the heirs, not on behalf of the legatee.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in estate distribution: the executor (wasi) dividing the deceased's property among heirs and a legatee (musa lahu). The key is whose interests the executor represents. The passage uses the concept of 'khalifa' (successor) to determine the validity of the division. Understanding this sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi rules on wills and executorship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the matn: 'and his division on behalf of the heirs with the legatee is valid, but the reverse is not.' Note the two scenarios.
The reverse means the executor dividing with the heirs on behalf of the legatee.
2
Analyze the 'Khalifa' Concept
The passage explains why: the heir is a khalifa (successor) of the deceased, so he stands in the deceased's place regarding liabilities. The executor is also a khalifa. The legatee is not a khalifa; he acquires the bequest by a new cause (sabab jadid).
This distinction determines who can be bound by the executor's division.
3
Apply to the Two Scenarios
When the executor divides with the legatee on behalf of the heirs, he acts for the successors, so the division binds the heirs. When he divides with the heirs on behalf of the legatee, he acts for a non-successor, so the division does not bind the legatee.
Practical consequence: an absent heir cannot later challenge a division made on his behalf, but the legatee retains rights if the division was not on his behalf.
4
Synthesize the Rule
Conclude: The executor's valid role is to represent the heirs (the successors). He cannot represent the legatee because the legatee is not a khalifa.
Memorize: بخشة الوصي عن الورثة جائزة، وعن الموصى له لا.

The Executor's Appointed Executor is Executor for Both Estates
Hanafi proof that the second executor oversees both original and first executor's estates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the executor's appointed executor, state the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate the Hanafi response using the analogy of the grandfather.

Core Idea
The second executor oversees both estates because guardianship transfers fully — unlike an agent, he can delegate.

In this passage, we encounter a classic inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. The ruling at issue: when an executor appoints another executor before his death, is the second executor valid for the original testator's estate as well? The matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq states the Hanafi position that he is executor of both estates. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, arguing that the testator did not authorize delegation. The Hanafi response relies on a key principle: the transfer of guardianship (wilaya) from the testator to the executor, which grants the executor the same scope of authority, including the power to appoint a successor.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that the second executor is executor of both the first executor's estate and the original testator's estate.
This is the clear ruling in the matn: 'وصي الوصي وصي التركتين'.
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the testator only authorized the first executor, not his appointee, so the second has no authority over the original estate.
The objection is based on lack of express authorization.
3
Examine the Shafi'i analogy
Shafi'i compares the executor to an agent (wakil) or mudarib who cannot delegate.
This analogy implies that the executor's authority is personal and non-transferable.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school distinguishes by noting that the executor receives a full transfer of guardianship (wilaya) from the testator, akin to a grandfather inheriting guardianship from the father. This allows delegation.
The key is the nature of wilaya: full transfer vs. limited agency.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under Hanafi, the second executor can manage the original estate directly; under Shafi'i, a new executor must be appointed by the judge or heirs.
Thamarah al-khilaf: direct authority vs. court intervention.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i. Core Idea
Executor's division is like sale; its validity depends on property type and authority, with a key dispute on land.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a detailed analysis of the rulings regarding division (qisma) by the executor. It begins by clarifying the matn: the executor's division with the legatee on behalf of the heirs is valid, but if he divides with the heirs on behalf of the legatee, it is not valid. The reason is that the heir is the deputy of the deceased, so the executor's division is effective against him, while the legatee is a new owner and not a deputy, so the division does not bind him. If property perishes after division, the legatee gets a third of the remainder because he remains a partner. The executor is a trustee and not liable. The passage then discusses the executor's authority to sell movables of minors for preservation, but not land. It then presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa and Zufar do not allow division of land for an absent legatee, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow it. Abu Yusuf's reasoning is that the testator gave the executor full authority for necessary actions, and delivering the estate to heirs is a source of reward. The passage also covers division among minors: it is not directly allowed because it is like selling one minor's property to another without benefit; a legal stratagem is provided by selling undivided shares. For mixed groups (minors, absent majors, present majors), different rules apply: division of land is not allowed for absent majors, but for movables it is allowed because they are treated like minors. If majors are present, division is valid for them. This lesson illustrates the nuanced application of qiyas (analogy to sale) and the role of benefit and necessity in Hanafi fiqh.

The legatee is a partner with the heirs; an invalid division gives him the right to recover from remaining property or hold the executor or heirs liable.

Core Idea
When funds for a bequeathed Hajj are lost after division, Abu Hanifa insists on performing Hajj from the remaining third, while his two disciples differ.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a specific scenario: a person bequeaths that Hajj be performed for them, the executor divides the estate, and the funds designated for Hajj are lost. What happens? The three founding imams of the Hanafi school give three different answers. This lesson unpacks their positions and the reasoning behind them.

Practical differences: Under Abu Hanifa, Hajj always performed; under Abu Yusuf, possibly void; under Muhammad, never.

Judge's Division for Absent Legatee: Measurable Goods Only
Explains when a judge's division of estate for an absent legatee is valid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a judge's division for an absent legatee and distinguish between measurable and non-measurable goods.

Core Idea
Judge's division for absent legatee is valid only for measurable goods, where division is confirmation, not exchange.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains a key rule in estate division: when a legatee is absent, the judge may take their share. However, this power is limited. The distinction hinges on whether the goods are measurable by volume or weight. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The judge's division for an absent legatee is ruled valid (sahih). This is the core ruling.
The ruling is conditional on the type of property.
2
Understand the rationale
The judge acts as guardian for the absent legatee. The division is considered confirmation (iqrar) of the legatee's share, not an exchange.
Iqrar means the share is already owned, so no consent needed.
3
Distinguish property types
Goods sold by measure (volume) or weight are 'makīl wa mawzūn'. Other goods (e.g., land, unique items) are not.
Measurable goods are fungible; exact replacement is possible.
4
Apply the distinction
For measurable goods, division is valid. For non-measurable goods, it is not, because division becomes an exchange (mubādalah) like a sale, which requires the owner's consent.
Selling absent owner's property is impermissible.
5
Note the consequence
If the goods perish in the judge's custody, the legatee has no claim against the heirs or judge.
This applies only to valid division (measurable goods).

Executor's Sale of Slave for Creditors
Validates sale of a slave by executor for creditors even without their presence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of an executor selling a slave from the estate for creditors in their absence, derive it via qiyas, and distinguish it from the case of a slave permitted to trade.

Core Idea
The executor stands in the deceased's place; creditors' right is to value, not specific assets.

This ruling clarifies that an executor may sell estate property to satisfy creditors even without their presence. The reasoning is grounded in the principle that the executor represents the deceased, and the creditors' claim is on the financial value, not the specific item. Understanding this distinction is crucial for proper estate administration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that the sale of a slave by the executor for the creditors is valid (sahih), even if the creditors are absent.
The ruling is explicitly given: 'بيع الوصي عبداً من التركة بغيبة الغرماء يصح'.
2
Recognize the qiyas structure
The executor is analogized to the deceased: as the deceased could sell while alive, so can the executor. This is qiyas jaliy with a clear illa: the executor stands in the place of the deceased (الوصي قائم مقام الموصي).
Qiyas jaliy is used because the illa is explicit and evident.
3
Understand the illa and its application
The illa is that the executor is a substitute for the deceased. Just as the deceased's sale during terminal illness without creditors present is valid, so is the executor's sale.
The passage emphasizes that the creditors' right is to the wealth (مال), not the specific item (صورة). The sale substitutes the price for the item, preserving value.
4
Distinguish the counterexample
The passage contrasts this with a slave permitted to trade. In that case, the master cannot sell him because creditors have a direct right of collection from the slave's earnings. Here, creditors' right is only to the value, not to the slave's person or earnings.
This distinction clarifies the limits of the executor's authority.

The sale does not destroy the value but replaces it with the price. This is distinguished from a slave who is permitted to trade; in that case, the master cannot sell him because the creditors have a direct right of collection from the slave's earnings.

Executor's Liability for Reclaimed Slave
States executor's liability when slave sold by order is reclaimed after price lost

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that the executor is personally liable to the buyer when a bequeathed slave is reclaimed after the price is lost, and explain the basis in contract law (uhdah).

Core Idea
The executor bears liability because he is the contracting party; warranty follows the seller.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario from the chapter on bequests: a testator directs the executor to sell a slave and give the proceeds to charity. The executor sells the slave, but the price is lost, and then the slave is reclaimed by a rightful owner. Who bears the loss? The passage states clearly that the executor himself is liable, not the estate. We will examine the reasoning behind this ruling, which turns on the principle of uhdah (warranty) attaching to the seller.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The testator bequeaths that his slave be sold and the price given in charity. The executor sells the slave and receives the price, but the price is lost in his possession before he can give it to charity.
2
Recognize the event triggering liability
After the price is lost, the slave is reclaimed (istaḥaqqa) by a third party who proves rightful ownership. The buyer loses the slave and demands the price back.
Reclamation means the slave was never the testator's property to begin with.
3
Determine who is liable
The passage rules that the executor (al-waṣī) is liable for the price to the buyer. The reason given: 'because he is the contracting party (al-ʿāqid), so the liability (al-ʿuhdah) is upon him.'
The ʿuhdah is the seller's responsibility to deliver good title; if it fails, the seller must refund the price.
4
Apply the principle of uhdah
In Hanafi sales law, the seller bears the risk of the sold item being reclaimed. Here, the executor stands as the seller, so he must return the price he took from the buyer, even though the price was lost through no fault of his own (it was lost before reclamation).
The loss of the price does not shift liability to the buyer or the estate; the executor took the buyer's money without delivering a valid item.

Core Idea
The executor who frees a bequeathed criminal slave becomes personally liable for the indemnity; he cannot shift liability to the estate.

This lesson examines the obligations of an executor (waṣī) when a slave, whose emancipation was bequeathed, commits a crime after the testator's death. Distinguish Knowledge Levels
The ruling splits based on whether the executor knew of the crime at the time of freeing. If he knew, he is liable for the arsh. If he did not know, he is liable for the slave's value only.

The executor may choose to retain the slave, sell him, and pay the arsh from the sale price. If he formally declares this choice before a judge, he cannot later change his mind. If the slave dies after the choice, the debt becomes a liability on the orphans (heirs).

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses the legal authority of the guardian (wasi) over a minor's property. It begins with the ruling that the guardian may sell the minor's real estate (عقار) subject to three exceptions: if the deceased (the minor's father) had unpaid debts, if a buyer is willing to pay double the market price, or if the minor has an urgent need for the cash. This indicates that the primary concern is the minor's welfare. Imam al-Sadr al-Shahid confirms that this is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi). The author of Kanz al-Daqaiq made the ruling general for all types of property.

The text then introduces the three categories of the guardian's statements regarding expenses. The first category covers actions the guardian is legally empowered to perform: spending on slaves who are part of the minor's inheritance, purchasing slaves, hiring a man to recover a runaway slave. In these, the guardian is believed without the need for witnesses, because he is doing what he is authorized to do for the minor's benefit.

The second category covers actions outside his authority: spending from his own money with the intention of reimbursement, or paying indemnities for the minor's slaves. The guardian is not believed in these because he is not empowered to assume liability from his own pocket or to spend on the minor's relatives without a judge's order.

The third category involves disputed cases, such as claiming to have paid a reward for recovering a runaway slave or paying land tax (خراج) for ten years. Here, Abu Yusuf believes the guardian, while Imam Muhammad does not. Abu Yusuf's reasoning is that the guardian is empowered to spend the minor's wealth for its preservation, and paying tax or reward directly benefits the minor's property. Imam Muhammad considers these as extraneous obligations. The correct position is according to the dispute.

Finally, the text presents a case where the guardian brings a man to the judge and asks to pay the reward from the minor's wealth. There is a distinction: if the guardian claims the reward is due in his own wealth, he is not believed; if he claims it is from the minor's wealth directly, he is believed because he is authorized to manage the minor's assets.

The Guardian’s Authority to Sell on Behalf of a Major Absentee
Expansion of matn on guardian's sale for absentee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule regarding a guardian's sale on behalf of a major absentee and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute when selling real estate for a partial debt.

Core Idea
A guardian may sell an absentee's movables by istihsan, but real estate only for debt; Abu Hanifa allows selling all, the Sahibayn limit to the debt amount.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks a ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq: a guardian (wasi) can sell an absentee's movable property but not real estate, based on istihsan. However, when a debt is involved, real estate may be sold, and a dispute arises between Abu Hanifa and his two companions on how much may be sold when the debt is partial. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi approach to balancing authority and necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling and its basis
State the default rule: the guardian can sell movable property for the major absentee by istihsan, but not real estate (except for debt). Understand the qiyas that would deny this authority and why istihsan overrides it.
Istihsan is based on preserving the movable property that might spoil; real estate is self-preserving.
2
Identify the exception for debt
When the absentee has a debt, real estate may be sold. First, if the debt covers the full value, there is consensus (ijma) that the guardian sells all. If partial, the dispute arises.
The consensus is among the Hanafi school.
3
State the point of dispute (partial debt)
According to Abu Hanifa, the guardian may sell the entire property. According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), he may only sell the portion equivalent to the debt.
Both positions are transmitted within the Hanafi school.
4
Compare the evidence (dalil) for each position
Abu Hanifa argues that authority (wilayah) is indivisible: if established for part, it applies to the whole. The two companions argue there is no need to sell more than necessary.
The dispute is methodological: indivisibility of authority vs. principle of necessity.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In practice, according to Abu Hanifa, the entire property is sold and the surplus returned to the owner. According to the two companions, only the needed portion is sold, with the remainder left untouched.
The different rulings affect the scope of the sale and the risk to the owner's property.

However, if the testimony is about a bequest, it is not accepted. Abu Yusuf disagrees, saying it is not accepted even for debt. Several narrations from Abu Hanifa are mentioned: one with Muhammad, one with Abu Yusuf, and one from al-Hasan that if all come together and testify, the testimony is void; but if they testify separately, it is accepted. The author then quotes from al-'Inaya a classification of these issues into four aspects based on the presence of 'suspicion of partnership' (تهمة الشركة). The first aspect (disputed) is testimony about debt; the second (agreed to be invalid) is testimony about a common part (e.g., one-third); the third (agreed to be valid) is where each group testifies about a specific item (e.g., slave-girl and slave); the fourth (also invalid) is where one group testifies about a specific item and the other about a common part. The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq (Ibn Nujaym) criticizes this classification as confused: the true general categories are three (agreed valid, agreed invalid, disputed), and examples are five. He then cites al-Nihaya and al-Kifaya with a different fourfold division, and finally mentions the correct threefold division given by earlier scholars like Abu al-Layth and al-Sarakhsi. The passage ends mid-explanation of Muhammad's argument for accepting debt testimony: debt is in the dhimma (a legal liability) which can accommodate multiple claims without creating partnership, unlike bequest which attaches to specific assets.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the acceptance of testimony when two teams of witnesses testify for each other in a will case. The ruling is that such testimony is not accepted due to mutual interest. Testimony of the Ambiguous Khuntha
Ruling on suspension of khuntha's testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the acceptance of testimony from an ambiguous khuntha and identify the condition for its validity.

Core Idea
The testimony of a khuntha is suspended until his gender becomes clear.

In Hanafi fiqh, the legal capacity of a khuntha (a person with both male and female traits) is uncertain until the gender is resolved. This uncertainty directly affects the validity of their testimony in court. The text from Al-Bahr al-Raiq states that the testimony of an ambiguous khuntha is suspended both before and after puberty until his gender becomes known. This lesson explains the ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue: testimony of the ambiguous khuntha
Recognize that the passage addresses the shahada of a khuntha and the condition for its acceptance.
The khuntha is a hermaphrodite with ambiguous gender traits.
2
Understand the suspension of testimony
Even after puberty, if the gender remains unclear, the khuntha's testimony is not accepted. It is suspended indefinitely until clarification.
The text says: 'يوقف أمره في حق الشهادة حتى يتبين'.
3
Identify the condition for validity
The condition for the testimony to be valid is that the gender of the witness must be known as either male or female.
Gender affects legal capacity and credibility in testimony.
4
State the outcome of clarification
Once gender is determined, the testimony of a male khuntha is accepted (subject to other conditions) and similarly for a female. While ambiguous, the testimony is ineffective.
The ruling is precautionary due to uncertainty.

Bequest to an Ambiguous Khuntha
Ruling on distribution of bequest to khuntha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the provisional bequest amount given to a khuntha under the two Hanafi opinions and explain the adjustment after gender clarification.

Core Idea
A bequest to a khuntha is given partly now and partly suspended, with two opinions on the exact split.

When a testator bequeaths a sum to a khuntha without specifying gender, the inheritance law requires a cautious approach. The Hanafi scholars present two opinions: one gives half immediately and suspends half, the other gives three-quarters and suspends one-quarter. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the final adjustment after gender becomes known.

Guided Path
1
Assume a bequest amount: 1000 dirhams
The text uses this figure as the total bequest. Female share is 500, male share is 1000 (assuming male gets double).
This is the standard Hanafi differential for a son vs daughter.
2
Apply the first opinion (قول علمائنا)
The scholars 'عندنا' say give 500 now and suspend 500. This is the lower possible share.
This cautious approach avoids overpaying if the khuntha turns out female.
3
Apply the second opinion (الشيخ)
The Shaykh (scholar) says give 750 now and suspend 250. This is calculated as half of the male share (1000) + half of the female share (500) = 500 + 250 = 750.
This gives the average.
4
Determine the adjustment after clarification
If the khuntha is male, he receives the full suspended amount; if female, she must return the excess (if more than 500).
The text explicitly describes both outcomes.

Execution of Bequests: The One-Third Rule
Explains that bequests are executed from one-third after burial and debt, and legatee shares proportionally.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on execution of bequests and apply the one-third limit after burial and debt.

Core Idea
A bequest is taken from one-third of the estate after burial and debt; legatees share proportionally with heirs.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies a key rule in Hanafi inheritance and wills: bequests (wasiyya) are executed from one-third of the net estate after deducting burial expenses and debts. The legatee is not a priority; rather, they become a partner with the heirs in the residual estate. Understanding this rule ensures proper distribution according to Sharia.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its limits
The bequest is executed from one-third of what remains after burial and debt. Any excess is invalid without heirs' consent.
2
Understand the legatee's role
The legatee is not given priority; they share the estate proportionally with heirs. If the bequest is one-third, heirs get two-thirds.
This contrasts with burial and debt which are taken out first.
3
Distinguish from burial and debt
Burial expenses and debts are taken out in full before any distribution; bequest and inheritance are from the remainder.
