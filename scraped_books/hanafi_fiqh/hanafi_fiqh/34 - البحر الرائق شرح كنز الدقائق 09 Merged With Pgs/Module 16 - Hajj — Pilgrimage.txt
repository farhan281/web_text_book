When is Jaza' Obligatory for a Muhrim?
Ruling on condition for jaza' obligation based on state at shooting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for when jaza' is wajib for a muhrim who shoots game and distinguish it from the exceptional case mentioned by the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Obligation of jaza' depends on the state at the moment of shooting, not when the game falls.

This passage clarifies a common confusion: one might think the obligation of compensation (jaza') depends on the hunter's state when the animal is hit, but the Hanafi school holds that the decisive factor is the state of the hunter at the time of shooting. The general principle is agreed upon by all, but there is a nuance in cases of apostasy, where Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine when compensation (jaza') is obligatory for a Muhrim who shoots game. The passage states the ruling: jaza' is wajib if shooting occurs in ihram.
2
Identify the principle
The time of shooting is considered, not the time of hitting. This is the agreed-upon rule.
The Arabic phrase 'يعتبر وقت الرمي' encapsulates this.
3
Apply the principle to two scenarios
Scenario 1: Muhrim shoots then becomes halal before hit → wajib. Scenario 2: Halal shoots then becomes muhrim before hit → not wajib.
The transgression (ta'addi) occurs at shooting.
4
Note the exception
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad deviate in the case where a Muslim shoots then apostatizes before hit, considering it a discharge (ibra'), so no jaza'.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference.
5
Understand the justification
Jaza' is due to transgression (shooting in ihram); transgression occurs at the moment of shooting, not impact.
This logic supports the general principle.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the ruling on a Muhrim (one in state of ihram) who hunts. The general principle, agreed upon by all, is that the time of shooting determines the obligation of compensation (jaza') for hunting. If a Muhrim shoots and then becomes halal (exits ihram) before the arrow hits, he is still liable because the transgression (shooting while in ihram) occurred. Conversely, if a halal person shoots and then enters ihram before the hit, he is not liable because at the time of shooting he was not in ihram. The exception mentioned is from Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding apostasy: they considered that apostasy acts like a discharge (ibra'), making the game halal despite the shooter's state at the time of shooting. But the general rule holds for ihram cases.

Performing Hajj on Behalf of the Deceased through Bequest
Ruling on bequest to perform hajj on behalf of the deceased and related details.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for performing Hajj on behalf of a deceased via bequest and apply them to a scenario.

Core Idea
A bequest to perform Hajj obligates the estate to hire a proxy who rides and starts from the deceased's town.

This lesson addresses the ruling when a person leaves a bequest (wasiyyah) that the obligatory Hajj of Islam be performed on his behalf after his death. The Hanafi school holds that this bequest must be fulfilled from the deceased's estate, and the proxy must observe certain conditions such as starting from the deceased's hometown and riding (not walking). We also explore the limits on spending the deceased's wealth for the proxy's needs during the journey.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is performing the obligatory Hajj (Hajjat al-Islam) on behalf of a deceased person who left a bequest. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) upon the executor or heirs from the estate's third.
2
List the conditions for the proxy
From the text, extract the conditions: the proxy must be a man from the deceased's town, he must ride (i.e., not walk), and the journey must start from the deceased's hometown.
The condition of riding is because the deceased was not obligated to walk, so the proxy's mode of travel must match the deceased's obligation.
3
Analyze the expense rules
Distinguish between permissible and prohibited expenses from the deceased's wealth. The proxy can spend on necessities directly related to Hajj (e.g., washing clothes, body, head from dirt) but not on personal medical treatments (cupping, medicine) or water for wudu/ghusl (since these are personal purification needs).
The reasoning is that medical treatment and water for purification are considered personal benefits, whereas cleaning items are necessary for the Hajj journey.
4
Consider a case from Nawazil
Examine the scenario where the heir himself performs Hajj on behalf of the deceased and dies en route. If there is no other heir, the Hajj must be performed from the deceased's homeland, and the deceased heir's estate must pay the incurred expenses.
This illustrates that the obligation is fulfilled from the original estate, not the heir's personal money.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Given a modern scenario (e.g., a man bequeaths Hajj for his father, specifying a proxy from his village who will travel by plane), determine which conditions are met and what expenses are permissible.
Modern transportation like riding a plane satisfies the 'riding' condition. The proxy should still start from the deceased's town.

Ruling on Bequest for Hajj: Must Be from the Deceased's Town and Riding
Ruling on performing Hajj on behalf of deceased from his town

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling and conditions for fulfilling a bequest for Hajj and explain why the representative must be from the deceased's town and ride.

Core Idea
A bequest for Hajj must fulfill the exact obligation: from the deceased's town and riding, because the deceased was obligated to do so.

When a Muslim dies having never performed the obligatory Hajj, and leaves a bequest for it, the executor must ensure Hajj is performed on his behalf exactly as he would have done it. The Kanz al-Daqaiq matn rules that a man must be hired from the deceased's town to ride for Hajj. In this lesson, we unpack the legal reasoning behind this ruling and the conditions that make the bequest valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is fulfilling a bequest for Hajj (Hajjat al-Islam). The ruling is wajib upon the executor to carry it out as the deceased specified or, by default, in the manner that was obligatory upon the deceased.
The bequest is an obligation (wajib) because it aims to discharge a duty the deceased owed.
2
Understand the Proof (Dalil)
The commentator states: 'because he (the deceased) was obligated to perform Hajj from his town'. The proof is the deceased's own obligation; the bequest must replicate that obligation. This is a form of qiyas: the representitive's duty mirrors the deceased's duty.
The proof is aqli (rational) based on the nature of obligation.
3
Extract the Conditions
Two conditions are explicit: the representative must be from the deceased's town (baladihī) and must travel riding (rākiban). Walking is not obligatory on the deceased, so the representative need not walk.
These conditions ensure the act matches the deceased's obligation.
4
Note the Subsidiary Case (al-Nawazil)
If the representative dies en route, the heir must ensure Hajj is still performed from the homeland and bear the travel expenses already incurred.
This confirms the primacy of the location condition.

Insufficient Funds for Bequest of Hajj: Istihsan Overrides Qiyas
Ruling on insufficient funds for Hajj bequest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of istihsan to a bequest for Hajj when funds are insufficient, and distinguish it from the case of manumission.

Core Idea
If a bequest for Hajj falls short, perform it from the nearest possible point—istihsan saves the bequest.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi school regarding the execution of a bequest for Hajj when the funds are insufficient to perform it from the deceased's town. The passage demonstrates how the Hanafi principle of istihsan (juristic preference) overrides strict analogical reasoning (qiyas) to preserve the testator's intent as much as possible. Understanding this case clarifies the school's approach to fulfilling bequests partially.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The testator bequeaths Hajj from his town but the bequest is insufficient to cover the expense from that location.
The matn says 'otherwise, from wherever it reaches'.
2
State the qiyas
Strict analogy (qiyas) would void the bequest because the specified condition (Hajj from the town) cannot be fulfilled.
The text explicitly mentions 'والقياس أن لا يحج عنه'.
3
Apply istihsan
Istihsan overrides qiyas because the testator's objective is to have Hajj performed; partial fulfillment is better than complete voidance.
The text says 'جاز ذلك استحساناً لأن مقصوده تنفيذ الوصية'.
4
Contrast with manumission
Unlike Hajj, a bequest to buy a specific slave for manumission becomes void if funds are short, as partial ownership is not possible according to Abu Hanifa.
The text notes 'بخلاف العتق' and refers to a known distinction.

Bequest for Hajj When Property Perishes
Ruling on bequest for Hajj when property perishes after division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a bequest for Hajj when the property is lost after division, and explain the differences among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.

Core Idea
A bequest for Hajj attaches to one-third of the estate; if the property perishes, Abu Hanifa holds the obligation transfers to the remaining third.

When a testator bequeaths a Hajj, the executor must set aside up to one-third of the estate. If after division with the heirs the property allocated for the Hajj perishes, what happens? The Hanafi school has three distinct views. This lesson walks through the scenario and the reasoning of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The deceased bequeathed a Hajj. The executor divided the estate with the heirs, and the property in the executor's hand (the share for the Hajj) perished. Alternatively, the executor gave the money to an agent who lost it.
This is the starting scenario common to all three positions.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and its basis
Abu Hanifa rules that Hajj must still be performed from one-third of what remains of the estate. His reasoning is analogous to partnership: the legatee (the Hajj) is a partner in the third, so loss is shared and the remaining third still bears the obligation.
This is the relied-upon position in the school.
3
State Abu Yusuf's conditional ruling
Abu Yusuf distinguishes: if the original bequest consumed the entire third (i.e., the bequest equalled the third and all was lost), the bequest is void. If it consumed less than the third, Hajj is performed from what remains of the third up to its full amount.
Abu Yusuf looks at whether the entire third was allocated to the bequest.
4
State Muhammad's ruling
Muhammad holds that no Hajj is performed at all, because the property was lost before the act was carried out, so the bequest is simply forfeited.
This is the strictest view.
5
Note the practical consequence
In fatwa, the view of Abu Hanifa is preferred. The executor must use the remaining third to hire someone for Hajj, even if the amount is less than the original bequest.
The text mentions that this ruling has been established in al-Manask.

Remaining: If the deceased bequeathed a Hajj, and the executor divided with the heirs, and what was in his hand perished, or he paid it to a person to perform Hajj on his behalf, and what he paid perished, then Hajj is performed for him from one-third of what remains. Similarly, if he paid it to a man to perform Hajj on his behalf, and what he paid was lost, then Hajj is performed from one-third of the remainder. This according to Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf said: If the [designated?] amount consumes the entire third, the bequest is void and no Hajj is performed; if it does not consume the entire third, Hajj is performed from what remains of the third up to completion of the third. Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof regarding a bequest for Hajj (wasiyya bi al-hajj). The matn states: if the deceased bequeathed a Hajj, and the executor divided the estate with the heirs, and the property in the executor's hand (including the share allocated for Hajj) perishes, then the Hajj is to be performed from one-third of the remaining estate. The same applies if the executor gave the amount to an agent who lost it. This is Abu Hanifa's position: the Hajj is a bequest that attaches to one-third of the estate, and if the specific property is lost, the obligation transfers to the remaining third. Abu Yusuf distinguishes: if the bequest fully exhausts the third, then it is void because the entire third is gone; if it does not exhaust the third, the Hajj is performed from what remains of the third up to its completion. Muhammad holds that no Hajj is performed at all because the property was lost before performance and the bequest is considered unfulfilled. The commentator notes that this ruling has been established earlier in the book al-Manask (on Hajj rituals). The practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifa, the executor must use the remaining third to hire someone for Hajj, even if the amount is less than the original bequest.

Key Terms
حجة (hajja): Pilgrimage to Mecca; here refers to a bequest to perform Hajj on behalf of the deceased.
الثلث (al-thuluth): One-third of the estate; the maximum amount a testator can bequeath to non-heirs.
مقرر (muqarrar): The designated amount set for the Hajj bequest; possibly the sum fixed by testator.

Fear of Bandits as an Excuse for Hajj
Ruling on bandit threat as excuse from Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that unsafe roads excuse a pilgrim from Hajj and identify the condition that triggers this concession.

Core Idea
Road safety is a condition for Hajj; without it, the obligation is lifted.

Hajj is a fundamental obligation, but the Hanafi school recognizes that certain obstacles excuse a Muslim from performing it in a given year. This lesson examines the ruling that the threat of bandits on the road makes Hajj non-obligatory due to the absence of road safety, a key condition for the act.
