Dispute on Kaffarah in Intentional Killing: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Presents the dispute on whether kaffarah is required in intentional killing, with Hanafi response to Shafi'i.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on kaffarah in intentional killing, explain the Hanafi proof, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the distinction of offense against place vs. act.

Core Idea
Kaffarah is not wajib for intentional killing because it is a purely major sin, unlike accidental killing which mixes permissibility.

In this passage, the author of Al-Bahr al-Raiq presents the Hanafi ruling that no kaffarah is required for intentional killing (qatl al-'amd). He then engages with Imam al-Shafi'i's opposing view, which argues for kaffarah by analogy with accidental killing. The Hanafi proof relies on the nature of kaffarah as oscillating between worship and punishment, requiring its cause to oscillate between permissible and prohibited. The passage also addresses an objection regarding hunting in ihram and includes a critique of the standard response. Your task is to trace the reasoning and understand why the Hanafi school maintains its position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling and the Shafi'i opposing view
The text begins with the Hanafi ruling: 'لا' meaning no kaffarah. Then states the Shafi'i view that it is wajib. Identify these two positions clearly.
The Shafi'i view is introduced with 'قال الشافعي'.
2
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof: kaffarah oscillates between worship and punishment, so its cause must oscillate between permissible and prohibited. Intentional killing is purely prohibited (major sin), so it cannot be a cause, just like other major sins (adultery, theft, usury).
This is the core Hanafi reasoning: kabirah mahdah (purely major sin).
3
Examine the objection from muhrim's hunting and the response
An objection is raised: killing game in ihram is also a major sin, yet kaffarah is required. The response distinguishes between offense against the act (jinaayah 'ala al-fi'l) and offense against the place (jinaayah 'ala al-mahal). Hunting in the Haram is an offense against the place, so kaffarah attaches to the place, not the act.
Taj al-Shari'ah's response uses this distinction to preserve the general rule.
4
Evaluate the commentator's critique and additional Hanafi arguments
The commentator (Ibn Nujaym) critiques the response: (1) It does not refute the objection to the proof itself. (2) According to usul, kaffarah is a recompense for the act, not the place. Additional arguments: the Quranic threat of Hell (wa'eed muhkam) cannot be removed by kaffarah; kaffarah cannot be established by qiyas because it is a legislated matter with specific causes.
The verse 'fajazaa'uhu jahannam' is cited to show that kaffarah would abrogate the threat, which is impermissible.

Al-Shafi'i, may Allah have mercy on him, said: It is obligatory, by analogy with accidental killing, nay it is more appropriate because it (kaffarah) was legislated to erase sin, and sin is greater in intentional killing, so it is more deserving of being obligatory. Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the obligation of kaffarah (expiation) in intentional killing (qatl al-'amd). The Hanafi position (stated in the matn 'لا' meaning 'no') is that kaffarah is not obligatory. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that it is obligatory by analogy with accidental killing (khata'), and even more so because the sin is greater. The Hanafi proof is based on the nature of kaffarah: it oscillates between worship and punishment. Its cause must therefore be something that oscillates between permissible and prohibited acts. Intentional killing is a purely prohibited major sin (kabirah mahdah), analogous to other major sins like adultery and theft, for which no kaffarah is prescribed. The text then includes an objection raised by Taj al-Shari'ah (a Hanafi scholar) regarding the kaffarah for a muhrim who kills game in the Haram. That act is also a major sin, yet kaffarah is required. The response distinguishes between an offense against the act (jinaayah 'ala al-fi'l) and an offense against the sanctity of the place (jinaayah 'ala al-mahal). Killing game in the Haram is an offense against the place, not the act; kaffarah is attached to the place, and therefore the rule differs. The commentator then critiques this response on two grounds: (1) The objection targets the proof (that kaffarah is not attached to purely major sins) rather than the original claim, and the response does not fully rebut it. (2) According to usul, kaffarah is a recompense for the act, not for the place; if it were for the place, kaffarah would be unsuitable. Additionally, the commentator argues that intentional killing carries a severe threat (wa'eed muhkam) in the Quran (e.g., 'his recompense is Hell'), and kaffarah cannot remove such a definitive threat. Establishing kaffarah by analogy is invalid because kaffarah is a legislated matter (maqdooraat) not subject to qiyas in this way. Finally, adding kaffarah would be like abrogating the clear threat of Hell without evidence. Therefore, the Hanafi position stands: no kaffarah for intentional killing.

Key Terms
الكفارة (al-kaffarah): An expiatory act (e.g., freeing a slave, fasting, feeding the poor) required for certain offenses; in intentional killing, Hanafis do not require it.
قتل العمد (qatl al-'amd): Intentional killing; the Hanafi view holds it does not require kaffarah.
جناية على المحل (jinaayah 'ala al-mahal): An offense against the sanctity of a place (e.g., the Haram) rather than the act itself; used to explain why kaffarah is due for hunting in ihram.

Ruling on Intention for Qada' Fasts Without Specifying Day
Intra-Hanafi dispute on intention specificity for qada' fasts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on intention for qada' fasts without specification, and apply the more correct view (al-ashahh) with its thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
For multiple missed Ramadans, intention without specification fails; the asahh view requires specifying the year.

When making up missed Ramadan fasts, the intention must be present, but how specific must it be? Some Hanafi scholars allow a general intention even for multiple missed Ramadans. However, the more correct view draws a sharp distinction between a single missed Ramadan and multiple ones. This lesson unpacks the dispute, its evidence, and the practical consequence you need to know for issuing fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
First position (some mashayikh): general intention suffices even for multiple Ramadans. Second (al-ashahh): valid for one Ramadan only; multiple require specifying the year.
Both positions are within the Hanafi school.
2
Examine the proof for the lenient view
The lenient view analogizes to qada' prayers, where a general intention for missed prayers is valid without specifying each prayer or day.
Analogy (qiyas) is the core of their argument.
3
Examine the rebuttal for the stricter view
The stricter view argues that multiple obligations require distinction to avoid confusion; this is supported by qiyas on multiple missed prayers where specification is needed.
The rebuttal focuses on preventing uncertainty (gharar) in the intention.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If one owes days from two Ramadans and fasts without specifying, the lenient view counts it for one; the stricter view counts it for neither.
This practical difference dictates how the mufti advises.
5
Apply the mufta bihi
The more correct view (al-ashahh) is that for one Ramadan, no specification needed; for multiple, specify the year. In practice, always advise specification for multiple missed Ramadans.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa.

Swallowing Another's Saliva While Fasting: Kaffarah vs. Qada
Ruling on breaking fast by swallowing another person's saliva

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on swallowing another person's saliva while fasting and determine whether kaffarah or qada is required based on the relationship to the person.

Core Idea
Friend's saliva = desired → kaffarah; stranger's saliva = loathed → qada only.

This short passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a clear ruling that hinges on the psychological perception of the swallowed substance. The Hanafi school distinguishes between saliva that is naturally desirable (from a friend) and saliva that is repulsive (from a non-friend), assigning the heavy penalty of kaffarah only when the substance is akin to food. Understanding this distinction is essential for applying the correct expiation in fasting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its two scenarios
The act is swallowing saliva of another person while fasting. Two scenarios: if the saliva is from a friend (صديق) or from a non-friend (غير صديق).
2
State the ruling for each scenario
If from a friend: kaffarah (expiation) is obligatory. If from a non-friend: qada (make-up) only, no kaffarah.
Both scenarios invalidate the fast, but the penalty differs.
3
Analyze the underlying rationale (ta'lil)
The reasoning is based on the soul's reaction: friend's saliva is not loathed, so it is like a desirable food (bread); non-friend's saliva is loathed, so it is like a repulsive substance (dough).
This shows that the Hanafi school considers the psychological element of desirability.
4
Apply the ruling to a new case
Test: If a fasting person swallows the saliva of their spouse, is that a friend? The spouse is a friend, so kaffarah applies. If they swallow the saliva of a stranger, qada only.
The key is whether the person would typically find the saliva desirable.

The distinction hinges on the psychological aspect of the fasting person: what the soul desires triggers major violation, what it loathes triggers a minor one.

Distinguishing Kaffarah and Qada for Swallowing Another's Saliva While Fasting
Commentary expanding on the matn's ruling for swallowing another's saliva.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two scenarios of swallowing another's saliva while fasting and determine whether kaffarah or qada is required based on the relationship with the person.

Core Idea
Swallowing a friend's saliva requires kaffarah; swallowing a non-friend's requires only qada.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling in Hanafi fiqh regarding a fasting person who swallows another person's saliva. The key distinction is the relationship: the saliva of a close friend is considered desirable, while that of a stranger is repulsive, leading to different legal consequences. This ruling highlights how psychological factors can affect the application of kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between swallowing the saliva of a close friend (ṣadīq) and that of a non-friend. The ruling differs in each case.
The term ṣadīq implies a very close relationship, not just a casual acquaintance.
2
Understand the underlying rationale (ʿilla)
The soul instinctively finds the saliva of a stranger repulsive, whereas it does not for a friend. This psychological reaction determines whether the act is akin to eating something undesirable (which only breaks the fast without kaffarah) or something desirable (which requires full kaffarah).
The ʿilla is the soul's reaction; if repulsive, no kaffarah; if not, kaffarah is due.
3
Apply the ruling to each scenario
For a friend's saliva: qada and kaffarah are required. For a non-friend's saliva: only qada is required, no kaffarah.
The base ruling is that any substance entering the stomach breaks the fast, but the compensation (kaffarah vs qada) depends on the nature of the substance.
4
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) do not make this distinction; they consider all saliva as not breaking the fast or require only qada. This nuance is specific to Hanafi fiqh.
This is a mukhtalaf fih issue.

He said, may Allah have mercy on him: (And if he swallows the saliva of another, kaffarah applies if it is from his close friend, otherwise not). Meaning: if the fasting person swallows the saliva of another, if it is the spit of his close friend, kaffarah is obligatory upon him; but if it is not from his close friend, only qada is obligatory without kaffarah, because the soul instinctively dislikes and finds repulsive the saliva of someone who is not a close friend, so it is like dough and the like which the soul dislikes; whereas the saliva of a close friend is not disliked, so it is like bread and similar things which souls desire. Allah said: (Killing some pilgrims is an excuse for abandoning Hajj) because safety of the road is a condition for obligation or for performance, as we explained in the Manasik, and this is not attained when some pilgrims are being killed on the road, so he is excused for abandoning Hajj; he does not sin by that, and we have covered it fully in the Manasik, and we mentioned the disagreement, so we will not repeat it. And you may say: the chosen opinion regarding when there is a sea between you and Mecca – if safety is predominant, Hajj is obligatory; otherwise not. So it should be known here and it is said: if safety is predominant on the road, Hajj is obligatory; otherwise not.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expanding on the matn's ruling about a fasting person swallowing another person's saliva. The commentator explains that the distinction between requiring kaffarah (expiation) and only qada (make-up) depends on whether the saliva is from a close friend (sadiq) or not. The reasoning is psychological: a person's soul naturally finds the saliva of a non-friend repulsive, so swallowing it is akin to eating something disgusting, which does not break the fast in a way that requires kaffarah; it only requires qada. Conversely, the saliva of a friend is not repulsive, so swallowing it is like eating desirable food, which necessitates both qada and kaffarah. The commentator then attaches a separate ruling about Hajj: if pilgrims are being killed on the road, the obligation of Hajj is excused because safety of the road is a condition for either the obligation or the performance. He notes that this has been detailed in the Manasik section and that the relied-upon position is to judge based on the predominant condition regarding safety.

Key Terms
كفارة (kaffārah): Expiation required for deliberately invalidating a fast without valid excuse; here it is due when swallowing a non-repulsive substance.
قضاء (qaḍā’): Making up a missed fast due to unintentional or less severe invalidations.
صديق (ṣadīq): Close friend; used here to determine the ruling on saliva.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school distinguishes between friend and non-friend saliva in fasting, with different compensation requirements: kaffarah for friend's saliva, only qada for non-friend's.
