Scope of 'Walad' in Bequests: Includes Both Genders
Ruling on the scope of 'walad' in bequests.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the term 'walad' in a bequest includes both male and female children, and that the entire bequest goes to a single child if only one exists.

Core Idea
'Walad' means child of either sex; one child takes the full bequest.

In this passage, the great Hanafi jurist discusses the interpretation of the word 'walad' (child) in the context of a bequest. The ruling clarifies that the term encompasses both sons and daughters, and that it applies to singular as well as plural. This is crucial for drafting and executing wills in accordance with Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest phrase
The testator says 'to the child of so-and-so'. Recognize the key term 'walad'.
2
Analyze the term 'walad' linguistically
The word literally means child and includes both sexes (الذكور والإناث) in reality (حقيقة).
Refer to the Arabic phrase 'دخل الذكور والإناث لأن الولد يتناول الكل حقيقة'.
3
Apply the rule to the heirs
All children of the named person, male and female, are included in the bequest.
No distinction in share unless otherwise specified.
4
Consider the case of one child
If the named person has only one child, that child receives the entire bequest because 'walad' covers singular.
The passage states: 'ولو لم يكن له إلا ولد واحد فكل الثلث له'.

Core Idea
A bequest to an unborn child is valid if born alive; conditional bequests are allowed.

This passage also addresses bequests to a fetus (الجنين). The Hanafi ruling is that such a bequest is valid, but it is contingent upon the fetus being born alive. This reflects a broader principle that bequests can be suspended on conditions and that a bequest can be made to a non-existing entity in anticipation of its existence.

The bequest is valid only if the fetus is born alive (بشرط أن ينفصل حياً). If stillborn, the bequest lapses.

This shows the flexibility of bequests in Hanafi law.
4
Apply the ruling
If the fetus is born alive, it takes the bequest; if stillborn, the bequest is void or returns to the estate.
No share for the fetus until live birth.

Direct Children vs. Grandchildren in Bequests
Ruling on precedence of direct children over grandchildren in bequests.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine that direct children take precedence over grandchildren via a son in a bequest using the term 'walad', and that grandchildren via a daughter are excluded.

Core Idea
In bequests, 'walad' refers literally to direct children; son's children are included only when no direct children exist; daughter's children are never included.

The passage continues to address a common scenario: a testator has both direct daughters and grandchildren from a predeceased son. The ruling clarifies that the bequest goes to the direct daughters because they are the literal children (البنات الصلبية), while the grandchildren are only metaphorical children. If there are no direct children, then grandchildren via the son take the bequest, but grandchildren via the daughter are excluded altogether based on principles of lineage attribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heir categories
The testator has direct daughters (بنات صلبية) and sons of a son (بنو ابن). Recognize that both are potential recipients.
The son of a son is a grandson via a son.
2
Apply the literal vs. metaphorical distinction
The term 'walad' literally applies to direct children (البنات الصلبية), not to grandchildren. Grandsons via a son are included metaphorically only when no direct children exist.
The passage states: 'اسم الولد يتناوله البنات الصلبية حقيقة وولد الابن مجازاً'.
3
Determine the precedence
Direct children take priority. The bequest goes entirely to the direct daughters, excluding the grandsons.
4
Consider the scenario of no direct children
If the testator has no direct child, then the bequest goes to the son's children (males and females) metaphorically.
The passage says: 'فإن لم يكن له ولد صلب فالوصية لولد الابن الذكور والإناث'.
5
Exclude daughter's children
Grandchildren via a daughter are not included because they are attributed to the father's side, not the testator's side (lineage passes through fathers).
Passage: 'ولا شيء لولد البنت'.

Interpreting 'Elders' in Bequests: The Age Threshold
Ruling on interpreting 'akabir' in bequests

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi definition of 'akabir' (elders) in a bequest to determine which beneficiaries receive a share.

Core Idea
When a testator says 'elders,' Hanafi law defines it as those above fifty or in the first half of life.

This lesson examines how the Hanafi school resolves ambiguity in a bequest when the testator uses the term 'akabir' (elders). The passage establishes a clear age threshold based on customary understanding (urf), ensuring that the testator's intent is realized without uncertainty. Understanding this principle helps you correctly distribute bequests in similar cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguity
The testator bequeaths to 'the elders of so-and-so's children' without specifying which children. This creates a need to define 'akabir' (elders).
The term is ambiguous because it could refer to the oldest, the most senior, or another criterion.
2
Determine the Hanafi interpretive method
The Hanafi school uses customary practice (urf) to resolve such ambiguity. Here, the customary understanding is anchored to an age threshold: fifty years or the first half of life (middle-age).
This threshold is not arbitrary but derived from common usage and analogy of similar rulings.
3
Apply the threshold to the given case
In the example, some children are seventy, sixty, and forty. Applying the threshold (above fifty), the seventy and sixty-year-old qualify, while the forty-year-old does not.
The text also mentions 'or in the first half' as an alternative criterion, but the dominant reading is the age threshold.
4
Extend the principle to analogous cases
The same principle applies when a master says 'the elders of my slaves are free': the elder slaves are those above fifty or in the first half of life.
This shows consistency across different types of statements (bequest and manumission).

Plural Nouns in Bequests: The Two-Minimum Rule
Rulings on bequests to plural groups: distribution when actual count differs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a plural noun in a bequest requires at least two individuals to fully take that share, and calculate the distribution when only one exists.

Core Idea
A plural word needs two; one gets half, half returns to heirs.

When a testator uses a plural noun like 'sons', 'uncles', or 'sisters', the Hanafi school interprets it as requiring at least two individuals. If only one exists, that person takes half of the designated share and the other half goes back to the testator's heirs. This lesson walks you through several scenarios from the classical text to master this key principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the plural term
In each scenario, find the Arabic plural noun (e.g., بني, أعمامي, أخوالي, أخواتي, بني فلان). This is the category set by the testator.
Common plural forms: بني (sons), أعمام (paternal uncles), أخوال (maternal uncles), أخوات (sisters).
2
Count the actual individuals
Determine how many people actually belong to that category at the time of distribution (often at the testator's death). For example, 'sons of so-and-so' might be three or one.
The number may differ from what the testator assumed; actual count matters.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule of jam' = 2
If the actual count is two or more, the full share for that category is divided equally among them (per capita). If only one exists, that person takes half of the category's share, and the other half reverts to the testator's heirs.
This rule is specific to bequest and inheritance; outside these contexts, plural may mean three or more.
4
Distribute and handle reversion
Allocate the shares accordingly. Any portion that does not go to the named beneficiaries because the plural condition was not fully met returns to the testator's residuary heirs (the 'asaba).
Reversion is a key difference from gifts inter vivos, where the intent might be given more weight.

Core Idea
A bequest to 'relatives' includes clients and allies because the Prophet said they are from among the people.

When a testator leaves a bequest to the relatives of a tribe, it may not be immediately clear whether freed slaves (mawali) and allies (hulafa') qualify. Guided Path

2

The text states that mawali (clients), their clients, and allies (hulafa') are included in the bequest. This is based on the principle that they are considered part of the group.
Mawali include freed slaves and their descendants; allies are those who have sworn allegiance.
3

The hadith 'Inna mawla al-qawm minhum' (the client of a people is from among them) and 'Halif al-qawm minhum' (the ally of a people is from among them) are cited as the basis for inclusion.
This hadith establishes that clientage and alliance create a legal relationship equivalent to lineage for certain purposes.
4

This rule applies when the named person is a tribal figure (e.g., 'sons of so-and-so' as a tribe). If the person is a specific father, the bequest goes only to his direct sons, and mawali are excluded.
The distinction hinges on whether 'fulan' is a progenitor of a tribe or an individual.
5
Apply the distribution method
The bequest is divided equally among those eligible recipients whom the executor can reach. The hadith supports equal treatment.
If the group is uncountable, the executor distributes to those he can identify, giving each an equal share from the one-third.

Core Idea
Istihsan validates a bequest of future fruit even when qiyas would void it.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a classic Hanafi application of istihsan overriding qiyas. By strict analogy (qiyas), a bequest of non-existent fruit would be void because the object does not exist at death.

Core Idea
When the house is divisible, the one-third share is physically partitioned among legatees for their respective periods.

When a testator bequeaths the right to dwell in a house for different durations, and the house is the only asset, the heirs' refusal forces a strict application of the one-third rule. The case from al-Jami‘ shows how to physically divide the house so that each legatee gets his share of usufruct.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequests
The testator bequeaths dwelling to one man for 1 year and to another for 2 years. The house is the only property.
2
Apply the one-third rule
Since heirs refuse consent, the bequest is limited to one-third of the house value. Here, the house is divided physically into three equal parts: two-thirds for heirs, one-third for legatees.
The division is based on area, not time.
3
Divide the legatees' one-third
The one-third is split equally between the two legatees: each gets one-sixth of the house.

Both legatees dwell in their respective one-sixth portions for the first year. At the end of the first year, the one-year legatee vacates his portion, which goes to the two-year legatee for the second year.

Final return
After the second year, the entire one-third share reverts to the heirs, ending the bequest.

Bequest of Slave Service: Within or Beyond One-Third
Ruling with proof on bequest of slave service, with two cases and qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two cases of a bequeathed slave's service and apply the rule of muhaya'a when the slave exceeds one-third.

Core Idea
When a bequest of slave service exceeds one-third, the usufruct is divided by time in proportion to the shares of the heirs and legatee.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a bequest of a slave's service. The matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq, with commentary from al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah, presents two clear cases: if the slave's value falls within the one-third of the estate, the legatee receives full service; otherwise, the service is shared in time (muhaya'a) according to the respective shares. Death of the Legatee: Return of Bequest to Testator's Heirs
Commentary expanding two sub-rulings from Kanz al-Daqa'iq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi rulings on the death of a legatee and explain the reasoning (talil) behind each.

Core Idea
A bequest is a personal right; the legatee's death extinguishes it, and the property returns to the testator's heirs.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq expands on two scenarios from the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq: when the legatee dies after the testator, and when he dies before. The rulings hinge on the nature of a bequest as a contingent transfer of ownership that cannot be inherited by the legatee's heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers death of the legatee (1) after the testator and (2) before the testator. For each, note the different legal consequence.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
After testator: the bequested item returns to the testator's heirs. Before testator: the bequest becomes void (batil).
3
Analyze the reasoning (talil) for the first scenario
The testator gave the legatee the right to usufruct by virtue of ownership. If the legatee's heir inherited that right, they would own it permanently from the testator's property without his consent, which is impermissible.
The right is personal to the legatee, not transferable.
4
Analyze the reasoning for the second scenario
A bequest is a transfer of ownership deferred to after the testator's death. Since the legatee dies before that event, he cannot own anything after his death; the bequest is therefore void ab initio.
Ownership cannot be acquired by a deceased person.
5
Note the unanimity within the Hanafi school
This is the standard position (zahir al-riwayah) from the core Hanafi texts, with no intra-madhhab dispute mentioned.

Distinguishing Thamara and Ghalla in Bequests
Definition and linguistic analysis of bequest terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a bequest of thamara and ghalla, and determine what the legatee receives based on the wording used.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, bequest of thamara only covers existing fruit; ghalla covers both existing and future produce.

When drafting or interpreting a bequest involving fruit or produce, the precise wording matters. The Hanafi school distinguishes between thamara (fruit) and ghalla (yield/produce). This lesson examines the linguistic and legal basis for this distinction, and how adding the word 'abadan' (perpetually) changes the scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms
Understand the linguistic and legal definitions of thamara and ghalla. Thamara refers to fruit existing at a given time; ghalla refers to recurring yield including future produce.
2
Apply the default scope
If the testator bequeaths thamara and dies when fruit exists, the legatee receives only that existing fruit. No future fruit is included unless specified.
This is based on haqiqa (literal meaning).
3
Consider the condition of no fruit at death
If no fruit exists at death, thamara is used metaphorically and includes future fruit. This mirrors the ghalla rule.
The condition 'ifthamarat' in the text highlights this exception.
4
Examine the effect of 'abadan'
Adding 'abadan' (perpetually) to thamara extends it to include future fruit, making it like ghalla. This is by the generality of metaphor.
It does not combine literal and metaphorical but uses the scope of the word's metaphorical extension.
5
Contrast with ghalla
Ghalla inherently includes existing and future produce without needing extra wording. No condition of existence or 'abadan' is required.
The term ghalla is more comprehensive by custom and legal usage.

Commentary
This is a detailed linguistic and legal analysis of the difference between bequeathing 'thamara' (fruit) and 'ghalla' (produce/yield). The key distinction: 'thamara' by default refers only to fruit existing at the testator's death, while 'ghalla' includes both existing and future produce. The commentator explains this using the usul al-fiqh concepts of haqiqa (literal meaning) and majaz (metaphorical). But if 'abadan' is added, it covers both. This ruling is important for drafting wills accurately.

Key Terms

الغلة (al-ghalla): The annual yield or produce, including both what exists and what will come.

Bequest of Animal Produce: Wool, Offspring, and Milk
Brief matn on bequest of animal produce existing at death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default limit of a bequest of animal produce (wool, offspring, milk) and apply the principle of 'existing at death' to analogous cases.

Core Idea
A bequest of wool, offspring, or milk covers only what exists at the testator's death, not future yields.

This lesson clarifies a concise matn ruling from Kanz al-Daqaiq: when someone bequeaths produce from their livestock—such as wool, offspring, or milk—the legatee's entitlement is limited to what is physically present at the moment of death. The ruling mirrors the earlier case of fruit on trees, establishing a consistent principle for all perishable or renewable produce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequeathed items
The matn specifies three types of animal produce: wool (suf), offspring (walad), and milk (laban). Recognize that these are analogous to fruit in the earlier ruling.
Other animal produce, like eggs or honey, may follow the same rule by qiyas.
2
Locate the limiting phrase
The key phrase is 'al-mawjud 'inda mawtihi'—'that exist at his death.' This restricts the bequest to what is already present, not future growth or production.
The same phrase was used for fruit, showing a consistent Hanafi approach.
3
Understand the default ruling
Without additional wording (e.g., 'perpetually' or 'every year'), the bequest is limited to the existing stock. Any future wool sheared, lambs born, or milk produced after death does not belong to the legatee.
This prevents gharar (uncertainty) in bequests of future, non-existent property.
4
Recognize the exception
If the testator specifies 'perpetually' or similar wording, the bequest extends to future produce as it arises, analogous to the fruit case. The matn does not state this, but the school's position is clear from fuller commentaries.
This exception is based on the testator's explicit intent to include future yields.

Bequest of Usufruct: Return and Void
Rulings on bequest of usufruct: return and void conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on when a bequest of usufruct (service or dwelling) is void and when it returns to the testator's heirs.

Core Idea
A bequest of usufruct ends upon the legatee's death and is void if the legatee predeceases the testator.

This lesson explains the fundamental rules that govern bequests of service and dwelling. The Hanafi school treats these as valid bequests of usufruct, but their duration is limited and they automatically revert to the testator's heirs upon the legatee's death. Understanding these boundary conditions is essential for drafting valid bequests and resolving disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of bequest
The passage addresses bequests of service (of a slave) and dwelling (of a house), and also fruit of an orchard. Recognize that each is a form of usufruct bequest.
The term 'usufruct' (manfa‘a) is central.
2
State the default duration and limitation
The legatee has a right to 'one day' (a share) of the usufruct. Upon the legatee's death, the right returns to the testator's heirs. The bequest is personal to the legatee.
3
Analyze the void condition
If the legatee dies before the testator, the bequest is void because it is a postponed transfer (tamlik mudāf) that never takes effect.
The condition is the testator's death for the transfer to vest.
4
Examine the fruit bequest analogy
For a bequest of fruit, the legatee takes the fruit existing at death and, if specified 'forever', all future fruit as well. Then the house itself returns to the heirs. This mirrors the service/dwelling rule.

Commentary
This passage presents rulings on bequests of usufruct (service and dwelling). The author first states the basic rule: if the bequest is of service or dwelling, the legatee gets a share proportional to the one-third. If the object (slave or house) is within the one-third, it is handed over to the legatee outright. If it exceeds the one-third, the heirs retain their two-thirds share, but since the object is indivisible, the Hanafi scholars prescribe time-sharing (muhāyā'a): the heirs use it two days, the legatee one day. Upon the legatee's death, the usufruct right reverts to the testator's heirs. If the legatee dies before the testator, the bequest is void because the ownership transfer was contingent on the testator's death. The commentary also discusses the case of a house that cannot be divided, equating its ruling to that of a slave. The basis for all these rulings is the principle that the bequest operates within one-third of the estate, and the heirs cannot be compelled to give more than their share without consent.

الخدمة (al-khidma): The right to the service of a slave or employee; here specifically bequest of a slave's service.
السكنى (al-suknā): The right to dwell in a house; bequest of dwelling right.

Time-Sharing (Muhāyā'a) When the Bequest Exceeds One-Third
Rulings on time-sharing for bequests exceeding one-third.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi time-sharing (muhāyā'a) method to distribute usufruct of an indivisible asset bequeathed to multiple parties with different shares.

Core Idea
When a bequest of usufruct exceeds one-third, the indivisible asset is shared by time proportional to shares.

When a testator bequeaths the service of a slave or the dwelling of a house and the value of the bequest exceeds one-third of the estate, the Hanafi school does not invalidate the bequest. Instead, they prescribe time-sharing (muhāyā'a): the heirs and the legatee use the asset on a rotational basis proportional to their shares. This lesson details that mechanism and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for time-sharing
Time-sharing is applied when the bequest exceeds one-third of the estate and the asset is indivisible (e.g., a slave or a house that cannot be divided).
If the bequest is within one-third, the asset is handed over fully to the legatee.
2
Determine the shares
The legatee's share is one-third; the heirs' share is two-thirds. These proportions determine the time allocation.
This mirrors the principle in bequest of a specific item (waṣiyya bi al-‘ayn).
3
Apply time-sharing to a slave
Since the slave cannot be divided physically, the right to service is shared by time: the heirs use the slave two days, the legatee one day.
The passage states: 'خدم الورثة يومين والموصى له يوماً'.
4
Apply to an indivisible house
The same ruling applies to a house that cannot be divided: time-sharing based on the same two-thirds to one-third ratio.
In al-Zahiriyya: 'ولو كانت الدار لا تتحمل القسمة كان الحكم فيها كالحكم في العبد'.
5
Note the exception when the bequest is within one-third
If the bequest is within one-third (or if heirs consent), the asset is handed over to the legatee outright, and time-sharing does not apply.
The passage: 'فإن خرج العبد من سلم إليه ليخدمه'.

Upon the legatee's death, the usufruct right reverts to the testator's heirs. If the legatee dies before the testator, the bequest is void because the ownership transfer was contingent on the testator's death. The commentary also discusses the case of a house that cannot be divided, equating its ruling to that of a slave. The basis for all these rulings is the principle that the bequest operates within one-third of the estate, and the heirs cannot be compelled to give more than their share without consent.

الخدمة (al-khidma): The right to the service of a slave or employee; here specifically bequest of a slave's service.

The author of al-Inayah said after quoting this from al-Nihayah: The apparent is that there is no contradiction between his two statements because he said there 'correct' and here 'more correct', and both can be true. End quote. I say: This is nothing, for there is no doubt that whoever says in disputed matters 'it is correct' means preference of this statement over the other, not mere statement of its correctness while the other is more preponderant. Rather, his saying 'it is correct' is more indicative of preference than his saying 'it is more correct'. There is no doubt that preference of one over the other negates preference of the other, and they cannot both be true. The one hoping for his Lord's pardon said: The more similar is that she is like the dhimmiyya; her will is valid because she is not killed, and therefore all her dispositions are valid, and likewise the will. It is as if he meant by 'author of the book' the author of al-Hidayah. And al-Sighnaqi mentioned that whoever apostatizes from Islam to Christianity, Judaism, or Zoroastrianism, the ruling on his wills is like the ruling on those he converted to; whatever is valid from them is valid from him. And this is according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). As for Abu Hanifa, his will is suspended, and the wills of an apostate woman are effective by consensus because she is not killed in our view. And Qadi Khan said: The correct view is that the apostate woman is like the dhimmiyya; whatever is permissible from the dhimmiyya is permissible from her, and whatever is not, is not.

Commentary
This passage discusses the validity of bequests from dhimmis (non-Muslim subjects of an Islamic state) and apostates. The main principle is that a bequest is valid if it amounts to a transfer of ownership (tamlik), even if the designated purpose is not considered a religious good deed in Islam. The author distinguishes between three cases: (1) When the dhimmi bequeaths for purposes that are good deeds in Islam (like Hajj or building mosques) but not in his own religion, the bequest is void because it is not in his belief, unless it is given to specific individuals, in which case it is valid as ownership. (2) When he bequeaths for a purpose that is good in his own religion but not in Islam (like building a church), there is a dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa permits it because it is a transfer of ownership to the beneficiaries; the Sahibayn (Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) disallow it because it is for a sinful purpose. (3) The same applies to an innovator (sahib al-bid'ah): if his innovation does not make him a disbeliever, he is treated as a Muslim; if it does, he is like an apostate. The passage then discusses the apostate woman (murtaddah). Imam al-Hidayah states that the most correct view is that her will is valid because she remains upon apostasy and is not killed, unlike the male apostate. Al-Sighnaqi reports disagreement, and some hold that she is not like a dhimmiyya because she is not left upon her belief. The author of al-Inayah tries to reconcile the views, but the commentator rejects this, arguing that 'sahih' (correct) and 'asahh' (more correct) are not equal. The preferred view is that the apostate woman is like a dhimmiyya, so her will is valid. For conversion to another religion, the ruling on wills is according to the new religion. Abu Hanifa suspends the will of a male apostate until his return or execution. The overall ruling is that a dhimmi's bequest for a purpose not good in Islam is valid only as transfer of ownership; for churches, Abu Hanifa permits, Sahibayn prohibit. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) likely follows the Sahibayn due to later preference, but the text does not explicitly state tarjih.

Key Terms
تمليك (tamlik): Transfer of ownership, the basis for validating a bequest even if the purpose is not a religious good deed in Islam.
قربة (qurba): A religious good deed that brings one closer to God; here used to distinguish between acts that are good deeds in Islam vs. in the testator's religion.
