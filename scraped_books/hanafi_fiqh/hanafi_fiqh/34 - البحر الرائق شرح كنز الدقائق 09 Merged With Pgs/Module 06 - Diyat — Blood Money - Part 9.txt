Who Bears Qasamah: Owner or Resident? The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute with talil reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf on Qasamah liability when a body is found in a house or camp, and apply their reasoning to distinguish between owner and resident responsibility.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the owner bears Qasamah for a corpse found in his house; Abu Yusuf adds residents due to their authority of residence.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explores the application of Qasamah when a body is found in a house or camp. It presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute: while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold the owner solely responsible, Abu Yusuf extends liability to residents based on their protective authority (wilayat al-tadbir). Understanding this dispute illuminates Hanafi methodology of 'illah and istihsan. The passage also covers related cases of a wounded person moved between localities and the liability of a carrier.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize the two main contexts: a dead body found in a house, and in a camp (military encampment). Note that the camp is compared to a residential area.
The camp example sets a baseline: by consensus, Qasamah is on the camp commander (owner) not individual soldiers.
2
State the Hanafi Majority Position
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that only the owner of the house and his 'aqilah bear Qasamah and Diyah. The residents are not liable because the house is in the owner's hand and control.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
3
Present Abu Yusuf's View
Abu Yusuf argues that residents also have authority of management (wilayat al-tadbir) through residence, so they share liability.
His dalil is that residence alone establishes protective authority over the premises.
4
Analyze the Evidence for Each Side
Examine the proofs: For the majority, the owner's permanent settlement and exclusive benefit create stronger control and thus negligence. For Abu Yusuf, the act of dwelling grants a form of authority.
The text emphasizes custom ('urf) and durability of stay as supporting the majority.
5
Apply to the Camp and Wounded Person Cases
Apply the same principles: In a camp, liability is on the commander (like owner) because soldiers are transient. For a wounded person moved from one locality to another, liability is on the locality where the wound was inflicted. For a carrier of a wounded person, Abu Hanifa analogizes the carrier's hand to a locality, making him liable; Abu Yusuf rejects this analogy.
These extensions show how the principle of control/authority is applied consistently.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the ruling on Qasamah and Diyah when a dead body is found in a house or in a military camp. The author first compares a camp to a residential locality, stating that by consensus, in a camp the Qasamah and Diyah are on the owners (the commanders) of the place, not the individual soldiers. Then the text presents the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on one side, and Abu Yusuf on the other, regarding a house. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that only the owner and his 'aqilah bear Qasamah and Diyah, because the house is in his possession and control; the residents have no authority. Abu Yusuf argues that residents also share authority (wilayat al-tadbir) as they reside there, so they too are liable. The Hanafi response emphasizes that owners are the ones who customarily benefit from the property, their stay is more permanent, and they have the authority of management, so negligence is attributed to them. The passage also includes a case of a wounded person moved from one locality to another, where the Qasamah falls on the locality of the wound, and a case where a man carries a wounded person who later dies; Abu Hanifa analogizes the carrier's hand to a locality, making him liable, while Abu Yusuf does not. This shows the application of principles of responsibility and custody.

Key Terms
القسامة (al-qasāmah): A legal procedure of oaths taken by the relatives (wli) of a deceased when a dead body is found and the killer is unknown; it establishes liability for blood-money (diyah) if the oaths are sworn fifty times.
العاقلة (al-ʿāqilah): The male agnate relatives (asabah) of the person liable for diyah; they collectively pay blood-money.
ولاية التدبير (wilāyat al-tadbīr): The authority of management and protection over a property, which determines responsibility for negligence.

Liability in a Locality: The Role of Ahl al-Khattah and Buyers
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for Qasamah in a locality with original inhabitants and buyers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on who bears Qasamah and Diyah when a dead body is found in a locality with original inhabitants and buyers, and distinguish Abu Hanifa and Muhammad's view from Abu Yusuf's.

Core Idea
Liability for Qasamah follows custodial authority: the original inhabitants are primarily liable, but if absent, the buyer bears it.

In this lesson, we explore the first major scenario mentioned in the text: the discovery of a dead body in a house within a locality that has both original inhabitants (ahl al-khattah) and later buyers (mustarun). The Hanafi school has an internal dispute about who is responsible for the Qasamah and Diyah. The passage from al-Asl and its reconciliation by al-Karkhi provide the key to understanding the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify Key Terms
First, understand the terms 'ahl al-khattah' (original inhabitants) and 'mustari' (buyer). The original inhabitants are those granted land by the ruler; the buyer is a newcomer. Their legal authority over the locality differs.
Ahl al-khattah are considered the primary custodians of security.
2
Default Rule from al-Asl
The base rule: if a body is found in a house, Qasamah is on the owner and Diyah on his 'aqilah, meaning the ahl al-khattah. This is the agreed starting point.
The owner here refers to the original inhabitant, not the buyer.
3
The Dispute: Narrations about the Buyer
There are two narrations: one puts Qasamah on the buyer alone, another on his 'aqilah. Al-Karkhi reconciles them by linking to presence or absence of the buyer's tribe.
Reconciliation is a common Hanafi method to harmonize riwayat.
4
Position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They hold that if the buyer's tribe is absent, Qasamah is on the buyer alone; if present, on his tribe. This is the relied-upon view.
This view treats the buyer as the substitute custodian.
5
Abu Yusuf's Two Narrations
In one narration, Abu Yusuf puts liability on the residents (ahl al-khattah) only; in another, on both residents and buyers. Compare and contrast.
The thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in who pays.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on Qasamah and Diyah from the previous lesson, now focusing on more specific scenarios. It begins with a reference to al-Asl (of Muhammad al-Shaybani) which states that when a dead body is found in a house, the Qasamah is on the owner and the Diyah on his 'aqilah, meaning the original inhabitants (ahl al-khattah) of the locality. Then the text presents a reconciliation by al-Karkhi of two narrations: one that limits Qasamah to the buyer (mustari) alone, and another that includes his 'aqilah; the reconciliation is based on whether the buyer's tribe is present or absent. It then affirms that the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is that Qasamah is on the buyer (owner) when his tribe is absent, and on his tribe when present. The text then contrasts Abu Yusuf's two narrations, one blaming only residents and the other both residents and buyers. Next, a scenario is given where the owner of the house confesses to killing a thief; Abu Hanifa has two opinions, one exempting him and the other requiring Diyah but not Qisas. The passage then covers various other cases: when a dead body is found in a shop, vineyard, or land, the ruling is like that of a house; when found in a ruined locality, the nearest prosperous locality bears responsibility; when found in the house of a person whose testimony is not accepted (like a non-Muslim or a woman), Qasamah and Diyah are still due; when found in a woman's house, she takes the oaths and her 'aqilah pays; when two men are in a room and one is found dead, Abu Yusuf imposes liability on the other while Muhammad refuses due to doubt; and finally, a locked house found with a dead body imposes Qasamah and Diyah on the 'aqilah of the owner. Throughout, the principle is that liability follows the person(s) responsible for maintaining security in the place where the body is found.

أهل الخطة (ahl al-khaṭṭah): The original inhabitants of a locality who were granted land by the Imam (ruler) for settlement; considered the custodians responsible for security.
المشتري (al-mushtarī): The buyer who purchases a house from the original inhabitants; considered the owner in this context.
الدخيل (al-dakhīl): A newcomer or outsider who is not part of the original inhabitants; thus having lesser authority over the locality.

Special Scenarios of Qasamah: Ruined Places, Shops, and Two Men in a Room
Collection of special scenarios for Qasamah, including an intra-Hanafi dispute on two men in a room.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for Qasamah and Diyah when a dead body is found in a ruined locality, a shop, a woman's house, and in cases involving two men in a room, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute in the two-men scenario.

Core Idea
Liability for Qasamah extends to the nearest inhabited locality for ruins, and to the husband's 'aqilah for a woman's house, but two men in a room create doubt that suspends liability.

The text from Bahr al-Raiq continues with a series of special cases that expand the application of Qasamah beyond the simple house scenario. These include a ruined locality, a shop, a woman's house, and the puzzling case of two men in a locked room with one found dead. Each scenario illustrates how the principle of custodial authority is adapted to different circumstances, and the two-men case reveals a further intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
List the Special Scenarios
Identify the different scenarios: (1) owner confesses to killing a thief, (2) shop/vineyard/land, (3) ruined locality near a prosperous one, (4) house of a person whose testimony is not accepted, (5) woman's house, (6) two men in a room, (7) locked house with no one inside.
Each scenario modifies the default rule.
2
Analyze the Thief Confession Case
When the owner admits killing a thief, Abu Hanifa has two views: one exempts him, another imposes Diyah but not Qisas. Note the ambiguity.
This is about self-defense and property protection.
3
Apply the Default Ruling to Shops and Land
The text says shops, vineyards, and land follow the same ruling as a house: Qasamah on the owner, Diyah on his 'aqilah.
Consistency across property types.
4
Understand the Ruined Locality Rule
If a body is found in a ruined locality with no inhabitants, the nearest prosperous locality bears the Qasamah and Diyah.
Liability shifts to the closest responsible community.
5
Examine the Two Men Case Dispute
Two men in a room, one found dead. Abu Yusuf imposes Diyah on the survivor because the apparent case is murder. Muhammad refuses due to doubt – the survivor could have killed himself.
This is a clear intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the role of probability vs certainty.

Qasamah Liability in a Designated Plot (Khitta): Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qasamah liability in a khitta

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on who bears qasamah and diyah when a body is found in a khitta, and evaluate the differing positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
In a khitta, qasamah falls on the original settlers (ahl al-khitta), not on later residents — unless no original settlers remain.

When a dead body is found in a designated settlement area (khitta), the Hanafi school holds that the liability for qasamah (oaths) and diyah (blood money) rests primarily on the original inhabitants (ahl al-khitta) rather than on later residents or buyers. This ruling, attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, is contested within the school by Abu Yusuf, who argues for equal liability among all inhabitants. In this lesson, we examine the reasoning behind each position and the practical consequences of the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What is the legal question when a body is found in a khitta? Determine who must swear qasamah and pay diyah.
The khitta is a plot designated by the Imam for a specific group.
2
State the majority Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: qasamah and diyah are on ahl al-khitta only, not on residents or buyers.
This is the mufta bihi position.
3
State Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf says all residents are equally liable because all have the same capacity to protect.
He cites equal authority of protection.
4
Analyze the proofs
The majority relies on custom ('urf) that ahl al-khitta are the original protectors (asil); buyers are secondary (dakhil). Abu Yusuf relies on equal capacity.
Custom governs the hierarchy.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: if both ahl al-khitta and buyers are present, only ahl al-khitta pay per majority; all share per Abu Yusuf.
The ruling changes based on which view is followed.

Qasamah in Shared Property: Division by Heads vs. Shares
Ruling on dividing diyah and qasamah among joint owners with unequal shares

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for dividing diyah in shared property and explain why it is divided by heads rather than shares, and also identify the intra-Hanafi dispute with Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
In shared property, diyah is divided equally among owners by heads, not shares—because all have equal authority over the property.

When a dead body is found in a house owned by several people with unequal shares, who pays the diyah? The natural assumption might be that liability follows ownership proportion, but the Hanafi school—following Imam Muhammad from Abu Hanifa—holds that all owners are equally liable, regardless of their share size. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the intra-madhhab dispute with Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and issue
Recognize that the core situation is a slain body found in a jointly owned property where owners hold unequal shares. The legal issue is how to divide the diyah (blood money) among them.
The term 'qasamah' includes the oath-taking process, but here the focus is on financial liability.
2
State the Hanafi position (Muhammad from Abu Hanifa)
The relied-upon view divides the diyah equally among all owners by number of heads (owners), totally disregarding the size of each owner's share.
This is the position in al-Jami' al-Saghir and is considered zahir al-riwayah.
3
Recognize the opposing position (Abu Yusuf)
Abu Yusuf holds that the diyah should be divided in proportion to each owner's share of the property.
This is a valid ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
4
Analyze the proof for the majority position
The proof is that all owners, regardless of share size, have equal authority (tadbir) to manage and protect the property. Since they share equal control, they are equally able to be negligent or protective, hence equal liability.
The text compares this to pre-emption (shuf'a), which is also divided by heads.
5
Note the practical difference and tarjih
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is clear: with shares of 2/3 and 1/3, the majority says 50-50; Abu Yusuf says 2/3-1/3. The relied-upon view is the majority because it is based on equal authority and is recorded in zahir al-riwayah.
The commentator (in al-Bahr al-Raiq) adopts Muhammad's view.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Liability for blood money follows physical possession, not ownership, according to Abu Hanifa.

This lesson examines a specific scenario: a house is sold, but before the buyer takes possession, a dead body is discovered within it. The question is whether the seller's or buyer's 'Aqila bears the blood money and qasamah. The Hanafi school has an internal dispute: Abu Hanifa prioritizes physical possession (yad), while his two companions prioritize ownership. Understanding this disagreement highlights the legal method for determining liability in cases of negligence in preservation.

Liability for diya follows actual custody in movable property and public responsibility in public places.

The Hanafi school addresses the delicate question of who bears the blood money when a victim is found in a place with multiple potential custodians. Within a market, if shopkeepers spend the night in their shops (permanent presence), they are liable; otherwise, the entire locality bears the diya.
This is based on the idea that protection is incumbent on those who are present.

Diyah for a Corpse Found on a Ship
Ruling on diyah with intra-Hanafi expansion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah for a corpse found on a ship, identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, and explain the legal basis of yad (actual control) distinguishing movable and immovable property.

Core Idea
Liability for diyah follows actual physical control (yad), not ownership.

This lesson explores a specific ruling from the Hanafi school: when a dead body is found on a ship, who is liable for the blood money (diyah)? The matn provides a clear rule, but the accompanying commentary reveals an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other, grounded in how 'yad' (hand/control) applies to movable versus immovable property. By the end, you will not only know the ruling but also understand the legal reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling in the Matn
Read the matn: 'وفي الفلك على من فيها من الركاب والملاحين'. State the ruling: the diyah is obligatory on the riders and sailors (passengers and crew) who are on the ship.
The matn does not mention the owner, implying liability falls on those with actual presence and control.
2
Understand the Legal Basis ( Dalil)
The proof given is 'لأنه في أيديهم' (because it is in their hands). The concept of yad (hand/dominion) is key. The ship is movable, so those on it have actual physical control, making them liable.
This contrasts with a house (real estate) where both owner and resident may be liable.
3
Examine the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf analogizes the ship to a house: both owner and resident are liable. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (lahuma) distinguish: the ship moves, so it is genuinely in the hands of the riders, unlike real estate.
This distinction affects who pays: only the passengers/crew, or also the ship owner.
4
Review Additional Opinions from Later Texts
Al-Muhit says the liability is on passengers only, not the owner. Sharh al-Tahawi conditions liability on the owner having a known owner. Some say it is absolutely on the passenger. Muhammad's absolute statement in al-Nawazil is the relied-upon answer.
The mufta bihi is that the diyah is on the passenger (rider) regardless of owner knowledge.
5
Apply the Ruling to a New Scenario
Consider a passenger ferry where a dead body is found. According to the mufta bihi, the passengers and crew (riders and sailors) are collectively liable for the diyah, not the ship owner. This is because they have yad over the ship during the voyage.
If the ship is privately owned and the owner is also on board, he is included among the riders.

Liability for a Corpse Found in Public vs. Private Places
Ruling on diyah for corpse in mosque and street

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between public and private locations and determine which party pays diyah when a corpse is found, applying the Hanafi rule of protection-based liability.

Core Idea
The diyah for a corpse in a public place is on Bayt al-Mal; in a private place, it is on those responsible for protection.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a corpse is found and the killer is unknown, the liability for blood money (diyah) depends on the nature of the location. Public places accessible to all, such as the Friday mosque and main streets, are under the authority of the Iamam, so the public treasury (Bayt al-Mal) pays. In contrast, neighborhood mosques and private markets are under the protection of the residents or owners, making them liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of location
Determine if the place is open to the general public (e.g., Friday mosque, public street) or restricted to a specific group (e.g., neighborhood mosque, private market).
Public places are under the Iamam's administration; private places are under the protection of residents or owners.
2
Determine who is responsible for protection
The party charged with safeguarding the location is liable for diyah if a corpse is found there and the killer is unknown.
Protection duty creates a presumption of liability.
3
Apply the general ruling
If the location is public, Bayt al-Mal pays. If private, the residents, owners, or shopkeepers pay.
The Iamam acts as deputy for public places; residents/owners for private.
4
Note special cases
For markets, liability shifts if shopkeepers sleep in their shops (they are liable) or if there are residents in a remote market.
Al-Muntaqa distinguishes based on overnight stay.
5
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
Liability follows the obligation to protect; qasamah (oaths) cannot be applied to the entire public, so diyah falls on the treasury.
The Iamam administers public property on behalf of all Muslims.

Mosques, Bridges, and Public Spaces: Liability on Bayt al-Mal or Community
Expansion of ruling to specific public and community locations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the liability for diyah when a corpse is found in various types of mosques, on a bridge, or in a blind alley, applying the Hanafi rule that public places lead to Bayt al-Mal liability and private/community places shift to specific groups.

Core Idea
Public places (mosques, bridges) → Bayt al-Mal; community places (tribal mosques, alleys) → community 'aqila.

The passage expands from wilderness to human constructions. When a corpse is found in the Sacred Mosque (al-Masjid al-Haram) or at Arafat without crowd chaos, the diyah is on Bayt al-Mal without qasamah. For a Friday mosque (jami'), if the killer is unknown, Bayt al-Mal pays. But for a mosque built by a known person, the liability falls on his 'aqila. Similarly, for blind alleys or single prayer places, the 'aqila of the house owners are liable. Bridges and vaulted bridges are treated as public property: Bayt al-Mal pays. This lesson unpacks these nuanced rulings.

Guided Path
1
Classify the location as public or private
The first distinction is whether the place is public property (e.g., Masjid al-Haram, Friday mosque, bridge) or private/community (mosque of a tribe, blind alley).
Public → Bayt al-Mal; private/community → specific persons.
2
Apply the rule for mosques
If the mosque is a general Friday mosque (jami') and killer unknown, the diyah is on Bayt al-Mal. If the mosque was built by a known person and that person's identity is known, the diyah is on his 'aqila. If the mosque belongs to a tribe, the nearest houses pay. If the tribe has multiple mosques, the whole tribe pays.
The text even specifies that if the builder is known, his 'aqila is liable.
3
Apply the rule for dead-end alleys
A blind alley (darb ghayr nafidh) or a single prayer place (musalla wahid) is under the control of the houses opening into it. Their 'aqila are collectively liable.
This is analogized to a shared space.
4
Apply the rule for bridges and waqf
A bridge (jisr) or vaulted bridge (qantara) is a public utility, so Bayt al-Mal pays. For waqf, if the waqf is general (for the public), Bayt al-Mal; if specific (for a known group), the beneficiaries pay.
Waqf ruling is mentioned in al-Sighnaqi.

Core Idea
A dead body in a public place shifts diyah from the family to the community or Bayt al-Mal.

Rulings on diyah for found bodies by location type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine who bears responsibility (diyah and qasamah) for a found body by analyzing the location's ownership, custody, and public or private status according to Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Liability follows effective control: who manages the land or water bears the burden of unexplained deaths.

When a dead body is found and the killer is unknown, Hanafi law assigns responsibility based on the location's custody. The key principle is that the party with effective control over the place—whether an individual, a community, or the public treasury—must pay diyah. This lesson walks through the rulings for various locations: wilderness, rivers, mosques, alleys, bridges, and riverbanks, showing how ownership and proximity determine liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the location type
First, classify the place where the body was found: wilderness, large river, small river, mosque, alley, bridge, riverbank, etc. Each category has its own rule.
The text distinguishes between ownerless and owned lands, public and private waters, and communal versus individual spaces.
2
Determine ownership or control (yad)
Ask: Is the place in someone's hand (yad)? A wilderness has no owner, so no one has yad. A small river owned by specific people gives them yad. A mosque might be public or private.

If no one has yad (wilderness, large river), the death is hader (no diyah) or falls on Bayt al-Mal (public treasury) if the area is under Muslim control. If someone has yad, that party (owner, 'aqilah, community) bears diyah and qasamah.

Identify the responsible party
Specifically name who pays diyah: Bayt al-Mal (for public mosques, large rivers, bridges), the 'aqilah of the owner (for private mosques, small rivers, owned banks), the residents of an alley (for dead-end alleys), or the whole tribe (for tribal mosques).
Qasamah (oaths) accompanies diyah when the responsible party is a group or individual.

Liability for Bodies Found in Houses of Persons with Partial Legal Capacity
Expands rulings on bodies found in homes of persons with partial legal capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on liability (diyah and qasamah) when a body is found in the houses of a mukatab, slave, mudabbar, umm walad, or partial-free person, and explain the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A mukatab is his own 'aqilah; liability for a body found in his house is limited. A slave's death in his master's house usually incurs no liability.

The Hanafi school provides detailed rulings for cases where a dead body is discovered in a home owned by persons with varying degrees of freedom. The original matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq) did not address partial-free persons, so later commentators expanded. This lesson explores the key scenarios and the internal disagreements among the founding Imams, particularly regarding the liability of the community versus the owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Categories
First, distinguish the status of the house owner or resident: mukatab (slave under contract for freedom), slave (full slave), mudabbar (slave whose freedom is deferred), umm walad (mother of master's child), partial-free (mu'taq al-ba'd), and a fully free person. Each has different 'aqilah arrangements.
The 'aqilah is the group that pays blood money. A mukatab has no 'aqilah except himself.
2
Distinguish the Locus of the Body
Note whether the body is found in the house of the person themselves (e.g., a mukatab found dead in his own house) or in the house of another (e.g., a free person found dead in a mukatab's house). The rulings differ based on ownership and effective possession.
If found in one's own house, Ibn Abi Malik narrates no qasamah/diyah; the majority view (Hasan ibn Ziyad) imposes liability on the community.
3
Trace the Intra-Hanafi Disputes
For each category, identify the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. For example, for a slave found dead in a locality, al-Hasan ibn Ziyad reports that qasamah and value are due; another narration from Abu Yusuf treats it like animal homicide (no liability). For a partial-free person, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad treat him as free (community liable), while Abu Hanifa treats him like a mukatab.
Pay attention to the conflicting narrations: Ibn Abi Malik vs. al-Hasan ibn Ziyad. Later texts (al-Dhakhirah) seem to favour the Hasan narration.
4
Determine the Operative Rulings
Synthesize which position is mufta bihi. The text suggests that for slaves and mukatabs, the majority (including later texts) favours qasamah and value on the community's 'aqilah. For a slave found in his master's house, no liability unless the slave had debts; then the master pays the lesser of value and debt.
The ruling for a body found in one's own house remains disputed; the stronger view appears to be that the community is liable if no specific culprit is identified.

Liability of the Locality: Qasamah and Diyah After a Sword Fight
Fiqh ruling with commentary and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for a body found after a sword fight, explain the condition that shifts liability, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and the two imams.

Core Idea
The locality bears qasamah and diyah unless the guardian identifies a specific killer; the school is divided on whether this clears the locality.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a specific scenario: two groups fight with swords, and a dead body is left behind. Who is liable for the blood money and oath? The matn gives the default rule and an exception. The commentary then clarifies the exception according to different Hanafi views and adds a distinction for cases involving polytheists or Khawarij.

Guided Path
1
State the default ruling
Identify the general rule: when two groups fight and a body is found, the people of the locality (where the body is) must perform qasamah (oath) and pay diyah (blood money).
The reason: the body is among them and they are responsible for protection.
2
Identify the exception and its two interpretations
The exception: if the guardian (wali) claims the killer against a specific person, the locality is cleared. The commentary explains that this phrase can mean either (a) one of the residents of the locality, or (b) one of the fighters. The ruling differs accordingly.
Interpretation (a) leads to an intra-Hanafi dispute; interpretation (b) is by consensus.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
If the accused is a resident (interpretation a): Abu Yusuf holds that the locality is cleared (qiyas). Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the two) hold that the locality is not cleared (istihsan). Understand the basis: qiyas vs istihsan.
The text does not give a tarjih but leans toward the two's view as the relied-upon istihsan.
4
Apply the additional ruling from Abu Ja'far
The passage ends with a note from Kashf al-Ghawamid: this ruling applies only if the two groups are Muslims not from the enemy. If they were polytheists or Khawarij, no liability – it is treated as enemy action. Also when Muslims fight polytheists, the rule differs.
This limits the scope of the default rule.
5
Summarize the legal outcome
Consolidate: the locality is presumptively liable. The guardian can shift liability by naming a specific person, but according to the two imams, the locality remains liable if the accused is a resident, unless the accused is one of the fighters (consensus).
The burden of proof remains on the locality to exonerate themselves fully.

Qasamah and Diyah for a Dead Body Found Among Fighters
Explaining the ruling on qasamah when a dead body is found among fighters, distinguishing between Muslims fighting non-Muslims and Muslims fighting Muslims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasamah and diyah when a dead body is found among fighters, distinguishing between battles against non-Muslims and internal Muslim conflicts, and explain the role of the textual command (nas) versus presumption (zahir) in establishing liability.

Core Idea
When a body is found among Muslim fighters: no liability if against non-Muslims (presumed war casualty); liability if among Muslims (text commands qasamah).

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses a nuanced ruling in the law of qasamah (mutual oaths for unsolved killings). The default rule is that the residents of a locality where a dead body is found must swear oaths or pay blood money. However, an exception arises when the deceased was part of a battle against non-Muslims. The text explains why the presumption is reversed and defends the distinction against criticisms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
When a dead body is found in a locality and the killer is unknown, the residents are liable for qasamah and diyah.
This is based on the Prophetic nas attributing the killing to the locality.
2
Recognize the exception
If the deceased was engaged in a battle against non-Muslims, no liability because the presumption (zahir) is that the killer was a non-Muslim.
The death is considered a war casualty (hady).
3
Understand the objection
Critics argue that the same presumption should also protect residents when the fighting was between Muslims, because the apparent (zahir) that the killer is from outside the locality should be a defense.
They claim that al-zahir is a proof for defense (daf'), not for entitlement (istihqaq).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The liability is not based on presumption (zahir) but on a textual command (nas) from the Prophet (ﷺ) that attributes the killing to the locality when uncertain. This nas applies only when there is no strong counter-presumption, which exists in the war scenario.
Thus, the nas is overridden by the stronger presumption of enemy action in war.
5
Apply the distinction
In practice, if a Muslim is killed in a battle against non-Muslims, no compensation; if killed in a Muslim-on-Muslim conflict, the locality must pay diyah or swear oaths.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is clear.

Core Idea
Naming a specific killer does not absolve the defendant of the oath for himself and unknown others.

In qasamah, when the defendant accuses a specific person of the murder, the matn requires a specific oath. However, the Sahibayn differed on whether an oath on knowledge is necessary. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical consequence.

Core Idea
The 'Aqila system is an exception to individual liability, rooted in prophetic judgment and practice, to prevent undue hardship on the unintentional killer.

Guided Path
1

5

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It addresses the situation where the killer belongs to a group that receives government grants ('ata'). The ruling states that if the grants are distributed over a period longer or shorter than three years, the blood-money is still taken from those grants. However, if there is no official register (diwan), the liability falls on the 'aqila (the tribe or group responsible for paying blood-money), and it is spread over three years. The maximum amount taken from each individual per year is one dirham or one and a third dirhams, and the total over three years does not exceed four dirhams per person. The proof comes from the practice of Caliph 'Umar, who, when he established registers, placed the blood-money on the people of the register in the presence of the Companions without objection. This was not abrogation but an application of the same principle: originally, blood-money was on the tribe that provided support; under 'Umar, it shifted to those registered in the diwan. The commentator notes that if today people support each other by profession, the 'aqila would be the people of that profession. The estimation of three years is from the Prophet (peace be upon him) and from 'Umar. The author then discusses the principle that when a legal ruling comes contrary to analogy (qiyas), it is particular to its specific case. Here, since the direct obligation of blood-money in exchange for life is against analogy, and the Law only obligated it deferred over three years, the deferment is restricted to three years. Various branches are then derived: if the grants are for past years and come after the judgment, they are not taken; if for future years and come in one year, all is taken. The obligatory amount per year is determined by the total: one-third or less in one year, up to two-thirds in two years, and full in three. The same applies to the killer's own liability when retaliation is converted to money. If multiple killers are involved, each's 'aqila pays a proportionate share over three years. The period starts from the judgment date.

Distribution of Blood-Money Over Three Years Without a Register
States the ruling on how 'aqila pays blood-money in three years with maximum per person.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the distribution of blood-money by the 'aqila over three years, including the maximum annual and total contributions per person.

Core Idea
Blood-money is spread over three years with minimal contributions to ease the burden on the 'aqila.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a person is killed by mistake, the blood-money is the responsibility of the 'aqila (the killer's clan or group of supporters). To prevent hardship, the payment is deferred over three years. This lesson clarifies the exact limits set by the Hanafi school for each member's contribution, especially when there is no official register (diwan) to determine the number of payers.

Guided Path

Identify the time period
The blood-money is divided over three years. This is the standard period for deferment.
The three-year period is fixed, not negotiable.
3

Per year, each member of the 'aqila pays a maximum of one dirham and one third (1 1/3). Nothing is taken beyond this amount annually.
Al-Quduri reported an alternative view of 4 dirhams annually, but the relied-upon view is 1 1/3.

The total cap is not annual; it is the cumulative amount for the whole period.

The correct position is from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani: annual limit 1 1/3 dirhams, total limit 4 dirhams. This is the mufta bihi.

Who Contributes to 'Aqila? The Killer's Obligation and Adding Tribes
Explains adding tribes and killer's obligation with proof and dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on the killer's contribution to blood-money, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and apply the order of adding tribes when the primary 'aqila is insufficient.

Core Idea
The killer pays his share as part of the 'aqila because blood-money is based on mutual support, not on guilt.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq tackles two interconnected rulings within the Hanafi school: (1) when a tribe cannot cover the full blood-money (diyah) for accidental homicide, how are additional paying groups selected? (2) does the killer himself contribute? The Hanafi school holds that the killer is like any other 'aqila member and must pay, while Imam al-Shafi'i exempts him entirely. This difference reveals deeper principles about the nature of 'aqila as a system of solidarity rather than penalty.

This ensures the principle of alleviation (takhfif).
The order of 'asabat is the same as in inheritance (e.g., son, father, brother, uncle).
2
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on including close relatives
There is a difference of opinion regarding including the killer's father and sons. Some say they are included due to their closeness; others say they are excluded because they are few in number, and the purpose of alleviation is already achieved by adding other tribes. The passage does not give a definitive tarjih.
This ikhtilaf stems from balancing closeness versus numerical abundance.
3
Contrast the Hanafi and Shafi'i views on the killer's obligation
The Hanafi position (stated as 'qala' by the commentator) is that the killer is like one of the 'aqila and must pay his share. The Shafi'i position (attributed to Imam al-Shafi'i) is that the killer is excused (ma'dhur) and pays nothing, because if the whole debt is not on him, the part also is not.
This is the core dispute (mukhtalaf fih) between the two schools.
4

The Hanafi response: obligating the whole blood-money would be oppressive (ijhaf), but a part is not. Moreover, blood-money is based on nusrah (support), and the killer supports others more than himself, so he should contribute. The verse 'No bearer shall bear another's burden' (Al-An'am 6:164) is cited by Shafi'i, but the Hanafis interpret it as referring to bearing sin, not one's own obligation.
The Shafi'i also argue by analogy to zakat (half-dinar each).

For Arabs, the addition rule works because they preserve genealogies. For non-Arabs, there are two opinions: consider the nearest locality or defer to the imam's ijtihad. If the 'aqila are salaried employees, the blood-money is taken from their salaries over three years, preferring easier sources like grants (ata') over salaries.
This shows practical flexibility in applying the rules.

'Aqila for Freed Slaves and Mawla, and Non-Covered Crimes

Core Idea
The 'aqila pays only for free persons and only for non-intentional killings, never for the slave's acts or the killer's own settlements.

This lesson clarifies two foundational rulings about the 'aqila (the support group liable for blood-money) in Hanafi fiqh. First, who constitutes the 'aqila for a freed slave and for a free client (mawla al-muwalah)? Second, which types of financial obligations are the 'aqila not responsible for? Understanding these boundaries is essential for correctly assigning diyah in cases of accidental and intentional harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the 'aqila of a freed slave
The freed slave's 'aqila is the tribe of his patron (the one who freed him). This is based on the hadith 'The mawla of a people is from among them.' The freed slave gains support and identity from the patron's tribe.
Freed slave = mu'taq, patron = al-mu'tiq.
2
Identify the 'aqila of a mawla al-muwalah
For a free client (mawla al-muwalah), the 'aqila is his patron (the one who contracted the alliance) and that patron's tribe. This is analogous to the case of the freed slave because the client becomes part of the patron's group.
Mawla al-muwalah is a free ally, not a freed slave.

The 'aqila does not pay for: (1) crimes committed by a slave, (2) intentional killing (al-'amd), and (3) liabilities arising from settlement (sulh) or confession (i'tiraf) by the killer. The reasons are that slaves do not provide support, intentional killing is not covered by the 'aqila's obligation, and the 'aqila is not bound by the killer's voluntary agreements.

Resolving the Ruling on Aqila for Dhimmis
Critical refinement of a previous ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi linguistic principle of the negated indefinite noun to distinguish between factual non-occurrence and legal prohibition, and state that aqila among dhimmis is permissible if they establish it.

Core Idea
A negated indefinite noun negates existence, not permissibility.

This lesson tackles an apparent contradiction in Hanafi texts: one passage says dhimmis have no aqila, another allows it. The key lies in understanding the linguistic force of a negated indefinite noun. By mastering this usul tool, you will see that the earlier text merely reports historical practice, not a legal ban.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contradictory texts
Locate the two statements: earlier text (from 'Jinaayat al-Mamluk') says 'la aqilata' (no aqila); later text (here) says they may have aqila among themselves. Recognize the apparent conflict.
The earlier text is from a different chapter; the author is reconciling across chapters.
2
Examine the first attempted reconciliation
Some scholars answered that the earlier ruling was based on the majority case (ghalib). The author rejects this because the phrasing 'la aqilata' is absolute.
The rejected answer fails to account for linguistic generality.
3
Apply the linguistic principle (al-nakira al-munfiyya)
Recall the usul rule: an indefinite noun in a negative context indicates absolute generality (umum). Here, 'la aqilata' literally means 'no aqila at all'—impossible to be literal. Therefore it must be interpreted figuratively.
This is a Hanafi principle: al-nakira fi siyaq al-nafs tufid al-umum.
4
Derive the correct interpretation
Since a total absence of aqila is impossible, the phrase must refer to non-occurrence in practice, not impermissibility. Thus the earlier text describes what did not happen, and the later text clarifies that if it were to happen, it is permissible.
The author's preferred answer: 'la aqilata' means 'it did not occur', not 'it is unlawful'.
5
State the mufta bihi position
The operative ruling: if dhimmis choose to establish mutual aqila, it is valid and effective, even if their religions differ. This is the relied-upon view.
This is the view of the later Hanafi authorities (fatawa mutakhkhirin).

Recovering Compensation from a Guaranteed Portion: The Case of Two Walis
Case ruling with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on how an absent wali recovers his share when a slave dies after being partially given as fida', and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two companions.

Core Idea
The absent wali can only recover from the guaranteed (madhmun) portion held by the present wali, not from the trust (amanah) with the master.

This case from al-Mabsut illustrates how the concept of guarantee (daman) versus trust (amanah) governs the distribution of compensation when a slave dies after being partially handed over as ransom. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on whether the absent wali must sue the present wali or the master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
A slave kills a man by mistake. The victim has two guardians (walis). One present wali takes half of the slave as fida' (ransom). The other is absent. Then the slave dies, leaving no other property.
This sets up the need to determine how the absent wali recovers his share.
2
Understand the legal nature of the two halves
The half taken by the present wali is considered a guaranteed substitute (madhmun) for the diya. The half remaining with the master is a trust (amanah), not guaranteed.
This distinction is key: the absent wali cannot claim from a trust that perishes without guarantee.
3
Calculate the absent wali's recovery
The absent wali is entitled to half of the guaranteed portion. Since the present wali holds half the slave (guaranteed), the absent wali gets half of that half, i.e., a quarter of the total slave value.
The calculation reflects that only the guaranteed part is recoverable.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa holds that the choice of fida' by one wali does not bind the absent wali, so the absent wali still sues the master for half the diya. The two companions hold that the absent wali can only recover from the present wali (the quarter).
The practical consequence differs: who is sued?

The payer's statement about the khuntha's gender is accepted, and the diyah of a woman is paid immediately while the remainder is held back.

This lesson examines two interrelated rulings from Hanafi fiqh: first, when a father's testimony about his child's gender is accepted; second, how to determine the diyah when a khuntha is accidentally killed before gender signs appear. ولو كان الخنثى أبوه حياً فقال هو غلام ولا يعرف ذلك إلا بقوله لكان القول قوله وكذلك لو قال هي جارية فالقول قوله إذا لم يكن مشكل الحال لأن الوصي قائم مقام الأب وإن كان مشكل الحال لم يصدق. وإن قتل الخنثى خطأ قبل أن يستبين أمره قال القول في ذلك قول القاتل أنه ذكر أو أنثى إن كانت الدية تجب على القاتل إن لم يكن له عاقلة، وإن كان له عاقلة فالقول قول العاقلة وإن قالوا لا ندري فالقول قولهم ووجب عليهم دية وإن قالوا إنه أنثى وورثة الخنثى ادعوا أنه ذكر فالقول قول العاقلة لأنهم يدعون على القاتل والعاقلة زيادة خمسة آلاف درهم والقاتل والعاقلة ينكرون ذلك فيقضى عليهم بدية المرأة ويتوقف العقل إلى أن يستبين أمره أنه ذكر أو أنثى
And if the khuntha's father is alive and he says 'He is a boy' and that is not known except by his statement, then the statement is his statement. Likewise, if he says 'She is a girl', then the statement is his statement, provided the khuntha is not ambiguous (mushkil) in condition, because the guardian (wasi) takes the place of the father. But if it is ambiguous, he is not believed. And if the khuntha is killed accidentally before the matter becomes clear, he said: The statement in that matter is the statement of the killer whether it is male or female, if the diyah is obligatory upon the killer if he has no 'aqilah. And if he has an 'aqilah, then the statement is the statement of the 'aqilah. And if they say 'We do not know', then the statement is their statement and the diyah is obligatory upon them. And if they say it is female, and the heirs of the khuntha claim it is male, then the statement is the statement of the 'aqilah, because they (the heirs) are claiming against the killer and the 'aqilah an additional five thousand dirhams, and the killer and 'aqilah deny that. So they are adjudged to pay the diyah of a woman, and the payment is held back until the matter becomes clear whether it is male or female.

Commentary
This passage deals with two scenarios: (1) determining the gender of a khuntha for legal purposes, and (2) determining the diyah when a khuntha is accidentally killed. First, the father's testimony about his child's gender is accepted if the child is not already ambiguous in condition. This is because the father is the natural guardian and has superior knowledge. However, if the child is already known as ambiguous (mushkil al-hal), the father's statement is not accepted. Second, in an accidental killing of a khuntha before gender signs appear, the question arises whether the diyah is 10,000 dirhams (for a male) or 5,000 dirhams (for a female). The killer or his 'aqilah (if he has one) have the right to state the gender. Their statement is accepted, supported by an oath if necessary. If they claim the khuntha was female, they pay the lower diyah. The heirs of the khuntha may claim male, but the burden of proof is on them to prove the greater amount. The ruling is that the statement of the 'aqilah is taken, and only the diyah of a woman is due immediately; the additional amount is held in abeyance until the truth becomes clear (e.g., by subsequent signs). This ensures the killer is not penalized for uncertainty.

Key Terms
دية (diyah): Blood money paid for accidental killing: 10,000 dirhams for a male, 5,000 for a female.
عاقلة (ʿāqilah): The group of male agnates who pay diyah on behalf of the killer.
خنثى (khuntha): A person with both male and female reproductive organs, or whose gender is ambiguous.
يتوقف العقل (yatawaqqaf al-ʿaql): The payment (of diyah) is held back or suspended until clarification.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi law, when there is uncertainty about the gender of a deceased khuntha for diyah, the statement of the party liable to pay (the 'aqilah) is accepted, and only the lower amount is due immediately.
