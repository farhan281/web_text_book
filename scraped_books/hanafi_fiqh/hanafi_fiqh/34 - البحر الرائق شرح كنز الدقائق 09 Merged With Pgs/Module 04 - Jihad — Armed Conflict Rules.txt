Proof that Qisas is Only for Intentional Killing
Discussion of the verse on killing and its restriction by the hadith 'al-amdu qawad'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that qisas is wajib only for intentional killing, and evaluate the argument that the hadith 'al-amdu qawad' restricts the general Quranic command.

Core Idea
The hadith 'al-amdu qawad' restricts the general command of qisas in the Quran to intentional killing only.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the proof for qisas (retaliation) in the Hanafi school. While the Quran commands retaliation for killing, the Prophet's statement 'al-amdu qawad' specifies that it only applies to intentional homicide. This lesson also explores a subtle objection about the hadith's scope and the response to it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two proofs
The Hanafi school relies on the Quranic verse (2:178) and the hadith 'al-amdu qawad' as the primary proofs for qisas being wajib for intentional killing.
The verse commands retaliation, but does not distinguish between intentional and unintentional killing.
2
Understand the apparent generality of the verse
The verse 'Prescribed for you is retaliation in killing: free for free' appears general, covering all killings.
If taken alone, it would imply qisas for both intentional and unintentional homicide.
3
Recognize the restriction by the hadith
The hadith 'al-amdu qawad' (intentional killing requires retaliation) adds the condition of intentionality, restricting the general command.
This is a classic example of a specific hadith (khass) delimiting a general Quranic text (amm).
4
Analyze the objection from al-Kifayah
The author of al-Kifayah questions whether the hadith actually restricts the verse, since it only mentions intentional killing without negating other types.
The response is that mentioning 'intentional' would be pointless if it did not restrict the general rule.
5
Consider the commentator's alternative explanation
The commentator notes that the Prophet might have been asked specifically about intentional killing, so the hadith is an answer to that question, not necessarily a restriction.
This possibility is considered but does not change the established ruling that qisas is only for amd.

The book of Jinayat. Regarding killing a believer, unless it is said: the mentioned verse although it conveys sin for killing a believer intentionally only by its wording, yet it also conveys sin for killing a dhimmi alternatively based on the establishment of inviolability between Muslim and dhimmi considering obligation or abode as will come in detail. If it is said: the specificity of the proof remains with the generality of the claim from another angle, namely that the madhhab of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah is that a believer does not abide eternally in Hell even if he commits a major sin, and it is not proven, and the apparent meaning of 'whoever kills' in the mentioned verse is the one who deems it lawful, indicated by 'abiding eternally therein', so killing without deeming it lawful is outside the meaning of the verse. We say: we do not accept the apparent meaning of 'whoever kills' in the mentioned verse being the one who deems it lawful; it is possible that the intended meaning of 'eternity' mentioned therein is long stay as mentioned in the exegeses, so it does not contradict the generality of the madhhab of Ahl al-Sunnah. And even if we concede that the intended meaning is the one who deems it lawful as mentioned in theological books and exegeses, then the verse indicates the enormity of that crime and the certainty of sin in killing a believer intentionally without deeming it lawful as well; otherwise, his deeming it lawful would not necessitate eternity in Hell. As for retaliation, it is due to his statement (peace be upon him) 'al-amdu qawad' and the statement of the Most High {Prescribed for you is retaliation in killing: free for free} [al-Baqarah 2:178] etc. However, it is restricted by the description of intentionality due to his statement (peace be upon him) 'al-amdu qawad' meaning its obligator, for the apparent meaning of the verse necessitates retaliation by qisas wherever killing occurs and does not distinguish between intentional and unintentional, but it is restricted by the description of intentionality by the famous hadith accepted by the Ummah, which is his statement (peace be upon him) 'al-amdu qawad', i.e., its obligator is retaliation; similarly in the commentaries. The author of al-Kifayah said after that: It cannot be said that his statement (peace be upon him) 'al-amdu qawad' does not necessitate restriction because it is specification by mention and does not indicate negation of other than it, for we say: if this report did not necessitate restriction of the verse, then retaliation would not be obligatory for intentional only, so there would be no benefit in mentioning the word 'intentional'. End. I say: an objection apparently valid, and it should occur to anyone with sound nature, but I have not seen anyone other than him mention it. As for its answer, it is considered by me, for it is possible that the Prophet (peace be upon him) was asked only about the ruling of intentional killing, as the crime was intentional killing, so his statement (peace be upon him) 'al-amdu qawad' was an answer to their question. In that case, the benefit of mentioning the word 'intentional' is to match the answer to the question. With this possibility, how can the Book of Allah be restricted by the aforesaid hadith?

Commentary
This passage is a ruling_with_proof. It begins with a discussion on the verse about killing a believer and its extension to dhimmis, then addresses an objection about eternal punishment and istihlal. The main point is that qisas (retaliation) is proven by the hadith 'al-amdu qawad' (intentional killing requires retaliation) and the Quranic verse (2:178). However, the general verse is restricted to intentional killing by this hadith. The author of al-Kifayah raises a subtle objection: the hadith does not explicitly negate retaliation for unintentional killing; it just specifies intentional. The commentator responds that if the hadith did not restrict the verse, then mentioning 'intentional' would be pointless. He further notes a possible alternative: the hadith might have been an answer to a specific question about intentional killing, so it does not necessarily restrict the general verse. The passage guards against misunderstanding that the Quranic verse alone covers all killings; rather, retaliation is only for intentional murder.

Key Terms
عمد (amd): Intentional killing with a weapon or instrument likely to cause death.
قود (qawad): Retaliation, specifically qisas (the taking of life for life).
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation, the equal punishment for a crime.

Equality of Limbs between Muslim and Kafir in Qisas
Ruling and proof that limbs of Muslim and kafir are equal in qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that limbs of Muslim and kafir are equal in qisas and state the logical argument supporting the cutting of a healthy limb for a crippled one.

Core Idea
Limbs in qisas are treated as wealth; value parity allows equality between Muslim and kafir limbs.

This lesson addresses a key inter-madhhab dispute: whether qisas (retributive amputation) applies between a Muslim and a kafir. The Hanafi school holds that the limbs are equal based on financial value, while the Shafi'i school disagrees. We will follow the argument as presented in the commentary of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling from the matn: The limb of a disbeliever and a Muslim are equal in qisas. This means qisas is applicable between them.
This applies to dhimmī (protected non-Muslim) individuals.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The opponent (Shafi'i) objects that qisas between Muslim and kafir is not allowed because of value inequality and the principle that believers are protectors of one another.
The opposing proof is based on the verse 'al-mu'minun ba'duhum awliya' ba'd' (believers are allies of one another).
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi author concedes that value difference prevents cutting a more complete limb for a less complete one, but does not prevent the opposite. The example: a crippled hand is cut for a healthy hand. This is because the crippled hand is of lesser value, yet cutting it for a healthy one is permissible.
The argument is based on the analogy of limbs to wealth, where value difference only blocks the extraction of higher value for lower, not the reverse.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Hanafi law, if a kafir cuts off a Muslim's hand, the Muslim can demand qisas. Under Shafi'i law, qisas is not available; only blood money (diya) applies.
This difference affects actual judicial rulings in societies with non-Muslim citizens.

The Book of Crimes: Chapter of Retribution for Less Than Life. And the limb of the disbeliever and the Muslim are equal. And cutting a hand from half the forearm, and a penetrating wound that heals, and the tongue, and the penis, [are subject to qisas?]. We concede the existence of difference in value in the limbs and that it prevents execution, but what is rationally understood from it is prevention of executing the more complete by the less complete, not the opposite, for the crippled hand is cut from the healthy one, and you do not cut a woman's hand for a man's hand nor a slave's hand for a free person's hand. Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute. The matn states that the limb of a kāfir (dhimmī) and a Muslim are equal for qisas (retributive amputation). The commentator then addresses an objection: if we concede that value difference prevents qisas, why do we cut a crippled hand for a healthy one? The answer is that limbs are treated like property; value inequality is considered only when it is universal (prevalent), not in rare cases like a crippled hand. The difference between male/female and free/slave is cited as examples where value inequality prevents qisas. The opponent (Shafi'i) is addressed indirectly.

Key Terms
قصاص (qiṣāṣ): Retributive justice in bodily injuries, exact equivalent.
مماثلة (mumāthalah): Legal equality required for qisas; here defined by value in limbs.
