Hawala (Transfer of Debt) is Permissible
Opening statement of the Book of Hawala affirming its permissibility for debts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissibility of hawala (transfer of debt) in Hanafi fiqh and identify the legal nature of the contract as a valid transfer of debt.

Core Idea
Hawala is a valid transfer of debt; the creditor's right moves to a third party.

This brief passage opens the Book of Hawala in Al-Quduri's matn. Hawala is a contract that transfers a debt from one person (the transferor) to another (the transferee). The text declares it permissible for debts, establishing its basic legal validity in the Hanafi school.

2
Translate the key phrase
Translate 'ja'izah bi al-duyun' as 'permissible for debts'. Understand that 'ja'izah' indicates permissibility.
3
Memorize the ruling
Commit the Arabic phrase to memory, along with its meaning. This is the foundational statement for the chapter on hawala.

Consent of All Three Parties for Hawala
Ruling statement on a condition for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three parties in a hawala contract and state the condition of mutual consent for its validity.

Core Idea
A hawala is only valid when all three parties consent.

This lesson examines a foundational condition for the validity of hawala (transfer of debt) in Hanafi fiqh. The source text from Al-Lubab states succinctly that hawala is valid only with the consent of all three involved parties. Understanding who these parties are and why consent is required is essential for proper application of this contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract
Recognize that the passage is about hawala, a contract to transfer a debt from one debtor to another.
2
List the three parties
Identify the muhil (original debtor), muhtal (creditor), and muhal alayh (new debtor) as the three parties.
The Arabic terms are given in the text.
3
State the condition
The passage states that hawala is valid (tashihhu) with the consent (rida) of all three parties.
Consent must be explicit and free of coercion.
4
Understand the consequence
If any party does not consent, the hawala is invalid (batil). This protects each party's rights.
5
Reflect on the wisdom
The requirement of consent prevents injustice and ensures that no one is forced into a debt obligation they did not agree to.
This aligns with the general Hanafi principle of mutual agreement (taradi) in contracts.

Commentary
This passage outlines the condition for the validity of hawala: all three parties must consent. The transferor (muhil) is the debtor who transfers the debt; the creditor (muhtal) is the one to whom the debt is owed; the transferee (muhal alayh) is the person who takes over the debt. Without the consent of any of these parties, the hawala is not valid.

Key Terms

المحتال (al-muhtal): The person to whom the debt is transferred (the creditor)
المحال عليه (al-muhal alayh): The person who accepts the transfer of debt (the new debtor)

Effect of Completed Hawala: Transferor Freed
Ruling on the effect of completed hawala: transferor is freed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling of a completed hawala and identify the exception when the creditor retains recourse against the transferor.

Core Idea
Upon completion of hawala, the transferor is freed from debt unless recourse was intended.

Hawala is a debt transfer mechanism in Hanafi fiqh. The key question is whether the original debtor (muhil) remains liable after transfer. The matn provides a clear default ruling and one exception. Understanding this is essential for distinguishing hawala from guarantee (kafala).

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction
Recognise that hawala involves three parties: transferor (muhil), creditor (muhtal), and transferee (muhal alayh). The passage addresses the legal effect once the transfer is completed.
2
State the default ruling
The text declares 'the transferor is freed from the debt' (بري المحيل من الدين). This is the primary outcome: upon completion, the muhil's liability ceases entirely.
3
Identify the exception
The passage adds 'unless he intends his right' (إلا أن ينوى حقه). This means if the creditor explicitly intends to retain the right to claim from the transferor, the muhil remains liable.
This intention transforms the hawala into a form of guarantee (kafala).
4
Analyse the legal consequence
The default is full transfer (tabarru') freeing the muhil. The exception allows the creditor to preserve recourse, turning the arrangement into a secondary liability on the muhil.
Hanafi default is immediate release, but the exception shows party autonomy.

And when the hawala is completed, the transferor is freed from the debt, and the creditor does not have recourse against the transferor unless he intends his right (to do so).

Commentary
This passage explains the legal effect of a completed hawala. Upon completion, the original debtor (muhil) is released from the debt liability. The creditor (muhtal) cannot then claim the debt from the muhil. However, there is an exception: if the creditor explicitly intended to retain the right to claim from the muhil (that is, they agreed that the hawala was not a full release but a guarantee-like arrangement), then he may have recourse. The default rule is that the muhil is free.

Key Terms

المحيل (al-muḥīl): The original debtor who transfers the debt to another.
المحتال (al-muḥtāl): The creditor to whom the debt is owed.
المحال عليه (al-muḥāl ʿalayh): The person who assumes the debt (the transferee).

The Risk of Loss (Tawa) in Hawala: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conditions for tawa in hawala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for tawa according to Abu Hanifa and explain the third condition added by the Sahibayn, and identify the practical difference between their positions.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa limits tawa to two cases; the Sahibayn add a third: court-declared insolvency.

In hawala, the creditor's right to claim from the original debtor transfers to the transferee. But if the transferee fails to pay, under what circumstances does the creditor lose his right to revert? This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa recognizes only two scenarios, while his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), add a third. Understanding this difference is crucial for practicing fiqh in hawala transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept
What is tawa? It is the risk of loss meaning the creditor cannot return to the original debtor after hawala transfer.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
He says tawa occurs in two cases: (1) the transferee denies the hawala and takes an oath with no evidence, or (2) the transferee dies insolvent.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
They accept those two cases and add a third: the judge declares the transferee insolvent during his lifetime.
4
Analyze the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, if the transferee is declared insolvent by a judge but is still alive, the creditor can still revert to the original debtor. Under the Sahibayn, the court's declaration alone triggers tawa, cutting off recourse.
This affects whether the creditor can sue the original debtor after the transferee's court-declared insolvency.

Source Text

والتَّوَى عند أبي حنيفة بأحد أمرين: إما أن يجحد الحوالة ويحلف ولا بينة عليه، أو يموت مفلساً. وقالا : هذان، ووجه ثالث، وهو أن يحكم الحاكم بإفلاسه في حال حياته.
And the loss (tawa) according to Abu Hanifa is in one of two matters: either that the transferee denies the hawala and swears an oath while there is no evidence against him, or that he dies insolvent. And the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: these two, and a third aspect: that the judge declares his insolvency during his lifetime.
Source pages p. 185

Commentary
This passage discusses the concept of tawa (risk of loss) in hawala. When a hawala is completed, the creditor's right to claim from the original debtor is transferred to the transferee. However, if the transferee fails to pay, under what conditions does the creditor lose his right to revert to the original debtor? According to Abu Hanifa, the loss (tawa) occurs in only two scenarios: (1) the transferee denies the hawala and takes an oath, and there is no evidence to prove it, or (2) the transferee dies insolvent. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad add a third scenario: the judge declares the transferee insolvent during his lifetime. This means that if the transferee becomes insolvent and the court declares it, the creditor also loses the right to revert. The difference affects when the creditor can sue the original debtor again.

Key Terms
التوى (al-tawa): The loss or risk incurred by the creditor when the transferee fails to pay, meaning the creditor cannot revert to the original debtor.

Dispute Between Parties After Hawala: Transferee vs Transferor
Ruling on whose word is accepted in a dispute after hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on whose statement is accepted in a hawala dispute between the transferee and transferor, and distinguish between the two scenarios.

Core Idea
In hawala disputes, the word of the transferor is not accepted when sued by the transferee, but is accepted when sued by the creditor.

Hawala (transfer of debt) can lead to disputes between the parties about the nature of the transfer. This lesson analyzes two scenarios: when the transferee (muhal 'alayh) sues the transferor (muhil), and when the transferor sues the creditor (muhtal). The ruling turns on who bears the burden of proof and the default assumption about ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties involved
In each scenario, list the roles: transferor (muhil), transferee (muhal 'alayh), creditor (muhtal). Note that the transferee becomes the new debtor.
The same person can be transferor in one scenario and defendant in another.
2
Determine the claim and counterclaim
For each scenario, state what each party is claiming about the reason for the hawala. In scenario 1, transferor claims it was for a debt owed to him; in scenario 2, transferor claims it was for collection.
The counterclaim differs.
3
Apply the ruling: whose statement is accepted?
The text gives two opposite rulings. If the transferee sues the transferor, the transferor's word is not accepted. If the transferor sues the creditor, the transferor's word is accepted (with oath).
Memorize the distinction.
4
Understand the legal basis
In the first scenario, the hawala itself implies the transferor owes the transferee, so the transferor's claim contradicts the apparent. In the second, default is that the transferor retains ownership.
Default assumptions drive the rulings.
5
Note the role of the oath
In the second scenario, the transferor's statement is accepted with his oath, indicating a shifted but not absolute burden.
The oath is required for his statement to prevail.

And if the transferee claims from the transferor a claim equivalent to the amount of the hawala, and the transferor says: 'I transferred it because of a debt that you owed me,' his statement is not accepted, and he owes the like of the debt. And if the transferor claims from the creditor what he transferred to him, and he (the transferor) says: 'I only transferred it to you so that you could collect it for me,' and the creditor says: 'Rather you transferred it to me because of a debt you owed me,' then the statement is the statement of the transferor with his oath.
Source pages p. 185

Commentary
This passage deals with disputes that can arise after hawala. The first scenario: the transferee (muhal alayh) sues the transferor (muhil) for the amount of the hawala, claiming that the transferor owes him that amount. The transferor says that the hawala was because of a debt that the transferee owed him (i.e., it was a payment). The ruling: the transferor's statement is not accepted; he must pay the debt. This is because the hawala itself is evidence that the transferor owes the transferee, and the transferor's claim contradicts the apparent meaning. Second scenario: the transferor sues the creditor (muhtal) for the amount transferred, claiming that the hawala was only to collect the money on his behalf (not as payment of a debt). The creditor says it was payment of a debt. Here, the transferor's word is accepted with his oath, because the default is that the transferor retains ownership unless proven otherwise.

المحال عليه (al-muhal alayh): The person who is directed to pay the debt (the new debtor / transferee)
المحتال (al-muhtal): The person to whom the debt is transferred (the creditor)

Ruling on Suftaja (Bill of Exchange) as Disliked
Ruling on suftaja as makruh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define suftaja, state its ruling (makruh), and explain why it is disliked in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
A loan that yields a benefit to the lender is problematic — suftaja is disliked because it secures the lender from travel risk.

Suftaja is a financial instrument used in trade to transfer money safely across dangerous routes. While it seems practical, the Hanafi school rules it makruh (disliked) because the lender receives a benefit (safe passage) from the loan, which touches on the prohibition of riba. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that suftaja are makruh (disliked). Identify the act (suftaja) and the hukm level (makruh).
2
Define suftaja
Understand the definition: a loan where the lender gains safety from road dangers by having the loan repaid in a different location.
3
Recognize the illa (reason)
The reason for dislike is that the lender obtains a benefit (security) from the loan. In Hanafi fiqh, any loan that yields a benefit to the lender is akin to riba.
This is a key principle: qarḍ jarra nafʿan fa-huwa ribā.
4
Determine the degree of dislike
The ruling is makruh tanzihan (disliked but not haram), because the benefit is indirect and not certain. There is no explicit prohibition, but it is discouraged.
5
Differentiate from permissible transactions
Contrast suftaja with other forms of hawala (transfer) where a fee is charged for services unrelated to the loan itself. The benefit in suftaja is directly tied to the loan.

Commentary
This passage concludes the Book of Hawala with a ruling on suftaja. Suftaja refers to a bill of exchange or a kind of financial instrument used in trade, where a person gives a loan in one location to be repaid in another location, thereby avoiding the risk of carrying money during travel (e.g., bandits). The ruling is that it is makruh (disliked) because it involves a loan that yields a benefit (safety) to the lender. In Hanafi fiqh, any loan that brings a benefit to the lender is considered riba (usury) or leads to it, so it is prohibited or at least disliked. Here it is ruled makruh tanzihan (disliked but not haram).

Key Terms
السفاتج (al-sufatij): A bill of exchange or financial instrument used to transfer money safely
