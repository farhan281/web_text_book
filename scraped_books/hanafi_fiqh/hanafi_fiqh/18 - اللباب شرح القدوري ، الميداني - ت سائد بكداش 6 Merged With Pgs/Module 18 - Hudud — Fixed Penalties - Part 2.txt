Learning Objective

Core Idea
First theft: right hand; second: left foot; third: no more cutting, only imprisonment.

The hadd punishment for theft is applied in a specific order. The first theft results in amputation of the right hand. If the same person steals again, the left foot is amputated. A third theft does not lead to further amputation; instead, the thief is imprisoned until repentance. This lesson walks through that sequence and the rationale.

The text states that for the first theft, the right hand is cut from the wrist and cauterized. Note the precise location (al-zand) and the immediate aftercare (cauterization).
The wrist joint is specified to avoid cutting into the forearm.
2
Identify the second offence and its penalty
If the thief steals a second time, the left foot is amputated. This is the next limb in the sequence after the right hand.
The left foot is chosen to maintain symmetry and functionality.
3
Identify the third offence and its penalty
For a third theft, no further amputation occurs. Instead, the thief is imprisoned indefinitely until he repents.
The wisdom is to preserve the body from excessive mutilation and to give opportunity for tawbah.
4
Understand the rationale for stopping at three
The Hanafi view limits amputation to two limbs (right hand, left foot) to avoid total incapacitation and because the Qur'an mentions only the hand (Quran 5:38).
Other schools may differ, but this is the relied-upon Hanafi position.

The act under discussion is apostasy (irtidad). The ruling for a male apostate is that he must be killed after a specific procedure. For a female apostate, she is imprisoned indefinitely.
The killing is a hadd punishment, but it requires procedural safeguards.
2
List the procedural steps for a male apostate
The text states three steps: (1) Islam is presented to him, (2) any doubt (shubhah) is clarified, (3) he is imprisoned for three days. If he repents, he is freed; otherwise, he is killed.
If someone kills him before this procedure, it is makruh but no penalty on the killer.
3
Contrast the ruling for a female apostate
The text explicitly says a female apostate is not killed but imprisoned until she returns to Islam.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position; other schools differ.
4
Examine the property consequences
The apostate's ownership of property becomes suspended (zawāl murā'ī). If he returns to Islam, ownership is restored; if he dies or is killed, property acquired during Islam goes to Muslim heirs, and property acquired during apostasy becomes fay'.
This unique Hanafi ruling avoids immediate transfer of property.

Commentary
This lesson covers the procedure when a Muslim abandons Islam. For a male apostate: first, Islam is presented to him. If he has any scholarly doubt, it is resolved. He is then imprisoned for three days to reconsider. If he repents, he is released; if not, he is executed (killed as a hadd punishment for apostasy). If someone kills the apostate before this procedure, it is makruh (disliked) but no liability falls on the killer. For a female apostate: she is never executed; instead she is imprisoned indefinitely until she returns to Islam. Additionally, an apostate's ownership of property becomes suspended (zawāl murā'ī): if he returns to Islam, ownership is restored; if he dies or is killed on apostasy, the property acquired during Islam goes to Muslim heirs, and property acquired during apostasy becomes fay' (public treasury).

Key Terms
ارتد (irtadda): To abandon Islam and enter apostasy.
شبهة (shubhah): A doubt or ambiguity in the apostate's mind that might have led to apostasy.
زوال مراعي (zawāl murā'ī): A suspended or revocable cessation of ownership pending the apostate's fate.

The Apostate's Wealth and Bonds
Rulings on apostate's property, slaves, and contracts under three scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on an apostate's property, slaves, and contracts upon death, joining dar al-harb, or return, and distinguish the treatment of male and female apostates.

Core Idea
Property splits by acquisition period; contracts hover until the apostate's final fate.

This passage continues the fiqh of apostasy, detailing what happens to the apostate's wealth and contracts. A key distinction is drawn between assets earned while Muslim and those earned after apostasy. Contracts during apostasy are not automatically void but are suspended pending the apostate's death or return. Additionally, the status of certain slaves and the gender difference in legal capacity are addressed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The text covers: (1) death or execution on apostasy, (2) joining dar al-harb with a judge's ruling, and (3) returning to Islam after joining dar al-harb. For each, track property, slaves, debts, and contracts.
The scenarios determine how property is transferred and whether contracts are valid.
2
Trace the property division
Note that assets acquired during Islam go to Muslim heirs; assets acquired during apostasy become fay' (state property). Debts are similarly assigned based on when they were incurred.
This dual-track ensures that Islamic-law rights and apostasy liabilities are separated.
3
Examine slave freedom
When the apostate joins dar al-harb (death-equivalent), mudabbars and umm walads are freed immediately. This is because the master's status changes to that of a combatant, triggering freedom conditions.
The mudabbar's freedom was contingent on the master's death; joining dar al-harb is treated as death.
4
Understand contract suspense
All contracts (sales, purchases) made during apostasy are suspended (mawquf). They become valid if he returns to Islam, and void if he dies or stays in dar al-harb.
This suspension preserves the possibility of validity, unlike immediate voidness.
5
Note the gender exception
A female apostate's property transactions are valid during her apostasy, unlike the male's. The reason is that a female apostate is not killed but imprisoned, so her legal capacity remains intact.
This shows that the ruling hinges on the apostate's potential for execution, not the act of apostasy itself.

Commentary
This lesson continues the rules on apostasy, focusing on property and the consequence of joining enemy territory. If an apostate dies or is killed on apostasy, all assets earned while Muslim go to Muslim heirs, while assets earned during apostasy become fay' (state property). If he joins dar al-harb and a judge confirms this, his mudabbirs (slaves promised freedom upon owner's death) and umm walads (slave mothers of his children) become free, and his debts become immediately due. Debts contracted during Islam are paid from Islamic-acquired assets; debts contracted during apostasy are paid from apostasy-acquired assets. All contracts (sales, purchases, etc.) made during apostasy are suspended: if he returns to Islam, they become valid; if he dies or stays in dar al-harb, they are void. If he returns to Dar al-Islam a Muslim after the judge's ruling, he may reclaim any of his property still in the hands of his heirs. Finally, a female apostate's property transactions are valid during her apostasy, unlike the male whose transactions are suspended—this difference stems from the fact that a female apostate is not killed but imprisoned, so her legal capacity remains intact.

Key Terms
فَيْء (fay'): Property that Muslims acquire from non-Muslims without war (or as in this case, property of an apostate that goes to the state treasury).
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whose freedom is promised upon the death of the master.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who bears a child to her master, thereby becoming free upon his death.
