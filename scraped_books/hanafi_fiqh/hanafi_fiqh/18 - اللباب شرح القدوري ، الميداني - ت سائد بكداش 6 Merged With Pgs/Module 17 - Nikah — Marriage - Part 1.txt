Permissible and Impermissible Subject Matters for Settlement
Scope of sulh: permissible for property, usufruct, battery; not for hadd; marriage and slavery claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which claims can and cannot be settled (sulh) under Hanafi law, and explain the rulings for marriage and slavery claims.

Core Idea
Sulh is valid for property and battery claims, invalid for hadd, and asymmetrical for marriage claims.

Settlement (sulh) is a flexible tool for ending disputes, but it has limits. This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies what subject matters can be settled and which cannot, highlighting the Hanafi position that rights of Allah, such as hadd punishments, are not subject to compromise. It also addresses tricky cases involving marriage and slavery claims, where the settlement is treated as a different contract (khul' or manumission). Understanding these boundaries is key to applying sulh correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Scope
Read the first sentence: settlement is permissible for claims of properties, usufructs, and battery (intentional or accidental). This sets the broad rule.
Properties include cash, goods, debts; usufructs include rental rights; battery covers personal injury claims.
2
Note the Absolute Prohibition
Settlement is not permissible for any claim of hadd (fixed punishment). Hadd is a right of Allah that cannot be waived or compromised by individuals.
Examples of hadd: theft (sariqah), adultery (zina), false accusation (qadhf).
3
Analyze the Marriage Claim Cases
Case 1: Man claims marriage, woman denies. She pays him to drop the claim → valid, treated as khul'. Case 2: Woman claims marriage, man denies. He pays her → invalid. Understand the asymmetry: a man can divorce (khul') but a woman cannot force a divorce from a man who denies the marriage.
The ruling hinges on who is the potential divorcer: only the husband can initiate khul'.
4
Examine the Slavery Claim Case
Man claims another is his slave. The alleged slave pays the claimant to drop the claim → valid, treated as manumission for payment (itq 'ala mal). This is allowed because the claimant, if proven, would have the right to free the slave for payment.
The slave's payment is considered as purchasing freedom, not a settlement of a disputed claim.
5
Summarize the Principles
Sulh converts claims into new contracts: for property/battery, it's a straight settlement; for marriage, it becomes khul'; for slavery, it becomes itq 'ala mal. Hadd claims cannot be settled under any circumstances.
These conversions affect the legal consequences (e.g., khul' counts as a divorce, itq frees the slave).

Commentary
This passage sets the general boundaries of what can be settled. Settlement is permissible for claims involving property (e.g., money, goods) and usufructs (e.g., rental rights), as well as for claims of battery (jirah), whether intentional or accidental. However, it is not permissible for claims involving hadd punishments (fixed corporal penalties like those for theft or adultery), because these are rights of Allah that cannot be compromised. The passage then gives special cases: If a man claims marriage against a woman and she denies, and she pays him to drop the claim, this is valid as a form of khul' (divorce by redemption). But if a woman claims marriage against a man and he pays her to drop the claim, it is invalid because a man cannot divorce himself from a marriage that he does not acknowledge. Similarly, if a man claims another to be his slave and the alleged slave pays him to drop the claim, it is valid and treated as manumission for payment.

Key Terms

حد (hadd): A fixed punishment prescribed by Allah for certain crimes (e.g., theft, adultery).
جناية (jinayah): Battery or physical harm, whether intentional or accidental.
خلع (khul'): Divorce initiated by the wife in return for compensation.
عتق على مال (itq 'ala mal): Manumission of a slave in return for payment.

Rulings on the Missing Person (Mafqud)
Series of rulings on the missing person's estate, marriage, and inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the missing person's estate, marriage, and inheritance, and apply the 120-year death presumption.

Core Idea
A missing person is legally alive until 120 lunar years from birth – only then is death presumed for inheritance and iddah.

The missing person (mafqud) is someone whose whereabouts and life status are unknown. Hanafi fiqh provides detailed rulings to protect his wealth, family, and dependents while he is absent, and defines a clear procedure once he is legally presumed dead after 120 years from his birth. This lesson covers the key rulings from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the missing person's status
A person who disappears with no known location and unknown life/death status is called a mafqud. The judge preserves his rights by appointing a guardian.
The guardian manages property, collects debts, and supports dependents.
2
Recognize marriage preservation
The marriage of a missing person remains valid during his absence; no separation is granted based solely on disappearance.
The wife does not need to wait only because of absence; other conditions like lack of provision may change this.
3
Apply the 120-year death presumption
After 120 lunar years from the day of birth, the judge rules the missing person as dead. This triggers iddah for the wife and inheritance distribution.
The period is calculated from birth, not disappearance.
4
Determine inheritance timing
Only heirs alive at the time of the death ruling (after 120 years) inherit. Any heir who died before that does not inherit from the missing person.
Similarly, the missing person does not inherit from someone who died during his absence.
5
Understand the retroactive effect
The death ruling is effective only from the end of 120 years, not retroactively. Therefore, the missing person's death is not presumed earlier.
This avoids inheritance disruptions before the ruling.

Commentary

At that point, the wife's iddah begins (if the marriage is consummated, she observes iddah of death, i.e., 4 months and 10 days), and the estate is distributed among the heirs who are alive at that time. Anyone who died before the 120-year period does not inherit from the missing person. Conversely, the missing person does not inherit from someone who died during his absence, because his death is only retroactively declared from the 120-year mark.

Witness Requirements and the Dhimmī Witness Dispute
Witness conditions and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the witness conditions for a Muslim marriage and explain why Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow Dhimmī witnesses while Muhammad requires Muslim witnesses.

Core Idea
Two Muslim men suffice for marriage witnesses; Dhimmī witnesses acceptable when the bride is Dhimmī.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out the essential conditions for valid marriage witnesses and then presents a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: can a Muslim man marry a Dhimmī woman with only Dhimmī witnesses? The answer depends on whether witnessing is a purely Islamic function or can accommodate the non-Muslim party's status. Let's unpack the rule and the ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base witness rule
Note that marriage is not valid without two witnesses. They must be free, adult, sane, Muslim, either two men or one man and two women. Justice is not required.
This is the general rule for two Muslim parties.
2
Recognize the exception to the witness requirement
The text explicitly allows witnesses who are sinners or even those punished for qadhf. This shows that 'adalah is not a condition.
This is unique to marriage; for other contracts, witnesses may need to be 'adil.
3
Locate the specific dispute scenario
The scenario is a Muslim man marrying a Dhimmī (Scriptury) woman. The dispute is about the religion of the witnesses.
The marriage itself is allowed by ijma'.
4
Compare the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow two Dhimmī witnesses. Muhammad insists on two Muslim witnesses. Understand each dalil.
Implicit dalil for majority: the marriage involves a Dhimmī, so her community can witness. Muhammad: testimony is an Islamic act requiring Muslim witnesses.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities prefer the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) due to necessity and ease.
This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Nikah begins with the definition of the marriage contract: it requires an offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) using past-tense words (or one past and one future). The example given: 'Zawwijni' (marry me) and 'Zawwajtuka' (I marry you) – note that the offer is in the imperative (future) and acceptance in the past, which is valid. Then the text states the witness requirement: two free, adult, sane, Muslim men are required; or one man and two women are acceptable (as in financial transactions). The witnesses need not be 'adil (just) – even fasiq (sinful) persons can witness a marriage, and even those who have been punished for slander (qadhf) are not disqualified (contrary to their rejection in testimony for other claims). The exception is that the witnesses must be Muslims for a Muslim marriage. Then a specific case of a Muslim man marrying a Dhimmī (scriptury) woman: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf permit the marriage with the testimony of two Dhimmī witnesses, because the marriage itself is valid (a Muslim may marry a Kitabiyya). However, Imam Muhammad requires Muslim witnesses even for a Dhimmī bride because the witnesses validate the marriage, and non-Muslim testimony is not acceptable in Muslim affairs. The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, as the marriage of a Muslim to a Kitabiyya is a matter between them, and Dhimmīs can be trusted to witness it.

شاهدين (shāhidayn): Two witnesses required for the validity of a Muslim marriage contract.
محدودين في قَذْف (madhūdīn fī qadhf): Persons who have been subjected to the hadd punishment for false accusation of adultery (qadhf).

Lists female relatives permanently prohibited for marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the seven categories of women permanently prohibited for marriage in Hanafi fiqh and correctly apply the rules of nasab and musaharah.

Core Idea
The permanent prohibitions in marriage: mothers, daughters, sisters, aunts, nieces, grandmothers, and mothers-in-law.

Understanding who is permanently prohibited (muharramat mu’abbadah) is fundamental to Islamic marriage law. This passage from Al-Quduri’s Mukhtasar provides a concise list that forms the basis for all further study in the Hanafi school. Memorizing these categories is an essential first step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of prohibition
The passage lists seven categories: mother, grandmothers, daughter (including granddaughters), sister, nieces, aunts (paternal and maternal), and mother-in-law.
2
Classify as nasab or musaharah
Blood relations: mother, grandmothers, daughter, sister, nieces, aunts. In-law: mother-in-law.
3

This phrase includes all female descendants, not just direct daughters – i.e., granddaughters, great-granddaughters, etc.
4
Observe the timing of mother-in-law prohibition
The mother-in-law is prohibited immediately upon the marriage contract, whether or not consummation has occurred.
Contrast with stepdaughter who is only prohibited if consummation occurred.
5
Memorize the list using a mnemonic
Use the acronym: 'Mothers, Grandmothers, Daughters, Sisters, Nieces, Aunts, Mother-in-law'.
Repetition and written recall are recommended.

Rulings on contract formation and witness requirements.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Marriage requires a clear offer and acceptance in past tense, plus two witnesses; their uprightness is not required.

This passage introduces the fundamental building blocks of a valid marriage (nikāḥ) in the Hanafi school. The contract hinges on two declarations: the offer (ījāb) and acceptance (qabūl), both typically in past tense. Additionally, the presence of witnesses is a mandatory condition, though their moral uprightness is not necessary. Woman Authorizing a Man to Marry Her to Himself
Ruling on woman's authorization in marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ruling when a woman authorizes a man to marry her to himself and identify the condition that makes the marriage valid.

Core Idea
A woman can authorize a man to marry her to himself; the marriage is valid only if contracted before two witnesses.

The second ruling in this passage expands agency in marriage: a woman can appoint a specific man as her agent to marry her to himself. This might seem irregular, but the Hanafi school permits it with the safeguard of two witnesses. This lesson focuses on the condition that validates such a marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
A woman gives permission (idhn) to a man to marry her to himself. The man then performs the marriage contract.
2
State the ruling
The text says 'جاز' meaning the marriage is valid.
3
Identify the condition
The condition is that the contract must be performed in the presence of two witnesses ('بحضرة شاهدين').
4
Understand the implication
The woman's authorization is key; without it, the marriage would not be valid. The witnesses ensure transparency.

Source Text

ويجوز لابن العم أن يزوج بنت عمه من نفسه. وإذا أذنت المرأة لرجل أن يزوجها من نفسه، فعقد بحضرة شاهدين : جاز. وإذا ضَمِنَ الولي المهر للمرأة: صحٌ ضمانه، وللمرأة الخيار في مطالبة زوجها، أو وليها.
And it is permissible for a paternal cousin to marry the daughter of his paternal uncle to himself. And if a woman authorizes a man to marry her to himself, and he contracts in the presence of two witnesses, it is valid. And if the guardian guarantees the mahr for the woman, his guarantee is valid, and the woman has the choice to demand payment from her husband or from her guardian.
Source pages p. 225

Commentary
This passage covers three rulings. First, a paternal cousin can act as guardian and marry himself to his cousin; he can contract the marriage himself. Second, if a woman authorizes a specific man to marry her to himself (i.e., she appoints him as her agent and he marries her to himself), the marriage is valid as long as two witnesses are present. Third, if the guardian (wali) guarantees the dower for the woman, the guarantee is valid, and the woman can choose to demand the dower from either the husband or the guardian.

Key Terms
ضمان (daman): Guarantee, a promise to pay the mahr on behalf of the husband.
