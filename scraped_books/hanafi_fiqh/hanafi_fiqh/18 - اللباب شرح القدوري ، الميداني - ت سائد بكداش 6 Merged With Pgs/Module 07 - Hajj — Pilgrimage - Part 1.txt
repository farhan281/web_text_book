Conditions for the Obligation of Hajj
Conditions for Hajj obligation and child/slave rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the seven conditions that make Hajj obligatory and identify the additional condition for women.

Core Idea
Hajj is wajib only when you are free, Muslim, adult, sane, healthy, able, and the route is safe.

This passage lists the prerequisites that trigger the obligation of Hajj. Understanding these conditions is essential to determine when Hajj becomes compulsory and when it can be delayed. The text also addresses special cases like children, slaves, and women's travel requirements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The text begins by stating that Hajj is wajib (obligatory). This is the overarching ruling that applies when all conditions are met.
The obligation is a one-time duty for those who qualify.
2
List the general conditions for obligation
Seven conditions are mentioned: free (not a slave), Muslim, adult (baligh), sane (aql), healthy (from severe illness that prevents travel), ability to afford provisions and mount (zad and rahila), and safety of the route (amn al-tariq).
Ability also means having surplus beyond essential needs and dependents' expenses for the journey.
3
Understand the financial ability condition
The text specifies that the person must have resources beyond their dwelling, indispensable needs, and the maintenance of dependents until return. This ensures that Hajj does not cause hardship.
The surplus must be enough for both travel and provisions.
4
Recognize the additional condition for women
For a woman, Hajj is obligatory only if she has a mahram (unmarriageable male relative) or her husband to accompany her, provided the journey is three days or more. She may not travel with anyone else.
This condition ensures her safety and protection.
5
Understand the child/slave rule
If a child performs Hajj and reaches puberty after entering ihram, or a slave is freed and continues, that Hajj does not count as the obligatory Hajj of Islam. They must perform another Hajj later.
The obligation starts only when conditions are fully met at the time of ihram.

The Five Fixed Miqat for Entering Ihram
List of the five miqat locations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to name the five miqat and identify which miqat applies to a pilgrim based on their origin.

Core Idea
No pilgrim may pass the miqat without entering ihram; each region has a designated station.

This part of the text specifies the boundaries (mawaqit) for entering ihram. Pilgrims coming from different directions must assume ihram before crossing these points. The five locations cover Madinah, Iraq, Sham, Najd, and Yemen.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The text states that it is not permissible to pass these points without being in ihram. This is a binding requirement for the validity of Hajj and Umrah.
Pilgrims coming from beyond these points must enter ihram before crossing.
2
List the five miqat and their corresponding regions
Memorize each miqat with its region: Dhu al-Hulayfah for Madinah, Dhat 'Irq for Iraq, al-Juhfah for Sham, Qarn al-Manazil for Najd, and Yalamlam for Yemen.
These miqat are fixed and well-known.
3
Apply the miqat rule to a pilgrim
Determine the pilgrim's origin or route to know which miqat they must use. If passing multiple miqat, the nearest one to Makkah is sufficient, but it is better to enter ihram at the first.
Hanafis allow entering ihram before the miqat, but passing without ihram is prohibited.

Conditions for Hajj Becoming Obligatory
Stating the conditions for Hajj becoming wajib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the conditions of istita'ah that make Hajj obligatory and determine whether a given individual is obligated.

Core Idea
Hajj is obligatory only when istita'ah (ability) is present: provisions, transport, safety, and fulfilling basic needs.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn distills the Hanafi conditions for the obligation of Hajj. Unlike many other acts of worship, Hajj is not required unless one has the physical and financial means. Understanding these conditions precisely is essential for knowing when the obligation applies — and when it does not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text states that Hajj is wajib (obligatory) upon certain individuals who meet specific conditions.
The term 'wajib' here is used in the sense of fard (obligatory).
2
List the personal conditions
The person must be free, Muslim, adult, sane, and healthy.
These are the eligibility conditions related to the individual's status and capacity.
3
Identify the resource conditions
The individual must possess provisions (zad) and a mount (rahila) beyond what is needed for their dwelling, necessities, and family maintenance for the duration of travel.
This ensures that the obligation does not cause hardship to the individual or their dependents.
4
Identify the external condition
The road to Hajj must be safe (tariq amin).
Safety includes protection from harm and, in the case of women, the availability of a mahram.
5
Synthesize into istita'ah
All these conditions together constitute istita'ah (ability). If any condition is missing, Hajj is not obligatory.
Istita'ah is the basis for the obligation of Hajj.

The Mahram Requirement for a Woman's Hajj
Ruling statement on the mahram condition for a woman's Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of a mahram for a woman's Hajj and identify when the Hajj becomes obligatory or not based on distance.

Core Idea
A woman's Hajj is not obligatory if she lacks a mahram for a journey of three days or more.

This lesson addresses a specific condition for the obligation of Hajj upon women. The Hanafi school holds that a woman must be accompanied by a mahram or her husband if the journey to Mecca takes three days or longer. Understanding this condition is crucial for determining when Hajj becomes obligatory for women.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
The passage states that a woman must have a mahram or a husband to perform Hajj with her. A mahram is a male relative whom she cannot marry (e.g., father, brother, son). This is a condition for the obligation of Hajj.
The mahram condition applies only to the journey to Hajj, not to the rites themselves.
2
Note the distance threshold
The condition is triggered when the journey to Mecca is 'three days and nights' journey or more.' This is measured by travel time on foot or by camel, not by modern transport.
If the journey is less than three days, the mahram condition does not apply, and a woman may travel with a trustworthy group according to some views.
3
Understand the legal consequence
If a woman cannot find a mahram or husband for a journey of three days or more, she is not obligated to perform Hajj. The obligation is lifted due to inability to fulfill the condition.
This is a condition for obligation (shart al-wujub), not a condition for validity. Without mahram, the Hajj is not obligatory, but if she goes anyway, it may be valid.

Hajj of a Child or Slave Does Not Fulfill the Obligation
Ruling on child reaching puberty or slave being freed after entering ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the Hajj of a child who reaches puberty during ihram and identify whether it fulfills the obligatory Hajj.

Core Idea
A child's or slave's Hajj is valid as voluntary but does not replace the obligatory Hajj.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in Hajj: a child who enters ihram and then becomes an adult, or a slave who is freed during the rites. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that while their Hajj is valid, it does not count as the required Hajj of Islam. Understanding this distinction between validity (sihha) and sufficiency for obligation (ijza) is crucial for correct practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subjects
The ruling applies to two types of people: a child (sabi) who enters ihram before puberty, and a slave (abd) who enters ihram before being freed.
Both are considered lacking full legal capacity at the time of ihram.
2
Note the condition of continuity
After reaching puberty or being freed, they continue in their state of ihram and complete the rites. The ruling is about this scenario where they do not break ihram.
If they break ihram after the change, the ruling might differ.
3
Understand the ruling
The text states: 'it does not suffice for them as the Hajj of Islam.' This means the Hajj they performed does not fulfill the obligatory Hajj (Hajjat al-Islam).
The phrase 'lam yujzihima' indicates lack of sufficiency for the fard.
4
Recognize the nature of the performed Hajj
Though it does not count for obligation, the Hajj itself is valid (sahih) as a voluntary (nafl) act. They receive reward for it.
Validity (sihha) does not equal fulfilling the obligation (ijza).
5
Determine the consequence
Once they become legally responsible (adult/free), they must perform another Hajj with the intention of fulfilling the fard, with new ihram.
The nafl Hajj they performed does not remove the obligation.

Miqat Places and Rulings on Entering Ihram
Listing five miqat and rules for special cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five miqat places and apply the rulings for entering ihram based on one's location relative to Mecca.

Core Idea
The miqat is a binding boundary: you cannot cross it without ihram, but you may enter ihram earlier.

This passage establishes the five designated miqat places for entering ihram. Knowing these boundaries is essential for the validity of Hajj and 'Umrah. The passage also clarifies the rules for those who live beyond a miqat or inside Mecca itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five miqat
List each miqat and the region it serves: Dhul-Hulayfah for Madinah, Dhat 'Irq for Iraq, Al-Juhfah for Syria, Qarn al-Manazil for Najd, Yalamlam for Yemen.
These are fixed by the Prophet ﷺ and remain in use today.
2
Understand the prohibition of crossing without ihram
It is not permissible (haram) to pass the miqat without being in ihram. Doing so requires a sacrifice (dam) to compensate.
If someone crosses intentionally without ihram, the Hajj is still valid but a penalty applies.
3
Note the permissibility of early ihram
It is permissible to enter ihram before reaching the miqat, e.g., at home or en route.
This is a concession; the sunnah is to enter at the miqat.
4
Apply the ruling for residents beyond the miqat
If one's home is located between a miqat and Mecca (closer to Mecca), his miqat is al‑Hill (the area outside the Haram boundaries). He enters ihram there.
For example, a person living in Jeddah (which is in the Hill) enters ihram from Jeddah.
5
Apply the ruling for residents of Mecca
For Hajj, a Meccan enters ihram from the Haram (his home). For 'Umrah, he must go to the Hill (e.g., at‑Tan'eem) and enter ihram there.
This distinction ensures the 'Umrah ihram is entered from outside the Haram.

Rulings of Qiran (Combined Hajj and Umrah)
States the ruling of Qiran and its obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of Qiran, list the obligatory acts, and explain the Dam obligation and its substitute.

Core Idea
Qiran combines Hajj and Umrah in one Ihram; a sacrifice (Dam) is wajib, with fasting as an alternative.

In this passage from al-Lubab, al-Maydani presents the Hanafi ruling on Qiran, the most meritorious form of Hajj. He describes the niyyah, the sequence of Tawaf and Sa'i, the wajib Dam, and the fasting alternative. Understanding this lesson will help you distinguish Qiran from Ifrad and Tamattu'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its superiority
Recognize that Qiran is a form of Hajj combining Umrah and Hajj with one Ihram, and that Hanafis consider it superior to Tamattu' and Ifrad.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Learn the niyyah and initial acts
The qarin raises the voice (ihlal) from the miqat for both, and after prayer makes a specific du'a. Upon entering Makkah, he performs Tawaf (with raml in first three circuits) and Sa'i for Umrah.
Raml is walking briskly with chest out.
3
Distinguish the two Tawafs and Sa'is
After the Umrah Tawaf and Sa'i, he performs Tawaf al-Qudum and then Sa'i for Hajj. This duplication is unique to Qiran.
The mufrid (Ifrad) only does one Sa'i.
4
Understand the Dam obligation
On the Day of Nahr, after stoning the jamrah, the qarin must slaughter an animal (sheep, cow, or camel share). This is the Dam al-Qiran, wajib. If he cannot, he fasts three days during Hajj (by Day of Arafah) and seven after returning.
If the three-day fast is missed before Nahr, only the Dam suffices.
5

If the qarin goes directly to Arafat without entering Makkah, he becomes a 'rafid' (abandoner) of his Umrah. The Dam al-Qiran is waived, but he owes a Dam for the abandoned Umrah and must make it up later.
This shows that entering Makkah is a necessary condition for the validity of Qiran.

Conditions Invalidating Tamattu' and Timing of Umrah
Ruling statement on conditions for tamattu' validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions that invalidate tamattu' and determine whether a pilgrim is a mutamatti' based on the timing of Umrah tawaf.

Core Idea
Tamattu' is invalid if the pilgrim returns home without a sacrificial animal; partial Umrah before Hajj months can still count.

This passage from al-Quduri clarifies when the tamattu' form of Hajj is valid or invalid. It covers two critical scenarios: returning home after Umrah without the required sacrificial animal, and the timing of Umrah tawaf relative to the start of the Hajj months. Understanding these conditions ensures a pilgrim properly performs tamattu' and avoids having to change their intention or repeat rituals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition
Read the first sentence. It states that if the mutamatti' returns to his home country after completing Umrah without having brought a sacrificial animal (hady), his tamattu' becomes invalid. Note that the hady must be brought from outside the Haram.
Returning home breaks the connection between Umrah and Hajj if no hady is present.
2
Analyze the second scenario
The second sentence describes a pilgrim who enters ihram for Umrah before the Hajj months (i.e., before Shawwal) and performs less than four tawaf circuits. Then the Hajj months begin, and he completes the tawaf and enters ihram for Hajj. The judgment: he is a mutamatti'.
The key is that the Umrah is mostly performed within the Hajj months.
3
Contrast with the third scenario
If the pilgrim performs four or more tawaf circuits before the Hajj months, then completes the Umrah and later performs Hajj in the same year, he is NOT a mutamatti'. The Umrah is considered separate (ifrad).
Four circuits is the threshold: less than four preserves the linkage, four or more severs it.
4
Define the Hajj months
The passage concludes by defining the months of Hajj: Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, and the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah. This definition is crucial for the timing conditions.
Hanafis consider the entire months of Shawwal and Dhu al-Qa'dah as Hajj months, plus ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah.
5

Combine the conditions: tamattu' is invalid if (a) the pilgrim returns home without hady, or (b) the Umrah tawaf is largely completed (4+ circuits) before the Hajj months. Valid tamattu' requires either performing Umrah within the Hajj months or having performed less than four circuits before the months begin.
These rules ensure the Umrah and Hajj are linked as a single journey.

The pilgrim is now in ihram for Hajj only (if no Umrah intention remains) or becomes a qarin if he later adds Umrah.

Menstruating Woman's Ihram and Tawaf
Rulings for menstruating woman regarding ihram and tawaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a menstruating woman regarding ihram and tawaf during Hajj, and explain why Tawaf al-Sadr is waived.

Core Idea
Menstruation does not block ihram but blocks tawaf; farewell tawaf is excused after the essential tawaf.

This lesson presents two key scenarios from the classical Hanafi text. The first addresses when a woman begins her period at the time of entering ihram: she still enters ihram and performs all rites except tawaf. The second addresses when she menstruates after standing at Arafat and completing Tawaf al-Ziyarah: she may leave without Tawaf al-Sadr and owes no penalty. These rulings illustrate how the school balances ritual purity requirements with practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios

2
Analyse the first scenario
For a woman who menstruates at the time of ihram: she performs ghusl (recommended), enters ihram, and does everything a non-menstruating pilgrim does except tawaf (which requires purity).
Her ihram is valid; she must delay tawaf until she is pure.

Analyse the second scenario
If she menstruates after standing at Arafat and after performing Tawaf al-Ziyarah (the essential tawaf), she may depart from Makkah without performing Tawaf al-Sadr (farewell tawaf) and owes no compensation.
Tawaf al-Ziyarah is a rukn and must be done; Tawaf al-Sadr is wajib but excused here.

Understand the rationale
Tawaf requires ritual purity (tahara) because it involves circumambulation with prayer. The farewell tawaf is waived for menstruating women due to hardship and because it is not a rukn.
The ruling is based on the principle of removal of hardship (raf' al-haraj).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A dam for major violations, sadaqah for minor ones; the threshold is the extent of the action.

In this lesson, we examine the detailed penalties for common ihram violations as established by Imam al-Quduri in al-Mukhtasar. The rulings are straightforward: a major violation (perfuming a full limb, wearing sewn clothes all day, shaving a quarter of the head) requires a dam, while lesser acts require sadaqah. A notable intra-Hanafi difference exists for shaving cupping spots, which we will explore.

If the act meets or exceeds the stated threshold, penalty is a dam; otherwise it is sadaqah.

Abu Hanifa holds a dam because shaving cupping spots is like shaving part of the head; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold sadaqah because it is a minor act.
The mufta bihi position is Abu Hanifa's view.

Memorize the rulings for fatwa application
These penalties are wajib. Commit the thresholds to memory for quick reference.
Other schools may differ, but within the Hanafi school, these are the relied-upon positions.
