Sale of Game by a Muhrim is Void (Batil)
Declares the sale or purchase of game by a muhrim as void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a muhrim's sale or purchase of game and explain why it is batil (void).

Core Idea
A muhrim cannot sell or buy game: the contract is utterly void (batil) from inception.

This lesson examines a clear-cut ruling from the Hanafi school: any transaction involving game animals by a person in ihram is legally void. The prohibition on hunting during ihram extends to the commercial sphere, rendering the contract ineffective. We will unpack the meaning of batil and contrast it with fasid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and subject matter
The act involves a muhrim (one in ihram) and a game animal (wild animal). The muhrim can be either seller or buyer.
2
Determine the legal ruling
The sale or purchase is batil (void ab initio). This means the contract has no legal effect; ownership does not transfer.
Hanafi law distinguishes batil from fasid.
3
Understand the reason
The ruling is based on the prohibition of hunting and trading game during ihram. The subject matter is not permissible (mubah) for the muhrim to trade.
4
Apply the distinction
Batil means the contract is invalid from the start. It cannot be rectified later. Contrast with fasid, which might be corrected by removing a deficiency.
This ruling uses batil because an essential condition (lawful subject matter) is missing.

Commentary
This passage gives a straightforward ruling: any sale or purchase of game (wild animals) by a person in ihram is utterly void (batil). This is because a muhrim is prohibited from hunting, killing, or even indicating game to a hunter. Transactions involving game are therefore unlawful, and the contract does not produce any legal effect. The Hanafi school considers such a contract void from its inception (batil), not merely voidable (fasid), due to the fundamental prohibition. This applies whether the muhrim is the seller or the buyer. The game animal itself is considered not eligible for trade during ihram.

Formation and Conditions of Sale (Bay')
Defines sale formation and validity conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid sale contract and apply the rules of offer/acceptance, khiyar al-majlis, and price specification to given scenarios.

Core Idea
Sale is concluded by offer and acceptance in past tense; session option exists; price must be known.

This lesson covers the foundational rules of sale (bay') as stated in the matn of al-Quduri. We will explore how a sale contract is formed, the importance of offer and acceptance in past tense, the buyer's right to withdraw during the session, and the conditions regarding price and currency. Understanding these basics is essential for distinguishing valid sales from invalid ones in daily transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core formation requirement
Find the statement that explains how a sale is concluded: by offer and acceptance (ijab wa qabul) in past tense. This is the pillar (rukn) of the contract.
Past tense is a Hanafi condition; other schools may allow present tense.
2
Understand the option of the session (khiyar al-majlis)
After an offer is made, the other party has the option to accept or reject while both are still in the same gathering. If either leaves before acceptance, the offer becomes void.
This option protects the offeree from being bound prematurely.
3
Recognize post-contract options
Once offer and acceptance occur, the sale is binding. The only remaining options are option of defect (khiyar al-'ayb) and option of sight (khiyar al-ru'ya).
No general right to rescind without cause.
4
Learn price and currency conditions
The price must be known in quantity and quality. If unspecified, it defaults to the prevalent currency of the country. If multiple currencies exist, specify one; otherwise the sale is fasid.
Also, deferred payment is valid only if the term is known.
5
Understand permissibility of estimation (mujazafah)
Food and grains may be sold by guess (juzaf) or using a container/stone of unknown measure, as this is customary.
This is an exception to the general requirement of knowing the quantity.

General Rules of Formation of Sale
Codifies basic rules of sale formation and price conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid sale contract according to the Hanafi school, and identify when an offer becomes void and when a sale becomes binding.

Core Idea
A sale is concluded by past-tense offer and acceptance in the same session; the price must be known unless the object is specified.

This lesson covers the foundational rules for forming a valid sale contract (bay') in Hanafi fiqh. The text from al-Quduri's al-Mukhtasar lays out the essential conditions: the tense of the contract, the option of the session, the binding effect, and price requirements. Mastering these rules is essential for understanding all subsequent transactions in Islamic commercial law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the essential pillars (arkan) of sale
A sale is concluded by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul), both expressed in past tense verbs. This indicates finality and completion of the contract.
Past tense is a Hanafi requirement; other schools allow present or future tense.
2
Understand the option of the session (khiyar al-majlis)
After the offer, the offeree has the option to accept or reject while both parties remain in the contract session (majlis).
Khiyar al-majlis is a temporary right that ends when the session ends.
3
Determine when the offer lapses and when the sale becomes binding
If either party physically leaves the session before acceptance, the offer becomes void. Once both offer and acceptance occur, the sale is binding (lazim) with no right of rescission except for a defect or unseen item.
Leaving the session terminates the option; the contract is immediately binding upon acceptance.
4
Recognize the rules regarding price knowledge
For specified objects (e.g., a particular car), knowing the quantity is not necessary. For monetary prices (thaman), the amount and quality must be known for the sale to be valid.
This distinction avoids gharar (uncertainty) in the essential terms.
5
Apply the default rule for unspecified price and the exception for food/grain
If the price is left unspecified, it defaults to the dominant currency of the town. If multiple currencies exist, the buyer must specify which, or the sale is invalid. Food and grain can be sold by estimate, container, or weight even if the exact measure is unknown.
The exception for food/grain is based on customary practice and need.

Commentary
This passage begins the Book of Sales (كتاب البيوع). It is a foundational matn stating the essential rules for forming a valid sale contract. First, the definition: a sale is concluded by offer (إيجاب) and acceptance (قبول) using past tense verbs (ماضي). This is because past tense indicates finality and leaves no room for future negotiation. Second, the rule of khiyar al-majlis (option of the session): after the offer, the offeree has the option to accept or reject while both remain in the contract session. If any party leaves the place before acceptance, the offer lapses. Third, once offer and acceptance occur, the sale becomes binding (لازم) and no party has a right of rescission except for a hidden defect (عيب) or because the item was unseen (عدم رؤية). Fourth, regarding the price: for specified objects (أعواض مشار إليها), knowing the exact quantity is not necessary (e.g., selling a specific house without stating its area is valid). However, the term can be immediate or deferred if the period is known. If the price is left unspecified (e.g., 'sell you this for the dirham'), the default is the prevailing currency of the town; if multiple currencies exist, the buyer must specify which, otherwise the sale is invalid. Finally, it is permissible to sell food or grain by measure (mikayala), by estimation (mujazafa), by a specific container of unknown volume, or by a specific stone of unknown weight. This is an exception to the general requirement of knowing quantity, allowed because the object itself is known by indication.

Sale of Grain at a Rate per Unit - Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on selling a stack of grain per unit and buying with assumed quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling a stack of grain per unit without total, and distinguish between the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Sale per unit without total is valid for one unit only (Abu Hanifa) but Sahibayn allow all; flock/cloth sales are void entirely.

This lesson examines a common transaction: selling a commodity per unit without specifying the total quantity. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on grain sales, but is unanimous on discrete items like sheep and cloth. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to identify gharar in contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the commodity and contract terms
Note that the grain is sold per qafiz without total, while sheep and cloth are also sold per unit without total.
Distinguish between measurable (grain) and countable (sheep) items.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
For grain: only one qafiz is valid; the rest are invalid unless total stated. For sheep and cloth: entirely void.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling for grain
They say the entire sale is valid whether total is mentioned or not.
Their reasoning: the unit price is clear, and no dispute arises.
4
Apply the khiyar rule for quantity deficiency
If the buyer purchases based on an assumed total, and finds less, he has the option to take at proportionate price or cancel; if more, the surplus belongs to the seller.
This rule applies when total is specified.
5
Distinguish and note mufta bihi
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view for grain due to caution against gharar.

Commentary
This passage addresses sales where the price is set per unit but the total quantity is unspecified. The first case: a person sells a stack of grain saying 'each qafiz for a dirham' without stating the total number of qafizes. According to Imam Abu Hanifa (رحمة الله), such a contract is valid only for one qafiz (the first unit measured) because the sale is ambiguous regarding the remainder. The sale of the rest is invalid unless the seller specifies the total number. However, his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (Sahibayn), hold that the sale is valid for the entire stack, whether the total is mentioned or not, because the buyer can take however many qafizes he wants at that rate; the ambiguity does not lead to dispute. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) for fatwa is the view of Abu Hanifa in this case, as it is more precautionary. The second case is selling a flock of sheep 'each sheep for a dirham' or a cloth 'each cubit for a dirham' without specifying the total number. Here, the sale is void in all (fāsid fī jamī‘ihi) according to all three imams, because the exact number cannot be known until counting, and the contract is considered ambiguous (gharar). The difference from the grain case is that grain can be measured gradually, but sheep and cloth are not normally sold per unit without total. The third case is buying a stack with a stated total quantity (e.g., 'I buy this stack assuming it is 100 qafiz for 100 dirhams'). If the actual quantity is found less, the buyer has the option: either take what exists at its proportionate price (i.e., pay only for the actual qafizes at the unit rate) or cancel the sale. If the actual quantity is more, the surplus belongs to the seller; the buyer has no claim to the excess. This is because the contract was based on an assumption, and the seller's ownership of the excess remains.

Selling Flock or Cloth by Unit Without Total: Invalid
Ruling on unit sales of heterogeneous items without total

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that selling heterogeneous items (e.g., a flock of sheep or cloth by the cubit) by unit without specifying the total is invalid (fasid), and explain the reason: ignorance of the subject matter.

Core Idea
Selling distinct items per unit without total price invalid due to ignorance.

In the previous lesson, we saw that selling a homogeneous heap by the pound is valid. But here, the matn rules that selling a flock of sheep 'each sheep for a dirham' without stating the total number is completely invalid. The same applies to cloth sold 'each cubit for a dirham' without the total length. This distinction rests on whether the items sold are identical or distinct. The moment the units are heterogeneous, the total price becomes unknown, rendering the contract fasid due to prohibited ignorance (jahl).

1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is selling a flock of sheep, each sheep for a dirham, without specifying the total number. The ruling: the sale is fasid (invalid) in its entirety. The same applies to cloth sold by the cubit without total length.
The ruling is from the matn of Al-Quduri and agreed upon.
2
Distinguish from homogeneous goods
Unlike a heap of dates where each pound is identical, a flock contains distinct animals. Similarly, each cubit of cloth may differ in quality or pattern. Heterogeneity means each unit is a separate subject, and ignorance of the total leads to ignorance of the total price.
Homogeneous goods (e.g., grain from one sack) allow valid per-unit sale if total weight known.
3
Locate the cause of invalidity
The cause is jahl (ignorance) of the subject matter. In the flock, the total price depends on the number of sheep, which is unknown. In cloth, the total length is unknown. Ignorance of an essential term makes the contract fasid.
Jahl affecting the subject matter is prohibited (gharar).
4

The ruling says 'in its entirety' meaning the whole contract is void, not partially valid. The buyer does not acquire any sheep or cloth, and no price is owed.
If the contract is batil (void) it would be similar, but here it is fasid because it can be rectified by specification in principle? Actually, fasid means invalid due to missing condition; it can be corrected by adding the total. Batil would be intrinsically invalid. The matn uses fasid.

Any sale of heterogeneous items sold per unit without specifying total price or quantity follows the same ruling. Examples: selling assorted fruits by piece without total, or selling mixed nails by weight without total.
Always check if the units are homogeneous or heterogeneous.

Core Idea
Conditions contradicting the purpose of sale or vague deferments invalidate the sale; but if possession occurs, ownership transfers with duty to pay value.

ولا يجوز بيع ثوب من ثوبين.
ومن باع عبداً على أن يُعتقَه المشتري، أو يُدبِّرَه، أو يكاتبه، أو باع أمة على أن يستولدها: فالبيع فاسد.
وكذلك لو باع عبداً على أن يستخدمه البائع شهراً، أو داراً على أن يسكنها البائع مدةً معلومة، أو على أن يُقرضه المشتري درهماً، أو على أن يُهدى له هدية.
Chapter of Invalid Sale. And it is not permissible to sell a cloth from two cloths. And whoever sells a slave on condition that the buyer frees him, or makes him mudabbar, or enters a kitaba with him, or sells a slave-girl on condition that the buyer takes her as a concubine for offspring, then the sale is fasid. Similarly, if he sells a slave on condition that the seller uses him for a month, or a house on condition that the seller lives in it for a known period, or that the buyer loans him a dirham, or that he gives him a gift.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on invalid sales (al-bay' al-fasid) lists several examples where a contract is rendered fasid due to a stipulated condition that provides a benefit to the seller. The first ruling states that selling a garment that is part of two unseparated garments (thawb min thawbayn) is not permissible — this is because it involves gharar (uncertainty). The subsequent examples involve selling a slave or slave-girl with conditions like the buyer freeing or making a contract of kitaba, etc., which give the buyer a future right but were not part of the exchange. Similarly, conditions allowing the seller to benefit after sale (e.g., using the slave for a month, living in the house, obtaining a loan or gift) make the sale fasid because they are extra benefits not compensated in the price. All these conditions invalidate the contract in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
بيع فاسد (bay' fasid): A sale that is defective due to a missing condition or an extra condition that does not void the contract completely but makes it invalid in its current form; it may be corrected by removing the defect.
تدبير (tadbir): A declaration by the owner that a slave will be free upon the owner's death.
كتابة (kitabah): A contract between a master and slave whereby the slave earns money to purchase his freedom.

A sale is fasid if it includes a condition that provides an uncovenanted benefit to the seller, such as using the sold item or receiving a loan or gift.

Delayed Delivery Sale Is Fasid
Ruling on sale with delayed delivery condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that a sale with a condition of delayed delivery until a future time is fasid in the Hanafi school and explain the reason (gharar and resemblance to a loan with benefit).

Core Idea
A sale with a condition of deferred delivery is fasid due to gharar and resemblance to a loan with benefit.

In this lesson, we examine the first example from our passage: a sale where the seller stipulates that delivery will not occur until the end of the month. This condition renders the contract defective (fasid) in Hanafi law.

The item is known.
2
Identify the condition
The condition is that the seller will not deliver the item until the end of the month.
This is a future delivery condition.
3
Apply the ruling
The text states 'فالبيع فاسد' – the sale is fasid.
Fasid means defective, not void (batil).

ومن باع عَيْناً على أن لا يُسلّمها إلى رأس الشهر : فالبيع فاسد.
ومن باع جارية أو دابةٌ إلا حَمْلَهَا : فَسَد البيع.
ومن اشترى ثوباً على أن يَقطَعَه البائع، ويخيطه قميصاً، أو قباء، أو نعلاً على أن يَحْذُوها ، أو يُشَرِّكَها : فالبيعُ فاسد.
And whoever sells an item on condition that he does not deliver it until the month's end, the sale is fasid. And whoever sells a slave-girl or an animal except its fetus, the sale becomes fasid. And whoever buys a cloth on condition that the seller cuts it and sews it into a shirt or a cloak, or a sandal on condition that the seller makes it or repairs it, the sale is fasid.
Source pages p. 124

Commentary
This passage continues listing sales that are fasid due to specific conditions. First, selling with a stipulation that delivery is postponed until an indefinite future time (like the end of the month) is invalid because the timing is unknown and it resembles a loan with benefit. Second, selling a slave-girl or animal while excluding its unborn fetus is fasid because the fetus is unknown and cannot be independently sold. Third, buying cloth or leather with the condition that the seller performs manufacturing (cutting, sewing, making into a garment or sandal) is fasid because it combines a contract of sale with a contract of hire (ijarah) without proper separation; the condition renders the sale defective.

Key Terms
حمل (haml): Fetus, unborn offspring of an animal or slave-girl.
استثناء (istithna'): Exclusion of a part of the sold item from the contract.

Sale Excluding the Unborn Fetus Is Fasid
Ruling on sale with exception of unborn fetus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that selling an animal or slave-girl while excluding her unborn fetus is fasid and explain the reason (gharar).

Core Idea
Selling a pregnant animal 'except its fetus' is fasid because the fetus is unknown and cannot be a separate item of sale.

The second example in our passage addresses a sale where the seller attempts to exclude the unborn offspring from the transaction. This condition introduces uncertainty because the fetus is not yet a separate property.

3
Apply the ruling
The text says 'فَسَد البيع' – the sale becomes fasid.
Fasid because the excluded part is unknown.
