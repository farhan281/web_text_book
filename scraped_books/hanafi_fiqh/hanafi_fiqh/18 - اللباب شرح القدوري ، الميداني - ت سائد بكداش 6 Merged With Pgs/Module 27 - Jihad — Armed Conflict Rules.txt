Jihad as Collective Obligation with Conditions and Exemptions
Compilation of jihad rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of jihad as fard kifayah, list the exempted categories, and explain the pre-fighting da'wah obligations.

Core Idea
Jihad is a collective duty but becomes personal when defense is needed, and fighting requires prior invitation to Islam.

Jihad is often misunderstood as a perpetual personal duty. This passage from the Book of Siyar clarifies its legal category and the conditions that trigger different levels of obligation. You will learn who is exempt, how the obligation transforms during invasion, and the required protocol before engaging in combat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation type
The text opens by stating jihad is fard kifayah. Understand that if enough Muslims perform it, the rest are absolved; if none do, all sin.
Compare with fard 'ayn which is binding on every individual.
2
List the exempted categories
The passage lists six categories: minor, slave, woman, blind, crippled (muq'ad), one-handed (aqta'). Memorize them as mawani' (preventers) of obligation.
These exemptions are due to lack of ability or capacity.
3
Recognize the defensive exception
If the enemy attacks a Muslim town, the obligation becomes fard 'ayn on all, including women and slaves. Note the change in permission: they may go out without consent.
This shows that defense overrides standard exemptions.
4
Outline the da'wah protocol
Before fighting, Muslims must call the enemy to Islam. If they accept, fighting stops. If they refuse, call to jizyah. Those who haven't heard the call cannot be fought until invited.
This is a wajib (obligatory) step derived from the Prophet's practice.

Book of Siyar. Jihad is a collective obligation (fard kifayah); if a group of people undertake it, it falls from the rest. If no one undertakes it, all people sin by abandoning it. Fighting the disbelievers is obligatory even if they do not initiate (aggression against us). Jihad is not obligatory upon a minor, a slave, a woman, a blind person, a cripple (muq'ad), or a one-handed person (aqta'). If the enemy attacks a town, then defense becomes obligatory upon all Muslims. The woman goes out without the permission of her husband, and the slave without the permission of his master. When Muslims enter Dar al-Harb and besiege a city or fortress, they invite them to Islam; if they accept, they refrain from fighting them. If they refuse, they invite them to pay jizyah; if they pay it, then they have what Muslims have and upon them what Muslims have. It is not permissible to fight those who have not received the call to Islam, except after inviting them to Islam. It is recommended (mustahabb) to invite those who have received the call, but it is not obligatory.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Siyar outlines the basic rulings of jihad in the Hanafi school. It begins by establishing that jihad is fard kifayah (collective obligation), meaning if enough people fulfill it, others are absolved; otherwise everyone sins. Attacking the disbelievers is obligatory even without provocation, but certain categories are exempt from the obligation: minors, slaves, women, blind, crippled, and one-handed. However, if the enemy invades, defense becomes fard 'ayn on all, including women and slaves, who may go out without permission. The conduct of war includes calling to Islam first; if they accept, fighting stops; if not, call to jizyah; if they pay, they become dhimmis with similar rights and duties. Those who have not yet heard the call cannot be fought until they are invited. For those who have heard, the invitation is recommended but not obligatory.

Key Terms
فرض الكفاية (fard kifayah): Collective obligation: if some Muslims perform it, the rest are absolved of sin
الجهاد (jihad): Struggle in the path of Allah, specifically military struggle against disbelievers
المُقعد (muq'ad): A person who is confined to sitting due to paralysis or disability
الأقطع (aqta'): Person who has lost a limb (hand or foot)

Permissible Siege Tactics and Human Shields in Hanafi Jihad
Permissible siege actions and human shield rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissible siege tactics according to the Hanafi school and explain the ruling on targeting when the enemy uses Muslim human shields.

Core Idea
Siege warfare permits necessary destruction and even shooting at Muslims used as shields, but the intention must be to hit the disbelievers.

This lesson covers the Hanafi rulings on what actions are permissible during a siege. While Islam generally prohibits unnecessary destruction, war necessitates certain measures to compel the enemy. It also addresses the difficult scenario where the enemy uses Muslim captives or children as human shields—a situation that requires precision in intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the permissible siege actions
List the specific actions mentioned: setting up mangonels, burning, flooding, cutting trees, destroying crops. Note that these are allowed despite general prohibitions against destruction because they are necessary for war.
These actions are permissible (ja'iz) but not mandatory.
2
Analyze the ruling on shooting when Muslims are among the enemy
The text says there is no harm in shooting even if a Muslim captive or trader is present. This is because the primary target is the enemy; the Muslim’s presence does not stop the offensive.
This rule applies to both captives and traders.
3
Examine the human shield scenario (tatarrus)
When the enemy shields themselves with Muslim children or prisoners, Muslims still shoot but must deliberately aim at the disbelievers. The intention (qasd) is key—shooting is not indiscriminate.
Hanafi scholars emphasize that the act is permissible but the intention must be specific.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The permissibility here is based on necessity (darurah) and the fact that the harm to the enemy outweighs the potential harm to the Muslims used as shields.
This is an application of istihsan (juristic preference).
5
Contrast with general prohibitions
Note that these actions are exceptions to the general rules of not killing Muslims and not destroying property. They are limited to the context of active warfare.
These exceptions do not extend to peacetime.

Prohibited Acts and Protection of Non-Combatants in Hanafi Jihad
Prohibited acts and non-combatant immunity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the prohibited acts during war and identify which non-combatants may be killed under specific circumstances.

Core Idea
Treachery, embezzlement, and mutilation are strictly forbidden; women, children, elderly, blind, crippled, and mad are immune unless they participate in war.

This lesson addresses the ethical boundaries of warfare. While the previous lesson focused on permissible actions, here we learn the prohibitions that preserve the Islamic character of jihad. The text also specifies categories of non-combatants who must not be harmed, with exceptions for those who actively contribute to the war effort.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three universal prohibitions in war
Find the three acts: treachery (ghadr), embezzlement from booty (ghulul), and mutilation (tamthil). These are prohibited by the Sunnah and consensus.
These are major sins (kaba'ir) in the context of jihad.
2
List the categories of non-combatants who are immune
The text mentions: women, aged old men, children, blind, crippled, and mad persons. These are generally not to be killed.
This immunity is based on their typical non-participation in fighting.
3
Analyze the exceptions for women and others
Exceptions are made for those who have 'opinion in war' (i.e., combat strategy), or a woman who is a queen or sorceress. Similarly, if any of the listed persons directly participate, the immunity is lost.
The exception is based on actual combat value, not mere possibility.
4
Understand the ruling on women and slaves fighting
A woman may not fight without her husband's permission, nor a slave without his master's permission, unless there is a sudden enemy attack (defensive necessity).
Permission is needed because of authority structures; defensive necessity overrides.
5
Relate these rules to the earlier ones
The prohibitions and immunities complement the permissibility of siege actions. While destruction of property is allowed, direct harm to non-combatants is not, except for valid exceptions.
This balances the harshness of war with mercy.

Peace Treaties and Their Termination in Hanafi Fiqh
Conditions for peace treaties and termination procedure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the conditions for making peace with the enemy and the proper procedure for terminating a peace treaty according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Peace is permissible when beneficial; if broken, it must be openly declared before resuming combat.

This lesson covers the discretionary power of the imam regarding peace treaties. While the previous lessons focused on warfare itself, this section addresses the transition to peace and the ethical requirement of transparency if peace is ended. The Hanafi school emphasizes benefit (maslahah) as the guiding principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for making peace
The text states that peace is permissible (لا بأس) if the imam sees benefit (مصلحة) for the Muslims. Conditions: the imam's judgment and communal benefit.
Peace is not mandatory; it depends on tijarah (benefit).
2
Understand the nature of the peace agreement
Peace can be made with the entire enemy or a specific group. It is a contractual agreement (muhadana).
The term 'أهل الحرب' refers to the belligerent non-Muslims.
3
Examine the ruling on breaking the peace
If after making peace for a fixed period, the imam sees that breaking it is more beneficial for the Muslims, he must 'نبذ إليهم' (announce to them) before fighting.
The term 'نبذ' means to cast or throw, i.e., to give notice in a clear manner.
4
Analyze the ethical requirement of notification
The notification ensures that the enemy is not taken by surprise after a truce. This is based on the principle of avoiding treachery (ghadr), which was earlier prohibited.
This aligns with the prohibition of treachery in the previous lesson.
5
Differentiate from absolute surrender
Peace treaties are temporary; they do not require conversion to Islam. The imam has the authority to negotiate terms as long as they serve Muslim interests.
This is different from accepting jizyah or conversion.

The key trigger is the judge's ruling (hukm al-hakim) that the apostate has joined dar al-harb. Without this judicial determination, the consequences do not take effect.
The judge must be informed of the apostate's departure and verify it.
2
Enumerate the immediate consequences of the ruling
Once the ruling is issued, the apostate's mudabbar slaves and umm walad are freed, debts owed by him are dissolved, and whatever he earned during his Islamic period is transferred to his Muslim heirs.
The freeing is automatic; debts are cancelled, not transferred.
3
Distinguish debts by period
Debts that became binding during his Islamic state are paid exclusively from his Islamic earnings. Debts incurred during apostasy are paid only from what he earned while an apostate. The two pools do not mix.
This prevents valid Islamic claims from being burdened by apostasy-period liabilities.
4
Classify transactions during apostasy as suspended
All sales, purchases, and dispositions of wealth during apostasy are suspended (mawquf). They become valid if he repents and returns to Islam, but void if he dies, is killed, or remains in dar al-harb.
If he returns as a Muslim, he can reclaim specific property still in heirs' hands.

Zakat from Banu Taghlib Christians
Ruling that Banu Taghlib Christians pay double the zakat rate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the special tax rule for Banu Taghlib Christians and identify who is liable.

Core Idea
Banu Taghlib Christians pay double the Muslim zakat rate instead of jizya.

This lesson covers a unique Hanafi ruling regarding the Christian Arab tribe Banu Taghlib. Because they are Arabs, they are exempt from jizya, but they must pay a special tax equal to double the zakat rate of Muslims. This ruling clarifies that adult men and women are liable, while children are not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the tribe and its special status
Banu Taghlib is a Christian Arab tribe. In Hanafi law, Arab Christians do not pay jizya but are subject to a special tax.
This is an exception to the general rule that non-Muslims pay jizya.
2
Understand the tax rate
The tax is double the rate of zakat paid by Muslims. For example, if zakat on livestock is 1/40, Banu Taghlib pays 2/40 (or 1/20).
Exact rate depends on the type of wealth.
3
Determine liability among individuals
The tax is taken from adults, both men and women, not from children.
Unlike jizya which is only from adult free males.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider a Banu Taghlib woman with 200 dirhams. She must pay double the zakat due on that amount.
The nisab threshold for zakat applies.

Public Expenditure of Kharaj, Jizya, and Gifts
Ruling on disposal of specific public funds

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four sources of public funds (kharaj, Banu Taghlib wealth, gifts from harbis, jizya) and list the permissible categories of expenditure according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Kharaj, jizya, and gifts to the Imam are public funds for Muslim welfare, not personal wealth.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as explained by al-Maydani, clarifies the fiqh ruling on the disposal of certain public funds. The text specifies four sources—kharaj, wealth of Banu Taghlib, gifts from enemy combatants, and jizya—and mandates that they be spent on the general interests of the Muslims. This lesson will help you identify these categories and understand the underlying principle that the Imam is a trustee, not an owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sources
List the four sources of public funds mentioned: kharaj (land tax), wealth of Banu Taghlib (a Christian Arab tribe paying double zakat), gifts from harbis (enemy non-Muslims), and jizya (poll tax).
These are distinct from zakat and general charity, which have separate rules.
2
Note the ruling
These funds are to be spent on 'masalih al-Muslimin' (interests of the Muslims). This is an obligation upon the Imam.
Masalih includes general welfare, not personal benefit.
3
Enumerate specific expenditures
The text gives examples: fortifying frontier fortresses (thughur), building bridges and culverts, salaries for judges, officials, and scholars, and stipends for fighters and their families.
These are illustrative, not exhaustive.
4
Understand the principle
These funds belong to the public treasury (bayt al-mal). The Imam is a custodian, not an owner. Any misuse would be a breach of trust.
Gifts to the Imam from non-Muslims are not personal; they become public property.
5
Reflect on implications
The Imam must prioritize these expenditures over other needs if funds are limited. Surplus may be saved for future public needs.
This ensures continuity of welfare projects.
