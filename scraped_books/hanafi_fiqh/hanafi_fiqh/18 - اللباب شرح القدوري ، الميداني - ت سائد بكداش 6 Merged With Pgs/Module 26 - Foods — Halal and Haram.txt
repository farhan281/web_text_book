Rulings on Eating Fish, Eel, Marfish, and Locusts
States main rulings on permissible animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on eating aquatic animals (fish only), floating fish, eel, marfish, and locusts, and explain why slaughter is not required for fish and locusts.

Core Idea
Fish and locusts are halal without slaughter; carrion and pig can never be made halal.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents fundamental Hanafi rulings on which animals may be eaten. It clarifies that carrion and pig are absolutely forbidden regardless of slaughter, and among water animals, only fish is permissible—with a caveat for floating dead fish. It also addresses special cases like eel, marfish, and locusts, which are permissible without ritual slaughter. Understanding these distinctions is essential for daily fiqh, especially when selecting seafood or encountering unfamiliar creatures.

Guided Path
1
Identify the absolute prohibitions
Note that carrion and pig are mentioned as animals that cannot be made lawful by any form of slaughter. This is a core Hanafi principle.
The matn implies the previous discussion included carrion.
2
Determine the rule for aquatic animals
The text states 'nothing from water animals is eaten except fish'. This means all non-fish sea creatures (e.g., squid, crabs, shrimp) are not permissible.
This is a stricter position than some other schools.
3
Recognize the exception for floating fish
Fish that dies naturally and floats (ṭāfī) is disliked (makruh tanzihi). It is not haram, but avoiding it is recommended.
This ruling is based on istihsan.
4
Note permissible land-like water creatures
Eel (jarīth) and marfish (mār-māhī) are explicitly permitted. They are considered fish despite their appearance.
These are exceptions to the general rule of 'only fish'.
5
Understand the ruling on locusts
Locusts are halal without any slaughter (dhakāh). This is a special exemption agreed upon by all schools.
No requirement for Islamic method of killing.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn of al-Quduri, presenting key Hanafi rulings on prohibited and permissible animals. The first line likely continues from a previous page mentioning carrion (maytah) and pig, affirming that ritual slaughter (dhakāh) cannot make them lawful. Among aquatic animals, only fish is permissible; other sea creatures are not. Eating a fish that floats dead (al-ṭāfī) is makrūh tanzīhī (disliked). Eel (jarīth) and marfish (mār-māhī, a snake-like fish) are permissible. Locusts are entirely permissible without any requirement of slaughter (dhakāh), as they are considered a special case.

Key Terms
ذكاة (dhakāh): Ritual slaughter that makes an animal lawful to eat
طافي (ṭāfī): A fish that dies naturally and floats on water

Consumption of Forbidden Items Under Coercion
Ruling on eating carrion or drinking wine under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which consuming carrion or wine becomes permissible in Hanafi fiqh and apply the ruling to scenarios of coercion.

Core Idea
Only fear for life or limb permits eating haram; enduring the threat is sinful.

Under normal circumstances, carrion and wine are absolutely prohibited. However, when one is coerced with a credible threat to life or limb, the ruling changes. This passage from al-Lubab clarifies the threshold: lesser coercion does not lift the prohibition, but genuine fear for life or a body part makes consumption permissible—and in fact obligatory, as refusing would be sinful.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Recognize that eating carrion (maytah) and drinking wine (khamr) are normally haram in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Recognize non-justifying coercion
Understand that mere imprisonment, shackling, or beating does not make these items permissible.
These are considered lesser coercion that one should endure rather than sin.
3
Identify the valid exception
Only when the threat involves fear for one's life or loss of a limb does the prohibition lift.
The text says: 'إلا أن يُكره بما يَخافُ منه على نفسه، أو على عضو من أعضائه'.
4
Understand the obligation to comply
When valid coercion exists, it becomes not only permissible but obligatory to consume, because preserving life is a higher duty.
The text states that if one endures and refuses, they are sinful.
5
Apply the ruling to scenarios
Practice distinguishing between conditions that permit and those that do not.

Source Text

ومن أكره على أن يأكل الميتة، أو يشرب الخمر، وأكـره علـى ذلك بحبس، أو قيد أو ضَرْب لم يحل له ، إلا أن يُكره بما يَخافُ منه على نفسه، أو على عضو من أعضائه، فإذا خاف ذلك: وَسِعَهُ أن يُقدم على ما أكره عليه. ولا يَسَعُه أن يصبر على ما تُوعد به، فإن صبر حتى أوقعوا به، ولم يأكل: فهو آثم.
And whoever is coerced into eating carrion, or drinking wine, and is coerced into that by imprisonment, shackling, or beating, it is not permissible for him, unless he is coerced by something he fears for his life or a limb of his body. If he fears that, then it is permissible for him to proceed with what he is coerced into. And it is not permissible for him to endure what he is threatened with; if he endures until they inflict it upon him and he does not eat, then he is sinful.
Source pages p. 356

Commentary
This passage provides the ruling on consuming prohibited foods and drinks under duress. The default principle: coercion by lesser means such as imprisonment, shackling, or beating does not permit eating carrion or drinking wine. The exception is when the coercion involves a threat to life or limb. In that case, it becomes permissible (yasi'uhu) to consume the forbidden item to save oneself. The text further states that it is not permissible (la yasi'uhu) to patiently endure the threatened harm and refuse to eat; doing so would make the person sinful (athim). This is because one is obligated to preserve one's life. The ruling emphasizes that once a valid coercion is established (fear of death or limb loss), one must comply to avoid sin.

Key Terms
الميتة (al-maytah): Carrion, an animal that died without proper Islamic slaughter; consumption is normally haram.
الخمر (al-khamr): Wine or intoxicating beverage; consumption is normally haram.
وسعه (wasi'ahu): It is permissible for him; he is allowed to do it without sin.
لا يسعه (la yasi'uhu): It is not permissible for him; it is forbidden.

Key Takeaway
Under Hanafi fiqh, consuming normally haram food or drink becomes obligatory when one is coerced by a credible threat to life or limb, and refusing in such a case is sinful.
