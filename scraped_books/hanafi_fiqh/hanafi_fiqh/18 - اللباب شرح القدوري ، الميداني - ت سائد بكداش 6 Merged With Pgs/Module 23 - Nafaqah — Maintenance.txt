When Is a Wife Entitled to Maintenance? — Conditions of Nafaqah
States the conditions and exceptions for wife's maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a wife is entitled to maintenance (nafaqah) in Hanafi fiqh and identify the exceptional cases that suspend or terminate her right.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is wajib when the wife submits; it stops if she rebels or is too young.

This passage from al-Quduri opens the Book of Maintenance with the foundational principle: the husband must provide food, clothing, and housing for his wife, but this right is conditional upon her submission (tamkin). The author then lists several exceptional scenarios that either preserve or void her entitlement. We will work through these rules systematically so you can apply them correctly in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and its condition
The passage begins with a general statement: maintenance is obligatory for the wife when she submits herself in her husband's home. Identify the act (nafaqah), its ruling (wajib), and the key condition (tamkin).
Submitting means living in the marital home and being available for marital relations.
2
Analyze the effect of withholding submission for mahr
If the wife refuses to submit until she receives her dowry, she still has maintenance. Understand why: she has a legitimate right to withhold, so she is not considered rebellious.
This is not nushuz; it's a valid delay.
3
Define nushuz and its consequence
If the wife commits nushuz (disobedience by leaving the home without right), she loses maintenance until she returns and repents.
Nushuz is a mufsid (invalidator) of the right to maintenance.
4
Examine the exceptions regarding age of spouses
A minor wife who cannot be enjoyed has no maintenance even if she submits. But if the husband is a minor and the wife is adult, she gets maintenance from his wealth.
The illah (reason) is the actual possibility of marital enjoyment.
5
Apply rules for divorce and death
A divorced wife gets maintenance and housing during her ‘iddah, regardless of type (raj‘i or ba’in). A widow gets no maintenance (she inherits instead). Also, if separation is due to the wife's sin (e.g., apostasy), she loses maintenance.
Differentiate between divorce and death: the ‘iddah of death does not carry maintenance.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Maintenance (Nafaqat) from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It establishes the general principle: maintenance (including food, clothing, and housing) is obligatory for the wife upon her husband when she submits herself to his authority in his home. The obligation is independent of the husband's financial status—whether he is wealthy or poor, he must provide according to his means. However, if the wife refuses to submit until she receives her dowry (mahr), she is still entitled to maintenance because she has a valid right to withhold. But if she commits nushuz (disobedience by leaving the home without right), she forfeits maintenance until she returns. A minor wife who is not yet fit for sexual relations has no maintenance, even if she submits, because the purpose of submission (tamkin) is not fulfilled. Conversely, if the husband is a minor incapable of intercourse, the adult wife is entitled to maintenance from his assets because she is ready for marital life. The passage further clarifies that a divorced woman retains maintenance and housing during her ‘iddah, whether the divorce is revocable (raj‘i) or irrevocable (ba’in). A widow, however, has no maintenance from her deceased husband’s estate (she receives her inheritance instead). Finally, if the separation is caused by the wife’s disobedience (e.g., apostasy or sinful behavior), she loses her right to maintenance. These rulings establish the precise conditions for nafaqah in Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Obligatory maintenance including food, clothing, and housing for the wife.
نشوز (nushūz): A wife's disobedience by leaving the marital home without legitimate reason.
عدة (‘iddah): Waiting period after divorce or death during which the wife cannot remarry.

Obligation of Maintenance and Conditions of Wife's Surrender
Establishes spousal maintenance obligation and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on spousal maintenance (nafaqah) and identify the conditions that affect its obligation, including surrender (taslim) and rebellion (nushuz).

Core Idea
Maintenance is obligatory for the wife upon surrender; it is only suspended by her rebellion.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn establishes the fundamental ruling on spousal maintenance (nafaqah) in Hanafi fiqh. The obligation of the husband to provide food, clothing, and housing is directly tied to the wife's surrender (taslim nafsiha) in his home. Several important exceptions and conditions are also outlined, such as the effect of demanding the mahr, nushuz, and the age of the spouses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Spousal maintenance (nafaqah) is ruled as wajib (obligatory) upon the husband, regardless of the wife's religion or the husband's financial status.
The text begins: 'النفقة واجبةٌ للزوجة على زوجها'
2
List the conditions for obligation
The primary condition is the wife's surrender (taslim nafsiha) in the husband's home. If she hands herself over, maintenance becomes due.
Condition: 'إذا سلمت نفسها في منزله'
3
Note exceptions that preserve maintenance
If the wife refuses to surrender until she receives her mahr (dowry), she still has maintenance. Also, if the husband is a minor and the wife adult, maintenance is due from his property.
Exception for mahr: 'فإن امتنعت من تسليم نفسها حتى يعطيها مهرها : فلها النفقة'
4
Identify cases that suspend or negate maintenance
If the wife is nashiz (rebellious, e.g., leaving the marital home without right), maintenance is suspended until she returns. If the wife is a minor who cannot be sexually enjoyed, no maintenance is due even if she surrenders.
Nushuz: 'فلا نفقة لها حتى تعود إلى منزله'

Exceptional Cases in Spousal Maintenance
Lists exceptional scenarios affecting nafaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on maintenance in exceptional circumstances such as imprisonment, Hajj, illness, and poverty, and apply them to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Maintenance is suspended only when the wife is at fault or voluntarily absent; inability to pay does not dissolve the marriage.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn presents a series of specific scenarios that modify the general obligation of maintenance. Understanding these exceptions clarifies the boundaries of nafaqah and highlights the Hanafi emphasis on the wife's obedience and the husband's financial responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
For each scenario mentioned in the passage, determine whether maintenance is due or not. Note the key condition that changes the ruling.
The passage covers: stepson incident, imprisonment, kidnapping, Hajj, illness, servant requirement, housing, visitation rights, poverty, and absent husband.
2
Understand the principle behind exceptions
Maintenance is suspended when the wife's absence or action is voluntary or due to her own fault (e.g., enabling stepson, imprisonment for debt, going on Hajj). Involuntary illness does not suspend maintenance.
Kidnapping is non-voluntary but the ruling still denies maintenance – a notable exception.
3
Apply the housing and visitation rules
The wife has the right to independent housing, separate from the husband's family, unless she chooses otherwise. The husband may prevent her relatives from entering, but they may see and speak with her.
This balances her privacy with family ties.
4
Recognize the Hanafi position on poverty
Inability to pay does not lead to judicial separation. The wife must borrow for her necessities, and the husband owes that debt.
This differs from other schools that permit annulment.
5
Understand the judge's role for absent husband
If the husband is absent but has property in the hands of an admitting trustee, the judge can order maintenance from that property for the wife and child.
This ensures support without the husband's presence.

Wife's Maintenance: When Enabling Step-Son or Detained
Rulings on wife's maintenance in specific situations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a wife loses her right to maintenance (nafaqah) and apply them to the scenarios of enabling a stepson, imprisonment, kidnapping, and Hajj.

Core Idea
Maintenance is due only when the wife is available; loss of tamkin suspends it, even involuntarily.

In Hanafi fiqh, a wife's right to maintenance is conditional upon her enabling her husband to have exclusive access (tamkin). This passage from al-Lubab clarifies specific situations where that right is forfeited: when she enables her stepson, when she is imprisoned, when she is kidnapped, or when she travels for Hajj with a mahram. Understanding these exceptions helps you see that maintenance is not absolute—it hinges on the wife's practical availability.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
Recognise that maintenance (nafaqah) is an obligation on the husband in exchange for the wife's tamkin—her enabling him to have exclusive intimate access. Any breach of tamkin potentially suspends this obligation.
Tamkin is not merely physical presence; it includes legal availability (e.g., not being imprisoned or absent for Hajj).
2
Analyse the first case: stepson
If the wife voluntarily enables her husband's son (stepson) to have intercourse with her, the ruling depends on timing. Before divorce: she has denied her husband exclusive access, so no maintenance. After divorce: the marriage has already ended; the act does not affect the maintenance due during the iddah period—she still receives it.
‘After divorce’ likely means during the iddah, when maintenance is still obligated.
3
Analyse the second set of cases: detention and absence
If the wife is imprisoned for a debt (her own fault), kidnapped (forcibly, not her fault), or travels for Hajj with a mahram (voluntary but religiously commendable), in all three she is physically unavailable to her husband. Consequently, maintenance stops.
The ruling applies regardless of whether the absence is voluntary (Hajj) or involuntary (kidnapping).
4
Derive the underlying rule
The common thread is the absence of tamkin—the husband cannot have access to his wife. Maintenance is not a reward; it is an exchange for availability. Therefore, whenever the wife is not available (even for a valid reason like Hajj), the obligation on the husband ceases.
There is no distinction between voluntary and involuntary absence in this ruling.
5
Memorize the exceptions
Commit these four scenarios to memory: enabling stepson (before divorce), imprisonment, kidnapping, and Hajj with mahram. In each, the wife loses maintenance. The only scenario where she retains maintenance after enabling stepson is after (or during) the iddah of a divorce.
These are specific rulings from the Hanafi school; other schools may differ.

Maintenance During Illness, Servant, Housing, and Visitation
Rulings on wife's maintenance, servant, housing, and visitation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligations of a husband regarding his wife's maintenance during illness, servant provision, separate housing, and visitation rights of her relatives.

Core Idea
A wife's right to maintenance continues even in illness; the husband must provide a separate dwelling and one servant if wealthy.

This passage from al-Lubab outlines several specific obligations of the husband in marital maintenance (nafaqah). While many rules are well-known, these details—especially regarding the wife's illness, the limit of one servant, and the husband's control over visitors—are crucial for accurate fatwa and practice. Identify the wife's maintenance during illness
The passage states that if the wife falls ill in the husband's house, she still receives nafaqah. This confirms that illness does not suspend her right to maintenance.

2
Determine the obligation of a servant
If the husband is wealthy (mūsir), he must provide a servant for his wife. However, only one servant is obligatory, even if he is very wealthy.
The condition is wealth, not the wife's need or illness.
3
Establish the housing requirement
The husband must provide a separate house (dār munfaridah) that does not contain any of his relatives. The wife may waive this right by choosing to live with them.
This ensures privacy and comfort for the wife.
4
Examine the rule about children from other marriages
If the husband has a child from another wife, he is not allowed to house that child with his current wife. The wife's consent is irrelevant here; it is prohibited.
This protects the wife from potential harm or discomfort.
5
Analyze the visitation rights
The husband may prevent the wife's parents, her child from another man, and other relatives from entering the house. However, he cannot stop them from looking at her or speaking with her at any time they choose.
This balances privacy with kinship rights.

Enforcement of Maintenance: Absent Son, Court Orders, and Slaves
Practical enforcement of maintenance: absent persons, court orders, and slave maintenance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rules regarding maintenance from an absent son's property, when court-awarded maintenance lapses, and the master's obligation to maintain slaves.

Core Idea
The court enforces maintenance by accessing property, but strict liability rules apply to third parties; slaves must be maintained or sold.

This lesson deals with practical enforcement scenarios when the obligated person is absent or refuses. It also covers the master's duty to maintain slaves. Understanding these rules is essential for applying nafaqah in real-world cases, especially when courts are involved.

Guided Path
1
Absent son with wealth
If a son is absent (غائب) but has wealth, that wealth is used to provide maintenance for his parents. This can be done through the court or by the father if he has access to the son's movable goods.
The rule distinguishes between movables and real estate.
2
Liability for spending from the absent son's property
If the parents spend from the son's wealth in their possession, they are not liable (لا يضمنان). But if a third party spends without court permission, they are liable (ضمن).
This protects parents but imposes strict liability on others to prevent misuse.
3
Court-awarded maintenance
Once the court awards maintenance for a specific period, it lapses after that period unless the court permitted borrowing. This means the recipient cannot claim past due maintenance after the period ends without prior authorization.
The phrase 'إلا أن يأذن لهم القاضي في الاستدانة عليه' provides an exception.
4
Master's obligation for slaves
The master must maintain his male and female slaves. If he refuses, and the slaves have earning capacity, they can earn and support themselves. Otherwise, the court compels the master to sell them or maintain them.
This ensures the slave's basic welfare and prevents neglect.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A qadi's maintenance decree lapses if time passes without collection, unless borrowing was permitted.

A qadi's decree is a binding judicial order, but Hanafi law balances that with the need for timely action. This lesson examines when a maintenance obligation drops and the sole exception that keeps it alive. Understanding this rule helps prevent indefinite accumulation of unclaimed debt and clarifies the rights of recipients.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the qadi's decree ordering maintenance for a child, parents, or relatives. The decree makes maintenance wajib (obligatory) on the liable person.
The decree itself creates a debt, but enforceability is conditional.
2
Determine the effect of time
If a period passes without the maintenance being paid or claimed, the obligation for that past period is waived (saqatat). This prevents indefinite liability.
The lapse applies only to past due amounts, not future maintenance.

Rulings on master's duty to feed and clothe slaves, and consequences of refusal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the master's wajib to maintain slaves and apply the judicial remedy when the master refuses.

Core Idea
A master must feed and clothe his slaves; refusal triggers earning or forced sale.

This passage from al-Quduri outlines the master's duty to provide nafaqah (food, clothing, shelter) for his slaves. It also gives practical remedies if the master neglects this obligation, balancing the slave's welfare with the master's property rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and parties
State the primary hukm: the master (mawla) must provide maintenance for both male and female slaves.
The obligation is wajib, not merely recommended.
2
Examine the conditional upon refusal
If the master refuses, the passage provides two scenarios. Determine what triggers each scenario.
The key variable is whether the slave has kasb (earning capacity).
3
Analyze the first scenario (kasb exists)
When the slave has an ability to earn (e.g., a craft or trade), they are permitted to work and spend on themselves.
The slave does not need the master's permission in this case.
4
Analyze the second scenario (no kasb)
If the slave cannot earn, the qadi compels the master either to sell the slave or to provide maintenance.
The master must choose one; he cannot simply abandon the slave.
5
State the judicial remedy
The qadi enforces the obligation by forcing the master to sell or maintain. This prevents neglect.
If sold, the new owner assumes the maintenance duty.
