Core Idea
A credit sale with an unknown term is fasid, but can be cured if both parties agree to remove the term before its arrival.

In this passage, al-Quduri explains that credit sales referencing vague future events (like festivals or harvests) are invalid due to uncertainty (jahalah). However, the defect can be fixed if the parties mutually decide to cancel the credit condition. Let us examine the two categories of uncertain terms and the legal remedy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of uncertain credit terms
The passage mentions two groups: events with fixed dates but unknown to the parties (e.g., Nowruz, Christian Lent) and events that are inherently variable (e.g., harvest, arrival of pilgrims).

For the first group, if the exact date is unknown, the sale is fasid. For the second group, the sale is not permissible (la yajuz) because the term is inherently uncertain.
In Hanafi fiqh, 'la yajuz' often means the contract is void due to a fundamental defect.

Before the uncertain event occurs, if both parties agree to drop the credit term (i.e., make the sale spot), the sale becomes permissible (ja'iz).
The remedy requires mutual consent and occurs before the event arrives.

Jahalah (ignorance) of the term invalidates a credit sale because it leads to potential dispute. Dropping the term removes the uncertainty and cures the defect.

Possession in a Fasid Sale: Ownership, Liability, and Rescission
First part of the passage: effects of qabd in fasid sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal effects of taking possession in a fasid sale according to Hanafi fiqh and explain why the buyer's subsequent sale is valid.

Core Idea
Possession with permission in a fasid sale transfers ownership but buyer pays market value and either party may rescind.

A fasid (invalid) sale does not normally transfer ownership. However, the Hanafi school makes an important exception: if the buyer takes possession with the seller's permission and both the item and the price are valid property, ownership transfers. The buyer must pay the item's market value (qimah), not the agreed price, and both parties retain the right to cancel the contract. This lesson unpacks these conditions and their practical consequences, including why the buyer can later resell the item.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions for ownership transfer
From the passage, two conditions are required: (1) possession occurs by the seller's command (بأمر البائع), and (2) the contract has two countervalues, each being property (كل واحد منهما مال). Confirm that both conditions are met.
The phrase 'وفي العقد عوضان، كل واحد منهما مال' ensures the item and price are legitimate property.
2
Determine the buyer's liability
The buyer becomes liable for the market value (قيمته) of the item at the time of possession, not the price agreed in the fasid contract. This is because the contract is invalid and the original price is not enforceable.
The text says 'ولزمته قيمته' – he is obligated to pay its value.
3
Analyze the right to rescind
Each contracting party has the right to rescind (فسخه). This means the transaction remains revocable even after possession. The fasid sale is not final until both parties accept it or it is executed.
The text states 'ولكل واحد من المتعاقدين فسخه'.
4
Apply the rule to the buyer's resale
If the buyer sells the item to a third party, that sale is valid (نَفَذ بيعه). Because ownership transferred upon possession, the buyer had the right to sell it. The buyer's liability to pay the qimah is a separate obligation.
This demonstrates that ownership was established despite the fasid nature of the first sale.

Composite Sales: Void and Partially Valid Combinations
Second part of the passage: rules for mixing different items in one sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a composite sale as void or partially valid based on the types of items combined, applying the Hanafi rule as stated in the passage.

Core Idea
Mixing a non-sellable item (free person, carrion) voids the entire contract; mixing a restricted item (mudabbar, another's slave) saves the sellable part.

When a single sale contract includes multiple items, the validity of each part depends on the nature of the items. The Hanafi school distinguishes sharply between items that cannot be sold at all (e.g., a free person or carrion) and items that can be sold but with legal limitations (e.g., a mudabbar slave or another's slave). This lesson applies the rule from the passage to determine when the entire contract is void and when only the saleable item stands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of items
The passage lists two categories: (1) items that can never be sold (free person, carrion) – combining these with a sellable item voids the entire contract; (2) items that have restrictions but can be part of a sale (mudabbar, slave owned by another) – combining these with a regular slave results in partial validity.
A mudabbar is a slave promised freedom after the master's death; he cannot be sold because the emancipation right is attached.
2
Analyze the effect of including a non-sellable item
If a free person (حر) or a slaughtered sheep that is carrion (ميتة) is combined with a sellable item, the sale is completely void (بَطَلَ البيع فيهما). The reason is that the non-sellable item taints the entire contract; the price cannot be apportioned validly.
The text uses 'بَطَلَ البيع فيهما' – the sale is void in both.
3
Analyze the effect of including a restricted item
If a mudabbar slave or a slave owned by another person is combined with a regular slave belonging to the seller, the contract is valid for the regular slave against its share of the price (بحصته من الثمن). The mudabbar or another's slave is excluded from the contract.
The text says 'صح العقد في العبد بحصته من الثمن' – the contract is valid for the slave with his share of the price.
4
Apply the principle to new cases
The underlying principle: if the invalid item cannot be separated from the sale (because it is inherently unsaleable), the whole contract fails; if the invalid item can be removed by apportioning the price, the sale stands for the valid part.
This distinction prevents injustice – the buyer can still acquire the sellable item.

Prohibited Acts That Do Not Invalidate a Sale
Five prohibited acts in sales that are makruh but do not invalidate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five prohibited acts in sales according to the Hanafi school and explain why each does not invalidate the contract.

Core Idea
Not every prohibited act makes a sale void; validity and morality are distinct.

In the Hanafi school, some actions in sales are forbidden or disliked but do not affect the contract's validity. This lesson presents five such practices from the hadith, each prohibited for its deceptive or unethical nature. You will learn the ruling for each and the principle that a prohibited act does not automatically render the contract fasid or batil.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the prohibition
For each of the five practices, note the specific action the Prophet forbade. Understand the nature of the deception or harm.
Najsh: bidding to inflate price without intent to buy. Sawm ala sawm: interfering after a deal is nearly done. Talaqqi al-jalb: intercepting traders at the road.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each act
The text says 'يكره' (disliked). Determine the precise level: mostly makruh tahrimi due to the explicit prohibition.
Even if makruh tahrimi, the contract remains valid.
3
Confirm that validity is unaffected
The key phrase is 'ولا يفسد به العقد' – the contract is not invalidated. Understand that sinfulness and validity are separate.
This is a major Hanafi principle in muamalat.
4
Relate to the hadith proof
The passage cites a hadith. Recognize that the prohibition (nahi) indicates tahrim but does not automatically make the contract fasid.
The Hanafis rely on dalala: nahi alone does not imply fasid unless the subject-matter itself is prohibited.

As a memorization anchor, commit these five examples to memory. They are often tested in fiqh exams.

Resale by the Buyer is Valid
Ruling on validity of resale by the original buyer

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Once you buy it, you own it, and your resale is valid without needing the first seller's consent.

This short but powerful ruling establishes a core principle of property transfer in Hanafi fiqh. Once a buyer acquires ownership through a valid purchase, he or she is free to dispose of the item, including selling it to another. The text makes this unconditional, emphasizing that the buyer's sale is effective (nafadha) without any additional approval. Understanding this simple rule prevents common misconceptions about delayed payment or seller's consent.

Guided Path

Iqalah: Permissibility, Conditions, and Legal Nature
Ruling statement on iqalah with conditions and legal nature

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of iqalah, identify its essential condition (same price), explain its dual legal nature (faskh for parties, new sale for third parties), and determine the effect of destruction of the price or sold item.

Core Idea
Iqalah with the same price dissolves the contract between parties but is a new sale for third parties.

Iqalah (mutual rescission) is a recommended act in Hanafi fiqh that cancels a sale by mutual consent. But it is not a simple cancellation—it has a unique legal nature that treats the parties as if the contract never existed, while protecting third-party rights by treating it as a new sale. Understanding iqalah helps you grasp how contracts can be undone and the balance between justice and security of transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that iqalah is permissible (ja'iz) in sale. Note that this means it is allowed and even recommended (mandub) based on the hadith 'He who grants a rescission to a seller, Allah will forgive his sins.'
The ruling is not fard or wajib but a recommended act of mutual kindness.
2
Locate the essential condition
The text says iqalah must be 'with the like of the first price' (bimithl al-thaman al-awwal). If a different price is stipulated, that condition is void, but the iqalah itself remains valid with the original price.
This preserves the original contractual equivalence; the return must mirror the original exchange.
3
Understand the legal nature (faskh vs. new sale)
Abu Hanifa holds that iqalah is a dissolution (faskh) between the contracting parties, meaning the contract is annulled retroactively. However, for third parties (e.g., a second buyer), it is treated as a new sale, so their rights are unaffected.
This dual nature prevents abuse: the original seller cannot revoke the contract to the detriment of a subsequent purchaser.
4
Examine the effect of destruction
If the price (money) is destroyed before iqalah, the obligation to return the same amount remains, so iqalah is valid. If the sold item is destroyed, iqalah is prevented because the seller cannot return what no longer exists. If only part is destroyed, iqalah is allowed for the remaining part.
Destruction of the sold item makes return impossible, so rescission fails; partial destruction allows partial rescission by proportion.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In Murabahah, buyer has an option; in Tawliyah, he deducts—but the Sahibayn differ.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the buyer's remedy when the seller misrepresents the cost in trust-based sales (Murabahah and Tawliyah). Abu Hanifa distinguishes between the two contracts: for Murabahah, the buyer may either accept the item at the full price or rescind; for Tawliyah, he deducts the dishonesty. Abu Yusuf argues for deduction in both, while Muhammad rejects deduction entirely. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of khiyar and the school's internal reasoning.

Murabahah is a sale with disclosed profit; Tawliyah is a sale at cost price without profit. Know which is which.
2

In Murabahah, the buyer has an option (khiyar): take at full price or rescind. In Tawliyah, the buyer deducts the dishonesty from the price.
3

Abu Yusuf holds that the buyer deducts in both Murabahah and Tawliyah.
4
Compare with Muhammad
Muhammad holds that the buyer does not deduct in either; the contract stands as is.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's: option in Murabahah, deduction in Tawliyah. This is the view adopted in major Hanafi texts like al-Hidayah.
The ikhtilaf is legitimate; all three Imams had sound reasoning.

Each sale requires its own measurement — no carryover, or the sale is invalid.

When you buy a commodity measured by volume or weight, you take possession after measuring. If you later sell that same commodity by the same standard, you cannot rely on the previous measurement. The Hanafi school requires you to remeasure or reweigh it for the new buyer. This ensures certainty of quantity and avoids gharar (ambiguity) in the second transaction.

The condition applies even if the original measurement was done by the seller himself.

If the condition is not met, the second sale is not permissible (lam yajuz) and is therefore fasid (voidable).
Fasid means the contract is defective but can be rectified if the condition is fulfilled before taking delivery.

Understand the Wisdom
Each contract must have a known quantity at the time of the contract to eliminate ambiguity (gharar). The previous measurement is linked to the first transaction and does not automatically transfer.
This rule applies to both kayl (volume) and wazn (weight).

The ruling is specific to sales by the same standard. If the second sale is by a different standard (e.g., by weight after buying by volume), the ruling may differ.

Disposing of Price Before Qabd
States ruling on disposing of price before qabd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on disposing of the price before qabd and apply it to a real scenario.

Core Idea
The buyer may use the price before receiving the goods; price qabd is not required for its use.

In Hanafi sales law, the general principle is that a person cannot dispose of an item before taking possession (qabd). However, the price (thaman) is treated differently from the sold item (mabi). This lesson clarifies that the buyer may dispose of the price even before physically receiving the purchased item. This ruling highlights the distinction between the price and the commodity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage states: 'And disposing of the price before possession is permissible.' The act is tasarruf (disposal) of the price before qabd. The ruling is ja'iz (permissible).
This is the explicit text.
2
Contrast with the sold item
In Hanafi law, the seller cannot dispose of the sold item before qabd. Why is the price different? Because the price is a monetary obligation, not the subject of the sale. The buyer's ownership of the price is not contingent on qabd of the item.
The sold item's qabd is a condition for its disposal.
3
Apply to a scenario
If a buyer agrees to buy a car for $10,000, before taking delivery, he may invest that $10,000 elsewhere. He is not prohibited from using the money.
This does not affect the seller's right to demand payment.

Deferment of Debt: General Permissibility and Exception of Qard
States ruling on deferment of debts and exception for qard

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on deferring immediate debts and identify the exception for qard with its rationale.

Core Idea
Any immediate debt can be deferred by agreement except a loan (qard), because deferring a loan resembles a sale of debt.

Debts that are currently due can be made payable later if both parties agree to a known term. This applies to sale price, compensation, etc. However, a loan (qard) cannot be deferred. The wisdom is that a loan is a gratuitous contract meant for immediate benefit; deferring it changes its nature and leads to prohibited riba-like transactions. This lesson examines the general rule and the exception.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The passage says: 'Whoever sells for an immediate price, then defers it for a known term, it becomes deferred.' This applies to any immediate debt.
The new term must be known (ma'lum).
2
Identify the exception
The exception is a loan (qard). The deferment of a loan is not valid (la yasihh).
The debt remains immediate despite the attempted deferment.
3
Explain the rationale
A qard is a gratuitous loan meant for immediate repayment. Deferring it transforms it into a sale of debt, which is prohibited due to riba. Even without interest, the deferment itself is invalid.
This is a Hanafi ruling; other schools may differ.
4
Practical application
If you lend $100 due now, you cannot later agree to receive it next year. The debt remains due now. However, you can structure a new sale on credit instead.
This prevents circumvention of riba rules.

The Two Attributes Framework and Permanent Categories
Explains the conditions for permissibility of excess and delay based on attributes and permanence of categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the two-attribute framework to determine when excess and delay are permissible, and identify which items are permanently considered measured or weighed.

Core Idea
If both kind and measure/weight are present, excess and delay are haram; if only one, only delay is haram.

This lesson builds on the 'illa by examining how the presence or absence of the two attributes (kind and measure/weight) affects the permissibility of excess and delay. We also learn that items explicitly mentioned by the Prophet are permanently categorized regardless of custom.

Guided Path
1
Review the two-attribute framework
The two attributes are: the kind (jins) and the associated meaning (ma'na al-madmum ilayh) which is measure or weight. When both are absent, both excess and delay are permissible.

2
Apply when both are present
If both kind and measure/weight are present, then excess (tafadul) and delay (nasa') are prohibited. This is the standard case of riba al-fadl.
3
Apply when only one is present
If only one attribute is present (e.g., same kind but different method, or same method but different kind), then excess is permissible but delay is prohibited. This covers cases like exchanging barley for wheat (same measure, different kind).
Delay is prohibited because it opens the door to riba al-nasi'ah.

Understand permanent categorization
Items explicitly mentioned by the Prophet as measured (wheat, barley, dates, salt) are always considered measured even if people stop measuring. Similarly, gold and silver are always weighed.
This prevents custom from overruling the nass.
5
Apply the custom rule for unspecified items
Items not explicitly mentioned follow the custom of the people: if they are customarily measured, they are makīl; if weighed, mawzūn. This determines their ribawi status.

Sarf and Specification in Ribawi Transactions
Clarifies the special rules for currency exchange (sarf) and the requirement of specification for other ribawi sales.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the contract of sarf and other ribawi sales, and identify the requirements of immediate delivery (taqabud) and specification (ta'yin).

Core Idea
Sarf requires both items to be handed over in the session; other ribawi sales only require specification of the item.

This lesson focuses on two key requirements in ribawi transactions: immediate hand-to-hand delivery for the exchange of gold/silver (sarf) and specification of the countervalue for other ribawi items. Understanding this distinction is crucial for valid currency exchanges and commodity trades.

Guided Path
1
Define the contract of sarf
Sarf is the exchange of gold for silver or any currency. The passage says it requires 'qabd al-'iwaydayn fi al-majlis' – hand-to-hand delivery of both exchanged items in the session.
This is to eliminate the possibility of riba al-nasi'ah.
2
Distinguish sarf from other ribawi sales
For sarf, both hand-to-hand delivery (taqabud) and specification (ta'yin?) Actually, the passage says sarf requires delivery, not specification. For other ribawi items, only specification (ta'yin) is required, not hand-to-hand delivery.
3
Understand the role of specification
Specification (ta'yin) means the specific item being sold must be identified precisely to avoid gharar. For example, when selling wheat for flour, the exact quantity and quality must be specified, but the items need not be exchanged on the spot.
This applies to ribawi sales other than sarf.
4
Apply to currency exchange
If you exchange a 10g gold coin for a 20g silver coin, it must be done immediately and both items must be handed over in that sitting. Any delay or credit makes it invalid.
5
Apply to commodity exchange
If you sell 1 kg of dates for 2 kg of salt, since they are different kinds, specification (e.g., 'this specific bag of dates for that specific bag of salt') is sufficient, but immediate delivery is not required unless there is a delay risk.

Salam (advance payment for future delivery) is a special contract permitted in Islam. Its validity hinges on several conditions that eliminate uncertainty (gharar). Rulings on trustee placement and fungible pledge liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on placing a pledge with a trustee and explain the liability for fungible pledges when destroyed in the trustee's custody.

Core Idea
A pledge placed with a neutral trustee is valid; if lost, the mortgagee bears the loss, and for fungibles, it is reduced by like.

This passage establishes two key rules in Hanafi pledge law. First, when both parties agree to deposit a pledge with a trusted third party (al-'adl), the arrangement is valid and neither party can unilaterally retrieve it. If the pledge is destroyed in the trustee's hands, the loss falls on the mortgagee—i.e., the debt is reduced. Second, fungible items such as dirhams, dinars, and goods sold by measure or weight may be pledged. If they are of the same genus as the debt and are destroyed, the debt is reduced by the like amount, regardless of differences in quality.

Understand the role of the adl
Recognize that the adl is a neutral trustee agreed upon by both parties. Once placed, neither pledgor nor mortgagee may take the pledge without the other's consent. The adl's possession is legally equivalent to the mortgagee's possession for liability purposes.
This prevents unilateral actions that could harm the other party.
2
Analyze liability upon destruction
If the pledge perishes in the adl's custody, the loss is borne by the mortgagee—i.e., the debt is reduced. This applies even if the trustee is negligent, because the adl is considered a representative of both parties, and possession by the adl is imputed to the mortgagee.
This differs from some schools where the trustee is liable only for negligence.
3
Apply the rule for fungibles
When fungibles (mithliyyat such as dirhams, dinars, measured or weighed goods) are pledged with their same genus and are destroyed, the debt is reduced by the like amount (mithl). Quality differences are ignored because fungibles are legally identical in value.
This ensures fairness: the creditor loses exactly the value of the destroyed item.

However, if this agency is made a condition in the original pledge contract, it transforms from a simple appointment to a binding term. This lesson explores the implications of such stipulation.

This is a special case where contract terms override the general revocability of agency.

Even if the pledgor dies, the stipulated agency does not terminate. The agent retains authority to sell the pledge after the debt matures.
Death normally ends agency, but here the condition in the contract makes it survive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three possible outcomes when a pledgor frees a pledged slave without the mortgagee's permission and explain the condition for each.

Core Idea
Freeing a pledged slave is valid even without the mortgagee's consent, but the mortgagee's right is preserved through alternative security.

This passage from al-Quduri outlines the Hanafi ruling on a pledgor who frees a pledged slave. The act of freeing (itq) is effective, but the mortgagee's security cannot be lost. The text clarifies how the debtor's obligations change depending on whether the debt is due, deferred, or the debtor is insolvent. Understanding this ruling is key to grasping how Hanafi fiqh balances the rights of parties in a pledge contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is freeing a pledged slave without the mortgagee's permission. The default ruling: it is effective (nāfidh).
The act is valid; the mortgagee's claim is not extinguished but redirected.
2
Check the debt's maturity
If the debt is due (حال), the pledgor must pay the debt immediately. If deferred (مؤجل), the court takes the slave's value as a substitute pledge.
3
Assess the pledgor's solvency
If the pledgor is insolvent, the freed slave must work (istis'ā') to earn his value to pay the debt, then recover from the master later.
The slave's earnings become the substitute security.
4
Recognize the pattern
In all cases, the mortgagee's right is secured: either by payment, substitution, or slave's earnings.
The freeing is never revoked.
