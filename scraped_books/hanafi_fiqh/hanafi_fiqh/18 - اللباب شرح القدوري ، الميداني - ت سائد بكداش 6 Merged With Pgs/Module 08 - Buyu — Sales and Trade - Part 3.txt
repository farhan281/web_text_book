Core Idea
If the pledgor destroys the pledge, he must compensate; if a stranger does, the mortgagee claims the value as new pledge.

This passage from Al-Lubab concludes the discussion of liability when a pledge (rahn) is destroyed. It distinguishes two scenarios: destruction by the pledgor himself and destruction by a third party. The ruling clarifies who bears liability and how the security interest is preserved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the Arabic text: 'إذا استهلك الراهن الرهن' (if the pledgor consumes/destroys the pledge) and 'إن استهلكه أجنبي' (if a stranger destroys it). These are the two distinct cases the passage addresses.
2
State the ruling for each scenario

Liability falls on the one who destroys, but the mortgagee has the right to sue the third party.
3
Understand the mortgagee's procedural right
The mortgagee, not the pledgor, is the proper plaintiff against the third party. This is because the mortgagee holds possession and has a security interest.
The compensation (value) replaces the original pledge and remains in the mortgagee's hand.

Core Idea
No hajr on a free adult even if wasteful; only a delay for the imprudent minor until 25.

This lesson introduces you to Imam Abu Hanifa's position on legal interdiction (al-hajr) for a reckless spendthrift (al-safih). Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi view on the legal capacity of adults versus minors, and the balance between individual freedom and financial protection.

Guided Path

Identify the subject and conditions
Note that the person in question must be an adult (baligh), sane (aqil), and free (hurr). The ruling applies only when these conditions are met.
If any condition is missing (e.g., a slave), the ruling differs.
2
State the general ruling
Abu Hanifa rules that no interdiction (hajr) is placed on such a person, and his disposal of wealth is valid (ja'iz) even if he is wasteful and destroys his wealth without any benefit.
3
Note the exception for minors
If a boy reaches puberty but is not prudent (rashid), his wealth is not handed over to him until he reaches 25 years. However, any transactions he makes before that age are valid.
After 25, the wealth is handed over even if he still lacks prudence.

Abu Hanifa distinguishes between legal capacity (ahliyya) and financial prudence. An adult has full capacity, so his actions are valid. For a minor, the lack of prudence temporarily delays control over wealth, but his transactions are still binding to protect third parties.
This contrasts with the Sahibayn's view, which allows interdiction of the adult safih.

Abu Hanifa refuses interdiction of the insolvent but permits indefinite imprisonment and forced sale.

When a debtor becomes insolvent (muflis), should the judge restrict his financial capacity (hajr) or take other measures? Abu Hanifa holds that interdiction is not applied; instead, the judge may imprison the debtor until he sells his assets to pay debts, and in some cases directly transfer currency. This ruling differs from the Sahibayn and later Hanafi practice.

If the debtor has dirhams and the debt is dirhams, the judge pays directly. If the debtor has dinars (different currency), the judge sells them first.
This shows flexibility in enforcement without interdiction.

Although not in this passage, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) allow interdiction. Later fatwa adopts their view as more practical.

Core Idea
An acknowledgement of debt from a sale binds the acknowledger even if the sale is invalid or goods unreceived, except when the creditor can deliver the goods.

This passage from al-Quduri presents four distinct rulings on a specific scenario: a person acknowledges owing money from a sale but claims the sold item (e.g., a slave) was never received, or disputes the quality of the currency. Each ruling depends on whether the item is specified, unspecified, prohibited, or disputed in quality. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying the law of iqrar (acknowledgement) in sale contracts.

Guided Path
1

Identify the ruling when the acknowledger mentions a specific slave he bought but did not take possession. The creditor has the option to either deliver the slave and take the money, or get nothing.

2
Case 2: Unspecified slave
If the acknowledger says 'from the price of a slave' without specifying which slave, the debt binds him absolutely (according to Abu Hanifa).
Lack of specification does not negate the obligation.

If the acknowledged debt is from the price of prohibited items (wine, pig), the debt still binds him, and his interpretation (attempt to explain away) is not accepted.
The iqrar is valid despite the underlying sale being invalid.

When the acknowledger says the owed dirhams are counterfeit (zuyuf) and the creditor claims they are good (jiyad), Abu Hanifa rules that the creditor's claim prevails, so good dirhams are due.
The creditor's assertion about his own right is given priority.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah arises from sale, is firmed by witness, and is owned by possession.

The right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) does not become final all at once. It develops through three distinct legal stages, each with its own effect. This lesson clarifies the moment the right arises, what makes it irrevocable, and how ownership is ultimately transferred.

Understand that the right becomes stable (tastaqirru) only when the shafi' performs ishhad—formally declaring his claim before witnesses.

4

Recognize that ownership (tamlik) is actualized by taking possession (akhdh), either through voluntary delivery by the buyer or a judge's decree.
Possession completes the transfer of ownership.
5
Summarize the sequence
Combine the stages: cause (sale) → stability (ishhad) → ownership (possession). Each stage depends on the previous.

Shuf'a is suspended by a seller's option or invalid sale, but immediately binding if the buyer holds the option.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) is a right to take over a sold property on the same terms. But not every sale triggers it immediately. This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri clarifies three distinct cases: when the seller has an option, when the buyer has an option, and when the sale is invalid (fasid). A fasid purchase does not give shuf'a because the sale is not legally valid. If both parties waive their right to rescind, then shuf'a becomes wajib.

The text uses 'وجبت الشفعة' for both but with different reasoning: in the seller's option, it becomes wajib after dropping; in the buyer's option, it is wajib from the start. Similarly, if the purchase is invalid (fasid), there is no shuf'a because the sale is not legally valid. Each party may rescind (فسخ). If the right to rescind is dropped (i.e., the parties waive their right to annul and treat the sale as valid), then the shafi's right is established as if the sale had been valid from the outset.

The rubble belongs to the buyer, not the pre-emptor.

This lesson explores an important intra-Hanafi disagreement about the boundaries of an agent's authority. When an agent is appointed to buy or sell, can he contract with his own close relatives? Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your awareness of how conflict of interest is balanced against commercial necessity in Hanafi law.

Why exclude the slave and mukatab? Because the agent has ownership or partial control over them, creating a stronger conflict of interest that fair price alone cannot neutralize.

This passage from the Lubab defines the legal limits for an agent authorized to purchase goods. Purchase by Agent: Specified vs Unspecified Item

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the item is specified, agent cannot buy for himself; if unspecified, it's his unless he intends or uses principal's money.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri addresses a common situation in agency (wakala): when does an agent's purchase belong to him personally versus his principal? The ruling hinges on whether the item was specifically identified or merely described generically. Understanding this distinction prevents disputes and protects both parties.

Guided Path

Distinguish specified vs unspecified
Identify whether the principal's instruction refers to a particular item ('this slave') or a generic description ('a slave').
Specified means identified by pointing or naming uniquely.
2
Apply the rule for specified item
If the item is specified, the agent cannot purchase it for himself. Any purchase he makes of that item is for the principal.
3
Apply the rule for unspecified item
If the item is unspecified, the agent's purchase is presumed to be for himself unless he explicitly states he intended for the principal or he uses the principal's money.
The default is ownership by the agent.
4
Identify the exceptions
The two exceptions that transfer ownership to the principal: (1) the agent's express intention (niat), (2) using the principal's funds.
