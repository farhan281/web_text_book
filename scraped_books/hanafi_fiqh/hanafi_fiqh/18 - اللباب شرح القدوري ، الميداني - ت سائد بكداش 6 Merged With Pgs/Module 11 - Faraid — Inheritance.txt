Acknowledging Collateral Relatives: Lineage vs Inheritance
Rulings on acknowledging lineage of collateral relatives and inheritance effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledging lineage of a brother or uncle and determine the inheritance outcome based on the presence or absence of a known heir.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a brother's lineage does not prove paternity, but it can entitle him to inherit if no known heir exists.

In earlier lessons, we saw that acknowledging lineage for parents, children, spouses, and patrons is accepted and establishes full inheritance rights. But what about relatives like brothers and uncles? This passage clarifies that such acknowledgments do not prove lineage, yet they can still affect inheritance under certain conditions. The key factor is whether the acknowledger already has a known heir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acknowledged relatives
The text mentions 'other than parents, child, husband, wife, and patron' and gives examples: brother (akh) and uncle (ʿamm). These are collateral relatives.
The earlier lessons covered the primary relatives; now we move to collaterals.
2
Understand the ruling on lineage
The acknowledgment is not accepted for establishing lineage (nasab). That means the acknowledged person does not become a legal relative for all purposes.
The reason is that lineage for collaterals requires stronger proof (like witnesses or testimony) than a simple acknowledgment.
3
Analyze the inheritance scenarios
Two cases: (1) If the acknowledger has a known heir (wārith maʿrūf), that heir takes priority over the acknowledged person. (2) If no known heir exists, the acknowledged person becomes entitled to inherit.
The 'known heir' can be near or distant; the key is that a recognized heir blocks the acknowledged claimant.
4
Apply the special case of a deceased father
If a man's father has died, and he acknowledges someone as his brother (i.e., his father's son), the brother's lineage is not proven from the father, but he still shares the inheritance with the acknowledger.
This is a specific application: the acknowledgment creates a right to inherit without establishing paternity.

Sulh Among Heirs: Conditions for Valid Settlement in Estate Distribution
States rules for sulh when heirs buy out one heir from the estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid sulh when heirs buy out one heir, and identify which conditions render the sulh void.

Core Idea
Sulh among heirs is valid for mixed assets but requires precise valuation when gold and silver are involved.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains the Hanafi rules for sulh when heirs agree to remove one heir from the estate by paying him a sum. The validity depends on the composition of the estate—whether it is real estate, chattels, silver, gold, or includes debts. Understanding these conditions ensures that the settlement does not fall into riba or gharar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is sulh (settlement) among heirs to exclude one heir by paying him money. The ruling is that it is valid (sahih) under certain conditions and void (batil) if those conditions are violated.
Sulh is a contract, so its validity depends on proper consideration and absence of prohibited elements.
2
List the estate scenarios
The text gives four scenarios: estate is real estate or chattels; estate is entirely one currency (silver or gold) and paid in another; estate contains both gold and silver along with other assets; and estate includes debts owed by third parties.
Each scenario has different conditions for valid sulh.
3
Extract conditions for each scenario
For real estate/chattels: any amount is valid. For one currency paid in another: valid unconditionally. For mixed gold/silver: payment in one metal must exceed the heir's share of that metal. For debts: sulh is valid only if debtors are discharged from the heir's share, not if other heirs take over debts.
The condition for mixed metals prevents riba (usury) by ensuring the excess represents the heir's share from other assets.
4
Identify the invalidator
The passage explicitly states that including debts in the sulh on condition that the other heirs take them over makes the sulh void (batil). This is because it involves uncertainty (gharar) in debt collection and potential riba.
The valid alternative is to simply discharge the debtors from the excluded heir's share.

Conditions for Buying Out an Heir from the Estate
States the permissibility and conditions of buying out an heir from the inheritance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissibility of buying out an heir and identify the condition required when the estate contains both gold and silver along with other assets.

Core Idea
Buying out an heir is permissible, but when the estate mixes gold and silver, the price must exceed his share of that metal.

In inheritance settlements, heirs often want to buy out one of their number rather than divide the assets. The Hanafi school permits this as a form of compromise (sulh), but with a careful condition when the estate contains both gold and silver. This lesson examines the ruling and its underlying rationale to prevent riba.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act
Recognize that the transaction is a compromise (sulh) where the remaining heirs give the exiting heir wealth to relinquish his share of the estate.
2
Classify the Composition of the Estate
Determine whether the estate is entirely real estate/trade goods, homogeneous in one metal, or a mix of gold and silver with other items. This classification drives the ruling.
The passage distinguishes three cases: pure aqar/urud, pure silver/gold, or mixed.
3
Apply the Corresponding Rule for Each Case
For aqar/urud: any amount is permissible. For homogeneous estate with payment in different metal: permissible. For mixed estate (gold + silver + others): the amount given in gold or silver must be more than the heir's share of that metal.
The condition applies only when the payment is in one of the metals present in the estate.
4
Understand the Rationale
The condition prevents riba al-fadl (inequality in exchange of the same genus). By exceeding the share, the extra compensates for the heir's portion of the other assets without violating riba rules.
Gold and silver are ribawi items; aqar and urud are not, so no parity condition is needed.

Compromise in Inheritance with Debts Receivable
Two-case ruling on sulh with debts receivable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two possible stipulations when including debts receivable in an inheritance compromise, apply the Hanafi rule to determine validity of each, and explain the legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Transferring an uncollected debt in a compromise is void; discharging the debtor from the exiting heir's share is valid.

Compromise (sulh) among heirs can simplify estate division, but when the estate includes debts owed to the deceased, the stipulations matter greatly. This passage from al-Lubab explains exactly which conditions make the compromise valid or invalid, based on whether the debt is transferred (batil) or the debtor is released from the exiting heir's share (sahih).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the debt scenario
The act is a compromise (sulh) among heirs where the estate includes a debt receivable (dayn) owed by third parties. One heir (musalih) exits in exchange for compensation from the other heirs.
The debt is an asset of the estate, but its collection is uncertain.
2
Examine the first stipulation: transfer of debt
If the other heirs stipulate that they will collect the debt and the exiting heir is released from any claim to it, and the debt becomes theirs, the compromise is batil.
This is essentially a sale of an uncollected debt, which involves gharar (uncertainty).
3
Examine the second stipulation: discharge of debtor
If the stipulation is that the debtors (ghurama') are discharged from the exiting heir's share (i.e., his portion of the debt is forgiven), and they will not be pursued for his share, then the compromise is valid.
This is a release (ibra') of a debt, which is permissible.
4
Determine the key difference
The invalid stipulation involves transferring ownership of an uncertain asset; the valid stipulation involves forgiving the debt, which is clear and final.
The difference turns on whether the exiting heir assumes risk (transfer) or simply forgives (ibra').
5
Apply the ruling to new scenarios
If the debt is already collected and held as cash, it is like any other asset and can be included in a compromise without issue. The ruling applies only to uncollected debts.
This is a common edge case.

Commentary
Passage type: Ruling statement. This section addresses a specific scenario in inheritance compromise where the estate includes debts owed to the deceased (receivables). If the heirs wish to include these debts in a compromise with an heir who is exiting, they must be careful with the stipulations. If they stipulate that the exiting heir is released from the debt (meaning he no longer has a claim to collect it) and the debt becomes the property of the other heirs, then the compromise is invalid (batil). Why? Because this amounts to transferring a debt that is not yet collected, and the exiting heir might be giving up his right to collect for an uncertain amount, which is not permissible. However, if they stipulate that the creditors (those who owe the debts) are discharged from the exiting heir's share (i.e., his claim on the debt is cancelled), and that the creditors will not be pursued for his share, then the compromise is valid. This is because the debt is effectively released, and the exiting heir is compensated with other assets. The key is whether the exiting heir is absolved of responsibility (which is valid) or the debt is transferred to others (which is invalid due to uncertainty).

مصالح (musalih): The heir who is making the compromise and exiting the estate.
إبراء (ibra'): Discharge of a debt; releasing the debtor from the obligation.

Core Idea
The khuntha's inheritance splits Hanafi imams: female-default vs half-male-half-female formula, yielding different ratios.

When a person dies leaving a son and a child of ambiguous gender (khuntha), the inheritance division depends on how we resolve the gender uncertainty. The Hanafi school presents three distinct positions: Abu Hanifa treats the khuntha as female, while the Sahibayn assign a fractional share based on the ambiguity, but they disagree on the exact calculation. In this lesson, you will trace the reasoning behind each position and learn which view is practically followed.

1

Identify the heirs: a son and a khuntha. The khuntha's gender is uncertain, which affects the share ratio (male:female is 2:1 in inheritance).
2

From the text, note that Abu Hanifa treats the khuntha as female unless proven otherwise. This gives a 2:1 ratio in favor of the son.
3

Inheritance of a Hermaphrodite (Khuntha): Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khuntha inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the inheritance of a hermaphrodite when a father dies leaving a son and a khuntha, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf between them.

Core Idea
In Hanafi inheritance, a hermaphrodite is presumed female unless proven male—this shifts the distribution ratio significantly.

When a father dies leaving a son and a hermaphrodite (khuntha), how is the estate divided? The Hanafi school has a rich internal dispute. Imam Abu Hanifa presumes the khuntha is female, giving the son two-thirds. But his two disciples disagree, leading to different computational methods. This lesson breaks down the three views and their practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling source
This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on khuntha inheritance. Note that the passage also begins with a separate ruling about buying a slave-girl for the khuntha from the treasury—that is not our focus here.
Focus on the inheritance dispute; the slave-girl ruling is a separate topic.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that the khuntha is female in inheritance unless proven otherwise (istishab). Therefore, with a son and a khuntha (as a daughter), the male receives twice the female share: the estate is divided into three shares, son gets 2, khuntha gets 1.

Further, the son, grandson, and brother are special asabah: they make their sisters asabah with them, giving the sister half the brother's share (2:1 ratio). Other asabah (e.g., uncles) do not share with sisters; only males inherit. Finally, if no blood asabah exists, the next in line is the manumitting master (a former owner who freed the deceased) and then his asabah; this is from the law of wala'.

Learning Objective

Surplus inheritance after fixed shares returns to the sharers except spouses when no residuary exists.

This lesson explains the principle of radd, a unique Hanafi rule that prevents surplus from going to the public treasury when there are eligible fixed-share heirs. Understanding radd is essential for accurate inheritance distribution.

Declares all disbelief as one millah and no cross-religious inheritance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that all non-Muslims inherit from each other as one millah, and that there is no inheritance between Muslims and non-Muslims.

Core Idea
All disbelief is one millah for inheritance; Muslims and non-Muslims never inherit from each other.

This passage from Al-Quduri lays down two foundational rules of Islamic inheritance law (faraid): first, that all forms of disbelief (kufr) are treated as one religious community for inheritance purposes, meaning different non-Muslim sects inherit from each other; second, that there is absolutely no inheritance between Muslims and non-Muslims in either direction. These rules are based on the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and are unanimously upheld in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the Arabic passage carefully. It contains two distinct rules. First, 'الكفر كله ملة واحدة يتوارث به أهله' (all disbelief is one millah, its people inherit by it). Second, 'ولا يرث المسلم من الكافر ولا الكافر من المسلم' (the Muslim does not inherit from the disbeliever, nor the disbeliever from the Muslim).
The first rule establishes mutual inheritance among all non-Muslims regardless of sect. The second rule prohibits cross-religious inheritance.
2
Understand the basis of the first rule
The Hanafi school considers all non-Muslims as one 'millah' for inheritance, meaning a Christian can inherit from a Jew, etc. This is based on the principle that all disbelief shares a common status regarding inheritance. The ruling is derived from the general implication of the hadith and the idea that inheritance follows the religion of the deceased.
Some other schools differentiate between People of the Book and polytheists, but Hanafis treat all alike.
3

Correcting the Base When Shares Do Not Divide Evenly
Rules for correcting the base in faraid calculations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of taṣḥīḥ al-mas’alah to adjust the base number when heirs' shares do not divide evenly.

Core Idea
When shares don't divide evenly, multiply the heir number by the base — or use the wafq if they share a common factor.

In inheritance calculations, the base number (aṣl al-mas’alah) often needs adjustment to give each heir whole shares. This passage from Al-Lubab presents the Hanafi method for correcting the base when shares from a group of heirs are not divisible by their count. The method uses multiplication, not addition, and considers whether the shares and headcount are coprime (tabāyun) or share a common factor (wafq).

Guided Path
1
Identify groups with indivisible shares
After assigning shares from the base, check each group of heirs (e.g., brothers, wives) to see if their total share is divisible by their number. If not, correction is needed.
2
Determine tabāyun or wafq
Compare the share amount with the number of heirs in that group. If they share a common divisor, find the wafq (the quotient of the number divided by the common divisor).
3
Apply the multiplication rule
If tabāyun (no common factor), multiply the full number of heirs by the original base. If wafq exists, multiply the wafq by the original base instead. This gives the corrected base.
For multiple groups, multiply sequentially: multiply one group's number (or wafq) by the next, etc., then by the base.
4
Handle special cases
If numbers are equal, one suffices. If one is a multiple of the other, use the larger. If there is wafq between two groups, multiply the wafq of one by the whole of the other.
These shortcuts avoid redundant multiplications.

Rules for Adjusting Inheritance Shares When They Do Not Divide Evenly
Explaining arithmetic correction methods in inheritance distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi arithmetic rules to correct an inheritance problem when the shares of a group of heirs do not divide evenly, including cases with common factors (muwafaqa).

Core Idea
When shares don't divide evenly, multiply the base by the number of heirs or its reduced part (wafq).

In inheritance distribution, after assigning fixed shares (e.g., 1/4, 1/8), the remaining shares often need to be split among multiple heirs. If the number of shares does not divide evenly by the number of heirs in a group, we must adjust the base problem (asl al-mas'alah) to ensure each heir receives a whole number. This lesson explains the simple arithmetic rules from Hanafi faraid, using examples to show when to multiply by the full number, by a reduced number (wafq), or by a combination when multiple groups are involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base of the problem (asl al-mas'alah)
Determine the base denominator from the fixed shares (e.g., for 1/4 and remainder, the base is 4). This is the starting denominator for the distribution.
The base is derived from the Quranic shares by taking the LCM of their denominators.
2
Calculate the remaining shares for the residuary heirs
Subtract the shares of fixed heirs from the base; the remainder goes to the residuary heirs (e.g., for woman 1/4, base 4, remainder 3 shares).
3
Check if the remaining shares divide evenly among the heirs
Divide the remaining shares by the number of heirs in that group. If it divides exactly (e.g., 3 shares for 3 heirs), no correction is needed. If not, proceed to the next step.
4
Determine the relationship between shares and number of heirs
If the shares and number of heirs share a common factor (muwafaqa), find the part (wafq) by dividing the number by the common factor. If they are mutually prime (tabayun), use the full number.
Common factor example: 3 shares and 6 heirs share factor 3; wafq = 2.
5
Multiply the base by the required number
If shares and heirs are mutually prime (tabayun), multiply the base by the number of heirs. If they share a common factor (muwafaqa), multiply the base by the wafq (reduced number). If there are multiple groups, multiply group sizes sequentially.
Example: base 4, 6 heirs with muwafaqa: multiply 4 by 2 (wafq) to get 8.

Commentary
This passage is a concluding practical note on the calculation of inheritance shares in cases of munasakha, which occur when a second heir dies before the distribution of the estate of the first deceased. The author provides a method for converting shares into a monetary unit known as 'dirham grains' (habbat al-dirham). In Hanafi faraid practice, a dirham is traditionally divided into 48 grains for computational ease. The method is: take the base number (asl al-mas'ala) after it has been corrected for the munasakha, and divide that number by 48. The resulting quotient is the value of one grain per unit share. Then, for each heir, you take that many grains from his or her share units. For example, if the corrected base is 48, dividing by 48 gives 1, meaning each share unit equals one grain. If the base is 24, dividing gives 0.5, so each share equals half a grain. This allows for precise monetary distribution. Key Terms
مناسخة (munāṣakhah): A mutation case in inheritance where one or more heirs die before the estate of the original deceased is distributed, requiring recalculation of shares.
حبات الدرهم (ḥabbāt al-dirham): Grains of the dirham; a unit of currency subdivision, where 1 dirham = 48 grains, used for fractional distribution of inheritance in monetary terms.
