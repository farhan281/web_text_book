The Fard and Sunan Acts of Wudu
Defines fard and sunan acts of wudu with Quran and hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fard and sunan acts of wudu according to the Hanafi school and identify the proof for wiping a quarter of the head.

Core Idea
Wiping a quarter of the head is fard in wudu — the Prophet's sunnah defines the minimal obligation.

This passage from the commentary on Al-Quduri opens the Book of Purification by defining the compulsory (fard) and recommended (sunan) acts of wudu. It grounds the fard acts in the Quranic verse (5:6) and then specifies the extent of the head wipe using a hadith. Understanding this distinction is fundamental to performing wudu correctly: omitting a fard invalidates wudu, while omitting a sunnah does not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fard acts from the Quranic verse
The verse commands washing the face, hands to elbows, wiping the head, and washing feet to ankles. The commentary clarifies that the elbows and ankles are included in the washing of the limbs.
The verse itself does not specify a quantity for the head wipe.
2
Note the specification for wiping the head
The author states that the fard in wiping the head is the amount of the forelock, i.e., a quarter of the head, citing the hadith where the Prophet wiped his forelock.
This is a Hanafi-specific ruling; other schools require wiping the entire head.
3
List the sunan acts mentioned
The passage lists: washing hands before inserting into vessel upon waking, saying Bismillah, siwak, rinsing mouth, sniffing water, wiping ears, combing through beard and fingers, and repeating washes up to three times.
These acts are recommended (sunnah muakkadah) but not obligatory.
4
Understand the consequence of omission
Omitting a fard act invalidates wudu, requiring a fresh wudu. Omitting a sunan act does not invalidate, but one loses the reward and completeness.

The Obligatory Measure of Wiping the Head
Ruling with proof on obligatory head-wipe measure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for the obligatory measure of wiping the head, identify the proof from the hadith of al-Mughirah, and explain the wajh al-dalalah linking the Prophet's action to the one-quarter requirement.

Core Idea
Wiping one-quarter of the head fulfills the fard; wiping the entire head is sunnah.

In wudu, wiping the head is a rukn, but how much must be wiped for the act to be valid? This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri gives the Hanafi position: the obligatory amount is the forelock, which is one-quarter of the head. The proof is a hadith where the Prophet ﷺ wiped only his forelock. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its evidence, and contrasts it with the view of Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on 'the obligatory in wiping the head' – stating that the obligatory amount is the forelock (one-quarter of the head). Note that wiping the head is itself a rukn of wudu; this lesson specifies the minimum amount that must be wiped.
The hukm is fard for that amount; wiping more is sunnah.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith from al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah describing the Prophet's wudu: he wiped his forelock (and his leather socks). The passage quotes key parts of the hadith.
The hadith is ahad (single-transmission), thus zanni al-thubut.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Hanafi inference: The Prophet ﷺ performed wudu and wiped only his forelock, not the entire head. Since he would not omit a fard, wiping only the forelock must be sufficient to fulfill the obligation. This sets the fard at that portion.
The dalala is zanni because the hadith could be interpreted differently (e.g., he wiped the forelock and then completed the rest, but the apparent action is only the forelock).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that wiping the entire head is obligatory. His proof is the Quranic verse 'wa imsahu bi ru'usikum' (wipe your heads) and a hadith where the Prophet wiped his entire head. The Hanafi response is that the verse is ambiguous and the hadith of al-Mughirah specifies the minimum.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in different usul principles regarding 'amm and bayan.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school relies on the hadith of al-Mughirah as a bayan (clarification) of the Quranic command. Since the Prophet's action is the best explanation, the fard is one-quarter. This is the mufta bihi position.
The tarjih is based on the Hanafi principle that actions of the Prophet (fi'l) can specify (takhsis) a general command.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the sunnah acts of wudu as per the Hanafi school and distinguish them from the obligatory acts.

Core Idea
Sunnah acts enhance wudu but do not invalidate; omitting them requires no compensation.

Wudu has obligatory (fard) and recommended (sunnah) components. This passage from al-Quduri enumerates the sunnah acts, which are emphasized in the Hanafi tradition. Understanding them helps you perfect your wudu and gain extra reward.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category: sunnah

2
List each sunnah act
Go through each: washing hands upon waking, saying Bismillah, using siwak, rinsing mouth (madmadah), sniffing water (istinshaq), wiping ears, doing khilal of beard and fingers, and washing each limb thrice.

3
Understand the ruling level
Sunnah means rewarded if done, not punished if omitted. It is preferred and loved by the Prophet (peace be upon him).
The Hanafi school considers all these as sunnah muakkadah (emphasized sunnah).
4
Note cross-madhhab differences
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) consider madmadah and istinshaq as wajib. Be aware that the status can vary.
This ikhtilaf stems from different interpretations of the same hadith evidence.

Recommended Acts in Wudu (Mustahabbat)
States the mustahabbat (recommended acts) of wudu

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four mustahabb acts of wudu in the Hanafi school and explain why they are recommended, not obligatory.

Core Idea
Intention, full head wipe, order, and starting with right are mustahabb — meritorious but not required for valid wudu.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between what is obligatory (fard or wajib) for wudu and what is recommended (mustahabb). The passage lists four acts that are highly recommended but do not affect validity: making the intention, wiping the entire head, following the sequence of the Quranic verse, and beginning with the right side. Understanding these enhances your practice without confusing them with obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the recommended acts
Read the passage carefully and list each mustahabb act: intention (niyyah), encompassing the whole head in masah (full wipe), ordering (tartib) as per the verse, and beginning with the right (yaman).
These are four distinct acts.
2
Contrast with the fard acts
Recall that the fard of wudu (from Quran and hadith) includes washing face, hands to elbows, wiping quarter of head, and washing feet. The mustahabb here go beyond the fard: e.g., full head wipe vs. quarter, intention vs. no intention required.
This contrast clarifies why they are not obligatory.
3
Examine the basis for each
Each mustahabb is rooted in the Prophet's practice (Sunnah). For example, tartib follows the Quranic order in verse 5:6; the right side is based on the Prophet's love for the right.
No specific dalil is cited here, but the practice is established.
4
Apply to a real wudu
Simulate performing wudu: form the intention in your heart, wipe the whole head, follow the verse order, and start with right hand and foot. Note that if any is missed, the wudu is still valid.
This solidifies the concept of mustahabb.

Nullifiers of Wudu (Nawāqiḍ al-Wuḍūʾ)
List of nullifiers (nawāqiḍ al-wuḍūʾ) with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the seven categories of nullifiers of wudu and identify the Hanafi conditions for each.

Core Idea
Anything from the two passages, flowing impurities, mouthful vomit, deep sleep, unconsciousness, and laughter in prayer break wudu.

Wudu is purity for prayer, but certain events invalidate it. This lesson enumerates the nullifiers as taught by Imam al-Quduri in the Hanafi school. Knowing these conditions ensures your worship remains valid.

Guided Path
1
List each nullifier
Read the passage and write down each item that nullifies wudu. There are seven main categories: exit from the two passages, blood/pus/sanies, vomit, sleep, fainting/insanity, and laughter in prayer.
Memorize the list in order.
2
Identify conditions for each
For each nullifier, note the added condition. Blood must flow beyond the wound site; vomit must fill the mouth; sleep must be deep (lying, reclining, or leaning unsupported); laughter must be loud and in a prayer with ruku and sujud.
These conditions are crucial for correct application.
3
Note intra-Hanafi and cross-madhhab differences
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that sleep only nullifies if deep; Abu Hanifa holds any sleep that relaxes the buttocks nullifies. The Shafi'is differ on sleep and laughter.
The text presents the relied-upon Hanafi position.
4
Apply to scenarios
Given a scenario, decide whether wudu is nullified by checking the relevant nullifier and its condition. For example, a small cut that drops blood but stays on the wound does not nullify.
Practice with multiple examples.
5
Memorize with conditions
Use the conditions as memory anchors. For instance, 'vomit only if mouthful' – remember أن يكون ملء الفم.
Repetition is key.

One vital question in taharah: when does mixing with a pure substance render water unusable for wudu? Building on the general rule that impure water cannot be used for purification, the Hanafi school makes a special exception for flowing water such as rivers and streams. This lesson explains the precise condition that permits wudu from such water after an impurity falls into it.

This contrast helps fix the unique Hanafi position on flowing water.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario in taharah: a large pond (ghadir) where impurity falls on one side. The Hanafi ruling clarifies that the water on the opposite side remains pure for wudu, provided the pond is so large that disturbing one side does not affect the other. The condition is not a fixed measurement but a pragmatic test of movement propagation.

If impurity falls into one side, it is permissible (جاز) to perform wudu from the other side. The water there remains pure (tahir).
The ruling is one of permission (mubah), not obligation.

The reason given is 'because the apparent [case] is that the impurity does not reach it' (لأن الظاهر أن النجاسة لا تصل إليه). This is based on reasonable presumption (ghalabat al-dhann).
This illa is a rational inference (dalil aqli) based on custom and distance.

If the pond were small such that moving one side moves the other, the ruling would be different: the entire water would become impure.
This contrast helps fix the condition in memory.

Commentary
This ruling permits tayammum for a healthy person residing in a city who has water, but fears missing the funeral prayer (janazah) if he performs wudu. The rationale is that these prayers are time-sensitive and communal; missing them has greater loss.

Tayammum is Not Allowed for Jumu'ah
Prohibiting tayammum for Friday prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using tayammum to attend Jumu'ah and explain the rationale behind the prohibition.

Core Idea
Tayammum for Jumu'ah is forbidden; wudu is obligatory even if Jumu'ah is missed.

In previous lessons, we saw that tayammum is permitted for funeral and Eid prayers if water is absent or harmful. However, for the Friday congregational prayer (Jumu'ah), the ruling is different. This lesson examines why the Hanafi school prohibits tayammum for Jumu'ah, even when a person fears missing the prayer entirely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the specific act being ruled on: performing tayammum to attend Jumu'ah. Then state the ruling: it is prohibited (haram) to do tayammum when water is available.
The act is tayammum for the sake of Jumu'ah; the ruling is prohibition.
2
Understand the prescribed alternative
The text commands performing wudu instead of tayammum. Recognize that wudu is obligatory (wajib) even if it causes one to miss Jumu'ah.
Wudu is not optional; it must be done.
3
Analyze the consequence of missing Jumu'ah
If Jumu'ah is missed after performing wudu, one must pray Zuhr (four rak'at) instead. Note that Zuhr is the substitute for Jumu'ah.
Jumu'ah has a substitute; if missed, the obligation is fulfilled by Zuhr.
4
Contrast with funeral and Eid prayers
Recall that for funeral and Eid prayers, tayammum is allowed if water is unavailable. Compare the rationale: those prayers have no substitute, so tayammum preserves the opportunity. Jumu'ah has a substitute (Zuhr), so the general obligation to use water takes precedence.
The presence of a substitute changes the ruling.

Commentary
This ruling contrasts with the previous allowance for funeral and Eid prayers. For Jumu'ah (Friday congregational prayer), tayammum is not allowed even if it means missing the prayer. Instead, he must perform wudu, even if that delays him. If he catches the Jumu'ah after wudu, he prays it; if he misses it, he prays Zuhr (four raka'at) instead. The reason is that Jumu'ah is a weekly obligation with a substitute (Zuhr), whereas funeral and Eid have no complete substitute if missed. The priority is to use water.

Key Terms
الجمعة (al-jumu'ah): Friday congregational prayer, a weekly obligation substituting for Zuhr.
