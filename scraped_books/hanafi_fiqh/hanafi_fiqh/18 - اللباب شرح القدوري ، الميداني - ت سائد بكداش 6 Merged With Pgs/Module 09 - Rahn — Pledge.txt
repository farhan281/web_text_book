Definition and Formation of Pledge (Rahn) in Hanafi Law
Defines the contract of pledge, formation, and liability upon loss.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of rahn, identify its pillars and conditions, and apply the liability rule for loss of the pledged item.

Core Idea
A rahn (pledge) is a security contract that is not binding until possession is delivered to the pledgee.

The Book of Pledge opens with the essential structure of the security contract. In this lesson, you will learn how a pledge is formed, what conditions must be met, and how liability is determined if the pledged item is lost. Understanding these fundamentals is crucial for any transaction involving collateral.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract's nature
Recognize that rahn is a security contract for a debt. It involves a pledgor (who owes) and a pledgee (who lends).
2
Understand formation
The contract is concluded by offer (ījāb) and acceptance (qabūl), but it is perfected only by possession (qabḍ). Until possession, the pledgor may revoke.
3
Learn possession requirements
The pledged item must be taken into the pledgee's secure, exclusive, and identifiable control (maḥjūz, mufargh, mumayyaz).
4
Apply liability rules
If the item perishes in the pledgee's hands, he is liable for the lesser of its value or the debt. Excess value is a trust; shortfall is still due.
Three scenarios: equal value → debt discharged; greater value → excess trust; lesser value → debt reduced by value.
5
Note the prohibitions
Undivided shares (mushā‘), fruit without trees, and crops without land cannot be pledged because they cannot be delivered exclusively.
These items lack the condition of distinct possession.

When the pledgee takes possession of the pledge in a protected, emptied, and distinct state: the contract becomes complete regarding it. As long as he does not take possession, the pledgor has the option: if he wishes he may hand it over to him, and if he wishes he may retract from the pledge. Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Pledge (al-Rahn). It begins by defining the contract: a pledge is concluded by offer (ījāb) and acceptance (qabūl), but it becomes fully effective only with possession (qabḍ). The pledgee must take possession of the pledged item in a state where it is 'mahūzan mufarghan mumayyazan' – secured, vacated, and separately identifiable. Until possession occurs, the pledgor may choose to deliver or withdraw. Once delivered and possessed, the pledged item enters the liability (ḍamān) of the pledgee – meaning he is responsible for it, but as a trustee (amīn) not as a guarantor. The text then states a condition: a pledge is valid only for a secured debt (i.e., the debt must be one that is guaranteed by the pledge). The liability of the pledgee in case of loss is limited to the lesser of the item's value or the debt. Three scenarios: if the value equals the debt, the pledgee is considered to have already collected his debt (so the debt is discharged); if the value exceeds the debt, the excess remains in the pledgee's hands as a trust (amāna); if the value is less, the debt is reduced by the value, and the pledgee may claim the remainder. Finally, three prohibitions: (1) a pledge cannot be undivided (mushā') because it cannot be delivered exclusively; (2) fruit on trees cannot be pledged without the trees, as they are not independently deliverable; (3) crops in the ground cannot be pledged without the land.

Key Terms
رهن (rahn): A contract whereby a pledgor gives an asset to a pledgee as security for a debt, with the condition that the asset is forfeited if the debt is not paid.
قبض (qabḍ): Physical or constructive possession required to perfect the pledge.
مشاع (mushāʿ): An undivided share in jointly owned property.

Contract Formation and Possession of Pledge
Ruling statement on the formation and completion of a pledge contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the formation and completion of a pledge contract and identify the three conditions for valid possession.

Core Idea
A pledge contract is formed by offer and acceptance, but completed only by taking possession with specific conditions.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational passage from al-Quduri on the contract of pledge (rahn). The text clearly distinguishes between the formation of the contract (in'aqud) through offer and acceptance, and its completion (tamamm) through actual possession. This distinction carries important legal consequences, including the pledgor's right to withdraw before possession and the transfer of guarantee to the pledgee after possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two stages: formation and completion
The passage states that rahn is concluded (yan'aqid) by offer and acceptance, and completed (yatimm) by possession. These are two distinct stages with different legal effects.
The contract is valid (sahih) at the first stage but not fully binding.
2
Recognise the pillars and condition
Offer and acceptance are the pillars (rukn) of the contract; possession is a condition (shart) for its completion, not a pillar.
This is unique to Hanafi fiqh; other schools may treat possession as a pillar.
3
List the three conditions for valid possession
The pledge must be: (1) mahuzan (safeguarded), (2) mufarraghan (vacated), (3) mumayyazan (distinguished). Each term has specific fiqh implications.
If any condition is missing, possession is not valid and the contract remains incomplete.
4
Understand the pledgor's option before possession
As long as possession has not taken place, the pledgor can either deliver the pledge or retract the contract. This shows the contract is not fully binding until possession.
This is a key practical consequence of the ruling.
5
Recognise the effect of possession: entry into guarantee
After valid possession, the pledge enters the pledgee's daman (guarantee), meaning the pledgee becomes responsible for its safekeeping.
The pledgee is not liable for loss due to force majeure, but is liable for negligence.

Valid Pledge: Condition of Guaranteed Debt and Guarantee by Lesser
States ruling that pledge is only valid for guaranteed debt and is guaranteed by lesser of value and debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for a valid pledge according to the Hanafi school and apply the rule of guarantee by the lesser of value and debt to scenarios of pledge loss.

Core Idea
A pledge is valid only for guaranteed debts and guarantees only the lesser of its value and the debt.

This lesson introduces two fundamental rulings in Hanafi muamalat regarding pledges (rahn). First, a pledge is only valid if the underlying debt is guaranteed (dain mamdun) — a condition that distinguishes binding obligations from trusts. Second, the pledge itself is a guarantee for the lesser of its market value and the debt amount, ensuring fairness if the pledge is lost. Understanding these principles is essential for correctly structuring secured transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Condition
Recognize that the passage addresses the pledge contract (rahn). The first sentence states that the pledge is not valid except for a guaranteed debt. Determine the nature of the debt: it must be a binding obligation like a loan or sale price, not a trust like a deposit or borrowed item.
Guaranteed debt = debt that must be repaid, regardless of the debtor's ability.
2
Understand the Guarantee Principle
The pledge is a guarantee (daman) for the lesser of two amounts: the value of the pledged item and the debt. This means the pledge does not automatically cover the entire debt; it secures only the smaller figure.
This prevents the pledgee from gaining more than the debt if the item is worth more.
3
Analyze the Three Loss Scenarios
The passage provides three cases when the pledge perishes in the pledgee's hands: (1) value equals debt — debt considered paid; (2) value exceeds debt — excess is a trust (amanah) in pledgee's hands; (3) value is less than debt — debt reduced by the value, pledgee claims the remainder.
In all cases, the pledgee does not benefit beyond the debt amount.
4
Apply to Hypotheticals
Practice with examples: If debt is $100 and pledge value is $80, loss reduces debt to $20 owed. If value is $150, loss fully discharges debt and pledgee must return $50 as trust.
The rule ensures the pledgee is compensated only up to the debt.
5
Note the Hanafi Specificity
This ruling is uniquely Hanafi. Other schools may allow pledges for non-guaranteed debts. Recognize that the condition 'guaranteed debt' is essential for validity in the Hanafi school.
Memorize: 'لا يصح الرهن إلا بدين مضمون' as a core principle.

Commentary
This passage provides two important rulings: first, a pledge is only valid when given for a debt that is guaranteed (dain maddun), meaning a debt that is binding and must be repaid. Second, the pledge itself is considered a guarantee (daman) for the lesser of its own market value and the amount of the debt. If the pledge perishes while in the pledgee's custody, the legal consequence depends on the relationship between value and debt. If equal, the pledgee is treated as having been paid. If value exceeds debt, the excess remains a trust (amanah) with the pledgee and must be returned. If value is less, the debt is reduced by that amount and the pledgee can still claim the balance. This ensures fairness and prevents unjust enrichment.

Prohibited Pledges: Undivided Shares and Inseparable Produce
States three specific prohibitions in the contract of pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the three prohibited types of pledge according to the Hanafi school and explain the legal basis for each prohibition.

Core Idea
A pledge must be a distinct, deliverable asset; undivided shares and unattached produce are invalid collateral.

This short passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri lays down three clear prohibitions in the contract of rahn (pledge). These restrictions ensure that the pledged asset is fully owned, physically separable, and capable of being delivered to the pledgee. Without these conditions, the contract risks ambiguity and potential disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and general rule
Recall that a pledge (rahn) requires the mortgagor to hand over a specific asset as security. The asset must be owned, distinct, and deliverable.
2
List the three prohibited items
From the text: 1) al-musha' (an undivided share), 2) fruit still on trees without the trees, 3) crops in the ground without the land.
Memorize these three as a set.
3
Explain the rationale for each prohibition
Undivided share: possession cannot be fully transferred to the pledgee. Fruit/crops without the underlying asset: they are not yet fully owned separately and may perish before delivery.
The core reason is the inability to deliver exclusive possession.
4
Note the later Hanafi relaxation
Some later Hanafi scholars allow pledging musha' with the consent of the co-owner or by appointing a proxy for possession. This is an exception; the strict view is the text's position.
The text follows the authoritative view of the early school.
5
Apply the rule to modern scenarios
For example, pledging shares in a joint-stock company is analogous to musha' because the share is an undivided interest in the company's assets.
Modern jurists often permit it due to custom and ease of transfer, but according to the text it would be invalid.

Commentary
This short passage lists three prohibitions in the contract of pledge: (1) Pledging a musha' (undivided share) in a property is not allowed because a pledge requires exclusive possession and clear ownership, which is not possible with a shared undivided interest. (2) Pledging fruit that is still on the trees, without also pledging the trees themselves, is invalid because the fruit is not yet fully owned separately and may not be delivered. (3) Similarly, pledging standing crops without the land is invalid. In all cases, the pledge must be a distinct, deliverable asset. These restrictions ensure that the pledgee can take proper possession and that the collateral is not subject to ambiguities that could lead to disputes.

Valid and Invalid Subjects of Pledge (Rahn)
Ruling on subjects of pledge: invalid for trusts, valid for guaranteed debts, with perishing rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish which debts or properties can be validly secured by a pledge (rahn) under Hanafi law by applying the criterion of guarantee, and state the special rule when the pledge perishes in the contract session.

Core Idea
Pledge secures only guaranteed debts; trusts are not pledgeable.

This lesson clarifies a key rule in the law of pledge (rahn): not every property or debt can be used as collateral. The Hanafi school distinguishes between trusts (amanat), where the holder is not liable for loss, and guaranteed debts (dayn), where the debtor is liable. Only the latter can be secured by a pledge. Additionally, there is a special facilitation if the pledged item perishes immediately after the contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the opening statement: pledges are invalid for trusts (amanat) but valid for certain debts. Trusts include deposits, mudaraba capital, and partnership property. Valid subjects include salam capital, currency exchange price, and the salam commodity.
The key distinction is whether the right is a guaranteed debt or a trust.
2
Understand the criterion: guarantee vs. trust
A trust is property held without liability for loss unless negligence occurs. A guaranteed debt is a fixed obligation that must be paid. The pledge is valid only for the latter.
Hanafi principle: rahn secures only a debt (dayn) that is established and guaranteed.
3
Apply the perishing rule
Study the final sentence: if the pledged item perishes in the contract session (majlis), the underlying contracts (sarf, salam) are completed, and the pledgee is deemed to have received his right. This facilitates transactions.
This rule applies only to contracts that are binding upon the session (lazim bi'l-majlis).
4
Memorise the examples
Commit to memory: invalid for deposits (wadi'ah), mudarabah, and partnership property. Valid for salam capital, sarf price, and the goods to be delivered (muslam fihi).
These are common cases in Hanafi fiqh.

For these, the debt is guaranteed. Placing a Pledge with a Trustee (Adl)
States ruling on placed pledge with trustee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on placing a pledge with a trusted third party and identify the rights and obligations of the pledgor, pledgee, and trustee.

Core Idea
Mutual agreement to appoint a trustee binds both parties; loss remains on the pledgee.

The pledgor and pledgee may mutually agree to deposit the pledged item with a neutral, trustworthy person called an adl. This arrangement protects the pledgor from exclusive possession by the pledgee while preserving the pledgee's security interest. The passage clarifies the legal effects, especially regarding who bears the risk of loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is placing the pledge with an adl by mutual agreement. The ruling is permissible (ja'iz).
2
Determine the conditions for validity
The condition is that both parties (pledgor and pledgee) agree. Unilateral action is not sufficient.
3
Analyze the rights and obligations
Once the pledge is with the adl, neither party has the right to take it back unilaterally. Only mutual agreement can remove it.
4
Understand the guarantee (daman) consequence
If the pledge perishes in the adl's custody, it is treated as if it perished under the pledgee's guarantee; the loss is borne by the pledgee.
This is because the adl's possession is considered like the pledgee's possession for liability.

This arrangement protects both parties: the pledgor's asset is not exclusively held by the pledgee, and the pledgee's security interest is preserved through the trustee.

Pledge of Fungible Goods: Currency and Measured/Weighed Items

The pawnee may take the pawn back at any time, and once he does, his liability resumes.

Returning the Pledge to the Pledgor and Death of Pledgor
Rulings on returning the pledge to the pledgor and executor's duty on death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the effect on liability when the pledgee returns the pledge to the pledgor, and explain the procedure for selling the pledge upon the pledgor's death.

Core Idea
If the pledgee lends the pledge back, liability shifts to the pledgor; upon the pledgor's death, the executor must sell the pledge to pay the debt.

This lesson covers two important scenarios: first, the pledgee voluntarily returning possession of the pledge to the pledgor (as a loan), which removes the pledgee's liability; second, what happens when the pledgor dies. The Hanafi school provides a clear mechanism for the executor or judge to sell the pledge and settle the debt. Understanding these rules is essential for proper execution of pledge contracts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the effect of returning the pledge
If the pledgee lends the pledge back to the pledgor, and the pledgor takes possession, the pledge leaves the liability of the pledgee.
The pledgee is no longer responsible for its safekeeping.
2
Note the consequences of loss in pledgor's hands
If the pledge perishes while with the pledgor, it perishes without liability (for the pledgee). The debt remains due.
The pledgor bears the loss, but the debt is not reduced.
3
Recognize the pledgee's right to reclaim
The pledgee may take the pledge back at any time; upon repossession, liability returns to him.
This restores the original state of security.
4
Apply the ruling on death of pledgor
If the pledgor dies, his executor must sell the pledge and pay off the debt from its proceeds.
If there is no executor, the judge appoints one to carry out the sale.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Pledge (Kitab al-Rahn) presents a series of rulings concerning the pledge contract. It begins by stating that the pledgee recovers his debt (from the pledge). Then it discusses a conditional sale: if someone sells a slave on the condition that the buyer pledges something specific for the price, and the buyer refuses to hand over the pledge, the buyer is not compelled to do so, but the seller has the option to either waive the pledge requirement or rescind the sale, unless the buyer pays the price immediately or pays the value of the pledge, which then becomes the pledge. Next, it explains that the pledgee may keep the pledge himself, his wife, his child, and his servant in his household. If he entrusts it to someone outside his household or deposits it with someone else, he becomes liable for any loss. If the pledgee misuses the pledge, he is liable as a usurper (ghasib) for its full value. If the pledgee lends the pledge back to the pledgor, and the pledgor takes possession, the pledge leaves the liability of the pledgee; if it perishes in the pledgor's hand, it perishes without any compensation to the pledgee (i.e., the debt remains). The pledgee may retrieve the pledge, and liability returns to him upon taking possession. Finally, if the pledgor dies, his executor must sell the pledge and pay off the debt; if there is no executor, the judge appoints one to sell it. These rulings follow the Hanafi school as per al-Quduri's Mukhtasar.
