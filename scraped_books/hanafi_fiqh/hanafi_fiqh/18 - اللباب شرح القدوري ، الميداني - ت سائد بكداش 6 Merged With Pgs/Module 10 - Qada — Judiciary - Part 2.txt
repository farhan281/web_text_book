Specific Oath Wordings for Sale and Usurpation Claims
Rulings on specific oath wording for sale and usurpation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the correct judicial oath wording in sale and usurpation claims as per Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
In sale disputes, the oath must negate the contract; in usurpation, it must deny the right to restitution, not the act.

The precise wording of a judicial oath must address the legal nature of the claim. For a sale, the claim is that a valid contract exists; the defendant swears there is no valid sale. For usurpation, the claim is the right to return; the defendant swears the claimant does not deserve return. This precision ensures the oath covers the legal issue at hand.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim type
Determine whether the dispute is over a sale (e.g., claimant says he bought an item) or usurpation (claimant says his property was taken wrongfully).
The text gives two examples: purchase of a slave and usurpation.
2
Determine the legal essence of the claim
For sale, the essence is the existence of a valid contract. For usurpation, the essence is the right to restitution (the duty to return).
The claimant asserts a contract or a right; the denial must negate that legal conclusion, not just the physical act.
3
Formulate the denial accordingly
In sale, the defendant swears: 'There is no valid sale between us' (negation of contract). In usurpation: 'You do not deserve its return from me' (negation of right).
The text contrasts with simple denials like 'I did not sell' or 'I did not usurp'.
4
Apply to other similar claims
For any claim that involves a contract or obligation, the oath should deny the existence of the contract or the right, not the underlying action. This principle can be extended by analogy.
For example, a claim of loan: the defendant should swear 'There is no debt due to you from me' rather than 'I did not borrow'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Theft evidence outweighs deposit evidence; the claim continues despite defendant's proof of trust.

When two parties each bring evidence supporting contradictory claims—one alleging theft, the other alleging deposit from a third party—the Hanafi school holds that the court does not summarily dismiss the case. This lesson examines the reasoning behind that ruling and its implications for judicial procedure.

Guided Path

Identify the two claims
The claimant alleges theft (suriqa minni) and the possessor alleges deposit (awdaʿanīhi fulān). Note that the claims are mutually exclusive in terms of ownership: theft implies the claimant owns it, deposit does not confer ownership on the possessor.
2
Recognize the evidence presented
Both parties bring evidence (bayyinah). The claimant's evidence supports his ownership through theft; the defendant's evidence only shows that a third party claimed deposit – it does not prove the defendant owns the property.
3
State the legal outcome
The text says: 'lam tandafiʿ al-khuṣūmah' – the lawsuit is not dismissed. The court must continue to hear the case.
4
Understand the underlying principle
Evidence of ownership (through theft claim) takes precedence over evidence of mere possession by trust. The defendant's deposit evidence does not negate the claimant's prima facie ownership claim.
This is based on the Hanafi principle that possession alone does not prove ownership; the defendant's evidence only shows how he obtained possession, not that he owns it.
5
Apply to similar scenarios
Consider: if the defendant claimed the property was a gift from the claimant, the ruling might differ. The deposit claim does not dispute the claimant's ownership, only the manner of possession.
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is mufta bihi.

When both parties derive their rights from different third persons, there is no direct legal conflict between them. Each claim is independent.

The defendant’s claim of deposit (amanah) is stronger than a bare claim of purchase, and the claimant’s case requires proving delivery to himself, which is not claimed. Thus the lawsuit falls.
