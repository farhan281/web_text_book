The Inviolable Conditions of Prayer: Purity, Awrah, Intention, and Qibla
Defines the preconditions for prayer: purity, covering awrah, intention, and facing qibla

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five conditions (shurut) of prayer according to the Hanafi school and explain the ruling on covering the awrah for a man, a free woman, and a slave woman.

Core Idea
Purity, covering awrah, intention, and facing Qiblah are inviolable conditions; missing any knowingly invalidates the prayer.

The early Hanafi masters listed the conditions that must precede every prayer. These conditions are not merely recommended; their absence, when avoidable, renders the prayer invalid. This passage from Al-Quduri introduces them concisely, with a detailed breakdown of the awrah rulings. Understanding these conditions is the first step in building a sound prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions listed
Read the opening lines and list the five conditions: purity from hadath and najasah, covering awrah, intention, and facing qiblah.
2
Analyze the awrah definitions
Note that the awrah differs for a man, a free woman, and a slave woman. Memorize each definition.
Hanafi view: free woman's entire body except face and hands is awrah.
3
Understand the role of intention
The intention must be made without a gap between it and the opening takbir. Any action separating the two invalidates the intention.
4
Recognize the qiblah requirement
Facing the qiblah is obligatory; the exception for fear comes later.
5
Apply the conditions
Given a scenario, determine which condition is missing and whether the prayer is valid.

Chapter of the conditions of prayer that precede it. It is obligatory upon the one praying to precede purity from hadath (minor and major impurities) and from najasat (filth) as we have explained. And to cover his/her awrah. And the awrah of a man is what is below the navel to the knee, and the knee is part of the awrah. And the body of a free woman is all awrah except her face and hands. And whatever is awrah of a man is awrah of a slave woman, and her belly and back are awrah, and besides that of her body is not awrah. And whoever does not find something to remove the impurity prays with it and does not repeat the prayer. And whoever does not find a garment prays naked sitting, making gestures for bowing and prostration. If he prays standing, it suffices, but the first is better. And he must intend for the prayer he enters with an intention that is not separated from the takbir al-tahrimah by an action. And he must face the qiblah, unless he is fearful: then he prays towards whichever direction he can.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri, enumerating the shurut (conditions) of prayer that must be present before the prayer begins. The author first states that it is wajib (obligatory) to ensure purity from both hadath (ritual impurity) and najasat (physical filth) according to what was previously explained. Then covering the awrah is wajib. The definition of awrah is given: for a man, from below the navel to the knees inclusive; for a free woman, her entire body except face and hands; for a slave woman, the same as a man plus her belly and back are awrah, while the rest of her body (like arms, legs) is not awrah. This reflects the Hanafi position that a slave woman has a lesser awrah than a free woman. Next, concessions: if one cannot remove impurity, prayer is still valid with that impurity and does not need to be repeated. If one has no clothing, one prays sitting and gesturing; standing is permissible but sitting is better to avoid exposing further. Intention must be made right before the takbir with no action separating them. Facing the qiblah is obligatory except for one in fear (e.g., danger, enemy) who prays as best he can. The passage is dense and each item requires parsing: the conditions are not all equal in obligation; some are shurut (conditions of validity), others are wujub (obligatory but not conditions). For example, covering awrah is a condition (shart) for validity, while facing qibla is a condition unless excused. Purity from hadath and najasat is a condition for validity. The intention is a condition for the prayer to be valid.

Key Terms
الْعَوْرَة (al-‘awrah): The parts of the body that must be covered during prayer and in front of non-mahram.
النِّيَّة (al-niyyah): The intention in the heart to perform a specific prayer, which must be present at the time of takbir without a gap.
الْقِبْلَة (al-qiblah): The direction of the Kaaba in Mecca, which must be faced during prayer.
الطَّهَارَة (al-ṭahārah): Ritual purity, including wudu/ghusl for hadath and removal of najasah.

Concessions in Prayer: When Conditions Are Excused
Details concessions for impurity, lack of clothing, and fear when facing qiblah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three concessions (impurity, lack of clothing, fear) that excuse the normal conditions of prayer and apply them to scenarios.

Core Idea
When unable to remove impurity, find clothing, or face the Qiblah due to fear, the prayer remains valid, but with modifications.

The lawgiver grants mercy when fulfilling a condition becomes impossible. This passage from Al-Quduri outlines three key concessions: praying with impurity if removal is impossible, praying naked if no clothing is available, and praying in any direction when in fear. Understanding these concessions prevents unnecessary hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first concession: impurity
The text says: 'whoever does not find something to remove the impurity prays with it and does not repeat the prayer.' This means if you cannot remove najasah (e.g., no water, no cloth to wipe, and the impurity is on the body/clothes), the prayer is valid and does not need repetition even if later able to remove it.
This applies to najasah; for hadath, there is no such concession except tayammum if water unavailable.
2
Identify the second concession: lack of clothing
If a person has no garment at all, they should pray sitting and make gestures (ima) for ruku' and sujud. Standing is allowed but less preferable because it exposes more of the awrah.
The default is sitting to minimize exposure; standing is permissible but makruh.
3
Identify the third concession: fear of enemy
If one is fearful (e.g., danger from enemy or predator), facing the qiblah is waived; one prays in any direction possible. This is a mercy from the lawgiver.
This applies to obligatory prayer as well.
4
Apply the concessions
Given a scenario, determine which concession applies and whether the prayer is valid.

Deathbed and Immediate Post-Mortem Actions
States rulings for deathbed and immediate post-mortem actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah actions for a dying person and immediately after death according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A dying Muslim faces qibla and hears shahada; after death, jaw is tied and eyes closed.

This lesson covers the initial steps in dealing with a Muslim at the point of death and after death. These actions are sunnah, showing respect and following the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The text from al-Quduri provides the foundational rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the position for the dying
The text states the dying person is turned to face the qibla on the right side. This is a sunnah act.
Right side facing qibla is preferred; if difficult, supine with feet towards qibla is also acceptable.
2
Recall the prompting
The dying person is prompted (talqin) with the shahada: 'La ilaha illallah' to help them remember Allah at the final moment.
Do not repeat excessively; just remind gently so their last words are the shahada.
3
Actions after death: tie jaw and close eyes
After death, the jaw is tied to prevent it from falling open, and the eyes are gently closed. These are sunnah acts of respect.
The jaw can be tied with a cloth; eyes are closed with light pressure.

Ghusl al-Mayyit (Washing the Deceased)
States rulings for washing and final preparations of the deceased

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Hanafi method of washing the deceased, including preparation, the three washes, drying, and application of hanut and camphor.

Core Idea
The deceased is washed with sidr water three times, dried, and hanut and camphor are applied to specific parts.

This lesson details the procedure for washing the deceased (ghusl al-mayyit) according to the Hanafi school as given in al-Quduri's matn. The process involves preparation, three washes using sidr water, gentle abdominal massage, drying, and application of perfumes. These steps are sunnah and ensure the deceased is purified with respect.

Guided Path
1
Prepare the body for washing
Place the body on a bed, cover the private parts with a cloth, remove clothes, and perform a wudu-like washing without rinsing the mouth and nose.
Covering the private parts is mandatory (fard) for modesty.
2
First wash (left side)
Lay the body on the left side and wash with water and sidr until water reaches the part beneath.
Sidr (lotus leaves) is recommended; if unavailable, plain water suffices.
3
Second wash (right side)
Lay the body on the right side and wash similarly with water and sidr.
Ensure all parts are washed; the head and beard are washed with marshmallow (khatmi).
4
Sit the body up and wipe the belly
Sit the body up, lean it gently, and wipe the belly softly. If any discharge occurs, wash it without repeating the full ghusl.
This is to expel any remaining impurities; if nothing comes out, proceed to drying.
5
Dry and apply hanut and camphor
Dry the body with a cloth, apply hanut (perfume) to the head and beard, and apply camphor to the prostration points (forehead, nose, hands, knees, feet).
Hanut typically contains camphor and other scents; camphor is placed on the seven points that touch the ground in sujud.

Funeral Rites for Neonates: Live-Born vs. Stillborn
Ruling statement on neonate funeral rites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for an infant who cries at birth versus one who does not, and apply the criteria to determine funeral rites.

Core Idea
Crying at birth determines full funeral rites; stillborn is wrapped and buried without prayer.

This lesson presents a clear distinction from the matn: the presence of a cry (istihlal) at birth triggers the full funeral rites – naming, washing, shrouding, and prayer. Without it, the infant is treated as stillborn, receiving only wrapping and burial. This ruling is foundational for taharah of neonates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The text presents two cases: the infant who cries after birth and the one who does not. Your task is to distinguish them.
2
Note the key indicator
The decisive factor is 'istihlal' (crying out). This is the Hanafi sign of life for funeral rites.
Other schools may use movement or heartbeat.
