Two Types of Agency Contracts in Hanafi Fiqh
Classifies agency contracts by rights and obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of agency contracts in Hanafi fiqh based on attribution and identify the rights and obligations for each.

Core Idea
Contracts in agency: rights attach to the named party — agent in property transactions, principal in personal status.

Agency contracts vary in who bears the rights and obligations. The Hanafi school classifies agency based on whom the agent attributes the contract to. This lesson explains the two types and their practical consequences.

Guided Path

Identify the two categories

For the first type: sale, purchase, lease. For the second: marriage, khul', settlement for intentional homicide. Note these examples.
These are key illustrations of the rule.

In the first type, the agent bears all contractual rights and duties (e.g., delivery, payment, litigation). In the second, the principal bears them.
This determines who must act or be sued.

Purchase Agency: Specifications, Defects, and Special Contracts
Details requirements for valid purchase agency and handling defects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the requirements for a valid purchase agency, explain the agent's right to return defective goods, and identify when a sarf or salam agency contract becomes void.

Core Idea
A purchase agent may return a defective item only while in his possession; general agency removes the need to specify type and price.

In this passage from Al-Lubab, we examine the rules governing purchase agency. The principal must specify the item adequately unless he grants a general agency. The agent has a limited right to return defective goods. Moreover, agency for currency exchange (sarf) and forward sale (salam) is valid but void if the agent leaves the session before taking possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the specification requirement
For a purchase agency to be valid, the principal must name the item's type and description, or type and price amount. However, if the principal says 'Buy for me what you see' (general agency), no further specification is needed.
Without specification and without general agency, the agency is invalid (fasid).
2
Understand the agent's right to return defects
If the agent buys an item and later discovers a defect, he may return it to the seller as long as the item is still in his possession. Once he delivers it to the principal, he can only return it with the principal's permission.
This protects the principal's right to accept or reject the defective goods.
3
Analyze agency for sarf and salam
Appointing an agent for a currency exchange (sarf) or forward sale (salam) is permissible. However, if the agent separates from the other party before taking possession (qabd), the contract becomes void. The principal's own separation from the session does not affect the contract.
This rule emphasizes the importance of hand-to-hand possession in sarf and prompt payment in salam.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Agent's Liability for Withheld Item
Ruling on agent's right to withhold and dispute on liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding an agent's liability for a withheld item and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
An agent's liability for a withheld item differs: pledge vs sold item analogy determines who bears the loss.

This passage from al-Lubab al-Quduri explores a specific dispute between the two leading disciples of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The question is: when an agent who has paid the purchase price withholds the item to secure repayment, and the item perishes, what is the nature of his liability? The answer determines whether the agent must compensate the principal or not. Let us unpack the ruling and the two analogies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agent's rights
The agent who pays his own money can reclaim from the principal and may withhold the item until paid. If item perishes before withholding, it's principal's loss; the agent's claim remains.
This is the agreed baseline.
2
Locate the dispute
If the agent withholds the item and it perishes, Abu Yusuf says it is guaranteed as a pledge (daman al-rahn); Muhammad says it is guaranteed as a sold item (daman al-mabee').
Two different analogies applied by the Sahibayn.
3
Understand the analogies
Abu Yusuf: agent is like a pledgee – if the pledge perishes without excuse, the pledgee is liable (compensates). Muhammad: agent is like a buyer who has taken possession – if the item perishes, the buyer still owes the price, but here the agent has already paid, so his liability is limited.
The practical effect is the opposite: Abu Yusuf makes agent liable; Muhammad makes principal bear the loss.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Yusuf, the agent must compensate the principal for the lost item. Under Muhammad, the agent does not compensate; his right to reclaim the price remains.
This difference has real financial implications.
5
Note the mufta bibi
The passage does not specify a preferred view; later Hanafi authorities may prefer one based on context. Both are from Zahir al-Riwayah.

A gift to a non-relative can be revoked, but four events block it; gifts to close relatives and spouses are always binding.

The Hanafi school grants the donor an exceptional right to revoke a gift even after delivery. However, this right is not absolute. This lesson from al-Quduri outlines the general rule and its key exceptions, equipping you with the precise conditions under which a gift becomes final.

Revocation is prevented if any of these four events occur: (1) the donee compensates the donor, (2) an inseparable increase (e.g., growth, built-in addition) occurs in the gifted item, (3) either party dies, (4) the gift leaves the donee's ownership (e.g., sold).

For these recipients, revocation is prohibited from the outset, regardless of other factors.

Given a concrete case, first determine if the recipient is ajnabi, mahram, or spouse. If ajnabi, check for any of the four exceptions. If an exception applies, or if the recipient is mahram/spouse, the gift is final.
This step-by-step approach ensures correct application.

Ruling on owner's option after usurped item reappears

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The basis of compensation determines whether the owner retains a choice after the item reappears.

A key question in ghasp (usurpation) is what happens when the usurper pays the owner compensation for a missing item, and then the item reappears. Does the owner get to choose between the compensation and the item? The answer depends on how the compensation amount was established. This lesson breaks down the two scenarios from the classical text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial transfer of ownership

2

The text lists three bases that give the owner no subsequent option: (a) compensation set by the owner's own statement, (b) by clear proof (baiyinah), or (c) by the usurper's refusal to swear (nukul). In these cases, the compensation is considered final.

The option arises because the usurper's own valuation might have been low; thus fairness allows the owner to revert.

Consider the factual condition: item reappears with higher value
The passage assumes the item's value at reappearance is higher than the compensation. This triggers the option in the second scenario. If the value is equal or lower, the owner might not exercise the option.
The text explicitly mentions 'its value is more than what he guaranteed'.

Offspring and Produce of Usurped Property as a Trust
Ruling on status of offspring and produce from usurped property as trust.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that offspring and produce of usurped property are a trust, identify the conditions that trigger the usurper's liability, and apply the rule to specific scenarios.

Core Idea
Offspring of usurped property is a trust; the usurper is only liable if he transgresses or refuses to return on demand.

This lesson examines a nuanced fiqh ruling: when a usurper takes property, any offspring or produce generated during his possession are considered a trust (amānah) in his hands. The Hanafi school holds that he is not automatically liable for their loss; liability only arises through transgression (ta'addī) or refusal to return after the owner's demand. Additionally, if a slave-girl gives birth, the decrease in her value is compensable, often by the value of the child. Understanding this distinction clarifies the boundaries of the usurper's responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the items covered by the ruling
The passage explicitly mentions offspring of a usurped female animal, produce (e.g., milk, wool) from such an animal, and fruit from a usurped garden. Note that these are separable benefits generated during usurpation.
The benefits are considered separate from the original property.
2
State the default ruling
These benefits are classified as amānah (a trust) in the usurper's hands. Therefore, if they perish or are lost without any fault or transgression by the usurper, he is not liable for compensation.
A trust reduces liability – the custodian is not an absolute guarantor.
3
Identify the exceptions that impose liability
The usurper becomes liable if (1) he acts transgressively (ta'addā) with the benefits – e.g., uses them improperly or destroys them, or (2) the owner demands their return and the usurper refuses (imtana'a). In those cases, the trust status is lost and the usurper must compensate.
Ta'addī can include misuse, delay after demand, or preventing access.
4
Apply the rule to the slave-girl scenario
When a usurped slave-girl gives birth, her value decreases due to childbirth. This decrease is placed on the usurper's liability (ḍamān). However, if the value of the newborn child (or its eventual value) is sufficient to cover that decrease, the child is used as compensation and the usurper's liability for the decrease is discharged.
This shows that the child is not automatically ownerless; it can offset the loss.
5
Understand the practical consequence
The Hanafi position protects the usurper from automatic liability for natural events, while holding him responsible for his actions (transgression) or refusal to restore the owner's property. This balances ownership rights with practical realities.
This ruling is a mercy for the usurper in case of involuntary loss, but still upholds the owner's rights.
