Ruling on Bankruptcy Declaration and Exception
States the effect of bankruptcy declaration and the condition for lifting restriction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling regarding the effect of a judge declaring a person bankrupt and identify the condition under which creditors can lift the restriction.

Core Idea
Bankruptcy restriction is triggered by judge's declaration; creditors can lift it by proving new wealth.

This lesson covers the first ruling from the chapter on Interdiction (Hajr) in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It explains what happens when a judge officially declares a person bankrupt, and the only way creditors can overcome the restriction: by proving that the debtor has acquired new assets.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger and effect
The trigger is a judge's declaration of bankruptcy (إذا فلسه الحاكم). The effect is that the judge prevents (حال) the bankrupt from dealing with his creditors. Understand that this is a legal restriction on the bankrupt's capacity to transact with creditors.
The restriction is not automatic upon insolvency; it requires a court declaration according to the Sahibayn.
2
Recognize the exception
The restriction is lifted if the creditors can establish proof that the bankrupt has subsequently obtained new wealth (إلا أن يقيموا البينة أنه قد حصل له مال). This exception allows creditors to pursue the new assets.
The burden of proof is on the creditors.
3
Note the attribution to Sahibayn
The text begins with (وقالا) indicating this ruling is from Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; Abu Hanifa's view is not stated here.
In Hanafi fiqh, when the Sahibayn agree against Abu Hanifa, the mufta bihi may be their view in later practice.

Commentary
This passage presents four rulings from the Hanafi school on the topic of hajr (interdiction). The first ruling, attributed to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the two companions), states that when a judge declares a person bankrupt (iflas), the bankruptcy triggers a restriction: the bankrupt is prevented from dealing with his creditors. However, if the creditors can prove that the bankrupt has subsequently acquired new wealth, the restriction is lifted and they can pursue that new asset. The second ruling clarifies that a person who is sinful (fasiq) is not automatically placed under interdiction; rather, the criterion is whether he manages his wealth properly. If he is a good manager (muslihan li malihi), he remains free to transact despite his sinfulness. The third ruling establishes that there is no distinction between original fisq (i.e., born into a non-Muslim family or inherently sinful) and acquired fisq (becoming sinful later in life); both are treated the same regarding the ruling. The fourth ruling addresses a scenario where a bankrupt person has in his possession specific goods (mut'ah) that he purchased from a particular seller. In such a case, the seller does not have a preferential right to reclaim those goods; instead, he stands as an ordinary creditor (uswah al-ghurama) alongside other creditors, sharing proportionately in the bankrupt's estate. These rulings reflect the Hanafi emphasis on protecting creditors while not unduly restricting personal freedom based on moral character.

Key Terms
الحجر (al-hajr): Interdiction or restriction placed by a judge on a person's ability to manage his wealth due to incompetence or bankruptcy.
الإفلاس (al-iflas): Bankruptcy, a legal declaration by the judge that a person's debts exceed his assets, leading to restrictions on his transactions.
الغرماء (al-ghurama'): Creditors who are owed debts by the bankrupt person.

States the condition for interdiction of a sinful person.

Core Idea
Sinfulness alone does not justify interdiction; proper financial management is the key.

This lesson focuses on the second ruling from the chapter on Hajr: a person who is sinful (fasiq) is not placed under interdiction if he manages his wealth properly. This reflects the Hanafi principle that hajr is based on financial incompetence, not moral character.

Identify the subject and condition

This distinguishes Hanafi from other schools that may interdict based on sin.
2
Recognize the negative inference
The phrase 'لا يحجر' implies that interdiction is valid when the condition fails: if the fasiq mismanages his wealth, he may be interdicted.
The criterion is financial prudence, not piety.

Ruling on Seller's Rights in Bankruptcy
States the status of a seller of specific goods in a bankruptcy case.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a seller of goods found in a bankrupt's possession is an ordinary creditor, not a preferential claimant.

Core Idea
Seller of specific goods is unsecured creditor; no right to reclaim.

This final ruling addresses a common situation: a person buys goods on credit and later becomes bankrupt while still in possession of those goods. The seller may think he can take them back, but the Hanafi rule puts him on par with other creditors.

Guided Path

Identify the scenario
The scenario: a person (عنده) has goods (متاع) that belong to a specific individual (لرجل بعينه) which he bought from him (ابتاعه منه). The buyer becomes bankrupt (أفلس).
The goods are still physically with the bankrupt.
2
State the ruling
The owner of the goods (صاحب المتاع) is like the other creditors (أسوة الغرماء) regarding that good. He does not get preferential treatment or the right to reclaim.
He shares proportionately in the bankruptcy estate.

Hanafi Rules on Confession (Iqrar) and Clarification of Ambiguous Terms
States general rule of confession and then explains rulings on ambiguous confessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid confession and apply the Hanafi rules for clarifying ambiguous confessions, including the minimum amounts implied by specific phrases.

Core Idea
A free adult's confession is binding even if vague; the confessor must clarify, and certain words impose minimum amounts to prevent undervaluation.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi rules on iqrar (confession). The source text from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar establishes that a valid confession is binding regardless of whether the subject is known or unknown, but the confessor must clarify vague terms. The judge can compel clarification, and specific phrases carry implied minimum amounts. Understanding these minimums is crucial for judges and lawyers when evaluating confessions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions for a valid confession
From the opening line, note the three conditions: freedom (hurr), adulthood (baligh), and sanity ('aqil). These are prerequisites for the confession to be binding.
A confession by a slave or minor is not binding in the Hanafi school.
2
Distinguish known vs. unknown confessions
The text states that a confession is binding whether the subject is known (ma'lum) or unknown (majhul). This means a vague confession like 'I owe you something' is initially effective.
3
Explain the obligation of clarification (bayan) and the judge's role
After a vague confession, the confessor is told to clarify. If he refuses, the judge compels him to specify. The confessor's statement with an oath is accepted unless the confessee proves a higher amount.
The judge's compulsion (ijbar) ensures that vague confessions are resolved.
4
Apply the specific rules for common vague phrases
Memorize the minimums: 'wealth' (mal) – left to the confessor; 'great wealth' – at least 200 dirhams; 'many dirhams' – at least 10; 'dirhams' alone – at least 3; 'such-and-such dirhams' – at least 11.
These minimums protect the creditor's rights against undervaluation.
5
Recognize the overall principle of the confessor's word with oath
In all these cases, the confessor's clarified statement is accepted with his oath unless the confessee provides evidence to the contrary.
This balances the confessor's duty to clarify with the right to contest excessive claims.

Acknowledgment of Lineage: Conditions and Inheritance Effects
Ruling on validity of lineage acknowledgment and its inheritance consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify from whom an acknowledgment of lineage is accepted in Hanafi fiqh and apply the inheritance rules that follow.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, only parents, children, spouses, and patrons may acknowledge lineage; others' acknowledgments do not establish lineage but may affect inheritance.

This passage from Kitab al-Iqrar (Book of Confession) clarifies a nuanced rule: not every claim of lineage is legally accepted. The school restricts acceptance to close family members and patrons, but then provides a pathway for inheritance rights even when lineage is not established. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying inheritance rules and avoiding invalid lineage claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of persons allowed to acknowledge lineage
Read the text and list the specific relatives from whom an acknowledgment of lineage is accepted: parents, child, husband, wife, and mawla (patron). Also note that for others like brother and uncle, the acknowledgment is not accepted.
The mawla refers to a freed slave or a patron in a contract of clientage.
2
Understand the inheritance effect when the person has a known heir
If the person making the acknowledgment (muqirr) has any known heir, even a distant one, that heir is more entitled to the inheritance than the person being acknowledged (muqarr lahu).
The existence of any heir blocks the muqarr lahu from inheriting.
3
Apply the rule when the muqirr has no heir
If the muqirr has no known heir at all, then the muqarr lahu inherits from him. This is an exception when there is no opposition.
This shows the acknowledgment still has some legal effect even though it does not establish lineage.
4
Analyze the special case of a son acknowledging a brother after the father's death
When a father dies and his son acknowledges a brother (i.e., another son of the father), the brother's lineage from the father is not established. However, the brother does share in the inheritance from the father (along with the son).
Lineage is not created by the son's acknowledgment, but inheritance rights are granted.

States three key procedural rules on oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three procedural rules regarding oaths in Hanafi litigation and explain the consequence of a defendant's refusal (nukul).

Core Idea
The oath is never returned to the claimant; the defendant's refusal results in judgment against him.

This passage presents three foundational procedural rules in Hanafi judicial procedure concerning oaths. The first rule states that the oath is not returned to the claimant if the defendant refuses it. The second rule declares that the possessor's evidence is not accepted in claims of absolute ownership. The third rule details the effect of the defendant's refusal (nukul) and the judge's proper conduct in offering the oath. Mastering these rules is essential for understanding how a Hanafi judge resolves disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rules
Read the text carefully and list the three distinct rulings: (1) oath not returned to claimant, (2) possessor's evidence not accepted, (3) refusal leads to judgment against defendant.
These are stated in three separate sentences, each starting with 'لا' (no/not).
2
Understand the first rule: oath not returned
Note that the text says 'ولا تُرَدُّ اليمينُ على المدعي' (the oath is not returned to the claimant). This means if the defendant refuses to swear, the judge does not invite the claimant to take the oath instead. This is a distinctive Hanafi position; other schools allow returning the oath.
3
Understand the second rule: possessor's evidence not accepted
The text states 'ولا تُقبل بينة صاحب اليد في الملك المطلق' (the evidence of the possessor is not accepted in absolute ownership). The reasoning is that possession itself is a form of evidence, so the possessor does not need additional testimony for a claim of absolute ownership.
This rule has details in longer works; here it is a general principle.
4
Understand the third rule: effect of nukul
The text says if the defendant refuses (nukul), judgment is given against him: 'قُضِيَ عليه بالنُّكول، ولَزِمَه ما ادعي عليه'. This is equivalent to an admission for the purpose of judgment.
5
Note the judge's procedure
The judge should offer the oath three times, explaining the consequence: 'إني أعرض عليك اليمين ثلاثاً، فإن حلفت، وإلا قضيت عليك بما ادعاه'. After three offers, if the defendant still refuses, judgment is entered.
The three offers are a recommended practice (yambaghi), not a mandatory requirement.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, the default rule is that a defendant who denies a claim must take an oath (yamin) to repel it. However, the school recognizes exceptions. The oath always goes to the defendant, never the plaintiff.

When claims are not contradictory, the case drops without evidence; when contradictory, the case continues.

Foundation of Judicial Oaths: Wording, Non-Muslims, and No Taghlīẓ
Ruling on oath wording and non-Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for judicial oath wording (by Allah alone, not by divorce/manumission), apply them to non-Muslims with adapted wording, and identify that taghlīz is not required for Muslims.

Core Idea
Judicial oaths must be by Allah alone; non-Muslims swear by Allah through their scriptures, and temporal/spatial enhancement is not required for Muslims.

The judge must administer the oath correctly, focusing on the essence of the claim. The Hanafi school specifies that an oath must invoke Allah's name directly, not through divorce or manumission. For non-Muslims, the oath is adapted to their beliefs. No additional solemnity (taghlīz) by time or place is obligatory for Muslims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is administering a judicial oath. The ruling is that the oath must be by Allah alone; oaths by divorce or manumission are invalid in court.
The text states 'ولا يُستحلف بالطلاق، ولا بالعتاق'.
2
Apply the ruling to non-Muslims
For Jews, Christians, and Zoroastrians, the oath is adapted: Jews swear by Allah who revealed the Torah, Christians by Allah who revealed the Gospel, Zoroastrians by Allah who created fire. They all still swear by Allah, not by their deities alone.
The wording includes 'بالله' for each.
3
Note the prohibition of taghlīz
For Muslims, it is not obligatory to make the oath heavier by time (e.g., after Asr) or place (e.g., in the mosque). Taghlīz is not required, though it may be permissible.
The text says 'ولا يجب تغليظ اليمين على المسلم بزمان، ولا بمكان'.
4
Understand the rationale
The oath is a legal tool to establish the truth, not a religious ritual. Therefore, it must be accessible and relevant to the party's belief system, but always centered on Allah.
5
Contrast with other practices
Some non-Hanafi schools require taghlīz or allow oaths by other means. The Hanafi position is more lenient, focusing on the essence rather than form.
This is an intra-madhhab or comparative point.

Commentary
This comprehensive passage details the Hanafi rules of judicial oaths (يمين). Key points: 1) Oaths must be by the name of Allah alone; oath by divorce or manumission (which are used as strong admonitions in some cultures) are not recognized in court. 2) For non-Muslims, the oath is tailored to their beliefs: Jews swear by Allah who revealed the Torah to Moses; Christians by Allah who revealed the Gospel; Zoroastrians by Allah who created the fire. However, they are not required to swear in their places of worship (e.g., synagogue, church) as an enhancement. 3) For Muslims, there is no requirement of temporal or spatial enhancement of the oath (e.g., swearing at a specific time or in mosque). 4) Specific oath wording: In a sale dispute where the claimant says he bought a slave for 1000, the defendant (if he denies) swears: 'There is no valid sale between us' (نفي البيع القائم), not simply 'I did not sell.' The reason is that the claimant's claim is of a contract; denial must negate the existence of the contract, not merely the act of selling. Similarly in usurpation, the defendant swears: 'You do not deserve its return from me' (denying the right to restitution), rather than 'I did not usurp.' This is because the claim is about the right to the item, not the act of taking. These precise wordings ensure the oath addresses the legal dispute accurately.

Key Terms
اليمين (al-yamīn): A judicial oath taken by a party to establish or deny a claim.
الطلاق والعتاق (al-ṭalāq wa al-'itāq): Divorce and manumission; used as forms of oath in some contexts, but not valid in court.
المجوسي (al-majūsī): A Zoroastrian; swears by Allah who created fire.
التغليظ (al-taghlīẓ): Making an oath more solemn by time, place, or wording.
