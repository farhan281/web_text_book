Kafalah bi al-Nafs: Definition, Conditions, and Discharge
Opening definitions and rulings on guarantee of the person.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition and conditions of kafalah bi al-nafs and identify when the guarantor is discharged.

Core Idea
Kafalah bi al-nafs obligates presenting the person; proper delivery discharges, but death extinguishes.

This lesson examines the opening section of the chapter on kafalah from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri. It clarifies the two types of guarantee and details the rulings specific to guarantee of the person—its formation, conditions for delivery, and grounds for discharge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of kafalah
The text begins by stating that kafalah is of two types: guarantee of the person (kafalah bi al-nafs) and guarantee of property (kafalah bi al-mal). Recognize that the lesson focuses on the first type.
2
Recognize the binding wording
The contract is formed by specific expressions such as 'takaffaltu binafsi fulan' or similar phrases meaning 'I guarantee the person of so-and-so'. Also note that variants like 'his neck', 'his spirit', etc. are valid.
3
Understand delivery conditions and discharge
If a time for delivery is stipulated, the guarantor must produce the person at that time; otherwise the judge may imprison him. The guarantor is discharged by delivering the person in a place where the claimant can bring him to court. Delivery in the market suffices if the condition was to deliver in the judge's court, but delivery in a wilderness does not discharge.
4
Note the effect of death
If the guaranteed person dies, the guarantor is discharged from the obligation because the duty was to present a living person.

Delivery Location, Death, and Combined Guarantee in Kafalah bi al-Nafs
Continues rulings on delivery location, death, and mixed guarantees.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a surety is discharged from kafalah bi al-nafs and distinguish between delivery in a market, wilderness, and combined guarantees.

Core Idea
Delivery in a market discharges the surety; in a wilderness it does not. Death discharges. Combined guarantee makes both obligations persist.

Kafalah bi al-nafs (suretyship for the person) involves specific conditions for discharge. The Hanafi school details how the location of delivery affects discharge, the effect of the guaranteed person's death, and the unique case where the surety also guarantees a debt. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out these rulings clearly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the delivery condition
Notice the explicit stipulation of delivery in the judge's assembly. The ruling hinges on whether the actual delivery location is a place of valid legal proceedings (e.g., market) or not (e.g., wilderness).
The market is considered a place where litigation can occur; wilderness is not.
2
Distinguish between market and wilderness
Delivery in the market results in discharge because the creditor can still claim his right. Delivery in a wilderness does not discharge because the creditor cannot pursue legal action there.
The wilderness (bariyyah) is uninhabited and lacks a governing authority for justice.
3
Apply the death ruling
If the guaranteed person dies, the suretyship for the person becomes void automatically. The surety is no longer obligated to produce him.
Death extinguishes the subject of the guarantee.
4
Analyze combined guarantee
If the surety guarantees the person and also promises to pay a debt if not produced, then failure to produce triggers the debt guarantee while the personal guarantee remains. Both obligations coexist.
The surety is not discharged from the personal guarantee even after paying the debt.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Kafalah bi al-Nafs in Hudud and Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on person-suretyship in penal cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on kafalah bi al-nafs in hudud and qisas and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prohibits person-suretyship in hudud; the Sahibayn permit it — a school-internal ikhtilaf.

This short passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri records an intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether a person can act as surety (kafalah bi al-nafs) in cases involving hadd punishments or qisas. While the general rule permits kafalah, Abu Hanifa makes an exception for these categories, arguing that they involve divine rights (Haqq Allah) that cannot be delegated. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani disagree, holding that suretyship is valid. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the boundaries of kafalah in penal matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is kafalah bi al-nafs (person-suretyship). The dispute is whether it is valid when the underlying claim involves hudud (fixed punishments) or qisas (retaliation).
The passage does not discuss other types of kafalah.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that it is not permissible (la tajuz). This is presented first in the text, indicating its primary status.
The reasoning is implied: hudud are rights of Allah that require personal presence.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say it is permissible (tajuz). They consider the general permissibility of kafalah contracts to apply.
Their reasoning focuses on the contractual nature of kafalah, not the nature of the underlying right.
4
Analyze the basis of the ikhtilaf
The difference stems from how each views hudud and qisas: Abu Hanifa sees them as pure divine rights (Haqq Allah) that cannot be delegated; the Sahibayn view the kafalah as a separate guarantee of presence that does not affect the punishment itself.
This is a classic example of usul influences on furu rulings.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is generally that of Abu Hanifa, due to caution (ihtiyat) regarding divine limits.
Some later Hanafi texts may mention the Sahibayn view for certain contexts, but the default fatwa is impermissibility.

Commentary
This short passage presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf. Abu Hanifa holds that kafalah bi al-nafs is invalid when the underlying claim involves hadd punishments or qisas (retaliation). His reasoning is that these are rights of Allah or rights that cannot be delegated, and the presence of the accused is required for the punishment itself; a surety cannot substitute for the person in such matters. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) argue that suretyship for the person is permissible even in hudud and qisas because the right of the claimant (to have the accused present) can be guaranteed, though the punishment itself is not affected. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is often that of Abu Hanifa due to the principle of avoiding delegation in divine rights.

Rulings on Suretyship for Property (Kafalah bi al-Mal)
States general permissibility, creditor's option, conditional suretyship, and recourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key rulings of kafalah bi al-mal: its permissibility, the creditor's option, conditional suretyship, dispute resolution, and recourse.

Core Idea
Kafalah bi al-mal is permissible for known or unknown valid debts; the creditor may choose whom to sue.

This lesson introduces the rulings on suretyship for property (kafalah bi al-mal). In Hanafi fiqh, this type of guarantee is a common transaction that provides security for creditors. The text from Al-Lubab covers its fundamental rules, from permissibility to the surety's right of recourse. By understanding these, you will grasp the legal framework that governs guarantees in Islamic finance and daily transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall ruling
The passage states that kafalah bi al-mal is permissible (jaiz). Note that this is the general ruling for all valid suretyship contracts.
Permissibility is unconditional as long as the underlying debt is valid.
2
Understand the debt requirement
The debt guaranteed can be known or unknown at the time of the contract, provided it is a valid debt (dayn sahih). Examples are given.
Unknown debts are allowed, unlike some other schools.
3
Recognize the creditor's option
The creditor (makful lahu) has the choice to demand payment from either the original debtor or the surety.
This is a key benefit for the creditor.
4
Examine conditional suretyship
The suretyship can be suspended on a condition, such as 'if you have not sold to so-and-so, then the debt is upon me.'
Conditional guarantees are valid in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Handle dispute on amount
If there is evidence of a specific debt, the surety must pay that amount. Without evidence, the surety's statement on what he admits is taken with his oath.
The principal debtor's admission of more does not bind the surety.
6
Apply recourse rules
If the surety pays with the order of the principal debtor, he can recover what he paid. Without order, there is no recourse unless an implied request exists.
Recourse is only with the debtor's order.

Kafalah bi al-Nafs in Hudud and Qisas: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on kafalah bi al-nafs in penal cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on kafalah bi al-nafs in hudud and qisas and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Personal surety is invalid for hudud and qisas according to Abu Hanifa, but valid according to the Sahibayn.

This lesson examines a notable intra-Hanafi dispute concerning kafalah bi al-nafs (personal surety, bail) in cases of hudud (fixed penalties) and qisas (retaliation). The matn concisely presents the disagreement: Abu Hanifa holds that such suretyship is not valid, while his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), consider it valid. Understanding the reasoning behind each position and the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf) is essential for applying the correct ruling in legal practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
What is the specific act being ruled upon? Here it is kafalah bi al-nafs — a contract where one person guarantees the presence of another person (the principal debtor/accused) before the authority. Is such a contract valid when the underlying claim involves a hadd penalty or qisas?
Kafalah bi al-nafs is distinct from kafalah bi al-mal (suretyship for debt).
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
According to the text, 'la tujuz' (it is not valid) according to Abu Hanifa. Identify that this is the default position in the matn before the exception.
The phrase 'عند أبي حنيفة' attributes the invalidity to Abu Hanifa alone.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The text uses 'وقالا: تجوز' — 'and the two said: it is valid.' Note that both Abu Yusuf and Muhammad agree against their teacher on this point.
This is one of several known issues where the Sahibayn differ from Abu Hanifa.
4
Analyze the underlying reasoning
Though not given in the matn, from other sources we know: Abu Hanifa argues that hudud are rights of Allah and cannot be compromised or represented; qisas, though a personal right, requires the actual perpetrator's presence for exact retaliation. The Sahibayn argue that the surety can secure the accused's presence, which is sufficient for execution of the penalty.
This reasoning is often explored in later commentaries like al-Hidayah and Bada'i' al-Sana'i'.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
What difference does this dispute make in practice? According to Abu Hanifa, the kafalah contract is batil (void) — no liability falls on the surety; the principal must appear on their own. According to the Sahibayn, the contract is sahih (valid) — the surety can be compelled to produce the accused or face consequences.

Kafalah: The Surety's Right of Recourse and Right to Demand Release
Ruling on recourse and release rights in kafalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the surety's right of recourse depending on the debtor's order and distinguish the condition under which the surety may demand release before payment.

Core Idea
The surety recovers only if he had the debtor's order; without order, payment is voluntary.

Kafalah (suretyship) is a common transaction where one person guarantees another's debt. A critical question is whether the surety can reclaim what he pays from the debtor. The Ḥanafī school draws a bright line between suretyship done with the debtor's order and without — the former gives the right of recourse, the latter does not. Equally important, the surety cannot demand payment from the debtor before he himself has paid the creditor, though he has a limited right to seek release when pressured.

2
State the ruling on recourse
If with order, the surety recovers (yarji‘) whatever he pays. If without order, he does not recover. Commit the rule: order → recourse; no order → no recourse.
This is a strict rule; the surety's payment without order is treated as a voluntary gift.

Explain the timing of recourse
The surety cannot demand the money from the debtor before he has paid the creditor. The right of recourse arises only after actual payment.

This mulāzamah right protects the surety from being trapped without means.

The Ḥanafī position balances the rights of all parties: the creditor is paid, the debtor is not prematurely sued by the surety, and the surety is not left helpless when pressured.
The ruling is a clear application of the maxim 'al-umūr bi-maqāṣidihā' — effects depend on intentions and circumstances.

B) It is not valid.
C) It is valid only if the creditors agree.
D) It is valid only if the guarantor is a relative.

Key Takeaway
When a debtor dies leaving no assets, Abu Hanifa invalidates the guarantee, but the Sahibayn validate it, highlighting that the debt's existence in the dhimmah is the crux of the dispute.
