Dissolution of Ijarah Due to Excusable Hardships
Ruling on rescission of lease due to serious hardship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two examples of excusable hardships that justify rescission of a lease contract and explain the judge's role in the process.

Core Idea
A lease is dissolved when a tenant loses capital or a lessor becomes bankrupt and must sell the asset.

Contracts are meant to be binding, but Islamic law recognizes that unforeseen hardships can make continued performance oppressive. This lesson presents specific cases where a lease (ijarah) may be rescinded to prevent harm. The judge oversees the rescission to ensure fairness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two excuses
The text gives two scenarios: a tenant who loses his capital (so he cannot benefit or pay), and a lessor who becomes bankrupt and needs to sell the leased property to pay debts.
Both involve financial inability that defeats the purpose of the contract.
2
Understand the underlying principle
The common factor is a serious hardship that makes continuation of the lease either impossible or extremely burdensome. The hardship must be genuine and severe.
Not every inconvenience qualifies; only those that fundamentally undermine the contract.
3
Note the judge's involvement
Rescission is not automatic; the judge must dissolve the contract and, in the second case, sell the property to satisfy creditors.
The judge ensures the process is lawful and protects the rights of all parties.

وتُفسخ الإجارة بالأعذار ، كمَن استأجر دكانـاً في السوق ليتجـر فيه، فذهب ماله ، وكمَن آجر داراً ، أو دكاناً، ثم أفلس، فلزمته ديون لا يقدر على قضائها إلا من ثمن ما آجر : فَسَخَ القاضي العقد، وباعها في الدين.
And the lease is rescinded due to excuses, such as a person who hires a shop in the market to trade in it, then his money is lost; and such as a person who leases out a house or a shop, then becomes bankrupt, and debts become incumbent upon him which he cannot repay except from the price of what he leased: then the judge voids the contract and sells it for the debt.

Commentary
This ruling allows the rescission (faskh) of a lease contract when one party faces a hardship that makes continued performance impossible or exceptionally burdensome. Two examples are given: (1) The tenant loses his capital, so he cannot pay rent or benefit; (2) The lessor becomes bankrupt and his only asset to pay debts is the property he leased out, so the judge may rescind the lease and sell the property to satisfy creditors. The judge's involvement is required for enforcement.

Key Terms
أعذار (a'dhar): Excuses or hardships that justify contract rescission

Change of Travel Plan as an Excuse in Ijarah of Animals
Ruling statement on cancellation of ijarah of animal due to change of travel plan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when a hirer may cancel an ijarah of an animal due to change of travel plan and explain why the carrier cannot.

Core Idea
Only the hirer's change of mind excuses cancellation; the carrier remains bound.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents a specific ruling from the Hanafi school on ijarah contracts. It distinguishes between the hirer (musta'jir) and the carrier (mukari) when travel plans change. Understanding this distinction helps appreciate how the contract's purpose and the parties' obligations are balanced in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The passage refers to two parties: the hirer (musta'jir) who hires the animal, and the carrier (mukari) who provides the animal for hire.
In modern terms, carrier is the service provider.
2
Identify the scenario
The scenario is that a person hires an animal for travel, then changes his mind about the journey.
3
State the ruling for the hirer
The hirer may rescind the lease; the change of plan is considered a valid excuse ('udhr).
Rescission is permissible without penalty.
4
State the ruling for the carrier
If the carrier changes his mind, that is not an excuse. He cannot cancel the contract.
The carrier remains obligated to provide the service.
5
Understand the rationale
The contract serves the hirer's need. When that need disappears, the contract loses its basis. The carrier's willingness is irrelevant; he has undertaken a binding obligation.
This asymmetry protects the hirer's interests.

Source Text

وكمن استأجر دابة ليسافر عليها، ثم بدا لـه مـن الـسفر: فله أن يفسخ الإجارة، فهو عُذر. وإن بدا للمكاري من السفر : فليس ذلك بعذرٍ.
And like a person who hires an animal to travel on, then he changes his mind about the journey: he may rescind the lease, so it is an excuse. But if the carrier changes his mind about the journey, that is not an excuse.
Source pages p. 159

Commentary
This ruling distinguishes between the hirer (musta'jir) and the carrier (mukari). If the hirer's need for travel disappears, he may cancel the lease without penalty, as it is a valid excuse ('udhr). However, if the carrier (the owner of the animal) decides not to travel, that is not an excuse for him to cancel, because the contract is for the hirer's benefit and the carrier is obligated to provide the service. The carrier's change of mind does not relieve him of his contractual duty.

Key Terms
مكاري (mukari): The owner who provides the animal for hire

Disputes in Lease Contracts (Ijara) Before and After Performance
Procedural rulings for lease disputes in three scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether mutual oaths (tahaluf) apply in a lease dispute based on the stage of performance and state the respective outcomes.

Core Idea
The stage of performance in a lease determines the dispute resolution: mutual oaths before or partial, lessee's word after full.

When a lessor and lessee disagree on the rent or terms after the lease has ended, the stage of performance critically affects how the dispute is resolved. This lesson unpacks the three scenarios outlined in Al-Quduri: before any use, after full use, and after partial use. Understanding these distinctions prevents unjust enrichment and ensures procedural fairness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the performance stage
Determine whether the lease's subject matter (e.g., property use) has been fully enjoyed, partially enjoyed, or not yet enjoyed. This is the key variable.
Performance stage is measured by the lessee's receipt of the benefit.
2
Apply the rule for that stage
Match the stage to the Hanafi ruling: (1) Before performance: mutual oaths and rescission. (2) After full performance: no mutual oaths, lessee's word is accepted. (3) After partial performance: mutual oaths for the unperformed part, rescission for that part, and lessee's word for the performed part.
The lessee is trusted post-performance because the benefit is consumed and the lessor seeks extra payment.
3
Determine the outcome
Conclude the legal effect: either rescission (taradd) or acceptance of the lessee's statement regarding the rent. In partial cases, the contract continues for the performed part and is rescinded for the remainder.
The goal is equitable resolution given the irreversible nature of consumed benefits.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
وإذا اختلفا في الإجارة قبل استيفاء المعقود عليه: تحالفا، وترادًا. وإن اختلفا بعد الاستيفاء لم يتحالفا، وكان القول قول المستأجر. وإن اختلفا بعد استيفاء بعض المعقود عليه: تحالفا، وفُسخ العقد فيما بقي، وكان القول في الماضي قول المستأجر.
And if they differ regarding a lease before the enjoyment (or performance) of the contracted subject matter: they take mutual oaths and return (the payment and the benefit). And if they differ after enjoyment (full performance), they do not take mutual oaths, and the statement is the statement of the lessee (hirer). And if they differ after enjoyment of part of the contracted subject matter: they take mutual oaths, and the contract is rescinded regarding the remainder, and the statement regarding the past (performed part) is the statement of the lessee.
Source pages p. 336

Commentary
This passage states the Hanafi rules for disputes in lease contracts (ijara). Three scenarios: (1) Before any performance (before the lessee uses the property): if they dispute the rent or terms, mutual oaths (tahaluf) are taken, and both parties restore what they have exchanged: the lessee returns the property and the lessor returns any rent paid. (2) After full performance (after the lessee has fully enjoyed the benefit): no mutual oaths; the lessee's word is accepted because the benefit has been consumed and the lessor's claim would be for additional payment; the default is that the rent is as agreed, and the lessee is the one who would be charged more, so his denial of extra is trusted. (3) After partial performance: they take mutual oaths regarding the unperformed part, and the contract is rescinded for that part only; for the already performed part, the lessee's word is taken regarding the rent due for it. This nuanced approach balances the interests when the lease is partially executed.

Key Terms
استيفاء (istifa'): Full receipt or enjoyment of the contracted benefit (e.g., occupying the leased property).
تراد (taradd): Mutual return: the lessor returns the rent, the lessee returns the property (rescission).

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint

Key Takeaway
The stage of performance in a lease dictates whether mutual oaths apply and whose word is accepted: before performance = mutual oaths and rescission; after full performance = no oaths, lessee's word; after partial = mutual oaths for the future, lessee's word for the past.
