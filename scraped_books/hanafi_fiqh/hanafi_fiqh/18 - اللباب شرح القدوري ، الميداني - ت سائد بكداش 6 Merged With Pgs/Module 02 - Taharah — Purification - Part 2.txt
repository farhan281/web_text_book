When Searching for Water is Not Required
Ruling on searching for water before tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a person performing tayammum is not required to search for water and explain the meaning of 'ghalabat al-zann'.

Core Idea
Tayammum is valid without searching unless you strongly believe water is near.

This short passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri clarifies a key point in taharah: when must you search for water before tayammum? The default is that you may perform tayammum without a fruitless search. However, if you have a strong indication that water is close, you must investigate. This lesson unpacks the condition of 'ghalabat al-zann' that triggers the duty to search.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is searching for water before tayammum. The default ruling is that it is not required (laysa 'ala... an yatlub). This means tayammum is valid without prior search.
The text explicitly negates the duty, establishing a default permissibility.
2
Identify the conditioning factor
The text conditions the absence of duty on 'if it does not dominate his assumption that water is near him' (lam yaghlib 'ala zannihi). This is the key trigger.
The condition is negative: if the assumption is dominant, searching becomes required.
3
Define ghalabat al-zann
Ghalabat al-zann is a strong belief or dominant assumption, based on signs or credible information. In Hanafi usul, it is a level of certainty short of 'ilm but sufficient for action.
The word 'ghalaba' implies overcoming doubt.
4
Apply to a practical scenario
If a traveler sees green vegetation in a desert, that may create ghalabat al-zann that water is nearby, making search necessary. If there are no signs, he may do tayammum.
The distance to search is detailed elsewhere (e.g., one mile).

Tayammum: Pure Earth Requirement and Recommended Timing
Opening the chapter on tayammum with basic ruling and timing recommendation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for valid tayammum (pure earth) and explain the recommended timing for a traveler without water who hopes to find water later.

Core Idea
Tayammum requires pure earth; delay prayer if you hope to find water.

This passage opens the chapter on tayammum with two key points: the essential condition that tayammum must be performed with pure earth, and a practical recommendation for someone who is without water at the start of the prayer time but expects to find it later. Both rulings derive from the Quran (5:6) and Sunnah, and this text lays the foundation for understanding when tayammum is valid and how to prioritize water when possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage first states that tayammum is not permissible except with pure earth. This is a general prohibition (la yajuz) indicating that tayammum is invalid without this condition. Identify the act: dry ablution (tayammum). The ruling: it is only valid with pure earth (sa'id tahir).
The negation 'la yajuz' is a strong prohibition, typical for conditions of validity.
2
Define the key terms: sa'id and tahir
Understand that 'sa'id' refers to the surface of the earth, including soil, sand, stone, and rock. 'Tahir' means pure, free from ritual impurity (najis). Hanafis hold that any part of the earth's surface is valid as long as it is pure.
Shafi'is restrict sa'id to dust that can stick to hands; Hanafis are more expansive.
3
Identify the second ruling: timing recommendation
The passage then discusses a situation: a person who has no water at the beginning of the prayer time but hopes to find it later. The ruling is that it is mustahabb (recommended) to delay the prayer to the end of the time.
This is not an obligation but a preference to avoid unnecessary tayammum.
4
Understand the consequence of delay
If water is found before the time ends, one performs wudu and prays. If not, one performs tayammum and prays. This ensures that water is used when possible.
5
Recognize the underlying principle
The ruling reflects the Hanafi principle of taysir (facilitation) and the preference for using water when available. Tayammum is a substitute, and the default is to use water unless it is genuinely unavailable.

One Tayammum for Multiple Prayers
Stating that a single tayammum allows multiple prayers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that one valid tayammum permits multiple obligatory and voluntary prayers.

Core Idea
One tayammum suffices for as many prayers as you wish, until water is found or it breaks.

This brief passage from al-Quduri's text presents a key Hanafi ruling that distinguishes tayammum from wudu. While wudu is required for each prayer only if broken, tayammum uniquely remains valid for multiple prayers as long as its conditions persist. Understanding this rule dispels a common misconception and provides practical ease for those unable to use water.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The passage states that a person who has performed tayammum can pray with it as many prayers as he wishes. This includes both obligatory (fara'id) and voluntary (nawafil) prayers.
The act is performing prayer using tayammum.
2
Determine the Condition
The ruling applies only while the tayammum itself remains valid. Tayammum is invalidated by finding sufficient water (if tayammum was due to lack of water) or by any act that would break wudu (e.g., urination, defecation, sleep).
The text does not list these conditions explicitly, but they are assumed from general Hanafi fiqh.
3
Scope of Application
The permission covers all prayers — fard (obligatory) and nafl (voluntary) — without limit as long as the tayammum is intact.
Other schools differ; for example, the Shafi'i school requires a new tayammum for each fard prayer.
4
Practical Consequence
A traveler or sick person who cannot use water can rely on a single tayammum for multiple daily prayers, reducing hardship.
If tayammum becomes invalid before finishing a prayer, that prayer must be repeated after renewing tayammum.

The Hanafi position is that tayammum remains valid until one finds water or until the event that necessitated tayammum ends.

Core Idea
When water is available, tayammum is never an option — even if it means delaying the prayer.

You might think that if prayer time is about to slip away, you can use tayammum to catch it. But the Hanafi school teaches otherwise: water takes priority over time. This short but powerful ruling protects the integrity of purification and forces us to prioritise wudu even at the cost of praying after the time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the act
The scenario is when the time is so narrow that performing wudu would cause the prayer time to expire. The act in question is the prayer that is about to be missed.
The prayer time is precious, but purification is a condition for validity.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is clear: he must perform wudu, not tayammum.
The text says 'lam yatayammam, wa lakin yatawadda' — he does not do tayammum but performs wudu.
3
Identify what is prohibited
Tayammum is prohibited in this situation despite the risk of missing the time.
Tayammum is only allowed when water is unavailable or harmful.
4
Understand the consequence
After performing wudu, he prays the prayer after the time has expired as qada (make-up).
The missed prayer is called fa'itah and must be made up.

Ruling on searching for water before tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of dominant assumption (ghalabat al-zann) to determine when searching for water is obligatory before performing tayammum.

Core Idea
Tayammum is only allowed after a reasonable search; dominant assumption of water triggers the duty to search.

Tayammum is a purification substitute for wudu when water is unavailable, but the Hanafi school imposes a condition: you must be reasonably certain that water is inaccessible. This lesson clarifies the threshold of certainty required and the steps you must take before resorting to tayammum, especially when water might be nearby.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key condition
The passage uses 'ghalabat al-zann' (dominant assumption) as the trigger. Determine whether the person has a strong belief that water exists nearby.
This is not certainty (yaqin) but a high probability; doubt (shakk) does not oblige search.
2
Apply to the first scenario
If no dominant assumption (i.e., no strong belief water is nearby), then no search is required and tayammum is permissible directly.
3
Apply to the second scenario
If a dominant assumption exists, tayammum is impermissible until a search is conducted. The search is an obligation.
The search must be reasonable given the circumstances (e.g., looking in the area where water is assumed).
4
Apply to the companion scenario
If a companion has water, one must request it before tayammum. If the companion refuses, tayammum becomes permissible.
This shows that water from others must be sought if accessible, even if not owned.
5
Conclude the ruling
After fulfilling the search requirement (or finding no water, or being refused), tayammum is valid and prayer with it is accepted.
The obligation to search is a condition for the permissibility of tayammum in these cases.

Wiping over Khuffs: Rulings and Conditions
Complete set of rules for wiping over khuffs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid mash over khuffs, apply the duration rules for resident and traveler, and identify what nullifies the wiping.

Core Idea
Wiping over khuffs is a sunnah concession with strict conditions: complete taharah, limited duration, and no janabah.

In this lesson, we explore the concession of wiping over leather socks (khuffs) instead of washing the feet during wudu. This practice is established by the Sunnah and has specific rules about when and how it is valid, including time limits for residents and travelers. Understanding these rules protects the validity of your purification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Basis
The text states that wiping over khuffs is 'ja'iz bi al-sunnah' — permissible and established by the Sunnah. This is not obligatory but a concession.
2
State the Prerequisite
The khuffs must be put on while in a state of 'taharah kamilah' (complete purity) — meaning after performing wudu, not after ghusl.
If you put them on after ghusl, they are not valid for later wiping.
3
Distinguish Durations
A resident (muqim) can wipe for one day and one night (24 hours) from the first hadath after wearing. A traveler (musafir) can wipe for three days and nights (72 hours).
The count starts from the moment of the first hadath, not from the time of wearing.
4
Specify the Method
Wiping is on the upper surface of the khuff in lines using the fingers, beginning at the toes and moving toward the shin. The obligatory amount is the width of three fingers of the hand.
The minimum is three fingers; wiping less than that is insufficient.
