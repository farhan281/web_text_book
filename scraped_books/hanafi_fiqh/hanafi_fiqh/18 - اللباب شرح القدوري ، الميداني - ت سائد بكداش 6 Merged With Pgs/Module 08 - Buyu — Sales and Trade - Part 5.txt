Evidence and Mutual Oaths in Sale Disputes
Rulings on evidence and mutual oaths in sale disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi procedure for resolving disputes over price or quantity in a sale, including the hierarchy of evidence and the mutual oaths (tahaluf) process.

Core Idea
When evidence conflicts, the proof of the higher claim wins; when none exists, mutual oaths lead to rescission.

Disputes after a sale over the price or quantity are common. Islamic law provides a clear, fair process. This lesson guides you through the Hanafi steps: first, evaluate evidence; if only one party proves their claim, they win; if both do, the evidence supporting the increase is preferred; if neither has evidence, the judge gives an ultimatum; if that fails, mutual oaths (tahaluf) are administered, and if both swear, the sale is rescinded. Understanding this ensures justice in market transactions.

Guided Path
1
Classify the dispute
Identify what is being disputed: price (buyer claims lower, seller higher) or quantity (seller admits some, buyer claims more). The same rules apply to both.
2
Assess evidence
If only one party brings evidence (bayyinah), that party wins. This is based on the principle that proof outweighs mere assertion.
3
Resolve conflicting evidence
If both bring evidence, the evidence establishing the increase (al-bayyinah al-muthbitah li al-ziyadah) is given precedence. Rationale: it includes the lesser claim and adds to it, so it is more comprehensive.
4
Handle absence of evidence
The judge gives an ultimatum: the buyer must accept the seller's claimed price, or the sale is rescinded; the seller must deliver the buyer's claimed quantity, or the sale is rescinded.
5
Implement mutual oaths (tahaluf)
If the ultimatum is rejected, the judge administers oaths starting with the buyer. If both swear, the sale is rescinded. If one refuses, the refusing party's claim is deemed admitted.
The order begins with the buyer's oath.

Mutual Oaths After Destruction: Abu Hanifa & Abu Yusuf vs. Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tahaluf after destruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding tahaluf after destruction and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
When the sold item is destroyed, the buyer's word prevails – no mutual oaths needed.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement on the procedure when the object of sale has been destroyed before the parties dispute the price. The two views reflect different assessments of the contract's status after destruction. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the rules of tahaluf and identify the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the legal issue
The sold item (mabee') is destroyed before any price dispute arises. The parties then disagree on the agreed price. The issue: should the judge administer mutual oaths (tahaluf) as in the normal case, or is a different procedure followed?
Destruction removes the possibility of returning the goods.
2
Identify the two opposing positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: no mutual oaths; the buyer's statement (with his oath) is accepted. Muhammad al-Shaybani: mutual oaths are taken, and if both swear, the sale is rescinded and the buyer pays the market value at the time of receipt.
3
Explain the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under the first view, the buyer's word is taken on the price, so he pays what he claims (subject to oath). Under the second view, the sale is voided and the buyer pays the market value, which may differ from the contractual price.
The difference affects both procedure and financial outcome.
4
Note the tarjih (relied-upon view)
The view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf is generally preferred in later Hanafi works because the goods have perished, making return impossible. The mutual oaths procedure is designed for cases where return is possible.
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah prefers this view.

Tahaluf When One of Two Slaves Dies: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tahaluf procedure after partial destruction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on tahaluf when one of two sold items perishes, and distinguish the view of Abu Hanifa from that of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
When one of two items perishes before price is settled, Abu Hanifa blocks mutual oaths unless the seller forfeits; the Sahibayn allow oaths and partial rescission.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the procedure of mutual oaths (tahaluf) when a sold item is partially destroyed. The scenario: two slaves are sold as a set; one dies before the parties agree on the price. The Imams differ on whether tahaluf can still occur, and the practical consequences for the contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Contested Issue
The scenario involves a sale of two slaves; one dies before the parties settle the price. The contested issue is whether mutual oaths (tahaluf) can be administered to resolve the price dispute.
Note that the dispute is about the price, not about which slave died.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position and Its Condition
Abu Hanifa holds that tahaluf does not occur unless the seller agrees to forgo (yatraku) the share of the price attributable to the deceased slave (hissat al-halik). Without such concession, no resolution through oaths.
This view treats the contract as indivisible regarding the dead item.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position and the Remedy
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that mutual oaths are administered, and if taken, the sale is rescinded with respect to the living slave (i.e., it is returned) and the buyer must pay the value of the dead slave (qimat al-halik).
This view treats the contract as divisible, allowing partial rescission.
4
Identify the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, unless the seller forfeits, the dispute remains unresolved; under the Sahibayn, the dispute is resolved by tahaluf and partial rescission.
The thamarah is the availability of a procedural remedy.
5
Note the Mufta Bihi (Preferred Position)
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Haskafi) prefer the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad because it provides a clear resolution to the dispute.
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn position.

Ruling on paternity claim with time-based presumption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a seller's paternity claim when a slave-girl gives birth within six months of sale, and explain the effects on the sale, price, and umm walad status.

Core Idea
Birth within six months of sale presumes pre-sale conception, rescinding the sale for the child.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out a common Hanafi scenario: a sold slave-girl gives birth, and the seller claims paternity. The ruling hinges on a simple time-based presumption that resolves conflicting claims and determines the child's status, the return of the price, and the mother's umm walad status. Understanding this requires careful attention to the six-month threshold and its exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario and parties involved
Recognize that the seller claims paternity of a child born to a slave-girl after the sale. The buyer may also claim the child. The key is the timing of birth relative to the sale date.
The child's lineage and freedom depend on whose claim is accepted.
2
Apply the time-based presumption
If the child is born within six months of the sale, the law presumes the pregnancy began before the sale, so the seller's paternity claim is accepted. If born after six months, the claim is rejected unless the buyer confirms it.
Six months is the minimum human gestation period in Hanafi law.
3
Trace the legal consequences when the seller's claim is accepted
The child is declared free and attributed to the seller, the mother becomes umm walad (free upon the seller's death), the sale is rescinded for the child, and the seller must return the full purchase price for the child.
The price is returned for the child's portion; the mother remains sold unless otherwise stated.
4
Handle the exceptions: child dies or mother dies
If the child dies, the mother does not become umm walad because the child must be alive to establish the status. If the mother dies, the seller's claim is still accepted within six months, and he takes the child and returns the full price according to Abu Hanifa.
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad may differ on returning full price when the mother dies.
5
Determine the hierarchy of claims
If both seller and buyer claim the child, the seller's claim takes precedence. The buyer's claim is secondary and only relevant if the seller's claim fails (e.g., after six months).
This reflects the principle that ownership prior to sale gives priority.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the six-month gestation rule to determine paternity and the consequences of a claim in the sale of a slave-girl.

Core Idea
A child born within six months of sale belongs to the seller; beyond that, the seller's claim requires buyer's confirmation.

When a man sells a slave-girl and she later gives birth, a paternity dispute may arise between seller and buyer. The Hanafi jurists have laid out clear rules based on the gestation period from the sale date. This lesson unpacks those rules, including the conditions for accepting the seller's claim, the precedence of claims, and the effects on the sale and the status of the mother and child. We will also examine an intra-Hanafi disagreement over how much of the price must be returned when the mother dies after the claim.

1
Identify the scenario and time frame
Begin by noting the key facts: a sale of a slave-girl, followed by her giving birth. The critical variable is the time between the sale and the birth: is it less than six months, more, or exactly six months?
The six-month period is based on the minimum possible human gestation in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Apply the rule for birth within six months
If she gives birth within six months of the sale (i.e., less than six months), the seller's claim of paternity is accepted. He must actively claim it. Consequences: the child is his son, the mother becomes his umm walad, the sale is rescinded, and he returns the full price.
The phrase 'أَوْلَى' indicates this is the preferred view.
3
Handle conflicting claims and birth after six months
If both seller and buyer claim paternity, the seller's claim takes precedence. If birth occurs more than six months after sale, the seller's claim is rejected unless the buyer explicitly confirms it.
The buyer's confirmation may be due to acknowledgment or lack of objection.
4
Consider death of the child or mother
If the child dies before the seller claims, the mother does not become umm walad. If the mother dies after the claim (and the child was born within six months), the seller takes the child and must return part or all of the price depending on which Hanafi view is followed.
This leads to the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf outlined in the next step.

Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on price return
Abu Hanifa holds that the seller returns the entire price because the mother and child are considered a single entity in the sale. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) say only the child's share of the price is returned, as the mother’s portion remains valid.
This dispute affects the buyer's reimbursement; the mufta bihi is not specified in this text.
