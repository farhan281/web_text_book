Types of Homicide in Hanafi Fiqh: Error, Quasi-Error, and Causation
Classifies homicide into three categories with respective consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the three categories of unintentional homicide (khaṭa', mā ujriya majrā al-khaṭa', and sabab) and state the legal consequences (diyah, kaffārah, sin) for each according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Unintentional killing carries expiation and blood money for direct error, but only blood money for indirect causation.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as expanded by al-Maydani, lays out the Hanafi classification of homicide beyond intentional murder. It is crucial to understand that not all accidental killings are treated equally. The school distinguishes between error in the act (khaṭa' fī al-fi'l), acts treated as error (e.g., a sleeping person rolling onto someone), and killing by cause (sabab). Each carries distinct obligations: expiation (kaffārah), blood money (diyah) on the 'āqilah, and the presence or absence of sin. This lesson will guide you through these categories and their consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the Arabic text carefully. Note the terms used: 'khaṭa' fī al-fi'l', 'mā ujriya majrā al-khaṭa'', and 'sabab'. The passage also implicitly excludes intentional murder ('amd), which is not covered here.
The three categories are: error in the act (خطأ في الفعل), what is treated as error (ما أُجري مجرى الخطأ), and killing by cause (القتل بسبب).
2
Extract legal consequences for each category
List what the passage says for each: 'khaṭa'' requires kaffārah and diyah on 'āqilah, no sin. 'Mā ujriya majrā al-khaṭa'' has the same ruling. 'Sabab' requires only diyah on 'āqilah, no kaffārah.
Note that diyah is always on the 'āqilah (paternal relatives) for unintentional killing.
3
Differentiate direct error from causation
The example of shooting at a target versus digging a well: in error, the action was aimed at a permissible target but hit a human; in causation, the action (digging) was not directly aimed at a person but created a deadly condition.
The key is whether the act was directly (mubāsharah) or indirectly (sabab) the cause of death.
4
Apply classification to new scenarios
Consider scenarios like a hunter aiming at an animal and hitting a person, or a person laying a trap in his own property. Determine which category and state the consequences.
Use the Hanafi definitions to avoid confusion with other schools.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as expanded by al-Maydani, classifies homicide into three categories beyond intentional murder. First, error in the act (khaṭa' fī al-fi'l): the actor intended a permissible act but mistakenly caused death, e.g., shooting at a target and hitting a person. The consequences are: expiation (kaffārah) on the killer, blood money (diyah) on the 'āqilah (the supporting male relatives), and no sin. Second, what is treated as error (mā ujriya majrā al-khaṭa'): acts like a sleeping person rolling onto and killing someone, which carry the same ruling. Third, killing by causation (sabab), such as digging a well or placing a stone on non-owned land leading to a death. This incurs only diyah on the 'āqilah and no kaffārah. The Hanafi school distinguishes these to assign liability appropriately: direct error requires expiation, while indirect causation does not.

Key Terms
خطأ (khaṭa'): Unintentional killing resulting from an action aimed at a permissible target.
عاقلة ('āqilah): The male paternal relatives of the killer who collectively pay the blood money (diyah) for unintentional killing.
كفارة (kaffārah): Expiation required for unintentional killing, typically fasting two consecutive months or equivalent.
سبب (sabab): Indirect causation of death, e.g., digging a well or placing an obstacle without ownership, leading to a person's death.

Diya for Shibh Amd and Khata: Core Rulings
Rulings on diya for shibh amd and khata with emphasis on taghliz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the diya amounts and categories for shibh amd and khata, and explain when taghliz applies.

Core Idea
Shibh amd diya is heavy only if paid in camels; khata diya is lighter and divided into fifths.

This lesson covers the foundational rules for diya in two types of homicide: shibh amd (quasi-intentional) and khata (mistake). The passage from al-Lubab explains the specific amounts, the division of camels, and the important principle that taghliz (heaviness) attaches only to camel payment. It also reminds us that kaffarah is obligatory on the killer separately, and that the diya amount is the same for Muslims and dhimmis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of homicide
Distinguish shibh amd from khata. Shibh amd: intentional act but unlikely to kill. Khata: accidental.
2
Recall the diya obligation for each
For shibh amd, diya mughallazah (heavy) on 'aqila; for khata, diya on 'aqila. Kaffarah on killer in both.
3
Learn the camel breakdowns
Shibh amd: 100 camels in four categories (25 each). Khata: 100 camels in five categories (20 each).
4
Understand taghliz condition
Taghliz (heavy) only when diya is paid in camels. If paid from other property (like gold or silver), it is not heavy.
5
Note the equal treatment of Muslim and dhimmi
Diya is equal regardless of faith.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Diyat. It first defines shibh amd (quasi-intentional homicide): the 'aqila must pay a heavy diya, and the killer must observe kaffarah. The diya for shibh amd is 100 camels divided equally into four categories by age. This heavy diya is only applicable when paid in camels; if the court assesses diya in other property, the surcharge of taghliz does not apply. Then it defines khata (homicide by mistake): the 'aqila pays the diya, the killer pays kaffarah. The diya for khata is 100 camels but divided into five categories, 20 each. Alternatively, the diya can be paid in gold (1,000 dinars) or silver (10,000 dirhams). Abu Hanifa restricts diya to these three types (camels, gold, silver). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad add cattle, sheep, and garments as permissible sources of diya, each with specific numbers. The passage concludes that the diya for a Muslim and a dhimmī is equal—a reminder that the sanctity of life is the same regardless of faith.

Key Terms
عاقلة (ʿāqila): The male agnatic relatives of the killer who collectively pay diya for homicide by mistake or quasi-intentional.
دية مغلظة (diyat mughallaẓa): Heavy diya for shibh amd, which is a full diya with additional severity (taghliz) due to the nature of the killing.
شبه عمد (shibh ʿamd): Quasi-intentional homicide: act is intentional but the means are not likely to kill.
تغليظ (taghliz): Heaviness or severity applied to diya, here only when paid in camels.
كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation required upon the killer for shibh amd or khata, separate from diya.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Property Types for Diya
Intra-Hanafi dispute on permissible property for diya payment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on permissible property for diya and explain the practical consequence.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa restricts diya to camels, gold, and silver; Sahibayn expand it to cattle, sheep, and garments.

While the basic diya amounts are agreed, the issue of which types of property can be used to pay diya created an important intra-Hanafi dispute. Abu Hanifa held that only camels, gold, and silver are valid, whereas Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani added cattle, sheep, and garments. This lesson examines their positions and the practical implications of this ikhtilaf.

Only three types: camels, gold, and silver.

4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
If a judge orders diya in cows, it is valid per Sahibayn but invalid per Abu Hanifa.
5
Consider the mufta bihi
Although not specified in the passage, later Hanafi practice tends to follow Abu Hanifa.

Diya for Specific Body Parts and Organs
Enumeration of diya amounts for specific body parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi diya amount for any specified body part or organ listed in the text.

Core Idea
Full diya for life and each paired organ; half for one; eyelids are a quarter each; all fingers equal at one-tenth.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as explained in al-Lubab, provides a comprehensive list of body parts and their prescribed diya (blood money) in Hanafi fiqh. Memorizing these amounts is essential for a faqih to properly adjudicate injury compensation. We will walk through the categories and their specific values.

Guided Path

2
Determine if paired or single
Check if the organ comes in pairs (eyes, hands, etc.) or is single (nose, tongue). The ruling then gives a full diya for both, and half diya for one.
Exceptions: eyelids are treated differently.
3
Apply the rule for paired organs
For paired organs, the text states full diya for both. For one of them, half diya. This applies to eyebrows, eyes, hands, feet, ears, lips, testicles, and women's breasts.
The text explicitly says: 'And for each one of these: half the diya.'
4
Handle special cases
Eyelids: full diya for both, but only quarter diya for one. Fingers: each finger one-tenth, regardless of type. Each finger has three joints; one joint is one-third of that finger's diya.
The beard and hair: full diya if permanently lost (shaved and does not grow).
5
Memorize the list
Familiarize yourself with every item in the list to be able to recall the diya for any given body part.
Use the passage as a memorization anchor.

Fixed Diya and Hukumat for Hand, Extra Finger, Child's Eye, and Mudihah
Rulings on fixed diyat and hukumat for specific injuries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fixed diya amounts for specific injuries and identify when hukumat ('arbitration by just persons') applies instead.

Core Idea
A fixed diya is prescribed for the palm and for complete senses, but hukumat applies for extra parts, minor limbs, and uncertain faculties.

In Hanafi fiqh, not every injury has a predetermined compensation. This lesson explains which injuries receive a fixed diya—like the palm—and which require valuation by just persons (hukumat). Understanding this distinction is crucial for accurate application of diyat law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the injury type
Read the passage and list each injury mentioned: hand with half forearm, extra finger, child's eye/penis/tongue when health unknown, mudihah with loss of mind/hair or senses.
Each injury is treated differently based on whether a fixed diya is defined or not.
2
Apply the fixed diya rule
For the palm cut with half forearm, the palm receives half the full diya (5000 dirhams). No fixed amount for the extra part (half forearm).
Half diya for a unilateral limb is standard in Hanafi law.
3
Recognize hukumat cases
Extra finger, child's eye/penis/tongue, and the extra part of the forearm all require hukumat (arbitration) because no fixed diya exists.
Hukumat is performed by trustworthy experts who evaluate the injury and determine compensation.
4
Understand mudihah consequences
If a mudihah (scalp wound exposing bone) causes loss of mind or hair, the arsh (compensation) for the mudihah is included in the overall diya. If it causes loss of hearing, sight, or speech, the arsh of mudihah is paid separately along with the full diya.

This lesson establishes the foundational distinction in diyah liability: when the killer pays from his own pocket and when his clan (aqila) pays. The passage from al-Quduri gives clear-cut rules for several scenarios, starting with intentional killings where qisas is waived due to legal doubt (shubha).

2

When an intentional killing escapes qisas due to a legal doubt (shubha), the diyah is on the killer's own wealth, not his aqila. Understand the reasoning: the killer's fault is at a level requiring personal liability.

If the killer confesses to a homicide, the diyah is on him alone, and his aqila are not implicated. Similarly, if the victim's family settles for diyah instead of qisas, the killer pays from his wealth.

Defines the 'aqila system for diyah payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for diyah payment by the 'aqila in cases of quasi-intentional and accidental homicide, and identify the different categories of 'aqila (diwan, tribe, freed slave, client of allegiance).

Core Idea
The 'aqila, not the killer alone, bears the diyah, paid over three years with an individual cap.

In Hanafi law, diyah (blood money) for quasi-intentional and accidental homicide is a communal responsibility. The passage from Kitab al-Ma'aqil explains who the 'aqila are, how the payment is structured, and special cases. Let's break down these rules to understand the system of collective liability that prevents the killer from being crushed by debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the homicide types
Note that diyah falls on the 'aqila only for shibh al-'amd (quasi-intentional) and khata' (accidental) homicide, not for intentional murder which involves qisas.
Intentional murder may also have diyah but not through 'aqila in all cases.
2
Determine the 'aqila category
Check whether the killer is a soldier registered in the diwan, a civilian from a tribe, a freed slave, or a mawla al-muwalah. Each has a different 'aqila.
For diwan soldiers, 'aqila = people of diwan; for tribesmen, 'aqila = his tribe; for freed slave, 'aqila = master's tribe; for mawla, 'aqila = master and his tribe.
3
Understand the payment method
The diyah is spread over three years. For soldiers, it is deducted from stipends. For tribes, it is distributed among members with a cap of 4 dirhams per person total (1 dirham 2 daniqs per year).
The cap ensures no one is overburdened; if the tribe cannot cover, nearby tribes are added.
4
Include the killer
The killer himself participates in the payment as one of the 'aqila, contributing like any other member.
This is a Hanafi feature; other schools exclude the killer.
5
Apply the minimum threshold
The 'aqila is not liable for less than half a tenth (1/20) of the full diyah. Smaller amounts are likely borne by the killer alone.
Full diyah is 100 camels or equivalent; 1/20 = 5 camels or equivalent.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of al-Ma'aqil sets out the Hanafi rules for the 'Aqila (the clan or group responsible for paying blood money) in cases of quasi-intentional homicide (shibh al-'amd) and accidental homicide (khata'). The key principles are: (1) Diyah that becomes obligatory by the killing itself falls on the 'Aqila, not on the killer. (2) If the killer is a soldier registered in the diwan (military register), his 'Aqila are the people of the diwan; the diyah is deducted from their state stipends over three years. (3) If the killer is not registered, his 'Aqila is his tribe (qabilah). The payment is spread over three years, with a cap on annual individual contribution: no one pays more than four dirhams total over three years, specifically one dirham and two daniqs per year. (4) If the tribe cannot cover the diyah, the closest neighboring tribes are brought in. (5) The killer himself joins the 'Aqila and contributes like a member. (6) For a freed slave, his 'Aqila is the tribe of his master. For a client of allegiance (mawla al-muwala), responsibility lies with his master and his tribe. (7) The minimum that the 'Aqila must bear is one-twentieth (half of one-tenth) of the full diyah. These rules ensure that the burden of blood money is distributed communally, preventing the killer from being crushed by debt.

Key Terms
شبه العمد (shibh al-'amd): Quasi-intentional homicide, where the act is intentional but the means is unlikely to cause death, or the intention is not to kill.
العاقلة (al-'aqilah): The group of male agnatic relatives (or tribe/register) who are responsible for paying diyah on behalf of the killer.
الديوان (al-diwan): The military register or government payroll list; those receiving state salaries.
مولى الموالاة (mawla al-muwalah): A client by contract of allegiance, not by freeing; someone who enters into a clientage relationship with a tribe for protection.
نصف عشر الدية (nisf 'ushr al-diyah): Half a tenth (1/20) of the full blood money; the minimum amount the 'aqila is obliged to pay.

Diyat — Blood Money
beginner
6 min
Rulings of the 'Aqila (Blood Money Payers)
States several rules regarding who bears the burden of blood money

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which the 'aqila bears liability for diyah and identify the exceptions.

Core Idea
The 'aqila pays only for accidental harm above half a tenth; lesser amounts and intentional harm fall on the offender.

This passage from Quduri's matn defines the scope of the 'aqila's obligation. Understanding when the 'aqila is liable—and when it is not—is essential for applying the diyat system correctly. The rules protect both the victim's right to compensation and the offender's accountability for their own actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline obligation
The 'aqila bears diyah for accidental homicide or injury when the amount is half of 'ushr (5 camels) or more. Lesser amounts are on the offender personally.
Half of 'ushr = 5 camels out of full diyah of 100 camels.
2
Note the four exceptions to 'aqila liability
The 'aqila is not liable for: (1) intentional injury, (2) injury to a slave, (3) a crime confessed to without witnesses unless the 'aqila affirms the confession, and (4) amounts settled by compromise (sulh).
Each exception has a rationale: intentional acts are solely on the perpetrator; slaves are property; confession is personal; sulh is a negotiated agreement.
3
Understand the rule for injury to a slave
If a free person accidentally injures a slave, the liability falls on the free person's 'aqila—not on the free person alone. This is because the 'aqila system applies to accidental harm to both free and slave.
The slave is considered property, but the 'aqila still bears the accidental harm.
4
Identify the fallback if no 'aqila exists
If the offender has no 'aqila (no relatives or tribe), the diyah is paid from the public treasury (bayt al-mal) to ensure the victim's family is compensated.
This reflects the state's role in ensuring justice.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of 'Aqila (the group responsible for paying blood money) outlines the limits of the 'aqila's liability under Hanafi law. The general rule is that the 'aqila bears the burden for accidental killing or injury if the diyah (blood money) amounts to half of a tenth or more. Lesser amounts are the personal responsibility of the offender. The 'aqila is not liable for intentional injury ('amd) because that falls solely on the perpetrator, nor for injuries involving a slave, since the slave's value is not covered by the 'aqila. If the offender confesses to a crime without witnesses, the 'aqila is not bound unless they choose to accept the confession. Similarly, if a settlement (sulh) has been reached, the 'aqila does not bear the amount. An important rule: if a free person accidentally injures a slave, the liability falls on the free person's 'aqila, not on the free person alone, because the slave is considered property but the 'aqila system applies to accidental harm to both free and slave. Finally, if the offender has no 'aqila (e.g., no relatives or tribe), the state treasury (bayt al-mal) pays the diyah, ensuring that the victim's family is not left uncompensated.

Key Terms
عاقلة ('aqila): The group of relatives (usually the 'asaba) responsible for paying blood money (diyah) on behalf of an accidental killer or injurer.
دية (diyah): Blood money paid to the victim's heirs for homicide or injury.
عشر ('ushr): Tenth. Here, half of a tenth refers to 5 camels out of 100 (full diyah).
