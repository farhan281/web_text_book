Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ownership of a spouse dissolves marriage automatically in Hanafi law.

In Hanafi fiqh, marriage and ownership are fundamentally incompatible. This lesson examines a clear ruling: when a husband or wife comes to own the other person—whether fully or through a share—the marriage ends immediately. This principle prevents a person from being both master and spouse simultaneously, which would create legal contradictions.

Guided Path

Identify the triggering event
Determine which spouse acquires ownership of the other: husband owns wife, wife owns husband, or either owns a share (shiqs) of the other.
The ruling applies equally regardless of gender or whether ownership is full or partial.
2
State the legal consequence
Recognize that the marriage immediately ends (tafarruq) upon acquisition of ownership. The separation is automatic, not requiring divorce or judicial decree.
3
Understand the rationale
Explain why marriage cannot coexist with ownership: a person cannot simultaneously be the master and spouse of the same individual, as these roles entail conflicting rights and obligations.
This is based on the principle of incompatibility (adam al-jam').

Conditions for Li'an and When Hadd Applies
States conditions for li'an obligation and exemption cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which li'an becomes wajib and identify when the husband receives hadd instead.

Core Idea
Li'an is only wajib when both spouses are eligible for testimony and the wife is immune to hadd for qadhf.

In this lesson, we examine the conditions that trigger the obligation of li'an when a husband accuses his wife of adultery or denies paternity. The passage from al-Lubab clarifies when the husband must undergo li'an and when he instead faces the hadd punishment for false accusation. Understanding these conditions is essential to correctly apply the law in marital disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Li'an becomes wajib when the husband accuses his wife of adultery or denies paternity and certain conditions are met. Determine the hukm level (wajib).
2
List the conditions for li'an to be wajib
Both spouses must be ahl al-shahadah (eligible for testimony: free, Muslim, just, adult, sane). The wife must be one whose accuser would be hadded for false accusation (not a slave, disbeliever, or previous hadd recipient). The wife must demand the legal consequence.
If the husband denies lineage, the demand may not be required.
3
Identify the consequences of refusal
If the husband refuses li'an, the judge imprisons him until he performs li'an or admits falsehood (then hadd). If the wife refuses after the husband's li'an, she is imprisoned until she performs li'an or admits truth.
4
Distinguish cases where hadd applies instead
If the husband is a slave, disbeliever, or previously hadded for qadhf, he receives hadd and no li'an. If the husband is eligible but the wife is a slave, disbeliever, or previously hadded, then no hadd on him and no li'an.
5
Summarize the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is as stated: li'an is wajib when conditions are met; otherwise, the case falls into one of the two exception categories.

The Exact Procedure of Li'an (Mutual Imprecation)
Describes the exact wording and steps of li'an.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exact wording and sequence of li'an as performed by the husband and wife according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Li'an requires exact wording: husband curses himself if lying; wife invokes Allah's anger if the husband is truthful.

Li'an is a solemn procedure when a husband accuses his wife of adultery without witnesses. The Qur'an (24:6-9) prescribes a specific exchange of oaths and invocations. This lesson presents the exact Hanafi procedure, emphasizing the precise Arabic wording that must be followed for the li'an to be valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the presiding authority
The judge (qadi) initiates the li'an, beginning with the husband.
2
List the husband's four testimonies
The husband testifies four times, swearing by Allah that he is truthful in his accusation. The wording is fixed: "Ashhadu billāhi innī lamin al-ṣādiqīn fīmā ramaytuhā bihī min al-zinā."
The phrase "fīmā ramaytuhā bihī min al-zinā" may be omitted if the context is clear, but the core is the testimony.
3
Identify the husband's fifth statement
After the fourth testimony, the husband says: "In la‘natullāhi ‘alayhi in kāna min al-kādhibīn fīmā ramāhā bihī min al-zinā." This invokes a curse upon himself if he is lying.
The word "la‘nah" (curse) is used here, not "ghadab" (anger).
4
Outline the wife's four testimonies
After the husband finishes, the wife testifies four times: "Ashhadu billāhi innahu lamin al-kādhibīn fīmā ramānī bihī min al-zinā."
She swears that her husband is among the liars.
5
Describe the wife's fifth statement
The wife then says: "In ghaḍaballāhi ‘alayhā in kāna min al-ṣādiqīn fīmā ramānī bihī min al-zinā." This invokes Allah's anger upon herself if her husband is truthful.
The wife does NOT invoke a curse (la‘nah); she invokes anger (ghadab). This is a common point of confusion.

The Legal Effects of Li'an: Separation and Special Cases
Explains consequences of li'an with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on separation after li'an, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, and identify the conditions for valid paternity denial.

Core Idea
Li'an results in separation; the Hanafi school disputes whether it is a ba'in divorce or permanent prohibition.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri details the legal effects after the spouses have performed li'an. It covers the obligatory separation, the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the nature of that separation, and special rules for paternity denial, retraction, and when the spouses lack capacity for li'an. Understanding these points is essential for applying this institution correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary effect of li'an
The passage begins by stating that when the couple completes li'an, the judge must separate them. This is the immediate legal consequence.
The separation is mandatory; the judge does not have discretion.
2
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on the nature of separation
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold the separation is a ba'in (irrevocable) divorce. Abu Yusuf argues it is a permanent prohibition (tahrim mu'abbad). The mufta bihi is the ba'in divorce view.
The practical difference: permanent prohibition forbids remarriage forever; ba'in divorce allows remarriage with a new contract.
3
Examine the rules for paternity denial
If the husband accuses his wife regarding a child, his denial of paternity must be made immediately after birth (or within nifas per Sahibayn). If done in time, li'an negates lineage; if delayed, lineage is established but li'an still occurs.
The timing is critical: immediately, at time of congratulations, or purchase of birth equipment.
4
Consider the exceptional cases where li'an is not applicable
Li'an is not performed if the wife is a minor or insane, if the husband is mute, or if the husband merely denies pregnancy without explicit adultery accusation. In these, no hadd applies either.
Li'an requires sanity and majority; the mute cannot perform the required verbal oath.
5
Understand the effect of the husband's retraction
If the husband later admits false accusation, he receives the hadd punishment for qadhf, and it becomes permissible for him to remarry his ex-wife (because the separation was ba'in, not permanent).
The hadd for qadhf is 80 lashes; this is the same as for false accusation of any chaste woman.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Denial of a twin triggers hadd or li'an, but both lineages are established.

Twin births present a unique legal puzzle under li'an. What happens when the husband accepts one child but rejects the other? This lesson examines two classic scenarios from the Hanafi school. Understanding these rulings clarifies how the law balances the presumption of paternity, spousal accusation, and the integrity of lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage presents two distinct scenarios: (1) husband denies the first twin and acknowledges the second; (2) husband acknowledges the first and denies the second. Note the symmetry.
2
Determine lineage in each case
In both scenarios, the ruling is that the lineage of both twins is established. This is a key principle: partial denial does not break paternity for either child.
Established by legal presumption (the first) or by admission (the second) depending on the case.
3

When the husband denies the first twin, he is subject to the hadd penalty for qadhf (false accusation). When he denies the second twin, he must perform li'an (mutual imprecation) regarding that child.
The difference hinges on whether the denial is considered slander against the mother (hadd) or repudiation of paternity (li'an).
4

The first twin is presumed legitimate due to the minimal time between births; denying him implies the mother committed adultery before marriage (if possible) or before the acknowledgment of the second. The second twin's denial is a direct negation of paternity, requiring li'an.
This logic preserves the mother's honor against slander while allowing the husband to legally disown a child through li'an.
5
Memorize the principle
In twin birth cases, the husband cannot selectively establish lineage; either both children are his or he must perform li'an for the denied one. The penalty (hadd or li'an) depends on which twin is denied.
This is a fixed Hanafi ruling with no intra-madhhab variation.

Ruling on evidence and oaths in mahr disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi tiers of evidence and mahr al-mithl when spouses dispute the mahr amount.

Core Idea
In a mahr dispute, the wife's evidence is preferred; if none, mutual oaths but marriage remains valid, and mahr al-mithl is imposed.

When a husband and wife disagree on the agreed mahr (dowry), how does Islamic law resolve the conflict without dissolving the marriage? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling from Al-Lubab, which establishes a clear hierarchy: first evidence, then mutual oaths, and finally an objective standard—mahr al-mithl (the proper dowry). You will learn the precise procedure and how the judge determines the amount.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of dispute
Recognize that the dispute is solely over the amount of mahr, not over the validity of the marriage or other terms. Both spouses agree a marriage occurred but differ on the agreed sum.
If the dispute were over the existence of the marriage itself, different rules apply.
2
Apply the evidence rule
If only one party brings evidence (bayyinah), that party wins. If both bring evidence, the woman's evidence is given precedence (unlike in sale disputes). If neither has evidence, proceed to tahaluf (mutual oaths).
The preference for the woman's evidence is based on the principle that the default mahr is not less than mahr al-mithl.
3
Apply tahaluf (mutual oaths) procedure
Each party swears an oath affirming their claim. The marriage is NOT dissolved; it remains valid. This differs from sales where tahaluf voids the contract.
The oath of the husband comes first, then the wife's oath.
4
Determine mahr al-mithl
The judge calculates mahr al-mithl based on the wife's family status, beauty, age, etc., by analogy to similar women.
Mahr al-mithl is an objective benchmark, not tied to the disputed claims.
5
Adjust actual payment to claims
Compare mahr al-mithl to the amounts claimed: if it equals or is less than the husband's admission, pay his admission; if it equals or exceeds the wife's claim, pay her claim; if it falls between, pay mahr al-mithl.
This ensures the wife receives at least mahr al-mithl, protecting her rights.
