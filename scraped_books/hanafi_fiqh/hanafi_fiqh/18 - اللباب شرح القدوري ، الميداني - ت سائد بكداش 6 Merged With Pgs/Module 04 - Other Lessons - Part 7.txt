Learning Objective

Core Idea
A musta’min cannot stay beyond one year without becoming a dhimmi; if he returns to dar al-harb, his property becomes vulnerable.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on the musta’min — a non-Muslim who enters Muslim territory with temporary safe conduct. The classical text from al-Quduri and al-Maydani sets clear limits: a one-year maximum stay, after which jizya is imposed and he becomes a permanent dhimmi. If he returns to dar al-harb, his assets in dar al-Islam are at risk, and if captured or killed, his debts are voided and deposits become fay'. Understanding this ruling clarifies the temporary nature of aman and its legal consequences.

This is a condition of the aman.
2

If the musta’min stays the full year, he must pay jizya, becomes a dhimmi, and is no longer allowed to return to dar al-harb.
This is a permanent change of status.
3
Consequence of returning to dar al-harb
If the musta’min returns to dar al-harb (without becoming dhimmi first?), his blood becomes permissible (i.e., he loses protection), and any property he left in dar al-Islam is at risk.
The text says 'his blood becomes permissible by his return'.

If the musta’min is captured or killed after returning, his debts are voided and any deposits he left become fay’ (state property).

Intra-Hanafi dispute on revived land classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi opinions on whether revived dead land becomes 'ushr or kharaj and apply the correct ruling based on either vicinity or water source.

Core Idea
Vicinity (Abu Yusuf) or water source (Muhammad) determines the tax status of revived dead land.

When a person revives dead land (mawat), a dispute arose between two giants of the Hanafi school: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Their disagreement centers on the factor that determines whether the revived land becomes 'ushr land (paying 10% produce tax) or kharaj land (paying a fixed land tax). This lesson examines both views and their practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
What is the dispute about? The classification of revived dead land as 'ushr or kharaj.
2
State Abu Yusuf's view
Based on vicinity (hiz). If near kharaj land, it is kharaj; if near 'ushr land, it is 'ushr. He cites Basra as 'ushr by consensus.
3
State Muhammad's view
Based on water source. If revived with water from an unowned source (well, spring, major river), it is 'ushr; if from man-made Persian canals, it is kharaj.
4
Identify the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: the tax rate (10% vs. fixed kharaj) on the revived land.
5
Note the operative ruling
Both views are transmitted; later Hanafi scholars often prefer Muhammad's view, but both are valid.
The mufta bihi may vary by region.

Kharaj Rates and Exemptions: The Ruling of Umar (RA)
Detail of kharaj rates and exemption conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the kharaj rates per jarib for irrigated land, alfalfa, vineyards, and date palms, and identify when kharaj is waived due to natural disasters.

Core Idea
Kharaj is a land tax tied to productivity: it is waived if natural disaster prevents farming, but not if the owner deliberately neglects the land.

The kharaj tax, established by Caliph Umar (RA) on the Sawad region, is not a fixed personal tax but varies by land type and productivity. Understanding the rates and the conditions for exemption is crucial for applying the correct rulings in agrarian taxation within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline rate for irrigated land
For every jarib of land that receives irrigation water, the kharaj is one hashimi qafiz (equivalent to one sa') and one dirham.
The qafiz hashimi is a measure of capacity, and the dirham is a silver coin.
2
Identify rates for alfalfa, vineyards, and date palms
For a jarib of alfalfa (ratbah), the kharaj is five dirhams. For a jarib of dense vineyards or dense date palms, it is ten dirhams.
Higher rates reflect higher profitability of these crops.
3
Note the principle of taxation according to capacity
For other types of land, the kharaj is set based on what the farmer can bear. If the tax is too burdensome, the Imam reduces it.
This shows flexibility and justice in Islamic fiscal policy.
4
Identify the exemptions from kharaj
Kharaj is waived if water overwhelms the land, if irrigation is cut off, or if a pestilence destroys the crop.
These are natural disasters beyond the owner's control.
5
Distinguish between disaster and owner's neglect
If the owner deliberately leaves the land uncultivated (ta'til), kharaj remains due, because the tax is on the land's potential productivity.
This prevents landowners from evading tax by willful neglect.

Bequest of a Fraction: Fungible vs. Non-Fungible Assets
Rulings on bequest of a third of a specific asset type when partially destroyed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between fungible (mithli) and non-fungible (qimi) assets in the context of a bequest of a fraction (thuluth) of a specific asset type, and apply the Hanafi rule to determine the legatee's entitlement when part of that asset is destroyed.

Core Idea
For fungibles, a bequest of a fraction gives the legatee all remaining after partial destruction; for non-fungibles, only the fraction of what remains.

Imagine a testator bequeaths 'one-third of my dirhams' or 'one-third of my garments.' Before the estate is distributed, two-thirds of that specific asset is accidentally destroyed. How much does the legatee receive? The Hanafi master jurists recognized that the nature of the asset—whether it is fungible (mithli) like money or sheep, or non-fungible (qimi) like garments—changes the ruling. This lesson unpacks the distinction and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest type
Determine whether the bequest is of a fraction of a specific asset class (e.g., 'one-third of my dirhams') or a fraction of the overall estate. Here it is the former.
The passage addresses bequests of a fraction of a specific type, not a general fraction of the estate.
2
Categorize the asset as fungible or non-fungible
Dirhams and sheep are fungible (mithli): they are interchangeable and typically replaced by similar items. Garments are non-fungible (qimi): each item is unique.
This categorization is key to the differentiation in the ruling.
3
Apply the partial destruction scenario
Assume two-thirds of the bequeathed asset type perishes. The remaining one-third is what is actually available for distribution.
The ruling states that the bequest 'comes out of one-third of what remains of his estate' – meaning the one-third limit applies to the net estate after debts, but here the focus is on the specific asset.
4
Determine the legatee's entitlement
For fungibles (dirhams/sheep), the legatee takes all the surviving third because the bequest is of a fraction of the genus, and the surviving part represents the entire bequeathed share. For non-fungibles (garments), the legatee takes only one-third of the surviving garments because the bequest is of a fraction of each item or of the set, not the whole.
The rationale: fungibles are considered replaceable; non-fungibles retain their individuality.

Whoever bequeaths to a man one-third of his dirhams or one-third of his sheep, then two-thirds of that perish and one-third remains, and it comes out of one-third of what remains of his estate: he gets all that remains. If he bequeaths to him one-third of his garments, then two-thirds of them perish and one-third remains, and it comes out of one-third of what remains of his estate: he is entitled to only one-third of what remains of the garments.
Source pages p. 379

Commentary
This ruling distinguishes between fungible goods (dirhams, sheep) and non-fungible (garments). For dirhams and sheep, the bequest is of one-third of that specific type. If two-thirds perish, the remaining one-third is the entire bequest because the bequest is taken from that type, and the legatee gets all of it. But for garments, the bequest is of one-third of the garments as a specific asset class. After two-thirds perish, the remaining one-third is still considered the entire bequest; however, the legatee only gets one-third of the surviving garments because the bequest is of a fraction (one-third) of that class, not the entire surviving stock. The difference arises because dirhams and sheep are considered a single genus that can be replaced, while garments are distinct items; the bequest of a third of garments means a third of each garment? Or a third of the total number? The Hanafi ruling treats garments as specific items such that the legatee gets a fraction of each item remaining.

Key Terms

مثلي (mithlī): Fungible asset; items that are interchangeable and replaceable by similar units.
قيمي (qīmī): Non-fungible asset; items that are unique and cannot be exactly replaced.

Paying a Bequest from an Estate with Cash and Debts
Ruling on paying a bequest of a fixed sum when the estate includes cash and debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for paying a bequest of a fixed sum when the estate includes both cash and debts.

Core Idea
A bequest of a fixed sum is paid first from cash; if insufficient, the legatee receives one-third of cash and then one-third of each debt collected.

When a person bequeaths a specific amount and leaves behind both cash and debts owed to him, the payment method depends on whether the bequest can be covered from one-third of the cash. This lesson explains the step-by-step process as outlined by Imam al-Quduri and the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest amount and estate components
Determine the fixed sum bequeathed and classify estate assets into cash (ayn) and debts (dayn).
Debts here are amounts owed to the deceased, not liabilities.
2
Check if the bequest fits within one-third of the cash
Compare the bequest amount to one-third of the cash alone. If the bequest is less than or equal to that, it can be paid immediately from cash.
The bequest must also be within one-third of the total estate; this ruling assumes a valid bequest.
3
If yes, pay the full amount from cash
The legatee receives the entire bequest from the cash portion immediately.
4
If no, pay one-third of cash now
The legatee receives one-third of the cash on hand immediately.
This is a partial payment, not the full bequest.
5
Collect debts and pay one-third of each collection
As debts owed to the deceased are collected, the legatee takes one-third of each collected amount until he has received the full bequest.
The process continues until the bequest is satisfied.

Commentary
This ruling details payment of a bequest of a fixed monetary amount when the estate includes both liquid cash (ayn) and outstanding debts owed to the deceased (dayn). The bequest is limited to one-third of the estate. First, attempt to pay the full thousand from one-third of the cash. If insufficient, the legatee receives one-third of the cash immediately, and then as debts are collected, he receives one-third of each collection until he receives the full thousand. This ensures the bequest is paid gradually as assets become liquid.

دين (dayn): A debt owed to the deceased, i.e., money receivable from others.
