The Recommended Nature of Wills in the Hanafi School
Opening ruling on the legal status of wills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wills as mustahabb and distinguish it from obligatory acts, explaining the legal basis and the practical consequence of this classification.

Core Idea
A will is not a duty but a recommended act — you incur no sin for omitting it, yet its neglect is a missed opportunity.

The opening line of the chapter on wills in classical Hanafi texts immediately clarifies a key principle: making a will is not a legal obligation. For many, this may be surprising, as the need to distribute one's estate after death seems pressing. However, the Hanafi school, based on the primary sources, classifies it as mustahabb (recommended). This lesson unpacks that ruling and its implications for every Muslim who wishes to plan their legacy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the first sentence: 'الوصية غير واجبة، وهي مستحبة'. The phrase 'غير واجبة' directly negates obligation, and 'مستحبة' affirms recommendation. Note the precise Arabic terms used.
The Hanafi school uses 'مستحبة' (mustahabb) for recommended acts; synonyms include 'مندوب' (mandub) and 'سنة' (sunnah) in other contexts.
2
Understand the hukm level
Mustahabb means an act that is rewarded if done but not punished if left. It is below wajib (obligatory) and sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah). The omission of a mustahabb is not a sin, but performing it earns divine reward.
Hanafi usul distinguishes five ahkam: fard, wajib, sunnah, mustahabb, mubah. This ruling falls under mustahabb.
3
Contrast with other schools
Some schools (e.g., Malikis in certain cases) consider a will obligatory when one has debts or rights to be settled. The Hanafi position is more permissive, leaving it as a recommendatory act.
This difference (mukhtalaf fih) arises from different interpretations of Quranic verses and hadith.
4
Apply the ruling to practice
A Muslim may choose not to make a will without sin, but it is encouraged to do so, especially if they have specific wishes for their property beyond the automatic inheritance shares.
The text later recommends bequeathing less than one-third, showing the practical guidance even for this recommended act.

The maximum that can be bequeathed is one-third of the estate, and anything beyond is invalid unless the heirs approve. A will for a killer (e.g., one who caused death) is void. Interestingly, a Muslim may bequeath to a non-Muslim and vice versa, as the basis is ownership, not religion. It is recommended to bequeath less than one-third to avoid burdening the heirs.

Limits and Conditions of Valid Bequests
Rules restricting bequests in the Hanafi school

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five key restrictions on bequests in the Hanafi school: prohibition for heirs (without consent), cap at one-third, prohibition for killers, permissibility across religions, and recommendation to be less than one-third.

Core Idea
A bequest cannot exceed one-third, must avoid heirs and killers, but crosses religious lines — a delicate balance of property and piety.

The opening passage of the chapter on wills does not stop at the basic ruling; it immediately lays out the boundaries within which a bequest is valid. These boundaries protect the rights of legal heirs, prevent abuse, and ensure that the will serves its recommended purpose without harming those whom the law already provides for. This lesson breaks down each restriction and explains the rationale behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five rules from the text
Read the passage sentence by sentence. Extract each distinct rule: (1) No bequest to an heir unless other heirs consent. (2) No bequest exceeding one-third of the estate. (3) No bequest to a killer. (4) Permissibility of bequests between Muslim and non-Muslim. (5) Recommendation to bequeath less than one-third.
The last sentence uses 'يستحب' (it is recommended), indicating a separate rule from the prohibition on exceeding one-third.
2
Understand the rationale for each rule
For each rule, think about the wisdom: (1) Heirs already have fixed shares; a bequest to them upsets the balance. (2) The one-third limit prevents depriving heirs. (3) The killer is excluded to prevent benefit from crime. (4) Property rights are not religion-dependent. (5) Leaving more for heirs is better, as per the hadith.
The Hanafi school bases these on hadith: 'The Prophet (ﷺ) said: A will for an heir is not valid unless the other heirs permit.' and 'Your heirs are better off wealthy than begging.'
3
Classify each rule as condition or prohibition
Determine whether each rule is a condition for validity (shart) or a prohibition (mani). For example, the heir restriction is a condition (requires consent). The one-third limit is a condition (maximum). The killer prohibition is a preventer (mani). The interfaith permission is a permission (rukhsah). The recommendation is not a condition but guidance.
Ranking helps when applying the rules to real cases.
4
Apply the rules to a hypothetical scenario
Take an example: A man dies leaving a wife and two children. He left a bequest of half his estate to a friend, and another bequest of 10% to his brother (an heir). Assess validity: Bequest to friend exceeds one-third -> invalid without heirs' consent. Bequest to brother (heir) -> invalid unless wife and children consent. Also, the brother is not a killer and is Muslim, so those do not apply.
This shows how multiple rules interact.

Acceptance of Will and Executor Qualifications
Rulings on acceptance of will, executorship, and ownership of bequest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for acceptance of a will, transfer of ownership of a bequest, and qualifications of an executor.

Core Idea
A will is only binding after death; acceptance by the legatee is required for ownership, and executors must be free, Muslim, and upright.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents several key rulings on wills (wasiyyah). It clarifies that acceptance or rejection of a will during the testator's lifetime has no legal effect. Ownership of the bequest is contingent on acceptance after death, with one exception. The passage also sets strict qualifications for an executor (wasi): a slave, non-Muslim, or sinful person cannot serve, and a testator cannot appoint his own slave as executor if there are adult heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what action is being ruled upon and its legal classification. The passage gives rules for acceptance of a will and appointment of an executor.
2
Analyze the timing of acceptance
Note that acceptance or rejection during the testator's life is void; only after death is it valid. This distinguishes the will from a contract that binds immediately.
3
Understand the transfer of ownership
The bequeathed property is owned by acceptance after death. There is one exception: if the legatee dies before accepting, the property passes to his heirs as if he had accepted.
This shows that acceptance is treated as occurring at death automatically in some cases.
4
Examine executor qualifications
The appointor cannot choose a slave, non-Muslim, or sinner as executor. If he does, the judge replaces them. Appointing one's own slave is invalid if there are adult heirs.
The executor must be capable of managing property and must be trustworthy (adil).

Core Idea
An executor's rejection is valid only if stated in the testator's presence.

This passage establishes a key condition for the validity of an executor's rejection of appointment. The ruling ensures that the testator receives clear, direct communication, preventing ambiguity and protecting the testator's intent. Understanding this principle helps in managing estate planning and related fiqh rulings.

Identify the act in question

2
Distinguish between acceptance and rejection scenarios

If the executor rejects in the testator's absence (e.g., tells a third party), the rejection is legally invalid. The appointment is not considered declined.
This protects the testator's reliance on the initial appointment.
4
Apply the ruling for rejection in presence
If the executor rejects directly in the testator's presence, the rejection is valid and the appointment is void.
5

Direct communication is essential for the validity of rejection. Silence or indirect communication does not constitute a legally binding rejection.

Ownership of Bequest by Acceptance (Except One Case)
General rule and exception for ownership of bequeathed property.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Acceptance is required to own a bequest; but if the legatee dies before accepting, his heirs inherit it.

In Hanafi law, a bequest (wasiyyah) does not automatically transfer ownership to the legatee at the testator's death. Instead, it requires the legatee's explicit acceptance. However, there is one critical exception: if the legatee dies before having the chance to accept, the bequest does not lapse but instead becomes part of the legatee's estate and passes to his own heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The passage states: 'The bequeathed property is owned by acceptance.' This means that ownership of a bequest is conditional upon the legatee's explicit acceptance after the testator's death.
Acceptance can be verbal or by conduct.
2
Locate the condition
The condition for ownership is 'acceptance' (qabul). Without acceptance, the legatee does not become owner.
Acceptance must occur after death; acceptance before death is ineffective.
3
Find the exception
The only exception is: if the legatee dies before acceptance, the bequest enters into the ownership of the legatee's heirs.
This applies even if the legatee was unaware of the bequest.
4
Understand the rationale
The bequest is considered to have been offered at death; the right to accept passes to the legatee's heirs if he dies before exercising it.
This is a unique Hanafi position that protects the legatee's beneficiaries.

This passage presents the Hanafi rules for distributing the one-third of an estate when a testator makes multiple bequests that together exceed that limit and the heirs do not approve. Three scenarios are examined: equal one-third bequests, one-third and one-sixth, and a bequest of 'all' combined with one-third. State the underlying rule

What happens if someone writes a will giving away one-third of their property, but at that moment they own nothing? Can the legatee still receive anything? In this lesson, we examine a clear Hanafi ruling: the bequest is not wasted — it attaches to whatever property the testator owns at death. This principle reflects the nature of a will as a posthumous transfer, not a present gift.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What did the testator do? He made a bequest of one-third of his property while having no property. The ruling: the bequest is valid and the legatee is entitled.
2
Determine the timing condition
The bequest takes effect only upon death. Therefore, property ownership at the time of the will is irrelevant; what matters is what the testator owns at death.
3
Apply the ruling to the acquisition scenario
If the testator later acquires property before death, the bequest applies to that property. The legatee receives one-third of whatever is owned at death.
4
Consider the negative case
If the testator never acquires property until death, the bequest yields nothing. The legatee gets nothing because there is no estate.

Commentary
This ruling addresses a scenario where a testator declares a bequest of one-third of his property but possesses no assets at that moment. Later, before death, he acquires wealth. The Hanafi position is that the bequest is valid and applies to the property he owns at the time of his death. The legatee is entitled to one-third of that acquired estate. This is because a bequest is a testamentary disposition that takes effect only after death, so the existence of property at the time of the will is not required; what matters is the property existing at death. However, if the testator never acquires property, the bequest is ineffective.

يملكه عند الموت (yamlikuhu 'inda al-mawt): What he owns at the time of death; the property that exists in the testator's estate at death.

Bequests: Joint Recipients and Future Wealth
Ruling on bequest scenarios with multiple beneficiaries and future property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on bequests to multiple recipients when one predeceases, and on bequests of future wealth.

Core Idea
Wording decides distribution when a beneficiary dies; future wealth bequests are valid if owned at death.

This lesson presents three key rulings from the Hanafi school on bequests (wasiyyah). Understanding these scenarios clarifies how the testator's wording and the timing of death affect distribution, and that a bequest of future wealth is binding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario type
Classify each scenario as joint bequest, distributive bequest, or bequest of future wealth.
The wording 'li-Zayd wa-'Amr' vs 'bayna Zayd wa-'Amr' is the key distinction.
2
Note the consequence of predecease
For joint bequest, the survivor takes the entire third. For distributive bequest, the survivor takes only half of the third.
In joint bequest, the share lapses to the other; in distributive, it reverts.
3
Understand future wealth bequest
If the testator had no wealth at the time of the bequest but later acquires property, the beneficiary is entitled to one-third of what is owned at death.
The bequest attaches to property existing at death, not at the time of the will.

كتاب الوصايا. ومن أوصى لزيد وعمرو بثلث ماله، فإذا عَمْرُو ميت: فالثلث كلُّه لزيد. وإن قال: ثلث مالي بين زيد وعمرو، وزيـد مـيـت: كان لعمرو نصف الثلث. ومن أوصى بثلث ماله ولا مال له ثم اكتسب مالاً: استحق الموصى له ثلث ما يملكه عند الموت.
Book of Bequests. And whoever makes a bequest to Zayd and ‘Amr of one-third of his wealth, and ‘Amr is dead: then the entire third is for Zayd. And if he said: one-third of my wealth is between Zayd and ‘Amr, and Zayd is dead: then for ‘Amr is half of the third. And whoever makes a bequest of one-third of his wealth and he has no wealth, then later acquires wealth: the beneficiary is entitled to one-third of what he owns at the time of death.
Source pages p. 381

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents three rulings on bequests (wasaya). The first scenario: a testator bequeaths one-third of his estate to both Zayd and ‘Amr jointly, but ‘Amr predeceases the testator. In Hanafi law, the entire third goes to Zayd, as the share of the predeceased lapses. The second scenario: the testator says 'one-third of my wealth is between Zayd and ‘Amr,' which implies equal division. If Zayd predeceases, his half lapses, so ‘Amr receives only half of the third (one-sixth of the estate), not the entire third, because the wording indicates separation. The third ruling: a person bequeaths one-third of his wealth but currently owns nothing; later he acquires wealth. The bequest is valid and attaches to whatever he owns at death, since capacity to bequeath exists at death. This follows the Hanafi principle that a bequest of future property is valid if the property exists at the testator's death.

Key Terms
وصية (wasiyyah): A disposition of property after death.
ثلث (thulth): The maximum portion that can be bequeathed without heirs' permission.
