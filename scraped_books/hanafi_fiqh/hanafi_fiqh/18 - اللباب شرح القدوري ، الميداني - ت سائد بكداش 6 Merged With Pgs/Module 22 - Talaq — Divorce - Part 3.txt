Feeding Expiation for Zihar: Amounts, Methods, and Ruling on Intercourse
Detailed rules for feeding as expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three valid methods of feeding expiation for Zihar, specify the required amounts per person, and explain why intercourse during feeding does not require restarting.

Core Idea
Feeding sixty poor persons for Zihar does not require restarting if intercourse occurs.

In the previous lesson, we learned that a person who cannot fast may feed sixty poor persons as expiation for Zihar. This lesson details the precise amounts, acceptable food types, and the three recognized methods of distribution. Importantly, the rule regarding intercourse differs from fasting—here it does not invalidate the ongoing expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three valid methods of feeding
The passage presents three distinct ways to fulfill the feeding expiation: (a) give each of sixty poor persons half a sa' of wheat (or equivalent) in one go, (b) provide two meals (lunch and dinner) to all sixty in one day, or (c) feed one poor person for sixty days.
These are all alternatives to the fasting option.
2
Determine the per-person amount
For wheat, the minimum is half a sa' per person; for dates or barley, it is one sa' per person. Alternatively, one may pay the monetary value.
The sa' is a standard Hanafi volume measure, approximately 2.5 kg for wheat.
3
Understand the meal option (lunch and dinner)
If the expiator gives all sixty persons both lunch and dinner on the same day, it suffices regardless of how much they eat. This is a single-day completion.
Quantity of food consumed is irrelevant; the act of feeding two meals satisfies.
4
Explain the one-person-for-sixty-days method
Feeding one poor person for sixty consecutive days is valid. However, giving all sixty portions to one person in a single day only counts as one day's portion.
The key is distribution over time: one person across sixty days or sixty people in one day.
5
Note the ruling on intercourse during feeding
If the man has intercourse with his wife during the feeding period, he does not restart the expiation; he may continue from where he left off. This differs from the fasting option where intercourse necessitates restarting.
The reasoning is that feeding is not invalidated by intercourse, unlike the continuous nature of fasting.

Fulfilling Multiple Zihar Expiation Obligations
Ruling on fulfilling multiple zihar expiations with a single act

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on discharging multiple zihar expiations with a single act and apply it to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
One sufficient act can discharge multiple identical obligations without specific intent.

In this lesson, we examine a clear Hanafi rule from the chapter of zihar: when a person owes two expiation obligations, performing an act that covers the total required amount fulfills both. The text distinguishes between acts equal to one full expiation and those that are partial, guiding when allocation of intention matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its quantity
Determine the act performed: freeing slaves, fasting months, or feeding poor. Check if the quantity equals the sum of one full expiation per obligation (e.g., two slaves for two expiations).
One full zihar expiation is: free a slave, or fast two months, or feed 60 poor.
2
Consider the intention (niyyah)
The Hanafi school does not require specifying which expiation the act fulfills, provided the quantity is sufficient. If the quantity is exactly the total needed, the act covers all obligations without allocation.
If the act is less than one full expiation per obligation, the person may choose which obligation it satisfies.
3
State the ruling
If the act equals the total required (e.g., two slaves for two expiations), it is valid (jaza) for all obligations. If it is insufficient (e.g., one slave for two expiations), it counts only for one, at the person's choice.
This ruling is based on the principle that multiple identical obligations can merge when fulfilled by a single sufficient act.
4
Apply to similar cases
Test with different numbers of obligations and different expiation levels (slave, fast, feeding). The same rule applies as long as the chosen act is valid for the person's ability level.
Remember: expiation options are sequential (slave → fast → feed) based on ability.

Conditions and Procedure of Li'an
Ruling statement on li'an conditions and procedure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions that make li'an wajib on the husband and identify the exceptions that nullify the obligation.

Core Idea
Li'an allows a husband to avoid hadd for false accusation by swearing four times, but only if both spouses are eligible to testify.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a husband accuses his wife of adultery without four witnesses, he faces the hadd for qadhf unless he performs li'an. However, li'an is not always an option—it is only available when both spouses meet specific qualifications. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out the conditions, exceptions, and the precise swearing procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering events
Notice that li'an is triggered either by an accusation of zina or by the husband's denial of the child's lineage.
2
Check the conditions for li'an to be wajib
Both spouses must be eligible to testify (ahl al-shahada), the wife must be a free Muslim chaste woman whose accuser would be subject to hadd, and she must demand the consequence.
If any condition fails, li'an is not wajib.
3
Understand the consequences of refusal
If the husband refuses to perform li'an, the judge imprisons him until he either performs it or retracts and receives hadd. Similarly, the wife is imprisoned if she refuses.
Imprisonment is a coercive measure, not a punishment.
4
Recognize the exceptions
Li'an is replaced by hadd if the husband is a slave, disbeliever, or previously punished for qadhf. If the wife is a slave, disbeliever, or previously punished, then no hadd and no li'an apply.
The key is eligibility to testify (ahl al-shahada).
5
Memorize the oath procedure
The husband swears four times, 'I swear by Allah that I am truthful,' and a fifth time adding, 'The curse of Allah be upon me if I am among the liars.' The judge begins with him, and he points at the wife.
This order is essential; the judge starts with the husband.

Core Idea
Li'an separation is a ba'in divorce (majority view), not an eternal prohibition.

This passage covers the woman's part in the li'an procedure and the legal consequence after both spouses complete the mutual imprecation. Within the Hanafi school, there is a difference of opinion on the nature of the separation: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad consider it a ba'in divorce, while Abu Yusuf considers it an eternal prohibition. Li'an Inapplicable: Minor, Insane, Mute, or Ambiguous Accusation
Explains conditions preventing li'an: minor wife, insane wife, or mute husband

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios where li'an is inapplicable according to the Hanafi school and explain why each prevents the procedure.

Core Idea
Li'an requires both spouses' legal capacity and a clear adultery accusation; absence of any invalidates the procedure.

This passage from Al-Lubab explains that li'an—the mutual oath procedure to negate paternity or prove adultery—cannot proceed if the wife is a minor or insane, if the husband is mute, or if the accusation is ambiguous. Each scenario lacks an essential element for the oaths to be valid, so no li'an occurs and the hadd for qadhf is also lifted.

Guided Path
1
Identify required capacities
Li'an requires both spouses to be legally capable: adulthood and sanity. A minor wife (ṣaghīrah) lacks adulthood; an insane wife (majnūnah) lacks sanity. Neither can perform the oaths.
Hanafis set puberty at 9 for girls, 12 for boys.
2
Analyze husband's capacity
A mute husband (akhras) cannot utter the verbal oaths required for li'an. Since li'an is a verbal procedure, muteness renders it invalid.
Some Hanafis allow written li'an if the mute can write, but the standard position is no li'an.
3
Examine the accusation
The husband's accusation must be explicit zina. Saying 'Your pregnancy is not from me' is ambiguous—it does not directly accuse her of adultery, so li'an is not triggered.
If the accusation is explicit zina, li'an may be valid (subject to other conditions).
4
Apply consequences
In all these cases, no li'an and no hadd for qadhf. Instead, the husband may be subject to tazir (discretionary punishment) for defamation.
The child's lineage is established if the accusation was ambiguous.

Mahr and Iddah After a Fasid Remarriage During Iddah
Presents a case of bain divorce and remarriage within iddah with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on mahr and iddah when a divorced woman remarries during iddah and divorces again, and explain the basis of the intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Core Idea
In a fasid second marriage during iddah, Abu Hanifa requires full mahr and a new iddah; Muhammad requires half mahr and completing the first.

This lesson examines a specific scenario: a woman receives an irrevocable (bain) divorce, then the same husband marries her again while she is still in her iddah. This second marriage is fasid (invalid) because a woman cannot remarry the same husband after a bain divorce during iddah. When the husband divorces her again before consummation, a dispute arises between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding the mahr and the iddah. Understanding this intra-Hanafi difference clarifies how the school treats invalid contracts and their effects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Legal Classification
The second marriage during iddah is fasid (defective) because the woman is still in the iddah of a bain divorce. Note that in Hanafi fiqh, a fasid contract is not completely void (batil) but is defective in its conditions.
A fasid marriage requires a new contract to be valid; it produces some legal effects.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position and Its Basis
Abu Hanifa rules that the husband must pay the full mahr and the woman must observe a new iddah. The basis: in a fasid marriage, the mahr becomes obligatory upon the contract itself (due to the woman's submission to marital authority), and the new marriage creates a legal bond that necessitates a new waiting period.
Full mahr is due even without consummation because the contract is considered effective for mahr purposes.
3
State Muhammad's Position and Its Basis
Muhammad al-Shaybani rules that only half the mahr is due and the woman must complete her first iddah. He analogizes the fasid marriage to a valid marriage in terms of mahr (half due before consummation) and argues that the first iddah is not interrupted by an invalid marriage.
Muhammad treats the second marriage as if it were valid for mahr purposes but invalid for iddah.
4
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf and the Mufta Bihi
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the woman receives full mahr and starts a new iddah; under Muhammad, she receives half mahr and completes the previous iddah. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's view.
Some later Hanafi scholars, like al-Kasani, incline toward Muhammad's view for ease, but the fatwa remains on Abu Hanifa's position.

Correct Wording of Oaths in Marriage and Divorce Claims
Procedural ruling on oath wording in marriage and divorce claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct wording of an oath in Hanafi fiqh for claims of marriage and divorce, and explain why the wording focuses on the present status rather than the past act.

Core Idea
Swear to the present status, not the past act—marriages end and divorces can be revocable.

When a party claims marriage or divorce and the defendant denies it, the court may require an oath. But what exact words should the defendant swear? This lesson from the Hanafi school gives two key formulations—and warns against two common mistakes. Understanding the reasoning behind each wording will sharpen your grasp of how oaths function in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two claims
The passage covers two distinct scenarios: a claim of marriage (dawā al-nikāḥ) and a claim of divorce (dawā al-ṭalāq). In each, the defendant must swear an oath.
The oath is part of court procedure (qadāʾ).
2
Note the prohibited oath wording
For marriage, the text says 'لا يُستحلف بالله ما تزوجتها' (he is not made to swear 'By Allah, I did not marry her'). For divorce, it says 'ولا يُستحلف بالله ما طلقتها' (he is not made to swear 'By Allah, I did not divorce her').
These formulations refer to past acts, not current status.
3
Note the permitted oath wording
For marriage, the correct wording is 'بالله ما بينكما نكاح قائم في الحال' (By Allah, there is no marriage standing between you two at the moment). For divorce, it is 'بالله ما هي بائن منك الساعة بما ذكرت' (By Allah, she is not irrevocably separated from you now as she mentioned).
These formulations address the present state.
4
Understand the legal rationale
A marriage may have ended by divorce or death, so a past marriage could have existed. Swearing 'I never married' would be overstatement and possibly perjury. Similarly, a revocable divorce (ṭalāq rajʿī) does not make the wife irrevocably separated (bāʾin) until the ʿidda expires, so swearing 'I did not divorce' is too broad. The oath must match the contested fact: the existence of marriage now or irrevocable separation now.
This shows the Hanafi care for precision in oaths.
5
Apply to a hypothetical
Example: A woman claims her husband divorced her irrevocably (bāʾin). The husband denies. The correct oath for him is: 'By Allah, she is not bāʾin from me now.' He should not be asked to swear 'I did not divorce her' because he may have given a revocable divorce.
Practice by creating similar scenarios.

Identify the acts and their legal nature

State the effectiveness under coercion
The passage states: 'What he was coerced into takes effect.' Thus, coerced divorce and emancipation are valid and binding.
The ruling is explicit: no option to rescind.

4
Distinguish from contracts under coercion
In sales, coercion grants the coerced party an option (khiyār) to annul. In īqā'āt, no such option exists because the act is effective immediately.
This difference stems from the nature of the legal act: unilateral vs. bilateral.
