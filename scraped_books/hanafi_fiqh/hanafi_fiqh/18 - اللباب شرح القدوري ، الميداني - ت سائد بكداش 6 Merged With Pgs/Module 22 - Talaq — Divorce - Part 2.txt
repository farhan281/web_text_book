Learning Objective

Core Idea
Exception in divorce reduces the number of divorces as long as it is uttered in the same breath.

A husband's divorce pronouncement can include an exception, such as 'You are divorced three except one.' The Hanafi school validates this exception, so only the reduced number of divorces takes effect. This lesson explains how to parse such statements, the condition of continuity, and the cross-madhhab difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Parse the utterance
Identify the total number pronounced before 'except' (must be a number) and the number stated after 'except'.
In 'three except one', total=3, exception=1.
2
Subtract the exception
Subtract the exception number from the total number. That result is the number of divorces that actually occur.
3 - 1 = 2 divorces.
3
Check the condition of continuity
For the exception to be valid, it must be uttered in the same continuous statement without a significant pause between the total and the exception.
If he pauses, the exception may be invalid and all three divorces might occur.
4
Compare with other schools
The Shafi'i school does not accept exception in divorce; they consider the entire number as pronounced. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi in the school.
This difference is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Definition and Ruling of Zihar in Hanafi Fiqh
Opening of Kitab al-Zihar with definition and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of zihar, identify its key conditions, and apply the ruling that the wife becomes forbidden until expiation is made.

Core Idea
Zihar makes intercourse haram until kaffara, not a divorce.

In this opening passage of Kitab al-Zihar, the Hanafi school presents the core definition and ruling of zihar. Understanding what constitutes zihar and its immediate legal consequences is essential before moving to expiation details. Pay attention to the precise wording required and the critical role of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its immediate ruling
When the husband says 'You are to me like the back of my mother,' the wife becomes forbidden (haram) for intercourse, touching, and kissing until he performs expiation.
This is the prototypical case.
2
Recognize the consequence of violating the prohibition
If the husband has intercourse before expiating, he must seek forgiveness but does not owe a second expiation; only the first kaffara is due.
No additional penalties beyond sin and the original expiation.
3
Understand the concept of 'awd (return)
'Awd is the husband's resolve to resume marital relations, which triggers the obligation of expiation.
Mental intention suffices; physical return is not required.
4
Identify the conditions for zihar to occur
Zihar is established if the husband compares his wife to any permanently prohibited female relative (by blood or fosterage) using words like 'back', 'belly', 'thigh', 'private part', or even fractions like 'half' or 'third'.
The comparison can be to a part or whole.
5
Apply the role of intention in ambiguous statements
If the husband says 'You are like my mother' without specifying 'back', intention determines the ruling: if he meant honor, it is not zihar; if he meant zihar, it is zihar.
Niyya is decisive in unclear expressions.

Core Idea
Zihar prohibits marital relations until expiation; the trigger for expiation is the resolve to resume intercourse, not the act itself.

In this lesson, we examine the definition and immediate legal effect of zihar as stated in the matn of al-Quduri. Zihar is a serious prohibition that resembles divorce but requires expiation. Understanding the precise moment when expiation becomes obligatory is key to applying the ruling correctly.

Identify the act and its ruling
The husband's statement 'You are to me like the back of my mother' constitutes zihar. The immediate ruling is that intercourse, touching, and kissing become unlawful (haram) until expiation is performed.

Determine the consequence of violation
If the husband violates the prohibition and has intercourse before expiating, he must seek forgiveness but no additional expiation is required; the original expiation remains obligatory.
This distinguishes zihar from other prohibitions where multiple violations might require multiple expiations.

Define the trigger for expiation (al-'awd)
The return (al-'awd) that makes expiation obligatory is not the act of intercourse but the husband's resolve to resume marital relations. This is the Hanafi position.
Note the contrast with other schools that require actual intercourse.

Expressions That Constitute Zihar
Lists expressions that constitute zihar and clarifies intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which expressions constitute zihar and explain how the husband's intention determines the ruling for ambiguous phrases.

Core Idea
Any comparison to a permanently prohibited relative constitutes zihar; intention resolves ambiguity.

Zihar is a pre-Islamic form of divorce that the Qur'an prohibited but attached expiation to. The Hanafi school provides a detailed list of phrases that trigger zihar. This lesson covers the explicit comparisons, comparisons to mahrams, and the crucial role of the husband's intention when the phrase is ambiguous.

Guided Path
1
Identify explicit comparisons
The text lists specific body parts: belly, thigh, private part, back. Any of these, when the wife is compared to the mother's part, constitutes zihar.
The phrase does not have to be 'back'; other body parts also count.
2
Extend to all permanently prohibited relatives
The comparison is not limited to the mother. Comparing the wife to any mahram (e.g., sister, aunt, foster mother) likewise constitutes zihar.
Mahram here means a relative forever prohibited in marriage.
3
Include any part or fraction of the wife
Specifying a part of the wife (head, face, neck) or a fraction (half, third) in the comparison also counts as zihar.
The ruling applies even if only a portion of the wife is likened to the mahram.
4
Analyze the ambiguous phrase 'like my mother'
When the phrase does not explicitly mention a body part, the husband's intention determines the ruling. If he intended honor, it is not zihar. If he intended zihar, it is zihar. If he intended divorce, it is a ba'in divorce. If no intention, the statement has no effect.
This is a Hanafi position; other schools may differ.
5
Apply to a scenario
Given a new case (e.g., 'You are to me like my sister's back'), classify it as zihar. If the phrase is ambiguous, ask what the husband intended.

General Rulings on Zihar: Scope and Expiation
States the scope of zihar and its expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the scope of zihar (applies only to free wives) and the sequence of expiation (free slave, fast two months, feed sixty poor) and explain why each is required.

Core Idea
Zihar applies only to free wives; for multiple wives, each requires a separate expiation.

This lesson introduces the foundational rules of zihar, a pre-Islamic form of divorce that Islam regulated. The Hanafi school specifies that zihar is only valid when declared against a free wife, not a slave woman. Additionally, if a man declares zihar against all his wives collectively, he must perform a separate expiation for each one. The expiation itself follows a graded sequence: freeing a slave, then if unable, fasting two consecutive months, then if unable, feeding sixty poor people. All expiation must be completed before any marital relations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its limitation
Understand that zihar is a specific verbal declaration ('You are like the back of my mother') and it only applies to a free wife. If said to a slave woman, it does not constitute zihar.
The word 'zihar' is derived from 'zahr' (back).
2
Understand the rule for multiple wives
If a man says the zihar declaration to all his wives collectively, he is considered to have declared zihar against each one individually. Therefore, he owes a separate expiation for each wife.
This prevents the act from being treated as a single declaration.
3
Memorize the expiation sequence
The expiation has three options in a fixed order: (1) freeing a slave, (2) if unable, fasting two consecutive months, (3) if unable, feeding sixty poor people. The sequence is obligatory; one cannot choose out of order.
The inability must be genuine and proven.
4
Note the timing
All expiation must be completed before any marital relations (touching) with the wife. Resuming intimacy without expiation is sinful and the obligation remains.
The expiation is wajib, not fard, but still binding.

Valid and Invalid Slaves for Zihar Expiation
Details the conditions for the slave to be freed in zihar expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which slaves are valid for zihar expiation and apply the conditions regarding disability and ownership status.

Core Idea
Only fully owned, able-bodied slaves free from severe disabilities or pending emancipation are valid for zihar expiation.

After establishing that freeing a slave is the primary expiation for zihar, this lesson details the specific conditions the slave must meet. The slave must be fully owned by the man and must be capable of work. The Hanafi school specifies which disabilities disqualify a slave and which are acceptable. Additionally, certain categories of slaves—like a mudabbar, umm walad, or a mukatib who has paid part—are not considered fully owned and thus cannot be used. However, a mukatib who has not paid anything is still fully owned. Interestingly, one may even buy his own father or son with the intention of freeing them for expiation.

Guided Path
1
List the basic requirements
The slave must be fully owned by the person performing the expiation. The slave's religion, gender, or age does not matter; a disbeliever, Muslim, male, female, young, or old are all acceptable.
Full ownership means the person has complete legal authority over the slave.
2
Identify disqualifying disabilities
A slave who is blind, missing both hands, missing both feet, or insane (lacking understanding) is not valid. Such disabilities impede the slave's ability to work, which is the legal purpose of freeing a slave for expiation (to restore productivity to society).
The text uses 'لا تجزىء' (does not suffice) for these.
3
Identify acceptable disabilities
A deaf slave is acceptable. Also, a slave missing one hand and one foot from opposite sides (e.g., left hand and right foot) is acceptable. The key is that the slave is still capable of some work.
The phrase 'من خلاف' means from opposite sides, not both on the same side.
4
Note invalid ownership statuses
A mudabbar (promised freedom upon master's death), an umm walad (female slave who has borne a child to the master), and a mukatib (slave who has a contract of emancipation and has paid part of the price) are not fully owned and thus cannot be freed for expiation. However, a mukatib who has paid nothing is still fully owned and valid.
Mudabbar and umm walad are already assured freedom, so freeing them is not a sacrifice.
5
Recognize special cases
If a person buys his own father or son with the intention of freeing them for expiation, it is valid. This is allowed because the relationship does not prevent ownership for the purpose of expiation.
This is a unique Hanafi ruling.

Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a slave declaring Zihar (a form of divorce-like oath). The ruling: the only valid expiation is fasting (sawm).
2
Note the limitation
A slave cannot free a slave or feed the poor because he does not own property. Hence, only fasting is available.
The standard expiation for a free person includes freeing a slave or feeding sixty poor; a slave cannot fulfill these.
3
Identify what is invalid
If the master frees a slave or feeds the poor on the slave's behalf, it does not suffice. The expiation must be performed by the slave himself.
4
Understand the rationale
The reason is that the slave lacks legal capacity to own or dispose of property; expiation requires direct personal action within one's means.
This is a matter of capacity: the slave's obligation is reduced to what he can perform.

If a slave declares Zihar, only fasting suffices for him in expiation. If the master frees [a slave] on his behalf, or feeds [poor], it does not suffice him.

Commentary
A slave's Zihar expiation is limited to fasting because he does not own property to free a slave or feed the poor. His master's actions (freeing a slave or feeding) are not valid on his behalf. The slave must fast personally.

Key Terms
العبد (al-'abd): A male slave who is under his master's ownership.
