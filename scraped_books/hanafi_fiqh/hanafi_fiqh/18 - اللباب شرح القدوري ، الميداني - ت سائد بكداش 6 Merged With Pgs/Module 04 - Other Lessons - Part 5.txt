Classification of Head Wounds (Shijaj) and Ruling of Qisas
Classification of head wounds and qisas for mudiha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the ten types of shijaj in order and identify which wound allows qisas if intentional.

Core Idea
Only the mudiha (bone-exposing) wound carries qisas; all other head wounds are compensated by diya or fair assessment.

Head wounds are classified into ten graded types in Hanafi law, from the most superficial to the most severe. This lesson introduces the classification and the critical rule that only the mudiha (the wound that exposes bone) permits retaliation (qisas) if committed intentionally. For all other head wounds, the remedy is financial compensation.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the ten terms in sequence
Learn the Arabic names from the text: al-harisah, al-dami'ah, al-damiyah, al-badi'ah, al-mutalahimah, al-simhaq, al-mudihah, al-hashimah, al-munaqqilah, al-ammah.
The sequence reflects increasing severity.
2
Distinguish superficial vs deep wounds
The first six are superficial (up to the membrane), the last four involve bone and skull.
This distinction affects whether qisas applies.
3
Identify the mudiha's unique status
Only the mudiha (bone-exposing) carries qisas if intentional. No other head wound carries qisas.
The text says: 'ففي الموضحة: القصاص إن كانت عمداً. ولا قصاص في بقية الشجاج.'
4
Apply hukumat adl to lower wounds
For wounds below mudiha (i.e., the first six), compensation is by 'fair arbitration' (hukumat adl), not a fixed amount.
Hukumat adl is assessed by a judge based on the injury's extent.

Fixed Diya Amounts for Head Wounds and Fingers
Fixed diya amounts for head wounds and fingers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fixed diya fractions for mudiha, hashimah, munqilah, ammah, ja'ifah, and the fingers of one hand.

Core Idea
Each graded head wound carries a specific diya fraction; the five fingers together count as half the full diya.

While some head wounds are compensated by fair arbitration (hukumat adl), five specific wounds and the ja'ifah have fixed diya amounts. Additionally, the five fingers of one hand collectively equal half the full diya. This lesson covers these fixed monetary compensations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wounds with fixed diya
Five wounds have fixed diya: mudiha, hashimah, munqilah, ammah, and ja'ifah (plus a second ja'ifah if through-and-through).
The remaining head wounds (first six) have hukumat adl.
2
Memorize the fractions for each wound
Mudiha: half-tenth (1/20); Hashimah: tenth (1/10); Munqilah: tenth + half-tenth (3/20); Ammah: one-third (1/3); Ja'ifah: one-third (1/3); Two Ja'ifah: two-thirds (2/3).
These fractions are of the full diya (100 camels or equivalent).
3
Understand the ja'ifah rule
Ja'ifah is a penetrating wound to the trunk, not head. If it goes through and through, it counts as two separate ja'ifah wounds, doubling the diya.
The text says: 'فإن نفذت: فهي جائفتان، ففيهما: ثلثا الدية'.
4
Note the diya for fingers of the hand
The five fingers of one hand together carry half the full diya. This is true even if cut with the palm; the palm does not add extra diya.
The phrase 'وإن قطعها مع الكف: ففيها نصف الدية' confirms no increase for the palm.

Qasamah: Liability Based on the Corpse's Location
Listing eight location-based rulings for qasamah liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasamah for each of the eight locations mentioned in the text and identify which locations exempt liability.

Core Idea
Liability for qasamah and diyah depends on the location's connection to a specific group.

In this passage, the Hanafi school presents a series of practical rulings determining who must take the oath of qasamah and who pays blood money when a killed person is discovered. The key variable is the location where the body is found. This lesson will guide you through each scenario, from private houses to public roads and remote wilderness, clarifying when the blood is 'wasted' (hadar) and when the community bears responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle for private property
Start with the first ruling: if a corpse is found in someone's house, qasamah is upon the owner (not the residents, per Abu Hanifa), and the diyah is upon the owner's 'aqilah.
Note the distinction between owner and resident.
2
Distinguish original settlers from later buyers
The text specifies that qasamah is upon the original settlers (ahl al-khattah) even after sale, and not upon buyers, even if only one original owner remains.
This emphasizes continuity of responsibility.
3
Examine special locations: ship and neighborhood mosque
For a corpse on a ship, qasamah is upon passengers and sailors collectively. For a neighborhood mosque, qasamah is upon the inhabitants of that neighborhood.
Note the collective nature.
4
Handle public spaces and wilderness
If found in a congregational mosque (jami') or main road (al-shari' al-a'zam), no qasamah is required and diyah is from the public treasury. In a remote wilderness with no habitation, the blood is hadar (no consequence).
Public spaces do not create private liability.
5
Apply proximity rules for rivers and between villages
If found between two villages, liability is on the closer one. If found in the middle of a river (e.g., Euphrates), it is hadar; but if stuck on the shore, it is upon the nearest village.
Proximity determines responsibility when location is ambiguous.

Procedural Rules in Qasamah: Accusation, Oath, and Testimony
Rules on how accusation affects qasamah liability, the taking of oaths, and acceptance of testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules on how accusation affects qasamah liability, describe the defendant's oath procedure, and explain why testimony of insiders against outsiders is rejected.

Core Idea
Accusing an insider does not drop qasamah from the community; accusing an outsider does.

In this lesson, we examine three procedural rules governing the conduct of qasamah cases. The first determines whether accusing a specific person from the liable community drops the collective liability. The second concerns the oath of the defendant when he names another person as the killer. The third addresses the rejection of testimony from members of the liable community against an outsider. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly applying qasamah proceedings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Recognize the wali (guardian), the mustakhlaf (defendant), and the community (ahl al-mahalla). These parties are central to the procedural rules.
The wali is the victim's heir; the mustakhlaf is the one who would normally take the oath of innocence.
2
Analyze the effect of accusation
If the wali accuses a specific insider (from the community), qasamah is NOT dropped from the community. If he accuses an outsider, qasamah is dropped.
Insider = same community liable for qasamah; outsider = not from that community.
3
Understand the defendant's oath
If the defendant says 'So-and-so killed him,' he must swear: 'By Allah, I did not kill him, nor do I know of any killer besides so-and-so.' This oath clears him while naming another.
The oath covers both direct denial and denial of knowing another killer besides the named person.
4
Evaluate the testimony rule
If two insiders testify against an outsider, their testimony is rejected. The reason is that they have a self-interest in shifting liability away from themselves.
Hanafi school considers this testimony biased and therefore inadmissible.
5
Reflect on the underlying principle
These rules prevent abuse of the qasamah process. Accusing an insider keeps pressure on the community, while accusing an outsider releases it. The oath and testimony rules ensure fairness and avoid false claims.

Who Bears the Blood-Money? The 'Aqila's Role
States rulings on 'aqila liability for blood-money.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for who bears diyah in shibh al-'amd and khata', and identify when the 'aqila is liable and when it is not.

Core Idea
Blood-money for unintended killings is borne by the 'aqila, not the killer alone.

This passage from al-Lubab lays out the core Hanafi principles of collective responsibility for blood-money. Understanding these rules is essential for grasping how Islamic law balances individual accountability with social solidarity. We will walk through the definition of 'aqila, how payment is collected, and the exceptions to their liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify triggering homicides
Note that the 'aqila is liable for diyah in cases of shibh al-'amd (semi-intentional) and khata' (mistake), not for 'amd (intentional).
The text explicitly states: 'الدية في شبه العمد، والخطأ'.
2
Define the 'Aqila
The 'aqila is either the people of the diwan (military register) for soldiers, or the tribe for others. Understand who constitutes the 'aqila in each case.
Two definitions: 'أهل الديوان' and 'قبيلته'.
3
Explain payment mechanism
Payment is spread over three years, taken from stipends (diwan) or apportioned among tribe members. Individual contribution is capped at four dirhams total (one dirham and two danigs per year).
The cap is 'لا يُزاد الواحد على أربعة دراهم'.
4
List exceptions to liability
The 'aqila does not bear: intentional injury ('amd), injury to a slave, confessions without affirmation, liability from settlement, and amounts below one-twentieth of diyah.
These are exceptions that shift liability back to the offender.
5
Identify fallback
If the killer has no 'aqila, the diyah is paid from the public treasury (bayt al-mal).
Final safety net: 'فالدية في بيت المال'.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the items exempt from the hadd of theft in the Hanafi school and state the rationale for each exemption.

Core Idea
Hadd cutting is only for property of value; the Quran, cross, games, free persons, and adult slaves are not such property.

Not every theft triggers the prescribed amputation. The Hanafi school identifies several categories of items whose theft does not incur hadd, either because they lack the status of valuable property (mal mutaqawwim) or because of a legal doubt (shubha). This lesson covers the key exemptions listed in the classical texts, including religious texts, instruments of disbelief and gambling, free persons, and different classes of slaves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Recall that hadd for theft requires the stolen item to be mal mutaqawwim (valuable property) and owned by someone. Exemptions arise when the item is not considered property or when ownership is doubtful.
2
Classify each exempted item
Go through each item: Quran, gold cross, chess, backgammon, free child, adult slave, notebooks. For each, ask: is this considered mal mutaqawwim? If not, no hadd.
The gold cross and games are not property due to their unlawful nature.
3
Understand the slave distinction
An adult slave is like a free person in some legal aspects, creating doubt (shubha) in ownership, so no hadd. A minor slave is fully property, so theft incurs cutting.
The age of majority for slaves follows the same criteria as free persons.
4
Note the exception for account ledgers
All notebooks are exempt except account ledgers (dafatir al-hisab), which record debts and are considered valuable property.
Ledgers themselves are not property, but the debts they represent are, so theft of the ledger facilitates taking the debt.
5
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Given an item not listed, determine if it shares the illa (effective cause) with any of the exempted categories. For example, a Torah or Bible is like the Quran in being a revealed scripture, but Hanafis may treat it differently.
This step prepares for fatwa and analogy.

Exceptions to Hadd for Theft: Shubha and Lack of Hirz
States that certain types of takers are not subject to cutting due to shubha or lack of hirz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories exempt from the hadd of cutting and explain the Hanafi rationale (shubha or lack of hirz) for each.

Core Idea
Shubha or lack of hirz waives the hadd of cutting for theft.

The hadd penalty for theft (cutting of the hand) is severe, and the Hanafi school strictly limits its application. This passage lists six types of takers who are not subject to cutting, either because their taking involves a legal ambiguity (shubha) or because the property was not taken from a secure place (hirz). Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly applying the law of theft.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling for theft
Recall that the hadd of cutting applies to a thief who takes property of nisab value from a secure place (hirz) with no shubha.
This is the baseline against which the exceptions are measured.
2
List the six exempt categories
From the passage: khain (traitor), nabbash (grave robber), muntahib (plunderer), mukhtalis (snatcher), thief from bayt al-mal, and thief from property in which he has a share.
Memorize these as the 'six exempt takers'.
3
Identify the rationale for each exemption
For each exempt category, determine whether the reason is shubha (legal ambiguity) or lack of hirz (no secure place). Khain: shubha due to prior trust. Nabbash: no hirz in grave and shubha in shroud ownership. Muntahib and mukhtalis: no hirz (open taking). Bayt al-mal and shared property: shubha due to thief's own right in the property.
Shubha can arise from ownership rights or contractual trust.
4
Apply the principle: no hadd when shubha is present
The Hanafi school consistently applies the maxim 'al-hudud tudra'u bi al-shubuhat' (penalties are repelled by ambiguities). If any shubha exists in the ownership, possession, or secure nature of the property, the hadd is not imposed.
This is a key usul principle in criminal fiqh.

Commentary
This passage lists types of individuals who are exempt from the hadd penalty even if they take property. A khain (traitor) is someone entrusted with property who betrays that trust, like a trustee who steals from the trust; this is not considered theft in the penal sense because there is shubha in the contract of trust. A nabbash is a grave robber who steals the shroud from a dead body; since the dead body is not in a hirz (the grave is not a secure place in the same way), and there is shubha regarding ownership of the shroud, no cutting. A muntahib is a plunderer who openly seizes property in a rebellious manner; this is not secret theft. A mukhtalis is a snatcher who snatches something openly and runs; this is not theft from hirz. Furthermore, stealing from bayt al-mal (public treasury) does not incur cutting because the thief may have a right in the treasury (as a Muslim), creating shubha. Similarly, if the thief has a share (shirkah) in the stolen property, there is shubha in his ownership of a part, so cutting is not applied.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the specific exemptions from hadd cutting and explain the shubha principle underlying each exemption.

Core Idea
Familial ties and shared rights create shubha in property, exempting the thief from hadd.

This passage lists several scenarios where the Shari‘ah does not apply the fixed penalty of hand-cutting for theft. The common thread is a special relationship—kinship, marriage, master-slave, or share in spoils—that casts doubt (shubha) on whether the property was truly exclusive to the victim. Understanding these exemptions helps us appreciate how the law balances punishment with consideration for overlapping rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exempt categories
Read the passage and list the specific relationships: theft from parents, child, mahram relative, spouse, slave from master (and his family), master from mukatib, and soldier from ghanimah before distribution.

For each category, identify the shubha
Consider why the law sees doubt in ownership: relatives may have customary mutual access; spouses share household property; a slave's keep is on the master; a soldier is entitled to a share of spoils. This shubha blocks the full hadd.
Shubha is a key Hanafi principle that prevents hudud punishments.

Conclude the legal outcome
The punishment is not cutting (no hadd), but the act remains sinful and is subject to ta'zir (discretionary punishment by the judge). Memorize the list as exemptions from hadd.
The absence of hadd does not imply the act is permissible.

Prohibited and Permissible Drinks: Hanafi Rulings
Lists prohibited and permissible drink categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four prohibited drinks and apply the conditions that make nabidh permissible according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Khamr is only from grapes; other intoxicants are haram but not khamr; non-intoxicating nabidh is halal.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out the foundational Hanafi taxonomy of drinks. It defines what is absolutely prohibited, what is conditionally allowed, and what is always permissible. Understanding these categories is essential for distinguishing between lawful and unlawful beverages in daily life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four prohibited drinks
Read the first part and list the four categories: khamr, boiled grape juice with less than 2/3 remaining, strong date/raisin infusion, and lightly cooked date/raisin nabidh when intoxicating and drunk with amusement.
The fourth category has additional conditions.
2
Understand the definition of khamr
Khamr is specifically grape juice that has boiled, become strong, and thrown foam. This is the only drink that is absolutely prohibited under all circumstances.
Other intoxicants are not called khamr in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Learn the conditions for nabidh permissibility
Date/raisin nabidh that is lightly cooked is permissible only if: (a) it does not intoxicate, and (b) it is not drunk with amusement or excitement. Other nabidh (honey, figs, etc.) is always halal even uncooked.
The key is the absence of intoxication and the intention.
4
Recognize permissible drinks
Permissible drinks include: grape juice cooked until 2/3 is gone (even if strong), honey/fig/grain nabidh (uncooked), and nabidh made in any container (gourds, jars, etc.)
The container prohibition was abrogated in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Apply the rule on turning khamr into vinegar
If khamr turns into vinegar naturally or by adding something, it becomes halal. It is not disliked to intentionally cause this transformation.
This is a Hanafi concession; other schools may differ.

Conditions for Lawful Hunting with Trained Animals
Rulings on hunting with trained predatory animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for lawful hunting with trained animals and identify which condition, if violated, renders the game haram.

Core Idea
Hunting is permissible only with trained animals that wound the prey, while mentioning Allah's name, and the dog must not eat the catch.

This passage from the Book of Hunting clarifies the detailed conditions for eating game caught by trained animals. It distinguishes between dogs and birds of prey, and emphasizes the importance of proper training and the mention of Allah's name. Understanding these rules ensures that the meat consumed is halal and not carrion.

Guided Path
1
Identify permissible hunting animals
List the animals that may be used for hunting: trained dogs, cheetahs, falcons, and other trained birds of prey (jawarih). Training is essential.
Not all predators; only those trained according to Hanafi definitions.
2
Understand the training criteria
For a dog, training means it refrains from eating the prey when commanded (tested three times). For a falcon, training is that it returns when called.
Training for birds is different from dogs.
3
List conditions at the time of sending
The hunter must mention Allah's name (Bismillah, Allahu Akbar) when sending the animal. The animal must wound the prey (draw blood).
The wound is necessary because the animal's killing is considered a substitute for slaughter.
4
Determine when the game is halal
If the animal wounds and kills the prey, and the dog does not eat from it, the meat is halal. For a falcon, eating does not render it haram.
Exception: birds of prey eating does not indicate lack of training.
5
Identify invalidating factors
If the hunter reaches the prey alive, he must slaughter it (tadhkiyah). If he leaves it to die, it is carrion. If the dog strangles without wounding, or an untrained dog or Magian's dog joins, or Allah's name is omitted, the game is not halal.
Each factor independently invalidates the catch.

Core Idea
Game is halal only if the projectile wounds, causes bleeding, and death results from the wound.

Hunting with projectiles is a common means of obtaining game, but its permissibility depends on strict conditions. The Hanafi school requires that the projectile wound the animal (cause bleeding), that the hunter mention Allah's name at the time of shooting, and that the animal dies from the wound itself, not from a fall or drowning. Essential Veins and Tools for Valid Slaughter
Rulings on the cutting of veins and tools for slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which veins must be cut in valid slaughter, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify permissible and impermissible slaughtering tools.

Core Idea
Cutting most of the four veins (trachea, esophagus, two jugulars) suffices according to Abu Hanifa; his students require at least three.

This lesson focuses on the physical act of slaughter. In the Hanafi school, the cutting of specific veins is required: the trachea, esophagus, and the two jugular veins. There is a well-known difference between Abu Hanifa and his two students on the exact number. Additionally, the text specifies that slaughter can be performed with any sharp object except a fixed tooth or nail, and it is recommended to sharpen the blade. Reaching the spinal cord or cutting off the head is disliked but does not invalidate the meat.

Guided Path
1
List the four veins
The four veins required for valid slaughter are: al-halqum (trachea), al-mari' (esophagus), and the two wadajan (jugular veins).
These are the primary passages for air and blood.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
If the slaughterer cuts most of these four veins, the meat is lawful.
Most means more than half; e.g., three out of four.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
They require cutting the trachea, esophagus, and at least one jugular vein.
This is a stricter requirement, ensuring the animal loses consciousness quickly.
4
Identify permissible tools
Slaughter is valid with any sharp object that causes blood to flow, such as a sharp stone (layt) or obsidian (marwah), except a fixed tooth (sinn qa'im) or a fixed nail (zifr qa'im).
These are excluded because they are not clean or may cause torture.
5
Note rulings on spinal cord and head
Reaching the spinal cord (nukha') with the knife or cutting off the head entirely is disliked (makruh tanzih), but the meat remains lawful.
This is based on the principle of avoiding unnecessary harm.

States the ruling for slaughter from the back of the neck

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for slaughtering an animal from the back of the neck and distinguish between permissible (with dislike) and forbidden outcomes based on the animal's survival.

Core Idea
Slaughter from the back is halal with dislike if the animal stays alive; haram if it dies before vein cut.

Slaughtering an animal from the back of the neck (qafā) rather than the throat deviates from the sunnah method. The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling that depends on whether the animal remains alive long enough for the essential act of cutting the jugular veins. Understanding this distinction helps avoid unnecessary suffering and ensures halal meat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the alternative
Recognize that the act described is slaughtering (dhabh) from the back of the neck instead of the throat. The standard method is to cut the throat, including the jugular veins.
Dhabh typically requires cutting the throat, but nahr (chest stab) is for camels.
2
State the two possible outcomes
The ruling presents two scenarios: (1) the animal stays alive after the back cut until the jugular veins are cut, (2) the animal dies before the veins are cut.
3
Apply the ruling to each scenario
For scenario one: jaza (permissible) and yukrah (disliked). For scenario two: lam tu'kal (not eaten, i.e., haram).
The disliked ruling is makruh tanzihi, not makruh tahrimi.
4
Understand the reason for dislike
The act is disliked because it deviates from the preferred sunnah method of throat cutting, even though the slaughter is technically valid if the veins are cut while the animal is alive.
Performing dhabh from the back may cause unnecessary pain and is contrary to the recommended practice.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may rule the meat haram even if the animal stays alive, but Hanafi permits with dislike.
This ruling is mukhtalaf fih (disputed).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prescribed method of slaughter for tame game and wild livestock according to the Hanafi school, and apply the rule to new cases.

Core Idea
The method of slaughter follows the animal's current state, not its origin.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario: how should you slaughter an animal that changes its nature? A wild animal that becomes tame must be treated like livestock, and a livestock animal that becomes wild must be treated like game. This ruling ensures the meat remains halal.

For tame game, the method is dhabh (throat-cut). For wild livestock, the method is aqr or jarh (wounding by any means). Memorize the exact phrases: 'fadhakatuhu adh-dhabh' and 'fadhakatuhu al-aqr wa al-jarh'.
3
Extract the underlying principle
The ruling shows that the method of slaughter (dhakat) depends on the animal's current condition, not its origin. A wild animal that becomes tame is no longer considered game; a domestic animal that becomes wild is no longer considered livestock.

Oaths about Drinking and Speaking with Conditions
Rulings on oaths with conditions on drinking and speaking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether an oath is broken based on the specific wording of the oath and the manner of the act.

Core Idea
The wording of an oath determines its scope—literal interpretation governs whether the oath is broken.

In Hanafi fiqh, oaths are interpreted strictly according to their wording. This passage from al-Quduri presents two cases: drinking from a river and speaking to someone. In each, the exact phrasing of the oath and the conditions under which the act is performed determine whether the oath is broken. Understanding these nuances helps avoid unnecessary expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two oaths
The passage covers two distinct types of oaths: one about drinking from a river, and one about speaking to a person. Each has its own conditions. Read the Arabic carefully to see the difference in wording.
The first oath is about 'drinking from the Tigris' vs 'drinking from the water of the Tigris'.
2
Analyze the drinking oath
Focus on the oath 'لا يشرب من دجلة' (not to drink from the Tigris). According to Abu Hanifa, this is only broken by direct lapping (كرع), not by using a vessel. Then contrast with the oath 'لا يشرب من ماء دجلة' (not to drink from the water of the Tigris), which is broken even by using a vessel.
The key distinction is whether the oath mentions the river itself or the water of the river.
3
Analyze the speaking oath
The second part deals with an oath not to speak to someone. If the person is asleep but can hear, speaking to him breaks the oath because he hears it. If the oath includes a condition 'except with his permission', and permission is given but the speaker does not know, the oath is still broken because the condition (knowledge) is not met.
The act of speaking itself breaks the oath unless the condition explicitly requires knowledge of permission.
4
State the ruling for each case
Summarize: For 'drink from the Tigris': no oath-breaking by vessel, only by lapping. For 'drink from the water of the Tigris': oath-breaking by any method of drinking. For speaking to a sleeping person who can hear: oath is broken. For speaking without knowledge of permission: oath is broken.
These rulings are specific to the Hanafi school, as per Abu Hanifa.

An oath to report troublemakers to a governor is binding only while he remains in office.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario where a governor requires a subject to swear an oath to inform him of corrupt individuals entering the city. The ruling clarifies that such an oath is tied to the governor's term of office and does not persist beyond it. Understanding this limitation prevents misconceptions about perpetual obligations.
