Definition and Conditions of Mudarabah
Definition and conditions of mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of mudarabah and identify its four essential conditions for validity.

Core Idea
Mudarabah is a partnership of capital and labor; profit must be shared as a common fraction, not a fixed sum.

Mudarabah (silent partnership) is a key contract in Hanafi commercial law. It allows one party to provide capital and another to provide labor, with profits shared according to an agreed fraction. This lesson examines the precise definition and the conditions that make such a contract valid, ensuring it remains a genuine partnership and not a loan or wage arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
The text defines mudarabah as a contract combining capital from one partner (rabb al-mal) and work from the other (mudarib). Note that the capital owner does not engage in work, and the mudarib does not contribute capital.
2
Understand the capital condition
The capital must be of a type that is valid for ordinary partnership – i.e., gold, silver, or currency, not perishable goods or debt. This ensures the capital is fungible and can be returned.
Refer to earlier lessons on partnership (shirkah) for what constitutes valid capital.
3
Understand the profit-sharing condition
Profit must be shared as a common fraction (mushaa), such as half or one-third. It cannot be a fixed sum of dirhams, as that would resemble a wage or interest and invalidate the partnership nature.
This condition prevents gharar (uncertainty) and riba (interest).
4
Understand delivery and control conditions
The capital must be physically delivered to the mudarib, giving him exclusive possession and control. The rabb al-mal must not retain any hand (yad) over the capital, as that would compromise the mudarib's agency.
If the rabb al-mal retains control, the contract becomes void (fasid).

Commentary
This is a definition and condition statement for mudarabah (silent partnership). Four conditions are mentioned: (1) The capital must be of a type valid for ordinary partnership (i.e., gold, silver, or currency; not perishable goods). (3) The capital must be delivered to the mudarib (tamleek), giving him possession and control. (4) The capital owner must not retain any control (yad) over the capital; otherwise, the mudarib’s agency is compromised. These conditions ensure that mudarabah is a form of partnership based on trust and profit-sharing, not a loan or wage contract.

Permissible Actions and Restrictions for the Mudarib
Lists actions allowed in absolute mudarabah and restrictions on place/commodity/time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five actions permitted in an absolute mudarabah and identify the effects of restrictions on the mudarib's authority.

Core Idea
In absolute mudarabah, the mudarib can buy, sell, travel, send goods, and appoint agents, but cannot sub-contract without permission.

Mudarabah is a silent partnership where one party provides capital (rabb al-mal) and the other provides labor (mudarib). The scope of the mudarib's authority depends on the contract terms. This passage from al-Lubab clarifies what actions are permitted in an unrestricted mudarabah and what restrictions the capital owner may lawfully impose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default scope of authority
In an absolute mudarabah (no restrictions), the mudarib has broad discretion. The passage lists specific actions that are automatically permitted.
2
List the specific permitted actions
Note each action: buying, selling, traveling, sending goods (ibdaa), and appointing an agent (tawkili). Understand the meaning of each term.
Ibdaa means to send goods to another place for sale; tawkili means to appoint a substitute agent.
3
Identify the prohibited action and its condition
The mudarib cannot give the capital to another as mudarabah (sub-contract) unless the capital owner explicitly permits or uses the phrase 'اعمل برأيك' (act according to your opinion).
4
Understand restrictions and their effect
If the capital owner limits the mudarib to a specific country, commodity, or time period, the mudarib must adhere. Exceeding such restrictions makes him liable for losses.
The contract remains valid during the specified period; it ends automatically after expiry.
5
Synthesize the complete ruling
The mudarib's authority is broad in absolute mudarabah but narrows when restrictions are imposed; the capital owner can define the scope, and the mudarib must operate within it.

Commentary

Mudarib Purchasing Relatives of the Capital Owner
Rules on mudarib buying capital owner's freed relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mudarib purchasing relatives of the capital owner and apply the rule to scenarios with and without profit.

Core Idea
A mudarib cannot buy the capital owner's freed relatives with profit; if no profit, it's allowed.

This passage addresses a potential conflict of interest in mudarabah: the mudarib using capital to buy the capital owner's relatives who would be automatically freed. The ruling hinges on whether the mudarabah has profit, protecting the capital owner's rights while allowing the mudarib to benefit when there is no loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited act and its condition
The mudarib may not purchase the capital owner's father, son, or any relative who would be freed upon ownership. The prohibition is conditional: it applies only when there is profit in the mudarabah capital.
'Ya'tiq 'alayh' refers to those whom the capital owner would be forced to free if he owned them (e.g., ascendants and descendants).
2
Distinguish between profit and no profit scenarios
If profit exists, the mudarib is prohibited from buying such relatives; if he does, the purchase is for himself (not the partnership) and he is liable for the capital. If no profit exists, the purchase is permissible.
The ruling changes based on the presence or absence of profit.
3
Determine the consequences of a valid purchase (no profit)
When there is no profit and the mudarib buys such a relative, the slave becomes partially free. The mudarib's share (corresponding to his profit share, which is zero here?) becomes free, and the slave must work to compensate the capital owner for his share of the value.
The slave works (yas'ā) to pay the capital owner's portion, similar to a mukātab.
4
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Consider a mudarib who buys the capital owner's brother. Brothers are not among those who are automatically freed (only direct ascendants/descendants). So this purchase would be treated differently. The key is identifying who is 'ya'tiq 'alayh'.
Only specific relatives trigger this rule.

Debts, Loss, and Permissibility of Credit Sales in Mudarabah
Rulings on debts, loss allocation, and permissibility of credit sales in mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules of debt collection, loss allocation, profit return, and permissible transactions in mudarabah.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, losses first reduce profit, not capital; debts must be collected if profit exists; prior profit returns cover subsequent loss.

This passage from al-Lubab explicates key post-termination and ongoing rules of mudarabah. It clarifies the mudarib's duty regarding receivables, the precedence of profit in absorbing losses, and the effect of profit distribution. It also permits credit sales and restricts use of capital for marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario of separation with debts
When mudarib and owner separate, there may be debts outstanding. The rule depends on whether profit exists.
2
Determine the mudarib's obligation to collect debts
If there is profit, the mudarib is compelled to collect; if no profit, he may delegate to the owner.
Profit makes collection beneficial for both.
3
Apply the loss allocation rule
Any loss is first subtracted from profit, not from the capital. Only when profit is exhausted does capital decrease.
This protects the principal.
4
Understand the effect of prior profit distribution on subsequent loss
If profits were distributed while the contract was ongoing and then a loss occurs, the profits must be returned to cover the loss.
Not applicable if the contract was terminated and renewed.
5
Note permissible and prohibited transactions
The mudarib may sell for cash or credit, but may not use capital to marry slaves, as marriage is not trade.
Credit sales are a common trade practice.

Commentary
