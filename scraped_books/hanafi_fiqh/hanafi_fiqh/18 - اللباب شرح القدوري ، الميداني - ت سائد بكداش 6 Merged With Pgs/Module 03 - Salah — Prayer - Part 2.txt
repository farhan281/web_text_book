Ruling on Doubt in Prayer: First-Time vs Frequent
Treatment of doubt in prayer distinguishing first occurrence from frequent occurrence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi ruling for the first-time doubter and the frequent doubter in prayer, and apply the correct action for each.

Core Idea
First-time doubt invalidates prayer; frequent doubt builds on predominant thought or certainty.

This passage presents a key Hanafi distinction in the rules of prayer. Doubt (shakk) about the number of rak'ahs is treated differently depending on whether it is a first occurrence or a frequent experience. Understanding this distinction prevents unnecessary repetition of prayer and provides ease for those who often doubt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt scenario
The doubt is about the number of rak'ahs prayed — specifically, whether three or four have been performed.
This is a specific type of doubt; other doubts may have different rulings.
2
Determine the frequency of doubt
Is this the first time the person has experienced such doubt, or does it occur frequently? The answer determines the ruling.
The Hanafi school defines 'frequent' as occurring often enough that the person cannot maintain certainty.
3
Apply the ruling for first-time doubter
If it is the first occurrence, the prayer must be restarted from the beginning (musta'naf).
This is because certainty (yaqin) is lost, and precaution requires a fresh start.
4
Apply the ruling for frequent doubter with predominant thought
If the person is a frequent doubter and has a predominant thought (ghalib al-dhann) about the number, he builds on that thought and completes the prayer accordingly, followed by sujood al-sahw.
Predominant thought is a strong inclination, not mere suspicion.
5
Apply the ruling for frequent doubter without predominant thought
If the frequent doubter has no predominant thought, he builds on certainty (al-yaqin), which is the lower number (three). He then performs sujood al-sahw.
Certainty is the lesser number because the original obligation is certain, and doubt cannot override it.

Forgetting the Final Sitting and Standing to the Fifth Rak'ah
Ruling on forgetting the final sitting in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a worshipper forgets the final sitting and stands for a fifth rak'ah, distinguishing the two scenarios based on whether prostration occurred, and identify the required corrective actions.

Core Idea
Forgetting the final sitting: if you haven't prostrated in the fifth, return; if you have, your fard becomes nafl and you must add a sixth.

In this passage, al-Quduri presents a specific scenario of forgetfulness in prayer: the worshipper omits the wajib final sitting and mistakenly stands for a fifth rak'ah. The ruling pivots on whether he has performed a prostration in that fifth rak'ah. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of Sajdah al-Sahw and avoiding invalidating the fard prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mistake
The worshipper forgets the final sitting (a wajib element) and stands up for an extra rak'ah (the fifth). This is an addition of a physical pillar (qiyam) and omission of a wajib.
The final sitting is wajib, not rukn, in the Hanafi school.
2
Distinguish the two conditions
The ruling changes based on whether the worshipper has performed a sajdah (prostration) in that fifth rak'ah. Two cases: before prostration, and after prostration.
The key moment is the performance of sujud in the fifth rak'ah.
3
Apply the ruling for the first condition (no prostration)
If he has not yet prostrated, he must immediately return to the sitting position, cancel the fifth rak'ah (it is ignored), and perform Sajdah al-Sahw at the end. The fard prayer remains valid.
He does not need to recite tashahhud again; just sits and completes.
4
Apply the ruling for the second condition (prostration performed)
If he has prostrated in the fifth, his fard prayer becomes void and transforms into a nafl (voluntary) prayer. He must then add a sixth rak'ah to complete a two-rak'ah nafl and perform Sajdah al-Sahw.
The addition of a prostration firmly incorporates the extra rak'ah into the prayer structure.
5
Understand the rationale
Prostration in the fifth signals that the worshipper has committed to the extra rak'ah. Since the original fard was incomplete (missing final sitting), it cannot remain fard; it becomes nafl. Adding a sixth ensures the nafl is complete.
This ruling prevents the prayer from being invalid without recompense.

Thinking the Last Sitting is the First Sitting and Standing
Ruling on standing after tashahhud mistaking it for first sitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling for standing after tashahhud due to mistaken intention, distinguishing the two sub-cases based on whether a prostration in the fifth rak'ah occurred.

Core Idea
Mistaking the final sitting for the first does not invalidate prayer; the extra rak'ahs become nafl if a prostration was made, otherwise one returns.

Imagine you are praying and after the fourth rak'ah you sit for tashahhud, but then you stand for a fifth rak'ah, mistakenly thinking it was only the first sitting. What happens to your prayer? This lesson covers the Hanafi ruling on this precise scenario, which depends on whether you have already prostrated in the fifth rak'ah. The ruling ensures the prayer remains valid while compensating for the forgetfulness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mistake
The person sat in the fourth rak'ah for the length of tashahhud (qadr al-tashahhud) but then stood up for a fifth rak'ah, thinking that sitting was the first sitting (after two rak'ahs). This is a case of forgetfulness (sahw) because he did not intend to add a rak'ah.
The key is that he sat long enough to recite tashahhud, meaning the sitting was complete in terms of the required duration.
2
Check prostration in the fifth
Determine whether he performed a sajdah (prostration) in the fifth rak'ah. If he has not yet prostrated, he can still return. If he has already prostrated, the fifth rak'ah is considered fully performed and cannot be undone.
In Hanafi fiqh, a rak'ah is considered complete with the performance of a sajdah.
3
Apply the ruling if no prostration
If he has not prostrated in the fifth, he returns to sitting (i.e., cancels the extra standing), recites the tashahhud, says salam, and performs the prostration of forgetfulness (sajdah al-sahw). His original prayer remains valid.
This is similar to cases where one stands after two rak'ahs by mistake.
4
Apply the ruling if prostration occurred
If he has prostrated in the fifth, he must add another rak'ah (a sixth) to complete his prayer. The original prayer is then considered complete, but the fifth and sixth rak'ahs together count as a voluntary (nafl) prayer. He also performs sajdah al-sahw for the forgetfulness.
The addition of a sixth rak'ah is necessary to make the extra set an even number, as odd numbers in nafl are disliked (makruh) according to Hanafi.
5
Recognize the principle
The ruling shows that the Hanafi school gives flexibility to correct mistakes during prayer without invalidating it. The intention is taken into account, but the physical action (prostration) triggers a different outcome. The sajdah al-sahw compensates for the forgetfulness.
This principle is consistent with the general Hanafi approach to sahw: the prayer is preserved whenever possible.

Hierarchical Rulings for the Sick Person's Prayer
Rules for prayer when unable to stand, sit, or gesture

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hierarchical rulings for a sick person's prayer and apply them to determine the correct posture and gestures based on the person's ability.

Core Idea
Prayer obligations adjust to capacity: stand if you can, else sit, else lie down; gestures replace actions if needed.

This lesson covers the rulings for a sick person's prayer. The Hanafi school provides a clear hierarchy of postures and gestures based on the person's physical ability. The key principle is that the obligation to pray remains, but the manner is reduced according to capacity, as derived from the Quranic verse 'So fear Allah as much as you are able' (At-Taghabun: 16).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Prayer (salah) is fard 'ayn, but its performance is adaptive. The ruling here is about the manner of performance, not the obligation itself.
The obligation is never dropped; only the physical form adjusts.
2
Determine the patient's ability
Assess the sick person's capacity: Can they stand? If not, can they sit? If not, can they lie on back or side? Can they gesture with head?
Use the hierarchy from most able to least able.
3
Apply the corresponding ruling from the hierarchy
Match the ability to the ruling: if standing is difficult -> pray sitting with normal ruku' and sujud; if can sit but cannot bow/prostrate -> gesture with head; if cannot sit -> lie on back with feet to qibla and gesture; if cannot lie on back -> lie on side facing qibla and gesture.
Prostration gesture must be lower than bowing gesture.
4
Identify the limit of concessions
If the person cannot even gesture with the head, the prayer is delayed until recovery or until it becomes possible. Gesturing with eyes, heart, or eyebrows is not valid.
Delaying means postponing until ability returns; missed prayers are made up later.

Adapting Prayer Posture Due to Illness Mid-Prayer
Ruling on changing posture mid-prayer due to illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on adapting posture during prayer due to illness and identify the hierarchical adjustments.

Core Idea
Illness during prayer does not nullify it; you adapt your posture to your ability.

Imagine you begin your prayer standing, as is normal for a healthy person. Suddenly, you are struck by severe dizziness or pain and cannot remain standing. Does this mean your prayer is invalid? The Hanafi school teaches that you may continue your prayer from where you left off, adjusting your posture according to your new capacity. This lesson will walk you through the hierarchical options and the reasoning behind this flexible ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial posture
The person begins prayer while healthy, standing. This is the default posture for a person who is able.
The prayer is validly commenced with the intention and takbir while standing.
2
Recognize the obstacle
During the prayer, an illness occurs that prevents the person from standing. The obstacle is genuine and involuntary.
The illness must be such that standing causes harm or is impossible.
3
Apply the hierarchy of postures
The person continues the prayer in the easiest possible posture: first sitting with normal ruku' and sujud, then sitting with gestures (ima') if ruku'/sujud are also impossible, and finally lying on the back if sitting is impossible.
The hierarchy is descending from most active to least active, maintaining the essence of prayer.
4
Note the ruling on continuity
The existing rak'ahs remain valid. There is no need to restart the prayer from the beginning. The change in posture does not break the continuity.
This is the distinctive Hanafi position; other schools may require restarting.
5
Cross-madhhab awareness
Be aware that some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) consider a change in posture due to a new condition as a change in intention, thus requiring a fresh start. The Hanafi school prioritizes the principle of continuity (bina').
The practical difference: a Hanafi can continue; a Shafi'i would restart if the posture changes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of travel that triggers shortened prayer, identify the distance and intention conditions, and explain the rulings on qasr and itmam.

Core Idea
For the Hanafi school, the traveler must shorten four-rak'ah prayers to two; the full prayer is not valid without a valid excuse.

This lesson covers the foundational rulings on the traveler's prayer (salat al-musafir) as taught in the Hanafi school. We will learn what constitutes a shar'i journey, when shortened prayer begins, and the critical distinction between shortening and completing for those intending to stay. Understanding these rules ensures that a Muslim fulfills his obligation correctly while traveling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is traveling. The ruling is that the fard in four-rak'ah prayers becomes two rak'ahs. This is a binding obligation (wajib) in the Hanafi school, not a choice.
2

The journey must be three days and nights by camel or on foot (approximately 48 farsakh). Travel by water is not considered in this definition. The journey must be permissible and the destination known.
Note: Some later Hanafi sources also allow measurement by distance.
3
Understand the effect of sitting for tashahhud
If a traveler prays four rak'ahs and sits for tashahhud in the second rak'ah, the first two count as the fard and the last two as nafl. If he does not sit, the entire prayer is invalid because he intentionally added without the proper pause.
4

Shortening begins when the traveler departs from the built-up area (biyut al-misr) of his town. Praying before that point requires full completion.
5
Note the termination of travel rules
The traveler remains under travel rulings until he intends to reside in a place for fifteen days or more. If he intends less, he continues shortening indefinitely, even if he stays for years without a firm intention of fifteen days.
This is a unique Hanafi position based on intention, not actual duration.

Traveler Joining a Resident in Prayer
Rulings on traveler joining resident in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a traveler following a resident in prayer when the prayer time is still remaining versus when it is a missed prayer.

Core Idea
A traveler following a resident must complete four rak'ahs if the time remains, but following in a missed prayer invalidates the traveler's prayer.

This lesson focuses on two specific sub-rulings: a traveler joining a resident in an on-time prayer must follow the resident and pray four rak'ahs; but if the prayer is a missed (qada) prayer, the traveler's prayer behind the resident is invalid. Understanding the distinction is crucial for proper congregational prayer.

1
Identify the two cases

When the prayer time is still remaining, the traveler follows the resident and prays the full four rak'ahs. This is because the traveler is considered a follower (muqtadi) and must follow the imam's prayer length.

Analyze the missed prayer case
If the traveler joins the resident in a missed prayer (qada), his prayer is invalid. This is because the traveler's missed prayer must be shortened (two rak'ahs), but the resident prays four, creating a conflict in obligation.
The traveler cannot follow a resident in qada because the ruling for missed prayers is based on the state of the missed prayer, not the current state.
4
State the principle
The validity of a traveler following a resident depends on the type of prayer: on-time prayers allow full following; missed prayers do not.

Core Idea
Friday prayer requires a city, sultan’s permission, Zuhr time, a khutbah, and congregation — but the minimums are disputed within the school.

This passage from al-Quduri’s Mukhtasar lays out the conditions (shurūṭ) for the validity of the Friday prayer. Salat al-Khawf: The Prayer of Fear in the Hanafi School
Ruling on Salat al-Khawf method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Hanafi method of performing Salat al-Khawf when fear is intense, including the division of the congregation, the number of rak'ahs each group prays, and the rulings on fighting during prayer and extreme fear.

Core Idea
Salat al-Khawf allows the obligatory prayer to be performed in shifts while guarding against the enemy.

In times of war or when under enemy threat, Muslims are still obligated to pray. The Prayer of Fear (Salat al-Khawf) provides a practical method to fulfill this obligation while maintaining defense. This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling on how to perform this prayer when fear is intense, based on the classical text of al-Quduri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario for Salat al-Khawf
The text begins 'إذا اشتد الخوف' (when fear is intense). Recognize that this method applies specifically when the fear of enemy attack prevents normal congregational prayer.
The condition of 'intense fear' triggers the allowance for this method.
2
Understand the division of the congregation
The imam divides the people into two groups (طائفتين): one group faces the enemy while the other stands behind the imam to pray.
This ensures continuous defense.
3
Trace the rak'ah sequence for each group
The imam leads the first group in one rak'ah (with sajdah). After the second sajdah, the first group goes to guard, and the second group comes. The imam leads them in one rak'ah, then completes tashahhud and salam. The imam's prayer is complete. Then each group completes their remaining individual rak'ahs (one each, without recitation for the first group, with recitation for the second).
The first group's second rak'ah is without recitation because they already recited behind the imam.
4

If the imam is a resident (muqim), he prays two rak'ahs with each group (for a four-rak'ah prayer). For Maghrib, he prays two rak'ahs with the first group and one rak'ah with the second group.
The imam's residency status affects the number of rak'ahs.
5
Learn the prohibitions and allowances
Fighting during the prayer invalidates it. In extreme fear, prayer may be performed individually while riding or walking, with gestures and without facing the qibla.
The ruling shows flexibility to preserve life while maintaining prayer obligation.

فإن دُفن ولم يُصلَّ عليه : صُلِّي على قبره إلى ثلاثة أيام في الشتاء، وسبعة في الصيف، ولا يُصلَّى بعد ذلك.
If he was buried and no prayer was performed over him: prayer is performed over his grave up to three days in winter, and seven in summer, and no prayer is performed after that.
Source pages p. 62

Commentary
This passage states the ruling for performing funeral prayer at the grave when the deceased was buried without prayer. In the Hanafi school, it is recommended (sunnah) to pray at the grave within three days in winter and seven days in summer, as the body is expected to remain intact during that time. After those periods, the prayer is not performed, due to the likelihood of decomposition. This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
الشتاء (al-shita'): Winter season, when the time limit for grave prayer is three days.
الصيف (al-sayf): Summer season, when the time limit for grave prayer is seven days.
