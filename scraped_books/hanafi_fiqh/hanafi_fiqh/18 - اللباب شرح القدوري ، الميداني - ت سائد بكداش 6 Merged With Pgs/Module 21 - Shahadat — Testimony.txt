Ruling on evidence for establishing fosterage

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Fosterage, like lineage, requires strong proof: the testimony of women alone is insufficient; two men or one man and two women are needed.

This passage from the Lubab al-Quduri addresses a specific point of evidence law: how to prove a fosterage relationship in court. Fosterage (rida') creates marital prohibitions akin to blood relations, so Islamic law demands reliable testimony. The Hanafi school prescribes a minimum witness requirement, which we will examine here.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act requiring proof
Recognize that the passage deals with proving fosterage (rida'), which affects marriage prohibitions. Understand that such matters require formal evidence in court.
2

In most civil and financial cases, women's testimony alone is not accepted; they must be accompanied by a man. Hudud and certain family matters have stricter rules. This passage applies the general standard to fosterage.
Exception: childbirth and related matters accept women alone due to necessity.
3
State the required combination of witnesses
The text explicitly states two acceptable combinations: two male witnesses (rajulān) OR one male and two female witnesses (rajul wa imra'atān). Any other combination, especially women alone, is rejected.
4
Apply the rule to a hypothetical scenario
Test your understanding: if only female witnesses come forward, the claim is not proven and no legal effect (e.g., marriage prohibition) can be established based on that testimony.
This reinforces the binding nature of the requirement.

Proof of Birth for a Divorced Woman: Witness Requirements
Intra-Hanafi dispute on witnesses for birth of a child in iddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the testimony needed to prove the birth of a child to a woman in iddah, and state the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires two male witnesses; the Sahibayn accept one woman's testimony.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement on the number of witnesses required to establish the lineage of a child born to a woman during her iddah. The ruling has practical implications for the child's paternity and inheritance. You will see how the Sahibayn's more lenient view is often preferred in later practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The case: a woman in iddah gives birth. The court needs to confirm the child's lineage to the ex-husband. What proof is needed?
The dispute is about the number of witnesses, not the principle of testimony.
2
State Abu Hanifa's rule
Abu Hanifa requires the standard shahada: two men or one man and two women. Without that, lineage is not proven — unless the pregnancy was visibly known or the husband admits paternity.
Exceptions: visible pregnancy (the community knows) or husband's acknowledgment waive the need for witnesses.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say: the testimony of a single woman is sufficient in all cases because women are the ones who typically witness births.
The Sahibayn's view is easier to apply, especially where only female witnesses are available.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
If only one woman witnesses the birth, Abu Hanifa would not establish lineage, but the Sahibayn would. This affects the child's legal status.
Later Hanafi fatwa often adopts the Sahibayn's view for facilitation (takhfif).
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Many later authorities (e.g., Ibn Abidin) consider the Sahibayn's view the operative ruling, though both are valid within the school.
The teacher's commentary notes that the Sahibayn's view is preferred due to necessity.

This lesson presents a specific ruling from the chapter of qasamah. The text is brief but encodes a key principle that distinguishes qasamah testimony from ordinary shahadah. Identify the conditions

The ruling applies only when both conditions are met.

The consequence is that such testimony is rejected entirely. In ordinary testimony, these conditions would not necessarily invalidate, but in qasamah the rules are stricter.

Core Idea
Testimony is a fard; required witnesses vary: four men for zina, two men for other hudud and qisas, two men or one man and two women for other rights, one woman for hidden female matters.

This passage from the Book of Testimonies establishes the basic framework for when testifying is obligatory and how many witnesses are needed. It distinguishes between cases where concealment is recommended (hudud) and where it is required to testify (property rights). Understanding these categories is essential for knowing the validity of testimony in court.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation ruling
Note that testimony is fard (obligatory) when demanded by the claimant; however, for hudud penalties the witness may choose to conceal, and concealment is better.
The obligation is not absolute – it depends on the type of case.
2
Distinguish the theft exception
In theft involving property, the witness must testify but should say 'he took' rather than 'he stole' to avoid directly testifying for a hadd penalty.
This preserves the option of shubha (doubt) for the accused.
3
List the four categories of witness requirements
Four categories: (a) Zina: four men; (b) Other hudud and qisas: two men; (c) Other rights (marriage, divorce, etc.): two men or one man and two women; (d) Hidden female matters (childbirth, virginity): one woman.
Women's testimony is wholly excluded from hudud and qisas.
4
Identify the universal conditions
All testimony requires 'adalah (upright character) and the proper wording of shahada (e.g., 'ashhadu').
Without these, testimony is invalid.

States the obligation of testimony and rule for hudud cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligation of testimony when demanded and explain the distinction between hudud and financial rights in testimony.

Core Idea
Testimony is obligatory when demanded, but in hudud concealment is better except for financial rights.

This passage establishes two core principles of testimony in Hanafi fiqh. First, when a claimant demands your testimony, it becomes an obligation you must fulfill. Second, for hudud punishments, the default recommendation is to conceal the sin, except when a financial right is involved—then you must testify, but carefully avoid wording that triggers the hadd penalty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general obligation
The passage begins by stating that testimony is fard (obligatory) when the claimant demands it. Concealment is not permissible in that situation.
2
Recognize the hudud exception
For hudud cases, the witness has a choice. Concealment is better (mustahab) because Allah loves covering sins. This is the default rule.
3
Understand the financial rights exception
Even in hudud, if the case involves a financial right (e.g., theft of property), the witness must testify about the property. However, they should say 'he took' not 'he stole' to avoid triggering the hadd penalty unnecessarily.
This balances the individual's right to property with the societal interest in leniency for hudud.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Witnesses are liable for retraction only if their testimony caused a loss that would not have occurred otherwise.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri lists numerous cases in which witnesses retract their testimony after a judgment has been based on it. The general rule is that liability (ضمان) arises only when the retraction reveals that the testimony caused a harm that would not have occurred without it. We will examine each scenario to see how this principle is applied, including cases involving marriage, sale, divorce, manumission, retaliation, and sub-witnesses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle
Understand that liability for retraction is based on causation of harm. If the testimony merely affirmed an existing obligation (e.g., a proper dower), retraction causes no new loss. If it created a false obligation or undervalued an asset, the witnesses must compensate the difference.
This principle underlies all the specific rulings.
2

For testimony to a marriage with a proper dower (مهر المثل), no liability because the marriage is valid and the dower is already due. For excessive dower, witnesses owe the excess. These cases hinge on whether the testimony created a new financial obligation.

Testimony to a sale at or above market value: no loss, no liability. Testimony below market value: witnesses are liable for the deficiency (the difference between market value and the testified price).
The loss is the underpayment caused by the false testimony.
4

Testimony to manumission: witnesses owe the slave's value because the master lost an asset. Testimony to retaliation (qisas) followed by execution: witnesses owe blood money (diyah) but are not subject to qisas because multiple persons cannot be executed for one life.
Qisas is not applied to more than one in Hanafi fiqh.

Witnesses to ihsan (حصانة) are not liable because their testimony did not cause the hadd penalty (stoning requires four witnesses; their retraction does not affect it).
Liability follows from the causal link between the testimony and the loss.

Liability for Retraction: Oath Witnesses vs. Condition Witnesses
Ruling on liability when witnesses of oath and condition retract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on liability when witnesses of oath and condition retract, and explain the difference in legal effect between the two types of testimony.

Core Idea
Liability falls only on those who directly caused the legal outcome, not those who proved a prerequisite.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in witness retraction: two witnesses testify to the taking of an oath, and two others testify to the existence of a condition that triggers the oath. When both pairs retract, the Hanafi ruling limits liability to the oath witnesses. This distinction rests on the principle that causation of the legal effect determines liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sets of witnesses
The passage describes witnesses of the oath (yamin) and witnesses of the condition (shart). Understand that they testify to different facts.
The condition 'shart' is a prerequisite for the oath.
2
Determine the legal consequence of each testimony
The oath witnesses directly establish that the oath was taken, which changes the legal outcome (e.g., the defendant is absolved). The condition witnesses only prove a preliminary fact, not the act itself.
The oath is the decisive act; the condition merely enables it.
3
Apply the ruling on retraction liability
According to the text, liability (daman) falls only on the oath witnesses upon retraction. The condition witnesses are not liable because their testimony did not directly cause the harm.
The key phrase 'الضمان على شهود اليمين خاصة' indicates exclusivity.
4
Understand the underlying principle
Hanafi law holds a witness liable only if their testimony directly caused harm; here, only the oath witnesses' retraction reverses the initial judgment. This is consistent with the general rule that liability follows direct causation (al-ghurm bi al-ghunm).
Compare with rules on direct vs. indirect causes in other chapters.

Testimony of Partitioners and Claims of Error
Ruling on accepting partitioners' testimony and claims of mistake

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting partitioners' testimony and identify when a claim of error requires evidence.

Core Idea
The partitioners' testimony is binding; a claim of error after acknowledged receipt requires proof.

This lesson addresses disputes that arise after the partition of jointly owned property. In Hanafi law, the testimony of the two partitioners (qāsimān) is given significant weight to ensure the finality of the division. However, if one co-partitioner later claims a mistake, especially after having acknowledged full receipt, the law imposes a strict evidential burden. This balance maintains justice while preventing endless litigation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct situations: first, when the co-partitioners disagree and the partitioners testify; second, when one party claims error after acknowledging receipt.
These are sequential: the testimony of the partitioners is accepted first, then a claim of error is handled differently.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For the first: the partitioners' testimony is accepted (precedent testimony). For the second: the claimant is not believed unless he brings evidence (bayyinah).
The second ruling is a restriction on the first; the acknowledgment of receipt overrides the claim unless proven otherwise.
3
Identify the conditions
For the first ruling, the condition is that the partitioners testify and the co-partitioners disagree. For the second, the condition is that the claimant had previously acknowledged receipt before witnesses.
These conditions trigger the respective rulings.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is that partition is meant to be final. Acknowledgment of receipt (ashhad ‘alā nafsihi bi al-istīfā’) creates a strong presumption that cannot be overturned by a mere claim.
This protects the reliability of transactions and prevents frivolous re-litigation.
5
Apply to a concrete example
Imagine two brothers divide inherited land. The partitioners testify that each received his share. Brother A signed a receipt. Later, A claims part of his share is with B. The judge will not accept A's claim unless A produces witnesses or documentary evidence of the error.
This demonstrates the practical effect of the ruling.

This protects the finality of partition and prevents frivolous claims after settlement.

Acceptance of Statements from Child, Slave, Sinner, and Just Person
Rulings on whose statements are accepted in different contexts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify in which contexts the statements of a child, slave, fasiq, and just person are accepted according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, acceptance of a statement depends on context: low-stakes for gifts, moderate for transactions, strict for religion.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out a key principle: not every person's word carries the same weight in all legal matters. The Hanafi school distinguishes three contexts—gifts and permissions, commercial transactions, and religious reports—each with its own threshold for whose statement is considered reliable. Understanding this gradation prevents both the rejection of valid evidence and the acceptance of unreliable testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three contexts
The passage mentions three distinct legal contexts: (1) gifts and permissions, (2) commercial transactions, and (3) religious reports (akhbar al-diyanat).
These contexts correspond to increasing levels of religiosity and legal consequence.
2
Match each context with the accepted declarant
For gifts/permissions: the statement of a child and slave is accepted. For transactions: the statement of a fasiq is accepted. For religious reports: only the statement of a just person ('adl) is accepted.
Memorize these pairings—they are the core of the ruling.
3
Derive the underlying principle
The stringency of acceptance correlates with the religious significance of the matter. Mundane matters (gifts, transactions) require less probity; religious reports require the highest probity ('adalah).
This principle explains why a fasiq is accepted in transactions but not in religious reports.
4
Compare with the common misconception
A common mistake is to think a fasiq is never accepted. The passage explicitly restricts the rejection to religious contexts, not transactions.
This distinction is a hallmark of Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage classifies the acceptability of statements based on context. In Hanafi fiqh, for gifts (hibah) and permissions (idhn), the statement of a discerning child or a slave is accepted because no high level of reliability is required. In commercial transactions (mu'amalat), even a fasiq (someone known for sin) is accepted as long as he is not known to lie. However, in matters of religious reports (akhbar al-diyanat), such as reports about hadith or witnessing in religious matters, only a 'adl (just, upright) person is accepted.

Key Terms
الفاسق (al-fasiq): A person who commits major sins or persists in minor sins; not trustworthy in religious matters but may be accepted in transactions.
العدل (al-'adl): A just, upright person who avoids major sins and is reliable in testimony and reports.
