Effect of Third Party Compensation on Gift Revocation
Rulings on compensation by a third party and its effect on the donor's right to revoke.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi rulings on gift revocation when a third party voluntarily compensates the donee and apply them to scenarios involving partial claims on the gift or compensation.

Core Idea
Accepting voluntary third-party compensation drops the donor's right to revoke the gift.

This lesson examines three precise rulings from the Hanafi school on how a third party's voluntary compensation affects the donor's ability to revoke a gift. The rulings distinguish between full compensation, a partial claim on the gift itself, and a partial claim on the compensation, ensuring equitable adjustments between donor and donee.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage describes a situation where a third party (ajnabī) voluntarily gives compensation (ʿiwaḍ) to the donee on behalf of the donor. The donor then accepts (qabaḍa) this compensation.
The third party is not obligated; the compensation is a gift to the donee.
2
Determine the effect on revocation
The text states that once the donor takes the compensation, the right to revoke (rujūʿ) drops. This is the baseline ruling: full compensation extinguishes the right to revoke.
The compensation is treated as an implied satisfaction, making the gift irrevocable.
3
Handle a partial claim on the gift
If half of the gifted property is later claimed by a rightful owner (istiḥqāq), the donor can take back half of the compensation he received. The compensation is adjusted proportionally.
The gift is partially invalidated, so the compensation is partially returned.
4
Handle a partial claim on the compensation
If half of the compensation itself is claimed (e.g., the third party's property was not entirely his), the donor cannot revoke the gift unless he first returns the remaining compensation to the donee. After returning it, he may revoke the entire gift.
The donor must restore the part of compensation that is valid before revoking.
5
Apply the rules consistently
These rulings maintain fairness: the donor cannot both keep compensation and revoke, and partial claims require proportional adjustments.
Memorize the three cases: full compensation, half gift claimed, half compensation claimed.

Conditions for Valid Revocation of Gift
Requirement of mutual consent or court order for gift revocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions that make gift revocation valid in Hanafi law and explain why unilateral revocation is invalid.

Core Idea
Gift revocation requires mutual consent or a judge's order; unilateral donor action is invalid.

This short passage from al-Lubāb clarifies a key procedural rule in Hanafi gift law. Once a gift is delivered, the donor's right to revoke is not absolute. The text specifies two and only two pathways to a valid revocation: mutual agreement of both parties, or a binding court order. Understanding this rule prevents disputes and protects the stability of completed transfers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is revoking a gift. The ruling is that it is not valid (lā yaṣiḥḥ) unless either of two conditions are met.
The negative particle 'lā' indicates prohibition of invalidity.
2
State the first condition
Mutual consent (tarāḍin) of both donor and donee. The donee must voluntarily agree to return the gift.
Consent can be explicit or implied.
3
State the second condition
A judge's ruling (ḥukm al-ḥākim). If the donee refuses but there is a legitimate cause (e.g., ingratitude), the donor can seek a court order.
The judge's ruling is binding and overrides the donee's objection.
4
Understand the legal effect
If neither condition exists, the revocation is invalid and the gift remains with the donee. The donor cannot take back by force or unilateral declaration.
This maintains stability in completed gifts.

No Recourse for Donee After Gifted Property Destroyed
Ruling when gifted property is destroyed and then claimed by a third party.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation when a gifted item is destroyed and then claimed by a rightful owner, and explain why the donee cannot seek recourse from the donor.

Core Idea
Donee bears loss of destroyed gifted property even when title proves defective.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in gift law (hiba): what happens if the gifted property is destroyed while in the donee's possession, and then a third party proves they are the true owner? The donee must compensate the rightful owner, but may wonder if he can recover that amount from the original donor. The text gives a clear answer, grounded in the principles of possession and liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and events
Recognize the three roles: donor (wāhib), donee (mawhūb lah), and rightful claimant (mustaḥiqq). The item is first given, then destroyed, then claimed.
2
State the ruling on recourse
The donee, after compensating the rightful owner, cannot (lam yarjiʿ) take back anything from the donor.
3
Understand the legal basis
The donor's lack of ownership makes the gift void (fāsid), but the donee's possession at the time of destruction grounds liability on him, not the donor.

Rulings on ʿUmra and Ruqba
Hanafi rulings on life grant and life tenancy with intra-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ʿumra and ruqba, distinguish the intra-madhhab dispute, and identify the muftā bihī position.

Core Idea
ʿUmra is valid and passes to heirs; ruqba is invalid due to uncertainty—Abu Yusuf alone dissents.

This lesson examines two types of property grants: ʿumra (life grant) and ruqba (life tenancy with reversion). The Hanafi school unanimously validates ʿumra, but disagrees on ruqba. Understanding this dispute clarifies the madhhab's approach to conditional gifts and uncertainty (gharar).

Guided Path
1
Understand ʿumra and its ruling
Identify the legal definition of ʿumra: a grant of property for the grantee's lifetime. According to the text, it is valid (jā'iz) for the grantee during his life and for his heirs after him. The condition of reversion to the donor is void.
The hadith 'al-ʿumrā hiya lil-muʿmar lahu wa li-waraṣatihi' supports this.
2
Understand ruqba and its ruling
Define ruqba as a conditional grant where the survivor (donor or donee) keeps the property. The text states it is invalid (bāṭila) according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
The invalidity stems from uncertainty (gharar) in the conditional transfer.
3
Identify the intra-madhhab dispute
Note that Abu Yusuf differs: he considers ruqba valid. The text presents his view but does not adopt it.
4
Determine the muftā bihī
The relied-upon position is the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: ruqba is invalid.
This is the position taken in later Hanafi works like al-Hidayah.
5
Apply the distinction to new cases
Given a grant, classify it as ʿumra (valid, passes to heirs) or ruqba (invalid, remains with donor) based on the condition used.

Source Text

Commentary
This passage presents two types of grants: ʿumra (life grant) and ruqba (life tenancy with reversion). In ʿumra, the owner grants a property to another for the grantee's lifetime, stipulating that it reverts to the donor (or his heirs) after the grantee's death. The Hanafi school rules that ʿumra is valid: the grantee enjoys the property during his life, and after his death, it goes to his heirs, not back to the donor. This is based on a hadith that ʿumra is for the grantee and his heirs. As for ruqba, which is a grant where the surviving party (donor or donee) takes the property (e.g., 'if you die first, it goes to me; if I die first, it goes to you'), there is an intra-Hanafi dispute. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani rule it invalid (bāṭila) because it is a conditional transfer based on an uncertain future event, resembling a gharar. Abu Yusuf, however, considers it valid because it can be seen as a form of ʿumra. The relied-upon position (muftā bihī) is that ruqba is invalid, following the majority of the school.

Key Terms
عمرى (ʿumrā): A grant of property for the lifetime of the grantee, with ownership vesting in the grantee and passing to his heirs.
رقبى (ruqbā): A conditional grant where the survivor (either donor or donee) keeps the property; literally 'waiting'.

Ruling on gift of pregnant slave

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The unborn child is part of the mother; an attempt to exclude it from a gift is void.

In Hanafi contract law, a gift (hiba) transfers ownership. When the gifted item is a pregnant female slave, the donor may try to keep the unborn child. This lesson clarifies that the gift remains valid, but the exclusion fails because the pregnancy is considered inseparable from the mother. The donee thus receives both the slave and the child upon birth.

The exclusion is void (baṭala al-istithnā).
5

The pregnancy is juzʾ minhā (a part of her). In Hanafi law, an inseparable part cannot be separated by an exclusion clause.

Rulings on Charity (Sadaqah)
Rulings on charity requiring possession, prohibition in divisible undivided property, and no revocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four rulings on sadaqah and apply the conditions for validity.

Core Idea
Charity is like a gift: requires possession, cannot be in divisible undivided property, cannot be revoked after possession.

Charity (ṣadaqah) is a meritorious act in Islam, but its validity depends on specific conditions. The Hanafi school treats charity like a gift in requiring possession, but with stricter rules regarding undivided property and irrevocability. This lesson unpacks four key rulings that every Muslim should know to ensure their charitable giving is legally sound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal nature of charity
The passage opens by equating charity to a gift (hiba) in requiring possession (qabḍ). Recognize this as the foundational rule: without transfer of possession, the charity is not valid.
This is true even if the intention and declaration are present.
2
Understand the condition regarding undivided property
The second ruling prohibits charity in an undivided share (mushāʿ) that is capable of division. The reason is uncertainty (gharar) about the exact portion. If the property is indivisible, the charity may be valid.
Divisibility is determined by whether partitioning is physically possible without significant loss.
3
Recognize the exception for joint charity
The third ruling states that giving a single item to two poor persons as charity is valid. This shows that the prohibition on undivided property does not apply when the charity is given jointly to multiple recipients.
The item is considered to have been delivered to both in common.
4
State the rule of irrevocability
The fourth ruling declares that revocation after possession is invalid. Unlike a gift (hiba), which may sometimes be revoked under Hanafi law, charity is irrevocable because it is an act of worship seeking reward.
Revocation here means taking back the item from the recipient.

Vows to Give Charity: Obligation and Concession
Rulings on two charity vow scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of charity vows (nadhr) and explain the obligation for each, including the concession to keep living expenses.

Core Idea
A vow to give all you own is binding, but you may keep enough to survive and repay later.

Vows (nadhr) are serious commitments in Islamic law. When someone vows to give charity from his property, the type of property matters. But if he vows to give everything he owns, the law imposes a full obligation while also providing a compassionate concession for basic needs. This lesson unpacks these two scenarios and their practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of charity vows
Distinguish between a vow to give 'property' (mālihi) and a vow to give 'entire possession' (milkihi). These trigger different obligations.
The Arabic term 'milk' implies full ownership of all assets.
2
Determine the ruling for each vow
For mālihi: give from zakatable types (e.g., gold, silver, trade goods). For milkihi: give all of it, but with a concession to keep living expenses.
Zakatable types are those with a recognized value in Sharīʿa.
3
Apply the concession for the second vow
The mufti advises the person to keep only enough for his own and his dependents' basic expenses until he earns more. Later, when he earns, he gives charity equal to what he kept.
This prevents hardship while fulfilling the vow.
4
Understand the underlying principle
Vows are binding (wajib), but necessity (ḥāja) allows temporary retention. The principle balances obligation with the preservation of life.
This aligns with the Hanafi legal maxim: 'al-Ḥājā tanzilu manzilat al-ḍarūrā' – need is treated like necessity.

Proof of Zina and Punishment for the Muhsan
Ruling statement on proof and punishment of zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two methods of proving zina, list the conditions for each, and identify the punishment for a muhsan fornicator.

Core Idea
Zina requires four witnesses or four confessions; the muhsan is stoned to death.

The Hanafi school applies the strictest evidentiary standards for the crime of zina, grounded in the Qur'anic requirement of four witnesses. This passage from the Quduri matn details the two exclusive methods of proof: testimony (bayyinah) and confession (iqrar). For each, specific questions and conditions must be met before the hadd punishment becomes binding. The most severe penalty—stoning to death—is reserved for the muhsan fornicator, reflecting the gravity of the offense within a valid marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods of proof
The text states that zina is established by two means: bayyinah (testimony) and iqrar (confession). Note that no other evidence, such as pregnancy or circumstantial evidence, is accepted.
2
Examine the conditions for testimony
Testimony requires exactly four male witnesses who must describe the act with graphic detail (e.g., like a rod in a kohl container) and answer five questions from the judge. The judge also verifies their uprightness ('adala) in public and private.
If the witnesses do not meet these conditions, they are liable for the hadd of qadhf (false accusation).
3
Examine the conditions for confession
The accused must be an adult, sane, and confess four times in four separate sessions. After each confession, the judge should turn him away (to allow retraction). After the fourth, the judge asks the same five questions; if detailed, the hadd becomes binding.
Confession in one session or fewer than four times is insufficient.
4
Determine the punishment based on status
If the fornicator is muhsan (free, adult Muslim who has had valid marital intercourse), the punishment is stoning until death. This passage focuses on stoning; flogging for ghayr muhsan is covered elsewhere.
The Hanafi definition of muhsan includes freedom, Islam, adulthood, sanity, and consummated marriage.

Zina is established by proof (bayanah) and by confession (iqrar).

The proof: that four witnesses testify against a man or a woman for zina. The imam asks them: what is zina? How was it? Where did he commit zina? With whom did he commit zina? And when did he commit zina?

If they explain that and say: "We saw him having intercourse with her in her private part like a kohl stick in the kohl container," and the judge investigates about them and they are found to be trustworthy in private and public, then he rules by their testimony.

Confession: that a mature, sane person confesses to zina against himself four times. In four sittings of the confessor. Whenever he confesses, the judge rebukes him. When his confession is complete four times, the judge asks him about zina: what is it? How was it? Where did he commit zina? With whom? And when? If he explains that, then the hadd becomes obligatory upon him.

If the fornicator is muhsan (married and free), he is stoned with stones until death. They take him out to an open land. The witnesses initiate the stoning, then the imam, then the people. If the witnesses refuse to initiate, the hadd is dropped. If the fornicator is a confessor, the imam initiates, then the people. He is washed, shrouded, and the funeral prayer is offered for him.

If he is not muhsan and is free, his hadd is one hundred lashes. The imam orders him to be struck with a whip that has no knots, a moderate beating. His clothes are removed (except to cover awrah), and the beating is distributed over his limbs except his head, face, and private parts. If he is a slave, he is lashed fifty similarly.

If the confessor retracts his confession before the hadd is carried out or during it, his retraction is accepted and he is released. It is recommended for the imam to prompt the confessor to retract, saying: "Perhaps you only touched or kissed."

The man and woman are equal in that, except that the woman's clothes are not removed except the fur and padding. And if a pit is dug for her in stoning, it is permissible. The master does not carry out the hadd on his male or female slave except with the permission of the imam.

A confession must be made four times in separate sessions, with the judge rebuking each time—protecting against false guilt.

Confession is the second method to prove zina, but it is not taken lightly. The accused must confess four times in four different court sessions, and after each confession the judge must rebuke or discourage the confession. This process ensures that only a truly willing and guilty person is punished. This lesson explores the strict conditions for a valid confession.

Identify the confessor's qualifications

The Hadd Penalty for Qadhf (Slander)
Chapter on the hadd for qadhf with definitions and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the hadd of qadhf and distinguish between explicit slander and other insults.

Core Idea
Only explicit accusation of zina against a muhsan incurs the hadd of eighty (free) or forty (slave) lashes.

This chapter introduces the fixed penalty (hadd) for falsely accusing a chaste person of zina. Unlike ta'zir for general insults, qadhf carries a specific number of lashes if the slandered person meets the conditions of ihsan. The text also details who can demand the penalty, how it is executed, and what counts as explicit slander.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine that qadhf is an explicit accusation of zina against a muhsan person. The ruling is a hadd of 80 lashes for a free person, 40 for a slave.
The hadd is wajib when conditions are met and demand is made.
2
List the conditions for ihsan
The slandered person must be free, sane, adult, Muslim, and chaste from zina. This is the definition of muhsan for qadhf.
Note: Ihsan here does not require marriage, unlike the ihsan for zina.
3
Note the execution and exceptions
The lashes are distributed over limbs, and the person is not stripped but heavy clothing (padding and fur) is removed. Recantation after confession does not stop the hadd.
This differs from other hudud where retraction may stop the penalty.
4
Recognize who can demand the hadd
Only the slandered person if alive. If deceased, only the one whose lineage is affected can demand. A son (even non-Muslim) or a slave can demand if the deceased is muhsan.
But a slave cannot demand against his own master for slandering his mother.
5
Distinguish explicit slander from non-hadd insults
Insults like 'ya nabati' (O Nabati) do not incur hadd because they do not explicitly accuse of zina.
Such insults fall under ta'zir, not hadd.

Hadd al-Qadhf and Tazir: Conditions and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Rulings on hadd al-qadhf and tazir with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the hadd of qadhf, identify expressions that incur tazir, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the maximum tazir punishment.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school distinguishes between hadd for qadhf and tazir for lesser accusations, with a dispute on tazir's maximum.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri outlines the rulings on the fixed punishment for false accusation of zina (qadhf) and the discretionary punishment for other insults. It clarifies who is liable for hadd, what expressions trigger tazir, and the limits of tazir according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Understanding these distinctions is essential for a mufti to apply the correct punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify expressions that are NOT qadhf
The text gives examples like 'son of rainwater' or attributing a person to his uncle; these do not constitute false accusation of zina and thus no hadd.
2
Recognize when hadd for qadhf is suspended
Accusing someone who has committed unlawful intercourse without ownership, or accusing a woman after li'an with a child, does not trigger hadd. However, li'an without a child triggers hadd.
3
Distinguish between hadd and tazir
Accusing slaves, disbelievers, or Muslims of non-zina (e.g., 'transgressor') leads to tazir. Words like 'donkey' or 'pig' incur no punishment at all.
4
Memorize tazir limits and the dispute
Maximum tazir is 39 lashes (Abu Hanifa), minimum 3. Abu Yusuf allows up to 75 lashes. The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's.
5
Understand consequences on testimony
A Muslim who receives hadd for qadhf permanently loses his testimony; a disbeliever who receives hadd and then converts has his testimony accepted.
