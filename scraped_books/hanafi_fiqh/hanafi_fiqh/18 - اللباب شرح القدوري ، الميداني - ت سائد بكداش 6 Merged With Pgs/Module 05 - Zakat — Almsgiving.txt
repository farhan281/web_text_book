Conditions and Exemptions for Zakat (Hanafi)
Sets out conditions and exemptions for zakat obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for zakat obligation and identify who is exempt from zakat according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Zakat is wajib on free, adult, sane Muslims owning nisab for a year; exemptions apply to minors, insane, mukatab, and debt-ridden.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri lays out the foundational rules of zakat. Understanding who is obligated and who is exempt is critical for correct practice. We will examine each condition and exemption as taught by the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act is being discussed? It is zakat. What is its hukm level? According to Hanafi school, it is wajib.
Zakat is wajib, not fard, because the evidence is zanni al-thubut according to Hanafi usul.
2
List the conditions for obligation
Identify each condition mentioned: free, Muslim, adult, sane, complete ownership of nisab, and passage of one lunar year (hawl).
These are shurut al-wujub.
3
List the exemptions
Who or what is exempt? Minors, insane, mukatab (slave with contract), and one whose debt encompasses wealth.
Also assets like houses, clothes, furniture, riding animals, etc. are exempt.
4
Note the requirement of intention
Zakat is not valid without niyyah at the time of payment or setting aside the amount.
Intention is a wajib, not a shart.
5
Understand the consequence of voluntary charity
If someone gives all wealth in charity without intending zakat, the zakat obligation is dropped.
But caution: this should not be used to evade zakat.

Book of Zakat. Zakat is obligatory upon the free, the Muslim, the adult, the sane, when he owns a complete nisab and a year has passed. There is no zakat upon a minor, nor an insane person, nor a mukatab. And whoever has a debt that encompasses his wealth: there is no zakat upon him. And if his wealth is more than the debt: he gives zakat on the surplus if it reaches a nisab. There is no zakat on residential houses, body garments, household furniture, riding animals, servant slaves, and weapons of use. And whoever gives all his wealth in charity without intending zakat: the obligation of zakat falls from him.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri states the general rules for the obligation of zakat. It opens by listing five conditions for zakat to be wajib: being free, Muslim, adult, sane, owning a complete nisab in full ownership, and the passage of one lunar year (hawl). Then it lists exemptions: minors, the insane, and a mukatab (a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom) are not required to pay zakat. A person whose debt encompasses his wealth also pays no zakat; but if his wealth exceeds the debt, he pays zakat on the excess if it reaches nisab. Certain assets are exempt from zakat: dwellings, clothes, furniture, riding animals, domestic slaves, and weapons. The passage then emphasizes the necessity of niyyah (intention) – it must be made at the time of payment or at the time of setting aside the zakat amount. Finally, if someone gives away all his wealth voluntarily without intending zakat, the obligation of zakat is dropped (but one should be careful not to avoid zakat through this).

Key Terms
زكاة (zakāh): A mandatory charitable contribution on specific wealth, one of the five pillars of Islam.
نصاب (niṣāb): The minimum amount of wealth that triggers the obligation of zakat.
حول (ḥawl): The passage of one lunar year from the time the nisab is possessed in full ownership.
مكاتب (mukātab): A slave who has made a contract to purchase his freedom by paying installments.

Zakat on Camels: Schedule and Nisab
Lists zakat obligations for camels by number

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the nisab for camels and identify the zakat due for any number of camels from 5 to 200+ using the Hanafi schedule.

Core Idea
Zakat on camels starts at five grazing camels; the due amount escalates with number and age categories.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents the detailed schedule for zakat on camels, a key obligation for wealthy camel owners. The text covers the nisab, the conditions of sa'imah and hawl, and the precise animal due for each range of camel numbers. Understanding this schedule is essential for fulfilling zakat correctly according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify nisab and conditions
Note that zakat is not due on fewer than five camels (dhawd). The camels must be sa'imah (grazing) and a full lunar year (hawl) must pass. These are the necessary conditions for zakat to be wajib.
Nisab is five camels; fewer than five means no zakat.
2
Understand the first cycle (5-120 camels)
Map each range of camel numbers to the due item. From 5-9 camels: one sheep; 10-14: two sheep; 15-19: three sheep; 20-24: four sheep; 25-35: bint makhad (1-year-old female camel); 36-45: bint labun (2-year-old); 46-60: hiqqah (3-year-old); 61-75: jadha'ah (4-year-old); 76-90: two bint labun; 91-120: two hiqqah.
Memorize these ranges and corresponding animals.
3
Recognize the restart pattern after 120
After 120 camels, the schedule restarts: for every five camels, the due is one sheep, but the previously due higher categories are also included. For example, from 121-125 camels, the due is one sheep plus the two hiqqah from the previous bracket? Actually the text says 'then the obligation is restarted, so on five camels is one sheep along with the two hiqqah' meaning the pattern resets but the base obligation includes the camel categories.
This restarting continues every fifty camels.
4
Apply the rule for 196-200 and beyond
For 196-200 camels, the due is four hiqqah. Then the cycle restarts again as it did after 150. The text states that bukht (Bactrian) and 'irab (Arabian) camels are treated equally—no distinction.
The schedule repeats indefinitely every fifty camels after 150.

Zakat on Camels: Fixed Obligations and the Restart Rule
Lists zakat obligations for camels at fixed thresholds and the restart rule after 150

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the zakat obligations for camels at each threshold from 5 to 200 and apply the restart rule after 150 camels.

Core Idea
After 150 camels, the zakat scale restarts from a base of 5 camels.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn is a memorization anchor that lists the fixed zakat payments due at specific camel counts. It covers the scale up to 200 camels, including a crucial restart rule after 150. Understanding this scale is essential for correctly calculating zakat on camels.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base thresholds
List the key numbers: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, up to 150. At each, note the animal due (e.g., at 25: bint makhad).
Memorize the pattern: for 5-20, pay sheep; at 25, begin camel categories.
2
Note the animals for each threshold
For 25-150, the animals are bint makhad (25-35), bint labun (36-45), hiqqah (46-60), etc. The text gives later nodes: at 150, three hiqaq.
The categories change at 36, 46, 61, etc. Consult a full table if needed.
3
Understand the restart rule at 150
After 150 camels, the fard (obligation) is restarted. This means the next 5 camels above 150 pay 1 sheep, the next 10 pay 2 sheep, and so on, following the same pattern as from 5 to 150.
The restart is a new cycle, not a continuation.
4
Recognize the special case at 196
At 196 camels, the obligation is four hiqaq, up to 200. This is an exception within the restart cycle.
From 196 to 200, the payment is fixed at 4 hiqaq, not following the incremental pattern.
5
Apply the equality of camel types
The text states that hybrid camels (bukht) and pure Arab camels (arab) are treated equally for zakat. All camel types count together.
This prevents evasion by mixing breeds.

Zakat on Cattle: The Dispute on the Increment (40–60)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on incremental zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on zakat for cattle between 40 and 60 and explain the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) of the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
The increment between 40 and 60 cattle is disputed: Abu Hanifa imposes proportional zakat, while the Sahibayn defer to 60.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn sets out the zakat thresholds for cattle and introduces an important intra-Hanafi dispute. While the basic rulings at 30, 40, 60, 70, and 80 are agreed, the treatment of the increment from 41 to 59 is contested between Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this dispute clarifies both the method of zakat calculation and the nature of ikhtilaf within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify Agreed Thresholds
Find the rulings at 30, 40, 60, 70, and 80 cattle. At 30: one tabi'. At 40: one musinnah. At 60: two tabi's. At 70: musinnah + tabi'. At 80: two musinnahs.
These are not disputed between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
2
Locate the Disputed Zone
The dispute is specifically for numbers between 40 and 60 (41–59). The text says: 'When they exceed forty, zakat is obligatory on the increase proportionally up to sixty, according to Abu Hanifa.'
The Sahibayn disagree on this proportional obligation.
3
State Abu Hanifa's View
Abu Hanifa holds that for each extra cow beyond 40, a proportional fraction of a musinnah is due: for 1 extra, 1/40 musinnah; for 2 extra, 1/20 musinnah; for 3 extra, 3/40 musinnah; for 4 extra, 1/10 musinnah.
The fraction is based on the ration 1 musinnah per 40 cattle.
4
State the Sahibayn's View
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that no additional zakat is due on the increment until the total reaches 60. At 60, two tabi's are due. It is as if the 40–59 zone is a blank.
The Sahibayn treat 40–59 as a single bracket with no change in zakat.
5
Determine Practical Difference
Calculate for a herd of 45: Under Abu Hanifa, zakat = 1 musinnah (for the first 40) + 5/40 musinnah = 1.125 musinnah. Under Sahibayn, zakat = 1 musinnah (only for the 40). The extra 5 cows incur no zakat until they reach 60.
This thamarah al-khilaf directly affects the amount due.

Commentary
This passage from the matn of Al-Quduri presents the rulings for zakat on cattle (domestic cows) and includes an intra-Hanafi dispute. First, it establishes the general rule: no zakat on fewer than 30 grazing cattle. At 30, after a year, one tabi' (a one-year-old calf, male or female) is due. At 40, one musinnah (a two-year-old or older cow) is due. Then comes the dispute: when the herd increases beyond 40 up to 60, Abu Hanifa holds that zakat is due on each extra head proportionally. For example, one extra cow requires one quarter of a tenth of a musinnah (i.e., 1/40 of a musinnah), two extra require half of a tenth, etc. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that nothing is due on the increase until the total reaches 60, at which point two tabi's are due. The text then continues with the agreed-upon rulings for higher numbers: at 70, one musinnah and one tabi'; at 80, two musinnahs.

مسنة (musinnah): A female cow that has entered its third year.
السائمة (al-sa'imah): Grazing cattle that are let to pasture freely for most of the year.

Zakat of Cows: Nisab and Amounts
Ruling statement on zakat thresholds for cows

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the zakat due for 30, 40, 90, and 100 cows, and explain the alternation rule for every ten cows beyond that.

Core Idea
For every 30 cows, one tabi'; for every 40, one musinnah; the obligation alternates every ten.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn presents the concise rule for zakat on cows. Instead of memorizing every number, you learn a simple pattern that applies to all herd sizes above the nisab. Understanding this rule is essential for every Muslim cattle owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify base thresholds
Learn that for 30 cows, 1 tabi' (two-year-old) is due; for 40 cows, 1 musinnah (three-year-old) is due.
These are the foundational amounts.
2
Study the given examples
The text explicitly states: for 90 cows, 3 tabi'; for 100 cows, 2 tabi' and 1 musinnah.
90 = 30+30+30, 100 = 30+30+40
3
Understand the alternation rule
The obligation changes every ten cows, from tabi' to musinnah and vice versa. This pattern allows you to calculate for any number.
For 110 cows, it becomes 1 tabi' + 2 musinnah; for 120, 3 musinnah.
4
Apply to other numbers
Using the pattern, calculate zakat for numbers like 50, 60, 70, 80. Remember: break into groups of 30 and 40.
For 70 cows: 30 (1 tabi') + 40 (1 musinnah) = 1 tabi' + 1 musinnah.
5
Include buffaloes
Buffaloes (jawaamis) are treated identically to cows for zakat purposes.
No separate calculation needed.

Core Idea
Zakat on sheep starts at 40 heads; from 40–120: one; 121–200: two; 201–399: three; then one per hundred.

This passage from Al-Quduri sets the foundational thresholds for zakat on small livestock. It provides the exact amounts due at each bracket, clarifying that sheep and goats are treated identically. Understanding these precise numbers is essential for correct zakat calculation.

For forty to one hundred twenty sheep, the zakat is one sheep.
This bracket is inclusive of both ends.

The increase is triggered by one additional sheep beyond 120.

For 201 to 399 sheep, the zakat is three sheep.
Again, crossing 200 triggers the next bracket.
5
Apply the rule from 400 onwards
At 400, zakat is four sheep. Then for every additional hundred, one extra sheep is due. For example, 500 gives five sheep, 600 gives six, etc.
The phrase 'then for every hundred, one sheep' applies after 400, not from the start.

Zakat of Horses and Young Animals: Hanafi Positions
Rulings on zakat of horses, mules, donkeys, and young animals with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on zakat of horses, mules, donkeys, and young animals, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding young animals.

Core Idea
Zakat on horses is optionally per head or value; no zakat on male-only horses; young animals alone are exempt according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

This lesson covers the rulings on zakat for horses, mules, donkeys, and young animals (camels, sheep, cows). The passage presents Abu Hanifa's position with a khiyar (option) for mixed or female horses, the Sahibayn's disagreement on horses entirely, and an intra-Hanafi dispute on young animals between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf. Understanding these details is crucial for correct zakat calculation.

Identify the ruling on horses

Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on young animals

Read: 'ومن وجب عليه سن، فلم توجد عنده : أَخَذَ المُصَدِّقُ أعلى منها، ورد الفضل، أو أخذ دونَها، وأخذ الفضل. ويجوز دَفْعُ القيمة في الزكاة.' The collector may take a higher or lower age with adjustment, and paying monetary value (qimah) is allowed.

Commentary
This passage from the Lubab (Sharh al-Quduri) presents the rulings on zakat pertaining to horses, mules, donkeys, and young livestock, along with several intra-madhhab disputes within the Hanafi school. 

The chapter begins with the ruling on pasturing (sa'ima) horses. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, if pasturing horses include males and females, or females only, and a full lunar year passes, the owner has an option: either give one dinar per horse, or evaluate the horses and pay 2.5% of their value (5 dirhams per 200 dirhams). The relied-upon position in the school is Abu Hanifa's view, though the ruling has nuances.

Paying cash value instead of the animal is permitted. Working animals, pregnant animals, and those that are stall-fed (not sa'ima) have no zakat. The collector must take the average quality, not the best or worst. 

Additional rulings: if one possesses the nisab and acquires more of the same type during the year, it is added to the nisab and zakat is paid on the total at the end of the year. The definition of sa'ima is given: an animal that depends on grazing for most of the year; if fed for half the year or more, it is not sa'ima and no zakat is due. 

On the issue of surplus (al-afw) beyond the nisab, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that zakat is only on the nisab, not on the surplus. Muhammad holds that zakat is obligatory on both the nisab and the surplus (within the same type). If the wealth (subject to zakat) perishes after zakat becomes obligatory (e.g., at the end of the year), the obligation drops. If one pays zakat before the completion of the year while owning the nisab, it is valid (jazz).

عوامل (a'amila): Working animals used for labor, e.g., oxen for plowing or horses for transport.
العفو (al-'afw): The surplus beyond the nisab that is not subject to zakat according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on a Woman Giving Zakat to Her Husband
Intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat to husband.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a woman giving zakat to her husband, identify the evidence for each side, and state the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prohibits a woman from giving zakat to her husband; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow it, but the relied-upon fatwa is prohibition.

Among the prohibitions on relatives is the rule concerning spouses. The text records a well-known intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa holds that a woman cannot give her zakat to her husband, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, permit it. This lesson analyzes the arguments and the resulting practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Ruling
Locate the sentence: 'Wa la tadfa' al-mar'atu ila zawjiha 'inda Abi Hanifah, wa qala: tadfa'u ilayhi.' This is the core of the dispute.
The context is the prohibition of giving zakat to close relatives.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Argument
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: The husband's maintenance is obligatory upon the wife if she is wealthy. Giving him zakat would effectively benefit herself (since her obligation is reduced), negating the purpose of zakat. Also, zakat cannot be given to those whose maintenance is obligatory (like parents/children), and by analogy, the husband falls under this.
The key principle: avoiding benefit to oneself via zakat.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's Argument
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that the husband's maintenance is not a direct obligation on the wife; the prohibition for parents/children is clear, but the husband is not mentioned. She gives him zakat as a separate act of worship. The benefit to herself is indirect and not prohibited.
They rely on the general permissibility of giving to needy relatives.
4
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah)
Under Abu Hanifa's view, a woman must give her zakat to other categories. Under the Sahibayn's view, she may give to her husband if he is poor. This affects many families where the husband is in need.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, but the Sahibayn's view may be used for necessity.

Note that Abu Hanifa permits using silk as a pillow (no harm) while the Sahibayn consider it makruh. For war, Abu Hanifa considers it makruh while the Sahibayn permit it. Understand the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).
The mufta bihi for pillow is makruh; for war, it is permissible due to necessity.
3
Recognize the ruling on mixed fabrics (mulham)
A fabric with silk warp and cotton weft is permissible for men. This is an exception based on composition.
Other mixes with a non-silk weft are also permissible.
4
State the specific permissions for men regarding silver
Men may wear a silver ring, a silver belt, and silver decoration on a sword. All other gold and silver adornment is prohibited for men.
Women are permitted all gold and silver adornment.
5
Apply the ruling on dressing boys in gold and silk
It is makruh to dress a young boy in gold or silk. This is not haram but disliked.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn of Al-Quduri, commented on by Al-Maydani. It presents a series of rulings on silk and gold adornment. The first ruling is clear: silk is haram for men and halal for women. Then there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Abu Hanifa, using silk as a pillow is permissible (no harm), but the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it makruh. Similarly, wearing silk brocade in war is permissible according to the two companions but makruh according to Abu Hanifa. Mixed silk-cotton fabric (mulham) is permissible if the warp is silk and weft is cotton, or if it is a pure silk fabric (khazz). For gold and silver adornment, men are generally prohibited except for a silver ring, a silver belt, and silver decoration on a sword. Women may wear gold and silver. It is makruh to dress a boy in gold and silk, meaning it is not absolutely forbidden but disliked.

Key Terms

الديباج (al-dibaj): Silk brocade, a type of silk fabric with patterns.
الملحم (al-mulham): A fabric with silk warp and cotton weft, considered permissible for men as it is not pure silk.
