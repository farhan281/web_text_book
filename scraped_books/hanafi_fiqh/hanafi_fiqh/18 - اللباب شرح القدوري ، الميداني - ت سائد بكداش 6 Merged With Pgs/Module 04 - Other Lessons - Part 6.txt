Rulings on oaths involving wheat, flour, and bread

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oaths involving wheat and its derivatives, and explain how transformation (tabdeel) and custom (urf) determine whether an oath is broken.

Core Idea
Transforming wheat into bread changes its legal identity; flour to bread does not.

Oaths are interpreted according to common usage. This passage from al-Lubab presents three cases involving wheat, flour, and bread, showing that the transformation of the original substance and customary consumption determine whether the oath is broken. Understanding these distinctions helps avoid unintended oath-breaking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the three oaths: (1) swearing not to eat wheat, then eating bread from it; (2) swearing not to eat flour, then eating bread from it; (3) swallowing flour dry. These are the three rulings.
The third scenario is about consuming the flour in its raw form.
2
Analyze the first case: wheat to bread
According to Abu Hanifa, swearing not to eat wheat and then eating bread from that wheat does not break the oath. Why? Because bread is a different substance (transformation). The wheat has been fundamentally changed.
Other schools may differ; the text attributes this to Abu Hanifa.
3
Analyze the second case: flour to bread
Swearing not to eat flour and then eating bread from that flour breaks the oath. Why? Because bread is customarily considered consumption of the flour. Flour is the main ingredient, and in common usage, eating bread is eating flour.
This shows the role of custom (urf).
4
Analyze the third case: eating flour dry
If someone swallows (istafaha) the flour as it is (raw), the oath is not broken. Why? Because this is not the usual way of consuming flour; custom does not consider this 'eating' flour in the normal sense.
The term 'istafaha' means to swallow dry.
5
Summarize the principle
The key factor is whether the consumed item is customarily considered the same as the sworn item. Transformation (wheat to bread) changes identity; non-transformation (flour to bread) does not. Unusual consumption (dry swallowing) is not considered 'eating'.
This illustrates the Hanafi reliance on urf in oaths.

Rights of Way, Water Channels, and Floor Valuation
Rules for rights of way, water channels, and multi-floor property valuation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine how pre-existing rights of way and water channels affect a partition, and apply the Hanafi rule for valuing multi-floor properties.

Core Idea
Rights of way and water channels must be respected; if rerouting is impossible, the partition is voided.

Partition can be complicated when one co-owner has a pre-existing right to use a path or water channel across another's share. The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: if the right can be relocated, the partition stands; if not, the partition is voided. Similarly, when a building has multiple floors that are not identical, each floor must be valued separately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the existence of a right
Determine if one co-owner has a right of way (tariq) or water channel (masil) in the other's property, and whether this right was stipulated in the partition agreement.
2
Assess feasibility of rerouting
If the right was not stipulated but can be relocated without harm, the right is lost; the owner cannot use the path or channel on the other's share.
3
Determine if partition is voided
If rerouting is impossible, the partition is voided because the right effectively attaches to the share, making separation incomplete.
4
Apply floor valuation rule
For multi-floor properties, each floor is valued separately and divided by value, not by floor number.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Qisma (Book of Partition) covers several rulings on dividing jointly owned property. The text begins by stating that mixed property types (e.g., a house and a plot) are valued and divided separately per share. The divider (qasim) should map the property, equalize portions, appraise buildings, and ensure each share has independent access to path and water to prevent entanglement. Shares are labeled sequentially, names placed in lots, and drawn by lot. Currency (dirhams/dinar) is not included unless all parties agree. If one partner has a right of passage or water channel across another's share and that right was not stipulated in the partition, the rule depends on feasibility: if the path/channel can be rerouted, the partner loses the right; if not, the partition is voided. For buildings with multiple floors, each floor is valued separately and assigned by value. When parties disagree, the testimony of the two dividers (qasiman) is accepted. If a partner claims a mistake and that some of his share is in the other's possession, and he previously executed a written acknowledgment of receipt, he is not believed without proof. If he acknowledges receipt but later says he only took part, the other partner's statement with oath prevails. If he claims the property was assigned to him but not delivered, and no acknowledgment exists, and the partner denies, both swear and the partition is voided. Finally, if a third party successfully claims ownership of a specific portion of one partner's share (istihqaq), there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the partition remains valid, and the affected partner recovers compensation from the other partner's share; Abu Yusuf holds that the partition is voided. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) according to later Hanafi authorities is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as it preserves the validity of the division while allowing recovery.

Disputes After Partition: Error, Receipt, and Oath
Rules for resolving disputes after partition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the procedures for resolving disputes after partition, including when testimony of dividers is accepted, how claims of error are handled, and the role of oaths and voiding.

Core Idea
After partition, disputes are resolved by testimony of dividers, oaths, and possibly voiding the partition, depending on the nature of the claim.

Even after a careful partition, disputes may arise. Co-owners might claim errors, that they did not receive their share, or that they only received part. Hanafi law provides clear rules: the testimony of the official dividers is accepted, claims of error require evidence, and conflicting oaths can lead to voiding the partition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of dispute
Determine whether the dispute is about an error in the division, about receipt of share, or about non-delivery.
2
Apply rule for divisor testimony
If the two dividers testify, their testimony is accepted; they are presumed trustworthy.
3
Handle claim of error with prior acknowledgment
If a co-owner claims error and had previously acknowledged receipt (ashhada 'ala nafsihi), he is not believed without evidence.
4
Handle partial receipt claim
If a co-owner says 'I received my right' then later says 'I took only part', the opponent's statement with oath is believed.
5
Handle non-delivery claim without acknowledgment
If a co-owner says 'it came to me but you didn't hand it over' and no acknowledgment exists, both swear and the partition is voided.

Third-Party Claim (Istihqaq) After Partition: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of third-party claim (istihqaq) on partition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) and Abu Yusuf's minority view on the effect of a third-party claim on a completed partition, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).

Core Idea
A third-party claim to a specific share does not void the partition according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad; compensation is given instead.

Sometimes after a partition, a third party proves that part of one co-owner's share actually belongs to them. This raises a critical question: does the partition collapse, or does the affected co-owner get compensation? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute. This lesson dissects the positions of the founding Imams and the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
A specific portion of one co-owner's share is successfully claimed by a third party (istihqaq) after the partition is complete.
2
State the majority Hanafi position
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the partition is not voided. The affected co-owner recovers compensation for that portion from the share of his partner.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that the partition is voided entirely because the claim affects the subject matter and the division was defective from the start.
4
Analyze the proofs
The majority argue that partition is like a sale: once executed, retroactive voiding is avoided; compensation is sufficient. Abu Yusuf emphasizes that the claim reveals a defect in the subject matter, nullifying the division.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
Practical difference: under the majority, the partition stands; under Abu Yusuf, it is undone. The relied-upon position for fatwa is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Finally, if a third party successfully claims ownership of a specific portion of one partner's share (istihqaq), there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the partition remains valid, and the affected partner recovers compensation from the other partner's share; Abu Yusuf holds that the partition is voided. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) according to later Hanafi authorities is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as it preserves the validity of the division while allowing recovery.

استحقاق (istihqaq): A claim by a third party that a portion of the property belongs to them, proven by evidence.
حصة (hissa): Portion or share. In the context, compensation equivalent to the lost portion.
فسخ (faskh): Voiding or rescission of a contract or transaction.
