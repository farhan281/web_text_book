Intention Rules for Different Types of Fasting
Types of fasting and their intention rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between time-specific obligatory fasting and liability-based fasting, and state the correct intention time for each according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Intention must be from night for liability fasts; time-specific fasts allow intention until midday.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri introduces the types of fasting and the critical rulings on when intention (niyyah) must be made. Understanding these distinctions is essential to ensure the validity of one's fast according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories of fasting
The text states that fasting is divided into obligatory (wajib) and voluntary (nafl). Note that the author uses 'wajib' to include fard acts like Ramadan.
2
Differentiate the two subtypes of obligatory fasting
Obligatory fasting is further split into time-specific (e.g., Ramadan, specified vow) and liability-based (e.g., qada, kaffarat, unspecified vow). This determines the intention rule.
The key is whether the fast is tied to a specific day or is a debt upon the person.
3
Apply the intention rule to time-specific fasts
For Ramadan and specified vows, intention can be made from the night, but if omitted until morning, it suffices until zawal (midday) provided no nullifying acts occurred.
Zawal is the time when the sun passes the meridian.
4
Apply the intention rule to liability-based fasts
For qada, unspecified vows, and expiations, intention must be made from the night (before Fajr). It is not valid if made after dawn.
This is a stricter requirement because these fasts are a debt (fi al-dhimmah).
5
Identify the rule for voluntary fasting
Voluntary fasting is permitted with intention before zawal, even if one did not intend from the night.
This flexibility applies only to nafl, not to any obligatory fast.

Commentary
This passage begins the Book of Fasting. It first defines the types of fasting: obligatory (wajib) and voluntary (nafl). Obligatory fasting is further divided into two subtypes. If the sky is clear, the testimony of a large group that yields certain knowledge is required.

النذر المطلق (al-nadhr al-mutlaq): A vow to fast without specifying a time; it becomes a debt upon the person.
النذر المعين (al-nadhr al-muayyan): A vow to fast on a specific day or days.

Crescent Sighting and Testimony Rules
Crescent sighting, individual obligation, and testimony rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for sighting the crescent of Ramadan and the conditions for accepting testimony regarding its sighting.

Core Idea
The crescent must be sought on the 29th of Sha'ban; if obscured, complete 30 days; testimony rules depend on sky conditions.

The second part of this passage details the procedure for determining the start of Ramadan. It covers the night of doubt, individual sighting, and the criteria for accepting testimony from witnesses. This is crucial for establishing the beginning of the fasting month correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the starting point: 29th of Sha'ban
The text says people should search for the crescent on the evening of the 29th of Sha'ban. If seen, they fast the next day.
This is the sunnah practice.
2
Understand the rule when the sky is obscured (ghumma)
If clouds or dust prevent sighting, Sha'ban is completed as 30 days based on the hadith 'complete the count of Sha'ban as thirty'.
3
Individual sighting: the person who sees the crescent must fast
A single person who sees the crescent is obligated to fast even if the imam rejects his testimony. This is based on personal certainty.
4
Conditions for accepting public testimony
The passage states two scenarios: (a) with an obstruction ('illa) in the sky: the imam accepts the testimony of one just person (man, woman, free, or slave). (b) with a clear sky: a large crowd is required to cause certain knowledge.
'Illa means any hindrance like clouds, dust, or haze.
5
Apply the distinction to determine if testimony is accepted
In cloudy weather, one reliable witness suffices; in clear weather, many witnesses are needed to achieve certainty.
This ensures accuracy and avoids false claims.

Classification of Fasting and Intention Rules
Classification of fasting with intention conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify fasting into its types and state the intention requirement for each type according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Intention timing varies: before dawn for liability fasts, before midday for time-specific obligatory and all supererogatory fasts.

Fasting is divided into obligatory and supererogatory, each with distinct rules about when the intention must be made. This passage from al-Quduri's Matn outlines these categories and the crucial timing of niyyah, which is essential for the validity of the fast. Understanding these distinctions will enable you to correctly apply the intention conditions in different fasting scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two primary categories of fasting
The text states fasting is of two types: obligatory (wajib) and supererogatory (nafl). Note that 'wajib' here encompasses fard (e.g., Ramadan) and other obligatory fasts.
The matn uses the term 'wajib' broadly; in later Hanafi terminology, there is a technical difference between fard and wajib, but this text does not make that distinction.
2
Subdivide obligatory fasting
Obligatory fasting is further divided into two subcategories: (1) time-specific (e.g., Ramadan, specified vow) and (2) liability-based (e.g., make-up of Ramadan, absolute vow, expiations).
Time-specific fasts are tied to a particular day, while liability fasts are obligations that are not linked to a specific time.
3
Apply the intention rule to each category
For time-specific obligatory fasts, intention may be made from the night (before dawn) or up to midday. For liability-based obligatory fasts, intention must be made from the night; it is not valid after dawn. For supererogatory fasts, intention before midday is sufficient.
The midday cut-off is when the sun passes its zenith (zawal). If one intends after zawal, the fast is not valid for that day except in the case of a missed supererogatory fast? Actually, for nafl, intention before zawal is permissible; after zawal, it becomes invalid.

One reliable witness suffices in cloud; a multitude is needed in clear sky.

This lesson explains the procedure: when to look, what to do if obscured, and the difference in witness requirements between cloudy and clear skies.

Individual certainty creates personal obligation.

The illa (obstruction) lowers the evidentiary threshold.

The Legal Definition of Fasting: Time, Abstention, and Intention
Defines fasting, its time, and the requirement of intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of fasting, including its time, the three elements of abstention, and the condition of intention, and apply it to determine the validity of a given fast.

Core Idea
Fasting is abstention from eating, drinking, and intercourse from dawn to sunset, with intention.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn lays down the precise legal definition of fasting (sawm). Knowing this definition is essential because it distinguishes a valid Islamic fast from mere hunger or dieting. Every element—time, abstention, and intention—must be present. Let's unpack each component.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the definition
The passage defines 'sawm' (fasting) as a legal (shar'i) term. Note that it is not a linguistic or medical definition but a juristic one.
2
Pinpoint the time boundaries
The fasting day begins at the second dawn (al-fajr al-thani) and ends at sunset. The second dawn is the true dawn—a horizontal light spread across the horizon.
The false dawn (vertical light) does not start the fast.
3
Enumerate the elements of abstention
Three specific acts must be avoided: eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse. These are the core 'mufsidat' (invalidators) when done intentionally.
Other acts like vomiting deliberately also break the fast but are covered elsewhere.
4
Recognize the role of intention
Intention (niyyah) is a necessary condition for validity. Without it, even full abstention is not considered a valid fast in the sight of Shari'ah.
Intention distinguishes worship from habit.
5
Distinguish true dawn from false dawn
The 'second dawn' is the horizontal light that spreads across the horizon; the 'first dawn' is a vertical pillar of light that does not mark the start of the fast. Only the second dawn is the correct time marker.
This distinction is explained in hadith and is agreed upon by all schools.

The mention of 'the second dawn' distinguishes it from the false dawn (al-fajr al-awwaal) which appears vertically and does not mark the start of the fast.

Forgetful Acts During the Fast: No Break, No Penalty
Ruling on forgetful acts during fast and mistaken belief

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on forgetful eating, drinking, or intercourse during a fast and apply it to a scenario involving mistaken belief.

Core Idea

Expiatory Fasting for a Slave Who Pronounces Zihar
Ruling on expiation for a slave who performs zihar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exclusive expiation for a slave who pronounces zihar and explain why wealth-based expiations are invalid.

Core Idea
For a slave, zihar expiation must be fasting; freeing or feeding is invalid due to his lack of ownership.

Zihar is a form of divorce-like pronouncement that requires expiation (kaffarah). While a free person has three options—freeing a slave, fasting two months, or feeding sixty poor—a slave's situation differs because he owns no property. This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling that only fasting is acceptable for a slave, and why the master cannot intervene.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the Arabic phrase 'لم يجز في الكفارة إلا الصوم'—nothing suffices in expiation except fasting. This is the explicit ruling.
The ruling is categorical: only fasting is valid.
2
Understand the reason
A slave does not own property. Freeing a slave is impossible because he himself is property, and feeding requires wealth he does not possess.
The inability to own or dispose of property is the key legal cause (illah).
3
Analyze the master's role
The text states that if the master frees a slave or feeds on his behalf, it does not suffice. The expiation is personal to the slave.
Expiation is an obligation on the slave, not transferable to his master.
4
Apply the ruling
Whenever a slave pronounces zihar, the only permissible expiation is fasting two consecutive months. No alternative is accepted.
This rule is strict; no exception for partial ability or permission.

Source Text

If a slave pronounces zihar, nothing suffices him in expiation except fasting. If his master frees a slave on his behalf, or feeds, it does not suffice him.

If his master attempts to free a slave or feed the poor on his behalf, it is not valid; the expiation must be performed by the slave himself through fasting.
