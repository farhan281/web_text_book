Defining Luqata and Its Recognition Period
Defines luqata and sets announcement periods based on value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of luqata, identify the condition for it being an amanah, and apply the correct announcement period based on the item's value.

Core Idea
Luqata is a trust only if formally declared; otherwise it is a liability.

When you find a lost item, Islamic law does not permit you to simply take it as your own. The classical Hanafi scholars, as summarised by al-Quduri, set clear rules to protect the owner's right while encouraging preservation. This lesson unpacks the definition of luqata and the precise announcement periods required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
Recognise that picking up found property (luqata) is the act under discussion. Understand that its default ruling is not automatic ownership but rather a trust (amanah) if conditions are met.
The default assumption is that the finder is not the owner.
2
Note the condition for amanah status
The finder must formally declare (with witnesses) that he is taking the item to preserve and return it. Without this declaration, the finder is liable (daman).
The Arabic term 'ashhada' is key; it means to take witnesses.
3
Distinguish the two value categories
If the item is worth less than ten dirhams, the announcement period is 'some days' (generally three days in the Hanafi school). If it is ten dirhams or more, the period is one full year.
The threshold is based on the item's market value, not weight.
4
Understand the post-announcement charity and owner's choice
After the announcement period, if no owner appears, the finder may give it in charity on behalf of the owner. If the owner later appears, he has the option to either let the charity stand or demand compensation from the finder.
This protects both the finder (who acted in good faith) and the owner.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as explained in al-Lubab, opens the chapter on luqata (found property). The definition states that luqata becomes an amanah (trust) in the finder's hands only if he formally declares (ashhada) that he is taking it for preservation and return. Without this declaration, it remains a liability (daman) on him. The ruling then distinguishes two cases based on value: if the item is worth less than ten dirhams, the finder must announce it for a few days (generally three days in the Hanafi school). If it is ten dirhams or more, he must announce it for a full year. After the year, if no owner appears, he may give it in charity on behalf of the owner. If the owner later appears, he has the option to either let the charity stand (and the finder is free) or demand compensation from the finder (tadmin).

Key Terms
اللقطة (al-luqata): Found property that is picked up by someone (the finder) with the intention of preserving it for the owner.
أمانة (amanah): A trust; the finder is not liable for loss or damage if he acted properly.
التعريف (al-ta'rīf): Announcing the found item publicly to find the owner.

Rulings on Found Animals: Permissibility and Spending
Ruling on picking up and spending on found large animals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on picking up found animals and explain the consequences of spending with or without judicial permission.

Core Idea
The finder of a large animal must seek the judge's permission to spend; without it, he is a volunteer and cannot reclaim costs.

Found animals (luqatah) are subject to specific rules to protect the owner's property. In this passage, the Hanafi school outlines which animals may be picked up and what happens when the finder incurs expenses. Understanding these rules is essential for anyone who might encounter a lost animal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the permissible animals
The text explicitly states that picking up is permissible for sheep, cows, and camels. Note that these are large animals that can be guarded.
Other small animals (e.g., goats) may have different rulings.
2
Distinguish spending with vs. without judge's permission
Without permission: the finder is a volunteer (mutabarri‘) and cannot demand reimbursement. With permission: expenses become a debt on the owner.
The key phrase is 'bi-ghayr idhn al-ḥākim' (without the judge's permission).
3
List the judge's three options
If the animal can be used (e.g., for milk or work), rent it and use proceeds. If it has no utility and costs may exceed value, sell it and keep the price. If keeping is best, permit spending as a debt.
The judge's decision is based on what is best (al-aghlab).
4
State the finder's right upon owner's return
The finder may prevent the owner from taking the animal until the expenses (authorized by the judge) are paid.
This right is a form of lien (ḥabs).

Claiming Luqata: Proof and Voluntary Return
Covers equality of hal and haram, claimant proof requirement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that luqata from the Haram is treated the same as elsewhere, and apply the rule that a claimant must provide proof (bayyinah) while a mere sign (alamah) permits voluntary return but not compulsion.

Core Idea
A claimant must bring witnesses; a sign alone allows return but cannot be forced.

This lesson clarifies two foundational rulings on luqata: its permissibility across sacred and non-sacred areas, and the procedure for returning it to a claimant. Understanding these distinctions protects the finder from false claims and ensures justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on luqata in the Haram
The text states that luqata found within the sacred precincts (al-haram) is treated identically to that found outside (al-hal). This refutes any misconception that picking up lost property in the Haram is forbidden.
This ruling applies regardless of the value or type of item.
2
Determine the requirement for forced return
When a person claims the item, the finder cannot hand it over until the claimant brings legal proof (al-bayyinah), typically two male witnesses. Without such proof, the finder is not compelled to return it.
Bayyinah is a condition for the return to be wajib upon the finder.
3
Understand the effect of a mere sign (al-alamah)
If the claimant provides a unique distinguishing mark (sign), the finder may return it voluntarily, but a judge cannot force him. This protects the finder from potential false claims based on guessable descriptions.
The sign must be specific and not easily known by others.

Luqata Charity: Restrictions on Recipient and Finder's Use
Covers charity restrictions and finder's personal use.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify that charity from luqata cannot be given to a rich person, that a poor finder may benefit from the item but a rich finder may not, and that a rich finder may give charity to his poor father, son, mother, or wife.

Core Idea
Luqata charity is restricted: not to the rich; finder's use depends on his own wealth; only specific poor relatives may receive it from a rich finder.

After the waiting period, if the owner does not appear, the finder may give the luqata in charity, but with important conditions regarding the recipient's wealth and the finder's own financial status. This lesson details those restrictions and the special allowance for giving to certain poor relatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibition on charity to a rich person
The text explicitly forbids giving luqata charity to a rich person. The recipient must be poor, otherwise the charity is not valid in the sense of fulfilling the finder's duty.
This applies after the mandatory waiting period.
2
Determine the rule for the finder's personal benefit
If the finder is rich, he cannot benefit from the item (e.g., use it for himself). If he is poor, he may use it without issue. This distinguishes the finder's right based on his own financial status.
The waiting period must have passed before personal benefit is allowed.
3
Learn the exception for charity to specific poor relatives
Even if the finder is rich, he may give the luqata as charity to his poor father, son, mother, and wife. This is a specific exemption from the general rule that the rich finder cannot benefit.
These are the only relatives mentioned; other relatives or strangers are not included.

Rulings on Found Property (Luqatah)
Series of rulings on found property (luqatah)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on found property (luqatah) for a rich and poor finder, and identify conditions for returning the property to a claimant.

Core Idea
Found property is a trust: the rich cannot use it, the poor may, and charity to relatives is allowed.

The Book of Found Property (Luqatah) from al-Quduri's matn presents a series of concise rulings that govern what a finder may and may not do with a lost item. These rulings distinguish between rich and poor finders and address scenarios such as returning the property to a claimant and giving charity from it. This lesson unpacks each ruling and explains the underlying principle that the property remains a trust for its owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling on expenses
Read the first sentence: 'And when its owner appears: the finder may prevent him from taking it until he takes the expenses.' This establishes the finder's right to recover costs (e.g., advertisement, care) before returning the item.
Expenses are reimbursable; withholding is a form of security.
2
Note the rule about location (Haram vs. elsewhere)
The text states: 'And the found property of the halal and the haram are the same.' This means that luqatah found within the Haram of Makkah is treated identically to elsewhere; no special rules apply.
Some scholars differentiate, but Hanafis treat all locations equally.
3
Understand the procedure for a claimant
The passage says: 'If a man appears and claims the found property is his: it is not handed over until he provides its description.' The finder may hand it over voluntarily if the description matches, but the qadi does not compel him. This emphasizes that ownership is not transferred merely by a claim.
The finder remains cautious; the qadi only enforces if there is clear proof.
4
Identify the rulings based on the finder's wealth
The text distinguishes: a rich finder cannot benefit from the luqatah; a poor finder may. Also, charity from the luqatah to a rich person is prohibited, but a rich finder may give it in charity to his poor relatives (father, son, mother, wife).
Wealth affects permissibility: the poor finder may use the item out of necessity, but it remains a trust.
5
Connect the charity exception
The ruling that a rich finder may give the luqatah in charity to his poor relatives shows that the finder is not allowed to personally benefit but can redirect the benefit to eligible recipients. This is a special concession.
Charity to relatives is valid even though the finder is rich; the item is given as if from the owner, but with intent to benefit the poor relatives.
