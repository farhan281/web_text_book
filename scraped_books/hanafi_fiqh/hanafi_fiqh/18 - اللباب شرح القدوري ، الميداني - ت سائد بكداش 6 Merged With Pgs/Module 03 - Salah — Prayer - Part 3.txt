Manner of the Funeral Prayer: Four Takbirs and Recitations
Ruling statement on funeral prayer integrals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four takbirs of the funeral prayer, describe the recitation after each, and identify the ruling on performing it in a mosque congregation.

Core Idea
The funeral prayer has four takbirs, each followed by a specific remembrance, and it is disliked in congregation inside a mosque.

The funeral prayer (salat al-janazah) is a collective obligation with a unique structure: four takbirs with no bowing or prostration. This passage from al-Quduri lays out the essential sequence: praise after the first, salawat after the second, du'a after the third, and taslim after the fourth. It also specifies the imam's position opposite the chest and warns against performing it in congregation inside a mosque, a nuanced fiqh point that students often overlook.

Guided Path
1
State the overall ruling
The funeral prayer is fard kifayah (collective obligation) according to the Hanafi school. If some Muslims perform it, the duty is fulfilled for all.
2
Identify the imam's position
The imam stands opposite the chest of the deceased (bi-hidha' sadr al-mayyit). This differs from other prayers where the imam leads from the front.
3
List the four takbirs with their actions
First takbir: praise Allah (e.g., al-Fatihah or any hamd). Second takbir: send salawat upon the Prophet. Third takbir: make du'a for self, deceased, and all Muslims. Fourth takbir: say taslim (al-salamu 'alaykum).
4
Apply the prohibition of mosque congregation
The passage concludes: 'No funeral prayer is performed over a deceased in a mosque in congregation.' In the Hanafi school, it is makruh tahrimi to pray janazah in congregation inside a masjid; the prayer should be in a musalla or open space.
5
Memorize the sequence
This passage serves as a memorization anchor for a foundational ibadah. Repeat the order until it becomes automatic.

Commentary
This passage explains the integral acts of the funeral prayer. The imam stands level with the chest of the deceased (not at the head or feet). The prayer consists of four takbirs. After the first, one praises Allah (usually by reciting al-Fatihah or a short praise). After the second, one sends salawat upon the Prophet. After the third, one makes du'a for oneself, the deceased, and all Muslims. After the fourth, one says the taslim. Additionally, it is makruh to perform the funeral prayer in congregation inside a mosque; it should be performed in the open or in a designated musalla.

Grave Shape and Infant Funeral Rulings
Two independent rulings: grave shape and infant funeral.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the sunnah grave shape is humped (musannam) and explain that an infant receives funeral prayer only if it cried (istahalla) after birth, applying the Hanafi criterion for life.

Core Idea
Grave is humped; infant funeral requires a cry after birth.

This lesson presents two concise rulings from classical Hanafi fiqh. The first describes the recommended shape of a grave, a sunnah often neglected. The second clarifies the sole criterion for life in a newborn that determines full funeral rites. Both rulings are practical and directly applicable.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling on grave shape
Identify the sunnah shape: the grave should be made humped (musannam). Note that making it flat (musattah) is contrary to sunnah.
This follows the practice of the Prophet ﷺ.
2
State the ruling on infant funeral
Determine if the infant cried (istahalla) after birth. If yes: name, wash, shroud, and pray. If no: wrap in cloth, no prayer.
Crying is the indicator of life per Hanafi school.
3
Understand the condition for life
The Hanafi school requires istihlal (crying) as definitive proof of life. Movement or other signs are insufficient.
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) accept any sign of life.

Definition and General Ruling of the Martyr (Shahid)
Definition and general ruling of shahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a shahid and apply the general ruling regarding washing, shrouding, and prayer.

Core Idea
A martyr is not washed; his blood is a testimony that remains.

This lesson introduces the chapter on the martyr (shahid). You'll learn who qualifies as a martyr in Hanafi fiqh and the general rule: the martyr is shrouded and prayed upon but not washed. The blood of the martyr is not removed; his clothes are only partially removed. Understanding this foundation is essential before exploring exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of shahid
Find the three categories of shahid in the text: killed by polytheists, found in battlefield with wounds, or killed unjustly by Muslims without blood-money.
Note the third category includes unjust killing by Muslims.
2
Extract the general ruling
The text says: yukaffan (shrouded), yusalla alayh (prayed upon), and la yughsal (not washed). That is the default ruling.
Washing is omitted due to the honor of martyrdom.
3
Identify what is removed and what remains
Blood is not washed off. Normal clothes remain, but fur, khuff, padding, and weapons are removed.
These items are considered extra or non-essential.
4
Recognize the exceptions
The text then lists exceptions: junub, sabi (disputed), murtathth, killed in hadd/qisas, rebel, highway robber, suicide.
This lesson focuses on the general ruling; exceptions will be covered in subsequent lessons.

الشهيدُ: مَن قَتَلَه المشركون، أو وُجد في المعركة وبـه أثـر الجراحة، أو قتله المسلمون ظلماً، ولم تجب بقتله دية.

ومَن قُتِل من البغاة، أو قطاع الطريق: لم يُغسل، ودفن، ولم يُصل عليه.
ومَن قَتَلَ نفسه : غُسل، وصُلِّي عليه.

So he is shrouded, prayed upon, and not washed. And if the junub (one in major impurity) is martyred, he is washed according to Abu Hanifa, and likewise the boy. And they (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: they are not washed. And his blood is not washed off the martyr, and his clothes are not removed from him, and there is removed from him the fur, the khuff, the padding, and the weapon. And whoever is killed in a prescribed punishment or retaliation: he is washed, and prayer is performed over him. And whoever is killed from the rebels (bughat) or highway robbers: he is not washed, and is buried, and prayer is not performed over him. And whoever kills himself: he is washed, and prayer is performed over him.

Commentary
This passage is the chapter on the martyr. It begins by defining the martyr (shahid) as one killed by the polytheists in battle, or found in the battlefield with traces of wounds, or killed unjustly by Muslims when no blood-money is due. The general ruling is that the martyr is shrouded (tukaffan) and prayed upon (yusalla alayh), but not washed (la yughsal). This is because the martyr's blood is considered a testimony and washing would remove it. However, there are exceptions. If the martyr is in a state of major impurity (junub), Abu Hanifa holds that he is still washed because the impurity of janabah is removed by washing, and the martyrdom does not remove it. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say they are not washed. Similarly for an adolescent boy (sabi) who is martyred: Abu Hanifa says washed, the two say not. The relied-upon position is the view of the two companions (they are not washed). Then it details what is removed from the martyr: his blood is not washed off, his clothes are not removed except for fur, khuff (leather socks), padding (hashw), and weapons. Then it discusses the one who is 'murtathth' (wounded but survives): if he eats, drinks, receives treatment, remains alive through a prayer period while conscious, or is moved from the battlefield alive, then he is washed and prayed upon. Also, one killed in a fixed punishment (hadd) or retaliation (qisas) is washed and prayed upon. Rebels (bughat) and highway robbers (qutta' al-tariq) who are killed: not washed, buried without prayer. And one who commits suicide: washed and prayed upon.

Key Terms
الشهيد (al-shahid): One who is killed by polytheists in battle, found with wounds in the battlefield, or killed unjustly by Muslims without blood-wit.

The Junub and Minor Martyr: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on washing junub/sabi martyr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the washing of a junub martyr and identify the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.

Core Idea
The Sahibayn's view that a junub martyr is not washed is the operative Hanafi position.

The general rule is that a martyr is not washed, but this lesson addresses an exception: the martyr who is in a state of major impurity (janabah) or is a minor. Abu Hanifa differed with his two disciples on whether such martyrs should be washed. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi methodology and the final tarjih within the school.

Guided Path
1
Recall the general ruling for shahid
Default: not washed.
2
Identify the case of junub and sabi
The text presents two views: Abu Hanifa says wash them; the two companions (Sahibayn) say do not wash.
The text says 'غسل عند أبي حنيفة' and 'وقالا: لا يغسلان'.
3
Understand the basis of disagreement
Abu Hanifa reasoned that janabah requires washing and martyrdom does not remove it. The Sahibayn considered the martyr's blood as a testimony that should not be removed even partially.
The text does not elaborate but this is the standard justification.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Hidayah, al-Lubab) adopt the view of the Sahibayn (not washed).
Thus, in fatwa, a junub or minor martyr is not washed.

Categories Beyond the Martyr: Murtathth, Hadd, Qisas, Bughat, and Suicide
Rulings for murtathth, hadd/qisas, bughat, highway robber, suicide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the correct funeral rulings (washing, shrouding, prayer) for a deceased person based on the cause of death: murtathth, killed in hadd/qisas, bughat, highway robber, and suicide.

Core Idea
The ruling for a martyr (not washed) only applies to those who die immediately in battle; other categories are washed and prayed upon, except rebels and highway robbers.

The previous lessons covered the general definition of the martyr and the dispute over junub. This lesson completes the picture by detailing the rulings for other categories of violent death: the wounded who survive (murtathth), those executed for a crime, rebels, highway robbers, and suicide. Each has a distinct ruling based on the nature of the death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the murtathth (surviving wounded)
The text defines irtithath: eating, drinking, receiving treatment, staying alive through a prayer time while conscious, or being moved from the battlefield alive. Such a person is treated as a regular deceased: washed and prayed upon.
The key is that he survived long enough to do these actions, indicating he was not killed instantly.
2
Ruling for those killed in hadd or qisas
They are washed and prayed upon. Their death is a penalty, not martyrdom.
Hadd = fixed punishment (e.g., for theft, zina). Qisas = retaliation for murder.
3
Ruling for rebels (bughat) and highway robbers (quṭṭāʿ al-ṭarīq)
They are not washed and not prayed upon, and are buried. This is a dishonorable burial.
They are considered sinners who died in rebellion or robbery.
4
Ruling for suicide
The one who kills himself is washed and prayed upon. Despite the sin, the obligation of washing and prayer remains.
Unlike rebels, the body of a suicide is treated with normal Islamic rites.

Core Idea

This lesson presents the Hanafi position on prayer performed inside the Kaaba, in congregation around it, and on its roof. Guided Path

This is the default Hanafi position.

Determine the conditions for valid congregation inside the Kaaba
When the imam prays inside the Kaaba with followers, the followers must orient themselves such that their backs are towards the back of the imam (i.e., they face the same direction). If a follower faces the imam (backs to faces), the prayer is invalid.
The key is the relative orientation, not absolute direction.

The dislike is due to showing less reverence by being above the Kaaba.
5

The Hanafi school considers a strong group as effectively representing a jihad expedition, while individuals do not. Thus, their spoils are subject to the standard khums rule.
This distinguishes Hanafi fiqh from other schools that require permission.

Read: 'ولا يجوز للرجال التحلي بالذهب والفضة، إلا الخاتم، والمنطَقةَ، وحلية السيف من الفضة.' Men cannot adorn themselves with gold or silver at all except for a silver ring, a silver belt, and a silver sword hilt.
The exceptions are specific: they are functional items, not purely ornamental.
2
Note the full permission for women
Read: 'ويجوز للنساء التحلي بالذهب والفضة.' Women are allowed to wear gold and silver without restriction.
This is a general concession; no exceptions are mentioned.
3
Understand the ruling for children
Read: 'ويكره أن يُلبس الصبي الذهب، والحرير.' It is disliked to dress a male child in gold or silk. This applies to boys who have not reached puberty.
The ruling is makruh tanzihi, not haram.

Rulings on Vessels and Quran Markings
Rulings on vessels and Quran decorations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between prohibited and permissible vessels, and state the ruling on writing numbers and dots in the Quran.

Core Idea
Gold and silver vessels are prohibited for all; silver-plated items are permissible according to Abu Hanifa; writing tenths in the Quran is disliked.

This lesson covers the materials that are allowed or prohibited for eating, drinking, and other uses, as well as the ruling on adding markings to the Quran. The Hanafi school distinguishes between pure gold/silver vessels (haram) and silver-plated ones (allowed by Abu Hanifa). It also considers writing page numbers or verse markers as makruh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general prohibition on gold and silver vessels
Read: 'ولا يجوز الأكل والشرب، والادهان، والتطيب في آنية الذهب والفضة، للرجال والنساء.' Using gold or silver vessels for any purpose is prohibited for everyone.
This includes eating, drinking, applying oil, and perfuming.
2
Note the permissibility of other materials
Read: 'ولا بأس باستعمال آنية الزجاج، والبَلُّور، والعقيق.' Vessels made of glass, crystal, and agate are permissible.
These materials are not problematic because they are not gold or silver.
3
Examine Abu Hanifa's ruling on silver-plated items
Read: 'ويجوز الشرب في الإناء المفضَّض عند أبي حنيفة، والركوب على السِّرْج المفضض، والجلوس على السرير المفضض.' Abu Hanifa allows drinking from a silver-plated vessel, riding on a silver-plated saddle, and sitting on a silver-plated bed.
The text only gives Abu Hanifa's view; the companions' view is not mentioned here.
4
Understand the ruling on Quran markings
Read: 'ويكره التعشير في المصحف، والنَّقْطُ.' Writing the number ten (or marking tenths) and dotting the Quran are disliked.
This is to preserve the sanctity of the Quran and avoid distraction.
