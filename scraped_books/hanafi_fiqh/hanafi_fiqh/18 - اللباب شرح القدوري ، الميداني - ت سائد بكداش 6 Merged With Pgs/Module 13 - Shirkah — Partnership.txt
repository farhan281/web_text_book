Classification of Partnership: Ownership and Contract
Definition and classification of partnership into two main types and four subtypes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define and distinguish between partnership of ownership and partnership of contract, and list the four subtypes of contractual partnership.

Core Idea
Partnership in Hanafi law is either by ownership or by contract; contractual partnership has four recognized forms.

Partnership (shirkah) is a fundamental concept in Hanafi commercial law. This lesson introduces the foundational classification found in the Quduri text: partnership is divided into two broad categories. Understanding this distinction is essential before studying the detailed rulings of each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main types
Read the Arabic text carefully. The author says 'al-shirkah 'ala darbayn: shirkat amlak, wa shirkat 'uqud'. Identify these two categories.
Shirkat al-amlak (ownership partnership) and shirkat al-'uqud (contractual partnership).
2
Analyze ownership partnership
Understand that shirkat al-amlak arises from inheritance or joint purchase. Each co-owner is like a stranger to the other's share; no disposal without permission.
This is not a true partnership in the contractual sense; it is co-ownership.
3
List the four contractual partnerships
The author then lists four types of shirkat al-'uqud: mufawada, 'inan, shirkat al-sana'i' (craftsmen), and shirkat al-wujuh (credit). Memorize them.
Each type has distinct rules regarding capital, work, and liability.
4
Apply the classification
Given a real-life scenario, decide whether it falls under ownership or contractual partnership, and if contractual, which specific subtype.
This skill is crucial for further study of partnership law.

Commentary
This is the opening of the Book of Partnership in the Quduri text. It defines two fundamental categories: (1) partnership in ownership (shirkat al-amlak), which arises from joint inheritance or purchase, where each co-owner is a stranger to the other's share unless authorized; (2) partnership by contract (shirkat al-'uqud), which is a mutual agreement to share profits and losses. The latter is further divided into four types: mufawada (full investment and equal partnership), 'inan (limited partnership), partnership of craftsmen (for skilled workers), and partnership of credit (based on creditworthiness). This classification is foundational for understanding Hanafi partnership law.

Conditions and Basic Rulings of Mufawada Partnership
Ruling statement defining conditions of mufawada partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three requirements of equality in mufawada partnership and identify the conditions for its validity (free, Muslim, adult, sane).

Core Idea
Mufawada partnership requires absolute equality between partners in capital, disposal, and debt.

Mufawada is the most comprehensive form of partnership in Hanafi fiqh. It is founded on mutual agency and suretyship. This lesson lays out the conditions for its validity and the implications of equality. Understanding these conditions is essential for distinguishing mufawada from other partnership forms.

Guided Path
1
Define mufawada
State the definition: two partners are equal in capital (mal), disposal (tasarruf), and debt (dayn).
Equality is the core condition.
2
Identify the conditions of the partners
The partners must be free, Muslim, adult, and sane. The text explicitly lists these.
These are conditions for the validity of the contract.
3
Recognize exclusions
Mufawada is not valid between a free person and a slave, between a minor and an adult, or between a Muslim and a non-Muslim.
These are invalid combinations.
4
Understand the legal basis
The contract is founded on mutual agency (wakalah) and mutual suretyship (kafalah). Each partner acts as agent for the other and guarantees the other's obligations.
This explains the equality in disposal and debt.
5
Apply the partnership to purchases
All purchases made by either partner belong to the partnership except for the food and clothing of each partner's family, which are personal expenses.
This is an exception to the general rule.

Types of Shirkah and Conditions of Mufawada Partnership
Defines partnership types and details Mufawada conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Shirkat al-Amwal and Shirkat al-Uqud, list the four types of contractual partnership, and state the conditions for a valid Mufawada partnership according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Mufawada requires absolute equality in capital, action, and debt liability, only among free, Muslim, adult, sane men.

Partnership in Islamic law is not merely a financial arrangement; it is a contract with specific legal rules. This passage from al-Quduri's matn introduces the two fundamental categories of shirkah and then zooms in on the most comprehensive form: Mufawada. Understanding these distinctions is essential to correctly classify and form business partnerships according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Categorize partnership types
Identify the two main categories: Shirkat al-Amwal (joint ownership) and Shirkat al-Uqud (contractual partnership). Note that the first is not a true partnership contract.
Shirkat al-Amwal is co-ownership, not a contract.
2
List the four contractual partnerships
Recall the four sub-types of Shirkat al-Uqud: Mufawada, Inan, partnership in crafts, and partnership of faces.
These are all based on mutual agency.
3
Identify Mufawada's core conditions
State that Mufawada requires equality in three things: capital (ra's al-mal), right to act (tasarruf), and debt liability (dayn). Also note the personal conditions: free, Muslim, adult, sane.
Equality is essential; without it, it becomes Inan.
4
Understand the legal basis and effects
Recognize that Mufawada is built on agency (wakalah) and suretyship (kafalah). All purchases belong to the partnership except household necessities; both are jointly liable for partnership debts.
Each partner is agent and guarantor for the other.
5
Note capital requirements and invalidation triggers
Note that capital must be dirhams, dinars, or current fulus (or customary equivalents). If one partner inherits or receives a gift of partnership-eligible property, Mufawada dissolves into Inan.
Goods require a prior sale to convert into currency.

Commentary
This passage from the Kitab al-Shirkah (Book of Partnership) in al-Quduri's matn begins by defining two fundamental categories of shirkah in Hanafi fiqh. The first is Shirkat al-Amwal (partnership in property), which is joint ownership of a tangible asset that arises from inheritance or joint purchase. In this type, each partner is considered a stranger regarding the other's share, so neither can dispose of the other's portion without explicit permission. This is essentially co-ownership, not a contractual partnership.

The second category, Shirkat al-Uqud (partnership by contract), is the focus of the chapter. It has four sub-types: (1) Mufawada (full partnership), (2) Inan (limited partnership), (3) Shirkat al-Sana'i' (partnership in crafts/trades), and (4) Shirkat al-Wujuh (partnership of faces/credit). The passage then elaborates on Mufawada.

Mufawada requires complete equality between the partners in three things: capital, right to act/dispose, and liability for debts. It is only valid between free, Muslim, adult, and sane individuals. It cannot be between a free man and a slave, a child and an adult, or a Muslim and a disbeliever. The contract is based on agency (wakalah) and suretyship (kafalah), meaning each partner is both agent and guarantor for the other.

All purchases made by either partner become partnership property, except for necessary household expenses like food and clothing for their families. Any debts incurred in a valid partnership transaction are jointly guaranteed. If one partner inherits or receives a gift of such property, the Mufawada is dissolved and automatically becomes an Inan partnership (where equality is no longer required).

Importantly, Mufawada partnership can only be contracted in dirhams, dinars, or current fulus (copper coins). It is invalid with other commodities unless people customarily deal in them, such as uncoined gold (tibr) and silver (nuqrah). If partners wish to contribute goods (urud), they must first sell half of their goods to the other for half of the other's goods, effectively converting the goods into cash, and then contract the partnership. This preserves the requirement that capital be in currency.

شركة المفاوضة (shirkat al-mufawada): Full partnership where partners are equal in capital, actions, and debt liability, based on mutual agency and suretyship.
الوكالة والكفالة (al-wakalah wa al-kafalah): Agency and suretyship: the foundations of Mufawada; each partner is agent and guarantor for the other.

Rules of Inan Partnership
States the rules and conditions of Inan partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key rules of Inan partnership and apply them to determine validity and liability.

Core Idea
Inan partnership: agency only, no guarantee; capital and profit can be unequal.

This lesson covers the basic rules of Shirkat al-'Inan, a limited partnership that gives great flexibility. Unlike Mufawada, Inan does not require equality in capital or profit, and each partner is simply an agent—not a guarantor—for the other. Understanding these rules is essential for structuring commercial partnerships in accordance with Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of Inan
Note that Inan is founded on agency (wakalah) alone, not on suretyship (kafalah). This means partners are agents for each other but not liable for each other's debts.
This distinguishes Inan from Mufawada, which includes both agency and suretyship.
2
Recognise permissible variations in capital and profit
Inequality in capital is valid. The partners may also be equal in capital but differ in profit sharing. They may contract using only part of their wealth.
Profit ratios are agreed upon; they do not have to mirror capital ratios.
3
Understand validity conditions inherited from Mufawada
Inan is valid only under the same conditions as Mufawada regarding the type of capital: it must be dirhams, dinars, or current equivalents (fulus). Mixed currencies are allowed in Inan.
One partner may contribute dirhams, the other dinars.
4
Analyse liability for purchases
Each partner is individually liable for his own purchases made for the partnership. The seller only demands payment from the purchasing partner, who then recovers the partner's share.
No joint liability; each partner is an agent only.
5
Apply the rule on destruction of capital
If the entire partnership capital or one partner's share is destroyed before any purchase, the partnership becomes void. The contract terminates because the subject matter is lost.
This applies only before any trading activity.

Commentary
This passage continues the chapter on partnership by discussing the Inan (limited) partnership. Unlike Mufawada, Inan is based solely on agency (wakalah) without suretyship (kafalah). This means each partner is an agent for the other in business transactions but is not a guarantor for the other's debts. The key feature is flexibility: partners may contribute unequal amounts of capital, and they may agree to share profits unevenly even if capital is equal. Each partner may also contribute only part of his wealth to the partnership, retaining the rest privately.

The validity conditions for Inan are the same as for Mufawada regarding the type of capital: it must be dirhams, dinars, or current fulus (or items customarily used as currency). However, Inan allows mixed currencies: one partner can contribute dirhams and the other dinars, which is not permissible in Mufawada due to the equality requirement.

In terms of liability, if a partner purchases goods for the partnership, the seller demands payment from that partner alone; the other partner is not directly liable. The purchasing partner then recovers the partner's share from him. This contrasts with Mufawada where both are jointly liable.

Finally, if the partnership capital (or one partner's capital) is destroyed before any purchase is made, the partnership is automatically void. This is because the capital is the subject matter, and its destruction ends the contract.

The passage sets out the basic rules of Inan, which is the most common form of commercial partnership in Hanafi fiqh due to its flexibility.

التفاضل في المال (al-tafadul fi al-mal): Inequality in capital contribution; permissible in Inan.
الوكالة (al-wakalah): Agency; one partner acts as agent for the other in trade.

Invalidation of Mufawada by Inheritance or Gift
Ruling on invalidation of Mufawada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that inheritance or gift of partnership-eligible property invalidates Mufawada and explain why it becomes an 'Inan partnership.

Core Idea
Mufawada requires continuous equality; separate property acquisition breaks it.

This passage introduces a key rule unique to the Hanafi Mufawada partnership: if one partner receives partnership-eligible wealth through inheritance or gift and takes possession, the Mufawada is automatically voided. It transforms into an 'Inan partnership, which does not require full equality. Understanding this helps distinguish the two types of partnership in Hanafi law.

Understand the condition of Mufawada
Mufawada requires equality in capital and liability at all times. A partner acquiring separate property breaches that equality.
This condition is a shart of Mufawada.
3
State the consequence
The Mufawada does not become void entirely but reverts to an 'Inan partnership, which only requires agency (wakalah) and not guarantee or equality.
The text says 'بطلت المفاوضة، وصارت الشركة عناناً'.

Given a scenario where a Mufawada partner inherits gold coins, state that the partnership transforms to 'Inan and the inherited property belongs solely to the heir.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on guarantee in zakat payment by partners

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi dispute on guarantee when partners pay each other's zakat and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Mutual permission to pay zakat does not automatically exempt from liability; the second payer may be a guarantor.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, commented upon by al-Maydani in al-Lubab, presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement (ikhtilaf) regarding zakat payment in a partnership. The core issue is whether ignorance of the first payment excuses the second payer from liability. Understanding this dispute sharpens your appreciation of how the Hanafi masters balance textual principles and practical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The rule forbids one partner from paying the other's zakat without permission. The case assumes permission is given mutually, and then both partners actually pay.
2
State the Common Ground
Both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree that a second payment made with knowledge of the first is wasteful and the second payer is a guarantor. The dispute is only when the second payer is unaware.
3
Explain Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa holds the second payer is a guarantor (damin) unconditionally, even if he did not know of the first payment. The permission given is general and does not include the right to make a redundant payment.
4
Explain the Sahibayn's Position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say: if the second payer was unaware of the first payment, he is not a guarantor, because he acted in good faith based on the general permission.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
If the second payer is a guarantor, he must compensate the first payer (or the treasury) the amount of zakat wasted. If not, no compensation is due.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) in later Hanafi works is typically that of Abu Hanifa, as it is more cautious regarding the rights of Allah.

وليس لواحد من الشريكين أن يؤدِّيَ زكاة مال الآخر إلا بإذنه. فإن أذِنَ كلٌّ واحدٍ منهما لصاحبه أن يؤدي زكاته، فأدَّى كـلُّ واحد منهما : فالثاني ضامِنٌ، سواء عَلم بأداء الأول، أو لم يعلم عنـد أبي حنيفة. وقالا : لا يضمن إذا لم يَعلَم.
And it is not permissible for either of the two partners to pay the zakat of the other’s wealth except with his permission. If each of them permits the other to pay his zakat, and each one pays, then the second is a guarantor, whether he knew of the first’s payment or not, according to Abu Hanifa. And the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: he is not a guarantor if he did not know.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf). The issue is when both partners authorize each other to pay zakat on their behalf, and both actually pay. According to Abu Hanifa, the second payer (the one who pays after the first) is a guarantor (damin) for the amount, meaning he must compensate the first payer or the treasury, regardless of whether he knew the first had already paid. This is because the permission is considered unconditional, and the second payment is without right. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) hold that if the second payer was unaware of the first payment, he is not a guarantor, because he acted in good faith based on the general permission. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa in most later works, but the text does not specify tarjih here.

Key Terms
زكاة (zakah): Obligatory alms on wealth.
ضامن (damin): Guarantor; one who must make good a loss due to wrongful payment.
أبو حنيفة (Abu Hanifah): Imam of the Hanafi school, whose opinion is stated first.
قالا (qala): They said; refers to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Settlement by an Agent, Unauthorized Person, and a Partner of a Joint Debt
Rules on settlement by agent, unauthorized person, and partner

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for settlement by an agent, settlement by an unauthorized person, and settlement by one partner of a joint debt, and identify the key conditions that determine validity.

Core Idea
An agent is not liable unless he guarantees; an unauthorized settlement is suspended until ratification; a partner's settlement gives the other partner an option.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents three sets of rules concerning settlement (sulh) in the context of agency, unauthorized action, and joint debt. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying the Hanafi law of transactions, especially when dealing with claims and debt recovery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the nature of the settlement
Determine who is the principal, agent, debtor, creditor, and whether the settlement is with or without permission.
Settlement (sulh) is a contract resolving a dispute by mutual agreement.
2
Distinguish authorized vs unauthorized agency
Note the two main sections: settlement by an agent (with permission) and settlement by a person without authorization.
The rules differ regarding liability of the agent/person.
3
Parse the four cases of unauthorized settlement
Break down the four scenarios based on payment and guarantee: (1) pays and guarantees, (2) says 'I settle for these two thousand' and hands over, (3) says 'I settle for one thousand' and hands over, (4) says 'I settle' but does not hand over.
Scenarios 1-3 result in immediate validity; scenario 4 results in a suspended contract (mawquf).
4
Apply the rule for partner settlement
Understand that when one partner settles his share of a joint debt for a garment, the other partner has an option: pursue the debtor for his half or take half the garment.
The option protects the non-consenting partner from being forced to accept the settlement property.
5
Note the legal effect of ratification
Recognize that in scenario 4, the settlement becomes valid only if the debtor (principal) ratifies it; otherwise it becomes void.
This is the concept of mawquf (suspended contract) common in Hanafi fiqh.

Source Text

ومَن وكَّل رجلاً بالصلح عنه، فصالحه : لم يلزم الوكيل ما صالحه عليه، إلا أن يضمنه، والمالُ لازم للموكل. فإن صالح عنه على شيء بغير أمره : فهو على أربعة أوجه: إن صالح بمال، وضَمِنَه : تم الصلح. وكذلك إن قال : صالحتك على ألفـي هـذه : تم الصلح، ولزمـه تسليمها . وكذلك لو قال : صالحتك على ألف، وسلَّمها إليه. وإن قال : صالحتُك على الف، ولم يسلمها إليه: فالعقد موقوف، فإن أجازه المدعى عليه : جاز، ولزمته الألف، وإن لم يُجزهُ: بَطَلَ . وإذا كان الدين بين شريكين، فصالح أحدهما مـن نـصـيبـه علـى ثوب: فشريكه بالخيار : إن شاء اتَّبَعَ الذي عليه الدين بنصفه، وإن شاء أَخَذَ نصف الثوب، إلا أن يضمن له شريكه ربُّعَ الدَّيْنِ. ولو استوفى نصف نصيبه من الدين كان الشريكه أن يشركه فيما
And whoever appoints a man as agent to settle on his behalf, and he settles: the agent is not obliged for what he settled upon, unless he guarantees it, and the wealth is binding on the principal. And if he settles on his behalf for something without his permission: it has four aspects: If he settles with wealth and guarantees it: the settlement is complete. Similarly, if he says: 'I settle with you for these two thousand': the settlement is complete, and its delivery becomes obligatory upon him. Similarly, if he says: 'I settle with you for one thousand' and hands it over to him. And if he says: 'I settled with you for one thousand' but did not hand it over to him: the contract is suspended; if the defendant ratifies it, it is valid and the thousand becomes binding on him; if he does not ratify it, it becomes void. And if a debt is between two partners, and one of them settles from his share for a garment: then his partner has an option: if he wishes, he can pursue the debtor for his half, and if he wishes, he can take half of the garment, unless his partner guarantees for him a quarter of the debt. And if he collected half of his share of the debt, his partner can share with him in what [he collected].
Source pages p. 188

Commentary
This passage covers two topics: settlement by an agent and settlement by an unauthorized person, and also settlement by one partner of a joint debt. First, if a person appoints an agent to settle a claim, the agent is not personally liable for the settlement payment; the payment is binding on the principal (the one who appointed him). The agent is only liable if he personally guarantees the payment. Second, if someone makes a settlement on behalf of another without authorization, there are four scenarios: (1) If he pays the money himself and guarantees it, the settlement is complete. (2) If he says 'I settle with you for these two thousand' and actually hands over the money, it is also complete. (3) If he says 'I settle for one thousand' and hands it over, similar. (4) If he says 'I settled for one thousand' but does not hand it over, the contract is suspended; if the debtor (the one on whose behalf the settlement is made) ratifies it, it becomes valid and he must pay; if he rejects it, it becomes void. Third, if two partners are owed a joint debt, and one partner settles his share for a garment, the other partner has an option: either pursue the debtor for his half of the original debt, or take half of the garment (unless the settling partner guarantees a quarter of the debt to the other partner). Finally, if one partner collected half of his share, the other partner can share in that collection.

Key Terms
موقوف (mawquf): Suspended contract that becomes effective upon ratification by the principal.
ضَمِنَه (daminahu): He guaranteed it, i.e., took personal responsibility for payment.
شريك (sharik): Partner in a debt, each owning a share of the joint claim.

The Validity and Conditions of Sharecropping (Muzāra'ah)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sharecropping with four forms and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on sharecropping and identify the four forms and conditions required for validity according to the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Sharecropping is invalid according to Abu Hanifa, but the Sahibayn allow it with specific conditions.

This passage from Kitāb al-Muzāra‘ah presents a major intra-Hanafi disagreement over sharecropping contracts. Imam Abu Hanifa rejects them entirely due to uncertainty (gharar), while his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, permit them based on customary practice and necessity. Understanding the four valid forms and essential conditions is critical for correctly applying the Hanafi ruling in agricultural partnerships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Note that Abu Hanifa declares sharecropping batil (invalid), while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider it ja’iz (permissible).
The Sahibayn's view is mufta bihi in later Hanafi works.
2
List the four forms according to the Sahibayn
Memorize the four combinations of factors (land, seed, oxen, labor). The first three are valid; the fourth is invalid.
Pay attention to who provides seed – it is the critical factor.
3
Identify the conditions of validity
Two conditions: (1) a fixed term, (2) the produce must be shared as an undivided fraction (mushā‘).
Specifying a fixed quantity from a particular plot invalidates the contract.
4
Understand invalid conditions
If they stipulate a qufrān (specified amount from a specific area) or allocate produce for canals/channels, the contract is batil.
Such conditions introduce gharar.

قال أبو حنيفة : المزارعةُ بالثلث والربع باطلة، وقال أبو يوسف ومحمد : جائزة. وهي عندهما على أربعة أوجه : إذا كانت الأرض والبذر لواحد، والعمل والبقر من آخر: جازت المزارعة. وإذا كانت الأرض لواحد والعمل والبقرُ والبَدْرُ لَآخَرَ: جازت المزارعة. وإذا كانت الأرض والبقرُ والبَدْرُ لواحد، والعمل لآخر: جازت. وإذا كانت الأرض والبقر لواحد ، والبَدْرُ والعمل لآخر: فهي باطلة. ولا تصح المزارعةُ إلا على مدة معلومة. ومن شرائطها : أن يكون الخارج مشاعاً بينهما، فإن شَرَطا لأحدهما قُفْراناً مسماة: فهي باطلة. وكذلك إن شرطا ما على الماديانات والسواقي. وإذا صحت المزارعةُ : فالخارج بينهما على الشرط.
Abu Hanifa said: Sharecropping with one-third or one-fourth is invalid. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: It is permissible. And according to them, there are four forms: If the land and seed are from one, and the labor and oxen from another: the sharecropping is permissible. If the land is from one, and the labor, oxen, and seed from another: permissible. If the land, oxen, and seed are from one, and the labor from another: permissible. If the land and oxen are from one, and the seed and labor from another: it is invalid. And sharecropping is not valid except for a fixed period. Among its conditions: that the produce be joint (undivided) between them; if they stipulate for one of them a specified portion (qufrān) from the plot, it is invalid. Similarly, if they stipulate what is upon the irrigation canals and channels. And if sharecropping is valid, the produce is between them according to the stipulation.
Source pages p. 215

Commentary
This passage opens Kitāb al-Muzāra‘ah (Sharecropping) with a major intra-Hanafi dispute. Imam Abū Ḥanīfa considers sharecropping with a fraction (one-third or one-fourth of the crop) invalid due to uncertainty (gharar) and the lack of a fixed wage, as it resembles a lease with uncertain consideration. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the two companions) deem it permissible based on customary practice (ta‘āmul) and the need of people. According to them, there are four valid forms: (1) land and seed from one party, labor and oxen from the other; (2) land from one, everything else from the other; (3) land, oxen, and seed from one, labor from the other; (4) is invalid: land and oxen from one, seed and labor from the other, because the seed is the essential element for growth, and if one party provides only labor and oxen, he bears no risk. Additionally, a fixed term is required for validity, and the produce must be shared as a fraction of the total yield (mushā‘). Specifying a fixed quantity from a specific plot (qufrān) or allocating the produce for maintenance of canals is invalid. Once valid, the produce is distributed as agreed.

Key Terms
المزارعة (al-muzāra‘ah): An agricultural contract where land is given to another party to cultivate in return for a share of the harvest.
بذر (badhr): Seed; one of the factors in land cultivation.
بقر (baqar): Oxen; the animals used for plowing.
مشاع (mushā'): Joint, undivided share in the total produce.
قُفْراناً مسماة (qufrānan musammāh): A specified quantity from a particular plot (e.g., a fixed number of bushels from a specific field).
