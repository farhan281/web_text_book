This lesson examines a specific agency scenario: an agent authorized to buy a whole slave purchases only half. The Hanafi ruling states that this purchase is suspended—it does not immediately bind the principal. However, if the agent subsequently buys the remaining half, the entire purchase becomes binding. The purchase of half is ruled as mawqūf (suspended). It does not become binding (lāzim) on the principal immediately.

3

If the agent later buys the remainder of the slave, the entire purchase becomes binding on the principal.
The condition is completing the purchase of the whole slave.
4
Understand the Rationale
The agent's initial deviation can be rectified by subsequent full compliance. This reflects the Hanafi principle that a suspended act can become binding when its missing condition is fulfilled.
The purchase is not void; it is merely pending.
5

Consider other agency scenarios: e.g., agent authorized to buy a specific property buys a share; the ruling is analogous.
The key element is whether the agent ultimately completes the authorized purchase.

Agent Exceeds Authorized Quantity: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Ruling on agent buying double the authorized quantity of meat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on agency exceeding quantity and explain the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) for the principal's liability.

Core Idea
An agent's exceeding authorization divides the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa limits liability; Sahibayn expand it.

A principal sends an agent to buy 10 pounds of meat for 1 dirham. The agent returns with 20 pounds for the same price. Did the agent act inside or outside the authorization? Imam Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn give different answers, affecting how much the principal must pay. This lesson dissects their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Authorization and the Agent's Action
The principal authorized a specific quantity (10 lbs) at a total price (1 dirham). The agent bought double the quantity at the same total price. The market rate is 10 lbs per dirham, so 20 lbs are worth 2 dirhams, but the agent paid only 1 dirham.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa holds that only 10 lbs are binding on the principal, payable at half a dirham (the proportionate share of the price). The remaining 10 lbs and the other half dirham are not binding because the agent exceeded the quantity limit.
He views the excess as a separate transaction not authorized.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that all 20 lbs are binding on the principal, payable at 1 dirham, because the agent obtained more of the same goods at a proportionate price, which is beneficial and within the spirit of the agency.
They see the purchase as a single deal that benefits the principal.
4
Analyze the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, the principal pays only half a dirham and gets 10 lbs; the agent must account for the other 10 lbs and half dirham. Under Sahibayn, the principal pays 1 dirham and gets 20 lbs.
The difference is whether the principal benefits from the agent's initiative.
5
Note the Operative Ruling (Mufta Bihi)
Later Hanafi jurists often adopt the Sahibayn's view when the excess is proportionate and beneficial, but the text does not specify a tarjih.
In practice, many fatwas follow the Sahibayn for public interest.

Deposit Delivery Upon Agency Claim
Ruling on deposit delivery when claimant asserts agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a claimant claims agency to receive a deposit and explain why the depository is not ordered to deliver.

Core Idea
A depository is not ordered to hand over a deposit based on a mere assertion of agency, even if believed.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in deposit (wadī‘a) contracts: when someone claims to be the agent of the depositor to receive the deposit, and the depository confirms the claimant's statement. The text gives a clear ruling that distinguishes deposits from debts. Understanding this ruling requires appreciating the nature of wadī‘a as a trust (amāna) and the depository's duty of caution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Parties
Identify the act under consideration: a depository being asked to hand over a deposit to a claimant who claims to be an agent of the depositor. The parties are the depository (holder), the claimant (alleged agent), and the absent depositor (principal).
2
Identify the Condition
The text states a condition: the depository confirms (fṣaddaqa) the claimant. This means the depository believes the claimant's statement that he is an agent.
Belief alone is not legal proof.
3
State the Ruling
The ruling is: the depository is not ordered to hand over the deposit (لم يؤمر بالتسليم إليه). This is a clear negative command.
The depository is not compelled to deliver.
4
Contrast with Debt Agency
Understand why this differs from debt: in debt, if the debtor confirms the claimant, he may be ordered to pay. In deposit, the depository's liability changes upon delivery, so more caution is required.
Deposit is an amāna (trust), debt is a liability.
5
Understand the Rationale
The rationale is that the depository's duty is to protect the deposit for the owner until clear evidence of agency is provided. Mere confirmation by the depository does not establish the claimant's authority; the depository could still be liable if the claimant was unauthorized.
Clear proof (e.g., written authorization, witness, owner's direct instruction) is needed.

Core Idea
Hawalah transfers the debt entirely, freeing the transferor unless he intends otherwise.

This lesson presents the foundational ruling on hawalah (transfer of debt) from the matn of al-Quduri. The passage establishes the permissibility, the required consent of all three parties, and the key consequence: the transferor is discharged. Understanding these core elements is essential before studying detailed conditions or disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that hawalah is a contract to transfer a debt. Its default ruling is permissible (mubah).
2
List the conditions for validity
The three parties must consent: the transferor (muhil), the transferee (muhtal), and the debtor (muhal 'alayh). Without any consent, the contract is invalid.
3
Understand the legal effect
Upon completion, the transferor is freed from the debt. The transferee can only claim from the debtor.
4
Note the exception
If the transferor intended to remain liable (as a guarantor), the transferee can recover from him. Otherwise, no recourse.
The exception is based on intention (niyyah).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for determining whose statement is decisive in disputes between the parties of a hawalah.

Core Idea
In hawalah disputes, the debtor's bare claim is rejected, but the transferor's claim of agency is accepted with oath.

This passage presents two critical scenarios that arise after a hawalah. Understanding which party's statement is accepted ensures that the hawalah contract remains effective and prevents unjust claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Familiarize yourself with the three parties: muhil (transferor), muhal 'alayh (debtor), and muhtal (transferee). Each has a different role in the dispute.
2
Analyze the first scenario
The debtor demands reimbursement from the transferor, claiming the transfer was based on a debt the transferor owed him. The text rules that the transferor's denial is not accepted; he must pay unless he proves the debt.
The default is that hawalah is a transfer of a debt from the transferor to the debtor, not a means for the debtor to collect from the transferor.
3
Analyze the second scenario
The transferor claims he only authorized the transferee to collect on his behalf (agency), not to satisfy a debt. The transferee says it was a debt transfer. The text says the transferor's word is taken with an oath.
The default in hawalah is that the transferee acquires the right to collect for himself, so the transferor must prove agency.
4
State the rule
In the first case, the transferor cannot avoid payment by a bare claim; in the second, his statement is given weight because the nature of the contract is in dispute.

Sulh (compromise) is a flexible tool to resolve disputes without litigation. The Hanafi school recognizes three types based on the defendant's stance: acknowledgment, silence, or denial. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rulings on sulh when the underlying claim is marriage by a man against a woman, marriage by a woman against a man, or a claim of slavery, and identify the analogous legal constructs (khul' and itq 'ala mal).

Core Idea
Sulh on a marriage claim by a man is valid and akin to khul'; sulh on a marriage claim by a woman is invalid; sulh on a slavery claim is valid and akin to emancipation for payment.

Al-Quduri presents three special scenarios that test the boundaries of sulh. The first involves a man claiming a marriage against a woman who denies it; if she pays him to drop the claim, it is valid and treated like khul' (divorce by redemption). The second involves a woman claiming marriage against a man who pays her; this is invalid because a woman cannot enforce a marriage claim. The third involves a man claiming another is his slave; if he accepts money to drop the claim, it is valid and treated like emancipation for payment (itq 'ala mal). These analogies integrate these cases into the broader fiqh framework.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimant and the subject
Determine who is making the claim (man or woman, slave owner or freeman) and what the claim is (marriage or slavery).
The gender of the claimant in marriage claims matters.
2
Apply the permissibility rule with analogy
If a man claims marriage against a woman and she pays to drop it, it is valid as khul'. If a woman claims marriage against a man and he pays her, it is invalid. If a man claims another is his slave and accepts payment to drop it, it is valid as itq 'ala mal.
The validity hinges on whether the claim could lead to a known legal outcome (marriage or emancipation).

Sulh Over a Debt: Partial Recovery and Forgiveness
Rule on characterization of sulh for existing debts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the legal nature of sulh when it involves a debt: it is not a sale or exchange but a partial recovery and forgiveness of the remaining debt.

Core Idea
When a creditor settles an existing debt for less, it is not an exchange but a recovery of part and forgiveness of the rest.

The final passage of this section clarifies a key distinction. If the subject of sulh is a debt that is already due (mustahaqq bi aqd al-mudayana), the settlement is not treated as a sale or exchange (mu'awada). Instead, the creditor is deemed to have collected part of his original debt and forgiven the balance. This prevents any implication of riba (usury) or gharar that might arise if it were treated as a sale of the debt. The example of one thousand dirhams settled for five hundred illustrates that the creditor recovers five hundred and waives five hundred.

1

Determine if the claim is based on a debt contract (e.g., loan, sale credit) where the debt is already established as due.
This applies when the subject of sulh is 'mustahaqq bi aqd al-mudayana' – a right due from a debt contract.
2
Apply the legal characterization
The sulh is not considered a new sale of the debt (which could be prohibited as riba). Instead, it is treated as the creditor recovering part of his original right and forgiving the remainder.
This protects the transaction from being treated as a sale of debt (bay' al-dayn) which may involve riba.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that when a third-party reclaims property subject to settlement, the plaintiff must return the consideration and may resume the lawsuit, including proportional partial reclaim.

Core Idea
If settlement property is reclaimed, the plaintiff returns the consideration and reopens the lawsuit—fully or proportionally.

This lesson unpacks the first scenario of istihqaq (third-party reclamation) after a sulh (settlement). When a third party proves ownership of the settled property, the original settlement is disturbed. The plaintiff must return the consideration he received, and he may resume his lawsuit. If only part of the property is reclaimed, he returns a proportional share and resumes the lawsuit only for that part.

The plaintiff (claimant in the original suit) returns the consideration (iwad) he received from the defendant.
3

The plaintiff can resume the lawsuit (khusumah) regarding that property.
4

If only part of the property is reclaimed, the plaintiff returns a proportional share of the consideration and can resume the lawsuit only for that part.

Settlement on Debts: The Principle of Partial Collection and Waiver
Ruling that sulh on a debt is treated as partial satisfaction, not exchange; examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that sulh on a debt is treated as partial satisfaction, and apply it to determine the validity of various settlement scenarios.

Core Idea
Sulh on a debt is not a sale; it is partial collection and forgiveness of the remaining.

When a creditor agrees to accept a lesser amount or different terms to settle a debt, the Hanafi school does not view this as a new exchange contract. Instead, it is seen as the creditor collecting part of his right and waiving the rest. This principle has important implications for validity, especially regarding riba. In this lesson, we will examine the ruling and several examples from the classical text.

Guided Path
1
Understand the legal nature
Recognize that sulh on a debt is not treated as an exchange (mu'awadah) but as partial collection (istifa') and waiver (ibra'). This is the foundational principle from which all rulings follow.
This is unique to Hanafi fiqh; other schools may treat it as a sale.
2
Analyze valid examples
Examine the examples where settlement is valid: same genus (dirhams for dirhams) even with different quality, and extension of term with the same amount. Understand why these are permissible.
Quality difference (good vs. deficient) does not affect validity as long as genus is the same.
3
Analyze invalid examples
Examine examples where settlement is invalid: change of genus (dirhams to dinars), reduction of amount when term is shortened (deferred to spot), and change from one subtype to another (black to white dirhams). Identify why each is invalid.
Invalidity arises because the settlement resembles riba under the exchange paradigm.
4
Identify the underlying rationale
Synthesize the pattern: any change that would constitute riba (in genus, amount, or term) if the transaction were treated as an exchange renders the sulh invalid. Since it is not an exchange, such changes are not permissible.
The principle protects against riba while allowing genuine hardship relief.

Core Idea
A gift with condition of compensation is a sale in disguise, governed by sale law.

Not every transfer of property is a pure gift. When a donor imposes a condition that the donee must give something in return, the transaction is fundamentally an exchange. Guided Path

This lesson examines a specific scenario within the Book of Usurpation: the usurper has already paid a guarantee (daman) for the property, but later the property itself is found. And the seller is told: either surrender what the buyer claims of the goods, otherwise we rescind the sale. Then if they do not agree, the judge makes each swear regarding the other's claim. He begins with the buyer's oath. If both swear, the judge rescinds the sale between them. If one refuses the oath, the other's claim is upheld against him.
Source pages p. 335

Commentary
This passage details the procedure when buyer and seller dispute the price or the quantity of goods. First scenario: one brings evidence, it prevails. Second: both bring evidence; the one proving the increase (higher price for seller, larger quantity for buyer) is preferred because he claims an addition beyond the common admission. Third: neither has evidence. The court first offers each a settlement: the buyer can agree to the seller's price, or the seller can agree to the buyer's quantity. If no agreement, a mutual oath (tahāluf) is administered: each swears concerning the other's claim. The buyer swears first. If both swear, the sale is rescinded. If one refuses, he loses: the one who swears (or whose claim is accepted) prevails.

No Mutual Oath in Disputes over Deferment, Option, or Partial Payment
Exception to mutual oath in sale disputes regarding deferred payment, option, or partial receipt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three exceptions to the mutual oath procedure in sale disputes and apply the rule to determine who is believed when parties disagree about non-essential terms.

Core Idea
Disputes about deferred payment, option, or partial receipt: no mutual oath; the denier is believed.

The general rule in sale disputes is that when the parties disagree about the price or the object, they take mutual oaths. However, this passage from al-Quduri outlines three exceptions where a different procedure applies. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly resolving disputes about additional conditions in a sale contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three exceptions
List the three scenarios where mutual oath does not apply: deferment (ajal), option (khiyār), and partial payment (istīfā' ba'd al-thaman).
These are all additional conditions beyond the essential elements.
2
Understand the rationale
These terms are not part of the core contract; the default is their absence. Therefore, the one who denies them is asserting the original state.
The contract is valid without these conditions.
3
Apply the rule
In each case, the party denying the extra condition is believed after taking an oath. The other party must bring evidence if they want to prove the condition.
No mutual oath is performed.
4
Distinguish from mutual oath
Mutual oath is for disputes about essential elements like price or subject matter. Here, the dispute is about ancillary terms, so the procedure differs.
This distinction is key to correct application.

Commentary
This passage specifies exceptions to the mutual oath procedure. If the dispute concerns whether the price was deferred (on credit), whether a stipulation of option (khiyār) existed, or whether part of the price was already paid, then the procedure is different: the parties do not perform mutual oaths. This is because these matters are not essential to the contract but are additional conditions; the default is their absence. The person denying the extra condition is given more weight.
