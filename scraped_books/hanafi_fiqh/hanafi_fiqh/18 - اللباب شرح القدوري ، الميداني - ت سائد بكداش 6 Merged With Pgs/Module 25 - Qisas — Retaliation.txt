General Rules of Retaliation (Qisas) for Protected Blood
Stating the requirement of qisas and the equivalences between victims and killers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that qisas is wajib for intentional killing of a protected person and identify the required equivalences between killer and victim.

Core Idea
Qisas is wajib only when victim and killer share equal protected status, freedom, and religion.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri lays out the foundational rules of qisas in Hanafi fiqh. It affirms that retaliation is obligatory for intentional killing of anyone whose blood is inviolable (mahdun ad-dam), but only when certain equalities between the parties are met. Understanding these equivalences is essential for correctly applying the law of qisas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The passage opens by stating that retaliation (qisas) is wajib for intentionally killing any person whose blood is protected (mahdun ad-dam). This is the foundational principle.
The term 'mahdun ad-dam' covers all persons whose life is inviolable under Islamic law.
2
List the equivalences
The passage lists specific pairings: free for free, free for slave, slave for free, slave for slave, Muslim for dhimmi. These indicate that the killer and victim must be equal in freedom status and in protected religious status.
Each pairing is a separate condition; all must be satisfied for qisas to apply.
3
Note the exception
The passage explicitly excludes the musta'min (a non-Muslim with temporary safe conduct): a Muslim is not killed for killing a musta'min.
This exception shows that the musta'min's blood is not considered equal to a Muslim's in qisas, though diyah may still apply.
4
Recognize that physical differences are irrelevant
The passage states that a man is killed for a woman, an adult for a child, a healthy person for a blind person, etc., establishing that qisas is not affected by gender, age, or physical condition.
Equality is based on protected status and freedom, not bodily attributes.

Exceptions to Qisas: Father, Child, and Slaves
Lists exceptions to qisas and execution method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the cases where qisas is not applied in Hanafi law and explain the rationale for each exception.

Core Idea
A father is never executed for killing his son; a master is never killed for killing his own slave.

The general rule of qisas—retaliation in kind for intentional murder—is subject to important exceptions. In Hanafi law, certain relationships and property rights bar the application of qisas. This lesson examines those exceptions and also the requirement that qisas execution be carried out only with a sword.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling structure
The passage first lists cases where qisas is not carried out: a man for his son, his slave, mudabbar, mukātib, or his child's slave. Then it addresses a special inheritance scenario. Finally, it mandates the method of execution.
These are mawāni' (preventers) that block the otherwise applicable qisas.
2
Understand the father-son exception
The hadith 'lā yuqtalu wālidun biwaladih' (a parent is not killed for a child) is the basis. The father's love and authority, and the child's entitlement to the father's wealth, make qisas inappropriate.
This is a definitive exception in Hanafi fiqh; no heinousness can override it.
3
Understand the master-slave exceptions
A man is not killed for killing his own slave, mudabbar (promised freedom on death), or mukātib (contract of manumission). The rationale is that the slave is his property; destroying one's own property does not incur qisas. The mudabbar and mukātib are still under his ownership during his life.
The mukātib is partly free but still considered property for this ruling.
4
Analyze the inheritance scenario
If the person who inherits the right to demand qisas against a killer is the killer's own father, the right to qisas drops. Instead, the father (killer) must pay dīyah (blood money). This prevents a son from executing his father.
The text: 'wa man waritha qiṣāṣan ʿalā abīhi saqaṭa wa ʿalayhi ad-diyah'.
5
Note the execution method requirement
Qisas can only be carried out with a sword. This is to ensure a swift death and minimize pain. Other instruments (e.g., stone, fire) are prohibited.
This is a procedural rule that ensures the punishment fulfills its purpose without excessive cruelty.

If the heir of a murder victim is the father of the killer, then the right to qisās drops and the father must pay the diyah instead. Finally, qisās must be carried out with a sword, not other instruments, to ensure swift death and minimize suffering.

Qisas for a Mukātib Slave
Ruling on qisas for a mukātib slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions under which the owner of a mukātib may demand qisas and identify the scenario where the owner loses that right.

Core Idea
The owner's right to qisas for a mukātib depends on no other heir and sufficient property (wafā').

This passage clarifies the right of a mukātib's owner to demand retaliation (qisas) when the mukātib is intentionally killed. The ruling turns on two key conditions: the absence of other heirs and the existence of property covering the kitābah. Understanding these conditions prevents misapplication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and key parties
Recognise that the act is intentional killing of a mukātib (a slave under contract of manumission). The parties are the mukātib, his owner (mawlā), and potential other heirs.
A mukātib is partly free but still owned until the kitābah is paid.
2
State the condition for owner's right
From the text: 'وإذا قُتِلَ المكاتب عمداً، وليس له وارث إلا المولى، وتَرَكَ وفاء : فله القصاص.' The owner has the right to qisas only if (1) the mukātib has no heir other than the owner, and (2) he leaves wafā' (property sufficient to pay the kitābah). Both must be present.
Wafā' means the estate covers the remaining kitābah amount.
3
Identify when the owner loses the right
The text continues: 'فإن ترك وفاءً، ووارثه غيرُ المولى : فلا قصاص لهم وإن اجتمعوا مع المولى.' If an heir other than the owner exists, that heir has the right to qisas, and the owner cannot demand it—even if the heir consents to join with the owner.
The heir's right supersedes the owner's; they may choose to forgive or take diyah.
4
Understand the rationale
The owner's interest in the mukātib is primarily financial (the kitābah). If the mukātib leaves no other heir, the owner inherits the right to qisas as the nearest relative. But if a free heir exists, that heir has the exclusive right because they are free and closer in kinship.
This follows general inheritance principles where the owner is only heir when no other heir exists.

This passage addresses two special cases. But if the mukātib has other heirs (e.g., a child who is free), they, not the owner, have the right to qisās; if they choose to forego it, the owner cannot claim it. Second, a pledged slave (rahn): both the pledgor (rāhin) and pledgee (murtahin) must consent to qisās; the creditor's interest is protected, so either can block the execution.

وفاء (wafā'): Property left by the mukātib sufficient to cover the kitābah (money owed for freedom).
مكاتب (mukātib): A slave who has entered a contract of manumission (kitābah) with his master, obligating him to pay a sum for freedom.

Qisas for a Pledged Slave (Rahn)
Ruling on qisas for a pledged slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why both the pledgor and the pledgee must consent for qisas to be obligatory when a pledged slave is killed.

Core Idea
Qisas for a pledged slave requires joint consent of pledgor and pledgee due to shared property interests.

This second part of the passage addresses the situation of a slave who has been pledged as collateral (rahn). Because the slave is subject to a security interest, the right to demand retaliation is not unilateral; both the owner (rāhin) and the creditor (murtahin) must agree. This ensures that neither party's rights are harmed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Two parties are involved: the rāhin (pledgor, owner of the slave) and the murtahin (pledgee, the creditor who holds the slave as security). The slave is referred to as 'abd al-rahn.
The rāhin retains ownership but cannot sell the slave without the murtahin's consent.
2
State the ruling
The text says: 'لم يجب القصاص حتى يجتمع الراهن والمرتهن.' Retaliation is not obligatory until both the rāhin and murtahin agree. This means if either party does not consent, qisas cannot be executed.
'لم يجب' indicates qisas is not wājib (obligatory) without joint consent; it remains a potential right.
3
Understand the reason
The murtahin has a financial interest in the slave as collateral. If qisas is executed, the slave (the body) is lost, depriving the murtahin of his security. Conversely, the rāhin has ownership and may wish to forgive for diyah or other reasons. Thus, both must agree to preserve their respective rights.
This ruling prevents one party from forcing qisas against the other's interest.
4
Consider implications if they disagree
If the rāhin wants qisas but the murtahin refuses, or vice versa, qisas cannot proceed. In such a case, the killer may be liable for diyah instead, or the parties may negotiate. The passage does not detail the alternative, but Hanafi fiqh provides that diyah is due when qisas is blocked.
The right to qisas is suspended until consensus; it is not nullified permanently.

Qisas for an Intentional Wound Leading to Death
Ruling on intentional wound causing death after bedrest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that an intentional wound causing death after bedrest is punishable by qisas for homicide and explain the condition that transforms it from wounding to murder.

Core Idea
An intentional wound that keeps the victim bedridden until death is treated as murder — qisas is wajib.

This lesson examines a specific Hanafi criminal ruling: when a deliberate wound does not kill instantly but leads to death after the victim becomes bedridden. The passage classifies this as intentional homicide, not mere injury, and prescribes retaliation (qisas). We will explore the condition of 'remaining bedridden' and its legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its object
The passage begins with 'man jaraha rajulan 'amdan' — a person intentionally wounds another. Identify the act: intentional wounding. Note that the object is a living person.
The wound must be intentional; accidental wounding falls under different rules (diyah).
2
Recognize the key condition
The text says 'falam yazal sahiba firash hatta mata' — the victim remained bedridden until death. This condition establishes that the wound was the direct cause of death. The victim did not recover in between.
'Sahib firash' denotes one confined to bed due to the injury.
3
Derive the ruling
Since the intentional wound causes death after a period of bedrest, the ruling is 'fa-'alayhi al-qisas' (retaliation upon him). This is the same as intentional murder (qatl 'amd).
The Hanafi position: such a wound is not a separate category; it is fully intentional homicide.
4
Understand the implication
If the victim had recovered and died later from another cause, qisas would not apply. The condition of continuous bedrest ensures causation. This ruling prevents the aggressor from claiming the death was unrelated.

Core Idea
For limbs, qisas is executed precisely from the joint to ensure exact equivalence.

This lesson covers qisas for amputation of specific body parts: hand, foot, the soft tip of the nose (marin), and the ear. The Hanafi ruling emphasizes that for hands and feet, the retaliation must be from the joint to guarantee precise reciprocity. For the nose tip and ear, the same principle applies: the exact same part is severed. We will unpack each ruling and its condition.

Guided Path
1
List the body parts mentioned
The passage mentions: hand (yad), foot (rijl), tip of the nose (marin al-anf), and ear (udhun). These are the sites where qisas for amputation is applicable.
The term 'marin' refers specifically to the soft, fleshy part of the nose.
2
Identify the condition for hand and foot
For the hand and foot, the text says 'min al-mafsil' (from the joint). This means the amputation in retaliation must be precisely at the joint, not above or below.
This ensures exact equality in qisas.
3
Apply the same principle to nose tip and ear
The text says 'wa kathalika al-rijl, wa marin al-anf, wa al-udhun' (and likewise the foot, the tip of the nose, and the ear). This means the same condition applies: the qisas must be from the exact corresponding location.
For the nose tip, it is the marin; for the ear, the entire ear is cut from its base.
4
Derive the ruling
If someone intentionally cuts off another's hand from the joint, his own hand is cut from the joint in retaliation. Similarly for the foot, nose tip, and ear. The ruling is qisas wajib.
If the cut is not from the joint (e.g., from the forearm), the ruling may differ (diyah).

Duress to Kill: The Hanafi Prohibition
Ruling on being forced to kill someone under pain of death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing under duress and explain who bears criminal liability (qisas).

Core Idea
You may not kill an innocent person even to save your own life; the coercer is liable for qisas.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses one of the most severe cases of duress: being forced to kill. Unlike other situations where duress may permit or mitigate, Hanafi law absolutely prohibits taking an innocent life. The ruling emphasizes that the coercer, not the coerced killer, bears full criminal responsibility. Let's unpack the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is killing another under duress. The ruling is that it is not permissible (la yasi'uhu); the person must endure death rather than kill.
2
Determine the consequence for the coerced killer
If the coerced person does kill, he is sinful (āthim). The act is not excused.
3
Identify liability for qisas
Retribution (qisas) is upon the coercer (mukrih), not the coerced killer, if the killing was intentional.
4
Understand the rationale
The coercer is the true cause (mutasabbib) of the death; the coerced person is merely a tool under overwhelming duress, yet the sanctity of life overrides any excuse to kill.
This is based on the principle that the indirect cause (mutasabbib) can be liable for murder.

Commentary
This is a ruling on the most severe form of duress: being forced to commit murder. Unlike other cases where compliance is permitted, here the coerced person must not kill, even if it means his own death. The lives of others are inviolable, and one cannot kill an innocent person to save oneself. If he does kill, he is sinful (āthim), and the legal responsibility for qisas (retribution for intentional murder) falls on the coercer (mukrih), not the coerced killer, because the coercer is the true cause. This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
القصاص (al-qiṣāṣ): Retributive punishment of the murderer by the legal authorities (death penalty).
المكره (al-mukrih): The person who coerces another to act, especially in the context of duress causing murder.
