Carrying the Bier and Burial Practices: Sunnah and Makruh in the Hanafi School
Rulings on carrying the bier and burial.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah and makruh actions related to carrying the bier and burial, and apply the ruling for each practice.

Core Idea
Carry the bier by its four corners, walk briskly, use sun-dried bricks, and raise the grave — these are sunnah.

The funeral procession and burial involve several actions that are guided by the sunnah. The Hanafi school distinguishes between recommended (sunnah), disliked (makruh), and permissible (mubah) practices. This lesson covers the key rulings from the matn, enabling you to perform and advise on funerary rites correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actions related to carrying the bier
The text specifies that the bier is carried by its four legs (corners) and that the pace is brisk but not jogging. Recognize these as sunnah.
Carrying by four corners distributes weight and honours the deceased.
2
Note the disliked action upon arrival
It is makruh tanzihi to sit before the bier is placed on the ground. Understand that this is a matter of adab.
Sitting prematurely shows disrespect.
3
Analyze the burial procedures
The grave is dug with a lahd (niche), the body is entered from the qibla side, the shroud knot is untied, and the person placing the body says the prescribed words. These are sunnah.
Lahd is preferred; if ground is soft, shaqq may be used.
4
Examine the materials for covering the niche
Sun-dried bricks (labin) are recommended; fired bricks (ajur) and wood (khashab) are disliked; reeds (qasab) are permissible.
Disliked does not mean haram; it is allowed if necessary.
5
Understand the shape of the grave
The grave is made hump-shaped (musannam) and not flat (musattah). This is sunnah.
The hump shape resembles the Prophet's grave.

Causes of Legal Interdiction (Hajr) and Their Effects
Defines the three causes of interdiction and explains effects on contracts and torts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three causes of legal interdiction (hajr) and explain why interdiction applies to statements but not to actions.

Core Idea
Interdiction restricts statements, not actions: a minor's divorce is void, but his tort liability remains.

In this lesson, we explore the concept of hajr (legal interdiction), which limits a person's capacity to perform certain legal acts. The Hanafi school identifies three causes: minority, slavery, and insanity. Understanding these causes is essential because they determine whether a person's contracts, acknowledgments, divorce, and torts are valid or binding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three causes
Read the opening line: 'The causes necessitating interdiction are three: minority, slavery, and insanity.' Memorize these three.
2
Analyze the capacity for transactions
For each cause, note the rule: minor needs guardian, slave needs master, insane person can never transact. But if they understand and intend, the guardian can ratify.
3
Distinguish statements from actions
The passage states interdiction applies to statements (aqwal) like contracts, divorce, etc., but not actions (af'al) like torts. They are liable for destruction.
4
Apply the rule to a slave's acknowledgments
Slave's statements are effective for himself: debt binds after freedom; but acknowledgment of hadd/qisas binds immediately. Divorce takes effect.

The disposal of a slave is not permissible except with permission of his master. The disposal of an insane person who has lost his reason is not permissible under any circumstance. Commentary
This passage from the Book of Interdiction (Kitab al-Hajr) classifies the causes that legally restrict a person's capacity to act (hajr). According to the Hanafi school (based on al-Quduri), the three causes are minority (saghar), slavery (riqq), and insanity (junoon). A minor cannot conduct transactions without guardian's permission; a slave cannot without master's permission; an insane person can never conduct transactions. If a transaction occurs with someone from these groups who understands and intends the sale, the guardian has the option to ratify (if beneficial) or rescind. Importantly, interdiction applies to statements (aqwal) like contracts, acknowledgments, divorce, and manumission, but not to actions (af'al): if they cause damage, they are liable for compensation. For a minor and insane person, their contracts, acknowledgments, divorce, and manumission are invalid. For a slave, his statements are valid regarding himself but not binding on his master; for example, if he acknowledges a debt, it binds him only after freedom; but if he acknowledges a hadd punishment or qisas, it binds immediately. His divorce is effective. These rulings are standard in Hanafi fiqh.

الصَّغَرُ (as-saghar): Minority; the state of being under the age of legal majority (bulugh).
الرق (ar-riqq): Slavery; the state of being owned by another person.
الجنون (al-junoon): Insanity; loss of reason that renders a person incapable of valid legal acts.

Causes and Effects of Legal Interdiction (Hajr)
States the three causes of hajr and their legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three causes of hajr and apply the rules of capacity for minors, slaves, and the insane in financial and personal matters.

Core Idea
Interdiction restricts statements, not actions; minors and insane lose capacity, slaves have partial capacity.

Legal interdiction (hajr) is a protective restriction on a person's capacity to perform transactions due to a deficiency in intellect or status. This passage from al-Quduri's matn lists the three grounds for interdiction and explains the legal consequences for each. Understanding these rules is essential for determining the validity of contracts, acknowledgments, and personal actions like divorce and manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three causes
Read the first line: the causes are minority (sughar), slavery (riqq), and insanity (junun).
These are the only grounds in the matn; other schools add more.
2
Distinguish between transactions and actions
Note that interdiction applies to 'statements' (aqwal) like contracts, not to 'actions' (af'al) like causing damage. For actions, they are liable.
This is a key Hanafi principle: hajr affects verbal acts, not physical ones.
3
Apply rules to minor and insane
Minors and the insane: their contracts, acknowledgments, divorce, and manumission are invalid. But if they cause damage, they must compensate.
The guardian may ratify a beneficial transaction if the minor understood it.
4
Apply rules to the slave
Slaves: their transactions are suspended on the master's permission. Their statements are binding on themselves personally. Acknowledgment of debt binds after freedom; acknowledgment of hadd/qisas binds immediately; divorce is valid.
Partial capacity: the slave can engage in personal matters but not financial ones without permission.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Interdiction of the Spendthrift (Safih)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on interdiction of the safih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on interdiction of the safih and explain the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) in the validity of a safih's transactions.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa allows a safih's transactions; the Sahibayn protect his wealth through interdiction.

In Hanafi fiqh, the legal capacity of a person who is wasteful (safih) raises a tension between personal autonomy and preservation of wealth. This lesson examines the internal Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples on whether and how to restrict such a person. The passage presents both views with specific applications, allowing us to see the practical consequences of each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common ground
Note that both sides agree the safih is an adult, sane, and free person. The dispute is over whether he should be interdicted (restricted from managing his wealth).
All agree on the basic definition of safih: one who wastes wealth without purpose.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that no interdiction is placed on a safih. His transactions are valid even if wasteful. However, there is an exception for a minor who reaches adulthood without rushd: his wealth is withheld until age 25, but any transactions before that are effective.
After 25, wealth is handed over unconditionally, even if rushd is not perceived.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the safih is interdicted. His sales are void unless the judge approves for benefit. Emancipation is valid but the slave must work for his value. Marriage is valid, but any dower beyond mahr al-mithl is void.
For the minor who reaches adulthood without rushd, they hold that wealth is never handed over until rushd is perceived.
4
Identify areas of agreement
Both sides agree that zakat is taken from the safih's wealth and that maintenance for his dependents (children, wife, relatives) is provided from his wealth.
These obligations are not affected by the dispute.
5
Extract the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, a sale by a safih is valid; under the Sahibayn, it is void unless judicially authorized. Under Abu Hanifa, a safih's emancipation frees the slave without obligation; under the Sahibayn, the slave must work to pay his value.
The dower difference: under Abu Hanifa, the entire dower is valid; under the Sahibayn, only mahr al-mithl is valid, excess is void.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), regarding the legal interdiction of a safih (spendthrift/foolish person) who is an adult, sane, and free. Abu Hanifa's position is that such a person should not be interdicted at all; his transactions are valid even if he wastes his wealth. However, Abu Hanifa makes an exception for a boy who reaches adulthood but is not yet rashid (mature in handling finances): his wealth is withheld until he turns 25, but if he transacts before that, it is effective. At 25, the wealth is handed over unconditionally. The Sahibayn hold the opposite: the safih is interdicted, and his sales in property are invalid unless the judge approves for benefit. They also hold that an adult who is not rashid never gets his wealth until maturity is perceived. Common rulings: zakat is taken from his wealth, and maintenance for dependents is provided. This dispute illustrates the tension between preserving autonomy and protecting property.

رشد (rushd): Maturity and prudence in managing one's finances; the ability to handle wealth wisely.
مهر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The proper dower for a woman of similar status in her family and society.
سعى (sa'a): To work and earn to pay a debt; here, the freed slave must work to pay his value to the master.

Age of Majority for Males and Females
Ruling on signs and age of puberty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the physical signs of puberty for males and females and distinguish between Abu Hanifa's age limit and the Sahibayn's age limit, applying the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Puberty is primarily proven by physical signs; age limits are only used when signs are absent.

Determining when a child reaches legal majority (bulugh) is crucial for assigning religious obligations, contractual capacity, and criminal responsibility. The Hanafi school identifies clear physical signs for both males and females, but also provides default ages when those signs do not appear. This lesson examines those criteria and a well-known difference within the school between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the physical signs for males
Read the passage and list the three signs that conclusively establish puberty for a boy: nocturnal emission (ihtilam), ejaculation (inzal) during waking hours, and impregnating a woman (ihbal).
2
Identify the physical signs for females
Do the same for a girl: menstruation (hayd), nocturnal emission, and pregnancy (habl). Note that pregnancy is an independent sign even without other signs.
3
Recognize the default age limits
If none of the physical signs appear, the school provides an age-based default. According to Abu Hanifa, a male becomes baligh at 18 and a female at 17. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) set 15 years for both. Identify the difference and the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view due to ease and widespread practice.
4
Understand the procedural rule for ambiguous cases
When a child is near puberty (rahaq) and it is unclear whether they have reached majority, if they claim to have reached it, their statement is accepted and they are treated as adults in all legal rulings.

In Hanafi fiqh, the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) is a strong claim, but it can be lost through specific actions. This lesson enumerates the situations that render the right void, emphasizing the requirement of prompt action (ishhad) and the personal nature of the right.

The shafi' must formally bear witness to his claim, and it must be done against the parties or at the property. Simply witnessing in the assembly is insufficient.
Ishhad is a condition for preserving the right; without it, the right is lost.

If the shafi' compromises (sulh) his right for compensation, the shuf'a is void and he must return the compensation because the right is not property.

The death of the shafi' voids the right; it does not pass to heirs. However, the death of the buyer does not affect the shafi's right; the claim continues against the estate.
The right is personal to the shafi' and extinguishes at his death.

If the shafi' sells his own property (which gave him the right) before the decree, his right is void because he severed his connection to the property.
The right is tied to ownership of the adjacent or shared property; selling it relinquishes the right.

وإذا وكَّل الحر، البالغ، أو المأذون مثلهما: جاز.
وإن ولا صبياً محجوراً يعقل البيع والشراء، أو عبداً محجوراً:
ولا تتعلق بهما الحقوق، وتتعلق بموكليهما.
Book of Agency. Every contract that a person is permitted to perform himself, he is permitted to appoint an agent for it. Agency is valid for litigation regarding all rights, and for establishing them. Agency is valid for execution (of claims) except in the case of hadd penalties and qisas, for agency is not valid for executing them in the absence of the principal from the session. Abu Hanifa said: Agency for litigation is not valid except with the consent of the opponent, unless the principal is sick or absent a distance of three days' journey or more. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: Agency is valid without the consent of the opponent. Among the conditions of agency: that the principal be one who has the capacity to dispose (of his affairs) and to whom legal rulings are binding. (It is valid.) And the agent must be one who understands the contract and intends it. If a free, adult (principal) or a permitted one (ma'dhun) appoints someone like himself, it is valid. And if (the principal) appoints an interdicted minor who understands sale and purchase, or an interdicted slave: rights do not attach to them (the agents), but attach to their principals.
Source pages p. 175

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri begins the Book of Agency (Kitab al-Wikalah). It opens with the general principle that agency is valid for any act that a person may perform himself, establishing the permissibility of delegation. The text then addresses three specific rulings: agency for litigation (khusumah), agency for executing claims (istifa'), and agency for hadd penalties and qisas. The key discussion is the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding agency for litigation. According to Abu Hanifa, such agency requires the opponent's consent unless the principal is sick or absent at a distance of three days or more. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) hold that agency is valid without the opponent's consent. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is that of the Sahibayn, as it facilitates legal proceedings. The text then lists conditions: the principal must be legally capable of disposition (mimm yamlik al-tasarruf) and the agent must be sane and understand the contract. It concludes with rulings on appointing a minor or slave as agent: if an interdicted minor who understands sale and purchase is appointed, or an interdicted slave, the contract is valid but rights attach to the principal, indicating the agency is valid with the principal bearing liability.

Key Terms
وكالة (wikalah): A contract by which a person authorizes another to perform a legal act on his behalf.
خصومة (khusumah): Litigation or legal dispute before a judge.
موكل (muwakkil): The principal who appoints an agent.
وكيل (wakil): The agent appointed.
