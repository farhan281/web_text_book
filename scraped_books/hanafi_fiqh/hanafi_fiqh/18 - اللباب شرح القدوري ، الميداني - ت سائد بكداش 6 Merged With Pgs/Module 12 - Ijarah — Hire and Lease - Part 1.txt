Definition and Validity Conditions of Ijarah (Hire Contract)
Defines ijarah and explains how benefits become known for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijarah and explain the three methods by which the benefit becomes known for the contract's validity.

Core Idea
Ijarah is valid only when the benefit and wage are known — and the benefit can be known by duration, work, or identification.

This lesson opens the Book of Ijarah (Hire/Lease) by laying down the foundational definition and essential conditions. The Hanafi school defines ijarah as a contract on usufruct, not on the asset itself. For the contract to be sound and free from prohibited uncertainty (gharar), both the benefit and the wage must be clearly known. The passage then explains the three distinct ways a benefit can be made known, followed by specific applications for houses and agricultural land.

Guided Path
1
State the definition
The text defines ijarah as a contract on benefits in exchange for compensation. Write out the definition in your own words.
2
Identify the two essential known elements
The contract is valid only if both the benefit (manfa'ah) and the wage (ujrah) are known. Note the principle linking wage to price in sale.
3
Classify the three methods of knowing the benefit
Benefits become known by duration (e.g., renting a house for a month), by work specification (e.g., dyeing a garment), or by identification (e.g., moving this specific food to a known place).
4
Apply the rules to houses and land
For houses, the tenant may do any work except three specific trades (blacksmithing, laundering, milling). For land, the contract is valid only if the crop is named or a general permission is given.

Commentary
This passage opens Kitab al-Ijarah (Book of Hire/Lease) by defining the contract and its essential conditions for validity. The definition: Ijarah is a contract on manafi' (benefits/usufruct) in exchange for a compensation ('iwad). For the contract to be valid, both the benefit and the wage must be ma'lum (known) to avoid gharar (uncertainty). The text then provides a principle: anything that can serve as a price (thaman) in a sale can serve as a wage (ujrah) in ijarah. The main focus is on how benefits become known (ilman al-manafi'), which occurs in three ways: (1) By duration (mudda): e.g., renting a house for a specific period or land for farming. The contract is valid for any known duration. (2) By the nature of the work and its specification: e.g., hiring someone to dye a garment or stitch it, or hiring an animal to carry a known weight or ride a named distance. (3) By identification and indication: e.g., hiring someone to move this specific food to a known location. The passage then gives specific rulings on renting houses/shops: it is permissible for residence even without specifying the activity, and the tenant may do any work except certain noisy or damaging trades (blacksmithing, laundering, milling). For leasing agricultural land, the contract is valid only if the tenant specifies what crop will be planted, or alternatively, stipulates that he may plant whatever he wishes. This flexibility ensures clarity while preventing disputes.

Key Terms
الإجارة (al-ijārah): A contract on usufruct (manafi') in exchange for compensation ('iwad).
منافع (manāfi‘): The benefits or usufruct of an object, e.g., the right to live in a house or ride an animal.
عوض (‘iwaḍ): Compensation or consideration given in exchange for the benefit, i.e., the wage or rent.
معلومة (ma‘lūmah): Known, specifically the benefit and wage must be free from uncertainty (gharar) for the contract to be valid.

Definition and Conditions of Ijarah
Defines ijarah and explains how usufruct becomes known

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijarah, list its two essential conditions (known usufruct and known wage), and apply the three methods of making usufruct known to example scenarios.

Core Idea
Ijarah requires certainty of usufruct and wage — uncertainty invalidates the contract.

The passage before us establishes the foundational definition of ijarah (lease/hire) according to the Hanafi school. As students of fiqh, you already know that contracts require clarity to avoid dispute. Ijarah is no exception: the very first condition is that both the benefit (manfa'ah) and the payment (ajr) must be known. This lesson will equip you to identify these conditions and the three ways the law ensures that knowledge. Pay attention to the analogical link to sale — it will help you recall the rules for permissible wages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act defined
The passage begins by defining ijarah as 'a contract on usufruct in exchange for compensation'. Note that the subject matter is usufruct (manafi'), not the asset itself.
Ijarah covers both hiring of services and leasing of property in Hanafi fiqh.
2
State the two essential conditions
The text says 'it is not valid unless the usufruct is known and the wage is known'. These are shurut al-sihhah (conditions of validity). Both must be specified to eliminate gharar.
Other schools may have additional conditions, but for Hanafis, these two are fundamental.
3
Connect to sale rules
Whatever is permissible as a price (thaman) in a sale is permissible as a wage (ajr) in ijarah. This linkage allows familiar rules from the sale chapter to be applied here.
This implies that items that cannot be sold (e.g., impure substances) cannot be given as wages.
4
Explore the three methods of making usufruct known
The passage lists three modes: (1) by duration (e.g., renting a house for a month), (2) by work and specification (e.g., hiring a tailor to sew a shirt), (3) by designation and pointing (e.g., hiring someone to carry a specific bag to a specific place).
Each method corresponds to a different type of usufruct: temporal, service, or directed task.

Renting Houses and Shops: Permitted Activities and Prohibited Trades
Ruling on renting houses and shops and excluded trades.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the permissible and prohibited trades when renting a house or shop according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Renting a house allows any use except blacksmithing, laundering, and grinding.

When you rent a house or shop, you may use it for any lawful purpose unless the landlord restricts it. However, Hanafi law automatically excludes three trades that cause excessive damage: blacksmithing, laundering, and grinding. This lesson explains the rule and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule for renting premises
The text states that renting a house or shop is valid even if the tenant does not specify the use. The default is that the tenant may do anything.
2
Identify the prohibited trades
Three trades are explicitly prohibited: blacksmithing (al-hidadah), laundering (al-qasarah), and grinding (al-tahanah). These cause excessive heat, water, or dust damage.
These are examples of trades that harm the property.
3
Understand the rationale
The prohibition is to protect the premises from damage. The landlord cannot object to other trades unless specifically restricted in the contract.
4
Determine the consequence of violating the prohibition
If the tenant engages in a prohibited trade, the landlord has the right to claim compensation for damage, but the lease contract remains valid.

Renting Land: Cultivation, Building, and End-of-Lease Obligations
Ruling on renting land for cultivation and building, and end-of-lease removal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi conditions for renting agricultural land and open land, and determine the end-of-lease obligations for buildings and plantations.

Core Idea
Renting land for farming requires specifying the crop; building/planting on open land must be removed at lease end unless the landowner pays.

Renting land for cultivation or development has specific conditions. For farmland, the crop must be named or the tenant reserves the right to choose. For open land (saha), the tenant may build or plant, but at lease expiry he must remove his structures unless the landowner chooses to pay for them or leave them in place. This lesson explains these rules and the options available to both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of land and its intended use
The passage distinguishes between agricultural land (al-ardi li al-zira'ah) and open land (al-saha) for building or planting. Determine which case applies.
2
Apply the conditions for agricultural land
For farmland, the contract is not valid unless the crop is specified (e.g., wheat) or the tenant says 'on condition that he plants whatever he wishes'.
3
Apply the rules for open land (saha)
For open land, the tenant may build or plant trees. The contract must define the duration and purpose.
4
Determine end-of-lease obligations
Upon expiry, the tenant must remove his building and plantation and return the land empty. However, the landowner has two options: pay the value of the structures as removed (and own them) or agree to leave them as is, with the building belonging to the tenant and the land to the landowner.
5
Consider the implications of each option
The landowner's choice affects ownership and compensation. If he pays, he gets the materials; if he leaves them, the tenant retains ownership but the landowner may charge rent or seek removal later? The text does not detail further, but it establishes the basic framework.

Renting Animals and Movables: Specification of User and Cargo
Ruling on renting animals and movables, user specification, cargo limits, and distinction from immovables.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine when a hirer of an animal or movable is liable for damage based on specification of user, and apply the rule that real estate (immovables) is not considered to vary with user.

Core Idea
If an animal is rented for riding without naming the rider, anyone may ride; if a specific person is named, deviation causes liability.

When renting an animal or a garment, the hirer's liability depends on how the user is specified. If unspecified, the hirer may use it himself or let another. If a specific person is named and another uses it, the hirer is liable for damage. However, this rule applies only to movables that vary with the user. For real estate, specification of a particular occupant does not restrict substitution. This lesson explains this distinction and the rules for cargo quantity and type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject: animal or movable vs. immovable
Determine whether the leased item is an animal, garment (movable) or real estate (immovable). The rule differs.
Movables: animals, garments. Immovables: houses, land.
2
Check the specification of user
If the contract does not specify a particular person (absolute), the hirer may use it himself or allow anyone. If a specific person is named, the hirer must ensure that person uses it, else he becomes liable.
3
Apply the exception for immovables
For real estate (al-'iqar), specification of a particular occupant is not considered; the hirer may substitute another without liability.
4
Apply the rules for cargo on animals
If the contract specifies a type and quantity (e.g., five qafizes of wheat), the hirer may carry equivalent or less harmful loads, but not more harmful ones.
Examples of equivalent/less harmful: barley, sesame. More harmful: salt, iron.
5
Determine liability for deviation
If the hirer deviates from the specified user or cargo (e.g., mounts a different person, carries more harmful load), and the animal perishes or cargo is damaged, the hirer is liable.

End-of-Lease Obligations for Building or Planting on Hired Land
Ruling on hiring land for construction or cultivation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring land for building or planting and identify the landowner's options at the end of the lease.

Core Idea
Lessee must remove building/plants at lease end unless landowner pays salvage value or lets them remain.

When you hire a vacant piece of land to build a structure or plant trees, what happens when the lease term ends? The Hanafi school gives a clear default rule: you must remove everything and return the land empty. However, the landowner has two alternatives that can change the outcome. This lesson walks through the ruling and the options available.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Understand the terms 'saḥah' (vacant land), 'yaqla‘' (to uproot/remove), and 'yaghram' (to pay compensation). These define the legal situation.
2
State the default rule
At the end of the lease, the lessee must remove any buildings or plants he placed on the land and hand it over empty.
This obligation is wajib (binding).
3
List the landowner's exceptions
The landowner may: (1) pay the lessee the value of the building/plants as if they were removed (salvage value) and take ownership, or (2) allow them to remain, so each party retains ownership of what they had (building to lessee, land to owner).
These are the only alternatives to the default removal.
4
Apply to a concrete scenario
Consider a lessee who builds a shed on hired land. At lease end, the landowner must either let the lessee remove it, pay for it, or agree to keep it. Practice identifying which option leads to which ownership outcome.

Restrictions and Liability in Hiring Animals and Clothes
Rules for hiring animals and clothes: restrictions and liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for unrestricted and restricted hire of animals and clothes, and determine liability when the hirer deviates from the contract conditions.

Core Idea
If you hire an animal or garment, restricting use to a specific person makes you liable if you let another use it, unless it's real estate.

This lesson covers the key rules for hiring animals and clothes. The contract may be unrestricted or restricted to a specific user or load. The Hanafi school establishes distinct rulings for items that vary with the user versus those that do not, such as real estate. Understanding these distinctions is essential for determining liability when the hirer deviates from the agreed terms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of hired item
Determine if it is movable property (animal, garment) or real estate. Movable items are affected by the user; real estate is not.
2
Classify the condition
Was the hire unrestricted or restricted to a specific person? If unrestricted, the hirer may let anyone use it. If restricted, using another makes the hirer liable for loss.
3
Apply the rule to load modifications
If a specific type and weight are stated, the hirer may carry something equal or less harmful, but not more harmful or heavier.
Examples: wheat vs. barley (equal/less), salt/iron (more harmful).
4
Determine liability for overloading or second rider
Overloading or adding a rider results in liability proportional to the excess.
For second rider: half the value. For overloading: liability for excess weight.
5
Note the exception for real estate
Restriction to a specific person does not apply; the hirer may allow others.

Liability for Beating or Pulling the Bridle: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for killing a hired animal by bridle pulling or beating

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding liability for a hirer's action causing the animal's death, and identify the practical difference.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa holds the hirer liable if he kills the animal by pulling the bridle or beating; the Sahibayn say no liability.

The final sentence in the passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement. When a hirer pulls the bridle (kabh) or beats the hired animal, causing its death, is he liable? Abu Hanifa imposes liability, while Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that he is not. This lesson examines the dispute and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed action
The hirer pulls the bridle or beats the animal, resulting in its death.
2
State Abu Hanifa's view
He holds the hirer liable (daman).
3
State the Sahibayn's view
They hold no liability.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: in courts following Abu Hanifa, the hirer must pay compensation; under the Sahibayn's view, no compensation.
5
Note the later tarjih
Later Hanafi works often side with Abu Hanifa when the action is deemed excessive, but the text does not give preference.

Types of Hired Workers and Their Liability
Definitions and liability rules for hired workers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a common and a specific hired worker, state the Hanafi rules of liability for loss, direct damage, and human harm, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on loss of goods.

Core Idea
Common hired worker: liability for lost goods is disputed; direct damage always liable, except human life.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri lays out the foundational classification of hired workers in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding this classification is crucial for determining wage entitlement, liability for damaged goods, and responsibility for harm — especially the nuanced dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two principal students.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Locate where the text divides hired workers into two types: common (ajir mushtarak) and specific (ajir khas). Note the defining characteristics: the common worker is paid per job, the specific per period.
Common worker: dyer, fuller; specific: personal driver (implied).
2
Understand wage entitlement
The common worker does not deserve wages until the work is completed. Contrast with the specific worker who deserves wages for the period, regardless of work output.
3
Analyze the dispute on lost goods
The text states that goods in the common worker's hand are a trust (amanah). Abu Hanifa says no liability for loss without negligence; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say liable. Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4
Apply the rule for direct damage
If the worker's action directly causes damage (tearing cloth, slipping, breaking rope, sinking ship), he is liable (madhmun). Exception: no liability for harm to humans (drowning, falling).
Human life is not subject to mere compensation.
5
Examine the medical exception
Phlebotomist and cupper are not liable if they perform correctly at the usual site. Exceeding the usual site triggers liability.
This is a specific rule for skilled professionals.

In Hanafi fiqh, the validity of a hire contract (ijarah) is sensitive to the conditions attached to it. Just as a sale can be corrupted by invalid conditions, so too can ijarah. The contract becomes fasid (voidable), not batil (void). It can be rectified by removing the condition.
Fasid contracts can be corrected if the condition is dropped before possession or during the term.

This lesson examines an internal Hanafi disagreement regarding the permissibility of leasing an undivided share in property (musha'). The source text presents two positions: Abu Hanifa restricts leasing to co-owners only, while the Sahibayn allow it generally. Understanding the basis of each view is crucial for applying the correct ruling in modern joint ownership scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute

Hiring a wet nurse is a common scenario where the child's welfare must be balanced with contractual rights. The hirer cannot prevent the wet nurse's husband from having intercourse with her, as that is her marital right.

If she becomes pregnant, the hirer (or the child's guardians) may rescind the contract, but only if they fear for the child from her milk (e.g., due to changes in quality or quantity).
Pregnancy does not automatically void the contract; rescission is conditional on fear of harm.

She must prepare the child's food (presumably when weaning begins). However, if she nurses the child using the milk of a sheep (i.e., animal milk) during the contract period, she is not entitled to any wage.
The wage is for direct breastfeeding; substitution with animal milk nullifies her compensation.

Artisan's Right of Retention (Habs)
Ruling that artisans who leave a trace on the object may retain it until payment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an artisan's right to retain the object and identify the condition that must be met.

Core Idea
An artisan whose work leaves a trace may keep the object until paid.

Did you know that an artisan who works on your clothes or goods can keep them until you pay? This lesson explores a specific Hanafi ruling that grants a right of retention (habs) to certain workers. We'll learn exactly who qualifies and why the condition of 'leaving a trace' matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the Arabic text. It states that every artisan whose work leaves a trace on the object (kullu sani'in lahu athar fi al-ayn) has the right (falahu) to withhold the object after finishing work until the wage is collected. So the act is 'retaining possession' and the ruling is that it is legally permissible (mubah) as a right.
This is a possessory lien, not ownership.
2
Determine the condition
The condition is that the work must leave a trace (athar) on the object. This includes physically altering the item, e.g., washing, dyeing, tailoring, repairing. It does not include work like transporting, guarding, or teaching where no physical trace is left.
The trace can be visible or invisible as long as it is a change.
3
Apply the ruling to examples
Given the examples of the washer (qassar) and dyer (sabbgh), extend to an upholsterer, mechanic, or carpenter. They qualify. A barber? Possibly, though subject to discussion. A tutor? No, no trace on an object.
4
Note the legal effect
The retention is analogous to a pledge (rahn hukmi). The artisan holds the item as security for the wage. If the customer does not pay, the artisan may eventually seek a court order to sell the item and recover the wage.
The artisan cannot simply sell it without judicial process.

Liability for Rent After Possession and Excuses
Rulings on rent after possession and excuses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four rulings on rent liability after possession and apply them to scenarios involving usurpation, defect, and destruction.

Core Idea
Possession triggers full rent; loss of benefit excuses payment.

This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri presents a sequence of rulings about the tenant's obligation to pay rent after taking possession of the rented property. It clarifies the baseline rule — rent is due from possession — and then carves out exceptions when the benefit is lost through no fault of the tenant. Understanding these exceptions is essential for fair application of ijara contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline rule
The text states: once the tenant takes possession (قبض) of the rented house, the rent becomes due even if he never occupies it. This establishes possession as the trigger for the full rent obligation.
Contrast with the pre-possession period: before possession, the tenant is not liable for rent.
2
Recognize the effect of usurpation
If a third party (ghasib) seizes the property from the tenant's hand, the future rent drops (سقطت). The obligation ceases because the tenant can no longer access the benefit.
The contract is not rescinded; it is suspended. If the property is returned, the rent obligation resumes.
3
Understand rescission for latent defect
If the tenant discovers a hidden defect that makes dwelling harmful (e.g., dangerous structural issue), he has the right to rescind (فسخ) the contract. He may choose to keep the contract and claim a rent reduction instead.
The defect must be existing at the time of contract or arise later, but it must be substantial enough to harm dwelling.
4
Identify automatic dissolution and proportional liability
If the house collapses, or the water supply for land is cut off, or the mill's water is cut off permanently, the lease is automatically dissolved (انفسخت). The tenant owes rent only for the period he actually occupied or used the mill, calculated proportionally.
Key difference: automatic dissolution (infisakh) vs. rescission (faskh). Infisakh occurs without any action; faskh requires the tenant's choice.
5
Apply the proportional calculation
After dissolution, the rent owed is apportioned to the time the tenant had the full benefit. For example, if the house collapsed after half the rental period, half the total rent is due.
This ensures the landlord is compensated for the benefit already received, but not for the lost future benefit.
