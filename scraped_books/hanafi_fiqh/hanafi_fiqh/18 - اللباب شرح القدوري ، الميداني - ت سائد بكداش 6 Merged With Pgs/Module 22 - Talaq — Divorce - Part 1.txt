Three Types of Divorce: Ahsan, Sunnah, Bid'ah
Defining categories of divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the three types of divorce (ahsan, sunnah, bid'ah) and state the ruling for each according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Divorce can be best (ahsan), normative (sunnah), or innovatory (bid'ah) — all are valid, but only the first two are recommended.

The Book of Divorce begins with a classification of Talaq into three categories based on how it is pronounced and the timing. This lesson defines each type and clarifies their legal status in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the Arabic text and list the three types of divorce: أحسن الطلاق (ahsan), طلاق السنة (sunnah), and طلاق البدعة (bid'ah).
2
Extract conditions for each type
For each type, note the number of divorces, the timing (one tuhr, three separate tuhrs, or one tuhr), and whether intercourse occurred in that tuhr.
Ahsan requires one divorce in a tuhr without intercourse, then waiting for 'iddah. Sunnah requires three divorces over three tuhrs. Bid'ah is either three in one utterance or three in one tuhr.
3
Determine the ruling
Identify the hukm for each type: ahsan is mustahabb (recommended), sunnah is permissible and rewarded, bid'ah is makruh tahrimi (sinful) but the divorce is valid.
Use the hanafi_position_box for correct hukm level.
4
Note the consequence
For all types, the divorce takes effect (talaq occurs and the wife becomes separated). The husband is sinful only in bid'ah.
The text explicitly says: 'فإذا فعل ذلك: وقع الطلاق، وبانت امرأته منه، وكان عاصياً'.
5
Apply to scenarios
Given a scenario, classify the type of divorce. For example, 'a man says to his wife: you are divorced, you are divorced, you are divorced, in three separate tuhrs' is sunnah; 'a man says: you are divorced three times' in one tuhr is bid'ah.

The Two Aspects of Sunnah Divorce: Time and Number
Explaining the two aspects of Sunnah divorce: timing and number, with special cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Sunnah in number and the Sunnah in time, and apply the correct ruling for consummated and non-consummated wives.

Core Idea
Sunnah divorce requires one talaq per purity period for consummated wives; for others, timing is flexible.

Divorce in Islam is not a free-for-all; it has recommended methods. The Hanafi school distinguishes two components: the number of divorces and the timing. This passage explains that the Sunnah in number is universal, while the Sunnah in time only applies to a woman whose marriage has been consummated. For non-consummated wives or those who do not menstruate, the rules adjust to accommodate their situation. Understanding this distinction prevents common mistakes when initiating divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two aspects
Read the first line: 'والسُّنَّةُ في الطلاق من وجهين' meaning 'the Sunnah in divorce is of two aspects'. This gives the structure: time and number.
Note that these are recommended (sunnah) not obligatory.
2
Apply Sunnah in number
The Sunnah in number applies to all women: one divorce per period of purity (tuhr) for consummated, and similarly one at a time for non-consummated, but sequence is recommended.
Number aspect is about the count of divorces in each statement.
3
Apply Sunnah in time
For a consummated wife (madkhul biha), the Sunnah time is to divorce her only when she is in a state of purity (tuhr) and he has not had intercourse with her during that purity. This prevents complications regarding her iddah.
This condition does not apply to non-consummated wife (ghayr madkhul biha).
4
Handle special cases
For a woman who does not menstruate (due to young age or menopause), her 'tuhr' is approximated by a month. The Sunnah method is to divorce her once each month, three times over three months.
This is based on the principle of measuring purity by the lunar month for those who do not have regular cycles.

Bid'ah Divorce and the Two Sunnahs in Talaq
Explaining that bid'ah divorce is valid but sinful, and distinguishing sunnah in time and number

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of bid'ah divorce and distinguish the two aspects of sunnah divorce: in time and in number.

Core Idea
Bid'ah divorce is valid but sinful; sunnah divorce requires proper timing for consummated wives and proper number for all.

In this passage, the author clarifies a common misconception: even though the husband commits a sin by divorcing in a forbidden manner, the divorce still takes legal effect. The passage then introduces two dimensions of the 'sunnah' divorce—one related to when the divorce is pronounced (time) and one related to how many divorces are given (number). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the Hanafi rulings on talaq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal status of bid'ah divorce
Read the first sentence: 'So if he does that: the divorce occurs, his wife becomes irrevocably separate, and he is sinful.' Note that the divorce is effective (it takes place and produces separation) but the act is sinful.
Bid'ah divorce refers to divorcing during menstruation or giving three divorces in one sitting.
2
Recognize the two aspects of sunnah divorce
The author says 'The sunnah in divorce is from two aspects: sunnah in time and sunnah in number.' Understand that 'sunnah' here means the recommended practice, not the legal category of sunnah as opposed to fard.
These two aspects are independent; one may fulfill one but not the other.
3
Distinguish the scope of each sunnah
For sunnah in number, it applies to both consummated and non-consummated wives equally. For sunnah in time, it applies only to the consummated wife. Note the specific condition: divorcing her once in a pure period (tuhr) in which he has not had intercourse.
For non-consummated, timing is irrelevant; she can be divorced during purity or menstruation.

Ruling on sunnah divorce for non-menstruating women

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah method for divorcing a non-menstruating woman and distinguish between the recommended and permissible methods.

Core Idea
For non-menstruating women, sunnah divorce uses monthly intervals, but immediate divorce remains valid.

The Hanafi school provides clear guidance for divorcing women who do not menstruate due to age. Since the usual sign of a pure period (tuhr) is absent, the sunnah timing substitutes lunar months. This lesson explains the recommended sequence and clarifies that a faster divorce is still legally valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the woman's condition
The woman is non-menstruating due to youth (pre-menarche) or old age (post-menopause). This absence of a menstrual cycle removes the usual purity marker.
If she menstruates, the rules differ—she must be in a state of purity.
2
State the sunnah method
The husband divorces her once per lunar month for three consecutive months. The first divorce is given, then after a month the second, and after another month the third.
Each pronouncement counts as one divorce; after the third, the divorce is irrevocable (bainunah kubra).
3
Note the immediate permissibility
The text asserts that it is permissible (ja'iz) to divorce her immediately after intercourse without any waiting period. This does not follow the sunnah but is legally valid.
Immediate divorce results in the same legal effect but misses the recommended spacing.
4
Understand the legal nuance
The sunnah method is strongly recommended (sunnah muakkadah), but not obligatory. If the husband chooses otherwise, the divorce still takes effect.
The Hanafi school balances between ideal practice and practical flexibility.

Divorce of a Pregnant Woman: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sunnah method for divorcing a pregnant woman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the sunnah method for divorcing a pregnant woman and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
For a pregnant woman, divorce is sunnah with monthly intervals according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, while Muhammad restricts it to one divorce only.

The divorce of a pregnant woman is a special case because pregnancy itself serves as a clear indicator of purity (absence of menstruation). The Hanafi school permits divorce immediately after intercourse. However, the imams differ on the sunnah (recommended) method: how many divorces may be given and how they should be spaced. This lesson examines the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf versus Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, and identifies the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the baseline ruling
Confirm that divorce of a pregnant woman is permissible immediately after intercourse, and identify that the dispute is only about the sunnah method, not the permissibility itself.
The passage opens by stating permissibility before the dispute.
2
Identify the majority Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the husband may give three divorces, separating each by a month, following the pattern of divorcing a non-menstruating woman.
This is the mufta bihi (operative) position.
3
Identify Imam Muhammad's position
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani limits the sunnah method to a single divorce, reasoning that pregnancy is one continuous state; after the first divorce, a new 'purity' does not arise until delivery.
This is a minority view within the Hanafi school.
4
Analyze the reasoning (illah)
The majority analogize pregnancy to a non-menstruating woman whose purity is approximated monthly. Muhammad insists on actual new purity (which only occurs after delivery).
The difference is over whether a pregnant woman can be considered to have multiple 'purities' through approximation.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the majority view, three spaced divorces are sunnah; under Muhammad's view, only one. The mufta bihi is the majority view, so a Hanafi may give three with monthly intervals.
This affects how a mufti advises a husband seeking the sunnah method.

Conditions for divorce and exceptions

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two essential conditions for a valid divorce and identify the three categories of persons whose divorce is invalid according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Only a mature, sane husband can divorce; a minor, insane, or sleeping man's divorce is void.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays down the foundational conditions for a valid divorce (talaq). In Hanafi law, the capacity to divorce hinges on two personal attributes: puberty and sanity. The text then lists three exceptions—the minor, the insane, and the sleeping person—whose pronouncements carry no legal weight. Finally, it clarifies the special case of a slave husband: his divorce is valid if he married with his master's permission, but the master cannot divorce the slave's wife.

Guided Path
1
Identify the positive condition
The passage states that divorce takes effect if the husband is 'balighan aqilan' (mature and sane). These two qualities are essential for a valid talaq.
'Baligh' means having reached puberty; 'aqil' means being of sound mind.
