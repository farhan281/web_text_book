Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Transfer of Waqf Ownership
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on transfer of ownership in waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on when ownership of waqf leaves the waqif and apply the thamarah al-khilaf to a concrete scenario.

Core Idea
Ownership of a waqf does not leave the waqif at the same moment for all Hanafis.

The moment when a waqf property ceases to be the personal property of the endower is a foundational question in Hanafi waqf law. This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri records the classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on this issue. Understanding these three positions is essential for determining the validity and timing of any endowment transaction.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi positions
Identify the three positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani as given in the text. Note the specific condition required by each.
Abu Hanifa: court order or death-suspension. Abu Yusuf: mere utterance. Muhammad: appointment of a trustee and delivery.
2
Analyze the scope of disagreement
Determine which element of the waqf contract each imam emphasizes: external validation (hakim), intention and speech (qawl), or delivery and management (tahweel).
3
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider a practical scenario: a waqif declares a waqf but dies before a judge rules or before handing over property. Under each position, what is the status?
The practical difference in when the property is considered transferred.
4
Note the consensus point
Observe that despite their differences, all agree that once the waqf is established, the property leaves the waqif's ownership and does not enter the beneficiary's ownership.
This is the hallmark of waqf: ownership is suspended or owned by Allah.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The later Hanafi school generally follows Muhammad's view, requiring delivery to a trustee, though Abu Yusuf's view is also used in some contexts.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Endowment (Kitab al-Waqf) presents the classic intra-Hanafi dispute on the critical question: at what point does the property leave the ownership of the waqif? Muhammad adopts a middle position: ownership leaves only when the waqif appoints a custodian (wali) and physically delivers the property. The final statement indicates that despite their differing views on the transfer of ownership, all agree that once the waqf is valid, the property is no longer the waqif's and also does not become the property of the beneficiary—it remains in the ownership of Allah as a charitable endowment.

Intra-Hanafi Disputes on Waqf: Musha', Perpetuity, and Movable Property
Intra-Hanafi dispute on three conditions of valid waqf: musha', perpetuity, and movable property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani on waqf of musha', the condition of perpetuity, and waqf of movable property, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf for each dispute.

Core Idea
Waqf of undivided property and movable assets divides the Hanafi school; Abu Yusuf's flexibility contrasts with the majority's strictness.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents three contested issues in Hanafi waqf law: whether one can endow a share of undivided property (musha'), whether the waqf must have a perpetual final destination, and whether movable property can be endowed. Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad take stricter views, while Imam Abu Yusuf adopts more permissive positions in each case. Understanding these ikhtilafat clarifies the Hanafi school's underlying principles of waqf: permanence and immovability as core conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Disputes
The passage covers three distinct issues: (1) waqf of undivided property (musha'), (2) the requirement that the waqf's ultimate destination be perpetual, and (3) waqf of movable property. Under each, note the positions of the Imams.
The text also hints at a fourth issue about endowing a farm with its oxen and slaves, but the passage is cut short.
2

For each dispute, record what each Imam says. For musha': Abu Yusuf permits, Muhammad prohibits. For perpetuity: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require a never-ceasing end; Abu Yusuf allows a temporary purpose, after which it goes to the poor. For movable property: generally prohibited, but Abu Yusuf permits in cases like a farm with its oxen and workers via istihsan.
The position of Abu Hanifa on musha' is not explicitly stated; he is grouped with Muhammad on perpetuity.
3
Analyze the Basis of Difference
Consider why the Imams differ. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) emphasize that waqf requires complete severance of ownership and perpetuity, which is difficult with undivided shares or movable assets. Abu Yusuf places greater weight on the waqif's intention and custom (urf), allowing flexibility through istihsan.
Abu Yusuf's more permissive approach may derive from his reliance on local practice.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
For each dispute, identify the practical consequence. For musha': if you own half a house, can you waqf that share? According to Muhammad, no; according to Abu Yusuf, yes. For perpetuity: can you waqf temporarily for a specific person? Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say no; Abu Yusuf says yes, then it goes to the poor. For movable property: can you waqf a car or equipment? Generally no, but Abu Yusuf allows if it is part of a going concern (like farm equipment).
The thamarah directly affects the validity of many modern waqf proposals.
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi
Although the passage does not specify a relied-upon position, later Hanafi fatwa has tended to follow Abu Yusuf in some matters (e.g., waqf of movable property in urban settings) due to changing custom. However, the strict view remains authoritative for classical waqf of land and buildings.
Consult later fatwa collections (e.g., Ibn Abidin) for the operative position in each case.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on waqf conditions

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf permits waqf of an undivided share and terminable causes; Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require full ownership and perpetual destination.

This passage presents two related yet distinct intra-Hanafi disputes: the validity of endowing an undivided share (musha') and the necessity of a perpetual final beneficiary. While Abu Yusuf takes a more flexible stance on both issues, Muhammad and Abu Hanifa impose stricter conditions. Understanding these differences is essential for applying the correct ruling in contemporary waqf practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two issues
The passage discusses two conditions: (1) the waqf property must not be an undivided share (musha'), and (2) the waqf must have a perpetual final cause. Note that each issue has its own set of positions.
Musha' means joint ownership without physical partition, e.g., half of a house.
2
State the positions on musha'
Recall: Abu Yusuf allows waqf of musha'; Muhammad prohibits it. Abu Hanifa's view on musha' is not mentioned in this passage.
This is a known difference within the Hanafi school; later fatwa often follows Abu Yusuf for practicality.
3
State the positions on perpetuity
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require the final destination to be perpetual (e.g., the poor, a mosque). Abu Yusuf allows a terminable cause (e.g., for one's children only), after which the waqf defaults to the poor.
The default to the poor is a default charitable purpose (jihah 'ammah).
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
For musha': under Abu Yusuf, endowing half a house is valid; under Muhammad it is void. For perpetuity: under Abu Hanifa, a waqf that ends with a named group is incomplete; under Abu Yusuf it is valid and automatically continues for the poor.
This directly affects the validity of many modern family waqfs (waqf dhurri).

Valid and Invalid Types of Waqf Property
Ruling on types of property valid for waqf with exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on waqf of real estate and movable property, and identify the exceptions made by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Waqf is valid only for real estate; movables are invalid except when attached to land or for jihad.

The Hanafi school generally restricts waqf (endowment) to immovable property. However, the school's founding jurists recognized that some movables serve essential purposes. This passage from al-Quduri presents the baseline rule and the key exceptions developed by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for modern waqf practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act being ruled on
The passage rules on the type of property that can be made waqf (endowment). The act is 'waqf' itself, but the focus is on the object of the waqf.
Waqf is a charitable endowment that is permanent and inalienable.
2
State the general rule
The text states: 'Waqf of real estate is valid; waqf of movable and transferable property is not permissible.' This is the default position in the Hanafi school.
Real estate includes land and buildings. Movable property includes anything that can be transported.
3
Note the exception from Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf allows waqf of a farm (real estate) together with its oxen and slave plowmen, even though oxen and slaves are movable, because they are accessories to the land.
This is an extension of the waqf of real estate to include its essential equipment.
4
Note the exception from Muhammad
Muhammad al-Shaybani allows 'habs' (confinement) of horses and weapons, which are movables, because they are dedicated for jihad and are considered analogous to real estate in their permanence of use.
Habs is a synonym for waqf in this context.
5
Understand the reasoning behind exceptions
The exceptions show that movables can be made waqf if they are attached to real estate (like farm equipment) or if they serve a specific charitable purpose with enduring benefit (like weapons for jihad). This reflects a flexible application of the principle that waqf requires permanence.
These exceptions have been accepted in later Hanafi fatwa practice.

Ruling on sale and partition of waqf property with Abu Yusuf's exception for musha'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi ruling prohibiting sale of waqf property and identify the exception for musha' according to Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Waqf property is inalienable, but an undivided share may be partitioned if a co-owner demands it.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents a foundational rule of Hanafi waqf law: once a waqf is validly established, the property cannot be sold or transferred. However, an important exception is noted according to Abu Yusuf: if the waqf property is an undivided share (musha') held jointly with another owner, the co-owner may demand a physical partition. Understanding this rule and its exception is essential for properly applying waqf regulations in practice.

The general rule is agreed upon, but Abu Yusuf's view on partition of musha' is a recognized difference within the school.

Maintenance Priorities in Waqf
Rulings on maintenance priority and repair of waqf property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligation to prioritize maintenance over distribution in waqf and apply the judge's procedure when a resident beneficiary fails to maintain.

Core Idea
Maintenance of waqf property must be paid from its revenue before any distribution, regardless of the donor's stipulation.

Waqf property generates revenue for beneficiaries, but its upkeep is paramount. This lesson clarifies the obligation to prioritize maintenance, the judge's role when a resident beneficiary neglects repairs, and the handling of collapsed materials. These rules ensure the permanence of the waqf as intended by the donor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The first sentence states the overriding principle: it is wajib to begin with maintenance (عمارة) from the waqf's revenue (ريع) before any distribution, regardless of any stipulation by the donor. This applies to all waqf properties.
This establishes a universal obligation, not a mere recommendation.
2
Distinguish the residence waqf scenario
When a house is endowed for the residence of specific beneficiaries (e.g., the donor's children), maintenance becomes the responsibility of the resident beneficiary. This is a different allocation of duty based on the nature of the benefit.
The resident has direct use, so they bear immediate upkeep costs.
3
Outline the judge's intervention procedure
If the resident refuses or is too poor to maintain, the judge (الحاكم) steps in: he rents out the property, uses the rent revenue for repairs, and after repairs are complete, returns possession to the resident. This protects the waqf asset.
The judge acts as a safeguard, not as a permanent manager.
4
Explain the rule for collapsed materials
If parts of the waqf building or its fixtures collapse, the judge may spend the proceeds from the salvage on needed repairs. If no repair is needed, he preserves the materials until a future need arises.
This prevents waste and ensures funds are available for future maintenance.
5
State the prohibition of distribution
Leftover revenue or materials after maintenance cannot be distributed among beneficiaries. They must be kept for future maintenance needs, preserving the waqf's perpetuity.
This reinforces that maintenance has continuous priority.

Ownership Transfer in Waqf: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on waqf ownership transfer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on ownership transfer in waqf and explain why Abu Yusuf's view is the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
In waqf, ownership leaves by declaration alone, not by judge's ruling or public use.

This passage from al-Quduri presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on the precise moment the founder's ownership of waqf property ceases. Understanding this disagreement is essential for applying waqf rules in practice, as the mufta bihi position determines the validity of subsequent transactions involving the property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and its Legal Context
The passage deals with waqf (endowment) of specific properties: a public drinking fountain, a guesthouse, a ribat, or land as a cemetery. The act is the declaration of waqf. The dispute is about when the founder's ownership transfers away.
The rulings apply to any waqf, not just these examples.
2
Parse the Three Positions
Abu Hanifa states ownership does not leave until a judge rules. Abu Yusuf says it leaves by the mere statement (verbal declaration). Muhammad says it leaves when the public actually uses the property (drinking, staying, burial).
Note the key verb: 'yazulu' (ceases) and the conditional particle 'hatta' (until).
3
Identify the Contested Element
The contested element is the legal cause (sabab) for ownership transfer: judge's ruling (Abu Hanifa), statement (Abu Yusuf), or public use (Muhammad).
4
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
In Abu Hanifa's view, the founder can revoke the waqf before a judge's ruling. In Abu Yusuf's view, it is irrevocable upon declaration. In Muhammad's view, it remains revocable until public use begins.
This directly affects the validity of any sale by the founder.
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi (Operative View)
The Hanafi school prefers Abu Yusuf's view: ownership transfers by declaration alone. This is the position relied upon for fatwa.
Tarjih authority: Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah and later scholars.

ومن بنى سقايةً للمسلمين، أو خاناً يسكنه بنو السبيل، أو رباطاً، أو جَعَلَ أرضه مقبرة : لم يَزُلُ ملكه عن ذلك عند أبي حنيفة حتى يَحكُمَ به حاكم.

Book of Waqf. And whoever builds a public drinking fountain for Muslims, or a guesthouse for wayfarers to stay, or a ribat, or makes his land a cemetery: his ownership of that does not leave according to Abu Hanifa until a judge rules upon it. Abu Yusuf said: his ownership leaves by the statement (of declaring waqf). Muhammad said: if people drink from the drinking fountain, stay in the guesthouse and ribat, and are buried in the cemetery, then ownership leaves.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the mechanism of ownership transfer in waqf (endowment). The matn (al-Quduri) states three positions. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the founder's ownership does not cease merely by declaring the waqf; it requires a judicial ruling (hukm al-hakim) to effectuate the transfer. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that ownership leaves immediately upon the verbal declaration (al-qawl) without needing a judge's decision. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani takes an intermediate position: ownership leaves when the public actually begins using the endowed property (drinking, staying, being buried). The relied-upon mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Yusuf, as it is the most practical and commonly adopted.

Key Terms
الوقف (al-waqf): Endowment: dedicating property for charitable or religious purposes, removing it from personal ownership.
سقاية (siqayah): A public drinking fountain built for Muslims to provide water.
خان (khan): A guesthouse or inn for travelers (ibn al-sabil) to stay.
رباط (ribat): A fortified outpost or hospice for travelers or soldiers, often used for religious retreat.
حكم حاكم (hukm al-hakim): A judicial ruling by a qualified judge to effectuate legal consequences.

Checkpoint
According to the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school, when does ownership of waqf property transfer from the founder?
A) Only after a judge's ruling.
B) Immediately upon verbal declaration.
C) After public use of the property.
D) Never; ownership remains with the founder.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, the mufta bihi view is that waqf ownership transfers immediately upon verbal declaration, making it irrevocable.
