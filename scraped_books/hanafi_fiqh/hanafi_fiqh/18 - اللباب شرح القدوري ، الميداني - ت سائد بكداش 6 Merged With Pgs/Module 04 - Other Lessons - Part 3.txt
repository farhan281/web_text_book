Liability for Benefits of Usurped Property
States the rule and exception for benefit liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for benefits (manāfi') of usurped property and distinguish when compensation is due.

Core Idea
The usurper is not liable for mere use; only for decrease caused by use.

This passage from al-Lubāb defines a key Hanafi principle: the usurper is not liable for the benefits (manāfi') of the usurped property unless the usage causes a physical decrease. This ruling differs from other schools and is based on the Hanafi view that benefits are not considered property (māl) subject to compensation. Understanding this helps avoid confusion about liability in cases of wrongful possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is usurpation (ghaṣb). The general ruling is that the usurper is not liable for the benefits (manāfi') of the property. This includes any usufruct such as living in a house, riding an animal, or using a tool.
No liability means the usurper does not have to pay for the value of the use itself.
2
Note the exception
Liability arises if the use causes a decrease in the property itself (al-naqṣ). The decrease can be physical wear, depletion of parts, or reduction in market value due to usage.
The decrease must be measurable and directly caused by the usurper's use.
3
Apply the distinction to a scenario
Example: If a usurper rides a horse without causing any injury or weight loss, he owes nothing. But if the horse becomes lame or loses value due to overwork, he must pay the amount of the decrease.
The burden of proof is on the owner to show that a decrease occurred.
4
Compare with cross-madhhab view
The Shafi'i school holds that the usurper is liable for the full value of the benefits regardless of decrease, treating benefits as compensable property. The Hanafi position is a clear departure, rooted in the concept that manāfi' are not māl.
This difference has practical implications in Islamic finance and damage calculations.

This brief passage clarifies liability for benefits (manāfi'). Core Idea
Dhimmi's wine and pork are protected property; Muslim's are not.

Islamic law makes a crucial distinction between property that is protected (mutaqawwim) and that which is not. While wine and pork are impure and valueless for Muslims, the dhimma contract grants non-Muslim subjects the right to possess and use these items. This lesson examines the legal consequence when a Muslim destroys such property belonging to a dhimmi versus a fellow Muslim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two scenarios
The act is consuming or destroying wine or pork. Two scenarios: the owner is a dhimmi (non-Muslim protected subject) or a Muslim.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
If the owner is a dhimmi, the Muslim must pay the value (damān). If the owner is a Muslim, there is no liability.
3
Identify the underlying principle
The ruling turns on whether the property is considered 'māl mutaqawwim' (protected wealth). For a Muslim, wine and pork are not māl mutaqawwim because they are impure and prohibited. For a dhimmi, they are, due to the dhimma contract.
4
Explore the rationale
The dhimma grants dhimmis freedom to practice their religion, including consuming wine and pork. Therefore, these items have legal value and are protected property in their hands.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
The Shafi'ī school holds that a Muslim is liable for a dhimmi's wine but not for his pig. The Mālikī school says no liability if the dhimmi is not allowed to trade in these items.

The Trust Nature of Deposit and Limits of Liability
States default rule and exceptions for deposit liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default rule of wadi'ah as amanah and identify the acts that transform it into a guarantee (daman).

Core Idea
A deposit is a trust — the depositary is not liable for loss unless he breaches the owner's implied consent.

This passage from the matn establishes the foundational principle of deposit (wadi'ah) in Hanafi fiqh: it is an amanah (trust), meaning the depositary is not liable if it is accidentally lost. However, if the depositary goes beyond the owner's implied consent—by entrusting it to a stranger or re-depositing it—he becomes liable. Two emergency exceptions allow transfer without liability: fire and shipwreck danger. Understanding these boundaries is essential for proper custodianship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Read the first line: 'The deposit is a trust in the hand of the depositary; if it perishes in his hand, he does not guarantee it.' Recognize that the default is amanah, not daman.
Liability only arises when the depositary oversteps his authority.
2
Permitted custody
Next, note that the depositary may keep the deposit himself or with his household members. This is within the owner's implicit consent.
Household members are considered extensions of the depositary.
3
Acts that trigger liability
If the depositary keeps it with others (non-household) or re-deposits it, he becomes liable. This is the rule: unauthorised transfer results in daman.
Liability applies even if the deposit is lost without negligence.
4
Emergency exceptions
Two exceptions: if there is a fire in the house, he may hand it to a neighbor; if he is on a ship and fears drowning, he may throw it to another ship. In these cases, no liability attaches.
The action is for preservation, not breach of trust.

Liability is triggered at the moment of mixing, not upon actual loss. The depositary becomes a guarantor (damin) from that instant.
This is a strict rule to protect the owner's rights.

Core Idea
Withholding a deposit after demand while able to deliver turns amanah into daman.

We often take deposits as trusts (amanah). However, when the owner demands it back and the depositary is physically able to return it, any refusal or delay without excuse changes the nature of the deposit: the depositary becomes liable for it. This lesson examines the precise conditions that trigger this liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage concerns 'habasa' (withholding) the deposit. The ruling is that the depositary becomes liable (ḍāmin).
This is a consequential ruling: the act of withholding after demand makes him a guarantor.
2
Identify the two conditions for liability
The text states: (1) the owner demands it, and (2) the depositary is able to deliver but withholds. Both must be present.
If either condition is absent (e.g., no demand, or inability to deliver), no liability arises from mere withholding.
3
State the legal consequence
When both conditions are met, the depositary is liable (ḍāmin). This means he must compensate the owner if the deposit is lost or damaged, even without his fault.
This is a key difference from the original amanah status, where he was only liable for negligence.
4
Understand the rationale
By withholding after demand, the depositary wrongfully detains the property; this is ta'addī (transgression). The liability protects the owner's right.
Hanafi fiqh considers the act of habs after demand as a constructive misuse.

Ruling on unintentional mixing: partnership results.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on unintentional mixing of a deposit and distinguish it from intentional mixing.

Core Idea
Unintentional mixing creates partnership, not liability.

This short passage from al-Quduri's Matn clarifies a key distinction in the law of deposits. When a depositary's property accidentally mixes with the deposit through no fault of his own, he does not become liable for the loss. Instead, the two become co-owners in proportion to their shares. This ruling reflects the Hanafi principle that liability arises only from transgression (ta'addi).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and condition
The act is mixing (ikhtilat) of the deposit with the depositary's property. The crucial condition: it occurs 'without his action' (min ghayr fi'lihi). This means the mixing is unintentional, due to external factors.
If the depositary intentionally mixes, the ruling differs: he becomes liable.
2
Determine the legal consequence
The consequence is that the depositary becomes a partner (sharik) with the owner. He is not liable to replace the deposit; instead, both share ownership of the mixture proportionally.
Co-ownership here is shirkat al-milk, not shirkat al-aqd (contractual partnership).
3
Contrast with intentional mixing
Intentional mixing without permission makes the depositary a transgressor (damin). He must either return the same property (if possible) or compensate. Unintentional mixing has no such liability.
This contrast is essential for applying the correct ruling.

Commentary
In contrast to intentional mixing, if the deposit becomes mixed with the depositary's property due to an external cause (e.g., flood, wind) and not through his own act, he does not become liable. Instead, both parties become co-owners of the mixture in proportion to their respective shares. This is because no transgression occurred on the depositary's part.

Ruling on depositary's liability when spending part and replacing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a depositary's liability when he spends part of the deposit and then replaces it, distinguishing the two scenarios.

Core Idea
Spending part of a deposit creates liability; replacing and mixing makes the whole deposit liable.

This lesson examines a common fiqh scenario in deposits (wadi'ah): what happens when the depositary uses part of the deposit for himself? The Hanafi school provides two distinct rulings based on whether the depositary simply spent part (and the rest perishes) or spent, then replaced and mixed. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining liability accurately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act of spending part
Recognize that the depositary (mūda‘) has used a portion of the deposit without the owner's permission. This act is ta‘addī (transgression) and turns that portion from amānah to ḍamān (liability).
The spent portion is now a debt owed by the depositary.
2
Analyze the first scenario: remainder perishes
If the remainder (the unspent part) perishes due to no fault of the depositary, he is liable only for the amount he spent. The rest remains amānah and its loss is not on him.
The spent part was already converted to a liability.
3
Analyze the second scenario: replacement and mixing
If the depositary returns an equivalent amount (mithl) and mixes it with the remaining deposit, and then the entire mixed amount perishes, he is liable for the whole. The mixing destroys the identity of the remaining amānah.
The replacement does not restore the original amānah; it creates a new mixed corpus where the depositary's control is total.
4
Formulate the principle
Mixing the replacement with the original remaining deposit after spending part makes the entire deposit subject to liability because the depositary's transgression is now connected to the whole.
This is a strict ruling to prevent abuse: if mixing absolved liability, depositors could easily circumvent.

Reward for Returning an Absconding Slave
States rulings for reward on returning an absconding slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for receiving a reward (ju'l) when returning an absconding slave and apply the rules to calculate the correct amount.

Core Idea
The reward for returning an absconding slave is fixed at forty dirhams for a three-day journey, but varies by distance and slave's value.

This passage from the Book of Absconding Slaves (Kitab al-Ibaq) presents the Hanafi rulings on the reward (ju'l) for returning a runaway slave. The rulings specify conditions under which the reward is due, its amount, and exceptions. Understanding these rules clarifies the obligations between the returnee and the master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions for reward
The reward is due when a slave absconds and a person returns him to his master. The key conditions are: the return must be from a distance of three days' travel or more, and the slave must be delivered to the master.
If the distance is less than three days, the reward is proportionate.
2
Determine the base amount
For a return from three days' journey or more, the fixed reward is forty dirhams. This is the standard ju'l set by the texts.
Forty dirhams is the default when the slave's value is at least that much.
3
Apply reduction for shorter distance
If the return is from less than three days, the reward is calculated proportionally (bi-hisabihi). For example, if one day's journey, the reward is one-third of forty dirhams, i.e., 13.33 dirhams.
The exact proportion is typically based on the ratio of the distance to three days.
4
Apply reduction when slave's value is low
If the slave's market value is less than forty dirhams, the reward is reduced to the value minus one dirham. This ensures the reward does not exceed the slave's worth.
E.g., if value is 30 dirhams, reward is 29 dirhams.
5
Consider special cases
If the slave absconds again from the returnee, no reward is due and the returnee is not liable. If the slave was a pledge (rahn), the reward is paid by the pledgee (the lender).
It is recommended to take witnesses when taking the slave to confirm intention to return.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An authorized slave's capacity is strictly limited to trade; he cannot marry, free, or give gifts beyond small food.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn on al-ma'dhun (the authorized slave) lays down the precise boundaries of the slave's legal capacity when permitted by his master to trade. Guided Path
1
Classify authorization types
First, recognize three levels: general permission (all trades), permission for one type (still covers all types because trade types are interdependent), and permission for a specific item (no general authority; only that transaction is valid).

The master's withdrawal of permission (hajr) only becomes effective after public announcement in the marketplace. Without publicity, the slave remains authorized.

Core Idea
Emancipation is valid only from a free, adult, sane owner of the slave.

This opening passage from the Book of Emancipation (Kitab al-Itaq) establishes the foundational prerequisites for a valid act of emancipation in Hanafi law. Core Idea
An indirect expression frees only with intention; but not all phrases qualify as kinayah.

In Hanafi law, emancipation (itq) can be effected by clear direct expressions (sarih) like 'anta hurr' (you are free). But when a master uses an indirect phrase—such as 'I have no ownership over you'—the emancipation only takes effect if he genuinely intended freedom. This lesson examines which phrases are valid kinayah and which are not, emphasizing the critical distinction between ownership (milk) and authority (sultan).

Intention is the decisive factor.

The phrase 'la sultana li 'alayka' (I have no authority over you) does not free the slave even with intention because it refers to discipline, not ownership.

This distinction prevents unintended emancipations.
