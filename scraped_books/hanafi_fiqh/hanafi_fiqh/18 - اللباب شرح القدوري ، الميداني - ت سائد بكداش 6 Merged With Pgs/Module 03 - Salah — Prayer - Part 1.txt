Prayer Times and Intra-Hanafi Disputes in Al-Lubab
Defines prayer times and records intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on Zuhr end time and shafaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the beginning and end times of the five daily prayers according to the Hanafi school and identify the two intra-madhhab disputes regarding the end of Zuhr and the definition of shafaq.

Core Idea
Prayer times are defined by natural signs; two key differences between Abu Hanifa and his disciples affect Zuhr and Maghrib/Isha times.

This passage from Al-Lubab presents the fixed prayer times for the five daily prayers. It also highlights two important internal disagreements within the Hanafi school: one on the moment Zuhr ends (based on shadow length) and another on the definition of twilight (shafaq) that determines Maghrib and Isha boundaries. Understanding these ikhtilafs is essential for accurate fatwa and practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prayer and its time indicators
For each of the five prayers, note the natural sign that marks its beginning and end. The text uses concrete phenomena: dawn, zenith, shadow length, sunset, twilight.
Focus on the terms: fajr thani, zawal, shafaq.
2
Locate the two disputes
Find the two places where the text says 'عند أبي حنيفة' and 'قال أبو يوسف ومحمد'. These mark the Zuhr-end and shafaq disagreements.
3
State each imam's position on Zuhr end time
Abu Hanifa: shadow twice the object's height (excluding zenith shadow). Sahibayn: shadow equal to the object's height. Asr begins immediately after Zuhr ends, per either view.
The practical difference: Zuhr time is longer in Abu Hanifa's view.
4
State each imam's position on shafaq
Abu Hanifa: shafaq is the whiteness after redness. Sahibayn: shafaq is the redness itself. This affects when Maghrib ends and Isha begins.
Maghrib time ends earlier (and Isha begins earlier) when shafaq is redness.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: for Zuhr, Abu Hanifa's ruling gives a wider window; for Maghrib/Isha, his ruling shifts times later relative to the Sahibayn. In later Hanafi fatwa, the views of the Sahibayn are often preferred.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Prayer defines the beginning and end times for each of the five daily prayers. For Fajr, the time starts with the second dawn (the true dawn, a whiteness spreading horizontally) and ends at sunrise. Zuhr begins at solar noon (when the sun passes its zenith). The end of Zuhr is disputed within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa holds it ends when the shadow of any object equals twice its height (excluding the zenith shadow), while the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it ends when the shadow equals the object's height. Asr time begins immediately when Zuhr time expires according to either view. Asr ends at sunset. Maghrib begins at sunset and ends with the disappearance of twilight (shafaq). The definition of shafaq is also contested: Abu Hanifa considers it the whiteness that remains after redness on the horizon, while the companions consider it the redness itself. Isha begins when the twilight (per the accepted definition) disappears and ends at the rise of the second dawn. No proofs are cited; this is a straightforward statement of the rulings and differences.

Key Terms
فجر ثان (fajr thānī): The true dawn: a horizontal whiteness spreading across the horizon, signaling the start of Fajr prayer.
شفق (shafaq): Twilight: the glow after sunset; in this passage, the subject of ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa (whiteness) and Sahibayn (redness).
زوال (zawāl): The sun's passing of its zenith (culmination); the beginning of Zuhr time.

Core Idea
Prayer times are fixed by the sun's position; the definition of shafaq divides Abu Hanifa from the Sahibayn on the end of Maghrib.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri gives the precise time boundaries for each mandatory prayer and witr. It also records a well-known difference between Abu Hanifa and his two principal disciples regarding when Maghrib ends — a difference that affects the entire prayer schedule for millions of Muslims. Let us examine each time period and understand the ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Salah of al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri states the prescribed times for the five daily prayers and witr, along with recommended timings and an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the definition of shafaq. الإبراد بالظهر (al-ibrad bi-ẓ-ẓuhr): Delaying the Dhuhr prayer until the intense heat subsides, recommended in summer.

The Adhan: Its Ruling and Procedure
States the ruling of adhan and explains its procedure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of adhan in the Hanafi school and describe its standard procedure, including the differences for Fajr and iqama.

Core Idea
Adhan is an emphasized sunnah for the five daily prayers and Jumu'ah, with a specific procedure that differs from iqama.

Adhan marks the time for prayer. This lesson covers its legal status (sunnah muakkada) and the precise method of calling it, including the phrases, when to turn, and the differences for Fajr. Understanding these details ensures the call is performed correctly according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal ruling
Read the opening sentence: 'الأذانُ سُنَةٌ مؤكَّدة للصلوات الخمس، والجمعة'. Observe that adhan is a sunnah, not fard. Note which prayers it applies to (five daily prayers and Jumu'ah) and which it does not (e.g., 'Eid, funeral).
This establishes the hukm level: sunnah muakkada.
2
Outline the standard adhan phrases
The text says 'أن يقول : الله أكبر، الله أكبر... إلى آخره'. Recognize the standard phrases: four takbirs, two shahadahs, two 'Hayya 'ala' calls, two final takbirs, and one tahlil. The text omits the full list for brevity.
A complete adhan is: Allahu Akbar (4x), Ashhadu an la ilaha illa Allah (2x), Ashhadu anna Muhammadan Rasul Allah (2x), Hayya 'ala al-salah (2x), Hayya 'ala al-falah (2x), Allahu Akbar (2x), La ilaha illa Allah (1x).
3
Note the Hanafi position on tarji'
The text states 'ولا ترجيع فيه'. Understand that tarji' (soft repetition of shahadahs before loud) is not part of the Hanafi adhan. This differs from the Shafi'i school.
Tarji' is a practice where the mu'adhdhin says the two shahadahs softly first, then repeats them aloud. Hanafis do not do this.
4
Identify the Fajr addition and iqama differences
For Fajr, the text adds 'الصلاة خير من النوم' after 'Hayya 'ala al-falah'. For iqama, it adds 'قد قامت الصلاة' after 'Hayya 'ala al-falah'. Both are said twice.
These are the only differences between adhan and iqama, and the special phrase for Fajr.
5
Understand tarsul in adhan and hadr in iqama
The text says 'ويترسلُ في الأذان، ويَحدُرُ في الإقامة'. Tarsul means to draw out the words slowly and deliberately; hadr means to hasten the recitation. This is the recommended manner.
The mu'adhdhin should elongate the adhan and recite the iqama quickly.
6
Apply the ruling for missed prayers and purity
If one prayer is missed, call adhan and iqama. If multiple, call adhan only for the first; for the rest, you may call adhan again or suffice with iqama. Purity (wudu) is recommended but not obligatory for adhan.
This shows the flexibility in the school.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the chapter on the adhan from Al-Lubab sharh Al-Quduri. It opens by stating the legal ruling: adhan is sunnah muakkada (an emphasized sunnah) for the five daily prayers and Friday prayer, but not for other prayers like 'Eid or funeral. The description follows: the mu'adhdhin says 'Allahu Akbar' four times, then the two shahadahs, then 'Hayya 'ala al-salah' and 'Hayya 'ala al-falah', and finally 'Allahu Akbar' and 'La ilaha illa Allah'. He should face the qibla, and when saying 'as-salah' and 'al-falah', he turns his face to the right and left respectively. For a missed prayer, both adhan and iqama are performed. If multiple prayers are missed, he calls adhan for the first and has a choice for the rest. It is preferable to be in a state of wudu, but it is permissible without.

ترجيع (tarji'): Repeating the two shahadahs softly before saying them aloud; not practiced in the Hanafi school.
ترسل (tarsul): To draw out the words slowly and deliberately in the adhan.
حدر (hadr): To hasten the iqama, reciting quickly without drawing out.

The Opening Takbir: Dispute on its Wording
Presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on the wording of the opening takbir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissible wordings for the opening takbir and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifah and Muhammad allow any Arabic exaltation; Abu Yusuf insists on 'Allahu Akbar'.

The passage from Al-Lubab presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: Can the opening takbir be said with other Arabic phrases like 'Allahu Ajal' or 'Allahu A'zam'? This lesson unpacks both positions, their bases, and the operative ruling in the school. Understanding this dispute sharpens our appreciation of how Hanafi imams derive rulings from the same sources.

Guided Path
1
Locate the dispute in the text
Find the sentence: 'فإن قال بدلاً من التكبير: الله أجل، أو : أعظم، أو : الرحمن أكبر: أجزأه عند أبي حنيفة ومحمد. وقال أبو يوسف : لا يجزئه إلا بلفظ التكبير.' This is the core of the dispute.
The phrase 'بدلاً من' indicates substitution of the standard wording.
2
Summarize the two positions
Abu Hanifah and Muhammad: any Arabic expression that glorifies Allah (like 'Allahu Ajal', 'Allahu A'zam', 'al-Rahman Akbar') suffices. Abu Yusuf: only the exact phrase 'Allahu Akbar' is valid.
Abu Yusuf's view is more restrictive.
3
Identify the underlying proofs
Abu Hanifah and Muhammad: the essence of takbir is glorification; the word 'Akbar' is not a fixed condition. Abu Yusuf: the Prophet's consistent practice establishes a precise wording; deviation breaks the link to the transmitted act.
Both rely on different usul principles.
4
Determine the practical difference (thamarah)
A person who says 'Allahu Ajal' in the opening takbir: according to Abu Hanifah and Muhammad, his prayer is valid; according to Abu Yusuf, it is invalid and must be repeated.
This affects actual prayer validity.
5
State the mufta bihi and its basis
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifah and Muhammad, due to the principle of ease (taysir) and the focus on meaning over form. The tarjih is by later Hanafi masters like al-Marghinani.
In fatwa, the broader permissibility is adopted.

No Fixed Surah Is Obligatory in Prayer
Ruling that no specific surah is obligatory in prayer and that fixing one surah is disliked.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that no specific surah is obligatory in any prayer and identify the disliked practice of fixing a surah.

Core Idea
No specific surah is required in prayer; habitual fixation on one surah is disliked.

Many Muslims assume that certain surahs must be recited in specific prayers, such as Surah al-Fatihah in every rak'ah or al-Ikhlas in witr. This passage clarifies that no particular surah is ever obligatory for any prayer. However, it warns against a habit that can lead to misunderstanding: consistently reciting the same surah for a given prayer to the exclusion of all others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling
The passage first states a negation: there is no prayer in which reciting a specific surah is required such that nothing else suffices. In other words, any surah or set of verses after al-Fatihah is valid.
This does not negate the obligation to recite some Quran after al-Fatihah in the first two rak'ahs of fard prayers; it only negates a fixed surah.
2
Recognize the disliked practice

This passage from Al-Lubab presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about posture in nafl prayer. Forgetting the Last Sitting and Adding a Fifth Rak'ah
Rules for forgetting last sitting and mistakenly standing for fifth rak'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a person forgets the last sitting and stands for a fifth rak'ah, and identify whether the prayer becomes nafl or can be corrected with sujood al-sahw.

Core Idea
Forgetting the last sitting and prostrating in a fifth rak'ah turns fard into nafl.

This lesson deals with a common mistake in prayer: forgetting the final tashahhud and standing for an extra rak'ah. The Hanafi school provides clear guidance on whether the prayer can be saved or becomes supererogatory. You will learn the two main scenarios and the correct remedial actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling category
The act is forgetting the last sitting (al-qa'dah al-akhirah), which is a wajib in Hanafi fiqh. Its omission triggers sujood al-sahw.
The last sitting is not a rukn; its omission does not invalidate the prayer if corrected.
2
Determine the stage of the extra rak'ah
Has the person performed a prostration (sajdah) in the fifth rak'ah? This is the key differentiator. If no prostration has occurred, the extra rak'ah can be cancelled. If a prostration has occurred, the fard becomes nafl.
The text uses 'قيد' which likely means 'performed' or 'completed' a prostration.
3
Apply the corresponding ruling
If no prostration: return to sitting, cancel the fifth, and perform sujood al-sahw. If prostration occurred: the fard prayer is invalid and becomes nafl; add a sixth rak'ah to make an even number, then perform sujood al-sahw.
In the case where the person sat for tashahhud but mistakenly stood for the fifth, the ruling is similar except that after returning, he says salam and then sujood al-sahw.

Commentary
This passage addresses a common scenario: forgetting the final tashahhud and mistakenly standing for a fifth rak'ah. There are two cases. Case one: The person forgets the last sitting entirely and stands for fifth. If he remembers before prostrating in the fifth, he returns to sitting, cancels the fifth rak'ah (it is as if it did not happen), completes his prayer with salam, and performs sujood al-sahw. If he prostrates in the fifth, his fard prayer is invalidated and becomes a nafl (supererogatory) prayer. He must then add a sixth rak'ah to make it an even number (since nafl is prayed in pairs) and perform sujood al-sahw. If he has prostrated, he adds another rak'ah (making it six total), and his prayer is considered complete, with the extra two rak'ahs as nafl. In both cases, sujood al-sahw is performed.
