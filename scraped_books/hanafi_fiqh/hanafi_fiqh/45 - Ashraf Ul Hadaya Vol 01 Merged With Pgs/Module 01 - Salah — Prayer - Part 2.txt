Traveler's Adhan and Iqamah: Ruling and Proof
Ruling on traveler’s adhan and iqamah with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a traveler's adhan and iqamah, identify the proof, and apply the distinction between adhan and iqamah.

Core Idea
The traveler may suffice with iqamah alone; adhan is not required because companions are already present.

When traveling, the call to prayer (adhan) and the second call (iqamah) serve different purposes. The hadith instructs travelers to give both, but the Hanafi school examines the underlying wisdom to determine the precise ruling. This passage from al-Hidayah presents the rule, its proof, and the legal reasoning for the permissibility of iqamah alone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What act is being ruled upon? What is the hukm level (sunnah muakkadah, makruh, etc.)?
The act is the traveler's performance of adhan and iqamah.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the hadith cited. Identify its source and chain of transmission.
The hadith is addressed to the sons of Abu Mulayka and also to Malik ibn Huwayrith.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the dalil prove the ruling? The imperative form indicates a command; why is it not wujub?
The permissibility of iqamah alone downgrades the command to sunnah.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools view adhan as obligatory for the traveler. How does the Hanafi school respond?
Hanafis distinguish based on the purpose: adhan summons the absent, iqamah announces commencement.
5
Apply to a scenario
Given a traveler with limited time, what is the minimum required? What is the better practice?
Minimum: iqamah only. Better: both adhan and iqamah.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the traveler's obligation regarding adhan and iqamah. The core ruling from the Hidayah is that a traveler should say both adhan and iqamah. The proof is the hadith of the Prophet to the sons of Abu Mulayka and also to Malik ibn Huwayrith. If the traveler leaves both, it is makruh tanzihi (mildly disliked) because it contradicts the command. If he only gives iqamah without adhan, it is permissible because the purpose of adhan—to gather those absent—is already fulfilled since the fellow travelers are all present. However, iqamah remains needed to signal the start of prayer. The commentary also cites additional hadith about the great reward for those who give adhan and iqamah even alone, showing that adhan is not merely a notification but also a means of exalting Allah's name. The author then restates the ruling, explaining that the dislike for leaving both is due to opposing the explicit sunnah.

Key Terms
اذان (adhan): The call to prayer announcing the entrance of the prayer time, used to summon those who are absent.
اقامت (iqamah): The second call immediately before the prayer starts, announcing the commencement of the prayer.
مسافر (musafir): A traveler, defined in Hanafi fiqh as one who intends a journey of three days (approx. 48 miles).

City Dweller Praying at Home: Adhan and Iqamah
Ruling on adhan and iqamah for a person praying at home in the city

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on adhan and iqamah for a city dweller praying at home and explain why it is permissible to omit them using the proof of Ibn Mas'ud.

Core Idea
The quarter's adhan suffices; the mu'adhdhin is your deputy.

When living in a city with an established mosque, the call to prayer is a communal act. This lesson examines whether a person praying alone at home must repeat the adhan and iqamah. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling based on the practice of the Companion Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, balancing the sunnah of imitating congregation with the practical sufficiency of the quarter's adhan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what is being ruled upon: a person praying at home in the city. State the recommended action and the permissible alternative.
The matn says 'he should pray with adhan and iqamah... if he leaves both, it is permissible.'
2
Locate the dalil
Find the proof cited: the statement of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, 'The adhan of the quarter suffices us.' This is a qawl sahabi (companion's statement) used as evidence.
The proof is from the companion's practice, not a direct Quranic or prophetic command.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Understand how the proof applies: the mu'adhdhin is appointed by the residents, so his adhan legally represents them. Thus, omitting adhan at home does not violate the sunnah.
The commentary explains that the mu'adhdhin acts as a deputy (na'ib) for the community.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Recognize that other schools may require adhan for every prayer, but the Hanafis differentiate based on the presence of a communal mu'adhdhin.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is relied upon (mufta bihi) because of the sound basis in companion practice and the legal principle of deputyship.
There is no intra-Hanafi dispute; the position is from zahir al-riwayah.

If he prays in his house in the city, he should pray with adhan and iqamah so that the performance is in the form of congregation; and if he leaves both, it is permissible because of the statement of Ibn Mas'ud: 'The adhan of the quarter suffices us.' Then if in the city he wants to pray in his house, he should still pray with adhan and iqamah, whether alone or in congregation, so that the prayer performance is in the form of congregation. And if he leaves both, it is permissible. The proof is that once Abdullah ibn Mas'ud led Alqamah and Aswad in prayer without adhan and iqamah. The reason is that the mu'adhdhin is the deputy of the people of the quarter in adhan and iqamah because they appointed him for that task. So whoever prays in the quarter without adhan and iqamah in reality is considered to have prayed with them legally, so there is no dislike. In contrast, the traveler, when he prays alone without adhan and iqamah, leaves both in reality and legally. So he is both a real and figurative abandoner of congregation, and abandoning congregational prayer is disliked. Similarly, abandoning the resemblance of congregation is disliked.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof for a person praying at home in the city (i.e., within a town or city where there is an established adhan from the mosque). The matn states that such a person should still say adhan and iqamah to imitate the form of congregation, even if praying alone. However, it is permissible to leave both, based on the practice of Ibn Mas'ud who led prayer without adhan and iqamah and said the quarter's adhan suffices. The commentary explains the reasoning: the mu'adhdhin of the quarter acts as a deputy for all residents. Thus, when a resident prays without adhan and iqamah, he is legally considered to have performed them because the mu'adhdhin fulfilled the duty on behalf of the community. Therefore, there is no dislike. This is contrasted with the traveler, who has no such deputy; hence, leaving both is makruh for him because he abandons the sunnah of congregational form entirely.

Key Terms
اذان الحى (adhan al-hayy): The adhan given by the mu'adhdhin of the locality or quarter (mahallah) which serves as a collective call.
تارك جماعت (tarik jama'at): One who abandons congregational prayer; in this context, referring to the traveler who leaves both adhan and iqamah, thereby abandoning the semblance of congregation.

Introduction to the Conditions of Prayer (Shurut al-Salah)
Chapter heading introducing conditions of prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the conditions (shurut), pillars (arkan), and obligations (wajibat) of Salah in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Conditions precede prayer; pillars constitute it; obligations perfect it.

This chapter opens with a clear distinction: the conditions that must be in place before Salah begins. In Hanafi fiqh, understanding this tripartite division is essential. We will explore the three categories that scholars use to structure the rules of prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
From the chapter heading and commentary, note that the author will discuss 'shurut' (conditions), 'arkan' (pillars), and 'wajibat' (obligations). Recognize that these are distinct legal concepts.
The Urdu commentary mentions 'three words'—likely shurut, arkan, wajibat.
2
Define 'condition' (shart)
A condition is a prerequisite that must be fulfilled before the prayer begins. If missing, the prayer is invalid from the start. Examples: purity, covering the awrah, facing the qiblah, intention.
Conditions exist outside the prayer itself.
3
Define 'pillar' (rukn)
A pillar is an integral part of the prayer performed during the act. Omitting a pillar invalidates the prayer and requires a full repetition. Examples: standing (qiyam), bowing (ruku), prostrating (sajdah).
Pillars cannot be compensated by prostration of forgetfulness.
4
Define 'obligation' (wajib)
An obligation is a required element that, if omitted, does not invalidate the prayer but requires compensation (sajdah al-sahw). Examples: reciting Surah al-Fatihah, sitting for tashahhud.
This is a distinct Hanafi category; others may consider these as pillars.
5
Apply the distinction
Given an action, classify it as condition, pillar, or obligation. For instance, performing wudu is a condition, bowing is a pillar, reciting al-Fatihah is an obligation.
Practice strengthens understanding of the boundaries.

The 'Awrah of a Man: Navel to Knee - Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Defines man's 'awrah and refutes Shafi'i position on navel and knee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a man's 'awrah, explain the hadith evidence, and evaluate the Shafi'i objection regarding the inclusion of the knee.

Core Idea
The knee is part of a man's 'awrah; the navel is not.

In this passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, the Hanafi position on a man's 'awrah is defined and defended against the Shafi'i view. The key dispute revolves around the interpretation of the preposition 'ila' (to) in the primary hadith. By examining a second narration and an explicit prophetic statement, the Hanafi school demonstrates that the knee is included and the navel is excluded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
State clearly that the Hanafi 'awrah for a man is from below the navel to the knee, including the knee but not the navel.

2
Examine the primary hadith and the Shafi'i objection
Read the first hadith: 'The 'awrah of a man is what is between his navel and his knee.' The word 'ila' (to) suggests a limit. The Shafi'i school argues that a limit excludes the limit itself, so the knee is not 'awrah and the navel is 'awrah because it is the starting point.

The combination of two hadiths and an explicit statement strengthens the Hanafi position.

Proof of Obligation to Cover the 'Awrah
Objection and response on proof strength

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers covering the 'awrah fard despite the hadith being khabar wahid, by applying the principle of combining qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala.

Core Idea
Two zanni proofs can combine to yield certainty for obligation.

This passage presents a common objection: the verse (7:31) only proves obligation for tawaf, and the hadith is a single-narrator report that cannot establish a binding ruling. The Hanafi response demonstrates a key usul principle: two pieces of evidence, each strong in one aspect, can together provide the certainty needed for an obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's two prongs
The objector argues that the verse is limited to tawaf and the hadith is khabar wahid, so neither proves obligation.
2
Analyze the verse: transmission and meaning
The verse (Qur'an) is qat'i al-thubut (certain transmission), but its imperative 'khudhu' could be for tawaf or prayer, making it zanni al-dalala.
In Hanafi usul, the Qur'an is qat'i al-thubut; its meaning may be open to interpretation.
3
Analyze the hadith: transmission and meaning
The hadith is khabar wahid, thus zanni al-thubut, but it contains a tool of restriction (adat al-hasr) that gives it qat'i al-dalala (clear, decisive meaning).
Khabar wahid can have qat'i dalala if the wording is unambiguous.
4
Combine the two proofs
The verse provides certainty in transmission; the hadith provides certainty in meaning. Together they compensate for each other's weakness and establish the obligation (fard) of covering 'awrah.
This is a distinctive Hanafi principle: combining zanni proofs to reach qat'.

Defines free woman's 'awrah and discusses feet dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a free woman's 'awrah, identify the proof, and explain the preferred view regarding the feet.

Core Idea
A free woman's 'awrah is all except face and hands; the foot is not 'awrah per the preferred Hanafi view.

This passage from the Hidayah defines the 'awrah of a free woman and then addresses a debate within the Hanafi school about whether her feet are included. Understanding this ruling is essential for ensuring the validity of salah and proper conduct.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and exception
Read the first line: 'And the entire body of a free woman is 'awrah except her face and her palms.' This is the foundational ruling. Note that the exception is limited to face (wajh) and hands (kaffayn) up to the wrists.
The term 'palms' includes the backs of the hands as well, but not the forearms.
2
Analyze the proof and the principle of necessity
The hadith 'al-mar'atu 'awrah' is cited. The exception of face and hands is justified by necessity (ibtila') in daily transactions and interactions. Understand that the proof is zanni in transmission and meaning, allowing for exceptions based on custom and necessity.
The hadith is mentioned in variant forms; the author of Hidayah adds the word 'masturah' which some scholars did not find in original narrations.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on the feet and the tarjih
The text states that the matn's wording indicates the foot is 'awrah (since only face and hands are excepted). However, a narration from Abu Hanifa via Imam Hasan states that the foot is not 'awrah. The passage then declares this narration as 'asahh' (more correct), and this is the relied-upon position. Note the reasoning: if the face, which provokes greater desire, is not 'awrah, then the foot a fortiori is not 'awrah.
Imam al-Karkhi also supported this view. The dispute is a valid ikhtilaf within the school.

Exposure of a Woman's Shin in Prayer: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Determining the threshold of shin exposure that invalidates prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on the threshold of shin exposure that requires repetition of prayer, and evaluate their respective evidence.

Core Idea
A quarter of a woman's shin exposed invalidates prayer according to Abu Hanifa; half according to Abu Yusuf.

In Hanafi fiqh, covering the awrah during prayer is wajib. But what if a small part of a woman's shin becomes uncovered? Does she have to repeat the prayer? This lesson explores a famous intra-Hanafi dispute between the founding Imams on the minimum amount of exposure that triggers invalidity. By examining the passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, you will understand how each Imam defined 'much' and 'little' and the reasoning behind their thresholds.

Guided Path
1
State the two competing thresholds
Identify the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (quarter) and that of Abu Yusuf (half). Note that for Abu Yusuf there are two narrations regarding exactly half.

Analyze Abu Yusuf's evidence
Abu Yusuf argues that 'much' and 'little' are relative terms – something is called 'much' only when the remaining part is smaller. Thus, half is the natural dividing line.
This is a linguistic argument based on common usage.
3
Analyze the evidence of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They analogize from other rulings: a quarter is treated as significant in masah of the head and shaving during ihram. Also, seeing a quarter of a face is considered seeing the whole face.
This is qiyas (analogy) using established precedents in fiqh.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah)
If exactly a quarter is exposed, Abu Hanifa requires repetition but Abu Yusuf does not; if half or more, all agree.
The mufta bihi (operative position) is the quarter threshold, adopted by later Hanafi scholars.

Commentary
This passage discusses the intra-Hanafi dispute on the amount of shin exposure that requires repetition of prayer for a free woman. Intra-Hanafi dispute on quarter vs half exposure

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A quarter of any separate limb exposed in prayer invalidates it – the mufta bihi from the Sahibayn.

This lesson examines an important intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: when does exposure of hair, belly, or thigh during prayer invalidate the prayer? The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) set the threshold at one-quarter of a limb, while Imam Abu Yusuf has a conflicting narration requiring half. Understanding this dispute clarifies the precise conditions for covering the 'awrah in prayer.

Guided Path
1

This narration weakens the threshold but is not the relied-upon view.

This is a practical consequence affecting whether a prayer needs to be repeated.
5
Determine the tarjih
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that quarter exposure invalidates, as this is the view of the two companions and the author of Hidayah.

The 'Awrah of a Slave Woman: Hanafi Ruling and Evidence
Ruling delineating the 'awrah of a slave woman as distinct from a free woman, with evidence from Umar's practice and juristic reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the 'awrah of a slave woman according to the Hanafi school and identify the evidence from Umar's practice and the principle of hardship removal.

Core Idea
Slave woman's 'awrah = navel to knee plus belly/back; head and limbs not 'awrah due to necessity.

This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling on the 'awrah of a slave woman, which is distinct from that of a free woman. The ruling is supported by the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab (a qawl sahabi) and the juristic reasoning that slave women face hardship if required to cover fully. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rules of covering in prayer and in front of non-mahram men.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the 'awrah of a slave woman according to the Hanafi school? The text states it is the same as a man's (navel to knee) plus the belly and back.
The belly and back are added because they are places of desire.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The primary evidence is the saying of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) to a slave woman named Difar: 'Remove from yourself the donkey [covering], O Difar! Do you wish to resemble free women?' This indicates that covering the head is not required for a slave woman.
This is a qawl sahabi, a statement of a Companion, which is a source of law in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Umar's command to uncover her head and his prohibition of imitating free women show that the head of a slave woman is not 'awrah. Free women cover their heads; since she is not to resemble them, her head is not 'awrah.
This is a clear indication (dalalah) from the wording.
4
Note the underlying reasoning (ta'lil) and qiyas
The ruling is also supported by the principle of removing hardship (raf' al-haraj). Slave women customarily go out for their master's needs in work clothes, so requiring full cover would cause hardship. Their situation is analogized (qiyas) to mahram women, who have relaxed 'awrah in front of non-mahram men.
The 'illah (effective cause) is the frequent need to go out for work, creating hardship.

A female slave's 'awrah is navel to knee plus belly and back; the rest, including head and arms, is not 'awrah.

The passage also refutes a potential objection from the hadith of Imran ibn Husayn ('Pray standing, and if unable, then sitting'), explaining that the naked person is legally unable to stand because covering his 'awrah takes priority. Thus, sitting with gestures is the preferred method, and standing is a valid but lesser option.

Timing of Niyyah: Before, With, or After Takbir
Timing rules and intra-Hanafi dispute on niyyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on the timing of niyyah in salah and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding intention after takbir.

Core Idea
Niyyah must be present at or before takbir; intention after takbir is invalid per the relied-upon position.

While the necessity of niyyah is established, its precise timing is crucial. This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling: intention should be concurrent with the opening takbir (tahrimah) or immediately before, provided no invalidating act intervenes. We also examine the dissenting view of Imam Karkhi, who allows intention after takbir, and contrast the ease of fasting with the wakefulness of prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ideal timing: concurrent with takbir
The original ruling is that intention must be at the same time as the takbir. Understand why simultaneity is the standard.
2
Understand the concession for prior intention
If intention is made before takbir, it is valid as long as no action contrary to prayer (e.g., eating) occurs between them. Examine the example of intending during wudu.
3
Learn the invalidity of post-takbir intention
If intention is made after the takbir, the parts before intention are not worship, and the rest depends on them, so the whole prayer is invalid. This is the relied-upon view.
4
Examine Imam Karkhi's view
Imam Karkhi holds that intention after takbir is valid, but with disagreement on how long after. The text does not adopt this view.
5
Contrast prayer with fasting
The passage compares prayer with fasting: for fasting, intention can be delayed until after fajr due to hardship (sleep), but prayer begins in wakefulness, so no hardship in requiring intention at the start.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The follower must intend both the prayer and following the imam; otherwise, the prayer is invalid.

In congregational prayer, the follower (muqtadi) does not simply mimic the imam; he must consciously commit to following him. This lesson examines the ruling from Ashraf al-Hidayah that the muqtadi must include in his intention not only the specific prayer but also the act of following the imam. The reason given is that the follower's prayer is linked to the imam's — if the imam's prayer becomes invalid, so does the follower's. Therefore, the follower must willingly accept this connection through intention.

This linking principle is key to Hanafi congregational rulings.

When fear prevents facing the qibla, any direction suffices — the obligation lifts when the excuse is certain.

Ijtihad for Qibla When Uncertain
Explains the obligation of ijtihad (taharrī) when the qibla is uncertain and no knowledgeable person is present.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions requiring taharrī and explain why it is wajib when no knowledgeable person is present.

Core Idea
When qibla is unclear and no one to ask, wajib to exert effort (taharrī); Taqrir of Companions proves validity.

Imagine being in an unfamiliar place, unsure of the qibla direction, and no one around to guide you. What should you do? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling: you must perform ijtihad (taharrī) to determine the direction based on available signs. This lesson explains the obligation, its evidence from the Sunnah, and the priority of asking over personal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
The text describes a person for whom the qibla is unclear (mushtabah) and no one is present to ask. The ruling: he must perform ijtihad (taharrī).
Taharrī means to exert effort to form a strong belief (ghalabat al-zann) about the qibla.
2
Locate the evidence (dalil)
The author cites the practice of the Companions: they made taharrī, prayed, and the Prophet (peace be upon him) did not disapprove. This tacit approval (taqrir) is a proof.
The exact dalil: 'أَنَّ الصَّحَابَةَ تَحَرُوا وَصَلُّوا وَلَمْ يُنْكِرُ عَلَيْهِم رَسُولُ اللهِ'
3
Understand the legal principle
The underlying principle: acting upon an apparent proof (dalil zahir) is obligatory when no stronger proof exists. Taharrī is an apparent proof, but inquiry (istikhbar) is stronger.
Thus, if someone knowledgeable is present, inquiry must be made before taharrī.
4
Apply the ruling to practice
In the absence of a knowledgeable person, taharrī is wajib. The prayer prayed based on taharrī is valid, even if later the actual qibla is found to be different.
If a person prays without taharrī, the prayer is invalid.

When Qibla Error is Discovered After Prayer: The Hanafi Principle of Capacity
Presents the Hanafi position—no repetition required—and the Shafi'i opposition with response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on repetition after taharrī and explain the principle of taklif bi qadr al-wus' that supports it.

Core Idea
Obligation is limited to capacity: if you did your best to find qibla, your prayer is valid even if wrong.

This lesson examines a classic dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the validity of a prayer performed after ijtihad (taharrī) when the worshipper later discovers he faced the wrong direction. The Hanafi school, relying on the principle that obligation is limited to capacity (التكليف بقدر الوسع), rules that the prayer is valid and no repetition is required. Understanding this principle is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to unavoidable uncertainty in worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Note the specific situation: a person is uncertain about the qibla, performs taharrī (ijtihad) to determine direction, prays, and later discovers he made an error in his ijtihad.

The Hanafi school says: the prayer is valid and does not need to be repeated. This is the position of the text: 'لا يُعيدها' (he does not repeat it).

The proof is the principle 'التكليف بقدر الوسع' (obligation is limited to capacity). Since the person had no way to know the actual qibla, his obligation was only to face the direction of his ijtihad. He fulfilled that, so the prayer is complete.

Imam al-Shafi'i argues that certainty of error (يقين بالخطأ) invalidates the prayer, and repetition is required. He considers that facing the correct qibla is a condition that must be fulfilled definitively.
5
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response is that the legal qibla for the praying person at that time was the direction he determined through ijtihad. He had no capacity to do more, so the obligation was satisfied. The later discovery does not retroactively invalidate the prayer.
This response highlights the Hanafi emphasis on ease and capacity in the law.

Validity of Following Imam with Differing Taharri Directions
Ruling with conditions on valid congregation with differing qibla directions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a follower's prayer is valid or invalid when the imam and followers face different directions after performing taharri on a dark night.

Core Idea
A follower's prayer is valid if they do not know the imam's direction, even if they face a different direction after their own taharri.

In a dark night where the qibla is unknown, both the imam and his followers must perform taharri (independent judgement) to determine the direction. This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a specific scenario: each follower prays in his own direction, unaware of the imam's choice. The key question is whether such a congregation is valid. The Hanafi ruling balances the obligation to face the qibla with the practical difficulty of coordination, drawing an analogy with prayer inside the Kaaba.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Read the description: a dark night, no one to ask about qibla, imam and followers each perform taharri and face different directions. The followers are all behind the imam and do not know what direction the imam chose.
This is the default case—followers unaware of the imam's direction.
2
State the Ruling for the Unaware Follower
The text says their prayer is valid (اجز). The reason given is that each faced the direction of their own taharri, and the difference in direction does not prevent validity. This is analogous to praying inside the Kaaba where each person faces a different internal direction.
Analogy (qiyas) with jawf al-Ka'bah: the building itself is qibla, so direction is not an issue.
3
Identify the Invalidating Condition: Knowledge
If a follower knows the imam's direction (e.g., knows he faced east) but still prays in his own direction, the prayer becomes invalid (تفسد). The reason: he considered his imam mistaken, which violates the required respect and unity in congregation.
Knowledge (علم) changes the ruling—ignorance is excused.
4
Identify the Invalidating Condition: Going Ahead
Even if the follower does not know the imam's direction, if he stands ahead of the imam (متقدما), his prayer becomes invalid. This is because standing behind or level with the imam is a requirement (فرض المقام) for the follower in congregational prayer.
This condition is independent of the direction issue.
5
Apply the Ruling: Fatwa Position
The mufta bihi (operative position) is as stated. This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is relied upon in the Hanafi school. The conditions are clear: valid if unaware and behind; invalid if aware or ahead.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is recorded for this specific case.

Turning Toward Qibla During Prayer After Realizing Error
Ruling on turning to correct qibla within prayer if ijtihad changes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on turning to the correct qibla during prayer and explain the dalil from the Quba incident.

Core Idea
If your ijtihad on qibla changes during prayer, you must turn immediately without breaking the prayer; delay invalidates it.

In prayer, facing the qibla is a condition of validity. But what if you realize you were facing the wrong direction after starting? This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling based on a famous incident from the time of the Prophet ﷺ, teaching us when and how to correct direction within prayer without restarting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is turning to the correct qibla during prayer after realizing an error or change in ijtihad. The ruling is that it is wajib to turn immediately.
The ruling is based on the remaining part of the prayer needing to be in the correct direction.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of the people of Quba who, while in ruku', turned to the Kaaba upon hearing of the qibla change. The Prophet ﷺ approved their action.
This hadith is found in Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's approval (taqrir) indicates permissibility. Since the remaining prayer must be correct, turning becomes obligatory (wajib) to fulfill that condition.
Hanafi usul: The Prophet's approval is a binding proof (sunnah).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that if an error in qibla is discovered after completing part of the prayer, the entire prayer is invalid and must be repeated.
The difference stems from whether facing qibla is a condition for every part of prayer or can be corrected.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school relies on the explicit taqrir from the Quba incident, which allows correction without breaking the prayer. This is the mufta bihi position.
No intra-Hanafi disagreement on this ruling.

Congregational Prayer with Differing Qibla Directions
Ruling on congregation with ijtihad-based qibla differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a follower's prayer is valid when qibla directions differ due to ijtihad, and identify the two factors that invalidate it.

Core Idea
When both imam and followers independently determine qibla, the prayer is valid as long as the follower does not know the imam's direction and does not go ahead.

Imagine leading prayer on a dark night where you cannot see landmarks. This lesson examines a specific Hanafi ruling: if the imam and each follower independently determine the qibla direction, resulting in different orientations, the congregation remains valid. The key is that each person has fulfilled the obligation of facing the qibla to the best of their ability, and the difference does not break the unity of prayer, provided certain conditions are met.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that the ruling applies to a situation where both imam and followers are uncertain of qibla and each performs ijtihad individually on a dark night, without knowing the other's direction.
The scenario assumes the followers are behind the imam and do not know which direction he faces.
2
State the Ruling and Conditions
The prayer of all is valid (sahih) because each faced their ijtihad. The two essential conditions are: (1) followers are behind the imam, (2) followers do not know the imam's direction.
If these conditions are met, difference in direction does not harm the congregation.
3
Identify the Invalidating Factors
Two specific factors invalidate a follower's prayer: (1) knowing the imam's direction, because then the follower believes the imam is in error; (2) stepping ahead of the imam, which abandons the obligation of standing behind (fard al-maqam).
Both factors are independent; either one invalidates.
4
Analyze the Analogy with Inside the Kaaba
The ruling is analogized to prayer inside the Kaaba, where followers can face different directions around the imam and the prayer remains valid. This shows that difference in orientation is not inherently a preventer.
The analogy reinforces the principle that facing the qibla (the Kaaba) is fulfilled, even if directions vary.
5
Examine Potential Objections
The commentary addresses why followers might not know the imam's direction despite audible recitation: it could be a qada prayer, the imam forgot to recite loudly, or there was fear of enemies. These explanations preserve the ruling's applicability.
Understanding this objection and its resolution strengthens the legal reasoning.

And whoever leads a people in a dark night and he determines the qibla by ijtihad and prays toward the east, and those behind him also make ijtihad and each of them prays toward a direction, and all of them are behind him and they do not know what the imam did, then their prayer is valid because they have faced the direction of their ijtihad, and this difference is not a preventer just as in the case inside the Kaaba. And whoever among them knows the situation of his imam, his prayer becomes invalid because he considers his imam to be in error. Explanation of the scenario: A person led a congregation in a dark night, whether in a house or in the wilderness or without anyone present from whom to ask the direction of qibla. So the imam made ijtihad and determined the qibla direction and prayed facing east. And those behind the imam also made ijtihad, and each of them prayed facing a direction, i.e., whichever direction his ijtihad led him to, and all of them are behind the imam. No one is ahead of the imam, whether they know or do not know, except they know that the imam is ahead, but they do not know what the imam did i.e., which direction he faced. If it is objected that the prayer is at night, so why do they not know from the loud voice? The answer is that perhaps it is a makeup prayer (qada), or he forgot to recite loudly, or from the voice they only knew that the imam is ahead but could not know his direction, or due to fear of enemy or bandits the imam did not recite loudly and all tried to be quiet. In any case, in this situation their prayer is valid because they faced the direction of their ijtihad, and that is what is necessary. As for the difference that the imam faces one direction and the congregation faces another, that is not a preventer just as in the issue of the interior of the Kaaba. Because according to us, obligatory and supererogatory prayer inside the Kaaba is valid. So when people pray congregationally inside the Kaaba and follow the imam around him, then the one who turns his back to the imam's back or faces his face to the imam's back, his prayer is valid. And the one who faces his face to the imam's face also valid but disliked if there is no screen between them. But the one who puts his back to the imam's face, his prayer is not valid. And the one who is to the imam's right or left, his prayer is valid provided that no one in the row facing the wall is closer to the wall than the imam. And among those followers, the one who knows the state of his imam, his prayer is invalid because he considers the direction he ijtihad-ed to be correct and others wrong, so following an imam whom he believes to be in error is not valid. Similarly, if the follower goes ahead of the imam, his prayer is also invalid because he abandoned the obligation of the place (i.e., standing in the proper position relative to the imam).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement with explanatory commentary on a specific scenario: congregational prayer on a dark night when both the imam and each follower independently determine the qibla direction through ijtihad, resulting in different directions. The author rules that the prayers of all are valid as long as two conditions are met: (1) the followers do not know which direction the imam is facing, and (2) no follower is ahead of the imam. The proof by analogy is the case of praying inside the Kaaba, where followers may face different directions relative to the imam but the prayer is still valid because each is facing the Kaaba. In the Hanafi school, the interior of the Kaaba is a valid place for prayer, and the direction for those inside is to face any wall, so differences in direction do not invalidate congregation. The author explains that if a follower knows the imam's direction and considers it wrong, his prayer is invalid because he believes the imam to be in error, which contradicts the validity of following him. Similarly, if a follower goes ahead of the imam, that breaks the required positioning and invalidates the prayer. The commentary addresses potential objections (e.g., why didn't they hear the imam's recitation?) and provides possible reasons. This ruling applies specifically to situations where each person is obligated to do ijtihad due to lack of knowledge of qibla, such as on a dark night without means to determine direction.

مقتدی (muqtadi): A follower in congregational prayer (ma'mum).

Prayer Inside the Kaaba and Following Imam with Uncertain Direction
Ruling with proof via qiyas to Kaaba interior

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of congregational prayer when the imam and followers face different directions, and apply the Kaaba analogy to identify valid and invalid follower positions.

Core Idea
Differing qibla direction between imam and follower does not invalidate the prayer as long as the follower performed taharri and is not in a prohibited position relative to the imam.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced scenario: what happens when the imam and followers face different directions in prayer due to uncertainty or constraint? The Hanafi school draws a powerful analogy to praying inside the Kaaba itself, where the usual qibla direction is suspended. You will learn the specific conditions that keep the prayer valid and the few cases that invalidate it.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Hanafi school. It addresses two scenarios: (1) when the direction of prayer (qibla) is uncertain and the imam and followers face different directions, and (2) the specific case of praying inside the Kaaba itself. The text begins by discussing a situation where the imam's direction is unknown due to some constraint. The ruling is that the prayer is valid because each person performed taharri (estimation) and faced that direction. The objection that the imam and followers face different directions is not a barrier; the author draws an analogy (qiyas) to the ruling of praying inside the Kaaba. According to the Hanafis, both fard and nafl prayers inside the Kaaba are valid. In congregational prayer inside the Kaaba, followers who position themselves with their backs or faces towards the imam's back are valid. Turning one's face towards the imam's face is valid but makruh if no sutra (screen) is between them. However, turning one's back to the imam's face invalidates the prayer. Followers to the right or left are valid as long as they are not closer to the wall in front of the imam than the imam himself (this would create a disparity in the row). The passage then discusses a separate issue: if a follower knows that the imam is mistaken about the qibla direction, his prayer becomes invalid because he believes the imam to be in error, which contradicts the necessary belief in the imam's correctness for valid iqtida. Similarly, if a follower steps ahead of the imam, the prayer is invalid because he abandons the obligatory station (fardh maqam) of standing in his place. This ruling is based on the general principle that the follower must remain behind the imam. The authority weight of this ruling is from the Hanafi madhhab as explained in the commentary of Hidayah.

Key Terms
تحری (taharri): The act of estimating the direction of the qibla when it is not certain, based on available evidence.
سترة (sutra): An object placed in front of a person praying to prevent passersby from crossing, and to focus attention.
اقتداء (iqtida'): Following the imam in congregational prayer, which requires the follower to adhere to the imam's actions and intentions.
فرض مقام (fardh maqam): The obligation of standing in one's designated place during prayer, specifically that the follower must stand behind the imam.
