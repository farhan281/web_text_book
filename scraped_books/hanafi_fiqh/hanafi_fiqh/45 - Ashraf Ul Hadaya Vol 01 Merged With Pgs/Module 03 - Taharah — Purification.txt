Conditions of Prayer: Taharah and Satr al-'Awrah
Defines conditions of prayer and proves obligation of purity and covering

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ritual purity and covering the private parts as conditions for prayer and identify the proofs from Quran and hadith.

Core Idea
Taharah from hadath and najas and covering the 'awrah are fard conditions for prayer validity.

In this lesson, we examine two fundamental conditions of prayer from the Hanafi perspective: purity (taharah) from ritual and physical impurities, and covering the private parts (satr al-'awrah). The text from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents the evidence from the Quran and hadith, and addresses objections raised about the proof for covering. By the end, you will be able to articulate why these are obligatory and how Hanafi usul combines evidence to establish the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions discussed
Note the passage states two conditions: taharah from hadath (ritual impurity) and najas (physical impurity), and covering the 'awrah. These are conditions of continuance (shart dawam).
The three categories of conditions (in'iqad, dawam, baqa') are mentioned but not elaborated here.
2
Locate the primary proofs
Find the Quran verses: 74:4 (garments purify) and 5:6 (if junub, purify), and 7:31 (adornment at every mosque). Also the hadith: 'No prayer for a menstruating woman without a headcover.'
3
Analyze the evidence
Understand how each proof is used: the command to purify is interpreted as a command to remove impurity (wajib). The word 'adornment' is interpreted as covering the 'awrah, and 'mosque' as prayer. The hadith uses a particle of restriction ('illa) making its meaning definitive.
The command form (amr) in Arabic indicates obligation per Hanafi usul.
4
Address the objection
The passage responds to the objection that the verse 7:31 was revealed about tawaf, not prayer, and that the hadith is a solitary report (khabar wahid) which cannot establish obligation alone.
5
Apply the Hanafi usul solution
The response: the generality of the wording ('every mosque') is applied, not the specific cause (tawaf). The verse is qat'i al-thubut (definitive transmission) but zanni al-dalalah. The hadith is zanni al-thubut but qat'i al-dalalah due to the particle of restriction. Together they establish obligation.
This is a key Hanafi principle: combining a qat'i al-thubut text with a qat'i al-dalalah text yields a fard ruling.

This is a condition according to us, and according to Imam Shafi'i, Imam Ahmad, and the majority of jurists. The proof is the statement of the Exalted: 'Take your adornment at every mosque.' Meaning: take your adornment near every mosque. In the verse, 'adornment' means something that covers the awrah, and 'mosque' means prayer. Now the translation becomes: Take what covers your awrah at every prayer. So from this verse, the obligation of covering the awrah in prayer is established. But it is narrated from Ibn Abbas that this verse was revealed regarding those who used to circumambulate naked, not regarding prayer. So how can the obligation of covering the awrah in prayer be established from this verse? The answer is that the generality of the wording is considered, not the specificity of the cause, and 'at every mosque' is general, with no specification for Masjid al-Haram. The second proof is that the Prophet ﷺ said: 'Allah does not accept the prayer of a menstruating woman (i.e., a mature woman) without a scarf.' Here an objection arises that the verse and hadith presented by the author of Hidayah do not prove the obligation of covering the awrah. Because the verse 'Take your adornment...' is beneficial for obligation in the context of tawaf; indeed, circumambulating naked is valid legally, though sinful. So if it gives the benefit of obligation for prayer, then the word 'khudhu' (take) would be used in both meanings of wajib and fard, which is impermissible because it would necessitate combining the literal and metaphorical meanings. As for the hadith, it is a khabar wahid (solitary report), and a khabar wahid does not give the benefit of obligation. Therefore, this hadith is not beneficial for obligation. The response is that the verse, although not qat'i al-dalalah (definitive in meaning), is qat'i al-thubut (definitive in transmission), and the hadith, due to being khabar wahid, is zanni al-thubut (probable in transmission), but due to the particle of restriction, it is qat'i al-dalalah (definitive in meaning). So through the combination of both, obligation is established.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah begins the Book of Prayer by classifying the conditions (shurut) of prayer into three categories: conditions of commencement (shart in'iqad), conditions of continuance (shart dawam), and conditions of preservation (shart baqa'). The author then focuses on two key conditions: purity (taharah) from ritual impurity (hadath) and physical impurity (najas), and covering the 'awrah (satr al-'awrah). For taharah, the evidence is drawn from the Qur'anic commands 'And your garments purify' (Surah al-Muddaththir, 74:4) which is interpreted as purifying oneself from impurity, and 'And if you are junub, then purify yourselves' (Surah al-Ma'idah, 5:6) which refers to ritual bath. For covering the 'awrah, the verse 'Take your adornment at every mosque' (Surah al-A'raf, 7:31) is cited, where 'adornment' is interpreted as covering the private parts and 'mosque' as prayer. Additionally, a hadith states that Allah does not accept the prayer of a mature woman without a headcover (khimar). The author then addresses an objection: the verse is specifically about tawaf, and the hadith is a solitary report (khabar wahid) which does not establish obligation on its own. The response is that the generality of the wording ('every mosque') is applied, not the specific cause (tawaf). Furthermore, the combination of the verse (definitive transmission, probable meaning) and the hadith (probable transmission, definitive meaning due to the particle of restriction 'illa') together establish the obligation. This is a standard Hanafi usul principle: a zanni al-thubut text with qat'i al-dalalah can combine with a qat'i al-thubut text to produce obligation.

Key Terms
شرط (shart): Something upon which the existence of another thing depends, without being part of it. Here it refers to prerequisites for the validity of prayer.
حدث (hadath): Ritual impurity that requires wudu (minor) or ghusl (major). This text refers to both.
نجس (najas): Physical impurity (e.g., blood, urine) that must be removed from the body and clothes for prayer validity.
عورة ('awrah): Parts of the body that must be covered in prayer. For men, from navel to knee including the knee (as per this text).
خبر واحد (khabar wahid): A hadith transmitted by a single chain of narrators, not reaching tawatur. Its transmission is zanni (probable).

Awrah of a Free Woman Except Face and Hands
Defining awrah of free woman with hadith and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the awrah of a free woman in prayer and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether the foot is included.

Core Idea
A free woman's awrah is everything except face and hands; the foot is not awrah per the more correct Hanafi view.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes a foundational rule for a woman's prayer: covering her awrah is wajib. The author defines the awrah as the entire body except the face and palms, based on the prophetic hadith 'al-mar'atu 'awrah masturah'. The passage then opens an important intra-Hanafi discussion about whether the foot is also awrah, ultimately preferring the narration that it is not. Understanding this ruling helps you grasp how Hanafi jurists balance textual evidence with necessity and internal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is covering the awrah in prayer. The ruling is that covering the awrah is wajib; the awrah for a free woman is defined as her entire body except face and hands.
The exception is based on necessity (al-ibtila').
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith: 'al-mar'atu 'awrah masturah' (the woman is an awrah, concealed). This is an ahad hadith.
The hadith is cited in the matn of al-Hidayah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The predicate 'awrah' indicates that concealment is required; 'masturah' emphasizes that the awrah is something that is covered by obligation. The exception for face and hands is derived from the necessity of exposing them in daily transactions.
Some later scholars argue the hadith is general, but the exception is established by necessity.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The matn implies the foot is awrah because it only excepts face and hands. However, a narration from Abu Hanifa (via Hasan ibn Ziyad) and Imam al-Karkhi holds that the foot is not awrah, using qiyas auli: the face is more attractive yet not awrah, so the foot is even less deserving.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
5
Determine the tarjih
The author of Ashraf al-Hidayah declares the narration that the foot is not awrah as asahh (more correct). This is the mufta bihi position for fatwa.
The tarjih is based on the weight of the narration and the strength of the analogy.

Commentary
This passage establishes the ruling on the awrah of a free woman. The author states that her entire body is awrah except the face and hands (palms). The evidence is the hadith 'al-mar'atu 'awrah masturah' (the woman is an awrah, concealed). The exception for face and hands is due to the general necessity of exposing them in daily transactions and interactions. The author then notes that the matn (text of the Hidayah) explicitly indicates that the foot is awrah, but there is a narration from Imam Hasan from Abu Hanifa that the foot is not awrah, and this latter view is considered the more correct (asahh) position. The commentary also discusses the rationale: the face is not awrah despite its stronger attraction, so the foot is even less likely to be awrah. This is an intra-Hanafi difference, with the relied-upon position being that the foot is not awrah.

Dispute on the Extent of Shin Exposure Invalidating Prayer
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the threshold of limb exposure in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the threshold of limb exposure invalidating prayer, explain Abu Yusuf's differing view and his linguistic proof, and apply the quarter-rule analogy to determine invalidation.

Core Idea
A quarter of a limb uncovered invalidates prayer — the quarter represents the whole in Hanafi coverage law.

This passage addresses an intra-madhhab disagreement: Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad consider exposure of a quarter or more of a woman's limb (e.g., shin) enough to invalidate prayer, while Imam Abu Yusuf sets the threshold at half. Understanding this dispute reveals how the Hanafi school uses relative terms (qalil/kathir) and analogical reasoning (qiyas) to define legal thresholds.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is prayer (salat) with part of a woman's limb uncovered. The ruling is invalidation (fasad) requiring i'adah (repetition) if the exposure exceeds a certain threshold.
Covering the 'awrah is wajib; failure to do so can invalidate the prayer.
2
State the Two Positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: exposure of a quarter or more of a limb (e.g., shin) invalidates. Abu Yusuf: only half or more invalidates; for exactly half, two narrations.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; both are within the school.
3
Examine Abu Yusuf's Linguistic Proof
Abu Yusuf argues that 'little' (qalil) and 'much' (kathir) are relational; something is 'much' only when its counterpart is less. Thus, less than half is 'little' and excused.
He uses the principle of linguistic relativity.
4

Ruling on Hanging Hair Being Part of the Body for Awrah
Objection-response over awrah status of hanging hair.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A part of the body remains part of the awrah even if its washing is waived due to hardship.

Praying with an Impure Garment in Necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding praying in an impure garment when no purifier is available, and state the preferred ruling (afdal) with its reasoning.

Core Idea
When only an impure garment is available, covering the ‘awrah is prioritized over purification because it is required always.

What happens when you have nothing to clean your only garment? This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya addresses a person who only has an impure garment and no means to purify it. Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage divides the case into two: (1) when at least a quarter of the garment is pure, and (2) when the pure part is less than a quarter.
The first scenario has a consensus ruling; the second is disputed.

This is agreed upon by all Hanafi imams.

When the pure part is less than a quarter, Muhammad holds that praying in the impure garment is obligatory (wajib). His proof: praying in it leaves only one obligation (purification of clothing), while praying naked leaves multiple obligations (covering, standing, etc.).

Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf give a choice between praying in the impure garment or naked, but consider the former better (afdal). Preference for the garment is because covering is obligatory always, while purification is only for prayer.
This is the position favored by the commentary.

The mufta bihi (relied upon) position according to Ashraf al-Hidaya is the view of the two shaykhs: one may choose but it is better to pray in the impure garment.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a person who has only an impure garment and no means to purify it. The ruling is given in two scenarios: (1) If at least a quarter of the garment is pure, one must pray in that garment; praying naked is invalid because the pure quarter suffices as a whole. Therefore, they are equivalent in preventing validity, so the person may choose. The preference for the impure garment is because covering one's body is required both in prayer and outside it, while purification is specific to prayer, making it more strongly tied to prayer itself.

Praying Naked When No Garment: Ruling and Preference
Ruling on praying naked without any garment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a person without any garment, explain why sitting with gestures is preferred over standing, and identify the evidence from the Companions' practice.

Core Idea
When no garment is available, praying sitting with gestures is preferred because covering the private parts takes priority over performing pillars fully.

Imagine you have no cloth to cover your body for prayer. What should you do? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling based on the practice of the Prophet's Companions. In this lesson, we will explore the ruling, the reasoning behind the preference for sitting, and the evidence from the early community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the situation and basic ruling
The text addresses a person who has no garment at all (neither pure nor impure). The ruling: he prays naked, sitting, making gestures (īmā’) for bowing and prostration.
The gesture is a head movement indicating ruku' and sujud.
2
Note the alternative and its validity
The text says if he prays standing, it suffices (aǧzāhu). So standing while naked is permitted and the prayer is valid.
This shows flexibility: both methods are legally acceptable.
3
Analyze the preference (afḍal)
The text states the first (sitting) is better (afḍal). Understand why: sitting better conceals the major private parts (‘awrah ghalīẓah).
The reasoning is that covering the ‘awrah is a right of both the prayer and human dignity.
4
Examine the evidence (dalīl)
The proof is the action of the Companions of the Prophet (peace be upon him) as narrated by Anas ibn Malik in the story of a shipwreck.
In Hanafi uṣūl, the practice of a Companion (madhhab al-ṣaḥābī) is a valid source when it does not contradict qiyās.
5
Understand the legal reasoning (ta‘līl)
Covering the ‘awrah is wājib and has no substitute, while gestures (īmā’) are a substitute for the physical pillars when one is unable to perform them. Therefore, the obligation to cover takes priority over performing the pillars fully, making sitting preferable.
This priority explains why sitting is better despite standing fulfilling the pillars.

Ruling for a Naked Person Praying Sitting or Standing
Ruling with proof and preference for sitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a naked person's prayer and explain why sitting is considered superior.

Core Idea
Covering the 'awrah outweighs standing; a naked person may pray sitting or standing, but sitting is better.

What happens when a person loses all clothing and must pray? Does the obligation to stand override the obligation to cover? This lesson examines a Hanafi ruling that prioritizes covering over standing, with evidence from the Companions' practice and istihsan. You will learn the legal reasoning behind the preference for sitting and how the school resolves apparent conflict with the hadith 'Pray standing.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal context
The act is obligatory prayer (salah) performed by a person who is naked (al-'ārī) and has nothing to cover the 'awrah. In normal circumstances, covering and standing are both required. Here, they conflict.
The legal context: covering is a condition (shart) for prayer; standing is a pillar (rukn).
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi school rules that a naked person may pray either sitting or standing. Both are permissible (jā'iz), but sitting is superior (afḍal).
This ruling is based on the principle that covering the 'awrah is more emphatic than the pillar of standing.
3
Examine the primary proof and objection
The hadith 'Pray standing; if not, then sitting' (صَلِّ قَائِمًا فَإِنْ لَمْ تَسْتَطِعْ فَقَاعِدًا) seems to require standing unless physically unable. An objector argues that a naked person is physically able to stand, so must stand.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut; the Hanafi response uses istihsan to override the literal implication.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response: legal inability
The Hanafi response is that the naked person is considered legally unable to stand because standing would expose the 'awrah, which cannot be covered without omitting bowing, prostration, or standing. Thus, he falls under the exception 'if not able' in a hukmī (legal) sense.
This is an application of the principle: al-umūr bi-maqāṣidihā (matters are judged by their objectives).
5
Identify the tarjih (preference) and its two reasons
Sitting is preferred for two reasons: (1) covering the 'awrah is more emphatic than arm movements—nafl prayer on a mount with gestures is valid without standing but never without covering; (2) covering has no substitute, whereas bowing and prostration have gestures as substitute. Abandoning with a substitute is better than abandoning without one.
The principle 'tark ilā khalaf awlā min tark lā ilā khalaf' (abandoning with substitute is better than abandoning without) is decisive.

Both standing and sitting are permissible, but sitting is superior (afdal) for two reasons: (1) covering the 'awrah is more emphatic than maintaining the pillars, as evidenced by the permissibility of gesture in nafl while riding but not without covering; (2) covering has no substitute (khalaf), while pillars have gesture as substitute, and abandoning with substitute is better. The commentary follows the text's order, explaining each argument.

The Condition of Niyyah in Prayer: Basis, Timing, and Quality
Expansion of the ruling on niyyah with proofs and details

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on niyyah in prayer, derive it from the dalil, apply the timing rules, and distinguish the quality requirements for fard vs. nafl.

Core Idea
Intention is the heart's resolve that distinguishes worship from habit, without which prayer is invalid.

Prayer is an act of devotion that begins with standing (qiyam), but standing can be either a habitual posture or a posture of worship. What distinguishes the two? Intention. This passage from al-Hidayah explains why intention (niyyah) is a condition for prayer, how the famous hadith 'innama al-a'malu bi al-niyyat' is interpreted by the Hanafi school, and when exactly the intention must be made. It also clarifies that intention is a matter of the heart, not the tongue, and that for obligatory prayers the specific fard must be identified.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on niyyah
Identify that niyyah is a condition (shart) for the validity of prayer. Note its level (wajib) and that it is required for all prayers, fard and nafl.
The passage calls it a 'shart' (condition). Some Hanafi scholars consider it fard; the mufta bihi is that it is wajib.
2
Analyze the primary proof: the hadith
Examine the hadith 'innama al-a'malu bi al-niyyat'. Understand the Hanafi argument that the implicit word 'hukm' (ruling) is general, covering both worldly validity and otherworldly reward. Then learn the response to the objection that it only refers to reward.
The passage also gives a rational proof: standing is ambiguous, and intention resolves ambiguity.
3
Understand the timing conditions
The optimum is simultaneous intention with takbir. Intention before takbir is valid if no act contradictory to prayer intervenes. Intention after takbir is invalid.
Note the intra-Hanafi difference: Imam al-Karkhi's view that intention after takbir is valid is rejected.
4
Apply the quality requirements
Niyyah is a resolve in the heart (iradah), not verbal utterance. For nafl and sunnah, a general intention to pray suffices. For fard, specify the exact prayer (e.g., Zuhr).
Verbal expression is recommended (sunnah) to help the heart's resolve.
5
Contrast with fasting
Notice the exception: for fasting, intention after the time starts is allowed due to necessity (sleep, heedlessness), but for prayer, intention at the start is required because prayer is begun in wakefulness.
This distinction is based on the principle of removing hardship (raf' al-haraj).

Commentary
This passage is an expansion (sharh) on the conditions of niyyah (intention) for prayer from the Hidayah. The author begins with the base ruling: one must make intention for the specific prayer he is entering, and there must be no gap between the intention and the takbir (opening Allahu Akbar) caused by an action that disrupts prayer. The primary evidence is the famous hadith 'Innama al-a'malu bi al-niyyat' (Actions are by intentions), but the author also provides a rational proof: since standing (qiyam) can be either habitual or worshipful, intention is the distinguishing factor. This passage then addresses a potential objection: the hadith might be interpreted as referring to reward (thawab) rather than validity (sihha). The Hanafi response is that 'hukm' (ruling) is general, encompassing both worldly validity and otherworldly reward. Moreover, since worship is purely for reward, if the reward is lost due to lack of intention, the act itself becomes void. This establishes intention as a condition (shart) for the validity of prayer. After proving the requirement of intention, the author discusses its time. The optimum is simultaneous occurrence with the takbir, but intention before the takbir is valid provided no act contradictory to prayer intervenes. An opinion of Imam Muhammad and the two shaykhs (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) is cited that intention made during ablution suffices if no intervening act occurs. However, intention after the takbir is invalid, as the beginning parts would be without intention. A rare view of Imam al-Karkhi that intention after takbir is valid is mentioned but not relied upon. Finally, the passage covers the quality of intention: it is a resolution in the heart ('irada), not verbal utterance. For supererogatory (nafl) and sunnah prayers, a general intention to pray suffices, but for obligatory (fard) prayers, the specific fard (e.g., Zuhr) must be distinguished because obligations differ. The author concludes by contrasting prayer with fasting, where intention after the time starts is permitted due to necessity (sleep, heedlessness), but prayer being performed in wakefulness requires intention at the start.

Key Terms
نیت (niyyah): The specific intention in the heart to perform a particular act of worship, distinguishing it from mere habit.
تحریمہ (tahrimah): The opening takbir (saying Allahu Akbar) that initiates the prayer and renders worldly actions prohibited.
ارادہ (iradah): Intention as a resolve of the heart; the essence of niyyah is a deliberate aim, not a verbal articulation.
منافی صلوٰۃ (munafi salat): An act that contradicts or nullifies the state of prayer, such as eating, drinking, or talking.
