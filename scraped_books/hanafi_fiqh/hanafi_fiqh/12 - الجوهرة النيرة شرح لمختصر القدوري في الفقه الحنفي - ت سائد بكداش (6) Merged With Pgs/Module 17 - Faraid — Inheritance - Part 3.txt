Tawafuq: Correcting the Base When Heir Group Sizes Share a Common Divisor
Explaining tawafuq as the third case for correcting the base number.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the tawafuq rule to correct the base number when the numbers of heirs in two groups share a common divisor greater than 1.

Core Idea
When two numbers share a common factor, use the reciprocal of one and the whole of the other to find the correction multiplier.

In inheritance calculations, tawafuq (agreement) occurs when the numbers of heirs in two groups share a common divisor, such as 2 for 4 and 6. The correction multiplier is found by multiplying half of one number by the whole of the other. This lesson demonstrates tawafuq with the example of four wives, one sister, and six uncles, where the numbers 4 and 6 share the divisor 2.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs and their shares
The heirs are four wives (fard 1/4), one sister (fard 1/2?), and six uncles (asabah). The text does not specify shares, but typical shares with no children: wives 1/4, sister 1/2, uncles 1/4. The base number from these shares is 4.
The text focuses on the number of heirs, not the shares, for the tawafuq rule.
2
Determine the numbers of heirs with broken shares
The wives' share is broken over 4 wives; the uncles' share is broken over 6 uncles. The sister is a single heir, so her share is not broken. The relevant numbers are 4 and 6.
3
Check for tawafuq: do the numbers share a common divisor greater than 1?
4 and 6 share a common divisor of 2. They are neither equal (tamathul) nor is one a multiple of the other (tadakhul). Thus, tawafuq applies.
The rule states: 'If one number agrees with the other, multiply the half of one by the whole of the other.'
4
Apply the tawafuq rule
Take the reciprocal (half) of one number: half of 4 is 2. Multiply by the whole of the other (6): 2 × 6 = 12. This is the correction multiplier.
Alternatively, half of 6 is 3, multiplied by 4 gives 12; same result.
5
Compute the corrected base
Multiply the base (4) by the multiplier (12) to get 48. The corrected base is 48. The final shares would be: wives get 12 (3 each), sister gets 24, uncles get 12 (2 each).
The source states: 'it becomes forty-eight, and from it [the case] is corrected.'

Distributing the Estate Using the Corrected Base Number
Explaining the distribution formula after base correction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate each heir's share from the estate using the corrected base number and the distribution formula: multiply shares by estate and divide by the corrected base.

Core Idea
The corrected base gives proportional shares; actual distribution requires multiplication by estate and division by the base.

After correcting the base number in a faraid case (tasḥīḥ al-masʾalah), you obtain integer shares for each heir. But these are only proportions relative to the whole. To convert them into actual amounts from the estate, you use a straightforward formula: multiply each heir's share count by the total estate value, then divide by the corrected base number. This lesson walks through the formula and its application using a classic example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the corrected base and each heir's shares
From the correction step, you have a base number (e.g., 48) and the number of shares assigned to each heir (e.g., wives 12, sister 24, uncles 12). Understand that these are the numerators over the base denominator.
The base is the total parts.
2
Set up the distribution formula
The rule: multiply each heir's share count by the total estate amount, then divide by the corrected base number. This yields that heir's portion.
Formula: (Heir's shares ÷ Base) × Estate = Heir's share.
3
Apply to the example
Take the given example: base = 48, wives shares = 12, sister shares = 24, uncles shares = 12. If the estate is 240 dirhams, calculate each share: wives = (12/48)×240 = 60, sister = (24/48)×240 = 120, uncles = (12/48)×240 = 60.
Verify that the sum matches the estate: 60+120+60=240.
4
Understand the significance of tashih
The correction (tashih) ensures the shares are integers, allowing clean multiplication without fractions. Without correction, you would have fractional shares for some heirs.
Tashih is essential for practical distribution.

Tashih of the Estate Base: Correcting Fractional Shares
Explaining tashih with an example and general rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the corrected base (al-asl al-musahhah) of an estate when the shares of a group of heirs are not divisible among their numbers, using the Hanafi method of multiplying half of one group's count by the whole of the other.

Core Idea
When a share cannot be divided among the heirs of that category, multiply half of one group by the whole of the other to correct the estate base.

In inheritance calculations, after assigning the original base (asl), we sometimes find that the allocated share for a group of heirs—like six paternal uncles—is a single unit that cannot be split evenly among them. This situation is called 'inksar al-siham' (fractional shares). The Hanafi school solves this by adjusting (tas-hih) the base number, ensuring all shares become integers while preserving the proportional rights of each heir. This lesson walks through the classic example of four wives, a sister, and six uncles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs and their shares
List the heirs and determine their Quranic or residuary shares. In this example: wives get 1/4, sister gets 1/2, uncles get the residue (1/4).
Ensure you know which shares are Quranic (fard) and which are residuary (asabah).
2
Set the original base (asl)
Find the least common multiple (LCM) of the share denominators. For 1/4 and 1/2, the LCM is 4. This becomes the original base.
The base must be a number from which all Quranic shares can be taken as whole numbers.
3
Identify the broken share (inksar al-siham)
After computing shares from the base, check if any share is not divisible by the number of heirs receiving it. Here, uncles' share is 1 (from base 4, residue 1/4 = 1), but there are 6 uncles, so 1 is not divisible by 6.
This is the condition that triggers tashih.
4
Apply the tashih multiplier
Find the common factor between the number of wives (4) and the number of uncles (6). They share a factor of 2 (half). Multiply half of one by the whole of the other: (4/2)*6 = 12 or (6/2)*4 = 12. This is the correction multiplier.
The rule: multiply half of one group's count by the full count of the other group when they share a common factor.
5
Compute the new base and distribute
Multiply the original base (4) by the multiplier (12) to get the corrected base (48). Then multiply each heir's share from the original base by the multiplier: wives 1×12=12, sister 2×12=24, uncles 1×12=12. Divide each group's total by their number: each wife gets 12/4=3, each uncle gets 12/6=2. The sister gets the full 24.
The corrected base preserves the original proportions.

Calculating Munasakhat: Successive Deaths Before Distribution
Explaining the ruling on successive deaths before distribution with a worked example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the final distribution of an estate where an heir dies before distribution, including tashih (correction) and base multiplication.

Core Idea
Munasakhat combines two inheritance calculations by multiplying corrected bases when an heir dies before distribution.

When an heir dies before the first estate is distributed, their share becomes a separate estate. The Hanafi method resolves this by first solving the original estate, then handling the second death. This lesson walks through a classic example from the Hanafi manuals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two deaths and their heirs
First death: a man leaves a wife, a full sister, and four paternal uncles. Second death: one of the uncles dies before distribution, leaving his three brothers as his only heirs.
The second deceased heir must have heirs of his own.
2
Solve the first estate and correct for indivisible shares
The first estate has a base of 4 (for the wife's 1/4, sister's 1/2, and residue for uncles). The uncles' share is 1, which cannot be divided among 4 uncles, so we multiply the base by 4 to get 16. New shares: wife=4, sister=8, uncles=4 (each 1).
This is the first tashih.
3
Identify the share of the deceased uncle and his heirs
The deceased uncle's share from the first estate is 1 (in the 16 base). His heirs are his 3 brothers, so his own base is 3 (number of his heirs). His share 1 is not divisible by 3.
The second estate base equals the number of his heirs, not a fractional share.
4
Combine the two bases by multiplication
Since the first corrected base (16) and the second base (3) have no common factor, we multiply them: 16 × 3 = 48. This is the final universal base.
If there were a common factor, we would reduce before multiplying.
5
Calculate final shares
The wife's share (4 from first) multiplied by 3 = 12. The sister's share (8) multiplied by 3 = 24. The remaining 12 goes to the three surviving uncles, each 4. The deceased uncle's share is absorbed.
Check that the total adds up to 48.

Calculating Manasakhah (Successive Deaths) in Inheritance
Expands al-Quduri's matn on manasakhah calculation methods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the final inheritance shares when an heir dies before distribution, applying the Hanafi method of manasakhah with both harmonious and non-harmonious cases.

Core Idea
When an heir dies before distribution, recalculate by multiplying base numbers — whole if shares don't harmonize, or common factor if they do.

Manasakhah occurs when an heir passes away before the estate is distributed among the original heirs. This creates a second inheritance problem that must be integrated with the first. Al-Quduri’s matn, expanded in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, provides a clear arithmetic procedure: if the shares of the second deceased do not divide evenly among his heirs (no harmony), you multiply the two corrected base numbers; if there is harmony, you multiply by the common factor (wafq). This lesson walks through both methods step by step.

Guided Path

Rule of Muwafaqa in Munasakha: Combining Compatible Bases
Explains the procedural rule for munasakha when bases have a common factor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the unified base in a munasakha case when the two bases have a common factor (muwafaqa).

Core Idea
When bases are compatible, take concord of second base and multiply by first to unify.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the procedure for handling munasakha when the shares from two deaths share a common factor (muwafaqa). Instead of using incompatible numbers, you find the concord (wifq) of the second problem and multiply it by the first base. The example illustrates with a husband and two brothers first, then the husband dying and leaving four sons. Mastering this rule is essential for combining inheritance problems correctly without fractions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two problems
Recognize that the first problem involves the original deceased (e.g., woman) and her heirs (husband, two brothers). The second problem arises when one of those heirs (husband) dies before distribution, leaving his own heirs (four sons).
Always list the heirs for each death separately.
2
Determine the bases for each problem
Calculate the base (asl al-mas'alah) for the first death. For the second death, the base is based on the heirs of the second deceased, considering only the estate he inherits from the first. In the example, first base = 4 (husband 2, brothers 2). Second base = 4 (four sons take all).
In munasakha, the second deceased's estate is only what he received from the first, not any separate wealth.
3
Check compatibility between the two bases
Find the greatest common divisor (GCD) of the two bases. If they share a divisor greater than 1, they are compatible (muwafaqa). In the example, both bases are 4, GCD = 2, so muwafaqa by half.
If no common divisor, it's mubayana (incompatibility) and a different rule applies.
4
Apply the rule of muwafaqa
Take the concord (wifq) of the second base. The concord is the second base divided by the GCD. For 4 with GCD 2, wifq = 2. Multiply this wifq by the first base: 2 × 4 = 8. This is the unified base.
The wifq ensures the shares from both problems become whole numbers when multiplied.
5
Distribute shares using the unified base
Heirs from the first problem: multiply their original share by the wifq of the second base (2). Heirs from the second problem: multiply the share of the second deceased from the first problem by the wifq of the second base. In the example: brothers (first) had 2, × 2 = 4; sons (second) inherit the husband's 2, × 2 = 4. Check that total shares (4+4=8) equal unified base.
If the second deceased had separate wealth, adjust accordingly; here he only had the inheritance.

Converting Inheritance Shares to Hubbat al-Dirham
Demonstrates how to convert shares into dirham coin units

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert fractional inheritance shares into hubbat al-dirham by dividing the total shares by 48 and multiplying each heir's shares by the resulting factor.

Core Idea
Each share equals 2/3 hubbah when total shares are 72 and a dirham has 48 hubbah.

In Hanafi faraid, after computing inheritance shares, you often need to express them in coin units for distribution. This lesson walks through a munasakha example where the combined base is 72, and demonstrates the method to convert each heir's share into hubbat al-dirham (a subunit of the dirham). The key insight is that the total shares represent a fraction of a dirham, and converting requires dividing the fixed 48 hubbah by the total shares.

Guided Path
1
Understand the munasakha problem
The problem involves two deaths: first deceased leaves husband, parents, and son (base 12). The son dies leaving a son, a father, two grandfathers; his estate includes 5 shares from first. Multiply bases: 6 × 12 = 72 total shares.
The specific heir distribution is secondary; focus on the total shares.
2
Recall dirham subunit conversion
One dirham equals 48 hubbah. The total estate in dirhams is total shares divided by 48. Here, total shares 72 represent 1.5 dirhams.
3
Compute value per share in hubbah
Divide the fixed 48 hubbah by total shares: 48 / 72 = 2/3 hubbah per share.
4
Convert each heir's shares
Multiply each heir's share count by 2/3 to get hubbah. For example, father's 12 shares yield 12 × 2/3 = 8 hubbah.
5
Express in standard coin subunits
Use known conversions: 1 daniq = 8 hubbah, 1 tassuj = 2 hubbah, etc. For father: 8 hubbah = 1 daniq. For mother: 17 shares = 11.33 hubbah = 1 daniq + 3.33 hubbah.

Key Terms

دائق (dāniq): A subunit of the dirham: 1/6 of a dirham, consisting of 8 habbas.
