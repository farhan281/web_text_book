Classification of Ushr and Kharaj Lands
Defining 'ushr and kharaj lands with boundaries and ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given land as 'ushr or kharaj according to Hanafi fiqh and explain the ownership status of each.

Core Idea
Land in Islam is either 'ushr or kharaj; Arabia is 'ushr, Sawad is kharaj, and ownership differs.

This lesson defines the two fundamental categories of land in Hanafi fiqh: 'ushr (tithe) land and kharaj (land tax) land. The classical texts specify the boundaries of each type and the historical basis for their tax and ownership rules. Understanding this classification is essential for applying zakat on agricultural produce and for property transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Name the two types of land: 'ushr and kharaj. Note that 'ushr land is further divided into the Arabian Peninsula and other lands that were peacefully converted or conquered and divided.
2
Locate the geographical scope
The passage specifies that all land of the Arabs (the Arabian Peninsula) is 'ushr land, with boundaries from al-'Udhayb to Yemen to Syria. Al-Sawad (the black land of Iraq) is kharaj land, with boundaries from al-'Udhayb to 'Aqabat Hulwan and from al-'Alth to 'Abbadan.
3
Determine ownership status
'Ushr land is typically owned by Muslims or dhimmis and can be freely transacted. Kharaj land (Sawad) is owned by its original inhabitants, who may sell it, despite being conquered by force.
4
Trace the historical cause (sabab)
Sawad became kharaj land because it was conquered by force ('anwah) and the inhabitants were allowed to stay, paying kharaj and jizya. 'Ushr land arises either from peaceful conversion or from conquest followed by division among the fighters (ghanimun).
5
Apply the rule to new lands
Any land whose people embrace Islam without conquest, or any land conquered and then divided among the fighters, becomes 'ushr land. This applies outside Arabia.

Commentary
This passage defines the two primary categories of land in Hanafi fiqh: 'ushr (tithe) land and kharaj (land tax) land. The commentator expands on the matn of al-Quduri. First, the land of the Arabs (the Arabian Peninsula) is entirely 'ushr land, meaning a tenth (or half-tenth) is levied on its produce. Its boundaries are specified from al-'Udhayb (near Kufa) to the farthest point in Yemen at Mahrah, and to the border of Syria. Second, al-Sawad (the cultivated region of Iraq) is kharaj land, meaning a fixed land tax is imposed. The commentator notes that al-Sawad is named for the greenness of its vegetation. The boundaries of Sawad are given: from al-'Udhayb to 'Aqabat Hulwan in width, and from al-'Alth to 'Abbadan in length, with an area of about 32 million jaribs. The ruling that the land of Sawad is owned by its inhabitants is based on the historical fact that it was conquered by force ('anwah), but the people were allowed to remain on their land in exchange for paying kharaj and jizya. Therefore, they own the land and can sell it. Finally, any land whose people accept Islam without conquest, or any land conquered by force and then divided among the fighters (ghanimun), becomes 'ushr land. This applies to lands other than Arabia itself.

Definition of Sawad and Ownership of Its Land
Defines Sawad and rules on land ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the geographical boundaries of Sawad and explain why its land is privately owned according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Sawad land is privately owned by its inhabitants because after conquest they were left in place and taxed, not dispossessed.

This lesson defines the Sawad region of Iraq and establishes a key Hanafi principle: that conquered land, when its people are allowed to remain and are subjected to kharaj, remains their private property. This ruling has significant implications for land taxation (ushr vs kharaj) and ownership rights. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly classifying land and applying the appropriate tax rules in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Locate the Sawad region
Identify the precise boundaries of Sawad as given in the text: between al-'Udhayb, 'Aqabat Hulwan, al-'Alth, and 'Abbadan. Note the dimensions: 180 farsakhs long, 80 wide, area 32-36 million jaribs.
This geographical definition is crucial for determining which lands fall under the special kharaj regime.
2
State the ruling on ownership
The text explicitly says: 'And the land of Sawad is owned by its people: their sale of it and their disposal of it are permissible.' Confirm this as the Hanafi position (mufta bihi).
This is the central legal rule of the passage.
3
Analyze the talil (reason)
The reason given: because it was conquered by force, the people were left on their land, and kharaj was imposed on the land and jizyah on their heads. This demonstrates that ownership was not transferred to the state; rather, the inhabitants retained possession subject to tax.
This is a talil argument based on the precedent of the Companions.
4
Distinguish 'ushr land from kharaj land
The passage then states that land where people embraced Islam upon it, or conquered by force and divided among the spoilers, is 'ushr land (tithed). Note that 'ushr is purification and worship, suitable for Muslims; kharaj is not initiated on Muslims.
This classification affects tax liability and ownership structure.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a key topic in Hanafi land taxation: how to determine whether land is subject to kharaj (land tax) or 'ushr (tithe). The ruling establishes two fundamental rules: one based on conquest method, and another on water source. Understanding these rules is essential for applying zakat and land tax correctly.

Commentary
This passage addresses the land tax status of Basra, a major city in Iraq. The Shārih (commentator) explains the ruling from the matn (base text) and expands on intra-Hanafi differences. The core ruling is that Basra is 'ushri land, meaning it is subject to the 'ushr (tithe) rather than kharaj (land tax). This is established by the consensus (ijmā') of the Companions, who conquered the land and imposed 'ushr upon it. Abu Hanifa (referred to as 'he' in the matn) holds this view. Abu Yusuf's initial qiyas (analogy) would have made Basra kharaji because it was conquered from the Persians and is in the territory of kharaj lands, but he abandoned qiyas in favor of the Companions' consensus. Imam Muhammad then adds a detail: if the land is revived using water from sources no one owns (like wells, springs, or major rivers), it remains 'ushri; but if revived using water from rivers dug by non-Muslims (like the Persians), it becomes kharaji. This distinction is based on the nature of the water: 'ushri water is naturally occurring or from sources not owned, while kharaji water is from artificial canals dug by former non-Muslim rulers. The passage also notes that if a dhimmī (non-Muslim subject) revives such land, kharaj is due regardless, because the land is in the territory of 'ushr but the reviver's status affects the tax. The commentary references al-Hidayah for further classification. This ruling illustrates the Hanafi methodology of prioritizing ijma over qiyas and the use of istihsan (juristic preference) in land tax law.

Explains how kharaj is assessed on crops other than those with fixed rates.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for assessing kharaj on non-standard crops and apply the principle of capacity (tāqah) to determine the maximum tax.

Core Idea
Kharaj on non-standard crops is assessed by land capacity, up to half the produce, as a fixed annual tax, not per harvest.

This lesson examines how the Hanafi school extends Caliph Umar's principle of capacity to crops not covered by fixed kharaj rates. You will learn that kharaj is a single annual levy tied to the land's ability to bear it, distinguishing it from 'ushr which applies per harvest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle for Non-Standard Crops
Recognize that for crops like saffron and lucerne, kharaj is imposed 'according to capacity' (ḥasab al-ṭāqah).
This differs from fixed-rate crops where Umar set specific amounts.
2
Understand the Capacity (Ṭāqah) Principle
The ruler estimates the land's productive capacity; if the imposed tax exceeds it, the ruler must reduce it.
Capacity is assessed on the land's yield, not on market fluctuations.
3
Note the Maximum Limit: Half the Produce
The text states that the maximum kharaj can reach half the harvest, as 'half is the essence of fairness'.
This prevents excessive taxation.
4
Distinguish Kharaj from 'Ushr
Kharaj is a single annual tax regardless of how many times the land is cultivated, whereas 'ushr is due on each harvest.
This is a key practical difference between the two types of land.

The Non-Combination of 'Ushr and Kharaj
Clarifying that 'ushr and kharaj do not combine on the same land in Hanafi law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the non-combination of 'ushr and kharaj, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Kharaj and 'ushr are mutually exclusive: a land cannot bear both a land tax and a tithe.

This lesson examines a key point of difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: whether 'ushr (tithe) and kharaj (land tax) can be collected from the same agricultural land. The Hanafi school firmly holds that they cannot, as the land is characterized by one status. We will explore the reasoning and the practical implications, including an ancillary ruling on switching crops and a caution about fatwa issuance.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the core Hanafi ruling: kharaj and 'ushr cannot be collected from the same land. If the land is kharaj land, only kharaj is due; if it is 'ushr land, only 'ushr is due.
This applies to both fixed (muwazzaf) and proportional (muqasamah) kharaj, with the latter requiring no additional tax.
2
Identify the opposing school and their view
Recognize that Imam al-Shafi'i holds that both 'ushr and kharaj can be collected simultaneously from the same land.
The Shafi'i view is based on different causes: kharaj on the land, 'ushr on the produce.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the Shafi'i dalil: they argue that 'ushr and kharaj are two different rights with different bases (sabab) – one on the land, one on the produce – so they do not conflict.
This is a classic example of ikhtilaf in fiqh due to different approaches to causation.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is implicit: the land itself is characterized by one status (kharaj or 'ushr) and cannot bear dual taxation. The text does not give an explicit rebuttal but clearly prefers the Hanafi view.
The Hanafi position relies on the practice of the Companions and the idea that land tax and tithe are mutually exclusive.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: In Hanafi law, a Muslim owning kharaj land pays only kharaj; in Shafi'i law, he would pay both kharaj and 'ushr on the produce.
This difference affects agricultural taxation in contemporary settings where Islamic land classifications are applied.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with an inter-madhhab dispute. It is a chapter on 'ushr and kharaj. Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah deals with the chapter on 'ushr (tithe) and kharaj (land tax). It addresses two key rulings. First, if an owner of kharaj land becomes Muslim, the kharaj continues to be taken from him as before because the land is characterized by kharaj and does not change with the owner's change of religion. Second, a Muslim may purchase kharaj land from a dhimmi, and he will pay kharaj, not 'ushr, and no 'ushr is due on the produce from such land. The author clarifies that this applies when kharaj is fixed (muwazzaf); if it is sharecropping kharaj (muqasama), then nothing is taken. There is also a ruling that if someone transfers to the lesser of two matters without excuse, he must pay the higher kharaj, but this is not given as a fatwa to prevent oppression. The central issue is the non-combination of 'ushr and kharaj. The Hanafi position is that they cannot combine on one land. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, arguing that they are two different rights: kharaj is a punitive burden, 'ushr is a worship burden; they are on different loci (kharaj on the liability, 'ushr on the produce); and they have different causes (real growth vs. estimated growth). Thus, they do not conflict. The Hanafi response relies on a hadith: 'No 'ushr and kharaj combine in a Muslim's land,' and on the reasoning that kharaj applies to land conquered by force, while 'ushr applies to land whose people accepted Islam voluntarily, and these two descriptions cannot coexist. An additional application: if one buys both 'ushr land and kharaj land for trade, only 'ushr or kharaj applies, not zakat of trade.

Ushr and Kharaj: Distinguishing the Two Land Taxes
Distinguishes ushr and kharaj by cause and location

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ushr and kharaj by their cause, locus, and legal nature, and apply the rule that they do not combine on Muslim land.

Core Idea
Ushr is worship on produce; kharaj is punitive on land – they never combine.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayira clarifies a fundamental distinction in Hanafi land taxation: ushr (tithe) and kharaj (land tax) are two different rights with separate causes and loci. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying zakat rules and avoiding double taxation. Let's unpack the commentator's explanation and the supporting proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rights and their legal nature
State that ushr is a religious obligation (like worship) while kharaj is a punitive levy. Note the commentator's phrase 'burden in the sense of punishment' for kharaj and 'burden in the sense of worship' for ushr.
This classification affects how each tax is treated in fiqh.
2
Locate their different loci (mahall)
Identify that kharaj is a debt on the person (dhimma), while ushr is on the produce (al-kharij). This means kharaj is owed by the individual regardless of harvest, whereas ushr is only due when produce exists.
The locus determines when the obligation is triggered.
3
Identify their different causes (sabab)
The cause for ushr is actual growth (al-nama' al-haqiqi) – the existence of produce. The cause for kharaj is estimated growth (al-nama' al-taqdiri) – the ability to cultivate, regardless of actual yield.
This distinction explains why kharaj is fixed annually while ushr varies with harvest.
4
Examine the dalil and its implication
The hadith 'لا يجتمع عُشر وخراج في أرض مسلم' is cited as proof that the two taxes never combine. The reasoning is that kharaj applies to land conquered by force, ushr to land voluntarily submitted – these two descriptions cannot coexist on the same land.
The hadith is ahad (zanni) but accepted in the Hanafi school.
5
Apply the rule to the case of zakat of trade
When land subject to ushr or kharaj is purchased for trade, only the applicable land tax is due, not zakat of trade. The land tax takes precedence because the land retains its original classification.
This is a practical application showing the rule's effect.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi legal commentary (sharh) discussing the treaty obligations of Banu Taghlib, a Christian Arab tribe. The matn states that double sadaqa (zakat) is taken from their women but not from their children, and that their clients (mawali) pay jizya and kharaj instead. Zufar al-Balkhi, a Hanafi scholar, disagrees on both points: he argues that mawali should also pay double sadaqa based on the hadith 'The client of a people is from them,' and that women should not pay because the payment is essentially jizya, which is not taken from women. The Hanafi response uses istihsan (juristic preference): the double sadaqa is a lightened obligation (absence of abasement), so mawali are not bound by the original people's treaty; analogously, a Muslim's Christian client pays jizya. For women, the Hanafis argue that the treaty was on zakat, and women are subject to zakat, so they pay the double. The passage highlights how the Hanafi school deduces rulings from the nature of the treaty (sulh) rather than a fixed category.

Key Terms
المولى (al-mawla): A client freed by a Taghlibi, or a non-Arab convert attached to the tribe by patronage.
المضاعفة (al-muda'afah): Double the usual zakat rate (e.g., 10% instead of 5% on crops) imposed on Banu Taghlib by treaty.
الجزية (al-jizyah): A poll tax on non-Muslims in exchange for protection; Zufar equates the double sadaqa to jizya.

Taxes Collected by Rebels: When Is Payment Sufficient?
Ruling on kharaj and 'ushr collected by rebels, with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on re-collection of taxes by the imam after rebels have collected them, and explain the conditions under which the payment suffices.

Core Idea
Taxes paid to rebels may suffice, but only if rebels spent them on rightful recipients; otherwise, the payer must repeat.

When rebels take over a territory and collect land tax (kharaj) or tithe ('ushr), does the just imam collect the same taxes again from the same people after regaining control? This passage answers that question and introduces a crucial condition: whether the rebels spent the collected funds correctly. Understanding this ruling protects the community from double taxation while honoring religious obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Read the first sentence of the passage: 'What the rebels collect from the lands they dominate of kharaj and 'ushr: the imam does not take it a second time.' This is the core Hanafi position: no re-collection.
The ruling applies to both kharaj and 'ushr unless specified otherwise.
2
Examine the analogy from al-Mabsut
The commentary cites a case from al-Mabsut: a person who lived among rebels (Khawarij) for years and failed to pay zakat, then repented, is not forced to pay the missed years because the imam's authority did not reach them. However, the person must still make it up between himself and Allah.
This establishes the principle that obligations remain, but enforcement is suspended when the imam has no power.
3
Analyze the condition for sufficiency
The passage then states: if the rebels spent the collected funds 'in its rightful place,' it suffices; if not, the people must pay again. Rightful place for kharaj includes the fighters (rebels themselves, as they are combatants), and for 'ushr includes the poor.
The Hidayah clarifies that for kharaj, no repetition is needed even if rebels spent on themselves because they are eligible recipients. For 'ushr, repetition is not needed only if the rebels were poor.
4
Apply to analogous cases
The same principle extends to a person who becomes Muslim in dar al-harb and delays zakat until leaving: they must make it up personally but are not forced by the imam.
This reinforces the distinction between obligation (dhimma) and enforcement (qada' al-imam).

Commentary
This passage presents rulings on taxes collected by rebels during their domination of territories. The core ruling from the matn is that the just imam does not collect kharaj (land tax) or 'ushr (tithe) a second time from the same properties if rebels had already collected them. Priority of Zakah over a Bequest: Muhammad's Rationale
Intra-Hanafi dispute on priority of zakah over will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Muhammad al-Shaybani prioritizes zakah over a will and identify the thamarah al-khilaf in estate distribution.

Core Idea
Zakah combines divine and human rights, making it stronger than a bequest when both are obligatory.

Both zakah and a bequest are Quranic obligations. Yet, when the estate is insufficient, which takes precedence? This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's view: zakah is given priority because it involves a human right (haqq al-adami). We will unpack his reasoning and see how this intra-Hanafi dispute affects actual estate distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Recognize that the issue is when a deceased person had unpaid zakah and also made a bequest, and the estate is not enough to cover both. Which obligation is fulfilled first?
Both are due from the same estate, but they compete for limited assets.
2
State the two Hanafi positions
The 'other narration' refers to a transmission from Abu Hanifa that does not prioritize zakah. Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that zakah is given precedence. Locate each position in the Arabic text.
The passage explicitly presents Muhammad's view; Abu Hanifa's position is implied by contrast.
3
Trace Muhammad's proof (talil)
Muhammad argues: both are from the Quran and equal in obligation (fardiyya). However, zakah has an additional human right (the poor's right to receive it), making it 'stronger' (aqwa). Identify the operator 'illa' and the reasoning.
The argument is a talil: the human right gives an extra weight.
4
Derive the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the estate is limited, the executor must first discharge the zakah before fulfilling the bequest. Consider how this affects heirs and the beneficiary of the will.
This is the operative difference between the two views.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding priority between two Quranic obligations: zakah and a bequest (wasaya). The 'other narration' refers to a transmission differing from Abu Hanifa's. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that zakah should be given precedence. His reasoning: both are equally obligatory (faridah) as both are from the Quran, but zakah additionally involves a right of the human (haqq al-adami)—the right of the poor—making it stronger. The practical implication: if the estate is insufficient to cover both, zakah is paid first. The text does not specify Abu Hanifa's view, but it is implied that he does not prioritize zakah in this manner.

Key Terms
زكاة (zakah): The obligatory alms, a fiscal worship due on wealth, considered a divine right (haqq Allah) and a human right of the poor.
وصية (wasaya): A bequest or will made by a person for after their death, executed from one-third of the estate.
حَق الآدمي (haqq al-adami): The right of a human being, as opposed to the right of Allah. In this context, it refers to the right of the poor to receive zakah.
