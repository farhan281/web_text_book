Bequest of All Property and One-Third to Another
States the ruling when bequeathing all property to one and one-third to another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conflicting bequests of all property and one-third, and apply the rule of equal division of the third when both legatees claim it.

Core Idea
When bequests conflict, the all-property legatee takes above the third; the third is split equally.

This lesson addresses a specific case of overlapping bequests: a testator leaves all his property to one person and one-third of his property to another. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, when there are no heirs or they approve, the property is divided by mutual claim. The all-property legatee receives everything above the third without contest, but the third itself is shared equally between both legatees. Understanding this ruling clarifies how the Hanafi school resolves conflicts between general and specific bequests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequests and condition
Recognize the two bequests: all property to legatee A, one-third to legatee B. The ruling applies only if there are no heirs, or if heirs exist and they approve the bequests.
The condition ensures the bequests are not limited to the one-third cap.
2
Determine the portion above the third
Any property exceeding one-third of the estate goes exclusively to legatee A (the 'all' legatee) without any claim from B.
This is because B's bequest is limited to one-third, so B cannot claim beyond that.
3
Resolve the dispute over the third
Both A and B have an equal claim to the one-third portion: A claims it as part of his all-encompassing bequest, and B claims it as his specific entitlement. The text states 'their dispute is equal,' so the third is divided equally between them.
Equal division means splitting the third in halves, giving each legatee half of the third.
4
Verify the final distribution
Calculate each legatee's share: A receives (entire estate - one-third) + (half of one-third) = five-sixths of the estate; B receives half of the one-third = one-sixth.
The total is the full estate, confirming the division is complete.

Core Idea
A bequest of a specific sum is assumed valid; a fractional bequest is not.

In this passage, al-Quduri clarifies a key distinction in the law of bequests. When a testator leaves a specific amount of money, the Hanafi school presumes it is valid because the testator may have additional property. But when he leaves a fraction like one-third, no such presumption applies. Let's explore the reasoning and the practical calculation.

The reason for the difference: with a specific sum, it is possible the testator has other property from which the amount can come out of the third. With a fraction, the wording itself is uncertain and depends on the estate's total.

Distinguish fractional bequests
If the testator had bequeathed one-third and one-half, the bequests would not be valid in principle; the distribution would be based on the actual estate and the third limit.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Bequest of All Property and One-Third
Intra-Hanafi dispute on bequest distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on distributing a bequest of all property and one-third, and apply the correct distribution when heirs consent or refuse.

Core Idea
When bequests exceed the third, Abu Hanifa divides the third equally, while the Sahibayn apply proportional distribution like 'awl.

In this lesson, we examine a classical Hanafi dispute about two conflicting bequests: one for the entire estate and another for one-third. The disagreement centers on how to allocate the available third when heirs consent or not. You will learn the precise methodologies of Abu Hanifa and his two companions.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and conditions
Identify the two legatees: one for all property, one for one-third. Note the two conditions: with heirs consent (or no heirs) and without heirs consent.
The scenario is hypothetical but legally significant.
2
Abu Hanifa's method with consent
The legatee of all gets everything above the third without contest. The third is contested equally and divided half-half.
The legatee of all does not multiply by more than the third in the contested portion.
3
Sahibayn's method with consent
Each legatee multiplies by the full bequest: the legatee of one-third by 1 share, the legatee of all by 3 shares. Total 4 shares, so the third is divided 1/4 and 3/4.
This is analogous to 'awl in inheritance.
4
Distribution without heirs consent
Only one-third is available. Abu Hanifa divides it equally (each gets 1/6 of total), while the Sahibayn divide it in quarters (legatee of all gets 1/4 of total, legatee of one-third gets 1/12).
The difference is significant: equal vs 3:1 ratio.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi scholars prefer the Sahibayn's view as it is more consistent with proportional distribution.
Tarjih authority: Al-Hidayah and later fatwa works.

Debt overrides bequest: a will is invalid if the estate is entirely consumed by debt, unless creditors waive.

This lesson examines a critical rule in Hanafi inheritance law: when a testator's debt consumes the entire estate, any will he makes is void unless the creditors voluntarily forgive the debt. The passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains both the ruling and the rational proof behind it, grounded in the principle that obligatory debts take precedence over voluntary bequests. Understanding this rule is essential for fulfilling creditors' rights and avoiding invalid distributions.

The act is a will (wasiyyah) made by a testator who owes debts covering all his property. The ruling is that the will is invalid (batil) – it does not take effect.

The proof is a rational analogy (qiyas): debt is an obligatory (wajib) claim, while the will is a voluntary (tabarru') act. The obligatory takes precedence over the voluntary.
The proof is based on the principle that wajib claims are prioritized over tatawwu'.

Since the obligatory debt covers the entire estate, there is no remaining property to which the voluntary will could apply. Therefore, the will cannot be executed.

Bequeathing 'Like the Share of a Son' – Validity and Division Dispute
Ruling on bequest of 'like the share of a son' and intra-Hanafi dispute on division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a bequest of 'like the share' and 'the share' itself, calculate the legatee's share by treating him as an additional child, and compare the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on dividing the one-third when bequests exceed it.

Core Idea
A bequest of 'like the share of a son' is valid because it quantifies an amount, not the heir's actual property.

In Hanafi law, a testator may bequeath a sum equivalent to the share of an heir, such as a son. This is valid because it is a quantified amount, unlike bequeathing the exact share which would transfer ownership of the heir's prospective inheritance. The calculation treats the legatee as an additional child for determining his share. However, when multiple legatees are involved and the one-third limit applies, Abu Hanifa and his two companions differ on how to divide the available one-third. This lesson unpacks that dispute and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest type
Distinguish between 'like the share of my son' (مثل نصيب ابني) and 'the share of my son' (نصيب ابني). The former is an amount equivalent to his share, the latter is the share itself. Hanafis hold the first valid and the second void.
The key is the word 'like' (مثل) which indicates a similar amount, not the identical item.
2
Calculate the legatee's share
Treat the legatee as an additional child. If the testator has one son, the legatee's bequest equals the son's inheritance share (e.g., if son inherits all, the bequest is the whole estate). However, bequests exceeding one-third require heir consent, so without consent the legatee receives only one-third.
With two sons, the legatee is like a third son, so he gets one-third of the estate automatically (within the one-third limit).
3
Apply the one-third limitation
When the bequest exceeds one-third and the heirs do not consent, the legatee is limited to one-third of the estate. Any excess is suspended pending consent.
This rule is standard in all madhhabs.
4
Examine the dispute scenario
Consider two legatees: L1 bequeathed one-third, L2 bequeathed all. Without consent, only one-third of the estate is available. Abu Hanifa caps each legatee's claim at one-third (so total 2/3), then reduces proportionally to fit the one-third, giving equal halves (1/6 each). The Sahibayn allow each legatee to claim his full bequest, then reduce proportionally like 'awl (inheritance reduction), giving L1 1/4 and L2 3/4 of the one-third (i.e., 1/12 and 1/4 of total).
The difference hinges on whether an excessive bequest is void or merely reduced to one-third.
5
Compare practical outcomes
Under Abu Hanifa, both legatees receive equal shares (1/6 of total estate each). Under the Sahibayn, L1 receives 1/12 and L2 receives 1/4 of total estate.
The dispute is recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah; later preference often leans toward the Sahibayn's proportional method.

This dispute highlights different considerations: Abu Hanifa emphasizes the quasi-contractual nature of muhabah, while the Sahibayn focus on the irrevocability and spiritual merit of freeing a slave.

Core Idea

Proof that a minor's will is invalid by analogy to gift

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In this passage, the Hanafi school establishes that a minor's will is invalid by drawing a clear analogy (qiyas jaliy) between will (wasiyyah) and gift (hibah). Both are gratuitous acts (tabarru'), and a minor is not qualified for such acts. The ruling is absolute: regardless of discernment, a minor's will is void.

Guided Path

Identify the act and its ruling
The text begins with 'ولا تصح وصية الصبي' (and the will of a minor is not valid). The act is a minor making a will; the ruling is batil (void).

2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas: the text analogizes will to gift. It notes that a gift during health is even stronger in validity than a will, yet a minor's gift is invalid by consensus.
The analogy is based on the common attribute (illah) of being a gratuitous act.

The text argues: if a gift (hibah) is invalid from a minor, then a will (which is weaker) must also be invalid. The strength argument: an adult can give away all property in health but only 1/3 by will, showing will is weaker.
This is a fortiori reasoning (al-awlawi) implicit in qiyas.

The Hanafi position is unanimous within the school. Some other schools (e.g., Hanbali) allow a minor's will with guardian permission, but Hanafis reject it absolutely.
The ikhtilaf stems from different views on legal capacity for tabarru'.

The Hanafi position is based on the clear analogy and the principle that a minor lacks full legal capacity. It is the mufta bihi (operative) position.
No tarjih needed within the school; all imams agree.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira discusses the ruling on a bequest made to 'relatives' (aqarib). The author begins by stating the established ruling: the bequest goes to the nearest mahram relatives in order of closeness. Parents and children are excluded because they are not considered 'relatives' in this legal context—parents are the source of kinship and children are related by themselves, so the term does not encompass them. The bequest is distributed among two or more recipients; the minimum plural in inheritance matters is two, as deduced from the Quranic verse on brothers (4:11). All recipients are equal regardless of free/slave status, wealth, or gender, unless the testator specifies otherwise.

The commentator then expands on the matn, explaining that the condition of mahram (being unmarriageable) is necessary because the bequest aims at maintaining family ties (silah), analogous to maintenance (nafaqah) and the obligation of manumission. He then presents the well-known intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the conditions for this bequest. Abu Hanifa stipulates six conditions: (1) kinship (qarabah), (2) non-inheritance (so that the bequest is not for heirs), (3) absence of offspring among the recipients, (4) plurality (the bequest is for a group), (5) mahram relationship, and (6) precedence by closeness (aqrab fa-aqrab). His two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), agree only on the first three, rejecting the last three as conditions. Thus, in their view, the bequest is valid even if the recipients are not mahram, and distribution is equal among all relatives regardless of degree of closeness.

The text then applies Abu Hanifa's view to specific cases: if the testator has two paternal uncles and two maternal uncles, the bequest goes entirely to the paternal uncles because they are closer in kinship. If there is one paternal uncle and two maternal uncles, the paternal uncle takes half (since the bequest is to a group, minimum two, so the single uncle cannot take more than half), and the remaining half goes to the maternal uncles as the next closest. This illustrates the practical consequence of considering proximity.

Throughout, the commentary maintains a teacherly tone, explaining the rationale behind each condition and the points of disagreement. In Hanafi law, a bequest (wasiyyah) is limited to one-third of the estate after death. A bequest of a non-existing specific item is void, but a bequest of value from wealth is valid.

The Hanafi school carefully distinguishes between a bequest attached to a specific property (ayn) and one that is a general obligation (dayn). Core Idea
What is included in a bequest depends on whether the accessory is considered a 'follow-up' (tabi') or a separate entity.

When a testator bequeaths a specific item like a sword or a Quran, does the legatee receive only the main object or also its accessories? The Hanafi school has a nuanced answer based on the principle of taba'iyyah (followership). In this lesson, we examine the positions of the three major Hanafi imams—Abu Hanifa's students Muhammad, Abu Yusuf, and Zufar—on this issue and identify the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the item and its accessories
For each item (sword, saddle, mus-haf, scale), list what counts as an accessory in the discussion.
Accessories may include scabbard, fittings, felt pad, cover, pans, etc.
2
State Muhammad and Zufar's position
They generally include accessories as part of the bequest, based on the item's customary use.
Muhammad: sword with scabbard and fittings; saddle with accompaniments; Zufar: mus-haf with cover.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
He excludes accessories unless explicitly named, arguing they are separate items, analogous to a house not including furniture.
Abu Yusuf: sword only blade; saddle only structural parts; mus-haf without cover; scale without weights/stand.
4
Identify the underlying principle
The difference is based on whether the accessory is considered a 'tabi' (follow-up) or an independent entity.
Abu Yusuf uses qiyas on house bequest; Muhammad and Zufar rely on custom and usage.
5
Note the mufta bihi
For the mus-haf, the fatwa is on Zufar's view (cover included). For other items, the text implies Muhammad's view is relied upon, though not explicitly.
The fatwa statement appears only for the mus-haf.

Commentary
This passage addresses what accessories are included when someone bequeaths a specific item by name. There is a disagreement among the Hanafi imams. For a sword: Imam Muhammad and Zufar hold that the legatee gets the sword along with its scabbard and all fittings; Abu Yusuf says only the blade, excluding the scabbard. For a saddle: the majority (Muhammad and Zufar) say the legatee gets the saddle and its typical accessories like the felt pad and saddlecloth; Abu Yusuf limits this to the structural parts (saddle flaps and stirrups) and excludes consumable or separate items like the felt pad and cushion. For a mus-haf (Quran copy): Zufar includes the cover in the bequest; Abu Yusuf excludes it, arguing that the cover is separate and not part of the mus-haf. The principle of Abu Yusuf is that separate items are not included unless explicitly named, analogous to a bequest of a house not including furniture. However, the fatwa (the relied-upon position) is on Zufar's view because the cover is considered a follow-up (tabi') to the mus-haf, just as the scabbard is considered part of the sword according to his principle. Finally, for a scale (balance): Abu Yusuf rules that the legatee gets the pans, beam, and tongue, but not the weights nor the stand, as those are separate.

Paying a Bequest from Mixed Assets: 'Ayn and Dayn
States the ruling on paying bequest when estate has both specific property and debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling for distributing a bequest when the estate includes both specific property and receivables.

Core Idea
The legatee is a partner with the heirs, taking a proportionate share of both cash and debts.

How do you pay a bequest when the estate has both ready cash and money owed to the deceased? The Hanafi school provides a clear method: treat the legatee as a partner, and pay him proportionally from both types of assets. This lesson unpacks that ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two asset categories
Determine the 'ayn (specific property, e.g., cash) and the dayn (debts owed to the testator). These are separate pools in the estate.
2
Compute one-third of the total estate
Add the value of 'ayn and all expected dayn (even if not yet collected). One-third of this total is the maximum that can be bequeathed.
If the bequest exceeds this, it is reduced to one-third of total estate.
3
Check if bequest is fully covered by one-third of 'ayn
If the bequest amount is ≤ one-third of the 'ayn, pay the full bequest from the 'ayn immediately.
4
Pay proportionally from 'ayn and dayn
If the bequest exceeds one-third of 'ayn, give the legatee one-third of the 'ayn now. Then, as each debt is collected, give him one-third of that collected amount. Continue until he receives the full bequest (limited to one-third of total estate).
This ensures the legatee does not gain an unfair advantage if 'ayn is large relative to debts.
5
Understand the rationale
The legatee is a partner of the heirs. If he were restricted to 'ayn only, he would get an unfair advantage if 'ayn is large relative to debts, harming the heirs. Proportional sharing ensures equity.

If a person bequeaths to a man one thousand dirhams, and he has specific property and debt: if the thousand comes out of one-third of the specific property, it is given to the legatee. If it does not come out, one-third of the specific property is given to him, and whenever any part of the debt is collected, he takes one-third of it until he receives the full thousand. * His saying: (If a person bequeaths to a man one thousand dirhams, and he has specific property and debt...) because the legatee is a partner of the heirs, and specifying him to the specific property is detrimental to the heirs because there is a surplus in the specific property over the debt.

Commentary
This is a ruling from the Book of Wills. The matn states that when a testator bequeaths a fixed amount (1000 dirhams) and his estate consists of both 'ayn (specific property) and dayn (debts owed to him), the bequest is to be paid from the third of the estate. The sharh explains the reasoning: the legatee is considered a partner with the heirs. If the legatee were restricted to the 'ayn only, that would harm the heirs, as the 'ayn may have a surplus over the debts. Therefore, the legatee takes a proportionate share of both, ensuring fairness.

Key Terms
العين (al-'ayn): Specific property of the deceased that is physically present at the time of death.
الدين (al-dayn): Debts owed to the deceased, which are collected after death and form part of the estate.
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): A bequest made by a testator to a non-heir, valid up to one-third of the estate.
