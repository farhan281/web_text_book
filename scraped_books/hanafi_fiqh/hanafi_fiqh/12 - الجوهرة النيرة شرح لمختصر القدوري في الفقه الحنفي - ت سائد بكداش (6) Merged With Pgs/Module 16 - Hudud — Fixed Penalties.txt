Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ta'zir for False Witness
Intra-Hanafi dispute on punishment for false witness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on ta'zir for false witness and apply the condition of persistence (israr) to reconcile the apparent disagreement.

Core Idea
A false witness may receive public exposure or ta'zir — persistence determines which.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the punishment for a false witness. Abu Hanifa initially limits the penalty to public exposure (shuhra), while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani mandate beating and imprisonment. The commentator resolves the conflict by limiting the Sahibayn's evidence — the action of 'Umar — to persistent offenders, thereby reconciling the views. Understanding this reconciliation is key to applying the correct ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core question
What is the appropriate punishment for a false witness? Is it public exposure only, or corporal punishment (ta'zir) as well?
The dispute focuses on ta'zir, not on the fact that false testimony is a major sin.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the false witness should be publicly exposed (shuhra) in the market, but not given ta'zir. Note the limit: 'I will not give him ta'zir.'
Abu Hanifa does not deny ta'zir entirely; he restricts it to persistent offenders.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad advocate for beating and imprisonment (ta'zir). Their evidence is the practice of Caliph 'Umar, who ordered ta'zir, blackening the face, parading, and imprisonment for a false witness.
The Sahibayn use a specific incident of a Companion (Qawl Sahabi) as a precedent.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The commentator reconciles by noting that 'Umar's action applied to a persistent (musirr) offender. Abu Hanifa agrees to ta'zir for such a person. For a non-persistent first-time offender, public exposure suffices.
The dispute is thus not absolute but conditioned on the factor of persistence (israr).
5
Apply the reconciliation
In practice, evaluate the false witness: if he is a repeat offender or persists in false testimony, apply ta'zir (beating and imprisonment) plus public exposure. If it is a first-time lapse, limit to public exposure.
This reflects the tarjih (preferred position) as given by the commentator.

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on the other hand, advocate for both beating and imprisonment. Duress to Kill a Relative: Qisas, Diya, and Inheritance
Ruling on murder under duress of a relative, and its effects on qisas and inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing under duress of a relative, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding diya and retaliation, and apply the rules of inheritance in such cases.

Core Idea
Duress to kill a relative exempts the coerced from qisas and diya, but the heir-killer may be barred from inheritance.

Islamic law recognizes that duress can negate criminal liability. This passage from al-Quduri addresses a complex scenario: a person compelled under threat of death to kill his own father or brother. We will explore the Hanafi position, the variant views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, and the nuanced inheritance consequences. Understanding these rulings requires careful attention to who is the coerced, who is the victim, and who is the heir.

Guided Path
1
State the general Hanafi position
Read the first part of the passage: the coerced killer is free from qisas (retaliation) and diya (blood money), and inheritance is not prevented. Identify the key elements: no qisas, no diya, inheritance not barred.
This applies when the coerced is not an heir himself.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi variant
Note the disagreement: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the two companions) hold that the heir who is the killer may kill the coercer in retaliation. Additionally, Abu Yusuf alone holds that the coerced must pay the diya. Distinguish the two separate issues: retaliation against the coercer, and diya liability.
The passage uses 'عندهما' (according to them) and 'وقال أبو يوسف عليه الدية'.
3
Analyze the inheritance condition
The passage adds: if the coerced person is himself an heir of the victim (i.e., the killer is the heir), then he is prevented from inheritance. Contrast this with the earlier statement that inheritance is not prevented. Understand the two distinct scenarios.
The first statement seems general; the second qualifies it when the coerced is an heir.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical difference: under the majority view (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), the heir can execute qisas against the coercer, and no diya is due on the coerced. Under Abu Yusuf's position, the coerced pays diya even though he is exempt from qisas. The inheritance rule is independent of this dispute: if the coerced is an heir, he is barred.
This affects how a judge would rule in such a case.

Coercion and Zina: Hadd Obligation and the Sultan Exception
Ruling on man coerced to zina with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on a man coerced to commit zina, explain the rational argument for it, and identify the disagreement among the Sahibayn and Zufar regarding coercion by the sultan.

Core Idea
For a man, true coercion is impossible in zina because arousal requires desire; thus hadd is obligatory unless the sultan coerces.

This passage from the Book of Coercion (Kitab al-Ikrah) of al-Quduri, as explained in al-Jawhara al-Nayrira, addresses a critical question: if a man is forced to commit zina under threat, is he still liable for the hadd punishment? Abu Hanifa argues that genuine coercion is impossible for a man due to the physical requirement of arousal, so he is treated as voluntary. However, when the coercer is the sultan (ruler), the Hanafi school splits: some uphold hadd, others drop it in favor of ta'zir and mahr. We will navigate this intra-madhhab dispute and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline ruling
State Abu Hanifa's position for a man coerced by a non-sultan: hadd is obligatory. The rational proof is that arousal is necessary for intercourse and cannot occur under genuine fear; it only occurs with pleasure and choice.
The matn says 'وجب عليه الحد عند أبي حنيفة إلا أن يكرِهَـه السلطان'.
2
Distinguish the woman's case
Explain why a coerced woman is not hadded: she only enables the act (tamkin), which can be coerced, unlike the man's arousal which requires desire.
The passage explicitly states: 'وليس كذلك المرأة... فإنها لا تُحَدّ'.
3
Analyze the sultan exception
Present the two narrations: (a) hadd still obligatory (Zufar and one riwayah) because the same logic applies; (b) no hadd, only ta'zir and mahr (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) because the sultan's power cannot be resisted and arousal can be involuntary.
The commentator notes two riwayat and quotes al-Bazdawi cautioning against acting under sultan coercion.
4
Apply the rule to lesser coercion
Coercion by imprisonment, shackles, or beating without fear of death does not excuse zina; hadd remains. Only coercion involving fear of death or loss of limb excuses prohibited acts.
The last sentence: 'لا إكراه فيها إلا بما يخاف منه تلف نفس أو عضو'.

Commentary

The Apostate's Repentance: General Rule and Special Case of Reviling the Two Sheikhs
States procedure for apostates and discusses acceptance of repentance for reviling Sheikhayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the general Hanafi ruling on apostate repentance and the specific mufta bihi ruling for those who revile the two Sheikhs, and explain the basis for the intra-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
Reviling Abu Bakr and Umar triggers an aggravated apostasy: the mufta bihi view holds that repentance is not accepted and execution is mandatory even after repentance.

Apostasy is a grave matter in Islamic law, with established procedures for calling the apostate to return. However, not all apostates are treated equally. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah delves into a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: what if the apostate reviles the first two Caliphs? The discussion reveals a stricter ruling that became the relied-upon position in the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for apostates
The text first states the general procedure: a Muslim who apostatizes is presented with Islam and any doubts are clarified. The commentary notes that this presentation is not obligatory because the call (da'wah) has already reached the person. If he repents, he is accepted; otherwise, he is killed.
This is standard Hanafi procedure for ordinary apostasy.
2
Locate the special case of reviling the two Sheikhs
The text then singles out a person who reviles or slanders Abu Bakr and Umar (al-Shaykhan). Such a person is deemed a disbeliever and it is obligatory to kill him. This is an aggravated form of apostasy.
The two Sheikhs are the first two Caliphs, highly revered in Sunni Islam.
3
Isolate the disputed point: acceptance of repentance
The dispute is whether, if the reviler repents and renews Islam, his repentance is accepted. The text attributes to al-Sadr al-Shahid, followed by Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi and Abu Nasr al-Dabusi, the view that repentance is not accepted. This is the chosen (mukhtar) view for fatwa.
Earlier Hanafi authorities may have accepted repentance based on the general principle that repentance wipes out sins.
4
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the mufta bihi view, if a reviler repents, he is still executed. The general rule for ordinary apostates would spare him upon repentance. Thus the dispute has a direct impact on the life of the individual.
The text also mentions a three-day delay if requested, but this is procedural, not a sign of acceptance.
5
Identify the basis for the stronger view
The later scholars based their stricter view on the extreme gravity of insulting the companions, which amounts to such severe disbelief that the usual principle of repentance does not apply. This was to serve as a deterrent.
The authority weight is from the later Hanafi tradition (fatawa mutakhkhirin).

Ruling on debt payment from earnings in Islam and apostasy with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two riwayas from Abu Hanifa regarding payment of an apostate's debts and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between them.

Core Idea
An apostate's debt payment depends on when it was incurred and which riwaya you follow — the earnings of Islam are for the heirs, the earnings of apostasy are the apostate's own.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about the source for paying an apostate's debts. While the obligation to repay debts is clear, the question is whether all debts are paid first from apostasy earnings or whether debts incurred during Islam are separated. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to weigh competing claims — creditors vs. heirs — within Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two periods
Recognize that the apostate has two distinct earning periods: the time when he was Muslim (kasb al-Islam) and the time after apostasy (kasb al-riddah). Debts are also categorized by when they were incurred.
2
Distinguish the two riwayas
First riwaya (from Abu Hanifa, also held by Zufar): debts from Islam are paid from Islamic earnings, debts from apostasy from apostasy earnings. Second riwaya (also from Abu Hanifa): all debts are paid first from apostasy earnings; if insufficient, the remainder is taken from Islamic earnings.
These are both attributed to Abu Hanifa, creating an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
3
Understand the underlying principle
Islamic earnings are considered the right of the heirs (in case of death), while apostasy earnings are the apostate's exclusive right. Therefore, paying debts from apostasy earnings first respects the heirs' claim, but if those earnings are insufficient, the debt must still be satisfied from Islamic earnings because the debt is incumbent (lazim).
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under the first riwaya, Islamic earnings are only used for debts incurred during Islam. Under the second riwaya, Islamic earnings are a secondary source for all debts. The practical outcome differs when Islamic and apostasy earnings and debts are of different amounts.
For example, if apostasy earnings are plentiful, the second riwaya may spare Islamic earnings entirely; if scarce, it may deplete Islamic earnings faster.
5
Note the authority weight
The text attributes both riwayas to Abu Hanifa but does not specify which is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa). This suggests the issue is from the nawadir (rare transmissions) and later Hanafi masters may have chosen one. Today, one must check later tarjih in works like Radd al-Muhtar.

Core Idea
Only face and hands are permissible to look at; even those cannot be touched.

Islamic law sets clear boundaries for interactions between men and non-mahram women. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira details those limits: permissible looks are confined to the face and hands only when needed, and any touch is strictly prohibited. Guided Path

Identify the main ruling on permissible looking
The text begins by stating that a man may only look at the face and palms (kaffayn) of a non-mahram woman. This is due to necessity for transactions and testimony.

If a man fears he may feel desire, even looking at the face is prohibited unless there is a genuine need, such as legal testimony. The hadith about molten lead supports this.

This is a classic example of changing rulings due to changing customs ('urf).

The author refutes the minority view and confirms the prohibition of looking at the foot, which is the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Apply the absolute prohibition of touching
The text emphatically states that touching a non-mahram woman's face or hands is haram even without desire, because there is no necessity comparable to looking.
