Determining the referent of 'roast' (shawa) in an oath.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default meaning of 'shawa' in Hanafi oath law and apply the exception when the swearer has a specific intention.

Core Idea
In oaths, words are taken by common usage; the swearer’s intention can restrict or expand the scope.

When someone swears not to eat a certain food, the meaning is governed by everyday language. In this lesson, we examine the word 'shawa' (roast). The Hanafi school establishes a default meaning—roasted meat—but also allows the swearer’s specific intention to override that default. This protects the oath from unintended breach while honoring the swearer's own restrictiveness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
Recognize that the person has taken an oath (yamin) not to eat 'shawa'.
The act of swearing is a legal act with consequences.
2
Determine the default interpretation
According to Hanafi law, 'shawa' by default means roasted meat. This is based on common usage (urf).
Thus, roasted vegetables or fish are not included unless intended.
3
Apply the default to the given scenario
If the eater consumes something that is not roasted meat (e.g., eggplant, carrots, fish), the oath is not broken (la yahanth).
The oath remains intact; no kaffarah required.
4
Recognize the exception for intention
If the swearer had a specific intention (niyyah) to include all roasted items, then the oath covers those items as well because he imposed a stricter restriction on himself.
The intention must be proven; without it, the default applies.
5
Understand the legal principle
Oaths are interpreted by common usage unless a clear intention to the contrary is established. This balances strictness and fairness.
The Hanafi school gives weight to both urf and niyyah.

Core Idea
The contract itself triggers an oath on sale, even if it can be rescinded via option.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani and Imam Abu Yusuf regarding the effect of a khiyar (option) condition on an oath not to sell. The question is whether the mere existence of the sale contract suffices to break the oath, or whether the non-binding nature of an option-laden contract delays the violation. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the Hanafi method of ta'lil (reasoning) to conditional contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
Read the source text: it states that if a person swears not to sell and then sells with an option (for buyer or seller), Muhammad says he breaks the oath, Abu Yusuf says he does not. Determine the precise legal question: does an option contract count as a 'sale' for purposes of an oath?
2
Analyze Muhammad's proof (dalil)
Muhammad says: 'li wujub al-shart, wa huwa al-bay'' – because the condition (the sale contract) is established. Abu Yusuf argues that the oath is not broken because the contract is not fully binding due to the option. The sale is conditional and reversible; until the option is finalized (whether exercised to confirm or let expire), the sale is not a complete act. Hence the oath remains intact.
Abu Yusuf differentiates between a binding sale (lazim) and a non-binding one (ghayr lazim).

The practical difference: if a person swears not to sell and then sells with an option, according to Muhammad he must pay kaffarah (expiation) immediately if he intended the general sale. According to Abu Yusuf, no kaffarah is due until the option period ends and the contract becomes binding (or unless the option is exercised to confirm the sale).
The dispute affects timing of obligation, not the ultimate ruling if the sale becomes final.

The source text does not mention a tarjih. Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah, Ibn Abidin) may prefer one view or discuss conditions. For fatwa, the mufti must check the operative position in his school's later works.

Negative vs. Positive Oaths: Permanent Abandonment vs. Single Act
Commentary on the distinction between negative and positive oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on negative and positive oaths and apply the distinction between permanent abandonment and single act fulfillment.

Core Idea
A negative oath binds forever; a positive oath is fulfilled by one act.

This lesson explains a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh regarding oaths: whether an oath requires a single action or perpetual avoidance. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly fulfilling one's oaths and avoiding unnecessary kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of oaths
Note that the passage discusses two forms: a negative oath (لا أفعل كذا) and a positive oath (لأفعلن كذا). Identify the ruling for each.
2
Analyze the reasoning for negative oaths
Consider why the Hanafi school rules that a negative oath requires permanent abandonment. The reason is that negation (النفي) is not tied to any specific time; therefore it applies to all times indefinitely.
This is an argument based on the nature of language (ta'lil).
3
Analyze the reasoning for positive oaths
For a positive oath, the purpose (المقصود) is to bring about the act. Once the act is performed once, the purpose is achieved, so the oath is fulfilled. The oath is only broken if the act becomes impossible (e.g., death or loss of the object).
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Take a concrete example: a person swears 'By Allah, I will not enter this house.' According to the Hanafi ruling, he must avoid entering forever. If he enters even once, he breaks his oath and must pay kaffarah. For a positive oath like 'By Allah, I will visit Zaid,' visiting once fulfills the oath.
Contrast with an oath specifying 'every day' which would require repetition.

Negative Oaths: Perpetual; Positive Oaths: Fulfilled by One Act
Rulings on indefinite negative and positive oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for negative and positive oaths and apply the reasoning to determine when an oath is broken or fulfilled.

Core Idea
A negative oath binds forever; a positive oath is fulfilled by doing the act once.

Oaths (ayman) are serious legal commitments. The Hanafi school distinguishes between oaths of negation and affirmation. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the duration and fulfillment conditions for each. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly determining when an oath is broken and expiation (kaffarah) is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of oath
Determine whether the oath is negative (لا يفعل) or positive (ليفعلن). This classification dictates the ruling.
2
Apply the rule for negative oaths
If the oath is negative, it is perpetual — the person must refrain from the act forever. Any performance breaks the oath.
Reason: negation has no specific time.
3
Apply the rule for positive oaths
If the oath is positive, performing the act once fulfills the oath. The oath is only broken if it becomes impossible to perform (e.g., death of the subject).
Reason: purpose is to bring about the act.

Commentary
This passage covers two fundamental principles of oaths (al-ayman). First, a negative oath (لا يفعل) is indefinite and binds for eternity. The reasoning is that negation has no time limit. He only breaks the oath if it becomes impossible (by death or the act's object ceases).
