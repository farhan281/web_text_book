Absolute and Time-Limited Oaths: Conditions and Disputes
Explains two types of oaths with rulings and intra-Hanafi disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between absolute and time-limited oaths, and apply the Hanafi rules on when an oath is broken, including the intra-Hanafi disagreement over the existence of the sworn object.

Core Idea
An oath’s validity requires the continued existence of its subject; if lost, the oath is void according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

This lesson examines the Hanafi classification of oaths into absolute (mursal) and time-limited (muwaqqat). You will learn the precise conditions that trigger a breach (hanth) and the resulting obligation of expiation (kaffarah). Special attention is given to the dispute between Abu Yusuf and the Sahibayn regarding whether the existence of the sworn object is a condition for the oath’s validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of oaths
Read the passage and note the definitions of absolute and time-limited oaths. Absolute oaths have no time restriction; time-limited oaths specify a deadline.
The passage uses 'mursal' and 'muwaqqat'.
2
Analyze absolute oaths: affirmative vs. negative
For an affirmative absolute oath (e.g., 'I will hit Zayd'), the breach occurs when either party perishes. For a negative absolute oath (e.g., 'I will not hit Zayd'), breach occurs only when the forbidden act is done.
Note the asymmetry between affirmative and negative.
3
Understand time-limited oaths
In a time-limited oath, the swearer must perform the action within the specified time. If the time expires without performance, the oath is broken, provided both the swearer and the subject exist.
Consensus: if the swearer dies before time expires, no breach.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
The Sahibayn (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) hold that the existence of the sworn object is a condition. If the object perishes (e.g., water spills), the oath becomes void. Abu Yusuf disagrees, considering the oath binding on the action itself.
This leads to different rulings on kaffarah.
5
Apply the rules to scenarios
Use the provided scenarios in the passage to test your understanding. For example, if someone swears to drink water from a jug but it is empty, the Sahibayn say no breach, while Abu Yusuf says immediate breach.
The passage includes a known-knowledge exception (if swearer knew it was empty, all except Abu Hanifa say breach).

Commentary
This passage defines two types of oaths from the Hanafi perspective. First, the absolute oath (al-yamin al-mursal) is unconditional; it does not specify a time. In a positive oath (e.g., 'By Allah, I will hit Zayd'), the oath is broken only if both the swearer and the subject (Zayd) continue to exist; if one dies, the oath is broken. In a negative oath (e.g., 'By Allah, I will not hit Zayd'), the oath is never broken by mere inaction; only if he actually hits him does he break the oath, and then the expiation is due. The oath cannot be repeated on the same matter. Second, the time-limited oath (al-yamin al-muwaqqat) specifies a timeframe. If the swearer does not perform the action within the time, he breaks the oath. Consensus exists on several points: no breach if the swearer dies before time expires, and breach if time expires while both swearer and subject exist. A dispute arises between Abu Yusuf and the Sahibayn (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad): if the subject (e.g., water) is destroyed before the time ends, the oath is void according to the Sahibayn (since the subject's existence is a condition for the oath's validity), so no breach. Abu Yusuf says the oath remains valid and is broken when time expires. Another scenario: if someone swears to drink water from a jug but there is no water, the Sahibayn say no breach because the subject didn't exist; Abu Yusuf says it's broken immediately. If the swearer knew there was no water, all agree he breaks it, except Abu Hanifa who says no breach even if he knew, and Zufar agrees with him.

Key Terms
اليمين المرسل (al-yamin al-mursal): An oath that is not restricted by time (neither the action nor its negation is tied to a specific time).
المؤقت (al-muwaqqat): An oath that is explicitly restricted by a time period (e.g., 'today').
حنث (hanath): Breaking the oath by failing to fulfill its condition (for affirmative) or by doing the prohibited act (for negative).
كفارة (kaffarah): The expiation due for breaking a valid oath: (a) feeding or clothing ten poor, or (b) freeing a slave, or (c) fasting three days.

The Hanafi Rule on Oaths and Change of Name or State
Explains when name change affects oath breaking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that an oath attaches to the object by its name and apply it to determine whether a change in name or state breaks the oath.

Core Idea
An oath remains with the name; if the name vanishes, the oath vanishes.

In Hanafi fiqh, oaths are binding promises. When someone swears regarding a specific object, the scope of the oath depends critically on whether the object is edible in itself and whether its name persists after transformation. This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents a series of examples that establish a clear maxim: the oath attaches to the substance (al-ayn) and stays as long as its name remains, but ceases when the name disappears. Understanding this maxim is key to correctly judging when an oath is broken, avoiding unnecessary expiation or false security.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of objects
Distinguish between objects edible in themselves (e.g., a sheep, grapes) and those not edible in themselves (e.g., a palm tree, a vine). This classification determines the default scope of the oath.
For edible objects, the oath is confined to the substance as it is at the time of swearing.
2
Learn the maxim for objects with changed names
The text states: 'إذا تعلقت اليمين بعين: بقيت ببقاء اسمه وزالت بزواله' – 'When the oath attaches to a substance, it remains with the survival of its name and ceases with its removal.' This is the core principle.
This applies to changes like a lamb becoming a ram, an unripe date becoming ripe, or milk becoming cheese.
3
Apply the maxim to the examples
Work through each example: swearing in the name of a lamb (no hanth when it becomes a ram); swearing not to eat from a palm tree (hanth for fruit, etc.); swearing not to eat unripe dates (no hanth for ripe dates); swearing not to eat from a sheep (oath only on meat, not derivatives).
Note that for inedible objects, the oath extends to all produce (fruit, pith, etc.) as per Muhammad's opinion.
4
Understand the exception: intention
If the swearer intended to include derivatives, he breaks his oath even if the name changed. The text says 'فإن نوى ما يكون من ذلك حنث' – 'If he intended what comes from it, he breaks his oath.'
This is because he voluntarily restricted himself more strictly.
5
Synthesize the cases into a coherent rule
Combine the category-based rules and the maxim: For edible substances, the oath stays on the specific named item; for inedible substances, it covers the edible output. In both cases, a change in name (e.g., by ripening, cooking, or transformation into a different substance) lifts the oath unless the swearer intended otherwise.
This rule is applied consistently across all fiqh chapters dealing with oaths.

Commentary
This passage, from the commentary on Quduri's Mukhtasar, deals with oaths concerning edible items and how changes in the object's state or name affect whether the oath is broken. The core principle is that an oath attaches to the substance as it is known by its name at the time of swearing. If the name changes, the oath no longer applies. The text distinguishes between items that are edible in themselves (like a sheep or grapes) and those that are not edible in themselves (like a palm tree). For edible items, the oath applies only to the meat or the fruit in its current form, not to derivatives. For non-edible items, the oath applies to what comes from them. Muhammad al-Shaybani adds that for a palm tree, eating any part like fruit, pith, spathe, or uncooked molasses breaks the oath. However, if the molasses is cooked, or if one drinks vinegar or wine made from dates, the oath is not broken because there has been a change through manufacturing. The same logic applies to a vineyard: it is like a palm tree, so the oath covers grapes, raisins, and juice. The commentary then provides a key legal maxim: 'If the oath attaches to a substance, it remains as long as its name remains, and ceases when its name ceases.' This is illustrated with the example of unripe dates (busr) becoming ripe dates (rutab): since the name changes, eating the ripe date does not break the oath. Similarly, oaths concerning milk do not extend to cheese, whey, or rennet; oaths concerning eggs do not include chicks; and oaths concerning wine do not include vinegar. An exception is if the swearer intended the derivatives, in which case the oath applies because he voluntarily restricted himself more strictly. This passage is important for understanding how Hanafi fiqh treats changes in the objects of oaths, emphasizing the role of customary names and the difference between things that are consumed directly versus those that yield products.

Receiving Debt in Two Weighings Does Not Break Oath
Ruling with rational proof for receiving debt in two weighings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on receiving debt in installments to determine whether an oath is broken.

Core Idea
Receiving debt in two weighings does not break an oath if only the act of weighing interrupts.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a common scenario: a person swears to receive a debt in one go but can only receive it in two installments due to practical constraints. The Hanafi school rules that this does not constitute breaking the oath, provided the only interruption is the necessary act of weighing. The reasoning relies on necessity and customary practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition of the oath
The oath requires receiving the entire debt at once. Determine what the person swore to do.
The condition must be clearly understood before analyzing the action.
2
Recognize the actual action
The person receives the debt in two separate weighings, with only the act of weighing between them. No other interruption occurs.
The key is that the interruption is only what is necessary for the transfer.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle
Necessity and custom (urf) except this action from the oath. Since it may be impossible to receive all at once, and debts are customarily received this way, the oath is not broken.
This is based on qiyas and urf, not explicit text.

Timing of Oath Breach Upon Death
Ruling on timing of oath breach upon death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an oath is broken if the swearer dies before performing the sworn act and explain the rational basis for this ruling.

Core Idea
An oath is breached at the last moment of life because until then, fulfillment was possible; death triggers the breach.

This lesson examines a specific case in oaths: a person swears to go to Basra but dies before going. The Hanafi school holds that the oath is broken at the very end of his life, not earlier. Understanding this ruling illustrates how Hanafi jurists apply rational reasoning (qiyas) to determine the timing of an oath's breach.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is swearing an oath to go to Basra. The ruling is that the oath is broken (hanth) at the last moment of life, not before.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is rational (qiyas): before death, fulfillment was possible (marju), so the oath was not broken earlier. At death, the possibility ceases, so breach occurs.
This is a qiyas based on the principle that an oath remains binding until impossibility by death.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The key point is that the obligation of the oath continues until the very end of life. The loss of capacity to fulfill by death triggers the breach, not the actual failure to perform before death if that failure was not due to death.
This differentiates from cases where the act becomes impossible during life (e.g., Basra is destroyed) – then the oath might be nullified.
