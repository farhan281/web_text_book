Hanafi Legal Definitions: Claimant, Denier, Witness, and Acknowledger
Linguistic and legal definitions of key terms in claims and evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the four key roles in Hanafi procedural law (mudda'i, munkir, shahid, muqir) and apply the rule of thumb based on possession and the claimed right to classify a person's statement.

Core Idea
The legal status of a speaker is determined by whether the property is in his hand or another's, and whether he claims it for himself or another.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, correctly classifying the parties and witnesses is the first step. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira provides precise linguistic and technical definitions of four fundamental terms: claimant, denier, witness, and acknowledger. Understanding these categories is essential for determining who bears the burden of proof, who must take an oath, and how evidence is evaluated.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and legal meaning of da'wa
The passage begins by defining 'da'wa' (claim) linguistically and legally. Legally, a claim (da'wa) is a statement for which the claimant has no proof. If the claimant has proof, he is called a muhaqq (one who has established the right), not a mudda'i.
This distinction is crucial: the term 'mudda'i' is reserved for those without evidence. The example of Musaylimah vs. the Prophet ﷺ illustrates this.
2
Learn the rule of thumb for each category
The passage provides a simple rule based on two variables: whose hand the property is in (yad) and to whom the right belongs (haqq). This yields four categories: mudda'i (claimant), munkir (denier), shahid (witness), muqir (acknowledger).
Memorize the four statements: 'Everyone who testifies that what is in another's hand belongs to himself is a claimant...'
3
Apply the rule to identify the mudda'i
Apply the rule to a given scenario: the mudda'i is the one who makes a statement for which he has no proof. Even if he later produces evidence, he is only called a mudda'i before that evidence is presented.
The passage notes that after evidence is presented, the party is no longer called a mudda'i.
4
Distinguish the other three categories
Using the same rule, distinguish munkir (denies claim against himself), shahid (testifies for another about another's possession), and muqir (acknowledges another's right over his own possession).
Note that munkir and muqir involve one's own possession; shahid and mudda'i involve another's possession.

Ruling on Partition and Option After Judicial Decision
Ruling with proof about partition and option

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the after-judgment option (khiyar) in a partition case and apply the distinction between declination before and after the judge's decision.

Core Idea
After the judge rules, if one claimant declines his half, the other may not take the whole; the seller must refund the price.

Imagine two people both buy the same house from a seller. The judge decides to give each half. One buyer then says, 'I don't want my half.' Can the other buyer take the entire house? According to the Hanafi school, after the judge's ruling, the answer is no. This lesson explains the ruling and its rationale based on istihsan (juristic preference), and highlights the critical difference between declination before and after judgment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two claimants each purchased the same property from a seller. The judge partitions it, giving half to each. Then one claimant says 'I do not choose' after the judgment.
The scenario assumes both claims are valid and the judge's partition is binding.
2
State the ruling
The other claimant may not take the whole property. The ruling is that his taking would be batil (invalid).
3
Understand the dalil (proof)
The proof is istihsan. Each claimant contracted for the whole property, but only half was delivered. Therefore each has a khiyar (option) to take or leave. After judgment, declining does not transfer ownership to the other; rather, the seller must return the price to the declining buyer.
The rationale (ta'lil) focuses on the completeness of the contract vis-à-vis delivery.
4

If the declination occurs before the judge's ruling, the other claimant may take the whole property by paying the full price. After judgment, the rights are fixed and the option is restricted.
This distinction is critical: before judgment, the sale is not yet finalized by the court's binding decision.

Possession as Proof of Prior Purchase
Ruling on possession as proof of prior purchase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that possession (qabd) serves as circumstantial evidence (dalala) of prior purchase, and identify when explicit testimony (sarih) overrides it.

Core Idea
Possession indicates prior purchase, but explicit testimony trumps indication — al-sarih yafuq al-dalala.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains a key evidentiary rule in property disputes. When two claimants both lack dates for their purchases, the one in possession is presumed to have bought first. However, this presumption is rebuttable: if the non-possessor can produce witnesses confirming his purchase preceded the possessor's, the explicit testimony wins.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its context
The passage addresses a dispute between two claimants with no purchase dates. The ruling: the one in possession (qabd) is more entitled to the property.
This is a default rule in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
The evidence (dalil) is the possession itself—it is a circumstantial indication (dalala) that the possessor's purchase was earlier.
Dalala is a type of zanni evidence.
3
Understand the principle of priority
The principle is that explicit evidence (sarih) outweighs circumstantial evidence (dalala). Thus, if the non-possessor brings clear testimony, the possessor loses.
This is a well-known Hanafi usul principle.
4
Note the exception
The exception: if the non-possessor produces witnesses testifying that his purchase was before the possessor's, the explicit testimony overrides the indication of possession.
The witnesses must explicitly state the earlier date.

Ruling on Purchase vs Gift Claims Without Dates
Ruling with proof about purchase vs gift claims without dates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that purchase is preferred over gift in competing claims when no dates are given, and explain the rationale based on ownership transfer.

Core Idea
Purchase transfers ownership immediately, gift requires possession — purchase prevails when dates are unknown.

When two people claim the same property from the same transferor — one by purchase, the other by gift with possession — and both present evidence without dates, the Hanafi school gives priority to the purchase claim. This ruling rests on the fundamental difference in how ownership transfers: sale is effective upon contract alone, while gift requires physical possession (qabd) to complete ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two claims
One party claims purchase (shira'), the other claims gift (hiba) with possession (qabd). Both are from the same transferor.
2
Apply the default assumption for missing dates
When no dates accompany the evidence, we assume both contracts occurred at the same time.
This is a procedural default in evidence law.
3
Compare the legal effect of each contract
Sale transfers ownership immediately upon the contract. Gift only transfers ownership when possession (qabd) is taken.
This is the key Hanafi principle.
4
Determine which ownership arises first
Under the assumption of simultaneous contracts, the sale’s ownership takes effect immediately, while the gift’s ownership is delayed until possession. Therefore, the sale ownership precedes.
Thus, the purchase claim is preferred.
5
State the ruling with conditions
The ruling: purchase claim is preferred. Exception: if the claims are from two different transferors, both testimonies are accepted and property is divided equally.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Purchase vs Marriage Claim on Property
Intra-Hanafi dispute on property claim priority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on a property claim dispute between a purchaser and a wife claiming mahr, and explain the legal reasoning that leads to each position.

Core Idea
Sale requires specification of the object; marriage does not. Therefore Muhammad prefers purchase.

When two claims conflict over the same property—one claiming purchase, the other claiming marriage with that property as mahr—how do we decide? Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the two leading disciples of Abu Hanifa, disagreed. This lesson walks through their reasoning and the key distinction between contracts that require an identified object and those that do not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting claims
One man claims he purchased the property. A woman claims the same man married her and gave her that property as mahr (dower). Both bring witnesses but no dates.
The property is the same asset in both claims.
2
State Abu Yusuf's ruling and reasoning
Abu Yusuf holds that both claims are equal; the property is divided equally between them.
He treats both testimonies as equally strong since no priority can be established.
3
State Muhammad's ruling and reasoning
Muhammad holds that the purchase claim is preferred; the woman gets the value from her husband (not the property).
He reasons that both testimonies can be reconciled: the sale is specific to the property, while marriage can have unspecified mahr.
4
Analyze the legal principle: reconciling testimonies
The principle is to validate both testimonies as much as possible. Muhammad applies this by noting that sale requires specification, marriage does not; therefore the sale contract is specifically attached to this property, while the marriage contract can be fulfilled with any property (the value is owed).
This uses the rule 'tashih al-bayyinah' (validate both proofs).
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Yusuf, the purchaser and the wife split the property. Under Muhammad, the purchaser keeps the property, and the wife gets its monetary value from the husband.
The difference lies in whether the wife gets the specific property or its value.

Possessor's Evidence in Non-Recurring Ownership Causes
Ruling on evidence preference in ownership disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on possessor's evidence in disputes over non-recurring causes of ownership and distinguish it from the views of Muhammad al-Shaybani and Isa ibn Aban.

Core Idea
Possessor's evidence prevails when both claim a non-recurring cause of ownership because possession reinforces the unique proof.

In disputes over ownership, both parties may present evidence of how they came to own the property. When the cause is a one-time event like production or weaving, the Hanafi school gives priority to the current possessor. This rule is rooted in the principle that possession itself is a strong indicator, especially when the claimed origin cannot recur. Let us examine the ruling, the intra-school disagreements, and its application to both recurring and non-recurring causes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Both the outsider (non-possessor) and the possessor present evidence of a specific cause of ownership, such as production (نتاج) or weaving. Determine which party is the possessor (صاحب اليد) and which is the outsider (خارج).
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The possessor's evidence is preferred (فصاحب اليد أولى). This is the correct view according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, and it is the mufta bihi position.
The reasoning is that the cause is non-recurring, making the possessor's evidence more credible alongside possession.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the evidence cancels out and the property is divided equally. Isa ibn Aban holds that the evidences cancel out but the property is left with the possessor, not by final judgment, allowing later evidence from the outsider.
These are legitimate ikhtilaf within the school; the mainstream view is the one relied upon for fatwa.
4
Analyse the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the mainstream view, the outsider cannot later bring new evidence; under Ibn Aban's view, he can. This difference affects the finality of the judgment.
5
Apply the principle to recurring vs non-recurring causes
Non-recurring causes (e.g., production, weaving once, broken vessels) → possessor's evidence preferred. Recurring causes (e.g., hair cloth weaving, building, planting) → judgment goes to outsider. If uncertain, consult experts; if still ambiguous, judgment goes to outsider.
This expansion shows the scope of the ruling.

When Purchase Evidence Overrides Absolute Ownership
Ruling that possessor's evidence of purchase from outsider overrides outsider's evidence of absolute ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the possessor's evidence of purchase from the claimant overrides the claimant's evidence of absolute ownership, and explain the reasoning.

Core Idea
Purchase evidence shows transfer of ownership, overriding earlier absolute ownership claims.

In Islamic courts, disputes over property often involve competing evidence. This lesson examines a key Hanafi rule: when an outsider (claimant) proves he once owned the property, but the possessor proves he bought it from that same outsider, the possessor's evidence wins. The reason is that the purchase indicates a later transfer, which supersedes the earlier ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Recognize the two litigants: the outsider (claimant) who does not possess the property, and the possessor who currently holds it.
In Hanafi terminology, the outsider is 'al-kharij' and the possessor is 'sahib al-yad'.
2
Identify the evidence
The outsider brings evidence of absolute ownership (milk mutlaq), meaning he owned it at some unspecified time. The possessor brings evidence of purchase from that very outsider.
Absolute ownership does not specify a transaction; purchase is a specific cause.
3
State the ruling
The possessor is preferred ('awla'). His evidence takes precedence over the outsider's evidence.
The text says 'kan sahib al-yad awla' – the possessor is more entitled.
4
Understand the rationale
Even if the outsider's evidence proves earlier ownership, the purchase evidence shows a transfer of ownership to the possessor, thereby overriding the earlier claim.
The commentary says 'fa hadha tulqa minhu' – this [purchase] overrides that [earlier ownership].
5
Apply the rule
In practice, this rule applies when both evidence sets are credible. The judge gives priority to the later transaction.
This is a specific application of the broader principle that a later transaction supersedes earlier rights.

Ruling on cancelling conflicting purchase evidences without dates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two purchase evidences conflict without dates and explain why the evidences cancel.

Core Idea
Without dates, contradictory purchase evidences cancel; possession decides.

This passage addresses a common dispute: two parties each claim to have purchased the same property from the other, but neither provides a date. The Hanafi school has a clear rule – the evidences cancel and the property stays with the current possessor. However, Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani offers a different view. We will explore the reasoning and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
Understand that there are two parties: the outsider (one claiming purchase from the possessor) and the possessor (who currently holds the property). Both provide evidence of purchase from each other, but without dates.
The term 'الخارج' (outsider) refers to the one not in possession.
2
Understand the term تهاترت البينتان
The word 'تهاترت' means the two evidences cancel each other out and are treated as non-existent. This is a specific legal outcome.
Cancellation means the judge ignores both evidences.
3
State the majority Hanafi ruling
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (عندهما), the evidences cancel and the property remains in the hands of the possessor (في يد ذي اليد).
This is the mufta bihi position.
4
Note Imam Muhammad's opposing view
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that both evidences are valid, so he presumes the outsider's purchase occurred later and awards the property to the outsider.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
5
Understand why the majority view is preferred
The majority argues that without dates, there is no reason to prefer one transaction over the other. Possession itself provides a basis for keeping the property status quo.
The judge cannot settle the contradiction, so the existing state prevails.

Two Witnesses Equal to Four
Ruling that two witnesses equal four in a claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that two witnesses are equivalent to four in a claim and explain the underlying principle.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, two male witnesses suffice; more do not strengthen the case.

In a legal dispute, it is natural to think that bringing more witnesses is better. However, Hanafi law establishes that once the minimum requirement of two witnesses is met, any additional witnesses do not increase probative value. If one claimant brings two witnesses and the other brings four, they are considered equal. This lesson examines this ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Two claimants each present witnesses: one brings two, the other brings four. Recognize the key question: does the greater number give an advantage?
2
State the Ruling
The text clearly states 'فهما سواء' (they are equal). Both claimants are treated identically despite the difference in witness count.
3
Understand the Principle
The reason given is 'شهادة الأربعة كشهادة الاثنين' (testimony of four is like that of two). The minimum for proof in property claims is two male witnesses (or one man and two women). Exceeding this does not enhance credibility.
This is based on the Hanafi rule that two witnesses are sufficient for proof; more are unnecessary.
4
Appreciate the Legal Effect
The judge will not favor one party over the other based solely on witness numbers. Other factors, such as credibility or documentary evidence, will decide the case.

Items that can be remanufactured are recurring; the outsider's claim prevails over the possessor's.

In the previous lesson, we learned that non-recurring causes of ownership (like animal production) favor the possessor. Now we examine recurring causes: items made from metals and glass. These can be melted and reshaped multiple times, so they are not like production. Consequently, in a dispute, the outsider's evidence is preferred, and judgment goes to him. This means the person not in possession is favored.
3
Understand the legal reason (ta'lil)
The reason is that these items can be fashioned again and again (تُصاغ مرةً بعد أخرى). This makes them recurring, unlike non-recurring causes where the possessor is preferred.
Recurring means the cause of ownership is not unique or fixed to one instance.
4
Apply to a specific example
The text explicitly mentions jewelry (حلي) as an example. A gold bracelet is recurring, so if there is a dispute, the outsider's claim is upheld.
This is consistent with the general rule.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Division by Munaza'a vs 'Awl
Intra-Hanafi dispute over division when both have evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's method of division (munaza'a) and the Sahibayn's method ('awl) in a joint possession dispute with conflicting claims, and apply the distinction between qada al-tark and qada al-ilzam.

Core Idea
Possession limits evidence: Abu Hanifa divides by possession first; the Sahibayn proportion claims as abstract shares.

This passage presents a classical dispute within the Hanafi school about how a qadi should divide property when two claimants both present evidence and both are in possession. One claims the entire house, the other claims half. The disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, centers on whether to give weight to possession or treat claims as abstract shares. Understanding this disagreement reveals deeper principles about evidence acceptance and judicial discretion in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimants and their claims
Note that one claimant (صاحب الجميع) claims the entire house, the other (صاحب النصف) claims half. Both have evidence (بينة) and both are in possession of the house jointly (each holds half de facto).
Possession here means each physically occupies half, not necessarily legal ownership.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's method (munaza'a)
Abu Hanifa first respects the de facto possession: each has half. Evidence from a claimant is accepted only on the portion possessed by the other party. Thus, the full claimant's evidence is accepted on the half in the other's hand, allowing him to take that half by judgment (qada al-ilzam). The remaining half, in his own hand, is not subject to the other's evidence, so it stays with him by default (qada al-tark). The other half (possessed by the full claimant) is then disputed equally, so it is split between them.
This leads to full claimant getting 3/4, half claimant 1/4.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's method ('awl)
The Sahibayn ignore possession and treat the claims as abstract shares. The full claimant claims two shares (the whole), the half claimant claims one share, total three shares. The house is divided proportionally: full claimant gets 2/3, half claimant gets 1/3.
This is analogous to 'awl in inheritance, where the denominator is increased to accommodate all shares.
4
Grasp the key distinction: qada al-tark vs qada al-ilzam
Qada al-ilzam (judgment of compulsion) requires the property to be handed over; evidence is accepted only if the opponent formally delivers. Qada al-tark (judgment of abandonment) allows evidence to be accepted and the property to remain with the claimant without formal transfer. In this case, the half taken from the partner is via qada al-ilzam; the half retained is via qada al-tark.
This distinction is critical in Hanafi procedure and reflects the strength of possession.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is the final distribution. Under Abu Hanifa, full claimant gets 3/4, half claimant gets 1/4. Under the Sahibayn, full claimant gets 2/3, half claimant gets 1/3. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Abu Hanifa's.
The difference of 1/12 arises from the weight given to possession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on the number of validators required for secret and public tazkiyah, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and apply the mufta bihi position to a practical scenario.

Core Idea
Secret tazkiyah: one suffices; public tazkiyah: two required; but Muhammad ibn al-Hasan insists on two for both, analogizing all tazkiyah to testimony.

The validation of witnesses (tazkiyah) is a crucial step before a judge accepts testimony. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on how many validators are needed and who can serve. Understanding this distinction between secret (outside court) and public (in court) tazkiyah is essential for applying Hanafi procedural law correctly.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish secret vs. public tazkiyah
Identify the two contexts: secret tazkiyah occurs outside the court, often as a report to the judge; public tazkiyah takes place in court with formal testimony. This distinction is key to the different rulings.

State the three imams' positions on the number of validators
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that one validator suffices in secret, but two are more precautionary. Muhammad ibn al-Hasan insists on two in all cases. Abu Hanifa also accepts one in secret but adds further conditions on who can validate.

Phrasing of Testimony and Hearing Behind a Veil in Hanafi Law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct phrasing for testimony in Hanafi law and identify the conditions under which hearing from behind a veil is acceptable.

Core Idea
Testimony must precisely match the witness's mode of knowledge; vague phrasing like 'I testify by hearing' is invalid.

In Hanafi fiqh, the wording of testimony is crucial for its acceptance. This passage explains the correct forms for testifying about sales, confessions, and judges' rulings, and addresses the tricky issue of hearing from behind a veil. You will learn the precise language required and the exceptional case where certainty overcomes visual identification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Correct Phrases for Testimony
Note the precise formulations: 'I testify that he sold' for explicit sales, 'I testify that he confessed' for confessions, and 'I testify that the judge ruled' for hearing a judge's verdict in a valid jurisdiction.
The witness must not say 'he made me testify' unless actually asked.
2

For sales conducted by exchange (mu`atah) without verbal offer and acceptance, the witness should describe the act (taking and giving) rather than testifying to the sale itself. An alternative view in al-Dhakhirah permits testifying to the sale.
This preserves accuracy about the witness's direct knowledge.
3

General rule: testimony from behind a veil is invalid because voices can be mistaken. Exception: if the witness is inside the house, knows it is empty, sits at the only door, and hears a confession without seeing, certainty is achieved and testimony is accepted.
The key is that the witness had certainty (yaqin) about the identity of the speaker.
4
Distinguish Valid and Invalid Phrasing
Vague phrasing like 'I testify by hearing' (ashhadu bi al-sama`) is rejected because it does not convey firsthand knowledge. The witness must specify what he witnessed, e.g., 'I testify that he sold' or 'I testify that he confessed.'
The judge cannot act on ambiguous testimony.
5
Apply the Exception for Certainty
The exception for hearing behind a veil requires four conditions: (1) witness is inside the house, (2) knows the house is empty, (3) sits at the only door, (4) hears the confession but does not see the speaker. This yields certainty and allows testimony.
If any condition is missing, testimony is invalid.

Core Idea
Testimony requires certainty; handwriting without memory lacks yaqin, though a sealed diwan may suffice.

This lesson explores an internal Hanafi dispute on whether a witness may testify based solely on recognizing his own handwriting. The majority of the school (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) insist on memory of the actual testimony, while Abu Yusuf allows handwriting-based testimony under certain conditions. Some scholars claim there is no real disagreement: all agree that testifying by handwriting without memory is haram. The actual ikhtilaf is only when a judge finds a testimony in his own sealed register (diwan), which is secure from tampering, giving certainty. An ordinary document (sakk) in another's hand does not provide that certainty.
This resolves the apparent conflict and clarifies the practical scope.
5

The relied-upon (mufta bihi) is the majority view: testifying by handwriting alone is impermissible unless the witness remembers. The practical consequence is that a witness cannot rely on document recognition alone; the judge, however, may use his sealed register.

His statement: (And it is not permissible for the witness, if he sees his handwriting, to testify, unless he remembers the testimony) because handwriting resembles handwriting, so he does not attain certain knowledge of it. This is their view (i.e., Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). And Abu Yusuf said: It is permissible for him to testify. In Al-Hidayah: Muhammad is with Abu Yusuf. And it is said: There is no disagreement among them in this issue; they all agree that it is not permissible for him to testify in the view of all our companions, unless he remembers the testimony. The disagreement is only when the judge finds testimony in his register (diwan), because what is in his box under his seal is safe from addition and subtraction, so he attains knowledge; unlike testimony in a document (sakk), because it is in the hand of another.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a witness may testify solely by seeing his own handwriting. The matn states it is not permissible unless he remembers the testimony. The commentator explains the reasoning: handwriting can be imitated, so there is no certainty. This is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani (qawlahuma). Abu Yusuf, however, permits it. Al-Hidayah aligns Muhammad with Abu Yusuf. Then a reconciling view is reported: there is no real disagreement; all agree that testifying by handwriting without memory is impermissible. The actual dispute concerns a judge finding testimony in his own official register (diwan) which is sealed and secure, giving certainty. Thus the judge could testify from it. But testimony in a private document (sakk) is not similarly reliable because it is in another's hand. The mufta bihi is that handwriting alone is insufficient; memory is required, though the later position (mutakhkhirin) may lean toward accepting handwriting under strict conditions.

Key Terms
الخط (al-khatt): Handwriting; here it refers to the witness's own handwriting that he sees.
ديوان (diwan): Official register or record, especially of a judge, kept under seal and thus considered more reliable.

Checkpoint
According to the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position of the Hanafi school, can a witness testify by simply seeing his own handwriting without remembering the testimony?
A) Yes, as Abu Yusuf permits it.
B) No, because handwriting is not certain evidence.
C) Yes, if the handwriting is in a private document.
D) No, but testimony from a sealed official register is permissible even without memory.

Key Takeaway
Testimony by handwriting alone is impermissible without memory; the only Hanafi exception is for a judge's sealed diwan, where certainty is assured.
