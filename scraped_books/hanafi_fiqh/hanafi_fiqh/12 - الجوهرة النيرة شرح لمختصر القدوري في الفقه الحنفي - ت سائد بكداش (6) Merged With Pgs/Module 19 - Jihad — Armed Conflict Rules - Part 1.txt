Jihad: A Collective Obligation (Fard Kifayah)
Ruling statement with Quranic proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on jihad as fard kifayah and explain its condition and Quranic proofs.

Core Idea
Jihad is a collective obligation—if a sufficient group undertakes it, the rest are absolved.

This passage from Kitab al-Siyar establishes the foundational ruling for jihad in the Hanafi school. It defines the term, states the legal classification, and provides the primary evidences from the Quran. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the obligation in both classical and modern contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states 'الجهاد فرض على الكفاية'. The act is jihad, and the ruling is fard kifayah (collective obligation).
Note the precise wording: 'فرض على الكفاية'.
2
Understand the condition for discharge
The obligation falls from the rest only if a group of people undertakes it and that group is sufficient for the purpose. If they are not sufficient, the obligation remains on all.
The condition is embedded in the phrase 'إذا قام به فريق من الناس: سقط عن الباقين'.
3
Locate the Quranic proofs
The author cites three verses: Quran 2:216 (prescription of fighting), 9:5 (command to kill polytheists), and 8:39 (fight until fitnah ceases). These are the primary evidence for obligation.
These verses are from the category of 'amm (general) commands.
4
Explain the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof establishes the ruling)
The verses use imperative forms that indicate obligation ('kutiba', 'faqtulu', 'qatiluhum'). However, the Hanafi school interprets this obligation as collective due to the context of community defense and the principle that not everyone can be expected to fight.
The Hanafi usul principle distinguishes between fard ayn and fard kifayah based on the nature of the commanded act.
5
Note the inter-madhhab ikhtilaf
Some schools (e.g., some Shafi'i positions) hold jihad to be fard ayn in certain circumstances, especially defensive war. Hanafis maintain it is fard kifayah unless the enemy directly invades a locality and numbers are insufficient.
This is a known ikhtilaf with practical consequences.

Definition of Siyar and Obligation of Jihad as Fard Kifayah
Defining siyar and establishing jihad as fard kifayah with Quranic proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of siyar, identify the Quranic proofs for jihad, and explain why jihad is classified as fard kifayah.

Core Idea
Jihad is a communal obligation, not individual — the duty falls on the community, not every person.

This lesson opens the Book of Siyar in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. We will first clarify what siyar means in Islamic law, then examine the scriptural basis for jihad and its classification as a communal obligation (fard kifayah). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rulings of jihad.

1
Define siyar

The passage says: 'الجهاد فرض على الكفاية'.

Verses use imperative forms indicating obligation in Hanafi usul.
4
Analyze the rationale for fard kifayah
Understand why jihad is not fard 'ayn: if everyone fought, it would disrupt essential societal functions like agriculture and livelihood. The community is responsible unless a sufficient force is lacking.

5
Determine the consequence of neglect
State what happens if no one performs jihad: all people sin. Also explain when the obligation shifts to become individual on those nearest the enemy.

The Obligation of Fighting Disbelievers Even Without Aggression
Explaining that fighting disbelievers is wajib even if they did not initiate hostilities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the obligation of fighting disbelievers and explain why it is not contingent on their aggression using the reasoning from the passage.

Core Idea
Fighting disbelievers is wajib because of their kufr, not because they attack first.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a key distinction in Hanafi jihad: the obligation to fight polytheists is not defensive like fighting among Muslims. Rather, it is based on the very condition of disbelief. We will unpack the logical reasoning and its implications for the nature of jihad in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the Hanafi position: fighting disbelievers is wajib (obligatory) even if they do not initiate hostilities.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the Argument
The commentator uses a logical conditional: if fighting were contingent on initiation, it would be purely defensive.
Read the Arabic carefully: 'لو وقف على مبادأتهم لنا لكان على وجه الدفع'.
3
Understand the Illah
The underlying cause (illah) for fighting polytheists is their kufr, not aggression. This distinguishes it from intra-Muslim fighting.
The illah is derived from general Qur'anic commands to fight until fitnah is removed.
4
Contrast with Intra-Muslim Fighting
Fighting among Muslims is only permissible in self-defense (دفع الأذى). This shows the different legal basis.
The text says: 'وقتال المشركين مخالف لقتال المسلمين'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Jihad is not obligatory on those lacking legal capacity, physical ability, or whose obligations conflict with a superior right.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira enumerates seven groups of people who are not required to participate in jihad. Understanding these exemptions is crucial for correctly applying the fiqh of jihad. The reasoning draws on broader Hanafi principles of legal accountability (taklif) and ability (istita'ah).

Analyze the textual proof for each
For the child and madman, the proof is the lifting of the pen. For the slave, it is the precedence of the master's right and analogy with Hajj and Jumu'ah. For the woman, analogy with Jumu'ah. For the disabled, analogy with Hajj.
4
Apply the principle to similar cases
Consider whether other disabilities or situations (e.g., chronic illness, old age) would also exempt a person using the same reasoning.
5
Confirm the hukm level
All categories are 'not obligatory,' but note that jihad may remain permissible or recommended for some under certain conditions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Accepting Islam grants the same rights and duties as any Muslim — no distinction.

This lesson examines a concise but powerful ruling from the chapter on jihad (siyar). When an enemy combatant expresses acceptance of Islam, their legal standing transforms entirely. Let's explore what this means and why it matters.

The condition is simply offering acceptance of Islam, not a formal process.

Consider what 'what is for the Muslims' includes: inviolability of blood, property, honor; right to marry; duty to pray, fast, etc. There is no partial status.
This is a comprehensive equalization.

Note the immediate effect
The ruling takes effect upon acceptance, not after any verification or waiting period. The person becomes a full Muslim from that moment.
No probationary period.
4
Relate to the broader principle
Islam abrogates previous disbelief (al-Islam yajubbu ma qablahu). This principle underlies the immediate grant of rights.
This is a key maxim in Hanafi fiqh.

Fighting Without Prior Invitation to Islam
Ruling on necessity of da'wah before combat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fighting without prior da'wah and identify the exceptional case when da'wah is not required.

Core Idea
Fighting without da'wah is sinful, but if Islam is already widespread, the imam may forgo a new call.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira establishes a core principle of Islamic warfare: the call to Islam (da'wah) must precede combat. The author cites a Quranic verse and then introduces a later opinion from Al-Yanabi' that adapts this rule for later times. Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing between the classical obligation and the practical concession for later generations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is fighting those who have not received the call of Islam. The ruling: haram (prohibited) and sinful if done without prior da'wah.
The ruling applies to the initiating party.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verse (48:16) is cited: 'We will fight them or they will submit.' This serves as the textual proof.
The exact wording in the source differs slightly from the standard recitation.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The verse gives a binary choice: fight or submit. Hanafi usul interprets the imperative to fight as contingent on the prior call; the call creates the excuse for war.
The verse is considered zanni al-dalala (ambiguous in meaning) regarding the sequence.
4
Identify the exception
Al-Yanabi' states that in later times, when Islam is widespread and known everywhere, the imam is not obliged to call anew; he may choose to fight directly.
This exception is a later Hanafi fatwa, not from the Zahir al-Riwayah.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The classical position requires da'wah, but the later opinion grants discretion to the imam. Both are acknowledged within the school.
The mufta bihi today may follow the later opinion in many contexts.

Renewing the Call to Islam: Recommended, Not Obligatory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on renewing da'wah before fighting and apply the proof from the Prophet's practice.

Core Idea
Once the call has reached, renewing it is mustahabb, not wajib.

This lesson tackles a precise fiqh question: after the message of Islam has already reached a people, must a Muslim commander call them again before engaging in combat? The Hanafi school answers with a clear distinction between the obligatory first call and the recommended renewal. We will examine the textual proof from the Prophet's raid on Banu al-Mustaliq and understand why the school classifies this act as mustahabb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on calling those who have already received the Islamic da'wah. The hukm is 'yustahabb' (recommended), not 'yajib' (obligatory). Recognize the fiqh terminology: mustahabb is below wajib in obligation.
Mustahabb in Hanafi fiqh is equivalent to sunnah ghayr muakkadah — omission is not sinful.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith of the Prophet's raid on Banu al-Mustaliq. He attacked them while they were unaware ('gharun'), without renewing the call. This provides evidence that repeating the call is not required.
The hadith is mutafaq 'alayh (Bukhari and Muslim), so its transmission is certain.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does this act prove the ruling? The Prophet's omission of a second call, despite the opportunity, shows that it is not obligatory. If it were obligatory, he would have done it. His action demonstrates permissibility and, when combined with the general command to call, yields recommendation.
The principle: the Prophet's tacit approval (taqrir) or his action indicates permissibility; if combined with a general virtue, it becomes mustahabb.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (difference of opinion)
Some scholars (notably al-Shafi'i) argue that da'wah before fighting is obligatory (fard kifayah) even if the message has reached, because there might be doubts or distortions. The Hanafi school responds that the purpose of da'wah—informing—has already been achieved.
This difference is thamarah al-khilaf: in practice, Hanafis allow immediate attack if the call is known to have reached; Shafi'is require an explicit renewed call.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi in the school, based on the clear prophetic practice and the principle that obligations are not repeated once fulfilled.
This is the position of the Zahir al-Riwayah and is relied upon in fatwa.

Learning Objective

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira provides a historical-legal commentary on the declaration of Bara'ah (disavowal) in Surah al-Tawbah. Treachery and Expiration of Truces in Hanafi Jihad Rulings
Ruling on fighting without notice and automatic expiration of timed truce

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When the enemy unanimously breaks the treaty, nabdh is not required; a timed truce ends automatically, but safety for those inside Muslim land continues until they reach their place of security.

Islamic law regulates the termination of treaties with non-Muslims with precision. The Hanafi school distinguishes two scenarios where a truce ends: when the enemy commits treachery unanimously, and when a fixed-term truce expires. In both cases, the safety of individuals who entered Muslim territory under the truce is protected by the Quranic command to grant them safe passage. This lesson unpacks these rulings and their proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios for ending a truce
The passage presents two distinct situations: (1) enemy treachery by unanimous action, and (2) expiration of a timed truce. Understand that each has different rules regarding notice (nabdh).
Hanafis require nabdh only when ending a truce without breach; here the breach itself dissolves it.
2
Analyze the condition for fighting without nabdh
When the enemy unanimously initiates treachery (khiyanah), they become covenant-breakers. The Muslim commander may fight them without giving formal notice because the breach itself terminates the treaty.
Unanimity is key: if only some break it, nabdh may still be required.
3
Understand the automatic expiration of a timed truce
For a truce set to a specific duration (muwada'ah bi waqt ma'lum), the treaty ends automatically when the time passes. No nabdh is needed; Muslims may attack after the period ends.
Contrast with an unconditional truce, which requires nabdh to terminate.
4
Apply the Quranic proof to the protection of entrants
Even after the truce expires, an enemy person who entered Muslim territory under the truce remains safe until he returns to his place of security. The proof is Quran 9:6: 'Then give him his place of security.' This verse commands safe conduct for those seeking protection.
The protection (aman) continues during the return journey, not just until treaty expiration.
5

Imam al-Shafi'i reportedly requires notice (nabdh) in all cases of treaty termination, regardless of breach. The Hanafi view, as stated, is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.
The ikhtilaf is based on the interpretation of 'breach' and whether Quran 8:58 (nabdh) applies universally.

Use of Spoils in Battle Without Division: Necessity and Liability
Rulings on using spoils during battle and liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using spoils during battle without division and apply the condition of necessity to determine permissibility and liability.

Core Idea
Using spoils in genuine need during battle is permissible without liability because the right of Muslims is not yet established.

This passage from Kitab al-Siyar addresses a practical battlefield question: may a soldier use captured weapons, horses, or food without waiting for formal division? The answer depends on the principle that ownership of spoils is not finalized until distribution. Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Locate the core ruling: soldiers may fight with weapons from spoils without division. Note that the matn states this absolutely, but the sharh adds the condition of need.
The ruling applies to movable property (weapons, horses, food) before the spoils are formally divided after the campaign.

The text explains: 'because the right of the Muslims has not become established in it.' Ownership is not yet fixed, so using an item in necessity does not create liability if it is lost or consumed.
This is a key Hanafi principle distinguishing pre‑division spoils from individually owned property.

Necessary uses (broken weapon, need for food/fodder, riding to fight) are permissible. Unnecessary uses (using spoils to protect one's own equipment) are blameworthy (makruh) but still carry no liability.
The hadith 'Beware of the compound interest of embezzlement' prohibits ghulul, but necessity overrides the general prohibition via istihsan.

Soldiers may feed their dependents (slaves, women, children). Hired servants may not eat because they are not owed maintenance. Women treating the wounded may eat because they have a right to a share of spoils.
The ruling flows from the obligation of maintenance or from having a right in the spoils.
5
Note the prohibition of selling and the return obligation
Spoils may not be sold for personal profit; only immediate consumption (e.g., selling for food) is allowed. Any item used must be returned after the need ends.
Returning is an obligation (wajib) because the spoils belong to the Muslim community.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which persons and properties a convert from enemy territory secures through Islam, and apply the Hanafi distinction between movable and immovable property.

Core Idea
A convert secures movable property in his valid hand, but land and unharvested crops become community property (fay').

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the legal effects of embracing Islam for a resident of enemy territory (dar al-harb) before the Muslims conquer that land. The ruling balances individual ownership with the community's right over territory, using the key concept of 'valid hand' (yad sahiha). The Prophet's hadith 'Whoever embraces Islam over property, it is his' forms the proof, but its application is nuanced by the nature of the property and who holds it.

1
Identify the persons secured
The convert secures his own life and his minor children. His children become Muslims and free by following his conversion.
This reflects the rule that a child's status follows the better of their parents.

Property under the convert's actual physical possession or deposited with a Muslim or dhimmi is secured because their hand is valid and protected. The hadith 'Man aslama 'ala malin fahuwa lahu' is cited as proof.
Property in the hand of a harbi is not secured; it becomes fay'.
3
Distinguish immovable property
Land and unharvested crops are not secured even if the convert had a claim; they become fay' because they are part of enemy territory under collective control.
Al-Khujandi adds that movable goods like money and slaves remain his, except a fighting slave who becomes fay'.
4
Apply the legal outcome
After conversion and before Muslim conquest, the convert keeps his movable possessions. After conquest, the land becomes community property, and the converts are free.
This prevents individuals from privatizing conquered land.

Validity of Aman by a Discerning Minor
Intra-Hanafi dispute on aman from a discerning minor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the validity of aman by a discerning minor and explain the proofs of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
Aman from a minor: valid if based on fighting eligibility (Muhammad), invalid if based on contractual capacity (Abu Hanifa).

This lesson examines an important intra-Hanafi disagreement: can a minor who has reached the age of discernment and understands Islam grant a valid aman? The dispute turns on whether aman is fundamentally a contract (requiring full legal capacity) or an act of warfare (where the minor's physical participation justifies giving protection). Understanding this ikhtilaf reveals how the Hanafi school weighs different analogies in deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the issue
The act is granting aman (safe passage) by a murahiq (discerning minor who understands Islam). The issue: is this aman valid (binding) or not?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and dalil
Abu Hanifa says the aman is invalid until puberty. His proof: the minor does not own contracts (la yamlik al-'uqood), and aman is a contract among contracts.
Abu Hanifa classifies aman as a contractual act requiring full capacity.
3
State Muhammad al-Shaybani's position and dalil
Muhammad says the aman is valid. His proof: the minor is among the people of fighting (min ahl al-qital), like an adult. Since he can be a combatant, he can also grant protection.
Muhammad analogizes aman to a military action, not a contract.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the minor grants aman, under Abu Hanifa it is not binding, so Muslims may still attack; under Muhammad, it is binding and the enemy is safe.
This difference has life-or-death implications on the battlefield.
5
Note the mufta bihi position
The text does not specify the relied-upon view. In Hanafi fatwa, Abu Hanifa's position generally prevails for contractual acts, but the Imam may assess the minor's maturity in practice.
Later Hanafi scholars often prefer the view that is safest for the community.

The Binding Sanctity of Aman and the Authority of the Imam
Ruling on not killing those granted aman with proof from sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on killing those granted aman, identify the exception of mafsada, and explain why only the Imam may repudiate the aman.

Core Idea
Aman given by any Muslim is inviolable; only the Imam can annul it to prevent grave harm.

This lesson examines a fundamental rule in the law of jihad: the sanctity of aman (safe conduct) granted by a Muslim to an enemy. The Hanafi school holds that aman is a binding contract that cannot be violated by any individual. However, if granting aman leads to mafsada (corruption or weakness for the Muslims), the Imam—not the individual—may formally repudiate the aman. The proof is the famous hadith of Umm Hani, whose aman was upheld by the Prophet ﷺ against her brother Ali's objection.
