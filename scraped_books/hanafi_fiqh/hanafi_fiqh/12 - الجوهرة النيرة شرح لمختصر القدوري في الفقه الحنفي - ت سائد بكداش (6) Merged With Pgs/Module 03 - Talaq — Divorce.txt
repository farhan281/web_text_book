The Drunk Person's Oath is Invalid
Ruling on a drunk person's conditional divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a drunk person's oath and explain why it is not binding.

Core Idea
A drunk person's oath is invalid because he lacks legal capacity (ahliyyah).

In Hanafi fiqh, a person who is intoxicated to the point of losing his intellect is considered to have no valid intent. Therefore, any oath, divorce, or contract he makes is void. This lesson examines a specific scenario from the matn to illustrate this principle and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act
Read the passage and determine what act the drunk person performed (giving a dirham, making a conditional divorce). The ruling: the divorce does not occur.
The act is a conditional divorce (ta'liq al-talaq).
2
Recognize the underlying cause
The drunk person lacks 'aql (intellect). In Hanafi usul, valid intent (niyyah) and understanding are prerequisites for binding statements.
Intoxication nullifies legal capacity (ahliyyat al-ada).
3
Apply the general principle
This ruling extends to all statements by a drunk person: contracts, oaths, divorces, and acknowledgments. They are all invalid.
There is no distinction between conditional and absolute statements.
4
Contrast with other schools
Some other madhhabs hold that if the drunk person was not forced to drink, his statements may be valid. Recognize that Hanafi position is mufta bihi.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.

Conditional Divorce Oath and Death of a Spouse
Rulings on conditional divorce oath when one spouse dies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a conditional divorce oath when the husband or wife dies, and explain the reasoning based on qiyas.

Core Idea
A conditional divorce oath triggers at the moment of death if death prevents fulfillment.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a conditional divorce oath when either the husband or the wife dies before the condition is fulfilled. The ruling builds on earlier principles regarding oaths and the moment of divorce. We explore why the divorce occurs at the last moment of the wife's life but not upon the husband's death, and the consequences for inheritance and waiting period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the husband has sworn a conditional divorce (e.g., 'You are divorced if you do not go to Basra') and then one spouse dies before the condition is fulfilled.
Two sub-cases: husband dies first, or wife dies first.
2
Apply the principle from earlier oath rulings
Recall the earlier ruling: if a condition becomes impossible due to death, the divorce triggers at the last moment of the person whose action is impossible. Here, the wife's action (going to Basra) becomes impossible upon her death.
The qiyas is to the oath to go to Basra: the condition is tied to the wife's action.
3
Distinguish husband's death vs. wife's death
If the husband dies first, the condition is not impossible from the wife's side; she could still fulfill it. Hence, no divorce. If the wife dies first, she can no longer fulfill, so divorce occurs at the last moment of her life.
The difference hinges on whose action is conditioned.
4
Determine inheritance and waiting period
If the husband dies first and consummation had occurred, the wife inherits and observes the longer waiting period (iddah ab'ad al-ajalayn). If the wife dies, the husband does not inherit because the divorce forfeits his right.
Waiting period for the wife is like that of a forsaken woman (al-far).
5
Understand the legal reasoning (qiyas)
The analogy is drawn to the case of an oath to go to Basra: the condition is considered fulfilled at the last moment if death prevents fulfillment. This ensures the divorce takes effect before death, clarifying inheritance rights.
No istihsan overrides this qiyas according to the text.

Effects of Suspended Divorce Oaths: Death and Inheritance
Rulings on consequences of divorce oaths and death of parties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on inheritance, 'iddah, and the validity of divorce when a suspended divorce oath is involved and one or both parties die.

Core Idea
A suspended divorce becomes effective at the last moment of life if the wife dies before fulfilling the condition, forfeiting inheritance.

This passage from al-Yanabee' and al-Karkhi addresses two key scenarios: first, the inheritance and 'iddah consequences of a suspended divorce oath when the marriage is or is not consummated; second, the effect of the death of either spouse before the condition is met. These rulings are essential for understanding how ta'liq al-talaq interacts with death, inheritance, and 'iddah in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary scenarios
The passage covers two main cases: (1) a husband swears by divorcing his wife, which suspends the divorce on a condition; (2) one of the spouses dies before the condition is fulfilled. Separate the rulings for each.
The first scenario is from al-Yanabee'; the second from al-Karkhi.
2
Distinguish between consummated and non-consummated marriages
Note that if the marriage was not consummated (lam yadkhul biha), the wife neither inherits nor observes 'iddah. If consummated, she inherits and observes the longer of two 'iddahs: the divorce 'iddah or the death 'iddah.
This is analogous to the deserting husband (al-farr).
3
Apply the ruling when the husband dies first
If the husband dies before the wife fulfills the condition, the suspended divorce does not occur (lam tutallaq). The condition is not considered breached because the husband's death ends the possibility of fulfillment.
The text says: 'because the condition for fulfillment becomes impossible by her death' in the previous sentence, but here it's the husband's death that prevents the condition from being fulfilled.
4
Apply the ruling when the wife dies first
If the wife dies before the husband and before fulfilling the condition, she is considered divorced in the very last moment of her life (talqat fi akhir juz' min ajza' hayatiha). Consequently, the husband does not inherit from her because his oath caused the divorce.
This is the key ruling: death does not prevent the suspended divorce from taking effect; it is deemed to occur at the instant before death.

Oath in a Claim of Divorce
Ruling on the form of oath for a divorce claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct form of oath for a husband denying a divorce claim and explain why it is based on istihsan.

Core Idea
The oath negates current irrevocable separation, not the historical divorce act.

When a wife claims divorce and the husband denies, the Hanafi school prescribes a specific form of oath that avoids falsehood. The husband swears about the present status of the marriage, not about whether he ever uttered the divorce. This ruling, grounded in istihsan, accounts for possibilities like revocable return or remarriage after triple divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The husband's denial of a divorce claim is a legal dispute. The ruling is that the husband must take an oath (wajib). Determine what exactly the oath should cover.
The act is the oath in a divorce claim.
2
Locate the dalil
The author provides reasoning based on istihsan (juristic preference). The proof is not a direct textual evidence but a rational consideration of avoiding false oaths and reconciling possibilities.

The proof focuses on the possibility that the husband may have divorced and taken back (raj'ah) or that a triple divorce was followed by remarriage to another man. The oath on 'she is not bain' is always true if the marriage is intact at present.

While the Hanafi position is clear, other schools might require a different oath. For example, the Shafi'i school might hold that the husband swears that he did not divorce, but the Hanafi approach considers the possibility of reconciliation.

Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih

Legal Stratagem and Maintenance in Marriage Claims
Ruling on legal stratagem and maintenance in marriage claims

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Hanafi legal stratagem to avoid an oath in a marriage claim and state the ruling on maintenance when marriage is denied.

Core Idea
A woman can avoid an oath by marrying another man; denial of marriage eliminates maintenance.

If a man claims a woman is his wife and she denies, she may use a permitted stratagem to avoid swearing. Additionally, during litigation, the wife receives no maintenance whether she is the claimant or defendant, because her denial of the marriage is considered worse than recalcitrance.

2
Identify the hila
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, the woman can marry another man. After that, she is no longer required to take an oath for the original claimant.
This is a legal stratagem permitted in Hanafi fiqh.
3

Once she marries another, the original claimant cannot force her to swear, because the marriage may have been valid.
The hila relies on the fact that she is now someone else's wife.

Cancellation of Jizyah upon Islam or Death: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Ruling on cancellation of jizyah upon conversion or death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that jizyah falls due upon conversion to Islam or death, explain the reasoning, and contrast with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Jizyah is a punishment/humiliation that is removed by Islam; Shafi'i sees it as a financial right that survives.

This lesson examines a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: if a dhimmi converts to Islam or dies while jizyah is due, does the obligation fall off? The Hanafi school says yes, because jizyah is a punishment or humiliation that is incompatible with Islam or death. The Shafi'i school says it is a financial obligation that is not cancelled. We will explore the proofs and practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on conversion and death
According to the text, if a dhimmi converts to Islam while having jizyah due, the jizyah falls from him. Similarly, if he dies as a dhimmi, the jizyah is cancelled and not taken from his estate.
This applies to the jizyah that was due but not yet collected.
2
Identify the two reasons given
The text provides two possible reasons: (1) jizyah is a punishment (uquba), so it falls with Islam like the death penalty (qatl), or (2) jizyah is for humiliation (idhlal), which is removed by the honor of Islam.
Both reasons lead to the same ruling.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Al-Shafi'i holds that jizyah does not fall in either case—if the dhimmi converts, the past due jizyah remains; if he dies, it is taken from his estate.
The text says 'هذا كله عندنا' (all this is according to us) indicating the Hanafi position.
4
Analyze the underlying principle
The Hanafi reasoning treats jizyah as a 'punitive' or 'humiliating' obligation, which is extinguished by the removal of the cause (kufr) through Islam or death. Shafi'i treats it as a financial right akin to a debt, which is not extinguished by the debtor's conversion or death.
This difference stems from the nature of jizyah: is it a penalty or a fiscal duty?
5
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If a dhimmi converts, the Hanafi school drops any outstanding jizyah; the Shafi'i school would still demand it. Similarly, if he dies, Hanafi does not take it from the estate, while Shafi'i does.
This impacts the estate of a deceased dhimmi and the treatment of converts.

Tadakhul of Jizyah Years: Merging of Obligations
Ruling on tadakhul (merging) of jizyah when consecutive years are unpaid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of tadakhul (merging) as applied to jizyah, and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa treats jizyah like punishment (only one due when years accumulate); Sahibayn treat it like a debt (each year due separately).

This lesson examines the ruling when two years of jizyah are due but unpaid. Does the obligation merge, so only one year's jizyah is taken? Imam Abu Hanifa says yes, analogizing to hudud punishments: multiple punishments of the same type merge into one. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) say no, each year's jizyah is a separate financial right, like debts or rent, so both must be paid. We will explore the reasoning and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Define tadakhul in this context
Tadakhul (intercalation) means that when multiple obligations of the same kind accumulate, they merge into a single obligation. Here, if two consecutive years of jizyah are unpaid, Abu Hanifa says only one jizyah is taken.
The term literally means 'one enters into the other'.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa: only one jizyah is due. Reason: jizyah is a punishment (uquba). When two punishments of the same type accumulate, they merge, just like hudud (e.g., multiple thefts only incur one hadd).
The analogy is to hudud, which are punishments.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: both years' jizyah must be paid. Reason: jizyah is a financial right (haqq fi mal). Financial rights do not merge; they accumulate like debts, kharaj, and rent.
Each year creates an independent obligation.
4
Note the exception: death
If the dhimmi dies during the period (whether at the end of the year or during the year), no jizyah is taken according to all (ittiqaq). This is because jizyah falls due upon death, as seen in the previous lesson.
This shows the interplay with the cancellation rule.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
If a dhimmi has not paid jizyah for two years and then pays, according to Abu Hanifa he pays only one year's amount; according to Sahibayn he pays two years. This affects the treasury and the dhimmi's liability.
Also affects the timing of when jizyah is considered due.

Likewise, if a dhimmi dies while having jizyah due, it falls from him for the past [period], and it is not taken from his estate, and all this is according to us. Al-Shafi'i said: It does not fall from him in both cases, I mean if he becomes Muslim or dies as a disbeliever. His statement: (And if two years accumulate on him, the jizyah intercalates), meaning one enters into the other, and only one jizyah is taken. This is according to Abu Hanifa, because when jizyah was due on him in the first year and was not taken until the next year entered and another jizyah became due, two punishments of the same kind have accumulated, so it is necessary to limit to one, like hudud. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: It is taken from him, because it is a right in property, so they do not intercalate, like debts, kharaj, and rent. If he dies at the completion of the year, it is not taken from him, in all of their statements. Likewise, if he dies during the year. It is said: Kharaj of land follows this difference, and it is said: There is no intercalation in it by agreement. It is said in al-Yanabi': Jizyah is due at the beginning of the year according to Abu Hanifa, except that it is taken at the end of the year before its completion, with a day or two remaining.

Commentary
This passage is from the chapter on jizyah and the rulings of ahl al-dhimmah. It addresses three main issues: exemptions from jizyah, cancellation of jizyah upon Islam or death, and whether jizyah accumulates (tadakhul) over multiple years. The author begins with the basic ruling: women, minors, the chronically ill, the blind, the poor unable to work, and monks who isolate themselves are exempt. The reasoning is that jizyah is a substitute for fighting, and these individuals lack the capacity to fight. Abu Yusuf argues that if such persons (like the blind or chronically ill) are wealthy, they should still pay because they could potentially be killed if they have judgment (i.e., they are capable of causing harm). The Hanafi response (the author's position) is that they are not fighters, so they resemble women and children. Next, slaves and those with special status (mukatib, mudabbar, umm walad) are also exempt, and their masters do not pay on their behalf. The ruling on monks is conditional: if they are able to work, they pay; if not, they do not. The second issue: if a dhimmi converts to Islam or dies, any jizyah due falls off; it is not taken from the estate. This is the Hanafi position. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, holding that jizyah is a financial right that is not cancelled by conversion or death. The third issue: if two years pass without payment, Abu Hanifa rules that only one jizyah is due (tadakhul), analogizing to hudud punishments. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that both years must be paid, analogizing to debts and rent. The author notes that if the person dies at year-end or during the year, no jizyah is taken by all. There is a separate opinion that kharaj follows the same dispute, but some say no tadakhul in kharaj by consensus. Finally, a quote from al-Yanabi' clarifies that Abu Hanifa considers jizyah due at the beginning of the year, but it is only taken at the end.

Key Terms
تداخل (tadakhul): The merging of obligations of the same type such that only one is due (e.g., jizyah over two years).
