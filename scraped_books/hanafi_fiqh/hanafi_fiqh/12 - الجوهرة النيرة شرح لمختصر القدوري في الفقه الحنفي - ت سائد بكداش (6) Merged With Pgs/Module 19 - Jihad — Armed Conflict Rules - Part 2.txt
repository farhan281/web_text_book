Aman of a Discerning Minor (Murahiq) – Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of aman from a discerning minor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the validity of a murahiq's aman, explain the proofs of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of their disagreement.

Core Idea
A discerning minor who fights like an adult may not contract like one — contractual capacity is stricter than combat eligibility.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his student Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. At issue is whether a discerning minor (murahiq) who understands Islam can grant a valid aman (safe conduct) to an enemy combatant. Abu Hanifa holds that the minor's aman is invalid because contractual capacity is required; Muhammad argues that the minor is eligible to fight, so his aman should be honored. Follow along as we unpack the reasoning and its practical implications for jihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
The passage deals with the validity of aman from a murahiq. Abu Hanifa says invalid until puberty; Muhammad says valid. State each position precisely.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the minor does not own contracts (la yamlik al-'uqud), and aman is a contract. Understand the principle: contractual capacity requires full legal capacity.
3
Examine Muhammad's proof
Muhammad argues that the minor is ahl al-qital (people of fighting), like an adult. Therefore, his aman is valid. Recognize the analogy: fighting ability implies ability to grant aman.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical consequence: if a murahiq gives aman, according to Abu Hanifa the enemy may still be fought; according to Muhammad they must be honored. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about the validity of aman given by a murahiq (a minor who is discerning, close to puberty, and capable of understanding Islam). According to Abu Hanifa, such a minor's aman is not valid until he reaches puberty. His reasoning: the minor lacks the legal capacity to enter into contracts, and aman is a contract. Therefore, just as a minor cannot make a sale or purchase, he cannot grant a binding aman. Imam Muhammad disagrees, arguing that the minor who understands Islam is like an adult in that he is eligible to participate in fighting (ahl al-qital). Since the minor can be a combatant, he should be able to grant aman, which is an act ancillary to warfare. Abu Hanifa's position relies on the principle that only those with full legal capacity can undertake contractual obligations; aman, though related to war, is still a contract. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa: the minor's aman is not valid until puberty, though it may be considered effective if ratified later. This dispute shows how the Hanafi masters balance general principles of legal capacity with specific considerations of jihad.

Aman from a dhimmi, captive, merchant under duress, or slave (unless permitted to fight) is invalid.

Aman (safe conduct) is a serious guarantee of security. However, not every Muslim can give it. The Hanafi school lists persons whose aman is invalid due to lack of authority or duress. Core Idea
Non-Muslims can validly own each other as captives; Islamic law recognizes this ownership.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a specific scenario: the Turks defeat the Romans and take them captive. The ruling is not about Muslim conduct but about the legal status of those captives in Islamic law. Understanding this helps clarify that Islamic law acknowledges property rights even between non-Muslim belligerents, which has implications for later Muslim acquisition.

Stray Animal Captured in Enemy Territory
Ruling with commentary on ownership transfer of stray animal to enemy territory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a stray animal captured by the enemy and explain the rationale based on the absence of independent will.

Core Idea
A stray animal captured by the enemy becomes their property because it has no independent will.

This lesson examines a specific property ruling from the Hanafi school: when a camel (or any animal without intellect) strays into enemy territory and is captured, ownership transfers to the enemy. The reasoning hinges on the concept of 'hand' (possession) and the animal's lack of independent will. We'll explore the ruling, its rationale, and how it differs from a slave who has intellect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize the case: a camel (ba'ir) strays (nadda) to enemy territory (dar al-harb) and is captured (akhadhū).
The term 'nadda' implies fleeing or escaping without human control.
2
State the Ruling
The text states 'malakūhu' (they own it). The Hanafi school holds that the enemy becomes the owner upon capture.
This ruling is agreed upon by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
Understand the Rationale
The rationale is that the animal has no 'hand' (yad) that appears when it goes out—i.e., it lacks independent will. The enemy's physical control (istīlā') transfers ownership, as they take it from the owner's hand.
The principle is that for animals without intellect, the owner's possession is not considered to persist after the animal strays.
4
Distinguish from a Slave
A slave has intellect ('aql) and independent will, so the owner's hand is presumed to continue, and the enemy does not automatically own a captured slave.
The text explicitly says 'bikhilāf al-'abd' (unlike a slave).

This lesson examines a nuanced scenario in the law of jihad: a slave escapes to enemy territory with valuable property, both are captured and sold to a Muslim, who then brings them back to Islamic lands. Ruling on a Harbi Purchasing a Muslim Slave and Taking Him to Dar al-Harb
Intra-Hanafi dispute on legal construction using qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the freedom of a Muslim slave taken to dar al-harb by a harbi, explain Abu Hanifa's qiyas to the dissolution of marriage upon conversion, and evaluate the Sahibayn's objection.

Core Idea
A change of abode can substitute for emancipation in special cases to free a Muslim captive.

Imagine a Muslim slave purchased by a harbi who enters dar al-Islam under aman and then takes the slave to dar al-harb. Is the slave free? The sale is valid, but freeing a Muslim from enemy territory is a duty. Abu Hanifa ingeniously uses a legal construction (hila) by analogizing to the case where a change of abode dissolves a marriage. His two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, reject this analogy. In this lesson, we explore the reasoning behind both positions and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal dilemma
Consider a harbi who comes to dar al-Islam with aman, buys a Muslim slave, and then takes the slave to dar al-harb. The slave is lawfully owned, but there is a religious duty to free Muslim captives. How can the slave be freed without violating property rights?
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and its basis
Abu Hanifa rules that the slave becomes free. His basis: freeing a Muslim is wajib. Since direct emancipation is not possible, the condition (tabayun al-darayn) is made to stand in place of the cause (the act of freeing).
This is a classic hila (legal construction).
3
Understand the qiyas used
Abu Hanifa analogizes to the case of spouses in dar al-harb when one converts to Islam. There, the marriage is dissolved after three menstrual periods because the difference of abodes substitutes for divorce. Similarly, here the condition substitutes for emancipation.
The asl is the spouse case, the far is the slave case, and the illa is tabayun al-darayn standing in place of a legal cause.
4
Note the Sahibayn's objection
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad reject the analogy. They hold that the condition cannot be equated with the act of emancipation; the slave remains a slave. Their view is based on a stricter reading of the requirement of explicit emancipation.
For the Sahibayn, property rights are not set aside by a mere change of location.
5
Consider the practical consequence
The disagreement leads to different outcomes: under Abu Hanifa, the slave is free and the harbi loses his property; under the Sahibayn, the harbi retains ownership. Later Hanafi fatwa often prefers the Sahibayn's view, but Abu Hanifa's position is a well-known exercise of istihsan.
This is a key example of intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf in siyar.

Nafal Rules: Phrasing and Khums Implications
Explains nafal conditions regarding khums

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on nafal given to a raiding party, distinguish between phrasing that includes 'after the fifth' and those that do not, and apply the rule that after ihraz, nafal is only from the khums.

Core Idea
Nafal is deducted before khums unless specified 'after the fifth'; after ihraz, only from khums.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the rules of nafal (additional incentive) given to a raiding party. The imam may promise a portion of the booty, but the phrasing determines whether that portion is subject to the khums deduction. After the booty is brought to dar al-Islam, the rights of all soldiers are fixed, so nafal can only come from the imam's share of the khums.

Guided Path
1
Understand 'after the fifth'
When the imam says 'I give you the quarter after the fifth', it means the quarter is taken from the booty after the khums (one-fifth for the public treasury) has been deducted. The recipients get that quarter without further khums deduction on it.
This ensures the public treasury's share is protected.
2
Contrast without 'after the fifth'
If the imam simply says 'you have the quarter', the quarter is taken from the total booty before khums, and the nafal (the quarter) itself is subject to khums. The recipients must pay a fifth of that quarter as khums.
The phrase 'after the fifth' shifts when the khums applies.
3
Apply the same rule to salb
The same distinction applies to the salb (spoils of a killed enemy): if the imam says 'whoever kills has his salb' without 'after the fifth', the salb is not subject to khums; if he adds 'after the fifth', the salb becomes subject to khums.
The salb is treated as a type of nafal.
4
State the rule after ihraz
Once the booty is safely moved to dar al-Islam (ihraz al-ghanimah), the rights of all soldiers are fixed. No nafal can be given from the general booty; nafal is only allowed from the imam's discretionary portion within the khums (the fifth of the fifth).
This prevents encroaching on soldiers' shares.

Salab: Definition and the Hanafi Position on Its Distribution
Explains the definition of salab and the dispute over its distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define salab in Hanafi fiqh, state the Hanafi ruling that salab is part of general booty unless explicitly assigned, and explain the Shafi'i counter-argument and the Hanafi response.

Core Idea
Salab is not automatically the killer's – it is general booty unless the imam stipulates otherwise.

A widespread misconception is that a warrior automatically keeps the belongings of the enemy he kills. The Hanafi school takes a different stance: the salab (clothes, weapons, mount) is just like other booty, subject to the imam's discretion. This lesson examines the definition and the scholarly difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Define salab
Identify what constitutes salab: items on the slain enemy's person (clothes, weapons) and his mount (the horse he rode). Exclude remount, servant, and items on other mounts.
The definition is crucial: what is not salab is general booty.
2
State the Hanafi default ruling
If the imam does not explicitly assign the salab to the killer, it is part of the general ghanimah, and the killer has no priority over others.
The phrase 'والقاتل وغيره فيه سواء' emphasizes equality.
3
Introduce the Shafi'i view
Al-Shafi'i holds that if the enemy is killed while advancing (muqbil, not mudbir), the killer automatically gets the salab.
This is based on a different interpretation of prophetic practice.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response using the story of al-Bara'
The story shows that Umar (RA) treated the salab as part of booty subject to khums when it was large, implying no automatic right for the killer.
Umar's words: 'We used not to khums the salabs' indicate the default was no automatic taking.
5
Determine the practical difference
In Hanafi, the killer gets the salab only if the imam says so; in Shafi'i, he automatically gets it for a advancing enemy. This changes how incentives are structured.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Distinction Between Salb and Ghanimah; Returning Surplus Spoils
Rules of salb and returning leftover spoils

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between salb (personal spoils) and general ghanimah, and apply the rules for returning leftover spoils based on wealth status and time of division.

Core Idea
Salb is the killer's exclusive prize; everything else is shared ghanimah. Leftovers must be returned, with obligations differing by wealth.

This passage continues the discussion on ghanimah by clarifying what belongs to the individual killer (salb) and what must be shared. It then addresses a practical scenario: a soldier has leftover food or fodder after leaving Dar al-Harb. The ruling distinguishes between wealthy and poor fighters, and before versus after the formal division of spoils.

Guided Path
1
Define salb vs ghanimah
Salb (personal spoils) includes items directly on the slain enemy's body at the time of killing (weapons, armor, jewelry). It belongs exclusively to the killer. Everything else from the battlefield is general ghanimah.
The text gives examples: items on a spare horse, servant's mount, or another horse are not salb.
2
Apply the rule to the story of al-Bara' ibn Malik
Al-Bara' killed the marzuban and took his valuable belt and gold. 'Umar initially notes they used not to take khums from salb, but because of the large value, they took khums. This illustrates that salb is not subject to khums unless the value is extraordinary.
This is a historical precedent for the rule of khums on salb.
3
Identify the ruling on leftover spoils
Whoever has leftover food or fodder from the ghanimah after leaving Dar al-Harb must return it to the common pool because necessity has ceased.
The ruling applies to any unused portion.
4
Differentiate between wealthy and poor, and before vs after division
For the wealthy: before division, return the item to the ghanimah; after division, give its value in charity. For the poor: before division, return; after division, nothing is required because he is eligible for charity.
The poor's obligation is lighter due to his poverty.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Horseman's Shares in Ghanimah
Ruling on division of spoils: khums and shares for horsemen and foot soldiers with dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the horseman's share in ghanimah and identify the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa gives the horseman two shares; the Sahibayn give three — the result is a one-share difference per horseman.

The division of ghanimah (spoils of war) follows a fixed procedure: one-fifth (khums) is set aside for specified recipients, and the remaining four-fifths are distributed among the fighters. While all agree on the foot soldier's single share, there is an important intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the horseman's allotment. This lesson unpacks that dispute and its practical impact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the procedure of ghanimah division
The Imam first extracts the khums (one-fifth) from the total spoils, then distributes the remaining four-fifths among those who participated in the battle.
The khums is wajib based on Quran 8:41.
2
State the share of the foot soldier (rajil)
All scholars agree that the foot soldier receives one share from the four-fifths portion. This is the baseline.
3
Present the position of Imam Abu Hanifa on the horseman
According to Abu Hanifa, the horseman receives two shares: one for himself and one for his horse. This view is also held by Zufar, al-Hasan ibn Ziyad, and the Kufan/Basran scholars.
The horse is considered a contributor to the effort, hence a separate share.
4
Present the position of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the horseman receives three shares. The exact allocation is not fully detailed in the text, but the total is three shares from the four-fifths.
The Sahibayn likely consider the horse's equipment or increased effectiveness as justifying an extra share.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The difference of one share per horseman affects the total distribution: if there are many horsemen, the overall shares shift significantly. The foot soldier's share remains unchanged.
This dispute does not affect the khums or the foot soldier's entitlement.

Spoils Shares: The Rider's Two Shares vs. the Hijazi View
Ruling on cavalry share with inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and defend the Hanafi ruling on spoils shares for cavalry and infantry, and explain the Hanafi response to the Hijazi position using hadith and rational argument.

Core Idea
The rider receives two shares based on prophetic practice, not analogy.

This lesson examines a key difference between the Hanafi school and the scholars of the Hijaz regarding the distribution of war spoils (ghanimah). While both agree that a cavalryman should receive more than an infantryman, they disagree on the horse's share. The Hanafis, following the Prophet's action at Khaybar, give the rider two shares (one for himself, one for his horse), while the Hijazis argue the horse deserves a larger share due to higher maintenance costs. We will explore the arguments and the Hanafi resolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
Determine the exact point of contention: the amount of the horse's share relative to the rider's share.
The Hijazis claim the horse's share should exceed the rider's; Hanafis disagree.
2
State the Hanafi ruling and its textual basis
Recall that the Hanafi ruling gives the rider two shares total (one for himself, one for his horse) and the infantry one share. Cite the Khaybar hadith as the primary proof.
The hadith is the main textual evidence, though it is ahad (zanni thubut).
3
Present the Hijazi argument
The Hijazis use qiyas: since the horse's maintenance is more costly, it should receive a larger share than the rider.
This is an argument from analogy based on expense (talil bi almuna).
4
Explain the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa responds that pure analogy would deny any share to a tool; we follow the hadith. Since reports vary (some say two shares, some say three), we take the agreed minimum. Additionally, the rider is more essential in combat.
The response has two parts: (a) textual reliance and (b) rational preference for the rider.
5
Calculate the practical consequence
In the Khaybar distribution, cavalry got one-third of the spoils and infantry two-thirds. This ratio follows from the two-share rule.
If the horse had a larger share, cavalry would receive a greater proportion.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah expands on the Hanafi ruling regarding the distribution of khums (one-fifth) from war booty. While the Quran (8:41) lists five categories, the Hanafi school consolidates them into three shares for needy recipients, with the poor among the Prophet's relatives (dhu al-qurba) being included and given priority. The text also clarifies that the Prophet's personal share and his right to a special selection (safi) fell away with his death, and identifies which clans among the Quraysh are entitled to the relatives' share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three shares
The text states that khums is divided into three shares: a share for orphans (with poverty as a condition), a share for the poor (masakin), and a share for wayfarers (ibn al-sabil). These are the only categories for distribution.
Contrary to the apparent five categories in the verse, Hanafis interpret 'ilallah' and 'lir-rasul' as not separate distributions.
2
Understand the inclusion of poor relatives
The poor among the relatives of the Prophet (dhu al-qurba) are included in these three shares according to their specific need (orphan, poor, or wayfarer). They are given priority over non-relatives because Allah mentioned them first in the verse.
Wealthy relatives receive nothing; entitlement is based on poverty and need.
3
Examine the Prophet's share and safi
The share of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and his right to select an item (safi) from the booty fell away with his death. The relatives now deserve the share only by poverty, not by virtue of support (nusrah) as during his lifetime.
The safi was a special privilege abrogated after the Prophet's death.
4
Identify the entitled clans
The relatives' share is restricted to Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib, to the exclusion of Banu Abd Shams and Banu Nawfal. This is supported by the hadith of Jubayr ibn Mut'im.
The Prophet explained that these two clans are one in help (nusrah) and lineage.

Khums Shares and the Basis for Relatives' Entitlement
Explains the shares of khums and the basis for entitlement of the Prophet's relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five allocations of khums, explain that the Prophet's share ceased upon his death, and apply the hadith evidence to identify Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib as the exclusive recipients of the relatives' share based on their continuous support (nusrah).

Core Idea
Entitlement to the Prophet's relatives' share of khums is based on continuous support (nusrah), not mere kinship.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies the distribution of the one-fifth of war booty (khums) as stated in the Quran. We learn that the share ostensibly reserved for Allah is merely a blessing opener, the Prophet's own share ceased upon his death, and the share for his relatives (dhawi al-qurba) depends on their support during his life and their poverty thereafter. The key point is that only Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib qualify, as proven by the Prophet's own words and actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five shares of khums mentioned in the Quran
Open Quran 8:41. Note the five recipients: Allah (for His cause), the Messenger, relatives, orphans, the needy, and the wayfarer. The passage explains that Allah's share is for opening the speech, and the Prophet's share is dropped after his death.
The Hanafi school treats the five shares as distinct categories, with the Prophet's share ceasing after his death.
2
Understand the status of the Prophet's share and the safi after his death
The Prophet's share (sahm al-rasul) and the special selection (safi) were specific to him during his lifetime. After his death, they are discontinued.
This is unique to the Prophet; no other leader has such rights.
3
Analyze the basis for the relatives' entitlement: nusrah then poverty
During the Prophet's lifetime, relatives qualified through their support (nusrah) of him. After his death, the criterion shifts to poverty. This ensures the share benefits those who truly need it.
Nusrah includes both material and moral support from pre-Islamic times through Islam.
4
Determine which relatives are included and why
The Hanafi position restricts the share to Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib, excluding other branches of Banu Abd Manaf (Banu Abd Shams and Banu Nawfal). The reason is found in the hadith: these two clans never separated from the Prophet in Jahiliyyah or Islam.
The proof is the Prophet's interlocking of his fingers and his statement that they are 'one thing'.
5
Examine the hadith proofs and their indication that nusrah is the criterion
The hadith of Jubayr ibn Mut'im and the second hadith with Uthman and Jubayr show that the Prophet justified giving to Banu al-Muttalib because of their continuous support, not merely blood relation. This establishes nusrah as the operative cause (illah).
Thus, entitlement is not by lineage alone but by actual historical support.

Types and Amounts of Jizyah: A Hanafi Classification
States the two types of jizyah and the amounts per economic class

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of jizyah, list the three economic categories and their annual amounts, and apply the majority-of-year rule to determine jizyah liability.

Core Idea
Jizyah is compensation for protection, varying by wealth; the majority of the year sets the rate.

Jizyah is one of the key obligations upon dhimmis in an Islamic state. In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi classification of jizyah into treaty-based and imam-imposed, along with the precise amounts based on the dhimmi's economic status. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly applying the rules of jizyah in contemporary and historical contexts.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of jizyah
Identify treaty-based jizyah (fixed by mutual consent) and imam-imposed jizyah (set by the Imam upon conquest). The first is determined by agreement, the second by the Imam's ijtihad based on economic status.
Treaty-based amounts are not changed unilaterally; the Prophet's treaty with Najran is the example.
2
Identify the three economic categories and their amounts
Memorize the three classes: rich (48 dirhams/year), middle (24 dirhams/year), working poor (12 dirhams/year). Note the monthly breakdown given in the text.
These amounts are for imam-imposed jizyah; treaty amounts may differ.
3
Define the thresholds for each category
The evidently rich is defined as owning 10,000 dirhams or more; the middle class owns 200 dirhams or more but still needs to work; the working poor owns less than 200 dirhams and works for a living.
These thresholds differ from zakat thresholds; they are based on ability to pay jizyah.
4
Apply the majority-of-year rule
If the dhimmi is rich for most of the year, he pays the rich rate; if poor for most of the year, he pays the poor rate. If sick for most of the year, no jizyah is due; if healthy for most of the year, jizyah is due.
The rule is based on the principle 'the majority takes the ruling of the whole' (للأكثر حكم الكل).
5
Understand the half-year sickness ruling
If sickness equals exactly half the year, the obligating factor (health) and canceling factor (sickness) are equal. Since jizyah has a notional punitive element (as a substitute for unbelief), the canceling factor prevails, analogous to doubt in hudud punishments.
This analogy (qiyas) to hudud is specific to the Hanafi school and illustrates their use of 'shubhah' in cancelling punishments.

The Dhimmi Covenant: What Breaks It and What Does Not
States conditions for upholding or breaking dhimmi covenant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on which acts break the dhimmi covenant and which do not, and explain the reasoning behind the ruling.

Core Idea
Only physically joining the enemy or fighting Muslims breaks the dhimmi covenant; other crimes are punished but do not nullify the agreement.

This passage from the Hidayah commentary clarifies a critical aspect of dhimmi law: the covenant of protection (ahd) is robust and not easily invalidated. While certain offenses by dhimmis are serious crimes, they do not automatically nullify the contractual relationship. The only acts that break the covenant are those that effectively end the dhimmi's status as a protected person. Understanding this distinction is important for proper application of Islamic law regarding non-Muslim subjects.

2
Identify the acts that DO break the covenant
Only two: joining the abode of war (dar al-harb) or overpowering a place and fighting Muslims.
These actions demonstrate a break in the protected status.
3
Analyze the reasoning for reviling the Prophet
The commentary says it is an act of disbelief, and since the covenant was made despite their disbelief, a new act of disbelief does not remove it.
This is a rational (aql) argument, not a textual proof.
4
Note the inter-madhhab difference on killing a Muslim
The text states that killing a Muslim does not break the covenant, but some Shafi'is disagree.
The Hanafi position is that the ruler can still enforce qisas.
5
Distinguish the additional prohibition on public sale of wine and pork
The passage prohibits dhimmis from openly selling wine and pork in Muslim lands because it displays sin and may accustom Muslims to it.
This is a separate ruling about public morality, not about the covenant.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In war, the Sahibayn allow brocade; Abu Hanifa prohibits it—necessity versus unconditional prohibition.

This passage addresses an intra-Hanafi disagreement over wearing silk brocade during battle. While Abu Hanifa maintains the general prohibition of silk for men, his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, make an exception for warfare. Understanding the reasoning behind each position sharpens our grasp of how necessity can modify a ruling within the school.

Ruling on Selling Weapons During Civil Strife
Ruling on selling weapons during fitna with an exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling weapons during fitna and identify the condition that determines whether it is makruh or permissible.

Core Idea
Selling weapons to known rebels is makruh; selling to unknown buyers is permissible.

This lesson examines a concise Hanafi ruling on the sale of weapons during civil strife. The ruling at first seems general, but the commentary clarifies a key condition: the seller's knowledge of the buyer's affiliation. Understanding this condition is essential to applying the ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its base ruling
The act is selling weapons during times of fitna. The base ruling is that it is makruh (disliked).
Makruh here refers to makruh tanzih (slightly disliked), not haram.
2
Locate the exception
The commentary explains: if it is not known that the buyer is from the people of fitna, then there is no harm (la ba'sa) – the sale becomes mubah (permissible).
The exception is based on the seller's knowledge of the buyer's affiliation.
3
Understand the legal rationale
The karahah is due to assisting sin (i'ana 'ala al-ma'siya). Selling to known rebels aids their rebellion.
The principle of blocking means of evil (sadd al-dharai') may also apply.
4
Apply the condition to examples
If the seller knows the buyer is a Kharijite or rebel, the sale is makruh. If the buyer's affiliation is unknown, the sale is permissible. If the seller suspects but has no proof, the default is permissibility.
Certainty (yaqin) is not required; it is about what is known or not known.
