Dismissal of Lawsuit When Both Parties Acknowledge Third-Party Ownership
Ruling on dismissal of lawsuit when claimant and possessor agree item belongs to a third party

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dismissal of a lawsuit when both parties agree the item belongs to a third party, and identify the exception that revives litigation.

Core Idea
When both parties agree the item belongs to someone else, the possessor's hand is trust, not litigation — unless agency is proven.

In judicial proceedings, a lawsuit requires a genuine dispute. This passage from Sharh al-Quduri addresses a scenario where the claimant and the possessor both acknowledge that a third party is the true owner. The ruling is straightforward: the case is dismissed without evidence. But there is an important nuance about when the possessor can still assert a right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Read the statements: the claimant says 'I bought it from so-and-so' and the possessor says 'So-and-so deposited it with me'. Both agree that a third person (e.g., Zayd) is the owner.
The key is that both parties reference the same third party and do not claim ownership for themselves.
2
Apply the base ruling
The lawsuit is dismissed automatically without requiring evidence from either party. The judge does not investigate further because there is no conflict of ownership between the claimant and possessor.
This is a procedural ruling that saves court resources.
3
Understand the legal reason
Because both agree the item belongs to a third party, the possessor's hand is a trust hand (yad amanah) from that third party, not a hand of litigation (yad khusumah). A lawsuit requires a claim against the possessor as if he were the owner or wrongdoer; here he is merely a trustee.
This distinction between yad amanah and yad khusumah is fundamental in Hanafi qada.
4
Note the exception
The ruling changes if the possessor brings evidence that the third party appointed him as an agent (wakil) to take possession. Then his hand becomes a litigation hand because he can assert a right derived from that agency against the claimant.
The burden of proof is on the possessor to show agency; otherwise the case remains dismissed.

Dismissal of Suit When Both Claim from Third Party
Ruling on dismissal of suit under agreement on third-party ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a lawsuit is dismissed without evidence when both parties agree the property belongs to a third party, and identify the exception when the plaintiff proves authorization.

Core Idea
If both parties agree the property belongs to a third party, the suit is dismissed without evidence—because the possessor's hand is not adversarial.

In Islamic court procedure, the concept of 'yad khusumah' (adversarial possession) is key. This lesson presents a clear Hanafi ruling: when both the plaintiff and the defendant (possessor) agree that the disputed property originally belongs to a third party, the lawsuit is immediately dismissed without requiring any evidence from the plaintiff. The reason is that their agreement removes the adversarial nature of the possession. However, an exception exists if the plaintiff can produce evidence that the third party authorized him to recover the property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The plaintiff claims he bought the item from a third party (Zayd), and the defendant (possessor) says Zayd deposited it with him. Both agree on Zayd's ownership.
The key is their agreement on external ownership.
2
State the ruling
The lawsuit is dismissed without the plaintiff needing to present any evidence (bayyinah).
This is the default Hanafi position.
3
Understand the reasoning
Because they agree the property belongs to another, the possessor's hand is not adversarial (yad khusumah). There is no conflict of ownership claim.
The absence of adversarial possession removes the need for evidence.
4
Recognize the exception
If the plaintiff can prove that the third party authorized him (e.g., a power of attorney) to take possession, then the defendant must hand over the property.
This is because the plaintiff then proves he has a superior right to hold it.

Rules of the Judicial Oath: By Allah, Attributes, and the Prohibition of Divorce
Explanation of how the judicial oath is administered in Hanafi court

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on judicial oaths, explain the proof from the hadith, and identify the conditions and exceptions regarding emphasis and swearing by divorce or emancipation.

Core Idea
A judicial oath must be by Allah alone; swearing by divorce is prohibited except when necessity demands.

In Hanafi court procedure, the oath (yamin) is a solemn affirmation by the defendant (or plaintiff) to confirm or deny a claim. This lesson explains the essential rules: the oath must be sworn by Allah Almighty alone, it may be emphasized by mentioning His awe-inspiring attributes, and it must not be administered by divorce or emancipation. It also discusses a later dispensation allowing the use of divorce oaths when the defendant is indifferent to swearing by Allah alone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling
The passage begins by stating that the oath is sworn by Allah alone, not by other beings or objects. This is the foundational rule for judicial oaths in Hanafi fiqh.
The hadith 'Whoever swears, let him swear by Allah or be silent' is cited as proof.
2
Understand the emphasis (taghleez)
The oath may be emphasized by mentioning Allah's attributes such as 'Knower of the unseen and the witnessed' to make it more solemn. Emphasis is discretionary based on the person's piety or the value of the claim.
The judge may choose not to emphasize for a righteous person or for small claims.
3
Note the prohibition of divorce and emancipation
The original ruling prohibits swearing by divorce (talaq) or emancipation (itaq) in judicial oaths. These are not valid forms of oath and are Shariah-prohibited in this context.
Swearing by such means was considered coercive and not in line with the dignity of the court.
4
Recognize the later dispensation
In later times, when defendants became disrespectful of oaths by Allah alone, some scholars (as in al-Hidayah) permitted the judge to administer an oath by divorce if the litigant insisted and the defendant was otherwise unwilling to swear by Allah.
This is a dispensation (rukhsah) based on necessity, not the original ruling.
5
Analyze the consequence of nukul (refusal)
If the judge uses a divorce oath and the defendant refuses, al-Nihayah states that no judgment is passed against the defendant because he refused something that is prohibited by Shariah.
This protects the defendant from being penalized for avoiding a sin.

Swearing by divorce/emancipation is originally prohibited but becomes permissible when the opponent insists due to societal disregard for Allah's name.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Physical control and matching circumstantial evidence determine preference when written evidence conflicts.

In property disputes, claimants may each present evidence. How does a judge decide? This passage from the commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out key principles: matching physical age to a date, and the strength of possession. You will learn to apply these rules to classic Hanafi scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Determine the type of property (animal, camel with load, shirt) and the nature of the conflicting evidence (dates of birth, physical control).

Initial Duties of a New Judge and the Ruling on Seeking Judgeship
Explains the initial responsibilities of a judge upon appointment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the initial duties of a newly appointed Hanafi judge and explain why seeking judgeship is makruh tanzih, citing the prophetic hadith.

Core Idea
Seeking judgeship is disliked; a new judge must review records, prisoners, and claims, releasing only after due process.

This passage from the Book of the Etiquette of the Judge outlines the immediate tasks for a newly appointed qadi. It also addresses the ethical dimension of seeking judicial office, citing a hadith that distinguishes between those who seek it and those who are compelled. Understanding these duties ensures that justice is administered with continuity and fairness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial duties upon appointment
The new judge receives the diwan (records) from the previous judge and inspects prisoners, deposits, and endowment revenues. These ensure continuity of cases and protection of rights.
The diwan includes case files, documents, appointments of guardians.
2
Analyze the handling of prisoners and evidence
If a prisoner confesses a right, the judge enforces it. If the prisoner denies and the previous judge claims a right, that claim is not accepted without evidence (bayyinah), because the dismissed judge is like any ordinary person.
Bayyinah in Hanafi law is typically two male witnesses or one male and two females.

Understand the case of absent claimants
If no evidence is established against a prisoner, the judge must announce publicly for several days. If no claimant appears, the judge releases the prisoner after taking a surety (kafil) for his future appearance.
This protects the rights of potential absent claimants.

Recognize the judge's role over deposits and endowments
The judge inspects deposits and endowment revenues, acting on evidence or the confession of the person holding them. The dismissed judge's word is only accepted if the holder confirms the handover.
This ensures accountability and prevents misuse by the previous judge.
5
Derive the ruling on seeking judgeship from the hadith
The hadith 'Whoever seeks the judiciary is entrusted to himself, and whoever is forced upon it, an angel descends to guide him' shows that actively seeking judgeship (talab or su'al) is makruh tanzih, while accepting it when compelled is praiseworthy.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a Hanafi judge may accept a gift and apply them to determine permissibility.

Core Idea
A judge may accept gifts only from mahram relatives or long-term friends, and only if they have no pending case.

Being a judge requires strict impartiality. Accepting gifts can create conflicts of interest. This ruling from the Hanafi school provides clear boundaries: a judge may accept gifts from close relatives or habitual givers, but only when no litigation is involved. This protects the integrity of the judiciary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general prohibition
The act is a judge accepting a gift. By default, this is prohibited (haram) because it may compromise justice. The text implies prohibition by stating the narrow exceptions.
The general principle is that judges should avoid any appearance of bias.
2
Recognize the two exceptions
The judge may accept from: (1) a mahram relative (e.g., parent, child, sibling), or (2) a person who had a habit of exchanging gifts before the judge's appointment.
The mahram relationship is based on permanent marriage prohibition, not just any relative.
3
Overriding condition: no pending litigation
Even from these exceptions, if the giver currently has a case in the judge's court, acceptance is forbidden. The gift is presumed to influence the judge.
The condition applies to both exceptions equally.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira comments on the well-established principle that a judge must treat both parties equally. It clarifies that hosting one litigant without the other is prohibited because it breaks equality, while hosting both together is permissible since equality is maintained.
