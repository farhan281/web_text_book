Ruling on an Oath to Commit a Sin and the Effect of Time Condition
Ruling on an oath to kill and the effect of time condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a person who swears to kill someone, explain the recommended way to break such an oath, and distinguish between a time-bound and an absolute oath in terms of when it is broken.

Core Idea
A binding oath to kill is best broken by performing contrary acts and expiating; if directly fulfilled, the oath is not broken but sin occurs.

In this lesson, we examine a classical Hanafi ruling on a specific type of oath: one made to commit a sinful act like killing. The passage clarifies that such an oath should be deliberately broken through alternative actions, and it distinguishes between oaths with and without a specified time. Understanding this ruling helps us see how Hanafi fiqh handles conflicts between an oath and a greater obligation to avoid sin.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
Identify the case: a person has sworn an oath to kill someone. The oath involves a sinful act (murder).
2
Identify the recommended action
The text advises the swearer to 'cause himself to break his oath' by doing acts contrary to killing, such as speaking to his father, praying, and resolving not to kill, and then to perform expiation (kaffarah) for the broken oath.
This action breaks the oath intentionally and removes the sin of fulfilling it.
3
Recognize the consequence of fulfilling the sworn act
If the person ignores the advice and actually kills, he becomes a sinner, must repent, but does not need to pay another expiation because he directly committed the act he swore to do – thus he did not break the oath; he fulfilled it sinfully.
The expiation is for breaking the oath, not for committing the sworn act.
4
Differentiate time-bound vs absolute oaths
If the oath is tied to a specific time (e.g., 'I will kill him today'), it is considered broken at that time if not fulfilled. If no time is specified (absolute), the oath is only broken at the very end of life when death prevents fulfillment.
This distinction affects when expiation becomes due.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Given a scenario, determine whether the oath is broken and whether expiation is required. For example, if someone swears to hit a person but does not specify time, the oath remains until death.
Practice using the rules derived from the passage.

Commentary
This lesson contains a ruling from the commentary on a passage about oaths. The author explains the case of a person who swore an oath to kill someone. The advice given is that the swearer should intentionally break his oath by doing acts contrary to the sworn act—such as speaking to his father, praying, and resolving not to kill—and then perform expiation (kaffarah) for his oath. If he instead fails to do those acts and actually kills the person, he becomes a sinner but does not need to pay another expiation because he directly committed the act he swore to do. The key point here is that the expiation is for breaking the oath, not for fulfilling it. The ruling then distinguishes between a time-bound oath (muqayyadah) and an absolute oath (mutlaq). If the oath is tied to a specific time, he breaks it at that time if he fails to fulfill the sworn act. If no time is specified, he only breaks it at the very end of his life when death prevents him from fulfilling it. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that an oath remains in force until it is impossible to perform the sworn act.

Key Terms
يمين (yamin): A legally binding oath invoking Allah's name to do or abstain from an action.
كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation for breaking a valid oath: feeding 10 poor, clothing them, or fasting 3 days.
يمين مؤقتة (yamin muqayyadah): A time-bound oath that specifies a period for fulfillment or abstention.
يمين مطلقة (yamin mutlaqah): An absolute oath with no time condition, remaining in force until death.

Vows to Slaughter: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Nadhr
Rulings on vows to slaughter with intra-Hanafi disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on vowing to slaughter a son, slave, or oneself, and explain the evidence behind each position.

Core Idea
Vowing to slaughter one's son is interpreted as a vow to slaughter a sheep, following Ibrahim's example.

Vows (nadhr) are binding only if they involve an act of obedience. But what happens when someone vows to slaughter his own son or himself? The Hanafi school has a fascinating internal disagreement about whether such a vow is valid and what must be done. This lesson unpacks the rulings of the three Hanafi imams, their proofs, and the practical consequences for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule on vows
First, note that the text states vows for permissible or sinful acts are not binding. This establishes the baseline: only vows of obedience are binding.
2
Present the specific case: vowing to slaughter one's son
The text then gives a special ruling: according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the swearer must slaughter a sheep, even though the literal object is his son. Understand why this is considered an act of obedience.
The key is legal interpretation (ta'wil) – 'slaughtering one's son' in Sacred Law means slaughtering a sheep.
3
Analyze the evidence for Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
Their proof is based on the story of Prophet Ibrahim (AS), who was commanded to slaughter a ram after his willingness to sacrifice his son. The Quranic verses (37:105 and 4:125) show that the command to slaughter includes the substitute, and we are commanded to follow Ibrahim's millah.
This is a dalil from the Quran, using istihsan (juristic preference) to override the literal meaning.
4
Examine Abu Yusuf's position and his evidence
Abu Yusuf disagrees, citing the hadith 'There is no vow in disobedience.' He considers vowing to kill a son inherently sinful, so the vow is invalid and nothing is required.
The dispute hinges on whether the vow is interpreted as a vow to slaughter a substitute or taken literally.
5
Extend the analysis to vows to slaughter a slave or oneself
Muhammad extends the obligation of a sheep to these cases based on ownership (for the slave) and analogy (for oneself). Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf restrict it to the son case, with additional reasoning about the limits of legal reinterpretation.
The son's son is treated like a son; father and grandfather are exempt by consensus.

Commentary
This lesson addresses the rulings on vows (nadhr) in Hanafi law, focusing on vows to slaughter a son, slave, or oneself. The text first establishes a general rule: a vow concerning a permissible (mubah) or sinful (ma'siyah) act is not binding. Examples include vowing to go to the market (mubah) or to divorce, hit, or insult (sinful). However, a vow to slaughter one's son is treated differently. According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the swearer must slaughter a sheep (by istihsan) because the Sacred Law interprets 'slaughtering one's son' as slaughtering a sheep, drawing on the story of Prophet Ibrahim (AS) who was commanded to fulfill his dream-vow by slaughtering a ram. Abu Yusuf disagrees, citing the hadith 'There is no vow in disobedience' – he considers vowing to kill a son sinful, hence invalid. The discussion then extends to vowing to slaughter a slave: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold nothing is required, but Muhammad argues for a sheep because the owner has more control over his slave than over his son. For vowing to slaughter oneself, the same split occurs: Muhammad requires a sheep, while the other two say nothing. Al-Khujandi adds a crucial nuance: these rulings apply only if the vow was not intended to enforce an existing oath; if it was meant as a conditional oath (e.g., 'I swear that I must slaughter my son if...'), then the vow is null because it becomes a sin (the act of slaughtering a human). This is a classic intra-Hanafi dispute showing how different usul (principles) lead to different furu' (branch rulings).

Key Terms
نذر (nadhr): A vow to perform an act, typically of worship or charity, binding if it is an act of obedience.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that allows departing from strict analogy (qiyas) in favor of a more equitable or public interest-based ruling.
عبارة عن (ibaratun 'an): In legal terminology, 'is an expression for' meaning that the term is legally interpreted as something else.

Non-Muslim's Oath and Ila: Inviolability and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on non-Muslim's oath invalidity and ila validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the invalidity of a non-Muslim's oath, explain why divorce and manumission oaths are binding, and distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on ila.

Core Idea
A non-Muslim's oath by Allah is invalid, but his ila is valid per Abu Hanifa, leading to divorce after four months.

This lesson examines a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: while a non-Muslim's oath by Allah is entirely invalid and incurs no expiation, certain other commitments like divorce and manumission remain binding. Furthermore, we explore the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding ila — a husband's vow to abstain from marital relations — where Imam Abu Hanifa holds it valid for a non-Muslim, while the two companions disagree. Understanding these rulings clarifies how a non-Muslim's legal capacity interacts with different types of oaths and related contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
Locate the ruling: a non-Muslim's oath (yamin) by Allah is invalid. Breaking it, whether while still a non-Muslim or after embracing Islam, requires no expiation (kaffarah).
2
Trace the legal reasoning (dalil aqli)
The text provides two rationales: (1) an oath is for exalting Allah, which a non-Muslim does not do; (2) expiation is an act of worship requiring intention (niyyah), and a non-Muslim's worship is invalid, like prayer and fasting.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) with zanni thubut but qati dalala.
3
Note the exception for divorce and manumission
Even though a non-Muslim's oath by Allah is invalid, if he swears by divorce (talaq) or manumission (itq), it is binding. This is because these are conditional contracts that take effect independently of the exaltation of Allah.
4
Analyze the ila dispute
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on ila (vow of abstinence). Abu Hanifa holds it valid: if the husband refrains for four months, the wife is divorced (ba'in). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold it invalid, as they consider ila essentially an oath.
The thamarah al-khilaf: divorce occurs per Abu Hanifa; no divorce per Sahibayn.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa. The fatwa is given accordingly, as affirmed in later texts like Al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary of Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses the ruling of oaths made by a non-Muslim. It begins by stating the primary ruling: if a non-Muslim swears an oath and then breaks it, whether while still a non-Muslim or after converting to Islam, there is no expiation (kaffarah) upon him. Additionally, he is not eligible for expiation because it is an act of worship that requires intention (niyyah), and a non-Muslim's worship is not valid, analogous to prayer and fasting. However, if the non-Muslim swears by divorce (talaq) or manumission (itq), it is binding upon him because those are conditional contracts that take effect regardless of his religious status. This shows the intra-Hanafi dispute, with the fatwa being according to Abu Hanifa's view as it is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.

Declaring One's Own Property Prohibited on Oneself: Oath, Not Real Prohibition
Ruling with proof: tahrim as oath, with qiyas and objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that declaring one's own property haram is an oath, and explain why the item does not become actually haram, using the qiyas to declaring a wife or slave-girl haram.

Core Idea
Declaring your own food haram is an oath; violation requires kaffarah, but the food remains halal in itself.

This lesson examines a unique type of oath: when a person declares something he owns as prohibited upon himself. The Hanafi school treats this as a valid oath, not a literal prohibition. You will learn the ruling, the evidence from analogy, and a rhetorical objection resolved by distinguishing essential prohibition from treatment as permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is declaring something owned (e.g., food) as 'haram' on oneself. The ruling: it does not become actually haram, but if the person uses it (istibaha), he must pay kaffarah al-yamin.
Note: The item's substance remains halal; only the oath is violated.
2
Understand the qiyas (analogy)
The ruling is analogized to a man declaring his wife or slave-girl haram upon himself. Both are treated as oaths: the prohibition is not real, and violation requires expiation.
The illa is the declaration of tahrim on something owned, which constitutes a yamin.
3
Analyze the objection and response
The objection: 'istabaha' (making permissible) implies the item was haram, contradicting 'it does not become haram'. The response: it does not become haram in its essence (li 'aynihi); 'istibaha' means treating it as permissible (using it) after the oath, not transforming its legal status.
The key is distinguishing between essential prohibition (like pork) and oath-based self-restraint.

The oath 'kullu halal' is a binding yamin; default coverage is food/drink, wife only if intended.

The Effect of Demolition on Oaths of Entry: Dar vs Bayt
Analyzing the effect of demolition on oaths of entry for dar and bayt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for oaths involving 'dar' and 'bayt' when the structure changes, and apply the principle that the oath is attached to the name (ism), not the description (sifah).

Core Idea
An oath to enter a 'dar' remains binding even if demolished; an oath to enter a 'bayt' only binds if the building (walls) remains.

Oaths in Hanafi fiqh are tied to the name of the object, not its description. But what if the object changes after the oath? This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains how the meaning of 'dar' (house including courtyard) and 'bayt' (the building structure) determines whether entering after demolition breaks the oath. Understanding this distinction is essential for issuing correct fatwas on oaths.

Guided Path
1
Understand the key principle
The oath is attached to the name (ism), not the description (sifah). For a present object (pointed to), the description is superfluous (laghw); for an absent object, both name and description are conditions. The existence of the name is a condition in both cases.
This principle is explicitly stated in the passage: 'Al-asl fi hadha: anna al-sifah fi al-hadir laghw, wa fi al-gha'ib sharṭ, wa qiyam al-ism sharṭ fihima jami'an.'
2
Distinguish between dar and bayt
'Dar' is a name for the courtyard (sahah) that includes the building; the name persists even if the building is demolished as long as the land remains. 'Bayt' is a name for the building itself; if the building is completely demolished (walls gone), the name ceases to apply.
The poet's couplet summarizes: 'Al-dar dar wa in zalat hawa'ituha, wa al-bayt laysa bi-bayt ba'da tahdim.'
3
Apply to specific scenarios for dar
If someone swears not to enter a specific dar (pointing to it), and the building is demolished into an empty plot, the name 'dar' remains, so entering breaks the oath (hanth). If the dar becomes a mosque, bathhouse, or garden, the name changes, so no hanth. If it becomes a desert and is later rebuilt as a dar, entering breaks the oath.
The passage states: 'Idha halafa la yadkhulu hadhihi al-dar... fadalatha ba'da ma inhadamat wa sarat saharan hanth.'
4
Apply to specific scenarios for bayt
If someone swears not to enter a specific bayt, and the roof collapses but walls remain, the name 'bayt' still applies (ruined house), so entering breaks the oath. If walls collapse, the name ceases, so no hanth. If the house is completely demolished and rebuilt, entering does not break the oath because the original name is gone.
For an unspecified bayt (absent object), the description (building) is a condition, so if the roof is missing, entering does not break the oath.
5
Consider the present vs absent distinction
For a present object (specific, pointed to), only the name matters. For an absent object (unspecified), both the name and the description must exist. This explains why for an unspecified dar or bayt, additional conditions apply.
Example: 'Law halafa la yadkhula daran bi-ghayr 'ayniha, fadalatha daran qad hadamat wa sarat saharan lam yahnth; li-anna al-sifah fi al-gha'ib sharṭ.'

When Does an Oath Not to Enter a House Break?
Ruling on entering a house occupied by a person by any legal right.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an oath not to enter a person's house is broken, and identify the three types of occupancy that trigger the breach.

Core Idea
An oath not to enter a person's house is broken by entering any house they occupy, whether owned, rented, or borrowed.

Oaths regarding entering homes are common in daily life. The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: the determining factor is the person's residence, not their ownership status. This lesson will clarify exactly when such an oath is broken.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
First, identify the act: swearing not to enter the house of a specific person. The ruling: if the oath-taker enters a house that the person occupies by any legal right, the oath is broken.
2
Recognize the Three Occupancy Types
The text specifies three bases of occupancy: ownership (milk), rental (ijarah), and loan (ariyah). If the person lives in the house under any of these, entering breaks the oath.
Ownership means full property rights; rental is a lease; loan is permission to use without rent.
3
Apply to Examples
Consider cases: if Zaid owns his house, enters owned house → breaks oath. If Zaid rents an apartment, enters that apartment → breaks oath. If Zaid borrows a friend's house, enters borrowed house → breaks oath. If Zaid is a guest (no legal right), entering does not break oath.
The key is the person's legal right to occupy, not mere presence.

Commentary
This is a straightforward ruling: if someone swears not to enter a specific person's house, entering any house that person resides in—whether he owns it, rents it, or borrows it—constitutes a breach. The key is the person's residence, not the legal status of the property.

Key Terms
ملك (milk): Ownership; the house is the property of the named person.
إجارة (ijārah): Rental; the person has a leasehold right to the house.
عارية (ʿāriyah): Loan; the person is allowed to use the house without rent.

B) Yes, because Zaid occupies it by rental.
C) No, because the oath is only for owned houses.
D) Yes, but only if Zaid has a written lease.

Key Takeaway
A sworn oath not to enter a person's house is broken by entering any house the person occupies with a legal right to do so—whether ownership, rental, or loan.

Oath Not to Marry a Future Daughter: Definite vs Indefinite Expressions
Ruling on oath regarding a daughter born after the oath, with ikhtilaf from Abu Hanifa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on whether an oath not to marry a daughter includes a daughter born after the oath, and distinguish between definite and indefinite expressions as per Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Definite 'the daughter' excludes future daughters; indefinite 'a daughter' includes them.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a common situation: a man swears not to marry the daughter of a specific person, but after the oath, a daughter is born. Does marrying her break the oath? The answer depends on whether the expression is definite or indefinite. Abu Hanifa provides the key distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two expressions
Locate the definite phrase 'bint fulan' (the daughter of so-and-so) and the indefinite phrases 'bintan li-fulan' (a daughter for so-and-so) or 'bintan min banat fulan' (a daughter from among the daughters).
Definite uses 'al-' or possessive construct indicating specificity.
2
Understand the linguistic principle
The definite implies a known existing entity at the time of the oath; the indefinite generalizes and includes future instances.
This is a linguistic argument (dalil aqli) from the Hanafi school.
3
Apply to the scenario
If the oath is definite, a later-born daughter is not covered, so marriage does not break the oath. If indefinite, the later-born daughter is covered, so marriage breaks the oath.
Abu Hanifa's ruling: 'حنث عند أبي حنيفة' (he breaks the oath) only for indefinite.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The passage only presents Abu Hanifa's view. Other Hanafi imams (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) may differ, but their positions are not mentioned here.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute.

Source Text

وإن حلف لا يتزوج بنت فلان، فولدت له بنت بعد اليمين، فتزوجها : لم يحنث ؛ لأن قوله : بنت فلان : يقتضي بنتاً موجودةً في الحال. وإن قال بنتاً لفلان، أو بنتاً من بنات فلان، ولا بنات له وقت اليمين، ثم ولد له بعد اليمين بنت ، فتزوجها: حنث عند أبي حنيفة.
And if he swore not to marry the daughter of so-and-so, and a daughter was born to him after the oath, and he married her: he does not break his oath, because his saying 'the daughter of so-and-so' implies an existing daughter at the time. And if he said 'a daughter for so-and-so' or 'a daughter from among the daughters of so-and-so,' and he had no daughters at the time of the oath, then a daughter was born to him after the oath, and he married her: he breaks his oath according to Abu Hanifa.
Source pages p. 46

Commentary
This passage distinguishes between definite and indefinite expressions in oaths regarding a person's daughters. When the oath uses the definite 'bint' (the daughter), it refers only to daughters already born at the time of the oath. Therefore, a daughter born later is not covered. However, if the oath uses indefinite 'bintan' (a daughter) or 'a daughter from among the daughters', it encompasses future daughters as well. This is the position of Abu Hanifa. The passage does not mention the views of Abu Yusuf or Muhammad, so it is an intra-Hanafi dispute as understood from the context.

Key Terms
بنت فلان (bintu fulān): The daughter of so-and-so in a definite sense, referring to an existing specific daughter.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint

A) No, because there were no daughters at the time.
B) Yes, because the indefinite expression includes future daughters.
C) No, because the oath is invalid.
D) Yes, but only if the daughter is the firstborn.

Key Takeaway
Definiteness in oaths matters: if the daughter is not specified as existing, future daughters are included.

Oath Not to Eat from Jointly Owned Food
Ruling on eating jointly owned food under oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that eating from food jointly owned with the named person violates an oath not to eat from that person's food.

Core Idea
Eating any part of jointly owned food breaks an oath not to eat from that person's food.

This passage addresses a common scenario: an oath regarding food that is shared. The Hanafi position is clear: the oath is violated if the named person has any ownership share, even if the food is also owned by others or even by the oath-maker himself. The key principle is that the term 'food of so-and-so' applies to every part of the food in which he has a share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath context
Determine the person named, the prohibited act (eating), and the object (food).
2
Examine the ownership structure
Is the food solely owned by the named person, or jointly owned with others? Note the co-owners (third party or the oath-maker).
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
If the named person has any share in the food, then eating any part of it breaks the oath. Reason: each part is called 'food' of the named person because it is undivided property.
This applies even if the oath-maker is a co-owner.
4
Confirm the consequence
The oath is broken (hanth), requiring expiation (kaffarah).

Source Text

ولو حلف لا يأكل من طعام فلان فأكل من طعام مشترك بينه وبين آخر، أو بينه وبين الحالف حنث؛ لأن كل جزء منه يسمى طعاماً، فقد أكل من طعام المحلوف عليه.
And if he swore not to eat from the food of so-and-so, then he ate from food jointly owned between him and another, or between him and the oath-maker: he breaks his oath; because every part of it is called food, so he has eaten from the food of the one sworn by.
Source pages p. 46

Commentary
This ruling clarifies that eating from food that is partially owned by the named person still violates the oath, because each portion of the food is considered 'food' and the person has a share in it. This applies whether the joint ownership is with a third party or with the oath-maker himself. The reasoning is that the term 'food' applies to every part, and the oath prohibits eating any food that belongs to the named person.

Key Terms
مشترك (mushtarak): Jointly owned; shared between two or more parties.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint

B) Yes, because every part of the bread is called food and Zaid owns a share.
C) No, because the oath is specific to Zaid's exclusive food.
D) Yes, but only if the joint owner is a non-Muslim.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi law, an oath not to eat from someone's food is broken by eating any food in which that person has a share, regardless of other co-owners.
