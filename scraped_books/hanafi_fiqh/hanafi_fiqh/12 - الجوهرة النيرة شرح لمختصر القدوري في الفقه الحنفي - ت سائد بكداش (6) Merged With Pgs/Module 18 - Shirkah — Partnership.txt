Conditions for Compulsory Partition Based on Benefit and Detriment
Rulings on forced partition conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios of benefit/harm in joint property partition and identify when a judge may compel partition.

Core Idea
Forced partition is only permitted when at least one partner benefits; harming both requires mutual consent.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra details the Hanafi conditions for compulsory partition. A judge can only force division when it serves a beneficial purpose. This lesson breaks down the three possible scenarios for partners in joint ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios of benefit/harm among partners
Note the three cases: both benefit, one benefits and one is harmed, both are harmed.
The benefit is measured by the usability of the obtained share.
2
Determine when partition is compulsory (jabr)
For each scenario, decide if a judge can force partition at the request of one partner.
Only if at least one partner benefits from division.
3
Recognize the alternative of muhayah (alternation)
When one partner is harmed by forced partition, muhayah is ordered instead.
Muhayah allows time-sharing to avoid waste.
4
Understand the rationale: partition completes benefit, not causes harm
The rule is based on preventing harm and achieving utility for all partners.
The concept of muta'annit (obstinate person) is key.

Commentary
This passage sets the conditions under which a judge can compel partition of jointly owned property. The guiding principle is that forced partition (jabr) is only justified to enable each partner to derive benefit from his share. Therefore, if all partners benefit from the division (i.e., they would each get a usable portion), a single partner's request suffices to compel the others. If one partner benefits while the other is harmed—typically because his share becomes too small to be useful—then only the benefiting partner can request forced division; the harmed partner’s request is considered obstinate (muta'annit) and is rejected. In such a case, instead of forced partition, the judge should impose muhayah (alternating use) to prevent waste. If both partners would be harmed by division (e.g., the property is too small to divide beneficially), then no forced partition occurs; they may only divide by mutual consent. This rule balances the right to benefit with the prevention of harm, a core theme in Hanafi property law.

مهايأة (muhāyāh): Alternation or time-sharing use of property when forced partition is not beneficial
متعنت (mutaʿannit): One who makes a frivolous or obstructive request for partition without benefit
جبر (jabr): Compulsion by the judge to enforce partition

Indivisible Shared Properties: Baths, Wells, Mills, and Walls
Statement that shared baths, wells, and mills are not divisible without consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the division of shared baths, wells, mills, and walls, and identify the condition under which division is permissible.

Core Idea
A shared well cannot be divided unless all owners agree—harm to utility prevents forced partition.

In Hanafi fiqh, not all jointly owned property can be forcibly divided. Some properties, by their nature, become useless if split into smaller shares. This lesson examines the ruling on four such properties: bathhouses, wells, mills, and shared walls. The key principle is that forced division is prohibited due to harm (darar), but mutual consent makes division valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the listed properties
Read the source text and list the four types of property mentioned: bathhouse (hammam), well (bi'r), mill (raha), and wall (ha'it) between two houses.
These are all examples of properties that lose utility if divided.
2
Understand the general ruling
The phrase 'لا يَقْسِمُ' (one does not divide) indicates that forced division is not permitted. The ruling is that such properties are indivisible without the consent of all partners.
This is a default ruling; the burden of proof for divisibility lies on those who want to divide.
3
Recognize the exception
The exception is 'إلا أن يتراضى الشركاء' (unless the partners mutually consent). If all owners agree, division becomes permissible and valid.
Mutual consent (taradi) is a condition for validity.
4
Note the underlying reasoning
The commentator adds 'لاشتمال الضرر في الطرفين' (because it involves harm to both sides) – each partitioned segment would be useless, causing loss.
The reasoning is based on preventing harm (darar), a key principle in Islamic law.
5
Apply to a new case
Consider a shared oven or parking space. Would the same rule apply? Use the illa (cause) of harm to test: if dividing would render each part unusable, the ruling is the same.
Analogical reasoning (qiyas) can extend this ruling to similar properties.

The commentator adds the example of a shared wall between two houses, which cannot be divided because each section would not serve its purpose.

Procedure for Partition with Lottery
Describes the process of dividing property among co-owners

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the steps for partitioning co-owned property according to the Hanafi school and explain why the lottery is not obligatory.

Core Idea
Partition requires fair valuation and separation of rights; lottery is recommended but not required.

This lesson covers the proper procedure for dividing jointly owned property among co-owners. The Hanafi school prescribes a clear method: value any buildings, separate each share unambiguously, and then label the shares. A lottery is used to assign the labeled shares fairly, but as the commentary emphasizes, the lottery is not a legal requirement—it is a tool to satisfy hearts and eliminate bias. Understanding this procedure helps ensure that partitions are valid and disputes are minimized.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prerequisite – valuation of buildings
The text begins with 'ويُقَوْمَ البناء' – 'And the building is valued.' Recognize that if buildings exist on the property, they must be valued separately to ensure fair compensation. When one co-owner's share includes a building, he must compensate the others with its value.
Valuation is needed only when the building cannot be evenly divided with the land.
2
Analyze the separation of shares
Each share is separated from the others with its own path (طريقه) and water right (شربه). This ensures no share remains entangled with another, cutting off potential disputes and realizing a complete partition.
This separation is essential for the validity and finality of the partition.
3
Examine the labeling and lottery procedure
The shares are labeled first, second, third, etc. Names are written into a lottery, and the lottery is drawn. The person whose name is drawn first receives the first share. This method assigns shares randomly but fairly.
The labeling order is fixed before the lottery to avoid manipulation.
4
Evaluate the legal status of the lottery
The commentary explicitly states: 'والقرعة ليست بواجبة' – the lottery is not obligatory. It is used to please hearts, calm the mind, and remove suspicion of bias. The judge may assign shares without lottery, and such a partition is valid because it falls under his judicial authority.
This distinction is crucial: the method is recommended but not a condition of validity.

Inclusion of Currency in Partition Requires Mutual Consent
Ruling that currency inclusion in partition requires consent, with an example and exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on including currency in a property partition and explain why mutual consent is required, including the exception for judicial intervention.

Core Idea
Currency cannot be forced into a partition because it turns it into a sale-like exchange; only mutual consent allows it.

In Hanafi law, dividing jointly owned property is normally a non-commutative act. But if currency is introduced as compensation, it becomes an exchange that requires every owner's agreement. This lesson examines the rule, its rationale, and the limited exception when land adjustment is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is including dirhams or dinars (currency) in a partition of jointly owned property. The ruling is that it is not allowed without the mutual consent of all co-owners.
The matn states: 'ولا يُدخِلُ في القسمة الدراهم، والدنانير إلا بتراضيهم'.
2
Understand the reason for the ruling
Including currency transforms the partition from a non-commutative separation of shares (qismah) into a commutative transaction (mu'awadah), like a sale. Such transactions cannot be compelled; they require free consent.
The commentary explains: 'لأن إدخال ذلك يَجعلُ العقد معاوضةً، والمعاوضة لا يُجبر عليها'.
3
Apply the rule to the given example
Example: A house to be divided has extra building on one side. One partner wants cash compensation for the building, another wants land. The default ruling is that compensation is taken from the land, not cash. The partner in whose share the building falls is not forced to pay cash.
The text says: 'فإنه يجعل عوض البناء من الأرض، ولا يُكلف الذي وقع البناء في نصيبه أن يرد بإزاء البناء دراهم'.
4
Note the exception to the rule
If compensation from land is impossible (e.g., insufficient land), the judge may compel cash payment. This is the only case where currency can be forced.
The exception: 'إلا إذا تعذر: فحينئذ للقاضي ذلك'.

Effect of Pre-existing Easements on Partition: Must Divert or Rescind
Ruling on partition when a co-owner has a pre-existing right of way or drainage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two rulings regarding a pre-existing drainage or pathway right after partition and apply the condition (diversion possible vs. impossible) to determine which ruling applies.

Core Idea
A pre-existing servitude does not automatically survive partition; if diversion is possible, it must be diverted; otherwise partition is rescinded.

When co-owners divide a property, what happens if one of them had a pre-existing right to drain water or walk across a portion that now belongs to another? The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: the right does not automatically bind the new owner unless it is impossible to redirect it. This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah explains the two scenarios and the legal reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the passage deals with a partition where one co-owner already has a right of drainage (masil) or pathway (tariq) that crosses the share assigned to another co-owner, and this right was not stipulated in the partition agreement.
2
Distinguish the two conditions
The passage splits the ruling based on whether the drainage/pathway can be diverted away from the other's share. Note the key phrase: 'فإن أمكن صَرْفُ الطريقِ، والمَسيلِ . عنه' (if it is possible to divert the path and drainage from him).
3
State the ruling for each condition
If diversion is possible: the owner loses the right to continue using the path/drain across the other's share (فليس له أن يستطرق، ويُسيل في نصيب الآخر). If diversion is impossible: the partition is rescinded (فسخ القسمة) and must be redone.
4
Understand the legal reason (illa)
The commentary explains: if diversion is possible, the partition can be achieved 'without necessity' (من غير ضرورة) — meaning no harm to the other co-owner. If not possible, the partition is defective (مختلة) because intermixing (اختلاط) remains, defeating the purpose of partition.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools may allow the partition to stand with the servitude intact, but the Hanafi school requires rescission if diversion is impossible, preserving the integrity of the division.

This passage deals with a situation where, after a partition, it is discovered that one co-owner has a pre-existing right to a drainage channel (مسيل) or pathway (طريق) that crosses another co-owner's newly assigned share, and this right was not stipulated in the partition agreement. The ruling has two branches: (1) If the drainage or pathway can be diverted away from the other's share without hardship, then the owner of the right must do so; he cannot continue to use the path or drain across the other's land. (2) If diversion is impossible, then the entire partition is rescinded (فسخ القسمة) because the intermixing (اختلاط) remains, defeating the purpose of partition. The commentary explains that the reason for allowing diversion is that the partition's goal can be achieved without causing harm to the other co-owner. The rescission is because the partition is defective (مختلة) and must be restarted. This ruling protects the integrity of partition and prevents ongoing servitudes from undermining the division.

فسخ القسمة (faskh al-qismah): Rescission of the partition, returning the property to its undivided state.
طريق (tariq): A pathway or right of way.

Ratios for Dividing Upper and Lower Floors in Shared Property
Intra-Hanafi dispute on partition ratios for upper and lower stories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on dividing upper and lower floors, explain the reasoning behind each, and apply the mufta bihi (value-based) method to a given scenario.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school splits on floor division: Abu Hanifa uses a 2:1 area ratio, Abu Yusuf says 1:1, but the fatwa follows Muhammad: divide by market value.

When two people own a building with a lower floor and an upper floor, how should they fairly divide it? The Hanafi school has three different answers, each based on a distinct understanding of utility. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents the positions, the reasoning, and the relied-upon view of Imam Muhammad.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the method of dividing shared property consisting of upper and lower floors (علو وسفل). The discussion begins by presenting the positions of the three major Hanafi imams on the ratio for dividing a lower floor (سفل) and an upper floor (علو) when they are separate properties owned in common. Abu Hanifa holds that one cubit of lower floor equals two cubits of upper floor, because the lower floor has more utility (it can be built upon, a well dug, etc.). Abu Yusuf argues they are equal in dwelling value, so one for one. Imam Muhammad takes a different approach: they should be valued according to market price, so no fixed ratio; the fatwa is on his view. The passage then applies these principles to a specific case: a complete house (بيت كامل) containing both lower and upper floors, plus a separate upper floor. Abu Hanifa's ratio gives 1 cubit of the complete house = 3 cubits of the separate upper floor. Abu Yusuf's ratio gives 1:2. A further case compares a complete house with a lower floor only. The calculations demonstrate the practical application: e.g., for 100 cubits of separate upper floor, Abu Hanifa gives 33 1/3 cubits of complete house, while Abu Yusuf gives 50 cubits (since the complete house's value is split equally between its floors). The commentary follows the author's order, first stating the general ruling, then the reasoning, then the specific cases, and finally the preferred view.
