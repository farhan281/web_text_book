Oath on a Wife by Description: Effect of Divorce
Clarifying the effect of divorce on an oath regarding a specific wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on an oath regarding a specified vs unspecified wife after divorce, and identify the intra-madhhab difference between the two shaykhs and Imām Muḥammad.

Core Idea
Specification by pointing preserves an oath after the description changes; without it the majority dissolves the oath.

Oaths often refer to people by description ('wife of so-and-so', 'friend of so-and-so'). What happens if the description ceases – divorce or enmity? The Hanafi school has a nuanced answer depending on whether the person was physically specified by pointing (muʿayyan) or just described. This lesson walks through the ruling and the internal Hanafi disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath scenario
The oath is 'I will not speak to the wife of so-and-so' (or friend/slave). Notice that the person is identified by a relation to another person, not by name or pointing.
2
Distinguish specified vs unspecified
If the person was physically pointed to (e.g., 'this wife of so-and-so'), the oath remains valid even after divorce. If not pointed to (only described), the ruling is disputed.
Specification (taʿyīn) by ishāra makes the individual the subject, not the description.
3
State the majority position (two shaykhs)
Abū Ḥanīfa and Abū Yūsuf hold that when the person is not specified, the oath does not break (lā yaḥnath) because the description 'wife' was the condition for the subject; once it ceases, the oath's object vanishes.
This is the muftā bihī position.
4
State Imām Muḥammad's opposing view
Imām Muḥammad holds that he does break the oath (yaḥnath) because the oath was on the person identified by the description; the person remains the same even if the description changes.
His reasoning: 'wife' is not a condition but a mere label; the subject is the individual.
5
Note the consensus on the slave case
The passage adds that for a slave (if unspecified), there is consensus that he does not break the oath – this aligns with the majority view that the description is not a condition for the absent.
The slave case shows that the disagreement is specific to free persons with relational descriptions.

Oath on Valid vs Invalid Marriage/Prayer
Ruling that oath applies only to valid acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that an oath regarding an act is only violated by a valid (sahih) performance, not a defective (fasid) one.

Core Idea
An oath is broken only by a valid act; an invalid act does not breach the oath.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayira presents a fundamental rule in Hanafi oath jurisprudence: when a person swears not to perform a specific act—such as marriage or prayer—the oath is only violated if the act is legally valid (sahih). A defective (fasid) performance does not breach the oath. The reasoning is that invalid marriage does not establish the marital relationship intended by the oath, and the same logic extends to prayer and other acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oaths mentioned
The passage speaks of swearing not to marry and not to pray. Note that these are two distinct acts, but the same principle applies to both.
The principle is general; it applies to any act covered by an oath.
2
Recognize the distinction
The text states: 'it applies to the valid form of that, not the invalid.' This is the core ruling: the oath is only concerned with the legally valid performance of the act.
The terms 'sahih' (valid) and 'fasid' (invalid) are crucial here.
3
Understand the reasoning
For marriage, the author gives the reason: 'because marriage is not possessed by its invalid form.' This means an invalid marriage contract does not create the marital bond that the oath refers to; hence, it does not violate the oath.
Analogously, an invalid prayer (e.g., missing a condition) is not considered 'prayer' in the legal sense relevant to the oath.
4
Apply the principle to other acts
The same rule extends to any act that can be performed validly or invalidly. When interpreting an oath, one must determine whether the performed act meets the legal conditions for validity. Only then is the oath broken.
This is a consistent Hanafi approach: oaths are interpreted according to legal reality, not mere linguistic or apparent performance.

Ruling on Oath in Marriage Claims
States and explains ruling on oath in marriage claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that no oath is offered to the defendant in a marriage claim and explain the reasoning based on the equivalence of nukul and bazl.

Core Idea
In marriage claims, no oath is offered because nukul equals bazl, and marriage cannot be settled by an offer.

This lesson examines a specific procedural rule in Hanafi fiqh: when the subject of a claim is marriage (nikah), the defendant is not required to take an oath. This departure from the general rule is based on Imam Abu Hanifa's understanding that refusal to swear (nukul) is equivalent to making an offer (bazl). Since marriage cannot be constituted or dissolved by mere payment or offer, the oath serves no purpose and is therefore not administered. The commentary from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on this reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State clearly what the ruling is: when the claim is about marriage, the defendant is not made to take an oath according to Abu Hanifa.
This is a procedural ruling, not a hukm taklifi.
2
Understand the principle: nukul as bazl
Recognize that Abu Hanifa considers nukul (refusal to swear) as equivalent to bazl (an offer to settle the claim by payment).
This principle is established elsewhere in the madhhab.
3
Apply the principle to marriage claims
Since marriage cannot be established or terminated by an offer of payment, the benefit of the oath (which is to obtain nukul) is absent. Therefore, the oath is not offered.
The benefit (fā'idah) of the oath is nukul leading to settlement.
4
Recognize authority and ikhtilaf
This is the view of Abu Hanifa, and it is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school. Note that other schools may differ.
The Sahibayn may have a different view based on nukul as iqrar, but the text does not elaborate.

Two Claimants for Marriage of a Woman
Ruling when two men each claim marriage to the same woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two men each claim marriage to the same woman and present evidence, and identify the conditions under which the woman's testimony is decisive.

Core Idea
When evidence is equal and no priority indicator exists, the woman’s own testimony decides the marriage — but if she refuses, the judge separates her from both.

In cases where two men each present evidence of marriage to the same woman, the judge cannot prefer one evidence over the other since they are equal. This lesson explores the Hanafi solution: recourse to the woman's affirmation, and the consequences when that is absent. We will also examine the exceptions where the woman's choice is overridden by prior indicators such as residence, consummation, or earlier time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem
Two men each claim marriage to the same woman and bring evidence. Since the evidence is equal and cannot be reconciled, the judge cannot rule based on it.
Marriage cannot be shared, so only one can be valid.
2
State the default rule
The judge asks the woman to affirm one of the men. If she does, that man is her husband. If she refuses, the judge separates her from both.
The woman's testimony is given weight when external evidence fails.
3
Trace financial consequences
If both consummated, each pays half the mahr. If both die, she gets half mahr and half inheritance from each. If she dies before consummation, each pays half the named mahr. If one dies and she identifies him as first, she gets full mahr and inheritance.
The half mahr reflects the annulled marriage but obligation due to consummation.
4
Identify exceptions from the commentary
The woman's choice only applies if she is not in the house of one, no consummation occurred, and the time of one is not earlier. If any of these exists, that man is entitled without her choice.
These priority indicators override the default rule.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a new scenario, check for exceptions, then apply the appropriate rule to determine the husband and financial consequences.
Always check for priority indicators first.

Resolving Conflicting Marriage Claims in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling on conflicting marriage claims and recourse to woman's testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two men each claim marriage to the same woman with evidence, and apply the procedure of recourse to the woman's affirmation or other indicators.

Core Idea
When two claimants have equal evidence, the woman's word decides – unless possession, consummation, or earlier time tips the scale.

Marriage claims present a unique conflict: two men each assert a valid contract with the same woman, both bring witnesses, but the woman cannot be the wife of two simultaneously. How does a Hanafi judge resolve this? The matn of Al-Quduri gives a clear procedure that prioritizes the woman's own testimony and certain external factors over the raw number of witnesses. This lesson parses that ruling and its underlying logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core conflict
Two men each claim valid marriage to the same woman. Both produce bayyinah (evidence). The question is: which claim prevails?
The woman is the same subject; marriage is exclusive.
2
Apply the default rule: no preference by evidence alone
When both evidences are presented, the judge does not give judgment to either. Why? Because the object (the woman) cannot be shared, so the claims cancel each other out procedurally.
This is specific to cases where no other factor distinguishes them.
3
Consider the woman's affirmation
The judge asks the woman which man she affirms as her husband. Her word is accepted (if no other indicator contradicts). If she affirms one, he is the husband.
Recourse to her testimony is a subsidiary source of proof.
4
Handle scenarios when no affirmation occurs
If she affirms neither, the judge separates her from both – both claims are rejected. Then consider financial consequences: if both consummated, they split the mahr; if one dies, she may still get inheritance if she later affirms the deceased as the first, etc.
Separation (tafriq) nullifies both alleged marriages.
5
Recognize overriding indicators
The commentary mentions three factors that overrule recourse to her affirmation: being in one man's house, consummation by only one, or one having an earlier marriage time. In such cases, that man is preferred without her word.
These indicators create a stronger claim (istihqaq).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri regarding a claim dispute in marriage. Two men both claim to have married the same woman, and each brings evidence (bayyinah). The Proper Oath in a Marriage Claim
Ruling on the form of oath for a marriage claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct Hanafi wording for the oath in a marriage claim and explain why the oath focuses on the present existence of the marriage rather than its past contracting.

Core Idea
The oath negates current marriage, not past contract, because divorce or khul' may have ended it.

When a woman claims marriage and the man denies, the court may require an oath. The wording of that oath is critical: it must reflect the possibility that the marriage existed but ended. In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on the exact formulation of the oath and the reasoning behind it, as recorded in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is the oath given by the defendant in a marriage claim. The ruling is that the oath is required (wajib) according to some scholars, and the specific wording is prescribed.
The passage states 'this is according to the opinion of one who makes an oath in marriage' indicating a difference of opinion on whether an oath is even taken.
2
State the Correct Wording
The proper wording is: 'By Allah, there is no marriage existing between you two at present.' The oath must negate the current existence of the marriage, not the past contract ('I married her').
The forbidden wording is 'By Allah, I married her' because it fails to account for a possible divorce or khul' after the contract.
3
Understand the Rationale
The reason for this wording is that the defendant may have married the woman and then divorced her or entered into a khul'. The oath about the past contract would be false if the marriage existed at that time but has since ended.
This is based on istihsan, giving priority to the practical possibility of a prior divorce.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf reports that the judge says 'I have separated you two' as part of the oath. Others say the judge declares 'If she is your wife, she is divorced' and the husband replies 'Yes.'
These are different formulations but share the same underlying concern: addressing the current status, not the past.
5
Identify the Legal Trick (Hilah)
To avoid the oath entirely, the woman can marry another man. After remarriage, she cannot be forced to swear for the original claimant. During the case, she loses her right to maintenance because her denial of marriage is considered worse than nushuz.
This ruling is from al-Dhakhira and illustrates a permissible legal stratagem.

Dispute Over Mahr: Hanafi Procedure and Mahr al-Mithl
Ruling on evidence and oath when spouses dispute dower amount

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for resolving a mahr dispute without evidence and identify the three possible outcomes based on mahr al-mithl.

Core Idea
In a mahr dispute without evidence, the marriage stands and mahr al-mithl is awarded; the outcome depends on how it compares to each spouse's claim.

When spouses disagree on the dower amount, Islamic law provides a structured resolution. The Hanafi school carefully balances evidence, oaths, and the default obligation of the marriage contract. This lesson walks you through the stepwise process and its three possible outcomes, all while preserving the validity of the marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
The husband claims a lower amount (e.g., 1000), the wife claims a higher amount (e.g., 2000). First, determine if either party has evidence (bayyinah).
Evidence includes documented agreement or reliable witnesses.
2
Apply the evidence rule
If only one spouse brings evidence, it is accepted. If both bring evidence, the woman's evidence is preferred because she affirms an additional amount, while the husband negates it.
The principle: affirmative evidence outweighs negative evidence (al-muthbit muqaddam).
3
No evidence: mutual oaths (tahaluf)
If neither has evidence, the spouses take mutual oaths. According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the marriage is not annulled; instead, the judge determines mahr al-mithl (fair dower).
Tahaluf here does not invalidate the contract; it merely resolves the financial dispute.
4
Compare mahr al-mithl to the claims
After determining mahr al-mithl, compare it to the amounts claimed by both spouses. There are three scenarios: (1) mahr al-mithl ≤ husband's claim → husband's claim accepted; (2) mahr al-mithl ≥ wife's claim → wife's claim accepted; (3) mahr al-mithl between the two → wife receives mahr al-mithl.
The underlying rationale: the contract's default is mahr al-mithl, so when the specified amount is disputed, the default applies unless a party's claim aligns with external presumption (zahir).
5
Render the judgment
Based on the comparison, the judge awards the appropriate amount, always accompanied by the oath of the benefiting party in the absence of clear evidence.
The husband's oath supports him when his claim matches or exceeds the mahr al-mithl; the wife's oath supports her when her claim matches or is less than mahr al-mithl.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Coercion (Kitab al-Ikrah) in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with commentary from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, begins by addressing two distinct scenarios: first, a sale where the buyer is coerced into purchasing, and second, the more central topic of coercion to commit forbidden acts such as eating carrion, drinking wine, committing disbelief, or abusing the Prophet ﷺ. The commentary follows the matn sequentially.

The opening ruling concerns a coerced buyer: if the purchased item perishes while in his possession, he is not liable because his possession was forced, making him a trustee (amin). However, if he was not coerced, he guarantees the value. The coerced person has the option to either hold the coercer liable (and the coercer then recovers from the buyer) or to hold the buyer directly, in which case the buyer has no recourse against the coercer.

The core of the passage then shifts to the permissibility of consuming haram items under duress. Importantly, the text states that it is not permissible (la yasi'uhu) for the coerced person to patiently endure the threat and refuse to eat. If he does so and dies, he is sinful. The analogy given is that carrion in this situation becomes like permissible food; refraining from permissible food until death is also sinful. Thus, when life is at stake, it is obligatory to take the available means to survive.

The same principle applies to coercion into disbelief (kufr) or abuse of the Prophet ﷺ. Mere threats of imprisonment or beating do not constitute sufficient coercion. Only a threat to life or limb qualifies. He must make an internal intention to dissemble (yuwari). If he does so with a heart firm in faith, there is no sin. This is contingent upon a prevailing belief that the threat will be executed; otherwise, compliance remains impermissible.

Thus, the passage draws a clear line: coercion nullifies sin only when the threat is of the highest degree—death or permanent loss of a limb—and the coerced person is convinced the threat is real. In all lesser forms, patience is required and sin is incurred by compliance.

The most severe test of faith is being forced to deny it. The Hanafi school provides a precise framework: only a threat to life or limb can make such utterances permissible, and even then the coerced person must maintain inner conviction and use verbal dissimulation (tawriya). This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah spells out the conditions and the crucial distinction between inner faith and outward compliance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts considered kufr
Disbelief in Allah (kufr billah) and abuse of the Prophet ﷺ (sabb al-nabi). Also analogously, slandering a Muslim.
These are the most serious sins; the ruling applies to any utterance of kufr.
2
Distinguish insufficient from sufficient coercion
Imprisonment, shackles, or beating alone do NOT constitute valid coercion for these acts. Only a threat to life or limb qualifies.
The threshold is the same as for carrion and wine.
3
Identify sufficient coercion and its condition
If the threat is to life or limb, and the coerced person's predominant belief (ghalabat al-zann) is that the threat will be carried out, then it is permissible to outwardly comply.
Predominant belief is defined as more than 50% certainty.
4
Understand the duty of tawriya (dissimulation)
The person must dissemble (yuwari) in his utterance, intending a different meaning that is not kufr. For example, saying 'I am a disbeliever' while meaning 'I am a disbeliever in the idols'.
This allows him to preserve his inner faith while outwardly complying.
5
Conclude the ruling
If he does so with a heart firm in faith and with tawriya, there is no sin. If the threat does not meet the threshold, compliance is sinful and constitutes apostasy.
Tawriya is a mandatory condition, not optional.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Coercion (Kitab al-Ikrah) in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with commentary from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, begins by addressing two distinct scenarios: first, a sale where the buyer is coerced into purchasing, and second, the more central topic of coercion to commit forbidden acts such as eating carrion, drinking wine, committing disbelief, or abusing the Prophet ﷺ. The commentary follows the matn sequentially.

The opening ruling concerns a coerced buyer: if the purchased item perishes while in his possession, he is not liable because his possession was forced, making him a trustee (amin). However, if he was not coerced, he guarantees the value. The coerced person has the option to either hold the coercer liable (and the coercer then recovers from the buyer) or to hold the buyer directly, in which case the buyer has no recourse against the coercer.

The core of the passage then shifts to the permissibility of consuming haram items under duress. The general rule is that being coerced by mere imprisonment, shackles, or beating does not make the act permissible. The exception is when the coercion involves a threat to life or loss of a limb. In that case, it becomes permissible (yasi'uhu) to eat carrion, drink wine, or consume pork or blood. The underlying principle is that preservation of life takes precedence over prohibition when there is genuine fear.

Importantly, the text states that it is not permissible (la yasi'uhu) for the coerced person to patiently endure the threat and refuse to eat. If he does so and dies, he is sinful. The analogy given is that carrion in this situation becomes like permissible food; refraining from permissible food until death is also sinful. Thus, when life is at stake, it is obligatory to take the available means to survive.

The same principle applies to coercion into disbelief (kufr) or abuse of the Prophet ﷺ. Mere threats of imprisonment or beating do not constitute sufficient coercion. Only a threat to life or limb qualifies. If such a threat exists and the coerced person believes it will be carried out, he may outwardly comply while maintaining faith in his heart (i.e., dissimulation, al-taqiyya). He must make an internal intention to dissemble (yuwari). If he does so with a heart firm in faith, there is no sin. This is contingent upon a prevailing belief that the threat will be executed; otherwise, compliance remains impermissible.

Thus, the passage draws a clear line: coercion nullifies sin only when the threat is of the highest degree—death or permanent loss of a limb—and the coerced person is convinced the threat is real. In all lesser forms, patience is required and sin is incurred by compliance.

Coercion to Divorce or Manumit: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Ruling that divorce and manumission under coercion are effective, contra Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on divorce and manumission under coercion, explain the Shafi'i opposing view, and identify the practical financial consequences (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Hanafis: coercion does not invalidate divorce or manumission; the coerced party can seek compensation from the coercer.

When a husband is forced to divorce his wife or free his slave, a critical disagreement arises between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. The Hanafi school rules that such verbal acts remain valid and binding, while the Shafi'i school considers them nullified by coercion. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi principle that certain verbal dispositions are effective by utterance alone, regardless of duress, though the victim may later recover compensation.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: The Hanafi school holds that if a person is coerced into divorcing his wife or manumitting his slave, the act takes effect and is valid (sahih). There is no requirement of free consent for the validity of these verbal dispositions.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees. He holds that coercion vitiates the free consent necessary for such acts, so the divorce or manumission is invalid (batil) and does not count.
3
Follow the opponent's basis
Al-Shafi'i's view is based on the general principle that valid consent is required for legal acts, and the prophetic hadith 'My ummah is forgiven for mistakes, forgetfulness, and what they are coerced into' is understood to apply broadly, excusing the coerced person from any legal effect.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafis distinguish between criminal liability (where the hadith applies) and civil/contractual effects. They argue that verbal dispositions like talaq and itq are effective upon utterance by their nature; coercion does not negate the legal effect but allows the coerced party to seek compensation from the coercer for any financial loss.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is clear: Under Hanafi law, a coerced divorce before consummation obligates the husband to pay half the dower (or a gift) to the wife, but he can recover that amount from the coercer. Under Shafi'i law, no divorce occurs, so no dower is owed.
This illustrates how a seemingly abstract usul disagreement has concrete financial consequences.

Core Idea
Looking with desire at a non-mahram woman's face or foot is haram; necessity is strictly defined.

This passage clarifies the rulings on looking at a non-mahram woman's face and foot. Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar deals with rules of looking at and touching female slaves. This shows that the back holds a special status. Since the stomach is even more desired, it is even more forbidden. Permission for 'Azl: Free Wife vs. Slave-Girl
Ruling on 'azl with slave-girl vs. free wife, with dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'azl with a free wife and a slave-girl, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding permission when the wife is a slave-girl.

Core Idea
Permission for 'azl differs by status: free wife must consent, slave-girl does not; but if she is a married slave-girl, the master's permission governs.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a key distinction in marital rights: the permissibility of 'azl depends on the wife's legal status. It also reveals an internal Hanafi difference on whose consent matters when the wife is a slave-girl, with implications for practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling for each category
The matn states two rules: a man may practice 'azl with his slave-girl without permission, but with his free wife only with her permission. Note the default: no permission for slave-girl, permission required for free wife.
2
Locate the controversial scenario
The commentary clarifies that the rule for a free wife applies only when she is free. If the wife is a slave-girl, the issue shifts: who must give permission? The two positions are introduced.
3
Present the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad)
They argue that permission for 'azl with a slave-girl wife must come from her master, not from herself. Their dalil: the master has the right to retain her child and provides her for intercourse.
4
Present Abu Yusuf's opposing view
Abu Yusuf holds that the slave-girl herself must give permission, because she enjoys intercourse and 'azl diminishes that enjoyment. He analogizes her to a free woman.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the master's permission is required. The practical effect (thamarah al-khilaf) is that 'azl without the master's permission is impermissible according to Abu Yusuf but permissible according to the majority.
