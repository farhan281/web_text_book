Plaintiff and Defendant: Definitions and Conditions for a Valid Claim in Hanafi Law
Definitions and conditions for a claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define plaintiff and defendant according to Hanafi procedural law and state the conditions for a claim to be accepted, including the requirement of specification of kind and quantity and production of a specific object in the defendant's possession.

Core Idea
A claim is only judicially cognizable when its subject matter is specified in kind and quantity.

This lesson from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah, a classical Hanafi commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, introduces the foundational procedural concepts of claim (da'wa) and the parties in litigation. It defines who the plaintiff (mudda'i) and defendant (mudda'a 'alayh) are based on compulsion to respond, and lays down the critical condition for the acceptance of a claim: the subject matter must be known in both type and amount. Without such specification, evidence cannot be established and no judgment can follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Understand the Hanafi procedural definitions: the plaintiff is one not compelled to litigation if he abandons it; the defendant is compelled to respond.
This compulsion distinction is the core criterion.
2
Note the alternative definition
The plaintiff claims inwardly to remove an outward appearance; the defendant affirms the outward state. This is a deeper legal perspective.
The inward/outward formulation helps in cases of apparent ownership.
3
Extract the condition for claim acceptance
The claim must mention something known in its kind (jins) and quantity (qadr). Without this, the claim is unknown (majhul) and evidence cannot be established.
Examples: gold or silver (kind); ten dirhams or five dinars (quantity).
4
Apply the rule for a specific object
If the claimed item is a tangible object (ayn) in the defendant's possession, he must produce it so the plaintiff can point to it. This is also required for testimony and oaths.
Pointing is the most effective identification for moveable property.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar defines the key procedural terms in litigation: plaintiff (mudda'i) and defendant (mudda'a 'alayh). The plaintiff is not forced to pursue the case; if he drops it, no compulsion. The defendant, however, is compelled to respond. The passage then lays down a fundamental condition for the acceptability of a claim: the subject matter must be specified in both type (jins) and quantity (qadr). For example, one must state whether it is gold or silver, and whether it is ten dirhams or five dinars. This is because if the claim is vague, evidence cannot be established upon it, and even if the defendant refuses to take an oath, no judgment can be passed against him. Further, if the subject matter is a tangible object in the defendant's possession, the defendant is required to produce it so that the plaintiff can point to it specifically in his claim. The same requirement applies for testimony and for swearing an oath: the witness must identify the exact object. The reason given is that identification to the fullest extent possible is a condition, and pointing is the most effective means of identification for movable property.

Key Terms
المدعي (al-mudda'i): The plaintiff: one who is not compelled to litigation if he abandons it. Also defined as one who claims (inwardly) to remove an outward appearance.
المدعى عليه (al-mudda'a 'alayh): The defendant: one who is compelled to litigation if he abandons it. Also called al-munkir (the denier).
الدعوى (al-da'wa): A claim: a statement for which the claimant has no proof. If the claimant has proof, he is called muhaqq (one who establishes a right), not mudda'i.
البينة (al-bayyina): Evidence: proof that establishes a right. In this context, it must be established upon a known (specified) subject matter.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school strictly requires a claim to be specified in kind and quantity; otherwise, it is legally ineffective.

Definition of Plaintiff and Defendant and Condition of Specificity
Defines parties and requires specification of genus and quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the plaintiff and defendant according to the Hanafi school and state the condition of specifying genus and quantity for a valid claim.

Core Idea
A claim must specify genus and quantity; without them, no evidence can be established.

In Islamic judicial procedure, identifying the parties and the subject matter is essential. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira defines who is considered the plaintiff and defendant and lays down the basic specification required for a claim to be accepted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parties and their definitions
Read the definition of defendant (one compelled to litigation) and the subsequent explanation distinguishing plaintiff (claims inwardly to remove an outward appearance) from defendant (claims outwardly, affirming the current state). Note that the defendant is also called the denier (munkir).
The plaintiff seeks to change the apparent situation; the defendant affirms it.
2
Understand the basis for distinguishing plaintiff and defendant
The distinction is based on who contradicts the apparent state. The plaintiff’s claim goes against the visible reality, while the defendant’s statement aligns with it.
This affects burden of proof: plaintiff must bring evidence.
3
State the condition for accepting a claim
The passage says a claim is not accepted until the claimant mentions something known in its genus (jins) and quantity (qadr). Examples: 'gold' or 'silver' for genus, 'ten dirhams' or 'five dinars' for quantity.
Without both, the claim is unknown and cannot be proven.
4
Recognize the consequence of not specifying
If the item is not specified, no evidence can be established. Even if the defendant refuses the oath, the judge does not rule against him.
This shows the requirement is a condition for validity, not just a formality.

Without this specification, the object is unknown, and proof cannot be established. If the defendant refuses to take an oath, the judge does not rule against him merely for that reason.

Claiming a Specific Movable Object in Court
Procedural rules for identifying a claimed movable object

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for claiming a specific movable object (ayn) by distinguishing the requirements when the object is present versus absent.

Core Idea
Pointing identifies a movable object; if absent, value and gender must be stated.

When a plaintiff claims a specific movable object (ayn), the court must identify the object with certainty. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the two scenarios: if the object is present, pointing is required; if absent, value and gender must be stated. Understanding these rules ensures procedural validity of the claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The text distinguishes between the case where the claimed movable is in the defendant's possession (present) and where it is not present (absent).
Each scenario has different identification requirements.
2
Understand the rationale for pointing
Pointing provides the most complete identification for movables because they can be physically produced. The text states that pointing is 'more comprehensive in identification'.
This applies to claim, testimony, and oath.
3
Analyze the absent scenario
When the object is absent, description alone is insufficient; the plaintiff must state its value and additionally mention the gender (male/female) if relevant.
Gender is a required element alongside value.
4
Apply to testimony and oath
The same identification requirements (pointing or value+gender) apply when the plaintiff's witness testifies or when the defendant is administered an oath.
Ensures consistency in court proceedings.

Commentary
This passage details the procedure when claiming a specific movable object (ayn). If the object is in the defendant's possession, the defendant must bring it to court so the plaintiff can point to it. The same applies to testimony and oath: the witness must point to the object and say it is the plaintiff's right. Pointing is required because it provides the most complete identification possible. If the object is not present (e.g., lost or not at hand), the plaintiff must state its value, because describing it alone is insufficient. Additionally, when stating the value, the gender (male/female) of the object must be mentioned if relevant.

Key Terms
العين (al-'ayn): A specific, tangible movable object that can be pointed to.
الإشارة (al-ishara): Pointing to the object physically as a means of identification.
القيمة (al-qima): The monetary value of the claimed object, stated when the object is absent.
الذكورة والأنوثة (al-dhukura wa al-unutha): The gender of the object (male or female), required when stating value.

Conditions for Claiming Real Estate
Explains the conditions for a valid real estate claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions required for a valid real estate claim in court and explain the specification of boundaries, including the Hanafi view on three boundaries.

Core Idea
To claim real estate, specify boundaries, state possession, and demand explicitly.

When a plaintiff claims ownership of real estate in court, he must do more than simply assert ownership. The law requires precise specification to avoid ambiguity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act is 'claiming real estate' (da'wa 'ala al-'aqar). Understand that this is a legal proceeding where the plaintiff seeks a ruling.
2
List the three conditions
The plaintiff must: (1) specify the boundaries, (2) state that the property is in the defendant's possession, (3) explicitly demand it. Each condition removes a potential ambiguity (e.g., mortgage or retention for price).
3
Analyze boundary specification
Boundaries must be four, with names and lineages of owners. According to Abu Hanifa, the grandfather's name is required; some say father suffices. For well-known persons, just the name suffices.
The requirement prevents vagueness in the claim.
4
Note the exception for three boundaries
Hanafi school accepts three boundaries because that identifies the majority of the property. Zufar disagrees and requires all four.
This is an intra-madhhab dispute; the Hanafi view is mufta bihi.
5
Apply to testimony
The same specification conditions apply to witnesses testifying about real estate.
Testimony is part of proof; the claim conditions ensure evidence is precise.

Commentary
This passage explains the requirements for claiming real estate ('aqar). The plaintiff must specify the boundaries of the property, state that it is in the defendant's possession, and explicitly demand it. This is necessary because the property might be held as collateral or retained for the price, and the demand removes that ambiguity. التحديد (al-tahdid): Specification of boundaries of the real estate.
الحدود الأربعة (al-hudud al-arba'a): The four boundaries of the property (north, south, east, west).

Conditions for Claiming a Debt
Explains the conditions for a valid debt claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for a valid debt claim in court: explicit demand and sufficient description.

Core Idea
For a debt claim, demand payment and describe the debt sufficiently so it is identifiable.

When a plaintiff seeks repayment of a debt (a right in the dhimma), the court requires a specific procedure. Since the debtor is present, the claim must include a demand and a description of the debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of right
The right is a 'haqq fi al-dhimma' – a debt or obligation that rests on the defendant's liability. It is intangible and not specific property.
2
State the condition of demand
The plaintiff must explicitly demand payment. The text says 'nothing remains except the demand' because the debtor is present.
3
State the condition of description
The debt must be described sufficiently (e.g., type, amount, reason) so that it may be known. This avoids confusion about what is being claimed.
Description is necessary even if the debtor knows the debt; it ensures formal clarity.

Conditions for a Valid Claim in Hanafi Court Procedure
Explaining requirements for a valid claim and subsequent judicial process

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid claim (da'wa) in Hanafi fiqh and explain the procedure when a defendant neither admits nor denies.

Core Idea
A valid claim requires precise specification; a non-responsive defendant is not a denier until he explicitly denies.

This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira details the essential requirements for filing a valid claim in a Hanafi court. It covers how to describe the claimed item, whether it is present, real estate, or a debt, and then outlines the judge's procedure upon confession, denial, or ambiguity from the defendant. Understanding these rules is crucial for anyone litigating in a Hanafi legal context.

The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's view.

Gender must be mentioned alongside value.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira explains the conditions for a valid claim in Hanafi fiqh. The passage is a commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar and provides essential rulings for the procedure of claims and evidence. The author first discusses the requirements for specifying the claimed item. If the item is not present (e.g., it is detained elsewhere), the plaintiff must state its current value rather than describe it, because describing an unseen specific item is insufficient. Additionally, for livestock, gender must be mentioned. For real estate (crop/land), the plaintiff must specify the four boundaries and identify the neighbors by name and lineage. According to Abu Hanifa, the grandfather's name must be included; some scholars say the father suffices. If the neighbor is well-known, naming him is enough. If three boundaries are mentioned, it is sufficient according to the Hanafis, contrary to Zufar who requires all four. This specification is also required in testimony. For a debt claim, since the debtor is present, the plaintiff only needs to state that he demands it, but the debt must be described so it is known. Once the claim is valid, the judge asks the defendant. If the defendant confesses, judgment is given. If he denies, the plaintiff is asked for evidence. If evidence is presented, judgment is given. If no evidence, the plaintiff may request the defendant's oath. The text includes an intra-madhhab dispute: if the defendant says 'I neither admit nor deny', according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) he is considered a denier and must take an oath. But according to Abu Hanifa, he is not a denier; he is imprisoned until he either admits (leading to judgment) or explicitly denies (then he is sworn). The principle behind this is that the oath is only for an explicit denier. Furthermore, if the plaintiff claims to have evidence present, the denier is not sworn immediately per Abu Hanifa's principle. The plaintiff may choose to delay the oath request until he can bring evidence. The oath is only administered at the plaintiff's request because it is his right; if the defendant takes the oath, the plaintiff might take the case to another judge who does not accept evidence after an oath, so the oath is conditional on the plaintiff's request.

Key Terms
دعوى (da'wā): A claim or lawsuit in court, asserting a right against another.
بينة (bayyinah): Evidence; in court, it typically refers to the testimony of two male witnesses or one male and two females.
يمين (yamīn): Oath taken by the defendant when the plaintiff lacks evidence; it is a right of the plaintiff.
منكر (munkir): Denier; a defendant who explicitly denies the claim.
إقرار (iqrār): Confession or admission of the claim by the defendant.

The Judge's Procedure and the Dispute on 'I Do Not Admit Nor Deny'
States the judge's procedure and an intra-Hanafi dispute on evasive answers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's rulings when a defendant says 'I do not admit nor deny,' and state the procedure for each position.

Core Idea
An evasive defendant who neither admits nor denies is not a denier in Abu Hanifa's view—imprisonment, not an oath.

In this passage, al-Quduri presents the basic procedure for a valid civil claim. The judge asks the defendant; if he admits, judgment follows; if he denies, the plaintiff must provide evidence or request an oath. But what happens when the defendant gives an evasive answer? Here, the commentary reveals an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two foremost students on how to treat the statement 'I do not admit nor deny.' Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the school's procedure correctly.

Ruling on Defendant's Oath When Plaintiff Claims Present Evidence
Explains that defendant is not sworn if plaintiff claims present evidence, and oath is only upon request

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule regarding the defendant's oath when the plaintiff claims to have present evidence and explain why the oath is only administered upon the plaintiff's request.

Core Idea
The defendant is not sworn if the plaintiff claims to have evidence; the oath is only upon the plaintiff's request.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, when a defendant denies a claim, the judge first requires the plaintiff to produce evidence. Only if the plaintiff fails and explicitly requests the defendant's oath is the oath administered. This lesson examines the rationale behind this rule, rooted in the principle of Abu Hanifa that prioritizes evidence over oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial judicial action
When the defendant denies the claim, the judge asks the plaintiff for evidence (bayyinah). This is the default step.
The passage states: 'إذا أنكر : سأل المدعي البينة'
2
Understand the principle when evidence is claimed
If the plaintiff says 'I have present evidence' (لي بينةٌ حاضرة), the judge does not swear the defendant. This is based on Abu Hanifa's principle (asl) that the denier is not sworn when evidence is claimed.
The principle is: 'أن لا يُحلف المنكر إذا قال المدعي: لي بينةٌ حاضرة'
3
Recognize the condition for the oath
The oath is not administered automatically. It requires the plaintiff's request after he fails to produce evidence. The rationale: the oath is the plaintiff's right; he may choose to delay it to gather evidence. Also, swearing prematurely could prejudice the plaintiff if another judge does not accept evidence after an oath.
Phrase: 'لا يستحلفه إلا بمطالبته'

Swearing the Defendant When Plaintiff Claims Present Evidence

Core Idea
If the plaintiff claims present evidence, the defendant is not sworn per Abu Hanifa — unless evidence is in court (consensus).

In judicial proceedings, the plaintiff may ask for an oath even when claiming to have evidence. This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute on administering the oath when the evidence is 'present' but not yet produced. The key distinction is whether the evidence is actually in the courtroom or merely in the city.

Core Idea

This lesson examines a key rule in Hanafi judicial evidence: if a person in possession of property claims absolute ownership (without specifying a cause), his own testimony in support of that claim is not accepted. Why? Because his possession already serves as proof. The Hanafi school thus gives priority to the evidence of an outsider (non-possessor) who claims ownership. The Shafi'i school disagrees, arguing that possession strengthens the possessor's evidence. Understanding this dispute reveals deeper Hanafi principles about the function of possession and evidence.

Core Idea
Oath is not administered when nukul would equal an invalid acknowledgment — marriage, lineage, hudud, etc.

The key is his principle that refusing to swear (nukul) is treated as an admission (iqrar). This lesson unpacks those cases and their practical resolution.

Guided Path

The general rule in Hanafi court procedure is that the denying party is offered an oath. The matn lists exceptions where this is not done. Start by noting that the default rule applies to most claims, but these specific matters are excluded.

The commentary explains the basis: according to Abu Hanifa, nukul (refusal to swear) is treated as an acknowledgment (iqrar). However, if the case is of a type where mere acknowledgment is legally insufficient to prove the right, then nukul is useless, so the oath is not administered.

Core Idea
A non-repetitive cause of ownership favours the possessor; a repetitive cause favours the outsider.

When two litigants each present evidence that an animal or garment originated with them (e.g., by birth or weaving), how does a Hanafi judge decide? The answer hinges on whether the cause of ownership can occur more than once. In this lesson, we examine the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and 'Isa ibn Aban, and the practical consequences of each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Both the outsider (claimant not in possession) and the possessor each bring a witness testimony (bayyinah) that the item in dispute originated with them through a specific cause (e.g., birth of an animal, weaving of cloth).
The cause is claimed as 'sabab al-milk' – a non-transfer origin.
2
Classify the cause
Determine if the cause is non-repetitive (e.g., birth occurs only once; a cotton garment is woven only once) or repetitive (e.g., a hair cloth can be rewoven; building can be rebuilt). This classification decides the default ruling.
Non-repetitive: the cause cannot happen again. Repetitive: it can.
3
State the main Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that for non-repetitive causes, the possessor is preferred (awlā). The evidence of the possessor outweighs that of the outsider because the cause is unique and the possessor has physical custody.
The text states 'fā ṣāḥib al-yad awlā' – the possessor is more entitled.
4
Contrast the opposing views
Muhammad al-Shaybani says the possessor's evidence is not accepted; the two evidences cancel and the item is divided. 'Isa ibn Aban says the evidences cancel, but the item stays with the possessor without a judicial verdict – meaning the possessor keeps it but no final judgment is issued.
The practical difference: Ibn Aban would later accept new evidence from the outsider; the majority would not.
5
Apply the rule to repetitive causes
For repetitive causes (e.g., building, planting, hair cloth that can be rewoven), the outsider is preferred. The logic: since the cause can be repeated, the outsider's claim is stronger because the possessor's cause could have occurred multiple times, weakening his evidentiary priority.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, when a claim is brought against a defendant, the judge may order him to provide a surety (kafil) or be physically accompanied (mulazama) by the plaintiff to ensure his presence. Defendant's Claim of Absent Owner's Right and Effect of Evidence
Explains rulings when defendant claims property belongs to an absent person with evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between accepted and rejected defendant evidence in property disputes involving an absent owner, and identify the Hanafi rulings with the intra-madhhab differences.

Core Idea
A defendant's evidence of a third-party right dismisses a lawsuit unless the claim involves theft or the defendant is untrustworthy.

This passage from the Book of Claims and Evidence addresses a procedural scenario: the defendant in a property dispute claims the property belongs to an absent third party as a deposit, pledge, or usurpation. The Hanafi school requires evidence for such a claim to dismiss the lawsuit, but the acceptance is nuanced. Within the school, Abu Yusuf conditioned acceptance on the defendant's trustworthiness, and in theft claims, the majority (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) hold that the defendant's evidence does not dismiss the case due to the hadd penalty. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for proper judicial procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defendant's claim and its evidentiary requirement
Note the types of claims (deposit, pledge, usurpation) that, when supported by evidence (bayyinah), dismiss the lawsuit. Mere assertion without evidence is insufficient.

2
Apply Abu Yusuf's character condition
Even with evidence, if the defendant is known for trickery (hiyal), the judge may reject the evidence. Only a trustworthy (salih/adi) defendant's evidence is accepted.
This prevents fraudulent use of the absent owner defense.

Distinguish the theft scenario from the usurpation scenario
When the plaintiff explicitly says 'stolen from me' (implying a known thief), the defendant's counterclaim of deposit is not accepted, unlike when the plaintiff says 'usurped from me' without naming a doer—then the evidence is accepted by consensus.
The key factor is the hadd (amputation) penalty for theft, which motivates concealment.

According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, in a theft case the lawsuit is not dismissed despite defendant's evidence. Muhammad al-Shaybani holds it is dismissed. The majority view is operative.
The thamarah al-khilaf is that in theft, evidence is rejected; in usurpation without naming doer, it is accepted.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on claims and evidence addresses a scenario where the defendant in a property dispute claims that the property in his possession actually belongs to an absent third party, either as a deposit (wadi'ah), pledge (rahn), or usurpation (ghasb) by the defendant himself. The commentator explains the ruling from the matn of al-Quduri: if the defendant presents evidence (bayyinah) supporting his claim, then the lawsuit between him and the plaintiff is dismissed. Mere assertion without evidence is insufficient. This principle applies also to cases of loan (i'arah) or lease (ijarah). The commentator then notes disagreements: Ibn Abi Layla held that the claim plus an oath suffices; Ibn Shubruma held that even evidence does not dismiss the lawsuit. Within the Hanafi school, Abu Yusuf qualified the ruling: if the defendant is a trustworthy person (salih/adi), the evidence is accepted and the case is dismissed; but if he is known for trickery (hiyal), the judge may reject his evidence due to the possibility of fraud. The passage then distinguishes a different scenario: if the defendant claims to have bought the property from the absent person, he becomes a litigant because he asserts ownership in himself, unlike the earlier cases where he asserts a right of possession for the absent owner. Next, the passage considers a situation where the plaintiff claims theft (sariqah) from him and presents evidence, while the defendant claims deposit from an absent person and also presents evidence. According to the two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), the lawsuit is not dismissed—the defendant's evidence is not accepted because the plaintiff has explicitly attributed the act of theft to the defendant, so the defendant's claim of deposit is contradictory. Muhammad al-Shaybani disagrees, arguing that since the plaintiff attributes the action to the defendant, it is like the plaintiff saying 'It was usurped from me' (without naming a doer), in which case the defendant's evidence of deposit would be accepted by consensus. The two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) counter that when the plaintiff says 'stolen from me,' the mention of theft necessarily implies a thief, and the apparent thief is the possessor, but the plaintiff deliberately avoids naming him to avert the hadd penalty (amputation) out of compassion. Therefore, the case is analogous to saying 'I was stolen from' (which implies a known thief), not 'usurped from me' where there is no hadd penalty, so there is no need for concealment. Thus, the dispute hinges on the presence of hadd in theft but not in usurpation.

بينة (bayyinah): Evidence, usually two male witnesses or one male and two females; in this context, proof of the claim.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A possessor who claims ownership becomes a valid opponent because he admits a right in the item.

In Hanafi procedural law (qada), determining who can be sued is essential. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a specific scenario: when a person holds an item and claims to have bought it from an absent owner. The commentary explains that such a claim makes him a valid opponent (khasm), unlike the case where he claims to be a mere trustee (e.g., deposit). The key is the possessor's acknowledgment of ownership, which establishes his standing as a defendant.

Core Idea
The passive voice in theft claims does not automatically dismiss the lawsuit because the claimant may be shielding the thief from hadd.

This lesson examines a classical Hanafi dispute on the effect of a possessor's evidence when the claimant uses passive language in a theft case. The difference between theft and usurpation is key to understanding why Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule differently from Muhammad al-Shaybani.
