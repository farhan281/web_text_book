Nukul in Qisas Claims: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Ruling on refusal to take oath in claims for retaliation (qisas)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the different rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf & Muhammad) regarding the consequence of a defendant's nukul in qisas claims and apply the underlying reasoning to determine the outcome in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Nukul in qisas claims yields qisas, imprisonment, or arsh depending on the imam and the severity of the claim.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a defendant refuses to take a decisive oath (nukul) in a retaliation claim, the legal outcome is not uniform. This lesson unpacks a key intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa's differential treatment of life and limb claims versus the Sahibayn's uniform award of monetary compensation. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to trace how varying degrees of certainty in evidence affect the application of hudud and qisas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal context
The act is nukul (refusal to swear) by a defendant in a claim for qisas (retaliation). Note that the plaintiff has already been made to swear an oath, but the defendant refuses.
Qisas is a severe penalty, so the evidentiary standard is high.
2
State Abu Hanifa's two positions
For claims less than life (limb injury), nukul leads to qisas. For claims of life, nukul leads to imprisonment until the defendant confesses or swears.
Abu Hanifa treats limbs like property, so nukul implies admission. For life, extreme caution prevents qisas without clear admission.
3
State the Sahibayn's uniform position
Both claimants (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that in both limb and life claims, nukul results in monetary compensation (arsh), not qisas.
They view nukul as a doubtful admission, insufficient for the severe penalty of qisas.
4
Identify the key dalil and its structure
Abu Hanifa uses analogy (qiyas): limbs are treated like property (مال), so the rule of nukul in property claims applies: refusal implies liability (here, qisas). The Sahibayn stress the doubt (شبهة) in nukul, barring qisas.
The debate centers on whether nukul is a full admission or a presumptive one.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, a defendant refusing to swear for a lost finger could face qisas (retaliatory amputation). Under the Sahibayn, he would only pay arsh. For murder, the difference is imprisonment versus arsh.
The mufta bihi position in later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn's more cautious approach.

Commentary
This passage presents the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal effect of a defendant's refusal to take an oath (nukul) in a qisas claim. According to Abu Hanifa, if the defendant refuses to swear in a case of limb injury (below life), qisas is imposed because limbs are treated like property (and refusal is akin to admission). However, in a case of life, refusal does not lead to qisas but to imprisonment until the defendant either confesses or swears. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they hold that nukul in either case leads to monetary compensation (arsh), not qisas, because nukul is only a presumptive admission with doubt, insufficient for the severe penalty of qisas.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for bodily harm or murder.
نكول (nukul): A defendant's refusal to take a sworn oath after the plaintiff demands it.
أرش (arsh): Monetary compensation for bodily harm, different from diyah in that it is for wounds less than full life value.

Coercion to Kill: Ruling and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on coercion to kill with intra-Hanafi dispute on liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coercion to kill, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding liability, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The coerced killer is sinful, but qisas falls on the coercer; the coerced is treated as an instrument.

In this lesson, we examine a difficult case: a person is threatened with death unless he kills another. Does the law permit him to save himself? And if he kills, who bears the criminal liability? The Hanafi school's answer reveals a deep intra-madhhab debate between its founding imams, with practical consequences for qisas (retaliation) versus diya (blood money).

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling on the coerced person's action
The text begins by stating that the coerced must not kill; if he does, he is sinful. This establishes that killing a Muslim is not permitted even under coercion.
The phrase 'لم يَسَعُه' indicates it is haram, not just makruh.
2
Locate the dispute on liability
The main disagreement is whether qisas (retaliation) falls on the coercer or only diya (blood money) is due. Three positions are presented: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad vs. Abu Yusuf vs. Zufar.
Zufar agrees with the majority but differs in reasoning.
3
Analyze the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They hold qisas on the coercer. Their proof includes the hadith 'رفع عن أمتي...' and the analogy of the coerced as an instrument ('كالآلة').
This is the mufta bihi as indicated by 'وهذا عندهما'.
4
Analyze Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf argues that the coerced is a cause (sabab), not a direct agent, analogous to one who digs a pit. Thus, only diya is due on the coercer, and no qisas.
He also notes that diya is from the coercer's own wealth, not from 'aqila.
5
Determine the practical outcome and mufta bihi
The relied-upon view in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: qisas on the coercer. The practical difference is whether the coercer faces execution or only monetary penalty.
The text restricts this to deliberate killing; accidental killing has different rules, agreed upon by consensus.

Coerced Killing with Victim's Consent
Ruling on coerced killing with victim's consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced killing when the victim consents, and distinguish between sin (ithm) and worldly liability (daman).

Core Idea
Victim's consent does not make killing lawful; the killer is sinful but may escape worldly penalties.

This lesson examines a specific but important scenario in Hanafi criminal law: what happens when a person is coerced into killing someone, and that victim explicitly releases the killer from liability beforehand? The passage presents a ruling that prohibits the killing (sinful) yet implies no worldly consequences such as qisas or diya. Understanding this distinction between sin and liability is central to Hanafi criminal jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Read the scenario: a coercer threatens to kill A unless A kills B. B tells A, 'You are free from my blood' (أنت في حل من دمي). A kills B deliberately.
2
Parse the ruling components
The text says: 'فهو آثم' (he is sinful) – that is the religious ruling (haram). 'ولا شيء عليه' (nothing upon him) – this indicates no qisas or diyah is owed to B’s heirs. 'وتجب ديته في...' (his diyah is obligatory in...) the text cuts off, but the context suggests diyah is not on the killer; likely on the coercer or from the state.
Be cautious: the incomplete sentence may alter the full picture.
3
Understand the legal effect of 'hill'
The victim's statement 'you are free from my blood' is a waiver of the right to qisas and diyah. Hanafi law does not treat this as permission to kill; it only removes the worldly claim of the victim.
The killer remains sinful because human life is inviolable.
4
Distinguish sin and liability
In Hanafi law, a sin (ithm) is established against Allah; worldly penalties (qisas/diyah) are rights of the victim or his heirs. The victim's consent can waive the latter but not the former.
