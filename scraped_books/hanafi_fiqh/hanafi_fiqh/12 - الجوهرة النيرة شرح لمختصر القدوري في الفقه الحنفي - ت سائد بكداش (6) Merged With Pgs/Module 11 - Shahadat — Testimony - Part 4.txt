Branch Witnesses' Retraction Not Accepted
Ruling when branch witnesses claim original witnesses lied

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when branch witnesses claim original witnesses lied, and explain why the judgment is not overturned.

Core Idea
A branch witness's statement about another's falsehood does not overturn the judgment; only retraction of one's own testimony counts.

In Hanafi testimony law, branch witnesses (shuhud al-far') testify based on the testimony of original witnesses. A common question arises: what if the branch witnesses later claim the originals lied or made a mistake? This passage clarifies that such a claim has no legal effect, because the branch witnesses did not retract their own testimony – they merely testified about someone else's error.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Understand who are the original witnesses (shuhud al-asl) and who are the branch witnesses (shuhud al-far'). Branch witnesses testify based on the testimony of the originals.
2
Understand the branch witness's claim
The branch witnesses say either 'the original witnesses lied' or 'they erred in their testimony'. Note what they are claiming.
3
Know the ruling
The text states 'no attention is paid to that' – the claim is legally irrelevant. The judgment already passed is not reopened.
4
Recognize the reason
The ruling is based on the fact that the branch witnesses did not retract their own testimony. They only testified that someone else retracted or erred. Liability does not arise because they are not recanting.
This distinction is crucial: retraction of one's own testimony has consequences; testifying about others does not.

Liability of Ihsan Witnesses Upon Retraction
Ruling that ihsan witnesses who retract are not liable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that ihsan witnesses who retract are not liable, and explain the legal reasoning that distinguishes between conditions and causes of punishment.

Core Idea
Ihsan is a condition for rajm, not its cause; thus witnesses to ihsan bear no liability upon retraction.

In cases of zina, the punishment of rajm requires both proof of the act and proof that the accused is muhsan. What happens if the witnesses who testified to the accused's ihsan later retract? The Hanafi school rules that they are not liable for any damages. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the reasoning, focusing on the distinction between the conditions that enable a punishment and the act that triggers it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the act
Four witnesses testify to zina, and two additional witnesses testify to ihsan (the state of being muhsan). The ihsan witnesses then retract their testimony.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that the ihsan witnesses are not liable (lam yadmanu) — they owe no financial compensation.
This is in contrast to the zina witnesses, who would be liable if they retracted.
3
Analyze the first proof: ihsan is not punishable
Ihsan consists of attributes (puberty, Islam, marriage, freedom) that are not themselves punishable. Punishment is for zina, not for these conditions.
The text emphasizes that these 'meanings' (ma'ani) are not subject to punishment.
4
Analyze the second proof: ihsan existed before the testimony
Ihsan was already present in the accused before any testimony. The rajm punishment becomes obligatory only after zina occurs, not due to the ihsan testimony. Thus, the ihsan witnesses' testimony was not the cause of the punishment.
The cause (sabab) of rajm is zina combined with ihsan, but the ihsan testimony merely confirmed a pre-existing state.
5
Distinguish condition from cause
Liability for retraction attaches only when the testimony was the direct cause of a legal consequence. Here, ihsan is a condition (shart), not the cause (sabab). Therefore, retraction incurs no liability.
This distinction is central to the reasoning and can be applied to other areas of fiqh.

Commentary
This passage discusses the case where four witnesses testify to zina (illegal sexual intercourse) and two other witnesses testify to the accused's ihsan (the state of being muhsan: free, adult, Muslim, married). If the ihsan witnesses later retract, they are not held liable for damages. The commentary gives multiple reasons: (1) The ihsan witnesses do not cause the rajm (stoning) penalty; ihsan is only a condition for rajm, similar to puberty and sanity. (2) Rajm is a punishment for zina, not for ihsan. Ihsan consists of attributes (puberty, Islam, marriage, freedom) which are not punishable. (3) The rajm was already obligatory due to the zina testimony combined with the existence of ihsan, not due to the ihsan testimony alone. Therefore, retraction of the ihsan witnesses has no financial liability.

Liability of Tazkiyah Witnesses Upon Retraction
Ruling and dispute on liability of tazkiyah witnesses upon retraction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on whether tazkiyah witnesses are liable upon retraction and identify the consensus exception.

Core Idea
Tazkiyah witnesses may be liable if their certification enabled a flawed judgment.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about the liability of those who certify the integrity of witnesses (tazkiyah). When such certifiers retract their statements after a judgment, are they responsible for damages? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples disagree, and a consensus exception clarifies the limits of liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Tazkiyah witnesses certify that other witnesses are upright or free. If they later retract, and the original judgment was executed, who bears the loss?
Focus on the act of tazkiyah as a precondition for testimony.
2
State the positions of the two sides
Abu Hanifa holds: they are liable because tazkiyah made the testimony effective. Sahibayn: no liability because it's only praise.
Know the analogy: Abu Hanifa compares them to direct witnesses; Sahibayn compare them to ihsan witnesses.
3
Analyze the reasoning (talīl)
Abu Hanifa argues that before tazkiyah, no ruling was attached to the testimony; it became effective only through tazkiyah. Sahibayn argue that mere praise does not cause liability.
The dispute hinges on the legal effect of tazkiyah.
4
Note the consensus exception
If the tazkiyah was about uprightness (not freedom) and the witnesses turned out to be slaves, there is no liability by consensus because a slave can be upright.
The dispute only applies when tazkiyah explicitly asserts freedom.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: In Abu Hanifa's view, if the accused was executed and later the original witnesses are found to be slaves (invalidating testimony), the tazkiyah witnesses pay diyah. In Sahibayn's view, they pay nothing.
This affects how judges assess the risk of relying on tazkiyah.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding tazkiyah witnesses (those who certify the integrity of other witnesses). According to Imam Abu Hanifa, if the tazkiyah witnesses retract their certification after a judgment based on those witnesses, they are liable for damages (diyah or blood money) if the executed punishment turns out to be wrongful. The reason is that the tazkiyah was instrumental in making the witnesses' testimony effective; without it, no ruling would have been based on that testimony. The two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, hold that there is no liability because they merely praised the witnesses, similar to ihsan witnesses. The dispute exists only when they explicitly stated that the witnesses were free.

التزكية (al-tazkiyah): The act of certifying the integrity and suitability of witnesses for testimony.

Liability of Certifiers Upon Retraction of Tazkiyah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on certifier liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi dispute on the liability of certifiers (muzzakkūn) upon retraction of tazkiyah and identify the analogy used by each side.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa holds certifiers liable because their certification gives legal effect; the Sahibayn compare them to witnesses of ihsan, who are not liable.

This passage presents an important intra-Hanafi disagreement (ikhtilaf) about the legal responsibility of those who certify witnesses (tazkiyah) after a judgment has been carried out and the certification is later retracted. The dispute turns on whether the certifiers are like the witnesses themselves (Abu Hanifa) or like mere praisers with no liability (Sahibayn). Understanding this nuance is key to grasping the Hanafi theory of testimony and causation of harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
The scenario involves four witnesses testifying against a man for adultery, certifiers certifying them, and the man being stoned. Later it emerges the witnesses were slaves (thus incompetent). The certifiers retract their certification, claiming they knew the witnesses were slaves.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa: certifiers are liable for blood money (diyah). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn): no liability on certifiers; blood money is on the public treasury (bayt al-mal).
This is a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that certification (tazkiyah) gives legal effect to testimony. Before tazkiyah, testimony has no effect; after tazkiyah, it becomes operative. Thus certifiers are like false witnesses when they retract, because their act caused the harm.
Key phrase in text: 'جعلوا شهادة الشهود شهادة' – they made the testimony into testimony.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's reasoning
The Sahibayn analogize certifiers to witnesses of ihsan (shuhūd al-iḥṣān) in a false accusation of adultery case. Those witnesses only praise the plaintiff's chastity; if they retract, they are not liable for hadd qadhf. Similarly, certifiers merely praised the witnesses; they did not cause the harm directly.
The analogy is to a different legal context, but the principle is that mere praise does not create liability.
5
Identify the consensus exception
The text notes a consensus: if certifiers only affirmed uprightness (ʿadāla) and not freedom, they are not liable even if the witnesses turn out to be slaves, because a slave can be upright.
This narrows the scope of the dispute: the ikhtilaf only applies when certifiers explicitly assert freedom (ḥurriyyah).

Liability of Witnesses on Oath and Condition: Direct Cause Principle
Ruling on liability when witnesses retract testimony on oath and condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when witnesses of an oath and condition retract, and explain why only the oath witnesses are liable using the principle of direct causality (ta'lil).

Core Idea
Only the witnesses to the oath are liable upon retraction because the legal ruling attaches to the oath, not to the condition.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah presents a nuanced rule in Hanafi fiqh concerning witness liability. When two groups testify—one to an oath and one to the fulfillment of its condition—and later retract, only the witnesses to the oath bear liability. The reasoning hinges on identifying which testimony is the direct cause of the legal consequence. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying principles of causality (ta'lil) in Islamic legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two testimonies
Two sets of witnesses: one testifies to an oath (e.g., a man swore to free a slave if a condition occurs), the other testifies that the condition occurred (e.g., the slave entered the house).
The oath is the direct cause of the legal effect; the condition is a trigger.
2
State the ruling on retraction
If all witnesses retract, only the oath witnesses are liable for damages (e.g., value of slave or mahr). The condition witnesses are not liable.
The text explicitly says 'al-ḍamān ʿalā shuhūd al-yamīn khāṣṣatan'.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil)
The legal ruling (manumission or divorce) is attached to the oath itself, not to the condition. The condition is merely a sign that the oath's effect is triggered.
Analogous to witnesses of ihsan (chastity) in a false accusation case: they are not liable because the hadd is attached to the accusation, not the chastity testimony.
4
Apply the analogy from the text
The author provides an analogy: if a master says to his slave 'If X hits you, you are free,' then X hits him, the slave is freed by the master's oath, not by the hitting. So the hitter is not liable.
This illustrates that the direct cause (oath) determines liability.
5
Distinguish the case of divorce after consummation
If witnesses testify to divorce after the marriage is consummated, retraction brings no liability because the divorce is already effective and liability does not arise.
The passage says 'no benefit appears' meaning the ruling only matters before consummation.
