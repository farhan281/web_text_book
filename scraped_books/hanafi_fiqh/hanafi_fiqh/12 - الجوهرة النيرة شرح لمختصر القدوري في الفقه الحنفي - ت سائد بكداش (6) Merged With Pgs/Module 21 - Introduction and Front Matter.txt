Opening of Kitab al-Hazr and Ruling on Men Wearing Silk
Defines hazr and states prohibition of silk for men with hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-hazr and al-mubah in Hanafi fiqh and state the ruling on men wearing silk with its dalil.

Core Idea
Silk for men is haram; the hadith threat proves prohibition.

This lesson opens Kitab al-Hazr wa al-Ibahah, clarifying key definitions of prohibition and permission. It then presents a clear ruling from the matn that men wearing silk is haram, supported by a prophetic hadith. Understanding these foundational terms is essential for navigating the chapter.

Guided Path
1
Define hazr and mubah
The commentator defines hazr as prevention from use by Shariah, and mubah as choice without reward/punishment. Note the Quranic citation (17:20) for lexical meaning.
Hazr is opposite of ibahah.
2
Note the term 'makruh' in Hanafi usage
The Hidayah titled this chapter 'Kitab al-Karahiyya'. The commentator explains intra-Hanafi views: Muhammad says every makruh is haram but not labeled so without decisive text; Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say it is closer to haram.
Later Hanafis distinguish makruh tahrimi and tanzihi.
3
Identify the ruling on silk
The matn states 'لا يحل للرجال لبس الحرير' – not permissible for men to wear silk.
Explicit prohibition.
4
Analyse the dalil
The hadith: 'Only he wears it who has no share in the Hereafter.' This severe threat indicates the act is haram.
Hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but meaning is qati.
5
Confirm the hukm level
Based on explicit text and the threat, the ruling is haram, not merely makruh.

Book of Prohibition and Permission. Prohibition: it is prevention and restraint. Allah the Exalted said: 'And whatever your Lord gives is not prohibited.' (Al-Isra 17:20), i.e., the provision of your Lord is not withheld from the righteous and the sinner. And it is here an expression for what is prevented from use according to the Shariah. And the prohibited is the opposite of the permissible. And the permissible is what the responsible person is given choice between doing and leaving, without deserving reward or punishment. And the author of Al-Hidayah titled this chapter as 'Book of Dislike.' Then he said: They discussed the meaning of makruh, and what is narrated from Muhammad: that every makruh is haram, except that as long as he does not find a decisive text, he does not apply the word haram to it. And from Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: that it is closer to haram. He (the author) said: 'It is not permissible for men to wear silk,' because of his saying, peace be upon him: 'Only those who have no share in the Hereafter wear it.'

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Prohibition and Permission (Kitab al-Hazr wa al-Ibahah) in the commentary of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira. The commentator first defines the term 'hazr' lexically and technically, citing Quran 17:20. He then distinguishes between prohibited and permissible. He mentions that the author of Al-Hidayah titled the chapter 'Kitab al-Karahiyya' and discusses the Hanafi technical meaning of makruh: according to Imam Muhammad, every makruh is haram but not labeled as such without definitive proof, while according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, it is closer to haram. Then the commentator states the matn ruling: men are not permitted to wear silk, supported by the prophetic hadith that those who wear silk in this world have no share in the Hereafter. This hadith indicates severe prohibition, establishing the ruling as haram for men.

Key Terms
الحظر (al-hazr): Prevention and restraint; legally, what is prohibited from use by Shariah.
المباح (al-mubah): What the responsible person is given choice between doing and leaving, without reward or punishment.
المكروه (al-makruh): Disliked; according to Muhammad, it is haram but not labeled as such without decisive text.
