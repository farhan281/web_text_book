The Legal Ruling of Bequest (Wasiyyah)
Opening legal ruling with proof from Quran and Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequest (wasiyyah) and identify the textual proofs from Quran and Sunnah that establish it as recommended (sunnah) rather than obligatory.

Core Idea
Bequest is encouraged, not required—the Hadith of Sa'd proves permission, not obligation.

When we open the Book of Bequests in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, the first question is: what is the legal status of making a will? The Hanafi school places it as a recommended act (sunnah), not an obligation. This lesson examines the precise ruling and the two dalils from the Quran and the Sunnah that support it. By understanding the legal category, you will appreciate how the Hanafi school determines the binding force of an act.

Guided Path
1
Extract the explicit ruling
Find the precise words in the Arabic text that describe the ruling of bequest: 'غير مفروضة ولا واجبة' and 'محثوث عليها، مرغب فيها'. State the hukm level.
The text clearly negates fard and wajib and uses encouraging language.
2
Analyze the Quranic proof
Look at the verse cited: ﴿مِنْ بَعْدِ وَصِيَّةٍ يُوصِي بِهَا أَوْ دَيْنٍ﴾. Explain why this verse does not impose an obligation but only prescribes a priority in distribution.
The verse orders execution of bequest if made, but does not command making one.
3
Analyze the Sunnah proof
Examine the hadith of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas. Identify the key exchange: the Prophet's permission for one-third and his statement 'to leave your heirs wealthy is better'. Explain how this shows recommendation, not obligation.
The Prophet allowed up to one-third but did not command even that.
4
Synthesize the ruling
Combine the two proofs to confirm the Hanafi position: bequest is mustahabb (sunnah). Understand that the mufta bihi is up to one-third without heirs' consent.
No intra-madhhab ikhtilaf on this basic ruling.

Commentary

Ruling on Will (Wasiyyah): Non-Obligatory but Recommended
States ruling on will, provides proofs, and clarifies precedence of debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wills, explain the evidence from Quran and Sunnah, and apply the priority rule between debt and will.

Core Idea
A will is recommended, not obligatory; debt takes precedence over wills and inheritance.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary clarifies that making a will is encouraged but not mandatory. It also resolves the priority between debt and will, and between will and inheritance, using Quranic evidence and the famous hadith of Sa'd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wasiyyah (will). The text explicitly states it is 'ghayr wajibah' (not obligatory) and 'mustahabbah' (recommended).
Hanafi fiqh classifies it as sunnah ghayr muakkadah/mustahabb.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The Quranic proof is verse 4:11 (mentioning will and debt before inheritance). The Sunnah proof is the hadith of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, where the Prophet allowed only one-third.
Both proofs indicate permissibility, not obligation.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The verse mentions will alongside debt, showing permission but not command. The hadith shows the Prophet limited bequest to one-third, implying it is optional.
If it were obligatory, the Prophet would have commanded it, not merely set a limit.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
Some scholars (e.g., Imam Ahmad) consider the will necessary for those with outstanding obligations, but the Hanafi school holds it is recommended.
The Hanafi position is based on the original permissibility and lack of a direct command.
5
Evaluate the thamarah (practical consequence)
A person may choose to make a will or not; if they do, it cannot exceed one-third of the estate for non-heirs. Debt must be paid before executing any will.
The priority of debt over will is derived from the obligatory nature of debt versus voluntary nature of will.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh al-Quduri (Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira) discusses the ruling of the will (wasiyyah) in Hanafi fiqh. It begins by stating that a will is encouraged (mahthuth 'alayha) and desired (muraghghab fiha) but not obligatory (ghayr mafrudah wa la wajibah). It is legislated by the Quran and Sunnah. The Quranic proof is verse 4:11, which mentions will and debt before inheritance. The Sunnah proof is the hadith of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, in which the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade bequeathing more than one-third and stated that leaving heirs wealthy is better than leaving them poor. The passage then explains that the ruling of non-obligation is because a will is a contract that establishes a right over wealth, similar to a gift or loan. It clarifies that a will is recommended (mustahabb) for a non-relative (ajnabi), not for an heir. The text then discusses the priority between debt and will: debt takes precedence over will and inheritance because debt is obligatory while will is voluntary. The word 'or' in the verse does not indicate sequence but rather postpones inheritance until either or both are fulfilled. Finally, regarding whether it is better to make a will for less than one-third or leave no will at all, the answer depends on the heirs' financial situation: if they are poor, leaving no will is better; if they are rich, a will is better. There is also a narration that the person is given a choice. The passage ends with a question posed to Abu Yusuf about a person with minor heirs, though the answer is not given here.

Key Terms
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): A legal disposition that takes effect after death, transferring ownership of property to a non-heir as a charitable act.
غير واجبة (ghayr wajibah): Not obligatory; the will is not a mandatory act in Hanafi fiqh.
مستحبة (mustahabbah): Recommended; performing it earns reward, but omitting it does not incur sin.
الدين (al-dayn): Debt owed by the deceased; given priority over will and inheritance.

Ruling on Will: Not Obligatory but Recommended
States basic ruling on will, debt precedence, and amount recommendations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wills, explain why debt takes precedence, and describe when a lesser bequest is recommended.

Core Idea
A will is recommended, not required; debt must be paid first, and the amount should consider heirs' needs.

This opening of Kitab al-Wasiyyah establishes the basic ruling: a will is not obligatory but is mustahabb (recommended). The commentator clarifies that it is for non-heirs only. The passage then addresses the priority of debt over the will, citing the Quranic verse (4:11), and resolves the apparent contradiction in word order. Finally, it discusses whether to bequeath less than one-third or leave no will, based on the financial condition of the heirs, with endorsements from Abu Bakr, Umar, and Aisha for smaller bequests like a quarter or a fifth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on will
The passage states: 'الوصية غير واجبة' (the will is not obligatory) and 'وهي مستحبة' (it is recommended). Understand the hukm level: mustahabb (sunnah ghayr muakkadah).
The ruling is clear: not wajib, but mustahabb.
2
Understand the restriction
The recommendation is specifically for bequests to non-heirs (الأجنبي), not to heirs (الوارث). This prevents prejudice to those who already inherit by law.
3
Analyze the priority of debt
Debt is obligatory (واجب), will is voluntary (تبرع), so debt takes precedence over will and inheritance. Quran 4:11 is the proof, but the word 'or' (أو) does not imply order.
The obligatory takes priority over the voluntary.
4
Resolve the word 'or' issue
The verse mentions will before debt, but 'aw' merely indicates that inheritance distribution is delayed until either or both are fulfilled. It does not prescribe temporal order.
The objection about word order is answered by the nature of the particle.
5
Evaluate the amount recommendation
If heirs are poor, leaving no will is better. If they are rich, a will is better. Narrations recommend quarter over third, fifth over quarter, showing moderation.
Consider the financial state of heirs.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter of wills (Kitab al-Wasiyyah) by stating the foundational ruling: a will (wasiyyah) is not obligatory (ghayr wajibah); rather it is recommended (mustahabbah). The commentator explains that a will is like a gift (hibah) or loan (ariyah) in that it establishes a right in wealth by contract, so it is not an obligation. The recommendation is specifically for bequests to non-heirs (ajnabi), not to heirs (warith). The text then addresses the precedence of debt over both will and inheritance. Debt is obligatory (wajib), while the will is voluntary (tabarru'), so the obligatory takes precedence. The proof is the Quranic verse (4:11) which mentions will before debt but the word 'or' (aw) does not indicate order; it simply means that inheritance distribution is delayed until either or both are fulfilled. A common question arises: is it better to make a will for less than one-third or to leave it entirely? The answer depends on the heirs' financial state. If the heirs are poor, leaving the will (i.e., not making it) is better. If they are rich, making a will is better. Some say the testator is given a choice. Abu Yusuf was asked about a man with minor heirs; he ruled that leaving the estate for the heirs is better. The passage concludes with narrations from Abu Bakr, Umar, and Aisha that preferring to bequeath a quarter is better than a third, and a fifth better than a quarter. These narrations encourage moderation in bequests, not exceeding one-third and preferring less when heirs are in need.

مستحبة (mustahabbah): Recommended act; not obligatory but meritorious.

Bequests to Heirs and Exceeding One-Third
Explains prohibition of bequest to heir and limit of one-third

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequests to heirs and the one-third limit, apply the timing rule for determining heirship, and explain the effect of heirs' consent.

Core Idea
A bequest to an heir is invalid unless all other heirs consent; the limit is one-third of the estate.

This lesson from Al-Jawharah Al-Nayyirah expands on two core prohibitions: bequeathing to an heir and exceeding one-third of the estate. These rules protect the fixed shares ordained by Allah. We will examine the textual evidence from hadith, the reasoning of injustice (hayf), and the critical timing rule that determines whether a beneficiary is an heir at death, not at the time of the bequest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling and its evidence
Find the explicit statement that a bequest to an heir is not valid, and note the hadith quoted as proof: 'No bequest to an heir.' Also note the additional proof that it is injustice (hayf), which is a major sin.
The hadith is from multiple sources (Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud, Ibn Majah).
2
Explain the reasoning (hayf) and the recommended practice
Understand that making a bequest to an heir or exceeding one-third is considered injustice. The Companions (Abu Bakr, Umar, Aisha) recommended bequeathing even less than one-third (e.g., one-fourth or one-fifth) to avoid any suspicion of injustice.
The preferred practice is to leave everything to the legal heirs.
3
Understand the timing rule for heirship
Heirship is determined at the moment of death, not at the time of the bequest. Work through the two examples: bequest to a wife who is later divorced (valid) vs. bequest to a stranger who later marries the testator (invalid).
This rule is crucial for applying the prohibition correctly.
4
Compare analogous transactions: gift and acknowledgment
A gift from a sick person to an heir is treated like a bequest (executed from one-third). But an acknowledgment of debt to an heir is valid from the entire estate because it is a present transaction. Distinguish the reasoning.
This contrast highlights the principle that dispositions taking effect after death are limited to one-third.
5
Apply the consent exception
Both prohibitions (bequest to heir and exceeding one-third) can be overcome if all other heirs consent after the testator's death, provided they are adults of sound mind. Also handle the case where a bequest is made to both a stranger and an heir.
Consent requires the heirs to be baligh (adult) and 'aqil (sane).

Bequest to a Person Who Becomes an Heir
Ruling statement with illustrative examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a bequest to a person who becomes an heir at death and apply it to scenarios involving changes in heir status.

Core Idea
A bequest to a non-heir becomes invalid if they become an heir by death.

In Hanafi law, the validity of a bequest depends on the legatee's status at the time of death, not at the time the bequest was made. This lesson examines a key ruling: if a person makes a bequest to someone who is not an heir, but that person later becomes an heir before the testator dies, the bequest is invalid. Heirs' Permission and Partial Consent in Bequests
Commentary on permission for bequests exceeding one-third and partial consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on heirs' permission for bequests exceeding one-third and calculate the distribution when some heirs permit and others do not.

Core Idea
Permission after death from adult heirs makes a bequest exceeding one-third valid; partial permission is valid proportionally.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on bequests that exceed the one-third limit. The matn from al-Quduri states that such bequests are invalid unless the heirs permit after the testator's death. The commentary expands on who can permit, when permission is effective, and how partial permission by some heirs affects distribution. Understanding these rules is essential for drafting valid wills and resolving inheritance disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
The matn states: a bequest of more than one-third is not permissible unless the heirs permit it. This establishes that the default ruling for excess is invalidity.
The one-third limit is a well-known Hanafi principle based on hadith.
2
Specify conditions for valid permission
Permission is only valid after the testator's death, and the heirs must be adult and healthy (i.e., legally competent). Permission during the testator's lifetime is revocable and has no effect.
The right of heirs vests only at death, so prior consent is irrelevant.
3
Understand partial permission
If some heirs permit and others do not, the bequest is valid regarding the consenting heir's share and void regarding the non-permitting heir's share. This is a legal fiction: treat each heir's share independently.
This avoids total invalidity and respects individual rights.
4
Work through the example
Consider: two sons, bequest of half the estate to a stranger. If one son permits and the other does not, we calculate using base 12. The non-permitting son is treated as if none permitted: he gets 1/3 (4/12). The permitting son is treated as if all permitted: he gets 1/4 (3/12). The remainder (5/12) goes to the legatee.
The base 12 is used because of the fractions 1/3 and 1/4.
5
Apply the rule to other scenarios
The same logic applies if the testator bequeaths to a non-heir and an heir simultaneously: the non-heir gets half the bequest, and the heir's part is void. Similarly for bequest to a killer and a non-heir.
These are specific applications of the general principle.

Bequest: Contingent on Acceptance After Death
Ruling that bequest is contingent on acceptance after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a bequest requires acceptance after the testator's death, explain the proof from qiyas (analogy to gift and sale), and distinguish the view of Imam Zufr.

Core Idea
A bequest is a contract of ownership; like gift and sale, it requires acceptance — only after death.

A bequest (wasiyyah) does not transfer ownership automatically at the testator's death. Instead, it remains in suspense until the legatee accepts after the testator has passed away. This lesson explores why the Hanafi school insists on post-mortem acceptance, the proof from qiyas, and the contrary opinion of Imam Zufr within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
State the question: Does a bequest require acceptance by the legatee, and if so, when? The matn says acceptance is 'after death.'
2
Examine the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafi position: the bequest is contingent on acceptance (waqf). The proof is qiyas: a bequest is a tamlik bi-'aqd (ownership transfer by contract), like gift and sale, which require acceptance.
The text explicitly states: 'wa la-nā: annahu tamlīk bi-'aqd, fa-waqafa 'alā al-qabūl, ka-l-tamlīk bi-l-hibat wa-l-bay'.'
3
Contrast with Imam Zufr's view
Imam Zufr held that the bequest transfers ownership automatically at death, like inheritance. The Hanafi response: inheritance is compulsory (qahr), not a contract.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is whether the legatee's pre-acceptance death voids the bequest.
4
Apply the distinction: acceptance before death vs. after death
Acceptance or rejection during the testator's life is void because ownership can only pass after death. Acceptance after death immediately vests ownership.
Al-Khujandi's classification: explicit acceptance (saying 'I accept') vs. circumstantial acceptance (legatee's death after testator counts as acceptance).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from al-Jawharah al-Nayrah on the sections of wills (Wasiyyah) from al-Mukhtasar of al-Quduri, focusing on the conditions for appointing an executor (wasi). Two Executors: Joint Action Required Except in Necessity
Intra-Hanafi dispute on independent action by one executor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi position on joint executors, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, and apply the exceptions where one executor may act independently.

Core Idea
Delegated authority respects the testator's condition of jointness; one executor is not like the pair.

When a testator appoints two executors, does each have full authority, or must they act together? The Hanafi school has a well-known internal difference. The majority position requires joint action except in certain urgent matters, while Abu Yusuf permits each to act alone. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents the dispute, the reasoning behind each view, and a detailed list of exceptions. Understanding this ruling is essential for anyone who will draft wills or administer estates under Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: it is not permissible for one executor to act without the other (la yajuz). Note that they make exceptions for certain urgent cases.
This is the position of the majority and is recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Identify the opponent and their proof
Abu Yusuf holds that each executor may act independently in all matters. His proof: executorship is a form of wilaya, which is indivisible, like the guardianship of marriage (two brothers each have full authority to give in marriage).
The qiyas is to wilaya by kinship.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad distinguish: wilaya here arises from delegation (tafwid), not kinship. The condition of jointness must be observed because the testator only consented to the pair acting together. One is not equivalent to two.
The distinction is based on the cause (sabab) of the authority.
4
Determine the exceptions and their rationale
List the exceptions: purchasing the shroud, preparing the deceased, feeding/clothing minor children, returning specific deposits, paying debts, executing specific bequests, freeing specific slaves, litigation, and selling perishable items. The rationale: necessity (delay would cause harm or impossibility of joint action).
In each case, the urgency overrides the general rule of jointness.
5
Note the consensus when appointed separately
If the testator appoints each executor separately (e.g., 'I appoint X to handle A and Y to handle B'), then each may act independently by consensus.
This is a crucial distinction that avoids confusion.

Death of a Co-Executor and Ratification of Unauthorized Acts
Rulings on death of co-executor and ratification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules when one co-executor dies and explain how an unauthorized act can be rectified by ratification.

Core Idea
Upon death of one co-executor, the survivor cannot act alone until a new executor is appointed; but ratification of an unauthorized act by the other executor makes it valid.

What happens if one co-executor dies? The authority does not transfer to the survivor. The deceased executor also cannot appoint the survivor alone; a judge must appoint a new co-executor. However, if one executor acts without permission (outside exceptions), the other's subsequent ratification validates the act without needing to repeat the contract.

Dividing the Bequest of All and One-Third: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on dividing the permissible third between two rival legatees.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on dividing the permissible third in cases of competing bequests and explain the reasoning behind each position.

Core Idea
The legatee of all and the legatee of one-third divide the permissible third equally or in a 3:1 ratio depending on how the claim is treated.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between the founder of the school, Abu Hanifa, and his two principal disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The issue arises when a testator makes two bequests: one for the entire estate and another for one-third of it, and the heirs do not consent to the excess over one-third. Both legatees have valid claims only within the effective third, but the method of apportioning that third differs. Understanding this dispute sharpens your skill in analyzing how the same facts can yield different legal conclusions based on the underlying juristic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the two bequests
The testator bequeaths his entire estate to one legatee and one-third of the estate to another. The heirs do not approve the bequest of all, so only one-third of the estate is executable.
The bequest of all is valid up to one-third; the excess is suspended pending approval.
2
State the Sahibayn's position and their method
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad divide the third into four shares: the legatee of all gets three shares (three-quarters of the third) and the legatee of the third gets one share (one-quarter).
They analogize to the 'awl principle in inheritance, treating the total bequests as four-thirds.
3
Derive the 3:1 ratio under Sahibayn
Total property = three-thirds. Adding the bequest of one-third gives four-thirds. The legatee of all effectively claims three-thirds, so his share of the third is 3/4.
The Sahibayn treat the claim of the legatee of all as a present claim to the entire property despite the suspension.
4
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa divides the third equally (halves). He argues that the legatee of all's excess over one-third is a right of others, so he cannot compete with the other legatee for that share. Thus both have only a claim to one-third each.
For Abu Hanifa, the suspended nature of the excess means the legatee of all is treated as if he were only bequeathed one-third.
5
Identify the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf is a real difference in distribution. Under the Sahibayn, the legatee of all receives 3/4 of the third; under Abu Hanifa, he receives 1/2. The difference is 1/4 of the third.
Later Hanafi fatwa generally adopts the view of the Sahibayn, though Abu Hanifa's view is also transmitted.

Division of Bequests When Heirs Disallow: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn and the Three Exceptions
Intra-Hanafi dispute on division of third, plus three exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on dividing the one-third when bequests exceed the limit, and identify the three exceptional cases where Abu Hanifa allows striking for more than one-third.

Core Idea
When heirs disallow, the third is divided proportionally (Abu Hanifa) or by full claim (Sahibayn); three exceptions bypass the cap.

When a testator makes multiple bequests that together exceed one-third of the estate and the heirs refuse to consent, the law limits the total bequest to one-third. But how should that one-third be divided among the legatees? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement. This passage from Sharh al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents Abu Hanifa’s method of proportional reduction and the Sahibayn’s method of allowing each legatee to claim his full bequest up to the third. It also reveals three special cases where Abu Hanifa himself abandons the proportional cap, treating the bequests differently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the core rule
The starting point is that bequests to non-heirs are valid only from one-third of the estate unless the heirs consent to more. When heirs disallow, only one-third is available. The passage considers two legatees: one bequeathed half the estate, the other bequeathed one-quarter. If the heirs consent, the division is straightforward. But if they disallow, how is the one-third split?
Remember: bequests to heirs require consent; bequests to non-heirs are capped at one-third.
2
Apply Abu Hanifa’s method: proportional reduction
Abu Hanifa rules that any bequest exceeding one-third is void (mulgha). So the half bequest is reduced to one-third; the quarter bequest remains at one-quarter. The third is then divided in the ratio of one-third to one-quarter, i.e., 4:3. Thus the half legatee receives 4/7 of the third, the quarter legatee 3/7. The whole estate becomes 21 parts: 7 for legatees, 14 for heirs.
Abu Hanifa’s reasoning: the excess over one-third is invalid, so the legatee’s claim is only for one-third of the property, not the original fraction.
3
Contrast with the Sahibayn’s method: full claim up to the third
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that each legatee strikes for his full bequest, but limited by the one-third ceiling. The half legatee strikes for half (which is three-sixths), the quarter legatee strikes for one-quarter (two-sixths). So they divide the third in the ratio 2:1 (half:quarter). The half legatee gets 2/3 of the third, the quarter legatee gets 1/3. The whole estate becomes 18 parts: 6 for legatees, 12 for heirs.
Sahibayn’s reasoning: the bequest is valid up to the third; the legatee should not lose his priority based on the original fraction.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the half legatee gets 4/21 of the whole estate; under the Sahibayn, he gets 2/9 (which is larger). Later Hanafi scholars preferred the Sahibayn’s view as more equitable and easier to apply, so it is the mufta bihi.
Thamarah al-khilaf = the concrete difference in shares received.
5
Examine the three exceptional cases
The text then notes that Abu Hanifa makes an exception for three types of bequests: (1) muḥābāh (a favorable sale during death illness, the difference counts as bequest), (2) sa‘āyah (manumission with service, the slave’s value counts as bequest), and (3) al-darāhim al-mursalah (a bequest of a fixed sum of money, not attached to a specific item). In these, Abu Hanifa does not cap the legatee’s claim at one-third; instead the third is divided proportionally to the full amount of the favor or value, because the valuation is not fixed and the excess cannot be simply annulled.
These exceptions show Abu Hanifa’s practical concern: in these cases, the ‘bequest’ is not a simple grant but a favor or a valuation that must be assessed as a whole.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri explains the Hanafi ruling on multiple bequests that exceed the one-third limit when the heirs do not approve. The author first presents an example: a testator bequeaths freedom to two slaves, one worth 1000 and the other 2000, but only the third of the estate (1000) is available. The legatees share the third proportionally to their bequests (1:2), and each works to pay the remaining value for freedom. The author then contrasts this with what the qiyas (analogy) would require if this were treated like ordinary bequests: each would only claim from the third proportionally, but here they claim their full bequest and work for the excess. The text then gives three scenarios (المحاباة—favoring in sale, السعاية—emancipation of slaves, and الدراهم المرسلة—unspecified amounts of dirhams) where the same rule applies: because the wording of each bequest is valid (a specific amount), the legatee may claim his full bequest from the third, and work for the remainder. The key distinction is whether the bequest is worded as a specific amount (valid) or as a fraction of the estate (invalid at the outset, because the actual amount is unknown). If the wording is a fraction, the whole bequest fails if it cannot be fully satisfied from the third.

Key Terms
الوصية في مخرجها صحيحة (al-wasiyyah fi makhrajiha sahihah): A bequest whose wording (e.g., a specific amount like 1000) is valid at the time of utterance, making it possible that it can be fulfilled from the third if the testator has other wealth.
السعاية (al-si'ayah): The work a partially freed slave must do to earn the remaining value of his freedom.
المحاباة (al-muhabah): A sale at a price less than the market value, treated as a bequest for the difference.

Key Takeaway
A bequest of a specific amount is valid in origin; if it exceeds the third, the legatee receives a proportional share of the third and works for the remainder.
