Touching a Woman When Buying: Between Necessity and Desire
Clarifies the ruling and conditions for touching a woman when purchasing her.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which touching a woman when intending to buy is permissible or impermissible in the Hanafi school, and explain the distinction between looking and touching regarding the presence of desire.

Core Idea
Touching a woman when buying is permissible only if no desire arises; with desire, it becomes prohibited as a form of pleasure.

The matn of al-Quduri states that touching a woman when intending to buy is permissible even if one fears desire. However, the later Hanafi authorities in al-Hidayah refine this ruling by distinguishing between looking and touching. Looking is allowed due to necessity, but touching is not allowed if desire is present, because it constitutes direct physical pleasure. In this lesson, we will unpack this distinction and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling from the matn
Read the matn: 'There is no harm in his touching that [body part] when he intends to buy, even if he fears he may desire.' This suggests a general permissibility.
The matn uses 'la ba's' indicating permissibility.
2
Locate the commentator's clarification
The commentator specifies that 'that' refers to body parts other than the belly and back, i.e., those permissible to look at.
This restricts the permissible touch to certain areas.
3
Analyze the Hidayah citation
Al-Hidayah reports that 'our shaykhs' distinguish looking from touching: looking is permitted even with desire due to necessity, but touching is not permitted if desire is present or expected.
The key difference: looking does not give direct pleasure like touching.
4
Determine the illa (effective cause) for prohibition
Touching with desire is prohibited because it is a type of istimta' (pleasure), which is not necessary for the purchase.
Necessity (darura) permits looking, but touching with desire goes beyond necessity.

Hoarding Essential Foodstuffs: Ruling and Evidence
Declares hoarding essential foodstuffs as disliked when harmful

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ihtikar (hoarding foodstuffs) and explain the evidence from the hadith, including the condition of harm.

Core Idea
Hoarding essential foodstuffs that harms the community is makruh tahrimi — the Prophet cursed the hoarder.

This lesson addresses the fiqh ruling on ihtikar — hoarding essential foodstuffs. While many assume all hoarding is forbidden, the Hanafi school makes a nuanced distinction based on harm. The primary evidence is a hadith that contrasts the blessed importer with the cursed hoarder. We will unpack the ruling, its conditions, and the proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is ihtikar (hoarding) of essential foodstuffs (aqwat) for humans and animals. The ruling is makruh (disliked), specifically makruh tahrimi when it causes harm.
The ruling is conditional: it applies only when the hoarding harms the people of the town.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the prophetic hadith: 'The one who brings goods is blessed, and the hoarder is cursed.' This is a sahih hadith, though some chains are weak.
The hadith is the core evidence; its wording directly links hoarding to a curse.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'mal'un' (cursed) indicates severe displeasure. In Hanafi usul, a curse implies a prohibition, but since the hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), the ruling is makruh tahrimi (close to haram), not absolute haram.
The strength of the evidence determines the level of ruling.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) consider ihtikar directly haram based on the same hadith and other proofs. The Hanafi school distinguishes because the hadith is ahad and the harm condition is essential.
The practical difference: Hanafi says makruh, others say haram.
5
Evaluate the condition of harm
The ruling only applies if the hoarding 'harms the people of the town'. If there is no shortage or harm, hoarding for legitimate needs (e.g., family storage) is not disliked.
Condition (shart) is key: without harm, the ruling does not attach.

The Scope of Hoarding (Ihtikar): Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Exposition of hoarding rules with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on what goods constitute hoarding, and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Hoarding is not limited to food; anything harmful to the public is prohibited.

The prohibition of hoarding (ihtikar) is well-established in Hanafi fiqh, but the school itself contains a significant internal dispute over what types of goods fall under this ruling. Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad each defined hoarding differently, with practical consequences for the permissibility of withholding gold, clothing, or other goods. This lesson unpacks their arguments and identifies the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Guided Path
1
Identify permissible confinement
Note that storing goods without causing harm, e.g., in a large city, is permissible. This sets the baseline: hoarding is only condemned when harm occurs.
2
Locate the core dispute on goods
Abu Hanifa limits hoarding to food staples (aqwat) like wheat, barley, qat, chaff, and fodder. Abu Yusuf extends it to anything whose confinement harms the public, even gold or clothing. Muhammad excludes clothing entirely.
The difference lies in the definition of harm and the scope of the prohibition.
3
Analyze the proof structure
Abu Hanifa relies on the custom and a hadith specifically mentioning food (forty days). Abu Yusuf invokes a general hadith: 'The hoarder is cursed' (محتكر ملعون) and the principle of public welfare. Muhammad follows custom that clothing is not hoarded.
The general hadith is used by Abu Yusuf to override the specific restriction.
4
Apply the exceptions
Hoarding one's own estate produce or goods brought from another country is not hoarding according to Abu Hanifa, because the public has no prior claim on them. Abu Yusuf holds it still disliked due to the generality of the hadith.
This exception shows how rights of the public are defined: only goods from the local market or brought into the city are subject to hoarding.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi jurists (like al-Haskafi and Ibn Abidin) prefer Abu Yusuf's view for its public welfare consideration, so the operative ruling is that hoarding includes anything harmful, not just food.
However, the ruling remains makruh tahrimi, not haram, due to the weakness of the hadith chain.

Who Is Not a Hoarder: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Defines who is not a hoarder and presents Abu Hanifa vs Abu Yusuf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hoarding one's own produce or imports and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Your own produce and imports are not hoarding; public right only attaches to local goods.

The Hanafi school establishes that not every act of withholding goods is hoarding (ihtikar). The passage defines two clear exceptions: produce from one's own estate and goods imported from another town. But is there disagreement? Imam Abu Yusuf challenges the second exception based on a general hadith. Let's examine the dispute and the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
The passage describes two situations where a person is not considered a hoarder: (1) hoarding the produce of one's own estate, and (2) hoarding goods brought from another town. Note the justification for each.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa rules that in both scenarios the person is not a hoarder. For own produce: it is his pure right; he may choose not to sell. For imports: the public right only attaches to goods gathered within the city.
The qiyas for own produce: just as he may refuse to plant, he may refuse to sell.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's Position
Abu Yusuf disagrees on imports: he considers it makruh (disliked) due to the general hadith 'The hoarder is cursed.' He applies the hadith broadly without limiting it to local goods.
This is a dalil naqli (textual proof) argument.
4
Understand the Hanafi Response to Abu Yusuf
The Hanafi response is that the hadith is general but is specified (takhsis) by the context: the public right only attaches to goods from within the city. Imported goods are treated like one's own produce.
The principle: 'al-amm yukhassas bi al-maslahah' or context.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi and Practical Consequence
The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's: the person is not a hoarder and the ruler cannot force sale. The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is that under Abu Yusuf's view the ruler may intervene to prevent harm.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.

Priority Between Preferential Sale and Emancipation in the One-Third: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on competing bequests

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the priority of muḥābāh and 'itq in the one-third, and apply the rule to determine the buyer's option and the slave's service (sʿāyah).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prioritizes preferential sale over emancipation; the Sahibayn prioritize emancipation, affecting buyer's option and slave's service.

This passage from Kitab al-Wasaya presents a scenario where a dying person makes both preferential sales (muḥābāh) and emancipations ('itq) that exceed the one-third. Abu Hanifa and his disciples Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree on which takes precedence. The practical outcome determines the buyer's option and whether the emancipated slave must perform service (sʿāyah) for the heirs. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for applying the correct ruling in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the competing bequests
A dying person has two slaves: one sold below market value (muḥābāh) and one emancipated. Total assets = 3000, one-third = 1000. The muḥābāh difference is 1000, the emancipation value is 1000, so total bequests exceed one-third.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the muḥābāh takes precedence because it involves consideration. The buyer takes the slave at the agreed price (1000), and the emancipation is valid but the slave must perform sʿāyah for the full value (1000) to compensate the heirs.
No buyer's option in this case because the muḥābāh is fully effective.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that emancipation takes precedence because it is an act of piety. The emancipation is effective, the slave is free, and the buyer has the option: either take the slave at the higher price (2000) or return him.
The buyer's option arises because the priority changes the available one-third for muḥābāh.
4
Analyze the alternative scenario (emancipation first)
If the dying person emancipates first, then makes a muḥābāh, Abu Hanifa still prioritizes muḥābāh? Actually the text shows that when 'itq precedes, under Abu Hanifa the one-third is divided equally, the slave pays sʿāyah for half his value, and the buyer has an option to take at 1500 or return. This shows the rule of equality when types differ.
The alternative scenario clarifies that the order matters but the underlying principle of priority remains.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical difference is: under Abu Hanifa, the buyer gets the slave at the original lower price (no option), and the slave works for the heirs for his full value. Under the Sahibayn, the slave is freed without full sʿāyah, and the buyer must pay more or return the slave.
Later Hanafi fatwa often follows the Sahibayn, prioritizing emancipation.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Wasaya discusses how to divide the one-third of a dying person's property when multiple transactions compete. The author presents Abu Hanifa's rule: transactions of the same type (e.g., two preferential sales) share equally; when transactions differ, the one with priority in time gets precedence. Then an example from al-Yanabiʿ illustrates the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn: if a man sells a slave for less than value (muḥābāh) and emancipates another, Abu Hanifa says the muḥābāh is prior because it is a transaction with consideration, while the Sahibayn prioritize emancipation because it tends to be more meritorious. The practical outcome involves the buyer's option and the slave's service (sʿāyah). This is an intra-Hanafi dispute with clear positions and consequences.
