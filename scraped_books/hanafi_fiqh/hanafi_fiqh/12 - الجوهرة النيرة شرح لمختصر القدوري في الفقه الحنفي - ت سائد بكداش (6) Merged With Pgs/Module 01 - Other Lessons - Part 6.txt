Istihqaq after Qisma: Three Scenarios and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on istihqaq after division with ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on istihqaq after qisma in three scenarios and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the third scenario.

Core Idea
Undivided claim annuls division; known claim gives option; undivided in one hand splits the school.

After partners divide jointly owned property, a third party may come forward with a rightful claim to some of the property. The Hanafi school distinguishes three cases: when the claim is to an undivided share of the whole, when it is to a known divided part in one partner's hand, and when it is to an undivided part in one partner's hand. The first two cases are settled by consensus; the third sparks an internal Hanafi ikhtilaf that turns on the nature of qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three factual scenarios
Read the text and isolate the three different types of istihqaq: (1) istihqaq shā'i' fil kull (undivided in the whole), (2) istihqaq ma'lum muqassam fī yad ahadihima (known divided in one hand), (3) istihqaq shā'i' fī yad ahadihima (undivided in one hand).
The first two are by consensus; the third is disputed.
2
Understand the consensus rulings and their rationales
For scenario 1, the division is annulled to prevent the claimant's share from being scattered. For scenario 2, the claimant has an option: either annul the division or keep it and seek compensation (a quarter). The compensation is derived by qiyas to the full claim case.
The rationale for scenario 1 is harm (darar) to the claimant.
3
Analyze the disputed scenario and the qiyas arguments
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad analogize scenario 3 to scenario 2 (known claim): the claimant should have the same option. Abu Yusuf analogizes scenario 3 to scenario 1 (undivided in whole): the undivided nature introduces a third partner, making division void without his consent.
The difference turns on which analogy (qiyas) is more appropriate.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
Under Abu Hanifa's view, the division may stand if the claimant chooses compensation; under Abu Yusuf, it is automatically void. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, giving the claimant the option.
This is the established tarjih in Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Qisma discusses the effect of a third party's rightful claim (istihqaq) on property that has already been divided between partners. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not specified in the text, but the Hanafi school typically follows Abu Hanifa's view in such cases.

قسمة (qisma): Division of jointly owned property into separate shares among partners.
شائع (mushā'): An undivided share in the whole property, not demarcated.

Definition and Conditions of Coercion (Ikrah) in Hanafi Law
Legal definition of coercion and its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ikrah and identify its two key effects (negation of consent vs. corruption of choice) with examples.

Core Idea
Coercion negates consent or corrupts choice, but the coerced person remains legally capable.

Coercion (ikrah) is a critical concept in Hanafi fiqh that affects the validity of actions like contracts and torts. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah defines ikrah and sets out the conditions for its legal effect, distinguishing between acts where the coerced person becomes a tool (āla) and acts where only consent is negated. Understanding this definition is essential to applying later rules on the consequences of coercion.

Guided Path
1
Define ikrah lexically and technically
Note that ikrah is an act by one person upon another that either negates consent (rida) or corrupts choice (ikhtiyar), while legal capacity remains. This is the core definition.
The definition excludes cases where the person loses capacity entirely (e.g., insanity).
2
Identify the condition for effective coercion
Ikrah takes effect only when the coercer is capable of carrying out the threat, regardless of whether he is a sultan, a private person, or a thief.
Capability means the coerced person genuinely fears the threat will be executed.
3
Distinguish the two effects: negation of consent vs. corruption of choice
For contracts (e.g., sale), the coerced person becomes a mere tool (āla); consent is negated but choice may remain intact. For destructive acts (e.g., killing, property damage), complete coercion (threat of death or amputation) corrupts choice due to natural compulsion.
This distinction determines legal consequences: contracts become invalid (fasid/batil), while destructive acts may absolve liability under certain conditions.
4
Understand the rationale for the distinction
The text explains that man is naturally inclined to preserve life, so a threat of death compels the action, corrupting free choice. In contracts, the action is merely directed by the coercer, so only consent is affected.
This aligns with the Hanafi concept of 'āla (tool) – the coerced person in contracts is like an instrument.

If the coercer lacks such capability, the threat is not considered effective coercion.

اختيار (ikhtiyar): Free choice; its corruption makes the person a mere tool, so actions like destruction are not attributed to him freely.

Hanafi Conditions for Duress (Ikrah) to be Legally Effective
Definition and conditions of duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of duress in Hanafi law and identify the conditions required for duress to be legally effective, applying the distinction between negation of consent and corruption of choice.

Core Idea
Duress is legally effective only when the threatener can execute the threat — capability, not status, matters.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi definition of duress (ikrah), a key concept affecting the validity of transactions. You will learn the precise conditions under which a coerced act has legal effect, and understand the critical distinction between contracts (where consent is negated) and acts of destruction (where choice is corrupted). The passage emphasizes that the threatener must be capable of carrying out the threat, regardless of their official standing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition
Extract the definition of ikrah from the text: an action that negates consent or corrupts choice while legal capacity remains.
The definition has two components: effect on consent/choice, and preservation of capacity.
2
Distinguish two effects
Note the dual effect: negation of consent applies to contracts (e.g., sale), while corruption of choice applies to acts where the coerced is a mere tool (e.g., destruction).
These are mutually exclusive based on the nature of the coerced act.
3
Determine the condition for establishment
Duress is legally effective only if the threat comes from someone able to execute the threat. The text explicitly mentions 'whether a ruler or other, or a robber' to show that status is irrelevant.
This condition is about capability, not authority.
4
Recognize the threshold for complete duress
Complete duress (ikrah mulji') involves threat of death or mutilation, causing both negation of consent and corruption of choice due to the natural instinct to preserve life.
Incomplete duress (ghayr mulji') only affects consent or choice partially.
5
Apply to hypothetical scenarios
Test whether a coerced sale or destruction is effective by checking: (1) Is the threatener capable? (2) Is the duress complete? (3) Does it negate consent or corrupt choice?
This application clarifies the practical legal outcome.

Outward Compliance with Kufr under Coercion
Commentary expanding on coercion rulings for eating carrion and uttering kufr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which outward compliance with kufr is permitted under coercion and identify the superior option.

Core Idea
Outward compliance with kufr under threat of death or limb loss is permissible if the heart remains faithful; patient endurance is superior.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah expands on the rulings of coercion (ikrah) in matters of faith and forbidden food. It clarifies when a Muslim may outwardly comply with demands of kufr or eating carrion without sin, and when such compliance is prohibited. The key distinction is the severity of the threat and the state of the heart.

Guided Path
1
Identify the threatened harm
Determine whether the coercion involves a threat of death, loss of a limb, or lesser harm (imprisonment, shackling). Only the first two constitute genuine ikrah that permits outward compliance.
Lesser threats do not justify outward kufr; the person must refuse even if it leads to harm.
2
Evaluate the probability of execution
The permissibility requires overwhelming probability (ghalabat al-zann) that the threat will be carried out. If not, compliance is not allowed.
Apply the standard of ghalabat al-zann: more than 50% chance.
3
Assess inner faith
The heart must remain firmly settled in faith (mutma'in bil-iman). Outward words do not affect true belief if the inner conviction remains intact.
This is based on the Quranic verse (16:106) and the example of 'Ammar ibn Yasir.
4
Apply the ruling on permissibility
If all conditions are met, outward compliance is permissible (mubah). However, patient endurance and acceptance of death is more meritorious (mustahabb) and brings greater reward.
The example of Habib ibn 'Adiyy illustrates the superior option.
5
Note the exception for minor coercion
If one utters kufr under minor coercion (imprisonment, shackles) and later claims inner faith, he is not believed. Only genuine ikrah (threat of death/limb loss) excuses the outward act.
This refinement comes from al-Khujandi.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expanding on the rulings of compulsion (ikrah) from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It covers three main scenarios: compulsion to free a slave, compulsion to marry, and compulsion to commit zina. 

First, if someone is compelled to free his slave, the compeller must pay the slave's value to the owner (the one compelled). This is regardless of whether the compeller is wealthy or poor. The patronage (wala') of the freed slave goes to the original master (the owner who was compelled), and the slave does not have to perform sa'yah (earning to compensate the master) because the freeing was from the master's side and ownership is complete. This differs from the case of a pledged slave freed by an insolvent pledgor, where sa'yah is required due to the pledgee's right. Also, if someone is compelled to purchase a close blood relative (mahram), that relative becomes free upon purchase, and the compeller is not liable for the value because the compulsion was for the purchase, not the freeing.

Second, compulsion to marry: the marriage contract is valid. If the named dower is equal to or less than the proper dower (mahr al-mithl), it is valid and the compelled husband cannot recourse against the compeller for anything, because he was compensated with exactly what he gave. If the named dower exceeds the proper dower, the excess is void and only the proper dower is payable. It becomes as if they named the proper dower, so if divorce occurs before consummation, half the proper dower is due. The husband can recourse against the compeller for half the dower if divorce happened before consummation, because the dower was threatened with dropping (e.g., if the woman apostatized) and became confirmed only by the compeller's action (the divorce). After consummation, the full dower is established in the husband's liability due to consummation, not divorce, so no recourse against the compeller.

Third, compulsion to commit zina: According to Abu Hanifa, the compelled man is subject to the hadd punishment for zina, unless the compeller is the sultan (the ruler). This is because compulsion is not conceivable in zina for a man; erection requires desire and choice, not fear. Hence, he is considered to have committed zina voluntarily. A woman, however, is not subject to hadd if compelled, because she only submits, which can occur under compulsion. If the sultan compels a man, there are two narrations: one (held by Zufar) says hadd is obligatory; the second (implied but not stated) likely says no hadd. The text notes that the basis for Zufar's view is the same reasoning as above.

In Hanafi siyar, a peace treaty (sulh) is binding and must be honored. However, if breaking it becomes more beneficial for the Muslims, the imam may terminate it, but only after following a specific procedure: he must 'throw back' (nabdh) the covenant to the enemy and allow sufficient time for the news to reach all parties. Renouncing a Peace Treaty: The Obligation of Notification (Nabdh)
Ruling on breaking a treaty with non-Muslims after notice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on renouncing a peace treaty and apply the requirement of notification to determine the lawful commencement of hostilities.

Core Idea
Breaking a treaty requires prior notification; otherwise it is treachery (ghadr), which is forbidden.

This lesson examines a crucial principle in Hanafi siyar: the obligation to notify the enemy before resuming warfare after a peace treaty. The imam may renounce a treaty if it is more beneficial for Muslims, but he must give sufficient notice to avoid treachery. We will analyze the ruling, its proof from the Quran and Prophetic practice, and the practical period required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? Renouncing a peace treaty (nabdh). What is the ruling? Wajib (obligatory) to do it with notification; without notice it is treachery (ghadr), which is haram.
The ruling is derived from the need to avoid ghadr.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verses of Bara'ah (Surah at-Tawbah 9:1-10) and the incident of Ali (ra) at the Day of Nahr. The Quran commands a four-month period; the Prophet (peace be upon him) implemented this by sending Ali to announce the renunciation.
The dalil is Quran (qati al-thubut) but the interpretation of the period is zanni.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command to grant a four-month period implies that renunciation must be preceded by a period allowing the enemy to learn of the treaty's annulment and respond.
The specific period may vary; the principle is sufficiency for notification.
4
Determine the practical period
The period is sufficient for the enemy's king to inform the farthest reaches of his territory after learning of the renunciation.
This was the practice of the Prophet; the four months in the Quran was a specific case.
5
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Some schools may not require notification if the enemy has breached the treaty; the Hanafi school insists on notification even then to avoid treachery.
This difference stems from the weight given to the principle of avoiding ghadr.

When Notification is Not Required: Four Rulings on Truce Termination
Quranic basis and four fiqh rulings on truce termination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four Hanafi rulings on termination of truce and identify which cases require prior notice (nabdh) and which do not.

Core Idea
A truce ends by betrayal or expiry without notice, but enemy safe passage continues until return.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, a commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, explains the Quranic verses from Surah al-Tawbah that set the stage for dealing with polytheists after the declaration of disavowal. It then derives four specific fiqh rulings: when betrayal nullifies the need for notice, when a fixed-term truce automatically expires, the safety of an enemy who entered under truce, and the freedom of enemy slaves who escape. Let's work through these rulings step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic context
Read the initial Quranic commentary: God grants a four-month grace period except for those who kept their treaty. After those months, fighting is permitted. This sets the background for the rulings.
The four months are Shawwal, Dhu al-Qi'dah, Dhu al-Hijjah, and Muharram—not the sacred months.
2
Extract the first ruling: betrayal without notice
Focus on: 'If they initiate betrayal by mutual agreement, fight them and do not give them notice.' The key condition is 'bi-ittifaqihim'—the betrayal must be unanimous from the enemy side.
Notice (nabdh) is normally required before hostilities; this is an exception.
3
Extract the second ruling: fixed-term expiry
When the truce is for a known time and the time passes, the covenant is void without notice. Time-bound contracts end automatically.
This applies even if the enemy did not commit any betrayal.
4
Extract the third ruling: safety of the truce entrant
If an enemy entered Muslim lands under the truce and the term expires while he is still there, he remains safe until he returns to his own place of safety. The proof is Qur'an 9:6.
His blood and property are inviolable during that period.
5
Extract the fourth ruling: freedom of escaping slaves
If enemy slaves escape to the Muslim army, they become free immediately. Similarly, if they accept Islam in their land and Muslims conquer that land, they are free—this is legal emancipation (itq hukmi).
No wala' (patronage) is established over them.

Ransoming Muslim captives with polytheist captives is prohibited according to Abu Hanifa because it strengthens the enemy.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah presents an important intra-Hanafi dispute on the treatment of war captives. Guided Path
1

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Conquered land: imam may divide or keep with kharaj; preference depends on combatants' need.

This passage from the commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri explains the ruling on land conquered by force ('anwah). The imam has two equally valid choices: distribute the land as spoils or leave it with its owners and impose kharaj. Mann is prohibited because it strengthens the enemy.

The imam has three options for captives, but Arab polytheists and apostates may never be freed as dhimmis.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on the treatment of captives (asara) taken in jihad. The central principle is that ownership transfer depends not just on capture but on bringing the property into the conqueror's territory (dar al-harb) – a concept called ihraz. This lesson unpacks that principle, its evidence from hadith, and the contrast with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Scenarios
The passage discusses: (1) non-Muslims conquering other non-Muslims, (2) Muslims conquering non-Muslims, and (3) non-Muslims conquering Muslims. Note the different rulings for each.
Scenarios 1 and 2 are straightforward; scenario 3 is the disputed one.
2
Understand the Concept of Ihraz
Ihraz means taking possession of property and bringing it into the conqueror's territory (dar al-harb). For Hanafis, this is the condition that finalizes ownership transfer. Without ihraz, the property remains owned by the original owner.
Hanafis require ihraz; Shafi'is do not.
3
Apply the Analogy to Purchase
The commentary states that taking from them (the non-Muslims) is like purchasing. If we purchase from them, we own it; so when we overpower them, we own it similarly.
This analogy strengthens the Hanafi position on mutual ownership transfer.
4
Analyze the Hadith Evidence
The hadith 'Whoever accepts Islam over something, it is his' is cited. If non-Muslims capture Muslim property and later accept Islam, the property remains theirs. This confirms that their prior possession (after ihraz) was valid ownership.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted as evidence here.
5
Contrast with the Shafi'i View
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that non-Muslims never own Muslim property, even after ihraz. The practical difference: if the non-Muslims later accept Islam, Hanafis say the property stays theirs, while Shafi'is say it must be returned.
This ikhtilaf has real-world implications for post-war property disputes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When enemy secures Muslim property in dar al-harb, ownership transfers to them; the original owner's right is severed.

This lesson examines a key rule of Hanafi siyar: what happens to Muslim property when captured by the enemy and taken into their territory. The passage from Mukhtasar al-Quduri and its commentary establish the ruling that the enemy owns such property upon securing it (iḥrāz) in dar al-harb. We will study the proof from hadith, the analogy to purchase, and the important disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The matn presents two symmetrical cases: Muslims taking enemy property (halal for Muslims) and enemy taking Muslim property (enemy owns it). Identify the legal effect in each.
Focus on the second scenario (enemy taking Muslim property) as it is the subject of dispute.
2
Understand the concept of iḥrāz
The key condition for ownership transfer is 'iḥrāz'—securing the property in dar al-harb. Explore why this is sufficient for ownership according to Hanafis.
Iḥrāz implies that the property is beyond immediate recapture, analogous to taking possession in a purchase.
3
Recognize the analogy and hadith proof
The sharh draws an analogy: 'taking from them is like purchase'. Also, the hadith 'Man aslama 'ala mali fa huwa lahu' supports that conversion confirms ownership acquired through iḥrāz.
Both are used to justify the Hanafi position.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i counterargument
Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, arguing that ownership does not transfer until formal division (qismah). Understand the basis of his view.
The thamarah al-khilaf is practical: whether a Muslim reconquering must pay for his own property or can take it for free.
5

If the enemy converts to Islam while holding the property, the original owner has no claim. Apply the hadith and the principle of ownership transfer.
This confirms that ownership was already established before conversion.

Recovering Property Taken by the Enemy: Hanafi Rules
Explains Hanafi rules when Muslims recover property from enemy before/after division, trader case, and slave freedom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a Muslim recovering property from the enemy in each of three scenarios: before division, after division, and when purchased by a trader.

Core Idea
When enemy spoils are recovered, the original owner's right depends on division: free before, by value after, and optional reimbursement when bought by a trader.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains how the Hanafi school treats Muslim property that was taken by the enemy and later recovered. The key factor is whether the enemy had already divided the spoils among themselves. The rules also cover special cases like fungible goods, slaves, and property bought by a Muslim trader. Understanding these distinctions protects the rights of the original owner without unfairly harming new possessors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the timing of recovery
The ruling changes based on whether the property is found before or after the enemy has divided the spoils among themselves. Before division, the enemy's ownership is not fully established; after division, it is.
Division (qismah) crystallizes individual ownership among the enemy fighters.
2
Apply the rule for before division
If Muslims recover the property before division, the original owner takes it back for free. The enemy had no individual title to the property.
This is because the property is still considered part of the collective booty of the enemy, not yet owned by any individual.
3
Apply the rule for after division
If the property is recovered after it has been divided among the enemy and is in the hands of an individual, the original owner may take it by paying its value to the new owner. The owner may also choose to leave it.
Paying the value compensates the new owner for his loss, as he acquired it legitimately through the division.
4
Handle special cases: fungibles and slaves
For fungible goods (mithli), the owner does not take the specific item but receives an equivalent from the Muslim who acquired it in division. If the property is a slave and the new owner frees him, the freeing is valid and the original owner loses all rights. If the new owner sells the slave, the original owner may reclaim the slave by paying the sale price to the buyer.
Fungibles are replaced by equivalents because the benefit is identical. Freeing a slave extinguishes the original owner's right.
5
Analyze the trader scenario
If a Muslim trader buys the property from the enemy and brings it to dar al-Islam, the original owner has an option: take the property by paying the trader the price he paid, or leave it. This protects the trader from loss.
The trader paid consideration, so taking it for free would be unjust.

Ruling on recovered war spoils before and after division.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Before spoils are divided, property goes back free; after division, you must pay.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a common scenario in classic fiqh: when the enemy captures property of a Muslim, and then the Muslims re-capture it. The ruling changes dramatically depending on whether the division of spoils (qisma) has taken place. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the principles of property rights and compensation.

Separate the rule for property found before qisma and after qisma. Note the different consequences.
Before qisma: free return. After qisma: payment of price.

Understand the rationale
Why is there a difference? Before division, the property is still considered owned by the original owner (the enemy's possession did not transfer ownership). After division, the spoils have become owned by the fighters, so the original owner must compensate.
The enemy's possession in dar al-harb does not change ownership according to Hanafi.

Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a hypothetical case, determine whether the owner can reclaim for free or must pay.
Focus on the timing relative to qisma.

The Cavalryman's Shares: Resolving Conflicting Hadith
Intra-Hanafi dispute on cavalry shares in ghanima

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over cavalry shares and evaluate Abu Hanifa's method of resolving conflicting hadith reports.

Core Idea
When hadith reports conflict, the Hanafi school adopts the minimal agreed-upon ruling—two shares for the cavalryman.

This lesson examines a foundational dispute within the Hanafi school: how many shares should a cavalryman receive from the spoils of war? Abu Hanifa and his two major companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagree on this point. The dispute hinges on how to interpret conflicting hadith reports and the role of qiyas. By studying this case, you will see how Hanafi usul principles are applied to a concrete fiqh problem.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Abu Hanifa says the cavalryman gets two shares (one for himself, one for his horse); the infantryman gets one. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the cavalryman gets three shares (one for himself, two for his horse).
All agree the infantryman gets one share.
2
Examine the evidence for each position
The Sahibayn rely on the higher cost of maintaining a horse and on a hadith report giving the cavalryman three shares. Abu Hanifa uses qiyas to show the horse is a tool like a bow, but abandons qiyas in favor of hadith. Since hadith reports conflict (some say two, some say three), he takes the number agreed upon by all reports (two) and discards the disputed extra.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle of taking the minimal agreed-upon when reports conflict.
3
Understand the tarjih (preference) within the school
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Abu Hanifa's view. Later Hanafi authorities like al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah prefer this because it is based on stronger evidence: the Prophet's actual practice at Khaybar (giving two shares) and the principle that no animal should receive more than its human rider.
The Sahibayn's view is still considered, but it is not the operative position for fatwa.
4
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
Imagine a modern army with cavalry units (horses). The commander must divide spoils. According to Hanafi fatwa, the cavalryman receives two shares and the infantryman one. The extra share for the horse is valid but only one additional share, not two.
The same principle would apply to any mount or vehicle if analogized, though classical fiqh limits it to horses.

Commentary
This passage addresses the division of the four-fifths of the spoils after removing the fifth (khums) for the state. The central dispute is between Abu Hanifa and his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding how many shares a cavalryman (faris) receives. Abu Hanifa holds that the cavalryman gets two shares: one for himself and one for his horse, while the infantryman (rajil) gets one. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the cavalryman gets three shares: one for himself and two for his horse. The supporters of each position are noted: Abu Hanifa's view is upheld by Zufar and al-Hasan ibn Ziyad and is the view of the Iraqis, Kufans, and Basrans. The companions' view is that of the people of Hijaz. Their reasoning is that the horse's maintenance cost is higher, so it deserves more. Abu Hanifa argues that analogically (qiyas), the horse should not entitle its owner to any additional share because it is merely a tool of war, like a bow or sword. However, this analogy is overridden by hadith reports. Since the reports are conflicting (some say two shares, some say three), Abu Hanifa takes the minimal agreed-upon amount (two) and discards the disputed extra. Additionally, he argues that the cavalryman's contribution is greater than the horse's, as the horse alone cannot fight. He supports this with the precedent of the Prophet's distribution at Khaybar giving two shares to the cavalryman.

Share of a Soldier Who Enters on Foot and Later Acquires a Horse
States the share status and effect of buying/selling a horse after entry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the share of a soldier who enters on foot and later acquires a horse, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of his subsequent actions.

Core Idea
A soldier's share in spoils is determined by his entry status; later acquisition of a horse does not upgrade his share.

This passage addresses a specific question in the law of ghanimah (spoils): does a soldier who initially enters the battlefield on foot but later obtains a horse — whether by purchase, gift, loan, or rental — become entitled to a horseman's share? The main Hanafi view firmly says no, but a narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad reports a different opinion from Abu Hanifa himself. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the school determines entitlement and the weight given to initial status versus subsequent actions.

Guided Path
1
State the main Hanafi position
Identify the ruling given by the text: 'man dakhala rajilan, fashtara farasan: istahaqqa sahmi rajil' — he gets the foot soldier's share. This is the relied-upon view.
The same applies to borrowing, renting, or being gifted a horse.
2
Identify the conflicting narration
Find the report from al-Hasan ibn Ziyad: that Abu Hanifa said he gets a horseman's share if he buys and fights on the horse before spoils are taken.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, not a cross-madhhab dispute.
3
Compare the proofs
The main view argues that entitlement is fixed at entry (halat al-dukhul). The opposing view says the cause (sabab) of entitlement — entering as a horseman — occurs when he fights on the horse.
The main view treats sale of the horse as consent to forfeiture; death does not.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the main view, the soldier gets one share (foot). Under the narration, he gets two shares (horse share).
This affects the distribution of spoils.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text indicates that the soundest view (al-asahh) is that selling during battle forfeits the horseman share, implying the main view is relied upon. The foot share for the foot entrant is the operative position.
The author does not explicitly choose, but the context supports the main view.

Commentary
This passage deals with the determination of a soldier's share in the spoils (ghanimah) based on his status at the time of entering the battlefield. The main ruling from Abu Hanifa is that a person who enters on foot gets the share of a foot-soldier (sahm raajil) even if he later acquires a horse by purchase, gift, loan, or rental. The reasoning is that entitlement is based on the state at entry. If he later sells the horse, that is considered consent to forfeit the horseman's share (if he had one), but if the horse dies, no forfeiture occurs because there is no consent. However, a narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad reports that Abu Hanifa also said that if he buys a horse before the spoils are taken and then fights on it, he gets the horseman's share, as the cause of entitlement (entry as a horseman) is present. There is also a discussion on selling the horse during fighting versus after: the soundest view is that selling during battle forfeits the horseman's share because it indicates a commercial motive, but if sold after the fighting ends, the share is retained. This section shows the intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether subsequent action can change the share category.

Ruling on Horseman's Share When Forced to Fight on Foot
Ruling on horseman's share with consensus proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a horseman who fights on foot due to narrow space still receives the horseman's share, and identify the conditions that trigger this ruling.

Core Idea
A horseman's share is fixed upon entry; necessity of terrain does not alter it.

In the distribution of spoils, the share of a horseman is greater than that of a foot soldier. But what if a soldier enters as a horseman but is forced to dismount and fight on foot due to narrow terrain? This lesson extracts a clear ruling from Al-Hidayah that such a soldier retains his horseman's share by consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Recognize that this ruling relates to the distribution of spoils (ghanimah) for soldiers in military expeditions.
2
State the ruling
The soldier deserves the share of a horseman (sahm al-fursan), not a foot soldier.
3
Identify the condition
The servant is given a piece of cloth or garment to cover his body, and the condition for the ruling is that he entered as a horseman and the reason for fighting on foot is narrowness of the place (dayq al-makan).
The condition is specific to terrain, not injury or choice.
4
Recognize the proof
The ruling is supported by ijma' (consensus), indicated by 'bil-ittifaq' (by agreement).
Consensus makes the ruling binding and certain.
5
Note the practical application
When distributing spoils, the imam must give him the horseman's share even though he fought on foot.

Commentary
This is a brief but definitive ruling from Al-Hidayah, stating an agreed-upon (bil-ittifaq) position. The rule is: if a soldier enters the battle as a horseman, but due to terrain constraints (such as narrow streets or mountainous area) he has to dismount and fight on foot, his share remains that of a horseman. The rationale is that his entitlement was established upon entry, and the necessity of dismounting does not change his category. The phrase 'by agreement' indicates there is no disagreement among the early authorities on this point, making it a unanimous ruling (ijma'). This is a straightforward rule with no exceptions or ikhtilaf.

Ruling on Shares for Slaves, Women, Children, and Dhimmis
Expands on the ruling that certain categories get radkh instead of sahm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which categories of participants receive radkh instead of a full share and identify the conditions under which they receive radkh.

Core Idea
Full shares in spoils are reserved for free Muslim fighters; others get discretionary gratuity (radkh) only if they actively contribute.

The distribution of spoils (ghanima) follows strict rules. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that not all participants are eligible for a full share (sahm). Slaves, women, children, insane persons, and dhimmis do not receive a standard share. Instead, the Imam may award them a discretionary portion called radkh, but only if they have engaged in meaningful combat or support. Understanding these distinctions ensures fair distribution in accordance with the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
List excluded categories
Identify the five types: slave, woman, child, insane person, dhimmi. Note that a mukatab (contract-slave) is treated like a slave.
The text explicitly names these groups.
2
Understand radkh and its discretionary nature
Radkh is a gratuity less than a full share, given at the Imam's discretion. It is not a right (not wajib). The Imam decides the amount based on the individual's contribution.
This is the core ruling: no full share for these categories.
3
Identify conditions for radkh for slaves and women
A slave receives radkh only if he fights. A woman receives radkh only if she treats the wounded and tends to the sick. Mere service (e.g., for husband or master) without combat or healing gives nothing.
Active contribution is key.
4
Identify conditions for dhimmis
A dhimmi receives radkh only if he fights or acts as a guide. The Imam is advised not to enlist dhimmis due to risk of betrayal, but if they participate with permission, radkh applies and never a full share.
Dhimmis have lower status; their radkh is capped below the share of foot-soldiers or horsemen.
5
Apply the rule to the mukatab
A mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) is legally still a slave; his master can prevent him from fighting. Therefore, he receives radkh like a slave, not a full share.
The basis is the existence of ownership (riqq) and potential incapacity.

Commentary
This passage details the rules regarding distribution of spoils to participants who are not full-fledged Muslim free adult males. The general principle is that slaves, women, children, insane persons, and dhimmis do not receive a full share (sahm). Instead, the Imam may award them a discretionary gratuity called radkh, which is less than a full share. The reason is that these individuals are either legally incapable (women, children) or their participation is optional and subject to owner's permission (slaves). For a slave or a woman to receive radkh, they must have actually contributed meaningfully: fighting for a slave, or nursing/treating wounds for a woman. If they simply accompany for service without combat or medical aid, they get nothing. A mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) is treated like a slave in this regard. For dhimmis, the Imam is advised not to enlist them due to risk of betrayal, but if they do fight with permission, they receive radkh, not a full share, due to their lower status. The passage also includes a cross-reference from Al-Khujandi regarding forfeiture of the horseman's share if the horse is disposed of after entry - this appears as a marginal note. The overall emphasis is on maintaining the hierarchy: full shares are reserved for free Muslim males who are the primary fighters.

مملوك (mamluk): A slave, not entitled to a full share.
ذمي (dhimmi): A non-Muslim subject of the Islamic state, given radkh if they participate.
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave under a contract of manumission; treated like a slave for spoils.
