Dispute Resolution in Ijara Before Performance
Ruling on evidence and oath-taking in lease disputes before service

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the procedural rules for resolving a lease dispute before service is rendered, including the order of oaths and the preference of evidence based on whether the dispute is over rent or benefit.

Core Idea
In lease disputes before service, mutual oath and rescission apply; the burden of oath depends on whether the dispute is over rent or benefit.

This lesson examines a specific procedural scenario in Hanafi fiqh: a dispute between the lessor (mu'jir) and lessee (musta'jir) before the service or benefit under the lease has been received. Since the contract is not yet performed, the court must determine how to resolve conflicting claims. The text lays out a detailed procedure involving mutual oaths (tahalf), rescission (tarada), and rules for when either party presents evidence. Understanding these rules is crucial for anyone studying Hanafi qada (judicial procedure) and contract law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute type
First, determine whether the parties are disagreeing over the rent (badal/ujra) or over the benefit/service (mubdal/manfa'a). This classification dictates the order of oaths and the preference of evidence.
2
Apply the oath order
If the dispute is over rent, the lessee swears first because he is denying the obligation to pay the claimed rent. If over benefit, the lessor swears first because he is denying the lessee's claim of a specific benefit.
The party who swears first is the one who is the denier (munkir) of the other's claim in that specific context.
3
Handle refusal to swear (nukul)
If either party refuses to take the oath, the claim of the opposing party is established and judgment is given accordingly.
Nukul leads to immediate liability for the refuser.
4
Assess evidence
If one party produces evidence (bayyinah), it is accepted and judgment is based on it. If both produce evidence, the lessor's evidence is preferred if the dispute is over rent; the lessee's evidence is preferred if the dispute is over benefit.
This preference is based on which party is affirming a variance or has a stronger claim in that context.
5
Conclude with rescission if no evidence and mutual oaths
If neither has evidence and both swear (or one refuses), the contract is rescinded (tarada) and the parties return to their original positions.
The rescission is the final outcome when the dispute cannot be resolved through oaths or evidence.

Commentary
This passage continues the Book of Claims and Evidence, addressing disputes in an ijara (lease or hire) contract before the service or benefit has been rendered. The matn states: if the parties (the lessor/mu'jir and lessee/musta'jir) disagree—for example, on the amount of rent or the nature of the service—they take mutual oaths (tahalf) and then rescind the contract (tarada), i.e., it is annulled. The commentary expands: the dispute can be over the price (badal, i.e., the rent) or over the object (mubdal, i.e., the benefit/service). The procedural rules are: If the dispute is over the rent, the lessee begins the oath because the lessee is denying the obligation to pay the rent (i.e., the lessor claims a higher rent, the lessee denies it). If the dispute is over the benefit (e.g., the type or duration of service), the lessor begins the oath because he is denying the lessee's claim of a specific benefit. If one party refuses to swear (nukul), the other's claim is deemed established. If either party has evidence, it is accepted. If both have evidence, the evidence of the lessor is preferred in rent disputes (because the rent is his claim, analogous to the woman's evidence in mahr), and the evidence of the lessee is preferred in benefit disputes (because he is claiming a specific service that the lessor denies). The reasoning is based on who is affirming a variance from the default or who is the plaintiff in each scenario. This ruling ensures a fair procedure when the contract details are disputed before the service has begun.

Key Terms
الإجارة (al-ijārah): A lease or hire contract where one party provides a benefit (manfa'a) in exchange for rent (ujra).
البدل / المبدل (al-badal / al-mubdal): Badal is the price/rent (ujra) and mubdal is the object (the benefit/manfa'a). The parties may dispute either.
تحالفا وترادا (taḥālafā wa tarāddā): They take mutual oaths (tahalf) and then rescind the contract (tarada), meaning they annul it and return to their original positions.
نكل (nakala): Refusal to take an oath (nukul); the party who refuses loses the case and the opponent's claim is established.

Disputes in Ijarah Before Performance: Mutual Oath and Rescission
Ruling on disputes in lease before the leased benefit is received

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for resolving disputes in a lease before performance, apply the rules for who swears first, and determine the outcome when evidence is presented.

Core Idea
When lessor and lessee dispute before performance, mutual oath leads to rescission; who swears first depends on the subject of dispute.

In lease contracts (ijarah), before the lessee has received any of the benefit, disputes may arise over the rent or over the nature of the benefit. Hanafi fiqh provides a clear procedure: first, the parties take mutual oaths (tahāluf), and then the contract is rescinded (tarādd). The order of oaths depends on who is the denier in the specific claim. If one party brings evidence, it takes precedence over oaths. This lesson walks through the rulings as stated by Imam al-Quduri and explained in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of dispute
Determine whether the disagreement is about the rent (al-ujrah) or about the benefit (al-manfa'ah). The subject affects who is considered the denier.
The passage states: 'اختلفا في البدل، أو في المبدل' (they differ in the consideration or in the thing given in exchange).
2
Determine the denier
In a rent dispute, the lessor claims a certain rent; the lessee denies that obligation, so the lessee is the denier. In a benefit dispute, the lessee claims a certain benefit; the lessor denies that, so the lessor is the denier.
The denier is the one who would lose if no proof is offered; hence they swear first.
3
Apply the oath order
The denier swears first. If both take oaths, the contract is rescinded (tarādd). If one refuses, the opponent's claim is upheld.
Refusal results in liability: 'لزمه دعوى صاحبه' (the claim of his opponent is binding on him).
4
Handle evidence
If either party brings evidence (bayyinah), it is accepted and the case is decided by the evidence, not by oaths.
Evidence takes precedence over oaths even if one had already sworn.
5
Resolve conflicting evidence
If both present evidence, the lessor's evidence is preferred in rent disputes (he claims the rent), and the lessee's evidence is preferred in benefit disputes (he claims the benefit).
This preference is based on the principle that the one claiming additional entitlement needs stronger proof when conflicting.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling when the lessor and lessee disagree about the terms of a lease before any part of the leased benefit has been received. The principle is that they take mutual oaths (tahāluf) and then the contract is rescinded (tarādd). The commentary explains: the dispute may be over the rent (the monetary consideration) or over the benefit itself (e.g., the duration or nature of use). The order of oaths: if the dispute is about the rent, the lessee is the denier (since the lessor claims a specific rent, the lessee denies owing that amount), so the lessee swears first. If the dispute is about the benefit, the lessor is the denier (since the lessee claims a certain benefit, the lessor denies that), so the lessor swears first. If a party refuses to swear, the opponent's claim is upheld. If one party brings evidence (bayyinah), it is accepted. If both bring evidence, then for a dispute about rent, the lessor's evidence is preferred because he is the one claiming the additional amount; for a dispute about benefit, the lessee's evidence is preferred because he is the one claiming the additional benefit. This ruling is based on the principle that each party's claim is supported by apparent circumstances (istihsan or qiyas).

المبدل (al-mubdal): The thing given in exchange, i.e., the benefit (manfa'ah) in a lease.

Disputes in Ijarah After Full or Partial Performance
Ruling on disputes after full or partial receipt of the leased benefit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on disputes in ijarah after full or partial benefit receipt and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) that the contract is renewed moment by moment.

Core Idea
After full benefit, lessee's word; after partial, mutual oath for future, lessee's word for past.

This lesson deals with disputes that arise after the lessee has already used the leased property. The Hanafi school distinguishes between full and partial use, applying different rules of evidence. The key is understanding that the lease contract is considered to be renewed moment by moment, which determines the dispute resolution procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note whether the lessee has received the entire benefit (istīfā') or only part of it.
Full receipt means the lease period has ended; partial means only some time has passed.
2
Ruling for full receipt
If full receipt: no mutual oath (taḥāluf); the lessee's statement is accepted with his oath.
The lessee is the debtor (rent is owed), so his word is taken.
3
Ruling for partial receipt
If partial receipt: mutual oath (taḥāluf) is taken for the remaining part, and the contract is rescinded for that part. For the past part, the lessee's statement is accepted with his oath.
Past is settled; future is an ongoing contract requiring mutual oath.
4
Understand the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The contract is concluded moment by moment (yan'qid sā'atan fa sā'atan). Each unit of benefit (time) is treated as a separate contract.
This is the basis for distinguishing past (completed contracts) from future (pending contracts).
5
Apply the proof to the rulings
For past portions: they are completed contracts, so the lessee's word is accepted (as in a completed sale). For future portions: they are new contracts in dispute, so mutual oath applies.
Each moment's benefit is an independent 'contract'.

Commentary
This passage explains the ruling when the dispute arises after the lessee has already received the benefit. If the entire benefit has been received (e.g., the lease period has ended), no mutual oath is taken; instead, the lessee's statement is accepted with his oath, because he is the one who owes the rent (the debt is on him). If only part of the benefit has been received, the parties take mutual oaths (tahāluf) regarding the remaining part, and the contract is rescinded for the future. However, for the past portion that has already been used, the lessee's statement is accepted (with his oath), not mutual oaths. The reason given: the contract is considered to be renewed moment by moment; each portion of the benefit becomes a separate contract. Therefore, the past portion is like a completed contract where the lessee's word is accepted, and the future portion is like a new contract where mutual oaths apply.

Key Terms
استيفاء (istīfā'): Receipt or enjoyment of the entire benefit contracted for in the lease.
