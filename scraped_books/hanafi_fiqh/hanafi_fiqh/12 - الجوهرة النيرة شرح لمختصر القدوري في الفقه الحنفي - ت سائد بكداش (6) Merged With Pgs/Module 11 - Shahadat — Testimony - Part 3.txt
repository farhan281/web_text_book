Conditions and Disputes in Hearsay Testimony
Expansion of hearsay testimony with conditions and disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid hearsay testimony (shahada 'ala al-shahada) in Hanafi fiqh and explain why the school permits two hearsay witnesses for two original witnesses over the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Hearsay testimony is accepted only in rights immune to legal ambiguity; two hearsay witnesses suffice for two originals.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on the Hanafi ruling for hearsay testimony. It lays out the general rule, its conditions, and significant intra- and inter-madhhab disputes. As you read, pay attention to how the Hanafi school balances practicality (e.g., deathbed testimony) with the requirement for certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and its exception
The text states that shahada 'ala al-shahada is permissible in every right that is not dropped by shubha (legal ambiguity). Note that hudud and qisas are excluded because shubha affects them.
The key term 'haqq la yasqut bi shubha' is central to Hanafi theory of proofs.
2
List the conditions for hearsay testimony
The text gives several conditions: (1) the informing witnesses must be two just men or one man and two women whom the testifier trusts, (2) the information must be in the wording of testimony (not mere narration), (3) the testifier should not explain to the judge that it is hearsay. There is also a special dispensation for deathbed testimony where a single informant suffices.
Note that the condition of trust (yathiqa bihim) is subjective, based on the testifier's confidence.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on wala and waqf
The Shaykh restricted hearsay to five matters, excluding wala and waqf. But Abu Yusuf allowed hearsay in wala (clientage) comparing it to lineage, and Muhammad allowed it in waqf (endowment) due to its enduring nature. Al-Marghinani adds that waqf testimony must specify the beneficiary (jihah).
This shows that later Hanafi scholars expanded the scope beyond the early restriction.
4
Analyze the inter-madhhab dispute on number of witnesses
The text contrasts the Hanafi and Shafi'i views on whether the same two hearsay witnesses can testify upon the testimony of two different original witnesses. The Hanafis allow it because each original witness has two witnesses testifying on his testimony; the Shafi'is require four hearsay witnesses (two for each original).
The dispute is based on whether hearsay witnesses stand in place of the original witnesses individually or collectively.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical difference is the number of witnesses needed: Hanafi view requires two hearsay witnesses, Shafi'i requires four. This affects the feasibility of proving rights through secondary testimony.
The Hanafi position is more lenient, making it easier to establish rights that would otherwise be difficult to prove.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the ruling on hearsay testimony (shahada 'ala al-shahada) from the Hanafi perspective. The author begins by stating the general rule: hearsay testimony is permissible in all rights that are not subject to shubha (ambiguity/legal doubt). This excludes hudud (fixed corporal punishments) and qisas (retaliation), because shubha can prevent their execution. Conditions for hearsay testimony: (1) The informing witnesses must be two just men or one man and two women, and the testifying witness must trust them and feel their truthfulness. (2) The information must be in the wording of testimony (e.g., 'I testify that so-and-so testified'), not mere narration. There is a special dispensation in cases of death: since it is rare for more than one person to be present at someone's deathbed due to fear and aversion, a single informant (man or woman) suffices. However, this exception does not apply to marriage and lineage, which require stronger proof. The text also cautions that the witness should not qualify his testimony by saying 'I testify by hearsay,' as that would render it invalid. The author then references a view that restricts hearsay testimony to five specific matters (not listed here), excluding wala' (clientage) and waqf (endowment). There is a dispute within the Hanafi school: Abu Yusuf allows hearsay in wala' due to its similarity to lineage, while Muhammad allows it in waqf due to its enduring nature. Al-Marghinani adds that in waqf testimony, the specific beneficiary (e.g., mosque or cemetery) must be stated. The passage concludes with a discussion on the number of witnesses: two witnesses are needed to testify upon the testimony of two original witnesses; one witness upon one is not accepted because one witness does not constitute a complete proof. This leads to a dispute with al-Shafi'i, who requires that for each original witness there must be two separate hearsay witnesses, whereas the Hanafis allow the same two hearsay witnesses to testify upon multiple original witnesses. The Hanafi position is based on the principle that two hearsay witnesses are sufficient for the entire case, as they stand in place of the two original witnesses.

Key Terms
الشهادة على الشهادة (ash-shahada 'ala ash-shahada): Testimony given by a witness based on the testimony of another witness, i.e., hearsay or secondary testimony.
حق لا يسقط بالشبهة (haqq la yasqut bi ash-shubha): A right that is not invalidated by legal ambiguity (shubha), such as financial rights, as opposed to hudud and qisas.
التسامع (at-tasammu'): Hearsay; testifying based on common knowledge or widespread report, distinct from shahada 'ala al-shahada.

Testimony Not Accepted in Hudud and Qisas
States that testimony on testimony is not accepted in hudud and qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that testimony on testimony is not accepted in hudud and qisas and explain the rational proof based on the principle that hudud are repelled by doubts.

Core Idea
Hudud and qisas require certainty; testimony on testimony is not certain enough, so it is rejected.

In this lesson, we study a specific rule from the fiqh of testimony: in the Hanafi school, testimony on testimony (shahadah 'ala shahadah) is not admissible when the case involves a hadd punishment or qisas. The commentator explains that this is because hudud and qisas are repelled by doubts (shubuhat), and testimony on testimony is a weaker form of evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is giving testimony on testimony (shahadah 'ala shahadah) in cases of hudud and qisas. The ruling is that it is not accepted (la tuqbal).
This applies specifically to testimony on testimony, not to direct testimony.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is rational (ma'qul) based on the established principle that hudud are repelled by doubts (yudra'u bi al-shubuhat).
This principle is derived from hadith: 'Idra'u al-hudud bi al-shubuhat'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Because hudud and qisas are repelled by doubts, they require strong, certain evidence. Testimony on testimony is inherently weaker than direct testimony, so it is not sufficient to establish such severe penalties.
The phrase 'qā'im maqām al-ghayr' indicates it is a substitute, hence less reliable.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school differs: they accept testimony on testimony in hudud under certain conditions. Their reasoning is based on analogy with other cases.
This ikhtilaf is recognized as legitimate.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) because of the strength of the principle that hudud are repelled by doubts and the weakness of secondary testimony.
No explicit tarjih authority is given in the text, but the ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Testimony of Two Witnesses on Two Witnesses
Ruling on accepting two witnesses testifying on the testimony of two others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the validity of two witnesses testifying on multiple original testimonies and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
The same two witnesses can testify on multiple testimonies; each original testimony gets its own pair.

In shahadah 'ala shahadah, a critical issue arises: can the same two branch witnesses testify on the testimony of two different original witnesses? The Hanafi school permits it, while Imam al-Shafi'i requires distinct pairs. This lesson examines the reasoning behind each position and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
Determine the exact point of disagreement: whether the same two branch witnesses can testify on behalf of two different original witnesses.
2
State the Hanafi position
Articulate the Hanafi ruling: it is permissible for the same two witnesses to testify on multiple original testimonies because each original testimony is supported by two witnesses.
3
Examine the Shafi'i objection
Present the Shafi'i argument: each pair of branch witnesses stands in place of one original witness, so two originals require two distinct pairs (four witnesses total).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal: the condition is having two witnesses on each original testimony, which is fulfilled even if the same two testify on both.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical difference: in Hanafi, the same two witness can serve in multiple cases; in Shafi'i, distinct pairs are needed for each original testimony.

One Witness on the Testimony of One Not Accepted
Ruling that one witness cannot testify on the testimony of one other

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on branch testimony and identify the condition that the number of branch witnesses must match the number of original witnesses.

Core Idea
One branch witness cannot replace two; the testimony of one is not a proof.

This lesson addresses a specific rule in the Hanafi law of evidence: when a person testifies about the testimony of another (shahadat al-far'), they must come in pairs. Just as direct testimony requires two witnesses (in property claims), indirect testimony also requires two. A single branch witness is insufficient because his word alone does not establish a legal proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is 'giving testimony about the testimony of another.' The ruling is that it is not accepted if done by only one witness.
2
Understand the rationale
The text gives the reason: 'because the testimony of one does not constitute a hujjah (proof).' This mirrors the rule for direct testimony: one witness is insufficient in financial and civil claims.
In Hanafi, a single witness in non-hudud cases is supplemented by the plaintiff's oath to become a hujjah, but not for branch testimony.
3
Apply the condition for validity
For branch testimony to be valid, the number of branch witnesses must equal the number of original witnesses. If the original testimony required two witnesses, then two branch witnesses are needed.
If the original case involved a single witness with an oath, the branch testimony would still require two.
4
Distinguish from other types
This rule applies only to indirect testimony (shahadat al-far'). Direct testimony has its own rules. Also, in hudud cases, different standards apply (four witnesses for zina, etc.).

In Hanafi fiqh, testimony on testimony (shahada ‘ala shahada) must follow a precise wording to be valid. The original witness must explicitly instruct the branch witness to 'testify on my testimony.' Ruling on subsidiary testimony wording and condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct wording for a subsidiary witness and identify the condition that original witnesses must remain qualified.

Core Idea
Subsidiary testimony requires explicit commission wording and the original witnesses' continuous eligibility.

In Hanafi fiqh, subsidiary testimony (shahada ala al-shahada) is a valid means of proof under strict conditions. This lesson explains the precise wording the subsidiary must utter and the critical requirement that the original witnesses remain qualified throughout.

Guided Path

Identify the three essential components in the subsidiary's wording

The original witnesses must remain qualified (adl, sighted, speaking) from the time they gave their testimony until the subsidiary performs. If they become fasq, blind, or mute, the subsidiary's testimony is rejected.

If the original witnesses become sinful (fasq), blind, or mute after giving their testimony, the subsidiary's testimony is invalid because the originals lose the capacity to testify.

Resolves the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether subsidiary witnesses must certify original witnesses' integrity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether subsidiary witnesses must certify the original witnesses' integrity and apply Abu Yusuf's view to new fact patterns.

Core Idea
Subsidiary witnesses need only transmit testimony; the judge, not they, must verify the originals' integrity.

This lesson addresses a practical question in testimony law: when subsidiary witnesses relay the testimony of original witnesses, must they also certify that the originals are upright? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute, and the relied-upon position dispenses with such a requirement. Understanding this distinction clarifies the subsidiary's limited duty and the judge's role in character verification.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Both actions are permissible: subsidiary witnesses may certify the originals' integrity, or they may remain silent. The text says 'jāz' (permissible) in both cases.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
The disagreement is between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Abu Yusuf holds that certification is not required; Muhammad holds it is necessary.
3
Follow the proof for each
Abu Yusuf: the subsidiary's obligation is to transmit (naql), not to certify; they may not know the originals' integrity. Muhammad: testimony requires integrity (ʿadāla), so the subsidiary must know the originals are upright before transmitting.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The matn and commentary adopt Abu Yusuf's view, allowing the judge to investigate the originals' character separately. This protects subsidiaries from an obligation that may be difficult.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Yusuf, subsidiary testimony is accepted even without certification, as long as the court later verifies the originals' integrity. Under Muhammad, such testimony is rejected unless certification is provided.
The mufta bihi position follows Abu Yusuf.

Commentary
This passage addresses two sub-issues: First, whether subsidiary witnesses are allowed to certify the integrity (tazkiya) of the original witnesses. The commentator confirms that this is valid, as the subsidiary is a qualified certifier; their act of transmitting does not preclude them from giving a separate certification. This is analogous to one witness certifying another in the same case. Second, whether such certification is required. The matn states that if the subsidiary remain silent about certification, it is still permissible, and the judge must investigate the character of the original witnesses. The commentator clarifies that this is the view of Abu Yusuf: the subsidiary's duty is only to transmit, not to certify. Muhammad al-Shaybani disagrees: since testimony requires integrity, the subsidiary witnesses, by transmitting, implicitly vouch for the originals' integrity; if they do not know it, they should not have transmitted, and their testimony is rejected. Branch Witness Testimony: Validation and Silence
Intra-Hanafi dispute on branch witness testimony conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on accepting branch witness testimony when branch witnesses explicitly validate or remain silent about the original witnesses' integrity, and distinguish the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Branch witnesses need not validate originals; if silent, the judge investigates — Abu Yusuf's view prevails.

The acceptance of branch witness testimony depends on the integrity of the original witnesses. This lesson examines the conditions under which branch testimony is accepted, focusing on the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding whether branch witnesses must explicitly validate the originals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note the two cases: branch witnesses explicitly validate originals (jaza) and branch witnesses remain silent (jaza with investigation).
2
Understand the principle of tazkiyah
Branch witnesses are capable of tazkiyah; their validation is valid because transmission does not prevent validation.
The commentator clarifies that validating originals does not 'correct' the branch's own testimony.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's reasoning
Abu Yusuf holds that validation is not obligatory; branch witnesses are only required to transmit. Their silence is acceptable, but the judge must investigate the originals' integrity.
4
Analyze Muhammad's objection
Muhammad argues that testimony requires known integrity; if branches cannot vouch for originals, the testimony is invalid.
5
Note the practical procedure
According to Abu Yusuf, if branches are silent but upright, the judge asks about the originals; if validated, he accepts the testimony.
The text also mentions secret inquiry and public validation if the judge is unaware.
6
Apply to the imprisoned witness case
When the original witness is imprisoned, branch testimony is permissible if the original cannot be brought (e.g., in the governor's prison).
This illustrates the practical application of the rule.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses the rules of accepting testimony, specifically focusing on branch witnesses (shuhud al-far') who transmit testimony from original witnesses (shuhud al-asl). The passage is a mixture of rulings and intra-Hanafi disputes. It first states a base ruling: if branch witnesses explicitly validate (ta'dil) the original witnesses, their testimony is accepted. The commentator explains that branch witnesses are themselves capable of tazkiyah (validation) and that their validation of original witnesses does not correct their own testimony. The second ruling addresses what happens if branch witnesses remain silent about the integrity of the original witnesses. Here, Abu Yusuf holds that their testimony is still accepted, but the judge must investigate the integrity of the originals. Muhammad disagrees, arguing that without knowledge of integrity, the branch witnesses cannot transmit testimony. The commentator then discusses a scenario where the judge is unaware of the parties' integrity: he should inquire secretly and then validate publicly. The passage also includes a fatwa from al-Dhakhira regarding an imprisoned original witness: if the witness is in the judge's own prison, the judge can summon him, so branch testimony is not needed; if in the governor's prison and cannot be brought, branch testimony is permissible. Finally, the passage presents a second intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf accept a single person for tazkiyah (validation or discrediting of witnesses), comparing it to a report (khabar) rather than formal testimony. They also accept tazkiyah from a parent or child without requiring an opponent, and note that tazkiyah in a case of adultery does not require four witnesses. Muhammad requires at least two. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is typically that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, as their reasoning is more flexible and practical.

Key Terms
شهود الأصل (shuhud al-asl): The original witnesses who directly witnessed the event and whose testimony is being transmitted by branch witnesses.
شهود الفرع (shuhud al-far'): Branch witnesses who testify based on the testimony of the original witnesses, i.e., they transmit the testimony.
تعديل (ta'dil): The act of validating a witness's integrity (tazkiyah). In this text, it refers to branch witnesses confirming that original witnesses are upright.
تزكية (tazkiyah): Validation of a witness's uprightness; synonymous with ta'dil.

Number of Witnesses for Tazkiyah: One or Two?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the number of witnesses for tazkiyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf accept one witness for tazkiyah while Muhammad requires two, and apply the distinction between testimony (shahada) and report (khabar).

Core Idea
Tazkiyah is a report, not testimony — one person suffices, unlike formal shahada which needs two.

The validation (tazkiyah) of a witness's integrity is a critical step in court proceedings. This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute: how many witnesses are needed to validate or discredit a witness? The answer hinges on whether tazkiyah is classified as testimony (shahada) or as a report (khabar).

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: one person is accepted for tazkiyah. Muhammad: requires at least two.
2
Understand the classification
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf argue that tazkiyah is not testimony (shahada) but a report (khabar). Testimony requires specific formalities; reports do not.
Evidence: tazkiyah does not require the wording of testimony and can be established by communication (risala).
3
Note the implications
Because it is a report, tazkiyah from a parent for a child or child for parent is accepted, and the presence of an opponent is not required.
4
Analyze Muhammad's objection
Muhammad views tazkiyah as a form of testimony and therefore requires two witnesses, similar to other testimony.
The text does not elaborate Muhammad's proof, but the dispute is clear.
5
Consider the special case of zina
Tazkiyah for witnesses of adultery does not require four witnesses, indicating it is not treated like the original testimony of zina.
This supports the Hanafi position that tazkiyah is a separate, less formal act.

The open tazkiyah is done publicly and requires two by consensus, but secret tazkiyah—when the judge lacks personal knowledge—divides the school. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) accept one valid testimony, while Muhammad ibn al-Hasan insists on two, analogizing tazkiyah to testimony itself.

1
Distinguish secret vs open tazkiyah
Identify the two contexts: open tazkiyah is done publicly and requires two witnesses by consensus; secret tazkiyah is when the judge does not know the witness's character and seeks validation in private.
The passage explicitly states that for open tazkiyah, two are necessary with the word 'shahadah'.
2
Identify the Hanafi positions
State that according to Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), a single person's statement suffices; according to Muhammad ibn al-Hasan, two are required.
The text: 'وعلى هذا يُقبل فيه عندهما قولُ الواحد، وعند محمد: لا بد من اثنين'.
3
Analyze the analogy with the translator
The same difference appears for accepting a translator when the judge does not understand the litigant. Sahibayn accept one translator; Muhammad requires two.
This qiyas (analogy) shows that tazkiyah is treated like translation (ikhbar) rather than testimony (shahadah) by Sahibayn.
4
Determine the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of Sahibayn: one witness suffices for secret tazkiyah, as mentioned in al-Hidayah and other later texts.
The author of al-Jawharah al-Nayrah and al-Hidayah prefer the Sahibayn view.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, a Hanafi judge may accept secret tazkiyah from a single reliable person, unless the case involves zina (where four witnesses are needed for tazkiyah) or other exceptions.
The passage notes that for zina, four tazkiyah witnesses are required (from al-Hidayah).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from al-Jawharah al-Nayrah on the Mukhtasar al-Quduri, concerning the chapter on whose testimony is accepted and whose is not. The author begins by distinguishing between secret and open tazkiyah (validation of witnesses). In open tazkiyah, two witnesses are required by consensus, and the word 'shahadah' must be used. The dispute is about secret tazkiyah, when the judge does not know the witness's character. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), a single person's statement suffices, while Muhammad ibn al-Hasan (another Hanafi imam) requires two. This difference is analogous to their dispute regarding the translator: if the judge does not understand the litigant, one translator suffices according to the Sahibayn, but Muhammad requires two. Similarly, the tazkiyah of a woman is accepted by the Sahibayn, but Muhammad does not accept it. Abu Hanifa's position is more nuanced: he accepts a woman's tazkiyah in non-hudud cases, but for hudud, the witness must be male. The reasoning is that tazkiyah is the 'cause of the cause' (illat al-illa): the cause of the ruling is testimony, and the cause of testimony's acceptance is tazkiyah. The passage then explains the wording for tazkiyah: the muqazzin (one performing tazkiyah) says 'he is upright, acceptable' without specifying 'for him' or 'against him', as this encompasses both. A quote from al-Yanabi' says that if the plaintiff hires animals for witnesses to travel, their testimony is not accepted according to Abu Yusuf (due to suspicion of bias), but if the witnesses eat from the plaintiff's food on the way without being hired, it is accepted. The commentary then addresses a scenario where original witnesses deny having testified: the subsidiary witnesses (who testify on their behalf) are not accepted because the basis (tahammul) is not established. Muhammad says he does not accept their testimony in both cases (denial or absence). Nasr ibn Yahya and Abu al-Layth provide qualifications regarding providing mounts. Finally, several masa'il (issues) are given: (1) if sinful witnesses repent, their testimony is still not accepted because the suspicion of laundering their credibility remains; (2) if a husband testifies for his wife, then divorces her, his testimony is not accepted because he may have divorced to remove the impediment; similarly if a wife testifies for her husband and then is divorced; (3) a slave, disbeliever, insane person, or minor's testimony is initially rejected, and if they later become qualified, the ruling is not stated here but implied.

Woman's Tazkiyah: Intra-Hanafi Dispute and Hudud Exception
Intra-Hanafi dispute on woman's tazkiyah with hudud exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on a woman's tazkiyah and identify the mufta bihi with its hudud exception.

Core Idea
A woman's tazkiyah is generally accepted in the Hanafi school, but Muhammad ibn al-Hasan rejects it, and Abu Hanifa excludes hudud.

The acceptance of a woman's validation of a witness (tazkiyah) reveals another important intra-Hanafi dispute. While the school generally accepts her tazkiyah, three distinct views emerge: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani accept it unconditionally; Abu Hanifa accepts it except in hudud (fixed punishments); and Muhammad ibn al-Hasan rejects it entirely. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each view and identifies the operative ruling for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
Sahibayn accept woman's tazkiyah unconditionally. Abu Hanifa accepts it in all cases except hudud (where male witnesses are required). Muhammad ibn al-Hasan rejects it altogether.
The passage states: 'وعلى هذا يُقبل تعديل المرأة عندهما، وقال محمد: لا يجوز. ثم عند أبي حنيفة : إنما يُقبل تعديلها في غير العقوبات.'
2
Understand the reasoning behind each position
Sahibayn see tazkiyah as information (ikhbar), and women are reliable informants. Abu Hanifa treats hudud as a matter of ihtiyat (caution), requiring male witnesses. Muhammad sees tazkiyah as testimony, and women are not full witnesses in hudud, so he rejects it entirely.
The passage cites Abu Hanifa's principle: التزكية علة العلة (tazkiyah is the cause of the cause).
3
Analyze the concept of 'illat al-'illah'
Abu Hanifa argues that testimony (shahadah) is the direct cause of the ruling, and tazkiyah is the cause of testimony's acceptance. Since testimony in hudud requires male witnesses, the cause of its acceptance (tazkiyah) also requires males in hudud.
This is a unique argument linking the gender requirement from testimony to tazkiyah only in hudud.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that a woman's tazkiyah is accepted except in hudud, combining the Sahibayn's general acceptance with Abu Hanifa's hudud exception.
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Hidayah, al-Jawharah) prefer this combined view.
5
Apply the ruling
In a non-hudud case, a judge should accept a woman's tazkiyah for a witness. In a hudud case (e.g., theft, zina), the judge must only accept male tazkiyah.
Hudud cases are rare and have additional strict requirements.

Subsidiary Witnesses When Originals Deny
Intra-Hanafi dispute on subsidiary testimony when originals deny

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for accepting subsidiary testimony and apply the intra-Hanafi dispute on when originals deny or are absent.

Core Idea
Subsidiary testimony (shahadat al-farʿ) requires established tahammul; if originals deny, their testimony fails.

Islamic courts allow 'testimony upon testimony' (shahadat al-farʿ) when original witnesses cannot testify (e.g., death or absence). But what if the original witnesses deny ever having given that testimony? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling: subsidiary testimony is rejected when the originals deny, because the condition of tahammul (bearing the testimony) is not proven. Muhammad ibn al-Hasan goes further, rejecting subsidiary testimony altogether unless the originals explicitly acknowledge it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Original witnesses (shuhud al-asl) gave testimony, but later deny it, or are absent/dead. Subsidiary witnesses (shuhud al-farʿ) come to testify about the originals' testimony.
The passage: 'وإن أنكر شهود الأصل الشهادة : لم تُقبل شهادة شهودِ الفرع.'
2
Understand the condition of tahammul
Tahammul (bearing testimony) is a prerequisite for subsidiary testimony. It is established when the subsidiary witnesses actually heard the originals testify.
The text states: 'لأن التحمل لم يثبت، وهو شرط.'
3
Apply the condition when originals deny
If the originals deny having testified, tahammul is not established, so subsidiary testimony is rejected.
This is the relied-upon position (Sahibayn).
4
Note Muhammad ibn al-Hasan's stricter view
Muhammad rejects subsidiary testimony in both cases: when originals deny and when they are absent/dead. He requires explicit acknowledgment or another proof of tahammul.
The passage: 'وقال محمد : لا أقبل شهادتهم في الوجهين جميعاً.'
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Sahibayn: subsidiary testimony is accepted if tahammul is proven (originals acknowledge or it is witnessed), but rejected if originals deny or if tahammul is unproven.
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Hidayah) follow the Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from al-Jawharah al-Nayrah on the Mukhtasar al-Quduri, concerning the chapter on whose testimony is accepted and whose is not. The author begins by distinguishing between secret and open tazkiyah (validation of witnesses). In open tazkiyah, two witnesses are required by consensus, and the word 'shahadah' must be used. The dispute is about secret tazkiyah, when the judge does not know the witness's character. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), a single person's statement suffices, while Muhammad ibn al-Hasan (another Hanafi imam) requires two. This difference is analogous to their dispute regarding the translator: if the judge does not understand the litigant, one translator suffices according to the Sahibayn, but Muhammad requires two. Similarly, the tazkiyah of a woman is accepted by the Sahibayn, but Muhammad does not accept it. Abu Hanifa's position is more nuanced: he accepts a woman's tazkiyah in non-hudud cases, but for hudud, the witness must be male. The reasoning is that tazkiyah is the 'cause of the cause' (illat al-illa): the cause of the ruling is testimony, and the cause of testimony's acceptance is tazkiyah. The passage then explains the wording for tazkiyah: the muqazzin (one performing tazkiyah) says 'he is upright, acceptable' without specifying 'for him' or 'against him', as this encompasses both. The commentary then addresses a scenario where original witnesses deny having testified: the subsidiary witnesses (who testify on their behalf) are not accepted because the basis (tahammul) is not established. Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on secondary witnesses when original deny

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when original witnesses deny, and explain the intra-Hanafi difference between Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
If original witnesses deny, secondary testimony fails—tahammul is a condition that cannot be established without original confirmation.

This passage addresses a critical procedural issue: what happens when secondary witnesses (shuhud al-far') come forward but the original witnesses (shuhud al-asl) deny having ever testified? The Hanafi school holds that secondary testimony is not accepted in this case, because the chain of bearing testimony (tahammul) is broken. However, Imam Muhammad takes a stricter view, rejecting secondary testimony in all cases, even when the original are absent or dead.

Testimony After Change: Tuhmah vs. Capacity
Ruling with proof: cases distinguishing tuhmah from ahl.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between rejection of testimony due to tuhmah (suspicion) and due to lack of ahl al-shahada (capacity), and apply the rule for re-testimony accordingly.

Core Idea
Testimony rejected due to tuhmah stays rejected; rejection due to incapacity is cured when capacity is restored.

This lesson examines a set of cases (masa'il) from the Hanafi school that clarify a key distinction in the law of testimony. The central question: if a person's testimony is initially rejected, does a subsequent change in personal circumstances make it acceptable? The answer depends entirely on the reason for the original rejection—whether it was due to persistent suspicion (tuhmah) or a removable incapacity (ahl al-shahada). Understanding this principle is essential for correctly evaluating the validity of re-testimony in legal proceedings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
The passage establishes that the outcome of re-testimony hinges on the reason for the original rejection. If due to tuhmah, rejection is permanent; if due to non-ahl (incapacity), it is temporary and curable.
Tuhmah includes suspicion of bias, collusion, or partiality.
2
Analyze the first set of cases
Examine the examples of two sinful persons (fasiq) who repent, and a husband who divorces his wife then later testifies for her. In both, the original rejection was due to tuhmah, which persists despite the change in status (repentance or divorce). The testimony remains rejected.
The tuhmah is that the witness might be colluding to validate the testimony.
3
Analyze the second set of cases
Examine the examples of a slave, disbeliever, insane person, or minor. Their original rejection was due to lack of ahl al-shahada (e.g., not free, not Muslim, not sane, not adult). Once that incapacity is removed (freedom, Islam, sanity, puberty), they become ahl and their re-testimony is accepted.
No tuhmah is involved; the defect was purely technical and now removed.
4
Apply the principle to novel scenarios
Given a scenario where a witness's testimony was rejected, first determine the cause: was it tuhmah or lack of ahl? If tuhmah, the change in status does not help; if lack of ahl, once ahl is achieved, the testimony can be re-offered and accepted.
This distinction is crucial for judges and muftis evaluating testimony.

Core Idea
Retraction after judgment does not undo the judgment; it triggers liability and punishment.

The chapter on retraction of testimony (al-ruju' 'an al-shahadah) addresses what happens when a witness withdraws his testimony. The ruling depends crucially on timing: before or after the judge's verdict. This lesson unpacks the legal elements (pillar, condition, ruling) and their practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its two scenarios
The act is retraction of testimony. The law distinguishes two situations: retraction before the judge passes judgment, and retraction after judgment. Each has different consequences.
Timing is the key variable.
2
Extract the pillar (rukn) and condition (shart) of valid retraction
From the text: the pillar is the witness's verbal statement of retraction (e.g., 'I retract' or 'I testified falsely'). The condition is that this statement must be made before the judge.
Without the condition, the retraction is not legally operative.
3
Distinguish the rulings for each scenario
Before judgment: retraction nullifies the testimony, no liability (daman) because no loss was caused. After judgment: the judgment stands, but the witness must compensate for any property loss caused by the false testimony and receives discretionary punishment (ta'zir).
The judgment is not invalidated because it was based on valid testimony at the time.

Retraction from Testimony: Before and After Judgment
Ruling on retraction from testimony with pillar, condition, and rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for retraction from testimony before and after judgment, including the pillar, condition, and consequences (ta'zir and daman), and apply them to scenarios with multiple witnesses.

Core Idea
Retraction after judgment does not annul the ruling but imposes liability; retraction before judgment voids testimony without penalty.

A witness's change of heart can have serious legal consequences. The Hanafi school carefully distinguishes between retraction before and after a judge has passed sentence. This lesson unpacks the pillar and condition of a valid retraction, the mandatory discretionary punishment (ta'zir), and the circumstances under which the witnesses must financially compensate the wronged party. We will also explore how these rules apply when only one witness retracts.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two temporal scenarios
Identify the key difference: retraction before judgment causes the testimony to be dropped (sakatat) with no liability; retraction after judgment leaves the judgment intact but obligates ta'zir and daman (guarantee) on the witnesses.
This distinction is the axis of the entire ruling.
2
Extract the rukn and shart of retraction
The pillar (rukn) is the witness's verbal statement of retraction (e.g., 'I retract my testimony' or 'I testified falsely'). The condition (shart) is that the retraction must take place in the presence of a judge (any judge, not necessarily the one who passed the verdict).
Without these, the retraction is legally ineffective.
3
Derive the hukm: ta'zir always, daman conditionally
The ruling (hukm) is that discretionary punishment (ta'zir) is wajib in every case of retraction. Additionally, if retraction occurs after judgment, the subject matter was property, and the plaintiff has taken possession, daman (financial compensation) is also wajib.
Daman is based on the principle that causing loss through transgression (ta'addi) triggers liability, analogous to wrongful possession (yad).
4
Apply the causation principle to multiple witnesses
If both witnesses retract after judgment, they jointly owe the entire property. If only one retracts, he owes half, because the remaining witness's testimony still stands for half the claim. The guarantee only applies if the plaintiff actually took possession.
The principle: consideration is given to the continued existence of the one who remains, not to the retraction of the one who retracts.
5
Analyze the rationale (talil) and cross-madhhab note
The underlying proof is qiyas: the original case (asl) is wrongful possession (yad), the new case (far') is false testimony, and the common effective cause (illa) is causation of loss through transgression. Note that other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may treat retraction after judgment differently, but the Hanafi view is that the judgment stands for finality.
This reasoning is explicit in the text: 'because causation by way of transgression is a cause for guarantee, as with the hand.'

Commentary
This passage from the commentary al-Jawhara al-Nayira on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar discusses the rulings of retraction from testimony in the Hanafi school. The passage begins by distinguishing two scenarios: retraction before judgment and retraction after judgment. Before judgment, retraction voids the testimony entirely, and no liability (daman) is incurred because no loss was caused. However, after judgment, the judgment itself is not overturned (contra some other schools), but the retracting witnesses are liable for any loss they caused, meaning they must compensate the defendant if the property was taken and consumed. The text then explains the rukn (pillar) of retraction: the witness's statement of retraction, and the shart (condition): that it be in the presence of a judge. The hukm (ruling) includes mandatory discretionary punishment (ta'zir) in both cases, and additionally guarantee (daman) if after judgment. A key point is that retraction is only valid before a judge, and it need not be the same judge who heard the case. If both witnesses retract after judgment, they jointly owe the property to the defendant. If only one retracts, he owes half, as the remaining witness's testimony stands for half the claim. The underlying principle is that causation of loss through transgression (ta'addi) necessitates guarantee, analogous to wrongful possession (yad). The guarantee is only triggered if the plaintiff actually took possession of the property, because that is when the loss occurs.

Key Terms
رجوع (rujū'): Retraction; a witness's formal withdrawal of their testimony, which annuls the testimony if done before judgment and triggers liability if done after.
ضمان (ḍamān): Legal liability to compensate for loss caused by one's action; here, the witnesses must pay the value of the property lost due to their testimony.
تعزير (taʿzīr): Discretionary punishment administered by the judge for acts that do not have a fixed hadd penalty; here, mandatory for retraction in all cases.
تسبب (tasabbub): Causation; indirect action that leads to a result. In this context, false testimony is a cause (sabab) for the loss, making the witness liable.

Liability Division upon Retraction: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability division upon retraction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding liability distribution when a man and multiple women retract testimony, and explain the rationale based on testimonial weight.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, retracting witnesses are liable proportionally to their testimonial weight; two women equal one man.

When witnesses retract after a judgment based on their testimony, they must compensate the loss. This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute on how to divide liability when a man and several women testify together and then retract. The disagreement hinges on whether multiple women collectively hold the testimonial weight of one man or whether each pair equals one man. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi approach to witness credibility and proportional liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Consequence
The act is retraction (ruju') from testimony after a judgment. The consequence is financial liability (daman) for the loss caused. Note that only retraction after judgment triggers liability.
Retraction before judgment removes the testimony without liability.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position and Rationale
Abu Hanifa holds that the man bears 1/6 of the claim and the women bear 5/6. This is because each pair of women is equivalent to one man in testimonial weight. Thus, if there is one man and ten women, it is as if there are six male witnesses, and each retracting unit (man or pair of women) bears 1/6.
The analogy (qiyas) is made to six men retracting; each would bear 1/6.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position and Rationale
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the man bears 1/2 and the women bear 1/2 collectively. Their reasoning: women, no matter how many, are considered like one witness. Therefore, the testimonial weight is divided equally between the man (one witness) and the women (collectively one witness).
This view treats the group of women as a single unit regardless of number.
4
Compute the Practical Difference
Consider a case with one man and ten women testifying on a claim of 600 dirhams. According to Abu Hanifa, man pays 100 dirhams (1/6), women pay 500 dirhams (5/6). According to the Sahibayn, man pays 300 dirhams (1/2), women pay 300 dirhams (1/2). This numerical example shows the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence of the difference).
The dispute matters when the number of women is not a multiple of two that makes the total equivalent to an integer number of men.

The two Hanafi views on testimonial weight yield different liability fractions in mixed retraction cases, as shown in multiple subcases.

Retraction in Marriage Testimony: No Liability for Mahr al-Mithl
Ruling that witnesses to a marriage with proper mahr are not liable if they retract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness liability upon retraction in marriage testimony and explain the reasoning based on compensation through al-bud'.

Core Idea
Witnesses are not liable if the husband received the marital right; it compensates the mahr.

When witnesses testify to a marriage and then retract, one might assume they are liable for the mahr paid. However, Hanafi law distinguishes based on whether the husband received the marital benefit (al-bud'). In this passage, we examine two cases: testimony for mahr al-mithl or more, and testimony for less than mahr al-mithl. Both lead to the same conclusion—no liability—because the husband's enjoyment of the marital right offsets any loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for mahr al-mithl or more
Read the first scenario: two witnesses testify that a woman is married with a mahr equal to or exceeding her mahr al-mithl. After retracting, the ruling is no liability. Ask yourself: what is the legal reason (illah)?
The text explicitly says 'la daman'. Focus on the reasoning: they caused loss of property but with compensation.
2
Analyze the compensation argument
The marital right (al-bud') is considered a valuable property (mutaqawwam) once it enters the husband's ownership upon consummation. Therefore, the husband received a benefit that compensates for the mahr he paid. Trace this logic: loss of mahr offset by gain of bud'.
In Hanafi law, bud' is treated as a commodity for liability purposes after consumption.
3
Examine the second scenario (deficient mahr)
Now consider the case where witnesses testify to a mahr less than mahr al-mithl. The text states they are not liable for the deficiency (the difference). Note the reasoning: the usufruct of bud' is not considered valuable at the time of loss. But since the husband still received the marital right, there is no net loss.
The commentary clarifies that the ruling is actually the same as the first: no liability at all.
4
Synthesize the overarching principle
From both cases, derive the principle: witnesses to a marriage are never liable upon retraction because the husband has already benefited from the marital relationship. The amount of mahr does not affect liability, only the fact that consummation occurred.
This principle is specific to marriage testimony; other witness retraction cases may differ.

Retraction after Testimony of Marriage with Proper Dower: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Liability
Intra-Hanafi dispute on witness liability upon retraction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on witness liability upon retraction after testifying to a dower less than the proper dower, state the basis of each position (husband's vs. wife's word), and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Witness liability depends on whose word is taken in a dower dispute: husband's (no liability) or wife's (liability for shortfall).

This lesson unpacks a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute within the Book of Retraction of Testimonies. The basic ruling in the matn (al-Quduri) states that witnesses who testify to a marriage dower and then retract are not liable because the husband's own admission establishes the dower. However, the commentary from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira introduces a deeper disagreement among the founding imams: Abu Yusuf aligns with the matn, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that if the witnesses testified for an amount less than the proper dower, they become liable for the shortfall. Understanding this ikhtilaf requires grasping the underlying procedural rule of whose word is given weight when the husband and wife disagree on the dower amount.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling of the matn
Read the first part of the Arabic passage: 'Itlikewise, if two witnesses testify against a man that he married a woman for an amount equal to her proper dower... then they retract, they are not liable for anything.' Note the key reason given: 'because the payment is binding by the husband's admission.' This is the default Hanafi position presented in al-Quduri.
The matn does not mention any dispute; the ikhtilaf is introduced by the commentary.
2
Understand the specific scenario from al-Musaffa
The commentary provides a concrete example: husband claims dower of 100, wife claims 1000 (the proper dower), and witnesses testify for 100. After judgment for the wife, the witnesses retract. You must grasp that the judgment was based on their testimony for the lesser amount.
The proper dower (mahr al-mithl) is the default when the actual dower is unknown or disputed; here the wife claims the proper dower.
3
Distinguish the two opinions: Abu Yusuf vs. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
Abu Yusuf holds no liability; his reasoning: the husband's word is taken (qawl al-zawj) because the dower is a financial obligation, and in disputes over financial obligations, the defendant's word is given weight. Therefore, the witnesses did not cause any loss—the husband's admission already bound him. In contrast, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold the witnesses liable for the difference (900); their reasoning: in dower disputes, the wife's word is taken up to the proper dower, so without the testimony she would have received 1000. The witnesses' testimony for 100 caused her to lose 900.
This difference is not about the witnesses' truthfulness but about the procedural rule in a dower dispute.
4
Analyze the underlying principle: whose word is taken?
The entire dispute hinges on whether the qawl (word) of the husband or the wife is given priority when they disagree on the dower amount. Abu Yusuf applies the general rule for financial obligations (qawl al-mudda'ā 'alayh), while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad treat dower as a special case where the woman is the plaintiff and her claim is given weight up to the proper dower due to her vulnerability.
This distinction is found in other chapters of Hanafi fiqh, e.g., in dower disputes after consummation.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical outcome: if witnesses retract after testifying to a dower less than the proper dower, according to Abu Yusuf they owe nothing; according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad they owe the difference between the testified amount and the proper dower. Judges and muftis must be aware which view is operative in their context. The commentary notes that the matn's ruling aligns with Abu Yusuf, but the ikhtilaf is not definitively resolved.
Later Hanafi fatwa collections may prefer one view; this passage does not give a clear tarjih.

According to Muhammad, he has the choice: if he wants, he holds the branches liable, or the originals. His saying: (If branch witnesses say: 'The original witnesses lied' or 'They were mistaken in their testimony': it is not heeded) because what has been executed of the ruling is not overturned by their words. And liability is not obligatory on them because they have not retracted their testimony; they only testified against others regarding retraction. * His saying: (If four testify for zina and two for ihsan, and the ihsan witnesses retract: they do not guarantee) because the ihsan witnesses are not cause for stoning; ihsan is a condition like puberty and sanity, and because stoning is a punishment, and ihsan does not incur punishment, as it is puberty, Islam, marriage, and freedom, and these are meanings not punishable; punishment is deserved for zina, not for other things. And because ihsan existed in him before zina and did not necessitate stoning, so when zina occurred after ihsan, stoning is required, and if stoning is not required solely by the testimony of ihsan witnesses, they do not guarantee by retracting.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on rulings for retraction of testimony in Hanafi fiqh. It opens with the chapter on retraction of testimonies. The first ruling: if branch witnesses (those who testify based on the testimony of original witnesses) retract, they are liable for damages because their testimony directly caused the judgment. The second: if original witnesses retract and deny ever having appointed the branch witnesses, there is no liability on them, as they are essentially denying the basis of the branch testimony, and the judgment stands. The third: if original witnesses say 'we appointed them but we were mistaken,' they become liable according to Imam Muhammad; this is because their admission of appointing the branches makes them as if they testified themselves. However, according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two), the original witnesses are not liable because the judgment was based on the branch testimony alone. An inter-madhhab comparison: al-Shafi'i holds that both original witnesses are subject to retaliation (qisas) if their retraction leads to execution, while the Hanafi school holds that they only pay blood money from their own wealth over three years, as they have confessed to causing loss; the 'aqlah does not bear this, they owe no expiation, and they are not barred from inheritance if they are heirs of the deceased. Next, if branch witnesses claim the originals lied or erred, that is not heeded because the judgment has already been executed and cannot be overturned by such allegations; also, the branch witnesses are not retracting their own testimony. Finally, the case of zina: if four witnesses testify for zina and two for ihsan (the condition for stoning), and the ihsan witnesses retract, they do not guarantee. This is because ihsan is a condition like puberty or sanity, not a direct cause of punishment; stoning is incurred by zina alone, not by ihsan. Since their testimony was not the direct cause of the stoning, retraction does not create liability.

Key Terms
شهود الأصل (shuhūd al-aṣl): Original witnesses who directly witnessed the event and then appoint others (branch witnesses) to testify on their behalf.
شهود الفرع (shuhūd al-far'): Branch witnesses who testify based on the testimony of the original witnesses, not on their own direct knowledge.
ضمان (ḍamān): Liability to pay compensation (blood money or property) due to causing loss through testimony.

Key Takeaway
Liability for retracted testimony depends on who caused the judgment: branch witnesses are directly liable, while original witnesses are liable only if they admit causation.
