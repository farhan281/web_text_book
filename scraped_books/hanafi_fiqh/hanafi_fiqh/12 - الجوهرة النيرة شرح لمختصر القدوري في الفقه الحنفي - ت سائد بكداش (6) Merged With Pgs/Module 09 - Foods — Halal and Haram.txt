Definitions of Suhur and Ghadā' in Oaths
Defining the times for suhur and ghadā' when swearing oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of suhur, ghadā', and tasabbuh as they apply to oaths, and explain the use of custom ('urf) in defining legal terms.

Core Idea
In Hanafi oaths, suhur is from half the night to dawn; ghadā' is defined by custom; tasabbuh is from sunrise to mid-forenoon.

When someone swears an oath related to eating at a particular time, the legal consequence depends on how that time is defined. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies the precise definitions of suhur (pre-dawn meal), ghadā' (noon meal), and tasabbuh (morning meal) in the context of oaths. Understanding these definitions is essential for correctly determining whether an oath has been violated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms defined
The passage defines three terms: suhur, ghadā', and tasabbuh. Note that each has a specific temporal or customary definition.
Suhur and tasabbuh are time-based; ghadā' is custom-based.
2
State the definition of suhur
Suhur in the main text is from half the night to dawn. There is a variant from al-Karkhi that says from after half the night.
This slight difference can affect oath-breaking in borderline cases.
3
State the definition of ghadā'
Ghadā' is defined by the custom ('urf) of each locality. Additionally, it must be more than half-satiety to count as ghadā' for oath purposes.
Custom varies, so the ruling may differ by region.
4
State the definition of tasabbuh
Imam Muhammad defines tasabbuh as the period between sunrise and the end of the forenoon (al-duha al-akbar). This is used when someone swears not to take a morning meal.
This definition is specific to the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage defines two terms relevant to oaths about eating times. Suhur is defined with a slight difference between the main text (from half night to dawn) and a narration from al-Karkhi (from after half night). Ghadā' is defined customarily, with a condition that it exceeds half-satiety. Imam Muhammad gives a specific time for tasabbuh. These definitions are used to determine when an oath about eating at these times is broken.

Key Terms
سحور (suhūr): The pre-dawn meal; here it is defined as the time from half the night to dawn for oath purposes.
غداء (ghadā'): The noon meal; defined by custom and must be more than half-satiety to count as ghadā' in an oath.
تصبح (tasabbuh): The morning meal; specified by Muhammad as between sunrise and mid-forenoon.

Defining Idām in Oaths: Custom, Intention, and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on defining idām for oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of idām for oaths, identify the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad, and apply the rules of custom and intention to determine whether an item constitutes idām.

Core Idea
Idām in oaths is defined by custom and intention, not merely by physical property.

When someone swears not to eat idām, what exactly is being prohibited? The answer depends on a nuanced Hanafi framework that balances customary usage (‘urf) and the swearer's intention (niyyah). This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether items like roasted meat, dates, or ghee are considered idām, and how this affects the ruling on oath-breaking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline definition
Start with the core statement: idām is anything bread is dipped into and eaten with (like milk, vinegar, oil). This is the textual definition from al-Quduri.
2
Distinguish between dipped and non-dipped items
Note that items customarily dipped (e.g., broth) are idām by default. For items not dipped (e.g., roasted meat), there is a dispute: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf require intention to make them idām; Muhammad considers them idām regardless.
3
Apply consensus items
Salt is idām by consensus because it is not eaten alone. Fruit is not idām by consensus. Understand the reasoning behind these agreed rulings.
4
Examine the dispute on specific items
For items like ghee, dates, and walnuts, note the different positions: Muhammad considers them idām; Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf generally do not unless intended. For dates, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad cite a hadith where the Prophet described a date as idām.
5
Understand the role of custom and intention
The overall principle: custom (what people normally do) sets the default, but intention can broaden or narrow the oath's scope. The disagreement centers on how much weight to give custom versus general possibility.

ولو حلف لا يأتدم: فالإدام كلّ شيء يُصبغ به الخبز، ويؤكل معه به، كاللبن والخل والزيت والمرق والعسل. وأما ما لا يُصبغ به : فليس بإدام عند أبي حنيفة وأبي يوسف، إلا أن ينويه، مثل الشواء والجبن والبيض واللحم غير المطبوخ. وقال محمد: هو إدام وإن لم ينوه. والملح إدام، بالإجماع؛ لأنه لا يؤكل بانفراده، بخلاف اللحم، وما يضاهيه : فإنه يؤكل وحده، إلا أن ينويه. - وإن تُرد خبر بماء وملح : لم يكن إداماً؛ لأنه خلاف العرف. وأما السمن فذكر الخجندي أنه ليس بإدام عندهما. : وقال محمد: هو إدام. والفاكهة ليست بإدام، إجماعاً. والبقل والبطيخ والعنب ليس بإدام. والتمر والجوز ليس بإدام ؛ لأن التمر يُفرد بالأكل في الغالب. وعن أبي يوسف ومحمد : أن التمر إدام ؛ «لأن النبي عليه الصلاة والسلام أخذ لقمة بيده وتمرةً بيده الأخرى، وقال: هذه إدام هذه (۱). كذا في «الكرخي».
And if he swore that he does not eat idām: idām is everything that bread is dipped into and eaten with, such as milk, vinegar, oil, broth, and honey. As for what is not dipped: it is not idām according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, unless he intends it, such as roasted meat, cheese, eggs, and uncooked meat. Muhammad said: it is idām even if he did not intend it. Salt is idām by consensus because it is not eaten alone, unlike meat and what resembles it, for that is eaten alone, unless he intends it. And if one dips bread in water and salt, it is not idām because it is contrary to custom. As for ghee, al-Khujandi mentioned that according to the two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) it is not idām. And Muhammad said: it is idām. Fruit is not idām by consensus. Vegetables, watermelon, and grapes are not idām. Dates and walnuts are not idām because dates are usually eaten alone. And from Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: dates are idām, because the Prophet (peace be upon him) took a morsel with one hand and a date with the other and said: 'This is the idām of this.' Thus in al-Karkhi.

Commentary
This passage details the definition of idām for oath purposes. The main opinion of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf is that idām is only what is customarily used as a dip or flavoring; items eaten alone are not idām unless the swearer intends them. Muhammad expands it to include anything that could accompany bread. Citing a hadith, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider dates idām, though Abu Hanifa's position is that dates are normally eaten alone. The commentary shows how custom and intention affect the ruling. Salt is idām by consensus due to its inedibility alone. The text also notes that ghee is disputed between al-Khujandi's report and Muhammad's view.

Key Terms
إدام (idām): Anything that bread is dipped into and eaten with, like milk, vinegar, oil, etc. Also includes items that accompany bread by custom.
التصبيغ (al-tasbīgh): The act of dipping bread into a condiment; used to define idām.
العرف (al-‘urf): Custom; the habitual practice of people that serves as a legal indicator.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi oaths, idām is defined by custom and intention, leading to intra-school disagreement on borderline items like meat, dates, and ghee.

Classification of Fruits in Oaths: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Categorizing fruits into three groups for oath on eating fruit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi positions on what counts as fruit in an oath and apply the criterion of tafakkuh vs. shib' to determine breach.

Core Idea
In oaths, 'fruit' means what one eats for pleasure, not satiety — but the Sahibayn include precious fruits anyway.

When someone swears not to eat fruit, what exactly counts as fruit? The Hanafi school agrees on clear cases: apricots and melons are fruit, cucumbers are not. But pomegranate, ripe dates, and grapes are disputed. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the basis of the disagreement: Abu Hanifa defines fruit by the purpose of eating (tafakkuh vs. shib'), while his two disciples focus on the value and pleasure of the item. Let's break down the three categories and the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The text divides items into three: (1) consensus breach (apricot, peach, etc.), (2) consensus no breach (cucumber, carrot), and (3) disputed (pomegranate, ripe dates, grapes).
List examples from each group from the passage.
2
Extract Abu Hanifa's criterion
Abu Hanifa defines fruit (fākiha) as something eaten for enjoyment (tafakkuh) and not for satiety. He then explains why each disputed item fails: ripe dates are eaten for satiety; pomegranate is sucked, not eaten; grapes can be eaten for satiety.
Note the root: f-k-h is associated with delight.
3
Extract the Sahibayn's criterion
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider these items fruits because they are among the most precious fruits and the pleasure in them exceeds that of others.
They focus on the nature of the item, not the motive of eating.
4
Derive the practical consequence
If someone swears not to eat fruit and then eats grapes: Abu Hanifa says no breach; the Sahibayn say breach. This difference matters in real life for validity of oaths.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

وإن حلف لا يأكل فاكهة: فهو على ثلاثة أوجه : ١- في وجه: يحنث، إجماعاً، وهو ما إذا أكل المشمش أو الفرسك أو السفرجل أو الإجاص أو التين أو البطيخ أو نحوها، وكذا قصب السكر. ٢ـ وفي وجه : لا يحنث، بالإجماع، وهو ما إذا أكل القثّاء أو الخيار أو الجزر ونحو ذلك. ـ وفي وجه : اختلفوا فيه : وهو الرمان والرُّطَب والعنب: فعند أبي حنيفة : لا يحنث به؛ لأن الفاكهة عنده: ما يُقصد بأكله التفكه، ه، دون الشبع، والرُّطَب يؤكل للشبع، والرمان لا يقصد أكله، وإنما يُمَص، وكذا العنب. وعندهما : كلَّ ذلك فاكهة ؛ لأنه من أعزّ الفواكه، والتنعم به يفوق التنعم بغيره.
And if he swore that he would not eat fruit: it is on three aspects: 1) In one aspect: he breaks the oath by consensus, which is when he eats apricot, peach, quince, prune, fig, watermelon, or similar, and also sugar cane. 2) In another aspect: he does not break the oath by consensus, which is when he eats cucumber, gourd, carrot, or similar. 3) In another aspect: they differed about it, which is pomegranate, ripe dates (rutab), and grapes. According to Abu Hanifa: he does not break the oath, because fruit in his view is what is intended for enjoyment (tafakkuh) and not for satiety, and ripe dates are eaten for satiety, pomegranate is not intended for eating but sucked, and similarly grapes. According to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): all of that is fruit because they are among the most precious fruits and the pleasure in them exceeds pleasure in others.

Commentary
This passage treats the term 'fruit' (fākiha) in oaths. By consensus, true fruits like apricot, peach, etc., are considered fruit; vegetables like cucumber, etc., are not. The dispute is on pomegranate, ripe dates (rutab), and grapes. Abu Hanifa focuses on the purpose of eating: fruit is for enjoyment (tafakkuh) not for satiety; since ripe dates are eaten for satiety, pomegranate is only sucked, and grapes can be eaten for satiety, they are not fruit in his view. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider them fruits because they are among the most precious fruits and the pleasure in them is great. This shows the application of custom (within Hanafi) and the role of intent in defining terms in oaths.

Key Terms
فاكهة (fākiha): Fruit intended for enjoyment (tafakkuh) rather than satiety, according to Abu Hanifa. For Sahibayn, it includes precious fruits like pomegranate and grapes.
تفكه (tafakkuh): Eating for enjoyment or pleasure, not for nourishment.

Ruling on Castrating Animals (Khisa' al-Baha'im)
Permissibility of castrating animals for benefit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on castrating animals and explain the rationale based on benefit.

Core Idea
Castrating animals is permissible because it yields clear benefits without causing harm like human castration.

This lesson addresses the permissibility of castrating animals for utilitarian purposes. The Hanafi school permits it due to the tangible benefits of improved meat quality and fattening. We will explore the reasoning behind this ruling and distinguish it from human castration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states 'la ba's' which indicates permissibility (ibaha). Identify the act: castration of animals (khisa'). The ruling is mubah.
2
Understand the rationale (talil)
The commentary explains 'because it is done for benefit' and specifies the benefits: the animal becomes fatter and its meat improves. This is a causal reasoning linking benefit to permissibility.
Hanafi fiqh often uses talil (causal reasoning) to justify rulings.
3
Distinguish from human castration
While animal castration is permissible, human castration is haram because it involves changing Allah's creation without permissible benefit. The text implicitly contrasts the two.
This is a key distinction.
4
Note the scope and any conditions
The permissibility is general but may be restricted if the animal is harmed or the act is excessive. However, the text does not specify conditions; the default is mubah for benefit.
Some later scholars specify that it should be done with proper care (iha'lat) to avoid cruelty.

Commentary
The matn permits (la ba's) castration of animals. The commentary provides the rationale: it is for benefit (naf'), as the animal becomes fatter and its meat improves. This is in contrast to human castration which is muthla. Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between humans and animals here; human castration is haram, while animal castration is permissible for utilitarian purposes.

Key Terms
خصاء (khisa'): castration of animals
