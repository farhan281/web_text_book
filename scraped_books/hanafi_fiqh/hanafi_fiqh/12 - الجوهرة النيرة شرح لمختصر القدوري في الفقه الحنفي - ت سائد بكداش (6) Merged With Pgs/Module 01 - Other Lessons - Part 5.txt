Oath on Speaking: Defining Speech as Verbal Utterance
Specific cases: oath on speaking with conditions on parents' life

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of kalam in oath contexts and apply it to determine whether writing, gestures, or messengers break an oath.

Core Idea
Sending a letter does not break an oath not to speak — kalam means only verbal utterance.

When someone swears not to speak to a person, what counts as 'speaking'? Does sending a letter or a text message violate the oath? This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position: the term 'kalam' in legal oaths is strictly limited to spoken words (al-nutq). We will examine rulings from al-Waqi'at on conditional oaths and non-verbal communication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Oath and Its Condition
Consider the oath: 'I will not speak to you as long as your parents are alive.' What is the scope of the condition?
The condition is conjunctive: both parents must be alive.
2
Determine When the Condition is Fulfilled
The condition (both parents alive) is no longer fully present if one dies. Therefore, the restriction upon speech ceases.
Hanafis interpret conditions strictly – the entire condition must be present.
3
Define 'Kalam' (Speech)
According to the Hanafi school, 'kalam' is defined as verbal utterance (al-nutq). Non-verbal acts like writing, sending a messenger, gesturing, or signaling are not included.
This definition is crucial for determining oath violations.
4
Apply the Definition to Non-Verbal Acts
If the oath is not to speak, then writing a letter, sending a messenger, or using gestures does not break the oath because they are not 'kalam'.
Even if the messenger speaks on his behalf, the oath-breaker did not speak himself.
5
Extend to Similar Oaths (e.g., Muhaddatha)
An oath not to 'have a conversation' (muhaddatha) follows the same rule: it is limited to verbal exchange.
Consistency with the definition of kalam.

Oath on Wife's Exit: Permission and Conditions
Oath rulings on wife's exit: specific permissions vs. general permission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which an oath on a wife's exit is broken, apply the distinction between two oath formulations, and identify the Hanafi legal device (hila) to avoid breaking the oath.

Core Idea
An oath restricting exit needs separate permission per trip unless comprehensive phrasing is used.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies the fiqh of oaths regarding a wife's exit. It distinguishes between two common oath phrasings and explains when a single permission suffices for all future exits. It also introduces the Hanafi concept of hila (legal device) and highlights an intra-Hanafi dispute about the effectiveness of permission that the wife does not hear.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two oath formulations
The passage presents two key formulations: 'لا تخرج إلا بإذني' (she shall not go out except with my permission) and 'إلا أن آذن لك' (except that I permit you). Recognize that the first ties each exit to separate permission, while the second ties the exception to the act of permitting itself.
The difference hinges on the wording of the exception (istithna).
2
Apply the rule for repeated exits
Under the first formulation, if the husband permits once and she subsequently goes out again without a new permission, the oath is broken. Under the second, one permission covers all future exits.
The default is that each exit requires a new permission unless explicitly stated otherwise.
3
Understand the effect of intention (diyanatan wa qada'an)
If the husband intended his one permission to be for all exits, he is believed in conscience (diyanatan) but not in court (qada'an) according to the majority. Al-Karkhi holds he is believed in both.
This shows the Hanafi distinction between internal sincerity and external evidence.
4
Recognize the legal device (hila)
To avoid breaking the oath inadvertently, the husband can explicitly say: 'I have permitted you to go out every time' or 'I permit you whenever you go out.' This converts the permission into a general one.
The hila is a legitimate tool to adhere to the oath's conditions without repeated permissions.
5
Analyze the dispute on inaudible permission
The passage notes a disagreement: if the husband permits in a way the wife does not hear, the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) say the oath is broken because permission was not communicated. Abu Yusuf says it is not broken because the husband did permit.
This traces to different views on the requirement of communication for permission.

Oath terms like 'lunch' and 'dinner' are defined by local custom, not fixed times.

In oaths, the meaning of vague terms like 'lunch' and 'dinner' can determine whether the oath is broken. The Hanafi school relies on local custom (urf) to define these meals, considering both the time and the intention of satiety. This lesson examines the definitions and their application.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The genus of the coin, not its quality, determines oath fulfillment.

An oath to repay a debt can be tricky when the coins given turn out to be defective. Does a debtor break his oath if he pays with coins that are faulty, counterfeit, or even legally invalid? The Hanafi school gives a clear answer: as long as the coins are made of the same metal (silver), the oath is fulfilled. Only when the substance changes entirely—like copper or lead—does the oath break. This lesson unpacks that principle.

Effect of Child's Death on Establishing Umm Walad
Ruling statement with rationale based on dependency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a seller's claim of paternity after the child's death makes the mother an umm walad, and explain the legal rationale based on dependency.

Core Idea
Umm walad status depends on the child's paternity; if the child dies, his lineage is not established, so the mother's status does not arise.

This passage addresses a scenario where a slave woman gives birth to a child, and the child dies before the owner (seller) claims paternity. Normally, if the owner claims the child within six months, the child's lineage is established and the mother becomes an umm walad (free upon the owner's death). However, if the child dies before the claim, the Hanafi school rules that the mother's status does not follow because the child's lineage is not needed after death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
The child dies, the seller claims paternity, and the mother gave birth within less than six months. Normally, this would be a valid claim establishing lineage and umm walad status.
The six-month period is from the time of purchase, indicating the child was conceived while the woman was owned by the seller.
2
State the ruling
The mother does not become umm walad. The istilad (status of umm walad) is not established.
3
Analyze the rationale: dependency (tabi'ah)
The mother's status as umm walad is a consequence of the child's paternity. It is a secondary status that follows the primary (the child's lineage).
If the primary is not established, the secondary cannot arise.

Explain why the child's lineage is not established after death
After death, there is no need to establish the child's lineage (e.g., for inheritance, guardianship, payment of blood money). Therefore, the claim of paternity is not accepted to create a legal effect.
The purpose of establishing lineage is to attach legal consequences that are only relevant while the person is alive.
5
Note the practical consequence (thamarah)
The mother remains a slave. She does not become free upon the owner's death. The owner's claim is effectively void for this purpose.
Contrast with a living child: the claim would establish lineage and the mother would become umm walad.

The Principle of Rejecting Self-Interested Testimony

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Testimony tainted by personal gain or loss is legally invalid.

This passage from the Jawhara al-Nayra explains a fundamental rule in Hanafi testimony law: a witness cannot testify if doing so brings him a benefit or prevents a harm. The rule is applied to partners, employees, and other relationships. Studying this lesson will help you understand why certain testimonies are rejected and how to spot exceptions where suspicion is absent.

Appointment and Qualifications of the Divider (Qasim)
Rulings on appointing a divider and his required qualities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended appointment of a divider by the Imam and identify the three qualifications required for a qasim.

Core Idea
The divider is like a judge: the Imam should provide him, and he must be just, trustworthy, and knowledgeable.

Division of property among co-owners is a sensitive task that can prevent disputes. The Hanafi school treats it as a quasi-judicial function, so the Imam should appoint a qualified divider paid from the public treasury. If he does not, the divider may charge a fee from the parties. This lesson explores these two scenarios and the essential qualities a divider must possess.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
The text states that the Imam should (yambaghi) appoint a divider. In Hanafi terminology, 'yambaghi' indicates a strongly recommended act (sunnah muakkadah). This is the ruling for the act of appointing a qasim.
2
Understand the rationale for salary from Bayt al-Mal
Because division resolves disputes like a judge's work and benefits the entire community, the divider's salary should come from the public treasury. This is a case of 'ghurm bi ghanm' – risk in return for gain.
3
Distinguish the two appointment scenarios
If the Imam provides a salaried divider, the divider works without charging the parties. If the Imam does not, he appoints a divider who takes a fee from the dividing parties, since the benefit is specific to them.
This distinction avoids hardship and ensures division is possible even without state resources.
4
List the three essential qualifications
The divider must be 'adl (just) in his relationship with Allah, ma'mun (trustworthy) in dealings among people, and 'aliman bi al-qismah (knowledgeable of the laws of division).
These mirror the qualifications of a witness or judge, highlighting the seriousness of the role.
5
Define each qualification in practical terms
'Adl means avoiding major sins and not persisting in minor ones. Ma'mun means honesty and reliability. 'Alim means knowing the rules of partition, shares, and property rights.
A divider lacking any of these cannot validly perform the division.

Duties of the Qasim in Partitioning Property

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The qasim's method ensures fair partition through depiction, equalization, measurement, and separate valuation, with lots recommended but not required.

Partitioning jointly owned property requires careful procedure to avoid future disputes. In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi guidance for the appointed divider (qasim) — from documenting shares to evaluating buildings and assigning lots. The text uses 'yambaghi' (it is befitting), indicating sunnah.

Handle fractional shares

Buildings are valued separately, and each share is given its own pathway and water right to prevent future disputes over shared elements.

Consensus and Dispute: Undivided Claims and Compensation After Partition
Expansion on claim scenarios after partition with consensus and dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three post-partition claim scenarios and distinguish between consensus and dispute rulings, including the compensation calculation for a specific part of one hand.

Core Idea
An undivided claim on the whole rescinds by consensus; a specific part of one share triggers option; an undivided part of one share reopens the Hanafi dispute.

After partition, claims can take different forms: an undivided share in the whole property, a specific part of one partner's allocated portion, or an undivided part of that portion. Each scenario has its own ruling—some by consensus, some with a Hanafi ikhtilaf. This lesson walks through these variations and the compensation formula when the division is upheld.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the Three Scenarios
List: (a) undivided part of the whole property (e.g., half the house as an undivided share); (b) a specific known part of what is in one partner's hand (e.g., half of the lot in his hand, already divided and known); (c) an undivided part of what is in one partner's hand. Note which are consensus and which are disputed.
Scenario (a) is consensus: rescission. (b) is consensus: option. (c) is disputed.
2
Analyze Scenario (a): Undivided Part of the Whole
When an undivided share of the entire property is claimed, the division is rescinded by consensus. Why? Because the claimant's share would be scattered across both partners' portions, causing harm.
The claimant has a right in both shares, so the original division cannot stand.
3
Analyze Scenario (b): Specific Known Part of One Hand
If a known, divided part (e.g., half the specific lot) of one partner's share is claimed, the claimant has an option: either rescind the division (because his share is scattered by claiming part of one partition) or let the division stand and take compensation from the other partner. The compensation is calculated proportionally: if the claimed part is half of what was in that hand, the compensation is a quarter of what the other partner has.
Calculation: if all was claimed, he'd get half of the other's share; half of that gives quarter.
4
Analyze Scenario (c): Undivided Part of One Hand
Here, the claimed portion is an undivided part of one partner's share (e.g., an undivided third). Abu Hanifa and Muhammad give the claimant the option (as in scenario b). Abu Yusuf insists on rescission because an undivided portion introduces a third partner whose consent is needed for any division.
Dispute hinges on whether an undivided claim on one share is analogous to a specific claim (option) or an undivided claim on the whole (rescission).
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi
For scenario (c), the preferred view is Abu Hanifa and Muhammad's: option. This is consistent with the general principle of preserving partitions where possible.
The tarjih from Al-Hidayah supports the majority.
