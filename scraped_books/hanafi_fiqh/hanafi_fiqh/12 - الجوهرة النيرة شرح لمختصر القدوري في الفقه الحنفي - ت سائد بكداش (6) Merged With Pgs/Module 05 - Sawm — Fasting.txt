Counting Oaths and the Role of Intention
States rulings on multiple oath expressions and the role of intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on when multiple conditional phrases form one or multiple oaths and apply the rule of intention based on the status of the swearer.

Core Idea
Multiple descriptions of the same person form one oath; intention governs interpretation, but only if the swearer is wronged.

Have you ever made a complex oath with multiple conditions? The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines on how to count such oaths and whose intention matters. In this lesson, we explore a passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira that lays out the rulings and then presents a prophetic hadith as evidence. Understanding these rules prevents us from falling into false oaths and ensures justice in legal disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of oath expressions
Read the first part of the passage where different formulas are given: a series of descriptors ('Jew, Christian, Magian') vs. a single phrase with 'and' ('free from Allah and His Messenger') vs. separated phrases. Notice which are considered one oath and which are two.
Pay attention to the wording: repetition with 'and' can create multiple oaths if it restates the phrase, but a list of attributes is one.
2
Understand the rule of intention
The passage quotes al-Karkhi: the swearer's intention governs if he is wronged (mazlum), otherwise the intention of the person who demanded the oath governs. Grasp the distinction between a wronged (coerced) swearer and an unjust (voluntary) swearer.
'Wronged' means forced to swear; 'unjust' means swearing falsely of his own accord.
3
Analyze the hadith proof
The hadith warns against taking a Muslim's right by false oath. Consider how this proof supports the seriousness of oaths and the need for proper interpretation.
The hadith is a strong deterrent, even for a small object like a twig.
4
Note the exception for divorce/freeing oaths
The passage from al-Waqi'at states that for oaths involving divorce or freeing slaves, the swearer's intention always matters, regardless of being wronged or unjust. Understand why this is a special category.
Divorce and freeing are personal acts where the individual's intent is paramount.

Commentary
This passage presents rulings from the matn on how multiple expressions affect the number of oaths, followed by commentary on intention and a hadith warning against false oaths. The first ruling: when someone says 'He is a Jew, he is a Christian, he is a Magian if he does such-and-such,' it counts as a single oath because these are multiple descriptions of the same person. Similarly, saying 'He is free from Allah and His Messenger' is one oath, but if he separates them with 'and' (free from Allah and free from His Messenger), it becomes two oaths. The commentary from al-Karkhi distinguishes: if the one swearing is wronged (i.e., coerced to swear), the oath is interpreted according to his intention; if he is unjust (voluntarily swearing falsely), it is interpreted according to the intention of the person who asked him to swear. The hadith warns against taking a false oath to seize property: even a small thing like a twig leads to Hellfire. Finally, a note from al-Waqi'at: for oaths of divorce or freeing a slave, the intention of the one swearing always governs, regardless of whether he is unjust or wronged. This is because divorce and freeing are personal matters that depend on the individual's intent.

Key Terms
اليمين (al-yamin): An oath, a solemn affirmation invoking Allah or something sacred.
نية الحالف (niyyat al-halif): The intention of the person swearing the oath.
مظلوم (mazlum): One who is wronged; here, someone compelled or forced to swear an oath.

Key Takeaway

Conditions for Fasting in Kaffarah and Permissible Recipients
Sets rules for when fasting is valid and who may receive kaffarah payments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which fasting for kaffarah is invalid due to forgotten possession of means, and identify the permissible recipients of kaffarah including the intra-Hanafi difference regarding poor dhimmis.

Core Idea
Fasting for kaffarah is invalid if you possess the means, even if forgotten; kaffarah may be given to poor dhimmis per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah clarifies two important rulings regarding kaffarah of an oath. First, it explains that fasting only substitutes when one is truly unable to perform the primary expiation; if one forgets possessing a slave or food and then remembers, the fast is invalid by consensus. Second, it addresses to whom kaffarah may be given, highlighting a known intra-Hanafi difference regarding poor dhimmis. Understanding these details ensures correct performance of expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on forgetting means
The passage states that if a person fasts for kaffarah while having a slave or food in his possession that he forgot, then remembers after fasting, the fast does not suffice (lam yujzih) by consensus. The act is ruled invalid because the condition for fasting—inability to find the means—is absent.
The consensus (ijma') makes this ruling binding across schools.
2
Locate the dalil (Quranic proof)
The text cites Allah's restriction: 'faman lam yajid' (whoever does not find) from Quran 5:89. This conditions fasting on non-existence of means. Since the person actually possesses means (even if forgotten), he is not 'lam yajid', so fasting is invalid.
The dalil is qat'i al-thubut (Quran) and qat'i al-dalalah (explicit condition).
3
Define yasar for kaffarah
Yasar (affluence) is defined as having surplus beyond what suffices for one's dependents to cover the cost of kaffarah (freeing a slave, feeding or clothing ten poor). If one has such surplus, he must use it; fasting is only valid if he cannot afford any of the three options.
This definition prevents using fasting when one has the financial means, even if not the exact items.
4
Identify prohibited and permissible recipients
Kaffarah cannot be given to those to whom zakat is forbidden, such as parents, children, and spouses. However, it may be given to poor dhimmis (non-Muslim citizens) according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (al-Shaybani), though Abu Yusuf analogizes to zakat and forbids it.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is permissibility, as held by the majority of the Hanafi school.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
The disagreement centers on whether kaffarah, like zakat, is restricted to Muslims. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) rely on the general command to feed the poor, which includes dhimmis, while Abu Yusuf draws qiyas with zakat. The Hanafi response distinguishes kaffarah as expiation for a sin, not a wealth purification.
The practical consequence: if one gives kaffarah to a dhimmi, it is valid according to the mufta bihi view.

Commentary
This passage addresses the condition of inability ('ajz) for fasting as kaffarah. If a person cannot afford to free a slave, clothe ten poor, or feed ten poor, he must fast three consecutive days. However, if he has the means (a slave, clothing, or food) but has forgotten them, and then remembers after fasting, his fast is invalid by consensus (ijma'), because the Quran (5:89) conditions fasting on not finding the means, and here he actually possesses them. The definition of yasar (affluence) for kaffarah is having surplus wealth beyond one's own and dependents' basic needs sufficient to perform the kaffarah. If he possesses the specific items (a slave, cloth, or food) for kaffarah, he must use them; fasting is not valid until he is truly unable. 

The passage also discusses who may receive the kaffarah. It cannot be given to those to whom zakat is forbidden (i.e., ascendants, descendants, spouses according to Hanafi view). However, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), it is permissible to give kaffarah to poor dhimmis (non-Muslim citizens of the Islamic state), unlike zakat which is restricted to Muslims. Abu Yusuf disagrees, equating kaffarah with zakat in this regard. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the view of the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), as it is the majority. Additionally, kaffarah cannot be used for shrouds or building mosques; it must go directly to the poor.

Key Terms
يسار (yasar): Wealth/affluence: having surplus beyond basic needs sufficient to perform the kaffarah.
أهل الذمة (ahl al-dhimmah): Non-Muslim citizens of the Islamic state who pay jizyah and are under its protection.
إعسار (i'sar): Inability to perform the kaffarah due to lack of means.
