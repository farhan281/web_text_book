The Judge's Duty of Equality and Prohibition of Partiality
Explains the rule of equality and prohibited actions for judges

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on equality between two litigants and identify the prohibited actions for a judge in court.

Core Idea
Equality between litigants is wajib; any sign of partiality is prohibited.

A judge must be a neutral arbiter. This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out the core obligations of judicial conduct: the judge must treat both litigants equally in all aspects of demeanor, and avoid any action that could break the heart of one party or give the appearance of bias. These rules are essential to ensure that justice is both done and seen to be done.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on equality
The judge must equalize between the two litigants in seating, facing, looking, and speaking. This is wajib (obligatory) according to the Hanafi school.
Equality extends to all aspects of the judge's interaction with the parties.
2
List the prohibited actions
The judge must not whisper to one litigant, gesture to him, prompt him with an argument, raise his voice at one without raising it at the other, or smile at one without smiling at the other.
These actions are prohibited because they imply partiality and weaken the other party.
3
Examine the greeting rules
A litigant entering the court should not greet the judge; if he does, the judge is not obligated to return the greeting. However, a witness may greet and the judge must return it.
This exception prevents inequality: returning a greeting to one litigant would favor him.
4
Understand the rationale
All these rules are designed to prevent even the appearance of bias and to protect the hearts and rights of both parties, ensuring a fair trial.
The judge's impartiality is fundamental to the justice system.

Judgment in Absentia: The Dispute of the Sahibayn
Presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on judging after defendant's absence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding judgment in absentia and explain the practical difference.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf allows judgment in absentia; Muhammad insists on the defendant's presence.

When a defendant leaves after hearing evidence, should the judge proceed or wait? This short passage from al-Yanabi' records the classic intra-Hanafi disagreement between the two major disciples of Abu Hanifa. Understanding their reasoning illuminates the balance between procedural efficiency and the right to defense in Hanafi qada.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exact scenario
The judge has heard the plaintiff's evidence (baiyyina). The defendant was present at that point but then became absent before the judge pronounced judgment. Note: the dispute is about this specific sequence.
Not about the case when the defendant never appeared.
2
State the position of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf holds that the judge may rule on the evidence immediately and does not need to wait for the defendant's return. His basis: the defendant already had his opportunity to respond; absence after evidence is akin to default.
This view prioritizes efficiency and finality.
3
State the position of Muhammad al-Shaybani
Muhammad insists that the defendant must be brought back (or his presence secured) before judgment. His rationale: judgment in absentia may lead to injustice; the defendant's right to cross-examine or present a defense is lost.
This view emphasizes procedural fairness.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Yusuf, the judge can close the case quickly; under Muhammad, the case must be postponed until the defendant is located or returns.
This difference affects court administration and the rights of the plaintiff.
5
Note the status within the Hanafi school
Both views are considered valid transmissions (riwayatan) from the school. Many later Hanafi works prefer Abu Yusuf's view for its practicality, but Muhammad's view is often chosen for its caution.
The mufta bihi may vary by region and contextual consideration.

According to Abu Yusuf, the judge may proceed with judgment based on the evidence and does not need to wait for the defendant's return. His reasoning likely rests on the idea that the defendant had an opportunity to respond, and his absence after evidence is effectively a refusal to defend. Muhammad, however, insists that the defendant must be present (or brought back) for judgment, because the right to defense requires him to be present; judgment in absentia may lead to injustice. The text notes that this is recorded in the work 'al-Yanabi''. The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) in the Hanafi school is generally Abu Yusuf's view, especially when the defendant has already appeared and then fled, but Muhammad's view is considered more precautionary and is sometimes preferred for its justice. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that in courts following the Hanafi school, a judge may either wait for the defendant or proceed if he has left after evidence was given, depending on which ruling is adopted.

Encouraging Reconciliation Before Judgment
Recommends judge to encourage parties to settle before issuing judgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a judge encouraging reconciliation before judgment and explain the twice limit.

Core Idea
A judge should recommend reconciliation only when hopeful, and at most twice, to avoid delaying justice.

This lesson examines a specific adab (etiquette) for qadis: encouraging litigants to settle out of court. The source text from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah gives a balanced approach—reconciliation is good, but the judge must not delay proceedings excessively. Let's see the ruling, its proof, and the practical limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The act of the judge encouraging reconciliation. What is the hukm level? Mustahab (recommended), not wajib.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is from the saying of Umar ibn al-Khattab: 'رددوا الخصومَ كي يصطلحوا، فإن فَصْلَ القضاء يُورِثُ الضغائن' (Send back the litigants so that they may reconcile, for the passing of judgment begets grudges).
This is a Qawl Sahabi, which in Hanafi usul is a valid proof but zanni in transmission and indication.
3
Note the limit
The judge should not return them more than twice (ولا ينبغي أن يردهم أكثر من مرتين). Excessive delays can lead to injustice.
This limit is a directive to balance reconciliation with timely justice.
4
Reflect on the wisdom
Why is reconciliation encouraged? Because judgment often creates grudges. But why limit it? To protect the rights of litigants to a decision.
This shows the Hanafi concern for both social harmony and procedural justice.

Imprisonment of the Wealthy Debtor: Conditions and Proof
Explains when a judge may imprison a debtor based on how the debt is proven

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a Hanafi judge may imprison a wealthy debtor and distinguish the ruling when the debt is proven by acknowledgment versus evidence.

Core Idea
A debtor who can pay but delays is imprisoned only after refusal, with immediate imprisonment if denial is shown by evidence.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on imprisoning a debtor who refuses to pay while having the means. The passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies that imprisonment is not automatic; it depends on how the debt is proven and whether procrastination (mumāṭalah) is apparent. Understanding this distinction prevents injustice against debtors who may not be deliberately delaying.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of debt that qualify for imprisonment
List the debts for which the judge may imprison: debts that substitute for wealth received (price of sale, loan) or contractual obligations (dower, guarantee).
These debts establish the debtor's ability to pay because wealth came into his possession.
2
Determine the condition of procrastination (mumāṭalah)
The passage states that mumāṭalah must be apparent. Understand that this means the debtor's willful delay must be evident.
Mumāṭalah is a key condition for imprisonment when the debt is proven by acknowledgment.
3
Distinguish between proof by acknowledgment (iqrār) and evidence (bayyinah)
If the debtor acknowledges the debt, imprisonment is not immediate; the judge must first demand payment, and only upon refusal (showing mumāṭalah) may he imprison. If proven by evidence, imprisonment is immediate because the denial itself shows clear delay.
This distinction is based on the likelihood of mumāṭalah.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a case where a debtor admits owing money but asks for more time, the judge cannot imprison until after the debtor refuses to pay upon demand.
Contrast with a case where witnesses prove the debt and the debtor denies having received anything; the judge imprisons immediately.

The Judge's Encouragement of Reconciliation (Sulh)
Explains the judge's role in encouraging reconciliation before judgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a judge postponing judgment to encourage sulh, and identify the evidence from Umar's report and the limit of twice.

Core Idea
A judge may send litigants back up to twice to reconcile, as decisive judgment breeds grudges.

This lesson focuses on the judicial etiquette of encouraging reconciliation (sulh) before passing judgment. The passage from Al-Hidayah advises that if the judge has hope that the parties may settle, he may postpone judgment and send them back to negotiate, up to two times. The evidence is the reported statement of Umar (ra) that decisive judgment breeds enmity. This reflects the Islamic preference for amicable settlement over adversarial litigation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for postponing judgment
The judge must have hope (tama'a) that the litigants may reconcile. If there is no hope, he should not delay.
The hope must be reasonable; otherwise, postponement causes unnecessary delay.
2
Note the permissible actions: sending back and informing
The judge may send the parties back to negotiate, and he may also inform them that sulh is better. He does not impose a settlement.
The judge's role is facilitator, not mediator with authority to decide the terms.
3
Analyze the evidence: the report of Umar
The passage quotes Umar (ra) saying 'Send the litigants back so that they may reconcile, for the decisiveness of judgment breeds grudges.' This is a proof (dalil) for the permissibility of postponement.
In Hanafi usul, qawl al-sahabi (statement of a Companion) is a proof if not opposed.
4
Apply the limit of twice
The judge should not send them back more than two times. After that, if no settlement, he must proceed with judgment.
This limit prevents abuse and ensures timely justice.

Core Idea
A judge's letter is only accepted after rigorous verification, and never for hudud or qisas due to the risk of forgery (shubha).

Written testimony between judges is a powerful tool, but it carries risks of forgery. The Hanafi school imposes strict conditions to ensure authenticity. 1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the receiving judge's acceptance of a letter from another judge. The ruling is that acceptance is only valid if strict conditions are met; otherwise, the letter is not opened or enforced.

The Judge's Prohibition from Delegating Without Authorization
Stating the ruling that a judge cannot delegate without permission and proving it by analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the Hanafi ruling on a judge's delegation of judicial authority and apply the qiyas analogy to agency.

Core Idea
A judge cannot delegate unless authorized, just as an agent cannot sub-delegate.

This passage from the Jawhara al-Nayyira establishes a key principle of judicial delegation. While a judge holds significant authority, his power to appoint substitutes is restricted. The commentator uses analogy to agency law to explain the prohibition and its exceptions. We'll walk through the ruling, its proof, and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that a judge may not appoint a deputy over the judiciary unless that authority is specifically entrusted to him. This is the core ruling.
2
Locate the proof — qiyas to agency
The proof is analogy (qiyas) to an agent (wakil). Just as an agent cannot sub-delegate without permission, a judge cannot delegate judicial authority without authorization.
The illa (effective cause) is that both are representatives acting for another.
3
Trace the illa — being a representative
The judge is in the position of a deputy (na'ib) of the imam or the Muslim community. Since he is a representative, delegating without permission would be delegating his own delegation, which is not inherent in his appointment.
The illa is extracted using tanqih al-manat: the common element is representation.
4
Note the exceptions
Two exceptions: (1) if the deputy judges in the presence of the original judge (implying tacit approval), or (2) if the original judge ratifies the judgment after the fact, the delegation is valid.
These mirror exceptions in agency law.
5
Note the rule on continuity of office
Judges are not removed by the death of the prince or caliph; they are representatives of the Muslim community, which continues to exist. This reinforces their role as agents of the community.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Division of Slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on the divisibility of slaves and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Slaves cannot be divided without mutual consent because human differences prevent equitable partition—a Hanafi exception to general property rules.

The general rule in Hanafi fiqh is that jointly owned property of a similar kind can be divided by a judge even without the consent of the partners. However, slaves present a unique case because of the significant individual differences among humans. This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute where Abu Hanifa holds that slaves cannot be divided without mutual consent, while his two illustrious students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), argue that they can. We will trace the reasoning behind each position and understand which view is relied upon in the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions and the core issue
State clearly what Abu Hanifa rules and what the Sahibayn rule concerning the division of jointly owned slaves. The issue is whether a judge can force partition without the owners' mutual consent.
The dispute is within the Hanafi school itself, so both positions are from the same madhhab.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that slaves are not like other animals because human individuals vary greatly in value. Therefore, forced division would lead to inequity. Find where the text contrasts humans with other animals.
The key distinction: male and female humans are considered two species, whereas in animals they are one species.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn analogize slaves to livestock (camels, sheep) and to war booty slaves (raqiq al-maghanim). They argue that since the species is uniform, division is permissible.
They rely on the principle of 'uniformity of species' (ittihad al-jins) to argue for divisibility.
4
Examine the Hanafi rebuttal and distinction
The commentator distinguishes war booty slaves from jointly owned slaves: in war booty, ownership is over the monetary value (maliyyah), not the physical object (ayn). Hence, division of value is possible. But in joint ownership, ownership is of the actual slaves, so individual differences matter.
This rebuttal is crucial: it shows why the analogy to war booty does not hold.
5

The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa: slaves are not divided without mutual consent. Abu Bakr al-Razi's gloss confirms that the exception in al-Asl (where slaves are divided as subsidiary) is conditional on consent.
The practical effect: a judge cannot force partition of slaves; partners must agree.

In Hanafi fiqh, the court may order division of joint property among heirs or co-owners, but only when certain conditions are met. Core Idea
Each house in jointly-owned city property is divided separately, like different species cannot be mixed.

This lesson addresses a practical question in property law: how should a qadi divide multiple houses jointly owned by the same partners when all houses are in the same city? Abu Hanifa provides a clear rule that every house is treated independently, based on the principle that different houses are akin to different species (ajnas). Understanding this ruling helps ensure equitable division without mixing unlike properties.

According to Abu Hanifa, each house must be divided by itself (qusimat kullu darin 'ala hidatiha). This means the partners cannot pool all houses together and divide them as one unit; each house is a separate division unit.

The reason given is that different houses are like different species (al-jins). In Hanafi law, when dividing jointly owned property of different species, each species must be divided separately to avoid injustice. The word 'ajnas' indicates categories; each house is considered a separate jins.
4

If partners own two houses of different values, they cannot simply divide the total value and allocate shares arbitrarily. Instead, they must divide each house separately, probably by lot or mutual agreement, ensuring each partner receives a portion of each house or compensates for differences.
This prevents one partner from being forced to take a share in a house they may not want.

قوله : ( وزوجته فيء) ؛ لأنها كافرة حربية، لا تتبعه في الإسلام. قوله : ( وحملها فيء) ؛ لأنه ما دام متصلاً بأمه: فهو كعضو منها ؛ بدليل أنه يتبعها في البيع والعتق والتدبير والكتابة، فقلنا هو رقيق مسلم تبعاً للأب في الإسلام، ورقيق في الحكم تبعاً للأم، والمسلم قد يكون محلاً للتمليك تبعاً لغيره، بخلاف المنفصل: فإنه حر؛ لانعدام الجزئية. قوله : (وأولاده الكبار فيء؛ لأنهم كفار حربيون، ولا تبعية بينهم وبينه؛ لأنهم على حكم أنفسهم.
ومن قاتل من عبيده : فيء ؛ لأنه لما تمرد على مولاه: خرج من يده، وصار تبعاً لأهل الحرب.
And his wife is fay', and her unborn child is fay', and his adult children are fay'. His statement: (And his wife is fay') because she is a disbelieving Harbiyyah, she does not follow him in Islam. His statement: (And her unborn child is fay') because as long as it is attached to its mother, it is like a part of her; as evidenced by the fact that it follows her in sale, emancipation, tadbir, and kitabah. So we say: it is a Muslim slave as a dependent of the father in Islam, and a slave in ruling as a dependent of the mother, and a Muslim may be subject to ownership as a dependent of others, unlike the separate child: it is free due to the absence of partiality. His statement: (And his adult children are fay') because they are Harbi disbelievers, and there is no dependency between them and him; because they are subject to their own judgment. And whoever among his slaves fights: he is fay' because when he rebels against his master, he leaves his hand and becomes a dependent of the people of war.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on the convert from dar al-harb. The commentator explains the rulings on the convert's family. The wife of the convert remains a Harbi disbeliever and does not follow him in Islam; therefore, she becomes fay' (spoils) upon conquest. Similarly, the unborn child attached to her is considered fay'. The commentator justifies this by analogy to sale and emancipation, where the unborn child follows the mother. Interestingly, the child is considered a Muslim slave by dependency on the father's Islam, but a slave in ruling by dependency on the mother. The adult children of the convert are also fay' because they are independent Harbi disbelievers with no dependency on the father. Additionally, if any slave of the convert fights against Muslims, that slave becomes fay' because the act of fighting shows rebellion, removing him from the master's hand and making him a dependent of the warring faction.

Key Terms
حَمْل (haml): Unborn child in the womb; considered legally attached to the mother.
تَبَعِيَّة (taba'iyyah): Legal dependency; adult children of the convert have no legal dependency on him.
فَيْء (fay'): Spoils taken from non-Muslims without active warfare, or property that comes into Muslim possession through conquest.
