Swearing by Other Than Allah: Ruling and Exception
States ruling and exception for swearing by other than Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on swearing by other than Allah and identify the exceptional case of tabarri that constitutes a valid oath.

Core Idea
Swearing by a created being is never an oath; only disavowal of the Prophet or Quran counts.

In the Hanafi school, an oath is only valid when taken by Allah or His attributes. Swearing by the Prophet, the Quran, or the Kaaba does not create a legally binding oath. However, if someone expresses disavowal (tabarri) from the Prophet or the Quran, that statement is treated as an oath because it implies disbelief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
Read the first part of the passage: swearing by other than Allah is not an oath. Note that this includes the Prophet, Quran, and Kaaba.
The ruling is categorical: any created entity is excluded.
2
Recognise the Exception
The passage states that if someone says 'I am free from' the Prophet or Quran, they are considered an oath-taker. This is the only exception.
Tabarri (disavowal) is the key.
3
Understand the Reason
The reason given is that disavowing from the Prophet or Quran is an act of disbelief (kufr). Such a serious statement carries legal weight even if the form is unconventional.
This exception prevents a loophole for avoiding the consequences of blasphemy.
4
Apply the Rule
Given a statement, determine if it falls under the general rule or the exception. For non-tabarri statements, no oath. For tabarri statements, it is an oath.
Practice with examples.

Core Idea
The ba is the most general oath particle; the ta is restricted solely to the name 'Allah'.

Oaths in Islamic law are initiated using specific Arabic particles. The Hanafi school recognizes exactly three: waw, ba, and ta. Their scopes differ, and mastering these distinctions is essential for correctly understanding and formulating valid oaths.

Omitting the Particle of Oath
Rulings when the particle of oath is omitted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three vowel scenarios for omitted oath particles and apply the Hanafi ruling to determine whether an oath is formed.

Core Idea
The vowel on 'Allah' determines the oath—kasra or nasb binds, sukun does not.

In everyday speech, a person might omit the particle of oath, saying simply 'Allah, I will not do that.' Does this constitute a valid oath? The Hanafi school provides a precise grammatical test: the final vowel of the word reveals the intention and determines legal validity. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly identifying when a binding oath has been made.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is uttering a phrase intending an oath but without the explicit particle (wa, bi, ta). The ruling is that under certain conditions, it constitutes a valid oath (yamin).
The ruling depends on the inflection of the last letter.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verses from Surah Taha (20:71) and Surah al-A'raf (7:123) show that 'la-hu' and 'bi-hi' are used interchangeably, proving that the lam can substitute for the ba.
This supports the validity of saying 'Allah' (without particle) as an oath.
3
Distinguish the three vowel scenarios
If the last letter has sukun (no vowel), it is not an oath. If it has kasra, it is an oath. If it has nasb, there is disagreement, but the correct Hanafi view is that it is an oath.
The vowel acts as a sign of the omitted particle.
4
Understand the proof for lam substitution
The Quran uses 'la' (meaning 'to him') in place of 'bi' (meaning 'in him'), showing equivalence. Thus, the lam in 'Allah' stands for the omitted ba.
This proof is taken from the Arabic of the Quran.
5
Note the practical consequence
The mufta bihi (operative) position is that omitting the particle with kasra or nasb creates a binding oath; with sukun, no oath is formed.
This affects legal accountability, e.g., in court or for kaffarah.

Swearing by 'Haqqillah': Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on 'Wa haqqillah' as an oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on 'Wa haqqillah' as an oath and explain the reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's view.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, swearing by 'Haqqillah' is not an oath because it refers to obligations, not to Allah directly.

This passage addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: does saying 'Wa haqqillah' constitute a valid oath? Understanding this distinction is critical when a person makes such an utterance and then breaks it—does expiation become obligatory? The passage presents the positions of the leading Hanafi imams, helping you navigate this ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
State the issue and the main Hanafi position
What is being disputed? What is Abu Hanifa's ruling? Note that he says it is not an oath, and Muhammad agrees.
2
Identify the reasoning for the majority view
How does Abu Hanifa justify that it is not an oath? The term 'haqq' refers to obligations (acts of worship), not directly to Allah.
3
Identify the opposing positions
What do Abu Yusuf and Ibn Abi Mulayt argue? Abu Yusuf says it is an oath because 'haqq' is an attribute of Allah; Ibn Abi Mulayt says 'al-haqq' is a name of Allah based on Quranic evidence.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
What is the practical difference? If someone says 'Wa haqqillah' and breaks it, under Abu Hanifa no expiation is needed; under Abu Yusuf expiation is required.
5

Which view is relied upon? The matn gives Abu Hanifa's view as the default, and it is supported by Muhammad, so it is the operative position.

Core Idea
Intention determines whether mouth-rinsing is tasting or purification.

This lesson examines a subtle juristic distinction: when someone swears not to taste water, does a wudu mouth-rinse break the oath? The Hanafi school looks to the intention behind the act. We will see how the purpose of purification removes the act from the category of tasting, preserving the inviolability of the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Oath
Recognize the oath: 'I will not taste water.' The subsequent action is rinsing the mouth (madmadah) for wudu. The question is whether this constitutes tasting.
Oaths are interpreted according to common usage (urf).
2
Define 'Tasting' (Dhawq)
In legal context, dhawq means intentionally perceiving flavor. It is a deliberate act to know the taste, not incidental contact.
Hanafis consider intention (niyyah) essential for legal classification.
3
Determine the Purpose of Mouth-Rinse
The madmadah in wudu is an act of purification (taharah), done to follow the sunnah or fulfill the wajib. The purpose is not to taste but to cleanse.
Wudu mouth-rinse is a recommended sunnah, but its purpose remains purification.
4
Apply the Rule: Acts Judged by Intentions
Since the intention is purification and not tasting, the act does not fall under the oath. The person does not break the oath.
The maxim 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by intentions) governs here.

Conditions for Permissibility Under Duress: Carrion and Wine
States conditions for permissibility of eating haram under duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the specific condition under which consuming carrion or wine under duress becomes permissible in Hanafi fiqh, and identify which forms of duress are insufficient.

Core Idea
Only fear of death or limb loss permits carrion or wine; imprisonment or beating alone does not.

This passage from the Hanafi commentary Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah clarifies a critical distinction: not all forms of duress make haram consumption lawful. While common sense might suggest any threat should suffice, the Hanafi school requires a specific level of severity—fear for life or limb. Let's examine the exact ruling and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Recognize that eating carrion (maytah) and drinking wine (khamr) are fundamentally haram. The passage begins by listing means of duress that do not override this prohibition.
2
List the insufficient means of duress
Note the three means mentioned: imprisonment (habs), shackles (qayd), and beating (darb). These alone do not permit the prohibited act.
This is a key limitation: not all coercion is equal.
3
Identify the sufficient condition
The text states that only when the person fears for his life or the loss of a limb does the prohibition lift. This is the threshold for permissibility.
The Arabic uses 'yakhāfu minhu ʿalā nafsihi aw ʿalā ʿuḍwin min aʿḍāʾihī'.
4
Note the extension to other prohibited items
The commentary extends the same ruling to drinking blood or eating pork. The condition remains the same: only extreme fear permits.
5
Understand the liability ruling for carrion
If the person consumes carrion under duress, he is not liable to pay for it; it is considered a trust (amānah) because his possession was involuntary.
This protects the compelled person from financial responsibility.

A fasiq's word is trusted in business but not in religion—where only the just are accepted.

In Hanafi fiqh, the moral integrity of a witness matters differently depending on the context. While a person known for sin (fasiq) can be believed in financial transactions to facilitate daily dealings, the same person's word is not accepted in religious matters that affect worship, such as reports of impurity or moon sighting. 1
Identify the two domains
Recognize that the passage divides interactions into mu'amalat (transactions) and akhbar al-diyanat (religious news). The ruling for a fasiq's statement differs in each.
Mu'amalat include agencies, partnerships, and trade permissions. Diyanat include reports about purity, moon sighting, etc.
2
Understand the default ruling
In mu'amalat, the statement of a fasiq is accepted. In diyanat, only the statement of an adil (just person) is accepted.
The text specifies that even a slave or slave girl can be accepted if they are just.
3
Apply the rule of taharri
When a fasiq reports religious news (e.g., water impurity), one must investigate (taharri) and act based on predominant opinion.

Commentary
This passage expands on the rule that the statement of a fasiq (an open sinner) is accepted in mu'amalat (transactions like agency, partnership, and trading permissions) because such matters require ease and transactions are frequent. Ruling statements on acceptance of reports in religious matters

Core Idea
A just person's report is accepted; a fasiq's requires assessment, with precautionary tayammum after wudu.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It establishes the general principle that in matters of religion (diyanat), such as reports of water purity, the testimony of a just ('adl) person is accepted regardless of gender or legal status. If the informant is fasiq (sinful), one must engage in taharri (assessment of likelihood). If the informant is likely truthful, tayammum is done; if likely false, wudu is performed. The precautionary method combines both: wudu followed by tayammum. This ruling is from the Hanafi school, emphasizing the evidentiary weight of 'adalah.

Key Terms

تخري (taharri): Making a judgment based on predominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann) when the truth is uncertain.
الديانات (al-diyanat): Matters pertaining to religious obligations and rituals, as opposed to worldly transactions.
