Core Idea
Testimony by handwriting is not permissible unless memory or certainty is established, and delegation requires specific wording.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses two connected issues: the proper method for delegating testimony and the validity of testifying based solely on recognizing one's handwriting. While the matn gives the general rule, the commentary expands on the reasoning and presents a significant intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other.

The Testimony of a Blind Person: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Tahammul and Ada'
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on blind person's testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the testimony of a blind person and explain the three intra-Hanafi positions with their proofs.

Core Idea
A blind person's testimony is not accepted in any case — the majority Hanafi position denies both tahammul and ada' for the blind.

The testimony of a blind person presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute. While Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that blindness prevents both witnessing (tahammul) and testifying (ada'), Abu Yusuf distinguishes between the two, and Zafar carves out an exception for matters known through widespread reports (istifada). This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage discusses two aspects of a blind person's testimony: (1) the witness was sighted when witnessing but became blind before testifying; (2) the witness testified while sighted but became blind before the judge's ruling.
These scenarios test the conditions for tahammul and ada'.
2
State the majority and Abu Yusuf's positions on scenario 1
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say the testimony is invalid. Abu Yusuf says it is valid, analogizing to testimony about a deceased or absent person.
This is the core of the ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze the majority's dalil
The majority argues that blindness prevents tahammul by analogy with insanity, and since ada' is more stringent, it also prevents ada'. They also note that tahammul can be valid when ada' is not (e.g., sinner, slave, child), so prevention of tahammul implies prevention of ada'.
This uses qiyas jaliy and a rule about stringency.
4
Examine Abu Yusuf's opposing proof
Abu Yusuf argues that only the sight of the testifier is missing at delivery, which is not required; the tahammul is valid, so ada' follows, like testifying about an absent person.
He distinguishes the missing element as relevant only to the testifier's presence.
5
Understand scenario 2 and Zafar's exception
Scenario 2: Even if testimony is delivered while sighted, subsequent blindness before judgment invalidates the ruling because witnesses must remain eligible. Zafar adds an exception for istifada (widespread reports) like lineage and marriage, as the blind can gain knowledge through such reports. The mufta bihi rejects this exception.
Zafar's view is not the relied-upon position.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the testimony of a person who has been subjected to the hadd punishment for qadhf (false accusation of adultery). The passage opens by stating the Hanafi ruling: the testimony of such a person is not accepted, even if he repents. The proof is Quran 24:4, which commands never to accept their testimony. The rationale is that the rejection of testimony is part of completing the hadd punishment; unlike flogging for other offenses, where rejection is based on fisq (sinfulness) which is removed by repentance. The Shafi'i school disagrees, citing Quran 24:5 ('Except those who repent'), arguing that the exception includes the qadhf accuser. The Hanafi response is that the exception refers specifically to fisq (the sin of false accusation), not to the hadd itself. The hadd creates a permanent disqualification that repentance does not remove. The passage then discusses partial flogging, where the relied-upon (zahir al-riwaya) view is that testimony remains valid until all lashes are completed. Thereafter, various scenarios are addressed: a disbeliever flogged then accepting Islam (testimony accepted because a new capacity arises), a slave flogged then emancipated (testimony permanently rejected because he had no capacity originally), and mixed scenarios where part of the flogging occurs in disbelief and part in Islam (with three narrations, the relied-upon being that testimony is not invalidated unless the full hadd is completed in Islam). This passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of combining textual proof (Quran) with legal reasoning (ta'lil) and acknowledges internal differences (riwayat) within the school.

Testimony is rejected where strongest suspicion of bias exists — your wealth, your family, your spouse, your slave.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah presents a set of clear rulings on whose testimony is not accepted. The common thread is the Hanafi principle of tuhma (suspicion of bias). Each ruling is supported by a specific proof. The rulings cover parent-child (both directions), spouse-spouse, master-slave, and master-mukatab. Understanding these exceptions helps you apply the broader principle that testimony must come from a neutral party.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn with commentary explaining why certain testimonies are rejected. The underlying principle is that testimony is rejected when there is a strong suspicion (tuhma) of bias due to a connection of benefit or harm. The rulings are: (1) A parent cannot testify for his child or grandchild because the child's property is considered to belong to the father according to the hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father'; thus testifying for the child is like testifying for himself. However, a parent may testify against his child because the suspicion of partiality is absent. (2) A child cannot testify for his parents or grandparents because the benefits between them are interconnected, similar to why zakat cannot be given to them. (3) Spouses cannot testify for each other because of the typical connection of benefits. (4) A master cannot testify for his slave because the slave's property ultimately belongs to the master (if no debts), so it is testifying for himself; if the slave has debts, it is partially for himself. (5) Similarly, a master cannot testify for his mukatab (slave under contract of emancipation) because he remains under the master's ownership as per the hadith 'The mukatab is a slave as long as a dirham remains unpaid.' These rules aim to preserve the integrity of testimony by excluding those with obvious conflicts of interest.

According to Abu Hanifa, there must be agreement in both wording and meaning in matters of property and divorce. Therefore, if one witness testifies to 1,000 and the other to 2,000, their testimony is not accepted because they differ. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that it is accepted for the lesser amount (1,000) because it is included in the greater. The text then explains further scenarios: if one testifies to 1,000 and another to 1,500, and the claimant claims 1,500, the testimony is accepted for 1,000 by consensus (among Hanafi scholars) because 1,500 is two separate phrases and they agree on the 1,000. Analogously, in divorce cases, if witnesses testify to different numbers of divorces, the number agreed upon by all witnesses is established, but with conditions regarding consummation. The text references 'al-Nihayah' for elaboration. The teacher should note that Abu Hanifa's stricter requirement aims to avoid confusion and ensure precision, while the Sahibayn focus on the common element. The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) in the Hanafi school is often that of the Sahibayn in these matters, as it is more practical.

Conflicting Testimonies: Acts vs. Speech
Ruling on conflicting testimonies about acts vs. speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conflicting testimonies about a physical act and those about speech (iqrar), applying the Hanafi rule to determine admissibility.

Core Idea
A physical act cannot be repeated; conflicting testimonies about it are rejected. Speech can be repeated; conflicting testimonies about a confession are accepted.

The Hanafi school applies a logical distinction to conflicting testimonies: if the subject of testimony is a singular physical event, two contradictory accounts cannot both be true, so both are rejected. However, if the witnesses testify about a repeated speech act—like a confession—multiple consistent statements are possible, so the testimony is accepted. This principle, rooted in the nature of acts versus words, also governs cases like sale and marriage.

The key is the subject of testimony: act (fi'l) vs speech (qawl).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between testimonies concerning a single act and those concerning a statement, and apply the Hanafi rule that contradictory testimonies about an act are invalid (bāṭil) while those about a repeated statement are accepted.

Core Idea
Contradictory testimonies about an act are invalid because an act cannot occur twice; but testimonies about a statement are valid because statements can be repeated.

In this passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, the author explains a key principle of evidence law: when witnesses contradict each other about a physical act like a killing, their testimonies are rejected because only one can be true and the act cannot be repeated. However, if the contradiction concerns a statement (such as a confession), both testimonies can be accepted because statements can be made repeatedly. This lesson will guide you through the rationale and its application, including an important exception for marriage contracts.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the acceptance of testimony on negative conditions (الشهادة على النفي). The example: a man declares that if his slave does not enter the house today, the slave will be free. Two witnesses testify that the slave did not enter. This testimony is accepted and the slave is freed, because the condition of non-entry is a matter that can be known by observation and is subject to judicial cognizance (يدخل تحت القضاء). However, if the condition is about performing Hajj and the testimony is that the man sacrificed in Kufa (indicating he did not go for Hajj), then according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two, عندهما) the slave is not freed. Why? Because the testimony is based on negation (the fact that he sacrificed implies he did not perform Hajj), but the act of sacrificing is not a matter that directly falls under judicial cognizance. In contrast, Muhammad (al-Shaybani) holds that the slave is freed, because the testimony concerns a known fact (the sacrifice) from which the negation is inferred. The difference hinges on whether the fact testified to (the sacrifice) is sufficient to establish the negation in a judicial context.

Key Terms
الشهادة على الشروط في النفي (al-shahāda 'alā al-shurūṭ fī al-nafy): Testimony concerning conditions that involve negation, i.e., proving that a condition did not occur.

Conflicting Testimony on Witness Integrity
Ruling on contradictory witness character evaluations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conflicting testimony regarding a witness's character and apply it to determine whether a witness's testimony is accepted or rejected.

Core Idea
Two impeachments outweigh any number of certifications – once conclusive, integrity cannot be restored.

In Hanafi court procedure, the character of a witness can be challenged. When one person says the witness is upright and another says he is corrupt, the judge must seek a third opinion. This lesson explains the decisive rule: if two people have impeached the witness, his testimony is forever rejected, no matter how many later vouch for him.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting testimonies
The passage begins with one certification (tazkiya) and one impeachment (jarh). The judge does not decide immediately but asks a third person about the witness's character.
The judge must actively seek clarification.
2
Apply the outcome when the third certifies
If the third person certifies the witness's integrity, the judge accepts the witness's testimony. This shows that a single impeachment can be overcome by a single certification when there is no second impeachment.
3
Recognize the threshold for conclusive impeachment
If two people have already impeached the witness, then even if a thousand later certify his integrity, the judge does not accept his testimony. The two impeachments create a permanent bar.
The number two is the key threshold.
4
State the principle
The underlying principle: two impeachments outweigh any number of certifications. This gives finality to the impeachment process and prevents endless character evidence.
This is a default rule, not discretionary.

Hearsay Testimony: The Five Exceptions
Ruling with proof by istihsan on hearsay testimony exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule prohibiting hearsay testimony, list the five Hanafi exceptions, and explain the role of istihsan and the intra-madhhab differences regarding wala' and waqf.

Core Idea
Hearsay testimony is generally prohibited, but Hanafi law permits it for five matters by istihsan due to necessity.

In Islamic legal procedure, testimony must generally be based on direct observation. However, Hanafi jurists recognized that for certain matters, requiring eyewitnesses would cause undue hardship. This lesson examines the five exceptions to the hearsay rule, the condition of trustworthiness in the informant, and the scholarly differences within the Hanafi school on extending these exceptions to wala' and waqf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule and Its Basis
Locate the statement that a witness may not testify to something he did not directly witness. Understand that this is the default rule, rooted in the need for certainty in testimony.
The rule applies to all matters unless an exception is established.
2
List the Five Exceptions and Their Condition
Find the five matters excepted: lineage, death, marriage, marital relations, and appointment of a judge. Note the condition: the witness must have been informed by someone he trusts.
These matters are often known through reliable hearsay and are difficult to observe directly.
3
Trace the Dalil: Istihsan
The text explicitly states 'This is istihsan.' Understand that the exception is based on juristic preference, not strict analogy, due to necessity and common practice.
Istihsan is a Hanafi proof that allows departure from a general rule when compelling reasons exist.
4
Analyze the Intra-Hanafi Disagreements and Additional Condition
Note that the author restricted hearsay to the five matters, but Abu Yusuf allowed it for wala' (by analogy to lineage) and Muhammad allowed it for waqf (due to its enduring nature). Also, al-Marghinani added that for waqf testimony, the specific purpose must be stated.
These differences show how later jurists refined the exceptions.

Secondary Testimony: Conditions and Application
Explains permissibility and conditions of secondary testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid secondary testimony and identify which rights it covers, including the relaxation for death and the exception for marriage and lineage.

Core Idea
Secondary testimony is accepted for rights not nullified by doubt, with strict conditions on informants and wording.

Secondary testimony (al-shahada 'ala al-shahada) allows a witness to testify on behalf of another witness's testimony. This lesson explains the general rule, the required number and trustworthiness of informants, the necessity of using explicit testimony wording, and a special relaxation in cases of death. Understanding these conditions is crucial for determining when such testimony is valid in court.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling and its scope
The passage states that secondary testimony is permissible (ja'iz) for every right that is not forfeited by doubt (shubha). Note that hudud and qisas are excluded because they are nullified by doubt.
Shubha here means a doubt that leads to forfeiture of the right.
2
Extract the conditions for informants
The informants must be two just men or one man and two women, from among those the primary witness trusts and whose truthfulness he is convinced of. This ensures reliability.
3
Note the wording requirement
The information must be conveyed using the wording of testimony (لفظ الشهادة), e.g., 'I testify that so-and-so testified.' This is a condition mentioned by al-Khassaf.
4
Understand the relaxation for death
In cases of death, a single informant (man or woman) suffices because it is rare for more than one person to witness the death. This relaxation does not apply to marriage or lineage.
The reason is practical difficulty in finding multiple witnesses.
5
Recognize the prohibition on qualifying testimony
The witness should not specify that his testimony is based on hearsay. If he says 'I testify by hearsay,' his testimony is rejected because it undermines certainty.
The judge requires the testimony to be presented as direct knowledge.
