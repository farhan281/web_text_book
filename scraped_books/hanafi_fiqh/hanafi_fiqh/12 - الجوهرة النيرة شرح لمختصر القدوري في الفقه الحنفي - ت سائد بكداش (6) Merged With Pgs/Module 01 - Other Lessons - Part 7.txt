Radhkh: Discretionary Grants to Non-Combatants
States ruling on discretionary grants to non-combatants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which non-combatants may receive a discretionary grant (radhkh) from the spoils of war and distinguish it from a full share (sahm).

Core Idea
Non-combatants get radhkh, not sahm — only if they contribute meaningfully.

Spoils of war are primarily for free adult male Muslim combatants. But what about others who help? Hanafi fiqh introduces radhkh — a discretionary grant less than a full share — for slaves, women, children, and dhimmis who provide genuine assistance. This lesson unpacks the conditions for radhkh and when it is withheld.

Guided Path
1
Identify who is excluded from full shares
List the categories: slave, woman, child, dhimmi, insane person, mukatab. Understand the reason: lack of full capacity or control by master.
2
Define radhkh and its discretionary nature
Radhkh is a grant given at the imam's discretion, less than a full share, to encourage contribution.
The imam sets the amount; it is not wajib.
3
Determine conditions for receiving radhkh
For a slave: must fight. For a woman: must treat wounded or tend sick. For a dhimmi: must fight or guide. Mere service without benefit gives no radhkh.
4
Contrast radhkh with the horse sale example
If a fighter sells his horse after battle starts, the horse's share is forfeited; buying a new one restores it. This shows entitlement is tied to actual participation at distribution time.

Commentary
This passage addresses the distribution of spoils of war (ghanima) to individuals who are not full combatants in the Hanafi school. The general principle is that full shares in the spoils are reserved for free adult male Muslims who actively participate in the battle. However, the imam may give a discretionary grant (radhkh) to others such as slaves, women, children, the insane, and dhimmis if they provide some benefit to the Muslim forces, like fighting, treating wounded, guiding, or other helpful acts. The grant does not reach the level of a full share because these individuals lack full capacity or status. The mukatab (slave contracted for manumission) is treated like a slave in this rule. This shows the principle that entitlement is based on actual contribution at the time of distribution.

رضخ (radhkh): A discretionary grant from the spoils given by the imam to non-combatants who assist in the war effort, less than a full share.
سهم (sahm): A full share of the spoils allotted to a combatant (horseman or foot soldier).
مملوك (mamluk): A slave who belongs to a master; not entitled to a full share because the master can prevent participation.
ذمي (dhimmi): A non-Muslim subject of the Islamic state under protection; not trusted for combat but may receive radhkh for auxiliary services.

Division of the Khums: Three Shares with Priority for the Prophet's Poor Relatives
Explains the three shares and priority of Prophet's relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi division of khums into three shares, identify the condition of poverty for recipients, and explain why the poor among the Prophet's relatives are given priority.

Core Idea
The khums is divided into three shares for the needy, with the Prophet's poor relatives given priority due to Quranic order.

The khums (one-fifth of spoils) is not a private fund for the Prophet's family. Instead, it is distributed to three categories of needy people: orphans, the poor, and wayfarers. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains that the poor among the Prophet's relatives (Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib) are included in these categories and are given precedence over others, based on the order in Quran 8:41.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three shares
The passage states that khums is divided into three shares: one for orphans (sahm li'l-yatama), one for the poor (sahm li'l-masakin), and one for wayfarers (sahm li'abna' al-sabil).
Each share is conditional on poverty or need.
2
Note the condition of poverty
The commentary specifies that poverty is a condition for the orphan share (yushtarat fihim al-faqr) and that the wayfarer is defined as someone cut off from his wealth. Rich relatives receive nothing.
This ruling ensures khums goes to those in genuine need.
3
Understand inclusion of Prophet's relatives
The poor among the Prophet's relatives are included in these three categories: their orphans are counted with orphans, their poor with the poor, and their wayfarers with wayfarers.
They are not given a separate share but are subsumed under existing ones.
4
Recognize priority of relatives
The text explicitly says they are given priority (yuqaddamun) over the three categories because Allah mentioned them first in the verse.
Priority means they receive first from their respective share.
5
Connect to the Quranic proof
The author cites Quran 8:41 (wa li dhi al-qurba...) to show the order of recipients, which proves the priority of relatives.
The proof is dalil naqli, with qat'i thubut but zanni dalala regarding the exact division.

Commentary
This passage explains the division of the khums (one-fifth of the spoils) according to the Hanafi school. The khums is not divided into five shares as some might think, but into three categories: orphans, the poor, and wayfarers. The text explicitly states that poverty is a condition for receiving from the orphan share, and wayfarers are those stranded away from their wealth. Importantly, the poor among the Prophet's relatives (Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib) are included in these three categories: their orphans are included with orphans, their poor with the poor, and their wayfarers with wayfarers. Moreover, these relatives are given priority over other recipients because Allah mentions them first in the verse (Quran 8:41). The text references al-Mustasfa as a source. It concludes that wealthy relatives receive nothing because entitlement is based on poverty and need. This ruling clarifies that the khums is not a private fund for the Prophet's family but is distributed to the needy with preference given to the Prophet's poor relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on booty taken by one or two Muslims entering Dar al-Harb without the Imam's permission and distinguish it from ghanimah.

Core Idea
Booty taken individually without authorization is not ghanimah and incurs no khums.

This passage from Kitab al-Siyar addresses a critical distinction: not all property taken from enemy territory is war booty (ghanimah) subject to khums. The Hanafi school defines ghanimah strictly as property taken by overpowering force with official backing. When individuals act without the Imam's permission, their takings are akin to theft and do not trigger khums. The commentary also explores an internal Hanafi disagreement when permission is granted.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Siyar (the book of warfare) opens by stating a ruling about one or two Muslims entering Dar al-Harb (the abode of war) as raiders (مغيرين) without the permission of the Imam. If they take any property, it is not subject to khums (the one-fifth tax on war booty). The commentator explains that this is because such property does not qualify as ghanimah (war booty), which is defined as property taken by force and overwhelming power (قهراً وغلبة). In contrast, what is taken by stealth (اختلاساً) or theft (سرقة) is not ghanimah.

The text then includes a lengthy hadith about the Prophet's distribution of spoils among his relatives. The Prophet gave to Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib but not to Banu Nawfal and Banu Abd Shams, explaining that Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib had always supported him (نصرة). The commentator draws the principle that entitlement in distribution is based on support (نصرة), not mere kinship.

Returning to the main ruling, the commentator adds that if one or two enter with the Imam's permission, there are two narrations (روايتان). The well-known (مشهور) position is that khums applies, with the rest going to the captor. The second narration holds that it is not khumsed because it is still like larceny (تلصص). The commentator favors the first narration, arguing that the Imam's permission implies backing (بظهرة), making the taking akin to ghanimah.

تخميس (takhmis): The action of taking one-fifth (khums) of ghanimah for the public treasury.
اختلاس (ikhtilas): Taking property by stealth or snatching, without open force.

Khums for a Group with Strength Entering Dar al-Harb
Ruling on booty based on man'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on khums for a Muslim group entering Dar al-Harb and identify the condition (man'a) that determines whether khums is due.

Core Idea
Booty becomes ghanimah only if the group has force (man'a); otherwise, it remains mubah and no khums.

This lesson clarifies a crucial distinction in Hanafi siyar: not all booty taken from enemy territory is subject to khums. The determining factor is whether the group possesses man'a—strength and the ability to overpower. The text from Al-Quduri, with its commentary, presents two clearly opposing scenarios to illustrate this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) a group with man'a enters and takes something; (2) a group without man'a enters without permission and takes something. Note the ruling for each.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For the group with man'a: khums is wajib even without Imam's permission. For the group without man'a: no khums, each keeps what he personally took.
The ruling is binary: khums or no khums.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The commentary explains the 'illah (effective cause): man'a makes the taking an act of force (qahr), so it is considered ghanimah. Without man'a, the takers are like thieves—they act secretly—so no ghanimah, and the booty is permitted (mubah) to the individual taker.
The analogy in the text: 'like hunting and grass' (ibahah asliah).
4
Identify the condition for khums
The presence of man'a is the decisive condition (shart) for booty to be classified as ghanimah and thus subject to khums. The Imam's permission is not a condition.
5
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Consider a small group of Muslims who sneak into enemy land, take goods, and return unnoticed. According to the rule, they lack man'a, so no khums is due; each keeps what he personally took.
This scenario matches the second case.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi school regarding a Muslim who is granted safe conduct (aman) by a warring party and then betrays them by taking their property. The passage introduces the unique Hanafi concept of milk mahzur (prohibited ownership), which distinguishes between valid transfer of title and lawful use. Identify the act and its ruling

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When a musta'min dies or is captured, his deposit becomes fay', but his debts drop because the debtor's hand prevails.

In the law of war and aman, a musta'min who returns to dar al-harb is still under certain protections. However, if he is captured or killed, his property and obligations are settled according to specific rules. This ruling addresses two items: a deposit left in trust and any debts he owes. The rationale hinges on constructive possession and the priority of the debtor's hand over the public claim.

The deposit becomes fay' because it is considered to be in the musta'min's constructive possession; the depositor's hand is like his hand. Therefore, it follows his fate and becomes part of the fay' upon his death or capture.

The debtor is not required to pay the state.
4

These consequences apply only upon capture or death, not merely upon the musta'min's return to dar al-harb without capture.
The timing is crucial.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, explaining the chapter on 'Ushr and Kharaj. It begins by stating that dead land revived using water from rivers dug by non-Arabs (like the royal canals of the Sassanid kings) becomes kharajiyah land, subject to the kharaj tax. The author then discusses the kharaj set by Caliph Umar on the fertile lands of Sawad (Iraq) for each jarib (a measure of area equivalent to 60x60 cubits) that receives irrigation: one qafiz hashimi (a measure of grain such as wheat or barley, approximately three Iraqi ratls or four amnad) and one dirham (a silver coin weighing 14 qirats). The commentary clarifies that kharaj is of two types: (1) muqata'ah (fixed amount per land unit, regardless of cultivation) and (2) muqasamah (proportional share of actual produce, like 'ushr). The muqasamah is tied to output, not cultivability, so if the land is left fallow despite ability, no tax is due. The fixed kharaj (muqata'ah) should not exceed half or be less than one-fifth of the land's capacity. The reason is varying expenses: vineyards are least costly, grain most costly, and fodder in between. Thus, the tax rate reflects the crop's maintenance burden. This passage demonstrates the Hanafi school's systematic approach to land taxation, deriving from the precedent of Umar and rational analysis (qiyas) of costs.

Exemptions from Jizya: Women, Children, Disabled, and Poor Non-Workers
Listing exemptions from jizya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the categories of non-Muslims exempt from jizya in the Hanafi school and explain the reasoning that jizya is in lieu of fighting.

Core Idea
Jizya is a substitute for fighting; only those capable of fighting are liable.

Jizya is a poll tax imposed on certain non-Muslims in an Islamic state. The Hanafi school limits its imposition to free, adult, sane males who are capable of military service. This lesson examines the explicit exemptions listed in the text: women, children, the chronically ill, the blind, the poor non-earner, reclusive monks, and various categories of slaves. The key principle is that jizya serves as a substitute for fighting (badal al-qital), so those who are not fit to fight are not liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exempt categories
List all persons explicitly exempted: women, children, the chronically ill (zamin), the blind, the poor non-earner (faqir ghayr mu'timil), reclusive monks, slaves, mukatabs, mudabbirs, and umm walads. Note that Arab idol worshippers and apostates are not exempt but are not subject to jizya at all—they are given only the choice of Islam or the sword.
The exemption for monks is conditional on their inability to work.
2
Understand the rationale: jizya as badal al-qital
The text explicitly states: 'because jizya is imposed in lieu of fighting or killing, and these two are not killed nor do they fight due to lack of capacity.' This is the foundational logic. Women and children lack the capacity for combat, so they are exempt. The same reasoning extends to the chronically ill and blind—they are not of 'ahl al-qital' (people of fighting).
This is a qiyas jali: analogy to women and children.
3
Note the definition of mu'timil
A mu'timil is someone capable of earning the jizya amount by any means, even without a specific craft. The text from al-Hidayah adds that the mu'timil must be healthy for most of the year. This is the condition for a poor person to be liable: if they cannot earn, they are exempt.
The poor non-earner (faqir ghayr mu'timil) is explicitly exempt.
4
Apply to monks and slaves
Monks who do not mix with people are exempt only if they are unable to work; if they can work, they must pay. Slaves, mukatabs, etc., are exempt because they are owned and their master does not pay for them.
The text provides a caution: monks who waste their earning ability are likened to someone who neglects kharaj land.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on jizya for disabled wealthy

Core Idea
Being fit for fighting, not wealth, determines jizya liability.

The text presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on whether a chronically ill or blind person who is wealthy must pay jizya. Abu Yusuf holds that they should pay if wealthy because they could be killed in certain circumstances if they have sound opinion. The mufta bihi is the exemption.

The practical difference: a wealthy blind or chronically ill dhimmi, according to Abu Yusuf, must pay jizya; according to the majority, does not. This affects collection and justice.
The tarjih is on the side of the majority.

Jizyah Tadakhul and Dropping upon Islam or Death
Ruling on jizyah accumulation and dropping

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on jizyah accumulation and explain why Abu Hanifa applies tadakhul using qiyas to hudud.

Core Idea
Jizyah drops upon Islam or death because it is a punishment, not a debt.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah explains a key Hanafi rule: jizyah does not accumulate over years and drops upon conversion or death. The reason hinges on whether jizyah is a punishment ('uqubah) or a financial right (haqq mal). This classification affects how the law treats overlapping obligations and the consequences for the dhimmi's estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
Note that Abu Hanifa, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), and al-Shafi'i each have a different view on jizyah accumulation and dropping.
Abu Hanifa: tadakhul and dropping. Sahibayn: accumulation but still drop at year-end. Shafi'i: neither accumulation nor dropping.
2
Understand the legal basis
Each view is based on the nature of jizyah. Abu Hanifa classifies it as a punishment ('uqubah) for disbelief; the Sahibayn see it as a financial right (haqq mal); al-Shafi'i treats it as a debt.
The classification determines the application of tadakhul (overlap) and whether the obligation survives conversion or death.
3
Analyze the qiyas arguments
Abu Hanifa draws qiyas to hudud: like punishments, jizyah does not accumulate. The Sahibayn compare jizyah to debts, kharaj, and rent, which do accumulate. Identify the 'illa (effective cause) in each analogy.
For Abu Hanifa: the 'illa is 'being a punishment', leading to tadakhul. For Sahibayn: the 'illa is 'being a financial right', leading to accumulation.
4
Note the points of agreement
All three Hanafi imams agree that if the dhimmi dies at the end of the year, jizyah is not taken from his estate. This shows a core Hanafi principle that jizyah does not become a debt on the estate.
This agreement tempers the intra-madhhab difference.
5
Determine the thamarah (practical consequence)
The practical outcome: under the mufta bihi (Abu Hanifa's view), a dhimmi who becomes Muslim owes no past jizyah. Under Shafi'i, he would still owe it. Similarly, the estate is free of jizyah in the Hanafi school but liable in the Shafi'i school.
This difference matters in real cases of conversion or death.

Thus, if heir was a slave at apostasy, later freed, still no inheritance.
3

They say ownership ends only at actual death, so check eligibility at death. If the heir was a slave at apostasy but free at death, he inherits.
This is consistent with their view that ownership remains after apostasy.
4
Examine the two narrations from Abu Hanifa
First narration (al-Hasan): heir must survive until death. Second (Abu Yusuf from Abu Hanifa): heir's entitlement fixes at apostasy and passes to his own heirs if he dies before the apostate.
These are sub-variants within Abu Hanifa's school.

Rulings on Rebels: Calling Back, Fighting, and Pursuit

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Rebels must be called back first; fighting is defensive until their group disperses; post-battle violence depends on whether they have a rallying group.

This lesson examines the Hanafi legal framework for armed rebellion (baghy). The Imam must first seek a peaceful resolution by addressing grievances. Fighting is only permitted defensively, and the treatment of wounded and fleeing rebels hinges on the existence of a fi'a (rallying group). We also explore an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether preemptive strikes are ever allowed.

باغٍ (bāghī): A rebel against the legitimate Muslim ruler; one who rejects the authority and fights.
خراج (kharāj): Land tax imposed on non-Muslim farmers in conquered territories.
عشر ('ushr): Tithe (10%) produce of land owned by Muslims, given as zakat.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint
According to the Hanafi school, why is rebel-collected kharaj always considered sufficient?
A) Because kharaj is the property of the state and rebels are the de facto government.
B) Because kharaj is the right of the fighting force, and rebels are fighters.
C) Because the Imam's consent is not required.
D) Because kharaj is for the poor and rebels are considered poor.

Key Takeaway
Rebel-collected kharaj is always valid; 'ushr is valid only if the rebels were poor and spent it correctly.
