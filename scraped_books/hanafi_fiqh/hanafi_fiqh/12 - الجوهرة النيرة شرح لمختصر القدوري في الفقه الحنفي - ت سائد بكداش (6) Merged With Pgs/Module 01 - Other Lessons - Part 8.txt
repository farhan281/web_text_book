Inheritance When a Rebel is Killed: The Dispute
Ruling on inheritance from a killed rebel with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad versus Abu Yusuf on whether a rebel killer inherits from his victim, and apply the ruling based on the rebel's admission.

Core Idea
A rebel inherits only if he claims truth; Abu Yusuf says he never inherits.

This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding inheritance when a rebel kills someone. The key issue is whether the rebel's own belief about the righteousness of his action affects his right to inherit. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad link inheritance to the claim of being upon truth, while Abu Yusuf holds that the rebel's transgression always bars inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage considers two situations: a just person kills a rebel, and a rebel kills a just person.
We focus on the latter for the dispute.
2
Understand the default ruling for just killer
When a just person kills a rebel who is his heir, he inherits from him. The reason: killing by right does not prevent inheritance.
This is uncontested.
3
Analyse the rebel killer's inheritance per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
If the rebel claims he was upon the truth at the time of killing and still is, he inherits. If he admits he was upon falsehood, he does not inherit.
The intention matters: genuine belief in truth makes the killing justified.
4
Note Abu Yusuf's dissenting view
Abu Yusuf holds that the rebel never inherits, regardless of his claim. His reasoning: the rebel's act is inherently transgressive (baghy), so it always bars inheritance.
This is an intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Analyze the discussion on makruh

Using Silk as a Pillow: Intra-Hanafi Dispute and Related Rulings
States the ruling on using silk as a pillow and related issues with ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on using silk as a pillow and explain the reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's permissive view and the Sahibayn's dislike.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa permits using silk as a pillow because it shows disrespect, while the Sahibayn prohibit it due to resemblance to tyrants.

This passage from 'Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah' addresses a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: is it permissible for a man to use silk as a pillow? The matn records Abu Hanifa's permissive view, while the Sahibayn consider it disliked. Understanding their reasoning reveals the method of istihsan and the concept of 'urf in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling per Abu Hanifa
The matn states 'لا بأس بتوسده عند أبي حنيفة' – there is no harm in using silk as a pillow. Note that this is the base ruling recorded.
The commentary expands that sitting, sleeping, and spreading are also permitted.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's ruling and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say 'يكره توسده' – it is disliked. Their proof: it is from the dress of tyrants and kings, and imitating them is haram, supported by Umar's warning.
The opposition is based on tashabbuh (resemblance).
3
Compare the reasoning: istikhfaf vs. tashabbuh
Abu Hanifa argues that using silk as a pillow shows disrespect (istikhfaf) to the silk, so it does not fall under the prohibition of wearing for adornment. The Sahibayn see it as wearing because it is still contact with the body.
This hinges on whether the act of using a pillow counts as 'wearing' (libs).
4
Relate to broader silk rules for men
The passage also mentions that men are prohibited from wearing colored garments (mu'asfar, muz'afar) and that women are allowed silk. Abu Hanifa allows a small silk patch (alam) on clothing up to three or four fingers.
These rulings are consistent with the general prohibition of silk adornment for men.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
A man may use a silk pillow according to Abu Hanifa, while according to the Sahibayn he should avoid it. The mufta bihi is often Abu Hanifa's view, but caution is recommended.
This difference matters in everyday household items.

Silk and gold are haram for men, halal for women; only a small patch (3-4 fingers) is allowed for men.

This lesson examines the classical Hanafi position on silk and gold attire. While these are outright prohibited for men based on explicit hadith, women are permitted. Using Silk as a Cushion – Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Resolves permissibility of using silk for cushion, bedding, and sitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi positions on using silk as a cushion and evaluate the evidence behind each position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa permits silk cushions; the Sahibayn deem them disliked—a dispute rooted in whether imitation of tyrants overrides prophetic practice.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a classic intra-Hanafi debate: is it permissible to use silk as a cushion? Abu Hanifa sees no harm, citing prophetic precedent. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider it disliked (karahah) because it resembles the luxury of tyrants. This lesson unpacks their proofs and the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the Hanafi positions
The act is using silk as a cushion (tawassud), including bedding and sitting. Two positions: Abu Hanifa says mubah (no harm); Sahibayn say makruh tanzihi (disliked).
The dispute is within the Hanafi school, not with other madhhabs.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's dalil
Abu Hanifa relies on the Prophet's action of sitting on a silk cushion (murfqa) and Anas's use at a wedding. This indicates permissibility.
Prophetic action is a strong proof (fi'l al-nabi) in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's dalil
They argue silk cushions resemble the dress of tyrants (jababira) and kings (akasira), and imitating them is haram. They cite Umar's warning: 'Beware of the dress of non-Arabs.'
Their proof is based on qiyas (analogy) and a general prohibition of tashabbuh.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the Prophet's explicit practice overrides the analogy of resemblance. Since the Prophet used it, it cannot be inherently prohibited.
This illustrates the principle that a specific action can restrict a general rule.
5
Note the curtain exception
Using silk as a curtain (sitr) is not disliked by consensus according to al-'Uyun, though al-Hidayah reports a difference. This shows the ruling is context-dependent.
Curtains do not involve direct contact or contempt, hence the consensus.

ولا يجوز للمملوك أن ينظر من سيدته إلا إلى ما يجوز للأجنبي النظر إليه منها. وكذلك المحبوب؛ لأنه يساحق وينزل. وكذا المخنث؛ لأنه رجل فاسق.
* قوله : ( ولا يجوز للمملوك أن ينظر من سيدته إلا إلى ما يجوز للأجنبي أن ينظر إليه منها)؛ لأنه فحل غير محرم ولا زوج، والشهوة متحققة؛ لجواز النكاح في الجملة.
It is not permissible for a male slave to look at his female owner except as a stranger is permitted to look at her. Similarly, the passive male because he is used for sodomy and ejaculates. And likewise the effeminate man because he is a sinful man. [Commentary:] His saying: 'It is not permissible for a male slave to look at his female owner except as a stranger is permitted to look at her' because he is a virile male who is neither a mahram nor a husband, and desire is confirmed due to the permissibility of marriage in general.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling on looking by certain males at women. The main ruling is that a male slave (mamluk) cannot look at his female owner (sayyidah) except in the same limited way a non-mahram male (ajnabi) can. The reason is that he is a virile male (fahl) who is neither a mahram nor a husband, and the possibility of marriage exists, so desire is presumed. Then it extends the same ruling to the 'mahbub' (a passive male who submits to sodomy) and the 'mukhanath' (effeminate man). The mahbub is considered worse because he engages in sodomy and emits semen, while the mukhanath is a sinful man. Both are treated as non-mahram males for the purpose of looking.

Key Terms
مملوك (mamluk): A male slave owned by a woman.
أجنبي (ajnabi): A man who is not a mahram relative nor a husband.
محبوب (mahbub): A passive male who actively submits to anal intercourse.
مخنث (mukhanath): An effeminate man who imitates women in speech and movement.
