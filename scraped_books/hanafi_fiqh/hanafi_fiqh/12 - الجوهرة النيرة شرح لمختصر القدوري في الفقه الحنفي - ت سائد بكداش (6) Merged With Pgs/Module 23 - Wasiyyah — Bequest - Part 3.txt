Bequests to and for a Fetus in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling with proof: validity of bequest to/for a fetus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid bequest to a fetus and for a bequest for a fetus, and apply the timing rule to determine validity.

Core Idea
A fetus can be a legatee as a representative, not through ownership, if born within six months of the will.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah explains two types of bequest involving a fetus: to the fetus and for the fetus. The matn from Quduri states both are valid if the child is born within less than six months from the day of the will. The commentary provides the legal reasoning, including the concept of istikhalf (representation) and the broad scope of wasiyyah. Understanding these conditions is crucial for correctly applying Hanafi inheritance law.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of bequest
Identify the two scenarios: (1) bequest to a fetus (making the fetus a legatee), and (2) bequest for a fetus (giving something to be held for the fetus, like 'what is in the womb of my slave-girl').
The Arabic terms are الوصية للحمل (to the fetus) and الوصية بالحمل (for the fetus).
2
Understand the justification for bequest to a fetus
The commentary argues that bequest is a form of representation (istikhalf); the testator makes the legatee a representative over part of his wealth. Since a fetus can represent in inheritance, it can represent in bequest. This is a qiyas: asl=inheritance, far'=bequest, illa=representation.
This reasoning distinguishes bequest from gift (hiba), which is pure ownership and requires capacity.
3
Identify the condition for bequest for a fetus
Bequest for a fetus requires knowledge that the fetus existed at the time of the will. This is determined by the timing of birth: if the mother gives birth within less than six months from the will, existence is confirmed.
The passage mentions two opinions: from time of death (by some) or from time of will (al-Tahawi and the matn).
4
Apply the timing exception for iddah
If the mother is in iddah (e.g., after divorce or death of husband), the period extends to two years because lineage can be established up to two years. This exception overrides the six-month rule.
This shows Hanafi flexibility in ties of lineage.
5
Resolve a dual bequest conflict
If one bequests the slave-girl to one person and her fetus to another, the bequest is valid unless the birth occurs at six months or more, in which case both go to the legatee of the slave-girl, because the fetus would not have been separate at the time of will.
This is a practical scenario testing the timing condition.

Ruling on Bequest of a Slave-Girl Except Her Pregnancy
Ruling with proof and conditions for bequest of unborn child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on bequest of a slave-girl with exception of her pregnancy and apply the timing conditions for bequest of an unborn child.

Core Idea
A bequest of a slave-girl is valid even when excepting her pregnancy, and bequest of an unborn child is valid if existence is known within six months.

This lesson from Kitab al-Wasaya of Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a nuanced ruling: a testator can bequeath a slave-girl while excluding her unborn child. The Hanafi school validates such exceptions and also allows bequest of the unborn child directly, but with strict conditions on timing to establish its existence. The passage also clarifies the effect of the wife's waiting period on these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the Hanafi position on the validity of bequeathing a slave-girl with the exception of her pregnancy. The text affirms both the bequest and the exception are valid.
The exception (istithnā') is valid because the pregnancy can be independently bequeathed.
2
Understand the Proof
The proof is the general principle 'Bāb al-waṣiyyah wāsi‘' (the chapter of bequest is broad) due to the deceased's need and incapacity. This allows bequest of non-existent items like fruit, so by analogy it applies to a future unborn child.
This is an example of istihsan (juristic preference) based on necessity.
3
Analyze the Condition for Bequest of an Unborn Child
The bequest of what is in the womb (slave-girl or animal) is valid only if its existence is known. Knowledge is established if the child is born within six months of the testator's death (or from the time of bequest according to al-Tahawi).
Birth after six months raises the possibility that conception occurred after the bequest, making it invalid.
4
Recognize the Exception for 'Iddah
If the slave-girl is in her waiting period ('iddah) from a previous marriage, lineage can extend up to two years. Consequently, the bequest of the unborn child is considered valid if born within two years from death/bequest.
This parallels the rule for establishing paternity and ensures fairness.
5
Apply the Separate Bequest Rule
If the testator bequeaths the slave-girl to one person and her fetus to another, both bequests are valid. However, if she gives birth at six months or more after death/bequest, both mother and child go to the legatee of the slave-girl, because the fetus is deemed to have been conceived after the bequest.
This prevents contradiction in ownership.

Bequest of a Slave-Girl and Her Offspring Born After Death
Bequest ruling with proof and sub-rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a child born after the testator's death follows its mother into a bequest as a dependent, provided the birth occurs before acceptance and the total is within the one-third limit.

Core Idea
The offspring follows the mother as a dependent (taba‘) in the bequest, even if born after death but before acceptance.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains that when a slave-girl is bequeathed and gives birth after the testator's death but before the legatee accepts, the child is included in the bequest. The Hanafi school relies on the principle of taba‘iyyah (dependency) and the analogy with inheritance (al-wasiyyah ukht al-mirath). Understanding this ruling clarifies how the school treats property that becomes attached to a bequeathed item before the legatee takes possession.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its conditions
Identify the act: bequest of a slave-girl who gives birth after the testator's death but before the legatee accepts. The ruling: if both mother and child are within the one-third limit, both go to the legatee. Note the conditions: (1) birth occurs after death, (2) before acceptance, (3) total value within one-third.
The acceptance (qubul) is necessary for the bequest to be effective.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The commentary provides two proofs: first, the analogy with inheritance – bequest is the sister of inheritance, and inheritance applies to the fetus. Second, the principle of dependency (taba‘iyyah): the child is attached to the mother and thus follows her ruling.
The name 'slave-girl' does not literally include the pregnancy, but the child is entitled by default as a dependent.
3
Trace the legal reasoning (wajh al-dalalah)
From the inheritance analogy: just as a fetus inherits because it is considered part of the estate at death, here the unborn child enters the bequest because it was part of the bequeathed item at death. From the dependency principle: the child is considered an appendage of the mother; if the mother is validly bequeathed, the child follows.
This is a form of qiyas: the asl is inheritance of the fetus, the far‘ is bequest of the unborn child.
4
Apply the sub-rulings (furu‘)
The text gives two sub-cases under 'al-asl': (1) if the raqabah (corporeal substance) is bequeathed to one and the fetus to another, and the latter dies, the fetus goes to his heirs – not back to the testator. (2) If the raqabah is bequeathed to one and the usufruct to another, on the death of the usufruct legatee, the usufruct returns to the owner of the raqabah. This shows that ownership of the raqabah is primary.
The distinction: the fetus is a part of the raqabah, so it can be separated; the usufruct is an attribute, so it returns.
5
Articulate the principle
The overarching principle: 'Whatever can be isolated in a contract can be excepted from it, and what cannot be isolated cannot be excepted.' Since a pregnancy can be bequeathed separately (as per Hanafi permissibility), it can be excepted from the mother, but if not excepted, it follows as a dependent.
This principle guides similar cases in sales and bequests.

Apportioning a Bequest of a Slave Girl and Her Child: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on bequeathing a slave girl and her posthumous offspring with intra-madhhab disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the apportionment of a bequest when the combined value of a slave girl and her newborn child exceeds one-third of the estate, and calculate the distribution under both views.

Core Idea
When a bequeathed slave girl and her newborn exceed one-third, Abu Hanifa prioritizes the mother; the Sahibayn proportion both.

Imagine a testator bequeaths a slave girl to a friend, but before the friend accepts, the slave gives birth. If the total value of mother and child is within one-third of the estate, both go to the legatee. But what if their combined value exceeds one-third? The Hanafi school splits on how to allocate the legatee's one-third share. This lesson unpacks that dispute, focusing on the reasoning (talil) behind each view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline scenario and default ruling
Understand the core facts: a slave girl is bequeathed, she gives birth after the testator's death but before the legatee accepts. If the total value of mother and child is within one-third of the estate, both belong to the legatee. This is uncontested.
The default ruling applies when there is no shortfall.
2
State the problem: when the combined value exceeds one-third
Now consider that the mother and child together are worth more than one-third. The legatee can only take up to one-third. The dispute is about how to fill that one-third: from the mother only, or proportionally from both?
The one-third limit is a fixed rule in Islamic inheritance.
3
Present Abu Hanifa's position and his talil
Abu Hanifa holds that the legatee first takes his one-third from the mother (the primary object of the bequest). Only if the mother's value is less than one-third does he take the remainder from the child. The reasoning: the child enters as a follow-up (taba'an), so the primary attachment is to the mother.
The child is a subsidiary; the mother is the principal.
4
Present the Sahibayn's position and their talil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the legatee takes his one-third proportionally from both the mother and the child. Their reasoning: the bequest encompasses both, and the child's inclusion is not merely a backup but integrated into the same bequest. So the one-third should be split between them.
The Sahibayn see the child as co-object of the bequest, not an afterthought.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the legatee might receive the entire mother (if she alone is worth one-third) and none of the child. Under the Sahibayn, he receives a fraction of both. Later Hanafi scholarship often prefers the Sahibayn's view for greater equity.
The text does not explicitly choose, but the Hidayah favors the Sahibayn.

Ruling on timing of legatee's death relative to testator

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for the share of a legatee who dies before the testator versus after the testator.

Core Idea
If a legatee dies before the testator, his share returns to the testator's heirs; if after, it goes to his own heirs.

The previous lesson covered the case where one legatee was dead at the time of the bequest. Here, the law addresses what happens when both legatees are alive at the bequest, but one dies before the testator. The ruling turns on the timing of death relative to the testator's death, which determines whether the right to the bequest has vested.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline scenario
Both legatees are alive at the time the testator makes the bequest. This establishes that both have a potential right to half of the one-third each.
2
Apply the rule when one dies before the testator
If one legatee dies before the testator, his share becomes void. It does not pass to the surviving legatee but reverts to the testator's estate, going to the testator's heirs.
The surviving legatee still gets only his original half of the one-third, not the whole.
3
Apply the rule when one dies after the testator
Once the testator dies, the bequest right vests. If a legatee dies after that, his share becomes part of his own estate and is inherited by his heirs.
The key moment is the testator's death – the right becomes fixed at that point.

Core Idea
Byna divides the bequest equally; a dead legatee's share does not pass to survivors.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in Hanafi inheritance law: what happens when a testator uses the word 'between' (بين) to allocate a bequest to two individuals, but one of them is already dead. The choice of wording has a decisive impact on how the share is distributed, distinguishing it from the more straightforward 'to' (لـ) formulation.

The ruling follows directly from the meaning of the word.
3

If the testator had said 'to Zaid and Amr' and Zaid is dead, the entire one-third goes to Amr because the bequest was undivided. The contrast highlights the effect of 'byna'.
This difference is crucial for drafting wills.

Given a new case with three names and one dead, what would the living receive? Use the same principle: 'byna' divides equally among all named, so the dead's share goes to his heirs, not to the other legatees.
The rule extends to any number of legatees.

Ruling with rational proof on bequest validity

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A bequest is a contract attached to after death — wealth at death, not at bequest, determines entitlement.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi fiqh ruling: a person makes a bequest of one-third of his wealth while he owns nothing at that moment. Later, he acquires wealth before dying. Is the bequest valid? The Hanafi answer is yes — and the legatee receives one-third of what the testator owns at death. The proof is rooted in the nature of a bequest as a contract that takes effect only after death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is making a bequest (wasiyyah). The ruling is that the bequest is valid even with no current property; the legatee gets one-third of wealth at death.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a rational argument: the bequest is a contract of succession (istikhlaf) attached to after death, so its ruling establishes after death.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The key phrase: ‘mudaf ila ma ba‘d al-mawt’ (added to after death). Because the bequest takes effect only after death, the condition for entitlement is existence of wealth at death, not at the time of bequest.
4
Note potential misapplication
A common error is to think the bequest is void because no property existed at the time. This mistake arises by confusing the condition for a bequest with that for a sale or gift inter vivos.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi
The relied-upon Hanafi position is as stated; no alternative is given in this text. This is the standard view in Zahir al-Riwayah.

Proportional Reduction of Bequests Exceeding One Third
Method for proportional reduction when total bequests exceed the one-third limit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi proportional reduction method to distribute bequests that exceed the one-third limit.

Core Idea
When total bequests exceed one third, each bequest is reduced proportionally by the shortfall fraction.

A testator may make multiple bequests that collectively exceed the one-third limit of the estate. According to the Hanafi school, such bequests are not void; instead, they are proportionally reduced to fit within the one-third. This lesson explains the simple arithmetic method for that reduction, using a clear example.

Guided Path
1
Identify total bequests and one-third limit
First, determine the total value of all bequests and the value of one-third of the testator's estate. These are the two key figures.
The one-third limit is calculated after debts and funeral expenses.
2
Compute the shortfall (nuqsān)
Subtract the one-third limit from the total bequests. The result is the shortfall – the amount by which the bequests exceed the limit.
Shortfall = Total bequests – One-third of estate
3
Express shortfall as a fraction of total bequests
Divide the shortfall by the total bequests. This fraction will be applied to reduce each bequest.
Fraction = Shortfall ÷ Total bequests
4
Reduce each bequest by that fraction
Multiply each individual bequest by the fraction from step 3. Subtract that product from the original bequest to get the reduced amount.
Reduced bequest = Original bequest × (1 – fraction)
5
Verify the sum of reduced bequests equals one-third
Add all reduced bequests; the total must equal the one-third limit. This confirms the calculation.
If the sum does not match, recheck the fraction and reductions.

Core Idea
When heirs consent, the legatee of one-third gets 1/6 under Abu Yusuf, or 1/4 under Hasan.

When a testator bequeaths all his property to one person and one-third to another, the total exceeds the allowable one-third without consent. If the heirs consent, there is no explicit ruling from Abu Hanifa, so later Hanafi jurists derived their methods via analogy. This lesson walks through the two competing analogies and identifies the mufta bihi (operative opinion).

Guided Path
1
Understand the default rule without consent
Without heir consent, only one-third of the estate can be used for bequests. Abu Hanifa's principle divides this one-third equally between the two legatees, giving each 1/6 of the total estate.
This sets the baseline for comparison.
2
Identify the gap when consent is given
With consent, the entire estate is available for bequests. No explicit text from Abu Hanifa exists, so jurists must use qiyas (analogy) to derive the distribution.
This is the ijtihad space.

Follow Abu Yusuf's analogy (mufta bihi)
Abu Yusuf argues: the legatee of one-third has no claim to two-thirds of the estate (his bequest is only one-third), so that portion goes entirely to the legatee of all. The remaining one-third is split equally. Result: legatee of all gets 2/3 + 1/6 = 5/6; legatee of one-third gets 1/6.
Uses the concept of 'concession' (taslim) for the uncontested portion.

Contrast with Hasan ibn Ziyad's objection
Hasan objects that consent should not reduce the legatee of one-third's share below his baseline without consent (which was 1/6). He argues for a 1/4 share for that legatee, giving the other 3/4. His method implicitly treats the one-third as unaffected by consent and then divides the rest.
Hasan's view is marjuh (weaker) but demonstrates an alternative heuristic.
5

The author states Abu Yusuf's view is correct, citing al-Yanabi'. Thus the operative ruling for fatwa is: legatee of all gets 5/6, legatee of one-third gets 1/6.

When heirs refuse, the third is split equally between both legatees.

Bequests With Heir Consent: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute and Calculation Methods
Intra-Hanafi dispute on calculation when heirs consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Yusuf, al-Hasan, and the derived view of Abu Hanifa on distributing bequests when heirs consent, and apply the methods of munaza'a and 'awl.

Core Idea
When heirs consent, the legatee of the whole and the legatee of the third compete; Abu Yusuf's 5:1 ratio is the mufta bihi.

When heirs consent, the full bequests take effect, and the two legatees have competing claims on the entire estate. Since Abu Hanifa left no explicit text on this scenario, later scholars derived his position by qiyas. This lesson examines the three main positions—Abu Yusuf, al-Hasan, and the derived Abu Hanifa—and the two distinct calculation methods: munaza'a (competition) and 'awl (proportional increase).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties and Their Claims
Legatee A is bequeathed the entire estate. Legatee B is bequeathed one-third. Heirs consent, so both bequests are fully valid. A claims the whole estate; B claims one-third.
Consent removes the one-third limitation.
2
State Abu Yusuf's View and His Reasoning
Abu Yusuf divides the estate into sixths. He argues that B has no claim on two-thirds (since his bequest is only one-third), so those two-thirds go to A. The remaining third is contested equally, so each gets half of that third. Result: A gets 5/6, B gets 1/6.
His method is called munaza'a (competition) because claimants only compete for the overlapping portion.
3
State al-Hasan's Criticism and Alternative
Al-Hasan argues that B's share with consent should be at least as much as without consent (1/6), but he gives B 1/4 (i.e., 3/12) and A 3/4 (9/12). His reasoning is that after dividing the third equally (2/12 each), the remaining two-thirds are split such that B gets an additional 1/12 to make his total 1/4.
Al-Hasan's calculation is more favorable to B but was deemed incorrect.
4
Understand the Derived Abu Hanifa View and the Two Methods
The text reconciles Abu Hanifa's view with Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's (Sahibayn) view. They all agree on a 3:1 ratio (A gets 3/4, B gets 1/4). However, the derivation differs: Abu Hanifa uses munaza'a (competition step by step), while the Sahibayn use 'awl (proportional increase of the denominator). Both lead to the same result.
The final mufta bihi is Abu Yusuf's 5/6 : 1/6 division, as per al-Yanabi'.

Resolving Competing Wills: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn on Method

Learning Objective

Expenses of a Slave Bequeathed for Service
Hanafi ruling on who bears maintenance of a slave subject to a bequest of service

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on who bears the maintenance, clothing, and other expenses of a slave whose services are bequeathed, and explain the legal rationale based on exclusive benefit.

Core Idea
The beneficiary of a slave's service bears all expenses, because he alone enjoys the benefit.

When a testator bequeaths the right to use a slave's labor, ownership of the slave remains with the estate, but the beneficiary gets the usufruct. A practical question arises: who must feed, clothe, and maintain the slave? This lesson presents the clear Hanafi ruling and its logical basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the responsible party
The passage states: 'If someone bequeaths the service of a slave, then his maintenance, clothing, and what is needed for his well-being are upon the one bequeathed the service.' Identify that the act is providing maintenance and the responsible party is the legatee of service.
The Arabic uses 'على' indicating obligation.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason given: 'because he is the sole beneficiary without the heirs, so he becomes in the ruling of the owner.' Since the legatee alone enjoys the benefit, he is treated like the owner for maintenance purposes.
This is a principle of Hanafi fiqh: expenses follow benefit.
3
Note the scope of expenses
The passage explicitly mentions 'maintenance, clothing, and what is needed for his well-being.' This includes food, clothing, medical care, and any other necessary expenses.
The ruling is comprehensive for all essential needs.
4
Contrast with other scenarios
If the slave were bequeathed outright (ownership), expenses would fall on the new owner. Here, only the service is bequeathed, so the legatee bears expenses only as long as he enjoys the service.
This distinction is crucial for correct application.
