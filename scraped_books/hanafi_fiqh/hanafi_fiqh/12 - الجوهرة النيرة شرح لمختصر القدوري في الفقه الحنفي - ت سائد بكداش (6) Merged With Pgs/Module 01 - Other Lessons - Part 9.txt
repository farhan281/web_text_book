Kissing Between Men and the Classification of Kisses
Declares kissing between men disliked and classifies kisses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on kissing between men and classify each type of kiss according to its legal status.

Core Idea
Kissing between men is disliked unless for blessing from a scholar or parent; all kisses are defined by intention.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on physical affection between men and the broader classification of kisses in Islamic law. While the default ruling is dislike (karaha) for kissing another man, exceptions exist for greeting scholars or pious persons. Al-Faqih Abu al-Layth provides a systematic typology of kisses, each with its own ruling based on the relationship and intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Read the first sentence: what is the general rule for a man kissing another man? Note that it applies to the mouth, hand, or any part.
2
Recognize the exceptions
What concession did some later Hanafi scholars allow? Who else considered it sunnah?
Al-Sarakhsi reports permission; Sufyan al-Thawri says it is sunnah.
3
List the five types of kisses
From Abu al-Layth's classification, list each type, the parties involved, and the location of the kiss.
4
Determine the legal status of each type
Based on the default ruling and exceptions, decide whether each type is permissible, disliked, or something else.
Note that the types themselves are not rulings; the ruling depends on the individuals (e.g., between men vs. spouses).
5
Understand the sixth type
What is the sixth type added by some scholars, and how does it differ from the others?
Kissing the Black Stone is an act of devotion in worship, not between persons.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on kissing between men. The base ruling is that it is disliked (makruh) for a man to kiss another man's mouth, hand, or any part of him. However, there is a concession from some later Hanafi scholars, as reported by al-Sarakhsi, permitting kissing the hand of a scholar or a pious person as a blessing. Sufyan al-Thawri even considered it a sunnah. Then al-Faqih Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi classifies kisses into five types: (1) greeting kiss on the hand, (2) mercy kiss of parents on child's cheek, (3) compassion kiss of child on parents' head, (4) affection kiss between brothers on the forehead, (5) desire kiss between spouses or with a slave-girl on the mouth. Some scholars add a sixth type: the kiss of devotion, namely kissing the Black Stone in Hajj.

Key Terms
قبلة تحية (qiblat tahiyya): A kiss of greeting, usually on the hand as a sign of respect.
متنورع (mutawari'): A person who is pious and abstains from worldly pleasures.
قبلة رحمة (qiblat rahma): A kiss of mercy, typically parents kissing their child on the cheek.
قبلة شفقة (qiblat shafaqa): A kiss of compassion, typically a child kissing their parents on the head.
قبلة مودة (qiblat mawadda): A kiss of affection, typically between siblings on the forehead.
قبلة شهوة (qiblat shahwa): A kiss of desire, between spouses or with one's slave-girl on the mouth.
قبلة ديانة (qiblat diyana): A kiss of devotion, specifically kissing the Black Stone as an act of worship.

Partial Consent of Heirs to an Excessive Bequest
Ruling on partial consent by heirs to a bequest exceeding the one-third limit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partial consent of heirs to an excessive bequest and apply the binary treatment calculation when some heirs consent and others reject.

Core Idea
Partial consent of heirs to an excessive bequest makes it valid against consenting heirs and void against dissenting ones.

When a testator bequeaths more than one-third of the estate, heirs have the right to consent or reject after death. But what happens when only some consent? This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains the Hanafi rule: treat each heir independently based on their decision. The result is a nuanced distribution that respects both consent and rejection simultaneously.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal problem
A testator leaves a bequest exceeding one-third, and heirs are divided: some consent, some reject. The issue is how to treat the bequest given partial consent.
2
State the general rule for partial consent
The bequest is valid against the consenting heir to the extent of that heir's share, and void with respect to the dissenting heir's share.
This treats the consenting heir as if all had consented, and the dissenting as if none had consented.
3
Work through the classic example
Two sons are heirs; the testator bequeaths half his estate. If both consent, the legatee takes half, sons share half. If neither consents, legatee gets one-third, sons two-thirds. If one consents and the other rejects, apply the binary treatment.
4
Apply the calculation method (base of 12)
To determine shares, the property is divided into 12 parts. The consenting son's share is 1/4 (3 parts), the dissenting son's share is 1/3 (4 parts), and the legatee receives the remainder (5 parts).
This reconciles the need for halves and thirds.
5
Note the timing of consent from al-Hidayah
Consent given during the testator's lifetime is not considered because the heirs' right to object vests only at death. Post-death consent is binding and irrevocable.
This clarifies why pre-death consent does not affect the ruling.

The Spouse's Fixed Share and the Validity of a Bequest Exceeding One-Third
Ruling on bequest exceeding one-third when only spouse is heir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a bequest exceeding one-third is valid when the only heir is a spouse, and state the Hanafi position on this exception.

Core Idea
When the only heir is a spouse, the surplus after the fixed share has no heir, so it can be bequeathed freely.

The general rule is that a bequest exceeding one-third of the estate is invalid without the consent of the heirs. This passage introduces an important exception: when the only heir is a spouse, who receives a fixed share that cannot be increased. The surplus beyond that share is considered the testator's own property, and can be bequeathed beyond the one-third limit. We will explore the rationale and the Hanafi ruling that follows from it.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Recall that a bequest is typically limited to one-third of the estate unless all heirs consent to a larger amount.
This is a well-known Hanafi principle.
2
Identify the exception for spouses
The commentary explains that the rule applies when there is an heir who is entitled to the entire estate. A spouse, however, only receives a fixed share (1/4 or 1/2) and does not inherit the residue. Thus, the residue has no claimant.
The spouse's share is fixed by the Quran and cannot be increased.
3
Understand the rationale
Since the spouse's share is fixed, any amount beyond that share is the testator's disposable property. The testator may bequeath it to whomever they wish, even if it exceeds one-third of the total estate.
The key is that the residue has no legal heir.
4
Apply to the given example
Imam Muhammad's example: a woman leaves only a husband and bequeaths half to a stranger. The bequest is valid because after the husband's fixed share, the remainder has no heir.
This leads to a specific calculation we will examine in the next lesson.

Ownership Transfer in a Permitted Bequest to an Heir
Ruling with proof via qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a bequest to an heir when the heir permits it, explain the analogy to mortgagee permission, and apply the principle to determine ownership transfer.

Core Idea
Heir's permission removes impediment; ownership transfers directly from testator, not heir.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a key Hanafi principle: when a bequest is made to an heir, it is initially suspended due to the heir's competing inheritance right. However, if the heir later grants permission, the bequest becomes valid and the donee acquires ownership directly from the testator. The author uses an analogy to a mortgagee permitting sale to illustrate that permission merely lifts a bar, not creates a new transfer. Understanding this principle helps distinguish Hanafi fiqh from other schools on similar issues.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognise that the act is a bequest (وصية) made to an heir, which is normally invalid in Hanafi law due to the heir's presumptive right. However, if the heir gives permission (إجازة) after the testator's death, the bequest becomes valid (جائز).
The text uses 'كُلُّ مَا جَازَ' indicating all such cases.
2
Understand the cause and the role of permission
The cause (سبب) of the transfer is the testator's will. The heir's permission does not create a new cause; it merely removes the legal impediment (مانع) that existed due to the heir's inheritance right. Thus, ownership vests in the donee from the testator directly.
The text says: 'لأن السبب صدر من الموصي، والإجازة رفع المانع'.
3
Analyse the qiyas (analogy) to the mortgagee
The author analogises to a mortgagee (مرتهن) permitting the sale of mortgaged property. In that case, permission does not transfer ownership from mortgagee; it allows the mortgagor's sale to proceed, with buyer acquiring ownership from mortgagor. Similarly, heir's permission allows the bequest to take effect from testator.
The analogy highlights that the interfering right is removed, not a new transfer created.
4
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Consider a testator who bequeaths a specific asset to his son (an heir). The other heirs (e.g., daughters) are not affected. After death, the son (the heir) permits the bequest. According to this passage, the son owns the asset directly from the testator, and possession (قبض) is not required for ownership to transfer.
This contrasts with the Shafi'i school where the bequest becomes part of the estate and ownership passes through inheritance then donation.

The reason is that the cause of the transfer (the testator's will) originated from the testator, and the heir's permission merely removes the legal impediment (the heir's right to the property). Importantly, possession (qabd) is not required for the transfer to occur; the donee owns the property immediately upon the heir's permission. The author draws an analogy (qiyas) to the case of a mortgagee: if the mortgagee permits the mortgagor to sell the mortgaged property, the sale is valid and the buyer acquires ownership from the mortgagor, not from the mortgagee. Similarly, the heir's permission is like lifting the restriction, not creating a new transfer.

رفع المانع (raf' al-mani'): Removing the legal impediment that prevents the original cause from taking full effect.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In Hanafi fiqh, a bequest (wasiyya) is not automatically owned by the legatee at the testator's death; acceptance is required for ownership to transfer. However, there is a single exception: if the legatee dies after the testator but before accepting, the property becomes part of the legatee's estate and passes to his heirs. This ruling is derived by analogy (qiyas) to a sale with a condition of option (khiyar al-shart). We will examine the rule, the exception, and the reasoning behind it.

The will is completed from the testator's side at his death (cannot be rescinded). It is only suspended for the legatee's right. When the legatee dies, the right vests in his heirs.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a common scenario in estate administration: a testator appoints two executors. The passage also presents the dissenting view of Abu Yusuf, who likens executorship to marriage guardianship. Understanding this rule and its limits is essential for anyone involved in Islamic estate management.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses the scenario where a testator appoints two executors. The default ruling according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad is that neither executor may act independently; they must act jointly. This is because the testator's delegation (tafwid) is to both collectively, and the condition of collective action is tied to the testator's consent, which was only given for the pair. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that executorship is a form of guardianship (wilaya) that is indivisible; thus each executor possesses full authority just as two brothers jointly have guardianship over a sister's marriage. The response of the majority is that the analogy to brothers is flawed: in marriage guardianship, the cause is kinship, which inherently gives each brother full authority, whereas in executorship, the cause is the testator's delegation, which is explicitly to both together. However, exceptions are made for urgent matters where delay would cause harm: purchasing the shroud and preparing the deceased (to avoid decay), providing food and clothing for minor children (to prevent hunger or exposure), and returning specific deposits and trusts (to avoid liability). Also included are returning stolen goods, goods purchased through a void sale, preserving the wealth, and paying debts. These actions do not require the consent of the co-executor because they are immediate necessities. The commentary highlights that the Hanafi school distinguishes between the general rule of joint action and the exceptions based on necessity and the principle of avoiding harm.

Bequest of All Wealth and One-Third: Dispute Between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on dividing the one-third between two legatees

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's method of dividing the one-third when two conflicting bequests exceed the limit, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Without heirs' consent, only one-third is available; Abu Hanifa equalizes, Sahibayn proportionally reduce.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about how to divide the available one-third of an estate when a testator bequeaths all wealth to one person and one-third to another, and the heirs do not consent. Abu Hanifa treats the excess bequest as void, so both legatees effectively claim one-third each, leading to equal division. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider the total claims as four-thirds, applying 'awl to distribute the one-third in proportion: three-quarters to the all-legatee and one-quarter to the other. Understanding the reasoning behind each view is key to mastering Hanafi inheritance of bequests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the problem
The testator makes two bequests: all wealth and one-third. Without consent, only one-third of the estate can be bequeathed. The problem is how to divide that one-third between the two legatees.
The key constraint is the one-third limit (without consent).
2
Understand the Sahibayn's 'awl method
They treat the bequest of all as a claim to the entire estate (three-thirds) plus the other's one-third, total four-thirds. They apply 'awl (proportional reduction) to the available one-third, dividing it into four shares: three for the all-legatee, one for the other legatee.
This is analogous to 'awl in inheritance when shares exceed the fund.
3
Understand Abu Hanifa's equalization
Abu Hanifa argues that the excess beyond one-third is invalid without consent, so the all-legatee's valid claim is only one-third, equal to the other's. Thus both claim one-third each, so the one-third is split equally (half each).
This view emphasizes that an invalid excess cannot create a larger claim.
4
Compare outcomes and determine tarjih
Under Sahibayn, the all-legatee gets 3/4 of the one-third; under Abu Hanifa, 1/2. The text presents Abu Hanifa's view as the baseline (especially when heirs consent, his method yields five-sixths for the all-legatee), indicating it is the mufta bihi (operative) position.
Tarjih: Abu Hanifa's equal division is relied upon when heirs do not consent.

Dividing the One-Third Between Legatees of Half and Quarter: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on dividing the one-third between legatees of half and quarter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's methods to calculate the distribution of the one-third when bequests exceed the limit.

Core Idea
When bequests exceed one-third, Abu Hanifa caps each claim at one-third; Sahibayn proportionally reduce using full bequest ratios.

When a testator bequeaths conflicting shares exceeding one-third, the heirs' consent determines the outcome. Without consent, only one-third of the estate is available for bequests. The Hanafi masters differ on how to divide that one-third between legatees claiming half and quarter. This lesson unpacks the methodological difference between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the two positions
Identify the bequests: half to one legatee, quarter to another. Note that with heirs' consent the full bequests are honored, without consent only one-third is available. Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn propose different divisions of that one-third.
The consent case is straightforward; the dispute arises only when consent is withheld.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the half-legatee cannot press for more than one-third because the bequest cannot exceed one-third of the estate. Thus the half-legatee's effective claim is one-third, while the quarter-legatee's claim remains one-quarter. The ratio is 1/3 : 1/4 = 4:3.
This reflects the principle that bequests exceeding one-third are void without consent.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that both legatees press with their full bequests (half and quarter), so the ratio is 1/2 : 1/4 = 2:1. They do not cap the half-legatee's claim at one-third but proportionally reduce both claims to fit the available one-third.
The Sahibayn treat the bequests as competing claims that are reduced pro rata.
4
Perform the calculation
Under Abu Hanifa: find a common denominator for 1/3 and 1/4 = 12, so shares are 4 and 3, total 7. Thus the one-third is divided into 7 parts: 4 for half-legatee, 3 for quarter-legatee. Under Sahibayn: ratio 2:1, so total 3 parts: 2 for half-legatee, 1 for quarter-legatee.
Remember that these shares are fractions of the one-third, not the whole estate.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the half-legatee receives 4/7 of the one-third (≈57.1%), while under Sahibayn he receives 2/3 (≈66.7%). The quarter-legatee receives correspondingly less under the Sahibayn view.
This difference matters in actual estate distribution when heirs do not consent.

Interpreting 'Share' in a Bequest: Definition and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Definition and ruling for 'sahm' in a will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'sahm' in a bequest and identify the different opinions among Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn regarding its limits.

Core Idea
A vague bequest of 'a share' means the lowest heir's share, but its maximum differs within the school.

When a testator simply says 'I bequeath a share', the law must fix its amount. The Hanafi school resolves this by defaulting to the smallest share an heir would receive, but with a floor of one-sixth and a ceiling that varies among the founding imams. This lesson unpacks the definition and the internal disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the vague term
Recognize that 'sahm' (سهم) is undefined in the bequest, so it must be interpreted.
This is not a fixed fraction but a relative term.
2
Apply the default rule
The default interpretation is the 'lowest share of the heirs' (أخس سهام الورثة). Determine the smallest fractional share any heir receives under normal inheritance rules.
This is the baseline for the legatee's entitlement.
3
Apply the floor and ceiling adjustments
If the lowest heir's share is less than one-sixth, complete it to one-sixth (first narration of Abu Hanifa). If it exceeds one-sixth, cap it at one-sixth (second narration). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad instead cap at one-third.
The different caps create practical differences in the legatee's final share.

Core Idea
A minor's will is invalid because capacity for gratuitous transfers is lacking.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that a minor's will (wasiyya) is invalid. The proof is an analogy (qiyas) to gift (hiba): if a minor cannot give a valid gift during health, then a will – which is a weaker transaction – is also invalid. Capacity is evaluated at the moment of the act (mubasharah).

A slave or mukatib (slave contracting freedom) can attach a will to after freedom. Their capacity is complete; the obstacle is the master's right, which falls at freedom.

Mukatib's Will: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Ruling with intra-Hanafi difference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi difference regarding the validity of a mukatib's will and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A mukatib's will is invalid according to Abu Hanifa because his wealth is not fit for gratuitous transfers.

In this lesson, we examine a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: whether a mukatib (a slave under contract of manumission) can make a valid will. The matn of Al-Quduri apparently rules that it is not valid, but the commentary in Al-Jawhara al-Nira reveals that this is the view of Imam Abu Hanifa, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold that it is valid. Understanding this ikhtilaf and which view is relied upon in fatwa is essential for applying the correct ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal subject
The ruling concerns the will of a mukatib, a slave under a kitaba contract to earn manumission by paying instalments.
The mukatib has some property rights, but they are restricted.
2
Understand the matn's claim
The base text states 'والمكاتب وإن ترك وفاء' implying that even if he leaves enough to pay his contract and have surplus, the will is not valid.
3
Unpack the commentator's reason
The commentator explains: 'because his wealth does not accept gratuitous transfers' – meaning the mukatib lacks full ownership to give gifts or bequests.
This is the reason given for the ruling.
4
Distinguish the two views
The commentary then states: 'according to Abu Hanifa, it is not valid; according to the two (the Sahibayn), it is valid.'
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The phrase 'wa qila' indicates the second view is a weaker transmission. The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's: the will is invalid.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa.

Revocation of Will and Exceptions
Ruling on the testator's right to revoke the will with exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on revoking a will, identify the proof based on analogy to gifts, and distinguish the exceptional binding acts that cannot be revoked.

Core Idea
A will is revocable like an incomplete gift; tadbir, immediate muhabah, and delivered gifts to mahram are binding.

This lesson clarifies the general rule that a testator may revoke his will at any time before death. The underlying principle is that a will is a gratuitous transfer not fully completed, so it remains revocable. However, certain acts that have become binding are exceptions and cannot be revoked, even if they relate to the will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
Read the first line: it is permissible for the testator to revoke the will. This is the default position.
2
Examine the proof (dalil)
The commentator explains that the will is like a gift (hibah) before delivery, which is revocable. This is a rational analogy (qiyas).
3
Note the exceptions
The text lists three acts that are binding and cannot be revoked: an immediate preferential sale (muhabah munajjazah), tadbir (declaration of freedom after death), and a gift that has been received by a mahram relative.
These are considered completed and binding, unlike the will.
4
Understand methods of revocation
Revocation can be explicit (verbal statement) or implicit (any action that indicates revocation).
5
Apply the ruling to new cases
Use the three exceptions as a checklist: if the testator performed any of these binding acts, they stand regardless of a later will.

Understanding the Bequest to In-Laws (Akhthan)
Definition and expansion of 'akhtan' for bequest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'khatan' in Hanafi law, identify who is included under 'akhtan', and state the condition regarding the marital bond for an in-law to receive a bequest.

Core Idea
A bequest to 'akhtan' benefits the husbands of mahram female relatives, not the wives themselves; the marriage must exist at death.

When a testator makes a bequest to his 'akhtan' (in-laws), the precise definition of who qualifies is crucial. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah clarifies that 'khatan' refers specifically to the husbands of female mahram relatives, and explains how the status of the marriage at the time of death affects eligibility. Understanding this definition prevents common errors in application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its definition
Locate the definition of 'khatan' in the text. It is defined as 'the husband of every female relative who is a mahram'. Note that the term is specific to husbands of women who are mahram (unmarriageable kin) to the testator.
Mahram relatives include daughter, sister, aunt, etc.
2
Distinguish who is included and excluded
The commentator expands that 'akhtan' includes all such husbands, making them 'as-har' (in-laws). Importantly, the wives themselves (testator's wife, son's wife, father's wife) are not included because the term 'as-har' applies to the families of the wives, not the wives themselves.
Exclude: wife, daughter-in-law, mother-in-law (the wife herself).
3
Note the condition of marriage bond continuity
The passage specifies two scenarios: if the testator dies while the woman is in a valid marriage or in the iddah of a revocable divorce, the in-law (husband) receives the bequest. If she is in an irrevocable divorce iddah, he does not, because the marriage bond is severed.
Revocable divorce (talaq raj'i) keeps the bond; irrevocable (talaq ba'in) ends it.
4
Recognize the custom variation
The commentary from Al-Hidayah notes a difference in custom: originally the term 'akhtan' included all in-laws (including those who are mahram by marriage), but in later custom it may be narrower, excluding some spouses. This teaches that terminology can shift over time and context.
This does not change the core ruling; it highlights the need to understand the testator's intention.

Bequest to Relatives: Scope and Distribution
Ruling on bequest to 'aqarib', excluding parents and children

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequests to 'aqarib', explain which relatives are included and excluded, and apply the rule of nearest mahram relatives with a minimum of two recipients.

Core Idea
A bequest to 'aqarib' goes to the nearest mahram relatives, excluding parents and children, and must include at least two beneficiaries.

A bequest to one's 'aqarib' is a common scenario where the testator leaves one-third of his wealth to his relatives. But who exactly qualifies as a 'relative'? The Hanafi school provides precise guidance: the term excludes parents and children, includes only mahram relatives, and distribution is made to the nearest in degree. Furthermore, the plural form of 'aqarib' requires at least two recipients. This lesson unpacks these rules and their linguistic foundations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and the Act
The passage rules on a bequest (wasiyyah) where the testator says 'one-third of my wealth to my relatives.' The ruling: the bequest is valid and must be distributed to the nearest mahram relatives.
2
Determine the Scope of 'Aqarib'
According to the Hanafi school, 'aqarib' (relatives) refers specifically to mahram (unmarriageable) relatives other than parents and children. The commentary provides linguistic justification: parents are the source of relationship and children are directly related, so the term does not include them.
Exclusion of parents and children is also supported by Quranic usage.
3
Apply the Priority Rule
The bequest is given 'to the nearest then nearest' (al-aqrab fa al-aqrab) from among the mahram relatives. Closeness is determined by the degree of relation in inheritance hierarchy.
If multiple are at the same degree, they share equally.
4
Understand the Plural Minimum
Because the testator used the plural 'aqarib', the Hanafi school requires at least two recipients. This is derived from the Quranic verse on brothers (4:11), where 'ikhwa' (brothers) is interpreted as two or more.
If only one mahram relative exists, the bequest may need to be distributed differently, but the passage assumes at least two.
5
Recognize Equal Treatment
All recipients within the same category are equal: free and slave, near and distant, rich and poor, male and female. No preference is given unless the testator specifies otherwise.
This reflects the general principle that the word 'aqarib' applies uniformly.

Bequest to Unspecified Relatives: The Hanafi Dispute
Explains the Hanafi dispute on distributing a bequest to relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the distribution of a bequest to unspecified relatives, state the six conditions of Abu Hanifa and the broader view of the Sahibayn, and apply each position to a given family scenario.

Core Idea
A bequest to 'my relative' is not the same as 'my relatives' — and the two words split the Hanafi school.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses a specific question: when a testator leaves a bequest to 'his relative' (li dhi qarabatihi) without specifying who, how should it be distributed? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement. Abu Hanifa sets six conditions, treating the bequest like inheritance, while his companions Abu Yusuf and Muhammad take a more inclusive approach. Understanding this dispute clarifies how wording and intent interact in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's Position
Identify the six conditions and the distribution rule: bequest goes to the closest relative(s) based on closeness, with a minimum of two if multiple recipients.
The key condition is al-aqrab fa-l-aqrab (closest then closer).
2
Identify the Sahibayn's View
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree on three conditions: they do not require plurality, mahram, or closeness. Instead, they include all relatives traceable to the farthest Muslim ancestor.
Their rule: everyone to the farthest ancestor shares equally in the one-third.
3
Follow the Reasoning of Each
Abu Hanifa relies on analogy to inheritance and the singular wording 'dhi qarabatihi'. The Sahibayn rely on the testator's presumed intent to benefit all relatives.
The text does not give a direct rebuttal from either side.
4
Note Points of Agreement
All three agree on three conditions: the beneficiary must be a relative, must not be an heir, and there must be no children among them.
These are from the first three conditions.
5
Apply the Thamarah al-Khilaf
In a scenario with a paternal uncle and many distant cousins, Abu Hanifa gives only to the uncle; the Sahibayn give to all including the cousins.
The practical difference is huge.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a bequest (wasiyya) made to unspecified relatives. The author first presents the view of Abu Hanifa, who treats the bequest as analogous to inheritance ("the bequest is the sister of inheritance"). Abu Hanifa stipulates six conditions: (1) the beneficiaries must be relatives (qarabah), (2) they must not be heirs (since a bequest to an heir is invalid), (3) there must be no children among them (to avoid conflict with Quranic inheritance rules), (4) the bequest must be to a group (jam'iyya) meaning at least two recipients, (5) the relatives must be mahram (non-marriageable), and (6) the distribution follows closeness (al-aqrab fa-l-aqrab). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) agree on the first three conditions but reject the last three. According to them, the bequest includes all relatives traceable to the testator's farthest Muslim ancestor, regardless of closeness, number, gender, or religion. They share equally in one-third of the estate (the maximum allowable bequest). The text gives examples: with two paternal and two maternal uncles, Abu Hanifa gives the entire bequest to paternal uncles (being closer); with one paternal and two maternal uncles, the paternal uncle gets half and maternal uncles half (since the bequest to a group implies at least two, so the paternal uncle cannot take all). If only one paternal uncle exists under Abu Hanifa's view, he gets half and the remainder returns to heirs. Under the Sahibayn's view, the same scenario would include all relatives to the farthest ancestor, distributing the third equally among them. The discussion clarifies that the wording "li dhi qarabatihi" (to his relative) is singular and according to Abu Hanifa refers to one closest relative, whereas "li dhawiy qarabatihi" would be plural. The Sahibayn interpret "relatives" broadly.

Fractional Bequest After Partial Destruction: Same vs Different Types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on fractional bequests when part of the estate is destroyed, distinguishing between fungible and non-fungible items.

Core Idea
After destruction, the legatee's fraction attaches to what remains, not the original. Different types are indivisible; same type are like dirhams.

A testator bequeaths 'one-third of my clothes' to someone. Later, two-thirds of the clothes are damaged or destroyed. Does the legatee receive the entire remaining third? No — the bequest is fractional, so he gets only one-third of what survives. But there is a nuance: what if the clothes are of different types? This lesson unpacks that nuance and reveals a key Hanafi principle about divisibility of bequeathed items.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the loss
Recognize that the testator made a fractional bequest (one-third) of a specific set of items (clothes). After the will, two-thirds of those items are destroyed. The issue is: to what does the one-third fraction apply?
2
Distinguish between same-type and different-type items
The Hanafi ruling hinges on whether the clothes are of one type (jins wāḥid) or different types (ajnās mukhtalifah). Same-type items are fungible and divisible by count/measure; different-type items are not divisible among themselves.
3
Apply the rule for different types
If the clothes are different (e.g., a robe, a cloak, a shirt), the bequest is 'one-third of each garment'. After destruction, the legatee gets one-third of each surviving item. He does not get the whole remaining cloak and shirt — only a third each.
Rationale: different items cannot be divided proportionally across the whole set, so the fraction applies per item.
4
Apply the rule for same type
If all clothes are the same type (e.g., all seersucker shirts), they are like dirhams: divisible by number. The bequest of one-third applies to the total surviving quantity, so the legatee gets one-third of the number of surviving shirts.
This is because fungible items can be divided without affecting their nature.
5
Derive the rationale from the text
The commentator explains: 'because if clothes are different, they cannot be divided among themselves, so the remaining cannot be claimed by the legatee through division, so the bequest was not attached to the remainder.' Understand that the attachment of the bequest is to each item individually when items are heterogeneous.
This is a rational (ʻaqlī) proof based on the nature of divisibility.

Distributing a Bequest When the Estate Has Cash and Debt
Ruling on bequest distribution with cash and debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for distributing a bequest when the estate includes cash and debts, and explain the rationale based on partnership analogy.

Core Idea
When a bequest exceeds one-third of cash, the legatee shares proportionally in cash and debts as a partner.

This lesson examines a specific scenario from Hanafi wasiyya (bequest) law: a deceased person bequeaths a fixed sum, but his estate consists of both liquid cash and debts owed to him. How should the legatee be paid? The Hanafi ruling ensures fairness by preventing the legatee from taking only cash, which would harm the heirs. Instead, the legatee is treated as a partner, receiving a proportional share from both cash and debts as they are collected. We will walk through the rule, its application, and the underlying legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Bequest and Estate Composition
First, note the amount of the bequest (e.g., 1000 dirhams) and the estate's assets: cash (like liquid money) and debts (receivables).
The total estate is the sum of cash and debts, but the bequest is limited to one-third of the overall estate.
2
Calculate One-Third of the Cash
Compute one-third of the cash (e.g., if cash = 900, one-third = 300). This is the maximum that can be paid from cash initially without exceeding the bequest limit.
The one-third rule is based on the general rule that bequests are executed from one-third of the estate after debts.
3
Check if Bequest Can Be Fully Paid from That One-Third
If the bequest amount is less than or equal to one-third of cash, pay it in full from cash. If it exceeds, go to the next step.
Example: bequest 1000, cash 3000 → one-third of cash = 1000, so pay full from cash.
4
Apply Proportional Distribution
If the bequest exceeds one-third of cash, give the legatee only one-third of the cash. Then, as each debt is collected, give him one-third of that collected amount until the total bequest is satisfied.
The legatee thus receives a total that is one-third of all collected assets (cash and debts), mirroring a partner's share.
5
Understand the Rationale: Partnership Analogy
The legatee is considered a partner with the heirs. To give him only cash would harm the heirs because cash is more beneficial (immediate). By sharing proportionally, both parties are treated equitably.
This is a clear application of qiyas (analogy) in Hanafi fiqh.

For the fetus to make a bequest, its existence at the time of the will must be confirmed. The condition is that it is born alive within six months of the will – the maximum period of gestation in Hanafi law for this purpose.
If the fetus is born after six months or stillborn, the bequest is invalid.
5

The passage explicitly says acceptance is not needed for a bequest. This is because wasiyya is a form of succession, not a contract requiring mutual consent, unlike hiba.
This further supports the analogy with inheritance, where the heir inherits without acceptance.

The Follower Does Not Compete: Bequest of a Slave Woman Who Gives Birth Before Division
Intra-Hanafi dispute on division of bequest and offspring

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions regarding the bequest of a slave woman who gives birth before division and explain the underlying legal reasoning (talil) of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The follower does not compete with the original: the child born after death but before division is a tabi' and does not reduce the bequest of the mother.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a bequest that exceeds the one-third limit due to the birth of a child. You will learn how Abu Hanifa and his two disciples differ on the treatment of the child, and the principle of tabi' (follower) that underlies the disagreement. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of bequests and inheritance in cases where property grows before distribution.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the legatee receives the mother (full value within the one-third) and one-third of the child's value. Identify the numerical outcome: mother (300 dirhams) and 1/3 of child (100 dirhams) total 400 dirhams, but only 200 from the third is allowed, so effectively the legatee takes mother and 1/3 child within the 200 limit, with remaining value to heirs.
The mother alone is worth 300, which exceeds the 200 third, so the bequest is executed in the mother, and any remainder of the third (0 in this case) is not available; the one-third of child is taken from the child's value because the mother consumed the entire third.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the legatee gets two-thirds of each (mother and child) but limited to one-third of the estate. This means the legatee gets 200 dirhams worth from the combined mother and child, allocated proportionally: 2/3 of mother (200) and 2/3 of child (200) but both cannot be fully given; the actual distribution is that the legatee gets both items but only to the value of 200, i.e., two-thirds of each in value.
The Sahibayn consider the child as part of the bequest because it was attached at the time of death, so it shares proportionally.
3
Analyze the proof of Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa argues that the bequest was valid in the mother alone (since she fits the third), and once valid, it cannot be partially rescinded. The child is a follower (tabi'), and a follower does not compete with the original. If both were given, the bequest in part of the mother would be annulled, which is impermissible.

4
Analyze the proof of the Sahibayn
The Sahibayn argue that the child entered the bequest as a follower while attached (at the time of death), so its later separation by birth does not remove it from the bequest. Therefore, the child remains part of the bequest and shares proportionally.
They emphasize the continuity of the bequest from the moment of death to the time of division.
5

The practical difference is in the distribution: Abu Hanifa gives the entire mother (within third) and a fraction of the child; the Sahibayn give proportional shares of both. In the example, Abu Hanifa gives mother and 1/3 child; Sahibayn give 2/3 mother and 2/3 child (but limited to total third value).

Bequest of Usufruct: Services and Dwelling
Ruling plus distribution scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequeathing usufructs and apply the proportional distribution rule (muhaya'a) when the bequest exceeds one-third.

Core Idea
Usufructs like services can be bequeathed; when indivisible, rights are rotated proportionally.

This lesson examines the validity of bequeathing non-physical benefits—specifically the services of a slave or the right to dwell in a house. The Hanafi school, reasoning by analogy from lifetime transfers, affirms that such usufructs can be willed. The text then addresses a practical problem: what happens when the bequest exceeds the one-third limit? The answer involves a time-sharing arrangement (muhaya'a) for indivisible assets, contrasting with the physical division possible for a house.

2
Understand the dalil (qiyas)
The proof is analogy: since usufructs can be validly transferred during life (with or without compensation), they can be transferred after death via bequest. This is a straightforward qiyas jaliy.
The operative phrase: 'لأن المنافع يصح تمليكها في حال الحياة ببدل وبغير بدل، فكذا بعد الموت'.

Second, if the slave constitutes the only asset and exceeds one-third, and heirs do not consent, the service is rotated: two days for heirs, one day for legatee, reflecting their shares.
Rotation (muhaya'a) is necessary because the slave cannot be physically divided.
