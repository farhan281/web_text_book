Defining Yamin and Its Three Types
Definition of oath and its three types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of yamin and distinguish its three types (ghamus, mun'aqidah, laghw) based on their legal effects.

Core Idea
An oath in Hanafi fiqh is a binding contract that strengthens resolve, classified into three types with distinct consequences.

Before diving into the detailed rulings on oaths, we must first understand what an oath is and its different categories. The author begins the Book of Oaths with a dual definition: linguistic and legal. He then outlines three fundamental types that determine whether an oath is sinful, binding, or negligible. This classification sets the stage for all subsequent rules on violation and expiation.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish linguistic from legal meaning
Read the linguistic definition (strength) with its Quranic and poetic evidence. Then read the legal definition (a contract strengthening resolve). Note how the legal meaning builds on the linguistic.
The term 'yamin' literally means 'right hand' or 'strength'; the legal concept borrows the idea of firmness.
2
Identify the three types from the matn
The matn states: 'Oaths are of three types: yamin ghamus, yamin mun'aqidah, and yamin laghw.' Memorize these terms and their core features: ghamus = false past oath, mun'aqidah = future oath with kaffarah, laghw = unintentional idle oath.
3
Understand the legal consequence of each type
Ghamus is a major sin without kaffarah (repentance only); mun'aqidah requires kaffarah if broken; laghw has no sin or kaffarah. This practical difference is the thamarah (fruit) of the classification.
These consequences are derived from the Qur'an and Sunnah, explained later in the book.
4
Appreciate the expansion from matn to commentary
The commentator expands the matn by providing linguistic evidence (Qur'an and poetry) and adding explanatory details. Note how he maintains the concise structure of Quduri's matn while enriching it.
This is a classic expansion_probe: the sharh does not change the ruling but clarifies the foundations.

Commentary

غموس (ghamus): An oath that is deliberately false about a past event, causing the swearer to be plunged into sin.
منعقدة (mun'aqidah): An oath regarding a future action, which if broken requires kaffarah (expiation).
لغو (laghw): A vain oath that is unintentional or without binding effect, such as idle speech.

Definition and Ruling of Yamin Ghamus (False Oath)
Defines yamin ghamus, states ruling, and gives proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define yamin ghamus, state its ruling, identify the proof texts, and explain why no kaffara applies.

Core Idea
Yamin ghamus is a major sin with no expiation; only repentance and seeking forgiveness are valid.

In this passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra, the author classifies oaths into three types and focuses on the most severe: yamin ghamus. This is an oath about a past or present matter where the swearer lies intentionally. Understanding its ruling is crucial because many people mistakenly think they can expiate such an oath with kaffara, but the Hanafi school teaches that only sincere tawba and istighfar can erase it. Let's examine the definition, proof, and practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Yamin ghamus is swearing about a past or present matter with intentional lying. Its ruling is haram (major sin) with no kaffara; the only remedy is tawba and istighfar.
The text explicitly says 'la kaffara fiha illa al-istighfar'.
2
Locate the dalil
Two proofs: a hadith 'Whoever swears by Allah falsely, Allah enters him into the Fire' and Quran 3:77 which condemns those who sell Allah's covenant for a small price and omits mention of kaffara.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is clear (qati al-dalala).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith threatens entry into Fire, indicating the gravity of the sin; the Quranic verse censures such acts and does not prescribe expiation, implying that no kaffara exists.
The absence of kaffara in the verse is a key evidence for the Hanafi position.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools (e.g., certain Shafi'i views) hold that a false oath about a past matter may require kaffara if the swearer later repents. The Hanafi school maintains no kaffara.
The difference arises from the interpretation of the proofs and the definition of 'yamin' in the Quranic verses about kaffara.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is based on the clear absence of kaffara in the primary texts and is the mufta bihi (operative) ruling. It is supported by the majority of Hanafi scholars.
Later Hanafi authorities like al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah confirm this.

فاليمين الغموس : هي الحَلِفُ على أمرٍ ماض. الحَلِفُ على أمرٍ ماض يتعمد الكذب فيه). مثل أن يحلف على شيءٍ قد فَعَلَه : ما فَعَلَه، مع علمه بذلك، أو على شيء لم يفعله : لقد فَعَلَه ، مع علمه أنه لم يفعله.

The ghamus oath is: swearing about a past matter intentionally lying. So this oath, its doer commits a sin, and there is no expiation for it except seeking forgiveness. The ghamus oath is swearing about a past matter intentionally lying, such as swearing that he did not do something that he did, knowing that, or swearing that he did something he did not do, knowing that he did not. It may also occur in the present, not only the past, such as saying: By Allah, he does not owe me anything, while he is lying. Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling of the yamin ghamus (false oath). It is defined as swearing about a past matter with intentional lying. The key elements are: (1) the oath concerns a past event (or present, as the author clarifies), (2) the swearer knows the truth, and (3) the swearer deliberately lies. Examples include denying an action one has committed or affirming an action one has not committed. The ruling is that this oath is a major sin (kabirah) and carries no kaffara (expiation) except sincere tawba (repentance) and istighfar (seeking forgiveness). The author provides two proofs: a hadith stating that a false oath leads to hellfire, and Quranic verse 3:77 which censures those who sell Allah's covenant for a small price and omits mention of kaffara, indicating the gravity of the sin. The name 'ghamus' is derived from 'ghamasa' meaning to immerse, because it plunges the swearer into the fire of hell. The author also notes that this oath is particularly severe because it cuts off the rights of a Muslim and involves daring to disobey Allah.

يمين لغو (yamin laghw): A vain oath, such as an unguarded statement without intent; has no sin and no kaffara. Mentioned here as a type.

Binding and Destructive Oaths: Definitions and Rulings
Defines binding oath and proves destructive oath is a major sin via hadith and qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-yamin al-mun'aqidah and al-yamin al-ghamus, and explain why the latter is a major sin that does not require expiation.

Core Idea
The destructive oath is a major sin from the moment it is uttered; expiation cannot cleanse it—only repentance.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah clarifies the essential difference between two types of oaths: the binding oath concerning future acts and the destructive false oath about past events. By examining the Prophet's hadith and a powerful qiyas (analogy), you will understand why the destructive oath is a major sin requiring repentance rather than expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two oath types
Read the first two sentences to locate the definitions of al-yamin al-mun'aqidah (binding oath) and al-yamin al-ghamus (destructive oath). Note the key difference: one concerns future actions, the other past/false.
2
Locate the hadith proof
Find the hadith: 'Three are from the major sins: the destructive oath, disobedience to parents, and fleeing from battle.' This is the primary dalil for the destructive oath being a major sin (kabirah).
The hadith is narrated in Musnad Ahmad and Sahih al-Bukhari.
3
Analyze the illa (reason)
The text says the meaning that necessitates dissolution (hanth) accompanies the destructive oath at its inception. Understand that the breaking of the oath (hanth) occurs simultaneously with swearing, so the oath never becomes binding.
Contrast with the binding oath where hanth occurs later, triggering kaffarah.
4
Trace the qiyas (analogy)
The author compares the destructive oath to a sale that coincides with the emancipation of the slave being sold. Just as the sale is invalid because the emancipation prevents the contract from taking effect, the destructive oath is invalid because the hanth prevents it from binding.
The illa is the immediate presence of a contradictory factor.
5
Conclude on the ruling
From the above, derive that the destructive oath is haram (major sin) and does not require expiation; the remedy is sincere tawbah (repentance) with regret, desistance, and resolve not to return.
This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school.

Book of Oaths. The binding oath is: swearing concerning a future matter, that he will do it or not do it. So if he breaks that oath, kaffarah is incumbent upon him. And he (peace be upon him) said: Three are from the major sins: the destructive oath, disobedience to parents, and fleeing from battle. And because it is a major sin, expiation does not affect it. Commentary
This passage begins with the definition of a binding oath (al-yamin al-mun'aqidah) according to the Hanafi school. A binding oath is one sworn on a future act—either to do something or not to do it—and if the oath is broken (hanth), expiation (kaffarah) becomes obligatory. The text then moves to discuss the destructive oath (al-yamin al-ghamus), which is a false oath taken deliberately. The Prophet (peace be upon him) listed it among the major sins (kaba'ir). The commentator explains that this type of oath is a major sin and expiation does not remove its sinfulness, unlike the binding oath. The reasoning is that Allah made kaffarah obligatory for the binding oath, which involves a valid contract that can be dissolved. The destructive oath, however, is inherently false from the start; the breaking of the oath (hanth) occurs at the very moment of swearing, so the oath never becomes binding. An analogy is drawn: like a sale that coincides with the emancipation of the slave—it is invalid because the freeing of the slave prevents the sale from taking effect. Similarly, a prayer that coincides with an impurity is invalid. The commentator then explains the exception of seeking forgiveness (istighfar) for the destructive oath, which requires three conditions: regret, desisting from the sin, and firm resolve not to return to it. This is a simplification; note that in Hanafi fiqh, the destructive oath does not require expiation but requires tawbah (repentance).

Key Terms
اليمين المنعقدة (al-yamin al-mun'aqidah): A binding oath sworn on a future act, either to do or not do something, that if broken requires expiation.
اليمين الغموس (al-yamin al-ghamus): A false oath taken deliberately about a past or present event, knowing it is false; it is a major sin.
الحنث (al-hanth): Breaking or violating an oath by doing what was sworn not to do or not doing what was sworn to do.
الكفارة (al-kaffarah): Expiation for breaking a valid oath, which consists of feeding or clothing ten poor people or freeing a slave; if unable, fasting three days.

Types of Binding Oaths: al-Mursal and al-Muwaqqat
Parses two types of binding oaths with rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mursal and muwaqqat oaths and state the rulings for each, including the conditions that trigger breaking (hanth).

Core Idea
A mursal oath is broken by death; a muwaqqat oath by time expiry — but destroying the object before time? The two say no break, Abu Yusuf says yes.

This passage breaks down the two main types of binding oaths in Hanafi fiqh: mursal (unrestricted) and muwaqqat (time-limited). Understanding these distinctions is crucial because the moment of breaking (hanth) and the obligation of kaffarah change depending on the type. The text provides clear examples and even notes an important intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types and their definitions
First, note that binding oaths (al-yamin al-mun'aqidah) are three: mursal, muwaqqat, and fawr (immediate). The passage focuses on the first two. Read the definitions: mursal has no time condition; muwaqqat is tied to a specific time.
The third type (fawr) is mentioned but not elaborated here.
2
Analyze the mursal oath: affirmative vs. negative
For the affirmative mursal oath (e.g., 'By Allah, I will strike Zaid'), the key condition is the existence of both the swearer and the object (Zaid). As long as both are alive, no break. If either dies, break occurs. For the negative mursal oath (e.g., 'By Allah, I will not strike Zaid'), the oath is never broken until the swearer actually does the forbidden action once.
Affirmative: death triggers break. Negative: action triggers break.
3
Analyze the muwaqqat oath
The muwaqqat oath is tied to a time period. Using the example of swearing to drink water from a pitcher today, the oath is not broken as long as the day is ongoing. If the day passes without drinking, break occurs. If the swearer dies before the day ends, no break by consensus.
Time is the determining factor.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on destroying the object
What if the swearer pours out the water before the day passes? According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the two), no break occurs because the object no longer exists. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that by destroying the object, the swearer made fulfillment impossible, so break occurs when the time expires. The relied-upon position is that of the two.
This dispute is a classic example of how differing usul lead to different rulings.
5
Summarize the consensus points
The passage ends with a summary: as long as both swearer and object exist within the time, no break; if time expires alone, break; if swearer dies within time, no break. These points are by consensus.
Consensus (ijma) strengthens these rulings.

If the second condition is not fulfilled immediately after the first, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say the oath is broken (the slave is freed), whereas Abu Yusuf says the slave is not freed until the condition is violated at any time.

Bread, dates, or saweeq (barley porridge) suffice if such is the customary food of the expiator's family. If one feeds a single poor person for ten days with two meals daily, it is valid even if each meal is only a single loaf, because the aim is satiation, not a specific quantity. Tamkin is about feeding, not giving ownership, so quantities are not fixed per meal. However, if the expiator feeds ten persons lunch and a different ten persons dinner, it is invalid, as the twenty persons each receive only one meal instead of two. Similarly, feeding one poor person lunch and another dinner for ten days does not fulfill the kaffarah because the required two meals for each of ten persons have been spread over twenty persons. A mixed method is valid: giving lunch in kind and the value (qimah) of dinner in dirhams or fulus, whether for one or ten persons. Hisham narrates from Muhammad al-Shaybani that feeding a single poor person for twenty days (either lunch or dinner) suffices, analogizing that satiating one person over many days is like satiating many persons in one day. Finally, giving the entire food of ten poor persons to one poor person in a single day is invalid, as multiple deliveries on separate occasions are required, similar to the requirement of separate throws in stoning the jamrah.

تمكين (tamkin): Providing satiating meals to the poor directly, as opposed to giving ownership (tamlik).
إدام (idam): A condiment or something eaten with bread, like meat, oil, or vegetables.
سد الجوعة (sadd al-ju'ah): Filling the hunger/satiation, the goal of tamkin.

Due to his saying, peace be upon him: 'Whoever takes an oath and then sees something better than it, let him do that which is better and then expiate for his oath.' And because it involves abandoning the good for the compensator, which is expiation, and there is no compensator for the sin in its opposite. It is narrated that Abu Hanifa asked al-Sha'bi about this issue, and he said: No expiation is required because this is an oath in sin. He said: Did not Allah make zihar abominable speech and falsehood, yet required expiation for it? Al-Sha'bi said to him: You are one of the 'do you see' people, i.e., those who use opinion. And his statement: then he should break his oath: i.e., speak to his father, pray, resolve to abandon killing, and expiate for his oath. And if he leaves prayer, does not speak to his father, and kills so-and-so: then he is a sinner, and upon him is repentance and seeking forgiveness, and his matter is left to Allah, and no expiation is required because he did not actually perform the thing sworn upon.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on what to do when one swears to commit a sin (ma'siyah). The Hanafi position is that one should deliberately break the oath by doing the good act (e.g., praying, speaking to father, refraining from murder) and then expiate. The proof is the well-known hadith commanding that if one sees a better course, he should take it and expiate. The author provides a rational explanation (ta'lil): expiation is the compensator (jabir) for the good that was missed, but for the sin itself there is no compensator. The story of Abu Hanifa and al-Sha'bi illustrates a debate: al-Sha'bi initially argued no expiation is required for an oath on sin, but Abu Hanifa analogized it to zihar (a prohibited form of divorce that also requires expiation). Al-Sha'bi then criticized Abu Hanifa as an 'ara'iti' (one who uses personal opinion). The conclusion: if the person actually commits the sin without breaking the oath first, he is sinful and must repent, but no expiation is required because he did not actually do the act he swore upon? Actually the text says 'لم يباشر المحلوف عليه' meaning he did not directly do the thing sworn upon—if he swore not to pray and then did not pray, he did not break the oath; if he swore to kill and killed, he did break the oath, but then he sinned. The passage seems to indicate that if he breaks the oath by doing the sin, he is still a sinner and must repent, and no expiation is needed because the expiation is for the oath, not for the sin. Wait: the text says: if he leaves prayer, does not speak to father, and kills so-and-so: then he is a sinner, and no expiation. So he actually performed the sworn act (killing) and did not perform the sworn act of not praying (i.e., he left prayer, which is the sin). The point is that he went ahead and sinned; then the oath is considered broken, but he does not get expiation because he sinned. The proper course is to break the oath by doing the good (the opposite of the sin) and then expiate. So this is the relied-upon ruling.

حنث (hanath): Breaking the oath by doing the opposite; here it means to deliberately break the oath by doing the good act.

Timing of an Oath: Time-Bound vs. Absolute
Clarifies when an oath to sin is considered broken based on time restriction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a time-bound (mu'qqatah) and an absolute (mutlaqah) oath and state when each is considered broken according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
An absolute oath breaks only at death; a time-bound oath breaks when the time elapses without fulfillment.

In the previous discussion, we learned that one who swears an oath to sin should break the oath and expiate. However, that ruling applies specifically to time-bound oaths. For an absolute oath—one with no time limit—the breaking occurs only at the final moment of life. This lesson clarifies this crucial distinction, ensuring you apply the correct ruling based on the wording of the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Recognize that oaths can be time-bound (mu'qqatah) with a specified period (e.g., 'today') or absolute (mutlaqah) with no time restriction.
2
Understand the condition for time-bound
For a time-bound oath, the swearer must specify a time period. If the period ends without the act being done, the oath is broken at that moment.
Example: 'I swear I will not eat today' – time-bound.
3
Understand absolute oaths
An absolute oath has no time limit. The oath remains in effect until death. If the swearer never violates it, the oath is considered broken only at the last moment of life.
Example: 'I swear I will never eat meat' – absolute.
4
Apply the ruling to a sin oath
For a time-bound oath to sin, the recommended action is to break the oath (commit the sin? Actually, break the oath by not fulfilling it?) and expiate. For an absolute oath to sin, the person does not break it until death; the earlier recommendation does not apply.
The source text states: 'all this applies if the oath is time-bound; for absolute, he does not break it except at the last part of his life.'
5
Recognize practical implications
If someone makes an absolute oath to avoid a good deed, he must continue avoiding it until death, and expiation is due at death if he has not done the act. If he violates it, he must expiate immediately.
This distinction prevents unnecessary expiation before the time is due.
