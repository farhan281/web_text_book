Evidence Required for Division of Inherited Real Estate
Ruling with proof on evidence requirement for inherited real estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on evidence required for dividing inherited real estate and explain the rationale based on the distinction between real and movable property.

Core Idea
Division of inherited real estate requires proof of death and heirs because it is a judgment against the deceased.

When co-heirs come to a judge seeking division of a house or land they claim to have inherited, the Hanafi school requires formal evidence before proceeding. This lesson examines why Abu Hanifa insists on such proof for real estate but not for movable goods, and the reasoning behind this distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the judge being asked to do? What is Abu Hanifa's ruling on that request?
The act is dividing inherited real estate; the ruling is that the judge must not divide until evidence of death and heirs is presented.
2
Understand the ta'lil (rationale)
Why does Abu Hanifa consider division a judgment against the deceased? How does that affect the requirement of proof?
Because the estate remains in the deceased's ownership until debts and bequests are settled, division affects the deceased's rights.
3
Distinguish real estate from movables
Why is evidence required for real estate but not for movables? What is the principle of 'muhsan bi-nafsihi'?
Real estate is durable and not at risk of perishing, so delaying division for verification causes no harm.
4
Apply the legal consequence
What kind of evidence is required? Why is iqrar (admission) insufficient?
Bayyinah (formal testimony) is needed because an acknowledgment by the living is not binding on the deceased.

Commentary
This passage addresses a procedural rule when co-owners come to a judge seeking division of property they claim to have inherited. Abu Hanifa stipulates that for real estate (a house or landed estate), the judge must not proceed with division until the claimants provide formal evidence (bayyinah) of two facts: (1) the death of the alleged ancestor, and (2) the number of his heirs. The reasoning (ta'lil) is that division before the full settlement of inheritance is considered a judicial disposition against the deceased; the estate is still deemed to be in the deceased's ownership until debts and bequests are settled. Therefore, the judge's ruling on division affects the rights of the deceased and of absent or unknown heirs. Since an admission (iqrar) by the present parties is not binding on the deceased (who cannot speak or be produced), oral claims are insufficient; only evidence (witnesses) can satisfy the burden of proof. In contrast, for movable property (al-manqul) and other chattels, the judge may proceed with division without such evidence, because these items are perishable and could be lost or damaged if delayed. Real estate, being immovable and durable, is 'inherently safeguarded' (muhsin bi-nafsihi) and can wait for proper verification.

Key Terms
بينة (bayyinah): Formal legal evidence, typically the testimony of two male witnesses.
قضاء على الميت (qaḍā' 'alā al-mayyit): A judicial ruling that affects the rights and estate of a deceased person.
محصن بنفسه (muḥṣan bi-nafsihī): Inherently safeguarded; applied to real estate because it is immovable and unlikely to be lost or perish.

The Requirement of Evidence for Partition of Inherited Real Estate: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Ruling on partition of inherited property when partners claim inheritance without proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partition of inherited property when heirs lack evidence, distinguish Abu Hanifa's view from that of the Sahibayn, and explain the practical consequences of the difference.

Core Idea
A judge cannot partition inherited real estate based solely on heirs' admission; evidence of death is required because partition affects the deceased.

When heirs claim ownership of a house as an inheritance but have no witnesses, a judge must decide whether to divide the property. This lesson explores a key intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on whether the heirs' mere admission suffices, or whether formal proof is needed. Understanding this disagreement reveals deeper principles about the nature of inheritance ownership and the limits of judicial reliance on admissions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Determine the exact issue: can a judge partition inherited real estate based solely on the heirs' admission of inheritance, without external evidence of death and heir count?
The dispute is between Abu Hanifa (requires evidence) and the Sahibayn (accepts admission when no disputant exists).
2
Trace Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Understand why Abu Hanifa insists on evidence: partition is a legal disposition against the deceased (qada' ala al-mayyit) because the estate remains under his ownership until division. Admission (iqrar) is not binding on the deceased.
The estate remains in the deceased's ownership, so any change requires proof that affects him.
3
Contrast with the Sahibayn's analogy
Analyze how the Sahibayn analogize to purchased property: in purchase, the buyer's ownership is new and independent (milk musta'naf), so partition among buyers does not affect the seller. Similarly, here possession and admission suggest ownership with no disputant.
The Sahibayn's illa is the absence of a disputant; Abu Hanifa distinguishes because inheritance involves continuity of the deceased's ownership.
4
Evaluate the practical consequence
Examine the thamarah al-khilaf: if partition is based on admission alone, the ruling is limited to the heirs and does not affect third parties (e.g., the deceased's wife, mudabbars, debts). If based on evidence, the ruling extends fully.
The deed of division must specify the basis (bi bayyinah or bi iqrar) to determine its effect.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Partition (Kitab al-Qisma) addresses the procedure when partners (shuraka') come before a judge claiming to have inherited a house or land from a specific person. It presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The passage begins with the matn (primary text) from Mukhtasar al-Quduri, stating Abu Hanifa's position: the judge should not partition the property until the heirs provide evidence (bayyinah) of the death of the deceased and the number of heirs. The commentator then inserts a brief discussion about wages for digging a well and measuring/weighing in the context of partition, clarifying that if the work is not for partition itself, the wage is distributed proportionally to shares. This seems to be a tangential note. The main commentary then re-quotes the matn and explains the reasoning: partition of inherited property is a legal disposition (qada') against the deceased, because the estate remains under the deceased's ownership until division. Therefore, an admission (iqrar) by the heirs is not binding against the deceased; only evidence can establish the death and heirship. The passage then contrasts with movable property (manqul) and goods (urud): they are divided even without evidence due to the risk of loss (tawa), whereas real estate (aqar) is inherently secure. Abu Hanifa distinguishes between inheritance and purchase: in purchase, the buyer's ownership is new and independent of the seller, so partition among partners does not affect the seller. In inheritance, the heir's ownership stems from the deceased, so partition is a disposition against the deceased, requiring evidence. The Sahibayn argue that apparent possession (yad) and admission suffice because there is no disputant, and ownership is apparent. However, the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's view for real estate, requiring evidence, while for movables, the Sahibayn's view may be applied to prevent loss. The practical consequence (thamarah) is that if division is by admission only, the ruling is limited to the heirs and does not affect third parties like the deceased's wife (not divorced), mudabbars (not freed), or debts (not due).

Key Terms
قسمة (qisma): Partition or division of jointly owned property among co-owners.
بينة (bayyinah): Legal evidence, typically testimony of two male witnesses or one male and two females, required to establish a fact in court.
إقرار (iqrar): Admission or confession by a party of a right against themselves. Here, the heirs' admission that they inherited from a deceased person.

Coercion to Kill a Relative (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Rulings on killing a relative under coercion and cutting own hand

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the coerced killer of a relative and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf (Abu Yusuf's view) and its practical consequence.

Core Idea
No qisas or diyah on the coerced killer of an heir; the heir may kill the coercer.

This lesson examines a striking case: a person is forced under threat of death to kill his own father or brother. What is the fiqh ruling? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement, with Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other. You will learn the ruling, the dispute, and how the authority of the two prevails.

2
State the majority Hanafi position

This is indicated by 'عندهما'.

Abu Yusuf holds that the coerced must pay diyah; if the coerced is also an heir, he is deprived of inheritance.

Under Abu Yusuf, the coerced pays diyah; under the majority, no financial liability. Also, inheritance differs if the coerced is an heir.
Practical consequence of the dispute.

Coercion to Cut One's Own Hand
Rulings on killing a relative under coercion and cutting own hand

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cutting one's own hand under threat of death and explain the principle that necessity permits the act.

Core Idea
Cutting one's own hand under threat of death is permissible to save one's life.

This lesson presents a secondary scenario from the same passage: a person is threatened with death unless he cuts off his own hand. The Hanafi ruling is clear: it is permissible (وسعه) to do so because preserving life takes priority. You will see how the principle of necessity overrides the prohibition of self-harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A man threatens another: 'I will kill you or you must cut off your hand.' The question: may he cut his hand?
This is a case of coercion (ikrah) by threat of death.
2
State the ruling
The text says: 'وسعه قطع يده' – it is permissible for him to cut his hand.
The ruling is mubah (permissible).
3
Understand the rationale
The reason given: 'لأنه يصل بقطعها إلى إحياء نفسه' – because by cutting it he achieves saving his life.
The principle: saving life overrides preservation of a limb.
4
Consider the legal maxim
This is an application of the maxim: 'الضرورات تبيح المحظورات' – Necessities permit prohibited things.
Self-harm is normally prohibited, but necessity changes the ruling.
5
Note the scope
The permissibility is conditional: the threat must be genuine and the cutting must likely save his life.
If cutting does not save his life, it may not be permissible.

Inheritance of the Apostate: Timing of Heir's Status
Intra-Hanafi dispute on when heir's eligibility is determined

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on when the heir's status is considered for inheritance from an apostate, and apply the two narrations from Abu Hanifa to determine inheritance outcomes.

Core Idea
The heir's eligibility for inheritance from an apostate is fixed at apostasy (Abu Hanifa) or at death (Sahibayn).

Inheritance from an apostate raises a key question: at what moment do we check the heir's qualifications? Within the Hanafi school, Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold different views based on their underlying principles of ownership removal. This lesson dissects their positions, the two narrations from Abu Hanifa, and the practical consequences of each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Read the initial statement: the heir must be free and Muslim on the day of apostasy. This establishes the default position that the time of apostasy is relevant.
The phrase 'كان حراً مسلماً يومئذ' indicates the time of apostasy.
2
State the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa holds that the heir's status is considered at the moment of apostasy. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold it is considered at death, killing, or joining Dar al-Harb.
The Sahibayn's view is based on ownership not being removed by apostasy alone.
3
Trace the underlying principle
Abu Hanifa analogizes apostasy to death: ownership is removed at the last moment of Islam, just as a Muslim's ownership ends at the last moment of life. The Sahibayn argue ownership continues until death or equivalent.
This is a qiyas (analogy) on the nature of ownership.
4
Examine the two narrations from Abu Hanifa
First narration (from al-Hasan): the heir must survive until the apostate's death. Second narration (from Abu Yusuf): the heir's right vests at apostasy and passes to his own heirs if he dies before the apostate.
Both narrations agree on considering apostasy, but differ on the continuity of the right.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference occurs if the heir dies after apostasy but before the apostate's death. Under the first narration, the heir loses his share; under the second, his heirs inherit it. Under the Sahibayn, the heir must be alive at death.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, but the preferred narration is the second (from Abu Yusuf) in later fatwa.

For spouses (husband or wife), whose shares are fixed (1/4 or 1/8 normally, but here as sole heir the husband gets 1/2 and wife gets 1/4), the testator can bequeath more than one-third because the excess beyond the spouse's fixed share belongs to no one else.
2

A woman leaves only her husband and bequeaths half her property to a stranger. The bequest of one-third is executed first (donee gets 1/3). Then the husband inherits half of the remaining 2/3, giving him 1/3 of the total. The donee gets another 1/6 to complete half, leaving 1/6 for Bayt al-Mal.
The husband does not get his normal share (1/2) because the bequest is taken first.
3
Work Through Example 2: Wife, Husband, Bequest to Husband of Half
If the woman bequeaths half to her husband (an heir), he gets everything: half as inheritance, half as bequest. This is valid because there is no other heir to object.
When the legatee is the sole heir, the bequest is effective without permission.
4
Work Through Example 3: Husband, Wife, Bequest of All to Stranger
A man leaves only his wife and bequeaths all property to a stranger. The donee gets 1/3 first, then the wife inherits 1/4 of the remaining 2/3 (i.e., 1/6 of total). The remaining 1/2 (6/12) goes to the donee, making his total 5/6. The wife gets 1/6.
Base number (asl al-masalah) is 12 for easy calculation.
5
Work Through Example 4: Double Bequest to Wife and Stranger
If both the wife and a stranger are bequeathed all property, the stranger has priority for the first 1/3. After that, the wife inherits her share, then the remaining residue is divided proportionally between the two legatees based on their claims.
This illustrates the Hanafi method of using proportional division (qisma) when the bequest exceeds the estate.

The Ten Male Inheritors by Consensus
Listing the ten male inheritors by consensus with clarifications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the ten male heirs who inherit by consensus in Hanafi law and identify which 'grandfather' is included.

Core Idea
The ten male inheritors are fixed by consensus; only the paternal grandfather qualifies as an asabah heir.

This lesson presents the authoritative list of male heirs who inherit by consensus among the Companions and jurists. The commentary clarifies that 'grandfather' means only the paternal grandfather, not the maternal. Understanding this list is the first step in solving any inheritance case.

Guided Path
1
Recite the ten heirs from the matn
Read the list: the son, the son's son however low, the father, the grandfather (father's father) however high, the brother, the brother's son, the paternal uncle, the paternal uncle's son, the husband, and the freed slave (master of favor).
Memorize this order.
2
Understand 'however low' and 'however high'
These phrases mean all male descendants through males (for son's son) and all male ancestors through males (for grandfather). For example, grandson's son is included; great-grandfather is included.
3
Differentiate paternal vs. maternal grandfather
The passage explicitly says: 'What is meant by grandfather is the father's father; as for the mother's father, he is a dhawu arham, not an asabah.' Thus, the maternal grandfather is not in this list.
4
Identify the role of mawla al-ni'mah
This heir is the former master who freed a slave. Due to the contract of wala', the master inherits if there are no other heirs from the blood relatives.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Faraid (Inheritance) in the Hanafi tradition. It begins with a linguistic definition: 'al-fard' means estimation, as in a judge estimating maintenance. It then emphasizes the high status of the science of inheritance, citing three hadith: (1) 'Faraid is half of knowledge and the first to be withdrawn'; (2) Allah Himself divided inheritances, and there is no bequest for an heir; (3) Learn and teach faraid as it is half of knowledge and will be lost. The phrase 'half of knowledge' is explained: a person has two states, life and death; faraid deals with death, so it is one of two halves. Its connection to bequests is highlighted: bequests are during terminal illness, faraid after death. Then the matn lists the ten male heirs who inherit by consensus: son, son's son (however low), father, paternal grandfather (however high), brother, brother's son, paternal uncle, paternal uncle's son, husband, and freed slave (master of favor). The commentary clarifies that 'grandfather' means only paternal grandfather; maternal grandfather inherits only as a dhawu arham. The list includes those who may inherit in principle, though priority and shares differ. This lesson establishes the foundational categories of male inheritors in Hanafi faraid.

He explains the underlying principle—that inheritance requires ownership capacity—and then guides us through the nuanced exceptions for slaves who are partially free, such as the mukatab and mustas'a. This discussion reveals a classic intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifah and his two foremost students.

Guided Path
1
List the four categories
First, identify the four types of people who never inherit under any circumstance: the slave, the intentional killer of the deceased, the apostate, and those of two different religions (i.e., a non-Muslim heir and a Muslim deceased).
The fourth category actually refers to the rule that people of different religions do not inherit from each other.
2
Understand the slave's reasoning
The author gives the proof: inheritance is a transfer of ownership; a slave cannot own property (he himself is owned); his earnings belong to his master; and there is no kinship between the master and the deceased. Therefore, a full slave is barred from both inheriting and being inherited from.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli).
3
Distinguish full slave from partially free
Note that the bar applies to anyone with any degree of slavery (mukatab, mudabbar, umm walad). However, there is an exception for the mukatab: if he dies with sufficient funds to pay his kitabah, he is ruled free immediately before death, so he can inherit and be inherited from.
The same reasoning applies to mudabbar and umm walad: they are not excepted.
4
Analyze the mukatab exception
For the mukatab, if he dies with enough money, his freedom is retroactively established. The estate pays the kitabah first, then the remainder is distributed to his heirs. If he lacks sufficient funds, he dies a slave and no inheritance occurs.
This is the only partial freedom case that is explicitly excepted in the text.
5
Examine the ikhtilaf on mustas'a
The mustas'a (a slave allowed to work for freedom) is treated differently by the imams. Abu Hanifah equates him to a mukatab (inherits only if he has enough funds at death). The Sahibayn treat him as a free person with debts (inherits regardless, but debts are paid from the estate). The fatwa follows the Sahibayn.
This ikhtilaf also applies to a partially freed slave (mu'ataq ba'd).

The Apostate Does Not Inherit

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An apostate cannot inherit, but his Muslim heirs may inherit from him upon his death or legal death.

Inheritance rules are built on the bond of Islam. When a person leaves Islam (apostasy), that bond is severed. But what happens to the property the apostate leaves behind? This lesson explains the Hanafi position: the apostate loses all inheritance rights, yet his Muslim relatives may still inherit from him under specific circumstances.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The passage begins by declaring that an apostate does not inherit from anyone—neither from a Muslim, a dhimmī, nor another apostate. This is the absolute principle.
Apostasy nullifies the legal bond that is the basis for inheritance (wilāyat al-irth).
2
Identify the exception
The text then provides an exception: if the apostate is killed (as a penalty for apostasy or in battle) or if he flees to dar al-harb (enemy territory) and the judge rules him legally dead, then his Muslim heirs do inherit from him.
The key condition is that the apostate's death is either actual or legally presumed by a judge.
3
Understand the condition for Muslim heirs
For the Muslim heirs to inherit, there must be a definitive event—either execution or a judicial declaration of death upon joining the enemy. Without such an event, the apostate's property is held in abeyance (mawqūf) pending his return or death.
This ruling only applies to the apostate's own estate; his Muslim heirs are not affected in their own inheritance rights from other Muslims.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Imam al-Shafi'i (may Allah have mercy on him) holds that an apostate's property goes entirely to the bayt al-mal (public treasury) and his Muslim heirs do not inherit. The Hanafi position is based on the principle that apostasy does not completely sever the inheritance bond from the apostate's side, only from his right to inherit.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether the deceased apostate's property passes to his Muslim relatives or to the state.

Muslims and non-Muslims never inherit from each other; dhimmīs inherit among themselves, and harbīs are one millah unless at war.

Guided Path
1

2
Distinguish between dhimmīs and harbīs
Note the text explicitly states that a harbī does not inherit from a dhimmī and vice versa. Although both are non-Muslims, they belong to different millahs because the dhimmī is under Islamic protection and the harbī is from enemy territory.
The key difference is the legal relationship with the Islamic state.

Apply the rule to dhimmīs
The text says 'the people of dhimmah inherit from one another'. This means dhimmīs who share the same religion (e.g., Christians inherit from Christians, Jews from Jews) are considered one millah and inherit from each other.
If dhimmīs follow different religions (e.g., one Christian, one Jew), they are of two millahs and do not inherit from each other.

Harbīs are generally considered one millah (all enemy non-Muslims are one religious community) for inheritance, so they inherit from each other. However, if their territories are separate and they consider each other's blood lawful (i.e., they are at war with each other), they become different millahs and do not inherit.
The condition 'daruhum mukhtalifah' and 'yastahillu kullu wahidin minhuma dam al-akhar' triggers the exception.

The Hanafi position is that no inheritance occurs across any religious boundary (Muslim/non-Muslim or different non-Muslim communities if separate millahs). The Shafi'i school, however, holds that a Muslim can inherit from a non-Muslim but not vice versa.
The practical consequence (thamarah) is that in a Shafi'i jurisdiction, a Muslim heir might inherit a non-Muslim relative's estate, whereas in Hanafi law he would not.

Core Idea
People of different religions do not inherit from each other, but an apostate's Muslim heirs inherit his property regardless of when it was earned.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi position on inheritance involving apostates and non-Muslims. It begins with general rules based on the hadith 'People of two religions do not inherit from each other,' then presents a key dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding the treatment of the apostate's earnings. Understanding this dispute reveals the Hanafi school's internal methodology and its reliance on the preferred view of the Sahibayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal rule from the hadith
The hadith 'لا يتوارث أهل ملتين' establishes that people of two different religions do not inherit from each other. Note the exceptions: dhimmīs inherit among themselves (same religion), and harbīs from the same territory inherit among themselves. Harbīs from different territories do not inherit.
The hadith is the primary textual evidence for these rulings.
2
Apply the rule to the apostate
An apostate does not inherit from anyone (Muslim, dhimmī, or another apostate) because he is considered of a different 'millah' from Muslims. However, when the apostate dies or joins the abode of war and is legally considered as having joined, his Muslim heirs inherit from him according to the Hanafi school. This is the starting point of the dispute.
The apostate's own inheritance rights are severed, but his heirs may inherit from him.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on the apostate's property

Order of Residuaries and the Grandfather vs Brothers Dispute
Listing the priority of residuary heirs and the ijtihad of Abu Hanifa vs the Sahibayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi order of residuary heirs and explain the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the grandfather's entitlement relative to brothers, identifying the practical consequence of each view.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa gives the grandfather full priority over brothers; the Sahibayn share the estate – whose view prevails?

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents the canonical Hanafi sequence of residuary heirs (asabah) and then introduces a key internal disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two distinguished students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The dispute centers on whether the grandfather (father's father) takes precedence over full brothers or whether they inherit together. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for applying faraid correctly, as the resulting distribution changes significantly depending on which view is adopted.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the standard order
List the residuary heirs in descending priority: sons, then grandsons, then father, then grandfather, then full brothers, then paternal uncles, then great-uncles. This is the default Hanafi sequence as per the matn.
The order is crucial; any deviation can change inheritance shares.
2
Identify the point of dispute
The matn explicitly states that according to Abu Hanifa, the grandfather is more entitled than brothers and excludes them. The Sahibayn disagree, placing the grandfather to share with brothers.
This is a pure ijtihadi difference within the same school.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's analogical proofs (qiyas)
Abu Hanifa compares the grandfather to the father using three similarities: both have paternity (ولادة), both cause others to become residuaries (تعصيب), both may eat from a descendant's property in need without permission, and both cannot testify for a descendant. These shared traits make the grandfather more entitled.
The qiyas is based on a comprehensive set of attributes, not a single one.
4
Contrast the Sahibayn's view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the grandfather does not fully resemble the father; therefore he should not exclude the brothers. Instead, they treat the grandfather and brothers like co-residuaries, with the grandfather given the best of either sharing equally or taking one-third of the estate.
The Sahibayn consider the absence of a direct Quranic share for the grandfather as a key distinction.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, if a grandfather and full brother survive alone, the grandfather inherits the entire residue, excluding the brother. Under the Sahibayn, they share the residue equally, or the grandfather takes one-third if that is better. This directly affects the amounts received.
Calculators and fatwa must account for this ikhtilaf.

Explains the order of precedence among agnatic heirs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of precedence among agnatic heirs in Hanafi faraid and apply the rule that certain agnates share with females while others exclude them.

Core Idea
Full agnates precede half; son, grandson, and brothers share with sisters; other male agnates exclude females.

Inheritance in Islam often involves agnatic heirs (asabah) who receive the residue after Quranic shares. This passage from Al-Jawharah Al-Nayyirah outlines the Hanafi rules for prioritizing agnates when they are equal in degree, which agnates share with females, and what happens when no blood agnates exist. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly distributing the estate.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of asabah
Asabah are male agnates who inherit the residue after prescribed shares. They are prioritized by closeness to the deceased.
Agnatic relationship is through males (e.g., father, son, brother).
2
Learn the hierarchy among agnates
When agnates are equal in degree (e.g., both are sons of the grandfather), the one related through both parents (full sibling) takes priority over the half sibling. The passage states: 'فأولاهم من كان لأب وأم'.
Degree refers to the distance from the deceased (e.g., brother vs. cousin).
3
Identify agnates who share with females
The son, son's son, and full/paternal brothers share with their sisters in a 2:1 ratio (male:female). All other agnates (e.g., children of brothers, grandfather's children) only males inherit; females are excluded.
The passage explicitly lists these exceptions.

Know the order when no blood agnates
If no blood agnate exists, inheritance goes to the former master (mawla al-mu'tiq) who freed the deceased, then to the master's agnates, then to the master of emancipation (mawla al-'itaqah). This takes precedence over uterine relatives (dhawi al-arham).
Uterine relatives are blood relatives not through males (e.g., maternal uncle).
5
Recognize the last heir
The patron by contract (mawla al-muwalah) is the farthest heir, coming after uterine relatives. This completes the Hanafi order of inheritance.
Mawla al-muwalah is specific to Hanafi fiqh.

When the primary heirs are daughters, the surplus goes to the sons of the son (grandsons) and their sisters. When the primary heirs are full sisters, the surplus goes to the half-sisters and half-brothers from the father's side.
These are specific classes of residuary heirs (asabah) designated by the Hanafi school.

The surplus is divided among the residuary heirs with males receiving twice the share of females: for each male, two shares; for each female, one share.
This is analogous to the general inheritance rule for children.

In Hanafi faraid, full sisters are Quranic sharers (fard) when there are no children or father. However, when they coexist with paternal half-siblings (consanguine siblings), the sisters take their prescribed portion, and the half-siblings inherit the residue as residuaries (asabah). This lesson explains the distribution of that residue according to the principle 'for the male the share of two females.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs
List all surviving heirs: full sister(s) and paternal half-siblings (both brothers and sisters).
Full sisters are always from both parents; paternal half-siblings share only the father.
2
Determine the full sisters' prescribed share
If there are two or more full sisters, they receive 2/3 divided equally among them. If one full sister, she receives 1/2.
This is based on Quranic shares (Surah An-Nisa, verses 11 and 176).
3
Calculate the residue
Subtract the full sisters' total share from the whole estate (1). The remainder is the residue for the paternal half-siblings.
Example: two full sisters take 2/3, residue = 1/3. One full sister takes 1/2, residue = 1/2.
4

Paternal half-siblings inherit the residue as asabah. If both males and females exist, each male takes twice the share of each female. If only males, they share equally. If only females, they share the residue equally (since they become asabah ma' al-ghayr).
The rule 'for the male the share of two females' applies only when both genders are present.
5
Verify the total shares
Sum all shares to ensure they equal 1 (the whole estate). If not, adjust using 'awl (proportional reduction) if necessary, but in these scenarios no 'awl arises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate inheritance shares in the musharrakah case and explain why full siblings are excluded.

Core Idea
In the musharrakah, maternal siblings' prescribed share consumes the estate, leaving full siblings with nothing.

The musharrakah is a famous faraid puzzle that tests your understanding of prescribed shares vs. residuaries. In this lesson, you will work through the exact distribution that leaves full siblings empty-handed, a surprising result that challenges the assumption that full siblings always inherit.

Guided Path
1
Identify all surviving heirs
List every heir present: husband, mother, maternal brothers (children of the mother), and full brothers (from the father and mother).
The case assumes at least two maternal brothers to trigger the 1/3 collective share.
2
Apply hajb hirman (total exclusion)
Determine which heirs are completely blocked. Here, full brothers are excluded because no residue remains after distributing prescribed shares.
Typically full brothers exclude maternal brothers, but in this case maternal brothers take a prescribed share first.
3
Assign prescribed shares to fard heirs
Husband takes 1/2, mother takes 1/6 (due to presence of multiple siblings), maternal brothers take 1/3 collectively.
The mother's share is 1/6 because there are siblings; had there been fewer than two siblings, she would take 1/3.

Calculate asl al-masalah and verify total
Convert fractions to a common denominator: 1/2 = 3/6, 1/6 = 1/6, 1/3 = 2/6. Sum = 6/6. No residue.
The base number is 6 (LCM of 2, 6, 3).

Full brothers are asabah (residuaries), but since the estate is fully consumed, they receive zero.
This is the surprising outcome of the musharrakah case.

Core Idea
A person inheriting through two relationships takes both shares: fixed and residue.

Sometimes a person is related to the deceased in two ways at once. In this case, one of two paternal cousins is also a uterine brother. How should the estate be divided? The uterine brother receives his Quranic one-sixth, but he also shares the residue as a cousin. This lesson walks through the calculation and the reasoning behind it.
