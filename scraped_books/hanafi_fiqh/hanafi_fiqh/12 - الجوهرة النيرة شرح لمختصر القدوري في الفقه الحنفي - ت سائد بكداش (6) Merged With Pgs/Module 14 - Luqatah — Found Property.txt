Form of Oath in Property-Return Claims
Ruling on oath wording for property-return claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the proper form of oath in claims of ghasb, wadi'ah, and ariyah, and explain the legal reasoning (istihsan) behind it.

Core Idea
Oath in property-return claims is on entitlement to return, not on the act of taking – to avoid false swearing.

In judicial proceedings for usurpation, deposit, and loan, the defendant must swear an oath. The precise wording matters because it determines whether the defendant can truthfully swear without perjury. This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling based on istihsan to avoid false oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the form of oath in a judicial claim. The ruling is that the defendant must swear a specific wording. Identify what is being ruled upon: the wording of the oath.
The passage focuses on the wording, not the obligation to swear.
2
State the Hanafi position
For ghasb: the defendant swears 'By Allah, you are not entitled to the return of this specific item, nor its value.' For wadi'ah and ariyah: same form, mentioning value.
Value is included because the item may have perished due to transgression.
3
Examine the alternative oath
The alternative would be to swear 'By Allah, I did not usurp' or 'I did not take.' This is rejected because it may be false if the defendant took the item but later returned it, gifted, or bought it.
The alternative is avoided to prevent perjury.
4
Understand the proof: istihsan
The proof is istihsan (juristic preference) to avoid false swearing. The possibility that the defendant took the item but later returned or acquired ownership makes the alternative oath potentially false. The preferred wording avoids this.
Istihsan overrides strict qiyas that would require swearing on the act.
5
Apply to deposit and loan
The same reasoning applies to wadi'ah and ariyah. The defendant swears that the plaintiff is not entitled to return of the item or its value, not that the defendant did not receive it.
Value is mentioned because the item may have been destroyed by the defendant's transgression.

Commentary
This passage provides the ruling for the form of oath in claims involving usurpation (ghasb), deposit (wadi'ah), and loan (ariyah). The passage type is a ruling with proof. The defendant is made to swear that the plaintiff is not entitled to the return of the specific item or its value, not that he never took it. This is because the defendant may have taken the item but then returned it, gifted it, or bought it, so swearing 'I did not usurp' would be false. Similarly, for deposit and loan, the oath is on the right to return, not on the act of depositing or lending. The value is included because the item may have been destroyed due to the defendant's transgression, so the plaintiff might be entitled to compensation. This reasoning is istihsan to avoid perjury.

Key Terms
غصب (ghasb): Unlawful taking of another's property without consent.
وديعة (wadi'ah): Deposit: property left with another for safekeeping without compensation.
عارية (ariyah): Loan: giving property for free use with the obligation to return its specific object.
يمين (yamin): Oath sworn in a judicial context.
قيمة (qimah): Value (monetary compensation) for the item if it cannot be returned.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school prefers an oath that avoids perjury by focusing on current entitlement rather than past actions.
