Oath on a Specific Animal: Change of Description Does Not Nullify
Ruling on oath regarding a specific animal that changes its age designation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that an oath on a specific object remains binding despite a change in the object's description.

Core Idea
Oath on a specific object: the individual, not the attribute, binds.

Imagine you swear not to eat the meat of a particular lamb. Years later, that lamb is now a fully grown ram. Does eating its meat break your oath? In the Hanafi school, the answer is yes — because your oath was tied to the specific animal, not to its stage of life. This lesson explains the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of the oath
Look at the wording: 'I will not eat the meat of this lamb.' The demonstrative 'this' points to a specific individual animal, not to any lamb in general.
The key term is 'al-mushār ilayh' (the pointed-out object).
2
Distinguish between specific indication and generic description
The description 'lamb' is an attribute that can change, but the individual animal remains the same. The oath is legally attached to the individual, not to the attribute.
This is a fundamental Hanafi principle in oaths.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle
Since the oath is on the individual, a change in description (e.g., lamb → ram) does not change the identity of the object. Therefore, eating that animal's meat still fulfills the act prohibited by the oath.
The rational proof (dalīl ‘aqlī) is that the oath is tied to the specific entity.
4
Confirm the ruling
The text states: 'he breaks his oath' (ḥanth). The commentary adds that the oath was attached to the pointed-out thing, so the change is irrelevant.
This ruling is analogous to cases where a property changes form but the oath remains.

Scope of an Oath Not to Eat from a Date Palm
Intra-Hanafi ruling on oath scope and product transformation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the main Hanafi position on what an oath regarding a date palm covers, contrast it with Muhammad al-Shaybani's expanded view, and distinguish between included and excluded products.

Core Idea
The oath applies to fruit; only Muhammad includes core parts, but manufactured items are always excluded.

When someone swears not to eat from a specific date palm, the first question is: what exactly does the oath cover? The tree itself cannot be eaten, so the oath naturally refers to what the tree produces. But does 'produce' mean only the ripe fruit, or also the heart, the flower cluster, and the syrup? This lesson walks you through the Hanafi ruling on this question, including an important difference between the general view and Imam Muhammad's broader interpretation. You will also learn why manufactured items like vinegar and date wine fall outside the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the legal issue
The act is an oath (yamīn) not to eat from a specific date palm. The legal issue is the scope of the oath: what exactly is forbidden by the oath? Since the tree cannot be eaten, the oath must refer to something that comes from it.
2
State the main Hanafi position
The main position (without naming an imam) is that the oath applies only to the fruit (ثمر) of the palm. Eating the trunk or other parts of the tree does not break the oath.
3
Identify Imam Muhammad's expansion
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the oath also applies to the heart (جمار), the spadix (طلع), and unboiled syrup (دبس). These are all considered part of the tree's produce because they come from it.
The syrup must be unboiled; cooked syrup is excluded.
4
Distinguish excluded items
Vinegar (خل) and date wine (نبيذ) are not included even if made from dates, because they have undergone a transformation by human manufacture. They are no longer considered 'from the palm' in the relevant sense.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical example
If someone swears not to eat from a particular palm and then eats dates, they break the oath. If they eat the heart of the palm, only Muhammad's view would consider it a breach. If they drink date vinegar, according to all views, the oath is not broken.

Core Idea
An oath not to eat from a vine covers all its produce: grapes, raisins, and juice.

This lesson clarifies the scope of an oath made regarding a grapevine (karm). The ruling explicitly extends to grapes, raisins, and grape juice, based on an analogy with the date palm. Understanding this analogy is key to applying the ruling consistently across similar cases.

Guided Path

Identify the subject of the oath
The oath is about not eating 'from this grapevine' (min hadha al-karm). The word 'min' indicates origin or source.
The vine itself is not eaten; the oath concerns what the vine produces.
2
Determine the covered items

The ruling does not limit to fresh fruit only; dried and processed forms are included.

The date palm ruling serves as the asl (original case). By analogy, the farʿ (grapevine) takes the same ruling.

Core Idea
An oath on an edible animal binds only to its meat, not to its dairy or other products.

This lesson explores a specific Hanafi ruling on oaths: when someone swears not to eat from a sheep, the oath is interpreted narrowly to apply only to the meat, excluding milk, butter, cheese, and similar derivatives. The reasoning is based on the nature of the thing sworn upon—whether it is itself edible or not. Understanding this principle helps avoid unintended violations of oaths.

Guided Path

2

The text gives the reason: 'because the sheep is eaten in itself' (مأكولة في نفسها). This is the distinguishing characteristic.

Since milk, butter, cheese, etc. are not the sheep itself but products from it, they are not covered by the oath.

The date palm is not eaten in itself, so an oath on it applies to its fruit (e.g., dates). This contrast reinforces the principle.
The illa is jaliyyah (clear): edibility of the object.
5
Derive the general principle
For any edible animal (sheep, cow, goat), the oath on the animal itself covers only the meat. For non-edible plants (palm, tree), the oath covers what is produced.
This is a qiyas-based rule.

Oaths on Objects that Change Name and State: The Principle and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Mixed Forms
Rulings on oaths tied to changing names with an internal Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that an oath is tied to the name of an object, and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf on whether a mixture containing a small amount of the sworn substance breaks the oath.

Core Idea
An oath follows the name; if the name changes, the oath falls. But does a little bit of the sworn substance in a different-named thing break it?

In the Hanafi tradition, oaths are interpreted based on the common understanding of names. If a substance undergoes a complete transformation and acquires a new name, the oath on the original name is no longer binding. But what happens when the substance is only partially transformed, like a date that is mostly unripe but has a ripe tip? The great imams of the school differed on this subtle point, and their disagreement provides a rich study in legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle on name change
Locate the examples given: busr to rutab, milk to cheese, egg to chick, wine to vinegar. The underlying rule is that the oath is attached to the specific entity designated by its name; when the name ceases, the oath ceases.
The principle is: 'al-yaminu idha ta'allaqat bi-aynin baqiyat bi-baqa'i ismiha wa zilat bi-zawalihi'.
2
Recognize the exception for intention
Note that if the swearer intended the transformed product, the oath is broken because he made it binding on himself.
This shows that intention (niyya) can override the default linguistic interpretation.
3
State the dispute on mixed forms
When an oath is against a specific date type (e.g., rutab), and one eats a mixed form (e.g., busr mudhannab containing some rutab), the three imams diverge.
The dispute is between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (presence of any of the sworn substance breaks the oath) vs. Abu Yusuf (predominance is the criterion).
4
Analyze the reasoning of each side
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that the oath negates the act of eating rutab in any amount. Abu Yusuf argues that the mixed form is commonly called by the predominant name, so if the predominant part is not the sworn substance, the oath is not broken.
Abu Yusuf's position relies on customary usage ('urf) and the principle of ghalaba (predominance).
5
Apply the dispute to the four sub-issues
The passage lists four cases: (1) sworn against busr, ate busr mudhannab; (2) sworn against rutab, ate rutab with a little busr; (3) and (4) similar. Practice working through each.
In each case, determine the ruling according to each imam.

Commentary

It first presents the general principle: if an object changes its name due to a transformation, an oath attached to the original name is not broken by eating the transformed product. For example, busr (unripe dates) changing to rutab (ripe dates), milk changing to cheese or whey, eggs to chicks, and wine to vinegar. This is because the oath is tied to the specific entity designated by its name; when the name changes, the oath no longer applies. However, if the swearer intends the transformed product, he breaks his oath because he made it binding on himself. The passage summarizes that there are four sub-issues: (1) sworn against busr, ate busr mudhannab; (2) sworn against rutab, ate rutab with a little busr – according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, he breaks his oath; according to Abu Yusuf, he does not.

Core Idea
Riding a slave's animal does not break an oath not to ride the master's animal according to the two companions.

Oath on Entering a House: Roof, Hallway, and Doorway
Explaining what constitutes 'entry' for an oath, including roof, hall, and threshold.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the Hanafi criteria for what constitutes 'entry' (dukhul) in the context of an oath not to enter a house, including roof, hallway, threshold, and partial entry.

Core Idea
Roof and hallways are part of the house; threshold position matters; one foot usually not enough.

When someone swears not to enter a house, the exact definition of 'entry' becomes crucial. Is standing on the roof considered entry? What about the hallway or just one foot? This lesson examines the Hanafi position on these boundary cases, using analogies from i'tikaf and theft to clarify the rulings. The passage also highlights an intra-madhhab difference on partial foot entry, with the later reliance on al-Karkhi's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling
The matn states that standing on the roof or entering the drhliz (hallway/vestibule) breaks the oath because both are part of the house.
The roof is included by analogy with i'tikaf.
2
Understand the threshold rule
If one stands in the door arch (taq al-bab) such that closing the door leaves him outside, he does not break the oath; if inside, he does. This depends on the position relative to the door.
The threshold is a boundary: only being on the inside side counts.
3
Examine partial entry of the foot
The matn distinguishes between a depressed house (one steps down) and a level house. Inserting one foot into a depressed house breaks the oath; into a level house does not. Al-Karkhi holds that it never breaks the oath, and this is the relied-upon view.
The Karkhi view is considered 'sahih' (correct).
4
Analyze head and hand entry
Inserting the head without the feet, or taking something with the hand, does not constitute entry. This is argued by analogy with the thief's penalty: a thief is not amputated for such actions.
Custom ('urf) is also considered; in some places, roof access does not break the oath.
5
Synthesize the overall criteria
Summarize: roof, dihliz, and being inside the closed door break the oath; partial foot (head/hand) generally do not. The mufta bihi for foot entry is no break even if the house is depressed.
The analogy to i'tikaf and theft supports the rulings.

This passage provides detailed rules for what constitutes 'entry' (dukhul) in the context of an oath not to enter a house. The commentator begins by explaining that the matn ruling that standing on the roof or entering the dihliz (hallway/vestibule) breaks the oath because both are considered part of the house.
