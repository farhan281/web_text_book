Conditions and Disputes in Kaffarah Yamin
Detailed fiqh ruling with proof and disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid kaffarah fasting (consecutive days, night intention, ability timing) and distinguish the Hanafi positions from those of al-Shafi'i, Abu Yusuf, and Zufar.

Core Idea
Ability for kaffarah is judged at performance, not obligation; fasting requires three consecutive days and night intention.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayirah expands on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, detailing when fasting can replace feeding or freeing a slave for kaffarah yamin. It clarifies that ability is assessed at the time of performance, not when the oath was taken, and that consecutive fasting is a mandatory condition. You'll also encounter important intra-Hanafi disagreements and the Shafi'i counterposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three options and their order
The passage lists the three means of kaffarah: freeing a slave, feeding/clothing ten poor, or fasting three days. Note that fasting is only allowed if unable to do the first two, and ability is judged by ownership of specific items (slave, food, clothing) or surplus wealth.
The concept of 'yasar' (wealth) is defined as surplus beyond basic needs equal to the cost of expiation.
2
Understand the timing of ability
The text explicitly states that ability is considered at the time of performing the expiation, not when the oath was sworn. This leads to the ruling that a person who becomes poor after the oath may fast, but a person who becomes wealthy must use the higher options.
This is the Hanafi position, opposed by al-Shafi'i.
3
Analyze the condition of consecutive fasting
The word 'متتابعات' (consecutive) is taken as a condition (shart) for validity. Separating the days invalidates the fast. Contrast with the Shafi'i view that consecutive is recommended but not required.
Also note that intention at night is required for this fast.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi disputes and the mufta bihi
Abu Yusuf differs on giving kaffarah to poor dhimmis (prohibits), but the relied-upon view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) allows it. Zufar says qada is required if the fast is broken after becoming wealthy; the madhhab says no qada.
The text sides with the majority Hanafi view.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar details the expiation (kaffarah) for breaking an oath (yamin). It begins by stating the sequence of expiation: freeing a slave, feeding or clothing ten poor people, or fasting three consecutive days if unable to do the former. The author then addresses several subsidiary rulings.
- Giving the food of ten poor people to a single poor person in one day is insufficient because the requirement is to give to ten distinct individuals. This is compared to throwing all seven pebbles at the Jamrah at once, which only counts as one throw.
- If someone fasts while actually owning the means (slave or food) but has forgotten it, the fast is not valid by consensus; the Quranic condition is inability, and he is actually able.
- The expiation cannot be given to those prohibited from receiving zakat (parents, children, etc.), but according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the two), it may be given to poor dhimmis (non-Muslim subjects), unlike zakat. Abu Yusuf disagreed, equating it with zakat.
- Expiation cannot be used for shrouds or mosque construction.
- The commentary defines 'wealth' (yasar) for expiation as having surplus beyond basic needs equal to the cost of expiation. Ownership of the specific items (slave, clothing, food for ten) makes fasting invalid, even if he is in debt.
- The criterion of ability/poverty is evaluated at the time of performance, not when the oath was taken. Thus, if one was wealthy when the oath was taken but becomes poor later, he may fast; conversely, if poor at the time of obligation and later wealthy, he must use the other means. This Hanafi position differs from Shafi'i.
- Consecutive fasting is a condition (shart) for validity; separating the days invalidates it. Shafi'i allows separation.
- The fast requires intention (niyyah) at night. If one begins fasting and then becomes wealthy (yusr), it is recommended to complete the day; if he breaks it, no make-up is required according to the Hanafi school, contrary to Zufar.
- A poor woman's husband may prevent her from fasting this expiation, just as he can prevent any non-obligatory fast (since this fast is not originally obligatory like Ramadan).

Key Terms
اليسار والإعسار (al-yasār wa al-iʿsār): Wealth and poverty in the context of kaffarah: one is wealthy if he has surplus beyond his basic needs equal to the cost of expiation; otherwise poor.
التتابع (al-tatābuʿ): Consecutiveness: fasting three days without interruption. It is a condition for validity in the Hanafi school.
النية من الليل (al-niyyah min al-layl): Intention made during the night (before dawn) for the fast of kaffarah. This is a condition for its validity.

Oath Not to Speak: Reading Quran in Prayer is Not Speech
Reading Quran during prayer does not break an oath not to speak

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that reciting Quran and dhikr during prayer does not break an oath not to speak, cite the hadith proof, and apply the distinction between speech inside and outside prayer.

Core Idea
Reciting Quran in prayer is not speech for oath purposes.

This lesson examines a nuanced area of Hanafi fiqh: the intersection of oaths and prayer. When a person swears not to speak, does reciting Quran or performing dhikr during prayer break that oath? The answer hinges on a fundamental distinction between ordinary speech and ritual recitation, supported by a prophetic hadith and the principle of istihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is reciting Quran in prayer after swearing not to speak. The ruling: the oath is not broken (lam yahnath).
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the hadith: 'Indeed this prayer is not suitable for any of the speech of people; rather it is tasbih, takbir, and recitation of the Quran.' This hadith is from Sahih Muslim.
The hadith is ahad, so zanni al-thubut, but its meaning is clear.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith explicitly excludes ordinary human speech from prayer and defines the permissible actions as tasbih, takbir, and Quran recitation. Therefore, these actions are not considered 'kalam' (speech) in the legal context of oaths.
4
Expand to similar cases
The ruling extends to any dhikr in prayer (tasbih, tahlil, takbir). It also applies to reminding the imam through tasbih or recitation during prayer. However, the same actions outside prayer do constitute speech and break the oath.
5
Note the istihsan basis for praying itself
Even praying without any recitation (e.g., silent prayer) does not break the oath, by istihsan, because speech is not intended by the oath in that context.
Istihsan is a method of juristic preference based on custom or intention.

Commentary
This passage addresses oaths regarding speech. It states that if a person swears not to speak and then recites the Quran in prayer, the oath is not broken. The proof is the hadith that prayer is not for human speech but for tasbih, takbir, and Quran. Hence, the recitation is not considered 'kalam' (speech) in the legal sense. This extends to other dhikr like tasbih, tahlil, takbir in prayer. However, if these are done outside prayer, the oath is broken because they constitute speech. There is also a view based on custom that one is not called 'speaking' when reciting or glorifying. Additionally, if a person swears not to speak and simply prays, the oath is not broken by istihsan because speech is not intended. Finally, if a person swears not to speak to someone, and during prayer the imam makes a mistake and the swearer reminds him by tasbih or correcting recitation, the oath is not broken because such actions are not considered speech.

كلام (kalam): Speech that is considered breaking an oath; recitation and dhikr in prayer are excluded.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference based on custom or intention, leading to not breaking the oath.
تسبيح (tasbih): Glorification of Allah, e.g., saying 'Subhanallah'.
تهليل (tahlil): Saying 'La ilaha illallah'.
تكبير (takbir): Saying 'Allahu akbar'.

Oath Not to Speak: Prompting Imam in Prayer
Ruling on prompting imam during prayer under oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between speech and non-speech acts in prayer when evaluating oath violations, and apply the Hanafi criterion that acts which do not invalidate prayer are not considered speech.

Core Idea
Prompting the imam in prayer is not speech because it does not invalidate the prayer.

When a person swears not to speak to someone, but then prays behind that same person, a conflict may arise: does prompting the imam (by saying 'Subhānallāh' or correcting his recitation) break the oath? The Hanafi school answers by looking at the legal definition of speech. Since these acts are part of the prayer and do not nullify it, they are not considered speech, and the oath remains intact. However, if the same act occurs outside prayer, it is speech and breaks the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the oath
Note the oath is 'not to speak' and the act is prompting the imam during prayer (by tasbīḥ or tashīḥ or recitation).
2
Determine the legal definition of speech
In Hanafi fiqh, speech in prayer is defined by its effect: any action that invalidates prayer when done deliberately is considered speech. Acts that are part of the prayer itself (like dhikr, tilāwah, and salām) are not speech.
The proof: 'because speech nullifies prayer, and this does not nullify it.'
3
Apply the definition
Does prompting (tasbīḥ or opening) invalidate prayer? No, it is valid and part of the prayer. Therefore, it is not speech, and the oath is not broken.
4
Contrast with outside prayer
If the same prompting occurs outside prayer, it is considered ordinary speech and breaks the oath. This shows the context dependency.
5
Note the case of the imam giving salām
If the imam himself is the one who swore, and the person he swore about is behind him, the salām at the end of prayer is also not speech, by analogy to takbīr and recitation.

Oath Concerning Drinking from a River: The Literal vs Figurative Meaning
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on drinking from a river

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when drinking with a vessel breaks an oath, and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
An oath on drinking from a river is broken by direct drinking, but the Sahibayn also count indirect drinking with a vessel.

This passage from Kitab al-Ayman examines the intricacies of oaths related to drinking from a specific water source. Using the Tigris River as an example, the author introduces a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on whether using a vessel qualifies as 'drinking from' the river. Understanding this dispute is key to applying the Hanafi principle of haqiqah (literal meaning) vs majaz (figurative) in oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling from the matn
The matn states that if someone swears not to drink from the Tigris and then drinks with a vessel, he breaks his oath. This is the starting point.
2
Distinguish literal and figurative meanings
The sharh explains that 'kar'' (direct drinking) is the literal meaning (haqiqah), while 'ightiraf' (taking water with a vessel) is figurative (majaz). Both are commonly used.
3
Outline the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa restricts the oath to the literal meaning only; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) include both literal and figurative because both are common.
This dispute is the central analytical point of the lesson.
4
Apply to variant scenarios
The text considers cases like drinking from a branch river, drinking drawn water, and the effect of the particle 'min' for tab'eed (partiality). Note the by-consensus rulings.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The matn adopts the Sahibayn's view, making it the relied-upon position in the school.

Oath and Defective Coins: The Genus Principle
Ruling on oath with defective coins

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oath fulfillment with defective coins and apply the principle that a defect within the genus does not break an oath.

Core Idea
A defect that preserves the currency genus does not break an oath; a different substance does.

The Book of Oaths addresses a practical scenario: a debtor swears to repay a debt by a certain time, then pays with coins that turn out to be defective. Does the oath break? The Hanafi ruling distinguishes between defects that leave the coin within the genus (jins) of money and those that change its substance entirely. This distinction is based on the legal principle that a defect ('ayb) does not eliminate the genus, so the payment is considered valid for oath purposes. Understanding this ruling requires careful attention to the definitions of different coin types and the reasoning (ta'lil) that underpins the law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act: a person swears to pay a debt by a specific time, then pays. The ruling: if the coins are zuyuf (counterfeit but still silver), nabahrajah (off-mint silver), or mustahaqqah (claimed by another), the oath is not broken. If the coins are lead (rasas) or satuqah (brass plated with silver), the oath is broken.
The ruling is conditional on the type of defect.
2
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The 'illa (effective cause) is that a defect does not remove the genus (العيب لا يعدم الجنس). As long as the coin is still of the same genus as currency (silver or gold), the payment is valid. The creditor's acceptance is not required; even if he rejects them, the debt is considered paid and the oath is fulfilled.
This distinguishes between defects within the genus and those that change the genus entirely.
3
Distinguish between types of defects
Zuyuf are silver coins with adulteration (ghish) rejected by the treasury. Nabahrajah are silver coins struck outside the mint. Mustahaqqah are silver coins claimed by a third party. All are still silver (same genus). Lead and satuqah are different materials – lead is a base metal, satuqah is brass plated with silver – thus not of the currency genus.
Satuqah resembles silver but is not silver; it is called 'imitation' (المشبهة).
4
Apply the principle to analogous cases
Modern application: paying with damaged banknotes that are still legal tender does not break an oath (defect within genus). Paying with counterfeit notes that are not issued by the central bank (different genus) would break the oath. The key is whether the payment medium is still recognized as money of the same category.
The genus is determined by the material or the official recognition as currency.
5
Master the terminology
Learn the terms: zuyuf (زيوف), nabahrajah (نبهرجة), mustahaqqah (مستحقة), satuqah (ستوقة). Their definitions are essential for correct application.
These terms are specific to Hanafi fiqh on oaths and currency.

Rulings on Mixed Silk Fabrics and Thickness Requirement
Rulings for mixed silk fabrics and thickness condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the rulings for different compositions of mixed silk fabrics and identify the condition of thickness for permissibility in war.

Core Idea
Mixed silk with cotton is always permissible; pure silk is prohibited except in war with thick fabric.

This part of the passage provides detailed rules for mixed silk fabrics (ملحم) based on the warp and weft materials. It clarifies which combinations are permissible in peace and war, and emphasizes a critical condition: the fabric must be thick enough to provide actual protection. These distinctions help avoid common errors in applying silk regulations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fabric types
Note three cases: 1) warp silk + weft cotton, 2) weft silk + warp non-silk, 3) both warp and weft silk.
The terms سدى (warp) and لحمة (weft) are key.
2
State the ruling for each type
For case 1: permissible always (consensus). Case 2: prohibited outside war, permissible in war (consensus). Case 3: prohibited according to Abu Hanifa always; permissible in war according to Sahibayn.
The dispute on case 3 echoes the earlier pure silk dispute.
3
Apply the thickness condition
For pure silk in war (case 3, Sahibayn), the fabric must be thick (صفيق) to provide protection. Thin silk is prohibited by consensus.
Condition shared with all war exceptions: actual benefit.
4
Distinguish consensus vs. ikhtilaf
Cases 1 and 2 are by consensus (إجماع). Only case 3 has the intra-Hanafi dispute, but the thickness condition is agreed upon.
The consensus ensures these rulings are stable.
5
Apply to a real scenario
A man buying a shirt: if it has silk weft and cotton warp, it is permissible in all situations. If both warp and weft are silk, it is only permissible in war if thick.
The illa (effective cause) is protection, not mere silk presence.
